\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x938145.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 8, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 10, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Juan Smith was convicted of first-degree murder based on the testimony of a single eyewitness. During state postconviction relief proceedings, Smith obtained police files containing statements by the eyewitness contradicting his testimony. Smith argued that the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s failure to disclose those statements violated Brady v. Maryland, \n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83\n. Brady held that due process bars a State from withholding evidence that is favorable to the defense and material to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt or punishment. See id., at 87. The state trial court rejected Smith\xe2\x80\x99s Brady claim, and the Louisiana Court of Appeal and Louisiana Supreme Court denied review.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Brady requires that Smith\xe2\x80\x99s conviction be reversed. The State does not dispute that the eyewitness\xe2\x80\x99s statements were favorable to Smith and that those statements were not disclosed to Smith. Under Brady, evidence is material if there is a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable probability that, had the evidence been disclosed, the result of the proceeding would have been different.\xe2\x80\x9d Cone v. Bell, \n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 449\n\xe2\x80\x93470. A \xe2\x80\x9creasonable probability\xe2\x80\x9d means that the likelihood of a different result is great enough to \xe2\x80\x9cundermine[ ] confidence in the outcome of the trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Kyles v. Whitley, \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 419\n. Evidence impeaching an eyewitness\xe2\x80\x99s testimony may not be material if the State\xe2\x80\x99s other evidence is strong enough to sustain confidence in the verdict. United States v. Agurs, \n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 97\n\xe2\x80\x93113, and n. 21. Here, however, the eyewitness\xe2\x80\x99s testimony was the only evidence linking Smith to the crime, and the eyewitness\xe2\x80\x99s undisclosed statements contradicted his testimony. The eyewitness\xe2\x80\x99s statements were plainly material, and the State\xe2\x80\x99s failure to disclose those statements to the defense thus violated Brady. Pp. 2\xe2\x80\x934.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tReversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe State of Louisiana charged petitioner Juan Smith with killing five people during an armed robbery. At Smith\xe2\x80\x99s trial a single witness, Larry Boatner, linked Smith to the crime. Boatner testified that he was socializing at a friend\xe2\x80\x99s house when Smith and two other gunmen entered the home, demanded money and drugs, and shortly thereafter began shooting, resulting in the death of five of Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s friends. In court Boatner identified Smith as the first gunman to come through the door. He claimed that he had been face to face with Smith during the initial moments of the robbery. No other witnesses and no physical evidence implicated Smith in the crime.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe jury convicted Smith of five counts of first-degree murder. The Louisiana Court of Appeal affirmed Smith\xe2\x80\x99s conviction. State v. Smith, 797 So.\xc2\xa02d 193 (2001). The Louisiana Supreme Court denied review, as did this Court. 2001\xe2\x80\x932416 (La. 9/13/02), 824 So.\xc2\xa02d 1189; \n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1201 (2003)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSmith then sought postconviction relief in the state courts. As part of his effort, Smith obtained files from the police investigation of his case, including those of the lead investigator, Detective John Ronquillo. Ronquillo\xe2\x80\x99s notes contain statements by Boatner that conflict with his testimony identifying Smith as a perpetrator. The notes from the night of the murder state that Boatner \xe2\x80\x9ccould not . . . supply a description of the perpetrators other then [sic] they were black males.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 252\xe2\x80\x93253. Ronquillo also made a handwritten account of a conversation he had with Boatner five days after the crime, in which Boatner said he \xe2\x80\x9ccould not ID anyone because [he] couldn\xe2\x80\x99t see faces\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwould not know them if [he] saw them.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 308. And Ronquillo\xe2\x80\x99s typewritten report of that conversation states that Boatner told Ronquillo he \xe2\x80\x9ccould not identify any of the perpetrators of the murder.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 259\xe2\x80\x93260.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSmith requested that his conviction be vacated, arguing, inter alia, that the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s failure to disclose Ronquillo\xe2\x80\x99s notes violated this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Brady v. Maryland, \n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83 (1963)\n. The state trial court rejected Smith\xe2\x80\x99s Brady claim, and the Louisiana Court of Appeal and Louisiana Supreme Court denied review. We granted certiorari, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011), and now reverse.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder Brady, the State violates a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to due process if it withholds evidence that is favorable to the defense and material to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt or punishment. See 373 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 87. The State does not dispute that Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s statements in Ronquillo\xe2\x80\x99s notes were fa- vorable to Smith and that those statements were not dis- closed to him. The sole question before us is thus whether Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s statements were material to the determination of Smith\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. We have explained that \xe2\x80\x9cevidence is \xe2\x80\x98material\xe2\x80\x99 within the meaning of Brady when there is a reasonable probability that, had the evidence been disclosed, the result of the proceeding would have been different.\xe2\x80\x9d Cone v. Bell, \n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 449\n\xe2\x80\x93470 (2009). A reasonable probability does not mean that the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cwould more likely than not have received a different verdict with the evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d only that the likelihood of a different result is great enough to \xe2\x80\x9cundermine[] confidence in the outcome of the trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Kyles v. Whitley, \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 419, \n434 (1995)\n (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have observed that evidence impeaching an eyewitness may not be material if the State\xe2\x80\x99s other evidence is strong enough to sustain confidence in the verdict. See United States v. Agurs, \n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 97\n\xe2\x80\x93113, and n. 21 (1976). That is not the case here. Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s testimony was the only evidence linking Smith to the crime. And Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s undisclosed statements directly contradict his testimony: Boatner told the jury that he had \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o doubt\xe2\x80\x9d that Smith was the gunman he stood \xe2\x80\x9cface to face\xe2\x80\x9d with on the night of the crime, but Ronquillo\xe2\x80\x99s notes show Boatner saying that he \xe2\x80\x9ccould not ID anyone because [he] couldn\xe2\x80\x99t see faces\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwould not know them if [he] saw them.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 196, 200, 308. Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s undisclosed statements were plainly material.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe State and the dissent advance various reasons why the jury might have discounted Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s undisclosed statements. They stress, for example, that Boatner made other remarks on the night of the murder indicating that he could identify the first gunman to enter the house, but not the others. That merely leaves us to speculate about which of Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s contradictory declarations the jury would have believed. The State also contends that Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s statements made five days after the crime can be explained by fear of retaliation. Smith responds that the record contains no evidence of any such fear. Again, the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument offers a reason that the jury could have disbelieved Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s undisclosed statements, but gives us no confidence that it would have done so.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe police files that Smith obtained in state postconviction proceedings contain other evidence that Smith contends is both favorable to him and material to the verdict. Because we hold that Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s undisclosed statements alone suffice to undermine confidence in Smith\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, we have no need to consider his arguments that the other undisclosed evidence also requires reversal under Brady.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe judgment of the Orleans Parish Criminal District Court of Louisiana is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\nIt is so ordered.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Thomas, dissenting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court holds that Juan Smith is entitled to a new murder trial because the State, in violation of Brady v. Maryland, \n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83 (1963)\n, did not disclose that the eyewitness who identified Smith at trial stated shortly after the murders that he could not identify any of the perpetrators. I respectfully dissent. In my view, Smith has not shown a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable probability\xe2\x80\x9d that the jury would have been persuaded by the undisclosed evidence. United States v. Bagley, \n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 667, \n682 (1985)\n (opinion of Blackmun, J.). That materiality determination must be made \xe2\x80\x9cin the context of the entire record,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Agurs, \n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 97, \n112 (1976)\n, and \xe2\x80\x9cturns on the cumu- lative effect of all such evidence suppressed by the government,\xe2\x80\x9d Kyles v. Whitley, \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 419, \n421 (1995)\n. Applying these principles, I would affirm the judgment of the Louisiana trial court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe evidence presented at trial showed the following facts. On March 1, 1995, Larry Boatner and several friends gathered at Rebe Espadron\xe2\x80\x99s home in New Or- leans. Boatner and others were drinking and talking in the kitchen when Boatner heard the loud sound of a car without a muffler outside. As Boatner opened the kitchen\xe2\x80\x99s outside door to investigate the noise, armed men pushed their way through the door, demanding drugs and money. Tr. 153\xe2\x80\x93154 (Dec. 5, 1995). The first man though the door put a gun in Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s face and pushed him backwards. Id., at 154\xe2\x80\x93155. The men initially ordered Boatner and his friends to the floor, but then ordered Boatner to stand up. At that time, the man who had been the first one through the door placed his gun under Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s chin. Id., at 156\xe2\x80\x93157. When Boatner asked what the men wanted him to do, the first man struck Boatner on the back of the head with his gun, knocking Boatner back to the ground. Id., at 157\xe2\x80\x93158.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter hearing the commotion, Espadron emerged from a back bedroom, where she had been when the men entered the house. As Espadron opened an inside door leading to the kitchen, a man with a \xe2\x80\x9ccovering\xe2\x80\x9d over his mouth pointed his gun at her face and ordered her to the floor. Id., at 70\xe2\x80\x9371. Disregarding his command, Espadron ran back toward the bedroom, at which point the intruders opened fire. Id., at 71\xe2\x80\x9372, 159.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen the shooting was over, four people lay dead. A fifth person, 17-year-old Shelita Russell, was mortally wounded and died later at the hospital. Of those originally gathered in the house, the only survivors were Boatner, who suffered a severe laceration to his head from the first man\xe2\x80\x99s blow but was otherwise uninjured; Espadron, who escaped unharmed; and Reginald Harbor, who had remained in a back bedroom during the shooting. The police also found a man named Phillip Young at the scene. Young was alive but had suffered a gunshot wound to the head. Because Boatner, Espadron, and Harbor had never seen Young before, the police surmised that Young had been one of the perpetrators.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNew Orleans police officer Joseph Narcisse was a first responder to the scene of the shooting. He testified at trial that he encountered Boatner in the bathroom of Espadron\xe2\x80\x99s home, where Boatner was attempting to care for the laceration to his head. According to Narcisse, \xe2\x80\x9cMr. Boatner\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. had let inside the perpetrators and did see them.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 21 (Dec. 4, 1995). Narcisse further explained that Boatner \xe2\x80\x9chad a description\xe2\x80\x9d of the person that he saw, the details of which Narcisse could not recall. Id., at 32.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDetective John Ronquillo, the lead investigator of the shootings, testified that Boatner had described the first man through the kitchen door as having a \xe2\x80\x9cshort-type haircut,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ca lot of golds in his teeth,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cbrown-ski[n].\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Id., at 115 (Dec. 5, 1995). Ronquillo further testified that Boatner could describe no other perpetrator, but that Boatner had viewed the first man twice: once when the man initially came through the door and again when Boatner was ordered to stand up and the man held a gun to his chin. Id., at 117\xe2\x80\x93118.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRonquillo also testified that, during the four months following the shootings, Boatner viewed 14 six-person photograph arrays of potential suspects\xe2\x80\x94only one of which contained a picture of Smith. Id., at 89\xe2\x80\x93100. Three weeks after the crime, Ronquillo presented Boatner with one of the arrays that did not include a picture of Smith. Ronquillo recalled that Boatner noted that one man in the array had a \xe2\x80\x9csimilar haircut\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ca similar expression on his face\xe2\x80\x9d as the \xe2\x80\x9cgentleman that came into the house initially with the gun that [Boatner] confronted,\xe2\x80\x9d but that Boatner \xe2\x80\x9cwas positive this wasn\xe2\x80\x99t the individual.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 97; see also 5 Record 828. A few months later, Ronquillo presented Boatner with the array that included a photograph of Smith. Tr. 99\xe2\x80\x93101 (Dec. 5, 1995). Ronquillo testified that Boatner identified Smith \xe2\x80\x9cimmediately,\xe2\x80\x9d stating, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98This is it. I\xe2\x80\x99ll never forget that face.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 100. Of the 84 photographs that Boatner viewed, Smith\xe2\x80\x99s photograph was the only one that Boatner identified.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBoatner identified Smith again when he was called to the stand during Smith\xe2\x80\x99s trial. Boatner testified that Smith\xe2\x80\x99s face was the \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ame face,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 174, and that Smith\xe2\x80\x99s mouth was the \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ame mouth\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfull of gold,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., as that of the first man who came through the kitchen door on the night of the attack. Boatner also testified that Smith\xe2\x80\x99s hair at trial was \xe2\x80\x9cshaved on the sides\xe2\x80\x9d as it was during the crime, but that \xe2\x80\x9cthe top was a little bit lower\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the murders. Id., at 165. Boatner explain- ed that, during the attack, he had focused on the first man through the door\xe2\x80\x94who was unmasked\xe2\x80\x94but that he \xe2\x80\x9cdidn\xe2\x80\x99t notice\xe2\x80\x9d the faces of any of the other assailants or whether they were masked. Id., at 154. On cross-examination, Boatner testified that he had described the first man\xe2\x80\x99s build, haircut, and gold teeth jewelry to the police. Id., at 178.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBased on this evidence, the jury convicted Smith of first-degree murder. Following the conclusion of direct review, Smith petitioned the trial court for postconviction relief. Smith argued that the State had failed to disclose various police notes revealing favorable evidence material to Smith\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. As relevant here, those items include pretrial statements by Boatner; statements by victim Shelita Russell and Espadron\xe2\x80\x99s neighbor, Dale Mims; a pretrial statement by firearms examiner Kenneth Leary; statements by cosuspect Robert Trackling and Trackling\xe2\x80\x99s fellow inmate, Eric Rogers; and a statement by cosuspect Phillip Young. After holding a 4-day evidentiary hearing, the postconviction judge\xe2\x80\x94who had also presided over Smith\xe2\x80\x99s 2-day trial\xe2\x80\x94denied Smith\xe2\x80\x99s Brady claims.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLike the postconviction court below, I conclude that Smith is not entitled to a new trial under Brady. In my view, Smith has not established a reasonable probability that the cumulative effect of this evidence would have caused the jury to change its verdict.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSmith first identifies two undisclosed statements by Boatner, which the Court concludes are \xe2\x80\x9cplainly material.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3. First, a note by Ronquillo, documenting a conversation he had with Boatner at the scene, states that Boatner \xe2\x80\x9ccould not\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. supply a description of the perpetrators other th[a]n they were black males.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 Record 809. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSmith is correct that these undisclosed statements could have been used to impeach Boatner and Ronquillo during cross-examination. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe evidence showed that, notwithstanding Ronquillo\xe2\x80\x99s on-scene note, Boatner offered a description of the perpetrator at the scene. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn any event, Ronquillo\xe2\x80\x99s notes reflect that Boatner provided a description of the first perpetrator at the police station only a few hours after the shootings occurred. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOf course, had the jury been presented with Ronquillo\xe2\x80\x99s notes of Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s on-scene and March 6 statements, it might have believed that Boatner could not identify any of the perpetrators, but a possibility of a different verdict is insufficient to establish a Brady violation. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tInstead of requiring Smith to show a reasonable probability that Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s undisclosed statements would have caused the jury to acquit, the Court improperly requires the State to show that the jury would have given Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s undisclosed statements no weight. But Smith is not entitled to a new trial simply because the jury could have accorded some weight to Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s undisclosed statements. Smith\xe2\x80\x99s burden is to show a reasonable probability that the jury would have accorded those statements sufficient weight to alter its verdict. In light of the record as a whole\xe2\x80\x94which the Court declines to consider\xe2\x80\x94Smith has not carried that burden.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question presented here is not whether a prudent prosecutor should have disclosed the information that Smith identifies. Rather, the question is whether the cu- mulative effect of the disclosed and undisclosed evidence in Smith\xe2\x80\x99s case \xe2\x80\x9cput[s] the whole case in such a different light as to undermine confidence in the verdict.\xe2\x80\x9d Kyles, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 435. When, as in this case, the Court departs from its usual practice of declining to review alleged misapplications of settled law to particular facts, id., at 456 (Scalia, J., joined by Rehnquist, C.\xc2\xa0J., and Kennedy and Thomas, JJ., dissenting), the Court should at least consider all of the facts. And, the Court certainly should not decline to review all of the facts on the assumption that the remainder of the record would only further support Smith\xe2\x80\x99s claims, as the Court appears to have done here. Ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x934.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSuch an assumption is incorrect. Here, much of the record evidence confirms that, from the night of the murders through trial, Boatner consistently described\xe2\x80\x94with one understandable exception\xe2\x80\x94the first perpetrator through the door, that Boatner\xe2\x80\x99s description matched Smith, and that Boatner made strong out-of-court and in-court identifications implicating Smith. Some of the undisclosed evidence cited by Smith is not favorable to him at all, either because it is of no impeachment or exculpa- tory value or because it actually inculpates him. Because what remains is evidence of such minimal impeachment and exculpatory value as to be immaterial in light of the whole record, I must dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that the State violated Brady.\n\t\t\t
