\nSYLLABUS
\nOCTOBER TERM, 2007
\nMUNAF V. GEREN
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

MUNAF et\xc2\xa0al. v. GEREN, SECRETARY OF THE ARMY, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x931666.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 25, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 12, 2008*

\n

The Multinational Force\xe2\x80\x93Iraq (MNF\xe2\x80\x93I) is an international coalition force composed of 26 nations, including the United States. It operates in Iraq under the unified command of U. S. military officers, at the Iraqi Government\xe2\x80\x99s request, and in accordance with United Nations Security Council Resolutions. Pursuant to the U.\xc2\xa0N. mandate, MNF\xe2\x80\x93I forces detain individuals alleged to have committed hostile or warlike acts in Iraq, pending investigation and prosecution in Iraqi courts under Iraqi law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Shawqi Omar and Mohammad Munaf (hereinafter petitioners) are American citizens who voluntarily traveled to Iraq and allegedly committed crimes there. They were each captured by military forces operating as part of the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I; given hearings before MNF\xe2\x80\x93I Tribunals composed of American officers, who concluded that petitioners posed threats to Iraq\xe2\x80\x99s security; and placed in the custody of the U. S. military operating as part of the MNF-I. Family members filed next-friend habeas corpus petitions on behalf of both petitioners in the United States District Court for the District of Columbia.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Omar\xe2\x80\x99s case, after the Department of Justice informed Omar that the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I had decided to refer him to the Central Criminal Court of Iraq for criminal proceedings, his attorney sought and obtained a preliminary injunction from the District Court barring Omar\xe2\x80\x99s removal from United States or MNF-I custody. Affirming, the D. C. Circuit first upheld the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of habeas jurisdiction, finding that Hirota v. MacArthur, 338 U. S. 197, did not preclude review because Omar, unlike the habeas petitioners in Hirota, had yet to be convicted by a foreign tribunal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Meanwhile, the District Court in Munaf\xe2\x80\x99s case dismissed his habeas petition for lack of jurisdiction. The court concluded that Hirota controlled and required that the petition be dismissed for lack of jurisdiction because the American forces holding Munaf were operating as part of an international force\xe2\x80\x94the MNF-I. The D. C. Circuit agreed and affirmed. It distinguished its prior decision in Omar, which upheld jurisdiction over Omar\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition, on the grounds that Munaf had been convicted by a foreign tribunal while Omar had not.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The habeas statute extends to American citizens held overseas by American forces operating subject to an American chain of command. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the federal courts lack jurisdiction over the detainees\xe2\x80\x99 habeas petitions in such circumstances because the American forces holding Omar and Munaf operate as part of a multinational force is rejected. The habeas statute, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72241(c)(1), applies to persons held \xe2\x80\x9cin custody under or by color of the authority of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d The disjunctive \xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa72241(c)(1) makes clear that actual Government custody suffices for jurisdiction, even if that custody could be viewed as \xe2\x80\x9cunder \xe2\x80\xa6 color of\xe2\x80\x9d another authority, such as the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court also rejects the Government\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the District Court lacks jurisdiction in these cases because the multinational character of the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I, like the multinational character of the tribunal at issue in Hirota, means that the MNF-I is not a United States entity subject to habeas. The present cases differ from Hirota in several respects. The Court in Hirota may have found it significant, in considering the nature of the tribunal established by General MacArthur, that in that case the Government argued that General MacArthur was not subject to United States authority, that his duty was to obey the Far Eastern Commission and not the U. S. War Department, and that no process this Court could issue would have any effect on his action. Here, in contrast, the Government acknowledges that U. S. military commanders answer to the President. These cases also differ from Hirota in that they concern American citizens, and the Court has indicated that habeas jurisdiction can depend on citizenship. See e.g., Johnson v. Eisentrager, 339 U. S. 763, 781. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02. Federal district courts, however, may not exercise their habeas jurisdiction to enjoin the United States from transferring individuals alleged to have committed crimes and detained within the territory of a foreign sovereign to that sovereign for criminal prosecution. Because petitioners state no claim in their habeas petitions for which relief can be granted, their habeas petitions should have been promptly dismissed, and no injunction should have been entered. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9328.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The District Court abused its discretion in granting Omar a preliminary injunction, which the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit interpreted as prohibiting the Government from (1) transferring Omar to Iraqi custody, (2) sharing with the Iraqi Government details concerning any decision to release him, and (3) presenting him to the Iraqi courts for investigation and prosecution, without even considering the merits of the habeas petition. A preliminary injunction is an \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary and drastic remedy.\xe2\x80\x9d It should never be awarded as of right, Yakus v. United States, 321 U. S. 414, 440, and requires a demonstration of, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9ca likelihood of success on the merits,\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzales v. O Centro Esp\xc3\xadrita Beneficente Uni\xc4\x83o do Vegetal, 546 U. S. 418, 428. But neither the District Court nor the D. C. Circuit considered the likelihood of success as to the merits of Omar\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition. Instead, the lower courts concluded that the \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional issues\xe2\x80\x9d implicated by Omar\xe2\x80\x99s petition presented difficult and substantial questions. A difficult question as to jurisdiction is, of course, no reason to grant a preliminary injunction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The foregoing analysis would require reversal and remand in each of these cases: The lower courts in Munaf erred in dismissing for want of jurisdiction, and the lower courts in Omar erred in issuing and upholding the preliminary injunction. Our review of a preliminary injunction, however, \xe2\x80\x9cis not confined to the act of granting the injunctio[n].\xe2\x80\x9d City and County of Denver v. New York Trust Co., 229 U. S. 123, 136. Rather, a reviewing court has the power on appeal from an interlocutory order \xe2\x80\x9cto examine the merits of the case \xe2\x80\xa6 and upon deciding them in favor of the defendant to dismiss the bill.\xe2\x80\x9d North Carolina R. Co. v. Story, 268 U. S. 288, 292. In short, there are occasions when it is appropriate for a court reviewing a preliminary injunction to proceed to the merits; given that the present cases implicate sensitive foreign policy issues in the context of ongoing military operations, this is one of them. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Petitioners argue that they are entitled to habeas relief because they have a legally enforceable right not to be transferred to Iraqi authorities for criminal proceedings and because they are innocent civilians unlawfully detained by the Government. With respect to the transfer claim, they request an injunction prohibiting the Government from transferring them to Iraqi custody. With respect to the unlawful detention claim, they seek release but only to the extent it would not result in unlawful transfer to Iraqi custody. Because both requests would interfere with Iraq\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign right to \xe2\x80\x9cpunish offenses against its laws committed within its borders,\xe2\x80\x9d Wilson v. Girard, 354 U. S. 524, 529, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims do not state grounds upon which habeas relief may be granted. Their habeas petitions should have been promptly dismissed and no injunction should have been entered. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9328.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0Habeas is governed by equitable principles. Thus, prudential concerns may \xe2\x80\x9crequire a federal court to forgo the exercise of its habeas \xe2\x80\xa6 power.\xe2\x80\x9d Francis v. Henderson, 425 U. S. 536, 539. Here, the unusual nature of the relief sought by petitioners suggests that habeas is not appropriate. Habeas is at its core a remedy for unlawful executive detention. Hamdi v. Rumsfeld, 542 U. S. 507, 536. The typical remedy is, of course, release. See, e.g., Preiser v. Rodriguez, 411 U. S. 475, 484. But the habeas petitioners in these cases do not want simple release; that would expose them to apprehension by Iraqi authorities for criminal prosecution\xe2\x80\x94precisely what they went to federal court to avoid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The habeas petitioners do not dispute that they voluntarily traveled to Iraq, that they remain detained within the sovereign territory of Iraq today, or that they are alleged to have committed serious crimes in Iraq. Indeed, Omar and Munaf both concede that, if they were not in MNF\xe2\x80\x93I custody, Iraq would be free to arrest and prosecute them under Iraqi law. Further, Munaf is the subject of ongoing Iraqi criminal proceedings and Omar would be but for the present injunction. Given these facts, Iraq has a sovereign right to prosecute them for crimes committed on its soil, even if its criminal process does not come with all the rights guaranteed by the Constitution, see Neely v. Henkel, 180 U. S. 109, 123. As Chief Justice Marshall explained nearly two centuries ago, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he jurisdiction of the nation within its own territory is necessarily exclusive and absolute.\xe2\x80\x9d Schooner Exchange v. McFaddon, 7 Cranch 116, 136.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has twice applied that principle in rejecting claims that the Constitution precludes the Executive from transferring a prisoner to a foreign country for prosecution in an allegedly unconstitutional trial. Wilson, supra, at 529\xe2\x80\x93530; Neely, supra, at 112\xe2\x80\x93113, 122. Omar and Munaf concede that Iraq has a sovereign right to prosecute them for alleged violations of its law. Yet they went to federal court seeking an order that would allow them to defeat precisely that sovereign authority. But habeas corpus does not bar the United States from transferring a prisoner to the sovereign authority he concedes has a right to prosecute him. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9crelease\xe2\x80\x9d claim adds nothing to their \xe2\x80\x9ctransfer\xe2\x80\x9d claim and fails for the same reasons, given that the release they seek is release that would avoid transfer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is of course even more at issue here: Neely involved a charge of embezzlement and Wilson the peacetime actions of a serviceman. The present cases concern individuals captured and detained within an ally\xe2\x80\x99s territory during ongoing hostilities involving our troops. It would be very odd to hold that the Executive can transfer individuals such as those in the Neely and Wilson cases, but cannot transfer to an ally detainees captured by our Armed Forces for engaging in serious hostile acts against that ally in what the Government refers to as \xe2\x80\x9can active theater of combat.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 allegations that their transfer to Iraqi custody is likely to result in torture are a matter of serious concern but those allegations generally must be addressed by the political branches, not the judiciary. The recognition that it is for the democratically elected branches to assess practices in foreign countries and to determine national policy in light of those assessments is nothing new. As Chief Justice Marshall explained in the Schooner Exchange, \xe2\x80\x9cexemptions from territorial jurisdiction . . . must be derived from the consent of the sovereign of the territory\xe2\x80\x9d and are \xe2\x80\x9crather questions of policy than of law, \xe2\x80\xa6 they are for diplomatic, rather than legal discussion.\xe2\x80\x9d 7 Cranch, at 143, 146. In the present cases, the Government explains that it is the policy of the United States not to transfer an individual in circumstances where torture is likely to result and that the State Department has determined that the Justice Ministry\xe2\x80\x94the department which has authority over Munaf and Omar\xe2\x80\x94as well as its prison and detention facilities, have generally met internationally accepted standards for basic prisoner needs. The judiciary is not suited to second-guess such determinations. Pp.\xc2\xa023\xe2\x80\x9326.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(3)\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that, under Valentine v. United States ex rel. Neidecker, 299 U. S. 5, the Executive lacks discretion to transfer a citizen to Iraqi custody unless \xe2\x80\x9clegal authority\xe2\x80\x9d to do so \xe2\x80\x9cis given by act of Congress or by the terms of a treaty,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 9, is rejected. Valentine was an extradition case; the present cases involve the transfer to a sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s authority of an individual captured and already detained in that sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s territory. Wilson, supra, also forecloses petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 contention. A Status of Forces Agreement there seemed to give the habeas petitioner a right to trial by an American military tribunal, rather than a Japanese court, 354 U. S., at 529, but this Court found no \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional or statutory\xe2\x80\x9d impediment to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of its jurisdiction in light of Japan\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign interest in prosecuting crimes committed within its borders, id., at 530. Pp.\xc2\xa026\xe2\x80\x9328.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Multinational Force\xe2\x80\x93Iraq (MNF\xe2\x80\x93I) is an international coalition force operating in Iraq composed of 26 different nations, including the United States. The force operates under the unified command of United States military officers, at the request of the Iraqi Government, and in accordance with United Nations (U.\xc2\xa0N.) Security Council Resolutions. Pursuant to the U.\xc2\xa0N. mandate, MNF\xe2\x80\x93I forces detain individuals alleged to have committed hostile or warlike acts in Iraq, pending investigation and prosecution in Iraqi courts under Iraqi law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These consolidated cases concern the availability of habeas corpus relief arising from the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I\xe2\x80\x99s detention of American citizens who voluntarily traveled to Iraq and are alleged to have committed crimes there. We are confronted with two questions. First, do United States courts have jurisdiction over habeas corpus petitions filed on behalf of American citizens challenging their detention in Iraq by the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I? Second, if such jurisdiction exists, may district courts exercise that jurisdiction to enjoin the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I from transferring such individuals to Iraqi custody or allowing them to be tried before Iraqi courts?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude that the habeas statute extends to American citizens held overseas by American forces operating subject to an American chain of command, even when those forces are acting as part of a multinational coalition. Under circumstances such as those presented here, however, habeas corpus provides petitioners with no relief.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pursuant to its U.\xc2\xa0N. mandate, the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I has \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the authority to take all necessary measures to contribute to the maintenance of security and stability in Iraq.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. G to Pet. for Cert. in 07\xe2\x80\x93394, p.\xc2\xa074a, \xc2\xb610 (quoting U.\xc2\xa0N. Security Council, U.\xc2\xa0N. Doc. S/Res/1546, \xc2\xb610 (June 2004)). To this end, the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I engages in a variety of military and humanitarian activities. The multinational force, for example, conducts combat operations against insurgent factions, trains and equips Iraqi security forces, and aids in relief and reconstruction efforts.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0MNF\xe2\x80\x93I forces also detain individuals who pose a threat to the security of Iraq. The Government of Iraq retains ultimate responsibility for the arrest and imprisonment of individuals who violate its laws, but because many of Iraq\xe2\x80\x99s prison facilities have been destroyed, the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I agreed to maintain physical custody of many such individuals during Iraqi criminal proceedings. MNF\xe2\x80\x93I forces are currently holding approximately 24,000 detainees. An American military unit, Task Force 134, oversees detention operations and facilities in Iraq, including those located at Camp Cropper, the detention facility currently housing Shawqi Omar and Mohammad Munaf (herein-\nafter petitioners). The unit is under the command of United States military officers who report to General David Petraeus.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Shawqi Omar, an American-Jordanian citizen, voluntarily traveled to Iraq in 2002. In October 2004, Omar was captured and detained in Iraq by U. S. military forces operating as part of the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I during a raid of his Baghdad home. Omar is believed to have provided aid to Abu Musab al-Zarqawi\xe2\x80\x94the late leader of al Qaeda in Iraq\xe2\x80\x94by facilitating his group\xe2\x80\x99s connection with other terrorist groups, bringing foreign fighters into Iraq, and planning and executing kidnappings in Iraq. MNF\xe2\x80\x93I searched his home in an effort to capture and detain insurgents who were associated with al-Zarqawi. The raid netted an Iraqi insurgent and four Jordanian fighters along with explosive devices and other weapons.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The captured insurgents gave sworn statements implicating Omar in insurgent cell activities. The four Jordanians testified that they had traveled to Iraq with Omar to commit militant acts against American and other Coalition Forces. Each of the insurgents stated that, while living in Omar\xe2\x80\x99s home, they had surveilled potential kidnap victims and conducted weapons training. The insurgents explained that Omar\xe2\x80\x99s fluency in English allowed him to lure foreigners to his home in order to kidnap and sell them for ransom.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Following Omar\xe2\x80\x99s arrest, a three-member MNF\xe2\x80\x93I Tribunal composed of American military officers concluded that Omar posed a threat to the security of Iraq and designated him a \xe2\x80\x9csecurity internee.\xe2\x80\x9d The tribunal also found that Omar had committed hostile and warlike acts, and that he was an enemy combatant in the war on terrorism. In accordance with Article 5 of the Geneva Convention, Omar was permitted to hear the basis for his deten-\ntion, make a statement, and call immediately available witnesses.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to the review of his detention by the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I Tribunal, Omar received a hearing before the Combined Review and Release Board (CRRB)\xe2\x80\x94a nine-member board composed of six representatives of the Iraqi Government and three MNF\xe2\x80\x93I officers. The CRRB, like the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I Tribunal, concluded that Omar\xe2\x80\x99s continued detention was necessary because he posed a threat to Iraqi security. At all times since his capture, Omar has remained in the custody of the United States military operating as part of the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Omar\xe2\x80\x99s wife and son filed a next-friend petition for a writ of habeas corpus on Omar\xe2\x80\x99s behalf in the District Court for the District of Columbia. Omar v. Harvey, 479 F.\xc2\xa03d 1, 4 (CADC 2007). After the Department of Justice informed Omar that the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I had decided to refer him to the Central Criminal Court of Iraq (CCCI) for criminal proceedings, his attorney sought and obtained a preliminary injunction barring Omar\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cremov[al] \xe2\x80\xa6 from United States or MNF-I custody.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. in No. 07\xe2\x80\x93394, supra, at 59a. The order directed that

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cthe [United States], their agents, servants, employees, confederates, and any persons acting in concert or participation with them, or having actual or implicit knowledge of this Order \xe2\x80\xa6 shall not remove [Omar] from United States or MNF-I custody, or take any other action inconsistent with this court\xe2\x80\x99s memorandum opinion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States appealed and the Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit affirmed. Omar, 479 F.\xc2\xa03d 1. The Court of Appeals first upheld the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of habeas jurisdiction, finding that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Hirota v. MacArthur, 338 U. S. 197 (1948) (per curiam), did not preclude review. The Court of Appeals distinguished Hirota on the ground that Omar, unlike the petitioner in that case, had yet to be convicted by a foreign tribunal. 479 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 7\xe2\x80\x939. The Court of Appeals recognized, however, that the writ of habeas corpus could not be used to enjoin release. Id., at 11. It therefore construed the injunction only to bar transfer to Iraqi custody and upheld the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order insofar as it prohibited the United States from: (1) transferring Omar to Iraqi custody, id., at 11\xe2\x80\x9313; (2) sharing details concerning any decision to release Omar with the Iraqi Government, id., at 13; and (3) presenting Omar to the Iraqi Courts for investigation and prosecution, id., at 14.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Brown dissented. She joined the panel\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional ruling, but would have vacated the injunction because, in her view, the District Court had no authority to enjoin a transfer that would allow Iraqi officials to take custody of an individual captured in Iraq\xe2\x80\x94something the Iraqi Government \xe2\x80\x9cundeniably h[ad] a right to do.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 19. We granted certiorari. 552 U. S. ___ (2007).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Munaf, a citizen of both Iraq and the United States, voluntarily traveled to Iraq with several Romanian journalists. He was to serve as the journalists\xe2\x80\x99 translator and guide. Shortly after arriving in Iraq, the group\nwas kidnapped and held captive for two months. After\nthe journalists were freed, MNF\xe2\x80\x93I forces detained\nMunaf based on their belief that he had orchestrated the kidnappings.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A three-judge MNF\xe2\x80\x93I Tribunal conducted a hearing to determine whether Munaf\xe2\x80\x99s detention was warranted. The MNF\xe2\x80\x93I Tribunal reviewed the facts surrounding Munaf\xe2\x80\x99s capture, interviewed witnesses, and considered the available intelligence information. Munaf was present at the hearing and had an opportunity to hear the grounds for his detention, make a statement, and call immediately available witnesses. At the end of the hearing, the tribunal found that Munaf posed a serious threat to Iraqi security, designated him a \xe2\x80\x9csecurity internee,\xe2\x80\x9d and referred\nhis case to the CCCI for criminal investigation and\nprosecution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0During his CCCI trial, Munaf admitted on camera and in writing that he had facilitated the kidnapping of the Romanian journalists. He also appeared as a witness against his alleged co-conspirators. Later in the proceedings, Munaf recanted his confession, but the CCCI nonetheless found him guilty of kidnapping. On appeal, the Iraqi Court of Cassation vacated Munaf\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and remanded his case to the CCCI for further investigation. In re Hikmat, No. 19/Pub. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n/2007, p.\xc2\xa05 (Feb. 19, 2008). The Court of Cassation directed that Munaf was to \xe2\x80\x9cremain in custody pending the outcome\xe2\x80\x9d of further criminal proceedings. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Meanwhile, Munaf\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99s sister filed a next-friend petition for a writ of habeas corpus in the District Court for the District of Columbia. Mohammed v. Harvey, 456 F. \xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 115, 118 (2006). The District Court dismissed the petition for lack of jurisdiction, finding that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Hirota controlled: Munaf was \xe2\x80\x9cin the custody of coalition troops operating under the aegis of MNF\xe2\x80\x93I, who derive their ultimate authority from the United Nations and the MNF-I member nations acting jointly.\xe2\x80\x9d 456 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 122.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit affirmed. 482 F.\xc2\xa03d 582 (2007) (hereinafter Muraf). The Court of Appeals, \xe2\x80\x9c[c]onstrained by precedent,\xe2\x80\x9d agreed with the District Court that Hirota controlled and dismissed Munaf\xe2\x80\x99s petition for lack of jurisdiction. 482 F. \xc2\xa03d, at 583. It distinguished the prior opinion in Omar on the ground that Munaf, like the habeas petitioner in Hirota but unlike Omar, had been convicted by a foreign tribunal. 482 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 583\xe2\x80\x93584.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Randolph concurred in the judgment. Id., at 585. He concluded that the District Court had improperly dismissed for want of jurisdiction because \xe2\x80\x9cMunaf is an American citizen . . . held by American forces overseas.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Nevertheless, Judge Randolph would have held that Munaf\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition failed on the merits. Id., at 586. He relied on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in Wilson v. Girard, 354 U. S. 524, 529 (1957), that a \xe2\x80\x9csovereign nation has exclusive jurisdiction to punish offenses against its laws committed within its borders,\xe2\x80\x9d and concluded that the fact that the United States was holding Munaf because of his conviction by a foreign tribunal was conclusive. Ibid.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari and consolidated the Omar and Munaf cases. 552 U. S. ___ (2007).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Solicitor General argues that the federal courts lack jurisdiction over the detainees\xe2\x80\x99 habeas petitions because the American forces holding Omar and Munaf operate as part of a multinational force. Brief for Federal Parties 17\xe2\x80\x9336. The habeas statute provides that a federal district court may entertain a habeas application by a person held \xe2\x80\x9cin custody under or by color of the authority of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cin custody in violation of the Constitution or laws or treaties of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72241(c)(1), (3). MNF\xe2\x80\x93I forces, the argument goes, \xe2\x80\x9care not operating solely under United States authority, but rather \xe2\x80\x98as the agent of\xe2\x80\x99 a multinational force.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Federal Parties 23 (quoting Hirota, supra, at 198). Omar and Munaf are thus held pursuant to international authority, not \xe2\x80\x9cthe authority of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72241(c)(1), and they are therefore not within the reach of the habeas statute. Brief for Federal Parties 17\xe2\x80\x9318.[Footnote 2] \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States acknowledges that Omar and Munaf are American citizens held overseas in the immediate \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98physical custody\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of American soldiers who answer only to an American chain of command. Id., at 21. The MNF\xe2\x80\x93I itself operates subject to a unified American command. Id., at 23. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]s a practical matter,\xe2\x80\x9d the Government concedes, it is \xe2\x80\x9cthe President and the Pentagon, the Secretary of Defense, and the American commanders that control what \xe2\x80\xa6 American soldiers do,\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 15, including the soldiers holding Munaf and Omar. In light of these admissions, it is unsurprising that the United States has never argued that it lacks the authority to release Munaf or Omar, or that it requires the consent of other countries to do so.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We think these concessions the end of the jurisdictional inquiry. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument\xe2\x80\x94that the federal courts have no jurisdiction over American citizens held by American forces operating as multinational agents\xe2\x80\x94is not easily reconciled with the text of \xc2\xa72241(c)(1). See Duncan v. Walker, 533 U. S. 167, 172 (2001) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe begin, as always, with the language of the statute\xe2\x80\x9d). That section applies to persons held \xe2\x80\x9cin custody under or by color of the authority of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72241(c)(1). An individual is held \xe2\x80\x9cin custody\xe2\x80\x9d by the United States when the United States official charged with his detention has \xe2\x80\x9cthe power to produce\xe2\x80\x9d him. Wales v. Whitney, 114 U. S. 564, 574 (1885); see also \xc2\xa72243 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe writ . . . shall be directed to the person having custody of the person detained\xe2\x80\x9d). The disjunctive \xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa72241(c)(1) makes clear that actual custody by the United States suffices for jurisdiction, even if that custody could be viewed as \xe2\x80\x9cunder \xe2\x80\xa6 color of\xe2\x80\x9d another authority, such as the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s primary contention is that the District Courts lack jurisdiction in these cases because of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Hirota. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That slip of a case cannot bear the weight the Government would place on it. In Hirota, Japanese citizens sought permission to file habeas corpus applications directly in this Court. The petitioners were noncitizens detained in Japan. They had been convicted and sentenced by the International Military Tribunal for the Far East\xe2\x80\x94an international tribunal established by General Douglas MacArthur acting, as the Court put it, in his capacity as \xe2\x80\x9cthe agent of the Allied Powers.\xe2\x80\x9d 338 U. S., at 198. Although those familiar with the history of the period would appreciate the possibility of confusion over who General MacArthur took orders from, the Court concluded that the sentencing tribunal was \xe2\x80\x9cnot a tribunal of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court then held that, \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nder the foregoing circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d United States courts had \xe2\x80\x9cno power or authority to review, to affirm, set aside or annul the judgments and sentences\xe2\x80\x9d imposed by that tribunal. Ibid. Accordingly, the Court denied petitioners leave to file their habeas corpus applications, without further legal analysis. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government argues that the multinational character of the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I, like the multinational character of the tribunal at issue in Hirota, means that it too is not a United States entity subject to habeas. Reply Brief for Federal Parties 5\xe2\x80\x937. In making this claim, the Government acknowledges that the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I is subject to American authority, but contends that the same was true of the tribunal at issue in Hirota. In Hirota, the Government notes, the petitioners were held by the United States Eighth Army, which took orders from General MacArthur, 338 U. S., at 199 (Douglas, J., concurring), and were subject to an \xe2\x80\x9cunbroken\xe2\x80\x9d chain of U. S. command, ending with the President of the United States, id., at 207.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court in Hirota, however, may have found it significant, in considering the nature of the tribunal established by General MacArthur, that the Solicitor General expressly contended that General MacArthur, as pertinent, was not subject to United States authority. The facts suggesting that the tribunal in Hirota was subject to an \xe2\x80\x9cunbroken\xe2\x80\x9d United States chain of command were not among the \xe2\x80\x9cforegoing circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d cited in the per curiam opinion disposing of the case, id., at 198. They were highlighted only in Justice Douglas\xe2\x80\x99s belated opinion concurring in the result, published five months after that per curiam. Id., at 199, n.*. Indeed, arguing before this Court, Solicitor General Perlman stated that General MacArthur did not serve \xe2\x80\x9cunder the Joint Chiefs of Staff,\xe2\x80\x9d that his duty was \xe2\x80\x9cto obey the directives of the Far Eastern Commission and not our War Department,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cno process that could be issued from this court \xe2\x80\xa6 would have any effect on his action.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. in Hirota v. MacArthur, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1948, No. 239, pp. 42, 50, 51. Here, in contrast, the Government acknowledges that our military commanders do answer to the President.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if the Government is correct that the international authority at issue in Hirota is no different from the international authority at issue here, the present \xe2\x80\x9ccircumstances\xe2\x80\x9d differ in another respect. These cases concern American citizens while Hirota did not, and the Court has indicated that habeas jurisdiction can depend on citizenship. See Johnson v. Eisentrager, 339 U. S. 763, 781 (1950); Rasul v. Bush, 542 U. S. 466, 486 (2004) (Kennedy, J., concurring in judgment). See also Munaf, 482 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 584 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e do not mean to suggest that we find the logic of Hirota especially clear or compelling, particularly as applied to American citizens\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 585 (Randolph, J., concurring in judgment).[Footnote 3] \xe2\x80\x9cUnder the foregoing circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d we decline to extend our holding in Hirota to preclude American citizens held overseas by American soldiers subject to a United States chain of command from filing habeas petitions.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We now turn to the question whether United States district courts may exercise their habeas jurisdiction to enjoin our Armed Forces from transferring individuals detained within another sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s territory to that sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s government for criminal prosecution. The nature of that question requires us to proceed \xe2\x80\x9cwith the circumspection appropriate when this Court is adjudicating issues inevitably entangled in the conduct of our international relations.\xe2\x80\x9d Romero v. International Terminal Operating Co., 358 U. S. 354, 383 (1959). Here there is the further consideration that those issues arise in the context of ongoing military operations conducted by American Forces overseas. We therefore approach these questions cognizant that \xe2\x80\x9ccourts traditionally have been reluctant to intrude upon the authority of the Executive in military and national security affairs.\xe2\x80\x9d Department of Navy v. Egan, 484 U. S. 518, 530 (1988).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Omar, the District Court granted and the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit upheld a preliminary injunction that, as interpreted by the Court of Appeals, prohibited the United States from (1) effectuating \xe2\x80\x9cOmar\xe2\x80\x99s transfer in any form, whether by an official handoff or otherwise,\xe2\x80\x9d to Iraqi custody, 479 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 12; (2) sharing details concerning any decision to release Omar with the Iraqi Government, id., at 13; and (3) \xe2\x80\x9cpresenting Omar to the [Iraqi courts] for trial,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 14. This is not a narrow injunction. Even the habeas petitioners do not defend it in its entirety. They acknowledge the authority of the Iraqi courts to begin criminal proceedings against Omar and wisely concede that any injunction \xe2\x80\x9cclearly need not include a bar on \xe2\x80\x98information-sharing.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Habeas Petitioners 61. As Judge Brown noted in her dissent, such a bar would impermissibly \xe2\x80\x9cenjoin the United States military from sharing information with an allied foreign sovereign in a war zone.\xe2\x80\x9d Omar, supra, at 18.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin with the basics. A preliminary injunction is an \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary and drastic remedy,\xe2\x80\x9d 11A C. Wright, A. Miller, & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa72948, p. 129 (2d ed. 1995) (hereinafter Wright & Miller) (footnotes omitted); it is never awarded as of right, Yakus v. United States, 321 U. S. 414, 440 (1944). Rather, a party seeking a preliminary injunction must demonstrate, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9ca likelihood of success on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzales v. O Centro Esp\xc3\xadrita Beneficente Uni\xc4\x83o do Vegetal, 546 U. S. 418, 428 (2006) (citing Mazurek v. Armstrong, 520 U. S. 968, 972 (1997) (per curiam); Doran v. Salem Inn, Inc., 422 U. S. 922, 931 (1975)). But one searches the opinions below in vain for any mention of a likelihood of success as to the merits of Omar\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition. Instead, the District Court concluded that the \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional issues\xe2\x80\x9d presented questions \xe2\x80\x9cso serious, substantial, difficult and doubtful, as to make them fair ground for litigation and thus for more deliberative investigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Omar v. Harvey, 416 F. Supp. 2d 19, 23\xe2\x80\x9324, 27 (DC 2006) (internal quotation marks omitted; emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit made the same mistake. In that court\xe2\x80\x99s view, the \xe2\x80\x9conly question before [it] at th[at] stage of the litigation relate[d] to the district court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d 479 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 11. As a result, the Court of Appeals held that it \xe2\x80\x9cneed not address\xe2\x80\x9d the merits of Omar\xe2\x80\x99s habeas claims: those merits had \xe2\x80\x9cno relevance.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A difficult question as to jurisdiction is, of course, no reason to grant a preliminary injunction. It says nothing about the \xe2\x80\x9clikelihood of success on the merits,\xe2\x80\x9d other than making such success more unlikely due to potential impediments to even reaching the merits. Indeed, if all a \xe2\x80\x9clikelihood of success on the merits\xe2\x80\x9d meant was that the district court likely had jurisdiction, then preliminary injunctions would be the rule, not the exception. In light of these basic principles, we hold that it was an abuse of discretion for the District Court to grant a preliminary injunction on the view that the \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional issues\xe2\x80\x9d in Omar\xe2\x80\x99s case were tough, without even considering the merits of the underlying habeas petition.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What we have said thus far would require reversal and remand in each of these cases: The lower courts in Munaf erred in dismissing for want of jurisdiction, and the lower courts in Omar erred in issuing and upholding the preliminary injunction. There are occasions, however, when it is appropriate to proceed further and address the merits. This is one of them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our authority to address the merits of the habeas petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims is clear. Review of a preliminary injunction \xe2\x80\x9cis not confined to the act of granting the injunctio[n], but extends as well to determining whether there is any insuperable objection, in point of jurisdiction or merits, to the maintenance of [the] bill, and, if so, to directing a final decree dismissing it.\xe2\x80\x9d City and County of Denver v. New York Trust Co., 229 U. S. 123, 136 (1913). See also Deckert v. Independence Shares Corp., 311 U. S. 282, 287 (1940) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98If insuperable objection to maintaining the bill clearly appears, it may be dismissed and the litigation terminated\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Meccano, Ltd. v. John Wanamaker, N. Y., 253 U. S. 136, 141 (1920))). This has long been the rule: \xe2\x80\x9cBy the ordinary practice in equity as administered in England and this country,\xe2\x80\x9d a reviewing court has the power on appeal from an interlocutory order \xe2\x80\x9cto examine the merits of the case \xe2\x80\xa6 and upon deciding them in favor of the defendant to dismiss the bill.\xe2\x80\x9d North Carolina R. Co. v. Story, 268 U. S. 288, 292 (1925). Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he question whether an action should be dismissed for failure to state a claim is one of the most common issues that may be reviewed on appeal from an interlocutory injunction order.\xe2\x80\x9d 16 Wright & Miller, Jurisdiction and Related Matters, \xc2\xa73921.1, at 32 (2d ed. 1996).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Adjudication of the merits is most appropriate if the injunction rests on a question of law and it is plain that the plaintiff cannot prevail. In such cases, the defendant is entitled to judgment. See, e.g., Deckert, supra, at 287; North Carolina R. Co., supra, at 292; City and County of Denver, supra, at 136.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given that the present cases involve habeas petitions that implicate sensitive foreign policy issues in the context of ongoing military operations, reaching the merits is the wisest course. See Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer, 343 U. S. 579, 584\xe2\x80\x93585 (1952) (finding the case ripe for merits review on appeal from stay of preliminary injunction). For the reasons we explain below, the relief sought by the habeas petitioners makes clear under our precedents that the power of the writ ought not to be exercised. Because the Government is entitled to judgment as a matter of law, it is appropriate for us to terminate the litigation now.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The habeas petitioners argue that the writ should be granted in their cases because they have \xe2\x80\x9ca legally enforceable right\xe2\x80\x9d not to be transferred to Iraqi authority for criminal proceedings under both the Due Process Clause and the Foreign Affairs Reform and Restructuring Act of 1998 (FARR Act), div. G, 112 Stat. 2681\xe2\x80\x93761, and because they are innocent civilians who have been unlawfully detained by the United States in violation of the Due Process Clause. Brief for Habeas Petitioners 48\xe2\x80\x9352. With respect to the transfer claim, petitioners request an injunction prohibiting the United States from transferring them to Iraqi custody. With respect to the unlawful detention claim, petitioners seek \xe2\x80\x9crelease\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94but only to the extent that release would not result in \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful\xe2\x80\x9d transfer to Iraqi custody. Tr. of Oral Arg. 48. Both of these requests would interfere with Iraq\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign right to \xe2\x80\x9cpunish offenses against its laws committed within its borders.\xe2\x80\x9d Wilson, 354 U. S., at 529. We accordingly hold that the detainees\xe2\x80\x99 claims do not state grounds upon which habeas relief may be granted, that the habeas petitions should have been promptly dismissed, and that no injunction should have been entered.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Habeas corpus is \xe2\x80\x9cgoverned by equitable principles.\xe2\x80\x9d Fay v. Noia, 372 U. S. 391, 438 (1963). We have therefore recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cprudential concerns,\xe2\x80\x9d Withrow v. Williams, 507 U. S. 680, 686 (1993), such as comity and the orderly administration of criminal justice, may \xe2\x80\x9crequire a federal court to forgo the exercise of its habeas corpus power,\xe2\x80\x9d Francis v. Henderson, 425 U. S. 536, 539 (1976).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The principle that a habeas court is \xe2\x80\x9cnot bound in every case\xe2\x80\x9d to issue the writ, Ex parte Royall, 117 U. S. 241, 251 (1886), follows from the precatory language of the habeas statute, and from its common-law origins. The habeas statute provides only that a writ of habeas corpus \xe2\x80\x9cmay be granted,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72241(a) (emphasis added), and directs federal courts to \xe2\x80\x9cdispose of [habeas petitions] as law and justice require,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72243. See Danforth v. Minnesota, 552 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314). Likewise, the writ did not issue in England \xe2\x80\x9cas of mere course,\xe2\x80\x9d but rather required the petitioner to demonstrate why the \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary power of the crown\xe2\x80\x9d should be exercised, 3 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 132 (1768); even then, courts were directed to \xe2\x80\x9cdo as to justice shall appertain,\xe2\x80\x9d 1 id., at 131 (1765). The question, therefore, even where a habeas court has the power to issue the writ, is \xe2\x80\x9cwhether this be a case in which [that power] ought to be exercised.\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte Watkins, 3 Pet. 193, 201 (1830) (Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the outset, the nature of the relief sought by the habeas petitioners suggests that habeas is not appropriate in these cases. Habeas is at its core a remedy for unlawful executive detention. Hamdi v. Rumsfeld, 542 U. S. 507, 536 (2004) (plurality opinion). The typical remedy for such detention is, of course, release. See, e.g., Preiser v. Rodriguez, 411 U. S. 475, 484 (1973) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he traditional function of the writ is to secure release from illegal custody\xe2\x80\x9d). But here the last thing petitioners want is simple release; that would expose them to apprehension by Iraqi authorities for criminal prosecution\xe2\x80\x94precisely what petitioners went to federal court to avoid. At the end of the day, what petitioners are really after is a court order requiring the United States to shelter them from the sovereign government seeking to have them answer for alleged crimes committed within that sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s borders.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The habeas petitioners do not dispute that they voluntarily traveled to Iraq, that they remain detained within the sovereign territory of Iraq today, or that they are alleged to have committed serious crimes in Iraq. Indeed, Omar and Munaf both concede that, if they were not in MNF\xe2\x80\x93I custody, Iraq would be free to arrest and prosecute them under Iraqi law. See Tr. in Omar, No. 06\xe2\x80\x935126 (CADC), pp. 48\xe2\x80\x9349, 59 (Sept. 11, 2006); Tr. in Mohammed, No. 06\xe2\x80\x931455 (DC), pp. 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (Oct. 10, 2006). There is, moreover, no question that Munaf is the subject of ongoing Iraqi criminal proceedings and that Omar would be but for the present injunction. Munaf was convicted by the CCCI, and while that conviction was overturned on appeal, his case was remanded to and is again pending before the CCCI. The MNF\xe2\x80\x93I referred Omar to the CCCI for prosecution at which point he sought and obtained an injunction that prohibits his prosecution. See 479 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 16, n.\xc2\xa03 (Brown, J., dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[Omar] has not yet had a trial or even an investigative hearing in the CCCI due to the district court\xe2\x80\x99s unprecedented injunction\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Opposition to Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Emergency Motion for Injunctive Relief 18\xe2\x80\x9319, in Munaf v. Harvey, No. 06\xe2\x80\x935324 (CADC, Oct. 25, 2006))).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given these facts, our cases make clear that Iraq has a sovereign right to prosecute Omar and Munaf for crimes committed on its soil. As Chief Justice Marshall explained nearly two centuries ago, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he jurisdiction of the nation within its own territory is necessarily exclusive and absolute.\xe2\x80\x9d Schooner Exchange v. McFaddon, 7 Cranch 116, 136 (1812). See Wilson, supra, at 529 (\xe2\x80\x9cA sovereign nation has exclusive jurisdiction to punish offenses against its laws committed within its borders, unless it expressly or impliedly consents to surrender its jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d); Reid v. Covert, 354 U. S. 1, 15, n.\xc2\xa029 (1957) (opinion of Black, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] foreign nation has plenary criminal jurisdiction \xe2\x80\xa6 over all Americans \xe2\x80\xa6 who commit offenses against its laws within its territory\xe2\x80\x9d); Kinsella v. Krueger, 351 U. S. 470, 479 (1956) (nations have a \xe2\x80\x9csovereign right to try and punish [American citizens] for offenses committed within their borders,\xe2\x80\x9d unless they \xe2\x80\x9chave relinquished [their] jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d to do so).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is true with respect to American citizens who travel abroad and commit crimes in another nation whether or not the pertinent criminal process comes with all the rights guaranteed by our Constitution. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen an American citizen commits a crime in a foreign country he cannot complain if required to submit to such modes of trial and to such punishment as the laws of that country may prescribe for its own people.\xe2\x80\x9d Neely v. Henkel, 180 U. S. 109, 123 (1901).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The habeas petitioners nonetheless argue that the Due Process Clause includes a \xe2\x80\x9c[f]reedom from unlawful transfer\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cprotected wherever the government seizes a citizen.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Habeas Petitioners 48. We disagree. Not only have we long recognized the principle that a nation state reigns sovereign within its own territory, we have twice applied that principle to reject claims that the Constitution precludes the Executive from transferring a prisoner to a foreign country for prosecution in an allegedly unconstitutional trial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Wilson, 354 U. S. 524, we reversed an injunction similar to the one at issue here. During a cavalry exercise at the Camp Weir range in Japan, Girard, a Specialist Third Class in the United States Army, caused the death of a Japanese woman. Id., at 525\xe2\x80\x93526. After Japan indicted Girard, but while he was still in United States custody, Girard filed a writ of habeas corpus in the United States District Court for the District of Columbia. Ibid. The District Court granted a preliminary injunction against the United States, enjoining the \xe2\x80\x9cproposed delivery of [Girard] to the Japanese Government.\xe2\x80\x9d Girard v. Wilson, 152 F. Supp. 21, 27 (DC 1957). In the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, to permit the transfer to Japanese authority would violate the rights guaranteed to Girard by the Constitution. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, and vacated the injunction. 354 U. S., at 529\xe2\x80\x93530. We noted that Japan had exclusive jurisdiction \xe2\x80\x9cto punish offenses against its laws committed within its borders,\xe2\x80\x9d unless it had surrendered that jurisdiction. Id., at 529. Consequently, even though Japan had ceded some of its jurisdiction to the United States pursuant to a bilateral Status of Forces Agreement, the United States could waive that jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x94as it had done in Girard\xe2\x80\x99s case\xe2\x80\x94and the habeas court was without authority to enjoin Girard\xe2\x80\x99s transfer to the Japanese authorities. Id., at 529\xe2\x80\x93530.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Likewise, in Neely v. Henkel, supra, this Court held that habeas corpus was not available to defeat the criminal jurisdiction of a foreign sovereign, even when application of that sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s law would allegedly violate the Constitution. Neely\xe2\x80\x94the habeas petitioner and an American citizen\xe2\x80\x94was accused of violating Cuban law in Cuba. Id., at 112\xe2\x80\x93113. He was arrested and detained in the United States. Id., at 113. The United States indicated its intent to extradite him, and Neely filed suit seeking to block his extradition on the grounds that Cuban law did not provide the panoply of rights guaranteed him by the Constitution of the United States. Id., at 122. We summarily rejected this claim: \xe2\x80\x9cThe answer to this suggestion is that those [constitutional] provisions have no relation to crimes committed without the jurisdiction of the United States against the laws of a foreign country.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Neely alleged no claim for which a \xe2\x80\x9cdischarge on habeas corpus\xe2\x80\x9d could issue. Id., at 125. Accordingly, the United States was free to transfer him to Cuban custody for prosecution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the present cases, the habeas petitioners concede that Iraq has the sovereign authority to prosecute them for alleged violations of its law, yet nonetheless request an injunction prohibiting the United States from transferring them to Iraqi custody. But as the foregoing cases make clear, habeas is not a means of compelling the United States to harbor fugitives from the criminal justice system of a sovereign with undoubted authority to prosecute them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9crelease\xe2\x80\x9d claim adds nothing to their \xe2\x80\x9ctransfer\xe2\x80\x9d claim. That claim fails for the same reasons the transfer claim fails, given that the release petitioners seek is release in a form that would avoid transfer. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 47\xe2\x80\x9348; App. 40 (coupling Munaf\xe2\x80\x99s claim for release with a request for order requiring the United States to bring him to a U. S. court); App. 123 (same with respect to Omar). Such \xe2\x80\x9crelease\xe2\x80\x9d would impermissibly interfere with Iraq\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive jurisdiction to punish offenses against its laws committed within its borders,\xe2\x80\x9d Wilson, supra, at 529; the \xe2\x80\x9crelease\xe2\x80\x9d petitioners seek is nothing less than an order commanding our forces to smuggle them out of Iraq. Indeed, the Court of Appeals in Omar\xe2\x80\x99s case took the extraordinary step of upholding an injunction that prohibited the Executive from releasing Omar\xe2\x80\x94the quintessential habeas remedy\xe2\x80\x94if the United States shared information about his release with its military ally, Iraq. 479 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 13. Habeas does not require the United States to keep an unsuspecting nation in the dark when it releases an alleged criminal insurgent within its borders.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, because Omar and Munaf are being held by United States Armed Forces at the behest of the Iraqi Government pending their prosecution in Iraqi courts, Mohammed, 456 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 117, release of any kind would interfere with the sovereign authority of Iraq \xe2\x80\x9cto punish offenses against its laws committed within its borders,\xe2\x80\x9d Wilson, supra, at 529. This point becomes clear given that the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I, pursuant to its U.\xc2\xa0N. mandate, is authorized to \xe2\x80\x9ctake all necessary measures to contribute to the maintenance of security and stability in Iraq,\xe2\x80\x9d App. G to Pet. for Cert. in No. 07\xe2\x80\x93394, p. 74a, \xc2\xb610, and specifically to provide for the \xe2\x80\x9cinternment [of individuals in Iraq] where this is necessary for imperative reasons of security,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 86a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While the Iraqi Government is ultimately \xe2\x80\x9cresponsible for [the] arrest, detention and imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d of individuals who violate its laws, S.\xc2\xa0C. Res. 1790, Annex I, \xc2\xb64, p.\xc2\xa06, U.\xc2\xa0N. Doc. S/RES/1790 (Dec. 18, 2007), the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I maintains physical custody of individuals like Munaf and Omar while their cases are being heard by the CCCI, Mohammed, supra, at 117. Indeed, Munaf is currently held at Camp Cropper pursuant to the express order of the Iraqi Courts. See In re Hikmat, No. 19/Pub. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n/2007, at 5 (directing that Munaf \xe2\x80\x9cremain in custody pending the outcome\xe2\x80\x9d of further Iraqi proceedings). As that court order makes clear, MNF\xe2\x80\x93I detention is an integral part of the Iraqi system of criminal justice. MNF\xe2\x80\x93I forces augment the Iraqi Government\xe2\x80\x99s peacekeeping efforts by functioning, in essence, as its jailor. Any requirement that the MNF\xe2\x80\x93I release a detainee would, in effect, impose a release order on the Iraqi Government.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The habeas petitioners acknowledge that some interference with a foreign criminal system is too much. They concede that \xe2\x80\x9cit is axiomatic that an American court does not provide collateral review of proceedings in a foreign tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Habeas Petitioners 39 (citing Republic of Austria v. Altmann, 541 U. S. 677, 700 (2004)). We agree, but see no reason why habeas corpus should permit a prisoner detained within a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s territory to prevent a trial from going forward in the first place. It did not matter that the habeas petitioners in Wilson and Neely had not been convicted. 354 U. S., at 525\xe2\x80\x93526; 180 U. S., at 112\xe2\x80\x93113. Rather, \xe2\x80\x9cthe same principles of comity and respect for foreign sovereigns that preclude judicial scrutiny of foreign convictions necessarily render invalid attempts to shield citizens from foreign prosecution in order to preempt such nonreviewable adjudications.\xe2\x80\x9d Omar, 479 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 17 (Brown, J., dissenting in part).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To allow United States courts to intervene in an ongoing foreign criminal proceeding and pass judgment on its legitimacy seems at least as great an intrusion as the plainly barred collateral review of foreign convictions. See Banco Nacional de Cuba v. Sabbatino, 376 U. S. 398, 417\xe2\x80\x93418 (1964) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98To permit the validity of the acts of one sovereign State to be reexamined and perhaps condemned by the courts of another would very certainly \xe2\x80\x9cimperil the amicable relations between governments and vex the peace of nations\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Oetjen v. Central Leather Co., 246 U. S. 297, 303\xe2\x80\x93304 (1918); punctuation omitted)).[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is of course even more at issue here: Neither Neely nor Wilson concerned individuals captured and detained within an ally\xe2\x80\x99s territory during ongoing hostilities involving our troops. Neely involved a charge of embezzlement; Wilson the peacetime actions of a serviceman. Yet in those cases we held that the Constitution allows the Executive to transfer American citizens to foreign authorities for criminal prosecution. It would be passing strange to hold that the Executive lacks that same authority where, as here, the detainees were captured by our Armed Forces for engaging in serious hostile acts against an ally in what the Government refers to as \xe2\x80\x9can active theater of combat.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Federal Parties 16.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Such a conclusion would implicate not only concerns about interfering with a sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s recognized prerogative to apply its criminal law to those alleged to have committed crimes within its borders, but also concerns about unwarranted judicial intrusion into the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s ability to conduct military operations abroad. Our constitutional framework \xe2\x80\x9crequires that the judiciary be as scrupulous not to interfere with legitimate Army matters as the Army must be scrupulous not to intervene in judicial matters.\xe2\x80\x9d Orloff v. Willoughby, 345 U. S. 83, 94 (1953). Those who commit crimes within a sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s territory may be transferred to that sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s government for prosecution; there is hardly an exception to that rule when the crime at issue is not embezzlement but unlawful insurgency directed against an ally during ongoing hostilities involving our troops.

\n

B

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners contend that these general principles are trumped in their cases because their transfer to Iraqi custody is likely to result in torture. This allegation was raised in Munaf\xe2\x80\x99s petition for habeas, App. 39, \xc2\xb646, but not in Omar\xe2\x80\x99s. Such allegations are of course a matter of serious concern, but in the present context that concern is to be addressed by the political branches, not the judiciary. See M. Bassiouni, International Extradition: United States Law and Practice 921 (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cHabeas corpus has been held not to be a valid means of inquiry into the treatment the relator is anticipated to receive in the requesting state\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This conclusion is reflected in the cases already cited. Even with respect to claims that detainees would be denied constitutional rights if transferred, we have recognized that it is for the political branches, not the judiciary, to assess practices in foreign countries and to determine national policy in light of those assessments. Thus, the Court in Neely concluded that an American citizen who \xe2\x80\x9ccommits a crime in a foreign country\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccannot complain if required to submit to such modes of trial and to such punishment as the laws of that country may prescribe for its own people,\xe2\x80\x9d but went on to explain that this was true \xe2\x80\x9cunless a different mode be provided for by treaty stipulation between that country and the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 180 U. S., at 123. Diplomacy was the means of addressing the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s concerns.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By the same token, while the Court in Wilson stated the general principle that a \xe2\x80\x9csovereign nation has exclusive jurisdiction to punish offenses against its laws committed within its borders,\xe2\x80\x9d it recognized that this rule could be altered by diplomatic agreement in light of particular concerns\xe2\x80\x94as it was in that case\xe2\x80\x94and by a decision of the Executive to waive jurisdiction granted under that agreement\xe2\x80\x94as it was in that case. 354 U. S., at 529. See also Kinsella, 351 U. S., at 479 (alteration of jurisdictional rule through \xe2\x80\x9ccarefully drawn agreements\xe2\x80\x9d). This recognition that it is the political branches that bear responsibility for creating exceptions to the general rule is nothing new; as Chief Justice Marshall explained in the Schooner Exchange, \xe2\x80\x9cexemptions from territorial jurisdiction \xe2\x80\xa6 must be derived from the consent of the sovereign of the territory\xe2\x80\x9d and are \xe2\x80\x9crather questions of policy than of law, that they are for diplomatic, rather than legal discussion.\xe2\x80\x9d 7 Cranch, at 143, 146. The present concerns are of the same nature as the loss of constitutional rights alleged in Wilson and Neely, and are governed by the same principles.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Executive Branch may, of course, decline to surrender a detainee for many reasons, including humanitarian ones. Petitioners here allege only the possibility of mistreatment in a prison facility; this is not a more extreme case in which the Executive has determined that a detainee is likely to be tortured but decides to transfer him anyway. Indeed, the Solicitor General states that it is the policy of the United States not to transfer an individual in circumstances where torture is likely to result. Brief for Federal Parties 47; Reply Brief for Federal Parties 23. In these cases the United States explains that, although it remains concerned about torture among some sectors of the Iraqi Government, the State Department has determined that the Justice Ministry\xe2\x80\x94the department that would have authority over Munaf and Omar\xe2\x80\x94as well as its prison and detention facilities have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98generally met internationally accepted standards for basic prisoner needs.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Solicitor General explains that such determinations are based on \xe2\x80\x9cthe Executive\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of the foreign country\xe2\x80\x99s legal system and \xe2\x80\xa6 the Executive[\xe2\x80\x99s] \xe2\x80\xa6 ability to obtain foreign assurances it considers reliable.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Federal Parties 47.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Judiciary is not suited to second-guess such determinations\xe2\x80\x94determinations that would require federal courts to pass judgment on foreign justice systems and undermine the Government\xe2\x80\x99s ability to speak with one voice in this area. See The Federalist No. 42, p. 279 (J. Cooke ed. 1961) (J. Madison) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf we are to be one nation in any respect, it clearly ought to be in respect to other nations\xe2\x80\x9d). In contrast, the political branches are well situated to consider sensitive foreign policy issues, such as whether there is a serious prospect of torture at the hands of an ally, and what to do about it if there is. As Judge Brown noted, \xe2\x80\x9cwe need not assume the political branches are oblivious to these concerns. Indeed, the other branches possess significant diplomatic tools and leverage the judiciary lacks.\xe2\x80\x9d 479 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 20, n.\xc2\xa06 (dissenting opinion).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners briefly argue that their claims of potential torture may not be readily dismissed on the basis of these principles because the FARR Act prohibits transfer when torture may result. Brief for Habeas Petitioners 51\xe2\x80\x9352. Neither petitioner asserted a FARR Act claim in his petition for habeas, and the Act was not raised in any of the certiorari filings before this Court. Even in their merits brief in this Court, the habeas petitioners hardly discuss the issue. Id., at 17, 51\xe2\x80\x9352, 57\xe2\x80\x9358. The Government treats the issue in kind. Reply Brief for Federal Parties 24\xe2\x80\x9326. Under such circumstances we will not consider the question.[Footnote 6]

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the habeas petitioners raise the additional argument that the United States may not transfer a detainee to Iraqi custody, not because it would be unconstitutional to do so, but because the \xe2\x80\x9c[G]overnment may not transfer a citizen without legal authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Habeas Petitioners 54. The United States, they claim, bears the burden of \xe2\x80\x9cidentify[ing] a treaty or statute that permits it to transfer the[m] to Iraqi custody.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 49.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The habeas petitioners rely prominently on Valentine v. United States ex rel. Neidecker, 299 U. S. 5 (1936), where we ruled that the Executive may not extradite a person held within the United States unless \xe2\x80\x9clegal authority\xe2\x80\x9d to do so \xe2\x80\x9cis given by act of Congress or by the terms of a treaty,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 9. But Valentine is readily distinguishable. It involved the extradition of an individual from the United States; this is not an extradition case, but one involving the transfer to a sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s authority of an individual captured and already detained in that sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s territory. In the extradition context, when a \xe2\x80\x9cfugitive criminal\xe2\x80\x9d is found within the United States, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98there is no authority vested in any department of the government to seize [him] and surrender him to a foreign power,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in the absence of a pertinent constitutional or legislative provision. Ibid. But Omar and Munaf voluntarily traveled to Iraq and are being held there. They are therefore subject to the territorial jurisdiction of that sovereign, not of the United States. Moreover, as we have explained, the petitioners are being held by the United States, acting as part of MNF\xe2\x80\x93I, at the request of and on behalf of the Iraqi Government. It would be more than odd if the Government had no authority to transfer them to the very sovereign on whose behalf, and within whose territory, they are being detained.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The habeas petitioners further contend that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Wilson supports their argument that the Executive lacks the discretion to transfer a citizen absent a treaty or statute. Brief for Habeas Petitioners 54\xe2\x80\x9355. Quite the opposite. Wilson forecloses it. The only \xe2\x80\x9cauthority\xe2\x80\x9d at issue in Wilson\xe2\x80\x94a Status of Forces Agreement\xe2\x80\x94seemed to give the habeas petitioner in that case a right to be tried by an American military tribunal, not a Japanese court. 354 U. S., at 529. Nevertheless, in light of the background principle that Japan had a sovereign interest in prosecuting crimes committed within its borders, this Court found no \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional or statutory\xe2\x80\x9d impediment to the United States\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of its jurisdiction under the agreement. Id., at 530.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Munaf and Omar are alleged to have committed hostile and warlike acts within the sovereign territory of Iraq during ongoing hostilities there. Pending their criminal prosecution for those offenses, Munaf and Omar are being held in Iraq by American forces operating pursuant to a U.\xc2\xa0N. Mandate and at the request of the Iraqi Government. Petitioners concede that Iraq has a sovereign right to prosecute them for alleged violations of its law. Yet they went to federal court seeking an order that would allow them to defeat precisely that sovereign authority. Habeas corpus does not require the United States to shelter such fugitives from the criminal justice system of the sovereign with authority to prosecute them.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For all the reasons given above, petitioners state no claim in their habeas petitions for which relief can be granted, and those petitions should have been promptly dismissed. The judgments below and the injunction entered against the United States are vacated, and the cases are remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.
