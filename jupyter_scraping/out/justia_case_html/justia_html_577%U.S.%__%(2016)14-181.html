GOBEILLE, CHAIR OF THE VERMONT GREEN MOUNTAIN CARE BOARD v. LIBERTY MUTUAL INSURANCE CO.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93181.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 2, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 1, 2016

\n

Vermont law requires certain entities, including health insurers, to report payments relating to health care claims and other information relating to health care services to a state agency for compilation in an all-inclusive health care database. Respondent Liberty Mutual Insurance Company\xe2\x80\x99s health plan (Plan), which provides benefits in all 50 States, is an \xe2\x80\x9cemployee welfare benefit plan\xe2\x80\x9d under the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA). The Plan\xe2\x80\x99s third-party administrator, Blue Cross Blue Shield of Massachusetts, Inc. (Blue Cross), which is subject to Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s disclosure statute, was ordered to transmit its files on eligibility, medical claims, and pharmacy claims for the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s Vermont members. Respondent, concerned that the disclosure of such confidential information might violate its fiduciary duties, instructed Blue Cross not to comply and filed suit, seeking a declaration that ERISA pre-empts application of Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s statute and regulation to the Plan and an injunction prohibiting Vermont from trying to acquire data about the Plan or its members. The District Court granted summary judgment to Vermont, but the Second Circuit reversed, concluding that Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s reporting scheme is pre-empted by ERISA.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0ERISA pre-empts Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s statute as applied to ERISA plans. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0ERISA expressly pre-empts \xe2\x80\x9cany and all State laws insofar as they may now or hereafter relate to any employee benefit plan.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71144(a). As relevant here, the clause pre-empts a state law that has an impermissible \xe2\x80\x9cconnection with\xe2\x80\x9d ERISA plans, i.e., a law that governs, or interferes with the uniformity of, plan administration. Egelhoff v. Egelhoff,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 141\n. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The considerations relevant to the determination whether an impermissible connection exists\xe2\x80\x94ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s objectives \xe2\x80\x9cas a guide to the scope of the state law that Congress understood would survive,\xe2\x80\x9d New York State Conference of Blue Cross & Blue Shield Plans v. Travelers Ins. Co.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 645\n, and \xe2\x80\x9cthe nature of\xe2\x80\x9d the state law\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ceffect .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. on ERISA plans,\xe2\x80\x9d California Div. of Labor Standards Enforcement v. Dillingham Constr., N. A., Inc.,\n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 316\n\xe2\x80\x94lead to the conclusion that Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s regime, as applied to ERISA plans, is pre-empted. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0ERISA seeks to make the benefits promised by an employer more secure by mandating certain oversight systems and other standard procedures, Travelers, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 651, and those systems and procedures are intended to be uniform, id., at 656. ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s extensive reporting, disclosure, and recordkeeping requirements are central to, and an essential part of, this uniform plan administration system. Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s law and regulation, however, also govern plan reporting, disclosure, and recordkeeping. Pre-emption is necessary in order to prevent multiple jurisdictions from imposing differing, or even parallel, regulations, creating wasteful administrative costs and threatening to subject plans to wide-ranging liability. ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s uniform rule design also makes clear that it is the Secretary of Labor, not the separate States, that is authorized to decide whether to exempt plans from ERISA reporting requirements or to require ERISA plans to report data such as that sought by Vermont. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s counterarguments are unpersuasive. Vermont argues that respondent has not shown that the State scheme has caused it to suffer economic costs, but respondent need not wait to bring its pre-emption claim until confronted with numerous inconsistent obligations and encumbered with any ensuing costs. In addition, the fact that ERISA and the state reporting scheme have different objectives does not transform Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s direct regulation of a fundamental ERISA function into an innocuous and peripheral set of additional rules. Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s regime also cannot be saved by invoking the State\xe2\x80\x99s traditional power to regulate in the area of public health. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-existing reporting, disclosure, and recordkeeping provisions maintain their pre-emptive force regardless of whether the new Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act\xe2\x80\x99s reporting obligations also pre-empt state law. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

746 F.\xc2\xa03d 497, affirmed.

ALFRED GOBEILLE, in his official capacity as chair of the vermont green mountaincare board, PETITIONER v. LIBERTYMUTUAL INSURANCE COMPANY

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

[March 1, 2016]

\n

Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case presents a challenge to the applicability of a state law requiring disclosure of payments relating to health care claims and other information relating to health care services. Vermont enacted the statute so it could maintain an all-inclusive health care database.Vt. Stat. Ann., Tit. 18, \xc2\xa79410(a)(1) (2015 Cum. Supp.) (\xc2\xa0V.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A.). The state law, by its terms, applies to health plans established by employers and regulated by the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA),\n\n88Stat.\n829, as amended,\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001 et\xc2\xa0seq. The question before the Court is whether ERISA pre-empts the Vermont statute as it applies to ERISA plans.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Vermont requires certain public and private entities that provide and pay for health care services to report information to a state agency. The reported information is compiled into a database reflecting \xe2\x80\x9call health care utilization, costs, and resources in [\xc2\xa0Vermont], and health care utilization and costs for services provided to Vermont residents in another state.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 V. S. A. \xc2\xa79410(b). A database of this kind is sometimes called an all-payer claims database, for it requires submission of data from all health insurers and other entities that pay for health care services. Almost 20 States have or are implementing similar databases. See Brief for State of New York et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 1, and n.\xc2\xa01.

\n

Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s law requires health insurers, health care providers, health care facilities, and governmental agencies to report any \xe2\x80\x9cinformation relating to health care costs, prices, quality, utilization, or resources required\xe2\x80\x9d by the state agency, including data relating to health insurance claims and enrollment. \xc2\xa79410(c)(3). Health insurers must submit claims data on members, subscribers, and policyholders. \xc2\xa79410(h). The Vermont law defines health insurer to include a \xe2\x80\x9cself-insured .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. health care benefit plan,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa79402(8), as well as \xe2\x80\x9cany third party administrator\xe2\x80\x9d and any \xe2\x80\x9csimilar entity with claims data, eligibility data, provider files, and other information relating to health care provided to a Vermont resident.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa79410(\xc2\xa0j)(1)(B). The database must be made \xe2\x80\x9cavailable as a resource for insurers, employers, providers, purchasers of health care, and State agencies to continuously review health care utili-zation, expenditures, and performance in Vermont.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa79410(h)(3)(B).

\n

Vermont law leaves to a state agency the responsibility to \xe2\x80\x9cestablish the types of information to be filed under this section, and the time and place and the manner in which such information shall be filed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa79410(d). The law has been implemented by a regulation creating the Vermont Healthcare Claims Uniform Reporting and Evaluation System. The regulation requires the submission of \xe2\x80\x9cmedical claims data, pharmacy claims data, member eligibility data, provider data, and other information,\xe2\x80\x9d Reg. H\xe2\x80\x932008\xe2\x80\x9301, Code of Vt. Rules 21\xe2\x80\x93040\xe2\x80\x93021, \xc2\xa74(D) (2016) (CVR),in accordance with specific formatting, coding, and other requirements, \xc2\xa75. Under the regulation, health insurers must report data about the health care services provided to Vermonters regardless of whether they are treated in Vermont or out-of-state and about non-Vermonters who are treated in Vermont. \xc2\xa74(D); see also \xc2\xa71. The agency at present does not collect data on denied claims, \xc2\xa75(A)(8), but the statute would allow it to do so.

\n

Covered entities (reporters) must register with the State and must submit data monthly, quarterly, or annually, depending on the number of individuals that an entity serves. The more people served, the more frequently the reports must be filed. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa74, 6(I). Entities with fewer than 200 members need not report at all, ibid., and are termed \xe2\x80\x9cvoluntary\xe2\x80\x9d reporters as distinct from \xe2\x80\x9cmandated\xe2\x80\x9d reporters, \xc2\xa73. Reporters can be fined for not complying with the statute or the regulation. \xc2\xa710; 18 V. S. A. \xc2\xa79410(g).

\n

B

\n

Respondent Liberty Mutual Insurance Company maintains a health plan (Plan) that provides benefits in all 50 States to over 80,000 individuals, comprising respondent\xe2\x80\x99s employees, their families, and former employees. The Plan is self-insured and self-funded, which means that Plan benefits are paid by respondent. The Plan, which qualifies as an \xe2\x80\x9cemployee welfare benefit plan\xe2\x80\x9d under ERISA,\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71002(1), is subject to \xe2\x80\x9cERISA\xe2\x80\x99s comprehensive regulation,\xe2\x80\x9d New York State Conference of Blue Cross & Blue Shield Plans v. Travelers Ins. Co.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 645,\n650 (1995)\n. Respondent, as the Plan sponsor, is both a fiduciary and plan administrator.

\n

The Plan uses Blue Cross Blue Shield of Massachusetts, Inc. (Blue Cross) as a third-party administrator. Blue Cross manages the \xe2\x80\x9cprocessing, review, and payment\xe2\x80\x9d of claims for respondent. Liberty Mut. Ins. Co. v. Donegan, 746 F.\xc2\xa03d 497, 502 (CA2 2014) (case below). In its contract with Blue Cross, respondent agreed to \xe2\x80\x9chold [Blue Cross] harmless for any charges, including legal fees, judgments, administrative expenses and benefit payment requirements, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. arising from or in connection with [the Plan] or due to [respondent\xe2\x80\x99s] failure to comply with any laws or regulations.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 82. The Plan is a voluntary reporter under the Vermont regulation because it covers some 137 Vermonters, which is fewer than the 200-person cutoff for mandated reporting. Blue Cross, however, serves several thousand Vermonters, and so it is a mandated reporter. Blue Cross, therefore, must report the information it possesses about the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s members in Vermont.

\n

In August 2011, Vermont issued a subpoena ordering Blue Cross to transmit to a state-appointed contractor all the files it possessed on member eligibility, medical claims, and pharmacy claims for Vermont members. Id., at 33. (For clarity, the Court uses \xe2\x80\x9cVermont\xe2\x80\x9d to refer not only to the State but also to state officials acting in their official capacity.) The penalty for noncompliance, Vermont threatened, would be a fine of up to $2,000 a day and a suspension of Blue Cross\xe2\x80\x99 authorization to operate in Vermont for as long as six months. Id., at 31. Respondent, concerned in part that the disclosure of confidential information regarding its members might violate its fiduciary duties under the Plan, instructed Blue Cross not to comply. Respondent then filed this action in the United States District Court for the District of Vermont. It sought a declaration that ERISA pre-empts application of Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s statute and regulation to the Plan and an injunction forbidding Vermont from trying to acquire data about the Plan or its members.

\n

Vermont filed a motion to dismiss, which the District Court treated as one for summary judgment, see Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa012(d), and respondent filed a cross-motion for summary judgment. The District Court granted summary judgment to Vermont. It first held that respondent, despite being a mere voluntary reporter, had standing to sue because it was faced with either allegedly violating its \xe2\x80\x9cfiduciary and administrative responsibilities to the Plan\xe2\x80\x9d or assuming liability for Blue Cross\xe2\x80\x99 withholding of the data from Vermont. Liberty Mut. Ins. Co. v. Kimbell, No. 2:11\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x93204 (D Vt., Nov. 9, 2012), p.\xc2\xa012. The District Court then concluded that the State\xe2\x80\x99s reporting scheme was not pre-empted. Although that scheme \xe2\x80\x9cmay have some indirect effect on health benefit plans,\xe2\x80\x9d the court reasoned that the \xe2\x80\x9ceffect is so peripheral that the regulation cannot be considered an attempt to interfere with the administration or structure of a welfare benefit plan.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 31\xe2\x80\x9332.

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit reversed. The panel was unanimous in concluding that respondent had standing, but it divided on the merits of the pre-emption challenge. The panel majority explained that \xe2\x80\x9cone of ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s core functions\xe2\x80\x94reporting\xe2\x80\x94[cannot] be laden with burdens, subject to incompatible, multiple and variable demands, and freighted with risk of fines, breach of duty, and legal expense.\xe2\x80\x9d 746 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 510. The Vermont regime, the court held, does just that. Id., at 508\xe2\x80\x93510.

\n

This Court granted certiorari to address the important issue of ERISA pre-emption. 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

II

\n

The text of ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s express pre-emption clause is the necessary starting point. It is terse but comprehensive. ERISA pre-empts

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cany and all State laws insofar as they may now or hereafter relate to any employee benefit plan.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71144(a).

\n

The Court has addressed the potential reach of this clause before. In Travelers, the Court observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f \xe2\x80\x98relate to\xe2\x80\x99 were taken to extend to the furthest stretch of its indeterminacy, then for all practical purposes pre-emption would never run its course.\xe2\x80\x9d 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 655. That is a result \xe2\x80\x9cno sensible person could have intended.\xe2\x80\x9d California Div. of Labor Standards Enforcement v. Dillingham Constr., N.\xc2\xa0A., Inc.,\n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 316,\n336 (1997)\n (Scalia, J., concurring). So the need for workable standards has led the Court to reject \xe2\x80\x9cuncritical literalism\xe2\x80\x9d in applying the clause. Travelers, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 656.

\n

Implementing these principles, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s case law to date has described two categories of state laws that ERISA pre-empts. First, ERISA pre-empts a state law if it has a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reference to\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ERISA plans. Ibid. To be more precise, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here a State\xe2\x80\x99s law acts immediately and exclusively upon ERISA plans .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or where the existence of ERISA plans is essential to the law\xe2\x80\x99s operation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , that \xe2\x80\x98reference\xe2\x80\x99 will result in pre-emption.\xe2\x80\x9d Dillingham, supra, at 325. Second, ERISA pre-empts a state law that has an impermissible \xe2\x80\x9cconnection with\xe2\x80\x9d ERISA plans, meaning a state law that \xe2\x80\x9cgoverns .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a central matter of plan administration\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cinterferes with nationally uniform plan administration.\xe2\x80\x9d Egelhoff v. Egelhoff,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 141,\n148 (2001)\n. A state law also might have an impermissible connection with ERISA plans if \xe2\x80\x9cacute, albeit indirect, economic effects\xe2\x80\x9d of the state law \xe2\x80\x9cforce an ERISA plan to adopt a certain scheme of substantive coverage or effectively restrict its choice of insurers.\xe2\x80\x9d Travelers, supra, at 668. When considered together, these formulations ensure that ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s express pre-emption clause receives the broad scope Congress intended while avoiding the clause\xe2\x80\x99s susceptibility to limitless application.

\n

III

\n

Respondent contends that Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s law falls in the second category of state laws that are pre-empted by ERISA: laws that govern, or interfere with the uniformity of, plan administration and so have an impermissible \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98connection with\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ERISA plans. Egelhoff, supra, at 148; Travelers, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 656. When presented with these contentions in earlier cases, the Court has considered \xe2\x80\x9cthe objectives of the ERISA statute as a guide to the scope of the state law that Congress understood would survive,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and \xe2\x80\x9cthe nature of the effect of the state law on ERISA plans,\xe2\x80\x9d Dillingham, supra, at 325. Here, those considerations lead the Court to conclude that Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s regime, as applied to ERISA plans, is pre-empted.

\n

A

\n

ERISA does not guarantee substantive benefits. The statute, instead, seeks to make the benefits promised by an employer more secure by mandating certain oversight systems and other standard procedures. Travelers, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 651. Those systems and procedures are intended to be uniform. Id., at 656 (ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption clause \xe2\x80\x9cindicates Congress\xe2\x80\x99s intent to establish the regulation of employee welfare benefit plans \xe2\x80\x98as exclusively a federal concern\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Alessi v. Raybestos-Manhattan, Inc.,\n\n451 U.\xc2\xa0S. 504,\n523 (1981)\n)). \xe2\x80\x9cRequiring ERISA administrators to master the relevant laws of 50 States and to contend with litigation would undermine the congressional goal of \xe2\x80\x98minimiz[ing] the administrative and financial burden[s]\xe2\x80\x99 on plan administrators\xe2\x80\x94burdens ultimately borne by the beneficiaries.\xe2\x80\x9d Egelhoff, supra, at 149\xe2\x80\x93150 (quoting Ingersoll-Rand Co. v. McClendon,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 133,\n142 (1990)\n); see also Fort Halifax Packing Co. v. Coyne,\n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n9 (1987)\n.

\n

ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s reporting, disclosure, and recordkeeping requirements for welfare benefit plans are extensive. ERISA plans must present participants with a plan description explaining, among other things, the plan\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility requirements and claims-processing procedures. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71021(a)(1), 1022, 1024(b)(1). Plans must notify participants when a claim is denied and state the basis for the denial. \xc2\xa71133(1). Most important for the pre-emption question presented here, welfare benefit plans governed by ERISA must file an annual report with the Secretary of Labor. The report must include a financial statement listing assets and liabilities for the previous year and, further, receipts and disbursements of funds. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71021(b), 1023(b)(1), 1023(b)(3)(A)\xe2\x80\x93(B), 1024(a). The information on assets and liabilities as well as receipts and disbursements must be provided to plan participants on an annual basis as well. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71021(a)(2), 1023(b)(3)(A)\xe2\x80\x93(B), 1024(b)(3). Because welfare benefit plans are in the business of providing benefits to plan participants, a plan\xe2\x80\x99s reporting of data on disbursements by definition incorporates paid claims. See Dept. of Labor, Schedule H (Form 5500)Financial Information (2015) (requiring reporting of \xe2\x80\x9c[b]enefit claims payable\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[b]enefit payment and payments to provide benefits\xe2\x80\x9d), online at http://www.dol.gov/ebsa/pdf/2015-5500-Schedule-H.pdf (as last visited Feb. 26, 2016).

\n

The Secretary of Labor has authority to establish additional reporting and disclosure requirements for ERISA plans. ERISA permits the Secretary to use the data disclosed by plans \xe2\x80\x9cfor statistical and research purposes, and [to] compile and publish such studies, analyses, reports, and surveys based thereon as he may deem appropriate.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71026(a). The Secretary also may, \xe2\x80\x9cin connection\xe2\x80\x9d with any research, \xe2\x80\x9ccollect, compile, analyze, and publish data, information, and statistics relating to\xe2\x80\x9d plans. \xc2\xa71143(a)(1); see also \xc2\xa71143(a)(3) (approving \xe2\x80\x9cother studies relating to employee benefit plans, the matters regulated by this subchapter, and the enforcement procedures provided for under this subchapter\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

ERISA further permits the Secretary of Labor to \xe2\x80\x9crequir[e] any information or data from any [plan] where he finds such data or information is necessary to carry out the purposes of\xe2\x80\x9d the statute, \xc2\xa71024(a)(2)(B), and, when investigating a possible statutory violation, \xe2\x80\x9cto require the submission of reports, books, and records, and the filing of data\xe2\x80\x9d related to other requisite filings, \xc2\xa71134(a)(1). The Secretary has the general power to promulgate regulations \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary or appropriate\xe2\x80\x9d to administer the statute, \xc2\xa71135, and to provide exemptions from any reporting obligations, \xc2\xa71024(a)(3).

\n

It should come as no surprise, then, that plans must keep detailed records so compliance with ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s reporting and disclosure requirements may be \xe2\x80\x9cverified, explained, or clarified, and checked for accuracy and completeness.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71027. The records to be retained must \xe2\x80\x9cinclude vouchers, worksheets, receipts, and applicable resolutions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also \xc2\xa71135 (allowing the Secretary to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide for the keeping of books and records, and for the inspection of such books and records\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

These various requirements are not mere formalities. Violation of any one of them may result in both civil and criminal liability. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71131\xe2\x80\x931132.

\n

As all this makes plain, reporting, disclosure, and recordkeeping are central to, and an essential part of, the uniform system of plan administration contemplated by ERISA. The Court, in fact, has noted often that these requirements are integral aspects of ERISA. See, e.g., Dillingham, 519 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 327; Travelers, supra, at 651; Ingersoll-Rand, supra, at 137; Massachusetts v. Morash,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 107,\n113,\n115 (1989)\n; Fort Halifax, supra, at 9; Metropolitan Life Ins. Co. v. Massachusetts,\n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 724,\n732 (1985)\n.

\n

Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s reporting regime, which compels plans to report detailed information about claims and plan members, both intrudes upon \xe2\x80\x9ca central matter of plan administration\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinterferes with nationally uniform plan administration.\xe2\x80\x9d Egelhoff, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 148. The State\xe2\x80\x99s law and regulation govern plan reporting, disclosure, and\xe2\x80\x94by necessary implication\xe2\x80\x94recordkeeping. These matters are fundamental components of ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s regulation of plan administration. Differing, or even parallel, regulations from multiple jurisdictions could create wasteful administrative costs and threaten to subject plans to wide-ranging liability. See, e.g., 18 V.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa79410(g) (supplying penalties for violation of Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s reporting rules); CVR \xc2\xa710 (same). Pre-emption is necessary to prevent the States from imposing novel, inconsistent, and burdensome reporting requirements on plans.

\n

The Secretary of Labor, not the States, is authorized to administer the reporting requirements of plans governed by ERISA. He may exempt plans from ERISA report-ing requirements altogether. See \xc2\xa71024(a)(3); 29 CFR \xc2\xa72520.104\xe2\x80\x9344 (2005) (exempting self-insured health plans from the annual financial reporting requirement). And, he may be authorized to require ERISA plans to report data similar to that which Vermont seeks, though that question is not presented here. Either way, the uniform rule design of ERISA makes it clear that these decisions are for federal authorities, not for the separate States.

\n

B

\n

Vermont disputes the pre-emption of its reporting regime on several fronts. The State argues that respondent has not demonstrated that the reporting regime in fact has caused it to suffer economic costs. Brief for Petitioner 52\xe2\x80\x9354. But respondent\xe2\x80\x99s challenge is not based on the theory that the State\xe2\x80\x99s law must be pre-empted solely because of economic burdens caused by the state law. See Travelers, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 668. Respondent argues, rather, that Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s scheme regulates a central aspect of plan administration and, if the scheme is not pre-empted, plans will face the possibility of a body of disuniform state reporting laws and, even if uniform, the necessity to accommodate multiple governmental agencies. A plan need not wait to bring a pre-emption claim until confronted with numerous inconsistent obligations and encumbered with any ensuing costs.

\n

Vermont contends, furthermore, that ERISA does not pre-empt the state statute and regulation because the state reporting scheme has different objectives. This Court has recognized that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he principal object of [ERISA] is to protect plan participants and beneficiaries.\xe2\x80\x9d Boggs v. Boggs,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 833,\n845 (1997)\n. And \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n enacting ERISA, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 primary concern was with the mismanagement of funds accumulated to finance employee benefits and the failure to pay employees benefits from accumulated funds.\xe2\x80\x9d Morash, supra, at 115. The State maintains that its program has nothing to do with the financial solvency of plans or the prudent behavior of fiduciaries. See Brief for Petitioner 29. This does not suffice to avoid federal pre-emption.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[P]re-emption claims turn on Congress\xe2\x80\x99s intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Travelers, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 655. The purpose of a state law, then, is relevant only as it may relate to the \xe2\x80\x9cscope of the state law that Congress understood would survive,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 656, or \xe2\x80\x9cthe nature of the effect of the state law on ERISA plans,\xe2\x80\x9d Dillingham, supra, at 325. In Travelers, for example, the Court noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]oth the purpose and the effects of\xe2\x80\x9d the state law at issue \xe2\x80\x9cdistinguish[ed] it from\xe2\x80\x9d laws that \xe2\x80\x9cfunction as a regulation of an ERISA plan itself.\xe2\x80\x9d 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 658\xe2\x80\x93659. The perceived difference here in the objectives of the Vermont law and ERISA does not shield Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s reporting regime from pre-emption. Vermont orders health insurers, including ERISA plans, to report detailed information about the administration of benefits in a systematic manner. This is a direct regulation of a fundamental ERISA function. Any difference in purpose does not transform this direct regulation of \xe2\x80\x9ca central matter of plan administration,\xe2\x80\x9d Egelhoff, supra, at 148, into an innocuous and peripheral set of additional rules.

\n

The Vermont regime cannot be saved by invoking the State\xe2\x80\x99s traditional power to regulate in the area of public health. The Court in the past has \xe2\x80\x9caddressed claims of pre-emption with the starting presumption that Congress does not intend to supplant state law,\xe2\x80\x9d in particular state laws regulating a subject of traditional state power. Travelers, supra, at 654\xe2\x80\x93655. ERISA, however, \xe2\x80\x9ccertainly contemplated the pre-emption of substantial areas of traditional state regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Dillingham, 519 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 330. ERISA pre-empts a state law that regulates a key facet of plan administration even if the state law exercises a traditional state power. See Egelhoff, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 151\xe2\x80\x93152. The fact that reporting is a principal and essential feature of ERISA demonstrates that Congress intended to pre-empt state reporting laws like Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s, including those that operate with the purpose of furthering public health. The analysis may be different when applied to a state law, such as a tax on hospitals, see De Buono v. NYSA\xe2\x80\x93ILA Medical and Clinical Services Fund,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 806 (1997)\n, the enforcement of which necessitates incidental reporting by ERISA plans; but that is not the law before the Court. Any presumption against pre-emption, whatever its force in other instances, cannot validate a state law that enters a fundamental area of ERISA regulation and thereby counters the federal purpose in the way this state law does.

\n

IV

\n

Respondent suggests that the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (ACA), which created new reporting obligations for employer-sponsored health plans and incorporated those requirements into the body of ERISA, further demonstrates that ERISA pre-empts Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s reporting regime. See\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71185d; 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7300gg\xe2\x80\x9315a, 17; \xc2\xa718031(e)(3). The ACA, however, specified that it shall not \xe2\x80\x9cbe construed to preempt any State law that does not prevent the application of the provisions\xe2\x80\x9d of the ACA.\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa718041(d). This anti-pre-emption provision might prevent any new ACA-created reporting obligations from pre-empting state reporting regimes like Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s, notwithstanding the incorporation of these requirements in the heart of ERISA. But see\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71191(a)(2) (providing that the new ACA provisions shall not be construed to affect or modify the ERISA pre-emption clause as applied to group health plans);\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7300gg\xe2\x80\x9323(a)(2) (same).

\n

The Court has no need to resolve this issue. ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-existing reporting, disclosure, and recordkeeping provisions\xe2\x80\x94upon which the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion rests\xe2\x80\x94maintain their pre-emptive force whether or not the new ACA reporting obligations also pre-empt state law.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s express pre-emption clause requires invalidation of the Vermont reporting statute as applied to ERISA plans. The state statute imposes duties that are inconsistent with the central design of ERISA, which is to provide a single uniform national scheme for the administration of ERISA plans without interference from laws of the several States even when those laws, to a large extent, impose parallel requirements. The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit is

\n

Affirmed.

Doing so is likely to create serious administrative problems. The Court points out that the respondent\xe2\x80\x99s plan provides benefits to over 80,000 individuals living in 50 different States. See ante, at 3. In addition, amici curiae tell us that self-insured, ERISA-based health plans provide benefits to 93 million Americans. Brief for American Benefits Council et al. as Amici Curiae 8. If each State is free to go its own way, each independently determining what information each plan must provide about benefits, the result could well be unnecessary, duplicative, and conflicting reporting requirements, any of which can mean increased confusion and increased cost. Private standard setting can of course help alleviate these problems, but given the large number of different possible regulations, I do not believe that is sufficient. Cf. A. Costello & M. Taylor, APCD Council & NAHDO, Standardization of Data Collection in All-Payer Claims Databases 3\xe2\x80\x934 (Jan. 2011), online at https://www.apcdcouncil.org\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0publication/standardization-data-collection-all-payer-claims-databases (as last visited Feb. 26, 2016).

\n

I would also emphasize that pre-emption does not necessarily prevent Vermont or other States from obtaining the self-insured, ERISA-based health-plan information that they need. States wishing to obtain information can ask the Federal Government for appropriate approval. As the majority points out, the \xe2\x80\x9cSecretary of Labor has authority to establish additional reporting and disclosure requirements for ERISA plans.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8; see\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71135. Moreover, the Secretary \xe2\x80\x9cis authorized to undertake research and surveys and in connection therewith to collect, compile, analyze and publish data, information, and statistics relating to employee benefit plans, including retirement, deferred compensation, and welfare plans.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71143(a)(1). At least one other important statute provides the Secretary of Health and Human Services with similar authority. See\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7300gg\xe2\x80\x9317(a) (part of the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act that is applicable to group health insurance plans including ERISA plans); Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 4 (the Department of Labor, the Department of Health and Human Services, and the Department of Treasury are \xe2\x80\x9ccurrently considering a rulemaking to require health plans to report more detailed information about various aspects of plan administration, such as enrollment, claims processing, and benefit offerings\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

I see no reason why the Secretary of Labor could not develop reporting requirements that satisfy the States\xe2\x80\x99 needs, including some State-specific requirements, as appropriate. Nor do I see why the Department could not delegate to a particular State the authority to obtain data related to that State, while also providing the data to the Federal Secretary for use by other States or at the federal level.

\n

Although the need for federal approval or authorization limits to some degree the States\xe2\x80\x99 power to obtain information, requiring that approval has considerable advantages. The federal agencies are more likely to be informed about, and to understand, ERISA-related consequences and health-care needs from a national perspective. Their involvement may consequently secure for the States necessary information without unnecessarily creating costly conflicts\xe2\x80\x94particularly when compared with such alternatives as giving each State free rein to go its own way or asking nonexpert federal courts to try to iron out, regulation by regulation, such conflicts. Cf. Med-tronic, Inc. v. Lohr,\n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 470,\n506 (1996)\n (Breyer, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (reading a complex, ambiguous regulatory statute to permit \xe2\x80\x9cinformed agency involvement\xe2\x80\x9d is more likely to achieve Congress\xe2\x80\x99 general objectives).

\n

For these reasons, and others that the majority sets forth, I agree that Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s statute is pre-empted because it \xe2\x80\x9cinterferes with nationally uniform plan administration.\xe2\x80\x9d Egelhoff v. Egelhoff,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 141,\n148 (2001)\n.

I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion because it faithfully applies our precedents interpreting\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71144, the express pre-emption provision of the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA). I write separately because I have come to doubt whether \xc2\xa71144 is a valid exercise of congressional power and whether our approach to ERISA pre-emption is consistent with our broader pre-emption jurisprudence.

\n

I

\n

Section 1144 contains what may be the most expansive express pre-emption provision in any federal statute. Section 1144(a) states: \xe2\x80\x9cExcept as provided\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71144(b) ERISA \xe2\x80\x9cshall supersede any and all State laws insofar as they may now or hereafter relate to any employee benefit plan.\xe2\x80\x9d Under the ordinary meaning of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelate to,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71144(a) pre-empts all state laws that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98stand in some relation\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98have bearing or concern\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d on, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98pertain\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98refer\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to, or \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cbring into association with or connection with\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d an ERISA plan. Shaw v. Delta Air Lines, Inc.,\n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 85\n, n.\xc2\xa016 (1983) (quoting Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1158 (5th ed. 1979)). And \xc2\xa71144(b) seemingly acknowledges how broadly \xc2\xa71144(a) extends by excepting \xe2\x80\x9cgener-ally applicable criminal law[s]\xe2\x80\x9d and state laws \xe2\x80\x9cregulat[ing] insurance, banking, or securities\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94but not generally applicable civil laws\xe2\x80\x94from pre-emption. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71144(b)(2)(A), (b)(4). Section 1144, in sum, \xe2\x80\x9cis clearly expansive\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94so much so that \xe2\x80\x9cone might be excused for wondering, at first blush, whether the words of limitation (\xe2\x80\x98insofar as they .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. relate\xe2\x80\x99) do much limiting.\xe2\x80\x9d New York State Conference of Blue Cross & Blue Shield Plans v. Travelers Ins. Co.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 645,\n655 (1995)\n.

\n

Read according to its plain terms, \xc2\xa71144 raises constitutional concerns. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Supremacy Clause gives \xe2\x80\x98supreme\xe2\x80\x99 status only to those [federal laws] that are \xe2\x80\x98made in Pursuance\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of the Constitution. Wyeth v. Levine,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555,\n585 (2009)\n (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment) (quoting Art. VI, cl. 2). But I question whether any provision of Article I authorizes Congress to prohibit States from applying a host of generally applicable civil laws to ERISA plans. \xe2\x80\x9cThe Constitution requires a distinction between what is truly national and what is truly local.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Morrison,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 598\n\xe2\x80\x93618 (2000). If the Federal Government were \xe2\x80\x9cto take over the regulation of entire areas of traditional state concern,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9careas having nothing to do with the regulation of commercial activities,\xe2\x80\x9d then \xe2\x80\x9cthe boundaries between the spheres of federal and state authority would blur and political responsibility would become illusory.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Lopez,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549,\n577 (1995)\n (Kennedy, J., concurring). Just because Congress can regulate some aspects of ERISA plans pursuant to the Commerce Clause does not mean that Congress can exempt ERISA plans from state regulations that have nothing to do with interstate commerce. See Gonzales v. Raich,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9360 (2005) (Thomas, J., dissenting).

\n

II

\n

This Court used to interpret \xc2\xa71144 according to its text. But we became uncomfortable with how much state law \xc2\xa71144 would pre-empt if read literally. \xe2\x80\x9cIf \xe2\x80\x98relate to\xe2\x80\x99 were taken to extend to the furthest stretch of its indeterminacy,\xe2\x80\x9d we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cthen for all practical purposes pre-emption would never run its course.\xe2\x80\x9d Travelers, supra, at 655.

\n

Rather than addressing the constitutionality of \xc2\xa71144, we abandoned efforts to give its text its ordinary meaning. In Travelers, we adopted atextual but what we thought to be \xe2\x80\x9cworkable\xe2\x80\x9d standards to construe \xc2\xa71144. Ante, at 6. Thus, to determine whether a state law impermissibly \xe2\x80\x9crelates to\xe2\x80\x9d an ERISA plan due to some \xe2\x80\x9cconnection with\xe2\x80\x9d that plan, we now \xe2\x80\x9clook both to the objectives of the ERISA statute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as well as to the nature of the effect of the state law on ERISA plans.\xe2\x80\x9d Egelhoff v. Egelhoff,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 141,\n147 (2001)\n (citing Travelers; internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

We decided Travelers in 1995. I joined that opinion and have joined others applying the approach we adopted in Travelers. But our interpretation of ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s express pre-emption provision has become increasingly difficult to reconcile with our pre-emption jurisprudence. Travelers departed from the statutory text, deeming it \xe2\x80\x9cunhelpful.\xe2\x80\x9d 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 656. But, in other cases involving express pre-emption provisions, the text has been the beginning and often the end of our analysis. E.g., Chamber of Commerce of United States of America v. Whiting,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 582,\n594 (2011)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98focus[ing] on the plain wording\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to define the scope of the Immigration Reform and Control Act\xe2\x80\x99s express pre-emption clause); see also National Meat Assn. v. Harris, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2012) (slip op., at 4, 6\xe2\x80\x9310) (parsing the text to determine the scope of the Federal Meat Inspection Act\xe2\x80\x99s express pre-emption clause). We have likewise refused to look to policy limits that are not \xe2\x80\x9cremotely discernible in the statutory text.\xe2\x80\x9d Whiting, supra, at 599. We have not given a sound basis for departing from these principles and treating \xc2\xa71144 differently from other express pre-emption provisions.

\n

Travelers\xe2\x80\x99 approach to ERISA pre-emption also does not avoid constitutional concerns. We have continued to interpret \xc2\xa71144 as pre-empting \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial areas of traditional state regulation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpre-empt[ing] a state law .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. even if the state law exercises a traditional state power.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13 (internal quotation marks omitted). Until we confront whether Congress had the constitutional authority to pre-empt such a wide array of state laws in the first place, the Court\xe2\x80\x94and lower courts\xe2\x80\x94will continue to struggle to apply \xc2\xa71144. It behooves us to address whether Article I gives Congress such power and whether \xc2\xa71144 may permissibly be read to avoid unconstitutional results.

To better control health care outcomes and costs, Vermont requires all public and private entities that pay for health care services provided to Vermont residents to supply data to the State\xe2\x80\x99s all-payer claims database. Many States have similar databases in place or in development. The question presented in this case is whether Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s health care data-collection law is preempted by the Employer Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA),\n\n88Stat.\n832,\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001 et\xc2\xa0seq., the federal law regulating employee benefit plans. I would hold that Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s effort to track health care services provided to its residents and the cost of those services does not impermissibly intrude on ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s dominion over employee benefit plans.

\n

I

\n

In 2005, the Vermont Legislature established the Vermont Health Care Uniform Reporting and Evaluation System, a database populated by information on health care claims paid by insurers and other coverage providers. See Vt. Stat. Ann., Tit. 18, \xc2\xa79410 (2015 Cum. Supp.); Reg. H\xe2\x80\x932008\xe2\x80\x9301, Code Vt. Rules 21\xe2\x80\x93040\xe2\x80\x93021, \xc2\xa74(D) (2016) (directing insurers and other coverage providers to \xe2\x80\x9csubmit medical claims data, pharmacy claims data, member eligibility data, provider data, and other information related to health care provided to Vermont residents and health care provided by Vermont health care providers and facilities\xe2\x80\x9d). Health insurers and other coverage providers must report the required data if they cover at least 200 Vermont residents. \xc2\xa73(Ab).

\n

Seventeen other States have enacted similar database systems, called \xe2\x80\x9call-payer claims databases.\xe2\x80\x9d[1] These States, like Vermont, collect health-claims data to serve compelling interests, including identification of reforms effective to drive down health care costs, evaluation of relative utility of different treatment options, and detection of instances of discrimination in the provision of care. See Brief for National Governors Association et al. as Amici Curiae 11\xe2\x80\x9314; Brief for Harvard Law School Center for Health Law and Policy Innovation et al. as Amici Curiae 11\xe2\x80\x9318; Brief for State of New York et al. as Amici Curiae 12\xe2\x80\x9320. See also Vt. Stat. Ann., Tit. 18, \xc2\xa79410(a)(1) (Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law is designed to help \xe2\x80\x9cidentif[y] health care needs and infor[m] health care policy,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cevaluat[e] the effectiveness of intervention programs on improving patient outcomes,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccompar[e] costs between various treatment settings and approaches,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cdetermin[e] the capacity and distribution of existing resources,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[e] information to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. purchasers of health care\xe2\x80\x9d).[2]

\n

Respondent Liberty Mutual Insurance Company (Lib-erty), in common with legions of employers, provides health care to its employees through a self-insured plan, administered by Blue Cross/Blue Shield (Blue Cross).[3] Because Blue Cross administers thousands of health care policies in Vermont, the State requires it to report data for all of the plans it administers, and Blue Cross has complied with this mandate. In 2010, for example, Blue Cross reported data on over 7,000 Vermont health care-plan beneficiaries. Roughly half of the beneficiaries received coverage through self-insured employer policies. App. 205. In 2011, at Liberty\xe2\x80\x99s request, Blue Cross did not submit data on Vermont residents who received coverage through Liberty\xe2\x80\x99s plan. Id., at 21\xe2\x80\x9323. Vermont ordered Blue Cross to provide the claims data. Id., at 23, 31\xe2\x80\x9333. Lib-erty instructed Blue Cross not to comply and, shortly thereafter, filed the instant suit, seeking to block Vermont from obtaining the data.

\n

In defense of its resistance to Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law, Liberty relies on its plan\xe2\x80\x99s status as an ERISA-covered \xe2\x80\x9cemployee welfare benefit plan,\xe2\x80\x9d defined as \xe2\x80\x9cany plan, fund, or program .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. established or maintained by an employer .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for the purpose of providing for its participants or their beneficiaries, through the purchase of insurance or otherwise, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. medical, surgical, or hospital care or benefits, or benefits in the event of sickness.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71002(1). Because ERISA directs plan fiduciaries to conserve plan assets for the purpose of \xe2\x80\x9cproviding benefits to participants,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71104(a)(1)(A)(ii), Liberty maintains that ERISA preempts diverse state health-claims reporting laws. If there is to be mandatory health-claims reporting by ERISA plans, Liberty urges, the source of the mandate should be a uniform national reporting regime. See Brief for Respondent 26\xe2\x80\x9329; Tr. of Oral Arg. 32\xe2\x80\x9333.

\n

Opposing ERISA-grounded preemption of its data-collection law, Vermont points out that the efficacy of the State\xe2\x80\x99s law depends on comprehensive reporting, i.e., collecting data on numerous beneficiaries from each of several major segments of the health care market. See Brief for Petitioner 12; Brief for Harvard Law School Center for Health Law and Policy Innovation et al. as Amici Curiae 18\xe2\x80\x9319.[4] About half of Americans with health insurance receive coverage from their employers, Dept. of Commerce, Bureau of Census, J. Smith & C. Medalia, Health Insurance Coverage in the United States: 2013, p. 2 (2014), and 61% of such persons are covered by an employer\xe2\x80\x99s self-insured plan. Brief for Harvard Law School Center for Health Law and Policy Innovation et al. as Amici Curiae 20. In Vermont, about 20% of the database\xe2\x80\x99s total content originates from employer self-insured plans. Brief for Petitioner 12, and n. 10. Stopping States from collecting claims data from self-insured employer health care plans would thus hugely undermine the reporting regimes on which Vermont and other States depend to maintain and improve the quality, and hold down the cost, of health care services.

\n

The United States District Court for the District of Vermont rejected Liberty\xe2\x80\x99s plea for preemption. Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law, that court determined, served the State\xe2\x80\x99s undoubted interest in regulating health care markets, and did not substantially interfere with the operation of Liberty\xe2\x80\x99s ERISA plans. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 64\xe2\x80\x9366, 78\xe2\x80\x9379. The Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit reversed, two to one. Liberty Mut. Ins. Co. v. Donegan, 746 F.\xc2\xa03d 497 (2014). The majority acknowledged that the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s ERISA-preemption decisions of the 1990\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmarked something of a pivot\xe2\x80\x9d in starting with a presumption \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98that Congress does not intend to supplant state law,\xe2\x80\x99 especially if the \xe2\x80\x98state action [occurs] in fields of traditional state regulation,\xe2\x80\x99 like health care.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 506 (quoting New York State Conference of Blue Cross & Blue Shield Plans v. Travelers Ins. Co.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 645\n\xe2\x80\x93655 (1995)). Nonetheless, the majority con-cluded that ERISA preempted the application of Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law to Liberty\xe2\x80\x99s plan. 746 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 506, 508. The reporting of information about plan benefits, the majority reasoned, qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9ccore ERISA functio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d and, therefore, must be \xe2\x80\x9csubject to a uniform federal standard.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 505, 508. Judge Straub dissented, offering a concise critique of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe majority finds that the burden imposed by the Vermont reporting requirement warrants preemption of the [data-collection] statute. This conclusion falters for two primary reasons. First, the reporting requirement imposed by the Vermont statute differs in kind from the \xe2\x80\x98reporting\xe2\x80\x99 that is required by ERISA and therefore was not the kind of state law Congress intended to preempt. Second, Liberty Mutual has failed to show any actual burden, much less a burden that triggers ERISA preemption. Rather, the Vermont statute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does not interfere with an ERISA plan\xe2\x80\x99s administration of benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 511.

\n

II

\n

Essentially for the reasons Judge Straub identified, I would hold that ERISA does not preempt Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection statute. That law and ERISA serve different purposes. ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s domain is the design and administration of employee benefit plans: notably, prescriptions on the vesting of benefits, claims processing, and the designation of beneficiaries. See Travelers, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 656 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress intended to ensure that plans and plan sponsors would be subject to a uniform body of benefits law.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Its reporting requirements, geared to those functions, ensure that the plans in fact provide covered benefits. Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection statute, in contrast, aims to improve the quality and utilization, and reduce the cost, of health care in Vermont by providing consumers, government officials, and researchers with comprehensive data about the health care delivery system. Nor does Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s law impose burdens on ERISA plans of the kind this Court has found sufficient to warrant preemption.

\n

ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s preemption clause provides that the Act \xe2\x80\x9cshall supersede any and all State laws insofar as they may now or hereafter relate to any employee benefit plan.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71144(a). Lacking clear direction from the clause\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9copaque\xe2\x80\x9d text, De Buono v. NYSA\xe2\x80\x93ILA Medical and Clinical Services Fund,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 806,\n809 (1997)\n, the Court has sought to honor Congress\xe2\x80\x99 evident call for an expansive preemption principle without invalidating state regulations falling outside ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s domain. See Travelers, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 655\xe2\x80\x93656 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe governing text of [the] ERISA [preemption clause] is clearly expansive. .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [But] [i]f \xe2\x80\x98relate to\xe2\x80\x99 were taken to extend to the furthest stretch of its indeterminacy, then for all practical purposes pre-emption would never run its course, for really, universally, relations stop nowhere.\xe2\x80\x9d (some internal quotation marks omitted)).[5]

\n

Seeking to bring some measure of determinacy to ERISA preemption, the Court has stated: \xe2\x80\x9c[A] law \xe2\x80\x98relates to\xe2\x80\x99 an employee benefit plan .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. if it has a connection with or reference to such a plan.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 656 (some internal quotation marks omitted). In this case, the Court of Appeals found, and the parties do not here contest, that Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law lacks \xe2\x80\x9creference to\xe2\x80\x9d ERISA plans because the law applies to all health care payers and does not home in on ERISA plans. See 746 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 508, n.\xc2\xa09. The question, therefore, is whether the law has an impermissible \xe2\x80\x9cconnection with\xe2\x80\x9d ERISA plans. Because the term \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98connection with\xe2\x80\x99 is scarcely more restrictive than \xe2\x80\x98relate to,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the Court has \xe2\x80\x9ccautioned against .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. uncritical literalism,\xe2\x80\x9d Egelhoff v. Egelhoff,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 141,\n147 (2001)\n (internal quotation marks omitted), and has set out this further formulation: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]o determine whether a state law has the forbidden connection, we look both to the objectives of the ERISA statute as a guide to the scope of the state law that Congress understood would survive, as well as to the nature of the effect of the state law on ERISA plans.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

In framing preemption doctrine, the Court does not \xe2\x80\x9cassum[e] lightly that Congress has derogated state regulation, but instead .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. addresse[s] claims of pre-emption with the starting presumption that Congress does not intend to supplant state law,\xe2\x80\x9d Travelers, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 654, especially where the State\xe2\x80\x99s regulation deals with \xe2\x80\x9cmatters of health and safety,\xe2\x80\x9d De Buono, 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 814 (internal quotation marks omitted). In Travelers and subsequent decisions upholding state laws against preemption challenges, this Court made clear that this presumption plays an important role in ERISA cases. Travelers, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 654, 661; California Div. of Labor Standards Enforcement v. Dillingham Constr., N. A., Inc.,\n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 316\n\xe2\x80\x93331 (1997); De Buono, 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 814. Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law is a vital part of the State\xe2\x80\x99s control of its own health care market. See supra, at 1\xe2\x80\x932, 4; 746 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 513 (Straub, J., dissenting). The presumption against preemption should thus apply full strength, and Liberty has not rebutted it, i.e., it has not shown that ERISA demands the preemption of Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law. To the contrary, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s ERISA preemption precedent points against preemption in this case.

\n

A

\n

To determine whether Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law, as applied to Liberty\xe2\x80\x99s plan, has an impermissible \xe2\x80\x9cconnection with\xe2\x80\x9d ERISA plans, I look first to the \xe2\x80\x9cobjectives of the ERISA statute as a guide.\xe2\x80\x9d Egelhoff, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 147; Oneok, Inc. v. Learjet, Inc., 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 11) (emphasizing \xe2\x80\x9cthe importance of considering the target at which the state law aims\xe2\x80\x9d in applying ordinary field-preemption principles). Because ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s reporting requirements and the Vermont law elicit different information and serve distinct purposes, there is no sensible reason to find the Vermont data-collection law preempted.

\n

ERISA-covered benefit plans must, absent exemption, file annual reports containing financial and actuarial data to enable the Secretary of Labor to evaluate plans\xe2\x80\x99 management and solvency. See 29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71023, 1024(a)(2)(B); Dillingham, 519 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 326\xe2\x80\x93327 (Congress \xe2\x80\x9cestablished extensive reporting .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. requirements\xe2\x80\x9d to protect against \xe2\x80\x9cthe mismanagement of funds accumulated to finance employee benefits and the failure to pay employees\xe2\x80\x99 benefits from accumulated funds.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).[6]

\n

Beyond debate, Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law does not seek to regulate the management and solvency of ERISA-covered welfare plans. See supra, at 2 (reciting objectives of the Vermont data-collection law). Vermont requests no information on plan finances. See Reg. H\xe2\x80\x932008\xe2\x80\x9301, Code of Vt. Rules 21\xe2\x80\x93040\xe2\x80\x93021, \xc2\xa74(D); supra, at 2 (detailing the types of data collected by Vermont). The State collects data on paid health care claims, not denied claims. See \xc2\xa75(A)(8). Vermont seeks a better understanding of how its residents obtain health care and how effective that care is. Unlike ERISA superintendence, Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s interest does not lie in reviewing whether a self-insured provider is keeping its bargain to covered employees. Nor does Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s statute even arguably regulate relationships among the prime ERISA entities: beneficiaries, participants, administrators, employees, trustees and other fiduciaries, and the plan itself.

\n

Despite these significant differences between ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s reporting requirements and Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection regime, Liberty contends that Congress intended to spare ERISA plans from benefit-related reporting requirements unless those requirements are nationally uniform. In support of this contention, Liberty points to dicta from this Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinions and selections from ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history. See, e.g., Travelers, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 661 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[S]ubject matters covered by ERISA [include] reporting, disclosure, fiduciary responsibility, and the like.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Shaw v. Delta Air Lines, Inc.,\n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 85,\n98 (1983)\n)); Ingersoll-Rand Co. v. McClendon,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 133,\n137 (1990)\n (ERISA \xe2\x80\x9csets various uniform standards, including rules concerning reporting, disclosure, and fiduciary responsibility, for both pension and welfare plans.\xe2\x80\x9d); 120 Cong. Rec. 29942 (1974) (remarks of Sen. Javits) (\xe2\x80\x9cState laws compelling disclosure from .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. plans .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. will be superseded.\xe2\x80\x9d). Far from unambiguously endorsing Liberty\xe2\x80\x99s sweeping view of ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s preemptive scope, these statements can be read at least as reasonably for the unremarkable principle that ERISA preempts state reporting rules designed to serve the same purposes as ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s reporting requirements. This more limited understanding is consistent with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s admonition to pay close attention to the \xe2\x80\x9cobjectives of the ERISA statute as a guide.\xe2\x80\x9d Egelhoff, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 147.

\n

B

\n

Satisfied that ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s objectives do not require preemption of Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law, I turn to the \xe2\x80\x9cnature of the effect of the state law on ERISA plans.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The imposition of some burdens on the administration of ERISA plans, the Court has held, does not suffice to require preemption. See De Buono, 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 815. While a law imposing costs so acute as to effectively dictate how a plan is designed or administered could trigger preemption, see id., at 816, n.\xc2\xa016, no such extreme effects are present here. Moreover, no \xe2\x80\x9ccentral matter of plan administration,\xe2\x80\x9d Egelhoff, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 148, is touched by Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law. That law prescribes no vesting requirements, benefit levels, beneficiary designations, or rules on how claims should be processed or paid. Indeed, Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s law does not require Liberty to do anything. The burden of compliance falls on Blue Cross, which apparently provides the data without protest on behalf of other self-funded plans. See supra, at 3.

\n

Reporting and disclosure are no doubt required of ERISA plans, but those requirements are ancillary to the areas ERISA governs. Reporting and recordkeeping incident to state laws of general applicability have been upheld as they bear on ERISA plans. In De Buono, 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 809\xe2\x80\x93810, 816, for example, the Court held that a gross-receipts tax on patient services provided by a hospital operated by an ERISA plan was not preempted, even though administration of the tax required filing quarterly reports. And in Dillingham, 519 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 319, the Court held that California\xe2\x80\x99s prevailing-wage law was not preempted as applied to apprenticeship programs established by ERISA plans. Prevailing-wage laws typically require employees to keep records of the wages paid to employees and make them available for review by state authorities. See, e.g., Cal. Lab. Code Ann. \xc2\xa71776 (West 1989) (prevailing-wage law in Dillingham). The Second Circuit erred, then, in holding that ERISA preempts any state-law reporting obligation that is more than \xe2\x80\x9cslight.\xe2\x80\x9d See 746 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 508\xe2\x80\x93509.

\n

The Vermont data-collection statute keeps company with the laws considered in De Buono and Dillingham: It is generally applicable and does not involve \xe2\x80\x9ca central matter of plan administration.\xe2\x80\x9d Egelhoff, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 148. And, as Judge Straub emphasized in his dissent, Liberty \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to provide any details or showing of the alleged burden,\xe2\x80\x9d instead \xe2\x80\x9carguing only that \xe2\x80\x98all regulations have their costs.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 746 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 515 (quoting Liberty\xe2\x80\x99s appellate brief).

\n

As the United States explains, the supposition indulged by the Second Circuit that Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s law imposed a substantial burden \xe2\x80\x9cis not obvious, or even particularly plausible, without any factual support.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 28. The data-collection law \xe2\x80\x9cessentially requires Blue Cross [Liberty\xe2\x80\x99s third-party administrator] to take information generated in the ordinary course of its claims-payment operations and report that information in a prescribed format to the [State].\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court of Appeals majority accentuated the sheer number of data entries that must be reported to Vermont. See 746 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 509\xe2\x80\x93510, and n.\xc2\xa013. Accord ante, at 1 (opinion of Breyer, J.) Entirely overlooked in that enumeration is the technological capacity for efficient computer-based data storage, formatting, and submission. See Brief for National Association of Health Data Organizations et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 7\xe2\x80\x939, 13 (describing three-step electronic path data take from health provider, to insurer or health care plan, and ultimately to the State\xe2\x80\x99s database).[7] Where regulatory compliance depends upon the use of evolving technologies, it should be incumbent on the objector to show concretely what the alleged regulatory burden in fact entails.[8]

\n

Because data-collection laws like Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s are not uniform from State to State, compliance is inevitably burdensome, Liberty successfully argued in the Court of Appeals. The Court replays this reasoning in today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. See ante, at 7, 10. But state-law diversity is a hallmark of our political system and has been lauded in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinions. See, e.g., Arizona State Legislature v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 28) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis Court has long recognized the role of States as laboratories for devising solutions to difficult legal problems.\xe2\x80\x9d (citing New State Ice Co. v. Liebmann,\n\n285 U.\xc2\xa0S. 262,\n311 (1932)\n (Brandeis, J., dissenting); internal quotation marks omitted)). Something more than an inherent characteristic of our federal system, therefore, must underpin the ERISA-grounded preemption Liberty urges.[9]

\n

Liberty points to Egelhoff as exemplary. In Egelhoff, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 143\xe2\x80\x93144, a deceased ERISA-plan participant\xe2\x80\x99s ex-spouse challenged a state law that revoked her beneficiary status automatically upon her divorce, even though the ERISA plan\xe2\x80\x99s terms did not. The Court held that ERISA preempted the law because it \xe2\x80\x9cbinds ERISA plan administrators to a particular choice of rules for determining beneficiary status.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 147. In that context, the Court said: \xe2\x80\x9cRequiring ERISA administrators to master the relevant laws of 50 States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would undermine the congressional goal of minimizing the administrative and financial burdens on plan administrators\xe2\x80\x94burdens ultimately borne by the beneficiaries.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 149\xe2\x80\x93150 (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted).

\n

The Court took care, however, to confine Egelhoff to issues implicating \xe2\x80\x9ca central matter of plan administration,\xe2\x80\x9d in other words, \xe2\x80\x9ca core ERISA concern.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 147\xe2\x80\x93148. What does that category comprise? As earlier described, see supra, at 6, 11, prescriptions on benefit levels, beneficiary designations, vesting requirements, and rules on processing and payment of claims would rank under the central or core ERISA subject-matter rubric.[10] So, too, would reporting and disclosure obligations, but of what kind? Those that further regulation of the design and administration of employee benefit plans, i.e., reporting and disclosures tied to the areas ERISA governs. ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s reporting and disclosure requirements are thus concerned with mismanagement of funds, failure to pay employee benefits, plan assets or allocations, all information bearing on the financial integrity of the plan. See supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s data-collection law, eliciting information on medical claims, services provided to beneficiaries, charges and payment for those services, and demographic makeup of those receiving benefits, does not fit the bill any more than reporting relating to a plan\xe2\x80\x99s taxes or wage payments does.

\n

Numerous States have informed the Court of their urgent need for information yielded by their health care data-collection laws. See Brief for National Governors Association et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae; Brief for State of New York et al. as Amici Curiae; Brief for Connecticut Health Insurance Exchange as Amicus Curiae; Brief for State of New Hampshire as Amicus Curiae. Wait until the Federal Government acts is the Court\xe2\x80\x99s response. The Department of Labor\xe2\x80\x99s capacious grant of statutory authority, the Court observes, might allow it to collect the same data Vermont and other States seek about ERISA plan health-benefit payments. See ante, at 10; ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (opinion of Breyer, J.). Once the information is collected, the Court conjectures, the Department could pass the data on to the States. Cf. ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (opinion of Breyer, J.) (suggesting that States could seek the Department\xe2\x80\x99s permission to enforce reporting requirements like Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s). It is unsettling, however, to leave the States dependent on a federal agency\xe2\x80\x99s grace, i.e., the Department of Labor\xe2\x80\x99s willingness to take on a chore divorced from ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s objectives.[11]

\n

Declaring \xe2\x80\x9creporting,\xe2\x80\x9d unmodified, a central or core ERISA function, as the Second Circuit did, 746 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 508, passes the line this Court drew in Travelers, De Buono, and Dillingham when it reined in \xc2\xa71144(a) so that it would no longer operate as a \xe2\x80\x9csuper-preemption\xe2\x80\x9d provision. Bogan, Protecting Patient Rights Despite ERISA, 74 Tulane L. Rev. 951, 959 (2000); see supra, at 8. I dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s retrieval of preemption doctrine that belongs in the discard bin.
