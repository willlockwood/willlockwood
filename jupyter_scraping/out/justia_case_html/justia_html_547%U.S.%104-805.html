TEXACO INC. v. DAGHER et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x93805.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 10, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 28, 2006

\n

Petitioners, Texaco Inc. and Shell Oil Co., collaborated in a joint venture, Equilon Enterprises, to refine and sell gasoline in the western United States under the two companies\xe2\x80\x99 original brand names. After Equilon set a single price for both brands, respondents, Texaco and Shell Oil service station owners, brought suit alleging that, by unifying gas prices under the two brands, petitioners had violated the per se rule against price fixing long recognized under \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act, see, e.g., Catalano, Inc. v. Target Sales, Inc., 446 U. S. 643, 647. Granting petitioners summary judgment, the District Court determined that the rule of reason, rather than a per se rule, governs respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim, and that, by eschewing rule of reason analysis, respondents had failed to raise a triable issue of fact. The Ninth Circuit reversed, characterizing petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position as a request for an exception to the per se price-fixing prohibition, and rejecting that request.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0It is not per se illegal under \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act for a lawful, economically integrated joint venture to set the prices at which it sells its products. Although \xc2\xa71 prohibits \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very contract [or] combination \xe2\x80\xa6 in restraint of trade,\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71, this Court has not taken a literal approach to that language, recognizing, instead, that Congress intended to outlaw only unreasonable restraints, e.g., State Oil Co. v. Khan, 522 U. S. 3, 10. Under rule of reason analysis, antitrust plaintiffs must demonstrate that a particular contract or combination is in fact unreasonable and anticompetitive. See, e.g., id., at 10\xe2\x80\x9319. Per se liability is reserved for \xe2\x80\x9cplainly anticompetitive\xe2\x80\x9d agreements. National Soc. of Professional Engineers v. United States, 435 U. S. 679, 692. While \xe2\x80\x9chorizontal\xe2\x80\x9d price-fixing agreements between two or more competitors are per se unlawful, see, e.g., Catalano, supra, at 647, this case does not present such an agreement, because Texaco and Shell Oil did not compete with one another in the relevant market\xe2\x80\x94i.e., gasoline sales to western service stations\xe2\x80\x94but instead participated in that market jointly through Equilon. When those who would otherwise be competitors pool their capital and share the risks of loss and opportunities for profit, they are regarded as a single firm competing with other sellers in the market. Arizona v. Maricopa County Medical Soc., 457 U. S. 332, 356. As such, Equilon\xe2\x80\x99s pricing policy may be price fixing in a literal sense, but it is not price fixing in the antitrust sense. The court below erred in reaching the opposite conclusion under the ancillary restraints doctrine, which governs the validity of restrictions imposed by a legitimate joint venture on nonventure activities. That doctrine has no application here, where the challenged business practice involves the core activity of the joint venture itself\xe2\x80\x94the pricing of the very goods produced and sold by Equilon. Pp. 3\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

369 F.\xc2\xa03d 1108, reversed.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From 1998 until 2002, petitioners Texaco Inc. and Shell Oil Co. collaborated in a joint venture, Equilon Enterprises, to refine and sell gasoline in the western United States under the original Texaco and Shell Oil brand names. Respondents, a class of Texaco and Shell Oil service station owners, allege that petitioners engaged in unlawful price fixing when Equilon set a single price for both Texaco and Shell Oil brand gasoline. We granted certiorari to determine whether it is per se illegal under \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71, for a lawful, economically integrated joint venture to set the prices at which the joint venture sells its products. We conclude that it is not, and accordingly we reverse the contrary judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Historically, Texaco and Shell Oil have competed with one another in the national and international oil and gasoline markets. Their business activities include refining crude oil into gasoline, as well as marketing gasoline to downstream purchasers, such as the service stations represented in respondents\xe2\x80\x99 class action.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1998, Texaco and Shell Oil formed a joint venture, Equilon, to consolidate their operations in the western United States, thereby ending competition between the two companies in the domestic refining and marketing of gasoline. Under the joint venture agreement, Texaco and Shell Oil agreed to pool their resources and share the risks of and profits from Equilon\xe2\x80\x99s activities. Equilon\xe2\x80\x99s board of directors would comprise representatives of Texaco and Shell Oil, and Equilon gasoline would be sold to downstream purchasers under the original Texaco and Shell Oil brand names. The formation of Equilon was approved by consent decree, subject to certain divestments and other modifications, by the Federal Trade Commission, see In\xc2\xa0re Shell Oil Co., 125 F.\xc2\xa0T.\xc2\xa0C. 769 (1998), as well as by the state attorneys general of California, Hawaii, Oregon, and Washington. Notably, the decrees imposed no restrictions on the pricing of Equilon gasoline.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the joint venture began to operate, respondents brought suit in district court, alleging that, by unifying gasoline prices under the two brands, petitioners had violated the per se rule against price fixing that this Court has long recognized under \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act, ch. 647, 26 Stat. 209, as amended, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71. See, e.g., Catalano, Inc. v. Target Sales, Inc., 446 U. S. 643, 647 (1980) (per curiam). The District Court awarded summary judgment to Texaco and Shell Oil. It determined that the rule of reason, rather than a per se rule or the quick look doctrine, governs respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim, and that, by eschewing rule of reason analysis, respondents had failed to raise a triable issue of fact. The Ninth Circuit reversed, characterizing petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position as a request for an \xe2\x80\x9cexception to the per se prohibition on price fixing,\xe2\x80\x9d and rejecting that request. Dagher v. Saudi Refining, Inc., 369 F.\xc2\xa03d 1108, 1116 (2004). We consolidated Texaco\xe2\x80\x99s and Shell Oil\xe2\x80\x99s separate petitions and granted certiorari to determine the extent to which the per se rule against price fixing applies to an important and increasingly popular form of business organization, the joint venture. 545 U. S. ___ (2005).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 1 of the Sherman Act prohibits \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very contract, combination in the form of trust or otherwise, or conspiracy, in restraint of trade or commerce among the several States.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71. This Court has not taken a literal approach to this language, however. See, e.g., State Oil Co. v. Khan, 522 U. S. 3, 10 (1997) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]his Court has long recognized that Congress intended to outlaw only unreasonable restraints\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Instead, this Court presumptively applies rule of reason analysis, under which antitrust plaintiffs must demonstrate that a particular contract or combination is in fact unreasonable and anticompetitive before it will be found unlawful. See, e.g., id., at 10\xe2\x80\x9319 (concluding that vertical price-fixing arrangements are subject to the rule of reason, not per se liability). Per se liability is reserved for only those agreements that are \xe2\x80\x9cso plainly anticompetitive that no elaborate study of the industry is needed to establish their illegality.\xe2\x80\x9d National Soc. of Professional Engineers v. United States, 435 U. S. 679, 692 (1978). Accordingly, \xe2\x80\x9cwe have expressed reluctance to adopt per se rules \xe2\x80\xa6 \xe2\x80\x98where the economic impact of certain practices is not immediately obvious.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d State Oil, supra, at 10 (quoting FTC v. Indiana Federation of Dentists, 476 U. S. 447, 458\xe2\x80\x93459 (1986)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Price-fixing agreements between two or more competitors, otherwise known as horizontal price-fixing agreements, fall into the category of arrangements that are per se unlawful. See, e.g., Catalano, supra, at 647. These cases do not present such an agreement, however, because Texaco and Shell Oil did not compete with one another in the relevant market\xe2\x80\x94namely, the sale of gasoline to service stations in the western United States\xe2\x80\x94but instead participated in that market jointly through their investments in Equilon.[Footnote 1] In other words, the pricing policy challenged here amounts to little more than price setting by a single entity\xe2\x80\x94albeit within the context of a joint venture\xe2\x80\x94and not a pricing agreement between competing entities with respect to their competing products. Throughout Equilon\xe2\x80\x99s existence, Texaco and Shell Oil shared in the profits of Equilon\xe2\x80\x99s activities in their role as investors, not competitors. When \xe2\x80\x9cpersons who would otherwise be competitors pool their capital and share the risks of loss as well as the opportunities for profit \xe2\x80\xa6 such joint ventures [are] regarded as a single firm competing with other sellers in the market.\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona v. Maricopa County Medical Soc., 457 U. S. 332, 356 (1982). As such, though Equilon\xe2\x80\x99s pricing policy may be price fixing in a literal sense, it is not price fixing in the antitrust sense. See Broadcast Music, Inc. v. Columbia Broadcasting System, Inc., 441 U. S. 1, 9 (1979) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen two partners set the price of their goods or services they are literally \xe2\x80\x98price fixing,\xe2\x80\x99 but they are not per se in violation of the Sherman Act\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This conclusion is confirmed by respondents\xe2\x80\x99 apparent concession that there would be no per se liability had Equilon simply chosen to sell its gasoline under a single brand. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 34. We see no reason to treat Equilon differently just because it chose to sell gasoline under two distinct brands at a single price. As a single entity, a joint venture, like any other firm, must have the discretion to determine the prices of the products that it sells, including the discretion to sell a product under two different brands at a single, unified price. If Equilon\xe2\x80\x99s price unification policy is anticompetitive, then respondents should have challenged it pursuant to the rule of reason.[Footnote 2] But it would be inconsistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s antitrust precedents to condemn the internal pricing decisions of a legitimate joint venture as per se unlawful.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court below reached the opposite conclusion by invoking the ancillary restraints doctrine. 369 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1118\xe2\x80\x931124. That doctrine governs the validity of restrictions imposed by a legitimate business collaboration, such as a business association or joint venture, on nonventure activities. See, e.g., National Collegiate Athletic Assn. v. Board of Regents of Univ. of Okla., 468 U. S. 85, 113\xe2\x80\x93115 (1984); Citizen Publishing Co. v. United States, 394 U. S. 131, 135\xe2\x80\x93136 (1969). Under the doctrine, courts must determine whether the nonventure restriction is a naked restraint on trade, and thus invalid, or one that is ancillary to the legitimate and competitive purposes of the business association, and thus valid. We agree with petitioners that the ancillary restraints doctrine has no application here, where the business practice being challenged involves the core activity of the joint venture itself\xe2\x80\x94namely, the pricing of the very goods produced and sold by Equilon. And even if we were to invoke the doctrine in these cases, Equilon\xe2\x80\x99s pricing policy is clearly ancillary to the sale of its own products. Judge Fernandez, dissenting from the ruling of the court below, put it well:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIn this case, nothing more radical is afoot than the fact that an entity, which now owns all of the production, transportation, research, storage, sales and distribution facilities for engaging in the gasoline business, also prices its own products. It decided to price them the same, as any other entity could. What could be more integral to the running of a business than setting a price for its goods and services?\xe2\x80\x9d 369 F. 3d, at 1127.

\n

\nSee also Broadcast Music, supra, at 23 (\xe2\x80\x9cJoint ventures and other cooperative arrangements are \xe2\x80\xa6 not usually unlawful, at least not as price-fixing schemes, where the agreement on price is necessary to market the product at all\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the pricing decisions of a legitimate joint venture do not fall within the narrow category of activity that is per se unlawful under \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 antitrust claim cannot prevail. Accordingly, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.
