SCHRIRO, DIRECTOR, ARIZONA DEPARTMENT OF CORRECTIONS v. SUMMERLIN

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 03\xe2\x80\x93526. Argued April 19, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 24, 2004

\n

Respondent was convicted of first-degree murder and sentenced to death under Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s capital sentencing scheme then in effect, which authorized the trial judge, rather than the jury, to determine the presence of aggravating circumstances that make the defendant eligible for the death sentence. The State Supreme Court affirmed on direct review. While respondent\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent federal habeas case was pending in the Ninth Circuit, this Court decided that Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466, 490, required the existence of an aggravating factor to be proved to a jury rather than a judge under Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s scheme. Ring v. Arizona, 536 U. S. 584, 603\xe2\x80\x93609. The Ninth Circuit invalidated respondent\xe2\x80\x99s death sentence, rejecting the argument that Ring did not apply because respondent\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and sentence had become final on direct review before Ring was decided.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Ring does not apply retroactively to cases already final on direct review. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0A \xe2\x80\x9cnew rule\xe2\x80\x9d resulting from a decision of this Court applies to convictions that are already final only in limited circumstances. New substantive rules generally apply retroactively, but new procedural rules generally do not\xe2\x80\x94only \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98watershed rules of criminal procedure\xe2\x80\x99 implicating the fundamental fairness and accuracy of the criminal proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d are given retroactive effect. Saffle v. Parks, 494 U. S. 484, 495. Such a rule must be one \xe2\x80\x9cwithout which the likelihood of an accurate conviction is seriously diminished.\xe2\x80\x9d Teague v. Lane, 489 U. S. 288, 313. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Ring\xe2\x80\x99s holding is properly classified as procedural. It did not alter the range of conduct or the class of persons subject to the death penalty in Arizona, but only the method of determining whether the defendant engaged in that conduct. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Ring did not announce a watershed rule of criminal procedure. This Court cannot confidently say that judicial factfinding seriously diminishes accuracy. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

341 F.\xc2\xa03d 1082, reversed and remanded.

\n

DORA B. SCHRIRO, DIRECTOR, ARIZONA DEPART-\nMENT OF CORRECTIONS, PETITIONER v.\nWARREN WESLEY SUMMERLIN

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 24, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, we decide whether Ring v. Arizona, 536 U. S. 584 (2002), applies retroactively to cases already final on direct review.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In April 1981, Finance America employee Brenna Bailey disappeared while on a house call to discuss an outstanding debt with respondent Warren Summerlin\xe2\x80\x99s wife. That evening, an anonymous woman (later identified as respondent\xe2\x80\x99s mother-in-law) called the police and accused respondent of murdering Bailey. Bailey\xe2\x80\x99s partially nude body, her skull crushed, was found the next morning in the trunk of her car, wrapped in a bedspread from respondent\xe2\x80\x99s home. Police arrested respondent and later overheard him make incriminating remarks to his wife.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent was convicted of first-degree murder and sexual assault. Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s capital sentencing provisions in effect at the time authorized the death penalty if one of several enumerated aggravating factors was present. See Ariz. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x93703(E), (F) (West 1978), as amended by Act of May 1, 1979 Ariz. Sess. Laws ch. 144. Whether those aggravating factors existed, however, was determined by the trial judge rather than by a jury. \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x93703(B). In this case the judge, after a hearing, found two aggravating factors: a prior felony conviction involving use or threatened use of violence, \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x93703(F)(2), and commission of the offense in an especially heinous, cruel, or depraved manner, \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x93703(F)(6). Finding no mitigating factors, the judge imposed the death sentence. The Arizona Supreme Court affirmed on direct review. State v. Summerlin, 138 Ariz. 426, 675 P.\xc2\xa02d 686 (1983).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Protracted state and federal habeas proceedings followed. While respondent\xe2\x80\x99s case was pending in the Ninth Circuit, we decided Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466 (2000), and Ring v. Arizona, supra. In Apprendi, we interpreted the constitutional due-process and jury-trial guarantees to require that, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ther than the fact of a prior conviction, any fact that increases the penalty for a crime beyond the prescribed statutory maximum must be submitted to a jury, and proved beyond a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U. S., at 490. In Ring, we applied this principle to a death sentence imposed under the Arizona sentencing scheme at issue here. We concluded that, because Arizona law authorized the death penalty only if an aggravating factor was present, Apprendi required the existence of such a factor to be proved to a jury rather than to a judge. 536 U. S., at 603\xe2\x80\x93609.[Footnote 1] We specifically overruled our earlier decision in Walton v. Arizona, 497 U. S. 639 (1990), which had upheld an Arizona death sentence against a similar challenge. 536 U. S., at 609.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit, relying on Ring, invalidated respondent\xe2\x80\x99s death sentence. Summerlin v. Stewart, 341 F.\xc2\xa03d 1082, 1121 (2003) (en banc).[Footnote 2] It rejected the argument that Ring did not apply because respondent\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and sentence had become final on direct review before Ring was decided. We granted certiorari. 540 U. S. 1045 (2003).[Footnote 3]

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When a decision of this Court results in a \xe2\x80\x9cnew rule,\xe2\x80\x9d that rule applies to all criminal cases still pending on direct review. Griffith v. Kentucky, 479 U. S. 314, 328 (1987). As to convictions that are already final, however, the rule applies only in limited circumstances. New substantive rules generally apply retroactively. This includes decisions that narrow the scope of a criminal statute by interpreting its terms, see Bousley v. United States, 523 U. S. 614, 620\xe2\x80\x93621 (1998), as well as constitutional determinations that place particular conduct or persons covered by the statute beyond the State\xe2\x80\x99s power to punish, see Saffle v. Parks, 494 U. S. 484, 494\xe2\x80\x93495 (1990); Teague v. Lane, 489 U. S. 288, 311 (1989) (plurality opinion).[Footnote 4] Such rules apply retroactively because they \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily carry a significant risk that a defendant stands convicted of \xe2\x80\x98an act that the law does not make criminal\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or faces a punishment that the law cannot impose upon him. Bousley, supra, at 620 (quoting Davis v. United States, 417 U. S. 333, 346 (1974)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0New rules of procedure, on the other hand, generally do not apply retroactively. They do not produce a class of persons convicted of conduct the law does not make criminal, but merely raise the possibility that someone convicted with use of the invalidated procedure might have been acquitted otherwise. Because of this more speculative connection to innocence, we give retroactive effect to only a small set of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98watershed rules of criminal procedure\xe2\x80\x99 implicating the fundamental fairness and accuracy of the criminal proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Saffle, supra, at 495 (quoting Teague, 489 U. S., at 311). That a new procedural rule is \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental\xe2\x80\x9d in some abstract sense is not enough; the rule must be one \xe2\x80\x9cwithout which the likelihood of an accurate conviction is seriously diminished.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 313 (emphasis added). This class of rules is extremely narrow, and \xe2\x80\x9cit is unlikely that any \xe2\x80\xa6 \xe2\x80\x98ha[s] yet to emerge.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Tyler v. Cain, 533 U. S. 656, 667, n.\xc2\xa07 (2001) (quoting Sawyer v. Smith, 497 U. S. 227, 243 (1990)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit agreed with the State that Ring announced a new rule. 341 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1108\xe2\x80\x931109. It nevertheless applied the rule retroactively to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s case, relying on two alternative theories: first, that it was substantive rather than procedural; and second, that it was a \xe2\x80\x9cwatershed\xe2\x80\x9d procedural rule entitled to retroactive effect. We consider each theory in turn.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A rule is substantive rather than procedural if it alters the range of conduct or the class of persons that the law punishes. See Bousley, supra, at 620\xe2\x80\x93621 (rule \xe2\x80\x9chold[s] that a \xe2\x80\xa6 statute does not reach certain conduct\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmake[s] conduct criminal\xe2\x80\x9d); Saffle, supra, at 495 (rule \xe2\x80\x9cdecriminalize[s] a class of conduct [or] prohibit[s] the imposition of \xe2\x80\xa6 punishment on a particular class of persons\xe2\x80\x9d). In contrast, rules that regulate only the manner of determining the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpability are procedural. See Bousley, supra, at 620.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judged by this standard, Ring\xe2\x80\x99s holding is properly classified as procedural. Ring held that \xe2\x80\x9ca sentencing judge, sitting without a jury, [may not] find an aggravating circumstance necessary for imposition of the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d 536 U. S., at 609. Rather, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Sixth Amendment requires that [those circumstances] be found by a jury.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This holding did not alter the range of conduct Arizona law subjected to the death penalty. It could not have; it rested entirely on the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s jury-trial guarantee, a provision that has nothing to do with the range of conduct a State may criminalize. Instead, Ring altered the range of permissible methods for determining whether a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct is punishable by death, requiring that a jury rather than a judge find the essential facts bearing on punishment. Rules that allocate decisionmaking authority in this fashion are prototypical procedural rules, a conclusion we have reached in numerous other contexts. See Gasperini v. Center for Humanities, Inc., 518 U. S. 415, 426 (1996) (Erie doctrine); Landgraf v. USI Film Products, 511 U. S. 244, 280\xe2\x80\x93281 (1994) (antiretroactivity presumption); Dobbert v. Florida, 432 U. S. 282, 293\xe2\x80\x93294 (1977) (Ex\xc2\xa0Post Facto Clause).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent nevertheless argues that Ring is substantive because it modified the elements of the offense for which he was convicted. He relies on our statement in Ring that, \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated aggravating factors operate as \xe2\x80\x98the functional equivalent of an element of a greater offense,\xe2\x80\x99 the Sixth Amendment requires that they be found by a jury.\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 536 U. S., at 609 (citation omitted); see also Sattazahn v. Pennsylvania, 537 U. S. 101, 111 (2003) (plurality opinion). The Ninth Circuit agreed, concluding that Ring \xe2\x80\x9creposition[ed] Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s aggravating factors as elements of the separate offense of capital murder and reshap[ed] the structure of Arizona murder law.\xe2\x80\x9d 341 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1105.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A decision that modifies the elements of an offense is normally substantive rather than procedural. New elements alter the range of conduct the statute punishes, rendering some formerly unlawful conduct lawful or vice versa. See Bousley, 523 U. S., at 620\xe2\x80\x93621. But that is not what Ring did; the range of conduct punished by death in Arizona was the same before Ring as after. Ring held that, because Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s statutory aggravators restricted (as a matter of state law) the class of death-eligible defendants, those aggravators effectively were elements for federal constitutional purposes, and so were subject to the procedural requirements the Constitution attaches to trial of elements. 536 U. S., at 609. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that, because Arizona has made a certain fact essential to the death penalty, that fact must be found by a jury, is not the same as this Court\xe2\x80\x99s making a certain fact essential to the death penalty. The former was a procedural holding; the latter would be substantive. The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Ring nonetheless \xe2\x80\x9creshap[ed] the structure of Arizona murder law,\xe2\x80\x9d 341 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1105, is particularly remarkable in the face of the Arizona Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s previous conclusion to the contrary. See State v. Towery, 204 Ariz. 386, 390\xe2\x80\x93391, 64 P.\xc2\xa03d 828, 832\xe2\x80\x93833, cert. dism\xe2\x80\x99d, 539 U. S. 986 (2003).[Footnote 5]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent argues in the alternative that Ring falls under the retroactivity exception for \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98watershed rules of criminal procedure\xe2\x80\x99 implicating the fundamental fairness and accuracy of the criminal proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Saffle, 494 U. S., at 495 (quoting Teague, 489 U. S., at 311). He offers several reasons why juries are more accurate factfinders, including the tendency of group deliberation to suppress individual eccentricities; the jury\xe2\x80\x99s protection from exposure to inadmissible evidence; and its better representation of the common sense of the community. The Ninth Circuit majority added others, including the claim that a judge might be too acclimated to capital sentencing and that he might be swayed by political pressure. 341 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1109\xe2\x80\x931116. Respondent further notes that common-law authorities praised the jury\xe2\x80\x99s factfinding ability. See, e.g., 3 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 380 (1768); Georgia v. Brailsford, 3 Dall. 1, 4 (1794) (jury charge of Jay, C. J.).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question here is not, however, whether the Framers believed that juries are more accurate factfinders than judges (perhaps so\xe2\x80\x94they certainly thought juries were more independent, see Blakely v. Washington, ante, at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9312)). Nor is the question whether juries actually are more accurate factfinders than judges (again, perhaps so). Rather, the question is whether judicial factfinding so \xe2\x80\x9cseriously diminishe[s]\xe2\x80\x9d accuracy that there is an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98impermissibly large risk\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of punishing conduct the law does not reach. Teague, supra, at 312\xe2\x80\x93313 (quoting Desist v. United States, 394 U. S. 244, 262 (1969) (Harlan, J., dissenting)) (emphasis added). The evidence is simply too equivocal to support that conclusion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, for every argument why juries are more accurate factfinders, there is another why they are less accurate. The Ninth Circuit dissent noted several, including juries\xe2\x80\x99 tendency to become confused over legal standards and to be influenced by emotion or philosophical predisposition. 341 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1129\xe2\x80\x931131 (opinion of Rawlinson, J.) (citing, inter alia, Eisenberg & Wells, Deadly Confusion: Juror Instructions in Capital Cases, 79 Cornell L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1 (1993); Garvey, The Emotional Economy of Capital Sentencing, 75 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 26 (2000); and Bowers, Sandys, & Steiner, Foreclosed Impartiality in Capital Sentencing: Jurors\xe2\x80\x99 Predispositions, Guilt-Trial Experience, and Premature Decision Making, 83 Cornell L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1476 (1998)). Members of this Court have opined that judicial sentencing may yield more consistent results because of judges\xe2\x80\x99 greater experience. See Proffitt v. Florida, 428 U. S. 242, 252 (1976) (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.). Finally, the mixed reception that the right to jury trial has been given in other countries, see Vidmar, The Jury Elsewhere in the World, in World Jury Systems 421\xe2\x80\x93447 (N. Vidmar ed. 2000), though irrelevant to the meaning and continued existence of that right under our Constitution, surely makes it implausible that judicial factfinding so \xe2\x80\x9cseriously diminishe[s]\xe2\x80\x9d accuracy as to produce an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98impermissibly large risk\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of injustice. When so many presumably reasonable minds continue to disagree over whether juries are better factfinders at all, we cannot confidently say that judicial factfinding seriously diminishes accuracy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decision in DeStefano v. Woods, 392 U. S. 631 (1968) (per curiam), is on point. There we refused to give retroactive effect to Duncan v. Louisiana, 391 U. S. 145 (1968), which applied the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s jury-trial guarantee to the States. While DeStefano was decided under our pre-Teague retroactivity framework, its reasoning is germane. We noted that, although \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to jury trial generally tends to prevent arbitrariness and repression[,] \xe2\x80\xa6 \xe2\x80\x98[w]e would not assert \xe2\x80\xa6 that every criminal trial\xe2\x80\x94or any particular trial\xe2\x80\x94held before a judge alone is unfair or that a defendant may never be as fairly treated by a judge as he would be by a jury.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 392 U. S., at 633\xe2\x80\x93634 (quoting Duncan, supra, at 158). We concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he values implemented by the right to jury trial would not measurably be served by requiring retrial of all persons convicted in the past by procedures not consistent with the Sixth Amendment right to jury trial.\xe2\x80\x9d 392 U. S., at 634. If under DeStefano a trial held entirely without a jury was not impermissibly inaccurate, it is hard to see how a trial in which a judge finds only aggravating factors could be.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent contends that juries are more accurate because they better reflect community standards in deciding whether, for example, a murder was heinous, cruel, or depraved. Post, at 4 (opinion of Breyer, J.). But the statute here does not condition death eligibility on whether the offense is heinous, cruel, or depraved as determined by community standards. See Ariz. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x93703(F)(6) (West 1978). It is easy to find enhanced accuracy in jury determination when one redefines the statute\xe2\x80\x99s substantive scope in such manner as to ensure that result. The dissent also advances several variations on the theme that death is different (or rather, \xe2\x80\x9cdramatically different,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 6). Much of this analysis is not an application of Teague, but a rejection of it, in favor of a broader endeavor to \xe2\x80\x9cbalance competing considerations,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 4. Even were we inclined to revisit Teague in this fashion, we would not agree with the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s conclusions. Finally, the dissent notes that, in DeStefano, we considered factors other than enhanced accuracy that are no longer relevant after Teague. See post, at 8. But we held in that case that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll three factors favor only prospective application of the rule.\xe2\x80\x9d 392 U. S., at 633 (emphasis added). Thus, the result would have been the same even if enhanced accuracy were the sole criterion for retroactivity.[Footnote 6]

\n

* * *

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The right to jury trial is fundamental to our system of criminal procedure, and States are bound to enforce the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s guarantees as we interpret them. But it does not follow that, when a criminal defendant has had a full trial and one round of appeals in which the State faithfully applied the Constitution as we understood it at the time, he may nevertheless continue to litigate his claims indefinitely in hopes that we will one day have a change of heart. Ring announced a new procedural rule that does not apply retroactively to cases already final on direct review. The contrary judgment of the Ninth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Stevens, Justice Souter, and Justice Ginsburg join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Ring v. Arizona, 536 U. S. 584 (2002), this Court held that a jury, not a judge, must make the findings necessary to qualify a person for punishment by death. In my view, that holding amounts to a \xe2\x80\x9cwatershed\xe2\x80\x9d procedural ruling that a federal habeas court must apply when considering a constitutional challenge to a \xe2\x80\x9cfinal\xe2\x80\x9d death sentence\xe2\x80\x94i.e., a sentence that was already final on direct review when Ring was decided.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Teague v. Lane, 489 U. S. 288 (1989) (plurality opinion), sets forth the relevant retroactivity criteria. A new procedural rule applies retroactively in habeas proceedings if the new procedure is (1) \xe2\x80\x9cimplicit in the concept of ordered liberty,\xe2\x80\x9d implicating \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental fairness,\xe2\x80\x9d and (2) \xe2\x80\x9ccentral to an accurate determination of innocence or guilt,\xe2\x80\x9d such that its absence \xe2\x80\x9ccreates an impermissibly large risk that the innocent will be convicted.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 311\xe2\x80\x93313 (internal quotation marks omitted). In the context of a death sentence, where the matter is not one of \xe2\x80\x9cinnocence or guilt,\xe2\x80\x9d the second criterion asks whether the new procedure is \xe2\x80\x9ccentral to an accurate determination\xe2\x80\x9d that death is a legally appropriate punishment. Id., at 313 (emphasis added). See Sawyer v. Smith, 497 U. S. 227, 244 (1990); O\xe2\x80\x99Dell v. Netherland, 521 U. S. 151, 171, n.\xc2\xa03 (1997) (Stevens, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority does not deny that Ring meets the first criterion, that its holding is \xe2\x80\x9cimplicit in the concept of ordered liberty.\xe2\x80\x9d Cf. Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466, 499 (2000) (Scalia, J., concurring) (absent Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s rule jury trial right \xe2\x80\x9chas no intelligible content\xe2\x80\x9d); Ring, supra, at 610 (Scalia, J., concurring) (Apprendi involves the fundamental meaning of the jury trial guarantee); Blakely v. Washington, ante, at __ (slip op., at 5) (tracing Apprendi\xe2\x80\x99s conception of the jury trial right back to Blackstone); Duncan v. Louisiana, 391 U. S. 145, 157\xe2\x80\x93158 (1968) (Sixth Amendment jury trial guarantee is a \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental right\xe2\x80\x9d). Rather, the majority focuses on whether Ring meets the second criterion: Is its rule \xe2\x80\x9ccentral to an accurate determination\xe2\x80\x9d that death is a legally appropriate punishment? Teague, supra, at 313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As I explained in my separate concurrence in Ring, I believe the Eighth Amendment demands the use of a jury in capital sentencing because a death sentence must reflect a community-based judgment that the sentence constitutes proper retribution. See 536 U. S., at 614 (opinion concurring in judgment); see also Harris v. Alabama, 513 U. S. 504, 515\xe2\x80\x93526 (1995) (Stevens, J., dissenting); Spaziano v. Florida, 468 U. S. 447, 467\xe2\x80\x93490 (1984) (Stevens, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). And a jury is significantly more likely than a judge to \xe2\x80\x9cexpress the conscience of the community on the ultimate question of life or death.\xe2\x80\x9d Witherspoon v. Illinois, 391 U. S. 510, 519 (1968). As Justice Stevens has pointed out,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cJuries\xe2\x80\x94comprised as they are of a fair cross section of the community\xe2\x80\x94are more representative institutions than is the judiciary; they reflect more accurately the composition and experiences of the community as a whole, and inevitably make decisions based on community values more reliably, than can that segment of the community that is selected for service on the bench.\xe2\x80\x9d Spaziano, supra, at 486\xe2\x80\x93487 (footnote omitted).

\n

\nOn this view of the matter, the right to have jury sentencing in the capital context is both a fundamental aspect of constitutional liberty and also significantly more likely to produce an accurate assessment of whether death is the appropriate punishment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But my view is not the Ring majority\xe2\x80\x99s view. The majority held only that the jury must decide whether the special aggravating factors that make the offender eligible for death are present. 536 U. S., at 603\xe2\x80\x93609. And it rested its decision that a jury, not a judge, must make that determination upon the Court\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment holding in Apprendi that \xe2\x80\x9cany fact that increases the penalty for a crime beyond the prescribed statutory maximum must be submitted to a jury, and proved beyond a reasonable doubt,\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U. S., at 490.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the majority says that Ring\xe2\x80\x99s Apprendi-related rule cannot satisfy Teague\xe2\x80\x99s accuracy-enhancing requirement, for two reasons. First, it points out that for \xe2\x80\x9cevery argument why juries are more accurate factfinders, there is another why they are less accurate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. Hence, one cannot say \xe2\x80\x9cconfidently\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial factfinding seriously diminishes accuracy.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8 (emphasis in original). Second, it relies on DeStefano v. Woods, 392 U. S. 631 (1968) (per curiam), the case in which this Court considered whether Duncan v. Louisiana, supra, which extended the Sixth Amendment jury trial guarantee to the States, should apply retroactively. The Court decided that Duncan should not have retroactive effect. \xe2\x80\x9cIf,\xe2\x80\x9d the majority concludes, \xe2\x80\x9ca trial held entirely without a jury was not impermissibly inaccurate, it is hard to see how a trial in which a judge finds only aggravating factors could be.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority, however, overlooks three additional considerations that lead me to the opposite conclusion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the factfinder\xe2\x80\x99s role in determining the applicability of aggravating factors in a death case is a special role that can involve, not simply the finding of brute facts, but also the making of death-related, community-based value judgments. The leading single aggravator charged in Arizona, for example, requires the factfinder to decide whether the crime was committed in an \xe2\x80\x9cespecially heinous, cruel, or depraved manner.\xe2\x80\x9d Ariz. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x93703(F)(6) (West Supp. 2003); see Office of Attorney General, State of Arizona, Capital Case Commission Final Report (2002). Three of the other four Ring-affected States use a similar aggravator. See Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x931.3\xe2\x80\x931201(5)(j) (2003); Idaho Code \xc2\xa719\xe2\x80\x932515(h)(5) (Supp. 2003); Neb. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa729\xe2\x80\x932523(1)(d) (1995). Words like \xe2\x80\x9cespecially heinous,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccruel,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cdepraved\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94particularly when asked in the context of a death sentence proceeding\xe2\x80\x94require reference to community-based standards, standards that incorporate values. (Indeed, Nebraska\xe2\x80\x99s standard explicitly asks the factfinder to assess the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in light of \xe2\x80\x9cordinary standards of morality and intelligence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.) A jury is better equipped than a judge to identify and to apply those standards accurately. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, Teague\xe2\x80\x99s basic purpose strongly favors retroactive application of Ring\xe2\x80\x99s rule. Teague\xe2\x80\x99s retroactivity principles reflect the Court\xe2\x80\x99s effort to balance competing considerations. See 489 U. S., at 309\xe2\x80\x93313; Mackey v. United States, 401 U. S. 667, 675 (1971) (Harlan, J., concurring in two judgments and dissenting in one); Desist v. United States, 394 U. S. 244, 256 (1969) (Harlan, J., dissenting). On the one hand, interests related to certain of the Great Writ\xe2\x80\x99s basic objectives\xe2\x80\x94protecting the innocent against erroneous conviction or punishment and assuring fundamentally fair procedures\xe2\x80\x94favor applying a new procedural rule retroactively. Teague, supra, at 312\xe2\x80\x93313; Mackey, 401 U. S., at 693\xe2\x80\x93694. So too does the legal system\xe2\x80\x99s commitment to \xe2\x80\x9cequal justice\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., to \xe2\x80\x9cassur[ing] a uniformity of ultimate treatment among prisoners.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 689.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Where death-sentence-related factfinding is at issue, these considerations have unusually strong force. This Court has made clear that in a capital case \xe2\x80\x9cthe Eighth Amendment requires a greater degree of accuracy . . . than would be true in a noncapital case.\xe2\x80\x9d Gilmore v. Taylor, 508 U. S. 333, 342 (1993). Hence, the risk of error that the law can tolerate is correspondingly diminished. At the same time, the \xe2\x80\x9cqualitative difference of death from all other punishments\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94namely, its severity and irrevocability\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9crequires a correspondingly greater degree of scrutiny of the capital sentencing determination\xe2\x80\x9d than of other criminal judgments. California v. Ramos, 463 U. S. 992, 998\xe2\x80\x93999 (1983); see also Spaziano, 468 U. S., at 468 (Stevens, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (the Eighth Amendment mandates special safeguards to ensure that death is \xe2\x80\x9ca justified response to a given offense\xe2\x80\x9d); Ake v. Oklahoma, 470 U. S. 68, 87 (1985) (Burger, C.\xc2\xa0J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn capital cases the finality of the sentence imposed warrants protections that may or may not be required in other cases\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consider, too, the law\xe2\x80\x99s commitment to uniformity. Mackey, supra, at 689. Is treatment \xe2\x80\x9cuniform\xe2\x80\x9d when two offenders each have been sentenced to death through the use of procedures that we now know violate the Constitution\xe2\x80\x94but one is allowed to go to his death while the other receives a new, constitutionally proper sentencing proceeding? Outside the capital sentencing context, one might understand the nature of the difference that the word \xe2\x80\x9cfinality\xe2\x80\x9d implies: One prisoner is already serving a final sentence, the other\xe2\x80\x99s has not yet begun. But a death sentence is different in that it seems to be, and it is, an entirely future event\xe2\x80\x94an event not yet undergone by either prisoner. And in respect to that event, both prisoners are, in every important respect, in the same position. I understand there is a \xe2\x80\x9cfinality-based\xe2\x80\x9d difference. But given the dramatically different nature of death, that difference diminishes in importance.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Certainly the ordinary citizen will not understand the difference. That citizen will simply witness two individuals, both sentenced through the use of unconstitutional procedures, one individual going to his death, the other saved, all through an accident of timing. How can the Court square this spectacle with what it has called the \xe2\x80\x9cvital importance to the defendant and to the commu-\nnity that any decision to impose the death sentence\nbe, and appear to be, based on reason\xe2\x80\x9d? Beck v. Alabama, 447 U. S. 625, 637\xe2\x80\x93638 (1980) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s observation, in his concurring opinion in Ring, underscores the point. He wrote there that \xe2\x80\x9cthe repeated spectacle of a man\xe2\x80\x99s going to his death because a judge found that an aggravating factor existed\xe2\x80\x9d would undermine \xe2\x80\x9cour people\xe2\x80\x99s traditional . . . veneration for the protection of the jury in criminal cases.\xe2\x80\x9d 536 U. S., at 612 (emphasis in original). If that is so, it is equally so whether the judge found that aggravating factor before or after Ring.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the other hand, Teague recognizes that important interests argue against, and indeed generally forbid, retroactive application of new procedural rules. These interests include the \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in insuring that there will at some point be the certainty that comes with an end to litigation\xe2\x80\x9d; the desirability of assuring that \xe2\x80\x9cattention will ultimately be focused not on whether a conviction was free from error but rather on whether the prisoner can be restored to a useful place in the community\xe2\x80\x9d; and the fact that society does not have endless resources to spend upon retrials, which (where witnesses have become unavailable and other evidence stale) may well produce unreliable results. Mackey, supra, at 690\xe2\x80\x93691 (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Teague, 489 U. S., at 308\xe2\x80\x93310. Comity interests and respect for state autonomy point in the same direction. See id., at 308; Engle v. Isaac, 456 U. S. 107, 128, n. 33 (1982).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Certain of these interests are unusually weak where capital sentencing proceedings are at issue. Retroactivity here, for example, would not require inordinate expenditure of state resources. A decision making Ring retroactive would affect approximately 110 individuals on death row. Court Hears Arguments in Latest Death Case, 231 N.\xc2\xa0Y. L.\xc2\xa0J. 5 (2004). This number, however large in absolute terms, is small compared with the approximately 1.2 million individuals presently confined in state prisons. U. S. Dept. of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics Bulletin 2 (May 2004). Consequently, the impact on resources is likely to be much less than if a rule affecting the ordinary criminal process were made retroactive.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Further, where the issue is \xe2\x80\x9clife or death,\xe2\x80\x9d the concern that \xe2\x80\x9cattention . . . ultimately\xe2\x80\x9d should be focused \xe2\x80\x9con whether the prisoner can be restored to a useful place in the community\xe2\x80\x9d is barely relevant. Mackey, supra, at 690 (internal quotation marks omitted). Finally, I believe we should discount ordinary finality interests in a death case, for those interests are comparative in nature and death-related collateral proceedings, in any event, may stretch on for many years regardless. Cf. Teague, supra, at 321, n. 3 (Stevens, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cA major reason that Justice Harlan espoused limited retroactivity in collateral proceedings was the interest in making convictions final, an interest that is wholly inapplicable to the capital sentencing context\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, DeStefano v. Woods, 392 U. S. 631 (1968) (per curiam), fails to give the majority the support for which it hopes. DeStefano did decide that Duncan\xe2\x80\x99s holding\xe2\x80\x94that the Sixth Amendment jury trial right applies to the States\xe2\x80\x94should not have retroactive effect. But the Court decided DeStefano before Teague. And it explicitly took into account \xe2\x80\x9c(a) the purpose to be served by the new standards, (b) the extent of the reliance by law enforcement authorities on the old standards, and (c) the effect on the administration of justice of a retroactive application of the new standards.\xe2\x80\x9d 392 U. S., at 633 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The latter two factors, \xe2\x80\x9creliance\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ceffect on the administration of justice,\xe2\x80\x9d argued strongly against retroactivity. Retroactivity there, unlike here, would have thrown the prison doors open wide\xe2\x80\x94at least in Louisiana and possibly in other States as well. Id., at 634. The Court believed that the first factor\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthe purpose to be served by the new standards\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94also favored prospective application only. But the Court described that purpose broadly, as \xe2\x80\x9cprevent[ing] arbitrariness and repression\xe2\x80\x9d; it recognized that some judge-only trials might have been fair; and it concluded that the values served by the jury trial guarantee \xe2\x80\x9cwould not measurably be served by requiring retrial of all persons convicted in the past\xe2\x80\x9d without a jury. Id., at 633\xe2\x80\x93634 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By contrast, this case involves only a small subclass of defendants deprived of jury trial rights, the relevant harm within that subclass is more widespread, the administration of justice problem is far less serious, and the reliance interest less weighty. For these reasons, I believe the DeStefano Court would have come out differently had it been considering Ring\xe2\x80\x99s rule. Insofar as DeStefano has any relevance here, it highlights the importance, when making retroactivity decisions, of taking account of the considerations that underlie Teague\xe2\x80\x99s categorical rules. And, as shown above, those considerations argue in favor of retroactivity in this case. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As I have pointed out, the majority does not deny that Ring\xe2\x80\x99s rule makes some contribution to greater accuracy. It simply is unable to say \xe2\x80\x9cconfidently\xe2\x80\x9d that the absence of Ring\xe2\x80\x99s rule creates an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cimpermissibly large risk\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that the death penalty was improperly imposed. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. For the reasons stated, I believe that the risk is one that the law need not and should not tolerate. Judged in light of Teague\xe2\x80\x99s basic purpose, Ring\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that a jury, and not a judge, must apply the death sentence aggravators announces a watershed rule of criminal procedure that should be applied retroactively in habeas proceedings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I respectfully dissent.
