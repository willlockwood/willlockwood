HALL v. FLORIDA

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of florida

\n

No. 12\xe2\x80\x9310882.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 3, 2014\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x94Decided May 27, 2014

\n

After this Court held that the Eighth and\n \nFourteenth Amendments forbid the execution of persons with intellectual disability, see Atkins v. Virginia,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304\n, Hall asked a Florida state court to vacate his sentence, presenting evidence that included an IQ test score of 71. The court denied his motion, determining that a Florida statute mandated that he show an IQ score of 70 or below before being permitted to present any additional intellectual disability evidence. The State Supreme Court rejected Hall\xe2\x80\x99s appeal, finding the State\xe2\x80\x99s 70-point threshold constitutional.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The State\xe2\x80\x99s threshold requirement, as interpreted by the Florida Supreme Court, is unconstitutional. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0The\n \nEighth Amendment, which \xe2\x80\x9creaffirms the duty of the government to respect the dignity of all persons,\xe2\x80\x9d Roper v. Simmons,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551\n, prohibits the execution of persons with intellectual disability. No legitimate penological purpose is served by executing the intellectually disabled. Atkins, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 317, 320. Prohibiting such executions also protects the integrity of the trial process for individuals who face \xe2\x80\x9ca special risk of wrongful execution\xe2\x80\x9d because they are more likely to give false confessions, are often poor witnesses, and are less able to give meaningful assistance to their counsel. Id., at 320\xe2\x80\x93321. In determining whether Florida\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual disability definition implements these principles and Atkins\xe2\x80\x99 holding, it is proper to consider the psychiatric and professional studies that elaborate on the purpose and meaning of IQ scores and how the scores relate to Atkins, and to consider how the several States have implemented Atkins. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule disregards established medical practice. On its face, Florida\xe2\x80\x99s statute could be consistent with the views of the medical community discussed in Atkins and with the conclusions reached here. It defines intellectual disability as the existence of concurrent deficits in intellectual and adaptive functioning, long the defining characteristic of intellectual disability. See Atkins, supra, at 308. And nothing in the statute precludes Florida from considering an IQ test\xe2\x80\x99s standard error of measurement (SEM), a statistical fact reflecting the test\xe2\x80\x99s inherent imprecision and acknowledging that an individual score is best understood as a range, e.g., five points on either side of the recorded score. As interpreted by the Florida Supreme Court, however, Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule disregards established medical practice in two interrelated ways: It takes an IQ score as final and conclusive evidence of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual capacity, when experts would consider other evidence; and it relies on a purportedly scientific measurement of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s abilities, while refusing to recognize that measurement\xe2\x80\x99s inherent imprecision. While professionals have long agreed that IQ test scores should be read as a range, Florida uses the test score as a fixed number, thus barring further consideration of other relevant evidence, e.g., deficits in adaptive functioning, including evidence of past performance, environment, and upbringing. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (c)\xc2\xa0The rejection of a strict 70-point cutoff in the vast majority of States and a \xe2\x80\x9cconsistency in the trend,\xe2\x80\x9d Roper, supra, at 567, toward recognizing the SEM provide strong evidence of consensus that society does not regard this strict cutoff as proper or humane. At most, nine States mandate a strict IQ score cutoff at 70. Thus, in 41 States, an individual in Hall\xe2\x80\x99s position would not be deemed automatically eligible for the death penalty. The direction of change has been consistent. Since Atkins, many States have passed legislation to comply with the constitutional requirement that persons with intellectual disability not be executed. Two of those States appear to set a strict cutoff at 70, but at least 11 others have either abolished the death penalty or passed legislation allowing defendants to present additional intellectual disability evidence when their IQ score is above 70. Every state legislature, save one, to have considered the issue after Atkins and whose law has been interpreted by its courts has taken a position contrary to Florida\xe2\x80\x99s. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (d)\xc2\xa0Atkins acknowledges the inherent error in IQ testing and provides substantial guidance on the definition of intellectual disability. The States play a critical role in advancing the protections of Atkins and providing this Court with an understanding of how intellectual disability should be measured and assessed, but Atkins did not give them unfettered discretion to define the full scope of the constitutional protection. Clinical definitions for intellectual disability which, by their express terms, rejected a strict IQ test score cutoff at 70, and which have long included the SEM, were a fundamental premise of Atkins. See 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 309, nn. 3, 5. A fleeting mention of Florida in a citation listing States that had outlawed the execution of the intellectually disabled, id., at 315, did not signal the Atkins Court\xe2\x80\x99s approval of the State\xe2\x80\x99s current understanding of its law, which had not yet been interpreted by the Florida Supreme Court to require a strict 70-point cutoff. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (e)\xc2\xa0When a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s IQ test score falls within the test\xe2\x80\x99s acknowledged and inherent margin of error, the defendant must be able to present additional evidence of intellectual disability, including testimony regarding adaptive deficits. This legal determination of intellectual disability is distinct from a medical diagnosis but is informed by the medical community\xe2\x80\x99s diagnostic framework, which is of particular help here, where no alternative intellectual disability definition is presented, and where this Court and the States have placed substantial reliance on the medical profession\xe2\x80\x99s expertise. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9322.

FREDDIE LEE HALL, PETITIONER v. FLORIDA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of florida

\n

[May 27, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court has held that the Eighth and\n \nFourteenth Amendments to the Constitution forbid the execution of persons with intellectual disability. Atkins v. Virginia,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304,\n321 (2002)\n. Florida law defines intellectual disability to require an IQ test score of 70 or less. If, from test scores, a prisoner is deemed to have an IQ above 70, all further exploration of intellectual disability is foreclosed. This rigid rule, the Court now holds, creates an unacceptable risk that persons with intellectual disability will be executed, and thus is unconstitutional.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n On February 21, 1978, Freddie Lee Hall, petitioner here, and his accomplice, Mark Ruffin, kidnaped, beat, raped, and murdered Karol Hurst, a pregnant, 21-year-old newlywed. Afterward, Hall and Ruffin drove to a convenience store they planned to rob. In the parking lot of the store, they killed Lonnie Coburn, a sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s deputy who attempted to apprehend them. Hall received the death penalty for both murders, although his sentence for the Coburn murder was later reduced on account of insufficient evidence of premeditation. Hall v. Florida, 403 So.\xc2\xa02d 1319, 1321 (Fla. 1981) (per curiam).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Hall argues that he cannot be executed because of his intellectual disability. Previous opinions of this Court have employed the term \xe2\x80\x9cmental retardation.\xe2\x80\x9d This opinion uses the term \xe2\x80\x9cintellectual disability\xe2\x80\x9d to describe the identical phenomenon. See Rosa\xe2\x80\x99s Law,\n\n124Stat.\n2643 (changing entries in the U. S. Code from \xe2\x80\x9cmental retardation\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cintellectual disability\xe2\x80\x9d); Schalock et. al, The Renaming of Mental Retardation: Understanding the Change to the Term Intellectual Disability, 45 Intellectual & Developmental Disabilities 116 (2007). This change in terminology is approved and used in the latest edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, one of the basic texts used by psychiatrists and other experts; the manual is often referred to by its initials \xe2\x80\x9cDSM,\xe2\x80\x9d followed by its edition number, e.g., \xe2\x80\x9cDSM\xe2\x80\x935.\xe2\x80\x9d See American Psychiatric Association, Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders 33 (5th ed. 2013).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n When Hall was first sentenced, this Court had not yet ruled that the\n \nEighth Amendment prohibits States from imposing the death penalty on persons with intellectual disability. See Penry v. Lynaugh,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 302,\n340 (1989)\n. And at the time, Florida law did not consider intellectual disability as a statutory mitigating factor.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n After this Court held that capital defendants must be permitted to present nonstatutory mitigating evidence in death penalty proceedings, Hitchcock v. Dugger,\n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393\n\xe2\x80\x93399 (1987), Hall was resentenced. Hall then presented substantial and unchallenged evidence of intellectual disability. School records indicated that his teachers identified him on numerous occasions as \xe2\x80\x9c[m]entally retarded.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 482\xe2\x80\x93483. Hall had been prosecuted for a different, earlier crime. His lawyer in that matter later testified that the lawyer \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ouldn\xe2\x80\x99t really understand anything [Hall] said.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 480. And, with respect to the murder trial given him in this case, Hall\xe2\x80\x99s counsel recalled that Hall could not assist in his own defense because he had \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a mental . . . level much lower than his age,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d at best comparable to the lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s 4-year-old daughter. Brief for Petitioner 11. A number of medical clinicians testified that, in their professional opinion, Hall was \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly retarded,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 507; was \xe2\x80\x9cmentally retarded,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 517; and had levels of understanding \xe2\x80\x9ctypically [seen] with toddlers,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 523.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As explained below in more detail, an individual\xe2\x80\x99s ability or lack of ability to adapt or adjust to the requirements of daily life, and success or lack of success in doing so, is central to the framework followed by psychiatrists and other professionals in diagnosing intellectual disability. See DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 37. Hall\xe2\x80\x99s siblings testified that there was something \xe2\x80\x9cvery wrong\xe2\x80\x9d with him as a child. App. 466. Hall was \xe2\x80\x9cslow with speech and . . . slow to learn.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 490. He \xe2\x80\x9cwalked and talked long after his other brothers and sisters,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 461, and had \xe2\x80\x9cgreat difficulty forming his words,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 467.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Hall\xe2\x80\x99s upbringing appeared to make his deficits in adaptive functioning all the more severe. Hall was raised\xe2\x80\x94in the words of the sentencing judge\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cunder the most horrible family circumstances imaginable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53. Al-though \xe2\x80\x9c[t]eachers and siblings alike\xc2\xa0immediately recognized [Hall] to be significantly mentally retarded . . . [t]his retardation did not garner any sympathy from his mother, but rather caused much scorn to befall him.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 20. Hall was \xe2\x80\x9c[c]onstantly beaten because he was \xe2\x80\x98slow\xe2\x80\x99 or because he made simple mistakes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. His mother \xe2\x80\x9cwould strap [Hall] to his bed at night, with a rope thrown over a rafter. In the morning, she would awaken Hall by hoisting him up and whipping him with a belt, rope, or cord.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Hall was beaten \xe2\x80\x9cten or fifteen times a week sometimes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 477. His mother tied him \xe2\x80\x9cin a \xe2\x80\x98croaker\xe2\x80\x99 sack, swung it over a fire, and beat him,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cburied himin the sand up to his neck to \xe2\x80\x98strengthen his legs,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cheld a gun on Hall . . . while she poked [him] with sticks.\xe2\x80\x9d Hall v. Florida, 614 So.\xc2\xa02d 473, 480 (Fla. 1993) (Barkett, C. J., dissenting).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The jury, notwithstanding this testimony, voted to sentence Hall to death, and the sentencing court adopted the jury\xe2\x80\x99s recommendation. The court found that there was \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial evidence in the record\xe2\x80\x9d to support the finding that \xe2\x80\x9cFreddie Lee Hall has been mentally retarded his entire life.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 46. Yet the court also \xe2\x80\x9csuspect[ed] that the defense experts [were] guilty of some professional overkill,\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing of which the experts testified could explain how a psychotic, mentally-retarded, brain-damaged, learning-disabled, speech-impaired person could formulate a plan whereby a car was stolen and a convenience store was robbed.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 42. The sentencing court went on to state that, even assuming the expert testimony to be accurate, \xe2\x80\x9cthe learning disabilities, mental retardation, and other mental difficulties\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0cannot be used to justify, excuse or extenuate the moral culpability of the defendant in this cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 56. Hall was again sentenced to death. The Florida Supreme Court affirmed, concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cHall\xe2\x80\x99s argument that his mental retardation provided a pretense of moral or legal justification\xe2\x80\x9d had \xe2\x80\x9cno merit.\xe2\x80\x9d Hall, 614 So.\xc2\xa02d, at 478. Chief Justice Barkett dissented, arguing that executing a person with intellectual disability violated the State Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment. Id., at 481\xe2\x80\x93482.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In 2002, this Court ruled that the\n \nEighth Amendment prohibited the execution of persons with intellectual disability. Atkins v. Virginia, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 321. On November 30, 2004, Hall filed a motion claiming that he had intellectual disability and could not be executed. More than five years later, Florida held a hearing to consider Hall\xe2\x80\x99s motion. Hall again presented evidence of intellectual disability, including an IQ test score of 71. (Hall had received nine IQ evaluations in 40 years, with scores ranging from 60 to 80, Brief for Respondent 8, but the sentencing court excluded the two scores below 70 for evidentiary reasons, leaving only scores between 71 and 80. See App. 107; 109 So. 3d 704, 707 (Fla. 2012)). In response, Florida argued that Hall could not be found intellectually disabled because Florida law requires that, as a threshold matter, Hall show an IQ test score of 70 or below before presenting any additional evidence of his intellectual disability. App. 278\xe2\x80\x93279 (\xe2\x80\x9c[U]nder the law, if an I. Q. is above 70, a person is not mentally retarded\xe2\x80\x9d). The Florida Supreme Court rejected Hall\xe2\x80\x99s appeal and held that Florida\xe2\x80\x99s 70-point threshold was constitutional. 109 So. 3d, at 707\xe2\x80\x93708.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court granted certiorari. 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013).

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The\n \nEighth Amendment provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcessive bail shall not be required, nor excessive fines imposed, nor cruel and unusual punishments inflicted.\xe2\x80\x9d The\n \nFourteenth Amendment applies those restrictions to the States. Roper v. Simmons,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551,\n560 (2005)\n; Furman v. Georgia,\n\n408 U.\xc2\xa0S. 238\n\xe2\x80\x93240 (1972) (per curiam). \xe2\x80\x9cBy protecting even those convicted of heinous crimes, the\n \nEighth Amendment reaffirms the duty of the government to respect the dignity of all persons.\xe2\x80\x9d Roper, supra, at 560; see also Trop v. Dulles,\n\n356 U.\xc2\xa0S. 86,\n100 (1958)\n (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe basic concept underlying the\n \nEighth Amendment is nothing less than the dignity of man\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The\n \nEighth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cis not fastened to the obsolete but may acquire meaning as public opinion becomes enlightened by a humane justice.\xe2\x80\x9d Weems v. United States,\n\n217 U.\xc2\xa0S. 349,\n378 (1910)\n. To enforce the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s protection of human dignity, this Court looks to the \xe2\x80\x9cevolving standards of decency that mark the progress of a maturing society.\xe2\x80\x9d Trop, supra, at 101. The Eighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection of dignity reflects the Nation we have been, the Nation we are, and the Nation we aspire to be. This is to affirm that the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s constant, unyielding purpose must be to transmit the Constitution so that its precepts and guarantees retain their meaning and force.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The\n \nEighth Amendment prohibits certain punishments as a categorical matter. No natural-born citizen may be denaturalized. Ibid. No person may be sentenced to death for a crime committed as a juvenile. Roper, supra, at 578. And, as relevant for this case, persons with intellectual disability may not be executed. Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 321.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n No legitimate penological purpose is served by executing a person with intellectual disability. Id., at 317, 320. To do so contravenes the\n \nEighth Amendment, for to impose the harshest of punishments on an intellectually disabled person violates his or her inherent dignity as a human being. \xe2\x80\x9c[P]unishment is justified under one or more of three principal rationales: rehabilitation, deterrence, and retribution.\xe2\x80\x9d Kennedy v. Louisiana,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 407,\n420 (2008)\n. Rehabilitation, it is evident, is not an applicable rationale for the death penalty. See Gregg v. Georgia,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153,\n183 (1976)\n (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.). As for deterrence, those with intellectual disability are, by reason of their condition, likely unable to make the calculated judgments that are the premise for the deterrence rationale. They have a \xe2\x80\x9cdiminished ability\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cprocess information, to learn from experience, to engage in logical reasoning, or to control impulses . . . [which] make[s] it less likely that they can process the information of the possibility of execution as a penalty and, as a result, control their conduct based upon that information.\xe2\x80\x9d Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320. Retributive values are also ill-served by executing those with intellectual disability. The diminished capacity of the intellectually disabled lessens moral culpability and hence the retributive value of the punishment. See id., at 319 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf the culpability of the average murderer is insufficient to justify the most extreme sanction available to the State, the lesser culpability of the mentally retarded offender surely does not merit that form of retribution\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A further reason for not imposing the death penalty on a person who is intellectually disabled is to protect the integrity of the trial process. These persons face \xe2\x80\x9ca special risk of wrongful execution\xe2\x80\x9d because they are more likely to give false confessions, are often poor witnesses, and are less able to give meaningful assistance to their counsel. Id., at 320\xe2\x80\x93321. This is not to say that under current law persons with intellectual disability who \xe2\x80\x9cmeet the law\xe2\x80\x99s requirements for criminal responsibility\xe2\x80\x9d may not be tried and punished. Id., at 306. They may not, however, receive the law\xe2\x80\x99s most severe sentence. Id., at 318.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The question this case presents is how intellectual disability must be defined in order to implement these principles and the holding of Atkins. To determine if Florida\xe2\x80\x99s cutoff rule is valid, it is proper to consider the psychiatric and professional studies that elaborate on the purpose and meaning of IQ scores to determine how the scores relate to the holding of Atkins. This in turn leads to a better understanding of how the legislative policies of various States, and the holdings of state courts, implement the Atkins rule. That understanding informs our determination whether there is a consensus that instructs how to decide the specific issue presented here. And, in conclusion, this Court must express its own independent determination reached in light of the instruction found in those sources and authorities.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n That this Court, state courts, and state legislatures consult and are informed by the work of medical experts in determining intellectual disability is unsurprising. Those professionals use their learning and skills to study and consider the consequences of the classification schemes they devise in the diagnosis of persons with mental or psychiatric disorders or disabilities. Society relies upon medical and professional expertise to define and explain how to diagnose the mental condition at issue. And the definition of intellectual disability by skilled professionals has implications far beyond the confines of the death penalty: for it is relevant to education, access to social programs, and medical treatment plans. In determining who qualifies as intellectually disabled, it is proper to consult the medical community\xe2\x80\x99s opinions.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As the Court noted in Atkins, the medical community defines intellectual disability according to three criteria: significantly subaverage intellectual functioning, deficits in adaptive functioning (the inability to learn basic skills and adjust behavior to changing circumstances), and onset of these deficits during the developmental period. See id., at 308, n.\xc2\xa03; DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 33; Brief for American Psychological Association et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 12\xe2\x80\x9313 (hereinafter APA Brief). This last factor, referred to as \xe2\x80\x9cage of onset,\xe2\x80\x9d is not at issue.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The first and second criteria\xe2\x80\x94deficits in intellectual functioning and deficits in adaptive functioning\xe2\x80\x94are central here. In the context of a formal assessment, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he existence of concurrent deficits in intellectual and adaptive functioning has long been the defining characteristic of intellectual disability.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 11.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n On its face, the Florida statute could be consistent with the views of the medical community noted and discussed in Atkins. Florida\xe2\x80\x99s statute defines intellectual disability for purposes of an Atkins proceeding as \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly subaverage general intellectual functioning existing concurrently with deficits in adaptive behavior and manifested during the period from conception to age 18.\xe2\x80\x9d Fla. Stat. \xc2\xa7921.137(1) (2013). The statute further defines \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly subaverage general intellectual functioning\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cperformance that is two or more standard deviations from the mean score on a standardized intelligence test.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The mean IQ test score is 100. The concept of standard deviation describes how scores are dispersed in a population. Standard deviation is distinct from standard error of measurement, a concept which describes the reliability of a test and is discussed further below. The standard deviation on an IQ test is approximately 15 points, and so two standard deviations is approximately 30 points. Thus a test taker who performs \xe2\x80\x9ctwo or more standard deviations from the mean\xe2\x80\x9d will score approximately 30 points below the mean on an IQ test, i.e., a score of approximately 70 points.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n On its face this statute could be interpreted consistently with Atkins and with the conclusions this Court reaches in the instant case. Nothing in the statute precludes Florida from taking into account the IQ test\xe2\x80\x99s standard error of measurement, and as discussed below there is evidence that Florida\xe2\x80\x99s Legislature intended to include the measurement error in the calculation. But the Florida Supreme Court has interpreted the provisions more nar-rowly. It has held that a person whose test score is above 70, including a score within the margin for measurement error, does not have an intellectual disability and is barred from presenting other evidence that would show his faculties are limited. See Cherry v. State, 959 So.\xc2\xa02d 702, 712\xe2\x80\x93713 (Fla. 2007) (per curiam). That strict IQ test score cutoff of 70 is the issue in this case.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Pursuant to this mandatory cutoff, sentencing courts cannot consider even substantial and weighty evidence of intellectual disability as measured and made manifest by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s failure or inability to adapt to his social and cultural environment, including medical histories, behavioral records, school tests and reports, and testimony regarding past behavior and family circumstances. This is so even though the medical community accepts that all of this evidence can be probative of intellectual disability, including for individuals who have an IQ test score above 70. See APA Brief 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he relevant clinical authorities all agree that an individual with an IQ score above 70 may properly be diagnosed with intellectual disability if significant limitations in adaptive functioning also exist\xe2\x80\x9d); DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 37 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] person with an IQ score above 70 may have such severe adaptive behavior problems . . . that the person\xe2\x80\x99s actual functioning is comparable to that of individuals with a lower IQ score\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule disregards established medical practice in two interrelated ways. It takes an IQ score as final and conclusive evidence of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual capacity, when experts in the field would consider other evidence. It also relies on a purportedly scientific measurement of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s abilities, his IQ score, while refusing to recognize that the score is, on its own terms, imprecise.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The professionals who design, administer, and interpret IQ tests have agreed, for years now, that IQ test scores should be read not as a single fixed number but as a range. See D. Wechsler, The Measurement of Adult Intelligence 133 (3d ed. 1944) (reporting the range of error on an early IQ test). Each IQ test has a \xe2\x80\x9cstandard error of measurement,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., often referred to by the abbreviation \xe2\x80\x9cSEM.\xe2\x80\x9d A test\xe2\x80\x99s SEM is a statistical fact, a reflection of the inherent imprecision of the test itself. See R. Furr & V. Bacharach, Psychometrics 118 (2d ed. 2014) (identifying the SEM as \xe2\x80\x9cone of the most important concepts in measurement theory\xe2\x80\x9d). An individual\xe2\x80\x99s IQ test score on any given exam may fluctuate for a variety of reasons. These include the test-taker\xe2\x80\x99s health; practice from earlier tests; the environment or location of the test; the examiner\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor; the subjective judgment involved in scoring certain questions on the exam; and simple lucky guessing. See American Association on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities, R. Schalock et\xc2\xa0al., User\xe2\x80\x99s Guide To Accompany the 11th Edition of Intellectual Disability: Definition, Classification, and Systems of Supports 22 (2012) (hereinafter AAIDD Manual); A. Kaufman, IQ Testing 101, pp. 138\xe2\x80\x93139 (2009).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The SEM reflects the reality that an individual\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual functioning cannot be reduced to a single numerical score. For purposes of most IQ tests, the SEM means that an individual\xe2\x80\x99s score is best understood as a range of scores on either side of the recorded score. The SEM allows clinicians to calculate a range within which one may say an individual\xe2\x80\x99s true IQ score lies. See APA Brief 23 (\xe2\x80\x9cSEM is a unit of measurement: 1 SEM equates to a confidence of 68% that the measured score falls within a given score range, while 2 SEM provides a 95% confidence level that the measured score is within a broader range\xe2\x80\x9d). A score of 71, for instance, is generally considered to reflect a range between 66 and 76 with 95% confidence and a range of 68.5 and 73.5 with a 68% confidence. See DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 37 (\xe2\x80\x9cIndividuals with intellectual disability have scores of approximately two standard deviations or more below the population mean, including a margin for measurement error (generally +5 points). . . . [T]his involves a score of 65\xe2\x80\x9375 (70 \xc2\xb1 5)\xe2\x80\x9d); APA Brief 23 (\xe2\x80\x9cFor example, the average SEM for the WAIS-IV is 2.16 IQ test points and the average SEM for the Stanford-Binet 5 is 2.30 IQ test points (test manuals report SEMs by different age groupings; these scores are similar, but not identical, often due to sampling error)\xe2\x80\x9d). Even when a person has taken multiple tests, each separate score must be assessed using the SEM, and the analysis of multiple IQ scores jointly is a complicated endeavor. See Schneider, Principles of Assessment of Aptitude and Achievement, in The Oxford Handbook of Child Psychological Assessment 286, 289\xe2\x80\x93291, 318 (D. Saklofske, C. Reynolds, V. Schwean, eds. 2013). In addition, because the test itself may be flawed, or administered in a consistently flawed manner, multiple examinations may result in repeated similar scores, so that even a consistent score is not conclusive evidence of intellectual functioning.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Despite these professional explanations, Florida law used the test score as a fixed number, thus barring further consideration of other evidence bearing on the question of intellectual disability. For professionals to diagnose\xe2\x80\x94and for the law then to determine\xe2\x80\x94whether an intellectual disability exists once the SEM applies and the individual\xe2\x80\x99s IQ score is 75 or below the inquiry would consider factors indicating whether the person had deficits in adaptive functioning. These include evidence of past performance, environment, and upbringing.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A significant majority of States implement the protections of Atkins by taking the SEM into account, thus acknowledging the error inherent in using a test score without necessary adjustment. This calculation provides \xe2\x80\x9cobjective indicia of society\xe2\x80\x99s standards\xe2\x80\x9d in the context of the\n \nEighth Amendment. Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 563. Only the Kentucky and Virginia Legislatures have adopted a fixed score cutoff identical to Florida\xe2\x80\x99s. Ky. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7532.130(2) (Lexis Supp. 2013); Bowling v. Commonwealth, 163 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 361, 375 (Ky. 2005); Va. Code Ann. \xc2\xa719.2\xe2\x80\x93264.3:1.1 (Lexis Supp. 2013); Johnson v. Commonwealth, 267 Va. 53, 75, 591 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 47, 59 (2004), vacated and remanded on other grounds,\n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 901 (2005)\n. Alabama also may use a strict IQ score cutoff at 70, although not as a result of legislative action. See Smith v. State, 71 So. 3d 12, 20 (Ala. Crim. App. 2008) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Alabama Supreme Court . . . did not adopt any \xe2\x80\x98margin of error\xe2\x80\x99 when examining a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s IQ score\xe2\x80\x9d). Petitioner does not question the rule in States which use a bright-line cutoff at 75 or greater, Tr. of Oral Arg. 9, and so they are not included alongside Florida in this analysis.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In addition to these States, Arizona, Delaware, Kansas, North Carolina, and Washington have statutes which could be interpreted to provide a bright-line cutoff leading to the same result that Florida mandates in its cases. See Ariz. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x93753(F) (West 2013); Del. Code Ann. Tit. 11, \xc2\xa74209(d)(3) (2012 Supp.); Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa776\xe2\x80\x9312b01 (2013 Supp.); N.\xc2\xa0C. Gen. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa715A\xe2\x80\x932005 (Lexis 2013); Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa710.95.030(2)(c) (2012). That these state laws might be interpreted to require a bright-line cutoff does not mean that they will be so interpreted, however. See, e.g., State v. Vela, 279 Neb. 94, 126, 137, 777 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 266, 292, 299 (2010) (Although Nebraska\xe2\x80\x99s statute specifies \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n intelligence quotient of seventy or below on a reliably administered intelligence quotient test,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he district court found that [the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] score of 75 on the [IQ test], considered in light of the standard error of measurement, could be considered as subaverage general intellectual functioning for purposes of diagnosing mental retardation\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s statute appears to set a broad statutory cutoff at 70, Ariz. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x93753(F) (West 2013), but another provision instructs courts to \xe2\x80\x9ctake into account the margin of error for a test administered.\xe2\x80\x9d Id. at \xc2\xa714-753(K)(5). How courts are meant to interpret the statute in a situation like Hall\xe2\x80\x99s is not altogether clear. The principal Arizona case on the matter, State v. Roque, 141 P.\xc2\xa03d 368, (Ariz 2006), states that \xe2\x80\x9cthe statute accounts for margin of error by requiring multiple tests,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cif the defendant achieves a full-scale score of 70 or below on any one of the tests, then the court proceeds to a hearing.\xe2\x80\x9d Id. at 403. But that case also notes that the defendant had an IQ score of 80, well outside the margin of error, and that all but one of the sub-parts of the IQ test were \xe2\x80\x9cabove 75.\xe2\x80\x9d Id.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Kansas has not had an execution in almost five decades, and so its laws and jurisprudence on this issue are unlikely to receive attention on this specific question. See Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 316 (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven in those States that allow the execution of mentally retarded offenders, the practiceis uncommon. Some States . . . continue to authorize executions, but none have been carried out in decades. Thus there is little need to pursue legislation barring the execution of the mentally retarded in those States\xe2\x80\x9d). Delaware has executed three individuals in the past decade, while Washington has executed one person, and has recently suspended its death penalty. None of the four individuals executed recently in those States appears to have brought a claim similar to that advanced here.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Thus, at most nine States mandate a strict IQ score cutoff at 70. Of these, four States (Delaware, Kansas, North Carolina, and Washington) appear not to have considered the issue in their courts. On the other side of the ledger stand the 18 States that have abolished the death penalty, either in full or for new offenses, and Oregon, which has suspended the death penalty and executed only two individuals in the past 40 years. See Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 574 (\xe2\x80\x9c[The] Court should have considered those States that had abandoned the death penalty altogether as part of the consensus against the juvenile death penalty\xe2\x80\x9d). In those States, of course, a person in Hall\xe2\x80\x99s positioncould not be executed even without a finding of intellectual disability. Thus in 41 States an individual in Hall\xe2\x80\x99sposition\xe2\x80\x94an individual with an IQ score of 71\xe2\x80\x94would not be deemed automatically eligible for the death penalty.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n These aggregate numbers are not the only considerations bearing on a determination of consensus. Consistency of the direction of change is also relevant. See id., at565\xe2\x80\x93566 (quoting Atkins, supra, at 315). Since Atkins, many States have passed legislation to comply with the constitutional requirement that persons with intellectual disability not be executed. Two of these States, Virginia and Delaware, appear to set a strict cutoff at 70, although as discussed, Delaware\xe2\x80\x99s courts have yet to interpret the law. In contrast, at least 11 States have either abolished the death penalty or passed legislation allowing defendants to present additional evidence of intellectual disability when their IQ test score is above 70.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Since Atkins, five States have abolished the death penalty through legislation. See 2012 Conn. Pub. Acts no. 12\xe2\x80\x935; Ill. Comp. Stat. ch. 725, \xc2\xa7119\xe2\x80\x931 (West 2012); Md. Correc. Servs. Code Ann. \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x93901 et\xc2\xa0seq. (Lexis 2008); N.\xc2\xa0J. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa72C:11\xe2\x80\x933(b)(1) (West Supp. 2013); 2009 N. M. Laws ch. 11, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x937. In addition, the New York Court of Appeals invalidated New York\xe2\x80\x99s death penalty under the State Constitution in 2004, see People v. LeValle, 3 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 3d 88, 817 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 341 (2004), and legislation has not been passed to reinstate it. And when it did impose the death penalty, New York did not employ an IQ cutoff in determining intellectual disability. N.\xc2\xa0Y. Crim. Proc. Law Ann. \xc2\xa7400.27(12)(e) (West 2005).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In addition to these States, at least five others have passed legislation allowing a defendant to present additional evidence of intellectual disability even when an IQ test score is above 70. See Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa71376 (West Supp. 2014) (no IQ cutoff); Idaho\xc2\xa0Code \xc2\xa719\xe2\x80\x932515A (Lexis Supp. 2013) (\xe2\x80\x9cseventy (70) or below\xe2\x80\x9d); Pizzutto v. State, 146 Idaho 720, 729, 202 P.\xc2\xa03d 642, 651 (2008) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe alleged error in IQ testing is plus or minus five points. The district court was entitled to draw reasonable inferences from the undisputed facts\xe2\x80\x9d); La. Code Crim. Proc. Ann., Art. 905.5.1 (West Supp. 2014) (no IQ cutoff); Nev. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7174.098.7 (2013) (no IQ cutoff); Utah Code Ann \xc2\xa777\xe2\x80\x9315a\xe2\x80\x93102 (Lexis 2012) (no IQ cutoff). The U.\xc2\xa0S. Code likewise does not set a strict IQ cutoff. See\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73596(c). And no State that previously allowed defendants with an IQ score over 70 to present additional evidence of intellectual disability has modified its law to create a strict cutoff at 70. Cf. Roper, supra, at 566 (\xe2\x80\x9cSince Stanford v. Kentucky,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 361 (1989)\n, no State that previously prohibited capital punishment for juveniles has reinstated it\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In summary, every state legislature to have considered the issue after Atkins\xe2\x80\x94save Virginia\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x94and whose law has been interpreted by its courts has taken a position contrary to that of Florida. Indeed, the Florida Legislature, which passed the relevant legislation prior to Atkins, might well have believed that its law would not createa fixed cutoff at 70. The staff analysis accompanyingthe 2001 bill states that it \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not contain a set IQlevel\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0 Two standard deviations from these tests is ap-proximately a 70 IQ, although it can be extended up to 75.\xe2\x80\x9d Fla. Senate Staff Analysis and Economic Impact Statement, CS/SB 238, p. 11 (Feb. 14, 2001). But the Florida Supreme Court interpreted the law to require a bright-line cutoff at 70, see Cherry, 959 So.\xc2\xa02d, at 712\xe2\x80\x93713, and the Court is bound by that interpretation.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The rejection of the strict 70 cutoff in the vast majority of States and the \xe2\x80\x9cconsistency in the trend,\xe2\x80\x9d Roper, supra, at 567, toward recognizing the SEM provide strong evidence of consensus that our society does not regard this strict cutoff as proper or humane.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Atkins itself acknowledges the inherent error in IQ testing. It is true that Atkins \xe2\x80\x9cdid not provide definitive procedural or substantive guides for determining when a person who claims mental retardation\xe2\x80\x9d falls within the protection of the\n \nEighth Amendment. Bobby v. Bies,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825,\n831 (2009)\n. In Atkins, the Court stated:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cNot all people who claim to be mentally retarded will be so impaired as to fall within the range of mentally retarded offenders about whom there is a national consensus. As was our approach in Ford v. Wainwright with regard to insanity, \xe2\x80\x98we leave to the State[s] the task of developing appropriate ways to enforce the constitutional restriction upon [their] execution of sentences.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 317 (quoting Ford v. Wainwright,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399\n\xe2\x80\x93417 (1986); citation omitted).

\n

As discussed above, the States play a critical role in advancing protections and providing the Court with information that contributes to an understanding of how intellectual disability should be measured and assessed. But Atkins did not give the States unfettered discretion to define the full scope of the constitutional protection.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Atkins Court twice cited definitions of intellectual disability which, by their express terms, rejected a strict IQ test score cutoff at 70. Atkins first cited the definition provided in the DSM\xe2\x80\x93IV: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Mild\xe2\x80\x99 mental retardation is typically used to describe people with an IQ level of 50\xe2\x80\x9355 to approximately 70.\xe2\x80\x9d 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 308, n.\xc2\xa03 (citing Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders 41 (4th ed. 2000)). The Court later noted that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98an IQ between 70 and 75 or lower . . . is typically considered the cutoff IQ score for the intellectual function prong of the mental retardation definition.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 309, n.\xc2\xa05. Furthermore, immediately after the Court declared that it left \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to the States the task of developing appropriate ways to enforce the constitutional restriction,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 317, the Court stated in an accompanying footnote that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he [state] statutory definitions of mental retardation are not identical, but generally conform to the clinical definitions,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Thus Atkins itself not only cited clinical definitions for intellectual disability but also noted that the States\xe2\x80\x99 standards, on which the Court based its own conclusion, conformed to those definitions. In the words of Atkins, those persons who meet the \xe2\x80\x9cclinical definitions\xe2\x80\x9d of intellectual disability \xe2\x80\x9cby definition\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0have diminished capacities\xc2\xa0to understand and process information, to communicate, to abstract from mistakes and learn from experience, to engage in logical reasoning, to control impulses, and to understand the reactions of others.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 318. Thus, they bear \xe2\x80\x9cdiminish[ed] . . . personal culpability.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The clinical definitions of intellectual disability, which take into account that IQ scores represent a range, not a fixed number, were a fundamental premise of Atkins. And those clinical definitions have long included the SEM. See Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders 28 (rev. 3d ed. 1987) (\xe2\x80\x9cSince any measurement is fallible, an IQ score is generally thought to involve an error of measurement of approximately five points; hence, an IQ of 70 is considered to represent a band or zone of 65 to 75. Treating the IQ with some flexibility permits inclusion in the Mental Retardation category of people with IQs somewhat higher than 70 who exhibit significant deficits in adaptive behavior\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Respondent argues that the current Florida law was favorably cited by the Atkins Court. See Brief for Respondent 18 (\xe2\x80\x9cAs evidence of the national consensus, the Court specifically cited Florida\xe2\x80\x99s statute at issue here, which has not substantively changed\xe2\x80\x9d). While Atkins did refer to Florida\xe2\x80\x99s law in a citation listing States which had outlawed the execution of the intellectually disabled, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 315, that fleeting mention did not signal the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approval of Florida\xe2\x80\x99s current understanding of the law. As discussed above, when Atkins was decided the Florida Supreme Court had not yet interpreted the law to require a strict IQ cutoff at 70. That new interpretation runs counter to the clinical definition cited throughout Atkins and to Florida\xe2\x80\x99s own legislative report indicating this kind of cutoff need not be used.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument also conflicts with the logic of Atkins and the\n \nEighth Amendment. If the States were to have complete autonomy to define intellectual disability as they wished, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Atkins could become a nullity, and the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection of human dignity would not become a reality. This Court thus reads Atkins to provide substantial guidance on the definition of intellectual disability.

\n

D

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The actions of the States and the precedents of this Court \xe2\x80\x9cgive us essential instruction,\xe2\x80\x9d Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 564, but the inquiry must go further. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Constitution contemplates that in the end our own judgment will be brought to bear on the question of the acceptability of the death penalty under the\n \nEighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Coker v. Georgia,\n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 584,\n597 (1977)\n (plurality opinion). That exercise of independent judgment is the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judicial duty. See Roper, supra, at 574 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]o the extent Stanford was based on a rejection of the idea that this Court is required to bring its independent judgment to bear on the proportionality of the death penalty for a particular class of crimes or offenders, it suffices to note that this rejection was inconsistent with prior\n \nEighth Amendment decisions\xe2\x80\x9d (citation omitted).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In this Court\xe2\x80\x99s independent judgment, the Florida statute, as interpreted by its courts, is unconstitutional.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In addition to the views of the States and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent, this determination is informed by the views of medical experts. These views do not dictate the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, yet the Court does not disregard these informed assessments. See Kansas v. Crane,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 407,\n413 (2002)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he science of psychiatry . . . informs but does not control ultimate legal determinations\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). It is the Court\xe2\x80\x99s duty to interpret the Constitution, but it need not do so in isolation. The legal determination of intellectual disability is distinct from a medical diagnosis, but it is informed by the medical community\xe2\x80\x99s diagnostic framework. Atkins itself points to the diagnostic criteria employed by psychiatric professionals. And the professional community\xe2\x80\x99s teachings are of particular help in this case, where no alternative definition of intellectual disability is presented and where this Court and the States have placed substantial reliance on the expertise of the medical profession.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n By failing to take into account the SEM and setting a strict cutoff at 70, Florida \xe2\x80\x9cgoes against the unanimous professional consensus.\xe2\x80\x9d APA Brief 15. Neither Florida nor its amici point to a single medical professional who supports this cutoff. The DSM\xe2\x80\x935 repudiates it: \xe2\x80\x9cIQ test scores are approximations of conceptual functioning but may be insufficient to assess reasoning in real-life situations and mastery of practical tasks.\xe2\x80\x9d DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 37. This statement well captures the Court\xe2\x80\x99s independent assessment that an individual with an IQ test score \xe2\x80\x9cbetween 70 and 75 or lower,\xe2\x80\x9d Atkins, supra, at 309, n.\xc2\xa05, may show intellectual disability by presenting additional evidence regarding difficulties in adaptive functioning.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The flaws in Florida\xe2\x80\x99s law are the result of the inherent error in IQ tests themselves. An IQ score is an approximation, not a final and infallible assessment of intellectual functioning. See APA Brief 24 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is standard pyschometric practice to report the \xe2\x80\x98estimates of relevant reliabilities and standard errors of measurement\xe2\x80\x99 when reporting a test score\xe2\x80\x9d); ibid. (the margin of error is \xe2\x80\x9cinherent to the accuracy of IQ scores\xe2\x80\x9d); Furr, Psychometrics, at 119 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he standard error of measurement is an important psychometric value with implications for applied measurement\xe2\x80\x9d). SEM is not a concept peculiar to the psychiatric profession and IQ tests. It is a measure that is recognized and relied upon by those who create and devise tests of all sorts. Id., at 118 (identifying the SEM as \xe2\x80\x9cone of the most important concepts in measurement theory\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This awareness of the IQ test\xe2\x80\x99s limits is of particular importance when conducting the conjunctive assessment necessary to assess an individual\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual ability. See American Association on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities, Intellectual Disability: Definition, Classification, and Systems of Supports 40 (11th ed. 2010) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt must be stressed that the diagnosis of [intellectual disability] is intended to reflect a clinical judgment rather than an actuarial determination\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Intellectual disability is a condition, not a number. See DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 37. Courts must recognize, as does the medical community, that the IQ test is imprecise. This is not to say that an IQ test score is unhelpful. It is of considerable significance, as the medical community recognizes. But in using these scores to assess a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility for the death penalty, a State must afford these test scores the same studied skepticism that those who design and use the tests do, and understand that an IQ test score represents a range rather than a fixed number. A State that ignores the inherent imprecision of these tests risks executing a person who suffers from intellectual disability. See APA Brief 17 (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder the universally accepted clinical standards for diagnosing intellectual disability, the court\xe2\x80\x99s determination that Mr. Hall is not intellectually disabled cannot be considered valid\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court agrees with the medical experts that when a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s IQ test score falls within the test\xe2\x80\x99s acknowledged and inherent margin of error, the defendant must be able to present additional evidence of intellectual disability, including testimony regarding adaptive deficits.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is not sound to view a single factor as dispositive of a conjunctive and interrelated assessment. See DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 37 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] person with an IQ score above 70 may have such severe adaptive behavior problems\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0that the person\xe2\x80\x99s actual functioning is comparable to that of individuals with a lower IQ score\xe2\x80\x9d). The Florida statute, as interpreted by its courts, misuses IQ score on its own terms; andthis, in turn, bars consideration of evidence that must be considered in determining whether a defendant in a capital case has intellectual disability. Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule is invalid under the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Cruel and Unusual Punishments Clause.

\n

E

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Florida seeks to execute a man because he scored a 71 instead of 70 on an IQ test. Florida is one of just a few States to have this rigid rule. Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule misconstrues the Court\xe2\x80\x99s statements in Atkins that intellectually dis-ability is characterized by an IQ of \xe2\x80\x9capproximately 70.\xe2\x80\x9d 536U.\xc2\xa0S., at 308, n.\xc2\xa03. Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule is in direct opposition to the views of those who design, administer, and interpret the IQ test. By failing to take into account the standard error of measurement, Florida\xe2\x80\x99s law not only contradicts the test\xe2\x80\x99s own design but also bars an essential part of a sentencing court\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry into adaptive functioning. Freddie Lee Hall may or may not be intellectually dis-abled, but the law requires that he have the opportunity topresent evidence of his intellectual disability, including deficits in adaptive functioning over his lifetime.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The death penalty is the gravest sentence our society may impose. Persons facing that most severe sanction must have a fair opportunity to show that the Constitution prohibits their execution. Florida\xe2\x80\x99s law contravenes our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s commitment to dignity and its duty to teach human decency as the mark of a civilized world. The States are laboratories for experimentation, but those experiments may not deny the basic dignity the Constitution protects.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The judgment of the Florida Supreme Court is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

So ordered.

FREDDIE LEE HALL, PETITIONER v. FLORIDA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of florida

\n

[May 27, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Alito, with whom The Chief Justice, Justice Scalia, and Justice Thomas join, dissenting.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Atkins v. Virginia,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304 (2002)\n, the Court held that the\n \nEighth Amendment prohibits a death sentence for defendants who are intellectually disabled but does not mandate the use of a single method for identifying such defendants. Today, the Court overrules the latter holding based largely on the positions adopted by private professional associations. In taking this step, the Court sharply departs from the framework prescribed in prior\n \nEighth Amendment cases and adopts a uniform national rule that is both conceptually unsound and likely to result in confusion. I therefore respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach in this case marks a new and most unwise turn in our\n \nEighth Amendment case law. In Atkins and other cases, the Court held that the prohibition of cruel and unusual punishment embodies the \xe2\x80\x9cevolving standards of decency that mark the progress of a maturing society,\xe2\x80\x9d and the Court explained that \xe2\x80\x9cthose evolving standards should be informed by objective factors to the maximum possible extent.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 312 (internal quotation marks omitted). In addition, the Court \xe2\x80\x9cpinpointed that the clearest and most reliable objective evidence of contemporary values is the legislation enacted by the country\xe2\x80\x99s legislatures.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In these prior cases, when the Court referred to the evolving standards of a maturing \xe2\x80\x9csociety,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court meant the standards of American society as a whole. Now, however, the Court strikes down a state law based on the evolving standards of professional societies, most notably the American Psychiatric Association (APA). The Court begins its analysis with the views of those associations, see ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x9312, and then, after briefly discussing the enactments of state legislatures, see ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9316, returns to the associations\xe2\x80\x99 views in interpreting Atkins and in exercising the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cindependent judgment\xe2\x80\x9d on the constitutionality of Florida\xe2\x80\x99s law, see ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9322. This approach cannot be reconciled with the framework prescribed by our\n \nEighth Amendment cases.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s modern\n \nEighth Amendment precedents, whether a punishment is \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual\xe2\x80\x9d depends on currently prevailing societal norms, and the Court has long held that laws enacted by state legislatures provide the \xe2\x80\x9cclearest and most reliable objective evidence of contemporary values,\xe2\x80\x9d Penry v. Lynaugh,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 302,\n331 (1989)\n. This is so because \xe2\x80\x9cin a democratic society[,] legislatures, not courts, are constituted to respond to the will and consequently the moral values of the people,\xe2\x80\x9d Gregg v. Georgia, 428\xc2\xa0U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0153, 175\xe2\x80\x93176 (1976) (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.) (internal quotation marks omitted). Under this approach, as originally conceived, the Court first asked whether a challenged practice contravened a clear national consensus evidenced by state legislation, and only if such a consensus was found would the Court go on and ask \xe2\x80\x9cwhether there is reason to disagree with [the States\xe2\x80\x99] judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 313.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Invoking this two-step procedure, Atkins held that the \n \nEighth Amendment forbids the execution of defendants who are intellectually disabled. See id., at 315\xe2\x80\x93316. Critical to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis was the conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9ctoday our society views mentally retarded offenders as categorically less culpable than the average criminal.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 316. \xe2\x80\x9cThis consensus,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court continued, \xe2\x80\x9cunquestionably reflects widespread judgment about\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the relationship between mental retardation and the penological purposes served by the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 317.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n While Atkins identified a consensus against the execution of the intellectually disabled, the Court observed that there was \xe2\x80\x9cserious disagreement\xe2\x80\x9d among the States with respect to the best method for \xe2\x80\x9cdetermining which offenders are in fact retarded.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court therefore \xe2\x80\x9cle[ft] to the States the task of developing appropriate ways\xe2\x80\x9d to identify these defendants. Ibid. (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted). As we noted just five years ago, Atkins \xe2\x80\x9cdid not provide definitive procedural or substantive guides for determining when a person\xe2\x80\x9d is intellectually disabled. Bobby v. Bies,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825,\n831 (2009)\n.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Consistent with the role that Atkins left for the States, Florida follows the procedure now at issue. As we explained in Atkins, in order for a defendant to qualify as intellectually disabled, three separate requirements must be met: It must be shown that a defendant has both (1)\xc2\xa0significantly subaverage intellectual functioning and (2)\xc2\xa0deficits in adaptive behavior, and that (3)\xc2\xa0the onset of both factors occurred before the age of 18. See 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 318; ante, at 8. In implementing this framework, Flor-ida has determined that the first requirement cannot be satisfied if the defendant scores higher than 70 on IQ tests, the long-accepted method of measuring intellectual functioning.[1] The Court today holds that this scheme offends the\n \nEighth Amendment. The Court objects that Florida\xe2\x80\x99s approach treats IQ test scores as conclusive and ignores the fact that an IQ score might not reflect \xe2\x80\x9ctrue\xe2\x80\x9d IQ because of errors in measurement. The Court then concludes that a State must view a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s IQ as a range of potential scores calculated using a statistical concept known as the \xe2\x80\x9cstandard error of measurement\xe2\x80\x9d or SEM. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93B\xe2\x80\x931, infra. The Court holds that if this range includes an IQ of 70 or below (the accepted level for intellectual disability), the defendant must be permitted to produce other evidence of intellectual disability in addition to IQ scores.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I see no support for this holding in our traditional approach for identifying our society\xe2\x80\x99s evolving standards of decency. Under any fair analysis of current state laws, the same absence of a consensus that this Court found in Atkins persists today. It is telling that Hall himself does not rely on a consensus among States. He candidly argues instead that \xe2\x80\x9cthe precise number of States that share Florida\xe2\x80\x99s approach is immaterial.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 2.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis is more aggressive. According to the Court, a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant majority of States\xe2\x80\x9d reject Florida\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstrict 70 cutoff\xe2\x80\x9d and instead take \xe2\x80\x9cthe SEM into account\xe2\x80\x9d when deciding whether a defendant meets the first requirement of the intellectual-disability test. Ante, at 12, 16. On the Court\xe2\x80\x99s count, \xe2\x80\x9cat most nine States mandate a strict IQ score cutoff at 70\xe2\x80\x9d; 22 States allow defendants to present \xe2\x80\x9cadditional evidence\xe2\x80\x9d when an individual\xe2\x80\x99s test score is between 70 and 75, ante, at 20;[2] and 19 States have abolished the death penalty or have long suspended its operation. Ante, at 14. From these numbers, the Court concludes that \xe2\x80\x9cin 41 States\xe2\x80\x9d a defendant \xe2\x80\x9cwith an IQ score of 71\xe2\x80\x9d would \xe2\x80\x9cnot be deemed automatically eligible for the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[3] This analysis is deeply flawed.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To begin, in addition to the 8 other States that the Court recognizes as having rules similar to Florida\xe2\x80\x99s, 1 more, Idaho, does not appear to require courts to take the SEM into account in rejecting a claim of intellectual disability.[4] And of the remaining 21 States with the death penalty, 9 have either said nothing about the SEM or have not clarified whether they require its use.[5] Accordingly, of the death-penalty states, 10 (including Florida) do not require that the SEM be taken into account, 12 consider the SEM, and 9 have not taken a definitive position on this question. These statistics cannot be regarded as establishing a national consensus against Florida\xe2\x80\x99s approach.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Attempting to circumvent these statistics, the Court includes in its count the 19 States that never impose the death penalty, but this maneuver cannot be justified. It is true that the Court has counted non-death-penalty States in some prior\n \nEighth Amendment cases, but those cases concerned the substantive question whether a class of individuals should be categorically ineligible for the death penalty. In Roper v. Simmons,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551 (2005)\n, for example, the Court counted non-death-penalty States as part of the consensus against the imposition of a capital sentence for a crime committed by a minor. Id., at 574. The Court reasoned that a State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to abolish the death penalty necessarily \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrates a judgment that the death penalty is inappropriate for all offenders, including juveniles.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n No similar reasoning is possible here. The fact that a State has abolished the death penalty says nothing about how that State would resolve the evidentiary problem of identifying defendants who are intellectually disabled. As I explain below, a State may reasonably conclude that Florida\xe2\x80\x99s approach is fairer than and just as accurate as the approach that the Court now requires, and therefore it cannot be inferred that a non-death-penalty State, if forced to choose between the two approaches, would necessarily select the Court\xe2\x80\x99s. For all these reasons, it is quite wrong for the Court to proclaim that \xe2\x80\x9cthe vast majority of States\xe2\x80\x9d have rejected Florida\xe2\x80\x99s approach. Ante, at 16.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Not only are the States divided on the question whether the SEM should play a role in determining whether a capital defendant is intellectually disabled, but the States that require consideration of the SEM do not agree on the role that the SEM should play. Those States differ, for example, on the sort of evidence that can be introduced when IQ testing reveals an IQ over 70. Some require further evidence of intellectual deficits, while others permit the defendant to move on to the second prong of the test and submit evidence of deficits in adaptive behavior.[6] The fairest assessment of the current situation is that the States have adopted a multitude of approaches to a very difficult question.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In light of all this, the resolution of this case should be straightforward: Just as there was no methodological consensus among the States at the time of Atkins, there is no such consensus today. And in the absence of such a consensus, we have no basis for holding that Florida\xe2\x80\x99s method contravenes our society\xe2\x80\x99s standards of decency.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Perhaps because it recognizes the weakness of its arguments about a true national consensus, the Court places heavy reliance on the views (some only recently announced) of professional organizations, but the Court attempts to downplay the degree to which its decision is dependent upon the views of these private groups. In a game attempt to shoehorn the views of these associations into the national-consensus calculus, the Court reasons as follows. The views of these associations, the Court states, help in determining \xe2\x80\x9chow [IQ] scores relate to the holding in Atkins\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his in turn leads to a better understanding of how the legislative policies of various States, and the holdings of state courts, implement the Atkins rule\xe2\x80\x9d; and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hat understanding informs our determination whether there is a consensus that instructs how to decide the specific issue presented here.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I cannot follow the Court\xe2\x80\x99s logic. Under our modern\n \nEighth Amendment cases, what counts are our society\xe2\x80\x99s standards\xe2\x80\x94which is to say, the standards of the American people\xe2\x80\x94not the standards of professional associations, which at best represent the views of a small professional elite.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court also mistakenly suggests that its methodol-ogy is dictated by Atkins. See ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9319. On the con-trary, Atkins expressly left \xe2\x80\x9cto the States\xe2\x80\x9d the task of defining intellectual disability. And although the Atkins Court perceived a \xe2\x80\x9cprofessional consensus\xe2\x80\x9d about the best procedure to be used in identifying the intellectually dis-abled, the Atkins Court declined to import that view intothe law. 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 316, n.\xc2\xa021. Instead, the Court made clear that this professional consensus was \xe2\x80\x9cby no means dispositive.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 317, n.\xc2\xa021; see id., at 317, and n.\xc2\xa022.

\n

D

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the views of professional associations will also lead to serious practical problems. I will briefly note a few.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n First, because the views of professional associations often change,[7] tying\n \nEighth Amendment law to these views will lead to instability and continue to fuel pro-tracted litigation. This danger is dramatically illustrated by the most recent publication of the APA, on which theCourt relies. This publication fundamentally alters the first prong of the longstanding, two-pronged definition of intellectual disability that was embraced by Atkins and has been adopted by most States. In this new publication, the APA discards \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly subaverage intellectual functioning\xe2\x80\x9d as an element of the intellectual-disability test.[8] Elevating the APA\xe2\x80\x99s current views to constitutional significance therefore throws into question the basic approach that Atkins approved and that most of the States have followed.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is also noteworthy that changes adopted by professional associations are sometimes rescinded. For example, in 1992 the AAIDD extended the baseline \xe2\x80\x9cintellectual functioning cutoff\xe2\x80\x9d from an \xe2\x80\x9cIQ of 70 or below\xe2\x80\x9d to a \xe2\x80\x9cscore of approximately 70 to 75 or below.\xe2\x80\x9d AAIDD 11th ed. 10 (Table 1.3) (boldface deleted); see 2 Kaplan & Sadock\xe2\x80\x99s Comprehensive Textbook of Psychiatry 3449 (B. Sadock, V. Sadock, & P. Ruiz eds., 9th ed. 2009) (hereinafter Kaplan & Sadock\xe2\x80\x99s). That change \xe2\x80\x9cgenerated much controversy; by 2000, \xe2\x80\x9conly 4 states used the 1992 AAIDD definition, with 44 states continuing to use the 1983 definition.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And in the 2002 AAIDD, the baseline \xe2\x80\x9cIQ cut-off was changed\xe2\x80\x9d back to approximately \xe2\x80\x9c70 or less.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Second, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach implicitly calls upon the Judiciary either to follow every new change in the thinking of these professional organizations or to judge the validity of each new change. Here, for example, the Court tacitly makes the judgment that the diagnostic criteria for intellectual disability that prevailed at the time when Atkins was decided are no longer legitimate. The publications that Atkins cited differ markedly from more recent editions now endorsed by the Court. See 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 308, n.\xc2\xa03.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Third, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach requires the Judiciary to determine which professional organizations are entitled to special deference. And what if professional organizations disagree? The Court provides no guidance for deciding which organizations\xe2\x80\x99 views should govern.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Fourth, the Court binds\n \nEighth Amendment law to definitions of intellectual disability that are promulgated for use in making a variety of decisions that are quite different from the decision whether the imposition of a death sentence in a particular case would serve a valid penological end. In a death-penalty case, intellectual functioning is important because of its correlation with the ability to understand the gravity of the crime and the purpose of the penalty, as well as the ability to resist a momentary impulse or the influence of others. See id., at 318, 320. By contrast, in determining eligibility for social services, adaptive functioning may be much more important. Cf. DSM\xe2\x80\x93IV\xe2\x80\x93TR, at xxxvii (clinical \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderations\xe2\x80\x9d may not be \xe2\x80\x9crelevant to legal judgments\xe2\x80\x9d that turn on \xe2\x80\x9cindividual responsibility\xe2\x80\x9d); DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 20 (similar). Practical problems like these call for legislative judgments, not judicial resolution.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Because I find no consensus among the States, I would not independently assess the method that Florida has adopted for determining intellectual disability. But even if it were appropriate for us to look beyond the evidence of societal standards, I could not conclude that Florida\xe2\x80\x99s method is unconstitutional. The Court faults Florida for \xe2\x80\x9ctak[ing] an IQ score as final and conclusive evidence of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual capacity\xe2\x80\x9d and for failing to recognize that an IQ score may be imprecise. Ante, at 10. In my view, however, Florida has adopted a sensible standard that comports with the longstanding belief that IQ tests are the best measure of intellectual functioning. And although the Court entirely ignores this part of the Florida scheme, the State takes into account the inevitable risk of testing error by permitting defendants to introduce multiple scores.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In contrast, the Court establishes a standard thatconflates what have long been understood to be two independent requirements for proving intellectual disability:(1)\xc2\xa0significantly subaverage intellectual functioning and (2)\xc2\xa0deficits in adaptive behavior. The Court also mandates use of an alternative method of dealing with the risk of testing error without any hint that it is more accurate than Florida\xe2\x80\x99s approach.

\n

A

\n

1

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The first supposed error that the Court identifies is that Florida \xe2\x80\x9ctakes an IQ score\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cconclusive evidence\xe2\x80\x9d of intellectual functioning. Ante, at 10. As an initial matter, one would get the impression from reading the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion that Hall introduced only one test score (of 71). See ante, at 14. In truth, the Florida courts considered multiple scores, all above 70, on the particular IQ test that Hall has dubbed the \xe2\x80\x9cgold standard.\xe2\x80\x9d See Brief for Petitioner 50; App. 107\xe2\x80\x93108.[9] Florida\xe2\x80\x99s statute imposes no limit on the number of IQ scores that a defendant may introduce, so the Court is simply wrong to analyze the Florida system as one that views a single IQ score above 70 as \xe2\x80\x9cfinal and conclusive evidence\xe2\x80\x9d that a defendant does not suffer from subaverage intellectual functioning. See Brief for Respondent 44 (\xe2\x80\x9cFlorida\xe2\x80\x99s Rule allows for multiple evaluations, and if Hall believed a statistical error rate prevented any of his tests from reflecting his true score, he could have sought still more testing\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The proper question to ask, therefore, is whether Flor-ida\xe2\x80\x99s actual approach falls outside the range of discretionallowed by Atkins. The Court offers no persuasive reason for concluding that it does. Indeed, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion never identifies what other evidence of intellectual functioning it would require Florida to admit. As we recognized in Atkins, the longstanding practices of the States, and at least the previous views of professional organizations, seem to reflect the understanding that IQ scores are the best way to measure intellectual functioning. See 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 316.[10] Until its most recent publication, the APA, for example, ranked the severity of intellectual disability exclusively by IQ scores, necessarily pinpointing the onset of the disability according to IQ. See DSM\xe2\x80\x93IV\xe2\x80\x93TR, at 42.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We have been presented with no solid evidence that the longstanding reliance on multiple IQ test scores as a measure of intellectual functioning is so unreasonable or outside the ordinary as to be unconstitutional. The Court has certainly not supplied any such information.

\n

2

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n If the Court had merely held that Florida must permit defendants to introduce additional evidence (whatever that might be) of significantly subaverage intellectual functioning, its decision would be more limited in scope. But as I understand the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, it also holds that when IQ tests reveal an IQ between 71 and 75, defendants must be allowed to present evidence of deficits in adaptive behavior\xe2\x80\x94that is, the second prong of the intellectual-disability test. See ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, 12, 20. That is a remarkable change in what we took to be a universal understanding of intellectual disability just 12 years ago.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Atkins, we instructed that \xe2\x80\x9cclinical definitions of mental retardation require not only [(1)]\xc2\xa0subaverage intellectual functioning, but also [(2)]\xc2\xa0significant limitations in adaptive skills.\xe2\x80\x9d 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 318 (emphasis and alterations added). That is the approach taken by the vast majority of States.[11] As the Court correctly recognizes, most States require \xe2\x80\x9cconcurrent deficits\xe2\x80\x9d in intellectual functioning and adaptive behavior, requiring defendants to prove both. Ante, at 8 (emphasis added).[12]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Yet the Court now holds that when a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s IQ score is as high as 75, a court must \xe2\x80\x9cconsider factors indicating whether the person has deficits in adaptive functioning.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12; see ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, 20. In other words, even when a defendant has failed to show that he meets the first prong of the well-accepted standard for intellectual disability (significantly subaverage intellec-tual functioning), evidence of the second prong (deficits inadaptive behavior) can establish intellectual disability.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court offers little explanation for this sea change. It asserts vaguely that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is not sound to view a single factor as dispositive of a conjunctive and interrelated assessment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21. But the Court ignores the fact that deficits in adaptive behavior cannot be used to establish deficits in mental functioning because the two prongs are meant to show distinct components of intellectual disability. \xe2\x80\x9c[I]ntellectual functions\xe2\x80\x9d include \xe2\x80\x9creasoning, problem solving, planning, abstract thinking, judgment, academic learning, and learning from experience,\xe2\x80\x9d while adaptive functioning refers to the ability \xe2\x80\x9cto meet developmental and sociocultural standards for personal independence and social responsibility.\xe2\x80\x9d DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 33. Strong evidence of a deficit in adaptive behavior does not necessarily demonstrate a deficit in intellectual functioning. And without the latter, a person simply cannot be classified as intellectually disabled.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is particularly troubling to relax the proof requirements for the intellectual-functioning prong because that is the prong that most directly relates to the concerns that led to our primary holding in Atkins. There, we explained that \xe2\x80\x9cthe diminished ability to understand and process information, to learn from experience, to engage in logical reasoning, or to control impulses\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., diminished intellectual functioning\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cmake it less likely that [a defendant] can process the information of the possibility of execution as a penalty\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore be deterred from committing murders. 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320; see also id., at 318 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]hey often act on impulse rather than pursuant to a premeditated plan .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d); see also ante, at 6. A defendant who does not display significantly subaverage intellectual functioning is therefore not among the class of defendants we identified in Atkins.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Finally, relying primarily on proof of adaptive deficits will produce inequities in the administration of capital punishment. As far as I can tell, adaptive behavior is a malleable factor without \xe2\x80\x9cfirm theoretical and empirical roots.\xe2\x80\x9d See 2 Kaplan & Sadock\xe2\x80\x99s 3448. No consensus exists among States or medical practitioners about what facts are most critical in analyzing that factor, and its measurement relies largely on subjective judgments. Florida\xe2\x80\x99s approach avoids the disparities that reliance on such a factor tends to produce. It thus promotes consistency in the application of the death penalty and confidence that it is not being administered haphazardly.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s second \xe2\x80\x9cinterrelated\xe2\x80\x9d objection to Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule is that it fails to account for the risk of error inherent in IQ testing. In order to diminish this risk, the Court establishes a rule that if IQ testing reveals an IQ between 71 and 75, a claim of intellectual disability cannot be rejected on the basis of test scores alone. Ante, at 20. The Court both misunderstands how the SEM works and fails to explain why Florida\xe2\x80\x99s method of accounting for the risk of error (allowing a defendant to take and rely on multiple tests) is not as effective as the approach that the Court compels.

\n

1

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court begins with the simple and uncontroversial proposition that every testing situation is susceptible to error and thus may result in an imperfect measurement of \xe2\x80\x9ctrue\xe2\x80\x9d IQ. The Court then wades into technical matters that must be understood in order to see where the Court goes wrong.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n There are various ways to account for error in IQ testing. One way is Florida\xe2\x80\x99s approach (evaluate multiple test results). Another is to use a mathematical measurement called the \xe2\x80\x9cstandard error of measurement\xe2\x80\x9d or SEM. See AAMR 10th ed. 67\xe2\x80\x9371 (App. 4.1). Of critical importance, there is not a single, uniform SEM across IQ tests or even across test-takers. Rather, \xe2\x80\x9cthe [SEM] varies by test, subgroup, and age group.\xe2\x80\x9d User\xe2\x80\x99s Guide To Accompany AAIDD 11th ed.: Definition, Classification, and Systems of Supports 22 (2012).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Once we know the SEM for a particular test and a particular test-taker, adding one SEM to and subtracting one SEM from the obtained score establishes an interval of scores known as the 66% confidence interval. See AAMR 10th ed. 57. That interval represents the range of scores within which \xe2\x80\x9cwe are [66%] sure\xe2\x80\x9d that the \xe2\x80\x9ctrue\xe2\x80\x9d IQ falls. See Oxford Handbook of Child Psychological Assessment 291 (D. Saklofske, C. Reynolds, & V. Schwean eds. 2013). The interval is centered on the obtained score, and it includes scores that are above and below that score by the amount of the SEM. Since there is about a 66% chance that the test-taker\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctrue\xe2\x80\x9d IQ falls within this range, there is about a 34% chance that the \xe2\x80\x9ctrue\xe2\x80\x9d IQ falls outside the interval, with approximately equal odds that it falls above the interval (17%) or below the interval (17%).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n An example: If a test-taker scores a 72 on an IQ test with a SEM of 2, the 66% confidence interval is the range of 70 to 74 (72 \xc2\xb1 2). In this situation, there is approximately a 66% chance that the test-taker\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctrue\xe2\x80\x9d IQ is between 70 and 74; roughly a 17% chance that it is above 74; and roughly a 17% chance that it is 70 or below. Thus, there is about an 83% chance that the score is above 70.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Similarly, using two SEMs, we can build a 95% confidence interval. The process is the same except that we add two SEMs to and subtract two SEMS from the obtained score. To illustrate the use of two SEMs, let us hypothesize a case in which the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s obtained score is 74. With the same SEM of 2 as in the prior example, there would be a 95% chance that the true score is between 70 and 78 (74 \xc2\xb1 4); roughly a 2.5% chance that the score is above 78; and about a 2.5% chance that the score is 70 or below. The probability of a true score above 70 would be roughly 97.5%. As these two examples show, the greater the degree of confidence demanded, the greater the range of scores that will fall within the confidence interval and, therefore, the further away from 70 an obtained score could be and yet still have 70 fall within its confidence interval.

\n

2

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court misunderstands these principles and makes factual mistakes that will surely confuse States attempting to comply with its opinion.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n First, the Court unjustifiably assumes a blanket (or very common) error measurement of 5. See ante, at 20. That assumption gives rise to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that a defendant must be permitted to introduce additional evidence when IQ tests reveal an IQ as high as 75. See ibid. SEMs, however, vary by IQ test and test-taker, and there is no reason to assume a SEM of 5 points; indeed, it appears that the SEM is generally \xe2\x80\x9cestimated to be three to five points\xe2\x80\x9d for well-standardized IQ tests. AAMR 10th ed. 57. And we know that the SEM for Hall\xe2\x80\x99s most recent IQ test was 2.16\xe2\x80\x94less than half of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s estimate of 5. Brief for Petitioner 40, n.\xc2\xa017.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Relatedly, the Court misreads the authorities on which it relies to establish this cutoff IQ score of 75. It is true that certain professional organizations have advocated a cutoff of 75 and that Atkins cited those organizations\xe2\x80\x99 cutoff. See ante, at 12, 20. But the Court overlooks a critical fact: Those organizations endorsed a 75 IQ cutoff based on their express understanding that \xe2\x80\x9cone standard error of measurement [SEM]\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cthree to five points for well-standardized\xe2\x80\x9d IQ tests. AAMR, Mental Retardation 37 (9th ed. 1992) (hereinafter AAMR 9th ed.); Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., 309, n.\xc2\xa05 (citing AAMR 9th ed.; 2 Kaplan & Sadock\xe2\x80\x99s 2592 (B. Sadock & V. Sadock eds., 7th ed. 2000)); see also AAMR 10th ed. 57; AAIDD 11th ed. 36. In other words, the number 75 was relevant only to the extent that a single SEM was \xe2\x80\x9cestimated\xe2\x80\x9d to be as high as 5 points. AAMR 9th ed. 37. Here, by contrast, we know that the SEM for Hall\xe2\x80\x99s latest IQ test was less than half of that estimate; there is no relevance to the number 75 in this case. To blindly import a five-point margin of error when we know as a matter of fact that the relevant SEM is 2.16 amounts to requiring consideration of more than two SEMs\xe2\x80\x94an approach that finds no support in Atkins or anywhere else.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Because of these factual errors and ambiguities, it is unclear to me whether the Court concludes that a defendant is constitutionally entitled to introduce non-test evidence of intellectual disability (1) whenever his score is 75 or lower, on the mistaken understanding that the SEM for most tests is 5; (2) when the 66% confidence interval (using one SEM) includes a score of 70; or (3) when the 95% confidence interval (using two SEMs) includes a score of 70. In my view, none of these approaches is defensible.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n An approach tied to a fixed score of 75 can be dismissed out of hand because, as discussed, every test has a different SEM.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The other two approaches would require that a defendant be permitted to submit additional evidence when his IQ is above 70 so long as the 66% or 95% confidence interval (using one SEM or two SEMs, respectively) includes a score of 70, but there is no foundation for this in our\n \nEighth Amendment case law. As Hall concedes, the\n \nEighth Amendment permits States to assign to a defendant the burden of establishing intellectual disability by at least a preponderance of the evidence. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 12. In other words, a defendant can be required to prove that the probability of a 70 or sub-70 IQ is greater than 50%. Under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach, by contrast, a defendant could prove significantly subaverage intellectual functioning by showing simply that the probability of a \xe2\x80\x9ctrue\xe2\x80\x9d IQ of 70 or below is as little as 17% (under a one-SEM rule) or 2.5% (under a two-SEM rule). This totally transforms the allocation and nature of the burden of proof.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I have referred to the 66% and 95% confidence intervals only because they result from the most straightforward application of the SEM in this context: One SEM establishes the 66% confidence interval; two SEMs establish the 95% confidence interval. See AAIDD 11th ed. 36. But it would be simple enough to devise a 51% confidence interval\xe2\x80\x94or a 99% confidence interval for that matter. There is therefore no excuse for mechanically imposing standards that are unhinged from legal logic and that over-ride valid state laws establishing burdens of proof. The appropriate confidence level is ultimately a judgment best left to legislatures, and their judgment has been that a defendant must establish that it is more likely than not that he is intellectually disabled. I would defer to that determination.

\n

3

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court also fails to grasp that Florida\xe2\x80\x99s system already accounts for the risk of testing error by allowing the introduction of multiple test scores. The Court never explains why its criticisms of the uncertainty resulting from the use of a single IQ score apply when a defendant consistently scores above 70 on multiple tests. Contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s evident assumption, the well-accepted view is that multiple consistent scores establish a much higher degree of confidence.[13]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s only attempt to address this is to say that \xe2\x80\x9cthe analysis of multiple IQ scores jointly is a complicated endeavor,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11, but any evaluation of intellectual disability, whether based on objective tests or subjective observations, is \xe2\x80\x9ccomplicated.\xe2\x80\x9d If conducting the proper analysis of multiple scores produces an IQ as reliable as the approach mandated by the Court, there is no basis for rejecting Florida\xe2\x80\x99s approach.[14]
