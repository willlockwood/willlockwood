OBB PERSONENVERKEHR AG v. SACHS

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x931067.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 5, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided December 1, 2015

\n

Respondent Carol Sachs, a California resident, purchased a Eurail pass over the Internet from a Massachusetts-based travel agent. While using that pass to board a train in Austria operated by petitioner OBB Personenverkehr AG (OBB), the Austrian state-owned railway, Sachs fell to the tracks and suffered traumatic personal injuries. She sued OBB in Federal District Court. OBB moved to dismiss, claiming that her suit was barred by the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act, which shields foreign states and their agencies and instrumentalities from suit in United States courts, unless a specified exception applies. Sachs countered that her suit fell within the Act\xe2\x80\x99s commercial activity exception, which abrogates sovereign immunity for suits \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon a commercial activity carried on in the United States by [a] foreign state,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71605(a)(2), reasoning that her suit was \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the Massachusetts-based travel agent\xe2\x80\x99s sale of the Eurail pass in the United States, and that the travel agent\xe2\x80\x99s sale of that pass could be attributed to OBB through common law principles of agency. The District Court held that Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s suit did not fall within \xc2\xa71605(a)(2) and dismissed the suit, but the en banc Ninth Circuit reversed. The court first concluded that the Eurail pass sale by the travel agent could be attributed to OBB through common law principles of agency, and then determined that Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s suit was \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d that Eurail pass sale because the sale established a single element necessary to recover under each cause of action brought by Sachs.

\n

Held: Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s suit falls outside the commercial activity exception and is therefore barred by sovereign immunity. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(a) Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s suit is not \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the sale of the Eurail pass for purposes of \xc2\xa71605(a)(2). Therefore, the Court has no need to address whether the Act allows the travel agent\xe2\x80\x99s sale of the Eurail pass to be attributed to OBB through common law principles of agency. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(1) Although the Act does not elaborate on the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon,\xe2\x80\x9d Saudi Arabia v. Nelson,\n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 349\n, provides sufficient guidance to resolve this case. There, the Court held that the \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d inquiry requires a court to determine the \xe2\x80\x9cparticular conduct on which the action is \xe2\x80\x98based,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 356, and identified that conduct by looking to \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98gravamen of the complaint,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 357. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(2) The Ninth Circuit used a flawed approach when it found that the \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d inquiry would be satisfied if the sale of the Eurail pass provided \xe2\x80\x9can element\xe2\x80\x9d of each of Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s claims. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach in Nelson is flatly incompatible with such a one-element approach, which necessarily requires a court to identify all the elements of each claim before finding that the claim falls outside \xc2\xa71605(a)(2). The Nelson Court did not undertake such an exhaustive claim-by-claim, element-by-element analysis or engage in the choice-of-law analysis necessary to such an undertaking. See id., at 356\xe2\x80\x93358. P.\xc2\xa07.

\n

(3) As opposed to adopting a one-element test, the Nelson Court zeroed in on the core of the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 suit\xe2\x80\x94the conduct that actually injured the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x94to identify the conduct that the suit was \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 358. All of Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s claims turn on the same tragic episode in Austria, allegedly caused by wrongful conduct and dangerous conditions in Austria, which led to injuries suffered in Austria. However Sachs frames her suit, the incident in Innsbruck, Austria, remains at its foundation. Any other approach would allow plaintiffs to evade the Act\xe2\x80\x99s restrictions through artful pleading. See id., at 363. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(b) Sachs now contends that her claims are \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d OBB\xe2\x80\x99s entire railway enterprise. Because that argument was never presented to any lower court, it is forfeited. See Taylor v. Freeland & Kronz,\n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 638\n\xe2\x80\x93646. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

737 F.\xc2\xa03d 584, reversed.

\n

OBB PERSONENVERKEHR AG, PETITIONER v. CAROL P. SACHS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[December 1, 2015]

\n

\n

Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act shields foreign states and their agencies from suit in United States courts unless the suit falls within one of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s specifically enumerated exceptions. This case concerns the scope of the commercial activity exception, which withdraws sovereign immunity in any case \xe2\x80\x9cin which the action is based upon a commercial activity carried on in the United States by [a] foreign state.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71605(a)(2).

\n

Respondent Carol Sachs is a resident of Californiawho purchased in the United States a Eurail pass forrail travel in Europe. She suffered traumatic personal in-juries when she fell onto the tracks at the Innsbruck, Austria, train station while attempting to board a train operated by the Austrian state-owned railway. She sued the railway in Federal District Court, arguing that her suit was not barred by sovereign immunity because it is \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the railway\xe2\x80\x99s sale of the pass to her in the United States. We disagree and conclude that her action is instead \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the railway\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in Innsbruck. We therefore hold that her suit falls outside the commercial activity exception and is barred by sovereign immunity.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Petitioner OBB Personenverkehr AG (OBB) operates a railway that carries nearly 235 million passengers each year on routes within Austria and to and from points beyond Austria\xe2\x80\x99s frontiers. OBB is wholly owned by OBB Holding Group, a joint-stock company created by the Republic of Austria. OBB Holding Group in turn is wholly owned by the Austrian Federal Ministry of Transport, Innovation, and Technology. Sachs v. Republic of Austria, 737 F.\xc2\xa03d 584, 587 (CA9 2013).

\n

OBB\xe2\x80\x94along with 29 other railways throughout Europe\xe2\x80\x94is a member of the Eurail Group, an association responsible for the marketing and management of the Eurail pass program. Brief for International Rail Transport Committee as Amicus Curiae 12; 737 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 587. Eurail passes allow their holders unlimited passage for a set period of time on participating Eurail Group railways. They are available only to non-Europeans, who may purchase them both directly from the Eurail Group and indirectly through a worldwide network of travel agents. Brief for International Rail Transport Committee as Amicus Curiae 12\xe2\x80\x9313, and n.\xc2\xa03; Brief for Respondent4\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

Carol Sachs is a resident of Berkeley, California. In March 2007, she purchased a Eurail pass over the Internet from The Rail Pass Experts, a Massachusetts-based travel agent. The following month, Sachs arrived at the Innsbruck train station, planning to use her Eurail pass to ride an OBB train to Prague. As she attempted to board the train, Sachs fell from the platform onto the tracks. OBB\xe2\x80\x99s moving train crushed her legs, both of which had to be amputated above the knee. 737 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 587\xe2\x80\x93588.

\n

Sachs sued OBB in the United States District Court for the Northern District of California, asserting five causes of action: (1) negligence; (2) strict liability for design defects in the train and platform; (3) strict liability for failure to warn of those design defects; (4) breach of an implied warranty of merchantability for providing a train and platform unsafe for their intended uses; and (5) breach of an implied warranty of fitness for providing a train and platform unfit for their intended uses. App. 14\xe2\x80\x9318. OBB claimed sovereign immunity and moved to dismiss the suit for lack of subject matter jurisdiction. 737 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 588.

\n

B

\n

The Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act \xe2\x80\x9cprovides the sole basis for obtaining jurisdiction over a foreign statein the courts of this country.\xe2\x80\x9d Argentine Republic v. Amerada Hess Shipping Corp.,\n\n488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 428,\n443 (1989)\n. The Act defines \xe2\x80\x9cforeign state\xe2\x80\x9d to include a state \xe2\x80\x9cagency or instrumentality,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71603(a), and both parties agree that OBB qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9cforeign state\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of the Act. OBB is therefore \xe2\x80\x9cpresumptively immune from the jurisdiction of United States courts\xe2\x80\x9d unless one of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s express exceptions to sovereign immunity applies. Saudi Arabia v. Nelson,\n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 349,\n355 (1993)\n. Sachs argues that her suit falls within the Act\xe2\x80\x99s commercial activity exception, which provides in part that a foreign state does not enjoy immunity when \xe2\x80\x9cthe action is based upon a commercial activity carried on in the United States by the foreign state.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71605(a)(2).[1]

\n

The District Court concluded that Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s suit did not fall within \xc2\xa71605(a)(2) and therefore granted OBB\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss. 2011 WL 816854, *1, *4 (ND Cal., Jan. 28, 2011). A divided panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed. 695 F.\xc2\xa03d 1021 (2012). The full court ordered rehearing en banc and, with three judges dissenting, reversed the panel decision. 737 F.\xc2\xa03d 584.

\n

The en banc majority first observed that, \xe2\x80\x9cbased on the agreement of the parties,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe only relevant commercial activity within the United States was [Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s] March 2007 purchase of a Eurail pass from the Rail Pass Experts,\xe2\x80\x9d a Massachusetts company. Id., at 591, n.\xc2\xa04 (internal quotation marks omitted). The court concluded that The Rail Pass Experts had acted as OBB\xe2\x80\x99s agent and, using common law principles of agency, attributed that Eurail pass sale to OBB. Id., at 591\xe2\x80\x93598.

\n

The court next asked whether Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s claims were \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the sale of the Eurail pass within the meaning of \xc2\xa71605(a)(2). The \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d determination, the court explained, requires that the commercial activity within the United States be \xe2\x80\x9cconnected with the conduct that gives rise to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s cause of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 590. But, the court continued, \xe2\x80\x9cit is not necessary that the entire claim be based upon the commercial activity of OBB.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 599. Rather, in the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, Sachs would satisfy the \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d requirement for a particular claim \xe2\x80\x9cif an element of [that] claim consists in conduct that occurred in commercial activity carried on in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Applying California law, see id., at 600, n.\xc2\xa014, the court analyzed Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s causes of action individually and concluded that the sale of the Eurail pass established a necessary element of each of her claims. Turning first to the negligence claim, the court found that Sachs was required to show that OBB owed her a duty of care as a passenger as one element of that claim. The court concluded that such a duty arose from the sale of the Eurail pass. Id., at 600\xe2\x80\x93602. Turning next to the other claims, the court determined that the existence of a \xe2\x80\x9ctransaction between a seller and a consumer\xe2\x80\x9d was a necessary element of Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s strict liability and breach of implied warranty claims. Id., at 602. The sale of the Eurail pass, the court noted, provided proof of such a transaction. Ibid. Having found that \xe2\x80\x9cthe sale of the Eurail pass in the United States forms an essential element of each of Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s claims,\xe2\x80\x9d the court concluded that each claim was \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon a commercial activity carried on in the United States\xe2\x80\x9d by OBB. Ibid.

\n

We granted certiorari. 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

II

\n

OBB contends that the sale of the Eurail pass is not attributable to the railway, reasoning that the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act does not allow attribution through principles found in the common law of agency. OBB also argues that even if such attribution were allowed under the Act, Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s suit is not \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the sale of the Eurail pass for purposes of \xc2\xa71605(a)(2). We agree with OBB on the second point and therefore do not reach the first.

\n

A

\n

The Act itself does not elaborate on the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon.\xe2\x80\x9d Our decision in Saudi Arabia v. Nelson,\n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 349\n, however, provides sufficient guidance to resolve this case. In Nelson, a husband and wife brought suit against Saudi Arabia and its state-owned hospital, seeking damages for intentional and negligent torts stemming from the husband\xe2\x80\x99s allegedly wrongful arrest, imprisonment, and torture by Saudi police while he was employed at a hospital in Saudi Arabia. Id., at 351, 353\xe2\x80\x93354. The Saudi defendants claimed sovereign immunity under the Act, arguing, inter alia, that \xc2\xa71605(a)(2) was inapplicable because the suit was \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d sovereign acts\xe2\x80\x94the exercise of Saudi police authority\xe2\x80\x94and not upon commercial activity. See Brief for Petitioners in Saudi Arabia v. Nelson, O. T. 1992, No. 91\xe2\x80\x93552, pp. 12\xe2\x80\x9314. The Nelsons countered that their suit was \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 commercial activities in \xe2\x80\x9crecruit[ing] Scott Nelson for work at the hospital, sign[ing] an employment contract with him, and subsequently employ[ing] him.\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 358. We rejected the Nelsons\xe2\x80\x99 arguments.

\n

The Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d inquiry, we reasoned, first requires a court to \xe2\x80\x9cidentify[\xe2\x80\x85] the particular conduct on which the [plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s] action is \xe2\x80\x98based.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 356. Considering dictionary definitions and lower court decisions, we explained that a court should identify that \xe2\x80\x9cparticular conduct\xe2\x80\x9d by looking to the \xe2\x80\x9cbasis\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cfoundation\xe2\x80\x9d for a claim, id., at 357 (citing dictionary definitions), \xe2\x80\x9cthose elements .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that, if proven, would entitle a plaintiff to relief,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98gravamen of the complaint,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (quoting Callejo v. Bancomer, S.\xc2\xa0A., 764 F.\xc2\xa02d 1101, 1109 (CA5 1985)). Under that analysis, we found that the commercial activities, while they \xe2\x80\x9cled to the conduct that eventually injured the Nelsons,\xe2\x80\x9d were not the particular conduct upon which their suit was based. The suit was instead based upon the Saudi sovereign acts that actually injured them. 507 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 358. The Nelsons\xe2\x80\x99 suit therefore did not fit within \xc2\xa71605(a)(2). Id., at 361\xe2\x80\x93362.

\n

B

\n

The Ninth Circuit held that Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s claims were \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the sale of the Eurail pass because the sale of the pass provided \xe2\x80\x9can element\xe2\x80\x9d of each of her claims. 737 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 599. Under Nelson, however, the mere fact that the sale of the Eurail pass would establish a single element of a claim is insufficient to demonstrate that the claim is \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d that sale for purposes of \xc2\xa71605(a)(2).

\n

The Ninth Circuit apparently derived its one-element test from an overreading of one part of one sentence in Nelson, in which we observed that \xe2\x80\x9cthe phrase [\xe2\x80\x98based upon\xe2\x80\x99] is read most naturally to mean those elements of a claim that, if proven, would entitle a plaintiff to relief under his theory of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 357. We do not see how that mention of elements\xe2\x80\x94plural\xe2\x80\x94could be considered an endorsement of a one-element test, nor how the particular element the Ninth Circuit singled out for each of Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s claims could be construed to entitle her to relief.

\n

Be that as it may, our analysis in Nelson is flatly incompatible with a one-element approach. A one-element test necessarily requires a court to identify all the elementsof each claim in a complaint before that court may re-ject those claims for falling outside \xc2\xa71605(a)(2). But we did not undertake such an exhaustive claim-by-claim, element-by-element analysis of the Nelsons\xe2\x80\x99 16 causes of action, nor did we engage in the choice-of-law analysis that would have been a necessary prelude to such an undertaking. Compare id., at 356\xe2\x80\x93358, with 737 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 600, n.\xc2\xa014 (noting disagreement over whether state or federal common law principles govern suits under the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act).

\n

Nelson instead teaches that an action is \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cparticular conduct\xe2\x80\x9d that constitutes the \xe2\x80\x9cgravamen\xe2\x80\x9d of the suit. Rather than individually analyzing each of the Nelsons\xe2\x80\x99 causes of action, we zeroed in on the core of their suit: the Saudi sovereign acts that actually injured them. As the Court explained:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cEven taking each of the Nelsons\xe2\x80\x99 allegations about Scott Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s recruitment and employment as true, those facts alone entitle the Nelsons to nothing under their theory of the case. The Nelsons have .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. alleged .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. personal injuries caused by [the defendants\xe2\x80\x99] intentional wrongs and by [the defendants\xe2\x80\x99] negligent failure to warn Scott Nelson that they might commit those wrongs. Those torts, and not the arguably commercial activities that preceded their commission, form the basis for the Nelsons\xe2\x80\x99 suit.\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 358.

\n

Under this analysis, the conduct constituting the gravamen of Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s suit plainly occurred abroad. All of her claims turn on the same tragic episode in Austria, alleg-edly caused by wrongful conduct and dangerous conditions in Austria, which led to injuries suffered in Austria.

\n

Sachs maintains that some of those claims are not limited to negligent conduct or unsafe conditions in Austria, but rather involve at least some wrongful action in the United States. Her strict liability claim for failure to warn, for example, alleges that OBB should have alerted her to the dangerous conditions at the Innsbruck train station when OBB sold the Eurail pass to her in the United States. Under any theory of the case that Sachs pre-sents, however, there is nothing wrongful about the sale of the Eurail pass standing alone. Without the existence of the unsafe boarding conditions in Innsbruck, there would have been nothing to warn Sachs about when she bought the Eurail pass. However Sachs frames her suit, the incident in Innsbruck remains at its foundation.

\n

As we explained in Nelson, any other approach would allow plaintiffs to evade the Act\xe2\x80\x99s restrictions through artful pleading. For example, any plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9ccould recast virtually any claim of intentional tort .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as a claim of failure to warn, simply by charging the defendant with an obligation to announce its own tortious propensity before indulging it.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 363. To allow such \xe2\x80\x9crecast[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d of a complaint, we reasoned, would \xe2\x80\x9cgive jurisdictional significance to [a] feint of language,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby \xe2\x80\x9ceffectively thwart[ing] the Act\xe2\x80\x99s manifest purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

A century ago, in a letter to then-Professor Frankfurter, Justice Holmes wrote that the \xe2\x80\x9cessentials\xe2\x80\x9d of a personal injury narrative will be found at the \xe2\x80\x9cpoint of contact\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthe place where the boy got his fingers pinched.\xe2\x80\x9d Letter (Dec. 19, 1915), in Holmes and Frankfurter: Their Correspondence, 1912\xe2\x80\x931934, p. 40 (R. Mennel & C. Compston eds. 1996). At least in this case, that insight holds true. Regardless of whether Sachs seeks relief under claims for negligence, strict liability for failure to warn, or breach of implied warranty, the \xe2\x80\x9cessentials\xe2\x80\x9d of her suit for purposes of \xc2\xa71605(a)(2) are found in Austria.[2]

\n

III

\n

Sachs raises a new argument in this Court in an attempt to fit her claims within \xc2\xa71605(a)(2). In addition to arguing that her claims are \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the sale of the Eurail pass, she now contends that her suit is \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cOBB\xe2\x80\x99s overall commercial railway enterprise.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 24; see also Tr. of Oral Arg. 38.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ommercial activity carried on in the United States by the foreign state,\xe2\x80\x9d as used in \xc2\xa71605(a)(2), is defined to mean \xe2\x80\x9ccommercial activity carried on by such state and having substantial contact with the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71603(e). Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s new theory is that OBB\xe2\x80\x99s entire railway enterprise constitutes the \xe2\x80\x9ccommercial activity\xe2\x80\x9d that has the requisite \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial contact with the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d because OBB reaches out to American customers by marketing and selling Eurail passes in the United States.

\n

That argument was never presented to any lower court and is therefore forfeited. Sachs argued in the courts below only that her claims were \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the sale of the Eurail pass, and the lower courts resolved the case on that understanding. See, e.g., 737 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 591, n.\xc2\xa04 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe district court concluded, based on the agreement of the parties, that \xe2\x80\x98the only relevant commercial activity within the United States was plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s March 2007 purchase of a Eurail Pass from the Rail Pass Experts.\xe2\x80\x99 We consider only the relevant conduct as defined by the district court.\xe2\x80\x9d).[3] Indeed, when we granted certiorari, the relevant question presented for our review was whether Sachs\xe2\x80\x99s claims were \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9csale of the ticket in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i; accord, Brief for Respondent i. We have answered that question in the negative. Absent unusual circumstances\xe2\x80\x94none of which is present here\xe2\x80\x94we will not entertain arguments not made below. Taylor v. Freeland & Kronz,\n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 638\n\xe2\x80\x93646 (1992).

\n

We therefore conclude that Sachs has failed to demonstrate that her suit falls within the commercial activity exception in \xc2\xa71605(a)(2). OBB has sovereign immunity under the Act, and accordingly the courts of the United States lack jurisdiction over the suit.

\n

The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.
