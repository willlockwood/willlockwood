SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

AMGEN INC., et\xc2\xa0al. v. STEVE HARRIS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93278.\xe2\x80\x83Decided January 25, 2016

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

The Court considers for the second time the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s determination that respondent stockholders\xe2\x80\x99 complaint states a claim against petitioner fiduciaries for breach of the duty of prudence. The first time, the Court vacated and remanded in light of Fifth Third Bancorp v. Dudenhoeffer, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), a case which set forth the standards for stating a claim for breach of the duty of prudence against fiduciaries who manage employee stock ownership plans (ESOPs). On remand, the Ninth Circuit reiterated its conclusion that the complaint states such a claim. The Court now reverses and remands.

\n

The stockholders are former employees of Amgen Inc. and its subsidiary Amgen Manufacturing, Limited, who participated in plans that qualified under\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71107(d)(3)(A) as eligible individual account plans. Like ESOPs, these plans offer ownership in employer stock as an option to employees. The parties agree that the decision in Fifth Third is fully applicable to the plans at issue here. See 788 F.\xc2\xa03d 916, 935 (2014).

\n

All of the plans had holdings in the Amgen Common Stock Fund (composed, unsurprisingly, of Amgen common stock) during the relevant period. The value of Amgen stock fell, and in 2007, the stockholders filed a class action against petitioner fiduciaries alleging that they had breached their fiduciary duties, including the duty of prudence, under the Employee Retirement Income Secur-ity Act of 1974 (ERISA),\n\n88Stat.\n829, as amended,\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001 et\xc2\xa0seq. The District Court granted the fiduciaries\xe2\x80\x99 motion to dismiss, and the Ninth Circuit reversed, Harris v. Amgen, Inc., 738 F.\xc2\xa03d 1026 (2013). The fiduciaries sought certiorari.

\n

While that petition was pending, this Court issued a decision that concerned the duty of prudence owed by ERISA fiduciaries who administer ESOPs. That decision, Fifth Third, held that such ERISA fiduciaries are not entitled to a presumption of prudence but are \xe2\x80\x9csubject to the same duty of prudence that applies to ERISA fiduciaries in general, except that they need not diversify the fund\xe2\x80\x99s assets.\xe2\x80\x9d 573 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 1\xe2\x80\x932).

\n

Notwithstanding the lack of a presumption of prudence, Fifth Third noted that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress sought to encourage the creation of\xe2\x80\x9d employee stock-ownership plans, id., at ___ (slip op., at 14), a purpose that the decision recognized may come into tension with ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s general duty of prudence. Moreover, ESOP fiduciaries confront unique challenges given \xe2\x80\x9cthe potential for conflict\xe2\x80\x9d that arises when fiduciaries are alleged to have imprudently \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ed] to act on inside information they had about the value of the employer\xe2\x80\x99s stock.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 13). Fifth Third therefore laid out standards to help \xe2\x80\x9cdivide the plausible sheep from the meritless goats,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___ (slip op., at 15):

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cTo state a claim for breach of the duty of prudence on the basis of inside information, a plaintiff must plausibly allege an alternative action that the defendant could have taken that would have been consistent with the securities laws and that a prudent fiduciary in the same circumstances would not have viewed as more likely to harm the fund than to help it.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 18).

\n

It further clarified that courts should determine whether the complaint itself states a claim satisfying that liability standard:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[L]ower courts faced with such claims should also consider whether the complaint has plausibly alleged that a prudent fiduciary in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s position could not have concluded that stopping purchases\xe2\x80\x94which the market might take as a sign that insider fiduciaries viewed the employer\xe2\x80\x99s stock as a bad investment\xe2\x80\x94or publicly disclosing negative information would do more harm than good to the fund by causing a drop in the stock price and a concomitant drop in the value of the stock already held by the fund.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 20) (emphasis added).

\n

In the matter that is once again before the Court here, following the issuance of Fifth Third, the Court granted the fiduciaries\xe2\x80\x99 first petition for a writ of certiorari, va-cated the judgment, and remanded for further proceedings consistent with that decision. Amgen Inc. v. Harris, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014). On remand, the Ninth Circuit reversed again the dismissal of the complaint and denied the fiduciaries\xe2\x80\x99 petition for rehearing en banc. See 788 F.\xc2\xa03d 916. The fiduciaries once more sought certiorari.

\n

The Court now holds that the Ninth Circuit failed to properly evaluate the complaint. That court explained that its previous opinion (that is, the one it issued before Fifth Third was decided) \xe2\x80\x9chad already assumed\xe2\x80\x9d the standards for ERISA fiduciary liability laid out by this Court in Fifth Third. 788 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 940. And it reasoned that the complaint at issue here satisfies those standards because when \xe2\x80\x9cthe federal securities laws require disclosure of material information,\xe2\x80\x9d it is \xe2\x80\x9cquite plausible\xe2\x80\x9d that removing the Amgen Common Stock Fund \xe2\x80\x9cfrom the list of investment options\xe2\x80\x9d would not \xe2\x80\x9ccaus[e] undue harm to plan participants.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 937\xe2\x80\x93938. The Ninth Circuit, however, failed to assess whether the complaint in its current form \xe2\x80\x9chas plausibly alleged\xe2\x80\x9d that a prudent fiduciary in the same position \xe2\x80\x9ccould not have concluded\xe2\x80\x9d that the alternative action \xe2\x80\x9cwould do more harm than good.\xe2\x80\x9d Fifth Third, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 20).

\n

The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s proposition that removing the Amgen Common Stock Fund from the list of investment options was an alternative action that could plausibly have satisfied Fifth Third\xe2\x80\x99s standards may be true. If so, the facts and allegations supporting that proposition should appear in the stockholders\xe2\x80\x99 complaint. Having examined the complaint, the Court has not found sufficient facts and allegations to state a claim for breach of the duty of prudence.

\n

Although the Ninth Circuit did not correctly apply Fifth Third, the stockholders are the masters of their complaint. The Court leaves to the District Court in the first instance whether the stockholders may amend it in order to adequately plead a claim for breach of the duty of prudence guided by the standards provided in Fifth Third.

\n

The petition for certiorari is granted. The judgment of the Ninth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.
