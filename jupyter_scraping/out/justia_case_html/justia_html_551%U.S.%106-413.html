UTTECHT, SUPERINTENDENT, WASHINGTON STATE PENITENTIARY v. BROWN

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x93413.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 17, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 4, 2007

\n

A Washington jury sentenced respondent Brown to death, and the state appellate courts affirmed. Subsequently, the Federal District Court denied Brown\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition, but the Ninth Circuit reversed, finding that under Witherspoon v. Illinois, 391 U. S. 510, and its progeny, the state trial court had violated Brown\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth and Fourteenth Amendment rights by excusing \xe2\x80\x9cJurors Z\xe2\x80\x9d for cause on the ground that he could not be impartial in deciding whether to impose a death sentence.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Courts reviewing claims of error under Witherspoon and Wainwright v. Witt, 469 U. S. 412, especially federal habeas courts, owe deference to the trial court, which is in a superior position to determine a potential juror\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor and qualifications. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents establish at least four relevant principles. First, a criminal defendant has the right to an impartial jury drawn from a venire that has not been tilted in favor of capital punishment by selective prosecutorial challenges for cause. Witherspoon, supra, at 521. Second, the State has a strong interest in having jurors who are able to apply capital punishment within the framework state law prescribes. Witt, 469 U. S., at 416. Third, to balance these interests, a juror who is substantially impaired in the ability to impose the death penalty under the state-law framework can be excused for cause, but if the juror is not so impaired, removal for cause is impermissible. Id., at 424. Fourth, in determining whether a potential juror\xe2\x80\x99s removal would vindicate the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest without violating the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right, the trial court bases its judgment in part on the juror\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor, a judgment owed deference by reviewing courts. Id., at 424\xe2\x80\x93434. The trial court is in a superior position to assess demeanor, a factor critical in assessing the attitude and qualifications of potential jurors. Id., at 428. The Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996\xe2\x80\x99s requirements provide additional, and binding, directions to accord deference, creating an independent, high standard to be met before a federal court may issue a habeas writ to set aside state-court rulings. By not according the required deference here, the Ninth Circuit failed to respect the limited role of federal habeas relief in this area. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0In applying the Witherspoon-Witt rule, it is instructive to consider the entire voir dire in Brown\xe2\x80\x99s case and then turn to Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s questioning. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Here, 11 days of voir dire were devoted to determining whether potential jurors were death qualified. During that phase, 11 of the jurors the defense challenged for cause were excused. The defense objected to 7 of the 12 jurors the State challenged for cause, and only 2 of those 7 were excused. Before deciding a contested challenge, the court allowed each side to explain its position and recall a potential juror. It also gave careful and measured explanations for its decisions. Before individual oral examination, the court distributed a questionnaire asking jurors to explain their attitudes toward the death penalty and explained that Brown was only eligible for death or life in prison without possibility of release or parole. It repeated the sentencing options before Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s group was questioned. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The transcript reveals that, despite the preceding instructions and information, Juror Z had both serious misunderstandings about his responsibility as a juror and an attitude toward capital punishment that could have prevented him from returning a death sentence under the facts of this case. He was told at least four times that Brown could not be released from prison and stated six times that he could follow the law. But he also gave more equivocal statements that he would consider the death penalty only if there was no possibility that Brown would be released to reoffend. When the State challenged Juror Z on the grounds that he was confused about the conditions under which death could be imposed and seemed to believe it only appropriate when there was a risk of release and recidivism, the defense volunteered that it had no objection. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit erred in holding that both the state trial court\xe2\x80\x99s excusal of Juror Z and the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s affirmance were contrary to, or an unreasonable application of, clearly established federal law. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Contrary to the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion, the State Supreme Court explicitly found that Juror Z was substantially impaired. Even absent this explicit finding, the only fair reading of the opinion is that the state court applied the Witt standard in assessing his excusal. Regardless, there is no requirement in a case involving the Witt-Witherspoon rule that a state appellate court make particular reference to each juror\xe2\x80\x99s excusal, for it is the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling that counts. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0On this record, the trial court acted well within its discretion in granting the State\xe2\x80\x99s motion to excuse Juror Z. His answers, on their face, could have led the trial court to believe that he would be substantially impaired in his ability to impose the death penalty absent the possibility that Brown would be released and would reoffend. The trial court, furthermore, is entitled to deference because it had an opportunity to observe Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor. The State\xe2\x80\x99s challenge, Brown\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of an objection, and the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s excusal of Juror Z support the conclusion that the interested parties all felt that removal was appropriate under the Witherspoon-Witt rule. While there is no independent federal requirement that a state-court defendant object to the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to preserve a Witherspoon claim, voluntary acquiescence to, or confirmation of, a juror\xe2\x80\x99s removal can be taken into account. The defense did not just deny a conscientious trial judge an opportunity to explain his judgment or correct an error; it also deprived reviewing courts of further factual findings to help explain the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. The need to defer to the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor decision does not foreclose the possibility of reversal where the record discloses no basis for a substantial impairment finding, but the record here does not show the trial court exceeded its discretion in excusing Juror Z. The State Supreme Court recognized the deference owed and, contrary to the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s misreading of its opinion, identified the correct standard required by federal law and found it satisfied. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The Court is not persuaded by Brown\xe2\x80\x99s additional arguments to depart from the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination of the state law at issue or to ignore Brown\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object to Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s excusal. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

451 F.\xc2\xa03d 946, reversed and remanded.

JEFFREY UTTECHT, SUPERINTENDENT, WASH-\nINGTON STATE PENITENTIARY, PETITIONER v.\nCAL COBURN BROWN

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 4, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Cal Coburn Brown robbed, raped, tortured, and murdered one woman in Washington. Two days later, he robbed, raped, tortured, and attempted to murder a second woman in California. Apprehended, Brown confessed to these crimes and pleaded guilty to the California offenses, for which he received a sentence of life imprisonment. The State of Washington, however, sought the death penalty and brought Brown to trial. Based on the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdicts in the guilt and sentencing phases of the trial, Brown was sentenced to death. His conviction and sentence were affirmed by the Supreme Court of the State of Washington. State v. Brown, 132 Wash. 2d.\xc2\xa0529, 940 P.\xc2\xa02d 546 (1997) (en banc).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Brown filed a petition for writ of habeas corpus in the United States District Court for the Western District of Washington. The District Court denied the petition, App. to Pet. for Cert. 77a\xe2\x80\x9379a, 91a, but the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed. Brown v. Lambert, 451 F.\xc2\xa03d 946 (2006). The Court of Appeals considered, among other arguments for setting aside the capital sentence, the contention that under Witherspoon v. Illinois, 391 U. S. 510 (1968), and its progeny, the state trial court had violated Brown\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth and Fourteenth Amendment rights by excusing three potential jurors\xe2\x80\x94whom we refer to as Jurors X, Y, and Z\xe2\x80\x94for cause. The State moved to excuse these jurors due to the concern that they could not be impartial in deciding whether to impose a death sentence. The Court of Appeals held it was proper to excuse Jurors X and Y, but agreed with the defense that it was unconstitutional to excuse Juror Z for cause. On this premise the court held that Brown\xe2\x80\x99s death sentence could not stand, requiring that Brown receive a new sentencing trial more than a decade after his conviction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 549 U. S. __ (2007), and we reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When considering the controlling precedents, Witherspoon is not the final word, but it is a necessary starting point. During the voir dire that preceded William Witherspoon\xe2\x80\x99s capital trial, the prosecution succeeded in removing a substantial number of jurors based on their general scruples against inflicting the death penalty. The State challenged, and the trial court excused for cause, 47 members of the 96-person venire, without significant examination of the individual prospective jurors. 391 U. S., at 514\xe2\x80\x93515; see also Brief for Petitioner in Witherspoon v. Illinois, O. T. 1967, No. 1015, p.\xc2\xa04. The Court held that the systematic removal of those in the venire opposed to the death penalty had led to a jury \xe2\x80\x9cuncommonly willing to condemn a man to die,\xe2\x80\x9d 391 U. S., at 521, and thus \xe2\x80\x9cwoefully short of that impartiality to which the petitioner was entitled under the Sixth and Fourteenth Amendments,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 518. Because \xe2\x80\x9c[a] man who opposes the death penalty, no less than one who favors it, can make the discretionary judgment entrusted to him by the State,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 519, the Court held that \xe2\x80\x9ca sentence of death cannot be carried out if the jury that imposed or recommended it was chosen by excluding veniremen for cause simply because they voiced general objections to the death penalty,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 522. The Court also set forth, in dicta in a footnote, a strict standard for when an individual member of the venire may be removed for cause on account of his or her views on the death penalty. Id., at 522\xe2\x80\x93523, n. 21.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Wainwright v. Witt, 469 U. S. 412 (1985), the Court explained that \xe2\x80\x9cWitherspoon is best understood in the context of its facts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 418. The Court noted that in Witherspoon the trial court had excused half the venire\xe2\x80\x94every juror with conscientious objections to capital punishments. 469 U. S., at 416. Furthermore, the state sentencing scheme under which Witherspoon\xe2\x80\x99s sentence was imposed permitted the jury \xe2\x80\x9cunlimited discretion in choice of sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 421. When a juror is given unlimited discretion, the Court explained, all he or she must do to follow instructions is consider the death penalty, even if in the end he or she would not be able to impose it. Ibid. Rejecting the strict standard found in Witherspoon\xe2\x80\x99s footnote 21, the Court recognized that the diminished discretion now given to capital jurors and the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in administering its capital punishment scheme called for a different standard. The Court relied on Adams v. Texas, 448 U. S. 38, 45 (1980), which provided the following standard: \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hether the juror\xe2\x80\x99s views would prevent or substantially impair the performance of his duties as a juror in accordance with his instructions and his oath.\xe2\x80\x9d Witt, 469 U. S., at 424 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court in Witt instructed that, in applying this standard, reviewing courts are to accord deference to the trial court. Deference is owed regardless of whether the trial court engages in explicit analysis regarding substantial impairment; even the granting of a motion to excuse for cause constitutes an implicit finding of bias. Id., at 430. The judgment as to \xe2\x80\x9cwhether a veniremen is biased \xe2\x80\xa6 is based upon determinations of demeanor and credibility that are peculiarly within a trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s province. Such determinations [are] entitled to deference even on direct review; the respect paid such findings in a habeas proceeding certainly should be no less.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 428 (internal quotation marks, footnote, and brackets omitted). And the finding may be upheld even in the absence of clear statements from the juror that he or she is impaired because \xe2\x80\x9cmany veniremen simply cannot be asked enough questions to reach the point where their bias has been made \xe2\x80\x98unmistakably clear\xe2\x80\x99; these veniremen may not know how they will react when faced with imposing the death sentence, or may be unable to articulate, or may wish to hide their true feelings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 424\xe2\x80\x93425. Thus, when there is ambiguity in the prospective juror\xe2\x80\x99s statements, \xe2\x80\x9cthe trial court, aided as it undoubtedly [is] by its assessment of [the venireman\xe2\x80\x99s] demeanor, [is] entitled to resolve it in favor of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 434.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The rule of deference was reinforced in Darden v. Wainwright, 477 U. S. 168 (1986). There, the State had challenged a potential juror, and the defense had not objected to his removal. Without further questioning from the trial court, the juror was excused. Id., at 178. The petitioner argued to this Court that the transcript of voir dire did not show that the removed juror was substantially impaired because the critical answer he had given was ambiguous. The Court rejected this argument. \xe2\x80\x9c[O]ur inquiry does not end with a mechanical recitation of a single question and answer.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 176. Even when \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he precise wording of the question asked of [the venireman], and the answer he gave, do not by themselves compel the conclusion that he could not under any circumstance recommend the death penalty,\xe2\x80\x9d the need to defer to the trial court remains because so much may turn on a potential juror\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor. Id., at 178. The absence of an objection, and the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to engage in further questioning as it had prior to excusing other jurors, supported the conclusion that the juror was impaired. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Gray v. Mississippi, 481 U. S. 648 (1987), the Court addressed once more a case involving not the excusal of a single juror but rather systematic exclusion. The State had lodged for-cause or peremptory challenges against every juror who \xe2\x80\x9cexpressed any degree of uncertainty in the ability to cast . . . a vote\xe2\x80\x9d for the death penalty, id., at 652, and quickly exhausted all 12 of its peremptory challenges, id., at 653. The prosecution then challenged a juror who had expressed no opposition to the death penalty and had said many times that she could return a death sentence. The trial court denied the challenge. Id., at 654\xe2\x80\x93655. Arguing that the trial court had erroneously denied certain earlier challenges for cause, and thus had forced the State to waste peremptory challenges, the prosecution sought to reopen those previous challenges. The trial court refused to do so, but removed the current juror, over objection from the defense. Id., at 655. On appeal all of the state judges agreed the juror could not be excused for cause under either the Witherspoon or the Witt standard, but the majority held it was appropriate, under the circumstances, to treat the challenge in question as a peremptory strike. 481 U. S., at 656\xe2\x80\x93657.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court reversed, holding that the juror had been removed for cause and that she was not substantially impaired under the controlling Witt standard. 481 U. S., at 659. The error was not subject to harmlessness review, and thus the sentence could not stand. Ibid. Gray represents a rare case, however, because in the typical situation there will be a state-court finding of substantial impairment; in Gray, the state courts had found the opposite, which makes that precedent of limited significance to the instant case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These precedents establish at least four principles of relevance here. First, a criminal defendant has the right to an impartial jury drawn from a venire that has not been tilted in favor of capital punishment by selective prosecutorial challenges for cause. Witherspoon, 391 U. S., at 521. Second, the State has a strong interest in having jurors who are able to apply capital punishment within the framework state law prescribes. Witt, 469 U. S., at 416. Third, to balance these interests, a juror who is substantially impaired in his or her ability to impose the death penalty under the state-law framework can be excused for cause; but if the juror is not substantially impaired, removal for cause is impermissible. Id., at 424. Fourth, in determining whether the removal of a potential juror would vindicate the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest without violating the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right, the trial court makes a judgment based in part on the demeanor of the juror, a judgment owed deference by reviewing courts. Id., at 424\xe2\x80\x93434.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Deference to the trial court is appropriate because it is in a position to assess the demeanor of the venire, and of the individuals who compose it, a factor of critical importance in assessing the attitude and qualifications of potential jurors. Id., at 428; Darden, supra, at 178. Leading treatises in the area make much of nonverbal communication. See, e.g., V. Starr & M. McCormick, Jury Selection 389\xe2\x80\x93523 (3d ed. 2001); J. Frederick, Mastering Voir Dire and Jury Selection 39\xe2\x80\x9356 (2d ed. 2005).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The requirements of the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), 110 Stat. 1214, of course, provide additional, and binding, directions to accord deference. The provisions of that statute create an independent, high standard to be met before a federal court may issue a writ of habeas corpus to set aside state-court rulings. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2); Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362, 413 (2000).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By not according the required deference, the Court of Appeals failed to respect the limited role of federal habeas relief in this area prescribed by Congress and by our cases.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In applying the principles of Witherspoon and Witt, it is instructive to consider the entire voir dire in Brown\xe2\x80\x99s case. Spanning more than two weeks, the process entailed an examination of numerous prospective jurors. After the third day of the voir dire, during which few jurors were questioned, the trial court explained the process would \xe2\x80\x9chave to go a little bit faster.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 1398. The next day, the court reiterated this concern, for it had told the jury the trial would take no more than six weeks in order not to conflict with the Christmas holidays. Id., at 1426.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Eleven days of the voir dire were devoted to determining whether the potential jurors were death qualified. During that phase alone, the defense challenged 18 members of the venire for cause. Despite objections from the State, 11 of those prospective jurors were excused. As for the State, it made 12 challenges for cause; defense counsel objected seven times; and only twice was the juror excused following an objection from the defense. Before deciding a contested challenge, the trial court gave each side a chance to explain its position and recall the potential juror for additional questioning. When issuing its decisions the court gave careful and measured explanations. See, e.g., id., at 2601\xe2\x80\x932604 (denying the State\xe2\x80\x99s motion to excuse a juror following an objection for defense); App. 97\xe2\x80\x93100 (granting the State\xe2\x80\x99s motion to excuse Juror X despite an objection from defense).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before the State challenged Juror Z, the defense moved to excuse a potential juror who had demonstrated some confusion. After argument from both counsel, the trial court explained that it would be open to further questioning if one of the parties felt the juror\xe2\x80\x99s position could be clarified: \xe2\x80\x9cI thought at first the both of you were wanting to excuse [this juror] since he seemed kind of confused to both sides, but if there really is a question, let me know and I don\xe2\x80\x99t have any hesitation about bringing the juror out here and following up.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 26. Consistent with the need for an efficient voir dire, the court also told counsel: \xe2\x80\x9cLet me point something out to both sides. If you are going to agree on a challenge, \xe2\x80\xa6 we can shortcut some of what happens out here.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Setting aside the disputed circumstances of Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s removal, the defense refrained from objecting to the State\xe2\x80\x99s challenges for cause only when the challenged juror was explicit that he or she would not impose the death penalty or could not understand the burden of proof. See Tr. 1457, 1912, 2261, 2940. For other jurors, the defense objections were vigorous and, it seems, persuasive. The defense argued that the jurors\xe2\x80\x99 equivocal statements reflected careful thinking and responsibility, not substantial impairment. See, e.g., id., at 1791, 2111, 2815. The tenacity of Brown\xe2\x80\x99s counsel was demonstrated when, long after the trial court had overruled the defense objection and excused Juror Y, the defense moved in writing to have her returned for further questioning and rehabilitation. Id., at 3151\xe2\x80\x933154. The trial court denied this motion after argument from both parties. Id., at 3154.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The defense also lodged its own challenges for cause. In defending them against the State\xe2\x80\x99s objections, defense counsel argued, contrary to the position Brown takes in this Court, that a trial court cannot rely upon a potential juror\xe2\x80\x99s bare promises to follow instructions and obey the law. See, e.g., id., at 1713\xe2\x80\x931714, 1960\xe2\x80\x931961, 2772\xe2\x80\x932773, 3014\xe2\x80\x933016. With regard to one juror, defense counsel argued:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cAny time this individual was asked any questions about following the law, he will always indicate that he will. But when we look to see . . . his view[s] on the death penalty, . . . they [are] so strong that they would substantially impair his ability to follow the law and to follow his oath as a juror.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1960\xe2\x80\x931961.

\n

\nIn at least two instances this argument appears to have prevailed when the trial court overruled the State\xe2\x80\x99s objection to Brown\xe2\x80\x99s challenge for cause.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A final, necessary part of this history is the instruction the venire received from the court concerning the sentencing options in the case. Before individual oral examination, the trial court distributed a questionnaire asking jurors to explain their attitudes toward the death penalty. When distributing the questionnaire, the court explained the general structure of the trial and the burden of proof. It described how the penalty phase would function:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f you found Mr. Brown guilty of the crime of first degree murder with one or more aggravating circumstances, then you would be reconvened for a second phase called a sentencing phase. During that sentencing phase proceeding you could hear additional evidence [and] arguments concerning the penalty to be imposed. You would then be asked to retire to determine whether the death penalty should be imposed or whether the punishment should be life imprisonment without the possibility of parole.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIn making this determination you would be asked the following question: Having in mind the crime with which the defendant has been found guilty, are you convinced beyond a reasonable doubt that there are not sufficient mitigating circumstances to merit leniency? If you unanimously answered yes to this question, the sentence would be death. . . . [Otherwise] the sentence would be life imprisonment without the possibility of release or parole.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at. 1089\xe2\x80\x931090.

\n

\nAfter the questionnaires were filled out, the jurors were provided with handbooks that explained the trial process and the sentencing phase in greater depth. Small groups of potential jurors were then brought in to be questioned. Before Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s group began, the court explained once more that if Brown were convicted, \xe2\x80\x9cthere are only two penalties that a jury could return, one is life in prison without possibility of release or parole. And that literally means exactly that, a true life in prison without release or parole.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 2016.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With this background, we turn to Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s examination.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Juror Z was examined on the seventh day of the voir dire and the fifth day of the death-qualification phase. The State argues that Juror Z was impaired not by his general outlook on the death penalty, but rather by his position regarding the specific circumstances in which the death penalty would be appropriate. The transcript of Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s questioning reveals that, despite the preceding instructions and information, he had both serious misunderstandings about his responsibility as a juror and an attitude toward capital punishment that could have prevented him from returning a death sentence under the facts of this case.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the voir dire procedures, the prosecution and defense alternated in commencing the examination. For Juror Z, the defense went first. When questioned, Juror Z demonstrated no general opposition to the death penalty or scruples against its infliction. In fact, he soon explained that he \xe2\x80\x9cbelieve[d] in the death penalty in severe situations.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 58. He elaborated, \xe2\x80\x9cI don\xe2\x80\x99t think it should never happen, and I don\xe2\x80\x99t think it should happen 10 times a week either.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 63. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]here [are] times when it would be appropriate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The questioning soon turned to when that would be so. Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s first example was one in which \xe2\x80\x9cthe defendant actually came out and said that he actually wanted to die.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 59. Defense set this aside and sought another example. Despite having been told at least twice by the trial court that if convicted of first-degree murder, Brown could not be released from prison, the only example Juror Z could provide was when \xe2\x80\x9ca person is . . . incorrigible and would reviolate if released.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 62. The defense counsel replied that there would be no possibility of Brown\xe2\x80\x99s release and asked whether the lack of arguments about recidivism during the penalty phase would frustrate Juror Z. He answered, \xe2\x80\x9cI\xe2\x80\x99m not sure.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 63.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State began its examination of Juror Z by noting that his questionnaire indicated he was \xe2\x80\x9cin favor of the death penalty if it is proved beyond a shadow of a doubt if a person has killed and would kill again.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 69. The State explained that the burden of proof was beyond a reasonable doubt, not beyond a shadow of a doubt, and asked whether Juror Z understood. He answered, \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t would have to be in my mind very obvious that the person would reoffend.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 70. In response the State once more explained to Juror Z, now for at least the fourth time, that there was no possibility of Brown\xe2\x80\x99s being released to reoffend. Juror Z explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t wasn\xe2\x80\x99t until today that I became aware that we had a life without parole in the state of Washington,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 71, although in fact a week earlier the trial judge had explained to Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s group that there was no possibility of parole when a defendant was convicted of aggravated first-degree murder. The prosecution then asked, \xe2\x80\x9cAnd now that you know there is such a thing . . . can you think of a time when you would be willing to impose a death penalty . . . ?\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 71\xe2\x80\x9372. Juror Z answered, \xe2\x80\x9cI would have to give that some thought.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 72. He supplied no further answer to the question.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State sought to probe Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s position further by asking whether he could \xe2\x80\x9cconsider\xe2\x80\x9d the death penalty; Juror Z said he could, including under the general facts of Brown\xe2\x80\x99s crimes. Ibid. When asked whether he no longer felt it was necessary for the State to show that Brown would reoffend, Juror Z gave this confusing answer: \xe2\x80\x9cI do feel that way if parole is an option, without parole as an option. I believe in the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 72\xe2\x80\x9373. Finally, when asked whether he could impose the death penalty when there was no possibility of parole, Juror Z answered, \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f I was convinced that was the appropriate measure.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 73. Over the course of his questioning, he stated six times that he could consider the death penalty or follow the law, see id., at 62, 70, 72, 73, but these responses were interspersed with more equivocal statements.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State challenged Juror Z, explaining that he was confused about the conditions under which death could be imposed and seemed to believe it only appropriate when there was a risk of release and recidivism. Id., at 75. Before the trial court could ask Brown for a response, the defense volunteered, \xe2\x80\x9cWe have no objection.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The court then excused Juror Z. Ibid.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On federal habeas review, years after the conclusion of the voir dire, the Court of Appeals granted Brown relief and overturned his sentence. The court held that both the state trial court\xe2\x80\x99s excusal of Juror Z and the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s affirmance of that ruling were contrary to, or an unreasonable application of, clearly established federal law. 451 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 953. The Court of Appeals held that the Supreme Court of Washington had failed to find that Juror Z was substantially impaired; it further held that the State Supreme Court could not have made that finding in any event because the transcript unambiguously proved Juror Z was not substantially impaired. For these reasons, explained the Court of Appeals, the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to excuse Juror Z was contrary to the Witherspoon-Witt rule despite Brown\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object. Each of the holdings of the Court of Appeals is wrong.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As part of its exposition and analysis, the Court of Appeals found fault with the opinion of the Supreme Court of Washington. It stated that although the State Supreme Court had held that Jurors X and Y were substantially impaired, the same \xe2\x80\x9cfinding is missing from the state court\xe2\x80\x99s discussion\xe2\x80\x9d of Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s excusal. 451 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 950. The Court of Appeals therefore held \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Washington Supreme Court in this case applied the wrong standard with respect to Juror Z.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 953, n.\xc2\xa010. This is an erroneous summary of the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. The state court did make an explicit ruling that Juror Z was impaired. In a portion of the opinion entitled \xe2\x80\x9cSummary and Conclusions,\xe2\x80\x9d the court held: \xe2\x80\x9cThe trial court properly exercised its discretion in excusing for cause prospective jurors [X, Y, and Z] during voir dire. Their views would have prevented or substantially impaired their ability to follow the court\xe2\x80\x99s instructions and abide by their oaths as jurors.\xe2\x80\x9d Brown, 132 Wash. 2d, at 630, 940 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 598, 599. It is unclear why the Court of Appeals overlooked or disregarded this finding, and it was mistaken in faulting the completeness of the Supreme Court of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even absent this explicit finding, the Supreme Court of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s opinion was not contrary to our cases. The court identified the Witherspoon-Witt rule, recognized that our precedents required deference to the trial court, and applied an abuse-of-discretion standard. 132 Wash. 2d, at 601, 940 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 584. Having set forth that framework, it explained:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Brown] did not object at trial to the State\xe2\x80\x99s challenge of [Juror Z] for cause. At any rate, [Juror Z] was properly excused. On voir dire he indicated he would impose the death penalty where the defendant \xe2\x80\x98would reviolate if released,\xe2\x80\x99 which is not a correct statement of the law. He also misunderstood the State\xe2\x80\x99s burden of proof . . . although he was corrected later. The trial court did not abuse its discretion in excusing [Juror Z] for cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 604, 940 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 585.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The only fair reading of the quoted language is that the state court applied the Witt standard in assessing the excusal of Juror Z. Regardless, there is no requirement in a case involving the Witherspoon-Witt rule that a state appellate court make particular reference to the excusal of each juror. See Early v. Packer, 537 U. S. 3, 9 (2002) (per curiam). It is the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling that counts.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From our own review of the state trial court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, we conclude the trial court acted well within its discretion in granting the State\xe2\x80\x99s motion to excuse Juror Z.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s answers, on their face, could have led the trial court to believe that Juror Z would be substantially impaired in his ability to impose the death penalty in the absence of the possibility that Brown would be released and would reoffend. And the trial court, furthermore, is entitled to deference because it had an opportunity to observe the demeanor of Juror Z. We do not know anything about his demeanor, in part because a transcript cannot fully reflect that information but also because the defense did not object to Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s removal. Nevertheless, the State\xe2\x80\x99s challenge, Brown\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of an objection, and the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s excusal of Juror Z support the conclusion that the interested parties present in the courtroom all felt that removing Juror Z was appropriate under the Witherspoon-Witt rule. See Darden, 477 U. S., at 178 (emphasizing the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object and the judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to engage in further questioning as evidence of impairment).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s assurances that he would consider imposing the death penalty and would follow the law do not overcome the reasonable inference from his other statements that in fact he would be substantially impaired in this case because there was no possibility of release. His assurances did not require the trial court to deny the State\xe2\x80\x99s motion to excuse Juror Z. The defense itself had told the trial court that any juror would make similar guarantees and that they were worth little; instead, defense counsel explained, the court should listen to arguments concerning the substance of the juror\xe2\x80\x99s answers. The trial court in part relied, as diligent judges often must, upon both parties\xe2\x80\x99 counsel to explain why a challenged juror\xe2\x80\x99s problematic beliefs about the death penalty would not rise to the level of substantial impairment. Brown\xe2\x80\x99s counsel offered no defense of Juror Z. In light of the deference owed to the trial court the position Brown now maintains does not convince us the decision to excuse Juror Z was unreasonable.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true that in order to preserve a Witherspoon claim for federal habeas review there is no independent federal requirement that a defendant in state court object to the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s challenge; state procedural rules govern. We nevertheless take into account voluntary acquiescence to, or confirmation of, a juror\xe2\x80\x99s removal. By failing to object, the defense did not just deny the conscientious trial judge an opportunity to explain his judgment or correct any error. It also deprived reviewing courts of further factual findings that would have helped to explain the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. The harm caused by a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object to a juror\xe2\x80\x99s excusal was described well by a Washington appellate court in a different case:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a challenge for cause is made, opposing counsel can object either on the grounds that it is facially insufficient or that the facts needed to support it are not true. [Defendant] did neither. Had [defendant] objected immediately to the State\xe2\x80\x99s challenge for cause, the court could have tried the issue and determined the law and facts. Because [defendant] did not timely object to the excusal of Juror 30, the court had no opportunity to remedy whatever factual questions were in the mind of [defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Taylor, No. 16057\xe2\x80\x932\xe2\x80\x93III etc., 1998 WL 75648, *5 (Wash. App., Feb. 24, 1998) (unpublished opinion; citations omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The defense may have chosen not to object because Juror Z seemed substantially impaired. See 451 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 959 (Tallman, J., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc). Or defense counsel may have felt that Juror Z, a basketball referee whose stepbrother was a police officer, would have been favorable to the State. See App. 68, 74; 451 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 953, n.\xc2\xa09 (reasoning that \xe2\x80\x9cdefense counsel declined to object because he was glad to get rid of juror Z. After all, Z had described himself as pro-death penalty \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Defense counsel must have thanked his lucky stars when the prosecutor bumped Z\xe2\x80\x9d). Or the failure to object may have been an attempt to introduce an error into the trial because the defense realized Brown\xe2\x80\x99s crimes were horrific and the mitigating evidence was weak. Although we do not hold that, because the defense may have wanted Juror Z on the jury, any error was harmless, neither must we treat the defense\xe2\x80\x99s acquiescence in Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s removal as inconsequential.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The defense\xe2\x80\x99s volunteered comment that there was no objection is especially significant because of frequent defense objections to the excusal of other jurors and the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s request that if both parties wanted a juror removed, saying so would expedite the process. In that context the statement was not only a failure to object but also an invitation to remove Juror Z.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We reject the conclusion of the Court of Appeals that the excusal of Juror Z entitles Brown to federal habeas relief. The need to defer to the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s ability to perceive jurors\xe2\x80\x99 demeanor does not foreclose the possibility that a reviewing court may reverse the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s decision where the record discloses no basis for a finding of substantial impairment. But where, as here, there is lengthy questioning of a prospective juror and the trial court has supervised a diligent and thoughtful voir dire, the trial court has broad discretion. The record does not show the trial court exceeded this discretion in excusing Juror Z; indeed the transcript shows considerable confusion on the part of the juror, amounting to substantial impairment. The Supreme Court of Washington recognized the deference owed to the trial court and, contrary to the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 misreading of the state court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, identified the correct standard required by federal law and found it satisfied. That decision, like the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s, was not contrary to, or an unreasonable application of, clearly established federal law.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Brown raises two additional arguments that rely upon Washington state law. He first contends we should not consider his failure to object because Washington state law does not require a defendant to object to a challenge to a potential juror. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 35 (\xe2\x80\x9cAs to the . . . failure to object . . . we have admitted that what [defense counsel] said was I have no objection. . . . But [they] all knew that this issue could be raised for the first time on appeal\xe2\x80\x9d). In addition he asserts that even if Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s statements indicated that he would base his decision upon the risk of Brown reoffending, that requirement was consistent with the state sentencing scheme.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons explained above the defense\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object in this case has significance to our analysis even on the assumption that state law did not require an objection to preserve an error for review in the circumstances of this case. The Supreme Court of Washington, however, noted Brown\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object, suggesting it had significance for its own analysis. Brown, 132 Wash. 2d, at 604, 940 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 585. This is consistent with Washington law, which permits a party to \xe2\x80\x9cexcept\xe2\x80\x9d to the opposing party\xe2\x80\x99s challenge of a juror for cause, Wash. Rev. Code 4.44.230 (2006), and gives appellate courts discretion to bar \xe2\x80\x9cany claim of error which was not raised in the trial court\xe2\x80\x9d unless that error is a \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest error affecting a constitutional right,\xe2\x80\x9d Wash. Rule App. Proc. 2.5(a) (2006). See also 13 R. Ferguson, Washington Practice: Criminal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa74908, p.\xc2\xa0432 (3d ed. 2004) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn general, issues not raised in the trial court will not be considered for the first time on appeal. It is the purpose of this general rule to give the trial court an opportunity to correct the alleged error. Accordingly, it is the duty of counsel to call the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s attention to the alleged error \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d (footnotes omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Supreme Court of Washington also held that Juror Z misstated Washington\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing law. Brown, supra, at 604, 940 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 585. It is not for us to second-guess that determination, and our conclusion is, in any event, the same as that court\xe2\x80\x99s. Juror Z did not say that the likelihood of Brown\xe2\x80\x99s harming someone while in prison would be among his sentencing considerations. Rather, the sole reason Juror Z expressed for imposing the death penalty, in a case where the accused opposed it, was whether the defendant could be released and would reviolate. That is equivalent to treating the risk of recidivism as the sole aggravating factor, rather than treating lack of future dangerousness as a possible mitigating consideration. See Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa710.95.020 (2006) (setting forth aggravating factors); \xc2\xa710.95.070 (setting forth future dangerousness as one of eight mitigating factors).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, we are not persuaded to depart from the Supreme Court of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s determination of the state law at issue or to ignore Brown\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Capital defendants have the right to be sentenced by an impartial jury. The State may not infringe this right by eliminating from the venire those whose scruples against the death penalty would not substantially impair the performance of their duties. Courts reviewing claims of Witherspoon-Witt error, however, especially federal courts considering habeas petitions, owe deference to the trial court, which is in a superior position to determine the demeanor and qualifications of a potential juror. The Court of Appeals neglected to accord this deference. And on this record it was error to find that Juror Z was not substantially impaired. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Souter, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Breyer join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Millions of Americans oppose the death penalty. A cross section of virtually every community in the country includes citizens who firmly believe the death penalty is unjust but who nevertheless are qualified to serve as jurors in capital cases. An individual\xe2\x80\x99s opinion that a life sentence without the possibility of parole is the severest sentence that should be imposed in all but the most heinous cases does not even arguably \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98prevent or substantially impair the performance of his duties as a juror in accordance with his instructions and his oath.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Wainwright v. Witt, 469 U. S. 412, 420 (1985) (emphasis deleted). Moreover, an individual who maintains such a position, or even one who opposes the death penalty as a general matter, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98may not be challenged for cause based on his views about capital punishment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Today the Court ignores these well-established principles, choosing instead to defer blindly to a state court\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous characterization of a juror\xe2\x80\x99s voir dire testimony.[Footnote 1] Although this case comes to us under the standard of review imposed by the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), 110 Stat. 1214, the level of deference given by the Court to the state courts in this case is completely unwarranted based on the record before us. Because I find no justification in the record or elsewhere for the decision to strike Juror Z for cause, I must dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When the State challenged Juror Z, it argued that he was \xe2\x80\x9cconfused about the conditions under which [the death penalty] could be imposed and seemed to believe it only appropriate when there was a risk of release and recidivism.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. A more accurate characterization of Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s testimony is that although he harbored some general reservations about the death penalty, he stated that he could consider and would vote to impose the death penalty where appropriate.[Footnote 2] When asked for \xe2\x80\x9can idea \xe2\x80\xa6 of the underlying reason why you think the death penalty is appropriate [or] what purpose it serves,\xe2\x80\x9d Juror Z responded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe type of situation\xe2\x80\x9d in which the death penalty would be appropriate was \xe2\x80\x9cif a person was incorrigible and would reviolate if released.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 62 (emphasis added). After it was explained to Juror Z that the only two sentencing alternatives available under Washington law would be life imprisonment without the possibility of parole and a death sentence, Juror Z repeatedly confirmed that even if he knew the defendant would never be released, he would still be able to consider and vote for the death penalty. Id., at 62, 72, 73. As for any general reservations Juror Z may have had about the imposition of the death penalty, it is clear from his testimony that he was in no way categorically opposed to it. When asked whether he was \xe2\x80\x9ca little more comfortable that it is being used some of the time,\xe2\x80\x9d Juror Z responded in the affirmative. Id., at 63.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While such testimony might justify a prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s peremptory challenge, until today not one of the many cases decided in the wake of Witherspoon v. Illinois, 391 U. S. 510 (1968), has suggested that such a view would support a challenge for cause. The distinction that our cases require trial judges to draw is not between jurors who are in favor of the death penalty and those who oppose it, but rather between two sub-classes within the latter class\xe2\x80\x94those who will conscientiously apply the law and those whose conscientious scruples necessarily prevent them from doing so.[Footnote 3] As then-Justice Rehnquist explained in his opinion for the Court in Lockhart v. McCree, 476 U. S. 162, 176 (1986):

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIt is important to remember that not all who oppose the death penalty are subject to removal for cause in capital cases; those who firmly believe that the death penalty is unjust may nevertheless serve as jurors in capital cases so long as they state clearly that they are willing to temporarily set aside their own beliefs in deference to the rule of law.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nToday\xe2\x80\x99s opinion simply ignores the justification for this strict rule. As we explained 20 years ago:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe State\xe2\x80\x99s power to exclude for cause jurors from capital juries does not extend beyond its interest in removing those jurors who would \xe2\x80\x98frustrate the State\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate interest in administering constitutional capital sentencing schemes by not following their oaths.\xe2\x80\x99 Wainwright v. Witt, 469 U. S., at 423. To permit the exclusion for cause of other prospective jurors based on their views of the death penalty unnecessarily narrows the cross section of venire members. It \xe2\x80\x98stack[s] the deck against the petitioner. To execute [such a] death sentence would deprive him of his life without due process of law.\xe2\x80\x99 Witherspoon v. Illinois, 391 U. S., at 523.\xe2\x80\x9d Gray v. Mississippi, 481 U. S. 648, 658\xe2\x80\x93659 (1987).

\n

\nIn its opinion, the Court blindly accepts the state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusory statement that Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s views would have \xe2\x80\x9csubstantially impaired\xe2\x80\x9d his ability to follow the court\xe2\x80\x99s instructions without examining what that term means in practice and under our precedents. Ante, at 13. Even AEDPA does not permit us to abdicate our judicial role in this fashion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The high threshold that must be crossed to establish the kind of impairment that would justify the exclusion of a juror under the rule of Wainwright v. Witt is illustrated by Justice Powell\xe2\x80\x99s opinion for the Court in Darden v. Wainwright, 477 U. S. 168 (1986). In that case, we assumed that a prospective juror\xe2\x80\x99s affirmative answer to the following question would not suffice to support his exclusion for cause: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Do you have any moral or religious, conscientious moral or religious principles in opposition to the death penalty so strong that you would be unable without violating your own principles to vote to recommend a death penalty regardless of the facts?\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 178. We recognized that the juror\xe2\x80\x99s answer by itself did not compel the conclusion that he could not under any circumstances recommend the death penalty. See ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cThe precise wording of the question asked of [the juror], and the answer he gave, do not by themselves compel the conclusion that he could not under any circumstance recommend the death penalty\xe2\x80\x9d). We nevertheless upheld his exclusion because the trial judge had previously explained that he wanted to know if \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98you have such strong religious, moral or conscientious principles in opposition to the death penalty that you would be unwilling to vote to return an advisory sentence recommending the death sentence even though the facts presented to you should be such as under the law would require that recommendation?\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 176 (emphasis added). Our holding in Darden rested squarely on the distinction between mere opposition to the death penalty\xe2\x80\x94even when based on religious or moral principles\xe2\x80\x94and an inability to perform the legally required duties of a juror.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In contrast, in Gray, 481 U. S. 648, we reversed a death sentence where a juror had been impermissibly struck for cause. In that case, the trial court struck a juror who appeared confused and who at times seemed to equivocate, but who eventually acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9cshe could consider the death penalty in an appropriate case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 653; cf. voir dire testimony of Juror Z, App. 73 (\xe2\x80\x9cI could [impose the death penalty] if I was convinced that it was the appropriate measure\xe2\x80\x9d). The Court distinguishes Gray from the case now before us solely on the basis that in Gray there was no state-court finding of substantial impairment. Ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. In the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, this distinction is grounded in the fact that, here, there was \xe2\x80\x9can explicit ruling that Juror Z was impaired.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. That \xe2\x80\x9cruling\xe2\x80\x9d consists of a one-sentence conclusion included in the final summary section of the Washington Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. That conclusion is based on an earlier part of the court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, in which it found that during voir dire, Juror Z \xe2\x80\x9cindicated that he would impose the death penalty where the defendant \xe2\x80\x98would reviolate if released,\xe2\x80\x99 which is not a correct statement of the law.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Brown, 132 Wash. 2d 529, 604, 940 P.\xc2\xa02d 546, 585 (1997) (en banc). Under our precedents, a juror\xe2\x80\x99s statement that he would vote to impose a death sentence where there is a possibility that the defendant may reoffend, provided merely as an example of when that penalty might be appropriate, does not constitute a basis for striking a juror for cause.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the alternative, and perhaps recognizing the tenuous nature of the state court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cruling,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court relies on the fact that the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment is entitled to deference because it had the unique opportunity to observe Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor during voir dire. A ruling cannot be taken at face value when it is clear that the reasoning behind that ruling is erroneous in light of our prior precedents.[Footnote 5] There is absolutely nothing in the record to suggest\xe2\x80\x94even in light of the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s tendency to provide \xe2\x80\x9ccareful and measured explanations\xe2\x80\x9d for its decisions, ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x94that anything about Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor would dull the impact of his numerous affirmative statements about his ability to impose the death penalty in any situation. In effect, the Court reads something into nothing and defers to a finding that the trial court never made, instead of relying on the finding on which the Washington Supreme Court clearly based its own ruling and which finds no support in our decisions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In its analysis, the Court places great emphasis on defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object to Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s exclusion for cause, characterizing it as \xe2\x80\x9cvoluntary acquiescence to, or confirmation of\xe2\x80\x9d his removal. Ante, at 15. A closer look at the voir dire transcript, which the Court has included as an appendix to its opinion, reveals that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s statement is not necessarily accurate. Upon being asked by the judge if either party had any challenge to Juror Z, the State provided that it did and the defense responded to the judge that it had \xe2\x80\x9cno objection.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 75. Although the Court reads defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s statement to mean that defense counsel had no objection to Juror Z\xe2\x80\x99s exclusion, it is more clearly read to mean that the defense had no objection to Juror Z serving on the jury and therefore no reason to challenge him.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if we were to interpret defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s statement as the failure to provide an affirmative \xe2\x80\x9cdefense of Juror Z,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15, it is important to recognize that Washington law does not require an objection to preserve an error for review.[Footnote 7] Ante, at 17; see also State v. Levy, 156 Wash. 2d 709, 719, 132 P.\xc2\xa03d 1076, 1081 (2006) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe have long held that even if the defendant fails to object at trial, error may be raised on appeal if it \xe2\x80\x98invades a fundamental right of the accused\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting State v. Becker, 132 Wash. 2d 54, 64, 935 P.\xc2\xa02d 1321, 1326 (1997)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, whether defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s statement is taken as a failure to provide a defense of Juror Z or as acquiescence in his recusal, it is irrelevant to the ultimate disposition of this case. We said in Witt that the failure to object \xe2\x80\x9cin a situation later claimed to be so rife with ambiguity as to constitute constitutional error\xe2\x80\x9d is a factor that should be considered when assessing a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s claims, 469 U. S., at 431, n.\xc2\xa011, but in this case there was absolutely no basis for striking Juror Z. Thus, counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to provide an affirmative response to the State\xe2\x80\x99s motion, though perhaps not strategically sound, does not doom respondent\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional claim. Unlike Witt, in which there was arguably some ambiguity in the juror\xe2\x80\x99s voir dire responses, here Juror Z had unambiguously asserted his full capability to follow the law. See, e.g., App. 58 (\xe2\x80\x9cI do believe in the death penalty in severe situations\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 62 (responding to whether he could consider both available sentencing options, \xe2\x80\x9cYes, I could\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 63 (\xe2\x80\x9cI just felt that there were times when [the death penalty] would be appropriate\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 72 (responding to whether he could consider and impose the death penalty where the defendant would otherwise never be released from prison, \xe2\x80\x9cYes, sir\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 73 (responding to whether he could consider and vote for the death penalty where the alternative is a life sentence without the possibility of parole, \xe2\x80\x9cI could [impose it] if I was convinced that was the appropriate measure\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Witt, 469 U. S., at 438 (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cGiven \xe2\x80\xa6 [the juror\xe2\x80\x99s] somewhat timorous responses, it is entirely possible that her appearance and demeanor persuaded trial counsel that he would prefer a vigorous or more reluctant juror\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even a juror who is generally opposed to the death penalty cannot permissibly be excused for cause so long as he can still follow the law as properly instructed. The Court recognizes this principle, see ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, and yet the perverse result of its opinion is that a juror who is clearly willing to impose the death penalty, but considers the severity of that decision carefully enough to recognize that there are certain circumstances under which it is not appropriate (e.g., that it would only be appropriate in \xe2\x80\x9csevere situations,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 63), is \xe2\x80\x9csubstantially impaired.\xe2\x80\x9d It is difficult to imagine, under such a standard, a juror who would not be considered so impaired, unless he delivered only perfectly unequivocal answers during the unfamiliar and often confusing legal process of voir dire and was willing to state without hesitation that he would be able to vote for a death sentence under any imaginable circumstance. Cf. Adams v. Texas, 448 U. S. 38, 50\xe2\x80\x9351 (1980) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe repeat that the State may bar from jury service those whose beliefs about capital punishment would lead them to violate the law or violate their oaths. But [the Constitution does not allow the exclusion of] jurors whose only fault was to take their responsibilities with special seriousness or to acknowledge honestly that they might or might not be affected\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today, the Court has fundamentally redefined\xe2\x80\x94or maybe just misunderstood\xe2\x80\x94the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9csubstantially impaired,\xe2\x80\x9d and, in doing so, has gotten it horribly backwards. It appears to be under the impression that trial courts should be encouraging the inclusion of jurors who will impose the death penalty rather than only ensuring the exclusion of those who say that, in all circumstances, they cannot. The Court emphasizes that \xe2\x80\x9cthe State has a strong interest in having jurors who are able to apply capital punishment within the framework state law prescribes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. But that does not and cannot mean that jurors must be willing to impose a death sentence in every situation in which a defendant is eligible for that sanction. That is exactly the outcome we aimed to protect against in developing the standard that, contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s apparent temporary lapse, still governs today. See Gray, 481 U. S., at 658 (explaining that to permit the exclusion of jurors other than those who will not follow their oaths \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessarily narrows the cross section of venire members\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98stack[s] the deck against the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Witherspoon, 391 U. S., at 523)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Kozinski\xe2\x80\x99s opinion for the Court of Appeals in this case is solidly grounded on the entire line of our cases recognizing the basic distinction dramatically illustrated by Justice Powell\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in Darden and by Justice Rehnquist\xe2\x80\x99s statement in Lockhart. He surely was entitled to assume that the law had not changed so dramatically in the years following his service as a law clerk to Chief Justice Burger that a majority of the present Court would not even mention that basic distinction, and would uphold the disqualification of a juror whose only failing was to harbor some slight reservation in imposing the most severe of sanctions.

\n

App. 75. The words \xe2\x80\x9cno objection\xe2\x80\x9d meant in context at most what they say, namely that defense counsel did not object to the judge\xe2\x80\x99s excusing Juror Z for cause. Often States treat such a failure to object as waiving a point. But that is not so here. That is because the Washington Supreme Court has told us that, under state law, counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object is without significant legal effect. Ante, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319 (opinion of the Court); ante, at 8 (Stevens,\xc2\xa0J., dissenting); State v. Levy, 156 Wash. 2d 709, 719\xe2\x80\x93720, 132 P.\xc2\xa03d 1076, 1080\xe2\x80\x931081 (2006) (en banc). And that means we must treat this case as if a proper objection had been made.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority continues to rely upon the statement, however, not as proving an objection, but as helping to demonstrate courtroom \xe2\x80\x9catmospherics,\xe2\x80\x9d such as facial expressions or vocal hesitations or tones of voice sufficient to warrant excusing Juror Z for cause. Ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9318, 19. But in my view the majority reads too much into too little. What the words \xe2\x80\x9cno objection\xe2\x80\x9d suggest is simply that defense counsel did not have any objection. And to find more in those few words treats them like a Rorschach blot, permitting a reviewing judge to affirm (or to reverse) the trial judge on no more than the subjective view of the written record that the appellate judge may take. Or, it simply offers a backdoor way to avoid the effect of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s procedural rule. The latter would wrongly ignore Washington law. The former would too often make it impossible to obtain meaningful review of silent records. There is no need, after all, to stretch the significance of ordinary statements and thereby to assume special atmospherics that support (or undercut) a trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Where special courtroom atmospherics matter, a lawyer (or the judge) can always make appropriate remarks for the record.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Basing my conclusions, then, on the written record itself, and in particular upon what Juror Z said in response to questions, I believe, for the reasons Justice Stevens sets forth (and applying AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s strict standard), that the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision to excuse Juror Z was constitutionally erroneous and a new trial is necessary.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, I dissent.
