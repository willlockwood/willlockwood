SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

\n

Hall, as personal representative of the Estate of Hall and as successor trustee of the Ethlyn Louise Hall Family Trust v. Hall et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x931150.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 16, 2018\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 27, 2018

\n

Respondent Samuel Hall served as caretaker and legal advisor to his mother Ethlyn Hall, a property owner in the United States Virgin Islands. After falling out with Samuel, Ethlyn transferred her property into a trust and designated her daughter, petitioner Elsa Hall, as her successor trustee. Ethlyn sued Samuel and his law firm over the handling of her affairs (the \xe2\x80\x9ctrust case\xe2\x80\x9d). When Ethlyn died, Elsa took Ethlyn\xe2\x80\x99s place as trustee and as plaintiff. Samuel later filed a separate complaint against Elsa in her individual capacity (the \xe2\x80\x9cindividual case\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

On Samuel\xe2\x80\x99s motion, the District Court consolidated the trust and individual cases under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 42(a). The District Court held a single trial of the consolidated cases. In the individual case, the jury returned a verdict for Samuel, but the District Court granted Elsa a new trial. In the trust case, the jury returned a verdict against Elsa, and she filed a notice of appeal from the judgment in that case. Samuel moved to dismiss the appeal on jurisdictional grounds, arguing that the judgment in the trust case was not final and appealable because his claims against Elsa remained unresolved in the individual case. The Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit agreed and dismissed the appeal.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0When one of several cases consolidated under Rule 42(a) is finally decided, that decision confers upon the losing party the immediate right to appeal, regardless of whether any of the other consolidated cases remain pending. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Title\n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71291 vests the courts of appeals with jurisdiction over \xe2\x80\x9cappeals from all final decisions of the district courts,\xe2\x80\x9d except those directly appealable to this Court. Under \xc2\xa71291, \xe2\x80\x9cany litigant armed with a final judgment from a lower federal court is entitled to take an appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona v. Manypenny,\n\n451 U.\xc2\xa0S. 232\n. Here an appeal would normally lie from the judgment in the trust case. But Samuel argues that because the trust and individual cases were consolidated under Rule 42(a)(2), they merged and should be regarded as one case, such that the judgment in the trust case was merely interlocutory and not appealable before the consolidated cases in the aggregate are finally resolved. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Rule 42(a)(2) provides that if \xe2\x80\x9cactions before the court involve a common question of law or fact, the court may .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. consolidate the actions.\xe2\x80\x9d The meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate\xe2\x80\x9d in this context is ambiguous. But the term has a legal lineage stretching back at least to the first federal consolidation statute, enacted by Congress in 1813. Act of July 22, 1813, \xc2\xa73,\n\n3Stat.\n21 (later codified as Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7921 and\n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7734 (1934 ed.)). That history makes clear that one of multiple cases consolidated under the Rule retains its independent character, at least to the extent it is appealable when finally resolved, regardless of any ongoing proceedings in the other cases. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Under the consolidation statute\xe2\x80\x94which was in force for 125 years, until its replacement by Rule 42(a)\xe2\x80\x94consolidation was understood not as completely merging the constituent cases into one, but as enabling more efficient case management while preserving the distinct identities of the cases and rights of the separate parties in them. See, e.g., Rich v. Lambert, 12 How. 347; Mutual Life Ins. Co. v. Hillmon,\n\n145 U.\xc2\xa0S. 285\n; Stone v. United States,\n\n167 U.\xc2\xa0S. 178\n. Just five years before Rule 42(a) became law, the Court reiterated that, under the consolidation statute, consolidation did not result in the merger of constituent cases. Johnson v. Manhattan R. Co.,\n\n289 U.\xc2\xa0S. 479\n\xe2\x80\x93497. This body of law supports the inference that, prior to Rule 42(a), a judgment completely resolving one of several consolidated cases was an immediately appealable final decision. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0Rule 42(a) was expressly modeled on the consolidation statute. Because the Rule contained no definition of \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate,\xe2\x80\x9d the term presumably carried forward the same meaning ascribed to it under the statute and reaffirmed in Johnson.

\n

Samuel nonetheless asserts that \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate\xe2\x80\x9d took on a different meaning under Rule 42(a). He describes the Rule as permitting two forms of consolidation: consolidation for limited purposes and consolidation for all purposes. He locates textual authority for the former in a new provision, subsection (a)(1), which permits courts to \xe2\x80\x9cjoin for hearing or trial any or all matters at issue in the actions.\xe2\x80\x9d And he contends that subsection (a)(2), so as not to be superfluous, must permit the merger of cases that have been consolidated for all purposes into a single, undifferentiated case. But the narrow grant of authority in subsection (a)(1) cannot fairly be read as the exclusive source of a district court\xe2\x80\x99s power to consolidate cases for limited purposes, because there is much more to litigation than hearings or trials. Instead, that undisputed power must stem from subsection (a)(2). That defeats Samuel\xe2\x80\x99s argument that interpreting subsection (a)(2) to adopt the traditional understanding of consolidation would render it duplicative of subsection (a)(1), and that subsection (a)(2) therefore must permit courts to merge the actions into a single unit.

\n

Moreover, a Federal Rules Advisory Committee would not take a term that had long meant that separate actions do not merge into one, and silently and abruptly reimagine the same term to mean that they do. Nothing in the pertinent Committee proceedings supports the notion that Rule 42(a) was meant to overturn the settled understanding of consolidation; the Committee simply commented that Rule 42(a) \xe2\x80\x9cis based upon\xe2\x80\x9d its statutory predecessor, \xe2\x80\x9cbut insofar as the statute differs from this rule, it is modified.\xe2\x80\x9d Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on 1937 Adoption of Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 42(a), 28 U. S. C. App., p. 887. The limited extent to which this Court has addressed consolidation since adoption of Rule 42(a) confirms that the traditional understanding remains in place. See, e.g., Bank Markazi v. Peterson, 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___; Butler v. Dexter,\n\n425 U. S. 262\n\xe2\x80\x93267.

\n

This decision does not mean that district courts may not consolidate cases for all purposes in appropriate circumstances. But constituent cases retain their separate identities at least to the extent that a final decision in one is immediately appealable by the losing party. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

679 Fed. Appx. 142, reversed and remanded.

\n

Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., delivered the opinion for a unanimous Court.

ELSA HALL, as personal representative of the estate of ETHLYN LOUISE HALL and as successor trustee of the ETHLYN LOUISE HALL FAMILY TRUST, PETITIONER v. SAMUEL HALL, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

[March 27, 2018]

\n

\n

Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Three Terms ago, we held that one of multiple cases consolidated for multidistrict litigation under\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71407 is immediately appealable upon an order disposing of that case, regardless of whether any of the others remain pending. Gelboim v. Bank of America Corp., 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015). We left open, however, the question whether the same is true with respect to cases consoli- dated under Rule 42(a) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. Id., at ___, n.\xc2\xa04 (slip op., at 7, n.\xc2\xa04). This case presents that question.

\n

I

\n

Petitioner Elsa Hall and respondent Samuel Hall are siblings enmeshed in a long-running family feud. Their mother, Ethlyn Hall, lived and owned property in the United States Virgin Islands. Samuel, a lawyer in the Virgin Islands, served as Ethlyn\xe2\x80\x99s caretaker and provided her with legal assistance. But trouble eventually came to paradise, and Samuel and Ethlyn fell out over Samuel\xe2\x80\x99s management of Ethlyn\xe2\x80\x99s real estate holdings. During a visit from Elsa, Ethlyn established an inter vivos trust, transferred all of her property into the trust, and designated Elsa as her successor trustee. Ethlyn then moved to Miami\xe2\x80\x94under circumstances disputed by the parties\xe2\x80\x94to live with her daughter.

\n

The family squabble made its way to court in May 2011. Ethlyn, acting in her individual capacity and as trustee of her inter vivos trust, sued Samuel and his law firm in Federal District Court (the \xe2\x80\x9ctrust case\xe2\x80\x9d). Ethlyn\xe2\x80\x99s claims\xe2\x80\x94for breach of fiduciary duty, legal malpractice, conversion, fraud, and unjust enrichment\xe2\x80\x94concerned the handling of her affairs by Samuel and his law firm before she left for Florida.

\n

Then Ethlyn died, and Elsa stepped into her shoes as trustee and accordingly as plaintiff in the trust case. Samuel promptly filed counterclaims in that case against Elsa\xe2\x80\x94in both her individual and representative capacities\xe2\x80\x94for intentional infliction of emotional distress, fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, conversion, and tortious inter- ference. Samuel contended that Elsa had turned their mother against him by taking advantage of Ethlyn\xe2\x80\x99s alleged mental frailty. But Samuel ran into an obstacle: Elsa was not a party to the trust case in her individual capacity (only Ethlyn had been). So Samuel filed a new complaint against Elsa in her individual capacity in the same District Court (the \xe2\x80\x9cindividual case\xe2\x80\x9d), raising the same claims that he had asserted as counterclaims in the trust case.

\n

The trust and individual cases initially proceeded along separate tracks. Eventually, on Samuel\xe2\x80\x99s motion, the District Court consolidated the cases under Rule 42(a) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, ordering that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll submissions in the consolidated case shall be filed in\xe2\x80\x9d the docket assigned to the trust case. App. to Pet. for Cert. A\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

Just before the trial commenced, the District Court dismissed from the trust case Samuel\xe2\x80\x99s counterclaims against Elsa. Those claims remained in the individual case. The parties then tried the consolidated cases to- gether before a jury.

\n

In the individual case, the jury returned a verdict for Samuel on his intentional infliction of emotional distress claim against Elsa, awarding him $500,000 in compensatory damages and $1.5 million in punitive damages. The clerk entered judgment in that case, but the District Court granted Elsa a new trial, which had the effect of reopening the judgment. The individual case remains pending before the District Court.

\n

In the trust case, the jury returned a verdict against Elsa, in her representative capacity, on her claims against Samuel and his law firm. The clerk entered judgment in that case directing that Elsa \xe2\x80\x9crecover nothing\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe action be dismissed on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at A\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

Elsa filed a notice of appeal from the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in the trust case. Samuel and his law firm moved to dismiss the appeal on jurisdictional grounds, arguing that the judgment was not final and appealable because his claims against Elsa remained unresolved in the individual case. The Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit agreed. When two cases have been consolidated for all purposes, the court reasoned, a final decision on one set of claims is generally not appealable while the second set remains pending. The court explained that it considers \xe2\x80\x9cwhether a less-than-complete judgment is appealable\xe2\x80\x9d on a \xe2\x80\x9ccase-by-case basis.\xe2\x80\x9d 679 Fed. Appx. 142, 145 (2017). Here, the fact that the claims in the trust and individual cases had been \xe2\x80\x9cscheduled together and tried before a single jury\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel[ed] in favor of keeping the claims together on appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The court dismissed Elsa\xe2\x80\x99s appeal for lack of jurisdiction.

\n

We granted certiorari, 582 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

Had the District Court never consolidated the trust and individual cases, there would be no question that Elsa could immediately appeal from the judgment in the trust case. Title\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71291 vests the courts of appeals with jurisdiction over \xe2\x80\x9cappeals from all final decisions of the district courts,\xe2\x80\x9d except those directly appealable to this Court. A final decision \xe2\x80\x9cends the litigation on the merits and leaves nothing for the court to do but execute the judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ray Haluch Gravel Co. v. Central Pension Fund of Operating Engineers and Participating Employers,\n\n571 U.\xc2\xa0S. 177,\n183 (2014)\n. The archetypal final decision is \xe2\x80\x9cone[\xc2\xa0] that trigger[s] the entry of judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Mohawk Industries, Inc. v. Carpenter,\n\n558 U.\xc2\xa0S. 100,\n103 (2009)\n. Appeal from such a final decision is a \xe2\x80\x9cmatter of right.\xe2\x80\x9d Gelboim, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 1). Under \xc2\xa71291, \xe2\x80\x9cany litigant armed with a final judgment from a lower federal court is entitled to take an appeal,\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona v. Manypenny,\n\n451 U.\xc2\xa0S. 232,\n244 (1981)\n, which generally must be filed within 30 days,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107(a).

\n

Here the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict against Elsa resolved all of the claims in the trust case, and the clerk accordingly entered judgment in that case providing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe action be dismissed on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. A\xe2\x80\x9312. With the entry of judgment, the District Court \xe2\x80\x9ccompleted its adjudication of [Elsa\xe2\x80\x99s] complaint and terminated [her] action.\xe2\x80\x9d Gelboim, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 7). An appeal would normally lie from that judgment.

\n

But, Samuel contends, there is more to the litigation than the suit Elsa pursued against him in her representative capacity. There is also his suit against her in her individual capacity, which has not yet been decided. Because the District Court consolidated the trust and individual cases under Rule 42(a)(2), he argues, they merged and should be regarded as one case. Viewed that way, the judgment in the trust case was merely interlocutory, and more remains to be done in the individual case before the consolidated cases in the aggregate are finally resolved and subject to appeal.

\n

B

\n

Rule 42(a)\xe2\x80\x94entitled \xe2\x80\x9c[c]onsolidation\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94provides that if \xe2\x80\x9cactions before the court involve a common question of law or fact, the court may\xe2\x80\x9d take one of three measures. First, the court may \xe2\x80\x9cjoin for hearing or trial any or all matters at issue in the actions.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 42(a)(1). Second, the court may \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate the actions.\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 42(a)(2). Third, the court may \xe2\x80\x9cissue any other orders to avoid unnecessary cost or delay.\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 42(a)(3). Whether the judgment entered in the trust case is an immediately appealable final decision turns on the effect of consolidation under Rule 42(a).

\n

Samuel, looking to dictionary definitions, asserts that the \xe2\x80\x9cplain meaning of the phrase \xe2\x80\x98consolidate the actions\xe2\x80\x99 is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to unite two or more actions into one whole\xe2\x80\x94that is, to join them into a single case.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 23 (citing Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary (10th ed. 2014); some internal quotation marks and alterations omitted). But the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate\xe2\x80\x9d in the present context is am- biguous. When Rule 42(a) was adopted, the term was generally defined, as it is now, as meaning to \xe2\x80\x9cunite, as various particulars, into one mass or body; to bring together in close union; to combine.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 570 (2d ed. 1942). Consolidation can thus sometimes signify the complete merger of discrete units: \xe2\x80\x9cThe company consolidated two branches.\xe2\x80\x9d But the term can also mean joining together discrete units without causing them to lose their independent character. The United States, for example, is composed of States \xe2\x80\x9cunite[d], as various particulars, into one mass or body,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cbr[ought] together in close union,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccombine[d].\xe2\x80\x9d Yet all agree that entry into our Union \xe2\x80\x9cby no means implies the loss of distinct and individual existence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Texas v. White, 7 Wall. 700, 725 (1869). \xe2\x80\x9cShe consolidated her books\xe2\x80\x9d hardly suggests that the \xe2\x80\x9cbooks\xe2\x80\x9d became \xe2\x80\x9cbook.\xe2\x80\x9d The very metaphor Samuel offers\xe2\x80\x94that consolidation \xe2\x80\x9cmake[s] two one, like marriage\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94highlights this point. Tr. of Oral Arg. 56. However dear to each other, spouses would be surprised to hear that their union extends beyond the metaphysical. This is not a plain meaning case.

\n

It is instead about a term\xe2\x80\x94consolidate\xe2\x80\x94with a legal lineage stretching back at least to the first federal consolidation statute, enacted by Congress in 1813. Act of July 22, 1813, \xc2\xa73,\n\n3Stat.\n21 (later codified as Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7921 and\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7734 (1934 ed.)). Over 125 years, this Court, along with the courts of appeals and leading treatises, interpreted that term to mean the joining together\xe2\x80\x94but not the complete merger\xe2\x80\x94of constituent cases. Those authorities particularly emphasized that constituent cases remained independent when it came to judgments and appeals. Rule 42(a), promulgated in 1938, was expressly based on the 1813 statute. The history against which Rule 42(a) was adopted resolves any ambiguity regarding the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate\xe2\x80\x9d in subsection (a)(2). It makes clear that one of multiple cases consolidated under the Rule retains its independent character, at least to the extent it is appealable when finally resolved, regardless of any ongoing proceedings in the other cases.

\n

C

\n

Lord Mansfield pioneered the consolidation of related cases in England, and the practice quickly took root in American courts. See Mutual Life Ins. Co. v. Hillmon,\n\n145 U.\xc2\xa0S. 285,\n292 (1892)\n. In 1813, Congress authorized the newly formed federal courts, when confronted with \xe2\x80\x9ccauses of like nature, or relative to the same question,\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cmake such orders and rules concerning proceedings therein as may be conformable to the principles and usages belonging to courts for avoiding unnecessary costs or delay in the administration of justice\xe2\x80\x9d and to \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d the causes when it \xe2\x80\x9cshall appear reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73,\n\n3Stat.\n21. This consolidation statute applied at law, equity, and admi- ralty, see 1 W. Rose, A Code of Federal Procedure \xc2\xa7823(a) (1907) (Rose), and remained in force for 125 years, until its replacement by Rule 42(a).

\n

From the outset, we understood consolidation not as completely merging the constituent cases into one, but instead as enabling more efficient case management while preserving the distinct identities of the cases and the rights of the separate parties in them. In Rich v. Lambert, 12 How. 347 (1852), for example, we considered an appeal from several consolidated cases in admiralty. The appellees, the owners of cargo damaged during shipment, raised a challenge to our jurisdiction that turned on the nature of the consolidation. At the time, we could exercise appellate jurisdiction only over cases involving at least $2,000 in controversy. The damages awarded to the cargo owners in the consolidated cases surpassed $2,000 in the aggregate, but most of the constituent cases did not individually clear that jurisdictional hurdle. Id., at 352\xe2\x80\x93353.

\n

We declined to view the consolidated cases as one for purposes of appeal, concluding that we had jurisdiction only over those constituent cases that individually involved damages exceeding $2,000. Ibid. As we explained, \xe2\x80\x9calthough [a consolidated] proceeding assumes the form of a joint suit, it is in reality a mere joinder of distinct causes of action by distinct parties, arising out of a common injury, and which are heard and determined, so far as the merits are concerned, the same as in the case of separate libels for each cause of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 353. Consolidation was \xe2\x80\x9callowed by the practice of the court for its convenience, and the saving of time and expense to the parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The trial court\xe2\x80\x99s decree, we noted, had the effect of individually resolving each constituent case. Ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cThe same decree .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is entered as in the case of separate suits.\xe2\x80\x9d); see Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 532 (3d ed. 1933) (\xe2\x80\x9cdecree\xe2\x80\x9d is a \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment of a court of equity or admiralty, answering for most purposes to the judgment of a court of common law\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, we did \xe2\x80\x9cnot perceive .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any ground for a distinction as to the right of appeal from a decree as entered in these cases from that which exists where the proceedings have been distinct and separate throughout.\xe2\x80\x9d Rich, 12 How., at 353; see Hanover Fire Ins. Co. v. Kinneard,\n\n129 U.\xc2\xa0S. 176,\n177 (1889)\n (evaluating appellate jurisdiction over a writ of error in one of several consolidated cases without reference to the others).

\n

We elaborated on the principles underlying consolidation in Mutual Life Insurance Co. v. Hillmon,\n\n145 U.\xc2\xa0S. 285\n. Hillmon, a staple of law school courses on evidence, involved three separate actions instituted against different life insurance companies by one Sallie Hillmon, the beneficiary on policies purchased by her husband John. Sallie claimed she was entitled to the sizable proceeds of the policies because John had died while journeying through southern Kansas with two companions in search of a site for a cattle ranch. The three companies countered that John was in fact still alive, having conspired with one of the companions to murder the other and pass his corpse off as John\xe2\x80\x99s, all as part of an insurance fraud scheme. The trial court consolidated the cases and tried them together. Id., at 285\xe2\x80\x93287.

\n

The court, for purposes of determining the number of peremptory juror challenges to which each defendant was entitled, treated the three cases as though they had merged into one. Ibid. On appeal we disagreed, holding that each defendant should receive the full complement of peremptory challenges. Id., at 293. That was because, \xe2\x80\x9calthough the defendants might lawfully be compelled, at the discretion of the court, to try the cases together, the causes of action remained distinct, and required separate verdicts and judgments; and no defendant could be deprived, without its consent, of any right material to its defence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to which it would have been entitled if the cases had been tried separately.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. On remand, one case settled, and a consolidated trial of the others \xe2\x80\x9cresult[ed] in separate judgments\xe2\x80\x9d for Sallie. Connecticut Mut. Life Ins. Co. v. Hillmon,\n\n188 U.\xc2\xa0S. 208,\n209 (1903)\n.

\n

In Stone v. United States,\n\n167 U.\xc2\xa0S. 178,\n189 (1897)\n, we held that a party appealing from the judgment in one of two cases consolidated for trial could not also raise claims with respect to the other case. John Stone was the sole defendant in one case and one of three defendants in the other. Id., at 179\xe2\x80\x93181. After a consolidated trial, the jury returned a verdict in the case against Stone alone; its verdict in the multidefendant case was set aside. Id., at 181. Stone appealed from the judgment in his case, arguing that the failure to grant a peremptory challenge in the multidefendant case affected the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict in his. Id., at 189. We rejected that claim, punctiliously respecting the distinction between the constituent cases. There was \xe2\x80\x9cno merit in the objection,\xe2\x80\x9d we said, because in the case before us Stone had \xe2\x80\x9chad the benefit of the three peremptory challenges\xe2\x80\x9d to which he was entitled in that case. Ibid.; see Stone v. United States, 64 F. 667, 672 (CA9 1894) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe two cases, although consolidated, were separate and distinct. Defendant had exercised all the rights and privileges he was entitled to in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

And just five years before Rule 42(a) became law, we reiterated that, under the consolidation statute, consolidation did not result in the merger of constituent cases. Johnson v. Manhattan R. Co.,\n\n289 U.\xc2\xa0S. 479\n\xe2\x80\x93497 (1933). A major case of its day, Johnson arose from the \xe2\x80\x9cfinancial embarrassment\xe2\x80\x9d during the Great Depression of two companies involved in operating the New York subway system. Johnson v. Manhattan R. Co., 61 F.\xc2\xa02d 934, 936 (CA2 1932). In the resulting litigation, the District Court consolidated two suits, apparently with the intent to \xe2\x80\x9ceffect an intervention of the parties to the [first suit] in the [second] suit\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94in other words, to make the two suits one. Id., at 940. Judge Learned Hand, writing for the Second Circuit on appeal, would have none of it: \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidation does not merge the suits; it is a mere matter of convenience in administration, to keep them in step. They remain as independent as before.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We affirmed, relying on Hillmon and several lower court cases reflecting the same understanding of consolidation. Johnson, 289 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 497, n.\xc2\xa08. We explained once more that \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidation is permitted as a matter of convenience and econ- omy in administration, but does not merge the suits into a single cause, or change the rights of the parties, or make those who are parties in one suit parties in another.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 496\xe2\x80\x93497.

\n

Decisions by the Courts of Appeals, with isolated departures,[1]* reflected the same understanding in cases involving all manners of consolidation. See, e.g., Baltimore S.\xc2\xa0S. Co., Inc. v. Koppel Indus. Car & Equip. Co., 299 F. 158, 160 (CA4 1924) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe consolidation for convenience of trial did not merge the two causes of action\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cdeprive either party of any right or relieve it of any burden incident to the libel or cross-libel as a separate proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d); Taylor v. Logan Trust Co., 289 F. 51, 53 (CA8 1923) (parties to one constituent case could not appeal orders in the other because \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidation did not make the parties to one suit parties to the other\xe2\x80\x9d; cited in Johnson); Toledo, St. L. & K. C. R. Co. v. Continental Trust Co., 95 F. 497, 506 (CA6 1899) (consolidation \xe2\x80\x9coperates as a mere carrying on together of two separate suits supposed to involve identical issues\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not avoid the necessity of separate decrees in each case\xe2\x80\x9d; cited in Johnson).

\n

One frequently cited case illustrates the point. In Adler v. Seaman, 266 F. 828, 831 (CA8 1920), the District Court \xe2\x80\x9csought to employ consolidation as a medium of getting the two independent suits united,\xe2\x80\x9d but the Court of Appeals made clear that the consolidation statute did not authorize such action. The court explained that constituent cases sometimes \xe2\x80\x9cassume certain natural attitudes toward each other, such as \xe2\x80\x98in the nature of\xe2\x80\x99 a cross-bill or intervention.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 838. Be that as it may, the court continued, \xe2\x80\x9cthis is purely a rule of convenience, and does not result in actually making such parties defendants or interveners in the other suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The court described \xe2\x80\x9cthe result of consolidation\xe2\x80\x9d as instead \xe2\x80\x9cmerely to try cases together, necessitating separate verdicts and judgments or separate decrees,\xe2\x80\x9d and to \xe2\x80\x9cleave\xe2\x80\x9d the constituent cases \xe2\x80\x9cseparate, independent action[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 838, 840.

\n

Treatises summarizing federal precedent applying the consolidation statute also concluded that consolidated cases \xe2\x80\x9cremain distinct.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Rose \xc2\xa7823(c), at 758. They recognized that consolidated cases should \xe2\x80\x9cremain separate as to parties, pleadings, and judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d W. Simkins, Federal Practice 63 (rev. ed. 1923), and that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here must be separate verdicts, judgments or decrees, even although the consolidating party wished for one verdict,\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Rose \xc2\xa7823(c), at 758; see also G. Virden, Consolidation Under Rule 42 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, in 141 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D. 169, 173\xe2\x80\x93174 (1992) (Virden) (\xe2\x80\x9cas of 1933 and the Johnson case of that year, it was well settled that consolidation in the federal courts did not merge the separate cases into a single action\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Several aspects of this body of law support the inference that, prior to Rule 42(a), a judgment completely resolving one of several consolidated cases was an immediately appealable final decision. We made clear, for example, that each constituent case must be analyzed individually on appeal to ascertain jurisdiction and to decide its disposition\xe2\x80\x94a compartmentalized analysis that would be gratuitous if the cases had merged into a single case subject to a single appeal. We emphasized that constituent cases should end in separate decrees or judgments\xe2\x80\x94the traditional trigger for the right to appeal, for which there would be no need if an appeal could arise only from the resolution of the consolidated cases as a whole. We explained that the parties to one case did not become parties to the other by virtue of consolidation\xe2\x80\x94indicating that the right of each to pursue his individual case on appeal should not be compromised by the litigation conduct of the other. And, finally, we held that consolidation could not prejudice rights to which the parties would have been due had consolidation never occurred. Forcing an aggrieved party to wait for other cases to conclude would substantially impair his ability to appeal from a final decision fully resolving his own case\xe2\x80\x94a \xe2\x80\x9cmatter of right,\xe2\x80\x9d Gelboim, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 1), to which he was \xe2\x80\x9centitled,\xe2\x80\x9d Manypenny, 451 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 244.

\n

D

\n

Against this background, two years after Johnson, the Rules Advisory Committee began discussion of what was to become Rule 42(a). The Rule, which became effective in 1938, was expressly modeled on its statutory predecessor, the Act of July 22, 1813. See Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on 1937 Adoption of Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 42(a), 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 887. The Rule contained no definition of \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate,\xe2\x80\x9d so the term presumably carried forward the same meaning we had ascribed to it under the consolidation statute for 125 years, and had just recently reaffirmed in Johnson. See Frankfurter, Some Reflections on the Reading of Statutes, 47 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 527, 537 (1947) (\xe2\x80\x9cif a word is obviously transplanted from another legal source, whether the common law or other legislation, it brings the old soil with it\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Class v. United States, 583 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2018) (slip op., at 10) (Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 11(a)(2) did not silently alter existing doctrine established by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s past decisions).

\n

Samuel nonetheless asserts that there is a significant distinction between the original consolidation statute and Rule 42(a). The statute authorized district courts to \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate\xe2\x80\x9d related \xe2\x80\x9ccauses when it appears reasonable to do so\xe2\x80\x9d or to \xe2\x80\x9cmake such orders and rules .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as may be conformable to the usages of courts for avoiding unnecessary costs or delay in the administration of justice.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7734 (1934 ed.). Rule 42(a) permits district courts not only to \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate the actions\xe2\x80\x9d (subsection (a)(2)) and \xe2\x80\x9cissue any other orders to avoid unnecessary cost or delay\xe2\x80\x9d (subsection (a)(3)), but also to \xe2\x80\x9cjoin for hearing or trial any or all matters at issue in the actions\xe2\x80\x9d (subsection (a)(1)).

\n

Whatever \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate\xe2\x80\x9d meant under the statute, Sam- uel posits, it took on a different meaning under Rule 42(a) with the addition of subsection (a)(1). Samuel describes the Rule as \xe2\x80\x9cpermit[ting] two forms of consolidation\xe2\x80\x9d: consolidation that \xe2\x80\x9cextend[s] only to certain proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d such as discovery, and consolidation \xe2\x80\x9cfor all purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 4\xe2\x80\x935. He locates textual authority for the former in subsection (a)(1), which he says empowers courts to \xe2\x80\x9cjoin[\xc2\xa0] multiple actions for procedural purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 23. In light of this broad grant of authority, he contends, subsection (a)(2) must provide for something more if it is not to be superfluous. And Samuel sees that something more as the ability to merge cases that have been consolidated for \xe2\x80\x9call purposes\xe2\x80\x9d into a single, undifferentiated case\xe2\x80\x94one appealable only when all issues in each formerly distinct case have been decided. See id., at 22\xe2\x80\x9324 (to \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate\xe2\x80\x9d separate actions is \xe2\x80\x9cto join them into a single case\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmeld [them] into a single unit\xe2\x80\x9d (alterations omitted)).

\n

We disagree. It is only by substantially overreading subsection (a)(1) that Samuel can argue that its addition compels a radical reinterpretation of the familiar term \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate\xe2\x80\x9d in subsection (a)(2). The text of subsection (a)(1) permits the joining of cases only for \xe2\x80\x9chearing or trial.\xe2\x80\x9d That narrow grant of authority cannot fairly be read as the exclusive source of a district court\xe2\x80\x99s power to \xe2\x80\x9cjoin[\xc2\xa0] multiple actions for procedural purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 23. There is, after all, much more to litigation than hearings or trials\xe2\x80\x94such as motions practice or discovery. A district court\xe2\x80\x99s undisputed ability to consolidate cases for such limited purposes must therefore stem from subsection (a)(2). That defeats Samuel\xe2\x80\x99s argument that interpreting subsection (a)(2) to adopt the traditional understanding of consolidation would render it \xe2\x80\x9cwholly duplicative of [subsection] (a)(1),\xe2\x80\x9d and that subsection (a)(2) \xe2\x80\x9ctherefore must permit courts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to \xe2\x80\x98consolidate\xe2\x80\x99 the actions themselves into a single unit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 23\xe2\x80\x9324. Samuel\xe2\x80\x99s reinterpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate\xe2\x80\x9d is, in other words, a solution in search of a problem.

\n

We think, moreover, that if Rule 42(a) were meant to transform consolidation into something sharply contrary to what it had been, we would have heard about it. Congress, we have held, \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not alter the fundamental details\xe2\x80\x9d of an existing scheme with \xe2\x80\x9cvague terms\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csubtle device[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc.,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 457,\n468 (2001)\n; cf. Class, 583 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10). That is true in spades when it comes to the work of the Federal Rules Advisory Committees. Their laborious drafting process requires years of effort and many layers of careful review before a proposed Rule is presented to this Court for possible submission to Congress. See Report of Advisory Committee on Rules for Civil Procedure (Apr. 1937) (describing the exhaustive process undertaken to draft the first Federal Rules of Civil Procedure). No sensible draftsman, let alone a Federal Rules Advisory Committee, would take a term that had meant, for more than a century, that separate actions do not merge into one, and silently and abruptly reimagine the same term to mean that they do.

\n

Similarly, nothing in the pertinent proceedings of the Rules Advisory Committee supports the notion that Rule 42(a) was meant to overturn the settled understanding of consolidation. See United States v. Vonn,\n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 55\n, n.\xc2\xa06 (2002) (Advisory Committee Notes are \xe2\x80\x9ca reliable source of insight into the meaning of a rule\xe2\x80\x9d). In this instance, the Committee simply commented that Rule 42(a) \xe2\x80\x9cis based upon\xe2\x80\x9d its statutory predecessor, \xe2\x80\x9cbut insofar as the statute differs from this rule, it is modified.\xe2\x80\x9d Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on 1937 Adoption of Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 42(a), 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., at 887. The Committee did not identify any specific instance in which Rule 42(a) changed the statute, let alone the dramatic transformation Samuel would have us recognize. See Virden 174\xe2\x80\x93181 (evaluating the history of the development of Rule 42(a) and finding no evidence that the Committee intended a shift in meaning along the lines proposed by Samuel). This is significant because when the Committee intended a new rule to change existing federal practice, it typically explained the departure. See, e.g., Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on 1937 Adoption of Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 4, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 747 (a predecessor statute \xe2\x80\x9cis substantially continued insofar as it applies to a summons, but its requirements as to teste of process are superseded\xe2\x80\x9d); Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on 1937 Adoption of Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 18, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 802 (\xe2\x80\x9cIn respect to fraudulent conveyances the rule changes the former rule requiring a prior judgment against the owner .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to conform to the provisions of the Uniform Fraudulent Conveyance Act, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 9 and 10.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

As a leading treatise explained at the time, through consolidation under Rule 42(a) \xe2\x80\x9cone or many or all of the phases of the several actions may be merged. But merger is never so complete in consolidation as to deprive any party of any substantial rights which he may have possessed had the actions proceeded separately.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 J. Moore & J. Friedman, Moore\xe2\x80\x99s Federal Practice \xc2\xa742.01, pp. 3050\xe2\x80\x933051 (1938). Thus, \xe2\x80\x9cseparate verdicts and judgments are normally necessary.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3051, n.\xc2\xa012.

\n

The limited extent to which this Court has addressed consolidation since adoption of Rule 42(a) confirms the traditional understanding. Just recently in Bank Markazi v. Peterson, 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2016) (slip op., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320), for example, the Court determined that cases \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidated for administrative purposes at the execution stage .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. were not independent of the original actions for damages and each claim retained its separate character.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court quoted as authority a treatise explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cactions do not lose their separate identity because of consolidation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 20) (quoting 9A C. Wright & A. Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa72382, p.\xc2\xa010 (3d ed. 2008) (Wright & Miller)).

\n

In Butler v. Dexter,\n\n425 U.\xc2\xa0S. 262\n\xe2\x80\x93267 (1976) (per curiam), we dismissed an appeal because the constitutional question that supplied our jurisdiction had been raised not in the case before us, but instead only in other cases with which it had been consolidated. We explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach case .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. must be considered separately to determine whether or not this Court has jurisdiction to consider its merits\xe2\x80\x9d. Id., at 267, n.\xc2\xa012; see Rich, 12 How., at 352\xe2\x80\x93353. And in Alfred Dunhill of London, Inc. v. Republic of Cuba,\n\n425 U.S. 682\n, and n. 22 (1976) (Marshall, J., dissenting), four dissenting Justices\xe2\x80\x94reaching an issue not addressed by the majority\xe2\x80\x94cited Johnson for the proposition that actions are \xe2\x80\x9cnot merged\xe2\x80\x9d and do \xe2\x80\x9cnot lose their separate identities because of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. consolidation\xe2\x80\x9d under Rule 42(a).

\n

In the face of all the foregoing, we cannot accept Sam- uel\xe2\x80\x99s contention that \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidate\xe2\x80\x9d in Rule 42(a) carried a very different meaning\xe2\x80\x94with very different consequences\xe2\x80\x94than it had in Johnson, just five years before the Rule was adopted.

\n

None of this means that district courts may not consolidate cases for \xe2\x80\x9call purposes\xe2\x80\x9d in appropriate circumstances. District courts enjoy substantial discretion in deciding whether and to what extent to consolidate cases. See 9A Wright & Miller \xc2\xa72383 (collecting cases). What our decision does mean is that constituent cases retain their separate identities at least to the extent that a final decision in one is immediately appealable by the losing party. That is, after all, the point at which, by definition, a \xe2\x80\x9cdistrict court disassociates itself from a case.\xe2\x80\x9d Swint v. Chambers County Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35,\n42 (1995)\n. We thus express no view on any issue arising prior to that time.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

The normal rule is that a \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decision\xe2\x80\x9d confers upon the losing party the immediate right to appeal. That rule provides clear guidance to litigants. Creating exceptions to such a critical step in litigation should not be under- taken lightly. Congress has granted us the authority to prescribe rules \xe2\x80\x9cdefin[ing] when a ruling of a district court is final for the purposes of appeal under\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71291,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072(c), and we have explained that changes with respect to the meaning of final decision \xe2\x80\x9care to come from rulemaking, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not judicial decisions in particular controversies,\xe2\x80\x9d Microsoft Corp. v. Baker, 582 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2017) (slip op., at 15). If, as Samuel fears, our holding in this case were to give rise to practical problems for district courts and litigants, the appropriate Federal Rules Advisory Committees would certainly remain free to take the matter up and recommend revisions accordingly.

\n

Rule 42(a) did not purport to alter the settled understanding of the consequences of consolidation. That understanding makes clear that when one of several consolidated cases is finally decided, a disappointed litigant is free to seek review of that decision in the court of appeals.

\n

We reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
