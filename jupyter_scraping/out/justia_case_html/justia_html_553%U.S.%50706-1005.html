UNITED STATES v. SANTOS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x931005.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 3, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 2, 2008

\n

In an illegal lottery run by respondent Santos, runners took commissions from the bets they gathered, and some of the rest of the money was paid as salary to respondent Diaz and other collectors and to the winning gamblers. Based on these payments to runners, collectors, and winners, Santos was convicted of, inter alia, violating the federal money-laundering statute, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956, which prohibits the use of the \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d of criminal activities for various purposes, including engaging in, and conspiring to engage in, transactions intended to promote the carrying on of unlawful activity, \xc2\xa71956(a)(1)(A)(i) and \xc2\xa71956(h). Based on his receipt of salary, Diaz pleaded guilty to conspiracy to launder money. The Seventh Circuit affirmed the convictions. On collateral review, the District Court ruled that, under intervening Circuit precedent interpreting the word \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in the federal money-laundering statute, \xc2\xa71956(a)(1)(A)(i) applies only to transactions involving criminal profits, not criminal receipts. Finding no evidence that the transactions on which respondents\xe2\x80\x99 money-laundering convictions were based involved lottery profits, the court vacated those convictions. The Seventh Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is affirmed.

\n

461 F.\xc2\xa03d 886, affirmed.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, joined by Justice Souter, Justice Thomas, and Justice Ginsburg, concluded in Parts I\xe2\x80\x93III and V that the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71956(a)(1) means \xe2\x80\x9cprofits,\xe2\x80\x9d not \xe2\x80\x9creceipts.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9314, 16\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The rule of lenity dictates adoption of the \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d reading. The statute nowhere defines \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds.\xe2\x80\x9d An undefined term is generally given its ordinary meaning. Asgrow Seed Co. v. Winterboer, 513 U. S. 179, 187. However, dictionaries and the Federal Criminal Code sometimes define \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d and sometimes \xe2\x80\x9cprofits.\xe2\x80\x9d Moreover, the many provisions in the federal money-laundering statute that use the word \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d make sense under either definition. The rule of lenity therefore requires the statute to be interpreted in favor of defendants, and the \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d definition of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d is always more defendant-friendly than the \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d definition. Pp. 3\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation fails to give the money-laundering statute its intended scope begs the question; the Government\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation hinders effective enforcement of the law is exaggerated. Neither suffices to overcome the rule of lenity. Pp. 6\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0None of the transactions on which respondents\xe2\x80\x99 money-laundering convictions were based can fairly be characterized as involving the lottery\xe2\x80\x99s profits. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, joined by Justice Souter and Justice Ginsburg, concluded in Part IV that Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 position that \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d should be interpreted to mean profits for some predicate crimes, \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d for others, is contrary to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents holding that judges cannot give the same statutory text different meanings in different cases, see Clark v. Martinez, 543 U. S. 371. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens concluded that revenue a gambling business uses to pay essential operating expenses is not \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956. When, as here, Congress fails to define potentially ambiguous statutory terms, it effectively delegates the task to federal judges. See Commissioner v. Fink, 483 U. S. 89, 104. Because Congress could have required that \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d have one meaning when referring to some of the specified unlawful activities listed in \xc2\xa71956(c)(7) and a different meaning when referring to others, judges filling statutory gaps may also do so, as long as they are conscientiously endeavoring to carry out Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent. Section 1956\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history makes clear that \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d includes gross revenues from the sale of contraband and the operation of organized crime syndicates involving such sales, but sheds no light on how to identify the proceeds of an unlicensed stand-alone gambling venture. Furthermore, the consequences of applying a \xe2\x80\x9cgross receipts\xe2\x80\x9d definition of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d to respondents are so perverse that Congress could not have contemplated them: Allowing the Government to treat the mere payment of an illegal gambling business\xe2\x80\x99 operating expenses as a separate offense is in practical effect tantamount to double jeopardy, which is particularly unfair in this case because the penalties for money laundering are substantially more severe than those for the underlying offense of operating a gambling business. Accordingly, the rule of lenity may weigh in the determination, and in that respect the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is persuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x936.

\nOPINION OF SCALIA, J.
\nUNITED STATES V. SANTOS

UNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. EFRAIN\nSANTOS and BENEDICTO DIAZ

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[June 2, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia announced the judgment of the Court and delivered an opinion, in which Justice Souter and Justice Ginsburg join, and in which Justice Thomas joins as to all but Part IV.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We consider whether the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in the federal money-laundering statute, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(a)(1), means \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cprofits.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s until 1994, respondent Santos operated a lottery in Indiana that was illegal under state law. See Ind. Code \xc2\xa735\xe2\x80\x9345\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x933 (West 2004). Santos employed a number of helpers to run the lottery. At bars and restaurants, Santos\xe2\x80\x99s runners gathered bets from gamblers, kept a portion of the bets (between 15% and 25%) as their commissions, and delivered the rest to Santos\xe2\x80\x99s collectors. Collectors, one of whom was respondent Diaz, then delivered the money to Santos, who used some of it to pay the salaries of collectors (including Diaz) and to pay the winners.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These payments to runners, collectors, and winners formed the basis of a 10-count indictment filed in the United States District Court for the Northern District of Indiana, naming Santos, Diaz, and 11 others. A jury found Santos guilty of one count of conspiracy to run an illegal gambling business (\xc2\xa7371), one count of running an illegal gambling business (\xc2\xa71955), one count of conspiracy to launder money (\xc2\xa71956(a)(1)(A)(i) and \xc2\xa71956(h)), and two counts of money laundering (\xc2\xa71956(a)(1)(A)(i)). The court sentenced Santos to 60 months of imprisonment on the two gambling counts and to 210 months of imprisonment on the three money-laundering counts. Diaz pleaded guilty to conspiracy to launder money, and the District Court sentenced him to 108 months of imprisonment. The Court of Appeals affirmed the convictions and sentences. United States v. Febus, 218 F.\xc2\xa03d 784 (CA7 2000). We declined to review the case. 531 U. S. 1021 (2000).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thereafter, respondents filed motions under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255, collaterally attacking their convictions and sentences. The District Court rejected all of their claims but one, a challenge to their money-laundering convictions based on the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent decision in United States v. Scialabba, 282 F.\xc2\xa03d 475 (2002), which held that the federal money-laundering statute\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition of transactions involving criminal \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d applies only to transactions involving criminal profits, not criminal receipts. Id., at 478. Applying that holding to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 cases, the District Court found no evidence that the transactions on which the money-laundering convictions were based (Santos\xe2\x80\x99s payments to runners, winners, and collectors and Diaz\xe2\x80\x99s receipt of payment for his collection services) involved profits, as opposed to receipts, of the illegal lottery, and accordingly vacated the money-laundering convictions. The Court of Appeals affirmed, rejecting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s contention that Scialabba was wrong and should be overruled. 461 F.\xc2\xa03d 886 (CA7 2006). We granted certiorari. 550 U. S. ___ (2007).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The federal money-laundering statute prohibits a number of activities involving criminal \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds.\xe2\x80\x9d Most relevant to this case is 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(a)(1)(A)(i), which criminalizes transactions to promote criminal activity.[Footnote 1] This provision uses the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in describing two elements of the offense: the Government must prove that a charged transaction \xe2\x80\x9cin fact involve[d] the proceeds of specified unlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d (the proceeds element), and it also must prove that a defendant knew \xe2\x80\x9cthat the property involved in\xe2\x80\x9d the charged transaction \xe2\x80\x9crepresent[ed] the proceeds of some form of unlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d (the knowledge element). \xc2\xa71956(a)(1).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The federal money-laundering statute does not define \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds.\xe2\x80\x9d When a term is undefined, we give it its ordinary meaning. Asgrow Seed Co. v. Winterboer, 513 U. S. 179, 187 (1995). \xe2\x80\x9cProceeds\xe2\x80\x9d can mean either \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cprofits.\xe2\x80\x9d Both meanings are accepted, and have long been accepted, in ordinary usage. See, e.g., 12 Oxford English Dictionary 544 (2d ed. 1989); Random House Dictionary of the English Language 1542 (2d ed. 1987); Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 1972 (2d ed. 1957) (hereinafter Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 2d). The Government contends that dictionaries generally prefer the \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d definition over the \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d definition, but any preference is too slight for us to conclude that \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d is the primary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cProceeds,\xe2\x80\x9d moreover, has not acquired a common meaning in the provisions of the Federal Criminal Code. Most leave the term undefined. See, e.g., 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71963; 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7853. Recognizing the word\xe2\x80\x99s inherent ambiguity, Congress has defined \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in various criminal provisions, but sometimes has defined it to mean \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d and sometimes \xe2\x80\x9cprofits.\xe2\x80\x9d Compare 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72339C(e)(3) (2000 ed., Supp. V) (receipts), \xc2\xa7981(a)(2)(A) (2000 ed.) (same), with \xc2\xa7981(a)(2)(B) (profits).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since context gives meaning, we cannot say the money-laundering statute is truly ambiguous until we consider \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d not in isolation but as it is used in the federal money-laundering statute. See United Sav. Assn. of Tex. v. Timbers of Inwood Forest Associates, Ltd., 484 U. S. 365, 371 (1988). The word appears repeatedly throughout the statute, but all of those appearances leave the ambiguity intact. Section 1956(a)(1) itself, for instance, makes sense under either definition: one can engage in a financial transaction with either receipts or profits of a crime; one can intend to promote the carrying on of a crime with either its receipts or its profits; and one can try to conceal the nature, location, etc., of either receipts or profits. The same is true of all the other provisions of this legislation in which the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d is used. They make sense under either definition. See, for example, \xc2\xa71956(a)(2)(B), which speaks of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d represented by a \xe2\x80\x9cmonetary instrument or funds.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito\xe2\x80\x99s dissent (the principal dissent) makes much of the fact that 14 States that use and define the word \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in their money-laundering statutes,[Footnote 2] the Model Money Laundering Act, and an international treaty on the subject, all define the term to include gross receipts. See post, at 3\xe2\x80\x935. We do not think this evidence shows that the drafters of the federal money-laundering statute used \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d as a term of art for \xe2\x80\x9creceipts.\xe2\x80\x9d Most of the state laws cited by the dissent, the Model Act, and the treaty postdate the 1986 federal money-laundering statute by several years, so Congress was not acting against the backdrop of those definitions when it enacted the federal statute. If anything, they show that \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d is ambiguous and that others who believed that money-laundering statutes ought to include gross receipts sought to clarify the ambiguity that Congress created when it left the term undefined.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under either of the word\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary definitions, all provisions of the federal money-laundering statute are coherent; no provisions are redundant; and the statute is not rendered utterly absurd. From the face of the statute, there is no more reason to think that \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d than there is to think that \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cprofits.\xe2\x80\x9d Under a long line of our decisions, the tie must go to the defendant. The rule of lenity requires ambiguous criminal laws to be interpreted in favor of the defendants subjected to them. See United States v. Gradwell, 243 U. S. 476, 485 (1917); McBoyle v. United States, 283 U. S. 25, 27 (1931); United States v. Bass, 404 U. S. 336, 347\xe2\x80\x93349 (1971). This venerable rule not only vindicates the fundamental principle that no citizen should be held accountable for a violation of a statute whose commands are uncertain, or subjected to punishment that is not clearly prescribed. It also places the weight of inertia upon the party that can best induce Congress to speak more clearly and keeps courts from making criminal law in Congress\xe2\x80\x99s stead. Because the \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d definition of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d is always more defendant-friendly than the \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d definition, the rule of lenity dictates that it should be adopted.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Stopping short of calling the \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation absurd, the Government contends that the interpretation should nonetheless be rejected because it fails to give the federal money-laundering statute its proper scope and because it hinders effective enforcement of the law. Neither contention overcomes the rule of lenity.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0According to the Government, if we do not read \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9creceipts,\xe2\x80\x9d we will disserve the purpose of the federal money-laundering statute, which is, the Government says, to penalize criminals who conceal or promote their illegal activities. On the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he gross receipts of a crime accurately reflect the scale of the criminal activity, because the illegal activity generated all of the funds.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 21; see also post, at 5\xe2\x80\x937 (Alito, J., dissenting).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When interpreting a criminal statute, we do not play the part of a mind reader. In our seminal rule-of-lenity\ndecision, Chief Justice Marshall rejected the impulse to\nspeculate regarding a dubious congressional intent. \xe2\x80\x9c[P]robability is not a guide which a court, in construing a penal statute, can safely take.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Wiltberger, 5 Wheat. 76, 105 (1820). And Justice Frankfurter, writing for the Court in another case, said the following: \xe2\x80\x9cWhen Congress leaves to the Judiciary the task of imputing to Congress an undeclared will, the ambiguity should be resolved in favor of lenity.\xe2\x80\x9d Bell v. United States, 349 U. S. 81, 83 (1955).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The statutory purpose advanced by the Government to construe \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d is a textbook example of begging the question. To be sure, if \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d meant \xe2\x80\x9creceipts,\xe2\x80\x9d one could say that the statute was aimed at the dangers of concealment and promotion. But whether \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d is the very issue in the case. If \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cprofits,\xe2\x80\x9d one could say that the statute is aimed at the distinctive danger that arises from leaving in criminal hands the yield of a crime. A rational Congress could surely have decided that the risk of leveraging one criminal activity into the next poses a greater threat\nto society than the mere payment of crime-related expenses and justifies the money-laundering statute\xe2\x80\x99s harsh\npenalties.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If we accepted the Government\xe2\x80\x99s invitation to speculate about congressional purpose, we would also have to confront and explain the strange consequence of the \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation, which respondents have described as a \xe2\x80\x9cmerger problem.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Brief for Respondent Diaz 34. If \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d meant \xe2\x80\x9creceipts,\xe2\x80\x9d nearly every violation of the illegal-lottery statute would also be a violation of the money-laundering statute, because paying a winning bettor is a transaction involving receipts that the defendant intends to promote the carrying on of the lottery. Since few lotteries, if any, will not pay their winners, the statute criminalizing illegal lotteries, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71955, would \xe2\x80\x9cmerge\xe2\x80\x9d with the money-laundering statute. Congress evidently decided that lottery operators ordinarily deserve up to 5 years of imprisonment, \xc2\xa71955(a), but as a result of merger they would face an additional 20 years, \xc2\xa71956(a)(1). Prosecutors, of course, would acquire the discretion to charge the lesser lottery offense, the greater money-laundering offense, or both\xe2\x80\x94which would predictably be used to induce a plea bargain to the lesser charge.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The merger problem is not limited to lottery operators. For a host of predicate crimes, merger would depend on the manner and timing of payment for the expenses associated with the commission of the crime. Few crimes are entirely free of cost, and costs are not always paid in advance. Anyone who pays for the costs of a crime with its proceeds\xe2\x80\x94for example, the felon who uses the stolen money to pay for the rented getaway car\xe2\x80\x94would violate the money-laundering statute. And any wealth-acquiring crime with multiple participants would become money-laundering when the initial recipient of the wealth gives his confederates their shares.[Footnote 4] Generally speaking, any specified unlawful activity, an episode of which includes transactions which are not elements of the offense and in which a participant passes receipts on to someone else, would merge with money laundering. There are more than 250 predicate offenses for the money-laundering statute, see Dept. of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics, M. Motivans, Money Laundering Offenders 1994\xe2\x80\x932001, p.\xc2\xa02 (2003), online at http://www.ojp.usdoj.gov/bjs/pub/pdf/mlo01.pdf (as visited May 29, 2008, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file), and many foreseeably entail such transactions, see 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(c)(7) (establishing as predicate offenses a number of illegal trafficking and selling offenses, the expenses of which might be paid after the illegal transportation or sale).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government suggests no explanation for why Congress would have wanted a transaction that is a normal part of a crime it had duly considered and appropriately punished elsewhere in the Criminal Code to radically increase the sentence for that crime. Interpreting \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d eliminates the merger problem. Transactions that normally occur during the course of running a lottery are not identifiable uses of profits and thus do not violate the money-laundering statute. More generally, a criminal who enters into a transaction paying the expenses of his illegal activity cannot possibly violate the money-laundering statute, because by definition profits consist of what remains after expenses are paid. Defraying an activity\xe2\x80\x99s costs with its receipts simply will not be covered.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The principal dissent suggests that a solution to the merger problem may be found in giving a narrow interpretation to the \xe2\x80\x9cpromotion prong\xe2\x80\x9d of the statute: A defendant might be deemed not to \xe2\x80\x9cpromote\xe2\x80\x9d illegal activity \xe2\x80\x9cby doing those things \xe2\x80\xa6 that are needed merely to keep the business running,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 18, because promotion (presumably) means doing things that will cause a business to grow. See Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 2d, p.\xc2\xa01981 (giving as one of the meanings of \xe2\x80\x9cpromote\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o contribute to the growth [or] enlargement\xe2\x80\x9d of something). (This argument is embraced by Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s dissent as well. See post, at 2.) The federal money-laundering statute, however, bars not the bare act of promotion, but engaging in certain transactions \xe2\x80\x9cwith the intent to promote the carrying on of specified unlawful activity.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(a)(1)(A)(i) (emphasis added). In that context the word naturally bears one of its other meanings, such as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o contribute to the \xe2\x80\xa6 prosperity\xe2\x80\x9d of something, or to \xe2\x80\x9cfurther\xe2\x80\x9d something. See Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 2d, p.\xc2\xa01981. Surely one promotes \xe2\x80\x9cthe carrying on\xe2\x80\x9d of a gambling enterprise by merely assuring that it continues in business.[Footnote 5] In any event, to believe that this \xe2\x80\x9cnarrow\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cpromote\xe2\x80\x9d would solve the merger problem one must share the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s misperception that the statute applies just to the conduct of ongoing enterprises rather than individual unlawful acts. If the predicate act is theft by an individual, it makes no sense to ask whether an expenditure was intended to \xe2\x80\x9cgrow\xe2\x80\x9d the culprit\xe2\x80\x99s theft business. The merger problem thus stands as a major obstacle to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer admits that the merger problem casts doubt on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position, post, at 1, but believes there are \xe2\x80\x9cother, more legally felicitous\xe2\x80\x9d solutions to the problem, post, at 2. He suggests that the merger problem could be solved by holding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe money laundering offense and the underlying offense that generated the money to be laundered must be distinct in order to be separately punishable.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The insuperable difficulty with this solution is that it has no basis whatever in the words of the statute. Even assuming (as one should not) the propriety of a judicial rewrite, why should one believe that Congress wanted courts to avoid the merger problem in that unusual fashion, rather than by adopting one of the two possible meanings of an ambiguous term? Justice Breyer pins hope on the possibility, \xe2\x80\x9cif the \xe2\x80\x98merger\xe2\x80\x99 problem is essentially a problem of fairness in sentencing,\xe2\x80\x9d that the United States Sentencing Commission might revise its recommended sentences for money laundering. Post, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. See also principal dissent, post, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318 (in agreement). Even if that is a possibility, it is not a certainty. And once again, why should one choose this chancy method of solving the problem, rather than interpret ambiguous language to avoid it? In any event, as noted, supra, at 8, the merger problem affects more than just sentencing; it affects charging decisions and plea-bargaining as well.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government also argues for the \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation because\xe2\x80\x94quite frankly\xe2\x80\x94it is easier to prosecute. Proving the proceeds and knowledge elements of the federal money-laundering offense under the \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation will unquestionably require proof that is more difficult to obtain. Essentially, the Government asks us to resolve the statutory ambiguity in light of Congress\xe2\x80\x99s presumptive intent to facilitate money-laundering prosecutions. That position turns the rule of lenity upside-down. We interpret ambiguous criminal statutes in favor of defendants, not prosecutors.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true that the \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation demands more from the Government than the \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation. Not so much more, however, as to render such a disposition inconceivable\xe2\x80\x94as proved by the fact that Congress has imposed similar proof burdens upon the prosecution elsewhere. See 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71963(a) (criminal forfeiture provision requiring determination of \xe2\x80\x9cgross profits or other proceeds\xe2\x80\x9d); 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7853(a) (same).[Footnote 6] It is untrue that the added burdens \xe2\x80\x9cserve no discernible purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 12 (Alito, J., dissenting). They ensure that the severe money-laundering penalties will be imposed only for the removal of profits from criminal activity, which permit the leveraging of one criminal activity into the next. See supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, the Government exaggerates the difficulties. The \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds of specified unlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d are the proceeds from the conduct sufficient to prove one predicate offense. Thus, to establish the proceeds element under the \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation, the prosecution needs to show only that a single instance of specified unlawful activity was profitable and gave rise to the money involved in a charged transaction. And the Government, of course, can select the instances for which the profitability is clearest. Contrary to the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view, post, at 6, 11\xe2\x80\x9312, the factfinder will not need to consider gains, expenses, and losses attributable to other instances of specified unlawful activity, which go to the profitability of some entire criminal enterprise. What counts is whether the receipts from the charged unlawful act exceeded the costs fairly attributable to it.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When the Government charges an \xe2\x80\x9centerprise\xe2\x80\x9d crime as the predicate offense, see, e.g., 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(c)(7)(C), it will have to prove the profitability of only the conduct sufficient to violate the enterprise statute. That is typically defined as a \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing series of violations,\xe2\x80\x9d 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7848(c)(2), which would presumably be satisfied by three violations, see Richardson v. United States, 526 U. S. 813, 818 (1999). Thus, the Government will have to prove the profitability of just three offenses, selecting (again) those for which profitability is clearest. And of course a prosecutor will often be able to charge the underlying crimes instead of the overarching enterprise crime.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As for the knowledge element of the money-laundering offense\xe2\x80\x94knowledge that the transaction involves profits of unlawful activity\xe2\x80\x94that will be provable (as knowledge must almost always be proved) by circumstantial evidence. For example, someone accepting receipts from what he knows to be a long-continuing drug-dealing operation can be found to know that they include some profits. And a jury could infer from a long-running launderer-criminal relationship that the launderer knew he was hiding the criminal\xe2\x80\x99s profits. Moreover, the Government will be entitled to a willful blindness instruction if the professional money launderer, aware of a high probability that the laundered funds were profits, deliberately avoids learning the truth about them\xe2\x80\x94as might be the case when he knows that the underlying crime is one that is rarely unprofitable.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Concurring in the judgment, Justice Stevens expresses the view that the rule of lenity applies to this case because there is no legislative history reflecting any legislator\xe2\x80\x99s belief about how the money-laundering statute should apply to lottery operators. See post, at 3, 5. The rule of lenity might not apply, he thinks, in a case involving an organized crime syndicate or the sale of contraband because the legislative history supposedly contains some views on the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in those circumstances.[Footnote 8] See post, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, and n.\xc2\xa03. In short, Justice Stevens would interpret \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d for some predicate crimes, \xe2\x80\x9creceipts\xe2\x80\x9d for others.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 position is original with him; neither the United States nor any amicus suggested it; it has no precedent in our cases. Justice Stevens relies on the proposition that one undefined word, repeated in different statutory provisions, can have different meanings in each provision. See post, at 2, and n.\xc2\xa02. But that is worlds apart from giving the same word, in the same statutory provision, different meanings in different factual contexts. Not only have we never engaged in such interpretive contortion; just over three years ago, in an opinion joined by Justice Stevens, we forcefully rejected it. Clark v. Martinez, 543 U. S. 371 (2005), held that the meaning of words in a statute cannot change with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s application. See id., at 378. To hold otherwise \xe2\x80\x9cwould render every statute a chameleon,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 382, and \xe2\x80\x9cwould establish within our jurisprudence \xe2\x80\xa6 the dangerous principle that judges can give the same statutory text different meanings in different cases,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 386. Precisely to avoid that result, our cases often \xe2\x80\x9cgive a statute\xe2\x80\x99s ambiguous language a limiting construction called for by one of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s applications, even though other of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s applications, standing alone, would not support the same limitation. The lowest common denominator, as it were, must govern.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 380 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our obligation to maintain the consistent meaning of words in statutory text does not disappear when the rule of lenity is involved. To the contrary, we have resolved an ambiguity in a tax statute in favor of the taxpayer in a civil case because the statute had criminal applications that triggered the rule of lenity. See United States v. Thompson/Center Arms Co., 504 U. S. 505, 517\xe2\x80\x93518, and n.\xc2\xa010 (1992) (plurality opinion). If anything, the rule of lenity is an additional reason to remain consistent, lest those subject to the criminal law be misled. And even if, as Justice Stevens contends, post, at 1, statutory ambiguity \xe2\x80\x9ceffectively\xe2\x80\x9d licenses us to write a brand-new law, we cannot accept that power in a criminal case, where the law must be written by Congress. See United States v. Hudson, 7 Cranch 32, 34 (1812).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We think it appropriate to add a word concerning the stare decisis effect of Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 opinion. Since his vote is necessary to our judgment, and since his opinion rests upon the narrower ground, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding is limited accordingly. See Marks v. United States, 430 U. S. 188, 193 (1977). But the narrowness of his ground consists of finding that \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d when there is no legislative history to the contrary. That is all that our judgment holds. It does not hold that the outcome is different when contrary legislative history does exist. Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 speculations on that point address a case that is not before him, are the purest of dicta, and form no part of today\xe2\x80\x99s holding. Thus, as far as this particular statute is concerned, counsel remain free to argue Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 view (and to explain why it does not overrule Clark v. Martinez, supra). They should be warned, however: Not only do the Justices joining this opinion reject that view, but so also (apparently) do the Justices joining the principal dissent. See post, at 2, 17.

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The money-laundering charges brought against Santos were based on his payments to the lottery winners and his employees, and the money-laundering charge brought against Diaz was based on his receipt of payments as an employee. Neither type of transaction can fairly be characterized as involving the lottery\xe2\x80\x99s profits. Indeed, the Government did not try to prove, and respondents have not admitted, that they laundered criminal profits. We accordingly affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When Congress fails to define potentially ambiguous statutory terms, it effectively delegates to federal judges the task of filling gaps in a statute. See Commissioner v. Fink, 483 U. S. 89, 104 (1987) (Stevens, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn the process of legislating it is inevitable that Congress will leave open spaces in the law that the courts are implicitly authorized to fill\xe2\x80\x9d). Congress has included definitions of the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in some criminal statutes,[Footnote 1] but it has not done so in 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956 (2000 ed. and Supp. V), the money laundering statute at issue in this case. That statute is somewhat unique because it applies to the proceeds of a varied and lengthy list of specified unlawful activities, see \xc2\xa71956(c)(7) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cspecified unlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d to include, inter alia, controlled substance violations, murder, bribery, smuggling, various forms of fraud, concealment of assets, various environmental offenses, and health care offenses).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although it did not do so, it seems clear that Congress could have provided that the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d shall have one meaning when referring to some specified unlawful activities and a different meaning when referring to others. In fact, in the general civil forfeiture statute, \xc2\xa7981, Congress did provide two different definitions of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds,\xe2\x80\x9d recognizing that\xe2\x80\x94for a subset of activities\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d must allow for the deduction of costs. Compare \xc2\xa7981(a)(2)(A) (2000 ed.) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in cases involving illegal goods and services to mean \xe2\x80\x9cproperty of any kind obtained directly or indirectly \xe2\x80\xa6 not limited to the net gain or profit realized from the offense\xe2\x80\x9d) with \xc2\xa7981(a)(2)(B) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d with respect to lawful goods sold in an illegal manner as the amount of money acquired \xe2\x80\x9cless the direct costs incurred in providing the goods or services\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have previously recognized that the same word can have different meanings in the same statute.[Footnote 2] If Congress could have expressly defined the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d differently when applied to different specified unlawful activities, it seems to me that judges filling the gap in a statute with such a variety of applications may also do so, as long as they are conscientiously endeavoring to carry out the intent of Congress. Therefore, contrary to what Justice Alito and the plurality state, see post, at 17 (dissenting opinion); ante, at 15-16 (plurality opinion), this Court need not pick a single definition of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d applicable to every unlawful activity, no matter how incongruous some applications may be.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As Justice Alito rightly argues, the legislative history of \xc2\xa71956 makes it clear that Congress intended the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d to include gross revenues from the sale of contraband and the operation of organized crime syndicates involving such sales.[Footnote 3] But that history sheds no light on how to identify the proceeds of many other types of specified unlawful activities. For example, one specified unlawful activity is the conduct proscribed by \xc2\xa7541, \xe2\x80\x9cEntry of goods falsely classified.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 541 provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hoever knowingly effects any entry of goods, wares, or merchandise, at less than the true weight or measure thereof, or upon a false classification as to quality or value, or by the payment of less than the amount of duty legally due, shall be \xe2\x80\xa6 imprisoned not more than two years.\xe2\x80\x9d Conceivably the \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d stemming from a violation of \xc2\xa7541 could be either the money realized by misstating the value\xe2\x80\x94that is, the amount by which the criminal \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d by paying reduced duties\xe2\x80\x94or the total price at which the goods are later sold, even though the misclassification had only a trivial impact on that price.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Just as the legislative history fails to tell us how to calculate the \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d of violations of \xc2\xa7541, it is equally silent on the proceeds of an unlicensed stand-alone gambling venture. The consequences of applying a \xe2\x80\x9cgross receipts\xe2\x80\x9d definition of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d to the gambling operation conducted by respondents are so perverse that I cannot believe they were contemplated by Congress, particularly given the fact that nothing in Justice Alito\xe2\x80\x99s thorough review of the legislative history indicates otherwise.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Constrained by a holding that the payment of expenses constitutes \xe2\x80\x9cpromotion,\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 5] Justice Alito\xe2\x80\x99s opinion runs squarely into what can be characterized as the \xe2\x80\x9cmerger\xe2\x80\x9d problem. Allowing the Government to treat the mere payment of the expense of operating an illegal gambling business as a separate offense is in practical effect tantamount to double jeopardy, which is particularly unfair in this case because the penalties for money laundering are substantially more severe than those for the underlying offense of operating a gambling business. A money laundering conviction increases the statutory maximum from 5 to 20 years, and the Sentencing Commission has prescribed different Guidelines ranges for the two crimes.[Footnote 6] When a defendant has a significant criminal history or Guidelines enhancements apply, the statutory cap of five years in \xc2\xa71955 is an important limitation on a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence\xe2\x80\x94a limitation that would be eviscerated if Justice Alito\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d were applied in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito and Justice Breyer suggest that the advisory nature of the Guidelines post-Booker, United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220 (2005), or the possibility of an amendment to the money laundering Guideline, would soften this blow, post, at 17-18 (opinion of Alito,\xc2\xa0J.); post, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (opinion of Breyer,\xc2\xa0J.), and indeed they could. But the result in the case at hand might not be softened at all by resort to Booker because respondents\xe2\x80\x99 direct appeal was decided in 2000, several years prior to our decision in Booker. If Justice Alito\xe2\x80\x99s opinion were to carry the day, both respondents would return to prison to serve the remainder of their lengthy sentences.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The revenue generated by a gambling business that is used to pay the essential expenses of operating that business is not \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the money laundering statute. As the plurality notes, there is \xe2\x80\x9cno explanation for why Congress would have wanted a transaction that is a normal part of a crime it had duly considered and appropriately punished elsewhere in the Criminal Code, to radically increase the sentence for that crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. This conclusion dovetails with what common sense and the rule of lenity would require. Faced with both a lack of legislative history speaking to the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d when operating a gambling business is the \xe2\x80\x9cspecified unlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d and my conviction that Congress could not have intended the perverse result that would obtain in this case under Justice Alito\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, the rule of lenity may weigh in the determination. And in that respect the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is surely persuasive.[Footnote 7] Accordingly, I concur in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join Justice Alito\xe2\x80\x99s dissent while adding the following observations about what has been referred to as the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98merger problem.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8 (plurality opinion). Like the plurality, I doubt that Congress intended the money laundering statute automatically to cover financial transactions that constitute an essential part of a different underlying crime. Operating an illegal gambling business, for example, inevitably involves investment in overhead as well as payments to employees and winning customers; a drug offense normally involves payment for drugs; and bank robbery may well require the distribution of stolen cash to confederates. If the money laundering statute applies to this kind of transaction (i.e., if the transaction is automatically a \xe2\x80\x9cfinancial transaction\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cinvolves the proceeds of specified unlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d made \xe2\x80\x9cwith the intent to promote the carrying on of specified unlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d), then the Government can seek a heavier money laundering penalty (say, 20 years), even though the only conduct at issue is conduct that warranted a lighter penalty (say, 5 years for illegal gambling). 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(a)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is difficult to understand why Congress would have intended the Government to possess this punishment-transforming power. Perhaps for this reason, the Tenth Circuit has written that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress aimed the crime of money laundering at conduct that follows in time the underlying crime rather than to afford an alternative means of punishing the prior \xe2\x80\x98specified unlawful activity.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Edgmon, 952 F.\xc2\xa02d 1206, 1214 (1991). And, in 1997, the United States Sentencing Commission told Congress that it agreed with the Department of Justice that \xe2\x80\x9cmoney laundering cannot properly be charged for \xe2\x80\x98merged\xe2\x80\x99 transactions that are part of the underly-\ning crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Report to Congress: Sentencing Policy for\nMoney Laundering Offenses, including Comments on a Dept. of Justice Report, p. 16 (Sept. 1997), online at http://\nwww.ussc.gov/r_congress/launder.pdf (as visited May 20, 2008, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, like the plurality, I see a \xe2\x80\x9cmerger\xe2\x80\x9d problem. But, unlike the plurality, I do not believe that we should look to the word \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d for a solution. For one thing, the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of that word creates the serious logical and practical difficulties that Justice Alito describes. See post, at 7\xe2\x80\x9312 (dissenting opinion) (describing difficulties associated with proof and accounting). For another thing, there are other, more legally felicitous places to look for a solution. The Tenth Circuit, for example, has simply held that the money laundering offense and the underlying offense that generated the money to be laundered must be distinct in order to be separately punishable. Edgmon, supra, at 1214. Alternatively the money laundering statute\xe2\x80\x99s phrase \xe2\x80\x9cwith the intent to promote the carrying on of specified unlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d may not apply where, for example, only one instance of that underlying activity is at issue. (The Seventh Circuit on a prior appeal in this case rejected that argument, and thus we do not consider it here. See United States v. Febus, 218 F.\xc2\xa03d 784, 789 (2000).)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, if the \xe2\x80\x9cmerger\xe2\x80\x9d problem is essentially a problem of fairness in sentencing, the Sentencing Commission has adequate authority to address it. Congress has instructed the Commission to \xe2\x80\x9cavoi[d] unwarranted sentencing disparities\xe2\x80\x9d among those \xe2\x80\x9cfound guilty of similar criminal conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7991(b)(1)(B) (emphasis added); see also \xc2\xa7994(f) (instructing the Commission to pay particular attention to those disparities). The current money laundering Guideline, United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa72S1.1 (Nov. 2007) (USSG), by making no exception for a situation where nothing but a single instance of the underlying crime has taken place, would seem to create a serious and unwarranted disparity among defendants who have engaged in identical conduct. My hope is that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s past efforts to tie more closely the offense level for money laundering to the offense level of the underlying crime, see id., Supp. to App. C, Amdt. 634 (Nov. 2001), suggest a willingness to consider directly this kind of disparity. Such an approach could solve the \xe2\x80\x9cmerger\xe2\x80\x9d problem without resort to creating complex interpretations of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language. And any such solution could be applied retroactively. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(u).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of these alternative possibilities, I dissent.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fairly read, the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds,\xe2\x80\x9d as used in the principal federal money laundering statute, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(a), means \xe2\x80\x9cthe total amount brought in,\xe2\x80\x9d the primary dictionary definition. Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 1807 (1976) (hereinafter Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 3d). See also Random House Dictionary of the English Language 1542 (2d ed. 1987) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe total sum derived from a sale or other transaction\xe2\x80\x9d). The plurality opinion, however, makes no serious effort to interpret this important statutory term. Ignoring the context in which the term is used, the problems that the money laundering statute was enacted to address, and the obvious practical considerations that those responsible for drafting the statute almost certainly had in mind, that opinion is quick to pronounce the term hopelessly ambiguous and thus to invoke the rule of lenity. Concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cprofits,\xe2\x80\x9d the plurality opinion\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation would frustrate Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent and maim a statute that was enacted as an important defense against organized criminal enterprises.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fortunately, Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99s opinion recognizes that the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cinclude[s] gross revenues from the sale of contraband and the operation of organized crime syndicates involving such sales.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (opinion concurring in judgment).[Footnote 1] I cannot agree with Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99s approach insofar as it holds that the meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d varies depending on the nature of the illegal activity that produces the laundered funds, but at least that approach preserves the correct interpretation of the statute in most of the cases that were the focus of congressional concern when the money laundering statute was enacted.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While the primary definition of the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cthe total amount brought in,\xe2\x80\x9d I recognize that the term may also be used to mean \xe2\x80\x9cnet profit,\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 3d 1807, and I do not suggest that the question presented in this case can be answered simply by opening a dictionary. When a word has more than one meaning, the meaning that is intended is often made clear by the context in which the word is used, and thus in this case, upon finding that the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d may mean both \xe2\x80\x9cthe total amount brought in\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnet profit,\xe2\x80\x9d the appropriate next step is not to abandon any effort at interpretation and summon in the rule of lenity. Rather, the next thing to do is to ask what the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d customarily means in the context that is relevant here\xe2\x80\x94a money laundering statute.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The federal money laundering statute is not the only money laundering provision that uses the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds.\xe2\x80\x9d On the contrary, the term is a staple of money laundering laws, and it is instructive that in every single one of these provisions in which the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d is defined\xe2\x80\x94and there are many\xe2\x80\x94the law specifies that \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cthe total amount brought in.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The leading treaty on international money laundering, the United Nations Convention Against Transnational Organized Crime (Convention), Nov. 15, 2000, 2225\nU. N. T. S. 209 (Treaty No. I\xe2\x80\x9339574), which has been adopted by the United States and 146 other countries,[Footnote 2] is instructive. This treaty contains a provision that is very similar to \xc2\xa71956(a)(1)(B)(i). Article 6.1 of the Convention obligates signatory nations to criminalize \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he . . . transfer of property, knowing that such property is the proceeds of crime, for the purpose of concealing or disguising the illicit origin of the property or of helping any person who is involved in the commission of the predicate offence to evade the legal consequences of his or her action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 277 (emphasis added). The Convention defines the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cany property derived from or obtained, directly or indirectly, through the commission of an offence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 275 (Art. 2(e)). The money laundering provision of the Convention thus covers gross receipts.[Footnote 3] \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d is given a similarly broad scope in the Model Money Laundering Act (Model Act). See President\xe2\x80\x99s Commission on Model State Drug Laws, Economic Remedies, \xc2\xa7C (1993). Section 5(a)(1) of the Model Act criminalizes transactions involving property that is \xe2\x80\x9cthe proceeds of some form of unlawful activity,\xe2\x80\x9d and the Model Act defines \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cproperty acquired or derived directly or indirectly from, produced through, realized through, or caused by an act or omission \xe2\x80\xa6 includ[ing] any property of any kind,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa74(a).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fourteen States have money laundering statutes that define the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds,\xe2\x80\x9d and in every one of these laws the term is defined in a way that encompasses gross receipts. See Ariz. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa713\xe2\x80\x932314(N)(3) (West 2001), 13\xe2\x80\x932317(F)(4)(b) (West Supp. 2007); Ark. Code Ann. \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x9342\xe2\x80\x93203(5) (2006); Cal. Health & Safety Code Ann. \xc2\xa711370.9(h)(1) (West 2007); Haw. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7708A\xe2\x80\x932, 708A\xe2\x80\x933 (2007); Ind. Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa735\xe2\x80\x9345\xe2\x80\x9315\xe2\x80\x934, 35\xe2\x80\x9345\xe2\x80\x9315\xe2\x80\x935 (West 2004); Iowa Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7706B.1(1), 706B.2 (2005); La. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa714:230(A)(4) (West 2004); Mich. Comp. Laws Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7750.411j(f), 750.411j (West 2004); N.\xc2\xa0M. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa730\xe2\x80\x9351\xe2\x80\x932(E), 30\xe2\x80\x9351\xe2\x80\x934(A) (2004); Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71315.51(H), 1315.55 (Lexis 2006); Tex. Penal Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa734.01(4), 34.02 (West Supp. 2007); Utah Code Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa776\xe2\x80\x9310\xe2\x80\x931902(9), 76\xe2\x80\x9310\xe2\x80\x931903 (West 2007); Va. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa718.2\xe2\x80\x93246.2, 18.2\xe2\x80\x93246.3 (Lexis 2004); Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa79A.83.010(5), 9A.83.020 (2006). Cf. N.\xc2\xa0J. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa72C:21\xe2\x80\x9325(d) (West 2005).[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This pattern of usage is revealing. It strongly suggests that when lawmakers, knowledgeable about the nature and problem of money laundering, use the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in a money laundering provision, they customarily mean for the term to reach all receipts and not just profits.[Footnote 5]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is a very good reason for this uniform pattern of usage. Money laundering provisions serve two chief ends. First, they provide deterrence by preventing drug traffickers and other criminals who amass large quantities of cash from using these funds \xe2\x80\x9cto support a luxurious lifestyle\xe2\x80\x9d or otherwise to enjoy the fruits of their crimes. Model Act, Policy Statement, p. C\xe2\x80\x93105. See President\xe2\x80\x99s Commission on Organized Crime, Interim Report to President and Attorney General, The Cash Connection: Organized Crime, Financial Institutions, and Money Laundering 7\xe2\x80\x938 (Oct. 1984) (hereinafter Interim Report); Aranson, Bouker, & Hannon, Money Laundering, 31 Am. Crim. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 721, 721\xe2\x80\x93722 (1994); H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 99\xe2\x80\x93746, p. 16 (1986) (hereinafter H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep.). Second, they inhibit the growth of criminal enterprises by preventing the use of dirty money to promote the enterprise\xe2\x80\x99s growth. See, e.g., 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71956(a)(1)(A)(i), (a)(2)(A), and (a)(3)(A); Model Act \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa75(a)(2), (4); N.\xc2\xa0J. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa72C:21\xe2\x80\x9325(b)(1); Tex. Penal Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa734.02(a)(3)\xe2\x80\x93(4).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both of these objectives are frustrated if a money laundering statute is limited to profits. Dirty money may be used to support \xe2\x80\x9ca luxurious lifestyle\xe2\x80\x9d and to grow an illegal enterprise whenever the enterprise possesses large amounts of illegally obtained cash. And illegal enterprises may acquire such cash while engaging in unlawful activity that is unprofitable.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Suppose, for example, that a drug cartel sends a large shipment of drugs to this country, a good part of the shipment is intercepted, the remainder is sold, the cartel ends up with a net loss but with a large quantity of cash on its hands, and the cartel uses the cash in financial transactions that are designed to conceal the source of the cash or to promote further crime. There is no plausible reason why Congress would not have wanted the money laundering statute to apply to these financial transactions. If the cartel leaders use the money to live in luxury, this provides an incentive for these individuals to stay in the business and for others to enter. If the cartel uses the money to finance future drug shipments or to expand the business, public safety is harmed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is certainly true that Congress, in enacting the federal money laundering statute, was primarily concerned about criminal enterprises that realize profits. A criminal operation that consistently loses money will not last very long and thus presents a lesser danger than a profitable operation. But narrowing a money laundering statute so that it reaches only profits produces two perverse results that Congress cannot have wanted. First, it immunizes successful criminal enterprises during those periods when they are operating temporarily in the red. Second, and more important, it introduces pointless and difficult problems of proof. Because the dangers presented by money laundering are present whenever criminals have large stores of illegally derived funds on their hands, there is little reason to require proof\xe2\x80\x94which may be harder to assemble than the plurality opinion acknowledges\xe2\x80\x94that the funds represent profits.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The implausibility of a net income interpretation is highlighted in cases involving professionals and others who are hired to launder money. Those who are knowledgeable about money laundering stress the importance of prosecuting these hired money launderers. See, e.g., Depts. of Treasury and Justice, The 2001 National Money Laundering Strategy, pp. ix\xe2\x80\x93x, 1\xe2\x80\x932 (Sept. 2001), online at http://www.treas.gov/press/releases/docs/ml2001.pdf; Fi-nancial Action Task Force on Money Laundering, 1996\xe2\x80\x931997 Report on Money Laundering Typologies 7 (Feb. 1997), online at http://www.fatf-gafi.org/dataoecd/31/29/\n34043795.pdf; Butterworths International Guide to Money Laundering Law and Practice 629 (T. Graham 2d ed. 2003); Ratliff, Third Party Money Laundering: Problems of Proof and Prosecutorial Discretion, 7 Stan. L.\xc2\xa0& Policy Rev. 173 (1996); Sultzer, Money Laundering: The Scope of the Problem and Attempts to Combat It, 63 Tenn. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 143, 147\xe2\x80\x93148 (1995); H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep., at 16\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A net income interpretation would risk hamstringing such prosecutions. To violate 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(a), a defendant must \xe2\x80\x9ckno[w] the property involved in a financial transaction represents the proceeds of some form of unlawful activity.\xe2\x80\x9d A professional money launderer is not likely to know (or perhaps even to care) whether the enterprise is operating in the black when the funds in question were acquired. Therefore, under a net income interpretation, financial specialists and others who are hired to launder funds would generally be beyond the reach of the statute, something that Congress almost certainly did not intend.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is revealing that the money laundering statute explicitly provides that a money launderer need only know that \xe2\x80\x9cthe property involved in the transaction represented proceeds from some form, though not necessarily which form, of [specified illegal] activity.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71956(c)(1). Thus, the prosecution is not required to prove that a hired money launderer knew that funds provided for laundering derived from, say, drug sales as opposed to gambling. There is no reason to think that hired money launderers are more likely to know whether funds include profits than they are to know the nature of the illegal activity from which the funds were derived. Consequently, \xc2\xa71956(c) suggests that Congress did not intend to require proof that a hired money launderer knew that funds provided for laundering included profits.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality opinion dismisses these concerns with the observation that a jury may infer that a hired launderer knew that funds included profits if the launderer had a long-running relationship with the entity or person providing the funds or knew that the entity or person had been involved in the illegal enterprise for a lengthy period. See ante, at 14. But what about the case where the launderer accepts a million dollars of drug money on a single occasion? And even if there would be legally sufficient evidence to support an inference of the requisite knowledge under the circumstances that the plurality opinion posits, the requirement of convincing a jury to find beyond a reasonable doubt that the funds included profits would pose a troublesome and (in light of the aim of the money laundering statute) pointless obstacle.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even in cases in which the defendants are alleged to have been involved in the underlying criminal activity, a net income interpretation would produce nettlesome problems that Congress cannot have wanted. These problems may be especially acute in the very cases that money laundering statutes principally target, that is, cases involving large-scale criminal operations that continue over a substantial period of time, particularly drug cartels and other organized crime syndicates.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The federal money laundering statute was enacted in the wake of an influential report by the President\xe2\x80\x99s Commission on Organized Crime that focused squarely on criminal enterprises of this type. See Interim Report 7\xe2\x80\x938 (described in S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 99\xe2\x80\x93433, pp. 2\xe2\x80\x934 (1986) (hereinafter S.\xc2\xa0Rep.) and H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep., at 16). The Commission identified drug traffickers and other organized criminal groups as presenting the most serious problems. See Interim Report 7. The Commission found that \xe2\x80\x9cnarcotics traffickers, who must conceal billions of dollars in cash from detection by the government, create by far the greatest demand for money laundering services\xe2\x80\x9d but that \xe2\x80\x9cnumerous other types of activities typical of organized crime, such as loansharking and gambling, also create an appreciable demand for such schemes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. To illustrate the scope and nature of the money laundering problem, a section of the Interim Report was devoted to case studies, most of which involved the laundering of drug money. Id., at 29\xe2\x80\x9349.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a prime example of the problem of money laundering, the report discussed the so-called \xe2\x80\x9cPizza Connection\xe2\x80\x9d case that was prosecuted in federal court in New York City in the 1980\xe2\x80\x99s. In that case, the evidence showed that the Sicilian Mafia and organized crime elements in the United States, over a period of many years, imported huge amounts of heroin into this country, sold the heroin here, accumulated millions of dollars of cash, and then laundered the funds by smuggling them overseas in suitcases or funneling the money through a maze of bank accounts. See id., at 31\xe2\x80\x9335; United States v. Casamento, 887 F.\xc2\xa02d 1141, 1148\xe2\x80\x931149 (CA2 1989).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Following the issuance of the Interim Report, Congress turned its attention to the problem of money laundering, and much of the discussion focused on the need to prevent laundering by drug and organized crime syndicates. See, e.g., S.\xc2\xa0Rep., at\xc2\xa03 (discussing \xe2\x80\x9corganized crime \xe2\x80\x98businesses\xe2\x80\x99 such as gambling, prostitution, and loansharking\xe2\x80\x9d), 4 (\xe2\x80\x9cMoney laundering is a crucial financial underpinning of organized crime and narcotics trafficking\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Hearing on Money Laundering Legislation before the Senate Committee on the Judiciary, 99th Cong., 1st Sess., 1 (1985) (statement of Chairman Thurmond); id., at 29 (statement of Sen. Biden), 30 (statement of Sen. DeConcini), 31 (statement of Sen. D\xe2\x80\x99Amato), 53 (statement of Assistant Attorney General Trott).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of these concerns, it is most unlikely that Congress meant to enact a money laundering statute that would present daunting obstacles in the very sort of cases that had been identified as presenting the most pressing problems, that is, cases, like the \xe2\x80\x9cPizza Connection\xe2\x80\x9d case, in which law enforcement intercepts cash or wire transfers of funds derived from drug sales or other unlawful activity that occurred over a period of time. The plurality opinion\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds,\xe2\x80\x9d however, would often produce such problems. Tracing funds back to particular drug sales and proving that these sales were profitable will often prove impossible. See United States v. Bajakajian, 524 U. S. 321, 351\xe2\x80\x93352 (1998) (Kennedy,\xc2\xa0J., dissenting). Indeed, it will often be hard even to establish with any precision the period of time during which the drug sales occurred. But assuming that the Government can prove roughly when the funds were acquired, the next hurdle would be to show that the drug ring had net income during the time when the funds were acquired.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cNet income\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he excess of revenues over all related expenses for a given period.\xe2\x80\x9d R. Estes, Dictionary of Accounting 88 (1981) (emphasis deleted). There are no generally accepted accounting principles for determining the net income of illegal enterprises, and therefore, in order to apply a net income interpretation, special accounting rules would have to be developed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the drug-money cases that I have been discussing, the courts would have to decide whether the drug syndicate\xe2\x80\x99s net income should be calculated on an annual, quarterly, or some other basis. In addition, the courts would be forced to devise rules for determining the scope of the enterprise for which the net income calculation must be performed. Suppose, for example, that there were connections of an uncertain nature or degree between drug operations in different cities or countries. Rules would be needed to determine whether affiliated criminal groups should be regarded as one enterprise or several. And proof regarding the connections between such operations would often be very difficult to obtain. Criminal enterprises do not have papers of incorporation, partnership agreements, or (in most instances) other documents establishing precise business relationships.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rules would also be needed in order to determine whether particular illegal expenditures should be considered as expenses. In the \xe2\x80\x9cPizza Connection\xe2\x80\x9d case, the Sicilian Mafia used its income for such things as the murder of magistrates, police officers, witnesses, and rivals. See, e.g., Casamento, supra, at 1154\xe2\x80\x931156; United States v. Gambino, 809 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 1061, 1065\xe2\x80\x931068 (SDNY 1992). Are these expenditures simply a cost of engaging in the drug trade? Are they business expenses?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If a net income interpretation were taken to its logical conclusion, it presumably would be necessary as well to work out rules for the depreciation of instrumentalities of crime that must occasionally be replaced due to the efforts of law enforcement. But it seems quite implausible that Congress wanted courts or juries in money laundering cases to grapple with questions such as the useful life of, say, a drug processing plant or laboratory or the airplanes and boats that are used to smuggle drugs. And assuming that the accounting issues can ultimately be resolved by the courts, there would remain serious problems of proof. Illegal enterprises generally do not keep books and records like legitimate businesses do.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is tempting to dismiss many of the problems noted above on the ground that \xe2\x80\x9ceveryone knows\xe2\x80\x9d that drug cartels, organized crime syndicates, and the like make a profit. But such groups may not operate in the black at all times, and in any event, if net income is an element of the money laundering offense, the prosecution must prove net income beyond a reasonable doubt. The prosecution cannot simply ask the jury to take notice of the fact that these groups are profitable.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0My point in citing the accounting and proof problems that would be produced by a net income interpretation is not that the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98receipts\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation is preferable because \xe2\x80\x9cit is easier to prosecute,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11 (plurality opinion), but that creating these obstacles would serve no discernible purpose. Even if a drug or gambling ring was temporarily operating in the red during a particular period, the laundering of money acquired during that time would present the same dangers as the laundering of money acquired during times of profit. It is therefore implausible that Congress wanted to throw up such pointless obstacles.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality opinion attempts to minimize all these problems by stating that \xe2\x80\x9cto establish the proceeds element under the \xe2\x80\x98profits\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation, the prosecution needs to show only that a single instance of specified unlawful activity was profitable and gave rise to the money involved in a charged transaction.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. This suggestion ignores both the language of the money laundering statute, which makes no reference to an \xe2\x80\x9cinstance\xe2\x80\x9d of unlawful activity, and the realities of money laundering prosecutions. The prototypical money laundering case is not a case in which a defendant engages in a single, discrete criminal act and then launders the money derived from that act\xe2\x80\x94for example, a case in which a \xe2\x80\x9cfelon . . . uses . . . stolen money to pay for the rented getaway car.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. Rather, the prototypical case involves numerous criminal acts that occur over a period of time and the accumulation of funds from all these acts prior to laundering\xe2\x80\x94for example, the organized crime syndicate or drug cartel that amasses large sums before engaging in a laundering transaction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Take, for example, a case in which a defendant is charged with doing what was done in the \xe2\x80\x9cPizza Connection\xe2\x80\x9d case\xe2\x80\x94transferring millions of dollars of drug money overseas, knowing that the funds represent the proceeds of drug trafficking (\xe2\x80\x9csome form of unlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d) and that the transfer was designed to conceal the origin of the funds. See 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(a)(2)(B)(2). In such a case, it is unrealistic to think that individual dollars can be traced back to individual drug sales\xe2\x80\x94or that Congress wanted to require such tracing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the plurality opinion begins by touting the \xe2\x80\x9csingle instance\xe2\x80\x9d theory as a cure for the accounting and proof problems that a \xe2\x80\x9cprofits\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation produces, the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s application of the \xe2\x80\x9csingle instance\xe2\x80\x9d theory to the case at hand shows that this theory will not work. In this case, the \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d that produced the funds at issue in the substantive money laundering counts was the operation of the Santos lottery,[Footnote 6] and it is hardly apparent what constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9csingle instance\xe2\x80\x9d of running a gambling business. Did each lottery drawing represent a separate \xe2\x80\x9cinstance\xe2\x80\x9d? Each wager? And how long does each gambling \xe2\x80\x9cinstance\xe2\x80\x9d last? A day? A week? A month? \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When the plurality opinion addresses these questions, it turns out that \xe2\x80\x9ca single instance\xe2\x80\x9d means all instances that are charged, i.e., it means that the Government had to show that receipts exceeded costs during the time the defendant allegedly conducted, financed, etc., the gambling operation. See ante, at 13, n.\xc2\xa07. Here, since the Indictment alleged that the Santos lottery continued for more than 6 years (\xe2\x80\x9c[b]egining in or about January 1989 and continuing to in or about December 1994, the exact dates being unknown to the Grand Jury\xe2\x80\x9d),[Footnote 7] the plurality would apparently compel the Government to prove that the lottery was profitable over this entire period.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If this is where the \xe2\x80\x9csingle instance\xe2\x80\x9d theory leads, the theory plainly does not solve the accounting and proof problems we have noted. And the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that the Government had to show that the gambling operation was profitable for this entire period leads to preposterous results. Suppose that the lottery was profitable for the first five years and, at the end of each year, respondents laundered funds derived from the business. Suppose that in the sixth year the business incurred heavy losses\xe2\x80\x94losses so heavy that they wiped out all of the profits from the first five years. According to the plurality, if respondents were found to have operated the lottery during the entire 6-year period, then the financial transactions that occurred at the end of years one, two, three, four, and five would not violate the money laundering statute, even though an accounting done at those times would have come to the conclusion that the funds included profits. That result makes no sense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whenever a money laundering indictment charges that the laundered funds derived from an \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d that comprehends numerous acts that occurred over a considerable period of time\xe2\x80\x94and that is precisely the situation in many of the types of cases that the money laundering statute principally targeted\xe2\x80\x94the plurality opinion\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation will produce difficulties. I have already discussed drug and gambling cases, and similar problems will arise in cases in which the unlawful activity is a form of fraud. For example, the unlawful activity in mail fraud (18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341) is the scheme to defraud, not the individual mailings carried out in furtherance of the scheme. See Neder v. United States, 527 U. S. 1, 19 (1999); United States v. Mankarious, 151 F.\xc2\xa03d 694 (CA7 1998). In such a case, what will constitute the \xe2\x80\x9csingle instance of unlawful activity\xe2\x80\x9d? Will each mailing be a separate \xe2\x80\x9cinstance\xe2\x80\x9d? The same problem arises with other fraud predicates, including wire fraud (\xc2\xa71343), see, e.g., United States v. Zvi, 168 F.\xc2\xa03d 49 (CA2 1999), and financial institution fraud (\xc2\xa71344), see, e.g., United States v. Farr, 69 F.\xc2\xa03d 545 (CA9 1995).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality opinion suggests that the application of a profits interpretation will be easy in cases in which the financial transactions are payments of \xe2\x80\x9cexpenses.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. But it may be no small matter to determine whether particular payments are for \xe2\x80\x9cexpenses.\xe2\x80\x9d When the manager of a gambling operation distributes cash to those who work in the operation, the manager may be paying them the rough equivalent of a salary; that is, the recipients may expect to receive a certain amount for their services whether or not the operation is profitable. On the other hand, those who work in the operation may have the expectation of receiving a certain percentage of the gross revenue (perhaps even in addition to a salary), in which case their distribution may include profits. Such was the case in Santos\xe2\x80\x99 lottery, where the runners were paid a percentage of gross revenue. See Indictment 5; 16 Tr. 1399 (Oct. 9, 1997).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality opinion cites 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71963(a) and 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7853(a), for the proposition that Congress has \xe2\x80\x9celsewhere\xe2\x80\x9d imposed the burden of proving that illegally obtained funds represent profits, but the plurality opinion\xe2\x80\x99s examples are inapposite. Ante, at 12. Neither of these provisions, however, requires a determination of net income. Both provisions permit a fine in the amount of \xe2\x80\x9cnot more than twice the gross profits or other proceeds.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71963(a). Thus, the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d as used in these provisions is not limited to profits.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 For all these reasons, I am convinced that the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in the money laundering statute means gross receipts, not net income. And contrary to the approach taken by Justice Stevens, I do not see how the meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d can vary depending on the nature of the illegal activity that produced the laundered funds.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is apparent that a chief reason for interpreting the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d to mean net income in all money laundering cases (the approach taken in the plurality opinion) or in some money laundering cases (the approach taken by Justice Stevens) is the desire to avoid a \xe2\x80\x9cmerger\xe2\x80\x9d problem in gambling cases\xe2\x80\x94that is, to avoid an interpretation that would mean that every violation of \xc2\xa71955 (conducting an illegal gambling business) would also constitute a violation of the money laundering statute, which carries a much higher maximum penalty (20 as opposed to 5 years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment). This concern is misplaced and provides no justification for hobbling a statute that applies to more than 250 predicate offenses and not just running an illegal gambling business.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the so-called merger problem is fundamentally a sentencing problem, and the proper remedy is a sentencing remedy. While it is true that the money laundering statute has a higher maximum sentence than the gambling business statute, neither statute has a mandatory minimum. Thus, these statutes do not require a judge to increase a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence simply because the defendant was convicted of money laundering as well as running a gambling business. When the respondents were convicted, their money laundering convictions resulted in higher sentences only because of the money laundering Sentencing Guideline, United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa72S1.1 (Nov. 1997) (USSG), which, in the pre-Booker\xc2\xa0[Footnote 9] era, was mandatory. I agree with Justice Breyer, ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (dissenting opinion), that if a defendant is convicted of money laundering for doing no more than is required for a violation of 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71955, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence should be no higher than it would have been if the defendant had violated only that latter provision. Insofar as the Guidelines previously required\xe2\x80\x94and now advise in favor of\xe2\x80\x94a stiffer sentence, the obvious remedy is an amendment of the money laundering Guideline. And of course, now that the Guidelines are no longer mandatory, a sentencing judge could impose the sentence called for by the Guideline that applies to the gambling business provision, see USSG \xc2\xa72E3.1(a)(1) (Nov. 2007), or an entirely different sentence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the merger problem that the plurality opinion and Justice Stevens seek to avoid assumes the correctness of the interpretation of the promotion prong of the money laundering statute that the Seventh Circuit adopted in Santos\xe2\x80\x99 direct appeal, i.e., that a defendant \xe2\x80\x9cpromotes\xe2\x80\x9d an illegal gambling business by doing those things, such as paying employees and winning bettors, that are needed merely to keep the business running. As Santos\xe2\x80\x99 brief puts it, the merger problem arises when the interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d as gross receipts is \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ombined with the Government\xe2\x80\x99s broad application of the \xe2\x80\x98promotion\xe2\x80\x99 prong of the money laundering statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 6. But the meaning of the element of promotion is not before us in this case, and it would not make sense to allow our interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d to be dictated by an unreviewed interpretation of another statutory element.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, even if there is a merger problem, it occurs in only a subset of money laundering cases. The money laundering statute reaches financial transactions that are intended to promote more than 250 other crimes, ante, at 9 (plurality opinion), as well as transactions that are intended to conceal or disguise the nature, location, source, ownership, or control of illegally obtained funds. See 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956(a). The meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d cannot vary from one money laundering case to the next, and the plurality opinion and Justice Stevens inappropriately allow the interpretation of that term to be controlled by a problem that may arise in only a subset of cases.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality opinion defends its interpretation by invoking the rule of lenity, but the rule of lenity does not require us to put aside the usual tools of statutory interpretation or to adopt the narrowest possible dictionary definition of the terms in a criminal statute. On the contrary, \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause the meaning of language is inherently contextual, we have declined to deem a statute \xe2\x80\x98ambiguous\xe2\x80\x99 for purposes of lenity merely because it was possible to articulate a construction more narrow than that urged by the Government.\xe2\x80\x9d Moskal v. United States, 498 U. S. 103, 108 (1990) (citing McElroy v. United States, 455 U. S. 642, 657\xe2\x80\x93658 (1982)). As I have explained above, the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d in the money laundering statute emerges with reasonable clarity when the term is viewed in context, making the rule of lenity inapplicable.

Accordingly, the House seems to have understood \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d to include gross receipts.

See H.\xc2\xa0R. 5484, 99th Cong., 2d Sess., \xc2\xa71352, p. 48 (1986) (as enacted). There is no suggestion in the legislative history that the term \xe2\x80\x9ccriminally derived property\xe2\x80\x9d and the term \xe2\x80\x9cproceeds\xe2\x80\x9d were perceived as having different meanings.
