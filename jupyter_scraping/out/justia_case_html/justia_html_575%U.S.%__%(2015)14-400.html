HARRIS v. VIEGELAHN, CHAPTER 13 TRUSTEE

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93400.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 1, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 18, 2015

\n

Individual debtors may seek discharge of their financial obligations under either Chapter 7 or Chapter 13 of the Bankruptcy Code. In a Chapter 7 proceeding, the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets are transferred to a bankruptcy estate.\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7541(a)(1). The estate\xe2\x80\x99s assets are then promptly liquidated, \xc2\xa7704(a)(1), and distributed to creditors, \xc2\xa7726. A Chapter 7 estate, however, does not include the wages a debtor earns or the assets he acquires after the bankruptcy filing. \xc2\xa7541(a)(1). Chapter 13, a wholly voluntary alternative to Chapter 7, permits the debtor to retain assets during bankruptcy subject to a court-approved plan for payment of his debts. Payments under a Chapter 13 plan are usually made from a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfuture income.\xe2\x80\x9d 1322(a)(1). The Chapter 13 estate, unlike a Chapter 7 estate, therefore includes both the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s property at the time of his bankruptcy petition, and any assets he acquires after filing. \xc2\xa71306(a). Because many debtors fail to complete a Chapter 13 plan successfully, Congress accorded debtors a nonwaivable right to convert a Chapter 13 case to one under Chapter 7 \xe2\x80\x9cat any time.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71307(a). Conversion does not commence a new bankruptcy case, but it does terminate the service of the Chapter 13 trustee. \xc2\xa7348(e).

\n

Petitioner Harris, indebted to multiple creditors and $3,700 behind on his home mortgage payments to Chase Manhattan, filed a Chapter 13 bankruptcy petition. His court-confirmed plan provided that he would resume making monthly mortgage payments to Chase, and that $530 per month would be withheld from his postpetition wages and remitted to the Chapter 13 trustee, respondent Viegelahn. Trustee Viegelahn would make monthly payments to Chase to pay down Harris\xe2\x80\x99 mortgage arrears, and distribute remaining funds to Harris\xe2\x80\x99 other creditors. When Harris again fell behind on his mortgage pay-ments, Chase foreclosed on his home. Following the foreclosure, Viegelahn continued to receive $530 per month from Harris\xe2\x80\x99 wages, but stopped making the payments earmarked for Chase. As a result, funds formerly reserved for Chase accumulated in Viegelahn\xe2\x80\x99s possession. Approximately a year after the foreclosure, Harris converted his case to Chapter 7. Ten days after this conversion, Viegelahn distributed $5,519.22 in Harris\xe2\x80\x99 withheld wages mainly to Harris\xe2\x80\x99 creditors. Asserting that Viegelahn lacked authority to disburse his postpetition wages to creditors postconversion, Harris sought an order from the Bankruptcy Court directing refund of the accumulated wages Viegelahn paid to his creditors. The Bankruptcy Court granted Harris\xe2\x80\x99 motion, and the District Court affirmed. The Fifth Circuit reversed, concluding that a former Chapter 13 trustee must distribute a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s accumulated postpetition wages to his creditors.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A debtor who converts to Chapter 7 is entitled to return of any postpetition wages not yet distributed by the Chapter 13 trustee. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Absent a bad-faith conversion, \xc2\xa7348(f) limits a converted Chapter 7 estate to property belonging to the debtor \xe2\x80\x9cas of the date\xe2\x80\x9d the original Chapter 13 petition was filed. Because postpetition wages do not fit that bill, undistributed wages collected by a Chapter 13 trustee ordinarily do not become part of a converted Chapter 7 estate. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0By excluding postpetition wages from the converted Chapter 7 estate (absent a bad-faith conversion), \xc2\xa7348(f) removes those earnings from the pool of assets that may be liquidated and distributed to creditors. Allowing a terminated Chapter 13 trustee to disburse the very same earnings to the very same creditors is incompatible with that statutory design. Pp. 7\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0This conclusion is reinforced by \xc2\xa7348(e), which \xe2\x80\x9cterminates the service of [the Chapter 13] trustee\xe2\x80\x9d upon conversion. One service provided by a Chapter 13 trustee is disbursing \xe2\x80\x9cpayments to creditors.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71326(c). The moment a case is converted from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7, a Chapter 13 trustee is stripped of authority to provide that \xe2\x80\x9cservice.\xe2\x80\x9d P. 8.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0Section 1327(a), which provides that a confirmed Chapter 13 plan \xe2\x80\x9cbind[s] the debtor and each creditor,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xc2\xa71326(a)(2), which instructs a trustee to distribute \xe2\x80\x9cpayment[s] in accordance with the plan,\xe2\x80\x9d ceased to apply once the case was converted to Chapter 7. \xc2\xa7103(i). Sections 1327(a) and 1326(a)(2), therefore, offer no support for Viegelahn\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that the Bankruptcy Code requires a terminated Chapter 13 trustee to distribute to creditors postpetition wages remaining in the trustee\xe2\x80\x99s possession. Continuing to distribute funds to creditors pursuant to a defunct Chapter 13 plan, moreover, is not one of the trustee\xe2\x80\x99s postconversion responsibilities specified by the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(e)\xc2\xa0Because Chapter 13 is a voluntary alternative to Chapter 7, a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s postconversion receipt of a fraction of the wages he earned and would have kept had he filed under Chapter 7 in the first place does not provide the debtor with a \xe2\x80\x9cwindfall.\xe2\x80\x9d A trustee who distributes payments regularly may have little or no accumulated wages to return, while a trustee who distributes payments infrequently may have a sizable refund to make. But creditors may gain protection against the risk of excess accumulations in the hands of trustees by seeking to have a Chapter 13 plan include a schedule for regular disbursement of collected funds. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

757 F.\xc2\xa03d 468, reversed and remanded.

CHARLES E. HARRIS, III, PETITIONER v. MARY K. VIEGELAHN, CHAPTER 13 TRUSTEE

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[May 18, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case concerns the disposition of wages earned by a debtor after he petitions for bankruptcy. The treatment of postpetition wages generally depends on whether the debtor is proceeding under Chapter 13 of the Bankruptcy Code (in which the debtor retains assets, often his home, during bankruptcy subject to a court-approved plan for the payment of his debts) or Chapter 7 (in which the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets are immediately liquidated and the proceeds distributed to creditors). In a Chapter 13 proceeding, postpetition wages are \xe2\x80\x9c[p]roperty of the estate,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71306(a), and may be collected by the Chapter 13 trustee for distribution to creditors, \xc2\xa71322(a)(1). In a Chapter 7 proceeding, those earnings are not estate property; instead, they belong to the debtor. See \xc2\xa7541(a)(1). The Code permits the debtor to convert a Chapter 13 proceeding to one under Chapter 7 \xe2\x80\x9cat any time,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71307(a); upon such conversion, the service of the Chapter 13 trustee terminates, \xc2\xa7348(e).

\n

When a debtor initially filing under Chapter 13 exercises his right to convert to Chapter 7, who is entitled to post-petition wages still in the hands of the Chapter 13 trustee? Not the Chapter 7 estate when the conversion is in good faith, all agree. May the trustee distribute the accumu-lated wage payments to creditors as the Chapter 13 planrequired, or must she remit them to the debtor? That is the question this case presents. We hold that, under the governing provisions of the Bankruptcy Code, a debtor who converts to Chapter 7 is entitled to return of anypostpetition wages not yet distributed by the Chapter 13 trustee.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The Bankruptcy Code provides diverse courses overburdened debtors may pursue to gain discharge of their financial obligations, and thereby a \xe2\x80\x9cfresh start.\xe2\x80\x9d Marrama v. Citizens Bank of Mass.,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 365,\n367 (2007)\n (quoting Grogan v. Garner,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 279,\n286 (1991)\n). Two roads individual debtors may take are relevant here: Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 bankruptcy proceedings.

\n

Chapter 7 allows a debtor to make a clean break from his financial past, but at a steep price: prompt liquidation of the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets. When a debtor files a Chapter 7 petition, his assets, with specified exemptions, are immediately transferred to a bankruptcy estate. \xc2\xa7541(a)(1). A Chapter 7 trustee is then charged with selling the prop-erty in the estate, \xc2\xa7704(a)(1), and distributing the proceedsto the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s creditors, \xc2\xa7726. Crucially, however, a Chapter 7 estate does not include the wages a debtor earns or the assets he acquires after the bankruptcy filing. \xc2\xa7541(a)(1). Thus, while a Chapter 7 debtor must forfeit virtually all his prepetition property, he is able to make a \xe2\x80\x9cfresh start\xe2\x80\x9d by shielding from creditors his postpetition earnings and acquisitions.

\n

Chapter 13 works differently. A wholly voluntary alternative to Chapter 7, Chapter 13 allows a debtor to retain his property if he proposes, and gains court confirmation of, a plan to repay his debts over a three- to five-year period. \xc2\xa71306(b), \xc2\xa71322, \xc2\xa71327(b). Payments under a Chapter 13 plan are usually made from a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfuture earnings or other future income.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71322(a)(1); see 8 Col-lier on Bankruptcy \xc2\xb61322.02[1] (A. Resnick & H. Sommer eds., 16th ed. 2014). Accordingly, the Chapter 13 estate from which creditors may be paid includes both the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s property at the time of his bankruptcy petition, and any wages and property acquired after filing. \xc2\xa71306(a). A Chapter 13 trustee is often charged with collecting a portion of a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s wages through payroll deduction, and with distributing the withheld wages to creditors.

\n

Proceedings under Chapter 13 can benefit debtors and creditors alike. Debtors are allowed to retain their assets, commonly their home or car. And creditors, entitled to a Chapter 13 debtor\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdisposable\xe2\x80\x9d postpetition income, \xc2\xa71325(b)(1), usually collect more under a Chapter 13plan than they would have received under a Chapter 7 liquidation.

\n

Many debtors, however, fail to complete a Chapter 13 plan successfully. See Porter, The Pretend Solution: An Empirical Study of Bankruptcy Outcomes, 90 Texas L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 103, 107\xe2\x80\x93111 (2011) (only one in three cases filed under Chapter 13 ends in discharge). Recognizing that reality, Congress accorded debtors a nonwaivable right to convert a Chapter 13 case to one under Chapter 7 \xe2\x80\x9cat any time.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71307(a). To effectuate a conversion, a debtor need only file a notice with the bankruptcy court. Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 1017(f)(3). No motion or court order is needed to render the conversion effective. See ibid.

\n

Conversion from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7 does not commence a new bankruptcy case. The existing case continues along another track, Chapter 7 instead of Chapter 13, without \xe2\x80\x9ceffect[ing] a change in the date of the filing of the petition.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7348(a). Conversion, however, immediately \xe2\x80\x9cterminates the service\xe2\x80\x9d of the Chapter 13 trustee, replacing her with a Chapter 7 trustee. \xc2\xa7348(e).

\n

B

\n

In February 2010, petitioner Charles Harris III filed a Chapter 13 bankruptcy petition. At the time of filing, Harris was indebted to multiple creditors, and had fallen $3,700 behind on payments to Chase Manhattan, his home mortgage lender.

\n

Harris\xe2\x80\x99 court-confirmed Chapter 13 plan provided that he would immediately resume making monthly mortgage payments to Chase. The plan further provided that $530 per month would be withheld from Harris\xe2\x80\x99 postpetition wages and remitted to the Chapter 13 trustee, respondent Mary Viegelahn. Viegelahn, in turn, would distribute $352 per month to Chase to pay down Harris\xe2\x80\x99 outstanding mortgage debt. She would also distribute $75.34 per month to Harris\xe2\x80\x99 only other secured lender, a consumer-electronics store. Once those secured creditors were paid in full, Viegelahn was to begin distributing funds to Harris\xe2\x80\x99 unsecured creditors.

\n

Implementation of the plan was short lived. Harris again fell behind on his mortgage payments, and in November 2010, Chase received permission from the Bankruptcy Court to foreclose on Harris\xe2\x80\x99 home. Following the foreclosure, Viegelahn continued to receive $530 per month from Harris\xe2\x80\x99 wages, but stopped making the payments earmarked for Chase. As a result, funds formerly reserved for Chase accumulated in Viegelahn\xe2\x80\x99s possession.

\n

On November 22, 2011, Harris exercised his statutory right to convert his Chapter 13 case to one under Chapter 7. By that time, Harris\xe2\x80\x99 postpetition wages accumulated by Viegelahn amounted to $5,519.22. On December 1, 2011\xe2\x80\x94ten days after Harris\xe2\x80\x99 conversion\xe2\x80\x94Viegelahn disposed of those funds by giving $1,200 to Harris\xe2\x80\x99 counsel, paying herself a $267.79 fee, and distributing the remain-ing money to the consumer-electronics store and six of Harris\xe2\x80\x99 unsecured creditors.

\n

Asserting that Viegelahn lacked authority to disburse funds to creditors once the case was converted to Chapter 7, Harris moved the Bankruptcy Court for an order directing refund of the accumulated wages Viegelahn had given to his creditors. The Bankruptcy Court granted Harris\xe2\x80\x99 motion, and the District Court affirmed.

\n

The Fifth Circuit reversed. In\xc2\xa0re Harris, 757 F.\xc2\xa03d 468 (2014). Finding \xe2\x80\x9clittle guidance in the Bankruptcy Code,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 478, the Fifth Circuit concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderations of equity and policy\xe2\x80\x9d rendered \xe2\x80\x9cthe creditors\xe2\x80\x99 claim to the undistributed funds . . . superior to that of the debtor,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 478, 481. Notwithstanding a Chapter 13 debtor\xe2\x80\x99s conversion to Chapter 7, the Fifth Circuit held, a former Chapter 13 trustee must distribute a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s accumulated postpetition wages to his creditors.

\n

The Fifth Circuit acknowledged that its decision conflicted with the Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in In\xc2\xa0re Michael, 699 F.\xc2\xa03d 305 (2012), which held that a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s undistributed postpetition wages \xe2\x80\x9care to be returned to the debtor at the time of conversion [from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 307. We granted certiorari to resolve this conflict, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), and now reverse the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

Prior to the Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1994, courts divided three ways on the disposition of a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s undistributed postpetition wages following conversion of a proceeding from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7. Some courts concluded that undistributed postpetition wages reverted to the debtor. E.g., In\xc2\xa0re Boggs, 137 B.\xc2\xa0R. 408, 411 (Bkrtcy. Ct. WD Wash. 1992). Others ordered a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s undis-tributed postpetition earnings disbursed to creditors pur-suant to the terms of the confirmed (albeit terminated) Chapter 13 plan. E.g., In\xc2\xa0re Waugh, 82 B.\xc2\xa0R. 394, 400 (Bkrtcy. Ct. WD Pa. 1988). Still other courts, including several Courts of Appeals, held that, upon conversion, all postpetition earnings and acquisitions became part of the new Chapter 7 estate, thus augmenting the property available for liquidation and distribution to creditors. E.g., In\xc2\xa0re Calder, 973 F.\xc2\xa02d 862, 865\xe2\x80\x93866 (CA10 1992); In re Lybrook, 951 F. 2d 136, 137 (CA7 1991).

\n

Congress addressed the matter in 1994 by adding \xc2\xa7348(f) to the Bankruptcy Code. Rejecting the rulings of several Courts of Appeals, \xc2\xa7348(f)(1)(A) provides that in a case converted from Chapter 13, a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s postpetition earnings and acquisitions do not become part of the new Chapter 7 estate:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[P]roperty of the [Chapter 7] estate in the converted case shall consist of property of the estate, as of the date of filing of the [initial Chapter 13] petition, that remains in the possession of or is under the control of the debtor on the date of conversion.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

In \xc2\xa7348(f)(2), Congress added an exception for debtors who convert in bad faith:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf the debtor converts a case [initially filed] under chapter 13 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in bad faith, the property of the estate in the converted case shall consist of the property of the estate as of the date of the conversion.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Section 348(f), all agree, makes one thing clear: A debtor\xe2\x80\x99s postpetition wages, including undisbursed funds in the hands of a trustee, ordinarily do not become part of the Chapter 7 estate created by conversion. Absent a bad-faith conversion, \xc2\xa7348(f) limits a converted Chapter 7 estate to property belonging to the debtor \xe2\x80\x9cas of the date\xe2\x80\x9d the original Chapter 13 petition was filed. Postpetition wages, by definition, do not fit that bill.

\n

B

\n

With this background, we turn to the question presented: What happens to postpetition wages held by a Chapter13 trustee at the time the case is converted to Chapter 7? Does the Code require return of the funds to the debtor, or does it require their distribution to creditors? We conclude that postpetition wages must be returned to the debtor.

\n

By excluding postpetition wages from the converted Chapter 7 estate, \xc2\xa7348(f)(1)(A) removes those earnings from the pool of assets that may be liquidated and distributed to creditors. Allowing a terminated Chapter 13 trustee to disburse the very same earnings to the very same creditors is incompatible with that statutory design. We resist attributing to Congress, after explicitly exempting from Chapter 7\xe2\x80\x99s liquidation-and-distribution process a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s postpetition wages, a plan to place those wages in creditors\xe2\x80\x99 hands another way.

\n

Section 348(f)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s exception for bad-faith conversions is instructive in this regard. If a debtor converts in bad faith\xe2\x80\x94for example, by concealing assets in \xe2\x80\x9cunfair manipulation of the bankruptcy system,\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Siegfried, 219 B.\xc2\xa0R. 581, 586 (Bkrtcy. Ct. Colo. 1998)\xe2\x80\x94the converted Chapter 7 estate \xe2\x80\x9cconsist[s] of the property of the [Chapter 13] estate as of the date of conversion.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7348(f)(2) (emphasis added). Section 348(f)(2) thus penalizes bad-faith debtors by making their postpetition wages available for liquidation and distribution to creditors. Conversely, when the conversion to Chapter 7 is made in good faith, no penalty is exacted. Shielding a Chapter 7 debtor\xe2\x80\x99s postpetition earnings from creditors enables the \xe2\x80\x9chonest but unfortunate debtor\xe2\x80\x9d to make the \xe2\x80\x9cfresh start\xe2\x80\x9d the Bankruptcy Code aims to facilitate. Marrama, 549 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 367 (internal quotation marks omitted). Bad-faith conversions apart, we find nothing in the Code denying debtors funds that would have been theirs had the case proceeded under Chapter 7 from the start. In sum, \xc2\xa7348(f) does not say, expressly: On conversion, accumulated wages go to the debtor. But that is the most sensible reading of what Congress did provide.

\n

Section 348(e) also informs our ruling that undistrib-uted postpetition wages must be returned to the debtor.That section provides: \xe2\x80\x9cConversion [from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7] terminates the service of [the Chapter 13] trustee.\xe2\x80\x9d A core service provided by a Chapter 13 trustee is the disbursement of \xe2\x80\x9cpayments to creditors.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71326(c) (emphasis added). The moment a case is converted from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7, however, the Chapter 13 trustee is stripped of authority to provide that \xe2\x80\x9cservice.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7348(e).

\n

Section 348(e), of course, does not require a terminated trustee to hold accumulated funds in perpetuity; she must (as we hold today) return undistributed postpetition wages to the debtor. Returning funds to a debtor, however, is not a Chapter 13 trustee service as is making \xe2\x80\x9cpaymen[t] to creditors.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71326(c). In this case, illustratively, Chapter 13 trustee Viegelahn continued to act in that capacity after her tenure ended. Eight days after the case was converted to Chapter 7, she filed with the Bankruptcy Court a document titled \xe2\x80\x9cTrustee\xe2\x80\x99s Recommendations Concerning Claims,\xe2\x80\x9d recommending distribution of the funds originally earmarked for Chase to the remaining secured creditor and six of the 13 unsecured creditors. No. 10\xe2\x80\x9350655 (Bkrtcy. Ct. WD Tex., Nov. 30, 2011), Doc. 34. She then acted on that recommendation. She thus provided a Chapter 13 trustee \xe2\x80\x9cservice,\xe2\x80\x9d although barred fromdoing so by \xc2\xa7348(e). Returning undistributed wages to the debtor, in contrast, renders no Chapter 13-authorized \xe2\x80\x9cservice.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

C

\n

Viegelahn cites two Chapter 13 provisions in support of her argument that the Bankruptcy Code requires a termi-nated Chapter 13 trustee \xe2\x80\x9cto distribute undisbursed funds to creditors.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 21. The first, \xc2\xa71327(a), provides that a confirmed Chapter 13 plan \xe2\x80\x9cbind[s] the debtor and each creditor.\xe2\x80\x9d The second, \xc2\xa71326(a)(2), instructs a trustee to distribute \xe2\x80\x9cpayment[s] in accordancewith the plan,\xe2\x80\x9d and that, Viegelahn observes, is justwhat she did. But the cited provisions had no force here, for they ceased to apply once the case was converted to Chapter 7.

\n

When a debtor exercises his statutory right to convert, the case is placed under Chapter 7\xe2\x80\x99s governance, and no Chapter 13 provision holds sway. \xc2\xa7103(i) (\xe2\x80\x9cChapter 13 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. applies only in a case under [that] chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d). Harris having converted the case, the Chapter 13 plan was no longer \xe2\x80\x9cbind[ing].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71327(a). And Viegelahn, by then the former Chapter 13 trustee, lacked authority to distribute \xe2\x80\x9cpayment[s] in accordance with the plan.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71326(a)(2); see \xc2\xa7348(e).

\n

Nor can we credit the suggestion that a confirmed Chapter 13 plan gives creditors a vested right to funds held by a trustee. \xe2\x80\x9c[N]o provision in the Bankruptcy Code classifies any property, including post-petition wages, as belonging to creditors.\xe2\x80\x9d Michael, 699 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 312\xe2\x80\x93313.

\n

Viegelahn alternatively urges that a terminated Chapter 13 trustee\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cduty\xe2\x80\x9d to distribute funds to creditors is a facet of the trustee\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to \xe2\x80\x9cwind up\xe2\x80\x9d the affairs of the Chapter 13 estate following conversion. Brief for Respondent 25 (internal quotation marks omitted). The Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, however, specify what a terminated Chapter 13 trustee must do postconversion: (1) she must turn over records and assets to the Chapter 7 trustee, Rule 1019(4); and (2) she must file a report with the United States bankruptcy trustee, Rule 1019(5)(B)(ii). Continuing to distribute funds to creditors pursuant to the defunct Chapter 13 plan is not an authorized \xe2\x80\x9cwind-up\xe2\x80\x9d task.

\n

Finally, Viegelahn homes in on a particular feature of this case. Section 1327(b) states that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcept as otherwise provided in the [Chapter 13] plan . .\xc2\xa0. the confirmation of a plan vests all of the property of the estate in the debtor.\xe2\x80\x9d Harris\xe2\x80\x99 plan \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise provided\xe2\x80\x9d: It stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]pon confirmation of the plan, all property of the estate shall not vest in the Debto[r], but shall remain as property of the estate.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 31 (emphasis added). That plan language does not change the outcome here. Harris\xe2\x80\x99 wages may have been \xe2\x80\x9cproperty of the estate\xe2\x80\x9d while his case proceeded under Chapter 13, but estate property does not become property of creditors until it is distributed to them. See Michael, 699 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 313. Moreover, the order confirming Harris\xe2\x80\x99 plan provided that upon conversion to Chapter 7, \xe2\x80\x9c[s]uch property as may revest in the debtor shall so revest.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 48. Pursuant to that provision, property formerly in the Chapter 13 estate that did not become part of the Chapter 7 estate revested in Harris; here, Harris\xe2\x80\x99 postpetition wages so revested.

\n

D

\n

The Fifth Circuit expressed concern that debtors would receive a \xe2\x80\x9cwindfall\xe2\x80\x9d if they could reclaim accumulated wages from a terminated Chapter 13 trustee. 757 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 478\xe2\x80\x93481. As explained, however, see supra at 2\xe2\x80\x933, Chapter 13 is a voluntary proceeding in which debtors endeavor to discharge their obligations using postpetition earnings that are off-limits to creditors in a Chapter 7 proceeding. We do not regard as a \xe2\x80\x9cwindfall\xe2\x80\x9d a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s receipt of a fraction of the wages he earned and would have kept had he filed under Chapter 7 in the first place.

\n

We acknowledge the \xe2\x80\x9cfortuit[y],\xe2\x80\x9d as the Fifth Circuit called it, that a \xe2\x80\x9cdebtor\xe2\x80\x99s chance of having funds returned\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cdependent on the trustee\xe2\x80\x99s speed in distributing the payments\xe2\x80\x9d to creditors. 757 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 479, and n. 10. A trustee who distributes payments regularly may have little or no accumulated wages to return. When a trustee distributes payments infrequently, on the other hand, a debtor who converts to Chapter 7 may be entitled to a sizable refund. These outcomes, however, follow directly from Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decisions to shield postpetition wages from creditors in a converted Chapter 7 case, \xc2\xa7348(f)(1)(A), and to give Chapter 13 debtors a right to convert to Chapter 7 \xe2\x80\x9cat any time,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71307(a). Moreover, creditors may gain protection against the risk of excess accumulations in the hands of Chapter 13 trustees by seeking to include in a Chapter 13 plan a schedule for regular disbursement of funds the trustee collects.

\n

For the reasons stated, the judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.
