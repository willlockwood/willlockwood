Syllabus

\n

FRY et vir, as next friends of minor E. F. v. NAPOLEON COMMUNITY SCHOOLS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93497.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 31, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 22, 2017

\n

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) offers federal funds to States in exchange for a commitment to furnish a \xe2\x80\x9cfree appropriate public education\xe2\x80\x9d (FAPE) to children with certain disabilities,\n\n20 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71412(a)(1)(A), and establishes formal administrative procedures for resolving disputes between parents and schools concerning the provision of a FAPE. Other federal statutes also protect the interests of children with disabilities, including Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and \xc2\xa7504 of the Rehabilitation Act. In Smith v. Robinson,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 992\n, this Court considered the interaction between those other laws and the IDEA, holding that the IDEA was \xe2\x80\x9cthe exclusive avenue\xe2\x80\x9d through which a child with a disability could challenge the adequacy of his education. Id., at 1009. Congress responded by passing the Handicapped Children\xe2\x80\x99s Protection Act of 1986, overturning Smith\xe2\x80\x99s preclusion of non-IDEA claims and adding a carefully defined exhaustion provision. Under that provision, a plaintiff bringing suit under the ADA, the Rehabilitation Act, or similar laws \xe2\x80\x9cseeking relief that is also available under [the IDEA]\xe2\x80\x9d must first exhaust the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative procedures. \xc2\xa71415(l).

\n

Petitioner E.\xc2\xa0F. is a child with a severe form of cerebral palsy; a trained service dog named Wonder assists her with various daily life activities. When E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s parents, petitioners Stacy and Brent Fry, sought permission for Wonder to join E.\xc2\xa0F. in kindergarten, officials at Ezra Eby Elementary School refused. The officials reasoned that the human aide provided as part of E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s individualized education program rendered the dog superfluous. In response, the Frys removed E.\xc2\xa0F. from Ezra Eby and began homeschooling her. They also filed a complaint with the Department of Education\xe2\x80\x99s Office for Civil Rights (OCR), claiming that the exclusion of E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s service animal violated her rights under Title II and \xc2\xa7504. OCR agreed, and school officials invited E.\xc2\xa0F. to return to Ezra Eby with Wonder. But the Frys, concerned about resentment from school officials, instead enrolled E.\xc2\xa0F. in a different school that welcomed the service dog. The Frys then filed this suit in federal court against Ezra Eby\xe2\x80\x99s local and regional school districts and principal (collectively, the school districts), alleging that they violated Title II and \xc2\xa7504 and seeking declaratory and monetary relief. The District Court granted the school districts\xe2\x80\x99 motion to dismiss the suit, holding that \xc2\xa71415(l) required the Frys to first exhaust the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative procedures. The Sixth Circuit affirmed, reasoning that \xc2\xa71415(l) applies whenever a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s alleged harms are \xe2\x80\x9ceducational\xe2\x80\x9d in nature.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Exhaustion of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative procedures is unnecessary where the gravamen of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s suit is something other than the denial of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s core guarantee of a FAPE. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The language of \xc2\xa71415(l) compels exhaustion when a plaintiff seeks \xe2\x80\x9crelief\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cavailable\xe2\x80\x9d under the IDEA. Establishing the scope of \xc2\xa71415(l), then, requires identifying the circumstances in which the IDEA enables a person to obtain redress or access a benefit. That inquiry immediately reveals the primacy of a FAPE in the statutory scheme. The IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s stated purpose and specific commands center on ensuring a FAPE for children with disabilities. And the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative procedures test whether a school has met this obligation: Any decision by a hearing officer on a request for substantive relief \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d be \xe2\x80\x9cbased on a determination of whether the child received a free appropriate public education.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71415(f)(3)(E)(i). Accordingly, \xc2\xa71415(l)\xe2\x80\x99s exhaustion rule hinges on whether a lawsuit seeks relief for the denial of a FAPE. If a lawsuit charges such a denial, the plaintiff cannot escape \xc2\xa71415(l) merely by bringing the suit under a statute other than the IDEA. But if the remedy sought in a suit brought under a different statute is not for the denial of a FAPE, then exhaustion of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s procedures is not required. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(b) In determining whether a plaintiff seeks relief for the denial of a FAPE, what matters is the gravamen of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s complaint, setting aside any attempts at artful pleading. That inquiry makes central the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s own claims, as \xc2\xa71415(l) explicitly requires in asking whether a lawsuit in fact \xe2\x80\x9cseeks\xe2\x80\x9d relief available under the IDEA. But examination of a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s complaint should consider substance, not surface: \xc2\xa71415(l) requires exhaustion when the gravamen of a complaint seeks redress for a school\xe2\x80\x99s failure to provide a FAPE, even if not phrased or framed in precisely that way. In addressing whether a complaint fits that description, a court should attend to the diverse means and ends of the statutes covering persons with disabilities. The IDEA guarantees individually tailored educational services for children with disabilities, while Title II and \xc2\xa7504 promise nondiscriminatory access to public institutions for people with disabilities of all ages. That is not to deny some overlap in coverage: The same conduct might violate all three statutes. But still, these statutory differences mean that a complaint brought under Title II and \xc2\xa7504 might instead seek relief for simple discrimination, irrespective of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s FAPE obligation. One clue to the gravamen of a complaint can come from asking a pair of hypothetical questions. First, could the plaintiff have brought essentially the same claim if the alleged conduct had occurred at a public facility that was not a school? Second, could an adult at the school have pressed essentially the same grievance? When the answer to those questions is yes, a complaint that does not expressly allege the denial of a FAPE is also unlikely to be truly about that subject. But when the answer is no, then the complaint probably does concern a FAPE. A further sign of the gravamen of a suit can emerge from the history of the proceedings. Prior pursuit of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative remedies may provide strong evidence that the substance of a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim concerns the denial of a FAPE, even if the complaint never explicitly uses that term. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0This case is remanded to the Court of Appeals for a proper analysis of whether the gravamen of E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s complaint charges, and seeks relief for, the denial of a FAPE. The Frys\xe2\x80\x99 complaint alleges only disability-based discrimination, without making any reference to the adequacy of the special education services E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s school provided. Instead, the Frys have maintained that the school districts infringed E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s right to equal access\xe2\x80\x94even if their actions complied in full with the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s requirements. But the possibility remains that the history of these proceedings might suggest something different. The parties have not addressed whether the Frys initially pursued the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative remedies, and the record is cloudy as to the relevant facts. On remand, the court below should establish whether (or to what extent) the Frys invoked the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s dispute resolution process before filing suit. And if the Frys started down that road, the court should decide whether their actions reveal that the gravamen of their complaint is indeed the denial of a FAPE, thus necessitating further exhaustion. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

788 F.\xc2\xa03d 622, vacated and remanded.

STACY FRY, et vir, as next friends of minor E. F., PETITIONERS v. NAPOLEON COMMUNITYSCHOOLS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[February 22, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA or Act),\n\n84Stat.\n175, as amended,\n\n20 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71400 et seq., ensures that children with disabilities receive needed special education services. One of its provisions, \xc2\xa71415(l), addresses the Act\xe2\x80\x99s relationship with other laws protecting those children. Section 1415(l) makes clear that nothing in the IDEA \xe2\x80\x9crestrict[s] or limit[s] the rights [or] remedies\xe2\x80\x9d that other federal laws, including antidiscrimination statutes, confer on children with disabilities. At the same time, the section states that if a suit brought under such a law \xe2\x80\x9cseek[s] relief that is also available under\xe2\x80\x9d the IDEA, the plaintiff must first exhaust the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative procedures. In this case, we consider the scope of that exhaustion requirement. We hold that exhaustion is not necessary when the gravamen of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s suit is something other than the denial of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s core guarantee\xe2\x80\x94what the Act calls a \xe2\x80\x9cfree appropriate public education.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71412(a)(1)(A).

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The IDEA offers federal funds to States in exchange for a commitment: to furnish a \xe2\x80\x9cfree appropriate public education\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94more concisely known as a FAPE\xe2\x80\x94to all children with certain physical or intellectual disabilities. Ibid.; see \xc2\xa71401(3)(A)(i) (listing covered disabilities). As defined in the Act, a FAPE comprises \xe2\x80\x9cspecial education and related services\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94both \xe2\x80\x9cinstruction\xe2\x80\x9d tailored to meet a child\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cunique needs\xe2\x80\x9d and sufficient \xe2\x80\x9csupportive services\xe2\x80\x9d to permit the child to benefit from that instruction. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71401(9), (26), (29); see Board of Ed. of Hendrick Hudson Central School Dist., Westchester Cty. v. Rowley,\n\n458 U.\xc2\xa0S. 176,\n203 (1982)\n. An eligible child, as this Court has explained, acquires a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive right\xe2\x80\x9d to such an education once a State accepts the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s financial assistance. Smith v. Robinson,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 992,\n1010 (1984)\n.

\n

Under the IDEA, an \xe2\x80\x9cindividualized education program,\xe2\x80\x9d called an IEP for short, serves as the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary vehicle\xe2\x80\x9d for providing each child with the promised FAPE. Honig v. Doe,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 305,\n311 (1988)\n; see \xc2\xa71414(d). (Welcome to\xe2\x80\x94and apologies for\xe2\x80\x94the acronymic world of federal legislation.) Crafted by a child\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cIEP Team\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a group of school officials, teachers, and parents\xe2\x80\x94the IEP spells out a personalized plan to meet all of the child\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ceducational needs.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71414(d)(1)(A)(i)(II)(bb), (d)(1)(B). Most notably, the IEP documents the child\xe2\x80\x99s current \xe2\x80\x9clevels of academic achievement,\xe2\x80\x9d specifies \xe2\x80\x9cmeasurable an-nual goals\xe2\x80\x9d for how she can \xe2\x80\x9cmake progress in the general education curriculum,\xe2\x80\x9d and lists the \xe2\x80\x9cspecial education and related services\xe2\x80\x9d to be provided so that she can \xe2\x80\x9cadvance appropriately toward [those] goals.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71414(d)(1)(A)(i)(I), (II), (IV)(aa).

\n

Because parents and school representatives sometimes cannot agree on such issues, the IDEA establishes formal procedures for resolving disputes. To begin, a dissatisfied parent may file a complaint as to any matter concerning the provision of a FAPE with the local or state educational agency (as state law provides). See \xc2\xa71415(b)(6). That pleading generally triggers a \xe2\x80\x9c[p]reliminary meeting\xe2\x80\x9d involving the contending parties, \xc2\xa71415(f\xc2\xa0)(1)(B)(i); at their option, the parties may instead (or also) pursue a full-fledged mediation process, see \xc2\xa71415(e). Assuming their impasse continues, the matter proceeds to a \xe2\x80\x9cdue pro-cess hearing\xe2\x80\x9d before an impartial hearing officer. \xc2\xa71415(f\xc2\xa0)(1)(A); see \xc2\xa71415(f\xc2\xa0)(3)(A)(i). Any decision of the officer granting substantive relief must be \xe2\x80\x9cbased on a determination of whether the child received a [FAPE].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71415(f\xc2\xa0)(3)(E)(i). If the hearing is initially conducted at the local level, the ruling is appealable to the state agency. See \xc2\xa71415(g). Finally, a parent unhappy with the outcome of the administrative process may seek judicial review by filing a civil action in state or federal court. See \xc2\xa71415(i)(2)(A).

\n

Important as the IDEA is for children with disabilities, it is not the only federal statute protecting their interests. Of particular relevance to this case are two antidiscrimination laws\xe2\x80\x94Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA),\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa712131 et\xc2\xa0seq., and \xc2\xa7504 of the Rehabilitation Act,\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7794\xe2\x80\x94which cover both adults and children with disabilities, in both public schools and other settings. Title II forbids any \xe2\x80\x9cpublic entity\xe2\x80\x9d from discriminating based on disability; Section 504 applies the same prohibition to any federally funded \xe2\x80\x9cprogram or activity.\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa712131\xe2\x80\x9312132;\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7794(a). A regulation implementing Title II requires a public entity to make \xe2\x80\x9creasonable modifications\xe2\x80\x9d to its \xe2\x80\x9cpolicies, practices, or procedures\xe2\x80\x9d when necessary to avoid such discrimination. 28 CFR \xc2\xa735.130(b)(7) (2016); see, e.g., Alboniga v. School Bd. of Broward Cty., 87 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d 1319, 1345 (SD Fla. 2015) (requiring an accommodation to permit use of a service animal under Title II). In similar vein, courts have interpreted \xc2\xa7504 as demanding certain \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d modifications to existing practices in order to \xe2\x80\x9caccommodate\xe2\x80\x9d persons with disabilities. Alexander v. Choate,\n\n469 U.\xc2\xa0S. 287\n\xe2\x80\x93300 (1985); see, e.g., Sullivan v. Vallejo City Unified School Dist., 731 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 947, 961\xe2\x80\x93962 (ED Cal. 1990) (requiring an accommodation to permit use of a service animal under \xc2\xa7504). And both statutes authorize individuals to seek redress for violations of their substantive guarantees by bringing suits for injunctive relief or money damages. See\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7794a(a)(2);\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa712133.

\n

This Court first considered the interaction between such laws and the IDEA in Smith v. Robinson,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 992\n.[1] The plaintiffs there sought \xe2\x80\x9cto secure a \xe2\x80\x98free appropriate public education\xe2\x80\x99 for [their] handicapped child.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 994. But instead of bringing suit under the IDEA alone, they appended \xe2\x80\x9cvirtually identical\xe2\x80\x9d claims (again alleging the denial of a \xe2\x80\x9cfree appropriate public education\xe2\x80\x9d) under \xc2\xa7504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the\n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Equal Protection Clause. Id., at 1009; see id., at 1016. The Court held that the IDEA altogether foreclosed those additional claims: With its \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccarefully tailored\xe2\x80\x9d provisions, the Act was \xe2\x80\x9cthe exclusive avenue\xe2\x80\x9d through which a child with a disability (or his parents) could challenge the adequacy of his education. Id., at 1009; see id., at 1013, 1016, 1021.

\n

Congress was quick to respond. In the Handicapped Children\xe2\x80\x99s Protection Act of 1986,\n\n100Stat.\n796, it overturned Smith\xe2\x80\x99s preclusion of non-IDEA claims while also adding a carefully defined exhaustion requirement. Now codified at\n\n20 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71415(l), the relevant provision of that statute reads:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cNothing in [the IDEA] shall be construed to restrict or limit the rights, procedures, and remedies available under the Constitution, the [ADA], title V of the Rehabilitation Act [including \xc2\xa7504], or other Federal laws protecting the rights of children with disabilities, except that before the filing of a civil action under such laws seeking relief that is also available under [the IDEA], the [IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative procedures] shall be exhausted to the same extent as would be required had the action been brought under [the IDEA].\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The first half of \xc2\xa71415(l) (up until \xe2\x80\x9cexcept that\xe2\x80\x9d) \xe2\x80\x9creaffirm[s] the viability\xe2\x80\x9d of federal statutes like the ADA or Rehabilitation Act \xe2\x80\x9cas separate vehicles,\xe2\x80\x9d no less integral than the IDEA, \xe2\x80\x9cfor ensuring the rights of handicapped children.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 99\xe2\x80\x93296, p. 4 (1985); see id., at 6. According to that opening phrase, the IDEA does not prevent a plaintiff from asserting claims under such laws even if, as in Smith itself, those claims allege the denial of an appropriate public education (much as an IDEA claim would). But the second half of \xc2\xa71415(l) (from \xe2\x80\x9cexcept that\xe2\x80\x9d onward) imposes a limit on that \xe2\x80\x9canything goes\xe2\x80\x9d regime, in the form of an exhaustion provision. According to that closing phrase, a plaintiff bringing suit under the ADA, the Rehabilitation Act, or similar laws must in certain circumstances\xe2\x80\x94that is, when \xe2\x80\x9cseeking relief that is also available under\xe2\x80\x9d the IDEA\xe2\x80\x94first exhaust the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative procedures. The reach of that requirement is the issue in this case.

\n

B

\n

Petitioner E.\xc2\xa0F. is a child with a severe form of cerebral palsy, which \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly limits her motor skills and mobility.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Brief in Opposition 6, Complaint \xc2\xb619.[2] When E.\xc2\xa0F. was five years old, her parents\xe2\x80\x94petitioners Stacy and Brent Fry\xe2\x80\x94obtained a trained service dog for her, as recommended by her pediatrician. The dog, a goldendoodle named Wonder, \xe2\x80\x9chelp[s E.\xc2\xa0F.] to live as independently as possible\xe2\x80\x9d by assisting her with various life activities. Id., at 2, \xc2\xb63. In particular, Wonder aids E.\xc2\xa0F. by \xe2\x80\x9cretrieving dropped items, helping her balance when she uses her walker, opening and closing doors, turning on and off lights, helping her take off her coat, [and] helping her transfer to and from the toilet.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7, \xc2\xb627.

\n

But when the Frys sought permission for Wonder to join E.\xc2\xa0F. in kindergarten, officials at Ezra Eby Elementary School refused the request. Under E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s existing IEP, a human aide provided E.\xc2\xa0F. with one-on-one support throughout the day; that two-legged assistance, the school officials thought, rendered Wonder superfluous. In the words of one administrator, Wonder should be barred from Ezra Eby because all of E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cphysical and academic needs [were] being met through the services/programs/accommodations\xe2\x80\x9d that the school had already agreed to. Id., at 8, \xc2\xb633. Later that year, the school officials briefly allowed Wonder to accompany E.\xc2\xa0F. to school on a trial basis; but even then, \xe2\x80\x9cthe dog was required to remain in the back of the room during classes, and was forbidden from assisting [E.\xc2\xa0F.] with many tasks he had been specifically trained to do.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid., \xc2\xb635. And when the trial period concluded, the administrators again informed the Frys that Wonder was not welcome. As a result, the Frys removed E.\xc2\xa0F. from Ezra Eby and began homeschooling her.

\n

In addition, the Frys filed a complaint with the U.\xc2\xa0S. Department of Education\xe2\x80\x99s Office for Civil Rights (OCR), charging that Ezra Eby\xe2\x80\x99s exclusion of E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s service animal violated her rights under Title II of the ADA and \xc2\xa7504 of the Rehabilitation Act. Following an investigation, OCR agreed. The office explained in its decision letter that a school\xe2\x80\x99s obligations under those statutes go beyond providing educational services: A school could offer a FAPE to a child with a disability but still run afoul of the laws\xe2\x80\x99 ban on discrimination. See App. 30\xe2\x80\x9332. And here, OCR found, Ezra Eby had indeed violated that ban, even if its use of a human aide satisfied the FAPE standard. See id., at 35\xe2\x80\x9336. OCR analogized the school\xe2\x80\x99s conduct to \xe2\x80\x9crequir[ing] a student who uses a wheelchair to be carried\xe2\x80\x9d by an aide or \xe2\x80\x9crequir[ing] a blind student to be led [around by a] teacher\xe2\x80\x9d instead of permitting him to use a guide dog or cane. Id., at 35. Regardless whether those\xe2\x80\x94or Ezra Eby\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x94policies denied a FAPE, they violated Title II and \xc2\xa7504 by discriminating against children with disabilities. See id., at 35\xe2\x80\x9336.

\n

In response to OCR\xe2\x80\x99s decision, school officials at last agreed that E.\xc2\xa0F. could come to school with Wonder. But after meeting with Ezra Eby\xe2\x80\x99s principal, the Frys became concerned that the school administration \xe2\x80\x9cwould resent [E.\xc2\xa0F.] and make her return to school difficult.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Brief in Opposition 10, \xc2\xb648. Accordingly, the Frys found a different public school, in a different district, where administrators and teachers enthusiastically received both E.\xc2\xa0F. and Wonder.

\n

C

\n

The Frys then filed this suit in federal court against the local and regional school districts in which Ezra Eby is located, along with the school\xe2\x80\x99s principal (collectively, the school districts). The complaint alleged that the school districts violated Title II of the ADA and \xc2\xa7504 of the Rehabilitation Act by \xe2\x80\x9cdenying [E.\xc2\xa0F.] equal access\xe2\x80\x9d to Ezra Eby and its programs, \xe2\x80\x9crefus[ing] to reasonably accommodate\xe2\x80\x9d E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s use of a service animal, and otherwise \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[ing] against [E.\xc2\xa0F.] as a person with disabilities.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 15, \xc2\xb668, 17\xe2\x80\x9318, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb682\xe2\x80\x9383. According to the complaint, E.\xc2\xa0F. suffered harm as a result of that discrimination, including \xe2\x80\x9cemotional distress and pain, embarrassment, [and] mental anguish.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312, \xc2\xb651. In their prayer for relief, the Frys sought a declaration that the school districts had violated Title II and \xc2\xa7504, along with money damages to compensate for E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s injuries.

\n

The District Court granted the school districts\xe2\x80\x99 motion to dismiss the suit, holding that \xc2\xa71415(l) required the Frys to first exhaust the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative procedures. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 50. A divided panel of the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit affirmed on the same ground. In that court\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xc2\xa71415(l) applies if \xe2\x80\x9cthe injuries [alleged in a suit] relate to the specific substantive protections of the IDEA.\xe2\x80\x9d 788 F.\xc2\xa03d 622, 625 (2015). And that means, the court continued, that exhaustion is necessary whenever \xe2\x80\x9cthe genesis and the manifestations\xe2\x80\x9d of the complained-of harms were \xe2\x80\x9ceducational\xe2\x80\x9d in nature. Id., at 627 (quoting Charlie F. v. Board of Ed. of Skokie School Dist. 68, 98 F.\xc2\xa03d 989, 993 (CA7 1996)). On that understanding of \xc2\xa71415(l), the Sixth Circuit held, the Frys\xe2\x80\x99 suit could not proceed: Because the harms to E.\xc2\xa0F. were generally \xe2\x80\x9ceducational\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94most notably, the court reasoned, because \xe2\x80\x9cWonder\xe2\x80\x99s absence hurt her sense of independence and social confidence at school\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the Frys had to exhaust the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s procedures. 788 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 627. Judge Daugh-trey dissented, emphasizing that in bringing their Title II and \xc2\xa7504 claims, the Frys \xe2\x80\x9cdid not allege the denial of a FAPE\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cseek to modify [E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s] IEP in any way.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 634.

\n

We granted certiorari to address confusion in the courts of appeals as to the scope of \xc2\xa71415(l)\xe2\x80\x99s exhaustion requirement. 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016).[3] We now vacate the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision.

\n

II

\n

Section 1415(l) requires that a plaintiff exhaust the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s procedures before filing an action under the ADA, the Rehabilitation Act, or similar laws when (but only when) her suit \xe2\x80\x9cseek[s] relief that is also available\xe2\x80\x9d under the IDEA. We first hold that to meet that statutory standard, a suit must seek relief for the denial of a FAPE, because that is the only \xe2\x80\x9crelief\xe2\x80\x9d the IDEA makes \xe2\x80\x9cavail-able.\xe2\x80\x9d We next conclude that in determining whether a suit indeed \xe2\x80\x9cseeks\xe2\x80\x9d relief for such a denial, a court should look to the substance, or gravamen, of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s complaint.[4]

\n

A

\n

In this Court, the parties have reached substantial agreement about what \xe2\x80\x9crelief\xe2\x80\x9d the IDEA makes \xe2\x80\x9cavail-able\xe2\x80\x9d for children with disabilities\xe2\x80\x94and about how the Sixth Circuit went wrong in addressing that question. The Frys maintain that such a child can obtain remedies under the IDEA for decisions that deprive her of a FAPE, but none for those that do not. So in the Frys\xe2\x80\x99 view, \xc2\xa71415(l)\xe2\x80\x99s exhaustion requirement can come into play only when a suit concerns the denial of a FAPE\xe2\x80\x94and not, as the Sixth Circuit held, when it merely has some articulable connection to the education of a child with a disability. See Reply Brief 13\xe2\x80\x9315. The school districts, for their part, also believe that the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s exhaustion standard \xe2\x80\x9cgoes too far\xe2\x80\x9d because it could mandate exhaustion when a plaintiff is \xe2\x80\x9cseeking relief that is not in substance avail-able\xe2\x80\x9d under the IDEA. Brief for Respondents 30. And in particular, the school districts acknowledge that the IDEA makes remedies available only in suits that \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly implicate[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d a FAPE\xe2\x80\x94so that only in those suits can \xc2\xa71415(l) apply. Tr. of Oral Arg. 46. For the reasons that follow, we agree with the parties\xe2\x80\x99 shared view: The only relief that an IDEA officer can give\xe2\x80\x94hence the thing a plaintiff must seek in order to trigger \xc2\xa71415(l)\xe2\x80\x99s exhaustion rule\xe2\x80\x94is relief for the denial of a FAPE.

\n

We begin, as always, with the statutory language at issue, which (at risk of repetition) compels exhaustion when a plaintiff seeks \xe2\x80\x9crelief\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cavailable\xe2\x80\x9d under the IDEA. The ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9crelief\xe2\x80\x9d in the context of a lawsuit is the \xe2\x80\x9credress[\xc2\xa0] or benefit\xe2\x80\x9d that attends a favor-able judgment. Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1161 (5th ed. 1979). And such relief is \xe2\x80\x9cavailable,\xe2\x80\x9d as we recently explained, when it is \xe2\x80\x9caccessible or may be obtained.\xe2\x80\x9d Ross v. Blake, 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 8) (quoting Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 150 (1993)). So to establish the scope of \xc2\xa71415(l), we must identify the circumstances in which the IDEA enables a person to obtain redress (or, similarly, to access a benefit).

\n

That inquiry immediately reveals the primacy of a FAPE in the statutory scheme. In its first section, the IDEA declares as its first purpose \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure that all children with disabilities have available to them a free appropriate public education.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71400(d)(1)(A). That principal purpose then becomes the Act\xe2\x80\x99s principal command: A State receiving federal funding under the IDEA must make such an education \xe2\x80\x9cavailable to all children with disabilities.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71412(a)(1)(A). The guarantee of a FAPE to those children gives rise to the bulk of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s more specific provisions. For example, the IEP\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthe centerpiece of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s education delivery system\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94serves as the \xe2\x80\x9cvehicle\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmeans\xe2\x80\x9d of providing a FAPE. Honig, 484 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 311; Rowley, 458 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 181; see supra, at 2. And finally, as all the above suggests, the FAPE requirement provides the yardstick for measuring the adequacy of the education that a school offers to a child with a disability: Under that standard, this Court has held, a child is entitled to \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningful\xe2\x80\x9d access to education based on her individual needs. Rowley, 458 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 192.[5]

\n

The IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative procedures test whether a school has met that obligation\xe2\x80\x94and so center on the Act\xe2\x80\x99s FAPE requirement. As noted earlier, any decision by a hearing officer on a request for substantive relief \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d be \xe2\x80\x9cbased on a determination of whether the child received a free appropriate public education.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71415(f\xc2\xa0)(3)(E)(i); see supra, at 3.[6] Or said in Latin: In the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative process, a FAPE denial is the sine qua non. Suppose that a parent\xe2\x80\x99s complaint protests a school\xe2\x80\x99s failure to provide some accommodation for a child with a disability. If that accommodation is needed to fulfill the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s FAPE requirement, the hearing officer must order relief. But if it is not, he cannot\xe2\x80\x94even though the dispute is between a child with a disability and the school she attends. There might be good reasons, unrelated to a FAPE, for the school to make the requested accommodation. Indeed, another federal law (like the ADA or Rehabilitation Act) might require the accommodation on one of those alternative grounds. See infra, at 15. But still, the hearing officer cannot provide the requested relief. His role, under the IDEA, is to enforce the child\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive right\xe2\x80\x9d to a FAPE. Smith, 468 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 1010. And that is all.[7]

\n

For that reason, \xc2\xa71415(l)\xe2\x80\x99s exhaustion rule hinges on whether a lawsuit seeks relief for the denial of a free appropriate public education. If a lawsuit charges such a denial, the plaintiff cannot escape \xc2\xa71415(l) merely by bringing her suit under a statute other than the IDEA\xe2\x80\x94as when, for example, the plaintiffs in Smith claimed that a school\xe2\x80\x99s failure to provide a FAPE also violated the Rehabilitation Act.[8] Rather, that plaintiff must first submit her case to an IDEA hearing officer, experienced in addressing exactly the issues she raises. But if, in a suit brought under a different statute, the remedy sought is not for the denial of a FAPE, then exhaustion of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s procedures is not required. After all, the plaintiff could not get any relief from those procedures: A hearing officer, as just explained, would have to send her away empty-handed. And that is true even when the suit arises directly from a school\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of a child with a disability\xe2\x80\x94and so could be said to relate in some way to her education. A school\xe2\x80\x99s conduct toward such a child\xe2\x80\x94say, some refusal to make an accommodation\xe2\x80\x94might injure her in ways unrelated to a FAPE, which are addressed in statutes other than the IDEA. A complaint seeking redress for those other harms, independent of any FAPE denial, is not subject to \xc2\xa71415(l)\xe2\x80\x99s exhaustion rule because, once again, the only \xe2\x80\x9crelief\xe2\x80\x9d the IDEA makes \xe2\x80\x9cavailable\xe2\x80\x9d is relief for the denial of a FAPE.

\n

B

\n

Still, an important question remains: How is a court to tell when a plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cseeks\xe2\x80\x9d relief for the denial of a FAPE and when she does not? Here, too, the parties have found some common ground: By looking, they both say, to the \xe2\x80\x9csubstance\xe2\x80\x9d of, rather than the labels used in, the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s complaint. Brief for Respondents 20; Reply Brief 7\xe2\x80\x938. And here, too, we agree with that view: What matters is the crux\xe2\x80\x94or, in legal-speak, the gravamen\xe2\x80\x94of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s complaint, setting aside any attempts at artful pleading.

\n

That inquiry makes central the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s own claims, as \xc2\xa71415(l) explicitly requires. The statutory language asks whether a lawsuit in fact \xe2\x80\x9cseeks\xe2\x80\x9d relief available under the IDEA\xe2\x80\x94not, as a stricter exhaustion statute might, whether the suit \xe2\x80\x9ccould have sought\xe2\x80\x9d relief avail-able under the IDEA (or, what is much the same, whether any remedies \xe2\x80\x9care\xe2\x80\x9d available under that law). See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 20 (contrasting \xc2\xa71415(l) with the exhaustion provision in the Prison Litigation Reform Act,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71997e(a)). In effect, \xc2\xa71415(l) treats the plaintiff as \xe2\x80\x9cthe master of the claim\xe2\x80\x9d: She identifies its remedial basis\xe2\x80\x94and is subject to exhaustion or not based on that choice. Caterpillar Inc. v. Williams,\n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 386\n, and n.\xc2\xa07 (1987). A court deciding whether \xc2\xa71415(l) applies must therefore examine whether a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s complaint\xe2\x80\x94the principal instrument by which she describes her case\xe2\x80\x94seeks relief for the de-nial of an appropriate education.

\n

But that examination should consider substance, not surface. The use (or non-use) of particular labels and terms is not what matters. The inquiry, for example, does not ride on whether a complaint includes (or, alternatively, omits) the precise words(?) \xe2\x80\x9cFAPE\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cIEP.\xe2\x80\x9d After all, \xc2\xa71415(l)\xe2\x80\x99s premise is that the plaintiff is suing under a statute other than the IDEA, like the Rehabilitation Act; in such a suit, the plaintiff might see no need to use the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s distinctive language\xe2\x80\x94even if she is in essence contesting the adequacy of a special education program. And still more critically, a \xe2\x80\x9cmagic words\xe2\x80\x9d approach would make \xc2\xa71415(l)\xe2\x80\x99s exhaustion rule too easy to bypass. Just last Term, a similar worry led us to hold that a court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction under the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act turns on the \xe2\x80\x9cgravamen,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cessentials,\xe2\x80\x9d of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s suit. OBB Personenverkehr AG v. Sachs, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 6, 8, 9). \xe2\x80\x9c[A]ny other approach,\xe2\x80\x9d we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cwould allow plaintiffs to evade the Act\xe2\x80\x99s restrictions through artful pleading.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 8). So too here. Section 1415(l) is not merely a pleading hurdle. It requires exhaustion when the gravamen of a complaint seeks redress for a school\xe2\x80\x99s failure to provide a FAPE, even if not phrased or framed in precisely that way.

\n

In addressing whether a complaint fits that description, a court should attend to the diverse means and ends of the statutes covering persons with disabilities\xe2\x80\x94the IDEA on the one hand, the ADA and Rehabilitation Act (most notably) on the other. The IDEA, of course, protects only \xe2\x80\x9cchildren\xe2\x80\x9d (well, really, adolescents too) and concerns only their schooling. \xc2\xa71412(a)(1)(A). And as earlier noted, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s goal is to provide each child with meaningful access to education by offering individualized instruction and related services appropriate to her \xe2\x80\x9cunique needs.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71401(29); see Rowley, 458 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 192, 198; supra, at 11. By contrast, Title II of the ADA and \xc2\xa7504 of the Rehabilitation Act cover people with disabilities of all ages, and do so both inside and outside schools. And those statutes aim to root out disability-based discrimination, enabling each covered person (sometimes by means of reasonable accommodations) to participate equally to all others in public facilities and federally funded programs. See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. In short, the IDEA guarantees individually tailored educational services, while Title II and \xc2\xa7504 promise non-discriminatory access to public institutions. That is not to deny some overlap in coverage: The same conduct might violate all three statutes\xe2\x80\x94which is why, as in Smith, a plaintiff might seek relief for the denial of a FAPE under Title II and \xc2\xa7504 as well as the IDEA. But still, the statutory differences just discussed mean that a complaint brought under Title II and \xc2\xa7504 might instead seek relief for simple discrimination, irrespective of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s FAPE obligation.

\n

One clue to whether the gravamen of a complaint against a school concerns the denial of a FAPE, or instead addresses disability-based discrimination, can come from asking a pair of hypothetical questions. First, could the plaintiff have brought essentially the same claim if the alleged conduct had occurred at a public facility that was not a school\xe2\x80\x94say, a public theater or library? And second, could an adult at the school\xe2\x80\x94say, an employee or visitor\xe2\x80\x94have pressed essentially the same grievance? When the answer to those questions is yes, a complaint that does not expressly allege the denial of a FAPE is also unlikely to be truly about that subject; after all, in those other situations there is no FAPE obligation and yet the same basic suit could go forward. But when the answer is no, then the complaint probably does concern a FAPE, even if it does not explicitly say so; for the FAPE requirement is all that explains why only a child in the school setting (not an adult in that setting or a child in some other) has a viable claim.

\n

Take two contrasting examples. Suppose first that a wheelchair-bound child sues his school for discrimination under Title II (again, without mentioning the denial of a FAPE) because the building lacks access ramps. In some sense, that architectural feature has educational consequences, and a different lawsuit might have alleged that it violates the IDEA: After all, if the child cannot get inside the school, he cannot receive instruction there; and if he must be carried inside, he may not achieve the sense of independence conducive to academic (or later to real-world) success. But is the denial of a FAPE really the gravamen of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s Title II complaint? Consider that the child could file the same basic complaint if a municipal library or theater had no ramps. And similarly, an employee or visitor could bring a mostly identical complaint against the school. That the claim can stay the same in those alternative scenarios suggests that its essence is equality of access to public facilities, not adequacy of special education. See supra, at 7 (describing OCR\xe2\x80\x99s use of a similar example). And so \xc2\xa71415(l) does not require exhaustion.[9]

\n

But suppose next that a student with a learning disability sues his school under Title II for failing to provide remedial tutoring in mathematics. That suit, too, might be cast as one for disability-based discrimination, grounded on the school\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to make a reasonable accommo-dation; the complaint might make no reference at all to a FAPE or an IEP. But can anyone imagine the student making the same claim against a public theater or library? Or, similarly, imagine an adult visitor or employee suing the school to obtain a math tutorial? The difficulty of transplanting the complaint to those other contexts suggests that its essence\xe2\x80\x94even though not its wording\xe2\x80\x94is the provision of a FAPE, thus bringing \xc2\xa71415(l\xc2\xa0) into play.[10]

\n

A further sign that the gravamen of a suit is the denial of a FAPE can emerge from the history of the proceedings. In particular, a court may consider that a plaintiff has previously invoked the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s formal procedures to handle the dispute\xe2\x80\x94thus starting to exhaust the Act\xe2\x80\x99s remedies before switching midstream. Recall that a parent dissatisfied with her child\xe2\x80\x99s education initiates those administrative procedures by filing a complaint, which triggers a preliminary meeting (or possibly mediation) and then a due process hearing. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. A plain-tiff\xe2\x80\x99s initial choice to pursue that process may suggest that she is indeed seeking relief for the denial of a FAPE\xe2\x80\x94with the shift to judicial proceedings prior to full exhaustion reflecting only strategic calculations about how to maximize the prospects of such a remedy. Whether that is so depends on the facts; a court may conclude, for example, that the move to a courtroom came from a late-acquired awareness that the school had fulfilled its FAPE obligation and that the grievance involves something else entirely. But prior pursuit of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative reme-dies will often provide strong evidence that the substance of a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim concerns the denial of a FAPE, even if the complaint never explicitly uses that term.[11]

\n

III

\n

The Court of Appeals did not undertake the analysis we have just set forward. As noted above, it asked whether E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s injuries were, broadly speaking, \xe2\x80\x9ceducational\xe2\x80\x9d in nature. See supra, at 8; 788 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 627 (reasoning that the \xe2\x80\x9cvalue of allowing Wonder to attend [school] with E.\xc2\xa0F. was educational\xe2\x80\x9d because it would foster \xe2\x80\x9cher sense of independence and social confidence,\xe2\x80\x9d which is \xe2\x80\x9cthe sort of interest the IDEA protects\xe2\x80\x9d). That is not the same as asking whether the gravamen of E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s complaint charges, and seeks relief for, the denial of a FAPE. And that difference in standard may have led to a difference in result in this case. Understood correctly, \xc2\xa71415(l) might not require exhaustion of the Frys\xe2\x80\x99 claim. We lack some important information on that score, however, and so we remand the issue to the court below.

\n

The Frys\xe2\x80\x99 complaint alleges only disability-based discrimination, without making any reference to the ade-quacy of the special education services E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s school provided. The school districts\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9crefusal to allow Wonder to act as a service dog,\xe2\x80\x9d the complaint states, \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminated against [E.\xc2\xa0F.] as a person with disabilities .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by denying her equal access\xe2\x80\x9d to public facilities. App. to Brief in Opposition 15, Complaint \xc2\xb668. The complaint contains no allegation about the denial of a FAPE or about any deficiency in E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s IEP. More, it does not accuse the school even in general terms of refusing to provide the educational instruction and services that E.\xc2\xa0F. needs. See 788 F. 3d, at 631 (acknowledging that the Frys do not \xe2\x80\x9cstate that Wonder enhances E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s educational opportunities\xe2\x80\x9d). As the Frys explained in this Court: The school districts \xe2\x80\x9chave said all along that because they gave [E.\xc2\xa0F.] a one-on-one [human] aide, that all of her .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. educational needs were satisfied. And we have not challenged that, and it would be difficult for us to challenge that.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 16. The Frys instead maintained, just as OCR had earlier found, that the school districts infringed E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s right to equal access\xe2\x80\x94even if their actions complied in full with the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s requirements. See App. to Brief in Opposition 15, 18\xe2\x80\x9319, Complaint \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb6 69, 85, 87; App. 34\xe2\x80\x9337; supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

And nothing in the nature of the Frys\xe2\x80\x99 suit suggests any implicit focus on the adequacy of E.\xc2\xa0F.\xe2\x80\x99s education. Consider, as suggested above, that the Frys could have filed essentially the same complaint if a public library or theater had refused admittance to Wonder. See supra, at 16. Or similarly, consider that an adult visitor to the school could have leveled much the same charges if prevented from entering with his service dog. See ibid. In each case, the plaintiff would challenge a public facility\xe2\x80\x99s policy of precluding service dogs (just as a blind person might challenge a policy of barring guide dogs, see supra, at 7) as violating Title II\xe2\x80\x99s and \xc2\xa7504\xe2\x80\x99s equal access requirements. The suit would have nothing to do with the provision of educational services. From all that we know now, that is exactly the kind of action the Frys have brought.

\n

But we do not foreclose the possibility that the history of these proceedings might suggest something different. As earlier discussed, a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s initial pursuit of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s administrative remedies can serve as evidence that the gravamen of her later suit is the denial of a FAPE, even though that does not appear on the face of her complaint. See supra, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. The Frys may or may not have sought those remedies before filing this case: None of the parties here have addressed that issue, and the record is cloudy as to the relevant facts. Accordingly, on remand, the court below should establish whether (or to what extent) the Frys invoked the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s dispute resolution process before bringing this suit. And if the Frys started down that road, the court should decide whether their actions reveal that the gravamen of their complaint is indeed the denial of a FAPE, thus necessitating further exhaustion.

\n

With these instructions and for the reasons stated,we vacate the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n\n4\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0In reaching these conclusions, we leave for another day a further question about the meaning of \xc2\xa71415(l): Is exhaustion required when the plaintiff complains of the denial of a FAPE, but the specific remedy she requests\xe2\x80\x94here, money damages for emotional distress\xe2\x80\x94is not one that an IDEA hearing officer may award? The Frys, along with the Solicitor General, say the answer is no. See Reply Brief 2\xe2\x80\x933; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 16. But resolution of that question might not be needed in this case because the Frys also say that their complaint is not about the denial of a FAPE, see Reply Brief 17\xe2\x80\x94and, as later explained, we must remand that distinct issue to the Sixth Circuit, see infra, at 18\xe2\x80\x9320. Only if that court rejects the Frys\xe2\x80\x99 view of their lawsuit, using the analysis we set out below, will the question about the effect of their request for money damages arise.\n\t\n
\n\n5\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0A case now before this Court, Endrew F. v. Douglas County School Dist. RE\xe2\x80\x931, No. 15\xe2\x80\x93827, presents unresolved questions about the precise content of the FAPE standard.\n\t\n
\n\n6\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Without finding the denial of a FAPE, a hearing officer may do nothing more than order a school district to comply with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s various procedural requirements, see \xc2\xa71415(f\xc2\xa0)(3)(E)(iii)\xe2\x80\x94for example, by allowing parents to \xe2\x80\x9cexamine all records\xe2\x80\x9d relating to their child, \xc2\xa71415(b)(1).\n\t\n
\n\n7\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Similarly, a court in IDEA litigation may provide a substantive remedy only when it determines that a school has denied a FAPE. See School Comm. of Burlington v. Department of Ed. of Mass.,\n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 359,\n369 (1985)\n. Without such a finding, that kind of relief is (once again) unavailable under the Act.\n\t\n
\n\n8\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Once again, we do not address here (or anywhere else in this opinion) a case in which a plaintiff, although charging the denial of a FAPE, seeks a form of remedy that an IDEA officer cannot give\xe2\x80\x94for example, as in the Frys\xe2\x80\x99 complaint, money damages for resulting emotional injury. See n.\xc2\xa04, supra.\n\t\n
\n\n9\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0The school districts offer another example illustrating the point. They suppose that a teacher, acting out of animus or frustration, strikes a student with a disability, who then sues the school under a statute other than the IDEA. See Brief for Respondents 36\xe2\x80\x9337. Here too, the suit could be said to relate, in both genesis and effect, to the child\xe2\x80\x99s education. But the school districts opine, we think correctly, that the substance of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim is unlikely to involve the adequacy of special education\xe2\x80\x94and thus is unlikely to require exhaustion. See ibid. A telling indicator of that conclusion is that a child could file the same kind of suit against an official at another public facility for inflicting such physical abuse\xe2\x80\x94as could an adult subject to similar treatment by a school official. To be sure, the particular circumstances of such a suit (school or theater? student or employee?) might be pertinent in assessing the reasonableness of the challenged conduct. But even if that is so, the plausibility of bringing other variants of the suit indicates that the gravamen of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s complaint does not concern the appropriateness of an educational program.\n\t\n
\n\n10\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0According to Justice Alito, the hypothetical inquiries described above are useful only if the IDEA and other federal laws are mutually exclusive in scope. See post, at 1 (opinion concurring in part and concurring in judgment). That is incorrect. The point of the questions is not to show that a plaintiff faced with a particular set of circumstances could only have proceeded under Title II or \xc2\xa7504\xe2\x80\x94or, alternatively, could only have proceeded under the IDEA. (Depending on the circumstances, she might well have been able to proceed under both.) Rather, these questions help determine whether a plaintiff who has chosen to bring a claim under Title II or \xc2\xa7504 instead of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x94and whose complaint makes no mention of a FAPE\xe2\x80\x94nevertheless raises a claim whose substance is the denial of an appropriate education.\n\t\n
\n\n11\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0The point here is limited to commencement of the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s formal administrative procedures; it does not apply to more informal requests to IEP Team members or other school administrators for accommodations or changes to a special education program. After all, parents of a child with a disability are likely to bring all grievances first to those familiar officials, whether or not they involve the denial of a FAPE.\n\t\n

I join all of the opinion of the Court with the exception of its discussion (in the text from the beginning of the first new paragraph on page 15 to the end of the opinion) in which the Court provides several misleading \xe2\x80\x9cclue[s],\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15, for the lower courts.

\n

The Court first instructs the lower courts to inquire whether the plaintiff could have brought \xe2\x80\x9cessentially the same claim if the alleged conduct had occurred at a public facility that was not a school\xe2\x80\x94say, a public theater or library.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Next, the Court says, a court should ask whether \xe2\x80\x9can adult at the school\xe2\x80\x94say, an employee or visitor\xe2\x80\x94[could] have pressed essentially the same grievance.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. These clues make sense only if there is no overlap between the relief available under the following two sets of claims: (1) the relief provided by the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), and (2) the relief provided by other federal laws (including the Constitution, the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA), and the Rehabilitation Act of 1973). The Court does not show or even claim that there is no such overlap\xe2\x80\x94to the contrary, it observes that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he same conduct might vio-

\n

late\xe2\x80\x9d the ADA, the Rehabilitation Act and the IDEA. Ibid. And since these clues work only in the absence of overlap, I would not suggest them.

\n

The Court provides another false clue by suggesting that lower courts take into account whether parents, before filing suit under the ADA or the Rehabilitation Act, began to pursue but then abandoned the IDEA\xe2\x80\x99s formal procedures. Ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. This clue also seems to me to be ill-advised. It is easy to imagine circumstances under which parents might start down the IDEA road and then change course and file an action under the ADA or the Rehabilitation Act that seeks relief that the IDEA cannot provide. The parents might be advised by their attorney that the relief they were seeking under the IDEA is not available under that law but is available under another. Or the parents might change their minds about the relief that they want, give up on the relief that the IDEA can provide, and turn to another statute.

\n

Although the Court provides these clues for the purpose of assisting the lower courts, I am afraid that they may have the opposite effect. They are likely to confuse and lead courts astray.
