Syllabus

\n

Lamar, Archer & Cofrin, LLP v. Appling

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x931215.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 17, 2018\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 4, 2018

\n

Respondent R. Scott Appling fell behind on his bills owed to petitioner law firm Lamar, Archer & Cofrin, LLP, which threatened to withdraw representation and place a lien on its work product if Appling did not pay. Appling told Lamar that he could cover owed and future legal expenses with an expected tax refund, so Lamar agreed to continue representation. However, Appling used the refund, which was for much less than he had stated, for business expenses. When he met with Lamar again, he told the firm he was still waiting on the refund, so Lamar agreed to complete pending litigation. Appling never paid the final invoice, so Lamar sued him and obtained a judgment. Shortly thereafter, Appling and his wife filed for Chapter 7 bankruptcy. Lamar initiated an adversary proceeding against Appling in Bankruptcy Court, arguing that his debt to Lamar was nondischargeable pursuant to\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A), which bars discharge of specified debts arising from \xe2\x80\x9cfalse pretenses, a false representation, or actual fraud, other than a statement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s . . . financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Appling moved to dismiss on the ground that his alleged misrepresentations were \xe2\x80\x9cstatement[s] respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. financial condition,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B) requires to be \xe2\x80\x9cin writing.\xe2\x80\x9d The Bankruptcy Court disagreed and denied Appling\xe2\x80\x99s motion. Finding that Appling knowingly made two false representations on which Lamar justifiably relied and that Lamar incurred damages as a result, the court concluded that Appling\xe2\x80\x99s debt to Lamar was nondischargeable under \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A). The District Court affirmed, but the Eleventh Circuit reversed, holding that a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d may include a statement about a single asset. Because Appling\xe2\x80\x99s statements were not in writing, the court held, \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B) did not bar him from discharging his debt to Lamar.

\n

Held: A statement about a single asset can be a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7523(a)(2). Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The key word in the relevant statutory phrase here is the preposition \xe2\x80\x9crespecting.\xe2\x80\x9d In ordinary usage, \xe2\x80\x9crespecting\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cconcerning; about; regarding; in regard to; relating to.\xe2\x80\x9d Lamar contends that the definitions \xe2\x80\x9cabout,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cconcerning,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cwith reference to,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cas regards\xe2\x80\x9d denote a more limited scope than \xe2\x80\x9crelated to.\xe2\x80\x9d And under that more limited meaning, Lamar asserts, a formal financial statement providing a detailed accounting of one\xe2\x80\x99s assets and liabilities would qualify as \xe2\x80\x9ca statement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition,\xe2\x80\x9d but a statement about a single asset would not. But the overlapping and circular definitions of these words belie the clear distinction Lamar attempts to impose. And the firm gives no example of a phrase in a legal context similar to the one at issue here in which toggling between \xe2\x80\x9crelated to\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cabout\xe2\x80\x9d has any pertinent significance.

\n

Use of the word \xe2\x80\x9crespecting\xe2\x80\x9d in a legal context generally has a broadening effect, ensuring that a provision\xe2\x80\x99s scope covers not only its subject but also matters relating to that subject. Cf. Kleppe v. New Mexico,\n\n426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 529, 539. Indeed, this Court has typically read the phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94one of respecting\xe2\x80\x99s meanings\xe2\x80\x94expansively. See, e.g., Coventry Health Care of Mo., Inc. v. Nevils, 581 U. S. ___, ___. Appling and the United States, as amicus curiae, accordingly advance an expansive interpretation here. This Court agrees with them that, given the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9crespecting,\xe2\x80\x9d Lamar\xe2\x80\x99s statutory construction must be rejected, for it reads \xe2\x80\x9crespecting\xe2\x80\x9d out of the statute. See TRW Inc. v. Andrews,\n\n534 U. S. 19, 31. Had Congress intended \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B) to encompass only statements expressing the balance of a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets and liabilities, it could have so specified\xe2\x80\x94e.g., \xe2\x80\x9cstatement of the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court also agrees that a statement is \xe2\x80\x9crespecting\xe2\x80\x9d a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition if it has a direct relation to or impact on the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s overall financial status. A single asset has a direct relation to and impact on aggregate financial condition, so a statement about that asset bears on a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s overall financial condition and can help indicate whether a debtor is solvent or insolvent. A statement about a single asset, thus, can be a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Lamar\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation would yield incoherent results. For instance, on Lamar\xe2\x80\x99s view, a misrepresentation about a single asset made in the context of a formal financial statement or balance sheet would constitute a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d and trigger \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B)\xe2\x80\x99s heightened nondischargeability requirements, but the same misrepresentation made on its own, or in the context of a list of some but not all of the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets and liabilities, would not. Lamar does not explain why Congress would draw such seemingly arbitrary distinctions. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The statutory history of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d corroborates this Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading. Between 1926, when the phrase was introduced, and 1978, when Congress enacted the Bankruptcy Code, Courts of Appeals consistently construed the phrase to encompass statements addressing just one or some of a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets or liabilities. When Congress used the materially same language in \xc2\xa7523(a)(2), it presumptively was aware of this longstanding judicial interpretation and intended for the phrase to retain its established meaning. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0Lamar\xe2\x80\x99s additional arguments are unpersuasive. First, Lamar contends that Appling\xe2\x80\x99s construction gives \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B) an implausibly broad reach, such that little would be covered by \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s general rule rendering nondischargeable debts arising from \xe2\x80\x9cfalse pretenses, a false representation, or actual fraud.\xe2\x80\x9d But \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A) still retains significant function when the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d is interpreted to encompass a statement about a single asset. See, e.g., Husky Int\xe2\x80\x99l Electronics, Inc. v. Ritz, 578 U. S. ___, ___. Second, Lamar asserts that Appling\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is inconsistent with the overall principle that the Bankruptcy Code exists to afford relief only to the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98honest but unfortunate debtor.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Cohen v. de la Cruz, \n\n523 U. S. 213, 217. The text of \xc2\xa7523(a)(2), however, plainly heightens the bar to discharge when the fraud at issue was effectuated via a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d The heightened requirements, moreover, are not a shield for dishonest debtors. Rather, they reflect Congress\xe2\x80\x99 effort to balance the potential misuse of such statements by both debtors and creditors. See Field v. Mans,\n\n516 U.\xc2\xa0S. 59, 76\xe2\x80\x9377. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

848 F.\xc2\xa03d 953, affirmed.

LAMAR, ARCHER & COFRIN, LLP, PETITIONER v. R. SCOTT APPLING

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[June 4, 2018]

\n

\n

Justice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.[1]*

\n

The Bankruptcy Code prohibits debtors from discharging debts for money, property, services, or credit obtained by \xe2\x80\x9cfalse pretenses, a false representation, or actual fraud,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A), or, if made in writing, by a materially false \xe2\x80\x9cstatement .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. financial condition,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B).

\n

This case is about what constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Does a statement about a single asset qualify, or must the statement be about the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s overall financial status? The answer matters to the parties because the false statements at issue concerned a single asset and were made orally. So, if the single-asset statements here qualify as \xe2\x80\x9crespecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B) poses no bar to discharge because they were not made in writing. If, however, the statements fall into the more general category of \xe2\x80\x9cfalse pretenses, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. false representation, or actual fraud,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A), for which there is no writing requirement, the associated debt will be deemed nondischargeable.

\n

The statutory language makes plain that a statement about a single asset can be a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d If that statement is not in writing, then, the associated debt may be discharged, even if the statement was false.

\n

I

\n

Respondent R. Scott Appling hired petitioner Lamar, Archer & Cofrin, LLP (Lamar), a law firm, to represent him in a business litigation. Appling fell behind on his legal bills, and by March 2005, he owed Lamar more than $60,000. Lamar informed Appling that if he did not pay the outstanding amount, the firm would withdraw from representation and place a lien on its work product until the bill was paid. The parties met in person that month, and Appling told his attorneys that he was expecting a tax refund of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98approximately $100,000,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d enough to cover his owed and future legal fees. App. to Pet. for Cert. 3a. Lamar relied on this statement and continued to represent Appling without initiating collection of the overdue amount.

\n

When Appling and his wife filed their tax return, however, the refund they requested was of just $60,718, and they ultimately received $59,851 in October 2005. Rather than paying Lamar, they spent the money on their business.

\n

Appling and his attorneys met again in November 2005, and Appling told them that he had not yet received the refund. Lamar relied on that statement and agreed to complete the pending litigation and delay collection of the outstanding fees.

\n

In March 2006, Lamar sent Appling its final invoice. Five years later, Appling still had not paid, so Lamar filed suit in Georgia state court and obtained a judgment for $104,179.60. Shortly thereafter, Appling and his wife filed for Chapter 7 bankruptcy.

\n

Lamar initiated an adversary proceeding against Appling in Bankruptcy Court for the Middle District of Georgia. The firm argued that because Appling made fraudulent statements about his tax refund at the March and November 2005 meetings, his debt to Lamar was nondischargeable pursuant to\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A), which governs debts arising from \xe2\x80\x9cfalse pretenses, a false representation, or actual fraud, other than a statement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Appling, in turn, moved to dismiss, contending that his alleged misrepresentations were \xe2\x80\x9cstatement[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. respecting [his] financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d and were therefore governed by \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B), such that Lamar could not block discharge of the debt because the statements were not \xe2\x80\x9cin writing\xe2\x80\x9d as required for nondischargeability under that provision.

\n

The Bankruptcy Court held that a statement regarding a single asset is not a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d and denied Appling\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss. 500 B.\xc2\xa0R. 246, 252 (MD Ga. 2013). After a trial, the Bankruptcy Court found that Appling knowingly made two false representations on which Lamar justifiably relied and that Lamar incurred damages as a result. It thus concluded that Appling\xe2\x80\x99s debt to Lamar was nondischargeable under \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A). 527 B.\xc2\xa0R. 545, 550\xe2\x80\x93556 (MD Ga. 2015). The District Court affirmed. 2016 WL 1183128 (MD Ga., Mar. 28, 2016).

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit reversed. It held that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98statement[s] respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. financial condition\xe2\x80\x99 may include a statement about a single asset.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Appling, 848 F.\xc2\xa03d 953, 960 (2017). Because Appling\xe2\x80\x99s statements about his expected tax refund were not in writing, the Court of Appeals held that \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B) did not bar Appling from discharging his debt to Lamar. Id., at 961.

\n

The Court granted certiorari, 583 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2018), to resolve a conflict among the Courts of Appeals as to whether a statement about a single asset can be a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d[2] We agree with the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion and affirm.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

One of the \xe2\x80\x9cmain purpose[s]\xe2\x80\x9d of the federal bankruptcy system is \xe2\x80\x9cto aid the unfortunate debtor by giving him a fresh start in life, free from debts, except of a certain character.\xe2\x80\x9d Stellwagen v. Clum,\n\n245 U.\xc2\xa0S. 605, 617 (1918). To that end, the Bankruptcy Code contains broad provisions for the discharge of debts, subject to exceptions. One such exception is found in\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7523(a)(2), which provides that a discharge under Chapter 7, 11, 12, or 13 of the Bankruptcy Code \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not discharge an individual debtor from any debt .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for money, property, services, or an extension, renewal, or refinancing of credit, to the extent obtained by\xe2\x80\x9d fraud. This exception is in keeping with the \xe2\x80\x9cbasic policy animating the Code of affording relief only to an \xe2\x80\x98honest but unfortunate debtor.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Cohen v. de la Cruz,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213, 217 (1998).

\n

More specifically, \xc2\xa7523(a)(2) excepts from discharge debts arising from various forms of fraud. Subparagraph (A) bars discharge of debts arising from \xe2\x80\x9cfalse pretenses, a false representation, or actual fraud, other than a statement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Subparagraph (B), in turn, bars discharge of debts arising from a materially false \xe2\x80\x9cstatement .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d if that statement is \xe2\x80\x9cin writing.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

B

\n

1

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cOur interpretation of the Bankruptcy Code starts \xe2\x80\x98where all such inquiries must begin: with the language of the statute itself.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ransom v. FIA Card Services, N.\xc2\xa0A.,\n\n562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 61, 69 (2011). As noted, the relevant statutory text is the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Because the Bankruptcy Code does not define the words \xe2\x80\x9cstatement,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfinancial condition,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9crespecting,\xe2\x80\x9d we look to their ordinary meanings. See ibid.

\n

There is no dispute as to the meaning of the first two terms. A \xe2\x80\x9cstatement\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cthe act or process of stating, reciting, or presenting orally or on paper; something stated as a report or narrative; a single declaration or remark.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 2229 (1976) (Webster\xe2\x80\x99s). As to \xe2\x80\x9cfinancial condition,\xe2\x80\x9d the parties agree, as does the United States, that the term means one\xe2\x80\x99s overall financial status. See Brief for Petitioner 23; Brief for Respondent 25; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 12.

\n

For our purposes, then, the key word in the statutory phrase is the preposition \xe2\x80\x9crespecting,\xe2\x80\x9d which joins together \xe2\x80\x9cstatement\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cfinancial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d As a matter of ordinary usage, \xe2\x80\x9crespecting\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cin view of: considering; with regard or relation to: regarding; concerning.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 1934; see also American Heritage Dictionary 1107 (1969) (\xe2\x80\x9c[i]n relation to; concerning\xe2\x80\x9d); Random House Dictionary of the English Language 1221 (1966) (\xe2\x80\x9cregarding; concerning\xe2\x80\x9d); Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New Twentieth Century Dictionary 1542 (2d ed. 1967) (\xe2\x80\x9cconcerning; about; regarding; in regard to; relating to\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

According to Lamar, these definitions reveal that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98respecting\xe2\x80\x99 can be \xe2\x80\x98defined broadly,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but that the word \xe2\x80\x9cisn\xe2\x80\x99t always used that way.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 27. The firm contends that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98about,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98concerning,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98with reference to,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98as regards\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d denote a more limited scope than \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98related to.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 3, 18, 27. When \xe2\x80\x9crespecting\xe2\x80\x9d is understood to have one of these more limited meanings, Lamar asserts, a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9ca statement that is \xe2\x80\x98about,\xe2\x80\x99 or that makes \xe2\x80\x98reference to,\xe2\x80\x99 the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s overall financial state or well-being.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 27\xe2\x80\x9328. Under that formulation, a formal financial statement providing a detailed accounting of one\xe2\x80\x99s assets and liabilities would qualify, as would statements like \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Don\xe2\x80\x99t worry, I am above water,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I am in good financial shape.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 19, 28. A statement about a single asset would not.

\n

The Court finds no basis to conclude, however, at least in this context, that \xe2\x80\x9crelated to\xe2\x80\x9d has a materially different meaning than \xe2\x80\x9cabout,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cconcerning,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cwith reference to,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cas regards.\xe2\x80\x9d The definitions of these words are overlapping and circular, with each one pointing to another in the group. \xe2\x80\x9cRelate\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cto be in relationship: have reference,\xe2\x80\x9d and, in the context of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin relation to,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9creference, respect.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 1916; see also id., at 18a (Explanatory Note 16.2). \xe2\x80\x9cAbout\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cwith regard to,\xe2\x80\x9d and is the equivalent of \xe2\x80\x9cconcerning.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 5. \xe2\x80\x9cConcerning\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9crelating to,\xe2\x80\x9d and is the equivalent of \xe2\x80\x9cregarding, respecting, about.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 470. \xe2\x80\x9cReference\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cthe capability or character of alluding to or bearing on or directing attention to something,\xe2\x80\x9d and is the equivalent of \xe2\x80\x9crelation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crespect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1907. And \xe2\x80\x9cregard\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cto have relation to or bearing upon: relate to,\xe2\x80\x9d and is the equivalent of \xe2\x80\x9crelation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crespect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1911. The interconnected web formed by these words belies the clear distinction Lamar attempts to impose. Lamar also fails to put forth an example of a phrase in a legal context similar to the one at issue here in which toggling between \xe2\x80\x9crelated to\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cabout\xe2\x80\x9d has any pertinent significance.

\n

Use of the word \xe2\x80\x9crespecting\xe2\x80\x9d in a legal context generally has a broadening effect, ensuring that the scope of a provision covers not only its subject but also matters relating to that subject. Cf. Kleppe v. New Mexico,\n\n426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 529, 539 (1976) (explaining that the Property Clause, \xe2\x80\x9cin broad terms, gives Congress the power to determine what are \xe2\x80\x98needful\xe2\x80\x99 rules \xe2\x80\x98respecting\xe2\x80\x99 the public lands,\xe2\x80\x9d and should receive an \xe2\x80\x9cexpansive reading\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Indeed, when asked to interpret statutory language including the phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelating to,\xe2\x80\x9d which is one of the meanings of \xe2\x80\x9crespecting,\xe2\x80\x9d this Court has typically read the relevant text expansively. See, e.g., Coventry Health Care of Mo., Inc. v. Nevils, 581 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2017) (slip op., at 7) (describing \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98relate to\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cexpansive\xe2\x80\x9d and noting that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress characteristically employs the phrase to reach any subject that has \xe2\x80\x98a connection with, or reference to,\xe2\x80\x99 the topics the statute enumerates\xe2\x80\x9d); Morales v. Trans World Airlines, Inc.,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374, 378\xe2\x80\x93390 (1992) (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98relating to\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d has a \xe2\x80\x9cbroad\xe2\x80\x9d ordinary meaning and accordingly holding that the Airline Deregulation Act of 1978 provision prohibiting the States from enforcing any law \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98relating to rates, routes, or services\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of any air carrier pre-empted any fare advertising guidelines that \xe2\x80\x9cwould have a significant impact upon the airlines\xe2\x80\x99 ability to market their product, and hence a significant impact upon the fares they charge\xe2\x80\x9d); Ingersoll-Rand Co. v. McClendon,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 133, 139 (1990) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98A law \xe2\x80\x9crelates to\xe2\x80\x9d an employee benefit plan, in the normal sense of the phrase, if it has a connection with or reference to such a plan.\xe2\x80\x99 Under this \xe2\x80\x98broad common-sense meaning,\xe2\x80\x99 a state law may \xe2\x80\x98relate to\xe2\x80\x99 a benefit plan .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. even if the law is not specifically designed to affect such plans, or the effect is only indirect\xe2\x80\x9d (citation omitted)).

\n

Advancing that same expansive approach here, Appling contends that a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9ca statement that has a direct relation to, or impact on the balance of all of the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets and liabilities or the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s overall financial status.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 17 (internal quotation marks and citations omitted). \xe2\x80\x9cA debtor\xe2\x80\x99s statement describing an individual asset or liability necessarily qualifies,\xe2\x80\x9d Appling explains, because it \xe2\x80\x9chas a direct impact on the sum of his assets and liabilities.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cPut differently, a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s statement that describes the existence or value of a constituent element of the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s balance sheet or income statement qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x98statement respecting financial condition.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The United States as amicus curiae supporting Appling offers a slightly different formulation. In its view, a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d includes \xe2\x80\x9ca representation about a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s asset that is offered as evidence of ability to pay.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 11. Although Appling does not include \xe2\x80\x9cability to pay\xe2\x80\x9d in his proffered definition, he and the United States agree that their respective formulations are functionally the same and lead to the same results. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 50\xe2\x80\x9352, 58. That is so because to establish the requisite materiality and reliance, a creditor opposing discharge must explain why it viewed the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s false representation as relevant to the decision to extend money, property, services, or credit. If a given statement did not actually serve as evidence of ability to pay, the creditor\xe2\x80\x99s explanation will not suffice to bar discharge. But if the creditor proves materiality and reliance, it will be clear the statement was one \xe2\x80\x9crespecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Whether a statement about a single asset served as evidence of ability to pay thus ultimately always factors into the \xc2\xa7523(a)(2) inquiry at some point.

\n

We agree with both Appling and the United States that, given the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9crespecting,\xe2\x80\x9d Lamar\xe2\x80\x99s preferred statutory construction\xe2\x80\x94that a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d means only a statement that captures the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s overall financial status\xe2\x80\x94must be rejected, for it reads \xe2\x80\x9crespecting\xe2\x80\x9d out of the statute. See TRW Inc. v. Andrews,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 19, 31 (2001) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] statute ought .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to be so construed that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. no clause, sentence, or word shall be superfluous, void, or insignificant\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Had Congress intended \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B) to encompass only statements expressing the balance of a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets and liabilities, there are several ways in which it could have so specified, e.g., \xe2\x80\x9cstatement disclosing the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cstatement of the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d[3] But Congress did not use such narrow language.

\n

We also agree that a statement is \xe2\x80\x9crespecting\xe2\x80\x9d a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition if it has a direct relation to or impact on the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s overall financial status. A single asset has a direct relation to and impact on aggregate financial condition, so a statement about a single asset bears on a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s overall financial condition and can help indicate whether a debtor is solvent or insolvent, able to repay a given debt or not. Naturally, then, a statement about a single asset can be a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

2

\n

Further supporting the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion is that Lamar\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation would yield incoherent results. On Lamar\xe2\x80\x99s view, the following would obtain: A misrepresentation about a single asset made in the context of a formal financial statement or balance sheet would constitute a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d and trigger \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B)\xe2\x80\x99s heightened nondischargeability requirements, but the exact same misrepresentation made on its own, or in the context of a list of some but not all of the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets and liabilities, would not. Lamar does not explain why Congress would draw such seemingly arbitrary distinctions, where the ability to discharge a debt turns on the superficial packaging of a statement rather than its substantive content.

\n

In addition, a highly general statement like, \xe2\x80\x9cI am above water,\xe2\x80\x9d would need to be in writing to foreclose discharge, whereas a highly specific statement like, \xe2\x80\x9cI have $200,000 of equity in my house,\xe2\x80\x9d would not. This, too, is inexplicably bizarre.

\n

3

\n

Lastly, the statutory history of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d corroborates our reading of the text. That language can be traced back to a 1926 amendment to the Bankruptcy Act of 1898 that prohibited discharge entirely to a debtor who had \xe2\x80\x9cobtained money or property on credit, or obtained an extension or renewal of credit, by making or publishing, or causing to be made or published, in any manner whatsoever, a materially false statement in writing respecting his financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of May 27, 1926, \xc2\xa76,\n\n44Stat.\n663\xe2\x80\x93664.

\n

When Congress again amended this provision in 1960, it retained the \xe2\x80\x9cstatement in writing respecting .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d language. See Act of July 12, 1960, Pub. L. 86\xe2\x80\x93621, \xc2\xa72,\n\n74Stat.\n409. Congress then once more preserved that language when it rewrote and recodified the provision in the modern Bankruptcy Code as \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B).

\n

Given the historical presence of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition,\xe2\x80\x9d lower courts had ample opportunity to weigh in on its meaning. Between 1926, when the phrase was introduced, and 1978, when Congress enacted the Bankruptcy Code, Courts of Appeals consistently construed the phrase to encompass statements addressing just one or some of a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets or liabilities.[4] When Congress used the materially same language in \xc2\xa7523(a)(2), it presumptively was aware of the longstanding judicial interpretation of the phrase and intended for it to retain its established meaning. See Lorillard v. Pons,\n\n434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 575, 580 (1978) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress is presumed to be aware of an administrative or judicial interpretation of a statute and to adopt that interpretation when it re-enacts a statute without change\xe2\x80\x9d); Bragdon v. Abbott,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 624, 645 (1998) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen administrative and judicial interpretations have settled the meaning of an existing statutory provision, repetition of the same language in a new statute indicates, as a general matter, the intent to incorporate its administrative and judicial interpretations as well\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

III

\n

In addition to its plain-text arguments discussed and rejected above, see supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x937, Lamar contends that Appling\xe2\x80\x99s rule undermines the purpose of \xc2\xa7523(a)(2) in two ways. Neither argument is persuasive.

\n

A

\n

First, Lamar contends that Appling\xe2\x80\x99s construction gives \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B) an implausibly broad reach, such that little would be covered by \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s general rule rendering nondischargeable debts arising from \xe2\x80\x9cfalse pretenses, a false representation, or actual fraud.\xe2\x80\x9d That is not so. Decisions from this Court and several lower courts considering the application of \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A) demonstrate that the provision still retains significant function when the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d is interpreted to encompass a statement about a single asset.

\n

Section 523(a)(2)(A) has been applied when a debt arises from \xe2\x80\x9cforms of fraud, like fraudulent conveyance schemes, that can be effected without a false representation.\xe2\x80\x9d Husky Int\xe2\x80\x99l Electronics, Inc. v. Ritz, 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 3).[5] It also has been used to bar the discharge of debts resulting from misrepresentations about the value of goods, property, and services.[6]

\n

B

\n

Second, Lamar asserts that Appling\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is inconsistent with the overall principle that the Bankruptcy Code exists to afford relief only to the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98honest but unfortunate debtor,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Cohen, 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 217, because it leaves \xe2\x80\x9cfraudsters\xe2\x80\x9d free to \xe2\x80\x9cswindle innocent victims for money, property or services by lying about their finances, then discharge the resulting debt in bankruptcy, just so long as they do so orally.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 35.

\n

This general maxim, however, provides little support for Lamar\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation. The text of \xc2\xa7523(a)(2) plainly heightens the bar to discharge when the fraud at issue was effectuated via a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d[7] The heightened requirements, moreover, are not a shield for dishonest debtors. Rather, they reflect Congress\xe2\x80\x99 effort to balance the potential misuse of such statements by both debtors and creditors. As the Court has explained previously:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe House Report on the [Bankruptcy Reform Act of 1978] suggests that Congress wanted to moderate the burden on individuals who submitted false financial statements, not because lies about financial condition are less blameworthy than others, but because the relative equities might be affected by practices of consumer finance companies, which sometimes have encouraged such falsity by their borrowers for the very purpose of insulating their own claims from discharge.\xe2\x80\x9d Field v. Mans,\n\n516 U.\xc2\xa0S. 59, 76\xe2\x80\x9377 (1995).

\n

Specifically, as detailed in Field, the House Report noted that consumer finance companies frequently collected information from loan applicants in ways designed to permit the companies to later use those statements as the basis for an exception to discharge. Commonly, a loan officer would instruct a loan applicant \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to list only a few or only the most important of his debts\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d on a form with too little space to supply a complete list of debts, even though the phrase, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I have no other debts,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d would be printed at the bottom of the form or the applicant would be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98instructed to write the phrase in his own handwriting.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 77, n.\xc2\xa013. If the debtor later filed for bankruptcy, the creditor would contend that the debtor had made misrepresentations in his loan application and the creditor would threaten litigation over excepting the debt from discharge. That threat was \xe2\x80\x9coften enough to induce the debtor to settle for a reduced sum,\xe2\x80\x9d even where the merits of the nondischargeability claim were weak. H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93595, p. 131 (1977).

\n

Notably, Lamar\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d would not bring within \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B)\xe2\x80\x99s reach the very types of statements the House Report described, because those debts-only statements said nothing about assets and thus did not communicate fully the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s overall financial status. Yet in Field, the Court explained that the heightened requirements for nondischargeability under \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B) were intended to address creditor abuse involving such statements. 516 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 76\xe2\x80\x9377. Lamar\xe2\x80\x99s construction also would render \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B) subject to manipulation by creditors, frustrating the very end Congress sought to avoid when it set forth heightened requirements for rendering nondischargeable \xe2\x80\x9cstatements respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Finally, although Lamar tries to paint a picture of defenseless creditors swindled by lying debtors careful to make their financial representations orally, creditors are not powerless. They can still benefit from the protection of \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(B) so long as they insist that the representations respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition on which they rely in extending money, property, services, or credit are made in writing. Doing so will likely redound to their benefit, as such writings can foster accuracy at the outset of a transaction, reduce the incidence of fraud, and facilitate the more predictable, fair, and efficient resolution of any subsequent dispute.

\n

IV

\n

For the foregoing reasons, the Court holds that a statement about a single asset can be a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement respecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7523(a)(2) of the Bankruptcy Code. The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.
