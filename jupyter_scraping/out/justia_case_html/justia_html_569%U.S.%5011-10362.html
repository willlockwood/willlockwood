\n\t\t\t\tMILLBROOK v. UNITED STATES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x9310362.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 19, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 27, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Federal Tort Claims Act (FTCA) waives the Government\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity from tort suits, but excepts from that waiver certain intentional torts, \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72680(h). Section \xc2\xa72680(h), in turn, contains a proviso that extends the waiver of immunity to claims for six intentional torts, including assault and battery, that are based on the \xe2\x80\x9cacts or omissions\xe2\x80\x9d of an \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigative or law enforcement officer\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., a federal officer \xe2\x80\x9cwho is empowered by law to execute searches, to seize evidence, or to make arrests.\xe2\x80\x9d Petitioner Millbrook, a federal prisoner, sued the United States under the FTCA, alleging, inter alia, assault and battery by correctional officers. The District Court granted the Government summary judgment, and the Third Circuit affirmed, hewing to its precedent that the \xe2\x80\x9claw enforcement proviso\xe2\x80\x9d applies only to tortious conduct that occurs during the course of executing a search, seizing evidence, or making an arrest.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The law enforcement proviso extends to law enforcement officers\xe2\x80\x99 acts or omissions that arise within the scope of their employment, regardless of whether the officers are engaged in investigative or law enforcement activity, or are executing a search, seizing evidence, or making an arrest. The proviso\xe2\x80\x99s plain language supports this conclusion. On its face, the proviso applies where a claim arises out of one of six intentional torts and is related to the \xe2\x80\x9cacts or omissions\xe2\x80\x9d of an \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigative or law enforcement officer.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680(h). And by cross-referencing \xc2\xa71346(b), the proviso incorporates an additional requirement that the \xe2\x80\x9cacts or omissions\xe2\x80\x9d occur while the officer is \xe2\x80\x9cacting within the scope of his office or employment.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71346(b)(1). Nothing in \xc2\xa72680(h)\xe2\x80\x99s text supports further limiting the proviso to conduct arising out of searches, seizures of evidence, or arrests. The FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s only reference to those terms is in \xc2\xa72680(h)\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigative or law enforcement officer,\xe2\x80\x9d which focuses on the status of persons whose conduct may be actionable, not the types of activities that may give rise to a claim. This confirms that Congress intended immunity determinations to depend on a federal officer\xe2\x80\x99s legal author-ity, not on a particular exercise of that authority. Nor does the pro-viso indicate that a waiver of immunity requires the officer to be engaged in investigative or law enforcement activity. The text never uses those terms. Had Congress intended to further narrow the waiver\xe2\x80\x99s scope, it could have used language to that effect. See Ali v. Federal Bureau of Prisons, \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 214\n. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x88\x928.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t477 Fed. Appx. 4, reversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tKIM MILLBROOK, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Kim Millbrook, a prisoner in the custody of the Federal Bureau of Prisons (BOP), alleges that correctional officers sexually assaulted and verbally threatened him while he was in their custody. Millbrook filed suit in Federal District Court under the Federal Tort Claims Act, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71346(b), 2671\xe2\x80\x932680 (FTCA or Act), which waives the Government\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity from tort suits, including those based on certain intentional torts committed by federal law enforcement officers, \xc2\xa72680(h). The District Court dismissed Millbrook\xe2\x80\x99s action, and the Court of Appeals affirmed. The Court of Appeals held that, while the FTCA waives the United States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign immunity for certain intentional torts by law enforcement officers, it only does so when the tortious conduct occurs in the course of executing a search, seizing evidence, or making an arrest. Petitioner contends that the FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s waiver is not so limited. We agree and reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe FTCA \xe2\x80\x9cwas designed primarily to remove the sovereign immunity of the United States from suits in tort.\xe2\x80\x9d Levin v. United States, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 2) (internal quotation marks omitted). The Act gives federal district courts exclusive jurisdiction over claims against the United States for \xe2\x80\x9cinjury or loss of property, or personal injury or death caused by the negligent or wrongful act or omission\xe2\x80\x9d of a federal employee \xe2\x80\x9cacting within the scope of his office or employment.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71346(b)(1). This broad waiver of sovereign immunity is subject to a number of exceptions set forth in \xc2\xa72680. One such exception, relating to intentional torts, preserves the Government\xe2\x80\x99s immunity from suit for \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny claim arising out of assault, battery, false imprisonment, false arrest, malicious prosecution, abuse of process, libel, slander, misrepresentation, deceit, or interference with contract rights.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680(h). We have referred to \xc2\xa72680(h) as the \xe2\x80\x9cintentional tort exception.\xe2\x80\x9d Levin, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 2) (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1974, Congress carved out an exception to \xc2\xa72680(h)\xe2\x80\x99s preservation of the United States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign immunity for intentional torts by adding a proviso covering claims that arise out of the wrongful conduct of law enforcement officers. See Act of Mar. 16, 1974, Pub. L. 93\xe2\x80\x93253, \xc2\xa72, \n\n88Stat.\n50. Known as the \xe2\x80\x9claw enforcement proviso,\xe2\x80\x9d this provision extends the waiver of sovereign immunity to claims for six intentional torts, including assault and battery, that are based on the \xe2\x80\x9cacts or omissions of investigative or law enforcement officers.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680(h). The proviso defines \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98investigative or law enforcement officer\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cany officer of the United States who is empowered by law to execute searches, to seize evidence, or to make arrests for violations of Federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn January 18, 2011, Millbrook filed suit against the United States under the FTCA, asserting claims of negligence, assault, and battery. In his complaint, Millbrook alleged that, on March 5, 2010, he was forced to per- form oral sex on a BOP correctional officer, while another officer held him in a choke hold and a third officer stood watch nearby. Millbrook claimed that the officers threatened to kill him if he did not comply with their demands. Millbrook alleged that he suffered physical injuries as a result of the incident and, accordingly, sought compensatory damages.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government argued that the FTCA did not waive the United States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign immunity from suit on Millbrook\xe2\x80\x99s intentional tort claims, because they fell within the intentional tort exception in \xc2\xa72680(h). The Government contended that \xc2\xa72680(h)\xe2\x80\x99s law enforcement proviso did not save Millbrook\xe2\x80\x99s claims because of the Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s binding precedent in Pooler v. United States, 787 F.\xc2\xa02d 868 (1986), which interpreted the proviso to apply only to tortious conduct that occurred during the course of \xe2\x80\x9cexecuting a search, seizing evidence, or making an ar- rest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 872. The District Court agreed and granted summary judgment for the United States because the alleged conduct \xe2\x80\x9cdid not take place during an arrest, search, or seizure of evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Civ. Action No. 3:11\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9300131 (MD Pa., Feb. 16, 2012), App. 96.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The Third Circuit affirmed. 477 Fed. Appx. 4, 5\xe2\x80\x936 (2012) (per curiam).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), to resolve a Circuit split concerning the circumstances under which intentionally tortious conduct by law enforcement officers can give rise to an actionable claim under the FTCA. Compare Pooler, supra; and Orsay v. United States Dept. of Justice, 289 F.\xc2\xa03d 1125, 1136 (CA9 2002) (law enforcement proviso \xe2\x80\x9creaches only those claims asserting that the tort occurred in the course of investigative or law enforcement activities\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); with Ignacio v. United States, 674 F.\xc2\xa03d 252, 256 (CA4 2012) (holding that the law enforcement proviso \xe2\x80\x9cwaives immunity whenever an investigative or law enforcement officer commits one of the specified intentional torts, regardless of whether the officer is engaged in investigative or law enforcement activity\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe FTCA waives the United States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign immu- nity for certain intentional torts committed by law enforcement officers. The portion of the Act relevant here provides:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe provisions of this chapter and section 1346(b) of this title shall not apply to\xe2\x80\x94\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(h) Any claim arising out of assault, battery, false imprisonment, false arrest, malicious prosecution, abuse of process, libel, slander, misrepresentation, deceit, or interference with contract rights: Provided, That, with regard to acts or omissions of investigative or law enforcement officers of the United States Government, the provisions of this chapter and section 1346(b) of this title shall apply to any claim arising .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. out of assault, battery, false imprisonment, false arrest, abuse of process, or malicious prosecution.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa72680(h).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tOn its face, the law enforcement proviso applies where a claim both arises out of one of the proviso\xe2\x80\x99s six intentional torts, and is related to the \xe2\x80\x9cacts or omissions\xe2\x80\x9d of an \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigative or law enforcement officer.\xe2\x80\x9d The proviso\xe2\x80\x99s cross-reference to \xc2\xa71346(b) incorporates an additional requirement that the acts or omissions giving rise to the claim occur while the officer is \xe2\x80\x9cacting within the scope of his office or employment.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71346(b)(1). The question in this case is whether the FTCA further limits the category of \xe2\x80\x9cacts or omissions\xe2\x80\x9d that trigger the United States\xe2\x80\x99 liability.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe plain language of the law enforcement proviso answers when a law enforcement officer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cacts or omissions\xe2\x80\x9d may give rise to an actionable tort claim under the FTCA. The proviso specifies that the conduct must arise from one of the six enumerated intentional torts and, by expressly cross-referencing \xc2\xa71346(b), indicates that the law enforcement officer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cacts or omissions\xe2\x80\x9d must fall \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the scope of his office or employment.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72680(h), 1346(b)(1). Nothing in the text further qualifies the category of \xe2\x80\x9cacts or omissions\xe2\x80\x9d that may trigger FTCA liability.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA number of lower courts have nevertheless read into the text additional limitations designed to narrow the scope of the law enforcement proviso. The Ninth Circuit, for instance, held that the law enforcement proviso does not apply unless the tort was \xe2\x80\x9ccommitted in the course of investigative or law enforcement activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Orsay, supra, at 1135. As noted, the Third Circuit construed the law enforcement proviso even more narrowly in holding that it applies only to tortious conduct by federal officers during the course of \xe2\x80\x9cexecuting a search, seizing evidence, or making an arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d Pooler, 787 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 872. Court-appointed amicus curiae (Amicus) similarly asks us to construe the proviso to waive \xe2\x80\x9csovereign immunity only for torts committed by federal officers acting in their capacity as \xe2\x80\x98investigative or law enforcement officers.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Amicus 5. Under this approach, the conduct of federal officers would be actionable only when it \xe2\x80\x9caris[es] out of searches, seizures of evidence, arrests, and closely related exercises of investigative or law-enforcement authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNone of these interpretations finds any support in the text of the statute. The FTCA\xe2\x80\x99s only reference to \xe2\x80\x9csearches,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cseiz[ures of] evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9carrests\xe2\x80\x9d is found in the statutory definition of \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigative or law enforcement officer.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72680(h) (defining \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98investigative or law enforcement officer\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to mean any federal officer who is \xe2\x80\x9cempowered by law to execute searches, to seize evidence, or to make arrests for violations of Federal law\xe2\x80\x9d). By its terms, this provision focuses on the status of persons whose conduct may be actionable, not the types of activities that may give rise to a tort claim against the United States. The proviso thus distinguishes between the acts for which immunity is waived (e.g., assault and battery), and the class of persons whose acts may give rise to an actionable FTCA claim. The plain text confirms that Congress intended immunity determinations to depend on a federal officer\xe2\x80\x99s legal authority, not on a particular exercise of that authority. Consequently, there is no basis for concluding that a law enforcement officer\xe2\x80\x99s intentional tort must oc- cur in the course of executing a search, seizing evidence, or making an arrest in order to subject the United States to liability.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor does the text of the proviso provide any indication that the officer must be engaged in \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigative or law enforcement activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Indeed, the text never uses the term. Amicus contends that we should read the reference to \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigative or law-enforcement officer\xe2\x80\x9d as implicitly limiting the proviso to claims arising from actions taken in an officer\xe2\x80\x99s investigative or law enforcement capacity. But there is no basis for so limiting the term when Congress has spoken directly to the circumstances in which a law enforcement officer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct may expose the United States to tort liability. Under the proviso, an intentional tort is not actionable unless it occurs while the law enforcement officer is \xe2\x80\x9cacting within the scope of his office or employment.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72680(h), 1346(b)(1). Had Congress intended to further narrow the scope of the proviso, Congress could have limited it to claims arising from \xe2\x80\x9cacts or omissions of investigative or law enforcement officers acting in a law enforcement or investigative capacity.\xe2\x80\x9d See Ali v. Federal Bureau of Prisons, \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 214, \n227 (2008)\n. Congress adopted similar limitations in neighboring provisions, see \xc2\xa72680(a) (referring to \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny claim based upon an act or omission of an employee of the Government .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the execution of a statute or regulation\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)), but did not do so here. We, therefore, decline to read such a limitation into unambiguous text. Jimenez v. Quarterman, \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 113, \n118 (2009)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen the statutory language is plain, we must enforce it according to its terms\xe2\x80\x9d); Barnhart v. Sigmon Coal Co., \n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 438, \n450 (2002)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe inquiry ceases if the statutory language is unambiguous and the statutory scheme is coherent and consistent\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe hold that the waiver effected by the law enforcement proviso extends to acts or omissions of law enforcement officers that arise within the scope of their employment, regardless of whether the officers are engaged in investigative or law enforcement activity, or are executing a search, seizing evidence, or making an arrest. Accord- ingly, we reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

\n
