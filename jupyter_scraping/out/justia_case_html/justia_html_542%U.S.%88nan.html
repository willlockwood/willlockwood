HIBBS, DIRECTOR, ARIZONA DEPARTMENT OF REVENUE v. WINN et al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x931809. Argued January 20, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 14, 2004

\n

Plaintiffs-respondents, Arizona taxpayers, filed suit in federal court against the Director of Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s Department of Revenue (Director) seeking to enjoin the operation of Ariz. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 on Establishment Clause grounds. Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s law authorizes an income-tax credit for payments to nonprofit \xe2\x80\x9cschool tuition organizations\xe2\x80\x9d (STOs) that award scholarships to students in private elementary or secondary schools. Section 43\xe2\x80\x931089 provides that STOs may not designate schools that \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate on the basis of race, color, handicap, familial status or national origin,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089(F), but does not preclude STOs from designating schools that provide religious instruction or give religion-based admissions preferences. The District Court granted the Director\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss on the ground that the Tax Injunction Act (TIA), 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341, barred the suit. The TIA prohibits lower federal courts from restraining \xe2\x80\x9cthe assessment, levy or collection of any tax under State law where a plain, speedy and efficient remedy may be had in the courts of such State.\xe2\x80\x9d The Ninth Circuit reversed, holding that the TIA does not bar federal-court actions challenging state tax credits.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0 The Court rejects respondents\xe2\x80\x99 contention that the Director\xe2\x80\x99s certiorari petition was jurisdictionally untimely under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72101(c) and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 13(3). Section 2101(c) instructs that a petition must be filed \xe2\x80\x9cwithin ninety days after the entry of \xe2\x80\xa6 judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 13(3) elaborates on that statute\xe2\x80\x99s instruction. More than 90 days elapsed between the date the Ninth Circuit first entered judgment and the date the Director\xe2\x80\x99s petition was filed. That time lapse, respondents assert, made the filing untimely under Rule 13(3)\xe2\x80\x99s first sentence: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he time to file \xe2\x80\xa6 runs from the date of entry of the judgment or order sought to be reviewed.\xe2\x80\x9d Moreover, respondents submit, because no party petitioned for rehearing, the extended filing periods prescribed by the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s second sentence never came into play. This case, however, did not follow the typical course. The Ninth Circuit, on its own initiative, had recalled its mandate and ordered the parties to brief the question whether the case should be reheard en banc. That order, this Court holds, suspended the judgment\xe2\x80\x99s finality under \xc2\xa72101(c), just as a timely filed rehearing petition would or a court\xe2\x80\x99s appropriate decision to consider a late-filed rehearing petition, see Missouri v. Jenkins, 495 U. S. 33, 49. The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 order raised the question whether that court would modify the judgment and alter the parties\xe2\x80\x99 rights; thus, while the court-initiated briefing order was pending, there was no \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment\xe2\x80\x9d to be reviewed. See, e.g., id., at 46. The Director\xe2\x80\x99s certiorari petition was timely under the statute because it was filed within 90 days of the date the Ninth Circuit denied rehearing en banc. Were this Court to read Rule 13 as the sole guide, so that only a party\xe2\x80\x99s rehearing petition could reset the statute\xe2\x80\x99s 90-day count, the Court would lose sight of the congressional objective underpinning \xc2\xa72101(c): An appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s final adjudication, Congress indicated, marks the time from which the filing period begins to run. The statute takes priority over the \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural rules adopted by the Court for the orderly transaction of its business.\xe2\x80\x9d Schacht v. United States, 398 U. S. 58, 64. Because the petition was timely under \xc2\xa72101(c), the Court has jurisdiction. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The TIA does not bar respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suit. Pp. 8\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0To determine whether the TIA bars this litigation, it is appropriate, first, to identify the relief sought. Respondents seek prospective relief only: injunctive relief prohibiting the Director from allowing taxpayers to utilize the \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 tax credit for payments to STOs that make religion-based tuition grants; a declaration that \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089, on its face and as applied, violates the Establishment Clause; and an order that the Director inform such STOs that all funds in their possession as of the order\xe2\x80\x99s date must be paid into the state general fund. Taking account of the prospective nature of the relief requested, the Court reaches the dispositive question whether respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suit seeks to \xe2\x80\x9cenjoin, suspend or restrain the assessment, levy or collection of any tax under State law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71341. The answer turns on the meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d as employed in the TIA. For Internal Revenue Code (IRC) purposes, an assessment involves a \xe2\x80\x9crecording\xe2\x80\x9d of the amount the taxpayer owes the Government. 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76203. The Court does not focus on the word \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d in isolation, however, but follows \xe2\x80\x9cthe cardinal rule that statutory language must be read in context.\xe2\x80\x9d General Dynamics Land Systems, Inc. v. Cline, 540 U. S. ___, ___. In the TIA and tax law generally, an assessment is closely tied to the collection of a tax, i.e., the assessment is the official recording of liability that triggers levy and collection efforts. Complementing the cardinal rule just stated, the rule against superfluities instructs courts to interpret a statute to effectuate all its provisions, so that no part is rendered superfluous. If, as the Director asserts, the term \xe2\x80\x9cassessment,\xe2\x80\x9d by itself, signified the entire taxing plan, the TIA would not need the words \xe2\x80\x9clevy\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccollection\xe2\x80\x9d; the term \xe2\x80\x9cassessment,\xe2\x80\x9d alone, would do all the necessary work. In briefing United States v. Galletti, 541 U. S. ___, the Government made clear that, under the IRC definition, an \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d serves as the trigger for levy and collection efforts. The Government did not describe \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d as synonymous with the entire taxation plan, nor disassociate the word from the company (\xe2\x80\x9clevy or collection\xe2\x80\x9d) it keeps. Instead, and in accord with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s understanding, the Government related \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d to the term\xe2\x80\x99s collection-propelling function. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Congress modeled \xc2\xa71341 on earlier federal statutes of similar import, which in turn paralleled state provisions proscribing state-court actions to enjoin the collection of state and local taxes. Congress drew particularly on the Anti-Injunction Act (AIA), which bars \xe2\x80\x9cany court\xe2\x80\x9d from entertaining a suit brought \xe2\x80\x9cfor the purpose of restraining the assessment or collection of any [federal] tax.\xe2\x80\x9d 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77421(a). This Court has recognized, from the AIA\xe2\x80\x99s text, that the measure serves twin purposes: It responds to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s need to assess and collect taxes expeditiously with a minimum of preenforcement judicial interference; and it requires that the legal right to disputed sums be determined in a refund suit. E.g., Bob Jones Univ. v. Simon, 416 U. S. 725, 736. Lower federal courts have similarly comprehended \xc2\xa77421(a). Just as the AIA shields federal tax collections from federal-court injunctions, so the TIA shields state tax collections from federal-court restraints. In both 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77421(a) and 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341, Congress directed taxpayers to pursue refund suits instead of attempting to restrain collections. Third-party suits not seeking to stop the collection (or contest the validity) of a tax imposed on plaintiffs were outside Congress\xe2\x80\x99 purview. The TIA\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history shows that, in enacting the statute, Congress focused on taxpayers who sought to avoid paying their state tax bill by pursuing a challenge route other than the one specified by the taxing authority. Nowhere does the history announce a sweeping congressional direction to prevent federal-court interference with all aspects of state tax administration. The foregoing understanding of the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s purposes and legislative history underpins this Court\xe2\x80\x99s previous applications of that statute. See, e.g., California v. Grace Brethren Church, 457 U. S. 393, 408\xe2\x80\x93409. Id., at 410, distinguished. Contrary to the Director\xe2\x80\x99s assertion, Arkansas v. Farm Credit Servs. of Central Ark., 520 U. S. 821; National Private Truck Council, Inc. v. Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 515 U. S. 582; Fair Assessment in Real Estate Assn., Inc. v. McNary, 454 U. S. 100; and Rosewell v. LaSalle Nat. Bank, 450 U. S. 503, do not hold that state tax administration matters must be kept entirely free from lower federal-court \xe2\x80\x9cinterference.\xe2\x80\x9d Like Grace Brethren Church, all of those cases fall within \xc2\xa71341\xe2\x80\x99s undisputed compass: All involved plaintiffs who mounted federal litigation to avoid paying state taxes (or to gain a refund of such taxes). Federal-court relief, therefore, would have operated to reduce the flow of state tax revenue. Those decisions are not fairly portrayed cut loose from their secure, state-revenue-protective moorings. See, e.g., Grace Brethren Church, 457 U. S., at 410. This Court has interpreted and applied the TIA only in cases Congress wrote the statute to address, i.e., cases in which state taxpayers seek federal-court orders enabling them to avoid paying state taxes. The Court has read harmoniously the \xc2\xa71341 instruction conditioning the jurisdictional bar on the availability of \xe2\x80\x9ca plain, speedy and efficient remedy\xe2\x80\x9d in state court. The remedy inspected in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions was not designed for the universe of plaintiffs who sue the State, but was tailor-made for taxpayers. See, e.g., id., at 411. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0In other federal courts as well, \xc2\xa71341 has been read to restrain taxpayers from instituting federal actions to contest their liability for state taxes, but not to stop third parties from pursuing constitutional challenges to state tax benefits in a federal forum. Further, numerous federal-court decisions\xe2\x80\x94including decisions of this Court reviewing lower federal-court judgments\xe2\x80\x94have reached the merits of third-party constitutional challenges to tax benefits without mentioning the TIA. See, e.g., Byrne v. Public Funds for Public Schools of New Jersey, 442 U. S. 907; Griffin v. School Bd. of Prince Edward Cty., 377 U. S. 218. Consistent with the decades-long understanding prevailing on this issue, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suit may proceed without any TIA impediment. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

J. ELLIOTT HIBBS, DIRECTOR, ARIZONA DEPART-\nMENT OF REVENUE, PETITIONER v.\nKATHLEEN M. WINN et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Arizona law authorizes income-tax credits for payments to organizations that award educational scholarships and tuition grants to children attending private schools. See Ariz. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 (West Supp. 2003). Plaintiffs below, respondents here, brought an action in federal court challenging \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089, and seeking to enjoin its operation, on Establishment Clause grounds. The question presented is whether the Tax Injunction Act (TIA or Act), 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341, which prohibits a lower federal court from restraining \xe2\x80\x9cthe assessment, levy or collection of any tax under State law,\xe2\x80\x9d bars the suit. Plaintiffs-respondents do not contest their own tax liability. Nor do they seek to impede Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s receipt of tax revenues. Their suit, we hold, is not the kind \xc2\xa71341 proscribes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In decisions spanning a near half century, courts in the federal system, including this Court, have entertained challenges to tax credits authorized by state law, without conceiving of \xc2\xa71341 as a jurisdictional barrier. On this first occasion squarely to confront the issue, we confirm the authority federal courts exercised in those cases.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is hardly ancient history that States, once bent on maintaining racial segregation in public schools, and allocating resources disproportionately to benefit white students to the detriment of black students, fastened on tui-\ntion grants and tax credits as a promising means to circumvent Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U. S. 483 (1954). The federal courts, this Court among them, adjudicated the ensuing challenges, instituted under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, and upheld the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s equal protection requirement. See, e.g., Griffin v. School Bd. of Prince Edward Cty., 377 U. S. 218, 233 (1964) (faced with unconstitutional closure of county public schools and tuition grants and tax credits for contributions to private segregated schools, District Court could require county to levy taxes to fund nondiscriminatory public schools), rev\xe2\x80\x99g 322 F.\xc2\xa02d 332, 343\xe2\x80\x93344 (CA4 1963) (abstention required until state courts determine validity of grants, tax credits, and public-school closing), aff\xe2\x80\x99g Allen v. County School Bd. of Prince Edward Cty., 198 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 497, 503 (ED Va. 1961) (county enjoined from paying grants or providing tax credits to support private schools that exclude students based on race while public schools remain closed), and aff\xe2\x80\x99g 207 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 349, 355 (ED Va. 1962) (closure of public schools enjoined). See also Moton v. Lambert, 508 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 367, 368 (ND Miss. 1981) (challenge to tax exemptions for racially discriminatory private schools may proceed in federal court).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the instant case, petitioner Hibbs, Director of Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s Department of Revenue, argues, in effect, that we and other federal courts were wrong in those civil-rights cases. The TIA, petitioner maintains, trumps \xc2\xa71983; the Act, according to petitioner, bars all lower federal-court interference with state tax systems, even when the challengers are not endeavoring to avoid a tax imposed on them, and no matter whether the State\xe2\x80\x99s revenues would be raised or lowered should the plaintiffs prevail. The alleged jurisdictional bar, which petitioner asserts has existed since the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment in 1937, was not even imagined by the jurists in the pathmarking civil-rights cases just cited, or by the defendants in those cases, litigants with every interest in defeating federal-court adjudicatory authority. Our prior decisions command no respect, petitioner urges, because they constitute mere \xe2\x80\x9csub silentio holdings.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioner 8. We reject that assessment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We examine in this opinion both the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d as used in the TIA, and the question whether the Act was intended to insulate state tax laws from constitutional challenge in lower federal courts even when the suit would have no negative impact on tax collection. Concluding that this suit implicates neither \xc2\xa71341\xe2\x80\x99s conception of assessment nor any of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s underlying purposes, we affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs-respondents, Arizona taxpayers, filed suit in the United States District Court for the District of Arizona, challenging Ariz. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 (West Supp. 2003) as incompatible with the Establishment Clause. Section 43\xe2\x80\x931089 provides a credit to taxpayers who contribute money to \xe2\x80\x9cschool tuition organizations\xe2\x80\x9d (STOs). An STO is a nonprofit organization that directs moneys, in the form of scholarship grants, to students enrolled in private elementary or secondary schools. STOs must disburse as scholarship grants at least 90 percent of contributions received, may allow donors to direct scholarships to individual students, may not allow donors to name their own dependents, must designate at least two schools whose students will receive funds, and must not designate schools that \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate on the basis of race, color, handicap, familial status or national origin.\xe2\x80\x9d See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089(D)\xe2\x80\x93(F). STOs are not precluded by Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s statute from designating schools that provide religious instruction or that give admissions preference on the basis of religion or religious affiliation. When taxpayers donate money to a qualified STO, \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 allows them, in calculating their Arizona tax liability, to credit up to $500 of their donation (or $625 for a married couple filing jointly, \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089(A)(2)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In effect, \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 gives Arizona taxpayers an election. They may direct $500 (or, for joint-return filers, $625) to an STO, or to the Arizona Department of Revenue. As long as donors do not give STOs more than their total tax liability, their $500 or $625 contributions are costless.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Arizona Supreme Court, by a 3-to-2 vote, rejected a facial challenge to \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 before the statute went into effect. Kotterman v. Killian, 193 Ariz. 273, 972 P.\xc2\xa02d 606 (1999) (en banc). That case took the form of a special discretionary action invoking the court\xe2\x80\x99s original jurisdiction. See id., at 277, 972 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 610. Kotterman, it is undisputed, has no preclusive effect on the instant as-applied challenge to \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 brought by different plaintiffs.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 federal-court complaint against the Director of Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s Department of Revenue (Director) alleged that \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 \xe2\x80\x9cauthorizes the formation of agencies that have as their sole purpose the distribution of State funds to children of a particular religious denomination or to children attending schools of a particular religious denomination.\xe2\x80\x9d Complaint \xc2\xb6 ;13, App. 10. Respondents sought injunctive and declaratory relief, and an order requiring STOs to pay funds still in their possession \xe2\x80\x9cinto the state general fund.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7\xe2\x80\x938, App. 15.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Director moved to dismiss the action, relying on the TIA, which reads in its entirety:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe district courts shall not enjoin, suspend or restrain the assessment, levy or collection of any tax under State law where a plain, speedy and efficient remedy may be had in the courts of such State.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341.

\n

\nThe Director did not assert that a federal-court order enjoining \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 would interfere with the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax levy or collection efforts. He urged only that a federal injunction would restrain the \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d of taxes \xe2\x80\x9cunder State law.\xe2\x80\x9d Agreeing with the Director, the District Court held that the TIA required dismissal of the suit. App. to Pet. for Cert. 31.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed, holding that \xe2\x80\x9ca federal action challenging the granting of a state tax credit is not prohibited by the [TIA].\xe2\x80\x9d Winn v. Killian, 307 F.\xc2\xa03d 1011, 1017 (2002). Far from \xe2\x80\x9cadversely affect[ing] the state\xe2\x80\x99s ability to raise revenue,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals observed, \xe2\x80\x9cthe relief requested by [respondents] \xe2\x80\xa6 would result in the state\xe2\x80\x99s receiving more funds that could be used for the public benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1017, 1018. We granted certiorari, 539 U. S. 986 (2003), in view of the division of opinion on whether the TIA bars constitutional challenges to state tax credits in federal court. Compare 307 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1017, with ACLU Foundation v. Bridges, 334 F.\xc2\xa03d 416, 421\xe2\x80\x93423 (CA5 2003) (TIA bars federal action seeking to have any part of a State\xe2\x80\x99s tax system declared unconstitutional). We now affirm the judgment of the Ninth Circuit.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before reaching the merits of this case, we must address respondents\xe2\x80\x99 contention that the Director\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari was jurisdictionally untimely under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72101(c) and our Rules. See Brief in Opposition 8\xe2\x80\x9313. Section 2101(c) instructs that a petition for certiorari must be filed \xe2\x80\x9cwithin ninety days after the entry of \xe2\x80\xa6 judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d This Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 13(3) elaborates:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe time to file a petition for a writ of certiorari runs from the date of entry of the judgment or order sought to be reviewed, and not from the issuance date of the mandate (or its equivalent under local practice). But if a petition for rehearing is timely filed in the lower court by any party, the time to file the petition for a writ of certiorari for all parties (whether or not they requested rehearing or joined in the petition for rehearing) runs from the date of the denial of the petition for rehearing or, if the petition for rehearing is granted, the subsequent entry of judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nRespondents assert that the Director\xe2\x80\x99s petition missed the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s deadlines: More than 90 days elapsed between the date the Court of Appeals first entered judgment and the date the petition was filed, rendering the filing untimely under the first sentence of the Rule; and because no party petitioned for rehearing, the extended periods prescribed by the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s second sentence never came into play.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case, however, did not follow the typical course. The Court of Appeals, on its own motion, recalled its mandate and ordered the parties to brief the question whether the case should be reheard en banc. That order, we conclude, suspended the judgment\xe2\x80\x99s finality under \xc2\xa72101(c), just as a timely filed rehearing petition would, or a court\xe2\x80\x99s appropriate decision to consider a late-filed rehearing petition. Compare Young v. Harper, 520 U. S. 143, 147, n. 1 (1997) (appeals court agreed to consider a late-filed rehearing petition; timeliness of petition for certiorari measured from date court disposed of rehearing petition), with Missouri v. Jenkins, 495 U. S. 33, 49 (1990) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe time for applying for certiorari will not be tolled when it appears that the lower court granted rehearing or amended its order solely for the purpose of extending that time.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A timely rehearing petition, a court\xe2\x80\x99s appropriate decision to entertain an untimely rehearing petition, and a court\xe2\x80\x99s direction, on its own initiative, that the parties address whether rehearing should be ordered share this key characteristic: All three raise the question whether the court will modify the judgment and alter the parties\xe2\x80\x99 rights. See id., at 46 (\xe2\x80\x9cA timely petition for rehearing \xe2\x80\xa6 operates to suspend the finality of the \xe2\x80\xa6 court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, pending the court\xe2\x80\x99s further determination whether the judgment should be modified so as to alter its adjudication of the rights of the parties\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Department of Banking of Neb. v. Pink, 317 U. S. 264, 266 (1942) (per curiam) (alterations in original))). In other words, \xe2\x80\x9cwhile [a] petition for rehearing is pending,\xe2\x80\x9d or while the court is considering, on its own initiative, whether rehearing should be ordered, \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no \xe2\x80\x98judgment\xe2\x80\x99 to be reviewed.\xe2\x80\x9d Jenkins, 495 U. S., at 46.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this light, we hold that the Director\xe2\x80\x99s petition for a writ of certiorari was timely. When the Court of Appeals ordered briefing on the rehearing issue, 90 days had not yet passed from the issuance of the panel opinion. Because \xc2\xa72101(c)\xe2\x80\x99s 90-day limit had not yet expired, the clock could still be reset by an order that left unresolved whether the court would modify its judgment. The court-initiated briefing order had just that effect. Because a genuinely final judgment is critical under the statute, we must treat the date of the court\xe2\x80\x99s order denying rehearing en banc as the date judgment was entered. The petition was filed within 90 days of that date and was thus timely under the statute.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Were we to read Rule 13 as our sole guide, so that only a rehearing petition filed by a party could reset the statute\xe2\x80\x99s 90-day count, we would lose sight of the congressional objective underpinning \xc2\xa72101(c): An appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s final adjudication, Congress indicated, marks the time from which the period allowed for a certiorari petition begins to run. The statute takes priority over the \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural rules adopted by the Court for the orderly transaction of its business.\xe2\x80\x9d Schacht v. United States, 398 U. S. 58, 64 (1970). When court-created rules fail to anticipate unusual circumstances that fit securely within a federal statute\xe2\x80\x99s compass, the statute controls our decision. See, e.g., Kontrick v. Ryan, 540 U. S. ___, ___ (2004) (slip op., at 9) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[I]t is axiomatic\xe2\x80\x99 that [court-prescribed procedural rules] \xe2\x80\x98do not create or withdraw federal jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Owen Equipment & Erection Co. v. Kroger, 437 U. S. 365, 370 (1978))). Because the petition for a writ of certiorari was timely under \xc2\xa72101(c), we have jurisdiction to decide whether the TIA bars respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suit.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To determine whether this litigation falls within the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition, it is appropriate, first, to identify the relief sought. Respondents seek prospective relief only. Specifically, their complaint requests \xe2\x80\x9cinjunctive relief prohibiting [the Director] from allowing taxpayers to utilize the tax credit authorized by A.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0S. \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 for payments made to STOs that make tuition grants to children attending religious schools, to children attending schools of only one religious denomination, or to children selected on the basis of their religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Complaint 7, App. 15. Respondents further ask for a \xe2\x80\x9cdeclaration that A.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0S. \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x93\n1089, on its face and as applied,\xe2\x80\x9d violates the Establishment Clause \xe2\x80\x9cby affirmatively authorizing STOs to use State income-tax revenues to pay tuition for students attending religious schools or schools that discriminate on the basis of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Finally, respondents seek \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n order that [the Director] inform all [such] STOs that \xe2\x80\xa6 all funds in their possession as of the date of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s order must be paid into the state general fund.\xe2\x80\x9d Complaint 7\xe2\x80\x938, App. 15. Taking account of the prospective nature of the relief requested, does respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suit, in 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341\xe2\x80\x99s words, seek to \xe2\x80\x9cenjoin, suspend or restrain the assessment, levy or collection of any tax under State law\xe2\x80\x9d? The answer to that question turns on the meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d as employed in the TIA.[Footnote 1]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As used in the Internal Revenue Code (IRC), the term \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d involves a \xe2\x80\x9crecording\xe2\x80\x9d of the amount the taxpayer owes the Government. 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76203. The \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cessentially a bookkeeping notation.\xe2\x80\x9d Laing v. United States, 423 U. S. 161, 170, n.\xc2\xa013 (1976). Section 6201(a) of the IRC authorizes the Secretary of the Treasury \xe2\x80\x9cto make \xe2\x80\xa6 assessments of all taxes \xe2\x80\xa6 imposed by this title.\xe2\x80\x9d An assessment is made \xe2\x80\x9cby recording the liability of the taxpayer in the office of the Secretary in accordance with rules or regulations prescribed by the Secretary.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa76203.[Footnote 2] See also M. Saltzman, IRS Practice and Procedure \xc2\xb6 ;10.02, pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x934 to 10\xe2\x80\x937 (2d ed. 1991) (when Internal Revenue Service signs \xe2\x80\x9csummary list\xe2\x80\x9d of assessment to record amount of tax liability, \xe2\x80\x9cthe official act of assessment has occurred for purposes of the Code\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not focus on the word \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d in isolation, however. Instead, we follow \xe2\x80\x9cthe cardinal rule that statutory language must be read in context [since] a phrase gathers meaning from the words around it.\xe2\x80\x9d General Dynamics Land Systems, Inc. v. Cline, 540 U. S. ___, ___ (2004) (slip op., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314) (internal quotation marks omitted). In \xc2\xa71341 and tax law generally, an assessment is closely tied to the collection of a tax, i.e., the assessment is the official recording of liability that triggers levy and collection efforts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The rule against superfluities complements the principle that courts are to interpret the words of a statute in context. See 2A N. Singer, Statutes and Statutory Construction \xc2\xa746.06, pp.\xc2\xa0181\xe2\x80\x93186 (rev. 6th ed. 2000) (\xe2\x80\x9cA statute should be construed so that effect is given to all its provisions, so that no part will be inoperative or superfluous, void or insignificant \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d (footnotes omitted)). If, as the Director asserts, the term \xe2\x80\x9cassessment,\xe2\x80\x9d by itself, signified \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he entire plan or scheme fixed upon for charging or taxing,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 12 (quoting Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary of the English Language 166 (2d ed. 1934)), the TIA would not need the words \xe2\x80\x9clevy\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccollection\xe2\x80\x9d; the term \xe2\x80\x9cassessment,\xe2\x80\x9d alone, would do all the necessary work.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Earlier this Term, in United States v. Galletti, 541 U. S. ___ (2004), the Government identified \xe2\x80\x9ctwo important consequences\xe2\x80\x9d that follow from the IRS\xe2\x80\x99 timely tax assessment: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he IRS may employ administrative enforcement methods such as tax liens and levies to collect the outstanding tax,\xe2\x80\x9d see 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa76321\xe2\x80\x936327, 6331\xe2\x80\x936344; and \xe2\x80\x9cthe time within which the IRS may collect the tax either administratively or by a \xe2\x80\x98proceeding in court\xe2\x80\x99 is extended [from 3 years] to 10 years after the date of assessment,\xe2\x80\x9d see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa76501(a), 6502(a). Brief for United States in United States v. Galletti, O. T. 2003, No. 02\xe2\x80\x931389, pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x93\n16. The Government thus made clear in briefing Galletti that, under the IRC definition, the tax \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d serves as the trigger for levy and collection efforts. The Government did not describe the term as synonymous with the entire plan of taxation. Nor did it disassociate the word \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d from the company (\xe2\x80\x9clevy or collection\xe2\x80\x9d) that word keeps.[Footnote 4] Instead, and in accord with our understanding, the Government related \xe2\x80\x9cassessment\xe2\x80\x9d to the term\xe2\x80\x99s collection-propelling function.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress modeled \xc2\xa71341 upon earlier federal \xe2\x80\x9cstatutes of similar import,\xe2\x80\x9d laws that, in turn, paralleled state provisions proscribing \xe2\x80\x9cactions in State courts to enjoin the collection of State and county taxes.\xe2\x80\x9d S. Rep. No. 1035, 75th Cong., 1st Sess., 1 (1937) (hereinafter S.\xc2\xa0Rep.). In composing the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s text, Congress drew particularly on an 1867 measure, sometimes called the Anti-Injunction Act (AIA), which bars \xe2\x80\x9cany court\xe2\x80\x9d from entertaining a suit brought \xe2\x80\x9cfor the purpose of restraining the assessment or collection of any [federal] tax.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Mar. 2, 1867, ch. 169, \xc2\xa710, 14 Stat. 475, now codified at 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77421(a). See Jefferson County v. Acker, 527 U. S. 423, 434\xe2\x80\x93435 (1999). While \xc2\xa77421(a) \xe2\x80\x9capparently has no recorded legislative history,\xe2\x80\x9d Bob Jones Univ. v. Simon, 416 U. S. 725, 736 (1974), the Court has recognized, from the AIA\xe2\x80\x99s text, that the measure serves twin purposes: It responds to \xe2\x80\x9cthe Government\xe2\x80\x99s need to assess and collect taxes as expeditiously as possible with a minimum of preenforcement judicial interference\xe2\x80\x9d; and it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98require[s] that the legal right to the disputed sums be determined in a suit for refund,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (quoting Enochs v. Williams Packing & Nav. Co., 370 U. S. 1, 7 (1962)).[Footnote 5] Lower federal courts have similarly comprehended \xc2\xa77421(a). See, e.g., McGlotten v. Connally, 338 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 448, 453\xe2\x80\x93454 (DC 1972) (three-judge court) (\xc2\xa77421(a) does not bar action seeking to enjoin income-tax exemptions to fraternal orders that exclude nonwhites from membership, for in such an action, plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not contest the amount of his own tax, nor does he seek to limit the amount of tax revenue collectible by the United States\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)); Tax Analysts and Advocates v. Shultz, 376 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 889, 892 (DC 1974) (\xc2\xa77421(a) does not bar challenge to IRS revenue ruling allowing contributors to political candidate committees to avoid federal gift tax on contributions in excess of $3,000 ceiling; while \xc2\xa77421(a) \xe2\x80\x9cprecludes suits to restrain the assessment or collection of taxes,\xe2\x80\x9d the proscription does not apply when \xe2\x80\x9cplaintiffs seek not to restrain the Commissioner from collect-\ning taxes, but rather to require him to collect additional taxes according to the mandates of the law.\xe2\x80\x9d (emphases in original)).[Footnote 6]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Just as the AIA shields federal tax collections from federal-court injunctions, so the TIA shields state tax collections from federal-court restraints. In both 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77421(a) and 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341, Congress directed taxpayers to pursue refund suits instead of attempting to restrain collections. Third-party suits not seeking to stop the collection (or contest the validity) of a tax imposed on plaintiffs, as McGlotten, 338 F.\xc2\xa0Supp., at 453\xe2\x80\x93454, and Tax Analysts, 376 F.\xc2\xa0Supp., at 892, explained, were outside Congress\xe2\x80\x99 purview. The TIA\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history is not silent in this regard. The Act was designed expressly to restrict \xe2\x80\x9cthe jurisdiction of the district courts of the United States over suits relating to the collection of State taxes.\xe2\x80\x9d S. Rep., p.\xc2\xa01.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Specifically, the Senate Report commented that the Act had two closely related, state-revenue-protective objectives: (1) to eliminate disparities between taxpayers who could seek injunctive relief in federal court\xe2\x80\x94usually out-of-state corporations asserting diversity jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x94and taxpayers with recourse only to state courts, which generally required taxpayers to pay first and litigate later; and (2) to stop taxpayers, with the aid of a federal injunction, from withholding large sums, thereby disrupting state government finances. Id., at 1\xe2\x80\x932; see R. Fallon, D. Meltzer, & D. Shapiro, Hart and Wechsler\xe2\x80\x99s The Federal Courts and the Federal System 1173 (5th ed. 2003) (citing Rosewell v. LaSalle Nat. Bank, 450 U. S. 503, 522\xe2\x80\x93523, and nn.\xc2\xa028\xe2\x80\x9329, 527 (1981)). See also Jefferson County, 527 U. S., at 435 (observing that the TIA was \xe2\x80\x9cshaped by state and federal provisions barring anticipatory actions by taxpayers to stop the tax collector from initiating collection proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d). In short, in enacting the TIA, Congress trained its attention on taxpayers who sought to avoid paying their tax bill by pursuing a challenge route other than the one specified by the taxing authority. Nowhere does the legislative history announce a sweeping congressional direction to prevent \xe2\x80\x9cfederal-court interference with all aspects of state tax administration.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 20; post, at 11.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The understanding of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s purposes and legislative history set out above underpins this Court\xe2\x80\x99s previous applications of the TIA. In California v. Grace Brethren Church, 457 U. S. 393 (1982), for example, we recognized that the principal purpose of the TIA was to \xe2\x80\x9climit drastically\xe2\x80\x9d federal-court interference with \xe2\x80\x9cthe collection of [state] taxes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 408\xe2\x80\x93409 (quoting Rosewell, 450 U. S., at 522). True, the Court referred to the disruption of \xe2\x80\x9cstate tax administration,\xe2\x80\x9d but it did so specifically in relation to the \xe2\x80\x9cthe collection of revenue.\xe2\x80\x9d 457 U. S., at 410 (quoting Perez v. Ledesma, 401 U. S. 82, 128, n. 17 (1971) (Brennan, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part)). The complainants in Grace Brethren Church were several California churches and religious schools. They sought federal-court relief from an unemployment compensation tax that state law imposed on them. 457 U. S., at 398. Their federal action, which bypassed state remedies, was exactly what the TIA was designed to ward off. The Director and the dissent endeavor to reconstruct Grace Brethren Church as precedent for the proposition that the TIA totally immunizes from lower federal-court review \xe2\x80\x9call aspects of state tax administration, and not just interference with the collection of revenue.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 20; see post, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. The endeavor is unavailing given the issue before the Court in Grace Brethren Church and the context in which the words \xe2\x80\x9cstate tax administration\xe2\x80\x9d appear.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Director invokes several other decisions alleged to keep matters of \xe2\x80\x9cstate tax administration\xe2\x80\x9d entirely free from lower federal-court \xe2\x80\x9cinterference.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 17\xe2\x80\x9321; accord post, at 13. Like Grace Brethren Church, all of them fall within \xc2\xa71341\xe2\x80\x99s undisputed compass: All involved plaintiffs who mounted federal litigation to avoid paying state taxes (or to gain a refund of such taxes). Federal-court relief, therefore, would have operated to reduce the flow of state tax revenue. See Arkansas v. Farm Credit Servs. of Central Ark., 520 U. S. 821, 824 (1997) (corporations chartered under federal law claimed exemption from Arkansas sales and income taxation); National Private Truck Council, Inc. v. Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 515 U. S. 582, 584 (1995) (action seeking to prevent Oklahoma from collecting taxes State imposed on nonresident motor carriers); Fair Assessment in Real Estate Assn., Inc. v. McNary, 454 U. S. 100, 105\xe2\x80\x93106 (1981) (taxpayers, alleging unequal taxation of real property, sought, inter alia, damages measured by alleged tax overassessments); Rosewell, 450 U. S., at 510 (state taxpayer, alleging her property was inequitably assessed, refused to pay state taxes).[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our prior decisions are not fairly portrayed cut loose from their secure, state-revenue-protective moorings. See, e.g., Grace Brethren Church, 457 U. S., at 410 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf federal declaratory relief were available to test state tax assessments, state tax administration might be thrown into disarray, and taxpayers might escape the ordinary procedural requirements imposed by state law. During the pendency of the federal suit the collection of revenue under the challenged law might be obstructed, with consequent damage to the State\xe2\x80\x99s budget, and perhaps a shift to the State of the risk of taxpayer insolvency.\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Ledesma, 401 U. S., at 128, n. 17 (Brennan, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (emphases added))); Rosewell, 450 U. S., at 527\xe2\x80\x93\n528 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe compelling nature of these considerations\n[identified by Justice Brennan in Perez] is underscored by the dependency of state budgets on the receipt of local tax revenues\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. We may readily appreciate the difficulties encountered by the county should a substantial portion of its rightful tax revenue be tied up in injunction actions.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, this Court has interpreted and applied the TIA only in cases Congress wrote the Act to address, i.e., cases in which state taxpayers seek federal-court orders enabling them to avoid paying state taxes. See supra, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. We have read harmoniously the \xc2\xa71341 instruction conditioning the jurisdictional bar on the availability of \xe2\x80\x9ca plain, speedy and efficient remedy\xe2\x80\x9d in state court. The remedy inspected in our decisions was not one designed for the universe of plaintiffs who sue the State. Rather, it was a remedy tailor-made for taxpayers. See, e.g., Rosewell, 450 U. S., at 528 (\xe2\x80\x9cIllinois\xe2\x80\x99 legal remedy that provides property owners paying property taxes under protest a refund without interest in two years is a \xe2\x80\x98plain, speedy and efficient remedy\xe2\x80\x99 under the [TIA]\xe2\x80\x9d); Grace Brethren Church, 457 U. S., at 411 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] state-court remedy is \xe2\x80\x98plain, speedy and efficient\xe2\x80\x99 only if it \xe2\x80\x98provides the taxpayer with a \xe2\x80\x98full hearing and judicial determination\xe2\x80\x99 at which she may raise any and all constitutional objections to the tax.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Rosewell, 450 U. S., at 514)).[Footnote 10]

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In other federal courts as well, \xc2\xa71341 has been read to restrain state taxpayers from instituting federal actions to contest their liability for state taxes, but not to stop third parties from pursuing constitutional challenges to tax benefits in a federal forum. Relevant to the distinction between taxpayer claims that would reduce state revenues and third-party claims that would enlarge state receipts, Seventh Circuit Judge Easterbrook wrote trenchantly:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cAlthough the district court concluded that \xc2\xa71341 applies to any federal litigation touching on the subject of state taxes, neither the language nor the legislative history of the statute supports this interpretation. The text of \xc2\xa71341 does not suggest that federal courts should tread lightly in issuing orders that might allow local governments to raise additional taxes. The legislative history \xe2\x80\xa6 shows that \xc2\xa71341 is designed to ensure that federal courts do not interfere with states\xe2\x80\x99 collection of taxes, so long as the taxpayers have an opportunity to present to a court federal defenses to the imposition and collection of the taxes. The legislative history is filled with concern that federal judgments were emptying state coffers and that corporations with access to the diversity jurisdiction could obtain remedies unavailable to resident taxpayers. There was no articulated concern about federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 flogging state and local governments to collect additional taxes.\xe2\x80\x9d Dunn v. Carey, 808 F.\xc2\xa02d 555, 558 (1986) (emphasis added).

\n

\nSecond Circuit Judge Friendly earlier expressed a similar view of \xc2\xa71341:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe [TIA\xe2\x80\x99s] context and the legislative history lead us to conclude that, in speaking of \xe2\x80\x98collection,\xe2\x80\x99 Congress was referring to methods similar to assessment and levy, e.g., distress or execution \xe2\x80\xa6 that would produce money or other property directly, rather than indirectly through a more general use of coercive power. Congress was thinking of cases where taxpayers were repeatedly using the federal courts to raise questions of state or federal law going to the validity of the particular taxes imposed upon them\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Wells v. Malloy, 510 F.\xc2\xa02d 74, 77 (1975) (emphasis added).

\n

\nSee also In\xc2\xa0re Jackson County, 834 F.\xc2\xa02d 150, 151\xe2\x80\x93152 (CA8 1987) (observing that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa71341 has been held to be inapplicable to efforts to require collection of additional taxes, as opposed to efforts to inhibit the collection of taxes\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 11]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Further, numerous federal-court decisions\xe2\x80\x94including decisions of this Court reviewing lower federal-court judgments\xe2\x80\x94have reached the merits of third-party constitutional challenges to tax benefits without mentioning the TIA. See, e.g., Byrne v. Public Funds for Public Schools of New Jersey, 442 U. S. 907 (1979), summarily aff\xe2\x80\x99g 590 F.\xc2\xa02d 514 (CA3 1979) (state tax deduction for taxpayers with children attending nonpublic schools violates Establishment Clause), aff\xe2\x80\x99g 444 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 1228 (NJ 1978); Franchise Tax Board of California v. United Americans for Public Schools, 419 U. S. 890 (1974) (summarily affirming district-court judgment striking down state statute that provided income-tax reductions for taxpayers sending children to nonpublic schools); Committee for Public Ed. & Religious Liberty v. Nyquist, 413 U. S. 756 (1973) (state tax benefits for parents of children attending nonpublic schools violates Establishment Clause), rev\xe2\x80\x99g in relevant part 350 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 655 (SDNY 1972) (three-judge court); Grit v. Wolman, 413 U. S. 901 (1973), summarily aff\xe2\x80\x99g Kosydar v. Wolman, 353 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 744, 755\xe2\x80\x93756 (SD Ohio 1972) (three-judge court) (state tax credits for expenses relating to children\xe2\x80\x99s enrollment in nonpublic schools violate Establishment Clause); Finlator v. Powers, 902 F.\xc2\xa02d 1158 (CA4 1990) (state statute exempting Christian Bibles, but not holy books of other religions or other books, from state tax violates Establishment Clause); Luthens v. Bair, 788 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 1032 (SD Iowa 1992) (state law authorizing tax benefit for tuition payments and textbook purchases does not violate Establishment Clause); Minnesota Civil Liberties Union v. Roemer, 452 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 1316 (Minn. 1978) (three-judge court) (state law allowing parents of public or private school students to claim part of tuition and transportation expenses as tax deduction does not violate Establishment Clause).[Footnote 12]

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a procession of cases not rationally distinguishable from this one, no Justice or member of the bar of this Court ever raised a \xc2\xa71341 objection that, according to the petitioner in this case, should have caused us to order dismissal of the action for want of jurisdiction. See Mueller v. Allen, 463 U. S. 388 (1983) (state tax deduction for parents who send their children to parochial schools does not violate Establishment Clause); Byrne, 442 U. S. 907; United Americans for Public Schools, 419 U. S. 890; Committee for Public Ed. & Religious Liberty, 413 U. S. 756; Wolman, 413 U. S. 901; Griffin, 377 U. S. 218. Consistent with the decades-long understanding prevailing on this issue, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suit may proceed without any TIA impediment.[Footnote 13]

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the Court shows great skepticism for the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 ability to vindicate constitutional wrongs. Two points make clear that the Court treats States as diminished and disfavored powers, rather than merely applies statutory text. First, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of the Tax Injunction Act (TIA or Act), 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341, contrasts with a literal reading of its terms. Second, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that legislative histories support the conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hird-party suits not seeking to stop the collection (or contest the validity) of a tax imposed on plaintiffs . . . were outside Congress\xe2\x80\x99 purview\xe2\x80\x9d in enacting the TIA and the anti-injunction provision on which the TIA was modeled, ante, at 12, is not borne out by those sources, as previously recognized by the Court. In light of these points, today\xe2\x80\x99s holding should probably be attributed to the concern the Court candidly shows animates it. See ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (noting it was the federal courts that \xe2\x80\x9cupheld the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s equal protection requirement\xe2\x80\x9d when States circumvented Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U. S. 483 (1954), by manipulating their tax laws). The concern, it seems, is that state courts are second rate constitutional arbiters, unequal to their federal counterparts. State courts are due more respect than this. Dismissive treatment of state courts is particularly unjustified since the TIA, by express terms, provides a federal safeguard: The Act lifts its bar on federal court intervention when state courts fail to provide \xe2\x80\x9ca plain, speedy, and efficient remedy.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71341.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In view of the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s text, the congressional judgment that state courts are qualified constitutional arbiters, and the respect state courts deserve, I disagree with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s superseding the balance the Act strikes between federal and state court adjudication. I agree with the majority that the petition for certiorari was timely under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72101(c), see ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x938, and so submit this respectful dissent on the merits of the decision.

\n

I

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today is the first time the Court has considered\nwhether the TIA bars federal district courts from granting injunctive relief that would prevent States from giving citizens statutorily mandated state tax credits. There are cases, some dating back almost 50 years, which proceeded as if the jurisdictional bar did not apply to tax credit challenges; but some more recent decisions have said the bar is applicable. Compare, e.g., Mueller v. Allen, 463 U. S. 388 (1983); Committee for Public Ed. & Religious Liberty v. Nyquist, 413 U. S. 756 (1973); Griffin v. School Bd. of Prince Edward Cty., 377 U. S. 218 (1964), with, e.g., ACLU Foundation of La. v. Bridges, 334 F.\xc2\xa03d 416 (CA5 2003); In\xc2\xa0re Gillis, 836 F.\xc2\xa02d 1001 (CA6 1988). While unexamined custom favors the first position, the statutory text favors the latter. In these circumstances a careful explanation for the conclusion is necessary; but in the end the scope and purpose of the Act should be understood from its terms alone.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question presented\xe2\x80\x94whether the TIA bars the District Court from granting injunctive relief against the tax credit\xe2\x80\x94requires two inquiries. First, the term assessment, as used in \xc2\xa71341, must be defined. Second, we must determine if an injunction prohibiting the Director from allowing the credit would enjoin, suspend, or restrain an assessment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The word assessment in the TIA is not isolated from its use in another federal statute. The TIA was modeled on the anti-injunction provision of the Internal Revenue Code (Code), 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77421(a). See Jefferson County v. Acker, 527 U. S. 423, 434 (1999). That provision specifies, and has specified since 1867, that federal courts may not restrain or enjoin an \xe2\x80\x9cassessment or collection of any [federal] tax.\xe2\x80\x9d 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77421(a) (first codified by Act of Mar. 2, 1867, ch. 169, \xc2\xa710, 14 Stat. 475). The meaning of the term assessment in this Code provision is discernible by reference to other Code sections. Title 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 et seq.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chapter 63 of Title 26 addresses the subject of assessments and sheds light on the meaning of the term in the Code. Section 6201 first instructs that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Secretary [of the Internal Revenue Service] is . . . required to make the . . . assessments of all taxes . . . imposed by this title. . .\xe2\x80\x9d 26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76201(a). Further it provides, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Secretary shall assess all taxes determined by the taxpayer or by the Secretary . . .\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa76201(a)(1). Section 6203 in turn sets forth a method for making an assessment: \xe2\x80\x9cThe assessment shall be made by recording the liability of the taxpayer in the office of the Secretary.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Taken together, the provisions of Title 26 establish that an assessment, as that term is used in \xc2\xa77421(a), must at the least encompass the recording of a taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate tax liability. This is what the taxpayer owes the Government. See also Laing v. United States, 423 U. S. 161, 170, n. 13 (1976) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe \xe2\x80\x98assessment,\xe2\x80\x99 essentially a bookkeeping notation, is made when the Secretary or his delegate establishes an account against the taxpayer on the tax rolls\xe2\x80\x9d). Whether the Secretary or his delegate (today, the Commissioner) makes the recording on the basis of a taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s self-reported filing form or instead chooses to rely on his own calculation of the taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s liability (e.g., via an audit) is irrelevant. The recording of the liability on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s tax rolls is itself an assessment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The TIA was modeled on the anti-injunction provision, see Jefferson County, supra; it incorporates the same terminology employed by the provision; and it employs that terminology for the same purpose. It is sensible, then, to interpret the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s terms by reference to the Code\xe2\x80\x99s use of the term. Cf. Lorillard v. Pons, 434 U. S. 575, 581 (1978) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]here, as here, Congress adopts a new law incorporating sections of a prior law, Congress normally can be presumed to have had knowledge of the interpretation given to the incorporated law, at least insofar as it affects the new statute\xe2\x80\x9d). The Court of Appeals, which concluded that an assessment was the official estimate of the value of income or property used to calculate a tax or the imposition of a tax on someone, Winn v. Killian, 307 F.\xc2\xa03d 1011, 1015 (CA9 2002), placed principal reliance for its interpretation on a dictionary definition. That was not entirely misplaced; but unless the definition is considered in the context of the prior statute, the advantage of that statute\xe2\x80\x99s interpretive guidance is lost.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, the court defined the term in an unusual way. It relied on a dictionary that was unavailable when the TIA was enacted; it relied not on the definition of the term under consideration, \xe2\x80\x9cassessment,\xe2\x80\x9d but on the definition of the term\xe2\x80\x99s related verb form, \xe2\x80\x9cassess;\xe2\x80\x9d and it examined only a portion of that terms\xe2\x80\x99 definition. In the dictionary used by the Court of Appeals, the verb is defined in two ways not noted by the court. One of the alternative definitions is quite relevant\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c(2) to fix or determine the amount of (damages, a tax, a fine, etc.).\xe2\x80\x9d Compare 307 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1015, with Random House Dictionary of the English Language 90 (1979). Further,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cHad [the panel] looked in a different lay dictionary, [it] would have found a definition contrary to the one it preferred, such as \xe2\x80\x98the entire plan or scheme fixed upon for charging or taxing.\xe2\x80\x99 . . . Had the panel considered tax treatises and law dictionaries . . . it would have found much in accord with this broader definition. . . . Even the federal income tax code supports a broad reading of \xe2\x80\x98assessment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Winn v. Killian, 321 F.\xc2\xa03d 911, 912 (2003) (Kleinfeld, J., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc) (footnote omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Guided first by the Internal Revenue Code, an assessment under \xc2\xa71341, at a minimum, is the recording of taxpayers\xe2\x80\x99 liability on the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax rolls. The TIA, though a federal statute that must be interpreted as a matter of federal law, operates in a state-law context. In this respect, the Act must be interpreted so as to apply evenly to the 50 various state-law regimes and to the various recording schemes States employ. It is therefore irrelevant whether state officials record taxpayer liabilities with their own pen in a specified location, by collecting and maintaining taxpayers\xe2\x80\x99 self-reported filing forms, or in some other manner. The recordkeeping that equates to the determination of taxpayer liability on the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax rolls is the assessment, whatever the method. The Court seems to agree with this. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dictionary definition of assessment provides further relevant information. Contemporaneous dictionaries from the time of the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment define assessment in expansive terms. They would broaden any understanding of the term, and so the Act\xe2\x80\x99s bar. See, e.g., Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 139 (1927) (providing three context relevant definitions for the term assessment: It is the act of apportioning or determining an amount to be paid; a valuation of property for the purpose of taxation; or the entire plan or scheme fixed upon for charging or taxing). See also United States v. Galletti, 541 U. S. \xe2\x80\x94, \xe2\x80\x94 (2004) (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x938) (noting that under the Code the term assessment refers not only to recordings of tax liability but also to \xe2\x80\x9cthe calculation . . . of a tax liability,\xe2\x80\x9d including self-calculation done by the taxpayer). The Court need not decide the full scope of the term assessment in the TIA, however. For present purposes, a narrow definition of the term suffices. Applying the narrowest definition, the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s literal text bars district courts from enjoining, suspending, or restraining a State\xe2\x80\x99s recording of taxpayer liability on its tax rolls, whether the recordings are made by self-reported taxpayer filing forms or by a State\xe2\x80\x99s calculation of taxpayer liability.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The terms \xe2\x80\x9cenjoin, suspend, or restrain\xe2\x80\x9d require little scrutiny. No doubt, they have discrete purposes in the context of the TIA; but they also have a common meaning. They refer to actions that restrict assessments to varying degrees. It is noteworthy that the term \xe2\x80\x9cenjoin\xe2\x80\x9d has not just its meaning in the restrictive sense but also has meaning in an affirmative sense. The Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary current at the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment gives as a definition of the term, \xe2\x80\x9cto require; command; positively direct.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 663 (3d ed. 1933). That definition may well be implicated here, since an order invalidating a tax credit would seem to command States to collect taxes they otherwise would not collect. The parties, however, proceed on the assumption that enjoin means to bar. It is unobjectionable for the Court to make the assumption too, leaving the broader definition for later consideration.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents argue the TIA does not bar the injunction they seek because even after the credit is enjoined, the Director will be able to record and enforce taxpayers\xe2\x80\x99 liabilities. See Brief for Respondents 16. In fact, respondents say, with the credit out of the way the Director will be able to record and enforce a higher level of liability and so profit the State. Ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cThe amount of tax payable by some taxpayers would increase, but that can hardly be characterized as an injunction or restraint of the assessment process\xe2\x80\x9d). The argument, however, ignores an important part of the Act: \xe2\x80\x9cunder State law.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe district courts shall not enjoin, suspend or restrain the assessment . . . of any tax under State law\xe2\x80\x9d). The Act not only bars district courts from enjoining, suspending, or restraining a State\xe2\x80\x99s recording of taxpayer liabilities altogether; but it also bars them from enjoining, suspending, or restraining a State from recording the taxpayer liability that state law mandates.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 43\xe2\x80\x931089 is state law. It is an integral part of the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax statute; it is reflected on state tax forms; and the State Supreme Court has held that it is part of the calculus necessary to determine tax liability. See Kotterman v. Killian, 193 Ariz. 273, 279, 285, 972 P.\xc2\xa02d 606, 612, 618 (1999). A recording of a taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s liability under state law must be made in accordance with \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089. The same can be said with respect to each and every provision of the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax law. To order the Director not to record on the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax rolls taxpayer liability that reflects the operation of \xc2\xa743\xe2\x80\x931089 (or any other state tax law provision for that matter) would be to bar the Director from recording the correct taxpayer liability. The TIA\xe2\x80\x99s language bars this relief and so bars this suit.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court tries to avoid this conclusion by saying that the recordings that constitute assessments under \xc2\xa71341 must have a \xe2\x80\x9ccollection-propelling function,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11, and that the recordings at issue here do not have such a function. See also ante, at 11, n.\xc2\xa04 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he dissent would disconnect the word [assessment] from the enforcement process\xe2\x80\x9d). That is wrong. A recording of taxpayer liability on the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax rolls of course propels collection. In most cases the taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s payment will accompany his filing, and thus will accompany the assessment so that no literal collection of moneys is necessary. As anyone who has paid taxes must know, however, if owed payment were not included with the tax filing, the State\xe2\x80\x99s recording of one\xe2\x80\x99s liability on the State\xe2\x80\x99s rolls would certainly cause subsequent collection efforts, for the filing\xe2\x80\x99s recording (i.e., the assessment) would propel collection by establishing the State\xe2\x80\x99s legal right to the taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s moneys.

\n

II

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority offers prior judicial interpretations of the Code\xe2\x80\x99s similarly worded anti-injunction provision to support its contrary conclusions about the statutory text. See ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. That this Court and other federal courts have allowed nontaxpayer suits challenging tax credits to proceed in the face of the anti-injunction provision is not at all controlling. Those cases are quite distinguishable. Had the plaintiffs in those cases been barred from suit, there would have been no available forum at all for their claims. See McGlotten v. Connally, 338 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 448, 453\xe2\x80\x93454 (DC 1972) (three-judge court) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe preferred course of raising [such tax exemption and deduction] objections in a suit for refund is not available. In this situation we cannot read the statute to bar the present suit\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Tax Analysts and Advocates v. Shultz, 376 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 889, 892 (DC 1974) (\xe2\x80\x9cSince plaintiffs are not seeking to restrain the collection of taxes, and since they cannot obtain relief through a refund suit, [26 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.] \xc2\xa77421(a) does not bar the injunctive relief they seek\xe2\x80\x9d). The Court ratified those decisions only insofar as they relied on this limited rationale as the basis for an exception to the statutory bar on adjudication. See South Carolina v. Regan, 465 U. S. 367, 373 (1984) (holding the anti-injunction provision inapplicable to a State\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to the constitutionality of a federal tax exemption provision, \xc2\xa7103(a) of the Code (which exempts from a taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s gross income the interest earned on the obligations of any State) as amended by \xc2\xa7310(b)(1) of the Tax Equity and Fiscal Responsibility Act of 1982, 96 Stat. 596, because \xe2\x80\x9cthe [anti-injunction provision] was not intended to bar an action where . . . Congress has not provided the plaintiff with an alternative legal way to challenge the validity of a tax\xe2\x80\x9d). Even that strict limitation was not strict enough for four Members of the Court, one of whom noted \xe2\x80\x9cthe broad sweep of the [a]nti-[i]njunction [provision].\xe2\x80\x9d 465 U. S., at 382 (Blackmun, J., concurring in judgment). The other three Justices went further still. They would have allowed an exception to the anti-injunction provision\xe2\x80\x99s literal bar on nontaxpayer suits challenging tax exemption provisions only if due process rights were at stake. See 465 U. S., at 394 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cBob Jones University\xe2\x80\x99s recognition that the complete inaccessibility of judicial review might implicate due process concerns provides absolutely no basis for crafting an exception\xe2\x80\x9d to the anti-injunction act for a plaintiff who has \xe2\x80\x9cno due process right to review of its claim in a judicial forum\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In contrast to the anti-injunction provision, the TIA on its own terms ensures an adequate forum for claims it bars. The TIA specially exempts actions that could not be heard in state courts by providing an exception for instances \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a plain, speedy, and efficient remedy may [not] be had in the courts of [the] State.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341. The TIA\xe2\x80\x99s text thus already incorporates the check that Regan concluded could be read into the anti-injunction provision even though \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he [anti-injunction provision]\xe2\x80\x99s language \xe2\x80\x98could scarcely be more explicit\xe2\x80\x99 in prohibiting nontaxpayer suits like this one.\xe2\x80\x9d 465 U. S., at 385 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment) (quoting Bob Jones Univ. v. Simon, 416 U. S. 725, 736 (1974)). The practical effect is that a literal reading of the TIA provides for federal district courts to stand at the ready where litigants encounter legal or practical obstacles to challenging state tax credits in state courts. And this Court, of course, stands at the ready to review decisions by state courts on these matters.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court does not discuss this codified exception, yet the clause is crucial. It represents a congressional judgment about the balance that should exist between the respect due to the States (for both their administration of tax schemes and their courts\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of tax laws) and the need for constitutional vindication. To ignore the provision is to ignore that Congress has already balanced these interests.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents admit they would be heard in state court. Indeed a quite similar action previously was heard there. See Kotterman v. Killian, 193 Ariz. 273, 972 P.\xc2\xa02d 606 (1999). As a result, the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s exception (akin to that recognized by Regan) does not apply. To proceed as if it does is to replace Congress\xe2\x80\x99 balancing of the noted interests with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court and respondents further argue that the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s policy purposes and relatedly the federal anti-injunction provision\xe2\x80\x99s policy purposes (as discerned from legislative histories) justify today\xe2\x80\x99s holding. The two Acts, they say, reflect a unitary purpose: \xe2\x80\x9cIn both . . . Congress directed taxpayers to pursue refund suits instead of attempting to restrain [tax] collections.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. See also ante, at 14 (concluding that the Act\xe2\x80\x99s underlying purpose is to bar suits by \xe2\x80\x9ctaxpayers who sought to avoid paying their tax bill\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Brief for Respondents 18\xe2\x80\x9320. This purpose, the Court and respondents say, shows that the Act was not intended to foreclose relief in challenges to tax credits. The proposition rests on the premise that the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s sole purpose is to prevent district court orders that would decrease the moneys in state fiscs. Because the legislative histories of the Acts are not carefully limited in the manner that this reading suggests, the policy argument against a literal application of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s terms fails.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Taking the federal anti-injunction provision first, as has been noted before, \xe2\x80\x9c[its] history expressly reflects the congressional desire that all injunctive suits against the tax collector be prohibited.\xe2\x80\x9d Regan, 465 U. S., at 387 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment). The provision responded to \xe2\x80\x9cthe grave dangers which accompany intrusion of the injunctive power of the courts into the administration of the revenue.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 388. It \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally precludes judicial resolution of all abstract tax controversies,\xe2\x80\x9d whether brought by a taxpayer or a nontaxpayer. Id., at 392; see also id., at 387\xe2\x80\x93392 (reviewing the legislative history of the anti-injunction provision, its various amendments, and related enactments). Thus, the provision\xe2\x80\x99s object is not just to bar suits that might \xe2\x80\x9cinterrupt \xe2\x80\x98the process of collecting . . . taxes,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9c[s]imilarly, the language and history evidence a congressional desire to prohibit courts from restraining any aspect of the tax laws\xe2\x80\x99 administration.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 399.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history is also inconsistent with the interpretation of this same history in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s earlier cases. The Court has made clear that the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s purpose is not only to protect the fisc but also to protect the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax system administration and tax policy implementation. California v. Grace Brethren Church, 457 U. S. 393 (1982), is a prime example.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Grace Brethren Church the Court held that the TIA not only bars actions by individuals to stop tax collectors from collecting moneys (i.e., injunctive suits) but also bars declaratory suits. See id., at 408\xe2\x80\x93410. The Court explained that permitting declaratory suits to proceed would \xe2\x80\x9cdefea[t] the principal purpose of the Tax Injunction Act: \xe2\x80\x98to limit drastically federal district court jurisdiction to interfere with so important a local concern as the collection of taxes.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 408\xe2\x80\x93409 (quoting Rosewell v. LaSalle Nat. Bank, 450 U. S. 503 (1981)). It continued:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98If federal declaratory relief were available to test state tax assessments, state tax administration might be thrown into disarray, and taxpayers might escape the ordinary procedural requirements imposed by state law. During the pendency of the federal suit the collection of revenue under the challenged law might be obstructed, with consequent damage to the State\xe2\x80\x99s budget, and perhaps a shift to the State of the risk of taxpayer insolvency. Moreover, federal constitutional issues are likely to turn on questions of state tax law, which, like issues of state regulatory law, are more properly heard in the state courts.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Grace Brethren Church, supra, at 410 (quoting with approval Perez v. Ledesma, 401 U. S. 82, 128, n. 17 (1971) (Brennan, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part)).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While this, of course, demonstrates that protecting the state fisc from damage is part of the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s purpose, it equally shows that actions that would throw the \xe2\x80\x9cstate tax administration . . . into disarray\xe2\x80\x9d also implicate the Act and its purpose. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s concern with preventing administrative disarray puts in context its explanation that the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s principal concern is to limit federal district court interference with the \xe2\x80\x9ccollection of taxes.\xe2\x80\x9d The phrase, in this context, refers to the operation of the whole tax collection system and the implementation of entire tax policy, not just a part of it. While an order interfering with a specific collection suit disrupts one of the most essential aspects of a State\xe2\x80\x99s tax system, it is not the only way in which federal courts can disrupt the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax system:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he legislative history of the Tax Injunction Act demonstrates that Congress worried not so much about the form of relief available in the federal courts, as about divesting the federal courts of jurisdiction to interfere with state tax administration.\xe2\x80\x9d Grace Brethen Church, supra, at 409, n. 22.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions in Fair Assessment in Real Estate Assn., Inc. v. McNary, 454 U. S. 100 (1981), National Private Truck Council, Inc. v. Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 515 U. S. 582 (1995) (NPTC), and Rosewell, supra, make the same point. Though the majority says these cases support its holding because they \xe2\x80\x9cinvolved plaintiffs who mounted federal litigation to avoid paying state taxes,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 14, the language of these cases is too clear to be ignored and is contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding today. In Fair Assessment, the Court observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he [TIA] \xe2\x80\x98has its roots in equity practice, in principles of federalism, and in recognition of the imperative need of a State to administer its own fiscal operations.\xe2\x80\x99 This last consideration was [its] principal motivating force.\xe2\x80\x9d 454 U. S., at 110 (quoting Rosewell, supra, at 522 (in turn quoting Tully v. Griffin, Inc., 429 U. S. 68, 73 (1976)) (other citation omitted)). In NPTC, the Court said, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress and this Court repeatedly have shown an aversion to federal interference with state tax administration. The passage of the [TIA] in 1937 is one manifestation of this aversion.\xe2\x80\x9d 515 U. S., at 586 (summing up this aversion, generated also from principles of comity and federalism, as creating a \xe2\x80\x9cbackground presumption that federal law generally will not interfere with administration of state taxes,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 588). In Rosewell, the Court described the Act\xe2\x80\x99s language as \xe2\x80\x9cbroad\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprophylactic.\xe2\x80\x9d 450 U. S., at 524 (majority opinion of Brennan, J.). See also ibid. (the TIA was \xe2\x80\x9cpassed to limit federal-court interference in state tax matters\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Act is designed to respect not only the administration of state tax systems but also state court authority to say what state law means. \xe2\x80\x9c[F]ederal constitutional issues are likely to turn on questions of state tax law, which, like issues of state regulatory law, are more properly heard in the state courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Grace Brethren Church, supra, at 410 (internal quotation marks omitted). See also Rosewell, supra, at 527. This too establishes that the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s purpose is not solely to ensure that the State\xe2\x80\x99s fisc is not decreased. There would be only a diminished interest in allowing state courts to say what the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax statutes mean if the Act protected just the state fisc. The TIA protects the responsibility of the States and their courts to administer their own tax systems and to be accountable to the citizens of the State for their policies and decisions. The majority objects that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no disagreement to the meaning of\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d state law in this case, ante, at 15, n.\xc2\xa05. As an initial matter, it is not clear that this is a fair conclusion. The litigation in large part turns on what state law requires and whether the product of those requirements violates the Constitution. More to the point, however, even if there were no controversy about the statutory framework the Arizona tax provision creates, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s ruling has implications far beyond this case and will most certainly result in federal courts in other States and in other cases being required to interpret state tax law in order to complete their review of challenges to state tax statutes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our heretofore consistent interpretation of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history to prohibit interference with state tax systems and their administration accords with the direct, broad, and unqualified language of the statute. The Act bars all orders that enjoin, suspend, or restrain the assessment of any tax under state law. In effecting congressional intent we should give full force to simple and broad proscriptions in the statutory language.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the TIA\xe2\x80\x99s language and purpose are comprehensive, arguments based on congressional silence on the question whether the TIA applies to actions that increase moneys a state tax system collects are of no moment. Contra, Winn, 307 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1017\xe2\x80\x931018 (relying on Dunn v. Carey, 808 F.\xc2\xa02d 555, 558 (CA7 1986)); see also ante, at 18 (relying on Dunn). Whatever weight one gives to legislative histories, silence in the legislative record is irrelevant when a plain congressional declaration exists on a matter. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen terms are unambiguous we may not speculate on probabilities of intention.\xe2\x80\x9d Insurance Co. v. Ritchie, 5 Wall. 541, 545 (1867). Here, Congress has said district courts are barred from disrupting the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax operations. It is immaterial whether the State\xe2\x80\x99s collection is raised or lowered. A court order will thwart and replace the State\xe2\x80\x99s chosen tax policy if it causes either result. No authority supports the proposition that a State lacks an interest in reducing its citizens\xe2\x80\x99 tax burden. It is a troubling proposition for this Court to proceed on the assumption that the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in limiting the tax burden on its citizens to that for which its law provides is a secondary policy, deserving of little respect from us.

\n

IV

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The final basis on which both the majority and respondents rest is that years of unexamined habit by litigants and the courts alike have resulted in federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 entertaining challenges to state tax credits. See ante, at 19\xe2\x80\x9320 (citing representative cases). While we should not reverse the course of our unexamined practice lightly, our obligation is to give a correct interpretation of the statute. We are not obliged to maintain the status quo when the status quo is unfounded. The exercise of federal jurisdiction does not and cannot establish jurisdiction. See United States v. L. A. Tucker Truck Lines, Inc., 344 U. S. 33, 37\xe2\x80\x9338 (1952). \xe2\x80\x9c[T]his Court is not bound by a prior exercise of jurisdiction in a case where it was not questioned and it was passed sub silentio.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 38. In this respect, the present case is no different than Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. NRA Political Victory Fund, 513 U. S. 88 (1994). The case presented the question whether we had jurisdiction to consider a certiorari petition filed by the Federal Election Commission (FEC), and not by the Solicitor General on behalf of the FEC. The Court held that it lacked jurisdiction. See id., at 99. Though that answer seemed to contradict the Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior practices, the Court said:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cNor are we impressed by the FEC\xe2\x80\x99s argument that it has represented itself before this Court on several occasions in the past without any question having been raised about its authority to do so . . . . The jurisdiction of this Court was challenged in none of these actions, and therefore the question is an open one before us.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 97 (citations omitted).

\n

\nSee also Will v. Michigan Dept. of State Police, 491 U. S. 58, 63, n. 4 (1989) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[T]his Court has never considered itself bound when a subsequent case finally brings the jurisdictional issue before us.\xe2\x80\x99 Hagans v. Lavine, 415 U. S. 528, 535, n. 5 (1974)\xe2\x80\x9d (alteration in original)). These cases make clear that our failure to consider a question hardly equates to a thing\xe2\x80\x99s being decided. Contra, ante, at 2 (Stevens, J., concurring) (referring to prior silences of the courts with respect to the TIA as stare decisis and settled interpretation). As a consequence, I would follow the statutory language.

\n

* * *

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, \xe2\x80\x9c[n]ontaxpaying associations of taxpayers, and most other nontaxpayers, will now be allowed to sidestep Congress\xe2\x80\x99 policy against [federal] judicial resolution of abstract [state] tax controversies.\xe2\x80\x9d Regan, 465 U. S., at 394 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment). This unfortunate result deprives state courts of the first opportunity to hear such cases and to grant the relief the Constitution requires.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the foregoing reasons, with respect, I dissent.
