Syllabus\n

\n

A private cause of action under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 4 and 16 of the Clayton Act, as amended, does not lie for sales at unreasonably low prices for the purpose of destroying competition or eliminating a competitor, which are forbidden only by \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act. Pp. 355 U. S. 374-382.

\n

(a) Sections 4 and 16 of the Clayton Act permit private actions only for injuries resulting from practices forbidden by the "antitrust laws," as defined in \xc2\xa7 1 of that Act, and that definition, specifying certain Acts not including the Robinson-Patman Act, is exclusive. Pp. 355 U. S. 375-376.

\n

(b) The Robinson-Patman Act shows on its face that \xc2\xa7 3 does not amend the Clayton Act, but stands on its own footing and carries its own sanctions, which are penal in nature. Pp. 355 U. S. 376-380.

\n

(c) Section 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act contains only penal sanctions for violation of its provisions, and, in the absence of a clear expression of congressional intent to the contrary, these sanctions should be considered exclusive, rather than supplemented by civil sanctions of a distinct statute. P. 355 U. S. 377.

\n

(d) A different result is not required by the fact that there is a partial overlap between the price discrimination clauses of \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act and those of \xc2\xa7 2 of the Clayton Act, as amended by \xc2\xa71 of the Robinson-Patman Act. Pp. 355 U. S. 378-379.

\n

(e) A different result is not required by the fact that the United States Code codifies \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act as being among the "antitrust laws" embraced in \xc2\xa7 1 of the Clayton Act, since there was a palpable error in the codification, and the underlying statutes must prevail. Pp. 355 U. S. 379-380.

\n

(f) The conclusion here reached is supported by the legislative history of the Robinson-Patman Act. Pp. 355 U. S. 380-382.

MR. JUSTICE HARLAN delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Petitioner, alleging that it had been injured by respondent\'s sales at unreasonably low prices in violation of \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act, [Footnote 1] 49 Stat. 1526, 15 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 13a, sued the respondent for treble damages and injunctive relief under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 4 and 16 of the Clayton Act, 38 Stat. 730, as amended, 15 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 15, 26. The District Court dismissed the complaint on the ground that the private remedies afforded by \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 4 and 16 of the Clayton Act cannot be based on a violation of \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act. The Court of Appeals affirmed.

Page 355 U. S. 375

238 F.2d 86. We brought the case here, 352 U.S. 1023, to resolve a conflict between the ruling below and a decision of the Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit holding that such a private action does lie. Vance v. Safeway Stores, Inc., 239 F.2d 144.

\n

Sections 4 and 16 of the Clayton Act permit private actions of this kind [Footnote 2] only for injuries resulting from practices forbidden by the "antitrust laws" as defined in \xc2\xa7 1 of the Clayton Act, [Footnote 3] namely: (1) the Sherman Act (Act of July 2, 1890); (2) parts of the Wilson Tariff Act (Act of August 27, 1894); (3) the Act amending the Wilson Tariff Act (Act of February 12, 1913); and (4) the Clayton Act ("this Act"). In light of the much other so-called antitrust legislation enacted prior and subsequent

Page 355 U. S. 376

to the Clayton Act, [Footnote 4] it seems plain that the rule expressio unius exclusio alterius is applicable, and that the definition contained in \xc2\xa7 1 of the Clayton Act is exclusive. Therefore it is of no moment here that the Robinson-Patman Act may be colloquially described as an "antitrust" statute. And since no one claims that \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act can be regarded as an amendment to the Sherman Act or the Wilson Tariff Act, the precise issue before us is whether Congress made that section of the Robinson-Patman Act a part of the Clayton Act, thus making it one of the "antitrust laws" whose violation can lead to the private causes of action authorized by \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 4 and 16. For the reasons stated below we hold that this is not the case. [Footnote 5]

\n

I

\n

The Robinson-Patman Act, consisting of our sections, convincingly shows on its face that \xc2\xa7 3 does not amend the Clayton Act, but stands on its own footing and carries its own sanctions.

\n

The first section of the Act does expressly amend \xc2\xa7 2 of the Clayton Act, which prohibits certain kinds of price discriminations, and allied activities, on the part of those engaged in domestic or territorial commerce. The first paragraph of this section reads:

\n

"That section 2 of the [Clayton Act] . . . is amended to read as follows: . . ."

\n

"* * * *

Page 355 U. S. 377

"

\n

The section then sets forth in haec verba, and within quotation marks, all the provisions of \xc2\xa7 2, as modified by the amending language. 49 Stat. 1526, 15 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 13(a).

\n

Two other sections of the Act are not in point here. Section 2 simply applies the amending provisions of \xc2\xa7 1 to litigation commenced under the former provisions of \xc2\xa7 2 of the Clayton Act, 15 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 21a; and \xc2\xa7 4 deals with certain practices of cooperative associations. 15 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 13b.

\n

The only other section of the Act is \xc2\xa7 3, with which we are concerned here. It prohibits three kinds of trade practices, (a) general price discriminations, (b) geographical price discriminations, and (c) selling "at unreasonably low prices for the purpose of destroying competition or eliminating a competitor." The important thing to note is that this section, in contrast to \xc2\xa7 1 of the Robinson-Patman Act, does not on its face amend the Clayton Act. Further, \xc2\xa7 3 contains only penal sanctions for violation of it provisions; in the absence of a clear expression of congressional intent to the contrary, these sanctions should under familiar principles be considered exclusive, rather than supplemented by civil sanctions of a distinct statute. See D. R. Wilder Mfg. Co. v. Corn Products Refining Co., 236 U. S. 165, 236 U. S. 174-175.

\n

The conclusion that only \xc2\xa7 1 of the Robinson-Patman Act can be regarded as amendatory of the Clayton Act is further borne out by the title of the whole Robinson-Patman Act, which reads (49 Stat. 1526):

\n

"An Act"

\n

"To amend section 2 of [the Clayton Act] . . . and for other purposes."

\n

(Italics added.) The "other purposes" can only refer to the sections of the Act other than the first section.

Page 355 U. S. 378

\n

Because there is a partial overlap between the price discrimination clauses of \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act (see note 1 supra) and those of \xc2\xa7 2 of the Clayton Act, as amended by the first section of the Robinson-Patman Act, [Footnote 6] it is argued that it would be anomalous to allow a private cause of action for price discrimination in violation of \xc2\xa7 2 of the Clayton Act but to deny a private cause of action based on a violation of \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act. This argument, however, overlooks the fact that \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act includes a provision which is not found in \xc2\xa7 2 of the Clayton Act, namely, selling "at unreasonably low prices for the purpose of destroying competition or eliminating a competitor." It is not an idle conjecture that the possibility of abuse inherent in a private cause of action based upon this vague provision [Footnote 7] was among the factors which led Congress to leave the enforcement of the provisions of \xc2\xa7 3 solely in the hands

Page 355 U. S. 379

of the public authorities, except to the extent that violation of any of its provisions also constituted a violation of \xc2\xa7 2 of the Clayton Act, and as such was subject to private redress under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 4 and 16 of that Act. In any event, in the absence of a much clearer indication of congressional intent than is present in these statutory provisions and their legislative history (infra, p. 355 U. S. 380), we should not read the Robinson-Patman Act as subjecting violations of the "unreasonably low prices" provision of \xc2\xa7 3 to the private remedies given by the Clayton Act.

\n

Respondent calls our attention to the fact that the 1940 U.S. Code codifies \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act as being among the "antitrust laws" embraced in \xc2\xa7 1 of the Clayton Act. However, reference to the 1926 and 1934 Codes shows that the 1940 codification was a palpable error. [Footnote 8] Moreover, this codification seems to us, for the

Page 355 U. S. 380

reasons set forth in this opinion, to be manifestly inconsistent with the Robinson-Patman Act, and, in such circumstances, Congress has specifically provided that the underlying statute must prevail. Act of June 30, 1926, 32(a), vol. 1 U.S.C. (1952 ed.), p. LXIII; see Stephan v. United States, 319 U. S. 423, 319 U. S. 426.

\n

II

\n

What appears from the face of the Robinson-Patman Act finds full support in its legislative history. The fair conclusions to be drawn from that history are (a) that \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act was not intended to become part of the Clayton Act, and (b) that the section was intended to carry only criminal sanctions, except that price discriminations, to the extent that they were common to both that section and \xc2\xa7 2 of the Clayton Act, were also understood to carry, under the independent force of the Clayton, Act, the private remedies provided in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 4 and 16 of the Clayton Act. In other words, although price discriminations are both criminally punishable (under \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act) and subject to civil redress (under \xc2\xa7 2 of the Clayton Act), selling "at unreasonably low prices" is subject only to the criminal penalties provided in \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act. [Footnote 9] This is evident from the Conference Report on the bill, which states:

\n

"SECTION 2"

\n

"The provisions of section 2 of the House bill [Footnote 10] were agreed to without amendment by the Senate. . . . [I]t appears in the conference report as

Page 355 U. S. 381

section 2 of the bill itself, rather that as part of the amendment to section 2 of the Clayton Act which is provided for in section 1 to the present bill."

\n

"SECTION 3"

\n

"Subsection (h) of the Senate amendment . . . appears in the conference report as section 3 of the bill itself. It contains the operative and penal provisions of what was originally the Borah-Van Nuys bill (S. 4171). [Footnote 11] While they overlap in some respects, they are in no way inconsistent with the provisions of the Clayton Act amendment provided for in section 1. Section 3 authorizes nothing which that amendment prohibits, and takes nothing from it. On the contrary, where only civil remedies and liabilities attach to violations of the amendment provided in section 1, section 3 sets up special prohibitions as to the particular offenses therein described and attaches to them also the criminal penalties therein provided."

\n

H.R.Rep. No. 2951, 74th Cong., 2d Sess., p. 8. (Italics added.) Further excerpts from the legislative history, set forth in the margin, [Footnote 12] also bear out the conclusions stated at the outset of this part of our opinion.

Page 355 U. S. 382

\n

Finally, it is noteworthy, by way of epitomizing the conclusions to be drawn from the legislative history, that, in 1950, Representative Patman (a coauthor of the Robinson-Patman Act) stated in testimony before a Subcommittee of the House Committee on the Judiciary (Hearing on H.R. 7905, 81st Cong., 2d Sess., Serial No. 14, Part 5, p. 48):

\n

". . . it happens that section 3, the criminal section of the Robinson-Patman Act, was not, under the terms of that act, made an amendment to the Clayton Act. Moreover, section 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act has never been added to the list of laws designated as \'antitrust laws\' in section 1 of the Clayton Act."

\n

For the foregoing reasons, we hold that a private cause of action does not lie for practices forbidden only by \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act. To the extent that such practices also constitute a violation of \xc2\xa7 2 of the Clayton Act, as amended, they are actionable by one injured thereby solely under that Act. Since no such infringement of \xc2\xa7 2 is alleged here, the complaint in this case was properly dismissed.

\n

Affirmed.

MR. JUSTICE DOUGLAS, with whom THE CHIEF JUSTICE, MR. JUSTICE BLACK, and MR. JUSTICE BRENNAN concur, dissenting. *

\n

The question in these cases is whether a person injured by a violation of \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act, 49 Stat. 1526, 15 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 13a, may sue the wrongdoer for treble damages and an injunction under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 4 and 16 of the Clayton Act, 38 Stat. 730, 15 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 15, 26. A dictum in Bruce\'s Juices, Inc. v. American Can Co., 330 U. S. 743, 330 U. S. 750, indicated that the action would lie, and Moore v. Mead\'s Fine Bread Co., 348 U. S. 115, sustained a recovery on that theory, though the point now at issue was neither briefed nor considered.

\n

Section 4 of the Clayton Act allows suits for treble damages for acts forbidden by "the antitrust laws." Section 16 allows relief by injunction for violations of "the antitrust laws." The Court holds that \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act is not a part of "the antitrust laws" as used in the Clayton Act.

\n

We disagree. The legislative history, in our opinion, shows that Congress intended to permit private actions to be brought for violations of \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act.

\n

It is true that \xc2\xa7 1 of the Clayton Act defines "antitrust laws" as including, inter alia, the Sherman Act and the Clayton Act, and that the Robinson-Patman Act did not, in terms, amend \xc2\xa7 1. It is also true that \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act does not, in terms, amend \xc2\xa7 3 of the Clayton Act, while \xc2\xa7 1 of the Robinson-Patman Act does. 80 Cong.Rec. 9414. The legislative history is further clouded by the fact that certain types of price discriminations are forbidden by both \xc2\xa7 1 [Footnote 2/1] and \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman

Page 355 U. S. 384

Act. Suits for damages on account of these violations plainly are suits for damages under the "antitrust laws" within the meaning of the enforcement provisions of the Clayton Act. It is only when a violation of \xc2\xa7 3 alone is involved that the issue we are concerned with here arises. Yet why allow suits for treble damages for price discrimination under \xc2\xa7 2 and not allow them when the discrimination practiced is of the kind condemned by \xc2\xa7 3? There is no suggestion that any such line was being drawn by the Congress. The emphasis on the restrictive effect of \xc2\xa7 3 relates dimply to its criminal sanctions, not to the remedial provisions with which we are presently concerned. When the Conference Report was being considered in the House, Representative Miller, a House Conferee supporting the bill, made the following statements (80 Cong.Rec. 9421):

\n

"The penalty of triple damages is the old law. In other words, we made no change in that particular provision of the Clayton Act. Section 3, which the gentleman from New York talks about, is the Borah-Van Nuys amendment, and that is the criminal section of this bill. The first part of the bill has nothing to do with criminal offenses. It deals primarily, in my opinion, with the authority of the Federal Trade Commission to regulate and enforce the provisions of section 2 of the Clayton Act, as amended. Section 3 in the bill is placed in an effort to make the criminal offense apply only to that particular section, and I believe that is a reasonable construction, if you will look at the bill."

\n

"MR. MASSINGALE. There is no criminal offense involved for anything outside of what is contained in that section?"

\n

"MR. MILLER. In section 3. "

Page 355 U. S. 385

\n

"MR. HANCOCK of New York. Is it not perfectly clear that any vendor who discriminates in price between purchasers is guilty of a crime and is also subject to triple damages to anyone who claims to be aggrieved?"

\n

"MR. MILLER. That is true, but the criminal part is included in section 3 and section 3 only."

\n

"MR. HANCOCK of New York. But it is a part of the same act?"

\n

"MR. MILLER. Of course it is, but it is not a part of the Clayton Act as amended by section 2. It ought to be, as far as that is concerned, if a seller willfully discriminates."

\n

Yet \xc2\xa7 3, as well as \xc2\xa7 2, declares certain price discriminations unlawful, and suits for treble damages are as applicable to \xc2\xa7 3 situations as to those under \xc2\xa7 2, if words are to have their normal meaning.

"MR. CELLER. And he would also have to respond in triple damages under the provisions of the Clayton Act. Anyone aggrieved can sue."

\n

The treble damage provision of the Clayton Act was written into the law so as to provide incentives for private, as well as governmental, patrol of the antitrust field. Not a word in the legislative history of the Robinson-Patman Act suggests that this special remedy was to be denied to \xc2\xa7 3 actions and granted to those under \xc2\xa7 2. The fair intendment seems to have been that \xc2\xa7 3 was to be added to the body of "antitrust laws." The mechanical device used was an amendment to one section of the Clayton Act. [Footnote 2/2]

\n

In resolving all ambiguities against the grant of vitality to \xc2\xa7 3, we forget that the treble damage technique for law enforcement was designed as an effective, if not the most effective, method of deterring violators of the Act.

\n

The House Committee on the Judiciary is entrusted by Congress with the preparation and publication of the Code. 1 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 202. That Committee construed \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act as part of the antitrust laws, for it gave the section number 13a in the Code and provided in \xc2\xa7 12 that the term "antitrust laws" "includes sections 1-27 of this title." That codification establishes "prima facie the laws of the United States," 1 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 204(a), and the countermanding considerations relied on by the Court do not seem sufficiently persuasive to use to rebut that construction. It indeed accords with what we deem to be the prevailing sentiment in Congress at the time that \xc2\xa7 3 became as much a part of the "antitrust laws" as the other provisions of the Robinson-Patman Act.

\n

As the Court notes, it appears that the Department of Justice has never enforced the criminal provisions of \xc2\xa7 3 of the Robinson-Patman Act. Because of the Court\'s holding that \xc2\xa7 3 is not available in civil actions to private parties, the statute has, in effect, been repealed. It is apparent that the opponents of the Robinson-Patman Act have eventually managed to achieve in this Court what they could not do in Congress. We would reverse in No. 67, and affirm in No. 69.
