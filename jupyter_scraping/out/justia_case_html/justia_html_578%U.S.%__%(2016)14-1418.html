\n

Per Curiam.

\n

Petitioners are primarily nonprofit organizations that provide health insurance to their employees. Federal regulations require petitioners to cover certain contraceptives as part of their health plans, unless petitioners submit a form either to their insurer or to the Federal Government, stating that they object on religious grounds to providing contraceptive coverage. Petitioners allege that submitting this notice substantially burdens the exercise of their religion, in violation of the Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993,\n\n107Stat.\n1488,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000bb et seq.

\n

Following oral argument, the Court requested supplemental briefing from the parties addressing \xe2\x80\x9cwhether contraceptive coverage could be provided to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 employees, through petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 insurance companies, without any such notice from petitioners.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, p. ___. Both petitioners and the Government now confirm that such an option is feasible. Petitioners have clarified that their religious exercise is not infringed where they \xe2\x80\x9cneed to do nothing more than contract for a plan that does not include coverage for some or all forms of contraception,\xe2\x80\x9d even if their employees receive cost-free contraceptive coverage from the same insurance company. Supplemental Brief for Petitioners 4. The Government has confirmed that the challenged procedures \xe2\x80\x9cfor employers with insured plans could be modified to operate in the manner posited in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s order while still ensuring that the affected women receive contraceptive coverage seamlessly, together with the rest of their health coverage.\xe2\x80\x9d Supplemental Brief for Respondents 14\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

In light of the positions asserted by the parties in their supplemental briefs, the Court vacates the judgments below and remands to the respective United States Courts of Appeals for the Third, Fifth, Tenth, and D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuits. Given the gravity of the dispute and the substantial clarification and refinement in the positions of the parties, the parties on remand should be afforded an opportunity to arrive at an approach going forward that accommodates petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 religious exercise while at the same time ensuring that women covered by petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 health plans \xe2\x80\x9creceive full and equal health coverage, including contraceptive coverage.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1. We anticipate that the Courts of Appeals will allow the parties sufficient time to resolve any outstanding issues between them.

\n

The Court finds the foregoing approach more suitable than addressing the significantly clarified views of the parties in the first instance. Although there may still be areas of disagreement between the parties on issues of implementation, the importance of those areas of potential concern is uncertain, as is the necessity of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s involvement at this point to resolve them. This Court has taken similar action in other cases in the past. See, e.g., Madison County v. Oneida Indian Nation of N.\xc2\xa0Y.,\n\n562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 42,\n43 (2011)\n (per curiam) (vacating and remanding for the Second Circuit to \xe2\x80\x9caddress, in the first instance, whether to revisit its ruling on sovereign immunity in light of [a] new factual development, and\xe2\x80\x94if necessary\xe2\x80\x94proceed to address other questions in the case consistent with its sovereign immunity ruling\xe2\x80\x9d); Kiyemba v. Obama,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 131,\n132 (2010)\n (per curiam) (vacating and remanding for the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit to \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine, in the first instance, what further proceedings in that court or in the District Court are necessary and appropriate for the full and prompt disposition of the case in light of the new developments\xe2\x80\x9d); Villarreal v. United States, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014) (vacating and remanding to the Fifth Circuit \xe2\x80\x9cfor further consideration in light of the position asserted by the Solicitor General in his brief for the United States\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The Court expresses no view on the merits of the cases. In particular, the Court does not decide whether petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 religious exercise has been substantially burdened, whether the Government has a compelling interest, or whether the current regulations are the least restrictive means of serving that interest.

\n

Nothing in this opinion, or in the opinions or orders of the courts below, is to affect the ability of the Government to ensure that women covered by petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 health plans \xe2\x80\x9cobtain, without cost, the full range of FDA approved contraceptives.\xe2\x80\x9d Wheaton College v. Burwell, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 1). Through this litigation, petitioners have made the Government aware of their view that they meet \xe2\x80\x9cthe requirements for exemption from the contraceptive coverage requirement on religious grounds.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 2). Nothing in this opinion, or in the opinions or orders of the courts below, \xe2\x80\x9cprecludes the Government from relying on this notice, to the extent it considers it necessary, to facilitate the provision of full contraceptive coverage\xe2\x80\x9d going forward. Ibid. Because the Government may rely on this notice, the Government may not impose taxes or penalties on petitioners for failure to provide the relevant notice.

\n

The judgments of the Courts of Appeals are vacated, and the cases are remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.

Justice Sotomayor, with whom Justice Ginsburg joins, concurring.

\n

I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s per curiam opinion because it expresses no view on \xe2\x80\x9cthe merits of the cases,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cwhether petition-ers\xe2\x80\x99 religious exercise has been substantially burdened,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the current regulations are the least restrictive means of serving\xe2\x80\x9d a compelling governmental interest. Ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. Lower courts, therefore, should not construe either today\xe2\x80\x99s per curiam or our order of March 29, 2016, as signals of where this Court stands. We have included similarly explicit disclaimers in previous orders. See, e.g., Wheaton College v. Burwell, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]his order should not be construed as an expression of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s views on the merits\xe2\x80\x9d). Yet some lower courts have ignored those instructions. See, e.g., Sharpe Holdings, Inc., v. Department of Health and Human Servs., 801 F.\xc2\xa03d 927, 944 (CA8 2015) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n Wheaton College, Little Sisters of the Poor, and Zubik, the Supreme Court approved a method of notice to HHS that is arguably less onerous than [existing regulations] yet permits the government to further its interests. Although the Court\xe2\x80\x99s orders were not final rulings on the merits, they at the very least collectively constitute a signal that less restrictive means exist by which the government may further its interests\xe2\x80\x9d). On remand in these cases, the Courts of Appeals should not make the same mistake.

\n

I also join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion because it allows the lower courts to consider only whether existing or modified regulations could provide seamless contraceptive coverage \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 employees, through petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 insurance companies, without any\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. notice from petitioners.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3. The opinion does not, by contrast, endorse the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position that the existing regulations substantially burden their religious exercise or that contraceptive coverage must be provided through a \xe2\x80\x9cseparate policy, with a separate enrollment process.\xe2\x80\x9d Supp. Brief for Petitioners 1; Supp. Reply Brief for Petitioners 5. Such separate contraceptive-only policies do not currently exist, and the Government has laid out a number of legal and practical obstacles to their creation. See Supp. Reply Brief for Respondents 3\xe2\x80\x934. Requiring standalone contraceptive-only coverage would leave in limbo all of the women now guaranteed seamless preventive-care coverage under the Affordable Care Act. And requiring that women affirmatively opt into such coverage would \xe2\x80\x9cimpose precisely the kind of barrier to the delivery of preventive services that Congress sought to eliminate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 6.

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion does only what it says it does: \xe2\x80\x9cafford[s] an opportunity\xe2\x80\x9d for the parties and Courts of Appeals to reconsider the parties\xe2\x80\x99 arguments in light of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 new articulation of their religious objection and the Government\xe2\x80\x99s clarification about what the existing regulations accomplish, how they might be amended, and what such an amendment would sacrifice. Ante, at 4. As enlightened by the parties\xe2\x80\x99 new submissions, the Courts of Appeals remain free to reach the same conclusion or a different one on each of the questions presented by these cases.
