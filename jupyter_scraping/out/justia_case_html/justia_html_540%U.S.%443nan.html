KONTRICK v. RYAN

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x93819. Argued November 3, 2003\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 14, 2004

\n

A creditor in Chapter 7 liquidation proceedings has \xe2\x80\x9c60 days after the first date set for the meeting of creditors\xe2\x80\x9d to file a complaint objecting to the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s discharge. Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 4004(a). The bankruptcy court may extend that period \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d on motion \xe2\x80\x9cfiled before the time has expired.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 4004(b). Reinforcing Rule 4004(b)\xe2\x80\x99s restriction on extension of the Rule 4004(a) deadline, Rule 9006(b)(3) allows enlargement of \xe2\x80\x9cthe time for taking action\xe2\x80\x9d under Rule 4004(a) \xe2\x80\x9conly to the extent and under the conditions stated in [that rule],\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., only as permitted by Rule 4004(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On April 4, 1997, petitioner Kontrick filed a Chapter 7 bankruptcy petition. After gaining three successive time extensions from the Bankruptcy Court, respondent Ryan, Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s creditor, filed a complaint on January 13, 1998, objecting to Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s discharge. Ryan alleged that Kontrick had transferred property, within one year of filing his petition, with the intent to defraud creditors, and therefore did not qualify for discharge under 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7727(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x93(5). Ryan filed an amended complaint on May 6, 1998, with leave of court, but without seeking or gaining a court-approved time extension. The amended complaint alleged with particularity that Kontrick had fraudulently transferred money to his wife, first by removing his own name from the family\xe2\x80\x99s once-joint checking account, then by continuing regularly to deposit his salary checks into the account, from which his wife routinely paid family expenses (the \xe2\x80\x9cfamily-account\xe2\x80\x9d claim). Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s June 10, 1998, answer to the amended complaint did not raise the untimeliness of the family-account claim; on the merits, the answer admitted the transfers to the family account but denied that Kontrick had violated \xc2\xa7727(a)(2)(A). In response to Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s summary judgment motion, which appended a statement of material facts, Kontrick cross-moved to strike portions of Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s summary judgment filings, but did not ask the court to strike the amended complaint\xe2\x80\x99s family-account allegations. On February 25, 2000, the Bankruptcy Court awarded Ryan summary judgment on the family-account claim, concluding that Kontrick was not entitled to discharge because his transfers to the family account were made with intent to defraud at least creditor Ryan. Kontrick then moved for reconsideration. For the first time, Kontrick urged that the court was powerless to adjudicate the family-account claim. The amended complaint containing that claim, Kontrick observed, was untimely under Rules 4004(a) and (b) and 9006(b)(3). Those rules, Kontrick maintained, establish a mandatory, unalterable time limit of the kind Kontrick called \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d The Bankruptcy Court denied reconsideration and entered final judgment, holding that Rule 4004\xe2\x80\x99s complaint-filing time instructions are not \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d and that Kontrick had waived the right to assert the untimeliness of the amended complaint by failing squarely to raise the point before the court reached the merits of Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s objections to discharge. The District Court sustained the denial of discharge, and the Seventh Circuit affirmed. Both courts relied on decisions of sister Circuits holding that the timeliness provisions at issue are not \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A debtor forfeits the right to rely on Rule 4004 if the debtor does not raise the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s time limitation before the bankruptcy court reaches the merits of the creditor\xe2\x80\x99s objection to discharge. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Only Congress may determine a lower federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction. U. S. Const., Art. III, \xc2\xa71. Congress did so, as pertinent here, by instructing that \xe2\x80\x9cobjections to discharges\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ore proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d within the bankruptcy courts\xe2\x80\x99 jurisdiction. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7157(b)(2)(J). Congress did not build time constraints into that statutory authorization. Rather, the time constraints applicable to objections to discharge are contained in Bankruptcy Rules prescribed pursuant to \xc2\xa72075. Such rules \xe2\x80\x9cdo not create or withdraw federal jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Owen Equipment & Erection Co. v. Kroger, 437 U. S. 365, 370. As Bankruptcy Rule 9030 states, the Bankruptcy Rules \xe2\x80\x9cshall not be construed to extend or limit the jurisdiction of the courts.\xe2\x80\x9d The filing deadlines prescribed in Rules 4004 and 9006(b)(3) are claim-processing rules that do not delineate what cases bankruptcy courts are competent to adjudicate. Although Kontrick now concedes that those Rules are not properly labeled \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d in the sense of describing a court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction, he maintains that the Rules have the same import as provisions governing subject-matter jurisdiction. A litigant generally may raise a court\xe2\x80\x99s lack of subject-matter jurisdiction at any time in the same civil action. Mansfield, C.\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0M.\xc2\xa0R. Co. v. Swan, 111 U. S. 379, 382. Similarly, Kontrick urges, a debtor may challenge a creditor\xe2\x80\x99s objection to discharge as untimely under Rules 4004 and 9006(b)(3) at any time in the proceedings, even initially on appeal or certiorari. The equation Kontrick advances overlooks the critical difference between a rule governing subject-matter jurisdiction and an inflexible claim-processing rule. Characteristically, a court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction cannot be expanded to account for the parties\xe2\x80\x99 litigation conduct; a claim-processing rule, on the other hand, even if unalterable on a party\xe2\x80\x99s application, can nonetheless be forfeited if the party asserting the rule waits too long to raise the point. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0No reasonable construction of complaint-processing rules would allow a litigant situated as Kontrick is to defeat a claim, as filed too late, after the party has litigated and lost the case on the merits. The relevant claim-processing rules in this case, Bankruptcy Rules 4004(a) and (b) and 9006(b)(3), include, among their primary purposes, affording the debtor an affirmative defense to a complaint filed outside the Rules 4004(a) and (b) time limits. It is uncontested that Ryan filed his complaint objecting to Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s discharge outside those limits. Kontrick urges that nothing occurring thereafter counts, for the Rules\xe2\x80\x99 time prescriptions are unalterable, allowing no recourse to equitable exceptions. This case, however, involves no issue of equitable tolling or any other equity-based exception. Neither at the time Ryan filed the amended complaint containing the family-account claim nor anytime thereafter did he assert circumstances\xe2\x80\x94equitable or otherwise\xe2\x80\x94qualifying him for a time extension. The sole question is whether Kontrick forfeited his right to assert the untimeliness of Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s amended complaint by failing to raise the issue until after that complaint was adjudicated on the merits. In other words, how long did the affirmative defense Rules 4004(a) and (b) and 9006(b)(3) afforded Kontrick linger in the proceedings? The Seventh Circuit followed the proper path on this key question. It noted that time bars generally must be raised in an answer or responsive pleading. See Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 8(c) (made applicable to bankruptcy court adversary proceedings by Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 7008(a)). An answer may be amended to include an inadvertently omitted affirmative defense, and even after the time to amend \xe2\x80\x9cof course\xe2\x80\x9d has passed, \xe2\x80\x9cleave [to amend] shall be freely given when justice so requires.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 15(a) (made applicable to adversary proceedings by Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 7015). Kontrick not only failed to assert the time constraints of Rules 4004(a) and (b) and 9006(b)(3) in a pleading or amended pleading responsive to Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s amended complaint. In addition, Kontrick moved to delete certain items from Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s summary judgment filings, but, even that far into the litigation, he did not ask the Bankruptcy Court to strike the family-account claim. Ordinarily, a defense is lost if it is not included in the answer or amended answer. See Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 7012(b) (Fed. Rules Civ. Proc. 12(b)\xe2\x80\x93(h) apply in adversary proceedings). Rules 12(h)(2) and (3) prolong the life of certain defenses, but time prescriptions are not among them. Even if a defense based on Bankruptcy Rule 4004 could be equated to \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted,\xe2\x80\x9d the issue could be raised, at the latest, \xe2\x80\x9cat the trial on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 12(h)(2). Only lack of subject-matter jurisdiction is preserved post-trial. Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 12(h)(3). Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s resistance to the family-account claim is not of that order. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

295 F.\xc2\xa03d 724, affirmed.

ANDREW J. KONTRICK, PETITIONER v.\nROBERT A. RYAN

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[January 14, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns the duration of a right to object to a pleading on the ground that it was filed out of time. Under the Bankruptcy Rules governing Chapter 7 liquidation proceedings, a creditor has \xe2\x80\x9c60 days after the first date set for the meeting of creditors\xe2\x80\x9d to file a complaint objecting to the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s discharge. Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 4004(a). That period may be extended \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d on motion \xe2\x80\x9cfiled before the time has expired.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 4004(b). In the matter before us a creditor, in an untimely pleading, objected to the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s discharge. The debtor, however, did not promptly move to dismiss the creditor\xe2\x80\x99s plea as impermissibly late. Only after the Bankruptcy Court decided, on the merits, that the discharge should be refused did the debtor, in a motion for reconsideration, urge the untimeliness of the creditor\xe2\x80\x99s plea.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bankruptcy Rule 4004\xe2\x80\x99s time prescription, the debtor maintains, is \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., dispositive whenever raised in the proceedings. Rejecting the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d characterization, the courts below held that Rule 4004\xe2\x80\x99s time prescription could not be invoked to upset an adjudication on the merits. We agree that Rule 4004 is not \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Affirming the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit, we hold that a debtor forfeits the right to rely on Rule 4004 if the debtor does not raise the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s time limitation before the bankruptcy court reaches the merits of the creditor\xe2\x80\x99s objection to discharge.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A debtor in a Chapter 7 liquidation case qualifies for an order discharging his debts if he satisfies the conditions stated in \xc2\xa7727(a) of the Bankruptcy Code. 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7727(a).[Footnote 1] A discharge granted under \xc2\xa7727(a) frees the debtor from all debts existing at the commencement of the bankruptcy proceeding other than obligations \xc2\xa7523 of the Code excepts from discharge. \xc2\xa7727(b).[Footnote 2]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A debtor\xe2\x80\x99s discharge may be opposed by the trustee, the United States trustee, or any creditor. \xc2\xa7727(c)(1). Adjudication of \xe2\x80\x9cobjections to discharg[e],\xe2\x80\x9d Congress provided, is a \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ore proceedin[g]\xe2\x80\x9d within the jurisdiction of the bankruptcy courts. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7157(b)(2)(J). No statute, however, specifies a time limit for filing a complaint objecting to the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s discharge. Instead, the controlling time prescriptions are contained in the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, specifically, Rules 4004(a) and (b) and 9006(b)(3).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In relevant part, Bankruptcy Rule 4004(a) states: \xe2\x80\x9c[A] complaint objecting to the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s discharge under \xc2\xa7727(a) of the Code shall be filed no later than 60 days after the first date set for the meeting of creditors.\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 4004(b), governing extensions of the Rule 4004(a) filing deadline, provides: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he court may for cause extend the time [Rule 4004(a) allows] to file a complaint objecting to discharge\xe2\x80\x9d if the motion is \xe2\x80\x9cfiled before the time has expired.\xe2\x80\x9d Reinforcing Rule 4004(b)\xe2\x80\x99s restriction on extension of the Rule 4004(a) deadline, Rule 9006(b)(3) allows enlargement of \xe2\x80\x9cthe time for taking action\xe2\x80\x9d under Rule 4004(a) \xe2\x80\x9conly to the extent and under the conditions stated in [that rule],\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., only as permitted by Rule 4004(b).[Footnote 3]

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On April 4, 1997, petitioner, Dr. Andrew J. Kontrick, filed a Chapter 7 bankruptcy petition. Respondent, Dr. Robert A. Ryan, a major creditor and Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s former associate in a cosmetic and plastic surgery practice, opposed Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s discharge. After gaining three successive time extensions from the Bankruptcy Court, Ryan filed an original complaint on January 13, 1998, in which he objected to the discharge of any of Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s debts. Ryan alleged that Kontrick had transferred property, within one year of filing the bankruptcy petition, with intent to defraud creditors, and therefore did not qualify for a discharge under 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7727(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x93(5). App. to Pet. for Cert. 40.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ryan filed an amended complaint on May 6, 1998, with leave of court, ibid., but without seeking or gaining a court-approved time extension. The amended complaint particularized for the first time the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s violation of \xc2\xa7727(a)(2)(A) in this regard: Debtor Kontrick, creditor Ryan alleged, had fraudulently transferred money to Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s wife, first by removing Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s own name from the family\xe2\x80\x99s once-joint checking account, then by continuing regularly to deposit his salary checks into the account, from which his wife routinely paid family expenses (the \xe2\x80\x9cfamily-account\xe2\x80\x9d claim). Id., at 52\xe2\x80\x9353.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kontrick answered Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s amended complaint on June 10, 1998. His answer \xe2\x80\x9cdid not raise the untimeliness of [the family-account] claim,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 4; on the merits, he admitted the transfers to the family account but denied violating \xc2\xa7727(a)(2)(A). In March 1999, after the parties engaged in acrimonious discovery, Ryan moved for summary judgment. As Local Bankruptcy Rule 402(M) (Bkrtcy. Ct. ND Ill. 1994) instructs, Ryan appended to his motion \xe2\x80\x9ca statement of material facts as to which [he] contend[ed] there [was] no genuine issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Kontrick cross-moved, in August 1999, to strike portions of Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s summary judgment filings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s motion to strike sought deletion of \xe2\x80\x9cnew allegations,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., allegations making their first appearance in the litigation in Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s summary judgment submissions\xe2\x80\x94Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s statement of facts pursuant to Local Rule 402(M), accompanying exhibits, and corresponding portions of the summary judgment motion and memorandum. Motion to Strike and Response to [Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s] Statement of Facts Under Local Rule 402 N in No. 97 B 10353 (Bkrtcy. Ct. ND Ill.), pp. 2, 5, 26. Although Kontrick noted that the family-account allegations were stated only in the amended complaint and were absent from the original complaint, id., at 3\xe2\x80\x934, he did not ask the court to strike those allegations. His response, instead, and in line with Local Rule 402(N), addressed the substance of the family-account claim. He admitted taking his name off the account, but observed that he did so \xe2\x80\x9cover four years before bankruptcy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13. He also acknowledged that, thereafter, he \xe2\x80\x9cdeposited his paycheck into the account the same way he had always done.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On February 25, 2000, the Bankruptcy Court ruled on the cross-motions, granting in part Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s motion to strike, awarding summary judgment to Ryan on the family-account claim, and dismissing the remaining claims. The court used the amended complaint as its baseline; it struck as untimely \xe2\x80\x9callegations not included in [that] complaint.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 47; see id., at 48\xe2\x80\x9350. Homing in on Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s continuing deposits into the account from which he had removed his name, the court concluded that Kontrick had transferred property with intent \xe2\x80\x9cto hinder, delay or defraud at least [creditor] Ryan.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 55. That course of conduct, coupled with Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s testimony,[Footnote 5] the court concluded, sufficed to prove a violation of \xc2\xa7727(a)(2) (described supra, at 2, n.\xc2\xa01). App. to Pet. for Cert. 55, 64. Accordingly, the court held, Kontrick was not entitled to a discharge of his debts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kontrick moved for reconsideration. He argued that the Bankruptcy Court lacked jurisdiction over the sole claim on which the court had granted summary judgment, the family-account claim. See id., at 71. The court was powerless to adjudicate the claim, Kontrick insisted, because the amended complaint containing the claim was untimely. Governing Rules 4004(a) and (b) and 9006(b)(3), see supra, at 3, Kontrick maintained, establish a mandatory, unalterable time limit of the kind he then called \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 71. It was the first time Kontrick appended a jurisdictional label to any pleading he filed relating to the family-account claim.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Bankruptcy Court denied the reconsideration motion on June 8, 2000, and entered final judgment five days later. The court held that Rule 4004\xe2\x80\x99s complaint-filing time instructions are not \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d and that Kontrick had waived the right to assert the untimeliness of the amended complaint by failing squarely to raise the point before the court reached the merits of Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s objections to discharge.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court sustained the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision denying Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s discharge. App. to Pet. for Cert. 25\xe2\x80\x9338. The Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit, in turn, affirmed the judgment of the District Court. In\xc2\xa0re Kontrick, 295 F.\xc2\xa03d 724 (CA7 2002). Both courts relied on decisions of sister Circuits holding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe timeliness provisions at issue here are not jurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 733 (citing In\xc2\xa0re Benedict, 90 F.\xc2\xa03d 50, 54\xe2\x80\x9355 (CA2 1996), and Farouki v. Emirates Bank Int\xe2\x80\x99l Ltd., 14 F.\xc2\xa03d 244, 248 (CA4 1994)); accord, App. to Pet. for Cert. 31\xe2\x80\x9332. Both courts also agreed with the Bankruptcy Court that Kontrick had waived the right to challenge Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s amended complaint as impermissibly late.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Seventh Circuit found in Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s papers opposing summary judgment nothing that placed in issue the timeliness of allegations in the amended complaint. 295 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 735. Instead, according to the Court of Appeals, Kontrick apparently accepted creditor Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s amended complaint as properly filed; Kontrick used that complaint, not the original complaint, as a baseline to object to new allegations Ryan made for the first time in his statement of facts supporting summary judgment. Ibid. The Seventh Circuit further commented that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he policy concerns of expeditious administration of bankruptcy matters and the finality of the bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s decision hardly are fostered by requiring the bankruptcy court to consider the timeliness of an issue that it already has adjudicated.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari in view of the division of opinion on whether Rule 4004 is \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0[Footnote 6] 538 U. S. 998 (2003), and we now affirm the judgment of the Seventh Circuit.[Footnote 7]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Only Congress may determine a lower federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction. U. S. Const., Art. III, \xc2\xa71. Congress did so with respect to bankruptcy courts in Title 28 (Judiciary and Judicial Procedure); in cataloging core bankruptcy proceedings, Congress authorized bankruptcy courts to adjudicate, inter alia, objections to discharge. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7157(b)(1) and (b)(2)(I) and (J). Certain statutory provisions governing bankruptcy courts contain built-in time constraints. For example, \xc2\xa7157(c)(1) addresses de novo district court review of bankruptcy court findings and conclusions in noncore proceedings; that provision confines review to \xe2\x80\x9cmatters to which any party has timely and specifically objected.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 8] The provision conferring jurisdiction over objections to discharge, however, contains no timeliness condition. Section 157(b)(2)(J) instructs only that \xe2\x80\x9cobjections to discharges\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ore proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d within the jurisdiction of the bankruptcy courts.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The time constraints applicable to objections to discharge are contained in Bankruptcy Rules prescribed by this Court for \xe2\x80\x9cthe practice and procedure in cases under title 11.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72075; cf. \xc2\xa72072 (similarly providing for Court-prescribed \xe2\x80\x9crules of practice and procedure\xe2\x80\x9d for cases in the federal district courts and courts of appeals). \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is axiomatic\xe2\x80\x9d that such rules \xe2\x80\x9cdo not create or withdraw federal jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Owen Equipment & Erection Co. v. Kroger, 437 U. S. 365, 370 (1978). As Bankruptcy Rule 9030 states, the Bankruptcy Rules \xe2\x80\x9cshall not be construed to extend or limit the jurisdiction of the courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 9030\xe2\x80\x99s forerunner\xe2\x80\x94its counterpart in the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, Rule 82\xe2\x80\x94similarly states: \xe2\x80\x9cThese rules shall not be construed to extend or limit the jurisdiction of the United States district courts . . . .\xe2\x80\x9d See 12 C. Wright, A. Miller, & R. Marcus, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa73141, pp. 484\xe2\x80\x93485 (2d ed. 1997) (\xe2\x80\x9cRule 82 states [the] important principle\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he rules merely prescribe the method by which the jurisdiction granted the courts by Congress is to be exercised.\xe2\x80\x9d); Schacht v. United States, 398 U. S. 58, 64 (1970) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe procedural rules adopted by the Court for the orderly transaction of its business are not jurisdictional . . . .\xe2\x80\x9d). In short, the filing deadlines prescribed in Bankruptcy Rules 4004 and 9006(b)(3) are claim-processing rules that do not delineate what cases bankruptcy courts are competent to adjudicate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This much is common ground. Kontrick does not contend in this Court that the timing rules in question affect the subject-matter jurisdiction of the bankruptcy courts. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 9 (acknowledging that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his case does not deal with subject matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (explaining that counsel for Kontrick used the word \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cas a shorthand\xe2\x80\x9d to indicate a nonextendable time limit).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Courts, including this Court, it is true, have been less than meticulous in this regard; they have more than occasionally used the term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d to describe emphatic time prescriptions in rules of court. \xe2\x80\x9cJurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court has aptly observed, \xe2\x80\x9cis a word of many, too many, meanings.\xe2\x80\x9d Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U. S. 83, 90 (1998) (internal quotation marks omitted). For example, we have described Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 6(b), on time enlargement, and correspondingly, Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 45(b), on extending time, as \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory and jurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Robinson, 361 U. S. 220, 228\xe2\x80\x93229 (1960). But see Carlisle v. United States, 517 U. S. 416, 419\xe2\x80\x93433 (1996) (holding that, over the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s objection, a court may not grant a postverdict motion for a judgment of acquittal filed one day outside the time limit allowed by Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 29(c); this Court did not characterize the Rule as \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d); Taylor v. Freeland & Kronz, 503 U. S. 638, 642\xe2\x80\x93646 (1992) (similar ruling regarding Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 4003(b)). \xe2\x80\x9c[C]lassify[ing] time prescriptions, even rigid ones, under the heading \xe2\x80\x98subject matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d can be confounding. Carlisle, 517 U. S., at 434 (Ginsburg, J., concurring). Clarity would be facilitated if courts and litigants used the label \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d not for claim-processing rules, but only for prescriptions delineating the classes of cases (subject-matter jurisdiction) and the persons (personal jurisdiction) falling within a court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudicatory authority.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Though Kontrick concedes that Rules 4004 and 9006(b)(3) are not properly labeled \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d in the sense of describing a court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction, he maintains that the Rules have the same import as provisions governing subject-matter jurisdiction. A litigant generally may raise a court\xe2\x80\x99s lack of subject-matter jurisdiction at any time in the same civil action, even initially at the highest appellate instance. Mansfield, C. & L. M. R. Co. v. Swan, 111 U. S. 379, 382 (1884) (challenge to a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction may be made at any stage of the proceedings, and the court should raise the question sua sponte); Capron v. Van Noorden, 2 Cranch 126, 127 (1804) (judgment loser successfully raised lack of diversity jurisdiction for the first time before the Supreme Court); Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 12(h)(3) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhenever it appears by suggestion of the parties or otherwise that the court lacks jurisdiction of the subject matter, the court shall dismiss the action.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 9] Just so, Kontrick urges, a debtor may challenge a creditor\xe2\x80\x99s objection to discharge as untimely under Rules 4004 and 9006(b)(3) any time in the proceedings, even initially on appeal or certiorari. Tr. of Oral Arg. 10\xe2\x80\x9311 (a debtor may object after final judgment or on appeal \xe2\x80\x9cso long as it\xe2\x80\x99s within the same proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Petitioner 25, and n.\xc2\xa07 (same); Reply Brief 16, and n.\xc2\xa07 (citing lower court decisions supporting Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s argument on the longevity of time limits stated in Rules 4004 and 9006(b)(3), e.g., In\xc2\xa0re Poskanzer, 146 B.\xc2\xa0R. 125, 131 (DNJ 1992); In\xc2\xa0re Rinde, 276 B.\xc2\xa0R. 330, 333 (Bkrtcy. Ct. RI 2002); In\xc2\xa0re Barley, 130 B.\xc2\xa0R. 66, 69 (Bkrtcy. Ct. ND Ind. 1991); In\xc2\xa0re Kirsch, 65 B.\xc2\xa0R. 297, 300, 302 (Bkrtcy. Ct. ND Ill. 1986)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The equation Kontrick advances overlooks a critical difference between a rule governing subject-matter jurisdiction and an inflexible claim-processing rule. Characteristically, a court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction cannot be expanded to account for the parties\xe2\x80\x99 litigation conduct; a claim-processing rule, on the other hand, even if unalterable on a party\xe2\x80\x99s application, can nonetheless be forfeited if the party asserting the rule waits too long to raise the point.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn back now to the relevant claim-processing rules in this case. Bankruptcy Rules 4004(a) and (b) and 9006(b)(3), governing proceedings over which bankruptcy courts have subject-matter jurisdiction,[Footnote 10] serve three primary purposes. First, they inform the pleader, i.e., the objecting creditor, of the time he has to file a complaint. Second, they instruct the court on the limits of its discretion to grant motions for complaint-filing-time enlargements. Third, they afford the debtor an affirmative defense to a complaint filed outside the Rules 4004(a) and (b) limits. This case involves the third office of the Rules.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is uncontested that creditor Ryan filed his complaint objecting to debtor Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s discharge outside the Rules\xe2\x80\x99 time limits. Kontrick urges that nothing occurring thereafter counts, for the Rules\xe2\x80\x99 time prescriptions are unalterable, allowing no recourse to \xe2\x80\x9cequitable exceptions.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 13, n.\xc2\xa04; see id., at 8, 16\xe2\x80\x9318. This case, however, involves no issue of equitable tolling or any other equity-based exception. Neither at the time creditor Ryan filed the amended complaint containing the family-account claim nor anytime thereafter did he assert circumstances\xe2\x80\x94equitable or otherwise\xe2\x80\x94qualifying him for a time extension. Whether the Rules, despite their strict limitations, could be softened on equitable grounds\xc2\xa0[Footnote 11] is therefore a question we do not reach.[Footnote 12] See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 16 (\xe2\x80\x9c[M]uch of [Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s] argument is actually directed to an issue that is not presented in this case,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., whether the timing rules here in question are alterable by recourse to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98equitable exceptions imported from outside the rules.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d) (quoting Brief for Petitioner 13); Tr. of Oral Arg. 40 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhether [the bankruptcy court] would have had discretion to allow a late complaint . . . isn\xe2\x80\x99t before the Court, because [Ryan has not] claimed that [in this case] there is any equitable groun[d] for enlarging or extending the deadline, so that question isn\xe2\x80\x99t presented.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We can assume, arguendo, that had Kontrick timely asserted the untimeliness of Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s amended complaint, Kontrick would have prevailed in the litigation. The question, in that event, would have been \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the time restrictions in th[e] Rules are in such \xe2\x80\x98emphatic form\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d as to preclude equitable exceptions. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 16 (citation omitted). See, e.g., Carlisle, 517 U. S., at 419\xe2\x80\x93433 (upholding timely challenge to one-day-late filing under Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 29(c)); Taylor, 503 U. S., at 642\xe2\x80\x93646 (similar ruling regarding Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 4003(b)); Robinson, 361 U. S., at 222\xe2\x80\x93230 (similar ruling regarding Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 45(b)). Here, however, the sole question is whether Kontrick forfeited his right to assert the untimeliness of Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s amended complaint by failing to raise the issue until after that complaint was adjudicated on the merits.[Footnote 13] In other words, how long did the affirmative defense Rules 4004(a) and (b) and 9006(b)(3) afforded Kontrick linger in the proceedings?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals, we agree, followed the proper path on this key question. See 295 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 734\xe2\x80\x93735. Time bars, that court noted, generally must be raised in an answer or responsive pleading. See Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 8(c) (made applicable to adversary proceedings in bankruptcy courts by Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 7008(a)).[Footnote 14] An answer may be amended to include an inadvertently omitted affirmative defense, and even after the time to amend \xe2\x80\x9cof course\xe2\x80\x9d has passed, \xe2\x80\x9cleave [to amend] shall be freely given when justice so requires.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 15(a); see Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 7015 (\xe2\x80\x9cRule 15 F.R.Civ.P. applies in adversary proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kontrick not only failed to assert the time constraints of Rules 4004(a) and (b) and 9006(b)(3) in a pleading or amended pleading responsive to Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s amended complaint. As earlier recounted, see supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, Kontrick moved to delete certain items from Ryan\xe2\x80\x99s summary judgment filings, but, even that far into the litigation, he did not ask the Bankruptcy Court to strike the family- account claim.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ordinarily, under the Bankruptcy Rules as under the Civil Rules, a defense is lost if it is not included in the answer or amended answer. See Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 7012(b) (\xe2\x80\x9cRule 12(b)\xe2\x80\x93(h) F.R.Civ.P. applies in adversary proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d); 5A C. Wright & A. Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa71347, p. 184 (2d ed. 1990) (\xe2\x80\x9cA defense or objection that is not raised by motion or in the responsive pleading is waived unless it is protected by Rules 12(h)(2) or 12(h)(3) or by the successful invocation of the liberal amendment policy of Rule 15.\xe2\x80\x9d). Rules 12(h)(2) and (3) prolong the life of certain defenses, but time prescriptions are not among those provisions. Even if a defense based on Bankruptcy Rule 4004 could be equated to \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to state a claim upon which relief can be granted,\xe2\x80\x9d the issue could be raised, at the latest, \xe2\x80\x9cat the trial on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 12(h)(2). Only lack of subject-matter jurisdiction is preserved post-trial. Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 12(h)(3). And, as we earlier explained, see supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x9312, Kontrick\xe2\x80\x99s resistance to the family-account claim is not of that order. No reasonable construction of complaint-processing rules, in sum, would allow a litigant situated as Kontrick is to defeat a claim, as filed too late, after the party has litigated and lost the case on the merits.
