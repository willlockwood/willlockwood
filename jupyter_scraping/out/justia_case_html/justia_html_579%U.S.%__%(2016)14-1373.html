UTAH v. STRIEFF

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of utah

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x931373.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 22, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 20, 2016

\n

Narcotics detective Douglas Fackrell conducted surveillance on a South Salt Lake City residence based on an anonymous tip about drug activity. The number of people he observed making brief visits to the house over the course of a week made him suspicious that the occupants were dealing drugs. After observing respondent Edward Strieff leave the residence, Officer Fackrell detained Strieff at a nearby parking lot, identifying himself and asking Strieff what he was doing at the house. He then requested Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s identification and relayed the information to a police dispatcher, who informed him that Strieff had an outstanding arrest warrant for a traffic violation. Officer Fackrell arrested Strieff, searched him, and found methamphetamine and drug paraphernalia. Strieff moved to suppress the evidence, arguing that it was derived from an unlawful investigatory stop. The trial court denied the motion, and the Utah Court of Appeals affirmed. The Utah Supreme Court reversed, however, and ordered the evidence suppressed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The evidence Officer Fackrell seized incident to Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s arrest is admissible based on an application of the attenuation factors from Brown v. Illinois,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 590\n. In this case, there was no flagrant police misconduct. Therefore, Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s discovery of a valid, pre-existing, and untainted arrest warrant attenuated the connection between the unconstitutional investigatory stop and the evidence seized incident to a lawful arrest. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0As the primary judicial remedy for deterring\n \nFourth Amendment violations, the exclusionary rule encompasses both the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary evidence obtained as a direct result of an illegal search or seizure\xe2\x80\x9d and, relevant here, \xe2\x80\x9cevidence later discovered and found to be derivative of an illegality.\xe2\x80\x9d Segura v. United States,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 796\n. But to ensure that those deterrence benefits are not outweighed by the rule\xe2\x80\x99s substantial social costs, there are several exceptions to the rule. One exception is the attenuation doctrine, which provides for admissibility when the connection between unconstitutional police conduct and the evidence is sufficiently remote or has been interrupted by some intervening circumstance. See Hudson v. Michigan,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 586\n. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0As a threshold matter, the attenuation doctrine is not limited to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s independent acts. The doctrine therefore applies here, where the intervening circumstance is the discovery of a valid, pre-existing, and untainted arrest warrant. Assuming, without deciding, that Officer Fackrell lacked reasonable suspicion to stop Strieff initially, the discovery of that arrest warrant attenuated the connection between the unlawful stop and the evidence seized from Strieff incident to his arrest. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0Three factors articulated in Brown v. Illinois,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 590\n, lead to this conclusion. The first, \xe2\x80\x9ctemporal proximity\xe2\x80\x9d between the initially unlawful stop and the search, id., at 603, favors suppressing the evidence. Officer Fackrell discovered drug contraband on Strieff only minutes after the illegal stop. In contrast, the second factor, \xe2\x80\x9cthe presence of intervening circumstances, id., at 603\xe2\x80\x93604, strongly favors the State. The existence of a valid warrant, predating the investigation and entirely unconnected with the stop, favors finding sufficient attenuation between the unlawful conduct and the discovery of evidence. That warrant authorized Officer Fackrell to arrest Strieff, and once the arrest was authorized, his search of Strieff incident to that arrest was undisputedly lawful. The third factor, \xe2\x80\x9cthe purpose and flagrancy of the official misconduct,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 604, also strongly favors the State. Officer Fackrell was at most negligent, but his errors in judgment hardly rise to a purposeful or flagrant violation of Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFourth Amendment rights. After the unlawful stop, his conduct was lawful, and there is no indication that the stop was part of any systemic or recurrent police misconduct. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s counterarguments are unpersuasive. First, neither Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s purpose nor the flagrancy of the violation rises to a level of misconduct warranting suppression. Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s purpose was not to conduct a suspicionless fishing expedition but was to gather information about activity inside a house whose occupants were legitimately suspected of dealing drugs. Strieff conflates the standard for an illegal stop with the standard for flagrancy, which requires more than the mere absence of proper cause. Second, it is unlikely that the prevalence of outstanding warrants will lead to dragnet searches by police. Such misconduct would expose police to civil liability and, in any event, is already accounted for by Brown\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose and flagrancy\xe2\x80\x9d factor. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

2015 UT 2, 357 P.\xc2\xa03d 532, reversed.

\n

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 14\xe2\x80\x931373

UTAH, PETITIONER v. EDWARDJOSEPH STRIEFF, JR.

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of utah

[June 20, 2016]

Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

To enforce the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition against \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable searches and seizures,\xe2\x80\x9d this Court has at times required courts to exclude evidence obtained by unconstitutional police conduct. But the Court has also held that, even when there is a Fourth Amendment violation, this exclusionary rule does not apply when the costs of exclusion outweigh its deterrent benefits. In some cases, for example, the link between the unconstitutional conduct and the discovery of the evidence is too attenuated to justify suppression. The question in this case is whether this attenuation doctrine applies when an officer makesan unconstitutional investigatory stop; learns during that stop that the suspect is subject to a valid arrest warrant; and proceeds to arrest the suspect and seize incriminating evidence during a search incident to that arrest. We hold that the evidence the officer seized as part of the search incident to arrest is admissible because the officer\xe2\x80\x99s discovery of the arrest warrant attenuated the connection between the unlawful stop and the evidence seized incident to arrest.

I

This case began with an anonymous tip. In December 2006, someone called the South Salt Lake City police\xe2\x80\x99s drug-tip line to report \xe2\x80\x9cnarcotics activity\xe2\x80\x9d at a particular residence. App. 15. Narcotics detective Douglas Fackrell investigated the tip. Over the course of about a week, Officer Fackrell conducted intermittent surveillance of the home. He observed visitors who left a few minutes after arriving at the house. These visits were sufficiently frequent to raise his suspicion that the occupants were dealing drugs.

One of those visitors was respondent Edward Strieff. Officer Fackrell observed Strieff exit the house and walk toward a nearby convenience store. In the store\xe2\x80\x99s parking lot, Officer Fackrell detained Strieff, identified himself, and asked Strieff what he was doing at the residence.

As part of the stop, Officer Fackrell requested Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s identification, and Strieff produced his Utah identification card. Officer Fackrell relayed Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s information to a police dispatcher, who reported that Strieff had an outstanding arrest warrant for a traffic violation. Officer Fackrell then arrested Strieff pursuant to that warrant. When Officer Fackrell searched Strieff incident to the arrest, he discovered a baggie of methamphetamine and drug paraphernalia.

The State charged Strieff with unlawful possession of methamphetamine and drug paraphernalia. Strieff moved to suppress the evidence, arguing that the evidence was inadmissible because it was derived from an unlawful investigatory stop. At the suppression hearing, the prosecutor conceded that Officer Fackrell lacked reasonable suspicion for the stop but argued that the evidence should not be suppressed because the existence of a valid arrest warrant attenuated the connection between the unlawful stop and the discovery of the contraband.

The trial court agreed with the State and admitted the evidence. The court found that the short time between the illegal stop and the search weighed in favor of suppressing the evidence, but that two countervailing considerations made it admissible. First, the court considered the presence of a valid arrest warrant to be an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98extraordinary intervening circumstance.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 102 (quoting United States v. Simpson, 439 F.\xc2\xa03d 490, 496 (CA8 2006). Second, the court stressed the absence of flagrant misconduct by Officer Fackrell, who was conducting a legitimate investigation of a suspected drug house.

Strieff conditionally pleaded guilty to reduced charges of attempted possession of a controlled substance and possession of drug paraphernalia, but reserved his right to appeal the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of the suppression motion. The Utah Court of Appeals affirmed. 2012 UT App 245, 286 P.\xc2\xa03d 317.

The Utah Supreme Court reversed. 2015 UT 2, 357 P.\xc2\xa03d 532. It held that the evidence was inadmissible because only \xe2\x80\x9ca voluntary act of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s free will (as in a confession or consent to search)\xe2\x80\x9d sufficiently breaks the connection between an illegal search and the discovery of evidence. Id., at 536. Because Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s discovery of a valid arrest warrant did not fit this description, the court ordered the evidence suppressed. Ibid.

We granted certiorari to resolve disagreement about how the attenuation doctrine applies where an unconstitutional detention leads to the discovery of a valid arrest warrant. 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015). Compare, e.g., United States v. Green, 111 F.\xc2\xa03d 515, 522\xe2\x80\x93523 (CA7 1997) (holding that discovery of the warrant is a dispositive intervening circumstance where police misconduct was not flagrant), with, e.g., State v. Moralez, 297 Kan. 397, 415, 300 P.\xc2\xa03d 1090, 1102 (2013) (assigning little significance to the discovery of the warrant). We now reverse.

II

A

The Fourth Amendment protects \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures.\xe2\x80\x9d Because officers who violated the Fourth Amendment were traditionally considered trespassers, individuals subject to unconstitutional searches or seizures histori-cally enforced their rights through tort suits or self-help. Davies, Recovering the Original Fourth Amendment, 98 Mich. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 547, 625 (1999). In the 20th century, however, the exclusionary rule\xe2\x80\x94the rule that often requires trial courts to exclude unlawfully seized evidence in a criminal trial\xe2\x80\x94became the principal judicial remedy to deter Fourth Amendment violations. See, e.g., Mapp v. Ohio,367 U.\xc2\xa0S. 643,655 (1961).

Under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, the exclusionary rule encompasses both the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary evidence obtained as a direct result of an illegal search or seizure\xe2\x80\x9d and, relevant here, \xe2\x80\x9cevidence later discovered and found to be derivative of an illegality,\xe2\x80\x9d the so-called \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fruit of the poisonous tree.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Segura v. United States,468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 796,804 (1984). But the significant costs of this rule have led us to deem it \xe2\x80\x9capplicable only .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. where its deterrence benefits outweigh its substantial social costs.\xe2\x80\x9d Hudson v. Michigan,547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 586,591 (2006) (internal quotation marks omitted). \xe2\x80\x9cSuppression of evidence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. has always been our last resort, not our first impulse.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

We have accordingly recognized several exceptions to the rule. Three of these exceptions involve the causal relationship between the unconstitutional act and the discovery of evidence. First, the independent source doctrine allows trial courts to admit evidence obtained in an unlawful search if officers independently acquired it from a separate, independent source. See Murray v. United States,487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 533,537 (1988). Second, the inevitable discovery doctrine allows for the admission of evidence that would have been discovered even without the unconstitutional source. See Nix v. Williams,467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 431\xe2\x80\x93444 (1984). Third, and at issue here, is the attenuation doctrine: Evidence is admissible when the connection between unconstitutional police conduct and the evidence is remote or has been interrupted by some intervening circumstance, so that \xe2\x80\x9cthe interest protected by the constitutional guarantee that has been violated would not be served by suppression of the evidence obtained.\xe2\x80\x9d Hudson, supra, at 593.

B

Turning to the application of the attenuation doctrine to this case, we first address a threshold question: whether this doctrine applies at all to a case like this, where the intervening circumstance that the State relies on is the discovery of a valid, pre-existing, and untainted arrest warrant. The Utah Supreme Court declined to apply the attenuation doctrine because it read our precedents as applying the doctrine only \xe2\x80\x9cto circumstances involving an independent act of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98free will\xe2\x80\x99 in confessing to a crime or consenting to a search.\xe2\x80\x9d 357 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 544. In this Court, Strieff has not defended this argument, and we disagree with it, as well. The attenuation doctrine evaluates the causal link between the government\xe2\x80\x99s unlawful act and the discovery of evidence, which often has nothing to do with a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s actions. And the logic of our prior attenuation cases is not limited to independent acts by the defendant.

It remains for us to address whether the discovery of a valid arrest warrant was a sufficient intervening event to break the causal chain between the unlawful stop and the discovery of drug-related evidence on Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s person. The three factors articulated in Brown v. Illinois,422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 590 (1975), guide our analysis. First, we look to the \xe2\x80\x9ctemporal proximity\xe2\x80\x9d between the unconstitutional conduct and the discovery of evidence to determine how closely the discovery of evidence followed the unconstitutional search. Id., at 603. Second, we consider \xe2\x80\x9cthe presence of intervening circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 603\xe2\x80\x93604. Third, and \xe2\x80\x9cparticularly\xe2\x80\x9d significant, we examine \xe2\x80\x9cthe purpose and flagrancy of the official misconduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 604. In evaluating these factors, we assume without deciding (because the State conceded the point) that Officer Fackrell lacked reasonable suspicion to initially stop Strieff. And, because we ultimately conclude that the warrant breaks the causal chain, we also have no need to decide whether the warrant\xe2\x80\x99s existence alone would make the initial stop constitutional even if Officer Fackrell was unaware of its existence.

1

The first factor, temporal proximity between the ini-tially unlawful stop and the search, favors suppressing the evidence. Our precedents have declined to find that this factor favors attenuation unless \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial time\xe2\x80\x9d elapses between an unlawful act and when the evidence is obtained. Kaupp v. Texas,538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 626,633 (2003) (per curiam). Here, however, Officer Fackrell discovered drug contraband on Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s person only minutes after the illegal stop. See App. 18\xe2\x80\x9319. As the Court explained in Brown, such a short time interval counsels in favor of suppression; there, we found that the confession should be suppressed, relying in part on the \xe2\x80\x9cless than two hours\xe2\x80\x9d that separated the unconstitutional arrest and the confession. 422 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 604.

In contrast, the second factor, the presence of intervening circumstances, strongly favors the State. In Segura,468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 796, the Court addressed similar facts to those here and found sufficient intervening circumstances to allow the admission of evidence. There, agents had probable cause to believe that apartment occupants were dealing cocaine. Id., at 799\xe2\x80\x93800. They sought a warrant. In the meantime, they entered the apartment, arrested an occupant, and discovered evidence of drug activity during a limited search for security reasons. Id., at 800\xe2\x80\x93801. The next evening, the Magistrate Judge issued the search warrant. Ibid. This Court deemed the evidence admissible notwithstanding the illegal search because the information supporting the warrant was \xe2\x80\x9cwholly unconnected with the [arguably illegal] entry and was known to the agents well before the initial entry.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 814.

Segura, of course, applied the independent source doctrine because the unlawful entry \xe2\x80\x9cdid not contribute in any way to discovery of the evidence seized under the warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 815. But the Segura Court suggested that the existence of a valid warrant favors finding that the connection between unlawful conduct and the discovery of evidence is \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently attenuated to dissipate the taint.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That principle applies here.

In this case, the warrant was valid, it predated Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s investigation, and it was entirely unconnected with the stop. And once Officer Fackrell discovered the warrant, he had an obligation to arrest Strieff. \xe2\x80\x9cA warrant is a judicial mandate to an officer to conduct a search or make an arrest, and the officer has a sworn duty to carry out its provisions.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Leon,468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 897,920, n.21 (1984) (internal quotation marks omitted). Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s arrest of Strieff thus was a ministerial act that was independently compelled by the pre-existing warrant. And once Officer Fackrell was authorized to arrest Strieff, it was undisputedly lawful to search Strieff as an incident of his arrest to protect Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s safety. See Arizona v. Gant,556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 332,339 (2009) (explaining the permissible scope of searches incident to arrest).

Finally, the third factor, \xe2\x80\x9cthe purpose and flagrancy of the official misconduct,\xe2\x80\x9d Brown, supra, at 604, also strongly favors the State. The exclusionary rule exists to deter police misconduct. Davis v. United States,564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 229\xe2\x80\x93237 (2011). The third factor of the attenuation doctrine reflects that rationale by favoring exclusion only when the police misconduct is most in need of deterrence\xe2\x80\x94that is, when it is purposeful or flagrant.

Officer Fackrell was at most negligent. In stopping Strieff, Officer Fackrell made two good-faith mistakes. First, he had not observed what time Strieff entered the suspected drug house, so he did not know how long Strieff had been there. Officer Fackrell thus lacked a sufficient basis to conclude that Strieff was a short-term visitor who may have been consummating a drug transaction. Second, because he lacked confirmation that Strieff was a short-term visitor, Officer Fackrell should have asked Strieff whether he would speak with him, instead of demanding that Strieff do so. Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s stated purpose was to \xe2\x80\x9cfind out what was going on [in] the house.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 17. Nothing prevented him from approaching Strieff simply to ask. See Florida v. Bostick,501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429,434 (1991) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] seizure does not occur simply because a police officer approaches an individual and asks a few questions\xe2\x80\x9d). But these errors in judgment hardly rise to a purposeful or flagrant violation of Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s Fourth Amendment rights.

While Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s decision to initiate the stop was mistaken, his conduct thereafter was lawful. The officer\xe2\x80\x99s decision to run the warrant check was a \xe2\x80\x9cnegligibly burdensome precautio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d for officer safety. Rodriguez v. United States, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 7). And Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s actual search of Strieff was a lawful search incident to arrest. See Gant, supra, at 339.

Moreover, there is no indication that this unlawful stop was part of any systemic or recurrent police misconduct. To the contrary, all the evidence suggests that the stop was an isolated instance of negligence that occurred in connection with a bona fide investigation of a suspected drug house. Officer Fackrell saw Strieff leave a suspected drug house. And his suspicion about the house was based on an anonymous tip and his personal observations.

Applying these factors, we hold that the evidence discovered on Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s person was admissible because the unlawful stop was sufficiently attenuated by the pre-existing arrest warrant. Although the illegal stop was close in time to Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s arrest, that consideration is outweighed by two factors supporting the State. The outstanding arrest warrant for Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s arrest is a critical intervening circumstance that is wholly independent of the illegal stop. The discovery of that warrant broke the causal chain between the unconstitutional stop and the discovery of evidence by compelling Officer Fackrell to arrest Strieff. And, it is especially significant that there is no evidence that Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s illegal stop reflected flagrantly unlawful police misconduct.

2

We find Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s counterarguments unpersuasive.

First, he argues that the attenuation doctrine should not apply because the officer\xe2\x80\x99s stop was purposeful and flagrant. He asserts that Officer Fackrell stopped him solely to fish for evidence of suspected wrongdoing. But Officer Fackrell sought information from Strieff to find out what was happening inside a house whose occupants were legitimately suspected of dealing drugs. This was not a suspicionless fishing expedition \xe2\x80\x9cin the hope that something would turn up.\xe2\x80\x9d Taylor v. Alabama,457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 687,691 (1982).

Strieff argues, moreover, that Officer Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s conduct was flagrant because he detained Strieff without the necessary level of cause (here, reasonable suspicion). But that conflates the standard for an illegal stop with the standard for flagrancy. For the violation to be flagrant, more severe police misconduct is required than the mere absence of proper cause for the seizure. See, e.g., Kaupp, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 628, 633 (finding flagrant violation where a warrantless arrest was made in the arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s home after police were denied a warrant and at least some officers knew they lacked probable cause). Neither the officer\xe2\x80\x99s alleged purpose nor the flagrancy of the violation rise to a level of misconduct to warrant suppression.

Second, Strieff argues that, because of the prevalence of outstanding arrest warrants in many jurisdictions, police will engage in dragnet searches if the exclusionary rule is not applied. We think that this outcome is unlikely. Such wanton conduct would expose police to civil liability. See42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983; Monell v. New York City Dept. of Social Servs.,436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 658,690 (1978); see also Segura, 468 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 812. And in any event, the Brown factors take account of the purpose and flagrancy of police misconduct. Were evidence of a dragnet search presented here, the application of the Brown factors could be different. But there is no evidence that the concerns that Strieff raises with the criminal justice system are present in South Salt Lake City, Utah.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

We hold that the evidence Officer Fackrell seized as part of his search incident to arrest is admissible because his discovery of the arrest warrant attenuated the connection between the unlawful stop and the evidence seized from Strieff incident to arrest. The judgment of the Utah Supreme Court, accordingly, is reversed.

It is so ordered.

[June 20, 2016]

\n

Justice Kagan, with whom Justice Ginsburg joins, dissenting.

\n

If a police officer stops a person on the street without reasonable suspicion, that seizure violates the\n \nFourth Amendment. And if the officer pats down the unlawfully detained individual and finds drugs in his pocket, the State may not use the contraband as evidence in a criminal prosecution. That much is beyond dispute. The question here is whether the prohibition on admitting evidence dissolves if the officer discovers, after making the stop but before finding the drugs, that the person has an outstanding arrest warrant. Because that added wrinkle makes no difference under the Constitution, I respectfully dissent.

\n

This Court has established a simple framework for determining whether to exclude evidence obtained through a\n \nFourth Amendment violation: Suppression is necessary when, but only when, its societal benefits outweigh its costs. See ante, at 4; Davis v. United States,\n\n564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 229,\n237 (2011)\n. The exclusionary rule serves a crucial function\xe2\x80\x94to deter unconstitutional police conduct. By barring the use of illegally obtained evidence, courts reduce the temptation for police officers to skirt the\n \nFourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s requirements. See James v. Illinois,\n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 307,\n319 (1990)\n. But suppression of evidence also \xe2\x80\x9cexacts a heavy toll\xe2\x80\x9d: Its consequence in many cases is to release a criminal without just punishment. Davis, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 237. Our decisions have thus endeavored to strike a sound balance between those two competing considerations\xe2\x80\x94rejecting the \xe2\x80\x9creflexive\xe2\x80\x9d impulse to exclude evidence every time an officer runs afoul of the\n \nFourth Amendment, id., at 238, but insisting on suppression when it will lead to \xe2\x80\x9cappreciable deterrence\xe2\x80\x9d of police misconduct, Herring v. United States,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 135,\n141 (2009)\n.

\n

This case thus requires the Court to determine whether excluding the fruits of Officer Douglas Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s unjustified stop of Edward Strieff would significantly deter police from committing similar constitutional violations in the future. And as the Court states, that inquiry turns on application of the \xe2\x80\x9cattenuation doctrine,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x94our effort to \xe2\x80\x9cmark the point\xe2\x80\x9d at which the discovery of evidence \xe2\x80\x9cbecome[s] so attenuated\xe2\x80\x9d from the police misconduct that the deterrent benefit of exclusion drops below its cost. United States v. Leon,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 897,\n911 (1984)\n. Since Brown v. Illinois,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 590\n\xe2\x80\x93605 (1975), three factors have guided that analysis. First, the closer the \xe2\x80\x9ctemporal proximity\xe2\x80\x9d between the unlawful act and the discovery of evidence, the greater the deterrent value of suppression. Id., at 603. Second, the more \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose[ful]\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cflagran[t]\xe2\x80\x9d the police illegality, the clearer the necessity, and better the chance, of preventing similar misbehavior. Id., at 604. And third, the presence (or absence) of \xe2\x80\x9cintervening circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d makes a difference: The stronger the causal chain between the misconduct and the evidence, the more exclusion will curb future constitutional violations. Id., at 603\xe2\x80\x93604. Here, as shown below, each of those considerations points toward suppression: Nothing in Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s discovery of an outstanding warrant so attenuated the connection between his wrongful behavior and his detection of drugs as to diminish the exclusionary rule\xe2\x80\x99s deterrent benefits.

\n

Start where the majority does: The temporal proximity factor, it forthrightly admits, \xe2\x80\x9cfavors suppressing the evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. After all, Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s discovery of drugs came just minutes after the unconstitutional stop. And in prior decisions, this Court has made clear that only the lapse of \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial time\xe2\x80\x9d between the two could favor admission. Kaupp v. Texas,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 626,\n633 (2003)\n (per curiam); see, e.g., Brown, 422 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 604 (suppressing a confession when \xe2\x80\x9cless than two hours\xe2\x80\x9d separated it from an unlawful arrest). So the State, by all accounts, takes strike one.

\n

Move on to the purposefulness of Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, where the majority is less willing to see a problem for what it is. The majority chalks up Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFourth Amendment violation to a couple of innocent \xe2\x80\x9cmistakes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. But far from a Barney Fife-type mishap, Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s seizure of Strieff was a calculated decision, taken with so little justification that the State has never tried to defend its legality. At the suppression hearing, Fackrell acknowledged that the stop was designed for investigatory purposes\xe2\x80\x94i.e., to \xe2\x80\x9cfind out what was going on [in] the house\xe2\x80\x9d he had been watching, and to figure out \xe2\x80\x9cwhat [Strieff] was doing there.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 17\xe2\x80\x9318. And Fackrell frankly admitted that he had no basis for his action except that Strieff \xe2\x80\x9cwas coming out of the house.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 17. Plug in Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s and Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s names, substitute \xe2\x80\x9cstop\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9carrest\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9creasonable suspicion\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cprobable cause,\xe2\x80\x9d and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Brown perfectly describes this case:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is not disputed that [Fackrell stopped Strieff] without [reasonable suspicion]. [He] later testified that [he] made the [stop] for the purpose of questioning [Strieff] as part of [his] investigation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. The illegality here .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. had a quality of purposefulness. The impropriety of the [stop] was obvious. [A]wareness of that fact was virtually conceded by [Fackrell] when [he] repeatedly acknowledged, in [his] testimony, that the purpose of [his] action was \xe2\x80\x98for investigation\xe2\x80\x99: [Fackrell] embarked upon this expedition for evidence in the hope that something might turn up.\xe2\x80\x9d 422 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 592, 605 (some internal punctuation altered; footnote, citation, and paragraph break omitted).

\n

In Brown, the Court held those facts to support suppression\xe2\x80\x94and they do here as well. Swing and a miss for strike two.

\n

Finally, consider whether any intervening circumstance \xe2\x80\x9cbr[oke] the causal chain\xe2\x80\x9d between the stop and the evidence. Ante, at 6. The notion of such a disrupting event comes from the tort law doctrine of proximate causation. See Bridge v. Phoenix Bond & Indemnity Co.,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 639\n\xe2\x80\x93659 (2008) (explaining that a party cannot \xe2\x80\x9cestablish[\xc2\xa0] proximate cause\xe2\x80\x9d when \xe2\x80\x9can intervening cause break[s] the chain of causation between\xe2\x80\x9d the act and the injury); Kerr, Good Faith, New Law, and the Scope of the Exclusionary Rule, 99 Geo. L.\xc2\xa0J. 1077, 1099 (2011) (\n \nFourth Amendment attenuation analysis \xe2\x80\x9clooks to whether the constitutional violation was the proximate cause of the discovery of the evidence\xe2\x80\x9d). And as in the tort context, a circumstance counts as intervening only when it is unforeseeable\xe2\x80\x94not when it can be seen coming from miles away. See W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, B. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on Law of Torts 312 (5th ed. 1984). For rather than breaking the causal chain, predictable effects (e.g., X leads naturally to Y leads naturally to Z) are its very links.

\n

And Fackrell\xe2\x80\x99s discovery of an arrest warrant\xe2\x80\x94the only event the majority thinks intervened\xe2\x80\x94was an eminently foreseeable consequence of stopping Strieff. As Fackrell testified, checking for outstanding warrants during a stop is the \xe2\x80\x9cnormal\xe2\x80\x9d practice of South Salt Lake City police. App. 18; see also State v. Topanotes, 2003 UT 30, \xc2\xb62, 76 P.\xc2\xa03d 1159, 1160 (describing a warrant check as \xe2\x80\x9croutine procedure\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccommon practice\xe2\x80\x9d in Salt Lake City). In other words, the department\xe2\x80\x99s standard detention procedures\xe2\x80\x94stop, ask for identification, run a check\xe2\x80\x94are partly designed to find outstanding warrants. And find them they will, given the staggering number of such warrants on the books. See generally ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938 (Sotomayor, J., dissenting). To take just a few examples: The State of California has 2.5 million outstanding arrest warrants (a number corresponding to about 9% of its adult population); Pennsylvania (with a population of about 12.8 million) contributes 1.4 million more; and New York City (population 8.4 million) adds another 1.2 million. See Reply Brief 8; Associated Press, Pa. Database, NBC News (Apr. 8, 2007), online at http://goo.gl/3Yq3Nd (as last visited June 17, 2016); N.\xc2\xa0Y. Times, Oct. 8, 2015, p. A24.[1] So outstanding warrants do not appear as bolts from the blue. They are the run-of-the-mill results of police stops\xe2\x80\x94what officers look for when they run a routine check of a person\xe2\x80\x99s identification and what they know will turn up with fair regularity. In short, they are nothing like what intervening circumstances are supposed to be.[2] Strike three.

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s misapplication of Brown\xe2\x80\x99s three-part inquiry creates unfortunate incentives for the police\xe2\x80\x94indeed, practically invites them to do what Fackrell did here. Consider an officer who, like Fackrell, wishes to stop someone for investigative reasons, but does not have what a court would view as reasonable suspicion. If the officer believes that any evidence he discovers will be inadmissible, he is likely to think the unlawful stop not worth making\xe2\x80\x94precisely the deterrence the exclusionary rule is meant to achieve. But when he is told of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision? Now the officer knows that the stop may well yield admissible evidence: So long as the target is one of the many millions of people in this country with an outstanding arrest warrant, anything the officer finds in a search is fair game for use in a criminal prosecution. The officer\xe2\x80\x99s incentive to violate the Constitution thus increases: From here on, he sees potential advantage in stopping individuals without reasonable suspicion\xe2\x80\x94exactly the temptation the exclusionary rule is supposed to remove. Because the majority thus places\n \nFourth Amendment protections at risk, I respectfully dissent.

\n\n1\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0What is more, outstanding arrest warrants are not distributed evenly across the population. To the contrary, they are concentrated incities, towns, and neighborhoods where stops are most likely to occur\xe2\x80\x94and so the odds of any given stop revealing a warrant are even higher than the above numbers indicate. One study found, for example, that Cincinnati, Ohio had over 100,000 outstanding warrants with only 300,000 residents. See Helland & Tabarrok, The Fugitive: Evidence on Public Versus Private Law Enforcement from Bail Jumping, 47 J.\xc2\xa0Law & Econ. 93, 98 (2004). So the Court had no occasion to consider the question here: What happens when an unconstitutional act in fact leads to a warrant which then leads to evidence?\n\t\n

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

UTAH, PETITIONER v. EDWARDJOSEPH STRIEFF, JR.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of utah

\n

[June 20, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Sotomayor, with whom Justice Ginsburg joins as to Parts I, II, and III, dissenting.

\n

The Court today holds that the discovery of a warrant for an unpaid parking ticket will forgive a police officer\xe2\x80\x99s violation of your\n \nFourth Amendment rights. Do not be soothed by the opinion\xe2\x80\x99s technical language: This case allows the police to stop you on the street, demand your identification, and check it for outstanding traffic warrants\xe2\x80\x94even if you are doing nothing wrong. If the officer discovers a warrant for a fine you forgot to pay, courts will now excuse his illegal stop and will admit into evidence anything he happens to find by searching you after arresting you on the warrant. Because the\n \nFourth Amendment should prohibit, not permit, such misconduct, I dissent.

\n

I

\n

Minutes after Edward Strieff walked out of a South Salt Lake City home, an officer stopped him, questioned him, and took his identification to run it through a police database. The officer did not suspect that Strieff had done anything wrong. Strieff just happened to be the first person to leave a house that the officer thought might contain \xe2\x80\x9cdrug activity.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 16\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

As the State of Utah concedes, this stop was illegal. App. 24. The\n \nFourth Amendment protects people from \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable searches and seizures.\xe2\x80\x9d An officer breaches that protection when he detains a pedestrian to check his license without any evidence that the person is engaged in a crime. Delaware v. Prouse,\n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 648,\n663 (1979)\n; Terry v. Ohio,\n\n392 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n21 (1968)\n. The officer deepens the breach when he prolongs the detention just to fish further for evidence of wrongdoing. Rodriguez v. United States, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2015) (slip op., at 6\xe2\x80\x937). In his search for lawbreaking, the officer in this case himself broke the law.

\n

The officer learned that Strieff had a \xe2\x80\x9csmall traffic warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 19. Pursuant to that warrant, he arrested Strieff and, conducting a search incident to the arrest, discovered methamphetamine in Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s pockets.

\n

Utah charged Strieff with illegal drug possession. Before trial, Strieff argued that admitting the drugs into evidence would condone the officer\xe2\x80\x99s misbehavior. The methamphetamine, he reasoned, was the product of the officer\xe2\x80\x99s illegal stop. Admitting it would tell officers that unlawfully discovering even a \xe2\x80\x9csmall traffic warrant\xe2\x80\x9d would give them license to search for evidence of unrelated offenses. The Utah Supreme Court unanimously agreed with Strieff. A majority of this Court now reverses.

\n

II

\n

It is tempting in a case like this, where illegal conduct by an officer uncovers illegal conduct by a civilian, to forgive the officer. After all, his instincts, although unconstitutional, were correct. But a basic principle lies at the heart of the\n \nFourth Amendment: Two wrongs don\xe2\x80\x99t make a right. See Weeks v. United States,\n\n232 U.\xc2\xa0S. 383,\n392 (1914)\n. When \xe2\x80\x9clawless police conduct\xe2\x80\x9d uncovers evidence of lawless civilian conduct, this Court has long required later criminal trials to exclude the illegally obtained evidence. Terry, 392 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 12; Mapp v. Ohio,\n\n367 U.\xc2\xa0S. 643,\n655 (1961)\n. For example, if an officer breaks into a home and finds a forged check lying around, that check may not be used to prosecute the homeowner for bank fraud. We would describe the check as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fruit of the poisonous tree.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Wong Sun v. United States,\n\n371 U.\xc2\xa0S. 471,\n488 (1963)\n. Fruit that must be cast aside includes not only evidence directly found by an illegal search but also evidence \xe2\x80\x9ccome at by exploitation of that illegality.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

This \xe2\x80\x9cexclusionary rule\xe2\x80\x9d removes an incentive for officers to search us without proper justification. Terry, 392 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 12. It also keeps courts from being \xe2\x80\x9cmade party to lawless invasions of the constitutional rights of citizens by permitting unhindered governmental use of the fruits of such invasions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13. When courts admit only lawfully obtained evidence, they encourage \xe2\x80\x9cthose who formulate law enforcement polices, and the officers who implement them, to incorporate\n \nFourth Amendment ideals into their value system.\xe2\x80\x9d Stone v. Powell,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 465,\n492 (1976)\n. But when courts admit illegally obtained evidence as well, they reward \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest neglect if not an open defiance of the prohibitions of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Weeks, 232 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 394.

\n

Applying the exclusionary rule, the Utah Supreme Court correctly decided that Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s drugs must be excluded because the officer exploited his illegal stop to discover them. The officer found the drugs only after learning of Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s traffic violation; and he learned of Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s traffic violation only because he unlawfully stopped Strieff to check his driver\xe2\x80\x99s license.

\n

The court also correctly rejected the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the officer\xe2\x80\x99s discovery of a traffic warrant unspoiled the poisonous fruit. The State analogizes finding the warrant to one of our earlier decisions, Wong Sun v. United States. There, an officer illegally arrested a person who, days later, voluntarily returned to the station to confess to committing a crime. 371 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 491. Even though the person would not have confessed \xe2\x80\x9cbut for the illegal actions of the police,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 488, we noted that the police did not exploit their illegal arrest to obtain the confession, id., at 491. Because the confession was obtained by \xe2\x80\x9cmeans sufficiently distinguishable\xe2\x80\x9d from the constitutional violation, we held that it could be admitted into evidence. Id., at 488, 491. The State contends that the search incident to the warrant-arrest here is similarly distinguishable from the illegal stop.

\n

But Wong Sun explains why Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s drugs must be excluded. We reasoned that a\n \nFourth Amendment violation may not color every investigation that follows but it certainly stains the actions of officers who exploit the infraction. We distinguished evidence obtained by innocuous means from evidence obtained by exploiting misconduct after considering a variety of factors: whether a long time passed, whether there were \xe2\x80\x9cintervening circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d and whether the purpose or flagrancy of the misconduct was \xe2\x80\x9ccalculated\xe2\x80\x9d to procure the evidence. Brown v. Illinois,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 590\n\xe2\x80\x93604 (1975).

\n

These factors confirm that the officer in this case discovered Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s drugs by exploiting his own illegal conduct. The officer did not ask Strieff to volunteer his name only to find out, days later, that Strieff had a warrant against him. The officer illegally stopped Strieff and immediately ran a warrant check. The officer\xe2\x80\x99s discovery of a warrant was not some intervening surprise that he could not have anticipated. Utah lists over 180,000 misdemeanor warrants in its database, and at the time of the arrest, Salt Lake County had a \xe2\x80\x9cbacklog of outstanding warrants\xe2\x80\x9d so large that it faced the \xe2\x80\x9cpotential for civil liability.\xe2\x80\x9d See Dept. of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics,Survey of State Criminal History Information Systems, 2014 (2015) (Systems Survey) (Table 5a), online at https://www.ncjrs.gov/pdffiles1/bjs/grants/249799.pdf (all Internet materials as last visited June 16, 2016); Inst. for Law and Policy Planning, Salt Lake County Crim-inal Justice System Assessment 6.7 (2004), online at http://www.\xc2\xa0slco.org\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0cjac\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0resources\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0SaltLakeCJSAfinal.pdf. The officer\xe2\x80\x99s violation was also calculated to procure evidence. His sole reason for stopping Strieff, he acknowledged, was investigative\xe2\x80\x94he wanted to discover whether drug activity was going on in the house Strieff had just exited. App. 17.

\n

The warrant check, in other words, was not an \xe2\x80\x9cintervening circumstance\xe2\x80\x9d separating the stop from the search for drugs. It was part and parcel of the officer\xe2\x80\x99s illegal \xe2\x80\x9cexpedition for evidence in the hope that something might turn up.\xe2\x80\x9d Brown, 422 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 605. Under our precedents, because the officer found Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s drugs by exploiting his own constitutional violation, the drugs should be excluded.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

The Court sees things differently. To the Court, the fact that a warrant gives an officer cause to arrest a person severs the connection between illegal policing and the resulting discovery of evidence. Ante, at 7. This is a remarkable proposition: The mere existence of a warrant not only gives an officer legal cause to arrest and search a person, it also forgives an officer who, with no knowledge of the warrant at all, unlawfully stops that person on a whim or hunch.

\n

To explain its reasoning, the Court relies on Segura v. United States,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 796 (1984)\n. There, federal agents applied for a warrant to search an apartment but illegally entered the apartment to secure it before the judge issued the warrant. Id., at 800\xe2\x80\x93801. After receiving the warrant, the agents then searched the apartment for drugs. Id., at 801. The question before us was what to do with the evidence the agents then discovered. We declined to suppress it because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he illegal entry into petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 apartment did not contribute in any way to discovery of the evidence seized under the warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 815.

\n

According to the majority, Segura involves facts \xe2\x80\x9csimilar\xe2\x80\x9d to this case and \xe2\x80\x9csuggest[s]\xe2\x80\x9d that a valid warrant will clean up whatever illegal conduct uncovered it. Ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. It is difficult to understand this interpretation. In Segura, the agents\xe2\x80\x99 illegal conduct in entering the apartment had nothing to do with their procurement of a search warrant. Here, the officer\xe2\x80\x99s illegal conduct in stopping Strieff was essential to his discovery of an arrest warrant. Segura would be similar only if the agents used information they illegally obtained from the apartment to procure a search warrant or discover an arrest warrant. Precisely because that was not the case, the Court admitted the untainted evidence. 468 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 814.

\n

The majority likewise misses the point when it calls the warrant check here a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98negligibly burdensome precautio[n]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d taken for the officer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csafety.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8 (quoting Rodriguez, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 7)). Remember, the officer stopped Strieff without suspecting him of committing any crime. By his own account, the officer did not fear Strieff. Moreover, the safety rationale we discussed in Rodriguez, an opinion about highway patrols, is conspicuously absent here. A warrant check on a highway \xe2\x80\x9censur[es] that vehicles on the road are operated safely and responsibly.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 6). We allow such checks during legal traffic stops because the legitimacy of a person\xe2\x80\x99s driver\xe2\x80\x99s license has a \xe2\x80\x9cclose connection to roadway safety.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 7). A warrant check of a pedestrian on a sidewalk, \xe2\x80\x9cby contrast, is a measure aimed at \xe2\x80\x98detect[ing] evidence of ordinary criminal wrongdoing.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Indianapolis v. Edmond,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 32\n\xe2\x80\x9341 (2000)). Surely we would not allow officers to warrant-check random joggers, dog walkers, and lemonade vendors just to ensure they pose no threat to anyone else.

\n

The majority also posits that the officer could not have exploited his illegal conduct because he did not violate the\n \nFourth Amendment on purpose. Rather, he made \xe2\x80\x9cgood-faith mistakes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. Never mind that the officer\xe2\x80\x99s sole purpose was to fish for evidence. The majority casts his unconstitutional actions as \xe2\x80\x9cnegligent\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore incapable of being deterred by the exclusionary rule. Ibid.

\n

But the\n \nFourth Amendment does not tolerate an officer\xe2\x80\x99s unreasonable searches and seizures just because he did not know any better. Even officers prone to negligence can learn from courts that exclude illegally obtained evidence. Stone, 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 492. Indeed, they are perhaps the most in need of the education, whether by the judge\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s future guidance, or an updated manual on criminal procedure. If the officers are in doubt about what the law requires, exclusion gives them an \xe2\x80\x9cincentive to err on the side of constitutional behavior.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Johnson,\n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 537,\n561 (1982)\n.

\n

B

\n

Most striking about the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is its insistence that the event here was \xe2\x80\x9cisolated,\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9cno indication that this unlawful stop was part of any systemic or recurrent police misconduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. Respectfully, nothing about this case is isolated.

\n

Outstanding warrants are surprisingly common. When a person with a traffic ticket misses a fine payment or court appearance, a court will issue a warrant. See, e.g., Brennan Center for Justice, Criminal Justice Debt 23 (2010), online at https://www.brennancenter.org/sites/default/files/legacy/Fees%20and%20Fines%20FINAL.pdf. When a person on probation drinks alcohol or breaks curfew, a court will issue a warrant. See, e.g., Human Rights Watch, Profiting from Probation 1, 51 (2014), online at https://www.hrw.org/report/2014/02/05/profiting-probation/\xc2\xa0americas\xc2\xa0-\xc2\xa0offender\xc2\xa0-\xc2\xa0funded\xc2\xa0-\xc2\xa0probation-industry. The States and Federal Government maintain databases with over 7.8 million outstanding warrants, the vast majority of which appear to be for minor offenses. See Systems Survey (Table 5a). Even these sources may not track the \xe2\x80\x9cstaggering\xe2\x80\x9d numbers of warrants, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98drawers and drawers\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d full, that many cities issue for traffic violations and ordinance infractions. Dept. of Justice, Civil Rights Div., Investigation of the Ferguson Police Department 47, 55 (2015) (Ferguson Report), online at https://www.justice.gov/ sites\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0default\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0files\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0opa\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0press\xc2\xa0-\xc2\xa0releases\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0attachments\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa02015\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa003\xc2\xa0/ 04/ferguson_police_department_report.pdf. The county in this case has had a \xe2\x80\x9cbacklog\xe2\x80\x9d of such warrants. See supra, at 4. The Department of Justice recently reported that in the town of Ferguson, Missouri, with a population of 21,000, 16,000 people had outstanding warrants against them. Ferguson Report, at 6, 55.

\n

Justice Department investigations across the country have illustrated how these astounding numbers of warrants can be used by police to stop people without cause. In a single year in New Orleans, officers \xe2\x80\x9cmade nearly 60,000 arrests, of which about 20,000 were of people with outstanding traffic or misdemeanor warrants from neighboring parishes for such infractions as unpaid tickets.\xe2\x80\x9d Dept. of Justice, Civil Rights Div., Investigation of the New Orleans Police Department 29 (2011), online at https://www.justice.gov\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0sites\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0default\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0files\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0crt\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0legacy/2011/03/17/nopd_report.pdf. In the St.\xc2\xa0Louis metropolitan area, officers \xe2\x80\x9croutinely\xe2\x80\x9d stop people\xe2\x80\x94on the street, at bus stops, or even in court\xe2\x80\x94for no reason other than \xe2\x80\x9can officer\xe2\x80\x99s desire to check whether the subject had a municipal arrest warrant pending.\xe2\x80\x9d Ferguson Report, at 49, 57. In Newark, New Jersey, officers stopped 52,235 pedestrians within a 4-year period and ran warrant checks on 39,308 of them. Dept. of Justice, Civil Rights Div., Investigation of the Newark Police Department 8, 19, n.\xc2\xa015 (2014), online at https://www.justice.gov/sites\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0/default\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0files\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0crt\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0/legacy\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02014\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa007\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa022\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0newark\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0_\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0findings\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0_7-22-14.pdf. The Justice Department analyzed these warrant-checked stops and reported that \xe2\x80\x9capproximately 93% of the stops would have been considered unsupported by articulated reason-able suspicion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 9, n.\xc2\xa07.

\n

I do not doubt that most officers act in \xe2\x80\x9cgood faith\xe2\x80\x9d and do not set out to break the law. That does not mean these stops are \xe2\x80\x9cisolated instance[s] of negligence,\xe2\x80\x9d however. Ante, at 8. Many are the product of institutionalized training procedures. The New York City Police Department long trained officers to, in the words of a District Judge, \xe2\x80\x9cstop and question first, develop reasonable suspicion later.\xe2\x80\x9d Ligon v. New York, 925 F. Supp. 2d 478, 537\xe2\x80\x93538 (SDNY), stay granted on other grounds, 736 F.\xc2\xa03d 118 (CA2 2013). The Utah Supreme Court described as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98routine procedure\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98common practice\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the decision of Salt Lake City police officers to run warrant checks on pedestrians they detained without reasonable suspicion. State v. Topanotes, 2003 UT 30, \xc2\xb62, 76 P.\xc2\xa03d 1159, 1160. In the related context of traffic stops, one widely followed police manual instructs officers looking for drugs to \xe2\x80\x9crun at least a warrants check on all drivers you stop. Statistically, narcotics offenders are .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. more likely to fail to appear on simple citations, such as traffic or trespass violations, leading to the issuance of bench warrants. Discovery of an outstanding warrant gives you cause for an immediate custodial arrest and search of the suspect.\xe2\x80\x9d C. Remsberg, Tactics for Criminal Patrol 205\xe2\x80\x93206 (1995); C. Epp et\xc2\xa0al., Pulled Over 23, 33\xe2\x80\x9336 (2014).

\n

The majority does not suggest what makes this case \xe2\x80\x9cisolated\xe2\x80\x9d from these and countless other examples. Nor does it offer guidance for how a defendant can prove that his arrest was the result of \xe2\x80\x9cwidespread\xe2\x80\x9d misconduct. Surely it should not take a federal investigation of Salt Lake County before the Court would protect someone in Strieff\xe2\x80\x99s position.

\n

IV

\n

Writing only for myself, and drawing on my professional experiences, I would add that unlawful \xe2\x80\x9cstops\xe2\x80\x9d have severe consequences much greater than the inconvenience suggested by the name. This Court has given officers an array of instruments to probe and examine you. When we condone officers\xe2\x80\x99 use of these devices without adequate cause, we give them reason to target pedestrians in an arbitrary manner. We also risk treating members of our communities as second-class citizens.

\n

Although many Americans have been stopped for speeding or jaywalking, few may realize how degrading a stop can be when the officer is looking for more. This Court has allowed an officer to stop you for whatever reason he wants\xe2\x80\x94so long as he can point to a pretextual justification after the fact. Whren v. United States,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 806,\n813 (1996)\n. That justification must provide specific reasons why the officer suspected you were breaking the law, Terry, 392 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 21, but it may factor in your ethnicity, United States v. Brignoni-Ponce,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 873\n\xe2\x80\x93887 (1975), where you live, Adams v. Williams,\n\n407 U.\xc2\xa0S. 143,\n147 (1972)\n, what you were wearing, United States v. Sokolow,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x935 (1989), and how you behaved, Illinois v. Wardlow,\n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 119\n\xe2\x80\x93125 (2000). The officer does not even need to know which law you might have broken so long as he can later point to any possible infraction\xe2\x80\x94even one that is minor, unrelated, or ambiguous. Devenpeck v. Alford,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 146\n\xe2\x80\x93155 (2004); Heien v. North Carolina, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014).

\n

The indignity of the stop is not limited to an officer telling you that you look like a criminal. See Epp, Pulled Over, at 5. The officer may next ask for your \xe2\x80\x9cconsent\xe2\x80\x9d to inspect your bag or purse without telling you that you can decline. See Florida v. Bostick,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429,\n438 (1991)\n. Regardless of your answer, he may order you to stand \xe2\x80\x9chelpless, perhaps facing a wall with [your] hands raised.\xe2\x80\x9d Terry, 392 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 17. If the officer thinks you might be dangerous, he may then \xe2\x80\x9cfrisk\xe2\x80\x9d you for weapons. This involves more than just a pat down. As onlookers pass by, the officer may \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98feel with sensitive fingers every portion of [your] body. A thorough search [may] be made of [your] arms and armpits, waistline and back, the groin and area about the testicles, and entire surface of the legs down to the feet.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 17, n.\xc2\xa013.

\n

The officer\xe2\x80\x99s control over you does not end with the stop. If the officer chooses, he may handcuff you and take you to jail for doing nothing more than speeding, jaywalking, or \xe2\x80\x9cdriving [your] pickup truck .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with [your] 3-year-old son and 5-year-old daughter .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. without [your] seatbelt fastened.\xe2\x80\x9d Atwater v. Lago Vista,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 318\n\xe2\x80\x93324 (2001). At the jail, he can fingerprint you, swab DNA from the inside of your mouth, and force you to \xe2\x80\x9cshower with a delousing agent\xe2\x80\x9d while you \xe2\x80\x9clift [your] tongue, hold out [your] arms, turn around, and lift [your] genitals.\xe2\x80\x9d Florence v. Board of Chosen Freeholders of County of Burlington, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2012) (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x933); Maryland v. King, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 28). Even if you are innocent, you will now join the 65 million Americans with an arrest record and experience the \xe2\x80\x9ccivil death\xe2\x80\x9d of discrimination by employers, landlords, and whoever else conducts a background check. Chin, The New Civil Death, 160 U. Pa. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1789, 1805 (2012); see J. Jacobs, The Eternal Criminal Record 33\xe2\x80\x9351 (2015); Young & Petersilia, Keeping Track, 129 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1318, 1341\xe2\x80\x931357 (2016). And, of course, if you fail to pay bail or appear for court, a judge will issue a warrant to render you \xe2\x80\x9carrestable on sight\xe2\x80\x9d in the future. A. Goffman, On the Run 196 (2014).

\n

This case involves a suspicionless stop, one in which the officer initiated this chain of events without justification. As the Justice Department notes, supra, at 8, many innocent people are subjected to the humiliations of these unconstitutional searches. The white defendant in this case shows that anyone\xe2\x80\x99s dignity can be violated in this manner. See M. Gottschalk, Caught 119\xe2\x80\x93138 (2015). But it is no secret that people of color are disproportionate victims of this type of scrutiny. See M. Alexander, The New Jim Crow 95\xe2\x80\x93136 (2010). For generations, black and brown parents have given their children \xe2\x80\x9cthe talk\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94instructing them never to run down the street; always keep your hands where they can be seen; do not even think of talking back to a stranger\xe2\x80\x94all out of fear of how an officer with a gun will react to them. See, e.g., W. E. B. Du Bois, The Souls of Black Folk (1903); J. Baldwin, The Fire Next Time (1963); T. Coates, Between the World and Me (2015).

\n

By legitimizing the conduct that produces this double consciousness, this case tells everyone, white and black, guilty and innocent, that an officer can verify your legal status at any time. It says that your body is subject to invasion while courts excuse the violation of your rights. It implies that you are not a citizen of a democracy but the subject of a carceral state, just waiting to be cataloged.

\n

We must not pretend that the countless people who are routinely targeted by police are \xe2\x80\x9cisolated.\xe2\x80\x9d They are the canaries in the coal mine whose deaths, civil and literal, warn us that no one can breathe in this atmosphere. See L. Guinier & G. Torres, The Miner\xe2\x80\x99s Canary 274\xe2\x80\x93283 (2002). They are the ones who recognize that unlawful police stops corrode all our civil liberties and threaten all our lives. Until their voices matter too, our justice system will continue to be anything but.

\n

I dissent.
