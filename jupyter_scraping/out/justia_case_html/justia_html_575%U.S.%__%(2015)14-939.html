SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

STANLEY TAYLOR, et\xc2\xa0al. v. KAREN BARKES, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93939.\xe2\x80\x83Decided June 1, 2015

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

Christopher Barkes, \xe2\x80\x9ca troubled man with a long history of mental health and substance abuse problems,\xe2\x80\x9d was arrested on November 13, 2004, for violating his probation. Barkes v. First Correctional Medical, Inc., 766 F.\xc2\xa03d 307, 310\xe2\x80\x93311 (CA3 2014). Barkes was taken to the Howard R. Young Correctional Institution in Wilmington, Delaware. As part of Barkes\xe2\x80\x99s intake, a nurse who worked for the contractor providing healthcare at the Institution conducted a medical evaluation. Id., at 311.

\n

The evaluation included a mental health screening designed in part to assess whether an inmate was suicidal. The nurse employed a suicide screening form based on a model form developed by the National Commission on Correctional Health Care (NCCHC) in 1997. The form listed 17 suicide risk factors. If the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s responses and nurse\xe2\x80\x99s observations indicated that at least eight were present, or if certain serious risk factors were present, the nurse would notify a physician and initiate suicide prevention measures. Id., at 311, 313.

\n

Barkes disclosed that he had a history of psychiatric treatment and was on medication. He also disclosed that he had attempted suicide in 2003, though not\xe2\x80\x94as far as the record indicates\xe2\x80\x94that he had also done so on three other occasions. And he indicated that he was not cur-rently thinking about killing himself. Because only two risk factors were apparent, the nurse gave Barkes a \xe2\x80\x9croutine\xe2\x80\x9d referral to mental health services and did not initiate any special suicide prevention measures. Id., at 311.

\n

Barkes was placed in a cell by himself. Despite what he had told the nurse, that evening he called his wife and told her that he \xe2\x80\x9ccan\xe2\x80\x99t live this way anymore\xe2\x80\x9d and was going to kill himself. Barkes\xe2\x80\x99s wife did not inform anyone at the Institution of this call. The next morning, correctional officers observed Barkes awake and behaving normally at 10:45, 10:50, and 11:00 a.m. At 11:35 a.m., however, an officer arrived to deliver lunch and discovered that Barkes had hanged himself with a sheet. Id., at 311\xe2\x80\x93312.

\n

Barkes\xe2\x80\x99s wife and children, respondents here, brought suit under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, against various entities and individuals connected with the Institution, who they claimed had violated Barkes\xe2\x80\x99s civil rights in failing to prevent his suicide. At issue here is a claim against petitioners Stanley Taylor, Commissioner of the Delaware Department of Correction (DOC), and Raphael Williams, the Institution\xe2\x80\x99s warden. Although it is undisputed that neither petitioner had personally interacted with Barkes or knew of his condition before his death, respondents alleged that Taylor and Williams had violated Barkes\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional right to be free from cruel and unusual punishment. Barkes v. First Correctional Medical, Inc., 2008 WL 523216, *7 (D Del., Feb. 27, 2008). They did so, according to respondents, by failing to supervise and monitor the private contractor that provided the medical treatment\xe2\x80\x94including the intake screening\xe2\x80\x94at the Institution. Petitioners moved for summary judgment on the ground that they were entitled to qualified immu-nity, but the District Court denied the motion. Barkes v. First Correctional Medical, Inc., 2012 WL 2914915, *8\xe2\x80\x93*12 (D Del., July 17, 2012).

\n

A divided panel of the Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit affirmed. The majority first determined that respondents had alleged a cognizable theory of supervisory liability (a decision upon which we express no view). 766 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 316\xe2\x80\x93325. The majority then turned to the two-step qualified immunity inquiry, asking \xe2\x80\x9cfirst, whether the plaintiff suffered a deprivation of a constitutional or stat-utory right; and second, if so, whether that right was \xe2\x80\x98clearly established\xe2\x80\x99 at the time of the alleged misconduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 326.

\n

Taking these questions in reverse order, the Third Circuit held that it was clearly established at the time of Barkes\xe2\x80\x99s death that an incarcerated individual had an\n \nEighth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cright to the proper implementation of adequate suicide prevention protocols.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 327. The panel majority then concluded there were material factual disputes about whether petitioners had violated this right by failing to adequately supervise the contractor providing medical services at the prison. There was evidence, the majority noted, that the medical contractor\xe2\x80\x99s suicide screening process did not comply with NCCHC\xe2\x80\x99s latest standards, as required by the contract. Those standards allegedly called for a revised screening form and for screening by a qualified mental health professional, not a nurse. There was also evidence that the contractor did not have access to Barkes\xe2\x80\x99s probation records (which would have shed light on his mental health history), and that the contractor had been short-staffing to increase profits. Id., at 330\xe2\x80\x93331.

\n

Judge Hardiman dissented. As relevant here, he concluded that petitioners were entitled to qualified immu-nity because the right on which the majority relied was \xe2\x80\x9ca departure from\n \nEighth Amendment case law that had never been established before today.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 345.

\n

Taylor and Williams petitioned for certiorari. We grant the petition and reverse on the ground that there was no violation of clearly established law.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cQualified immunity shields government officials from civil damages liability unless the official violated a statutory or constitutional right that was clearly established at the time of the challenged conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Reichle v. Howards, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 5). \xe2\x80\x9cTo be clearly established, a right must be sufficiently clear that every reasonable official would have understood that what he is doing violates that right.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (brackets and internal quotation marks omitted). \xe2\x80\x9cWhen properly applied, [qualified immunity] protects all but the plainly incompetent or those who knowingly violate the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 12) (internal quotation marks omitted). \xe2\x80\x9cWe do not require a case directly on point, but existing precedent must have placed the statutory or constitutional question beyond debate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 9).

\n

The Third Circuit concluded that the right at issue was best defined as \xe2\x80\x9can incarcerated person\xe2\x80\x99s right to the proper implementation of adequate suicide prevention protocols.\xe2\x80\x9d 766 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 327. This purported right, however, was not clearly established in November 2004 in a way that placed beyond debate the unconstitutionality of the Institution\xe2\x80\x99s procedures, as implemented by the medical contractor.

\n

No decision of this Court establishes a right to theproper implementation of adequate suicide prevention pro-tocols. No decision of this Court even discusses suicide screening or prevention protocols. And \xe2\x80\x9cto the extent that a \xe2\x80\x98robust consensus of cases of persuasive authority\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in the Courts of Appeals \xe2\x80\x9ccould itself clearly establish the federal right respondent alleges,\xe2\x80\x9d City and County of San Francisco v. Sheehan, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 16), the weight of that authority at the time of Barkes\xe2\x80\x99s death suggested that such a right did not exist. See, e.g., Comstock v. McCrary, 273 F.\xc2\xa03d 693, 702 (CA6 2001) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe right to medical care for serious medical needs does not encompass the right to be screened correctly for suicidal tendencies\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Tittle v. Jefferson Cty. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 10 F.\xc2\xa03d 1535, 1540 (CA11 1994) (alleged \xe2\x80\x9cweaknesses in the [suicide] screening process, the training of deputies[,] and the supervision of prisoners\xe2\x80\x9d did not \xe2\x80\x9camount to a showing of deliberate indifference toward the rights of prisoners\xe2\x80\x9d); Burns v. Galveston, 905 F.\xc2\xa02d 100, 104 (CA5 1990) (rejecting the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9cthe right of detainees to adequate medical care includes an absolute right to psychological screening\xe2\x80\x9d); Belcher v. Oliver, 898 F.\xc2\xa02d 32, 34\xe2\x80\x9335 (CA4 1990) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe general right of pretrial detainees to receive basic medical care does not place upon jail officials the responsibility to screen every detainee for suicidal tendencies.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The Third Circuit nonetheless found this right clearly established by two of its own decisions, both stemming from the same case. Assuming for the sake of argument that a right can be \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established\xe2\x80\x9d by circuit precedent despite disagreement in the courts of appeals, neither of the Third Circuit decisions relied upon clearly established the right at issue. The first, Colburn I, said that if officials \xe2\x80\x9cknow or should know of the particular vulner-ability to suicide of an inmate,\xe2\x80\x9d they have an obligation \xe2\x80\x9cnot to act with reckless indifference to that vulnerability.\xe2\x80\x9d Colburn v. Upper Darby Twp., 838 F.\xc2\xa02d 663, 669 (1988). The decision did not say, however, that detention facilities must implement procedures to identify such vulnerable inmates, let alone specify what procedures would suffice. And the Third Circuit later acknowledged that Colburn I\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99s use of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cor should know\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94which might seem to nod toward a screening requirement of some kind\xe2\x80\x94was erroneous in light of Farmer v. Brennan,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825 (1994)\n, which held that\n \nEighth Amendment liability requires actual awareness of risk. See Serafin v. Johnstown, 53 Fed. Appx. 211, 213 (CA3 2002).

\n

Nor would Colburn II have put petitioners on notice of any possible constitutional violation. Colburn II reiter-ated that officials who know of an inmate\xe2\x80\x99s particular vul-nerability to suicide must not be recklessly indifferent to that vulnerability. Colburn v. Upper Darby Twp., 946 F.\xc2\xa02d 1017, 1023 (1991). But it did not identify any minimum screening procedures or prevention protocols that facilities must use. In fact, Colburn II revealed that the booking process of the jail at issue \xe2\x80\x9cinclude[d] no formal physical or mental health screening,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and yet the Third Circuit ruled for the defendants on all claims, see id., at 1025\xe2\x80\x931031.

\n

In short, even if the Institution\xe2\x80\x99s suicide screening and prevention measures contained the shortcomings that respondents allege, no precedent on the books in November 2004 would have made clear to petitioners that they were overseeing a system that violated the Constitution. Because, at the very least, petitioners were not contravening clearly established law, they are entitled to qualified immunity. The judgment of the Third Circuit is reversed.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.
