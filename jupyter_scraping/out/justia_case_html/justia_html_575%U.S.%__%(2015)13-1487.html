HENDERSON v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x931487.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 24, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 18, 2015

\n

After being charged with the felony offense of distributing marijuana, petitioner Tony Henderson was required as a condition of his bail to turn over firearms that he lawfully owned. Henderson ultimately pleaded guilty, and, as a felon, was prohibited under\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7922(g) from possessing his (or any other) firearms. Henderson therefore asked the Federal Bureau of Investigation, which had custody of his firearms, to transfer them to his friend. But the agency refused to do so. Henderson then filed a motion in federal district court seeking to transfer his firearms, but the court denied the motion on the ground that Henderson\xe2\x80\x99s requested transfer would give him constructive possession of the firearms in violation of \xc2\xa7922(g). The Eleventh Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A court-ordered transfer of a felon\xe2\x80\x99s lawfully owned firearms from Government custody to a third party is not barred by \xc2\xa7922(g) if the court is satisfied that the recipient will not give the felon control over the firearms, so that he could either use them or direct their use. Federal courts have equitable authority to order law enforcement to return property obtained during the course of a criminal proceeding to its rightful owner. Section 922(g), however, bars a court from ordering guns returned to a felon-owner like Henderson, because that would place the owner in violation of the law. And because \xc2\xa7922(g) bans constructive as well as actual possession, it also prevents a court from ordering the transfer of a felon\xe2\x80\x99s guns to someone willing to give the felon access to them or to accede to the felon\xe2\x80\x99s instructions about their future use.

\n

The Government goes further, arguing that \xc2\xa7922(g) prevents all transfers to a third party, no matter how independent of the felon\xe2\x80\x99s influence, unless that recipient is a licensed firearms dealer or other third party who will sell the guns on the open market. But that view conflates possession, which \xc2\xa7922(g) prohibits, with an owner\xe2\x80\x99s right merely to alienate his property, which it does not. After all, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa7922(g) would prohibit a felon from disposing of his firearms even when he would lack any control over and thus not possess them before, during, or after the disposition. That reading would also extend \xc2\xa7922(g)\xe2\x80\x99s scope far beyond its purpose; preventing a felon like Henderson from disposing of his firearms, even in ways that guarantee he never uses them again, does nothing to advance the statute\xe2\x80\x99s goal of keeping firearms away from felons. Finally, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s insistence that a felon cannot select a third-party recipient over whom he exercises no influence fits poorly with its concession that a felon may select a firearms dealer or third party to sell his guns. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa7922(g) is thus overbroad.

\n

Accordingly, a court may approve the transfer of a felon\xe2\x80\x99s guns consistently with \xc2\xa7922(g) if, but only if, the recipient will not grant the felon control over those weapons. One way to ensure that result is to order that the guns be turned over to a firearms dealer, himself independent of the felon\xe2\x80\x99s control, for subsequent sale on the open market. But that is not the only option; a court, with proper assurances from the recipient, may also grant a felon\xe2\x80\x99s request to transfer his guns to a person who expects to maintain custody of them. Either way, once a court is satisfied that the transferee will not allow the felon to exert any influence over the firearms, the court has equitable power to accommodate the felon\xe2\x80\x99s transfer request. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

555 Fed. Appx. 851, vacated and remanded.

TONY HENDERSON, PETITIONER v.UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[May 18, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Government agencies sometimes come into possession of firearms lawfully owned by individuals facing serious criminal charges. If convicted, such a person cannot recover his guns because a federal statute,\n\n18 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7922(g), prohibits any felon from possessing firearms. In this case, we consider what \xc2\xa7922(g) allows a court to do when a felon instead seeks the transfer of his guns to either a firearms dealer (for future sale on the open market) or some other third party. We hold that \xc2\xa7922(g) does not bar such a transfer unless it would allow the felon to later control the guns, so that he could either use them or direct their use.

\n

I

\n

The Federal Government charged petitioner Tony Henderson, then a U.\xc2\xa0S. Border Patrol agent, with the felony offense of distributing marijuana. See 21 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7841(a)(1), (b)(1)(D). A Magistrate Judge required that Henderson surrender all his firearms as a condition of his release on bail. Henderson complied, and the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) took custody of the guns. Soon afterward, Henderson pleaded guilty to the distribution charge; as a result of that conviction, \xc2\xa7922(g) prevents him from legally repossessing his firearms.

\n

Following his release from prison, Henderson asked the FBI to transfer the guns to Robert Rosier, a friend who had agreed to purchase them for an unspecified price. The FBI denied the request. In a letter to Henderson, it explained that \xe2\x80\x9cthe release of the firearms to [Rosier] would place you in violation of [\xc2\xa7922(g)], as it would amount to constructive possession\xe2\x80\x9d of the guns. App. 121.

\n

Henderson then returned to the court that had handled his criminal case to seek release of his firearms. Invoking the court\xe2\x80\x99s equitable powers, Henderson asked for an order directing the FBI to transfer the guns either to his wife or to Rosier. The District Court denied the motion, concluding (as the FBI had) that Henderson could not \xe2\x80\x9ctransfer the firearms or receive money from their sale\xe2\x80\x9d without \xe2\x80\x9cconstructive[ly] possessi[ng]\xe2\x80\x9d them in violation of \xc2\xa7922(g). No. 3:06\xe2\x80\x93cr\xe2\x80\x93211 (MD Fla., Aug. 8, 2012), App. to Pet. for Cert. 5a\xe2\x80\x936a, 12a. The Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed on the same ground, reasoning that granting Henderson\xe2\x80\x99s motion would amount to giving a felon \xe2\x80\x9cconstructive possession\xe2\x80\x9d of his firearms. 555 Fed. Appx. 851, 853 (2014) (per curiam).[1]

\n

We granted certiorari, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), to resolve a circuit split over whether, as the courts below held, \xc2\xa7922(g) categorically prohibits a court from approving a convicted felon\xe2\x80\x99s request to transfer his firearms to an-other person.[2] We now vacate the decision below.

\n

II

\n

A federal court has equitable authority, even after a criminal proceeding has ended, to order a law enforcement agency to turn over property it has obtained during the case to the rightful owner or his designee. See, e.g.,United States v. Martinez, 241 F.\xc2\xa03d 1329, 1330\xe2\x80\x931331 (CA11 2001) (citing numerous appellate decisions to that effect); Tr. of Oral Arg. 41 (Solicitor General agreeing). Congress, however, may cabin that power in various ways. As relevant here, \xc2\xa7922(g) makes it unlawful for any person convicted of a felony to \xe2\x80\x9cpossess in or affecting commerce[\xc2\xa0] any firearm or ammunition.\xe2\x80\x9d That provision prevents a court from instructing an agency to return guns in its custody to a felon-owner like Henderson, because that would place him in violation of the law. The question here is how \xc2\xa7922(g) affects a court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to instead direct the transfer of such firearms to a third party.

\n

Section 922(g) proscribes possession alone, but covers possession in every form. By its terms, \xc2\xa7922(g) does not prohibit a felon from owning firearms. Rather, it interferes with a single incident of ownership\xe2\x80\x94one of the proverbial sticks in the bundle of property rights\xe2\x80\x94by preventing the felon from knowingly possessing his (or an-other person\xe2\x80\x99s) guns. But that stick is a thick one, encompassing what the criminal law recognizes as \xe2\x80\x9cactual\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cconstructive\xe2\x80\x9d possession alike. 2A K. O\xe2\x80\x99Malley, J. Grenig, & W. Lee, Federal Jury Practice and Instructions, Criminal \xc2\xa739.12, p. 55 (6th ed. 2009) (hereinafter O\xe2\x80\x99Malley); see National Safe Deposit Co. v. Stead,\n\n232 U.\xc2\xa0S. 58,\n67 (1914)\n (noting that in \xe2\x80\x9clegal terminology\xe2\x80\x9d the word \xe2\x80\x9cpossession\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cinterchangeably used to describe\xe2\x80\x9d both the actual and the constructive kinds). Actual possession exists when a person has direct physical control over a thing. See Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1047 (5th ed. 1979) (hereinafter Black\xe2\x80\x99s); 2A O\xe2\x80\x99Malley \xc2\xa739.12, at 55. Constructive possession is established when a person, though lacking such physical custody, still has the power and intent to exercise control over the object. See Black\xe2\x80\x99s 1047; 2A O\xe2\x80\x99Malley \xc2\xa739.12, at 55. Section 922(g) thus prevents a felon not only from holding his firearms himself but also from maintaining control over those guns in the hands of others.

\n

That means, as all parties agree, that \xc2\xa7922(g) prevents a court from ordering the sale or other transfer of a felon\xe2\x80\x99s guns to someone willing to give the felon access to them or to accede to the felon\xe2\x80\x99s instructions about their future use. See Brief for United States 23; Reply Brief 12. In such a case, the felon would have control over the guns, even while another person kept physical custody. The idea of constructive possession is designed to preclude just that result, \xe2\x80\x9callow[ing] the law to reach beyond puppets to puppeteers.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Al-Rekabi, 454 F.\xc2\xa03d 1113, 1118 (CA10 2006). A felon cannot evade the strictures of \xc2\xa7922(g) by arranging a sham transfer that leaves him in effective control of his guns. And because that is so, a court may no more approve such a transfer than order the return of the firearms to the felon himself.

\n

The Government argues that \xc2\xa7922(g) prohibits still more\xe2\x80\x94that it bars a felon, except in one circumstance, from transferring his firearms to another person, no matter how independent of the felon\xe2\x80\x99s influence. According to the Government, a felon \xe2\x80\x9cexercises his right to control\xe2\x80\x9d his firearms, and thus violates \xc2\xa7922(g)\xe2\x80\x99s broad ban on possession, merely by \xe2\x80\x9cselect[ing] the[ir] first recipient,\xe2\x80\x9d because that choice \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine[s] who [will] (and who [will] not) next have access to the firearms.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 24. And that remains so even if a felon never retakes physical custody of the guns and needs a court order to approve and effectuate the proposed transfer. The felon (so says the Government) still exerts enough sway over the guns\xe2\x80\x99 disposition to \xe2\x80\x9chave constructive possession\xe2\x80\x9d of them. Id., at 25. The only time that is not true, the Government claims, is when a felon asks the court to transfer the guns to a licensed dealer or other party who will sell the guns for him on the open market. See id., at 20\xe2\x80\x9322; Tr. of Oral Arg. 18\xe2\x80\x9321. Because the felon then does not control the firearms\xe2\x80\x99 final destination, the Government avers, he does not constructively possess them and a court may approve the transfer. See ibid.

\n

But the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory wrongly conflates the right to possess a gun with another incident of ownership, which \xc2\xa7922(g) does not affect: the right merely to sell or otherwise dispose of that item. Cf. Andrus v. Allard,\n\n444 U. S. 51\n\xe2\x80\x9366 (1979) (distinguishing between entitlements to possess and sell property). Consider the scenario that the Government claims would violate \xc2\xa7922(g). The felon has nothing to do with his guns before, during, or after the transaction in question, except to nominate their recipient. Prior to the transfer, the guns sit in an evidence vault, under the sole custody of law enforcement officers. Assuming the court approves the proposed recipient, FBI agents handle the firearms\xe2\x80\x99 physical conveyance, without the felon\xe2\x80\x99s participation. Afterward, the purchaser or other custodian denies the felon any access to or influence over the guns; the recipient alone decides where to store them, when to loan them out, how to use them, and so on. In short, the arrangement serves only to divest the felon of his firearms\xe2\x80\x94and even that much depends on a court\xe2\x80\x99s approving the designee\xe2\x80\x99s fitness and ordering the transfer to go forward. Such a felon exercises not a possessory interest (whether directly or through another), but instead a naked right of alienation\xe2\x80\x94the capacity to sell or transfer his guns, unaccompanied by any control over them.[3]

\n

The Government\xe2\x80\x99s view of what counts as \xe2\x80\x9cpossession\xe2\x80\x9d would also extend \xc2\xa7922(g)\xe2\x80\x99s scope far beyond its purpose. Congress enacted that ban to keep firearms away from felons like Henderson, for fear that they would use those guns irresponsibly. See Small v. United States,\n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385,\n393 (2005)\n. Yet on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s construction, \xc2\xa7922(g) would prevent Henderson from disposing of his firearms even in ways that guarantee he never uses them again, solely because he played a part in selecting their transferee. He could not, for example, place those guns in a secure trust for distribution to his children after his death. He could not sell them to someone halfway around the world. He could not even donate them to a law enforcement agency. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 22. Results of that kind would do nothing to advance \xc2\xa7922(g)\xe2\x80\x99s purpose.

\n

Finally, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s expansive idea of constructive possession fits poorly with its concession that a felon in Henderson\xe2\x80\x99s position may select a firearms dealer or other third party to sell his guns and give him the proceeds. After all, the felon chooses the guns\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cfirst recipient\xe2\x80\x9d in that case too, deciding who \xe2\x80\x9cnext ha[s] access to the firearms.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 24; see supra, at 5. If (as the Government argues) that is all it takes to exercise control over and thus constructively possess an item, then (contrary to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view) the felon would violate \xc2\xa7922(g) merely by selecting a dealer to sell his guns. To be sure, that person will predictably convey the firearms to someone whom the felon does not know and cannot control: That is why the Government, as a practical matter, has no worries about the transfer. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 19\xe2\x80\x9321. But that fact merely demonstrates how the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view of \xc2\xa7922(g) errs in its focus in a case like this one. What matters here is not whether a felon plays a role in deciding where his firearms should go next: That test would logically prohibit a transfer even whenthe chosen recipient will later sell the guns to someone else. What matters instead is whether the felon will have the ability to use or direct the use of his firearms afterthe transfer. That is what gives the felon constructive possession.

\n

Accordingly, a court facing a motion like Henderson\xe2\x80\x99s may approve the transfer of guns consistently with \xc2\xa7922(g) if, but only if, that disposition prevents the felon from later exercising control over those weapons, so that he could either use them or tell someone else how to do so. One way to ensure that result, as the Government notes, is to order that the guns be turned over to a firearms dealer, himself independent of the felon\xe2\x80\x99s control, for subsequent sale on the open market. See, e.g., United States v. Zaleski, 686 F. 3d 90, 92\xe2\x80\x9394 (CA2 2012). Indeed, we can see no reason, absent exceptional circumstances, to disapprove a felon\xe2\x80\x99s motion for such a sale, whether or not he has picked the vendor. That option, however, is not the only one available under \xc2\xa7922(g). A court may also grant a felon\xe2\x80\x99s request to transfer his guns to a person who expects to maintain custody of them, so long as the recipient will not allow the felon to exert any influence over their use. In considering such a motion, the court may properly seek certain assurances: for example, it may ask the proposed transferee to promise to keep the guns away from the felon, and to acknowledge that allowing him to use them would aid and abet a \xc2\xa7922(g) violation. See id., at 94; United States v. Miller, 588 F. 3d 418, 420 (CA7 2009). Even such a pledge, of course, might fail to provide an adequate safeguard, and a court should then disapprove the transfer. See, e.g., State v. Fadness, 363 Mont. 322, 341\xe2\x80\x93342, 268 P.\xc2\xa03d 17, 30 (2012) (upholding a trial court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that the assurances given by a felon\xe2\x80\x99s parents were not credible). But when a court is satisfied that a felon will not retain control over his guns, \xc2\xa7922(g) does not apply, and the court has equitable power to accommodate the felon\xe2\x80\x99s request.

\n

Neither of the courts below assessed Henderson\xe2\x80\x99s motion for a transfer of his firearms in accord with these principles. We therefore vacate the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
