BOND v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

No. 12\xe2\x80\x93158.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 5, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 2, 2014

\n

To implement the international Convention on the Prohibition of the Development, Production, Stockpiling, and Use of Chemical Weapons and on Their Destruction, Congress enacted the Chemical Weapons Convention Implementation Act of 1998. The statute forbids, among other things, any person knowingly to \xe2\x80\x9cpossess[\xc2\xa0] or use .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any chemical weapon,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7229(a)(1). A \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c[a] toxic chemical and its precursors, except where intended for a purpose not prohibited under this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229F(1)(A). A \xe2\x80\x9ctoxic chemical\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cany chemical which through its chemical action on life processes can cause death, temporary incapacitation or permanent harm to humans or animals. The term includes all such chemicals, regardless of their origin or of their method of production, and regardless of whether they are produced in facilities, in munitions or elsewhere.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229F(8)(A). \xe2\x80\x9c[P]urposes not prohibited by this chapter\xe2\x80\x9d is defined as \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny peaceful purpose related to an industrial, agricultural, research, medical, or pharmaceutical activity or other activity,\xe2\x80\x9d and other specific purposes. \xc2\xa7229F(7).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Petitioner Bond sought revenge against Myrlinda Haynes\xe2\x80\x94with whom her husband had carried on an affair\xe2\x80\x94by spreading two toxic chemicals on Haynes\xe2\x80\x99s car, mailbox, and door knob in hopes that Haynes would develop an uncomfortable rash. On one occasion Haynes suffered a minor chemical burn that she treated by rinsing with water, but Bond\xe2\x80\x99s attempted assaults were otherwise entirely unsuccessful. Federal prosecutors charged Bond with violating, among other things, section 229(a). Bond moved to dismiss the chemical weapons charges on the ground that the Act violates the\n \nTenth Amendment. When the District Court denied her motion, she pleaded guilty but reserved the right to appeal. The Third Circuit initially held that Bond lacked standing to raise her\n \nTenth Amendment challenge, but this Court reversed. On remand, the Third Circuit rejected her\n \nTenth Amendment argument and her additional argument that section 229 does not reach her conduct.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section 229 does not reach Bond\xe2\x80\x99s simple assault. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0The parties debate whether section 229 is a necessary and proper means of executing the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s power to make treaties, but \xe2\x80\x9cnormally [this] Court will not decide a constitutional question if there is some other ground upon which to dispose of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d Escambia County v. McMillan,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48\n (per curiam). Thus, this Court starts with Bond\xe2\x80\x99s argument that section 229 does not cover her conduct. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0This Court has no need to interpret the scope of the international Chemical Weapons Convention in this case. The treaty specifies that a signatory nation should implement its obligations \xe2\x80\x9cin accordance with its constitutional processes.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. VII(1), 1974 U. N. T. S. 331. Bond was prosecuted under a federal statute, which, unlike the treaty, must be read consistent with the principles of federalism inherent in our constitutional structure. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (1)\xc2\xa0A fair reading of section 229 must recognize the duty of \xe2\x80\x9cfederal courts to be certain of Congress\xe2\x80\x99s intent before finding that federal law overrides\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cusual constitutional balance of federal and state powers,\xe2\x80\x9d Gregory v. Ashcroft,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452\n. This principle applies to federal laws that punish local criminal activity, which has traditionally been the responsibility of the States. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents have referred to basic principles of federalism in the Constitution to resolve ambiguity in federal statutes. See, e.g., United States v. Bass,\n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 336\n; Jones v. United States,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 848\n. Here, the ambiguity in the statute derives from the improbably broad reach of the key statutory definition, given the term\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is being defined, the deeply serious consequences of adopting such a boundless reading, and the lack of any apparent need to do so in light of the context from which the statute arose\xe2\x80\x94a treaty about chemical warfare and terrorism, not about local assaults. Thus, the Court can reasonably insist on a clear indication that Congress intended to reach purely local crimes before interpreting section 229\xe2\x80\x99s expansive language in a way that intrudes on the States\xe2\x80\x99 police power. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (2)\xc2\xa0No such clear indication is found in section 229. An ordinary speaker would not describe Bond\xe2\x80\x99s feud-driven act of spreading irritating chemicals as involving a \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon.\xe2\x80\x9d And the chemicals at issue here bear little resemblance to those whose prohibition was the object of an international Convention. Where the breadth of a statutory definition creates ambiguity, it is appropriate to look to the ordinary meaning of the term being defined (here, \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d) in settling on a fair reading of the statute. See Johnson v. United States,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 133\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading of section 229 would transform a statute concerned with acts of war, assassination, and terrorism into a massive federal anti-poisoning regime that reaches the simplest of assaults. In light of the principle that Congress does not normally intrude upon the States\xe2\x80\x99 police power, this Court is reluctant to conclude that Congress meant to punish Bond\xe2\x80\x99s crime with a federal prosecution for a chemical weapons attack. In fact, only a handful of prosecutions have been brought under section 229, and most of those involved crimes not traditionally within the States\xe2\x80\x99 purview, e.g., terrorist plots.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Pennsylvania\xe2\x80\x99s laws are sufficient to prosecute assaults like Bond\xe2\x80\x99s, and there is no indication in section 229 that Congress intended to abandon its traditional \xe2\x80\x9creluctan[ce] to define as a federal crime conduct readily denounced as criminal by the States,\xe2\x80\x9d Bass, supra, at 349. That principle goes to the very structure of the Constitution, and \xe2\x80\x9cprotects the liberty of the individual from arbitrary power.\xe2\x80\x9d Bond v. United States, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. The global need to prevent chemical warfare does not require the Federal Government to reach into the kitchen cupboard. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

681 F.\xc2\xa03d 149, reversed and remanded.

CAROL ANNE BOND, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

[June 2, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The horrors of chemical warfare were vividly captured by John Singer Sargent in his 1919 painting Gassed. The nearly life-sized work depicts two lines of soldiers, blinded by mustard gas, clinging single file to orderlies guiding them to an improvised aid station. There they would receive little treatment and no relief; many suffered for weeks only to have the gas claim their lives. The soldiers were shown staggering through piles of comrades too seriously burned to even join the procession.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The painting reflects the devastation that Sargent witnessed in the aftermath of the Second Battle of Arras during World War I. That battle and others like it led to an overwhelming consensus in the international commu-nity that toxic chemicals should never again be used as weapons against human beings. Today that objective is reflected in the international Convention on Chemical Weapons, which has been ratified or acceded to by 190 countries. The United States, pursuant to the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionally enumerated power to make treaties, ratified the treaty in 1997. To fulfill the United States\xe2\x80\x99 obligations under the Convention, Congress en-acted the Chemical Weapons Convention Implementation Act of 1998. The Act makes it a federal crime for a person to use or possess any chemical weapon, and it punishes violators with severe penalties. It is a statute that, like the Convention it implements, deals with crimes of deadly seriousness.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The question presented by this case is whether the Implementation Act also reaches a purely local crime: an amateur attempt by a jilted wife to injure her husband\xe2\x80\x99s lover, which ended up causing only a minor thumb burn readily treated by rinsing with water. Because our constitutional structure leaves local criminal activity primarily to the States, we have generally declined to read federal law as intruding on that responsibility, unless Congress has clearly indicated that the law should have such reach. The Chemical Weapons Convention Implementation Act contains no such clear indication, and we accordingly conclude that it does not cover the unremarkable local offense at issue here.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In 1997, the President of the United States, upon the advice and consent of the Senate, ratified the Convention on the Prohibition of the Development, Production, Stockpiling, and Use of Chemical Weapons and on Their Destruction. S. Treaty Doc. No. 103\xe2\x80\x9321, 1974 U. N. T. S. 317. The nations that ratified the Convention (State Parties) had bold aspirations for it: \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral and complete disarmament under strict and effective international control, including the prohibition and elimination of all types of weapons of mass destruction.\xe2\x80\x9d Convention Preamble, ibid. This purpose traces its origin to World War I, when \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ver a million casualties, up to 100,000 of them fatal, are estimated to have been caused by chemicals .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , a large part following the introduction of mustard gas in 1917.\xe2\x80\x9d Kenyon, Why We Need a Chemical Weapons Convention and an OPCW, in The Creation of the Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons 1, 4 (I. Kenyon & D. Feakes eds. 2007) (Kenyon & Feakes). The atrocities of that war led the community of nations to adopt the 1925 Geneva Protocol, which prohibited the use of chemicals as a method of warfare. Id., at 5.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Up to the 1990s, however, chemical weapons remained in use both in and out of wartime, with devastating consequences. Iraq\xe2\x80\x99s use of nerve agents and mustard gas during its war with Iran in the 1980s contributed to international support for a renewed, more effective chemical weapons ban. Id., at 6, 10\xe2\x80\x9311. In 1994 and 1995, long-held fears of the use of chemical weapons by terrorists were realized when Japanese extremists carried out two attacks using sarin gas. Id., at 6. The Convention was conceived as an effort to update the Geneva Protocol\xe2\x80\x99s protections and to expand the prohibition on chemical weapons beyond state actors in wartime. Convention Preamble, 1974 U. N. T. S. 318 (the State Parties are \xe2\x80\x9c[d]etermined for the sake of all mankind, to exclude completely the possibility of the use of chemical weapons, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. thereby complementing the obligations assumed under the Geneva Protocol of 1925\xe2\x80\x9d). The Convention aimed to achieve that objective by prohibiting the development, stockpiling, or use of chemical weapons by any State Party or person within a State Party\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction. Arts. I, II, VII. It also established an elaborate reporting process requiring State Parties to destroy chemical weapons under their control and submit to inspection and monitoring by an international organization based in The Hague, Netherlands. Arts.\xc2\xa0VIII, IX.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Convention provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(1) Each State Party to this Convention undertakes never \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n under any circumstances:

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x9c(a)\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To develop, produce, otherwise acquire, stockpile or retain chemical weapons, or transfer, directly or indirectly, chemical weapons to anyone;

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x9c(b) \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To use chemical weapons;

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x9c(c) \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To engage in any military preparations to use chemical weapons;

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x9c(d)\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To assist, encourage or induce, in any way, any-one to engage in any activity prohibited to a State Party under this Convention.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, id., at 319.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cChemical Weapons\xe2\x80\x9d are defined in relevant part as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]oxic chemicals and their precursors, except where intended for purposes not prohibited under this Convention, as long as the types and quantities are consistent with such purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II(1)(a), ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cToxic Chemical,\xe2\x80\x9d in turn, is defined as \xe2\x80\x9cAny chemical which through its chemical action on life processes can cause death, temporary incapacitation or permanent harm to humans or animals. This includes all such chemicals, regardless of their origin or of their method of production, and regardless of whether they are produced in facilities, in munitions or elsewhere.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II(2), id., at 320. \xe2\x80\x9cPurposes Not Prohibited Under this Convention\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9c[i]ndustrial, agricultural, research, medical, pharmaceutical or other peaceful purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II(9)(a), id., at 322, and other specific purposes not at issue here, Arts. II(9)(b)\xe2\x80\x93(d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Although the Convention is a binding international agreement, it is \xe2\x80\x9cnot self-executing.\xe2\x80\x9d W. Krutzsch & R. Trapp, A Commentary on the Chemical Weapons Convention 109 (1994). That is, the Convention creates obligations only for State Parties and \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not by itself give rise to domestically enforceable federal law\xe2\x80\x9d absent \xe2\x80\x9cimplementing legislation passed by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Medell\xc3\xadn v. Texas,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 491\n, n.\xc2\xa02 (2008). It instead provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach State Party shall, in accordance with its constitutional processes, adopt the necessary measures to implement its obligations under this Convention.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0VII(1), 1974 U. N. T. S. 331. \xe2\x80\x9cIn particular,\xe2\x80\x9d each State Party shall \xe2\x80\x9c[p]rohibit natural and legal persons anywhere .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. under its jurisdiction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. from undertaking any activity prohibited to a State Party under this Convention, including enacting penal legislation with respect to such activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0VII (1)(a), id., at 331\xe2\x80\x93332.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Congress gave the Convention domestic effect in 1998 when it passed the Chemical Weapons Convention Implementation Act. See\n\n112Stat.\n2681\xe2\x80\x93856. The Act closely tracks the text of the treaty: It forbids any person knowingly \xe2\x80\x9cto develop, produce, otherwise acquire, transfer directly or indirectly, receive, stockpile, retain, own, possess, or use, or threaten to use, any chemical weapon.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7229(a)(1). It defines \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d in relevant part as \xe2\x80\x9c[a] toxic chemical and its precursors, except where intended for a purpose not prohibited under this chapter as long as the type and quantity is consistent with such a purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229F(1)(A). \xe2\x80\x9cToxic chemical,\xe2\x80\x9d in turn, is defined in general as \xe2\x80\x9cany chemical which through its chemical action on life processes can cause death, temporary incapacitation or permanent harm to humans or animals. The term includes all such chemicals, regardless of their origin or of their method of production, and regardless of whether they are produced in facilities, in munitions or elsewhere.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229F(8)(A). Finally, \xe2\x80\x9cpurposes not prohibited by this chapter\xe2\x80\x9d is defined as \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny peaceful purpose related to an industrial, agricultural, research, medical, or pharmaceutical activity or other activity,\xe2\x80\x9d and other specific purposes. \xc2\xa7229F(7). A person who violates section 229 may be subject to severe punishment: imprisonment \xe2\x80\x9cfor any term of years,\xe2\x80\x9d or if a victim\xe2\x80\x99s death results, the death penalty or imprisonment \xe2\x80\x9cfor life.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229A(a).

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Petitioner Carol Anne Bond is a microbiologist from Lansdale, Pennsylvania. In 2006, Bond\xe2\x80\x99s closest friend, Myrlinda Haynes, announced that she was pregnant. When Bond discovered that her husband was the child\xe2\x80\x99s father, she sought revenge against Haynes. Bond stole a quantity of 10-chloro-10H-phenoxarsine (an arsenic-based compound) from her employer, a chemical manufacturer. She also ordered a vial of potassium dichromate (a chemical commonly used in printing photographs or cleaning laboratory equipment) on Amazon.com. Both chemicals are toxic to humans and, in high enough doses, potentially lethal. It is undisputed, however, that Bond did not intend to kill Haynes. She instead hoped that Haynes would touch the chemicals and develop an uncomfortable rash.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Between November 2006 and June 2007, Bond went to Haynes\xe2\x80\x99s home on at least 24 occasions and spread the chemicals on her car door, mailbox, and door knob. These attempted assaults were almost entirely unsuccessful. The chemicals that Bond used are easy to see, and Haynes was able to avoid them all but once. On that occasion, Haynes suffered a minor chemical burn on her thumb, which she treated by rinsing with water. Haynes repeatedly called the local police to report the suspicious substances, but they took no action. When Haynes found powder on her mailbox, she called the police again, who told her to call the post office. Haynes did so, and postal inspectors placed surveillance cameras around her home. The cameras caught Bond opening Haynes\xe2\x80\x99s mailbox, stealing an envelope, and stuffing potassium dichromate inside the muffler of Haynes\xe2\x80\x99s car.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Federal prosecutors naturally charged Bond with two counts of mail theft, in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71708. More surprising, they also charged her with two countsof possessing and using a chemical weapon, in violationof section 229(a). Bond moved to dismiss the chemical weapon counts on the ground that section 229 exceeded Congress\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated powers and invaded powers reserved to the States by the\n \nTenth Amendment. The District Court denied Bond\xe2\x80\x99s motion. She then entered a conditional guilty plea that reserved her right to appeal. The District Court sentenced Bond to six years in federal prison plus five years of supervised release, and ordered her to pay a $2,000 fine and $9,902.79 in restitution.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Bond appealed, raising a\n \nTenth Amendment challenge to her conviction. The Government contended that Bond lacked standing to bring such a challenge. The Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit agreed. We granted certiorari, the Government confessed error, and we reversed. We held that, in a proper case, an individual may \xe2\x80\x9cassert injury from governmental action taken in excess of the authority that federalism defines.\xe2\x80\x9d Bond v. United States, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (Bond I) (slip op., at 8). We \xe2\x80\x9cexpresse[d] no view on the merits\xe2\x80\x9d of Bond\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional challenge. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 14).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n On remand, Bond renewed her constitutional argument. She also argued that section 229 does not reach her conduct because the statute\xe2\x80\x99s exception for the use of chemicals for \xe2\x80\x9cpeaceful purposes\xe2\x80\x9d should be understood in contradistinction to the \xe2\x80\x9cwarlike\xe2\x80\x9d activities that the Convention was primarily designed to prohibit. Bond argued that her conduct, though reprehensible, was not at all \xe2\x80\x9cwarlike.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court of Appeals rejected this argument. 681 F.\xc2\xa03d 149 (CA3 2012). The court acknowledged that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading of section 229 would render the statute \xe2\x80\x9cstriking\xe2\x80\x9d in its \xe2\x80\x9cbreadth\xe2\x80\x9d and turn every \xe2\x80\x9ckitchen cupboard and cleaning cabinet in America into a potential chemical weapons cache.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 154, n.\xc2\xa07. But the court nevertheless held that Bond\xe2\x80\x99s use of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98highly toxic chemicals with the intent of harming Haynes\xe2\x80\x99 can hardly be characterized as \xe2\x80\x98peaceful\xe2\x80\x99 under that word\xe2\x80\x99s commonly understood meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 154 (citation omitted).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Third Circuit also rejected Bond\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional challenge to her conviction, holding that section 229 was \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary and proper to carry the Convention into effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 162. The Court of Appeals relied on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in Missouri v. Holland,\n\n252 U.\xc2\xa0S. 416 (1920)\n, which stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the treaty is valid there can be no dispute about the validity of the statute\xe2\x80\x9d that implements it \xe2\x80\x9cas a necessary and proper means to execute the powers of the Government,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 432.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We again granted certiorari, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013).

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In our federal system, the National Government possesses only limited powers; the States and the people retain the remainder. The States have broad authority to enact legislation for the public good\xe2\x80\x94what we have often called a \xe2\x80\x9cpolice power.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Lopez,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549,\n567 (1995)\n. The Federal Government, by contrast, has no such authority and \xe2\x80\x9ccan exercise only the powers granted to it,\xe2\x80\x9d McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 405 (1819), including the power to make \xe2\x80\x9call Laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into Execution\xe2\x80\x9d the enumerated powers, U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl. 18. For nearly two centuries it has been \xe2\x80\x9cclear\xe2\x80\x9d that, lacking a police power, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress cannot punish felonies generally.\xe2\x80\x9d Cohens v. Virginia, 6 Wheat. 264, 428 (1821). A criminal act committed wholly within a State \xe2\x80\x9ccannot be made an offence against the United States, unless it have some relation to the execution of a power of Congress, or to some matter within the jurisdiction of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Fox,\n\n95 U.\xc2\xa0S. 670,\n672 (1878)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Government frequently defends federal criminal legislation on the ground that the legislation is authorized pursuant to Congress\xe2\x80\x99s power to regulate interstate commerce. In this case, however, the Court of Appeals held that the Government had explicitly disavowed that argument before the District Court. 681 F. 3d, at 151, n. 1. As a result, in this Court the parties have devoted significant effort to arguing whether section 229, as applied to Bond\xe2\x80\x99s offense, is a necessary and proper means of executing the National Government\xe2\x80\x99s power to make treaties. U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl. 2. Bond argues that the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of Missouri v. Holland would remove all limits on federal authority, so long as the Federal Government ratifies a treaty first. She insists that to effectively afford the Government a police power whenever it implements a treaty would be contrary to the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 careful decision to divide power between the States and the National Government as a means of preserving liberty. To the extent that Holland authorizes such usurpation of traditional state authority, Bond says, it must be either limited or overruled.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Government replies that this Court has never held that a statute implementing a valid treaty exceeds Congress\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated powers. To do so here, the Government says, would contravene another deliberate choice of the Framers: to avoid placing subject matter limitations on the National Government\xe2\x80\x99s power to make treaties. And it might also undermine confidence in the United States as an international treaty partner.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Notwithstanding this debate, it is \xe2\x80\x9ca well-established principle governing the prudent exercise of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction that normally the Court will not decide a constitutional question if there is some other ground upon which to dispose of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d Escambia County v. Mc-Millan,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48,\n51 (1984)\n (per curiam); see also Ashwander v. TVA,\n\n297 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288,\n347 (1936)\n (Brandeis, J., concurring). Bond argues that section 229 does not cover her conduct. So we consider that argument first.

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Section 229 exists to implement the Convention, so we begin with that international agreement. As explained, the Convention\xe2\x80\x99s drafters intended for it to be a comprehensive ban on chemical weapons. But even with its broadly worded definitions, we have doubts that a treaty about chemical weapons has anything to do with Bond\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. The Convention, a product of years of worldwide study, analysis, and multinational negotiation, arose in response to war crimes and acts of terrorism. See Kenyon & Feakes 6. There is no reason to think the sovereign nations that ratified the Convention were interested in anything like Bond\xe2\x80\x99s common law assault.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Even if the treaty does reach that far, nothing prevents Congress from implementing the Convention in the same manner it legislates with respect to innumerable other matters\xe2\x80\x94observing the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s division of responsibility between sovereigns and leaving the prosecution of purely local crimes to the States. The Convention, after all, is agnostic between enforcement at the state versus federal level: It provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach State Party shall, in accordance with its constitutional processes, adopt the necessary measures to implement its obligations under this Convention.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0VII(1), 1974 U. N. T. S. 331 (emphasis added); see also Tabassi, National Implementation: Article VII, in Kenyon & Feakes 205, 207 (\xe2\x80\x9cSince the creation of national law, the enforcement of it and the structure and administration of government are all sovereign acts reserved exclusively for [State Parties], it is not surprising that the Convention is so vague on the critical matter of national implementation.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Fortunately, we have no need to interpret the scope of the Convention in this case. Bond was prosecuted under section 229, and the statute\xe2\x80\x94unlike the Convention\xe2\x80\x94must be read consistent with principles of federalism inherent in our constitutional structure.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view, the conclusion that Bond \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cuse[d]\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d in violation of section 229(a) is simple: The chemicals that Bond placed on Haynes\xe2\x80\x99s home and car are \xe2\x80\x9ctoxic chemical[s]\xe2\x80\x9d as defined by the statute, and Bond\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to assault Haynes was not a \xe2\x80\x9cpeaceful purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7229F(1), (8), (7). The problem with this interpretation is that it would \xe2\x80\x9cdramatically intrude[\xe2\x80\x82] upon traditional state criminal jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d and we avoid reading statutes to have such reach in the absence of a clear indication that they do. United States v. Bass,\n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 336,\n350 (1971)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Part of a fair reading of statutory text is recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress legislates against the backdrop\xe2\x80\x9d of certain unexpressed presumptions. EEOC v. Arabian American Oil Co.,\n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244,\n248 (1991)\n. As Justice Frankfurter put it in his famous essay on statutory interpretation, correctly reading a statute \xe2\x80\x9cdemands awareness of certain presuppositions.\xe2\x80\x9d Some Reflections on the Reading of Statutes, 47 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 527, 537 (1947). For example, we presume that a criminal statute derived from the common law carries with it the requirement of a culpable mental state\xe2\x80\x94even if no such limitation appears in the text\xe2\x80\x94unless it is clear that the Legislature intended to impose strict liability. United States v. United States Gypsum Co.,\n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 422,\n437 (1978)\n. To take another example, we presume, absent a clear statement from Congress, that federal statutes do not apply outside the United States. Morrison v. National Australia Bank Ltd.,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 247,\n255 (2010)\n. So even though section 229, read on its face, would cover a chemical weapons crime if committed by a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen in Australia, we would not apply the statute to such conduct absent a plain statement from Congress.[1] The notion that some things \xe2\x80\x9cgo without saying\xe2\x80\x9d applies to legislation just as it does to everyday life.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Among the background principles of construction that our cases have recognized are those grounded in the relationship between the Federal Government and the States under our Constitution. It has long been settled, for example, that we presume federal statutes do not abrogate state sovereign immunity, Atascadero State Hospital v. Scanlon,\n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 234,\n243 (1985)\n, impose obligations on the States pursuant to section 5 of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment, Pennhurst State School and Hospital v. Halderman,\n\n451 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9317 (1981), or preempt state law, Rice v. Santa Fe Elevator Corp.,\n\n331 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218,\n230 (1947)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Closely related to these is the well-established principle that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98it is incumbent upon the federal courts to be certain of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent before finding that federal law overrides\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cusual constitutional balance of federal and state powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Gregory v. Ashcroft,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452,\n460 (1991)\n (quoting Atascadero, supra, at 243). To quote Frankfurter again, if the Federal Government would \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98radically readjust[\xc2\xa0] the balance of state and national authority, those charged with the duty of legislating [must be] reasonably explicit\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d about it. BFP v. Resolution Trust Corporation,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 531,\n544 (1994)\n (quoting Some Reflections, supra, at 539\xe2\x80\x93540; second alteration in original). Or as explained by Justice Marshall, when legislation \xe2\x80\x9caffect[s] the federal balance, the requirement of clear statement assures that the legislature has in fact faced, and intended to bring into issue, the critical matters involved in the judicial decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Bass, supra, at 349.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We have applied this background principle when construing federal statutes that touched on several areas of traditional state responsibility. See Gregory, supra, at 460 (qualifications for state officers); BFP, supra, at 544 (titles to real estate); Solid Waste Agency of Northern Cook Cty. v. Army Corps of Engineers,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 159,\n174 (2001)\n (land and water use). Perhaps the clearest example of traditional state authority is the punishment of local criminal activity. United States v. Morrison,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 598,\n618 (2000)\n. Thus, \xe2\x80\x9cwe will not be quick to assume that Congress has meant to effect a significant change in the sensitive relation between federal and state criminal jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Bass, 404 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 349.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Bass, we interpreted a statute that prohibited any convicted felon from \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98receiv[ing], possess[ing], or transport[ing] in commerce or affecting commerce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any firearm.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 337. The Government argued that the statute barred felons from possessing all firearms and that it was not necessary to demonstrate a connection to interstate commerce. We rejected that reading, which would \xe2\x80\x9crender[\xc2\xa0] traditionally local criminal conduct a matter for federal enforcement and would also involve a substantial extension of federal police resources.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 350. We instead read the statute more narrowly to require proof of a connection to interstate commerce in every case, thereby \xe2\x80\x9cpreserv[ing] as an element of all the of-fenses a requirement suited to federal criminal jurisdiction alone.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 351.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Similarly, in Jones v. United States,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 848,\n850 (2000)\n, we confronted the question whether the federal arson statute, which prohibited burning \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98any .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. property used in interstate or foreign commerce or in any activity affecting interstate or foreign commerce,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d reached an owner-occupied private residence. Once again we rejected the Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cexpansive interpretation,\xe2\x80\x9d under which \xe2\x80\x9chardly a building in the land would fall outside the fed-eral statute\xe2\x80\x99s domain.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 857. We instead held that the statute was \xe2\x80\x9cmost sensibly read\xe2\x80\x9d more narrowly to reach only buildings used in \xe2\x80\x9cactive employment for commercial purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 855. We noted that \xe2\x80\x9carson is a paradigmatic common-law state crime,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 858, and that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s proposed broad reading would \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98significantly change[\xc2\xa0] the federal-state balance,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (quoting Bass, 404 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 349), \xe2\x80\x9cmak[ing] virtually every arson in the country a federal offense,\xe2\x80\x9d 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 859.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n These precedents make clear that it is appropriate to refer to basic principles of federalism embodied in the Constitution to resolve ambiguity in a federal statute. In this case, the ambiguity derives from the improbably broad reach of the key statutory definition given the term\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94being defined; the deeply serious consequences of adopting such a boundless reading; and the lack of any apparent need to do so in light of the context from which the statute arose\xe2\x80\x94a treaty about chemical warfare and terrorism. We conclude that, in this curious case, we can insist on a clear indication that Congress meant to reach purely local crimes, before interpreting the statute\xe2\x80\x99s expansive language in a way that intrudes on the police power of the States. See Bass, supra, at 349.[2]

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We do not find any such clear indication in section 229. \xe2\x80\x9cChemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d is the key term that defines the statute\xe2\x80\x99s reach, and it is defined extremely broadly. But that general definition does not constitute a clear statement that Congress meant the statute to reach local criminal conduct.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In fact, a fair reading of section 229 suggests that it does not have as expansive a scope as might at first appear. To begin, as a matter of natural meaning, an educated user of English would not describe Bond\xe2\x80\x99s crime as involving a \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon.\xe2\x80\x9d Saying that a person \xe2\x80\x9cused a chemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d conveys a very different idea than saying the person \xe2\x80\x9cused a chemical in a way that caused some harm.\xe2\x80\x9d The natural meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d takes account of both the particular chemicals that the defendant used and the circumstances in which she used them.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n When used in the manner here, the chemicals in this case are not of the sort that an ordinary person would associate with instruments of chemical warfare. The substances that Bond used bear little resemblance to the deadly toxins that are \xe2\x80\x9cof particular danger to the objectives of the Convention.\xe2\x80\x9d Why We Need a Chemical Weapons Convention and an OPCW, in Kenyon & Feakes 17 (describing the Convention\xe2\x80\x99s Annex on Chemicals, a nonexhaustive list of covered substances that are subject to special regulation). More to the point, the use of something as a \xe2\x80\x9cweapon\xe2\x80\x9d typically connotes \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n instrument of offensive or defensive combat,\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 2589 (2002), or \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n instrument of attack or defense in combat, as a gun, missile, or sword,\xe2\x80\x9d American Heritage Dictionary 2022 (3d ed. 1992). But no speaker in natural parlance would describe Bond\xe2\x80\x99s feud-driven act of spreading irritating chemicals on Haynes\xe2\x80\x99s door knob and mailbox as \xe2\x80\x9ccombat.\xe2\x80\x9d Nor do the other circumstances of Bond\xe2\x80\x99s offense\xe2\x80\x94an act of revenge born of romantic jealousy, meant to cause discomfort, that produced nothing more than a minor thumb burn\xe2\x80\x94suggest that a chemical weapon was deployed in Norristown, Pennsylvania. Potassium dichromate and 10-chloro-10H-phenoxarsine might be chemical weapons if used, say, to poison a city\xe2\x80\x99s water supply. But Bond\xe2\x80\x99s crime is worlds apart from such hypotheticals, and covering it would give the statute a reach exceeding the ordinary meaning of the words Congress wrote.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In settling on a fair reading of a statute, it is not un-usual to consider the ordinary meaning of a defined term, particularly when there is dissonance between that ordinary meaning and the reach of the definition. In Johnson v. United States,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 133,\n136 (2010)\n, for example, we considered the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98violent felony,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d which the Armed Career Criminal Act defined in relevant part as an offense that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98has as an element the use .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of physical force against the person of another.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Although \xe2\x80\x9cphysical force against .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. another\xe2\x80\x9d might have meant any force, however slight, we thought it \xe2\x80\x9cclear that in the context of a statutory definition of \xe2\x80\x98violent felony,\xe2\x80\x99 the phrase \xe2\x80\x98physical force\xe2\x80\x99 means violent force\xe2\x80\x94that is, force capable of causing physical pain or injury to another person.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 140. The ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d plays a similar limiting role here.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Government would have us brush aside the ordinary meaning and adopt a reading of section 229 that would sweep in everything from the detergent under the kitchen sink to the stain remover in the laundry room. Yet no one would ordinarily describe those substances as \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapons.\xe2\x80\x9d The Government responds that because Bond used \xe2\x80\x9cspecialized, highly toxic\xe2\x80\x9d (though legal) chemicals, \xe2\x80\x9cthis case presents no occasion to address whether Congress intended [section 229] to apply to common household substances.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 13, n.\xc2\xa03. That the statute would apply so broadly, however, is the inescapable conclusion of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position: Any parent would be guilty of a serious federal offense\xe2\x80\x94possession of a chemical weapon\xe2\x80\x94when, exasperated by the children\xe2\x80\x99s repeated failure to clean the goldfish tank, he considers poisoning the fish with a few drops of vinegar. We are reluctant to ignore the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d when doing so would transform a statute passed to implement the international Convention on Chemical Weapons into one that also makes it a federal offense to poison goldfish. That would not be a \xe2\x80\x9crealistic assessment[ ] of congressional intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In light of all of this, it is fully appropriate to apply the background assumption that Congress normally preserves \xe2\x80\x9cthe constitutional balance between the National Government and the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Bond I, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10). That assumption is grounded in the very structure of the Constitution. And as we explained when this case was first before us, maintaining that constitutional balance is not merely an end unto itself. Rather, \xe2\x80\x9c[b]y denying any one government complete jurisdiction over all the concerns of public life, federalism protects the liberty of the individual from arbitrary power.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading of section 229 would \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98alter sensitive federal-state relationships,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d convert an astonishing amount of \xe2\x80\x9ctraditionally local criminal conduct\xe2\x80\x9d into \xe2\x80\x9ca matter for federal enforcement,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinvolve a substantial extension of federal police resources.\xe2\x80\x9d Bass, 404 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 349\xe2\x80\x93350. It would transform the statute from one whose core concerns are acts of war, assassination, and terrorism into a massive federal anti-poisoning regime that reaches the simplest of assaults. As the Government reads section 229, \xe2\x80\x9chardly\xe2\x80\x9d a poisoning \xe2\x80\x9cin the land would fall outside the federal statute\xe2\x80\x99s domain.\xe2\x80\x9d Jones, 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 857. Of course Bond\xe2\x80\x99s conduct is serious and unacceptable\xe2\x80\x94and against the laws of Pennsylvania. But the background principle that Congress does not normally intrude upon the police power of the States is critically important. In light of that principle, we are reluctant to conclude that Congress meant to punish Bond\xe2\x80\x99s crime with a federal prosecution for a chemical weapons attack.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In fact, with the exception of this unusual case, the Federal Government itself has not looked to section 229 to reach purely local crimes. The Government has identified only a handful of prosecutions that have been brought under this section. Brief in Opposition 27, n.\xc2\xa05. Most of those involved either terrorist plots or the possession of extremely dangerous substances with the potential to cause severe harm to many people. See United States v. Ghane, 673 F.\xc2\xa03d 771 (CA8 2012) (defendant possessed enough potassium cyanide to kill 450 people); United States v. Crocker, 260 Fed. Appx. 794 (CA6 2008) (defendant attempted to acquire VX nerve gas and chlorine gas as part of a plot to attack a federal courthouse); United States v. Krar, 134 Fed. Appx. 662 (CA5 2005) (per curiam) (defendant possessed sodium cyanide); United States v. Fries, 2012 WL 689157 (D Ariz., Feb. 28, 2012) (defendant set off a homemade chlorine bomb in the victim\xe2\x80\x99s driveway, requiring evacuation of a residential neighborhood). The Federal Government undoubtedly has a substantial interest in enforcing criminal laws against assassination, terrorism, and acts with the potential to cause mass suffering. Those crimes have not traditionally been left predominantly to the States, and nothing we have said here will disrupt the Government\xe2\x80\x99s authority to prosecute such offenses.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is also clear that the laws of the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania (and every other State) are sufficient to prosecute Bond. Pennsylvania has several statutes that would likely cover her assault. See 18 Pa. Cons. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72701 (2012) (simple assault), 2705 (reckless endangerment), 2709 (harassment).[3] And state authorities regularly enforce these laws in poisoning cases. See, e.g., Gamiz, Family Survives Poisoned Burritos, Allentown, Pa., Morning Call, May 18, 2013 (defendant charged with assault, reckless endangerment, and harassment for feeding burritos poisoned with prescription medication to her husband and daughter); Cops: Man Was Poisoned Over 3 Years, Harrisburg, Pa., Patriot News, Aug. 12, 2012, p. A11 (defendant charged with assault and reckless endangerment for poisoning a man with eye drops over three years so that \xe2\x80\x9che would pay more attention to her\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Government objects that Pennsylvania authorities charged Bond with only a minor offense based on her \xe2\x80\x9charassing telephone calls and letters,\xe2\x80\x9d Bond I, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 2), and declined to prosecute her for assault. But we have traditionally viewed the exercise of state officials\xe2\x80\x99 prosecutorial discretion as a valuable feature of our constitutional system. See Bordenkircher v. Hayes,\n\n434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 357,\n364 (1978)\n. And nothing in the Convention shows a clear intent to abrogate that feature. Prosecutorial discretion involves carefully weighing the benefits of a prosecution against the evidence needed to convict, the resources of the public fisc, and the public policy of the State. Here, in its zeal to prosecute Bond, the Federal Government has \xe2\x80\x9cdisplaced\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cpublic policy of the Commonwealth of Pennsylvania, enacted in its capacity as sovereign,\xe2\x80\x9d that Bond does not belong in prison for a chemical weapons offense. Bond I, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 12); see also Jones, supra, at 859 (Stevens, J., concurring) (federal prosecution of a traditionally local crime \xe2\x80\x9cillustrates how a criminal law like this may effectively displace a policy choice made by the State\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n As we have explained, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has traditionally been reluctant to define as a federal crime conduct readily denounced as criminal by the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Bass, 404 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 349. There is no clear indication of a contrary approach here. Section 229 implements the Convention, but Bond\xe2\x80\x99s crime could hardly be more unlike the uses of mustard gas on the Western Front or nerve agents in the Iran-Iraq war that form the core concerns of that treaty. See Kenyon & Feakes 6. There are no life-sized paintings of Bond\xe2\x80\x99s rival washing her thumb. And there are no apparent interests of the United States Congress or the community of nations in seeing Bond end up in federal prison, rather than dealt with (like virtually all other criminals in Pennsylvania) by the State. The Solicitor General acknowledged as much at oral argument. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 47 (\xe2\x80\x9cI don\xe2\x80\x99t think anybody would say [that] whether or not Ms. Bond is prosecuted would give rise to an international incident\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This case is unusual, and our analysis is appropriately limited. Our disagreement with our colleagues reduces to whether section 229 is \xe2\x80\x9cutterly clear.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 5 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment). We think it is not, given that the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d in a particular case can reach beyond any normal notion of such a weapon, that the context from which the statute arose demonstrates a much more limited prohibition was intended, and that the most sweeping reading of the statute would fundamentally upset the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s balance between national and local power. This exceptional convergence of factors gives us serious reason to doubt the Government\xe2\x80\x99s expansive reading of section 229, and calls for us to interpret the statute more narrowly.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In sum, the global need to prevent chemical warfare does not require the Federal Government to reach into the kitchen cupboard, or to treat a local assault with a chemical irritant as the deployment of a chemical weapon. There is no reason to suppose that Congress\xe2\x80\x94in implementing the Convention on Chemical Weapons\xe2\x80\x94thought otherwise.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Convention provides for implementation by each ratifying nation \xe2\x80\x9cin accordance with its constitutional processes.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0VII(1), 1974 U. N. T. S. 331. As James Madison explained, the constitutional process in our \xe2\x80\x9ccompound republic\xe2\x80\x9d keeps power \xe2\x80\x9cdivided between two distinct governments.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 51, p. 323 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961). If section 229 reached Bond\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, it would mark a dramatic departure from that constitutional structure and a serious reallocation of criminal law enforcement authority between the Federal Government and the States. Absent a clear statement of that purpose, we will not presume Congress to have authorized such a stark intrusion into traditional state authority.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n By its clear terms, the statute at issue in this case regulates local criminal conduct that is subject to the powers reserved to the States. See ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment). That aggrandizement of federal power cannot be justified as a \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary and proper\xe2\x80\x9d means of implementing a treaty addressing similar subject matter. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. To the contrary, reading the Necessary and Proper Clause to expand Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power upon the ratification of every new treaty defies an indisputable first principle of our constitutional order: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[T]he Constitution created a Federal Government of limited powers.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d New York v. United States,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 144,\n155 (1992)\n (quoting Gregory v. Ashcroft,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452,\n457 (1991)\n). I accordingly join Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in full.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I write separately to suggest that the Treaty Power is itself a limited federal power. Cf. United States v. Lopez,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549,\n584 (1995)\n (Thomas, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e always have rejected readings of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the scope of federal power that would permit Congress to exercise a police power\xe2\x80\x9d). The Constitution empowers the President, \xe2\x80\x9cby and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate, to make Treaties, provided two thirds of the Senators present concur.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72. The Constitution does not, however, comprehensively define the proper bounds of the Treaty Power, and this Court has not yet had occasion to do so. As a result, some have suggested that the Treaty Power is boundless\xe2\x80\x94that it can reach any subject matter, even those that are of strictly domestic concern. See, e.g., Restatement (Third) of Foreign Relations Law of the United States, \xc2\xa7302, Comment c (1986). A number of recent treaties reflect that suggestion by regulating what appear to be purely domestic affairs. See, e.g., Bradley, The Treaty Power and American Federalism, 97 Mich. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 390, 402\xe2\x80\x93409 (1998) (hereinafter Bradley) (citing examples).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Yet to interpret the Treaty Power as extending to every conceivable domestic subject matter\xe2\x80\x94even matters without any nexus to foreign relations\xe2\x80\x94would destroy the basic constitutional distinction between domestic and foreign powers. See United States v. Curtiss-Wright Export Corp.,\n\n299 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304,\n319 (1936)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he federal power over external affairs [is] in origin and essential character different from that over internal affairs .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). It would also lodge in the Federal Government the potential for \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98police power\xe2\x80\x99 over all aspects of American life.\xe2\x80\x9d Lopez, supra, at 584 (Thomas, J., concurring). A treaty-based power of that magnitude\xe2\x80\x94no less than a plenary power of legislation\xe2\x80\x94would threaten \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cthe liberties that derive from the diffusion of sovereign power.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bond v. United States, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9). And a treaty-based police power would pose an even greater threat when exercised through a self-executing treaty because it would circumvent the role of the House of Representatives in the legislative process. See The Federalist No. 52, p. 355 (J. Cooke ed. 1961) (J. Madison) (noting that the House has a more \xe2\x80\x9cimmediate dependence on, & an intimate sympathy with the people\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I doubt the Treaty Power creates such a gaping loophole in our constitutional structure. Although the parties have not challenged the constitutionality of the particular treaty at issue here, in an appropriate case I believe the Court should address the scope of the Treaty Power as it was originally understood. Today, it is enough to highlight some of the structural and historical evidence suggesting that the Treaty Power can be used to arrange intercourse with other nations, but not to regulate purely domestic affairs.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Treaty Power was not drafted on a blank slate. To the contrary, centuries of experience\xe2\x80\x94reflected in treatises, dictionaries, and actual practice\xe2\x80\x94shaped the contours of that power.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Early treatises discussed a wide variety of treaties that nevertheless shared a common thread: All of them governed genuinely international matters such as war, peace, and trade between nations. See, e.g., 2 H. Grotius, De Jure Belli Ac Pacis 394\xe2\x80\x93396 (1646 ed., F. Kelsey transl. 1925) (treaties are made \xe2\x80\x9cfor the sake either of peace or of some alliance,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9cfor the restoration of captives and of captured property, and for safety\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9cthat neither signatory shall have fortresses in the territory of the other, or defend the subjects of the other, or furnish a passage to the enemy of the other\xe2\x80\x9d; and for \xe2\x80\x9ccommercial relations\xe2\x80\x9d and agreements on \xe2\x80\x9cimport duties\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)); 2 S. Pufendorf, De Jure Naturae et Gentium 1331 (1688 ed., C. Oldfather & W. Oldfather transls. 1934) (treaties are made \xe2\x80\x9cto form some union or society, the end of which is either commercial relations, or a united front in war\xe2\x80\x9d); 3 E. Vattel, The Law of Nations 165 (1758 ed., C. Fenwick transl. 1916) (treaties, which \xe2\x80\x9ccan be subdivided into as many classes as there are varieties in the character of national relations,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cdeal with conditions of commerce, with mutual defense, with belligerent relations, with rights of passage, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. stipulations not to fortify certain places, etc.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Founding-era dictionaries reflect a similar understanding. To be sure, some early dictionaries briefly defined \xe2\x80\x9ctreaty\xe2\x80\x9d simply as a \xe2\x80\x9ccompact of accommodation relating to public affairs.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., 2 S. Johnson, A Dictionary of the English Language 2056 (rev. 4th ed. 1773). More detailed definitions, however, recognized the particular character of treaties as addressing matters of intercourse between nations rather than domestic regulation. See, e.g., J. Buchanan, A New English Dictionary (1769) (defining \xe2\x80\x9ctreaty\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[a] covenant or agreement between several nations for peace, commerce, navigation, &c.\xe2\x80\x9d); N. Bailey, An Universal Etymological English Dictionary (26th ed. 1789) (same); J. Montefiore, A Commercial Dictionary (1803) (noting \xe2\x80\x9ctreaties of alliance\xe2\x80\x9d for military aid; \xe2\x80\x9ctreaties of subsidy\xe2\x80\x9d for the provision of soldiers; treaties of navigation and commerce; treaties governing fishing and timber rights; and treaties on import duties); 2 N. Webster, An American Dictionary of the English Language 97 (1828) (noting \xe2\x80\x9ctreaties for regulating commercial intercourse, treaties of alliance, offensive and defensive, treaties for hiring troops, [and] treaties of peace\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Treaty practice under the Articles of Confederation was also consistent with the understanding that treaties govern matters of international intercourse. The Articles provided: \xe2\x80\x9cThe United States in Congress assembled, shall have the sole and exclusive right and power of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. entering into treaties and alliances .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0IX. The Congress of the Confederation exercised that power by making treaties that fell squarely within the traditional scope of the power. See, e.g., Treaty with the Cherokee, Art.\xc2\xa0IV, Nov. 28,1785,\n\n7Stat.\n19,\n2 C. Kappler, Indian Affairs: Laws and Treaties 9 (1904) (territorial borders); Definitive Treaty of Peace, U.\xc2\xa0S.-Gr. Brit., Art.\xc2\xa0VII, Sept. 3, 1783,\n\n8Stat.\n83, T.\xc2\xa0S. No. 104 (peace); Contract for the Payment of Loans, U.\xc2\xa0S.-Fr., Arts. I\xe2\x80\x93IV, July 16, 1782,\n\n8Stat.\n614\xe2\x80\x93615, T.\xc2\xa0S. No. 831\xe2\x88\x954 (repayment of sovereign debt); Definitive Treaty of Peace, U.\xc2\xa0S.-Gr. Brit., Art.\xc2\xa0III, Sept. 3, 1783,\n\n8Stat.\n82, T.\xc2\xa0S. No. 104 (fishery rights in disputed waters); Treaty of Amity and Commerce, U.\xc2\xa0S.-Prussia, Arts. IV\xe2\x80\x93IX, Sept. 10, 1785,\n\n8Stat.\n86\xe2\x80\x9388, T. S. No. 292 (treatment of vessels in a treaty partners\xe2\x80\x99 waters); Convention Defining and Establishing the Functions and Privileges of Consuls and Vice-Consuls, U.\xc2\xa0S.-Fr., Arts. I\xe2\x80\x93III, Nov. 14, 1788,\n\n8Stat.\n106\xe2\x80\x93108, T. S. No. 84 (privileges and immunities of diplomatic officials); Treaty of Amity and Commerce, U.\xc2\xa0S.-Swed., Arts. III\xe2\x80\x93IV, Apr. 3, 1783,\n\n8Stat.\n60, T. S. No. 346 (rights of citizens of one treaty partner residing in the territory of the other).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n These treaties entered into under the Articles of Confederation would not have suggested to the Framers that granting a power to \xe2\x80\x9cmake Treaties\xe2\x80\x9d included authorization to regulate purely domestic matters. Whenever these treaties affected legal rights within United States territory, they addressed only rights that related to foreign sub-jects or foreign property. See, e.g., Treaty of Amity and Commerce, U.\xc2\xa0S.-Neth., Art.\xc2\xa0IV, Oct. 8, 1782,\n\n8Stat.\n34 (affording burial rights \xe2\x80\x9cwhen any subjects or inhabitants of either party shall die in the territory of the other\xe2\x80\x9d); Treaty with the Cherokee, Art.\xc2\xa0VII,\n\n7Stat.\n19,\n2 Kappler, supra, at 10 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf any citizen of the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall commit a robbery or murder, or other capital crime, on any Indian, such offender or offenders shall be punished in the same manner as if [the crime] had been committed on a citizen of the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d); Convention Relative to Recaptured Vessels, U.\xc2\xa0S.-Neth., Oct. 8, 1782,\n\n8Stat.\n50, T. S. No. 250 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe vessells of either of the two nations re-captured by the privateers of the other, shall be restored to the first proprietor .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). Preconstitutional practice therefore reflects the use of the treaty-making power only for matters of international intercourse; that practice provides no support for using treaties to regulate purely domestic affairs.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Debates preceding the ratification of the proposed Constitution confirm the limited scope of the powers possessed by the Federal Government generally; the Treaty Power was no exception. The Framers understood that most regulatory matters were to be left to the States. See The Federalist No. 45, at 313 (J. Madison) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe powers delegated by the proposed Constitution to the Federal Government, are few and defined\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Lopez, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 590\xe2\x80\x93592 (Thomas, J., concurring) (citing sources). Consistent with that general understanding of limited federal power, evidence from the ratification campaign suggests that the Treaty Power was limited and, in particular, confined to matters of intercourse with other nations.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In essays during the ratification campaign in New York, James Madison took the view that the Treaty Power was inherently limited. The Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s powers, Madison wrote, \xe2\x80\x9cwill be exercised principally on external objects, as war, peace, negotiation, and foreign commerce\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the traditional subjects of treaty-making. The Federalist No. 45, at 313. If the \xe2\x80\x9cexternal\xe2\x80\x9d Treaty Power contained a capacious domestic regulatory authority, that would plainly conflict with Madison\xe2\x80\x99s firm understanding that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he powers delegated by the proposed Constitution to the Federal Government, are few and defined.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Madison evidently saw no conflict, however, because the Treaty Power included authority to \xe2\x80\x9cregulate the intercourse with foreign nations\xe2\x80\x9d rather than all domestic affairs. Id., No. 42, at 279.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Madison reiterated that understanding at the 1788 Virginia ratifying convention, where the most extensive discussion of the proposed Treaty Power occurred, see Bradley 410; Golove, Treaty-Making and the Nation, 98 Mich. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1075, 1141\xe2\x80\x931142 (2000) (hereinafter Golove). There, Anti-Federalists leveled the charge that the Treaty Power gave the Federal Government excessive power. See, e.g., 3 Debates on the Federal Constitution 509 (J. Elliot 2d ed. 1876) (hereinafter Elliot\xe2\x80\x99s Debates) (G. Mason) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe President and Senate can make any treaty whatsoever\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 513 (P. Henry) (\xe2\x80\x9cTo me this power appears still destructive; for they can make any treaty\xe2\x80\x9d). But Madison insisted that just \xe2\x80\x9cbecause this power is given to Congress,\xe2\x80\x9d it did not follow that the Treaty Power was \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute and unlimited.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 514. The President and the Senate lacked the power \xe2\x80\x9cto dismember the empire,\xe2\x80\x9d for example, because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he exercise of the power must be consistent with the object of the delegation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cThe object of treaties,\xe2\x80\x9d in Madison\xe2\x80\x99s oft-repeated formulation, \xe2\x80\x9cis the regulation of intercourse with foreign nations, and is external.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Although Alexander Hamilton undoubtedly believed that the Treaty Power was broad within its proper sphere, see infra, at 8, the view he expressed in essays during the New York ratification campaign is entirely consistent with Madison\xe2\x80\x99s. After noting that the Treaty Power was one of the \xe2\x80\x9cmost unexceptionable parts\xe2\x80\x9d of the proposed Constitution, Hamilton distinguished the Treaty Power from the legislative power \xe2\x80\x9cto prescribe rules for the regulation of the society\xe2\x80\x9d and from the executive power to \xe2\x80\x9cexecut[e] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 75, at 503\xe2\x80\x93504. \xe2\x80\x9cThe power of making treaties,\xe2\x80\x9d he concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cis plainly neither the one nor the other.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 504. Rather, Hamilton explained that treaties \xe2\x80\x9care not rules prescribed by the sovereign to the subject, but agreements between sovereign and sovereign.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 504\xe2\x80\x93505. That description is difficult to square with a view of the Treaty Power that would allow the Federal Government to prescribe rules over all aspects of domestic life.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It did not escape the attention of the Framers that the Treaty Power was drafted without explicitly enumerated limits on what sorts of treaties are permissible. See, e.g., Hamilton, The Defence No. XXXVI, in 20 Papers of Alexander Hamilton 6 (H. Syrett ed. 1974) (\xe2\x80\x9cA power \xe2\x80\x98to make treaties,\xe2\x80\x99 granted in these indefinite terms, extends to all kinds of treaties and with all the latitude which such a power under any form of Government can possess\xe2\x80\x9d). The Articles of Confederation had, for example, explicitly restricted certain categories of treaties. See Art.\xc2\xa0IX(\xe2\x80\x9c[N]o treaty of commerce shall be made whereby the legis-lative power of the respective States shall be restrained from imposing such imposts and duties on foreigners, as their own people are subjected to, or from prohibiting the exportation or importation of any species of goods or commodities whatsoever\xe2\x80\x9d). The Constitution omitted those restrictions.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n That decision was not a grant of unlimited power, but rather a grant of flexibility; the Federal Government needed the ability to respond to unforeseeable varieties of intercourse with other nations. James Madison, for example, did \xe2\x80\x9cnot think it possible to enumerate all the cases in which such external regulations would be necessary.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 Elliot\xe2\x80\x99s Debates 514; see also id., at 363 (E. Randolph) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe various contingencies which may form the object of treaties, are, in the nature of things, incapable of definition\xe2\x80\x9d). But Madison nevertheless recognized that any exercise of the Treaty Power \xe2\x80\x9cmust be consistent with the object of the delegation,\xe2\x80\x9d which is \xe2\x80\x9cthe regulation of intercourse with foreign nations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 514; see also Hamilton, The Defence, supra, at 6 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hatever is a proper subject of compact between Nation & Nation may be embraced by a Treaty\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). That understanding of the Treaty Power did not permit the President and the Senate to exercise domestic authority commensurate with their substantial power over external affairs.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The understanding that treaties are limited to, in Madison\xe2\x80\x99s words, \xe2\x80\x9cthe regulation of intercourse with foreign nations,\xe2\x80\x9d endured in the years after the Constitution was ratified.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In 1796, an extended debate regarding the proper scope of the Treaty Power arose in the aftermath of a controversial treaty with Great Britain that addressed the validity of prerevolutionary debts and the property rights of British subjects. Treaty of Amity, Commerce and Navigation, Nov. 19, 1794,\n\n8Stat.\n116, T. S. No. 105. When President Washington requested appropriations to implement that so-called \xe2\x80\x9cJay Treaty\xe2\x80\x9d (after its chief negotiator, John Jay), the House of Representatives engaged in a month-long floor debate over its own role in the process of implementing treaties. See 5 Annals of Cong. 426 (1796); see generally D. Currie, The Constitution in Congress: The Fed-eralist Period 1789\xe2\x80\x931801, pp. 211\xe2\x80\x93217 (1997). SomeCongressmen argued that the House had a right to independently review the merits of the treaty. See, e.g., 5 Annals of Cong. 427\xe2\x80\x93428 (remarks of Rep. Livingston) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he House w[as] vested with a discretionary power of carrying the Treaty into effect, or refusing it their sanction\xe2\x80\x9d). Others insisted that \xe2\x80\x9cif the Treaty was the supreme law of the land, then there was no discretionary power in the House, except on the question of its constitutionality.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 436\xe2\x80\x93437 (Rep. Murray).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n That latter group relied in part on the observation that the Treaty Power was limited by its nature, and thus the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s failure to specify a role for the House did not pose a mortal threat to that Chamber\xe2\x80\x99s legislative prerogatives. Representative James Hillhouse of Connecticut expounded that position in the floor debate. Hillhouse recognized that the House had an \xe2\x80\x9cindispensable duty to look into every Treaty\xe2\x80\x9d to ensure that it is constitutional, i.e., \xe2\x80\x9cwhether it related to objects within the province of the Treaty-making power, a power which is not unlimited.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 660. He further explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he objects upon which it can operate are understood and well defined, and if the Treaty-making power were to embrace other objects, their doings would have no more binding force than if the Legislature were to assume and exercise judicial powers under the name of legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Hillhouse \xe2\x80\x9cadvert[ed] to the general definition of the Treaty-making power\xe2\x80\x9d to explain why the Treaty Power was not a threat to the House\xe2\x80\x99s legislative prerogatives:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f we look into our code of laws, we shall find few of them that can be affected, to any great degree, by the Treaty-making power. All laws regulating our own internal police, so far as the citizens of the United States alone are concerned, are wholly beyond its reach; no foreign nation having any interest or concern in that business, every attempt to interfere would be a mere nullity, as much as if two individuals were to enter into a contract to regulate the conduct or actions of a third person, who was no party to such contract.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 662.

\n

He accordingly denied that \xe2\x80\x9cthe President and Senate hav[e] it in their power, by forming Treaties with an Indian tribe or a foreign nation, to legislate over the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d concluding instead that the Treaty Power \xe2\x80\x9ccannot affect the Legislative power of Congress but in a very small and limited degree.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 663.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Other Representatives who participated in the Jay Treaty debates agreed with Hillhouse that the Treaty Power had a limited scope. See, e.g., id., at 516 (Rep. Sedgwick) (classifying the uses of the power as \xe2\x80\x9c1. To compose and adjust differences, whether to terminate or to prevent war. 2. To form contracts for mutual security or defence; or to make Treaties, offensive or defensive. 3. To regulate an intercourse for mutual benefit, or to form Treaties of commerce\xe2\x80\x9d). James Madison, who opposed the Jay Treaty as a Representative from Virginia, also took the opportunity to reiterate his view that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Treaty-making power was a limited power.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 777.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Other historical evidence from the postratification period is in accord. For example, Thomas Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s Senate Manual of Parliamentary Procedure, drafted while he was Vice President and therefore president of the Senate, Bradley 415, noted the need for a treaty to have a nexus to international intercourse. If a treaty did not \xe2\x80\x9cconcern the foreign nation, party to the contract,\xe2\x80\x9d then \xe2\x80\x9cit would be a mere nullity res inter alias acta.\xe2\x80\x9d Thomas Jefferson\xe2\x80\x99s Senate Manual (1801), in 9 The Writings of Thomas Jefferson 80\xe2\x80\x9381 (H. Washington ed. 1861). Later, Justice Story likewise anchored the Treaty Power in intercourse between nations. J. Story, Commentaries on the Constitution of the United States 552\xe2\x80\x93553 (abr. ed. 1833). (\xe2\x80\x9cThe power \xe2\x80\x98to make treaties\xe2\x80\x99 is by the constitution general; and of course it embraces all sorts of treaties, for peace or war; for commerce or territory; for alliance or succours; for indemnity for injuries or payment of debts; for the recognition or enforcement of principles of public law; and for any other purposes, which the policy or interests of independent sovereigns may dictate in their intercourse with each other\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The touchstone of all of these views was that the Treaty Power is limited to matters of international intercourse. Even if a treaty may reach some local matters,[1] it still must relate to intercourse with other nations. The Jay Treaty, for example, altered state property law, but only with respect to British subjects, who could hold and devise real property in the United States \xe2\x80\x9cin like manner as if they were natives.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0IX,\n\n8Stat.\n122. An 1815 treaty with Great Britain was held to pre-empt a state law authorizing the seizure of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98free negroes or persons of color\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d at ports in part because the state law applied to British sailors. See Elkison v. Deliesseline, 8 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 493, 495 (No. 4, 366) (CC SC 1823) (Johnson, Circuit Justice). And treaties with China and Japan, which afforded subjects of those countries the same rights and privileges as citizens of other nations, were understood to pre-empt state laws that discriminated against Chinese and Japanese subjects. See, e.g., Baker v. Portland, 2 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 472, 474 (No. 777) (CC Ore. 1879). Cf. Brief for United States 29, 33\xe2\x80\x9338.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The postratification theory and practice of treaty-making accordingly confirms the understanding that treaties by their nature relate to intercourse with other nations (including their people and property), rather than to purely domestic affairs.

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The original understanding that the Treaty Power was limited to international intercourse has been well represented in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents. Although we have not had occasion to define the limits of the power in much detail, we have described treaties as dealing in some manner with intercourse between nations. See, e.g., Holmes v. Jennison, 14 Pet. 540, 569 (1840) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe power to make treaties .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was designed to include all those subjects, which in the ordinary intercourse of nations had usually been made subjects of negotiation and treaty\xe2\x80\x9d); Holden v. Joy, 17 Wall. 211, 242\xe2\x80\x93243 (1872) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he framers of the Constitution intended that [the Treaty Power] should extend to all those objects which in the intercourse of nations had usually been regarded as the proper subjects of negotiation and treaty, if not inconsistent with the nature of our government and the relation between the States and the United States\xe2\x80\x9d). Cf. Power Auth. of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. Federal Power Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 247 F.\xc2\xa02d 538, 542\xe2\x80\x93543 (CADC 1957) (Bazelon, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cNo court has ever said .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the treaty power can be exercised without limit to affect matters which are of purely domestic concern and do not pertain to our relations with other nations\xe2\x80\x9d), vacated as moot,\n\n355 U.\xc2\xa0S. 64 (1957)\n (per curiam).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A common refrain in these cases is that the Treaty Power \xe2\x80\x9cextends to all proper subjects of negotiation with foreign governments.\xe2\x80\x9d In re Ross,\n\n140 U.\xc2\xa0S. 453,\n463 (1891)\n; see also Geofroy v. Riggs,\n\n133 U.\xc2\xa0S. 258,\n266 (1890)\n (same); Asakura v. Seattle,\n\n265 U.\xc2\xa0S. 332,\n341 (1924)\n (same). Those cases identified certain paradigmatic instances of \xe2\x80\x9cintercourse\xe2\x80\x9d that were \xe2\x80\x9cproper negotiating subjects\xe2\x80\x9d fit for treaty. See, e.g., Holmes, supra, at 569 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he treaty-making power must have authority to decide how far the right of a foreign nation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. will be recognised and enforced, when it demands the surrender of any [fugitive] charged with offences against it\xe2\x80\x9d); Geofroy, supra, at 266 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is also clear that the protection which should be afforded to the citizens of one country owning property in another, and the manner in which that prop-erty may be transferred, devised or inherited, are fitting subjects for such negotiation and of regulation by mutual stipulations between the two countries\xe2\x80\x9d); Asakura, supra, at 341 (\xe2\x80\x9cTreaties for the protection of citizens of one country residing in the territory of another are numerous, and make for good understanding between nations\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)). Nothing in our cases, on the other hand, suggests that the Treaty Power conceals a police power over domestic affairs.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Whatever its other defects, Missouri v. Holland, \n\n252 U.\xc2\xa0S. 416 (1920)\n, is consistent with that view. There, the Court addressed the constitutionality of a treaty that regulated the capture of birds that migrated between Canada and the United States. Convention with Great Britain for the Protection of Migratory Birds, Aug. 16, 1916,\n\n39Stat.\n1702, T. S. No. 628. Although the Court upheld a statute implementing that treaty based on an improperly broad view of the Necessary and Proper Clause, see ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9314 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment), Holland did not conclude that the Treaty Power itself was unlimited. See 252 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 433 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe do not mean to imply that there are no qualifications to the treaty-making power .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). To the contrary, the holding in Holland is consistent with the understanding that treaties are limited to matters of international intercourse. The Court observed that the treaty at issue addressed mi-gratory birds that were \xe2\x80\x9conly transitorily within the State and ha[d] no permanent habitat therein.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 435; see also id., at 434 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he treaty deals with creatures that [only] for the moment are within the state borders\xe2\x80\x9d). As such, the birds were naturally a matter of international intercourse because they were creatures in international transit.[2]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n At least until recently, the original understanding that the Treaty Power is limited was widely shared outside the Court as well. See Golove 1288 (\xe2\x80\x9c[V]irtually every authority, including the Supreme Court, has on countless occasions from the earliest days recognized general subject matter limitations on treaties\xe2\x80\x9d). The Second Restatement on the Foreign Relations Law of the United States, for example, opined that the Treaty Power is available only if the subject matter of the treaty \xe2\x80\x9cis of international concern.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7117(1)(a) (1964\xe2\x80\x931965). The Second Restatement explained that a treaty \xe2\x80\x9cmust relate to the external concerns of the nation as distinguished from matters of a purely internal nature.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., Comment b; see also Treaties and Executive Agreements: Hearings on S. J. Res. 1 before a Subcommittee of the Senate Committee on the Judiciary, 84th Cong., 1st Sess., 183 (1955) (Secretary of State Dulles) (Treaties cannot regulate matters \xe2\x80\x9cwhich do not essentially affect the actions of nations in relation to international affairs, but are purely internal\xe2\x80\x9d); Proceedings of the American Society of International Law 194\xe2\x80\x93196 (1929) (C. Hughes) (\xe2\x80\x9c[The Treaty Power] is not a power intended to be exercised .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with respect to matters that have no relation to international concerns\xe2\x80\x9d). But see Restatement (Third) of Foreign Relations Law of the United States \xc2\xa7302, Comment c (\xe2\x80\x9cContrary to what was once suggested, the Constitution does not require that an international agreement deal only with \xe2\x80\x98matters of international concern\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). At a minimum, the Second Restatement firmly reflects the understanding shared by the Framers that the Treaty Power has substantive limits. Only in the latter part of the past century have treaties challenged that prevailing conception by addressing \xe2\x80\x9cmatters that in the past countries would have addressed wholly domestically\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpurport[ing] to regulate the relationship between nations and their own citizens,\xe2\x80\x9d Bradley 396; see also ante, at 12 (opinion of Scalia, J.). But even the Solicitor General in this case would not go that far; he acknowledges that \xe2\x80\x9cthere may well be a line to be drawn\xe2\x80\x9d regarding \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the subject matter of [a] treaty is a proper subject for a treaty.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 43:10\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In an appropriate case, I would draw a line that respects the original understanding of the Treaty Power. I ac-knowledge that the distinction between matters of in-ternational intercourse and matters of purely domestic regulation may not be obvious in all cases. But this Court has long recognized that the Treaty Power is limited, and hypothetical difficulties in line-drawing are no reason to ignore a constitutional limit on federal power.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The parties in this case have not addressed the proper scope of the Treaty Power or the validity of the treaty here. The preservation of limits on the Treaty Power is nevertheless a matter of fundamental constitutional importance, and the Court ought to address the scope of the Treaty Power when that issue is presented. Given the increasing frequency with which treaties have begun to test the limits of the Treaty Power, see Bradley 402\xe2\x80\x93409, that chance will come soon enough.

So we are forced to decide\xe2\x80\x94there is no way aroundit\xe2\x80\x94whether the Act\xe2\x80\x99s application to what Bond did was constitutional.

\n

\n

I. The Statutory Question

\n

A. Unavoidable Meaning of the Text

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The meaning of the Act is plain. No person may knowingly \xe2\x80\x9cdevelop, produce, otherwise acquire, transfer directly or indirectly, receive, stockpile, retain, own, possess, or use, or threaten to use, any chemical weapon.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7229(a)(1). A \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c[a] toxic chemical and its precursors, except where intended for a purpose not prohibited under this chapter as long as the type and quantity is consistent with such a purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229F(1)(A). A \xe2\x80\x9ctoxic chemical\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cany chemical which through its chemical action on life processes can cause death, temporary incapacitation or permanent harm to humans or animals. The term includes all such chemicals, regardless of their origin or of their method of production, and regardless of whether they are produced in facilities, in munitions or elsewhere.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229F(8)(A). A \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose not prohibited\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny peaceful purpose related to an industrial, agricultural, research, medical, or pharmaceutical activity or other activity.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229F(7)(A).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Applying those provisions to this case is hardly complicated. Bond possessed and used \xe2\x80\x9cchemical[s] which through [their] chemical action on life processes can cause death, temporary incapacitation or permanent harm.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, she possessed \xe2\x80\x9ctoxic chemicals.\xe2\x80\x9d And, because they were not possessed or used only for a \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose not prohibited,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229F(1)(A), they were \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapons.\xe2\x80\x9d Ergo, Bond violated the Act. End of statutory analysis, I would have thought.[2]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court does not think the interpretive exercise so simple. But that is only because its result-driven antitextualism befogs what is evident.

\n

B. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s Interpretation

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s account of the clear-statement rule reads like a really good lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s brief for the wrong side, relying on cases that are so close to being on point that someone eager to reach the favored outcome might swallow them. The relevance to this case of United States v. Bass,\n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 336 (1971)\n, and Jones v. United States,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 848 (2000)\n, is, in truth, entirely made up. In Bass, we had to decide whether a statute forbidding \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98receiv[ing], possess[ing], or transport[ing] in commerce or affecting commerce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any firearm\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d prohibited possessing a gun that lacked any connection to interstate commerce. 404 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 337\xe2\x80\x93339. Though the Court relied in part on a federalism-inspired interpretive presumption, it did so only after it had found, in Part I of the opinion, applying traditional interpretive tools, that the text in question was ambiguous, id., at 339\xe2\x80\x93347. Adopting in Part II the narrower of the two possible readings, we said that \xe2\x80\x9cunless Congress conveys its purpose clearly, it will not be deemed to have significantly changed the federal-state balance.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 349 (emphasis added). Had Congress \xe2\x80\x9cconvey[ed] its purpose clearly\xe2\x80\x9d by enacting a clear and even sweeping statute, the presumption would not have applied.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Jones is also irrelevant. To determine whether an owner-occupied private residence counted as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98property usedin interstate or foreign commerce or in any activity affecting interstate or foreign commerce\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d under the federal arson statute, 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 850\xe2\x80\x93851, our opinion examined not the federal-jurisdiction-expanding consequences of answering yes but rather the ordinary meaning of the words\xe2\x80\x94and answered no, id., at 855\xe2\x80\x93857. Then, in a separate part of the opinion, we observed that our reading was consistent with the principle that we should adopt a construction that avoids \xe2\x80\x9cgrave and doubtful constitutional questions,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 857, and, quoting Bass, the principle that Congress must convey its purpose clearly before its laws will be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98deemed to have significantly changed the federal-state balance,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 858. To say that the best reading of the text conformed to those principles is not to say that those principles can render clear textambiguous.[3]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The latter is what the Court says today. Inverting Bass and Jones, it starts with the federalism-related consequences of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s meaning and reasons backwards, holding that, if the statute has what the Court considers a disruptive effect on the \xe2\x80\x9cfederal-state balance\xe2\x80\x9d of criminal jurisdiction, ante, at 14, that effect causes the text, even if clear on its face, to be ambiguous. Just ponder what the Court says: \xe2\x80\x9c[The Act\xe2\x80\x99s] ambiguity derives from the improbably broad reach of the key statutory definition .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the deeply serious consequences of adopting such a boundless reading; and the lack of any apparent need to do so .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). Imagine what future courts can do with that judge-empowering principle: Whatever has improbably broad, deeply serious, and apparently unnecessary consequences .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is ambiguous!

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The same skillful use of oh-so-close-to-relevant cases characterizes the Court\xe2\x80\x99s pro\xc2\xa0forma attempt to find ambiguity in the text itself, specifically, in the term \xe2\x80\x9c[c]hemical weapon.\xe2\x80\x9d The ordinary meaning of weapon, the Court says, is an instrument of combat, and \xe2\x80\x9cno speaker in natural parlance would describe Bond\xe2\x80\x99s feud-driven act of spreading irritating chemicals on Haynes\xe2\x80\x99s door knob and mailbox as \xe2\x80\x98combat.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. Undoubtedly so, but undoubtedly beside the point, since the Act supplies its own definition of \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon,\xe2\x80\x9d which unquestionably does bring Bond\xe2\x80\x99s action within the statutory prohibition. The Court retorts that \xe2\x80\x9cit is not unusual to consider the ordinary meaning of a defined term, particularly when there is dissonance between that ordinary meaning and the reach of the definition.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. So close to true! What is \xe2\x80\x9cnot unusual\xe2\x80\x9d is using the ordinary meaning of the term being defined for the purpose of resolving an ambiguity in the definition. When, for example, \xe2\x80\x9cdraft,\xe2\x80\x9d a word of many meanings, is one of the words used in a definition of \xe2\x80\x9cbreeze,\xe2\x80\x9d we know it has nothing to do with military conscription or beer. The point is illustrated by the almost-relevant case the Court cites for its novel principle, Johnson v. United States,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 133 (2010)\n. There the defined term was \xe2\x80\x9cviolent felony,\xe2\x80\x9d which the Act defined as an offense that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98has as an element the use .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of physical force against the person of another.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 135 (quoting \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(B)(i)). We had to figure out what \xe2\x80\x9cphysical force\xe2\x80\x9d meant, since the statute \xe2\x80\x9cd[id] not define\xe2\x80\x9d it. Id., at 138 (emphasis added). So we consulted (among other things) the general meaning of the term being defined, \xe2\x80\x9cviolent felony.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 140.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In this case, by contrast, the ordinary meaning of the term being defined is irrelevant, because the statute\xe2\x80\x99s own definition\xe2\x80\x94however expansive\xe2\x80\x94is utterly clear: any \xe2\x80\x9cchemical which through its chemical action on life proc-esses can cause death, temporary incapacitation or permanent harm to humans or animals,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229F(8)(A), unless the chemical is possessed or used for a \xe2\x80\x9cpeaceful purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229F(1)(A), (7)(A). The statute parses itself. There is no opinion of ours, and none written by any court or put forward by any commentator since Aristotle, which says, or even suggests, that \xe2\x80\x9cdissonance\xe2\x80\x9d between ordinary meaning and the unambiguous words of a definition is to be resolved in favor of ordinary meaning. If that were the case, there would hardly be any use in providing a definition. No, the true rule is entirely clear: \xe2\x80\x9cWhen a statute includes an explicit definition, we must follow that definition, even if it varies from that term\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Stenberg v. Carhart,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 914,\n942 (2000)\n (emphasis added). Once again, contemplate the judge-empowering consequences of the new interpretive rule the Court today announces: When there is \xe2\x80\x9cdissonance\xe2\x80\x9d between the statutory definition and the ordinary meaning of the defined word, the latter may prevail.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But even text clear on its face, the Court suggests, must be read against the backdrop of established interpretive presumptions. Thus, we presume \xe2\x80\x9cthat a criminal statute derived from the common law carries with it the requirement of a culpable mental state\xe2\x80\x94even if no such limitation appears in the text.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. And we presume that \xe2\x80\x9cfederal statutes do not apply outside the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Both of those are, indeed, established interpretive presumptions that are (1) based upon realistic assessments of congressional intent, and (2) well known to Congress\xe2\x80\x94thus furthering rather than subverting genuine legislative intent. To apply these presumptions, then, is not to rewrite clear text; it is to interpret words fairly, in light of their statutory context. But there is nothing either (1) realistic or (2) well known about the presumption the Court shoves down the throat of a resisting statute today. Who in the world would have thought that a definition is inoperative if it contradicts ordinary meaning? When this statute was enacted, there was not yet a \xe2\x80\x9cBond presumption\xe2\x80\x9d to that effect\xe2\x80\x94though presumably Congress will have to take account of the Bond presumption in the future, perhaps by adding at the end of all its definitions that depart from ordinary connotation \xe2\x80\x9cand we really mean it.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

C. The Statute as Judicially Amended

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n I suspect the Act will not survive today\xe2\x80\x99s gruesome surgery. A criminal statute must clearly define the conduct it proscribes. If it does not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98give a person of ordi-nary intelligence fair notice\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of its scope, United Statesv. Batchelder,\n\n442 U. S. 114,\n123 (1979)\n, it denies due process.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The new \xc2\xa7229(a)(1) fails that test. Henceforward, a person \xe2\x80\x9cshall be fined .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , imprisoned for any term of years, or both,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229A(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x94or, if he kills someone, \xe2\x80\x9cshall be punished by death or imprisoned for life,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229A(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x94whenever he \xe2\x80\x9cdevelop[s], produce[s], otherwise acquire[s], transfer[s] directly or indirectly, receive[s], stockpile[s], retain[s], own[s], possess[es], or use[s], or threaten[s] to use,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7229(a)(1), any chemical \xe2\x80\x9cof the sort that an ordinary person would associate with instruments of chemical warfare,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15 (emphasis added). Whether that test is satisfied, the Court unhelpfully (and also illogically) explains, depends not only on the \xe2\x80\x9cparticular chemicals that the defendant used\xe2\x80\x9d but also on \xe2\x80\x9cthe circumstances in which she used them.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The \xe2\x80\x9cdetergent under the kitchen sink\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe stain remover in the laundry room\xe2\x80\x9d are apparently out, ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x94but what if they are deployed to poison a neighborhood water fountain? Poisoning a goldfish tank is also apparently out, ante, at 17, but what if the fish belongs to a Congressman or Governor and the act is meant as a menacing message, a small-time equivalent of leaving a severed horse head in the bed? See ibid. (using the \xe2\x80\x9cconcerns\xe2\x80\x9d driving the Convention\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cacts of war, assassination, and terrorism\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94as guideposts of statutory meaning). Moreover, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s illogical embellishment seems to apply only to the \xe2\x80\x9cuse\xe2\x80\x9d of a chemical, ante, at 15, but \xe2\x80\x9cuse\xe2\x80\x9d is only 1 of 11 kinds of activity that the statute prohibits. What, one wonders, makes something a \xe2\x80\x9cchemical weapon\xe2\x80\x9d when it is merely \xe2\x80\x9cstockpile[d]\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpossess[ed]?\xe2\x80\x9d To these questions and countless others, one guess is as bad as another.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n No one should have to ponder the totality of the circumstances in order to determine whether his conduct is a felony. Yet that is what the Court will now require of all future handlers of harmful toxins\xe2\x80\x94that is to say, all of us. Thanks to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s revisions, the Act, which before was merely broad, is now broad and unintelligible. \xe2\x80\x9c[N]o standard of conduct is specified at all.\xe2\x80\x9d Coates v. Cincinnati,\n\n402 U. S. 611,\n614 (1971)\n. Before long, I suspect, courts will be required to say so.

\n

II. The Constitutional Question

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Since the Act is clear, the real question this case presents is whether the Act is constitutional as applied to petitioner. An unreasoned and citation-less sentence from our opinion in Missouri v. Holland,\n\n252 U.\xc2\xa0S. 416 (1920)\n, purported to furnish the answer: \xe2\x80\x9cIf the treaty is valid\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and no one argues that the Convention is not\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthere can be no dispute about the validity of the statute under Article I, \xc2\xa78, as a necessary and proper means to execute the powers of the Government.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 432.[4] Petitioner and her amici press us to consider whether there is anything to this ipse dixit. The Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s text and structure show that there is not.[5]

\n

A. Text

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Under Article I, \xc2\xa78, cl. 18, Congress has the power \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o make all Laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into Execution the foregoing Powers and all other Powers vested by this Constitution in the Governmentof the United States, or in any Department or Officer thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d One such \xe2\x80\x9cother Powe[r]\xe2\x80\x9d appears in Article II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02: \xe2\x80\x9c[The President] shall have Power, by and with the Advice and Consent of the Senate, to make Treaties, provided two thirds of the Senators present concur.\xe2\x80\x9d Read together, the two Clauses empower Congress to pass laws \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary and proper for carrying into Execution .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [the] Power .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to make Treaties.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is obvious what the Clauses, read together, do not say. They do not authorize Congress to enact laws for carrying into execution \xe2\x80\x9cTreaties,\xe2\x80\x9d even treaties that do not execute themselves, such as the Chemical Weapons Convention.[6] Surely it makes sense, the Government contends, that Congress would have the power to carry out the obligations to which the President and the Senate have committed the Nation. The power to \xe2\x80\x9ccarry into Execution\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cPower .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to make Treaties,\xe2\x80\x9d it insists, has to mean the power to execute the treaties themselves.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n That argument, which makes no pretense of resting on text, unsurprisingly misconstrues it. Start with the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cto make Treaties.\xe2\x80\x9d A treaty is a contract with a foreign nation made, the Constitution states, by the President with the concurrence of \xe2\x80\x9ctwo thirds of the Senators present.\xe2\x80\x9d That is true of self-executing and non-self-executing treaties alike; the Constitution does not distinguish between the two. So, because the President and the Senate can enter into a non-self-executing compact with a foreign nation but can never by themselves (without the House) give that compact domestic effect through legislation, the power of the President and the Senate \xe2\x80\x9cto make\xe2\x80\x9d a Treaty cannot possibly mean to \xe2\x80\x9center into a compact with a foreign nation and then give that compact domestic legal effect.\xe2\x80\x9d We have said in another context that a right \xe2\x80\x9cto make contracts\xe2\x80\x9d (a treaty, of course, is a contract) does not \xe2\x80\x9cextend .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to conduct .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. after the contract relation has been established .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Such postformation conduct does not involve the right to make a contract, but rather implicates the performance of established contract obligations.\xe2\x80\x9d Patterson v. McLean Credit Union,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 164,\n177 (1989)\n (emphasis added). Upon the President\xe2\x80\x99s agreement and the Senate\xe2\x80\x99s ratification, a treaty\xe2\x80\x94no matter what kind\xe2\x80\x94has been made and is not susceptible of any more making.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n How might Congress have helped \xe2\x80\x9ccarr[y]\xe2\x80\x9d the power to make the treaty\xe2\x80\x94here, the Chemical Weapons Convention\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cinto Execution\xe2\x80\x9d? In any number of ways. It could have appropriated money for hiring treaty negotiators, empowered the Department of State to appoint those negotiators, formed a commission to study the benefits and risks of entering into the agreement, or paid for a bevy of spies to monitor the treaty-related deliberations of other potential signatories. See G. Lawson & G. Seidman, The Constitution of Empire: Territorial Expansion and American Legal History 63 (2004). The Necessary and Proper Clause interacts similarly with other Article II powers: \xe2\x80\x9c[W]ith respect to the executive branch, the Clause would allow Congress to institute an agency to help the President wisely employ his pardoning power .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Most important, the Clause allows Congress to establish officers to assist the President in exercising his \xe2\x80\x98executive Power.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Calabresi & Prakash, The President\xe2\x80\x99s Power to Execute the Laws, 104 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 541, 591 (1994).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But a power to help the President make treaties is not a power to implement treaties already made. See generally Rosenkranz, Executing the Treaty Power, 118 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1867 (2005). Once a treaty has been made, Congress\xe2\x80\x99s power to do what is \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary and proper\xe2\x80\x9d to assist the making of treaties drops out of the picture. To legislate compliance with the United States\xe2\x80\x99 treaty obligations, Congress must rely upon its independent (though quite robust) Article I, \xc2\xa78, powers.

\n

B. Structure

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Constitutio[n] confer[s] upon Congress .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not all governmental powers, but only discrete, enumerated ones.\xe2\x80\x9d Printz v. United States,\n\n521 U. S. 898,\n919 (1997)\n. And, of course, \xe2\x80\x9cenumeration presupposes something not enumerated.\xe2\x80\x9d Gibbons v. Ogden, 9 Wheat. 1, 195 (1824). \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But in Holland, the proponents of unlimited congressional power found a loophole: \xe2\x80\x9cBy negotiating a treaty and obtaining the requisite consent of the Senate, the President .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may endow Congress with a source of legislative authority independent of the powers enumerated in Article I.\xe2\x80\x9d L. Tribe, American Constitutional Law \xc2\xa74\xe2\x80\x934, pp. 645\xe2\x80\x93646 (3d ed. 2000). Though Holland\xe2\x80\x99s change to the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s text appears minor (the power to carry into execution the power to make treaties becomes the power to carry into execution treaties), the change to its structure is seismic.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To see why vast expansion of congressional power is not just a remote possibility, consider two features of the modern practice of treaty making. In our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s early history, and extending through the time when Holland was written, treaties were typically bilateral, and addressed only a small range of topics relating to the obli-gations of each state to the other, and to citizens of the other\xe2\x80\x94military neutrality, for example, or military alliance, or guarantee of most-favored-nation trade treatment. See Bradley, The Treaty Power and American Federalism, 97 Mich. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 390, 396 (1998). But beginning in the last half of the last century, many treaties were \xe2\x80\x9cdetailed multilateral instruments negotiated and drafted at international conferences,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and they sought to regulate states\xe2\x80\x99 treatment of their own citizens, or even \xe2\x80\x9cthe activities of individuals and private entities,\xe2\x80\x9d A. Chayes & A. Chayes, The New Sovereignty: Compliance with International Regulatory Agreements 14 (1995). \xe2\x80\x9c[O]ften vague and open-ended,\xe2\x80\x9d such treaties \xe2\x80\x9ctouch on almost every aspect of domestic civil, political, and cultural life.\xe2\x80\x9d Bradley & Goldsmith, Treaties, Human Rights, and Condi-tional Consent, 149 U. Pa. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 399, 400 (2000).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Consider also that, at least according to some scholars, the Treaty Clause comes with no implied subject-matter limitations. See, e.g., L. Henkin, Foreign Affairs and the United States Constitution 191, 197 (2d ed. 1996); but see Bradley, supra, at 433\xe2\x80\x93439. On this view, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Tenth Amendment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does not limit the power to make treaties or other agreements,\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Third) of Foreign Relations Law of the United States \xc2\xa7302, Comment d, p. 154 (1986), and the treaty power can be used to regulate matters of strictly domestic concern, see id., at Comment c, p. 153; but see post, at 3\xe2\x80\x9316 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n If that is true, then the possibilities of what the Federal Government may accomplish, with the right treaty in hand, are endless and hardly farfetched. It could begin, as some scholars have suggested, with abrogation of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rulings. For example, the holding that a statute prohibiting the carrying of firearms near schools went beyond Congress\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated powers, United States v. Lopez,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549,\n551 (1995)\n, could be reversed by negotiating a treaty with Latvia providing that neither sovereign would permit the carrying of guns near schools. Similarly, Congress could reenact the invalidated part of the Violence Against Women Act of 1994 that provided a civil remedy for victims of gender-motivated violence, just so long as there were a treaty on point\xe2\x80\x94and some authors think there already is, see MacKinnon, The Supreme Court, 1999 Term, Comment, 114 Harv. L. Rev. 135, 167 (2000).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But reversing some of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions is the least of the problem. Imagine the United States\xe2\x80\x99 entry into an Antipolygamy Convention, which called for\xe2\x80\x94and Congress enacted\xe2\x80\x94legislation providing that, when a spouse of a man with more than one wife dies intestate, the surviv-ing husband may inherit no part of the estate. Constitu-tional? The Federalist answers with a rhetorical ques-tion: \xe2\x80\x9cSuppose by some forced constructions of its authority (which indeed cannot easily be imagined) the Federal Legislature should attempt to vary the law of descent in any State; would it not be evident that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. it had exceeded its jurisdiction and infringed upon that of the State?\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 33, at 206 (A. Hamilton). Yet given the Antipolygamy Convention, Holland would uphold it. Or imagine that, to execute a treaty, Congress enacted a statute prohibiting state inheritance taxes on real prop-erty. Constitutional? Of course not. Again, The Federalist: \xe2\x80\x9cSuppose .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [Congress] should undertake to abrogate a land tax imposed by the authority of a State, would it not be equally evident that this was an invasion of that concurrent jurisdiction in respect to this species of tax which its constitution plainly supposes to exist in the State governments?\xe2\x80\x9d No. 33, at 206. Holland would uphold it. As these examples show, Holland places Congress only one treaty away from acquiring a general police power.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Necessary and Proper Clause cannot bear such weight. As Chief Justice Marshall said regarding it, no \xe2\x80\x9cgreat substantive and independent power\xe2\x80\x9d can be \xe2\x80\x9cimplied as incidental to other powers, or used as a means of executing them.\xe2\x80\x9d McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 411 (1819); see Baude, Rethinking the Federal Eminent Domain Power, 122 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 1738, 1749\xe2\x80\x931755 (2013).No law that flattens the principle of state sovereignty, whether or not \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary,\xe2\x80\x9d can be said to be \xe2\x80\x9cproper.\xe2\x80\x9d As an old, well-known treatise put it, \xe2\x80\x9cit would not be a proper or constitutional exercise of the treaty-making power to provide that Congress should have a general legislative authority over a subject which has not been given it by the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 W. Willoughby, The Constitutional Law of the United States \xc2\xa7216, p. 504 (1910).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We would not give the Government\xe2\x80\x99s support of the Holland principle the time of day were we confronted with \xe2\x80\x9ctreaty-implementing\xe2\x80\x9d legislation that abrogated the freedom of speech or some other constitutionally protected individual right. We proved just that in Reid v. Covert,\n\n354 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1957)\n, which held that commitments made in treaties with Great Britain and Japan would not permit civilian wives of American servicemen stationed in those countries to be tried for murder by court-martial. The plurality opinion said that \xe2\x80\x9cno agreement with a foreign nation can confer power on the Congress, or on any other branch of Government, which is free from the restraints of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 16.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To be sure, the Reid plurality purported to distinguish the ipse dixit of Holland with its own unsupported ipse dixit. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he people and the States,\xe2\x80\x9d it said, \xe2\x80\x9chave delegated [the treaty] power to the National Government [so] the\n \nTenth Amendment is no barrier.\xe2\x80\x9d 354 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 18. The opinion does not say why (and there is no reason why) only the\n \nTenth Amendment, and not the other nine, has been \xe2\x80\x9cdelegated\xe2\x80\x9d away by the treaty power. The distinction between provisions protecting individual liberty, on the one hand, and \xe2\x80\x9cstructural\xe2\x80\x9d provisions, on the other, cannot be the explanation, since structure in general\xe2\x80\x94and especially the structure of limited federal powers\xe2\x80\x94is designed to protect individual liberty. \xe2\x80\x9cThe federal structure .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. secures the freedom of the individual.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. By denying any one government complete jurisdiction over all the concerns of public life, federalism protects the liberty of the individual from arbitrary power.\xe2\x80\x9d Bond v. United States, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Government raises a functionalist objection: If the Constitution does not limit a self-executing treaty to the subject matter delineated in Article I, \xc2\xa78, then it makes no sense to impose that limitation upon a statute implementing a non-self-executing treaty. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 32\xe2\x80\x9333. The premise of the objection (that the power to make self-executing treaties is limitless) is, to say the least, arguable. But even if it is correct, refusing to extend that proposition to non-self-executing treaties makes a great deal of sense. Suppose, for example, that the self-aggrandizing Federal Government wishes to take over the law of intestacy. If the President and the Senate find in some foreign state a ready accomplice, they have two options. First, they can enter into a treaty with \xe2\x80\x9cstipulations\xe2\x80\x9d specific enough that they \xe2\x80\x9crequire no legislation to make them operative,\xe2\x80\x9d Whitney v. Robertson,\n\n124 U.\xc2\xa0S. 190,\n194 (1888)\n, which would mean in this example something like a comprehensive probate code. But for that to succeed, the President and a supermajority of the Senate would need to reach agreement on all the details\xe2\x80\x94which, when once embodied in the treaty, could not be altered or superseded by ordinary legislation. The second option\xe2\x80\x94far the better one\xe2\x80\x94is for Congress to gain lasting and flexible control over the law of intestacy by means of a non-self-executing treaty. \xe2\x80\x9c[Implementing] legislation is as much subject to modification and repeal by Congress as legislation upon any other subject.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And to make such a treaty, the President and Senate would need to agree only that they desire power over the law of intestacy.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The famous scholar and jurist Henry St. George Tucker saw clearly the danger of Holland\xe2\x80\x99s ipse dixit five years before it was written:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[The statement is made that] if the treaty-making power, composed of the President and Senate, in discharging its functions under the government, finds that it needs certain legislative powers which Congress does not possess to carry out its desires, it may .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. infuse into Congress such powers, although the Framers of the Constitution omitted to grant them to Congress.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Every reputable commentator upon the Constitution from Story down to the present day, has held that the legislative powers of Congress lie in grant and are limited by such grant. . . . [S]hould such a construction as that asserted in the above statement obtain through judicial endorsement, our system of government would soon topple and fall.\xe2\x80\x9d Limitations on the Treaty-Making Power Under the Constitution of the United States \xc2\xa7113, pp. 129\xe2\x80\x93130 (1915).
