SPOKEO, INC. v. ROBINS

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x931339.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 2, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 16, 2016

\n

The Fair Credit Reporting Act of 1970 (FCRA) requires consumer reporting agencies to \xe2\x80\x9cfollow reasonable procedures to assure maximum possible accuracy of\xe2\x80\x9d consumer reports,\n\n15 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71681e(b), and imposes liability on \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person who willfully fails to comply with any requirement [of the Act] with respect to any\xe2\x80\x9d individual, \xc2\xa71681n(a).

\n

Petitioner Spokeo, Inc., an alleged consumer reporting agency, operates a \xe2\x80\x9cpeople search engine,\xe2\x80\x9d which searches a wide spectrum of databases to gather and provide personal information about individuals to a variety of users, including employers wanting to evaluate prospective employees. After respondent Thomas Robins discovered that his Spokeo-generated profile contained inaccurate information, he filed a federal class-action complaint against Spokeo, alleging that the company willfully failed to comply with the FCRA\xe2\x80\x99s requirements.

\n

The District Court dismissed Robins\xe2\x80\x99 complaint, holding that he had not properly pleaded injury in fact as required by Article III. The Ninth Circuit reversed. Based on Robins\xe2\x80\x99 allegation that \xe2\x80\x9cSpokeo violated his statutory rights\xe2\x80\x9d and the fact that Robins\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal interests in the handling of his credit information are individualized,\xe2\x80\x9d the court held that Robins had adequately alleged an injury in fact.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Because the Ninth Circuit failed to consider both aspects of the injury-in-fact requirement, its Article III standing analysis was incomplete. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0A plaintiff invoking federal jurisdiction bears the burden of establishing the \xe2\x80\x9cirreducible constitutional minimum\xe2\x80\x9d of standing by demonstrating (1) an injury in fact, (2) fairly traceable to the challenged conduct of the defendant, and (3) likely to be redressed by a favorable judicial decision. Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555\n\xe2\x80\x93561. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0As relevant here, the injury-in-fact requirement requires a plaintiff to show that he or she suffered \xe2\x80\x9can invasion of a legally protected interest\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete and particularized\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cactual or imminent, not conjectural or hypothetical.\xe2\x80\x9d Lujan, supra, at 560. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(1) The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s injury-in-fact analysis elided the independent \xe2\x80\x9cconcreteness\xe2\x80\x9d requirement. Both observations it made concerned only \xe2\x80\x9cparticularization,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., the requirement that an injury \xe2\x80\x9caffect the plaintiff in a personal and individual way,\xe2\x80\x9d Lujan, supra, at 560, n.\xc2\xa01, but an injury in fact must be both concrete and particularized, see, e.g., Susan B. Anthony List v. Driehaus, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Concreteness is quite different from particularization and requires an injury to be \xe2\x80\x9cde facto,\xe2\x80\x9d that is, to actually exist. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(2) The Ninth Circuit also failed to address whether the alleged procedural violations entail a degree of risk sufficient to meet the concreteness requirement. A \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete\xe2\x80\x9d injury need not be a \xe2\x80\x9ctangible\xe2\x80\x9d injury. See, e.g., Pleasant Grove City v. Summum,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 460\n. To determine whether an intangible harm constitutes injury in fact, both history and the judgment of Congress are instructive. Congress is well positioned to identify intangible harms that meet minimum Article III requirements, but a plaintiff does not automatically satisfy the injury-in-fact requirement whenever a statute grants a right and purports to authorize a suit to vindicate it. Article III standing requires a concrete injury even in the context of a statutory violation. This does not mean, however, that the risk of real harm cannot satisfy that requirement. See, e.g., Clapper v. Amnesty Int\xe2\x80\x99l USA, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ____. The violation of a procedural right granted by statute can be sufficient in some circumstances to constitute injury in fact; in such a case, a plaintiff need not allege any additional harm beyond the one identified by Congress, see Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Akins,\n\n524 U. S. 11\n\xe2\x80\x9325. This Court takes no position on the correctness of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate conclusion, but these general principles demonstrate two things: that Congress plainly sought to curb the dissemination of false information by adopting procedures designed to decrease that risk and that Robins cannot satisfy the demands of Article III by alleging a bare procedural violation. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

742 F.\xc2\xa03d 409, vacated and remanded.

\n

SPOKEO, INC., PETITIONER v. THOMAS ROBINS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[May 16, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case presents the question whether respondent Robins has standing to maintain an action in federal court against petitioner Spokeo under the Fair Credit Reporting Act of 1970 (FCRA or Act),\n\n84Stat.\n1127, as amended,\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71681 et\xc2\xa0seq.

\n

Spokeo operates a \xe2\x80\x9cpeople search engine.\xe2\x80\x9d If an individual visits Spokeo\xe2\x80\x99s Web site and inputs a person\xe2\x80\x99s name, a phone number, or an e-mail address, Spokeo conducts a computerized search in a wide variety of databases and provides information about the subject of the search. Spokeo performed such a search for information about Robins, and some of the information it gathered and then disseminated was incorrect. When Robins learned of these inaccuracies, he filed a complaint on his own behalf and on behalf of a class of similarly situated individuals.

\n

The District Court dismissed Robins\xe2\x80\x99 complaint for lack of standing, but a panel of the Ninth Circuit reversed. The Ninth Circuit noted, first, that Robins had alleged that \xe2\x80\x9cSpokeo violated his statutory rights, not just the statu-tory rights of other people,\xe2\x80\x9d and, second, that \xe2\x80\x9cRobins\xe2\x80\x99s personal interests in the handling of his credit information are individualized rather than collective.\xe2\x80\x9d 742 F.\xc2\xa03d 409, 413 (2014). Based on these two observations, the Ninth Circuit held that Robins had adequately alleged injury in fact, a requirement for standing under Article III of the Constitution. Id., at 413\xe2\x80\x93414.

\n

This analysis was incomplete. As we have explained in our prior opinions, the injury-in-fact requirement requires a plaintiff to allege an injury that is both \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete and particularized.\xe2\x80\x9d Friends of the Earth, Inc. v. Laidlaw Environmental Services (TOC), Inc.,\n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 167\n\xe2\x80\x93181 (2000) (emphasis added). The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis focused on the second characteristic (particularity), but it overlooked the first (concreteness). We therefore vacate the decision below and remand for the Ninth Circuit to consider both aspects of the injury-in-fact requirement.

\n

I

\n

The FCRA seeks to ensure \xe2\x80\x9cfair and accurate credit reporting.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71681(a)(1). To achieve this end, the Act regulates the creation and the use of \xe2\x80\x9cconsumer report[s]\xe2\x80\x9d[1] by \xe2\x80\x9cconsumer reporting agenc[ies]\xe2\x80\x9d[2] for certain specified purposes, including credit transactions, insurance, licensing, consumer-initiated business transactions, and employment. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71681a(d)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x93(C); \xc2\xa71681b. Enacted long before the advent of the Internet, the FCRA applies to companies that regularly disseminate information bearing on an individual\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccredit worthiness, credit standing, credit capacity, character, general reputation, personal characteristics, or mode of living.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71681a(d)(1).

\n

The FCRA imposes a host of requirements concerning the creation and use of consumer reports. As relevant here, the Act requires consumer reporting agencies to \xe2\x80\x9cfollow reasonable procedures to assure maximum possible accuracy of\xe2\x80\x9d consumer reports, \xc2\xa71681e(b); to notify providers and users of consumer information of their responsibilities under the Act, \xc2\xa71681e(d); to limit the circumstances in which such agencies provide consumer reports \xe2\x80\x9cfor employment purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71681b(b)(1); and to post toll-free numbers for consumers to request reports, \xc2\xa71681j(a).

\n

The Act also provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person who willfully fails to comply with any requirement [of the Act] with respect to any [individual[3]] is liable to that [individual]\xe2\x80\x9d for, among other things, either \xe2\x80\x9cactual damages\xe2\x80\x9d or statutory damages of $100 to $1,000 per violation, costs of the action and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, and possibly punitive damages. \xc2\xa71681n(a).

\n

Spokeo is alleged to qualify as a \xe2\x80\x9cconsumer reporting agency\xe2\x80\x9d under the FCRA.[4] It operates a Web site that allows users to search for information about other individuals by name, e-mail address, or phone number. In response to an inquiry submitted online, Spokeo searches a wide spectrum of databases and gathers and provides information such as the individual\xe2\x80\x99s address, phone number, marital status, approximate age, occupation, hobbies, finances, shopping habits, and musical preferences. App. 7, 10\xe2\x80\x9311. According to Robins, Spokeo markets its services to a variety of users, including not only \xe2\x80\x9cemployers who want to evaluate prospective employees,\xe2\x80\x9d but also \xe2\x80\x9cthose who want to investigate prospective romantic partners or seek other personal information.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 7. Persons wishing to perform a Spokeo search need not disclose their identities, and much information is available for free.

\n

At some point in time, someone (Robins\xe2\x80\x99 complaint does not specify who) made a Spokeo search request for information about Robins, and Spokeo trawled its sources and generated a profile. By some means not detailed in Robins\xe2\x80\x99 complaint, he became aware of the contents of that profile and discovered that it contained inaccurate information. His profile, he asserts, states that he is married, has children, is in his 50\xe2\x80\x99s, has a job, is relatively affluent, and holds a graduate degree. App. 14. According to Robins\xe2\x80\x99 complaint, all of this information is incorrect.

\n

Robins filed a class-action complaint in the United States District Court for the Central District of California, claiming, among other things, that Spokeo willfully failed to comply with the FCRA requirements enumerated above.

\n

The District Court initially denied Spokeo\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss the complaint for lack of jurisdiction, but later reconsidered and dismissed the complaint with prejudice. App. to Pet. for Cert. 23a. The court found that Robins had not \xe2\x80\x9cproperly pled\xe2\x80\x9d an injury in fact, as required by Article III. Ibid.

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed. Relying on Circuit precedent,[5] the court began by stating that \xe2\x80\x9cthe violation of a statutory right is usually a sufficient injury in fact to confer standing.\xe2\x80\x9d 742 F. 3d, at 412. The court recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Constitution limits the power of Congress to confer standing.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 413. But the court held that those limits were honored in this case because Robins alleged that \xe2\x80\x9cSpokeo violated his statutory rights, not just the statutory rights of other people,\xe2\x80\x9d and because his \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal interests in the handling of his credit information are individualized rather than collective.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis in original). The court thus concluded that Robins\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9calleged violations of [his] statutory rights [were] sufficient to satisfy the injury-in-fact requirement of Article III.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 413\xe2\x80\x93414.

\n

We granted certiorari. 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

The Constitution confers limited authority on each branch of the Federal Government. It vests Congress with enumerated \xe2\x80\x9clegislative Powers,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa71; it confers upon the President \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he executive Power,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa71, cl.\xc2\xa01; and it endows the federal courts with \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he judicial Power of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa71. In order to remain faithful to this tripartite structure, the power of the Federal Judiciary may not be permitted to intrude upon the powers given to the other branches. See DaimlerChrysler Corp. v. Cuno,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 332,\n341 (2006)\n; Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555\n\xe2\x80\x93560 (1992).

\n

Although the Constitution does not fully explain what is meant by \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he judicial Power of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa7 1, it does specify that this power extends only to \xe2\x80\x9cCases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cControversies,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa72. And \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[n]o principle is more fundamental to the judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s proper role in our system of government than the constitutional limitation of federal-court jurisdiction to actual cases or controversies.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Raines v. Byrd,\n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 811,\n818 (1997)\n.

\n

Standing to sue is a doctrine rooted in the traditional understanding of a case or controversy. The doctrine developed in our case law to ensure that federal courts do not exceed their authority as it has been traditionally understood. See id., at 820. The doctrine limits the category of litigants empowered to maintain a lawsuit in federal court to seek redress for a legal wrong. See Valley Forge Christian College v. Americans United for Separation of Church and State, Inc.,\n\n454 U.\xc2\xa0S. 464,\n473 (1982)\n; Warth v. Seldin,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 490\n\xe2\x80\x93499 (1975). In this way, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he law of Article III standing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. serves to prevent the judicial process from being used to usurp the powers of the political branches,\xe2\x80\x9d Clapper v. Amnesty Int\xe2\x80\x99l USA, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 9); Lujan, supra, at 576\xe2\x80\x93577, and confines the federal courts to a properly judicial role, see Warth, supra, at 498.

\n

Our cases have established that the \xe2\x80\x9cirreducible constitutional minimum\xe2\x80\x9d of standing consists of three elements. Lujan, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 560. The plaintiff must have (1) suffered an injury in fact, (2) that is fairly traceable to the challenged conduct of the defendant, and (3) that is likely to be redressed by a favorable judicial decision. Id., at 560\xe2\x80\x93561; Friends of the Earth, Inc., 528 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 180\xe2\x80\x93181. The plaintiff, as the party invoking federal jurisdiction, bears the burden of establishing these elements. FW/PBS, Inc. v. Dallas,\n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 215,\n231 (1990)\n. Where, as here, a case is at the pleading stage, the plaintiff must \xe2\x80\x9cclearly .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. allege facts demonstrating\xe2\x80\x9d each element. Warth, supra, at 518.[6]

\n

B

\n

This case primarily concerns injury in fact, the \xe2\x80\x9c[f\xc2\xa0]irst and foremost\xe2\x80\x9d of standing\xe2\x80\x99s three elements. Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83,\n103 (1998)\n. Injury in fact is a constitutional requirement, and \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is settled that Congress cannot erase Article III\xe2\x80\x99s standing requirements by statutorily granting the right to sue to a plaintiff who would not otherwise have standing.\xe2\x80\x9d Raines, supra, at 820, n.\xc2\xa03; see Summers v. Earth Island Institute,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 488,\n497 (2009)\n; Gladstone, Realtors v. Village of Bellwood,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 91,\n100 (1979)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIn no event .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may Congress abrogate the Art. III minima\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

To establish injury in fact, a plaintiff must show that he or she suffered \xe2\x80\x9can invasion of a legally protected interest\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete and particularized\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cactual or imminent, not conjectural or hypothetical.\xe2\x80\x9d Lujan, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 560 (internal quotation marks omitted). We discuss the particularization and concreteness requirements below.

\n

1

\n

For an injury to be \xe2\x80\x9cparticularized,\xe2\x80\x9d it \xe2\x80\x9cmust affect the plaintiff in a personal and individual way.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid., n.\xc2\xa01; see also, e.g., Cuno, supra, at 342 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98plaintiff must allege personal injury\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Whitmore v. Arkansas,\n\n495 U.\xc2\xa0S. 149,\n155 (1990)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98distinct\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Allen v. Wright,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 737,\n751 (1984)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cpersonal\xe2\x80\x9d); Valley Forge, supra, at 472 (standing requires that the plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98personally has suffered some actual or threatened injury\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Richardson,\n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 166,\n177 (1974)\n (not \xe2\x80\x9cundifferenti-ated\xe2\x80\x9d); Public Citizen, Inc. v. National Hwy. Traffic Safety Admin., 489 F.\xc2\xa03d 1279, 1292\xe2\x80\x931293 (CADC 2007) (collecting cases).[7]

\n

Particularization is necessary to establish injury in fact, but it is not sufficient. An injury in fact must also be \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete.\xe2\x80\x9d Under the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, however, that independent requirement was elided. As previously noted, the Ninth Circuit concluded that Robins\xe2\x80\x99 complaint alleges \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete, de facto\xe2\x80\x9d injuries for essentially two reasons. 742 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 413. First, the court noted that Robins \xe2\x80\x9calleges that Spokeo violated his statutory rights, not just the statutory rights of other people.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Second, the court wrote that \xe2\x80\x9cRobins\xe2\x80\x99s personal interests in the handling of his credit information are individualized rather than collective.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). Both of these observations concern particularization, not concreteness. We have made it clear time and time again that an injury in fact must be both concrete and particularized. See, e.g., Susan B. Anthony List v. Driehaus, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 8); Summers, supra, at 493; Sprint Communications Co. v. APCC Services, Inc.,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 269,\n274 (2008)\n; Massachusetts v. EPA,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497,\n517 (2007)\n.

\n

A \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete\xe2\x80\x9d injury must be \xe2\x80\x9cde facto\xe2\x80\x9d; that is, it must actually exist. See Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 479 (9th ed. 2009). When we have used the adjective \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete,\xe2\x80\x9d we have meant to convey the usual meaning of the term\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9creal,\xe2\x80\x9d and not \xe2\x80\x9cabstract.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 472 (1971); Random House Dictionary of the English Language 305 (1967). Concreteness, therefore, is quite different from particularization.

\n

2

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cConcrete\xe2\x80\x9d is not, however, necessarily synonymous with \xe2\x80\x9ctangible.\xe2\x80\x9d Although tangible injuries are perhaps easier to recognize, we have confirmed in many of our previous cases that intangible injuries can nevertheless be concrete. See, e.g., Pleasant Grove City v. Summum,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 460 (2009)\n (free speech); Church of Lukumi Babalu Aye, Inc. v. Hialeah,\n\n508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 520 (1993)\n (free exercise).

\n

In determining whether an intangible harm constitutes injury in fact, both history and the judgment of Congress play important roles. Because the doctrine of standing derives from the case-or-controversy requirement, and because that requirement in turn is grounded in historical practice, it is instructive to consider whether an alleged intangible harm has a close relationship to a harm that has traditionally been regarded as providing a basis for a lawsuit in English or American courts. See Vermont Agency of Natural Resources v. United States ex rel. Stevens,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 765\n\xe2\x80\x93777 (2000). In addition, because Congress is well positioned to identify intangible harms that meet minimum Article III requirements, its judgment is also instructive and important. Thus, we said in Lujan that Congress may \xe2\x80\x9celevat[e] to the status of legally cognizable injuries concrete, de\xc2\xa0facto injuries that were previously inadequate in law.\xe2\x80\x9d 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 578. Similarly, Justice Kennedy\xe2\x80\x99s concurrence in that case explained that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has the power to define injuries and articulate chains of causation that will give rise to a case or controversy where none existed before.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 580 (opinion concurring in part and concurring in judgment).

\n

Congress\xe2\x80\x99 role in identifying and elevating intangible harms does not mean that a plaintiff automatically satisfies the injury-in-fact requirement whenever a statute grants a person a statutory right and purports to authorize that person to sue to vindicate that right. Article III standing requires a concrete injury even in the context of a statutory violation. For that reason, Robins could not, for example, allege a bare procedural violation, divorced from any concrete harm, and satisfy the injury-in-fact requirement of Article III. See Summers, 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 496 (\xe2\x80\x9c[D]eprivation of a procedural right without some concrete interest that is affected by the deprivation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is insufficient to create Article III standing\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Lujan, supra, at 572.

\n

This does not mean, however, that the risk of real harm cannot satisfy the requirement of concreteness. See, e.g., Clapper v. Amnesty Int\xe2\x80\x99l USA, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ____. For example, the law has long permitted recovery by certain tort victims even if their harms may be difficult to prove or measure. See, e.g., Restatement (First) of Torts \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7569 (libel), 570 (slander per se) (1938). Just as the common law permitted suit in such instances, the violation of a procedural right granted by statute can be sufficient in some circumstances to constitute injury in fact. In other words, a plaintiff in such a case need not allege any additional harm beyond the one Congress has identified. See Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Akins,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 11\n\xe2\x80\x9325 (1998) (confirming that a group of voters\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cinability to obtain information\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress had decided to make public is a sufficient injury in fact to satisfy Article III); Public Citizen v. Department of Justice,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 440,\n449 (1989)\n (holding that two advocacy organizations\xe2\x80\x99 failure to obtain information subject to disclosure under the Federal Advisory Committee Act \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutes a sufficiently distinct injury to provide standing to sue\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In the context of this particular case, these general principles tell us two things: On the one hand, Congress plainly sought to curb the dissemination of false information by adopting procedures designed to decrease that risk. On the other hand, Robins cannot satisfy the demands of Article III by alleging a bare procedural violation. A violation of one of the FCRA\xe2\x80\x99s procedural requirements may result in no harm. For example, even if a consumer reporting agency fails to provide the required notice to a user of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s consumer information, that information regardless may be entirely accurate. In addition, not all inaccuracies cause harm or present any material risk of harm. An example that comes readily to mind is an incorrect zip code. It is difficult to imagine how the dissemination of an incorrect zip code, without more, could work any concrete harm.[8]

\n

Because the Ninth Circuit failed to fully appreciate the distinction between concreteness and particularization, its standing analysis was incomplete. It did not address the question framed by our discussion, namely, whether the particular procedural violations alleged in this case entail a degree of risk sufficient to meet the concreteness requirement. We take no position as to whether the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate conclusion\xe2\x80\x94that Robins adequately alleged an injury in fact\xe2\x80\x94was correct.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

The judgment of the Court of Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[May 16, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, concurring.

\n

The Court vacates and remands to have the Court of Appeals determine \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the particular procedural violations alleged in this case entail a degree of risk sufficient to meet the concreteness requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. In defining what constitutes a concrete injury, the Court explains that \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98real,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnot \xe2\x80\x98abstract,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but is not \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily synonymous with \xe2\x80\x98tan-gible.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. I write separately to explain how, in my view, the injury-in-fact requirement applies to different types of rights. The judicial power of common-law courts was historically limited depending on the nature of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s suit. Common-law courts more readily entertained suits from private plaintiffs who alleged a violation of their own rights, in contrast to private plaintiffs who asserted claims vindicating public rights. Those limitations persist in modern standing doctrine.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Standing doctrine limits the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial power\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98cases and controversies of the sort traditionally amenable to, and resolved by, the judicial process.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Vermont Agency of Natural Resources v. United States ex rel. Stevens,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 765,\n774 (2000)\n (quoting Steel Co. v. Citizens for a Better Environment,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83,\n102 (1998)\n). To understand the limits that standing imposes on \xe2\x80\x9cthe judicial Power,\xe2\x80\x9d therefore, we must \xe2\x80\x9crefer directly to the traditional, fundamental limitations upon the powers of common-law courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Honig v. Doe,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 305,\n340 (1988)\n (Scalia, J., dissenting). These limitations preserve separationof powers by preventing the judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s entanglement in disputes that are primarily political in nature. This concern is generally absent when a private plaintiff seeks to enforce only his personal rights against another private party.

\n

Common-law courts imposed different limitations on a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s right to bring suit depending on the type of right the plaintiff sought to vindicate. Historically,common-law courts possessed broad power to adjudicate suits involving the alleged violation of private rights, even when plaintiffs alleged only the violation of those rights and nothing more. \xe2\x80\x9cPrivate rights\xe2\x80\x9d are rights \xe2\x80\x9cbelonging to individuals, considered as individuals.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 W. Blackstone, Commentaries *2 (hereinafter Blackstone). \xe2\x80\x9cPrivate rights\xe2\x80\x9d have traditionally included rights of personal security (including security of reputation), property rights, and contract rights. See 1 id., at *130\xe2\x80\x93*139; Woolhander & Nelson, Does History Defeat Standing Doctrine?, 102 Mich. L. Rev. 689, 693 (2004). In a suit for the violation of a private right, courts historically presumed that the plaintiff suffered a de facto injury merely from having his personal, legal rights invaded. Thus, when one man placed his foot on another\xe2\x80\x99s property, the property owner needed to show nothing more to establish a traditional case or controversy. See Entick v. Carrington, 2 Wils. K.\xc2\xa0B. 275, 291, 95 Eng. Rep. 807, 817 (1765). Many traditional remedies for private-rights causes of action\xe2\x80\x94such as for trespass, infringement of intellectual property, and unjust enrichment\xe2\x80\x94are not contingent on a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s allegation of damages beyond the violation of his private legal right. See Brief for Restitution and Remedies Scholars as Amici Curiae 6\xe2\x80\x9318; see also Webb v. Portland Mfg. Co., 29 F. Cas. 506, 508 (No. 17,322) (Me. 1838) (stating that a legal injury \xe2\x80\x9cimports damage in the nature of it\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

Common-law courts, however, have required a further showing of injury for violations of \xe2\x80\x9cpublic rights\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94rights that involve duties owed \xe2\x80\x9cto the whole community, considered as a community, in its social aggregate capacity.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 Blackstone *5. Such rights include \xe2\x80\x9cfree navigation of waterways, passage on public highways, and general compliance with regulatory law.\xe2\x80\x9d Woolhander & Nelson, 102 Mich. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 693. Generally, only the government had the authority to vindicate a harm borne by the public at large, such as the violation of the criminal laws. See id., at 695\xe2\x80\x93700. Even in limited cases where private plaintiffs could bring a claim for the violation of public rights, they had to allege that the violation caused them \xe2\x80\x9csome extraordinary damage, beyond the rest of the [community].\xe2\x80\x9d 3 Blackstone *220 (discussing nuisance); see also Commonwealth v. Webb, 27 Va. 726, 729 (Gen. Ct. 1828).[1] An action to redress a public nuisance, for example, was historically considered an action to vindicate the violation of a public right at common law, lest \xe2\x80\x9cevery subject in the kingdom\xe2\x80\x9d be able to \xe2\x80\x9charass the offender with separate actions.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 Blackstone *219; see also 4 id., at *167 (same). But if the plaintiff could allege \xe2\x80\x9cspecial damage\xe2\x80\x9d as the result of a nuisance, the suit could proceed. The existence of special, individualized damage had the effect of creating a private action for compensatory relief to an otherwise public-rights claim. See 3 id., at *220. Similarly, a plaintiff had to allege individual damage in disputes over the use of public lands. E.g., Robert Marys\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 9 Co. Rep. 111b, 112b, 77 Eng. Rep. 895, 898\xe2\x80\x93899 (K. B. 1613) (commoner must establish not only injuria [legal injury] but also damnum [damage] to challenge another\xe2\x80\x99s overgrazing on the commons).

\n

B

\n

These differences between legal claims brought by private plaintiffs for the violation of public and private rights underlie modern standing doctrine and explain the Court\xe2\x80\x99s description of the injury-in-fact requirement. \xe2\x80\x9cInjury in fact\xe2\x80\x9d is the first of three \xe2\x80\x9cirreducible\xe2\x80\x9d requirements for Article III standing. Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555,\n560 (1992)\n. The injury-in-fact requirement often stymies a private plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to vindicate the infringement of public rights. The Court has said time and again that, when a plaintiff seeks to vindicate a public right, the plaintiff must allege that he has suffered a \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete\xe2\x80\x9d injury particular to himself. See Schlesinger v. Reservists Comm. to Stop the War,\n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 208\n\xe2\x80\x93223 (1974) (explaining this where plaintiffs sought to enforce the Incompatibility Clause, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa76, cl.\xc2\xa02, against Members of Congress holding reserve commissions in the Armed Forces); see also Lujan, supra, at 572\xe2\x80\x93573 (evaluating standing where plaintiffs sought to enforce the Endangered Species Act); Friends of the Earth, Inc. v. Laidlaw Environmental Services (TOC), Inc.,\n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 167\n\xe2\x80\x93184 (2000) (Clean Water Act). This requirement applies with special force when a plaintiff files suit to require an executive agency to \xe2\x80\x9cfollow the law\xe2\x80\x9d; at that point, the citizen must prove that he \xe2\x80\x9chas sustained or is immediately in danger of sustaining a direct injury as a result of that [challenged] action and it is not sufficient that he has merely a general interest common to all members of the public.\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte Levitt,\n\n302 U.\xc2\xa0S. 633,\n634 (1937)\n (\xc2\xa0per curiam). Thus, in a case where private plaintiffs sought to compel the U.\xc2\xa0S. Forest Service to follow certain procedures when it regulated \xe2\x80\x9csmall fire-rehabilitation and timber-salvage projects,\xe2\x80\x9d we held that \xe2\x80\x9cdeprivation of a procedural right without some concrete interest that is affected by the deprivation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is insufficient to create Article III standing,\xe2\x80\x9d even if \xe2\x80\x9caccorded by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Summers v. Earth Island Institute,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 488\n\xe2\x80\x93497 (2009).

\n

But the concrete-harm requirement does not apply as rigorously when a private plaintiff seeks to vindicate his own private rights. Our contemporary decisions have not required a plaintiff to assert an actual injury beyond the violation of his personal legal rights to satisfy the \xe2\x80\x9cinjury-in-fact\xe2\x80\x9d requirement. See, e.g., Carey v. Piphus,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 247,\n266 (1978)\n (holding that nominal damages are appropriate when a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights have been infringed but he cannot show further injury).

\n

The separation-of-powers concerns underlying our public-rights decisions are not implicated when private indi-viduals sue to redress violations of their own private rights. But, when they are implicated, standing doctrine keeps courts out of political disputes by denying private litigants the right to test the abstract legality of government action. See Schlesinger, supra, at 222. And by limiting Congress\xe2\x80\x99 ability to delegate law enforcement authority to private plaintiffs and the courts, standing doctrine preserves executive discretion. See Lujan, supra, at 577 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98To permit Congress to convert the undifferenti-ated public interest in executive officers\xe2\x80\x99 compliance with the law into an \xe2\x80\x98individual right\xe2\x80\x99 vindicable in the courts is to permit Congress to transfer from the President to the courts the Chief Executive\xe2\x80\x99s most important constitutional duty, to \xe2\x80\x98take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). But where one private party has alleged that another private party violated his private rights, there is generally no danger that the private party\xe2\x80\x99s suit is an impermissible attempt to police the activity of the political branches or, more broadly, that the legislative branch has impermissibly delegated law enforcement authority from the executive to a private individual. See Hessick, Standing, Injury in Fact, and Private Rights, 93 Cornell L. Rev. 275, 317\xe2\x80\x93321 (2008).

\n

C

\n

When Congress creates new private causes of action to vindicate private or public rights, these Article III principles circumscribe federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 power to adjudicate a suit alleging the violation of those new legal rights. Congress can create new private rights and authorize private plaintiffs to sue based simply on the violation of those private rights. See Warth v. Seldin,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 490,\n500 (1975)\n. A plaintiff seeking to vindicate a statutorily created private right need not allege actual harm beyond the invasion of that private right. See Havens Realty Corp. v. Coleman,\n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 363\n\xe2\x80\x93374 (1982) (recognizing standing for a violation of the Fair Housing Act); Tennessee Elec. Power Co. v. TVA,\n\n306 U.\xc2\xa0S. 118\n\xe2\x80\x93138 (1939) (recognizing that standing can exist where \xe2\x80\x9cthe right invaded is a legal right,\xe2\x80\x94one of property, one arising out of contract, one protected against tortious invasion, or one founded on a statute which confers a privilege\xe2\x80\x9d). A plaintiff seeking to vindicate a public right embodied in a federal statute, however, must demonstrate that the violation of that public right has caused him a concrete, individual harm distinct from the general population. See Lujan, supra, at 578 (noting that, whatever the scope of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power to create new legal rights, \xe2\x80\x9cit is clear that in suits against the Government, at least, the concrete injury requirement must remain\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, Congress cannot authorize private plaintiffs to enforce public rights in their own names, absent some showing that the plaintiff has suffered a concrete harm particular to him.

\n

II

\n

Given these principles, I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to vacate and remand. The Fair Credit Reporting Act creates a series of regulatory duties. Robins has no standing to sue Spokeo, in his own name, for violations of the duties that Spokeo owes to the public collectively, absent some showing that he has suffered concrete and particular harm. See supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. These consumer protection requirements include, for example, the requirement to \xe2\x80\x9cpost a toll-free telephone number on [Spokeo\xe2\x80\x99s] website through which consumers can request free annual file disclosures.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 23, First Amended Complaint \xc2\xb674; see\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71681j; 16 CFR \xc2\xa7610.3(a)(1) (2010).

\n

But a remand is required because one claim in Robins\xe2\x80\x99 complaint rests on a statutory provision that could argu-ably establish a private cause of action to vindicate the violation of a privately held right. Section 1681e(b) requires Robins to \xe2\x80\x9cfollow reasonable procedures to assure maximum possible accuracy of the information concerning the individual about whom the report relates.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71681e(b) (emphasis added). If Congress has created a private duty owed personally to Robins to protect his information, then the violation of the legal duty suffices for Article III injury in fact. If that provision, however, vests any and all consumers with the power to police the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable procedures\xe2\x80\x9d of Spokeo, without more, then Robins has no standing to sue for its violation absent an allegation that he has suffered individualized harm. On remand, the Court of Appeals can consider the nature of this claim.

\n

In the Fair Credit Reporting Act of 1970 (FCRA or Act),\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71681 et\xc2\xa0seq., Congress required consumer reporting agencies, whenever preparing a consumer report, to \xe2\x80\x9cfollow reasonable procedures to assure maximum possible accuracy of the information concerning the individual about whom the report relates.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71681e(b). To promote adherence to the Act\xe2\x80\x99s procedural requirements, Congress granted adversely affected consumers a right to sue noncomplying reporting agencies. \xc2\xa71681n (willful noncompliance); \xc2\xa71681o (negligent noncompliance).[1] Thomas Robins instituted suit against Spokeo, Inc., alleging that Spokeo was a reporting agency governed by the FCRA, and that Spokeo maintains on its Web site an inaccurate consumer report about Robins. App. 13.

\n

In particular, Robins alleged that Spokeo posted \xe2\x80\x9ca picture .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. purport[ing] to be an image of Robins [that] was not in fact [of him],\xe2\x80\x9d and incorrectly reported that Robins \xe2\x80\x9cwas in his 50s, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. married, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. employed in a professional or technical field, and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. has children.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14. Robins further alleged that Spokeo\xe2\x80\x99s profile of him continues to misrepresent \xe2\x80\x9cthat he has a graduate degree, that his economic health is \xe2\x80\x98Very Strong[,]\xe2\x80\x99 and that his wealth level [is in] the \xe2\x80\x98Top 10%.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Spokeo displayed that erroneous information, Robins asserts, when he was \xe2\x80\x9cout of work\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cactively seeking employment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Because of the misinformation, Robins stated, he encountered \xe2\x80\x9c[imminent and ongoing] actual harm to [his] employment prospects.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[2] As Robins elaborated on brief, Spokeo\xe2\x80\x99s report made him appear overqualified for jobs he might have gained, expectant of a higher salary than employers would be willing to pay, and less mobile because of family responsibilities. See Brief for Respondent 44.

\n

I agree with much of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. Robins, the Court holds, meets the particularity requirement for standing under Article III. See ante, at 8, 11 (remanding only for concreteness inquiry). The Court acknowledges that Congress has the authority to confer rights and delineate claims for relief where none existed before. Ante, at 9; see Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Akins,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 11\n\xe2\x80\x9320 (1998) (holding that inability to procure information to which Congress has created a right in the Federal Election Campaign Act of 1971 qualifies as concrete injury satisfying Article III\xe2\x80\x99s standing requirement); Public Citizen v. Department of Justice,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 440,\n449 (1989)\n (holding that plaintiff advocacy organizations\xe2\x80\x99 inability to obtain information that Congress made subject to disclosure under the Federal Advisory Committee Act \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutes a sufficiently distinct injury to provide standing to sue\xe2\x80\x9d); Havens Realty Corp. v. Coleman,\n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 363,\n373 (1982)\n (identifying, as Article III injury, violation of plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s right, secured by the Fair Housing Act, to \xe2\x80\x9ctruthful information concerning the availability of housing\xe2\x80\x9d).[3] Congress\xe2\x80\x99 connection of procedural requirements to the prevention of a substantive harm, the Court appears to agree, is \xe2\x80\x9cinstructive and important.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9; see Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555,\n580 (1992)\n (Kennedy, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cAs Government programs and policies become more complex and far reaching, we must be sensitive to the articulation of new rights of action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Restitution and Remedies Scholars et al. as Amici Curiae 3 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress cannot authorize individual plaintiffs to enforce generalized rights that belong to the whole public. But Congress can create new individual rights, and it can enact effective remedies for those rights.\xe2\x80\x9d). See generally Sunstein, Informational Regulation and Informational Standing: Akins and Beyond, 147 U. Pa. L. Rev. 613 (1999).

\n

I part ways with the Court, however, on the necessity of a remand to determine whether Robins\xe2\x80\x99 particularized injury was \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete.\xe2\x80\x9d See ante, at 11. Judged by what we have said about \xe2\x80\x9cconcreteness,\xe2\x80\x9d Robins\xe2\x80\x99 allegations carry him across the threshold. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion observes that time and again, our decisions have coupled the words \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete and particularized.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8 (citing as examples, Susan B. Anthony List v. Driehaus, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 8); Summers v. Earth Island Institute,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 488,\n493 (2009)\n; Sprint Communications Co. v. APCC Services, Inc.,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 269,\n274 (2008)\n; Massachusetts v. EPA,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497,\n517 (2007)\n). True, but true too, in the four cases cited by the Court, and many others, opinions do not discuss the separate offices of the terms \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cparticularized.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Inspection of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions suggests that the particularity requirement bars complaints raising generalized grievances, seeking relief that no more benefits the plaintiff than it does the public at large. See, e.g., Lujan, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 573\xe2\x80\x93574 (a plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cseeking relief that no more directly and tangibly benefits him than it does the public at large does not state an Article III case or controversy\xe2\x80\x9d (punctuation omitted)); Perkins v. Lukens Steel Co.,\n\n310 U.\xc2\xa0S. 113,\n125 (1940)\n (plaintiffs lack standing because they failed to show injury to \xe2\x80\x9ca particular right of their own, as distinguished from the public\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the administration of the law\xe2\x80\x9d). Robins\xe2\x80\x99 claim does not present a question of that character. He seeks redress, not for harm to the citizenry, but for Spokeo\xe2\x80\x99s spread of misinformation specifically about him.

\n

Concreteness as a discrete requirement for standing, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions indicate, refers to the reality of an injury, harm that is real, not abstract, but not necessarily tangible. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939; ante, at 1 (Thomas, J., concurring). Illustrative opinions include Akins, 524 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 20 (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ourts will not pass upon abstract, intellectual problems, but adjudicate concrete, living contests between adversaries.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and alterations omitted)); Diamond v. Charles,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 54,\n67 (1986)\n (plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cabstract concern does not substitute for the concrete injury required by Art[icle] III\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and ellipsis omitted)); Los Angeles v. Lyons,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 95,\n101 (1983)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cPlaintiffs must demonstrate a personal stake in the outcome .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Abstract injury is not enough.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Babbitt v. Farm Workers,\n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 289\n\xe2\x80\x93298 (1979) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe difference between an abstract question and a \xe2\x80\x98case or controversy\xe2\x80\x99 is one of degree, of course, and is not discernable by any precise test. Eastern Ky. Welfare Rights Organization,\n\n426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 26,\n40 (1976)\n (\xe2\x80\x9corganization\xe2\x80\x99s abstract concern .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does not substitute for the concrete injury required by Art.\xc2\xa0III\xe2\x80\x9d); California Bankers Assn. v. Shultz,\n\n416 U.\xc2\xa0S. 21,\n69 (1974)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThere must be .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. concrete adverseness\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9c[a]bstract injury is not enough.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Railway Mail Assn. v. (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t [is] not for courts to pass upon .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. abstract, intellectual problems but only .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. concrete, living contest[s] between adversaries call[ing] for the arbitrament of law.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Robins would not qualify, the Court observes, if he alleged a \xe2\x80\x9cbare\xe2\x80\x9d procedural violation, ante, at 10, one that results in no harm, for example, \xe2\x80\x9can incorrect zip code,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11. Far from an incorrect zip code, Robins complains of misinformation about his education, family situation, and economic status, inaccurate representations that could affect his fortune in the job market. See Brief for Center for Democracy & Technology et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 13 (Spokeo\xe2\x80\x99s inaccuracies bore on Robins\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cability to find employment by creating the erroneous impression that he was overqualified for the work he was seeking, that he might be unwilling to relocate for a job due to family commitments, or that his salary demands would exceed what prospective employers were prepared to offer him.\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Restitution and Remedies Scholars et al. as Amici Curiae 35 (\xe2\x80\x9cAn applicant can lose [a] job for being over-qualified; a suitor can lose a woman if she reads that he is married.\xe2\x80\x9d). The FCRA\xe2\x80\x99s procedural requirements aimed to prevent such harm. See 115 Cong. Rec. 2410\xe2\x80\x932415 (1969). I therefore see no utility in returning this case to the Ninth Circuit to underscore what Robins\xe2\x80\x99 complaint already conveys concretely: Spokeo\xe2\x80\x99s misinformation \xe2\x80\x9ccause[s] actual harm to [his] employment prospects.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 14.
