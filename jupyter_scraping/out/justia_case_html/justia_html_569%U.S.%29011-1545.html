\n\t\t\t\tCITY OF ARLINGTON, TEXAS, et\xc2\xa0al. v. FEDERAL COMMUNICATIONS COMMISSION et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x931545.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 16, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 20, 2013\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Communications Act of 1934, as amended, requires state or local governments to act on siting applications for wireless facilities \xe2\x80\x9cwithin a reasonable period of time after the request is duly filed.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii). Relying on its broad authority to implement the Communications Act, see \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7201(b), the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) issued a Declaratory Ruling concluding that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9creasonable period of time\xe2\x80\x9d is presumptively (but rebuttably) 90 days to process an application to place a new antenna on an existing tower and 150 days to process all other applications. The cities of Arlington and San Antonio, Texas, sought review of the Declaratory Ruling in the Fifth Circuit. They argued that the Commission lacked authority to interpret \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)\xe2\x80\x99s limitations. The Court of Appeals, relying on Circuit precedent holding that Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837\n, applies to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its own statutory jurisdiction, applied Chevron to that question. Finding the statute ambiguous, it upheld as a permissible construction of the statute the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s view that \xc2\xa7201(b)\xe2\x80\x99s broad grant of regulatory authority empowered it to administer \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Courts must apply the Chevron framework to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a statutory ambiguity that concerns the scope of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authority (i.e., its jurisdiction). Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9317.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Under Chevron, a reviewing court must first ask whether Congress has directly spoken to the precise question at issue; if so, the court must give effect to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 unambiguously expressed intent. 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 842\xe2\x80\x93843. However, if \xe2\x80\x9cthe statute is silent or ambiguous,\xe2\x80\x9d the court must defer to the administering agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the statute so long as it is permissible. Id., at 843. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0When a court reviews an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a statute it administers, the question is always, simply, whether the agency has stayed within the bounds of its statutory authority. There is no distinction between an agency\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnonjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d interpretations. The \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional-nonjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d line is meaningful in the judicial context because Congress has the power to tell the courts what classes of cases they may decide\xe2\x80\x94that is, to define their jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x94but not to prescribe how they decide those cases. But for agencies charged with administering congressional statutes, both their power to act and how they are to act is authoritatively prescribed by Congress, so that when they act improperly, no less than when they act beyond their jurisdiction, what they do is ultra vires. Because the question is always whether the agency has gone beyond what Congress has permitted it to do, there is no principled basis for carving out an arbitrary subset of \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d questions from the Chevron framework. See, e.g., National Cable & Telecommunications Assn., Inc. v. Gulf Power Co., \n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 327\n. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0This Court has consistently afforded Chevron deference to agencies\xe2\x80\x99 constructions of the scope of their own jurisdiction. See, e.g., Commodity Futures Trading Commission v. Schor, \n\n478 U.\xc2\xa0S. 833\n; United States v. Eurodif S.\xc2\xa0A., \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 305\n. Chevron applies to statutes designed to curtail the scope of agency discretion, see Chemical Mfrs. Assn. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 116\n, and even where concerns about agency self-aggrandizement are at their apogee\xe2\x80\x94i.e., where an agency\xe2\x80\x99s expansive construction of the extent of its own power would have wrought a fundamental change in the regulatory scheme, see FDA v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 120\n. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0The contention that Chevron deference is not appropriate here because the FCC asserted jurisdiction over matters of traditional state and local concern is meritless. These cases have nothing to do with federalism: The statute explicitly supplants state authority, so the question is simply whether a federal agency or federal courts will draw the lines to which the States must hew. P.\xc2\xa014.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(e)\xc2\xa0United States v. Mead Corp., \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218\n, requires that, for Chevron deference to apply, the agency must have received congressional authority to determine the particular matter at issue in the particular manner adopted. But Mead denied Chevron deference to action, by an agency with rulemaking authority, that was not rulemaking. There is no case in which a general conferral of rulemaking or adjudicative authority has been held insufficient to support Chevron deference for an exercise of that authority within the agency\xe2\x80\x99s substantive field. A general conferral of rulemaking authority validates rules for all the matters the agency is charged with administering. It suffices to decide this case that the preconditions to deference under Chevron are satisfied because Congress has unambiguously vested the FCC with general authority to administer the Communications Act through rulemaking and adjudication, and the agency interpretation at issue was promulgated in the exercise of that authority. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe consider whether an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a statutory ambiguity that concerns the scope of its regulatory authority (that is, its jurisdiction) is entitled to deference under Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWireless telecommunications networks require towers and antennas; proposed sites for those towers and antennas must be approved by local zoning authorities. In the Telecommunications Act of 1996, Congress \xe2\x80\x9cimpose[d] specific limitations on the traditional authority of state and local governments to regulate the location, construction, and modification of such facilities,\xe2\x80\x9d Rancho Palos Verdes v. Abrams, \n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 113, \n115 (2005)\n, and incorporated those limitations into the Communications Act of 1934, see \n\n110Stat.\n56,\n151. Section 201(b) of that Act empowers the Federal Communications Commission to \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe such rules and regulations as may be necessary in the public interest to carry out [its] provisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ch. 296, \n\n52Stat.\n588, codified at \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7201(b). Of course, that rulemaking authority extends to the subsequently added portions of the Act. See AT&T Corp. v. Iowa Utilities Bd., \n\n525 U.\xc2\xa0S. 366\n\xe2\x80\x93378 (1999).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Act imposes five substantive limitations, which are codified in \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B); only one of them, \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii), is at issue here. That provision requires state or local governments to act on wireless siting applications \xe2\x80\x9cwithin a reasonable period of time after the request is duly filed.\xe2\x80\x9d Two other features of \xc2\xa7332(c)(7) are relevant. First, subparagraph (A), known as the \xe2\x80\x9csaving clause,\xe2\x80\x9d provides that nothing in the Act, except those limitations provided in \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B), \xe2\x80\x9cshall limit or affect the authority of a State or local government\xe2\x80\x9d over siting decisions. Second, \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v) authorizes a person who believes a state or local government\xe2\x80\x99s wireless-siting decision to be inconsistent with any of the limitations in \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B) to \xe2\x80\x9ccommence an action in any court of competent jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn theory, \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii) requires state and local zoning authorities to take prompt action on siting applications for wireless facilities. But in practice, wireless providers often faced long delays. In July 2008, CTIA\xe2\x80\x94The Wireless Association,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t which represents wireless service providers, petitioned the FCC to clarify the meaning of \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that zoning authorities act on siting requests \xe2\x80\x9cwithin a reasonable period of time.\xe2\x80\x9d In November 2009, the Commission, relying on its broad statutory authority to implement the provisions of the Communications Act, issued a declaratory ruling responding to CTIA\xe2\x80\x99s petition. In\xc2\xa0re Petition for Declaratory Ruling, 24 FCC Rcd. 13994, 14001. The Commission found that the \xe2\x80\x9crecord evidence demonstrates that unreasonable delays in the personal wireless service facility siting process have obstructed the provision of wireless services\xe2\x80\x9d and that such delays \xe2\x80\x9cimpede the promotion of ad- vanced services and competition that Congress deemed critical in the Telecommunications Act of 1996.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14006, 14008. A \xe2\x80\x9creasonable period of time\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii), the Commission determined, is presumptively (but rebuttably) 90 days to process a collocation application (that is, an application to place a new antenna on an existing tower) and 150 days to process all other applications. Id., at 14005.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSome state and local governments opposed adoption of the Declaratory Ruling on the ground that the Commission lacked \xe2\x80\x9cauthority to interpret ambiguous provisions of Section 332(c)(7).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14000. Specifically, they argued that the saving clause, \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(A), and the judicial review provision, \xc2\xa7337(c)(7)(B)(v), together display a congressional intent to withhold from the Commission authority to interpret the limitations in \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B). Asserting that ground of objection, the cities of Arlington and San Antonio, Texas, petitioned for review of the Declaratory Ruling in the Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRelying on Circuit precedent, the Court of Appeals held that the Chevron framework applied to the threshold question whether the FCC possessed statutory authority to adopt the 90- and 150-day timeframes. 668 F.\xc2\xa03d 229, 248 (CA5 2012) (citing Texas v. United States, 497 F.\xc2\xa03d 491, 501 (CA5 2007)). Applying Chevron, the Court of Appeals found \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s effect on the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s author- ity to administer \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)\xe2\x80\x99s limitations ambiguous,\xe2\x80\x9d 668 F. 3d, at 250, and held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe FCC\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its statutory authority\xe2\x80\x9d was a permissible construction of the statute. Id., at 254. On the merits, the court upheld the presumptive 90- and 150-day deadlines as a \xe2\x80\x9cpermissible construction of \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii) and (v) .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. entitled to Chevron deference.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 256.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), limited to the first question presented: \xe2\x80\x9cWhether .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a court should apply Chevron to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an agency\xe2\x80\x99s determination of its own jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. in No. 11\xe2\x80\x931545, p. i.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs this case turns on the scope of the doctrine enshrined in Chevron, we begin with a description of that case\xe2\x80\x99s now-canonical formulation. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen a court reviews an agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the statute which it administers, it is confronted with two questions.\xe2\x80\x9d 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 842. First, applying the ordinary tools of statutory construction, the court must determine \xe2\x80\x9cwhether Congress has directly spoken to the precise question at issue. If the intent of Congress is clear, that is the end of the matter; for the court, as well as the agency, must give effect to the unambiguously expressed intent of Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 842\xe2\x80\x93843. But \xe2\x80\x9cif the statute is silent or ambiguous with respect to the specific issue, the question for the court is whether the agency\xe2\x80\x99s answer is based on a permissible construction of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 843.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChevron is rooted in a background presumption of congressional intent: namely, \xe2\x80\x9cthat Congress, when it left ambiguity in a statute\xe2\x80\x9d administered by an agency, \xe2\x80\x9cunderstood that the ambiguity would be resolved, first and foremost, by the agency, and desired the agency (rather than the courts) to possess whatever degree of discretion the ambiguity allows.\xe2\x80\x9d Smiley v. Citibank (South Dakota), N.\xc2\xa0A., \n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 735\n\xe2\x80\x93741 (1996). Chevron thus provides a stable background rule against which Congress can legislate: Statutory ambiguities will be resolved, within the bounds of reasonable interpretation, not by the courts but by the administering agency. See Iowa Utilities Bd., 525 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 397. Congress knows to speak in plain terms when it wishes to circumscribe, and in capacious terms when it wishes to enlarge, agency discretion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question here is whether a court must defer under Chevron to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a statutory ambiguity that concerns the scope of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authority (that is, its jurisdiction). The argument against deference rests on the premise that there exist two distinct classes of agency interpretations: Some interpretations\xe2\x80\x94the big, important ones, presumably\xe2\x80\x94define the agency\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Others\xe2\x80\x94humdrum, run-of-the-mill stuff\xe2\x80\x94are simply applications of jurisdiction the agency plainly has. That premise is false, because the distinction between \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnonjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d interpretations is a mirage. No matter how it is framed, the question a court faces when confronted with an agency\xe2\x80\x99s inter- pretation of a statute it administers is always, simply, whether the agency has stayed within the bounds of its statutory authority.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe misconception that there are, for Chevron purposes, separate \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d questions on which no deference is due derives, perhaps, from a reflexive extension to agen- cies of the very real division between the jurisdictional and nonjurisdictional that is applicable to courts. In the judicial context, there is a meaningful line: Whether the court decided correctly is a question that has different consequences from the question whether it had the power to decide at all. Congress has the power (within limits) to tell the courts what classes of cases they may decide, see Trainmen v. Toledo, P. & W. R. Co., \n\n321 U.\xc2\xa0S. 50\n\xe2\x80\x9364 (1944); Lauf v. E. G. Shinner & Co., \n\n303 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323, \n330 (1938)\n, but not to prescribe or superintend how they decide those cases, see Plaut v. Spendthrift Farm, Inc., \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 211\n\xe2\x80\x93219 (1995). A court\xe2\x80\x99s power to decide a case is independent of whether its decision is correct, which is why even an erroneous judgment is entitled to res judicata effect. Put differently, a jurisdictionally proper but substantively incorrect judicial decision is not ultra vires.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat is not so for agencies charged with administering congressional statutes. Both their power to act and how they are to act is authoritatively prescribed by Congress, so that when they act improperly, no less than when they act beyond their jurisdiction, what they do is ultra vires. Because the question\xe2\x80\x94whether framed as an incorrect application of agency authority or an assertion of author- ity not conferred\xe2\x80\x94is always whether the agency has gone beyond what Congress has permitted it to do, there is no principled basis for carving out some arbitrary subset of such claims as \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAn example will illustrate just how illusory the pro- posed line between \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnonjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d agency interpretations is. Imagine the following validly-enacted statute:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tCommon Carrier Act\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tSection 1. The Agency shall have jurisdiction to prohibit any common carrier from imposing an unreasonable condition upon access to its facilities.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThere is no question that this provision\xe2\x80\x94including the terms \xe2\x80\x9ccommon carrier\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable condition\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94defines the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction. Surely, the argument goes, a court must determine de\xc2\xa0novo the scope of that jurisdiction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsider, however, this alternative formulation of the statute:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tCommon Carrier Act\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tSection 1. No common carrier shall impose an unreasonable condition upon access to its facilities.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tSection 2. The Agency may prescribe rules and regulations necessary in the public interest to effectuate Section 1 of this Act.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNow imagine that the Agency, invoking its Section 2 authority, promulgates this Rule: \xe2\x80\x9c(1) The term \xe2\x80\x98common carrier\xe2\x80\x99 in Section 1 includes Internet Service Providers. (2) The term \xe2\x80\x98unreasonable condition\xe2\x80\x99 in Section 1 includes unreasonably high prices. (3) A monthly fee greater than $25 is an unreasonable condition on access to Internet service.\xe2\x80\x9d By this Rule, the Agency has claimed for itself jurisdiction that is doubly questionable: Does its authority extend to Internet Service Providers? And does it extend to setting prices? Yet Section 2 makes clear that Congress, in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 words, \xe2\x80\x9cconferred interpretive power on the agency\xe2\x80\x9d with respect to Section 1. Brief for Petitioners in No. 1545, p. 14. Even under petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 theory, then, a court should defer to the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the terms \xe2\x80\x9ccommon carrier\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable condition\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is to say, its assertion that its \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d extends to regulating Internet Service Providers and setting prices.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the first case, by contrast, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 theory would accord the agency no deference. The trouble with this is that in both cases, the underlying question is exactly the same: Does the statute give the agency authority to regulate Internet Service Providers and cap prices, or not?\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The reality, laid bare, is that there is no difference, insofar as the validity of agency action is concerned, between an agency\xe2\x80\x99s exceeding the scope of its authority (its \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d) and its exceeding authorized application of authority that it unquestionably has. \xe2\x80\x9cTo exceed authorized application is to exceed authority. Virtually any administrative action can be characterized as either the one or the other, depending on how generally one wishes to describe the \xe2\x80\x98authority.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Mississippi Power & Light Co. v. Mississippi ex rel. Moore, \n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 354, \n381 (1988)\n (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment); see also Monaghan, Marbury and the Administrative State, 83 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 29 (1983) (\xe2\x80\x9cAdministrative application of law is administrative formulation of law whenever it involves elaboration of the statutory norm.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis point is nicely illustrated by our decision in National Cable & Telecommunications Assn., Inc. v. Gulf Power Co., \n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 327 (2002)\n. That case considered whether the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d to regulate the rents utility-pole owners charge for \xe2\x80\x9cpole attachments\xe2\x80\x9d (defined as attachments by a cable television system or provider of telecommunications service) extended to attachments that provided both cable television and high-speed Internet access (attachments for so-called \xe2\x80\x9ccommingled services\xe2\x80\x9d). Id., at 331\xe2\x80\x93336. We held, sensibly, that Chevron applied. 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 333, 339. Whether framed as going to the scope of the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s delegated authority or the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s application of its delegated authority, the underlying question was the same: Did the FCC exceed the bounds of its statutory authority to regulate rents for \xe2\x80\x9cpole attachments\xe2\x80\x9d when it sought to regulate rents for pole attachments providing commingled services?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe label is an empty distraction because every new application of a broad statutory term can be reframed as a questionable extension of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction. One of the briefs in support of petitioners explains, helpfully, that \xe2\x80\x9c[j]urisdictional questions concern the who, what, where, and when of regulatory power: which subject matters may an agency regulate and under what conditions.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for IMLA Respondents 18\xe2\x80\x9319. But an agency\xe2\x80\x99s application of its authority pursuant to statutory text answers the same questions. Who is an \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman\xe2\x80\x9d? What is a \xe2\x80\x9cpole attachment\xe2\x80\x9d? Where do the \xe2\x80\x9cwaters of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d end? When must a Medicare provider challenge a reimbursement determination in order to be entitled to an administrative appeal? These can all be reframed as questions about the scope of agencies\xe2\x80\x99 regulatory jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x94 and they are all questions to which the Chevron framework applies. See Christopher v. SmithKline Beecham Corp., 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 2, 8); National Cable & Telecommunications Assn., supra, at 331, 333; United States v. Riverside Bayview Homes, Inc., \n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 121, \n123, \n131 (1985)\n; Sebelius v. Auburn Regional Medical Center, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 1, 11).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, judges should not waste their time in the mental acrobatics needed to decide whether an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a statutory provision is \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cnonjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Once those labels are sheared away, it becomes clear that the question in every case is, simply, whether the statutory text forecloses the agency\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of authority, or not. See H. Edwards & L. Elliott, Federal Standards of Review 146 (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn practice, it does not appear to matter whether delegated authority is viewed as a threshold inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d). The federal judge as haruspex, sifting the entrails of vast statutory schemes to divine whether a particular agency interpretation qualifies as \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d is not engaged in reasoned decisionmaking.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFortunately, then, we have consistently held \xe2\x80\x9cthat Chevron applies to cases in which an agency adopts a con- struction of a jurisdictional provision of a statute it administers.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 R. Pierce, Administrative Law Treatise \xc2\xa73.5, p. 187 (2010). One of our opinions explicitly says that no \xe2\x80\x9cexception exists to the normal [deferential] standard of review\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98jurisdictional or legal question[s] concerning the coverage\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of an Act. NLRB v. City Disposal Systems, Inc., \n\n465 U.\xc2\xa0S. 822, \n830, n. \n7 (1984)\n. A prime example of deferential review for questions of jurisdiction is Commodity Futures Trading Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Schor, \n\n478 U.\xc2\xa0S. 833 (1986)\n. That case involved a CFTC interpretation of \n\n7 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa718(c), which provides that before the Commission takes action on a complaint, the complainant must file a bond to cover \xe2\x80\x9cany reparation award that may be issued by the Commission against the complainant on any counterclaim by respondent.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) The CFTC, pursuant to its broad rulemaking authority, see \xc2\xa712a(5), interpreted that oblique reference to counterclaims as granting it \xe2\x80\x9cthe power to take jurisdiction over\xe2\x80\x9d not just federal-law counterclaims, but state-law counterclaims as well. Schor, supra, at 844. We not only deferred under Chevron to the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ceminently reasonable .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. interpretation of the statute it is entrusted to administer,\xe2\x80\x9d but also chided the Court of Appeals for declining to afford def- erence because of the putatively \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98statutory interpretation-jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x99 nature of the question at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d 478 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 844\xe2\x80\x93845.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSimilar examples abound. We have afforded Chevron deference to the Commerce Department\xe2\x80\x99s determination that its authority to seek antidumping duties extended to uranium imported under contracts for enrichment services, United States v. Eurodif S.\xc2\xa0A., \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 305, \n316 (2009)\n; to the Interstate Commerce Commission\xe2\x80\x99s view that courts, not the Commission, possessed \xe2\x80\x9cinitial jurisdiction with respect to the award of reparations\xe2\x80\x9d for unreasonable shipping charges, Reiter v. Cooper, \n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 258, \n269 (1993)\n (internal quotation marks and ellipsis omitted); and to the Army Corps of Engineers\xe2\x80\x99 assertion that its permitting authority over discharges into \xe2\x80\x9cwaters of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d extended to \xe2\x80\x9cfreshwater wetlands\xe2\x80\x9d adjacent to covered waters, Riverside Bayview Homes, supra, at 123\xe2\x80\x93124, 131. We have even deferred to the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that its broad regulatory authority extends to pre-empting conflicting state rules. City of New York v. FCC, \n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57, \n64 (1988)\n; Capital Cities Cable, Inc. v. Crisp, \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 691, \n700 (1984)\n.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur cases hold that Chevron applies equally to statutes designed to curtail the scope of agency discretion. For instance, in Chemical Mfrs. Assn. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 116, \n123 (1985)\n, we considered a statute prohibiting the Environmental Protection Agency from \xe2\x80\x9cmodify[ing] any requirement of this section as it applies to any specific pollutant which is on the toxic pollutant list.\xe2\x80\x9d The EPA construed the statute as not precluding it from granting variances with respect to certain toxic pollutants. Finding no \xe2\x80\x9cclear congressional intent to forbid EPA\xe2\x80\x99s sensible variance mechanism,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 134, we deferred to the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s construction of this express limitation on its own regulatory authority, id., at 125 (citing Chevron, \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837\n); see also, e.g., Japan Whaling Assn. v. American Cetacean Soc., \n\n478 U.\xc2\xa0S. 221\n\xe2\x80\x93234 (1986).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe U.\xc2\xa0S. Reports are shot through with applications of Chevron to agencies\xe2\x80\x99 constructions of the scope of their own jurisdiction. And we have applied Chevron where concerns about agency self-aggrandizement are at their apogee: in cases where an agency\xe2\x80\x99s expansive construction of the extent of its own power would have wrought a fundamental change in the regulatory scheme. In FDA v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 120 (2000)\n, the threshold question was the \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate framework for analyzing\xe2\x80\x9d the FDA\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction to regulate tobacco products,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 126, 132\xe2\x80\x94a question of vast \xe2\x80\x9ceconomic and political magnitude,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 133. \xe2\x80\x9cBecause this case involves an administrative agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction of a statute that it administers,\xe2\x80\x9d we held, Chevron applied. 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 132. Similarly, in MCI Telecommunications Corp. v. American Telephone & Telegraph Co., \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218, \n224, \n229, \n231 (1994)\n, we applied the Chevron framework to the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that the statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmodify any requirement\xe2\x80\x9d gave it authority to eliminate rate-filing requirements, \xe2\x80\x9cthe essential characteristic of a rate-regulated industry,\xe2\x80\x9d for long-distance telephone carriers.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe false dichotomy between \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnon- jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d agency interpretations may be no more than a bogeyman, but it is dangerous all the same. Like the Hound of the Baskervilles, it is conjured by those with greater quarry in sight: Make no mistake\xe2\x80\x94the ultimate target here is Chevron itself. Savvy challengers of agency action would play the \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d card in every case. See, e.g., Cellco Partnership v. FCC, 700 F.\xc2\xa03d 534, 541 (CADC 2012). Some judges would be deceived by the specious, but scary-sounding, \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d-\xe2\x80\x9cnonjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d line; others tempted by the prospect of making public policy by prescribing the meaning of ambiguous statutory commands. The effect would be to transfer any number of interpretive decisions\xe2\x80\x94archetypal Chevron questions, about how best to construe an ambiguous term in light of competing policy interests\xe2\x80\x94from the agencies that administer the statutes to federal courts.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t We have cautioned that \xe2\x80\x9cjudges ought to refrain from substituting their own interstitial lawmaking\xe2\x80\x9d for that of an agency. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA few words in response to the dissent. The question on which we granted certiorari was whether \xe2\x80\x9ca court should apply Chevron to review an agency\xe2\x80\x99s determination of its own jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Perhaps sensing the incoherence of the \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional-nonjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d line, the dissent does not even attempt to defend it, see post, at 5, but proposes a much broader scope for de\xc2\xa0novo judicial review: Jurisdictional or not, and even where a rule is at issue and the statute contains a broad grant of rulemaking authority, the dissent would have a court search provision-by-provision to determine \xe2\x80\x9cwhether [that] delegation covers the \xe2\x80\x98specific provision\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98particular question\xe2\x80\x99 before the court.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe dissent is correct that United States v. Mead Corp., \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218 (2001)\n, requires that, for Chevron deference to apply, the agency must have received congressional authority to determine the particular matter at issue in the particular manner adopted. No one disputes that. But Mead denied Chevron deference to action, by an agency with rulemaking authority, that was not rulemaking. What the dissent needs, and fails to produce, is a single case in which a general conferral of rulemaking or adjudicative authority has been held insufficient to support Chevron deference for an exercise of that authority within the agency\xe2\x80\x99s substantive field. There is no such case, and what the dissent proposes is a massive revision of our Chevron jurisprudence.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhere we differ from the dissent is in its apparent rejection of the theorem that the whole includes all of its parts\xe2\x80\x94its view that a general conferral of rulemaking authority does not validate rules for all the matters the agency is charged with administering. Rather, the dissent proposes that even when general rulemaking authority is clear, every agency rule must be subjected to a de\xc2\xa0novo judicial determination of whether the particular issue was committed to agency discretion. It offers no standards at all to guide this open-ended hunt for congressional intent (that is to say, for evidence of congressional intent more specific than the conferral of general rulemaking author- ity). It would simply punt that question back to the Court of Appeals, presumably for application of some sort of totality-of-the-circumstances test\xe2\x80\x94which is really, of course, not a test at all but an invitation to make an ad\xc2\xa0hoc judgment regarding congressional intent. Thirteen Courts of Appeals applying a totality-of-the-circumstances test would render the binding effect of agency rules unpredictable and destroy the whole stabilizing purpose of Chevron. The excessive agency power that the dissent fears would be replaced by chaos. There is no need to wade into these murky waters. It suffices to decide this case that the preconditions to deference under Chevron are satisfied because Congress has unambiguously vested the FCC with general authority to administer the Communications Act through rulemaking and adjudication, and the agency interpretation at issue was promulgated in the exercise of that authority.\n\t\t\t

Where Congress has established a clear line, the agency cannot go beyond it; and where Congress has established an ambiguous line, the agency can go no further than the ambiguity will fairly allow. But in rigorously applying the latter rule, a court need not pause to puzzle over whether the interpretive question presented is \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d If \xe2\x80\x9cthe agency\xe2\x80\x99s answer is based on a permissible construction of the statute,\xe2\x80\x9d that is the end of the matter. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa71, the latter in the \xe2\x80\x9cone supreme Court\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csuch inferior Courts as the Congress may from time to time ordain and establish,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa71. Agencies make rules (\xe2\x80\x9cPrivate cattle may be grazed on public lands X, Y, and Z subject to certain conditions\xe2\x80\x9d) and conduct adjudications (\xe2\x80\x9cThis rancher\xe2\x80\x99s grazing permit is revoked for violation of the conditions\xe2\x80\x9d) and have done so since the beginning of the Republic. These activities take \xe2\x80\x9clegislative\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial\xe2\x80\x9d forms, but they are exercises of\xe2\x80\x94indeed, under our constitutional structure they must be exercises of\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa71, cl.\xc2\xa01.\n\t\n
\n\n5\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0The dissent\xe2\x80\x94apparently with no attempt at irony\xe2\x80\x94accuses us of \xe2\x80\x9cmisunderstand[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d the question presented as one of \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 5. Whatever imprecision inheres in our understanding of the question presented derives solely from our having read it.\n\t\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Breyer, concurring in part and concurring in the judgment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI agree with the Court that normally \xe2\x80\x9cthe question a court faces when confronted with an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a statute it administers\xe2\x80\x9d is, \xe2\x80\x9csimply, whether the agency has stayed within the bounds of its statutory authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. In this context, \xe2\x80\x9cthe distinction between \xe2\x80\x98jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98non-jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x99 interpretations is a mirage.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDeciding just what those statutory bounds are, however, is not always an easy matter, and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s case law abounds with discussion of the subject. A reviewing judge, for example, will have to decide independently whether Congress delegated authority to the agency to provide interpretations of, or to enact rules pursuant to, the statute at issue\xe2\x80\x94interpretations or rules that carry with them \xe2\x80\x9cthe force of law.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Mead Corp., \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218, \n229 (2001)\n. If so, the reviewing court must give special leeway or \xe2\x80\x9cdeference\xe2\x80\x9d to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation. See id., at 227\xe2\x80\x93228.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have added that, if \xe2\x80\x9c[e]mploying traditional tools of statutory construction,\xe2\x80\x9d INS v. Cardoza-Fonseca, \n\n480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 421, \n446 (1987)\n, the court determines that Congress has spoken clearly on the disputed question, then \xe2\x80\x9cthat is the end of the matter,\xe2\x80\x9d Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837, \n842 (1984)\n. The agency is due no deference, for Congress has left no gap for the agency to fill. Id., at 842\xe2\x80\x93844. If, on the other hand, Congress has not spoken clearly, if, for example it has written ambiguously, then that ambiguity is a sign\xe2\x80\x94but not always a conclusive sign\xe2\x80\x94that Congress intends a reviewing court to pay particular attention to (i.e., to give a degree of deference to) the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation. See Gonzales v. Oregon, \n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 243\n\xe2\x80\x93269 (2006); Mead, supra, at 229.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI say that the existence of statutory ambiguity is sometimes not enough to warrant the conclusion that Congress has left a deference-warranting gap for the agency to fill be- cause our cases make clear that other, sometimes context-specific, factors will on occasion prove relevant. (And, given the vast number of government statutes, regulatory programs, and underlying circumstances, that variety is hardly surprising.) In Mead, for example, we looked to several factors other than simple ambiguity to help determine whether Congress left a statutory gap, thus delegating to the agency the authority to fill that gap with an interpretation that would carry \xe2\x80\x9cthe force of law.\xe2\x80\x9d 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 229\xe2\x80\x93231. Elsewhere, we have assessed\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cthe interstitial nature of the legal question, the re- lated expertise of the Agency, the importance of the question to administration of the statute, the complexity of that administration, and the careful consideration the Agency has given the question over a long period of time.\xe2\x80\x9d Barnhart v. Walton, \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 212, \n222 (2002)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe subject matter of the relevant provision\xe2\x80\x94for instance, its distance from the agency\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary statutory duties or its falling within the scope of another agency\xe2\x80\x99s authority\xe2\x80\x94has also proved relevant. See Gonzalez, supra, at 265\xe2\x80\x93266. See also Gellhorn & Verkuil, Controlling Chevron-Based Delegations, 20 Cardozo L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 989, 1007\xe2\x80\x931010 (1999).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text, its context, the structure of the statutory scheme, and canons of textual construction are relevant in determining whether the statute is ambiguous and can be equally helpful in determining whether such ambiguity comes accompanied with agency authority to fill a gap with an interpretation that carries the force of law. See Household Credit Services, Inc. v. Pfennig, \n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 232\n\xe2\x80\x93242 (2004); Zuni Public School Dist. No. 89 v. Department of Education, \n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 81\n\xe2\x80\x9399 (2007); FDA v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 120, \n133 (2000)\n; Dole v. Steelworkers, \n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 26, \n36 (1990)\n. Statutory purposes, including those revealed in part by legislative and regulatory history, can be similarly relevant. See Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., supra, at 143\xe2\x80\x93147; Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation v. LTV Corp., \n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 633, \n649 (1990)\n; Global Crossing Telecommunications, Inc. v. Metrophones Telecommunications, Inc., \n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45\n\xe2\x80\x9349 (2007). See also AT&T Corp. v. Iowa Utilities Bd., \n\n525 U.\xc2\xa0S. 366\n\xe2\x80\x93413 (1999) (Breyer, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough seemingly complex in abstract description, in practice this framework has proved a workable way to approximate how Congress would likely have meant to allocate interpretive law-determining authority between reviewing court and agency. The question whether Congress has delegated to an agency the authority to provide an interpretation that carries the force of law is for the judge to answer independently. The judge, considering \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional tools of statutory construction,\xe2\x80\x9d Cardoza-Fonseca, supra, at 446, will ask whether Congress has spoken unambiguously. If so, the text controls. If not, the judge will ask whether Congress would have intended the agency to resolve the resulting ambiguity. If so, deference is warranted. See Mead, supra, at 229. Even if not, however, sometimes an agency interpretation, in light of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s special expertise, will still have the \xe2\x80\x9cpower to persuade, if lacking power to control,\xe2\x80\x9d Skidmore v. Swift & Co., \n\n323 U.\xc2\xa0S. 134, \n140 (1944)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe case before us offers an example. The relevant statutory provision requires state or local governments to act on wireless siting applications \xe2\x80\x9cwithin a reasonable period of time after\xe2\x80\x9d a wireless service provider files such a request. \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii). The Federal Com- munications Commission (FCC) argued that this pro- vision granted it a degree of leeway in determining the amount of time that is reasonable. Many factors favor the agency\xe2\x80\x99s view: (1)\xc2\xa0the language of the Telecommunications Act grants the FCC broad authority (including rulemaking authority) to administer the Act; (2)\xc2\xa0the words are open-ended\xe2\x80\x94i.e. \xe2\x80\x9cambiguous\xe2\x80\x9d; (3)\xc2\xa0the provision concerns an interstitial administrative matter, in respect to which the agency\xe2\x80\x99s expertise could have an important role to play; and (4)\xc2\xa0the matter, in context, is complex, likely making the agency\xe2\x80\x99s expertise useful in helping to answer the \xe2\x80\x9crea- sonableness\xe2\x80\x9d question that the statute poses. See \xc2\xa7151 (creating the FCC); \xc2\xa7201(b) (providing rulemaking auth- ority); National Cable & Telecommunications Assn. v. Brand X Internet Services, \n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 967\n\xe2\x80\x93981 (2005) (acknowledging the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s authority to administer the Act).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn the other side of the coin, petitioners point to two statutory provisions which, they believe, require a different conclusion\xe2\x80\x94namely, that the FCC lacked authority altogether to interpret \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii). First, a nearby saving clause says: \xe2\x80\x9cExcept as provided in this paragraph, nothing in this chapter shall limit or affect the authority of a State or local government or instrumentality thereof over decisions regarding the placement, construction, and modification of personal wireless service facilities.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(A). Second, a judicial review provision, says: \xe2\x80\x9cAny person adversely affected by any final action or failure to act by a State or local government or any instrumentality thereof that is inconsistent with this subparagraph may, within 30 days after such action or failure to act, commence an action in any court of competent jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn my view, however, these two provisions cannot provide good reason for reaching the conclusion advocated by petitioners. The first provision begins with an exception, stating that it does not apply to (among other things) the \xe2\x80\x9creasonableness\xe2\x80\x9d provision here at issue. The second sim- ply sets forth a procedure for judicial review, a review that applies to most government actions. Both are consistent with a statutory scheme that gives States, localities, the FCC, and reviewing courts each some role to play in the location of wireless service facilities. And neither \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly describ[es] an exception\xe2\x80\x9d to the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s plenary authority to interpret the Act. American Hospital Assn. v. NLRB, \n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 606, \n613 (1991)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor these reasons, I would reject petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument and conclude that \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii)\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9creasonableness\xe2\x80\x9d statute\xe2\x80\x94leaves a gap for the FCC to fill. I would hold that the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s lawful efforts to do so carry \xe2\x80\x9cthe force of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Mead, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 229. The Court of Appeals ultimately reached the same conclusion (though for somewhat dif- ferent reasons), and the majority affirms the lower court. I consequently join the majority\xe2\x80\x99s judgment and such por- tions of its opinion as are consistent with what I have written here.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Kennedy and Justice Alito join, dissenting.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMy disagreement with the Court is fundamental. It is also easily expressed: A court should not defer to an agency until the court decides, on its own, that the agency is entitled to deference. Courts defer to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of law when and because Congress has conferred on the agency interpretive authority over the question at issue. An agency cannot exercise interpretive authority until it has it; the question whether an agency enjoys that authority must be decided by a court, without deference to the agency.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOne of the principal authors of the Constitution famously wrote that the \xe2\x80\x9caccumulation of all powers, legislative, executive, and judiciary, in the same hands, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may justly be pronounced the very definition of tyranny.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 47, p.\xc2\xa0324 (J. Cooke ed. 1961) (J. Madison). Although modern administrative agencies fit most comfortably within the Executive Branch, as a practical matter they exercise legislative power, by promulgating regulations with the force of law; executive power, by policing compliance with those regulations; and judicial power, by adjudicating enforcement actions and imposing sanctions on those found to have violated their rules. The accumulation of these powers in the same hands is not an occasional or isolated exception to the constitutional plan; it is a central feature of modern American government.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe administrative state \xe2\x80\x9cwields vast power and touches almost every aspect of daily life.\xe2\x80\x9d Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Bd., 561 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 18). The Framers could hardly have envisioned today\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cvast and varied federal bureaucracy\xe2\x80\x9d and the authority administrative agencies now hold over our economic, social, and political activities. Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he administrative state with its reams of regulations would leave them rubbing their eyes.\xe2\x80\x9d Alden v. Maine, \n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 706, \n807 (1999)\n (Souter, J., dissenting), quoted in Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. South Carolina Ports Authority, \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 743, \n755 (2002)\n. And the federal bureaucracy continues to grow; in the last 15 years, Congress has launched more than 50 new agencies. Compare Office of the Federal Register, United States Government Manual 1997/1998, with Office of the Federal Register, United States Government Manual 2012. And more are on the way. See, e.g., Congressional Research Service, C. Copeland, New Entities Created Pursuant to the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act 1 (2010) (The PPACA \xe2\x80\x9ccreates, requires others to create, or authorizes dozens of new entities to implement the legislation\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough the Constitution empowers the President to keep federal officers accountable, administrative agencies enjoy in practice a significant degree of independence. As scholars have noted, \xe2\x80\x9cno President (or his executive office staff) could, and presumably none would wish to, supervise so broad a swath of regulatory activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Kagan, Presidential Administration, 114 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 2245, 2250 (2001); see also S. Breyer, Making Our Democracy Work 110 (2010) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe president may not have the time or willingness to review [agency] decisions\xe2\x80\x9d). President Truman colorfully described his power over the administrative state by complaining, \xe2\x80\x9cI thought I was the president, but when it comes to these bureaucrats, I can\xe2\x80\x99t do a damn thing.\xe2\x80\x9d See R. Nathan, The Administrative Presidency 2 (1986). President Kennedy once told a constituent, \xe2\x80\x9cI agree with you, but I don\xe2\x80\x99t know if the government will.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 1. The collection of agencies housed outside the traditional executive departments, including the Federal Communications Commission, is routinely described as the \xe2\x80\x9cheadless fourth branch of government,\xe2\x80\x9d reflecting not only the scope of their authority but their practical independence. See, e.g., Administrative Conference of United States, D. Lewis & J. Selin, Sourcebook of United States Executive Agencies 11 (2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs for judicial oversight, agencies enjoy broad power to construe statutory provisions over which they have been given interpretive authority. In Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., we established a test for reviewing \xe2\x80\x9can agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the statute which it administers.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837, \n842 (1984)\n. If Congress has \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly spoken to the precise question at issue,\xe2\x80\x9d we said, \xe2\x80\x9cthat is the end of the matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. A contrary agency interpretation must give way. But if Congress has not expressed a specific intent, a court is bound to defer to any \xe2\x80\x9cpermissible construction of the statute,\xe2\x80\x9d even if that is not \xe2\x80\x9cthe reading the court would have reached if the question initially had arisen in a judicial proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 843, and n.\xc2\xa011.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen it applies, Chevron is a powerful weapon in an agency\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory arsenal. Congressional delegations to agencies are often ambiguous\xe2\x80\x94expressing \xe2\x80\x9ca mood rather than a message.\xe2\x80\x9d Friendly, The Federal Administrative Agencies: The Need for Better Definition of Standards, 75 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1263, 1311 (1962). By design or default, Congress often fails to speak to \xe2\x80\x9cthe precise question\xe2\x80\x9d before an agency. In the absence of such an answer, an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation has the full force and effect of law, unless it \xe2\x80\x9cexceeds the bounds of the permissible.\xe2\x80\x9d Barnhart v. Walton, \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 212, \n218 (2002)\n. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt would be a bit much to describe the result as \xe2\x80\x9cthe very definition of tyranny,\xe2\x80\x9d but the danger posed by the growing power of the administrative state cannot be dismissed. See, e.g., Talk America, Inc. v. Michigan Bell Telephone Co., 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (Scalia, J., concurring) (slip op., at 3) (noting that the FCC \xe2\x80\x9chas repeatedly been rebuked in its attempts to expand the statute beyond its text, and has repeatedly sought new means to the same ends\xe2\x80\x9d); Sackett v. EPA, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2012) (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310) (rejecting agency argument that would \xe2\x80\x9cenable the strong-arming of regulated parties into \xe2\x80\x98voluntary compliance\xe2\x80\x99 without the opportunity for judicial review\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhat the Court says in footnote 4 of its opinion is good, and true (except of course for the \xe2\x80\x9cdissent overstates\xe2\x80\x9d part). Ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314, n.\xc2\xa04. The Framers did divide governmental power in the manner the Court describes, for the purpose of safeguarding liberty. And yet .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the citizen confronting thousands of pages of regulations\xe2\x80\x94promulgated by an agency directed by Congress to regulate, say, \xe2\x80\x9cin the public interest\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94can perhaps be excused for thinking that it is the agency really doing the legislating. And with hundreds of federal agencies poking into every nook and cranny of daily life, that citizen might also understandably question whether Presidential oversight\xe2\x80\x94a critical part of the Constitutional plan\xe2\x80\x94is always an effective safeguard against agency overreaching. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is against this background that we consider whether the authority of administrative agencies should be augmented even further, to include not only broad power to give definitive answers to questions left to them by Congress, but also the same power to decide when Congress has given them that power.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBefore proceeding to answer that question, however, it is necessary to sort through some confusion over what this litigation is about. The source of the confusion is a familiar culprit: the concept of \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d which we have repeatedly described as a word with \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98many, too many, meanings.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Union Pacific R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Locomotive Engineers, \n\n558 U.\xc2\xa0S. 67, \n81 (2009)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court states that the question \xe2\x80\x9cis whether a court must defer under Chevron to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a statutory ambiguity that concerns the scope of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authority (that is, its jurisdiction).\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. That is fine\xe2\x80\x94until the parenthetical. The parties, amici, and court below too often use the term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d imprecisely, which leads the Court to misunderstand the argument it must confront. That argument is not that \xe2\x80\x9cthere exist two distinct classes of agency interpretations,\xe2\x80\x9d some \xe2\x80\x9cbig, important ones\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cdefine the agency\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and other \xe2\x80\x9chumdrum, run-of-the-mill\xe2\x80\x9d ones that \xe2\x80\x9care simply applications of jurisdiction the agency plainly has.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The argument is instead that a court should not defer to an agency on whether Congress has granted the agency interpretive authority over the statutory ambiguity at issue.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tYou can call that \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d if you\xe2\x80\x99d like, as petitioners do in the question presented. But given that the term is ambiguous, more is required to understand its use in that question than simply \xe2\x80\x9chaving read it.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15, n.\xc2\xa05. It is important to keep in mind that the term, in the present context, has the more precise meaning noted above, encompassing congressionally delegated authority to issue interpretations with the force and effect of law. See 668 F.\xc2\xa03d 229, 248 (CA5 2012) (case below) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe issue in the instant case is whether the FCC possessed statutory authority to administer \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii) and (v) by adopting the 90- and 150-day time frames\xe2\x80\x9d). And that has nothing do with whether the statutory provisions at issue are \xe2\x80\x9cbig\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9csmall.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cIt is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is.\xe2\x80\x9d Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 177 (1803). The rise of the modern administrative state has not changed that duty. Indeed, the Administrative Procedure Act, governing judicial review of most agency action, instructs reviewing courts to decide \xe2\x80\x9call relevant questions of law.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7706.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe do not ignore that command when we afford an agency\xe2\x80\x99s statutory interpretation Chevron deference; we respect it. We give binding deference to permissible agency interpretations of statutory ambiguities because Con- gress has delegated to the agency the authority to interpret those ambiguities \xe2\x80\x9cwith the force of law.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Mead Corp., \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218, \n229 (2001)\n; see also Monaghan, Marbury and the Administrative State, 83 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 27\xe2\x80\x9328 (1983) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe court is not abdicating its constitutional duty to \xe2\x80\x98say what the law is\xe2\x80\x99 by deferring to agency interpretations of law: it is simply applying the law as \xe2\x80\x98made\xe2\x80\x99 by the authorized law-making entity\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut before a court may grant such deference, it must on its own decide whether Congress\xe2\x80\x94the branch vested with lawmaking authority under the Constitution\xe2\x80\x94has in fact delegated to the agency lawmaking power over the ambiguity at issue. See ante, at 4 (Breyer, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe question whether Congress has delegated to an agency the authority to provide an interpretation that carries the force of law is for the judge to answer independently.\xe2\x80\x9d). Agencies are creatures of Congress; \xe2\x80\x9can agency literally has no power to act .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. unless and until Congress confers power upon it.\xe2\x80\x9d Louisiana Pub. Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. FCC, \n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 355, \n374 (1986)\n. Whether Congress has conferred such power is the \xe2\x80\x9crelevant question[ ] of law\xe2\x80\x9d that must be answered before affording Chevron deference. \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7706.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur precedents confirm this conclusion\xe2\x80\x94beginning with Chevron itself. In Chevron, the EPA promulgated a regulation interpreting the term \xe2\x80\x9cstationary sources\xe2\x80\x9d in the Clean Air Act. 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 840 (quoting \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77502(b)(6)(1982 ed.)). An environmental group petitioned for review of the rule, challenging it as an impermissible interpretation of the Act. 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 841, 859. Finding the statutory text \xe2\x80\x9cnot dispositive\xe2\x80\x9d and the legislative history \xe2\x80\x9csilent on the precise issue,\xe2\x80\x9d we upheld the rule. Id., at 862, 866.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn our view, the challenge to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation \xe2\x80\x9ccenter[ed] on the wisdom of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s policy, rather than whether it is a reasonable choice within a gap left open by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 866. Judges, we said, \xe2\x80\x9care not experts in the field, and are not part of either political branch of the Government.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 865. Thus, because Congress had not answered the specific question at issue, judges had no business providing their own resolution on the basis of their \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal policy preferences.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Instead, the \xe2\x80\x9cagency to which Congress ha[d] delegated policymaking responsibilities\xe2\x80\x9d was the appropriate political actor to resolve the competing interests at stake, \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the limits of that delegation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChevron\xe2\x80\x99s rule of deference was based on\xe2\x80\x94and limited by\xe2\x80\x94this congressional delegation. And the Court did not ask simply whether Congress had delegated to the EPA the authority to administer the Clean Air Act generally. We asked whether Congress had \xe2\x80\x9cdelegat[ed] authority to the agency to elucidate a specific provision of the statute by regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 843\xe2\x80\x93844 (emphasis added); see id., at 844 (discussing \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislative delegation to an agency on a particular question\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). We deferred to the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cstationary sources\xe2\x80\x9d based on our conclusion that the agency had been \xe2\x80\x9ccharged with responsibility for administering the provision.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 865 (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have never faltered in our understanding of this straightforward principle, that whether a particular agency interpretation warrants Chevron deference turns on the court\xe2\x80\x99s determination whether Congress has delegated to the agency the authority to interpret the statutory ambiguity at issue.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe made the point perhaps most clearly in Adams Fruit Co. v. Barrett, \n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 638 (1990)\n. In that case, the Department of Labor contended the Court should defer to its interpretation of the scope of the private right of action provided by the Migrant and Seasonal Agriculture Worker Protection Act (AWPA), \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71854, against employers who intentionally violated the Act\xe2\x80\x99s motor vehicle safety provisions. We refused to do so. Although \xe2\x80\x9cas an initial matter\xe2\x80\x9d we rejected the idea that Congress left a \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory \xe2\x80\x98gap\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for the agency to fill, we reasoned that if the \xe2\x80\x9cAWPA\xe2\x80\x99s language establishing a private right of action is ambiguous,\xe2\x80\x9d the Secretary of Labor\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its scope did not warrant Chevron deference. 494 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 649.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn language directly applicable to the question before us, we explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] precondition to deference under Chevron is a congressional delegation of administrative authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Although \xe2\x80\x9cCongress clearly envisioned, indeed expressly mandated, a role for the Department of Labor in administering the statute by requiring the Secretary to promulgate standards implementing AWPA\xe2\x80\x99s motor vehicle provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d we found \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o such delegation regarding AWPA\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement provisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 650 (emphasis added). It would therefore be \xe2\x80\x9cinappropriate,\xe2\x80\x9d we said, \xe2\x80\x9cto consult executive interpretations\xe2\x80\x9d of the enforcement provisions to resolve ambiguities \xe2\x80\x9csurrounding the scope of AWPA\xe2\x80\x99s judicially enforceable remedy.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Without questioning the principle that agency determinations \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the scope of delegated authority are entitled to deference,\xe2\x80\x9d we explained that \xe2\x80\x9cit is fundamental \xe2\x80\x98that an agency may not bootstrap itself into an area in which it has no jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Seatrain Lines, Inc., \n\n411 U.\xc2\xa0S. 726, \n745 (1973)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur subsequent cases follow the same approach. In United States v. Mead Corp., supra, for example, Chevron deference turned on whether Congress had delegated to the agency authority to interpret the statutory ambiguity by a particular means. The Customs Service had issued a \xe2\x80\x9cclassification ruling,\xe2\x80\x9d interpreting the term \xe2\x80\x9cdiaries\xe2\x80\x9d in a tariff schedule to include \xe2\x80\x9cday planners\xe2\x80\x9d of the type Mead imported, and on that basis subjected the planners to a four-percent tariff. Mead protested the imposition of the tariff, the Customs Service claimed Chevron deference for its interpretation, and the controversy made its way to our Court. Id., at 224\xe2\x80\x93226.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Mead, we again made clear that the \xe2\x80\x9ccategory of interpretative choices\xe2\x80\x9d to which Chevron deference applies is defined by congressional intent. Id., at 229. Chevron deference, we said, rests on a recognition that Congress has delegated to an agency the interpretive authority to implement \xe2\x80\x9ca particular provision\xe2\x80\x9d or answer \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a particular question.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Chevron, 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 844). An agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9ca particular statutory provision\xe2\x80\x9d thus qualifies for Chevron deference only \xe2\x80\x9cwhen it appears that Congress delegated authority to the agency generally to make rules carrying the force of law, and that the agency interpretation claiming deference was pro-mulgated in the exercise of that authority.\xe2\x80\x9d 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 226\xe2\x80\x93227.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court did not defer to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s views but instead determined that Congress had not delegated interpretive authority to the Customs Service to definitively construe the tariff schedule through classification rulings. Neither the statutory authorization for the classification rulings, nor the Customs Service\xe2\x80\x99s practice in issuing such rulings, \xe2\x80\x9creasonably suggest[ed] that Congress ever thought of [such] classification rulings as deserving the deference claimed for them.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 231. And in the absence of such a delegation, we concluded the interpretations adopted in those rulings were \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond the Chevron pale.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 234.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGonzales v. Oregon, \n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 243 (2006)\n, is in the same line of precedent. In that case, as here, deference turned on whether a congressional delegation of interpretive authority reached a particular statutory ambiguity. The Attorney General claimed Chevron deference for his interpretation of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate medical purpose\xe2\x80\x9d in the Controlled Substances Act (CSA) to exclude the prescribing and dispensing of controlled substances for the purpose of assisting suicide. Id., at 254, 258. No one disputed that \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate medical purpose\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cambiguous in the relevant sense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 258. Nor did any Justice dispute that the Attorney General had been granted the power in the CSA to promulgate rules with the force of law. Ibid.; see id., at 281 (Scalia, J., dissenting). Nevertheless, the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cChevron deference .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is not accorded merely because the statute is ambiguous and an administrative official is involved.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 258. The regulation advancing the interpretation, we said, \xe2\x80\x9cmust be promulgated pursuant to authority Congress has delegated to the official.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (citing Mead, supra, at 226\xe2\x80\x93227).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the CSA, Congress delegated to the Attorney General the authority to promulgate regulations \xe2\x80\x9crelating to the registration and control of the manufacture, distribution, and dispensing of controlled substances,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7821, or \xe2\x80\x9cfor the efficient execution of his functions under [the CSA],\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7871(b). After considering the text, structure, and purpose of the Act, the Court concluded on its own that interpreting \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate medical purpose\xe2\x80\x9d fell under neither delegation. Gonzales, 546 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 258\xe2\x80\x93269. Because the regulation \xe2\x80\x9cwas not promulgated pursuant to the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s authority, its interpretation of \xe2\x80\x98legitimate medical purpose\xe2\x80\x99 d[id] not receive Chevron deference.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 268.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAdams Fruit, Mead, and Gonzales thus confirm that Chevron deference is based on, and finds legitimacy as, a congressional delegation of interpretive authority. An agency interpretation warrants such deference only if Congress has delegated authority to definitively interpret a particular ambiguity in a particular manner. Whether Congress has done so must be determined by the court on its own before Chevron can apply. See H. Edwards, L. Elliot, & M. Levy, Federal Courts Standards of Review 168 (2d ed. 2013) (\xe2\x80\x9ca court decides de\xc2\xa0novo whether an agency has acted within the bounds of congressionally delegated authority\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Mead, supra, at 226\xe2\x80\x93227, and Gonzales, supra, at 258)); Sales & Adler, The Rest is Silence: Chevron Deference, Agency Jurisdiction, and Statutory Silences, 2009 U. Ill. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1497, 1564 (2009) (\xe2\x80\x9cif delegation really is antecedent to deference, as Mead insists, it cannot be that courts should defer to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s views on whether a delegation has taken place\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn other words, we do not defer to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of an ambiguous provision unless Congress wants us to, and whether Congress wants us to is a question that courts, not agencies, must decide. Simply put, that question is \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond the Chevron pale.\xe2\x80\x9d Mead, supra, at 234.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDespite these precedents, the FCC argues that a court need only locate an agency and a grant of general rulemaking authority over a statute. Chevron deference then applies, it contends, to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of any ambiguity in the Act, including ambiguity in a provision said to carve out specific provisions from the agency\xe2\x80\x99s general rulemaking authority. If Congress intends to exempt part of the statute from the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretive authority, the FCC says, Congress \xe2\x80\x9ccan ordinarily be expected to state that intent explicitly.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Federal Respondents 30 (citing American Hospital Assn. v. NLRB, \n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 606 (1991)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf a congressional delegation of interpretive authority is to support Chevron deference, however, that delegation must extend to the specific statutory ambiguity at issue. The appropriate question is whether the delegation covers the \xe2\x80\x9cspecific provision\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cparticular question\xe2\x80\x9d before the court. Chevron, 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 844. A congressional grant of authority over some portion of a statute does not necessarily mean that Congress granted the agency interpretive authority over all its provisions. See Adams Fruit, 494 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 650.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAn example that might highlight the point concerns statutes that parcel out authority to multiple agencies, which \xe2\x80\x9cmay be the norm, rather than an exception.\xe2\x80\x9d Gersen, Overlapping and Underlapping Jurisdiction in Administrative Law, 2006 S. Ct. Rev. 201, 208; see, e.g., Gonzales, 546 U.\xc2\xa0S, at 250\xe2\x80\x93251 (describing shared author-ity over the CSA between the Attorney General and the Secretary of Health and Human Services); Sutton v. United Air Lines, Inc., \n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 471, \n478 (1999)\n (authority to issue regulations implementing the Americans with Disabilities Act \xe2\x80\x9cis split primarily among three Government agencies\xe2\x80\x9d). The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act, for example, authorizes rulemaking by at least eight different agencies. See Con-gressional Research Service, C. Copeland, Rulemaking Requirements and Authorities in the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act 7 (2010). When presented with an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of such a statute, a court cannot simply ask whether the statute is one that the agency administers; the question is whether authority over the particular ambiguity at issue has been delegated to the particular agency.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBy the same logic, even when Congress provides interpretive authority to a single agency, a court must decide if the ambiguity the agency has purported to interpret with the force of law is one to which the congressional delegation extends. A general delegation to the agency to administer the statute will often suffice to satisfy the court that Congress has delegated interpretive authority over the ambiguity at issue. But if Congress has exempted particular provisions from that authority, that exemption must be respected, and the determination whether Congress has done so is for the courts alone.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe FCC\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Congress \xe2\x80\x9ccan ordinarily be expected to state that intent explicitly,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Federal Respondents 30 (citing American Hospital, supra), goes to the merits of that determination, not to whether a court should decide the question de\xc2\xa0novo or defer to the agency. Indeed, that is how the Court in American Hospital considered it. It was in the process of \xe2\x80\x9cemploying the traditional tools of statutory construction\xe2\x80\x9d that the Court said it would have expected Congress to speak more clearly if it had intended to exclude an entire subject area\xe2\x80\x94employee units for collecting bargaining\xe2\x80\x94from the NLRB\xe2\x80\x99s general rulemaking authority. Id., at 613, 614. The Court concluded, after considering the language, structure, policy, and legislative history of the Act on its own\xe2\x80\x94without deferring to the agency\xe2\x80\x94that the meaning of the statute was \xe2\x80\x9cclear and contrary to the meaning advanced by petitioner.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 609\xe2\x80\x93614. To be sure, the Court also noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ven if we could find any ambiguity in [the provision] after employing the traditional tools of statutory construction, we would still defer to Board\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 614 (emphasis added). But that single sentence of dictum cannot carry the day for the FCC here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs the preceding analysis makes clear, I do not understand petitioners to ask the Court\xe2\x80\x94nor do I think it necessary\xe2\x80\x94to draw a \xe2\x80\x9cspecious, but scary-sounding\xe2\x80\x9d line between \xe2\x80\x9cbig, important\xe2\x80\x9d interpretations on the one hand and \xe2\x80\x9chumdrum, run-of-the-mill\xe2\x80\x9d ones on the other. Ante, at 5, 12. Drawing such a line may well be difficult. Distinguishing between whether an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of an ambiguous term is reasonable and whether that term is for the agency to interpret is not nearly so difficult. It certainly did not confuse the FCC in this proceeding. Compare In\xc2\xa0re Petition for Declaratory Ruling, 24 FCC Rcd. 13994, 14000\xe2\x80\x9314003 (2009) (addressing the latter question), with id., at 14003\xe2\x80\x9314015 (addressing the former). Nor did it confound the Fifth Circuit. Compare 668 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 247\xe2\x80\x93254 (deciding \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the FCC possessed statutory authority to administer \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii)\xe2\x80\x9d), with id., at 254\xe2\x80\x93260 (considering \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the 90- and 150-day time frames themselves also pass muster under Chevron\xe2\x80\x9d). More importantly, if the legitimacy of Chevron deference is based on a congressional delegation of interpretive authority, then the line is one the Court must draw.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority\xe2\x80\x99s hypothetical Common Carrier Acts do not demonstrate anything different. Ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x938. The major-ity states that in its second Common Carrier Act, Section 2 makes clear that Congress \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98conferred interpretative power on the agency\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to interpret the ambiguous terms \xe2\x80\x9ccommon carrier\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable condition.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7 (quoting Brief for Petitioners in No. 1545, p.\xc2\xa014). Thus, it says, under anyone\xe2\x80\x99s theory a court must defer to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretations of those terms. Correct.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority claims, however, that \xe2\x80\x9cpetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 theory would accord the agency no deference\xe2\x80\x9d in its interpretation of the same ambiguous terms in the first Common Carrier Act. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. But as I understand petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument\xe2\x80\x94and certainly in my own view\xe2\x80\x94a court, in both cases, need only decide for itself whether Congress has delegated to the agency authority to interpret the ambiguous terms, before affording the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation Chevron deference.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor the second Common Carrier Act, the answer is easy. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s hypothetical Congress has spoken clearly and specifically in Section 2 of the Act about its delegation of authority to interpret Section 1. As for the first Act, it is harder to analyze the question, given only one section of a presumably much larger statute. But if the first Common Carrier Act is like most agencies\xe2\x80\x99 organic statutes, I have no reason to doubt that the agency would likewise have interpretive authority over the same ambiguous terms, and therefore be entitled to deference in con-struing them, just as with the second Common Carrier Act. There is no new \xe2\x80\x9ctest\xe2\x80\x9d to worry about, cf. ante, at 16; courts would simply apply the normal rules of statutory construction. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat the question might be harder with respect to the first Common Carrier Act should come as no surprise. The second hypothetical Congress has more carefully defined the agency\xe2\x80\x99s authority than the first. Whatever standard of review applies, it is more difficult to interpret an unclear statute than a clear one. My point is simply that before a court can defer to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the ambiguous terms in either Act, it must determine for itself that Congress has delegated authority to the agency to issue those interpretations with the force of law.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority also expresses concern that adopting petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position would undermine Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s stable background rule against which Congress legislates. Ante, at 5. That, of course, begs the question of what that stable background rule is. See Merrill & Hickman, Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s Domain, 89 Geo. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 833, 910 (2001) (\xe2\x80\x9cCourts have never deferred to agencies with respect to questions such as whether Congress has delegated to an agency the power to act with the force of law through either legislative rules or binding adjudications. Similarly, it has never been maintained that Congress would want courts to give Chevron deference to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s determination that it is entitled to Chevron deference, or should give Chevron deference to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s determination of what types of interpretations are entitled to Chevron deference\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tVI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court sees something nefarious behind the view that courts must decide on their own whether Congress has delegated interpretative authority to an agency, before deferring to that agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of law. What is afoot, according to the Court, is a judicial power-grab, with nothing less than \xe2\x80\x9cChevron itself\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe ultimate target.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court touches on a legitimate concern: Chevron importantly guards against the Judiciary arrogating to itself policymaking properly left, under the separation of powers, to the Executive. But there is another concern at play, no less firmly rooted in our constitutional structure. That is the obligation of the Judiciary not only to confine itself to its proper role, but to ensure that the other branches do so as well.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAn agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretive authority, entitling the agency to judicial deference, acquires its legitimacy from a delegation of lawmaking power from Congress to the Executive. Our duty to police the boundary between the Legislature and the Executive is as critical as our duty to respect that between the Judiciary and the Executive. See Zivotofsky v. Clinton, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 8). In the present context, that means ensuring that the Legislative Branch has in fact delegated lawmaking power to an agency within the Executive Branch, before the Judiciary defers to the Executive on what the law is. That concern is heightened, not diminished, by the fact that the administrative agencies, as a practical matter, draw upon a potent brew of executive, legislative, and judicial power. And it is heightened, not diminished, by the dramatic shift in power over the last 50 years from Congress to the Executive\xe2\x80\x94a shift effected through the administrative agencies.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe reconcile our competing responsibilities in this area by ensuring judicial deference to agency interpretations under Chevron\xe2\x80\x94but only after we have determined on our own that Congress has given interpretive authority to the agency. Our \xe2\x80\x9ctask is to fix the boundaries of delegated authority,\xe2\x80\x9d Monaghan, 83 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 27; that is not a task we can delegate to the agency. We do not leave it to the agency to decide when it is in charge.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn these cases, the FCC issued a declaratory ruling interpreting the term \xe2\x80\x9creasonable period of time\xe2\x80\x9d in \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii). The Fifth Circuit correctly recognized that it could not apply Chevron deference to the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation unless the agency \xe2\x80\x9cpossessed statutory authority to administer \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii),\xe2\x80\x9d but it erred by granting Chevron deference to the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s view on that antecedent question. See 668 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 248. Because the court should have determined on its own whether Congress delegated interpretive authority over \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii) to the FCC before affording Chevron deference, I would vacate the decision below and remand the cases to the Fifth Circuit to perform the proper inquiry in the first instance.\n\t\t\t
