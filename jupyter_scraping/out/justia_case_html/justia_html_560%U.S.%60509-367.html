DOLAN v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x93367.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 20, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 14, 2010

\n

Petitioner Dolan pleaded guilty to assault resulting in serious bodily injury and entered into a plea agreement, which stated that the District Court could order restitution for his victim. Dolan\xe2\x80\x99s presentence report also noted that restitution was required, but did not recommend an amount because of a lack of information on hospital costs and lost wages. The Mandatory Victims Restitution Act provides that \xe2\x80\x9cif the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses are not ascertainable by the date that is 10 days prior to sentencing,\xe2\x80\x9d the court \xe2\x80\x9cshall set a date for the final determination of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses, not to exceed 90 days after sentencing.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73664(d)(5). On July 30, the District Court held a sentencing hearing and imposed a sentence of imprisonment and supervised release. On August 8, the court entered a judgment, stating that restitution was \xe2\x80\x9capplicable\xe2\x80\x9d but leaving open the amount of restitution given that no information had yet \xe2\x80\x9cbeen received regarding possible restitution payments.\xe2\x80\x9d On October 5, 67 days later, an addendum documenting the restitution amount was added to the presentence report. The court did not set a hearing until February 4, about three months after the 90-day deadline had expired. At the hearing, Dolan argued that because that deadline had passed, the law no longer authorized restitution. Disagreeing, the court ordered restitution, and the Tenth Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0 A sentencing court that misses the 90-day deadline nonetheless retains the power to order restitution\xe2\x80\x94at least where, as here, that court made clear prior to the deadline\xe2\x80\x99s expiration that it would order restitution, leaving open (for more than 90 days) only the amount. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0To determine the consequences of a missed deadline where, as here, the statute does not specify them, this Court looks to the statutory language, to the relevant context, and to what they reveal about the deadline\xe2\x80\x99s purposes. A \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d deadline\xe2\x80\x99s expiration prevents a court from permitting or taking the action to which the statute attached the deadline. The prohibition is absolute. It cannot be waived or extended for equitable reasons. See John R. Sand & Gravel Co. v. United States, 552 U. S. 130, 133\xe2\x80\x93134. Other deadlines are \xe2\x80\x9cclaims-processing rules,\xe2\x80\x9d which do not limit a court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction, but regulate the timing of motions or claims brought before the court. Unless a party points out that another litigant has missed such a deadline, the party forfeits the deadline\xe2\x80\x99s protection. See, e.g., Kontrick v. Ryan, 540 U. S. 443, 454\xe2\x80\x93456. In other instances, a deadline seeks speed by creating a time-related directive that is legally enforceable but does not deprive the judge or other public official of the power to take the action even if the deadline is missed. See, e.g., United States v. Montalvo-Murillo, 495 U. S. 711, 722. In light of its language, context, and purposes, the statute at issue sets forth this third kind of limitation. The fact that a sentencing court misses the 90-day deadline, even through its own or the Government\xe2\x80\x99s fault, does not deprive the court of the power to order restitution. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Several considerations lead to this conclusion. First, where, as here, a statute \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not specify a consequence for noncompliance with\xe2\x80\x9d its \xe2\x80\x9ctiming provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfederal courts will not\xe2\x80\x9d ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9cimpose their own coercive sanction.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. James Daniel Good Real Property, 510 U. S. 43, 63. A statute\xe2\x80\x99s use of \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d alone, see \xc2\xa73664(d)(5), does not necessarily bar judges from taking the action to which the missed deadline refers. Second, the statute places primary weight on, and emphasizes the importance of, imposing restitution upon those convicted of certain federal crimes. See \xc2\xa73663A. Third, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s procedural provisions reinforce this substantive purpose. They reveal that the statute seeks speed primarily to help crime victims secure prompt restitution, not to provide defendants with certainty as to the amount of their liability. Fourth, to read the statute as depriving the sentencing court of the power to order restitution would harm the victims, who likely bear no responsibility for the deadline\xe2\x80\x99s being missed and whom the statute seeks to benefit. That kind of harm to third parties provides a strong indication that Congress did not intend a missed deadline to work a forfeiture. See Brock v. Pierce County, 476 U. S. 253, 262. Fifth, the Court has interpreted similar statutes, such as the Bail Reform Act of 1984, similarly. See Montalvo-Murillo, supra, at 721. Sixth, the defendant normally can mitigate potential harm by telling the court that he fears the deadline will be, or just has been, missed, and the court will likely set a timely hearing or take other statutorily required action. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0This Court has not understated the potential harm to a defendant of a missed deadline. Petitioner claims that because the sentence will not be a \xe2\x80\x9cfinal judgment\xe2\x80\x9d for appeal purposes without a definitive determination of the restitution amount, to delay that determination beyond the deadline is to delay his ability to appeal. But a defendant who knows that restitution will be ordered and is aware of the amount can usually avoid additional delay by asking for a timely hearing; if the court refuses, he could seek mandamus. And in the unlikely instance that delay causes the defendant prejudice, he remains free to ask the appellate court to take that fact and any other equitable considerations into account on review. This does not mean that the Court accepts petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s premise that a sentencing judgment is not \xe2\x80\x9cfinal\xe2\x80\x9d until the restitution amount is determined. Although that question need not be decided here, strong arguments favor the appealability of the initial judgment irrespective of the delay in determining the restitution amount. A judgment imposing \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98discipline\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d may still be \xe2\x80\x9cfreighted with sufficiently substantial indicia of finality to support an appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d Corey v. United States, 375 U. S. 169, 174. And several statutes say that a \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment of conviction\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cincludes\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cimprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d is a \xe2\x80\x9cfinal judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d E.g., 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(b). Moreover, \xc2\xa73664(o) provides that a \xe2\x80\x9csentence that imposes an order of restitution,\xe2\x80\x9d such as the later restitution order here, \xe2\x80\x9cis a final judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Even assuming that the rule of lenity could be applied to a statutory time provision in the criminal context, here there is no statutory ambiguity sufficiently grievous to warrant its application in this case. Muscarello v. United States, 524 U. S. 125, 139. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

571 F.\xc2\xa03d 1022, affirmed.

BRIAN RUSSELL DOLAN, PETITIONER v.\nUNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

[June 14, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns the remedy for missing a statutory deadline. The statute in question focuses upon mandatory restitution for victims of crimes. It provides that \xe2\x80\x9cthe court shall set a date for the final determination of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses, not to exceed 90 days after sentencing.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73664(d)(5). We hold that a sentencing court that misses the 90-day deadline nonetheless retains the power to order restitution\xe2\x80\x94at least where, as here, the sentencing court made clear prior to the deadline\xe2\x80\x99s expiration that it would order restitution, leaving open (for more than 90 days) only the amount.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On February 8, 2007, petitioner Brian Dolan pleaded guilty to a federal charge of assault resulting in serious bodily injury. 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7113(a)(6), 1153; App. 17. He entered into a plea agreement that stated that \xe2\x80\x9crestitution \xe2\x80\xa6 may be ordered by the Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 18. The presentence report, provided to the court by the end of May, noted that restitution was required. But, lacking precise information about hospital costs and lost wages, it did not recommend a restitution amount. Id., at 27.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On July 30, the District Court held Dolan\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing hearing. The judge sentenced Dolan to 21 months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment along with 3 years of supervised release. Id., at 38. The judge, aware that restitution was \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory,\xe2\x80\x9d said that there was \xe2\x80\x9cinsufficient information on the record at this time regarding possible restitution payments that may be owed,\xe2\x80\x9d that he would \xe2\x80\x9cleave that matter open, pending the receipt of additional information,\xe2\x80\x9d and that Dolan could \xe2\x80\x9canticipate that such an award will be made in the future.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 39\xe2\x80\x9340. A few days later (August 8) the court entered a judgment, which, among other things, stated:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cPursuant to the Mandatory Restitution Act, restitution is applicable; however, no information has been received regarding possible restitution payments that may be owed. Therefore, the Court will not order restitution at this time.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 49 (boldface deleted).

\n

\nThe probation office later prepared an addendum to the presentence report, dated October 5, which reflected the views of the parties, and which the judge later indicated he had received. Id., at 54. The addendum documents the \xe2\x80\x9ctotal amount of restitution\xe2\x80\x9d due in the case (about $105,000). Id., at 52. Its date, October 5, is 67 days after Dolan\xe2\x80\x99s July 30 sentencing and 23 days before the statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c90 days after sentencing\xe2\x80\x9d deadline would expire. \xc2\xa73664(d)(5).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The sentencing court nonetheless set a restitution hearing for February 4, 2008\xe2\x80\x94about three months after the 90-day deadline expired. As far as the record shows, no one asked the court for an earlier hearing. At the hearing, Dolan pointed out that the 90-day deadline had passed. Id., at 54\xe2\x80\x9355. And he argued that the law no longer authorized the court to order restitution. Id., at 60\xe2\x80\x9364.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court disagreed and ordered restitution. See Memorandum Opinion and Restitution Order in No. CR 06\xe2\x80\x9302173\xe2\x80\x93RB (D NM, Apr. 24, 2008), App. to Pet. for Cert. 47a. The Court of Appeals affirmed. 571 F.\xc2\xa03d 1022 (CA10 2009). And, in light of differences among the Courts of Appeals, we granted Dolan\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari on the question. Compare United States v. Cheal, 389 F.\xc2\xa03d 35 (CA1 2004) (recognizing court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to enter restitution order past 90 days) and United States v. Balentine, 569 F.\xc2\xa03d 801 (CA8 2009) (same), with United States v. Maung, 267 F.\xc2\xa03d 1113 (CA11 2001) (finding no such authority), and United States v. Farr, 419 F.\xc2\xa03d 621 (CA7 2005) (same).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no doubt in this case that the court missed the 90-day statutory deadline \xe2\x80\x9cfor the final determination of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(d)(5). No one has offered any excuse for the court\xe2\x80\x99s doing so. Nor did any party seek an extension or \xe2\x80\x9ctolling\xe2\x80\x9d of the 90 days for equitable or for other reasons. All the information needed to determine the requisite restitution amount was available before the 90-day period had ended. Thus, the question before us concerns the consequences of the missed deadline where, as here, the statute does not specify them.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In answering this kind of question, this Court has looked to statutory language, to the relevant context, and to what they reveal about the purposes that a time limit is designed to serve. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s answers have varied depending upon the particular statute and time limit at issue. Sometimes we have found that the statute in question imposes a \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d condition upon, for example, a court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to hear a case, to consider pleadings, or to act upon motions that a party seeks to file. See, e.g., Bowles v. Russell, 551 U. S. 205 (2007). But cf. Kontrick v. Ryan, 540 U. S. 443, 455 (2004) (finding bankruptcy rule did not show legislative intent to \xe2\x80\x9cdelineat[e] the classes of cases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpersons\xe2\x80\x9d properly \xe2\x80\x9cwithin a court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudicatory authority\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Reed Elsevier, Inc. v. Muchnick, 559 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 5\xe2\x80\x936) (discussing use of term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d). The expiration of a \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d deadline prevents the court from permitting or taking the action to which the statute attached the deadline. The prohibition is absolute. The parties cannot waive it, nor can a court extend that deadline for equitable reasons. See John R. Sand & Gravel Co. v. United States, 552 U. S. 130, 133\xe2\x80\x93134 (2008).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In other instances, we have found that certain deadlines are more ordinary \xe2\x80\x9cclaims-processing rules,\xe2\x80\x9d rules that do not limit a court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction, but rather regulate the timing of motions or claims brought before the court. Unless a party points out to the court that another litigant has missed such a deadline, the party forfeits the deadline\xe2\x80\x99s protection. See, e.g., Kontrick v. Ryan, supra, at 454\xe2\x80\x93456 (60-day bankruptcy rule deadline for creditor\xe2\x80\x99s objection to debtor discharge); Eberhart v. United States, 546 U. S. 12, 19 (2005) (per curiam) (7-day criminal rule deadline for filing motion for a new trial).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In still other instances, we have found that a deadline seeks speed by creating a time-related directive that is legally enforceable but does not deprive a judge or other public official of the power to take the action to which the deadline applies if the deadline is missed. See, e.g., United States v. Montalvo-Murillo, 495 U. S. 711, 722 (1990) (missed deadline for holding bail detention hearing does not require judge to release defendant); Brock v. Pierce County, 476 U. S. 253, 266 (1986) (missed deadline for making final determination as to misuse of federal grant funds does not prevent later recovery of funds); Barnhart v. Peabody Coal Co., 537 U. S. 149, 171\xe2\x80\x93172 (2003) (missed deadline for assigning industry retiree benefits does not prevent later award of benefits).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After examining the language, the context, and the purposes of the statute, we conclude that the provision before us sets forth this third kind of limitation. The\nfact that a sentencing court misses the statute\xe2\x80\x99s 90-day deadline, even through its own fault or that of the Government, does not deprive the court of the power to order restitution.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Several considerations lead us to this conclusion. First, where, as here, a statute \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not specify a consequence for noncompliance with\xe2\x80\x9d its \xe2\x80\x9ctiming provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfederal courts will not in the ordinary course impose their own coercive sanction.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. James Daniel Good Real Property, 510 U. S. 43, 63 (1993); see also Montalvo-Murillo, supra, at 717\xe2\x80\x93721. Cf., e.g., Speedy Trial Act, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73161(c)(1); \xc2\xa73162(a)(2) (statute specifying that missed 70-day deadline requires dismissal of indictment); Zedner v. United States, 547 U. S. 489, 507\xe2\x80\x93509 (2006) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe sanction for a violation of the Act is dismissal\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We concede that the statute here uses the word \xe2\x80\x9cshall,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(d)(5), but a statute\xe2\x80\x99s use of that word alone has not always led this Court to interpret statutes to bar judges (or other officials) from taking the action to which a missed statutory deadline refers. See, e.g., Montalvo-Murillo, supra, at 718\xe2\x80\x93719 (use of word \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d in context of bail hearing makes duty \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory\xe2\x80\x9d but does not mean that the \xe2\x80\x9csanction for breach\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9closs of all later powers to act\xe2\x80\x9d); Brock, supra, at 262 (same in context of misuse of federal funds); Barnhart, supra, at 158\xe2\x80\x93163 (same in context of benefits assignments). See also Regions Hospital v. Shalala, 522 U. S. 448, 459, n.\xc2\xa03 (1998) (same in respect to federal official\xe2\x80\x99s reporting date).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text places primary weight upon, and emphasizes the importance of, imposing restitution upon those convicted of certain federal crimes. Amending an older provision that left restitution to the sentencing judge\xe2\x80\x99s discretion, the statute before us (entitled \xe2\x80\x9cThe Mandatory Victims Restitution Act of 1996\xe2\x80\x9d) says \xe2\x80\x9c[n]otwithstanding any other provision of law, when sentencing a defendant convicted of [a specified] offense \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, the court shall order \xe2\x80\xa6 that the defendant make restitution to the victim of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73663A(a)(1) (emphasis added); cf. \xc2\xa73663(a)(1) (stating that a court \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d order restitution when sentencing defendants convicted of other specified crimes). The Act goes on to provide that restitution shall be ordered in the \xe2\x80\x9cfull amount of each victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwithout consideration of the economic circumstances of the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(f)(1)(A).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, the Act\xe2\x80\x99s procedural provisions reinforce this substantive purpose, namely, that the statute seeks primarily to assure that victims of a crime receive full restitution. To be sure speed is important. The statute requires a sentencing judge to order the probation office to prepare a report providing \xe2\x80\x9ca complete accounting of the losses to each victim, any restitution owed pursuant to a plea agreement, and information relating to the economic circumstances of each defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(a). The prosecutor, after consulting with all identified victims, must \xe2\x80\x9cpromptly provide\xe2\x80\x9d a listing of the amount subject to restitution \xe2\x80\x9cnot later than 60 days prior to the date initially set for sentencing.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(d)(1) (emphasis added). And the provision before us says:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses are not ascertainable by the date that is 10 days prior to sentencing, the attorney for the Government or the probation officer shall so inform the court, and the court shall set a date for the final determination of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses, not to exceed 90 days after sentencing.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(d)(5).

\n

\nBut the statute seeks speed primarily to help the victims of crime and only secondarily to help the defendant. Thus, in the sentence following the language we have just quoted, the statute continues:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf the victim subsequently discovers further losses, the victim shall have 60 days after discovery of those losses in which to petition the court for an amended restitution order.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\nThe sentence imposes no time limit on the victim\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent discovery of losses. Consequently, a court might award restitution for those losses long after the original sentence was imposed and the 90-day time limit has expired. That fact, along with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s main substantive objectives, is why we say that the Act\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to secure speedy determination of restitution is primarily designed to help victims of crime secure prompt restitution rather than to provide defendants with certainty as to the amount of their liability. Cf. S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x93179, p.\xc2\xa020 (1995) (recognizing \xe2\x80\x9cthe need for finality and certainty in the sentencing process,\xe2\x80\x9d but also stating that the \xe2\x80\x9csole due process interest of the defendant being protected \xe2\x80\xa6 is the right not to be sentenced on the basis of invalid premises or inaccurate information\xe2\x80\x9d); see also ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9c[J]ustice cannot be considered served until full restitution is made\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fourth, to read the statute as depriving the sentencing court of the power to order restitution would harm those\xe2\x80\x94the victims of crime\xe2\x80\x94who likely bear no responsibility for the deadline\xe2\x80\x99s being missed and whom the statute also seeks to benefit. Cf. \xc2\xa73664(g)(1) (\xe2\x80\x9cNo victim shall be required to participate in any phase of a restitution order\xe2\x80\x9d). The potential for such harm\xe2\x80\x94to third parties\xe2\x80\x94normally provides a strong indication that Congress did not intend a missed deadline to work a forfeiture, here depriving a court of the power to award restitution to victims. See Brock, 476 U. S., at 262 (parties concede and court assumes that official can \xe2\x80\x9cproceed after the deadline\xe2\x80\x9d where \xe2\x80\x9cinaction\xe2\x80\x9d would hurt third party); see also 3 N. Singer & J. Singer, Sutherland on Statutory Construction \xc2\xa757:19, pp.\xc2\xa073\xe2\x80\x9374 (7th ed. 2008) (hereinafter Singer, Statutory Construction) (missing a deadline does not remove power to exercise a duty where there is no \xe2\x80\x9clanguage denying performance after a specified time,\xe2\x80\x9d and especially \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a mandatory construction might do great injury to persons not at fault, as in a case where slight delay on the part of a public officer might prejudice private rights or the public interest\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fifth, we have previously interpreted similar statutes similarly. In Montalvo-Murillo, 495 U. S. 711, for example, we considered the Bail Reform Act of 1984, which states that a \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial officer shall hold a hearing\xe2\x80\x9d to determine whether to grant bail to an arrested person and that \xe2\x80\x9chearing shall be held immediately upon the person\xe2\x80\x99s first appearance before the judicial officer.\xe2\x80\x9d (A continuance of up to five days may also be granted.) 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73142(f) (emphasis added). The judicial officer missed this deadline, but the Court held that the judicial officer need not release the detained person. Rather, \xe2\x80\x9conce the Government discovers that the time limits have expired, it may [still] ask for a prompt detention hearing and make its case to detain based upon the requirements set forth in the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d 495 U. S., at 721.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9ca failure to comply\xe2\x80\x9d with the hearing deadline \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not so subvert the procedural scheme \xe2\x80\xa6 as to invalidate the hearing.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 717. Missing the deadline did not diminish the strength of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in preventing release to avert the likely commission of crimes\xe2\x80\x94the very objective of the Act. Id., at 720. Nor would mandatory release of the detained person \xe2\x80\x9cproportion[ately]\xe2\x80\x9d repair the \xe2\x80\x9cinconvenience and uncertainty a timely hearing would have spared him.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 721.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, as in Montalvo-Murillo, neither the language nor the structure of the statute requires denying the victim restitution in order to remedy a missed hearing deadline. As in Montalvo-Murillo, doing so would defeat the basic purpose of the Mandatory Victims Restitution Act. And, here, as in Montalvo-Murillo, that remedy does not \xe2\x80\x9cproportion[ately]\xe2\x80\x9d repair the harm caused the defendant through delay, particularly where, as here, the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cknew about restitution,\xe2\x80\x9d including the likely amount, well before expiration of the 90-day time limit. App. 62. Indeed, our result here follows from Montalvo-Murillo a fortiori, for here delay at worst postpones the day of financial reckoning. In Montalvo-Murillo, delay postponed a constitutionally guaranteed bail hearing with the attached risk that the defendant would remain improperly confined in jail. See 495 U. S., at 728 (Stevens, J., dissenting) (noting the seriousness \xe2\x80\x9cof the deprivation of liberty that physical detention imposes\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does Montalvo-Murillo stand alone. The Court there found support in similar cases involving executive officials charged with carrying out mandatory public duties in a timely manner. See id., at 718 (citing French v. Edwards, 13 Wall. 506, 511 (1872); Brock, supra, at 260). Those cases, in turn, are consistent with numerous similar decisions made by courts throughout the Nation. See, e.g., Taylor v. Department of Transp., 260 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 521, 522\xe2\x80\x93523 (Iowa 1977); Hutchinson v. Ryan, 154 Kan. 751, 756\xe2\x80\x93757, 121 P.\xc2\xa02d 179, 182 (1942); State v. Industrial Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 233 Wis. 461, 466, 289 N.\xc2\xa0W. 769, 771 (1940); see also 3 Singer, Statutory Construction \xc2\xa757:19, at 74 (citing cases).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sixth, the defendant normally can mitigate any harm that a missed deadline might cause\xe2\x80\x94at least if, as here, he obtains the relevant information regarding the restitution amount before the 90-day deadline expires. A defendant who fears the deadline will be (or just has been) missed can simply tell the court, which will then likely set a timely hearing or take other statutorily required action. See \xc2\xa73664(d)(4) (providing that \xe2\x80\x9ccourt may require additional documentation or hear testimony\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa73664(d)(5). Though a deliberate failure of the sentencing court to comply with the statute seems improbable, should that occur, the defendant can also seek mandamus. See All Writs Act, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71651(a); La Buy v. Howes Leather Co., 352 U. S. 249 (1957). Cf. Brock, 476 U. S., at 260, n.\xc2\xa07 (noting availability of district court action to compel agency compliance with time-related directive).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Dolan, however, believes we have understated the harm to a defendant that a missed deadline can cause. To show this he makes a three-part argument: (1) A defendant cannot appeal a sentence unless it is part of a \xe2\x80\x9cfinal judgment\xe2\x80\x9d; (2) a judgment setting forth a sentence is not \xe2\x80\x9cfinal\xe2\x80\x9d until it contains a definitive determination of the amount of restitution; and (3) to delay the determination of the amount of restitution beyond the 90-day deadline is to delay the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s ability to appeal for more than 90 days\xe2\x80\x94perhaps to the point where his due process rights are threatened. Brief for Petitioner 28\xe2\x80\x9333.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The critical problem with this argument lies in its third step. As we have said, a defendant who, like petitioner here, knows that restitution will be ordered and is aware of the restitution amount prior to the expiration of the 90-day deadline can usually avoid additional delay simply by pointing to the statute and asking the court to grant a timely hearing. That did not happen here. And that minimal burden on the defendant is a small cost relative to the prospect of depriving innocent crime victims of their due restitution. (Should the court still refuse, the defendant could seek mandamus\xe2\x80\x94which we believe will rarely be necessary.)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even in the unlikely instances where that delay does cause the defendant prejudice\xe2\x80\x94perhaps by depriving him of evidence to rebut the claimed restitution amount\xe2\x80\x94the defendant remains free to ask the court to take that fact into account upon review. That inquiry might also consider the reason for the delay and the party responsible for its cause, i.e., whether the Government or the victim. Cf., e.g., United States v. Stevens, 211 F.\xc2\xa03d 1, 4\xe2\x80\x936 (CA2 2000) (tolling 90-day deadline for defendant\xe2\x80\x99s bad-faith delay); United States v. Terlingo, 327 F.\xc2\xa03d 216, 218\xe2\x80\x93223 (CA3 2003) (same). Adopting the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approach, by contrast, would permit a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s bad-faith delay to prevent a timely order of restitution, potentially allowing the defendant to manipulate whether restitution could be awarded at all. But since we are not presented with such a case here, we need not decide whether, or how, such potential harm or equitable considerations should be taken into consideration.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In focusing upon the argument\xe2\x80\x99s third step, we do not mean to imply that we accept the second premise, i.e., that a sentencing judgment is not \xe2\x80\x9cfinal\xe2\x80\x9d until it contains a definitive determination of the amount of restitution. To the contrary, strong arguments favor the appealability of the initial judgment irrespective of the delay in determining the restitution amount. The initial judgment here imposed a sentence of imprisonment and supervised release, and stated that restitution would be awarded. This Court has previously said that a judgment that imposes \xe2\x80\x9cdiscipline\xe2\x80\x9d may still be \xe2\x80\x9cfreighted with sufficiently substantial indicia of finality to support an appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d Corey v. United States, 375 U. S. 169, 174, 175 (1963) (internal quotation marks omitted). And the Solicitor General points to statutes that say that a \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment of conviction\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cincludes\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9csentence to imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d is a \xe2\x80\x9cfinal judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(b). So is a judgment that imposes supervised release (which can be imposed only in conjunction with a sentence of imprisonment). Ibid.; \xc2\xa73583(a). So is a judgment that imposes a fine. \xc2\xa73572(c). See Tr. of Oral Arg. 33\xe2\x80\x9334.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, \xc2\xa73664(o) provides that a \xe2\x80\x9csentence that imposes an order of restitution,\xe2\x80\x9d such as the later restitution order here, \xe2\x80\x9cis a final judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, it is not surprising to find instances where a defendant has appealed from the entry of a judgment containing an initial sentence that includes a term of imprisonment; that same defendant has subsequently appealed from a later order setting forth the final amount of restitution; and the Court of Appeals has consolidated the two appeals and decided them together. See, e.g., United States v. Stevens, supra; United States v. Maung, 267 F.\xc2\xa03d 1113, 1117 (CA11 2001); cf. United States v. Cheal, 389 F.\xc2\xa03d 35, 51\xe2\x80\x9353 (CA1 2004).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That the defendant can appeal from the earlier sentencing judgment makes sense, for otherwise the statutory 90-day restitution deadline, even when complied with, could delay appeals for up to 90 days. Defendants, that is, would be forced to wait three months before seeking review of their conviction when they could ordinarily do so within 14 days. See Fed. Rule App. Proc. 4(b). Nonetheless, in light of the fact that the interaction of restitution orders with appellate time limits could have consequences extending well beyond cases like the present case (where there was no appeal from the initial conviction and sentence), we simply note the strength of the arguments militating against the second step of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument without deciding whether or when a party can, or must, appeal. We leave all such matters for another day.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent, however, creates a rule that could adversely affect not just restitution, but other sentencing practices beyond the narrow circumstances presented here. Consider, for example, a judge who (currently lacking sufficient information) wishes to leave open, say, the amount of a fine, or a special condition of supervised release. In the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view, the entry of any such judgment would immediately deprive the judge of the authority later to fill in that blank, in the absence of a statute specifically providing otherwise. See post, at 1\xe2\x80\x934 (opinion of Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J.). Thus, the sentencing judge would either have to (1) forgo the specific dollar amount or potential condition, or (2) wait to enter any judgment until all of the relevant information is at hand. The former alternative would sometimes deprive judges of the power to enter components of a sentence they may consider essential. The latter alternative would require the defendant to wait\xe2\x80\x94perhaps months\xe2\x80\x94before taking an appeal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As we have pointed out, our precedents do not currently place the sentencing judge in any such dilemma. See supra, at 5, 8\xe2\x80\x939. And we need not now depart from those precedents when this case does not require us to do so; when the issue has not been adequately briefed; when the lower court had no opportunity to consider the argument (which the petitioner may well have forfeited); and when the rule would foreclose the current practices of some district courts and unnecessarily cabin the discretion they properly exercise over scheduling and sentencing matters. Cf., e.g., United States v. Stevens, supra, at 3; United States v. Cheal, supra, at 47 (illustrating district court practices).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Certainly there is no need to create this rule in the context of restitution, for provisions to which the dissent refers are silent about whether restitution can or cannot be ordered after an initial sentencing. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73551(b), (c) (\xe2\x80\x9cA sanction authorized by [criminal forfeiture and restitution statutes] may be imposed in addition to the [rest of the] sentence\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa73663A(c)(1) (mandatory orders of restitution \xe2\x80\x9cshall apply in all sentencing proceedings [for specified offenses]\xe2\x80\x9d). And even on the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s theory, the statute elsewhere provides the necessary substantive authorization: \xe2\x80\x9cNotwithstanding any other provision of law, when sentencing a defendant convicted of [a specified] offense \xe2\x80\xa6 , the court shall order \xe2\x80\xa6 that the defendant make restitution to the victim of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73663A(a)(1) (emphasis added). The dissent cannot explain why a separate statutory provision regarding procedures as to when a \xe2\x80\x9ccourt shall set a date for the final determination of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(d)(5), automatically divests a court of this distinct substantive authority. While of course that provision does not \xe2\x80\x9cplainly\xe2\x80\x9d confer \xe2\x80\x9cpower to act after sentencing,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 5 (emphasis deleted), neither does it \xe2\x80\x9cplainly\xe2\x80\x9d remove it or require that all sentencing matters be concluded at one point in time. (And the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s assertion, see post, at 6\xe2\x80\x94that it uses the term \xe2\x80\x9cauthority\xe2\x80\x9d not in its \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d sense, but rather in the sense that a court lacks \xe2\x80\x9cauthority\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cimpose a sentence above the maximum\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94introduces a tenuous analogy that may well confuse this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents regarding the term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934.)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, unless one reads the relevant statute\xe2\x80\x99s 90-day deadline as an ironclad limit upon the judge\xe2\x80\x99s authority to make a final determination of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses, the statute before us itself provides adequate authority to do what the sentencing judge did here\xe2\x80\x94essentially fill in an amount-related blank in a judgment that made clear that restitution was \xe2\x80\x9capplicable.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 49 (boldface deleted). Since the sentencing judge\xe2\x80\x99s later order did not \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d an \xe2\x80\x9cerror\xe2\x80\x9d in the sentence, Rule 35 does not apply. Compare Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 35(a) with post, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. Hence the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s claim that there is no other statute that creates authority (even were we to assume all else in its favor, which we do not) is merely to restate the question posed in this case, not to answer it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reading creates a serious statutory anomaly. It reads the statute as permitting a sentencing judge to order restitution for a \xe2\x80\x9cvictim\xe2\x80\x9d who \xe2\x80\x9csubsequently discovers further losses\xe2\x80\x9d a month, a year, or 10 years after entry of the original judgment, while at the same time depriving that judge of the power to award restitution to a victim whose \xe2\x80\x9closses are not ascertainable\xe2\x80\x9d within 90 days. Compare \xc2\xa73664(d)(5) (first sentence) with \xc2\xa73664(d)(5) (second sentence). How is that a sensible reading of a statute that makes restitution mandatory for victims?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, petitioner asks us to apply the \xe2\x80\x9crule of lenity\xe2\x80\x9d in favor of his reading of the statute. Dolan has not provided us with an example of an instance in which the \xe2\x80\x9crule of lenity\xe2\x80\x9d has been applied to a statutory time provision in the criminal context. See United States v. Wiltberger, 5 Wheat. 76 (1820) (applying rule in interpreting substantive criminal statute); Bifulco v. United States, 447 U. S. 381, 387, 400 (1980) (applying rule in interpreting \xe2\x80\x9cpenalties\xe2\x80\x9d). But, assuming for argument\xe2\x80\x99s sake that the rule might be so applied, and after considering the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text, structure, and purpose, we nonetheless cannot find a statutory ambiguity sufficiently \xe2\x80\x9cgrievous\xe2\x80\x9d to warrant its application in this case. Muscarello v. United States, 524 U. S. 125, 139 (1998) (internal quotation marks omitted). See Caron v. United States, 524 U. S. 308, 316 (1998) (rejecting application of rule where the \xe2\x80\x9cambiguous\xe2\x80\x9d reading \xe2\x80\x9cis an implausible reading of the congressional\npurpose\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit is

\n

Affirmed.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Stevens, Justice Scalia, and Justice Kennedy join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The statute at issue in this case provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses are not ascertainable [at least] 10 days prior to sentencing, \xe2\x80\xa6 the court shall set a date for the final determination of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses, not to exceed 90 days after sentencing.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73664(d)(5). Under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, failing to meet the 90-day deadline has no consequence whatever. The Court reads the statute as if it said \xe2\x80\x9cthe court shall set a date for the final determination of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses, at any time after sentencing.\xe2\x80\x9d I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the absence of \xc2\xa73664(d)(5), any order of restitution must be imposed at sentencing, if it is to be imposed at all. Restitution \xe2\x80\x9cmay be imposed in addition to [a] sentence\xe2\x80\x9d of probation, fine, or imprisonment only if it is authorized under \xc2\xa73556. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73551(b)\xe2\x80\x93(c). Section 3556, in turn, authorizes courts to order restitution \xe2\x80\x9cin imposing a sentence on a defendant\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added), pursuant to yet other provisions requiring such orders to be made \xe2\x80\x9cwhen sentencing a defendant,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73663(a)(1)(A), (c)(1), 3663A(a)\n(1) (emphasis added). The mandatory restitution provisions of \xc2\xa73663A \xe2\x80\x9capply in all sentencing proceedings for convictions of\xe2\x80\x9d certain crimes. \xc2\xa73663A(c)(1) (emphasis added). And the court \xe2\x80\x9cat the time of sentencing\xe2\x80\x9d must \xe2\x80\x9cstate in open court the reasons for its imposition of the particular sentence\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94including its reasons for \xe2\x80\x9cnot order[ing] restitution\xe2\x80\x9d if it fails to do so. \xc2\xa73553(c).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These provisions authorize restitution orders at sentencing. They confer no authority to order restitution after sentencing has concluded. When Congress permits courts to impose criminal penalties at some time other than sentencing, it does so explicitly. See, e.g., \xc2\xa73552(b) (provisional sentence during a study period); \xc2\xa73582(d) (authorizing certain penalties \xe2\x80\x9cin imposing a sentence \xe2\x80\xa6 or at any time thereafter\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa73583(e) (permitting extension of supervised release); \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa74244(d)\xe2\x80\x93(e) (provisional sentencing for the mentally ill); see also Fed. Rules Crim. Proc. 32.2(b)(2)(B), (4)(A) (presentencing forfeiture orders); cf. Corey v. United States, 375 U. S. 169 (1963) (appeals from provisional and final sentences authorized by law).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once a sentence has been imposed, moreover, it is final, and the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s authority to modify it is narrowly circumscribed. We have stated that \xe2\x80\x9cthe trial courts had no such authority\xe2\x80\x9d prior to the adoption of Rule 35, United States v. Addonizio, 442 U. S. 178, 189, and n.\xc2\xa016 (1979), and Congress has since revoked the broad authority to correct illegal sentences originally set forth in that Rule. See Sentencing Reform Act of 1984, Pub. L. 98\xe2\x80\x93473, \xc2\xa7215(b), 98 Stat. 2015\xe2\x80\x932016; see also Historical Notes on 1984 Amendments to Rule 35, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A., p. 605 (2008). Today, an error may be corrected by the trial court only if it is \xe2\x80\x9cclear,\xe2\x80\x9d and only within 14 days after the sentence is announced. Rules 35(a), (c). The Rule of Criminal Procedure allowing extensions of time expressly provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he court may not extend the time to take any action under Rule 35, except as stated in that rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 45(b)(2). This Court has reiterated that time limits made binding under Rule 35 \xe2\x80\x9cmay not be extended,\xe2\x80\x9d Addonizio, supra, at 189, and that Rule 45(b)(2) creates \xe2\x80\x9cinflexible\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cclaim-processing rules,\xe2\x80\x9d Eberhart v. United States, 546 U. S. 12, 19 (2005) (per curiam).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, if the trial court fails to impose a mandatory term of imprisonment, see, e.g., \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A), or a mandatory fine, see, e.g., 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7844(a), or a mandatory order of restitution, see 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73663A, the Government cannot simply ask it to impose the correct sentence later. If the error is clear, and raised within 14 days, it might be corrected under Rule 35. Otherwise, the Government must appeal, and seek resentencing on remand. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73742(b)(1), (g).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 3664(d)(5) is a limited exception to these bedrock rules. It permits a trial court to go forward with sentencing while delaying any restitution order for up to 90 days. This provision is meaningful precisely because restitution must otherwise be ordered at sentencing, and because sentences are otherwise final unless properly corrected. If trial courts had power to amend their sentences at any time, \xc2\xa73664(d)(5) would be unnecessary.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, however, the District Court failed to make use of its limited authority under \xc2\xa73664(d)(5). Dolan was sentenced on July 30, 2007. The court declined to order restitution at that time or to set a date for a future restitution order. App. 35, 39\xe2\x80\x9340; see also id., at 49.[Footnote 1] The 90-day period elapsed on October 28. At no time did the Government seek timely relief, whether under Rule 35 or by appeal. Cf. Corey, supra, at 174; Berman v. United States, 302 U. S. 211, 212 (1937). Nor did it assert any claim that the deadline had been lawfully extended or equitably tolled, an issue that I agree is not before us, see ante, at 3, 11. But on April 24, 2008\xe2\x80\x94269 days after sentencing, and after Dolan had already been released from prison\xe2\x80\x94the District Court nonetheless ordered $104,649.78 in restitution. App. to Pet. for Cert. 32a, 47a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I cannot see where that court obtained authority to add additional terms to Dolan\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. That is the step the Court misses when it searches for the \xe2\x80\x9cremedy\xe2\x80\x9d for a violation of \xc2\xa73664(d)(5). Ante, at 1. The rule is that a trial court cannot alter a sentence after the time of sentencing. Section 3664(d)(5) is a limited exception to that rule. If the limits are exceeded the exception does not apply, and the general rule takes over\xe2\x80\x94the sentence cannot be changed to add a restitution provision. Section 3664(d)(5) is self-executing: It grants authority subject to a deadline, and if the deadline is not met, the authority is no longer available.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court appears to reason that \xc2\xa73664(d)(5) confers the authority to add a restitution provision for at least 90 days, and that once the camel\xe2\x80\x99s nose of some permitted delay sneaks under the tent, any further delay is permissible. Ante, at 3, 5. But that is not what \xc2\xa73664(d)(5) says. It provides 90 days for a final determination of the victims\xe2\x80\x99 losses, not a free pass to impose restitution whenever the trial court gets around to it. The court had no more power to order restitution 269 days after sentencing than it did to order an additional term of imprisonment and send Dolan back to prison.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To avoid this conclusion, the Court runs through a series of irrelevancies that cannot trump the clear statutory text. It notes, for example, that \xc2\xa73663A provides that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[n]otwithstanding any other provision of law, when sentencing a defendant convicted of [a specified] offense \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, the court shall order \xe2\x80\xa6 that the defendant make restitution to the victim of the offense.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6 (quoting \xc2\xa73663A(a)(1); emphasis in Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion). But the issue before us is when restitution should be ordered, so the language the Court should underscore is \xe2\x80\x9cwhen sentencing.\xe2\x80\x9d This provision plainly confers no power to act after sentencing. Any such power attaches only by virtue of \xc2\xa73663A(d), which incorporates the procedures of \xc2\xa73664, including the limited 90-day exception. See also \xc2\xa73556 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe procedures under section 3664 shall apply to all orders of restitution under this section\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court puts greater emphasis on its reading of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose, namely to provide restitution to victims of crime. Certainly that was a purpose Congress sought to promote. But \xe2\x80\x9cno legislation pursues its purposes at all costs,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cit frustrates rather than effectuates legislative intent simplistically to assume that whatever furthers the statute\xe2\x80\x99s primary objective must be the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Rodriguez v. United States, 480 U. S. 522, 525\xe2\x80\x93526 (1987) (per curiam).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress had to balance against the interest in restitution the contrary interest in promptly determining the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. The balance struck was clearly set forth in the statute: determine the victim\xe2\x80\x99s losses by a date \xe2\x80\x9cnot to exceed 90 days after sentencing.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73664(d)(5). Whether or not that limit was \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily designed to help victims of crime,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7, it does not cease to be law when invoked by defendants.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor can the Court find any support in the second sentence of \xc2\xa73664(d)(5). See ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315. That provision addresses a distinct issue\xe2\x80\x94what to do about newly discovered losses\xe2\x80\x94and sets a higher \xe2\x80\x9cgood cause\xe2\x80\x9d standard. The fact that Congress struck the balance between restitution and finality differently in that context does not justify overriding the balance it struck here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court also analogizes the 90-day limit to other provisions discussed in our precedents, most of which have nothing to do with the rights of criminal defendants (for whom procedural protections are of heightened importance), let alone the finality of criminal sentencing. The cited cases are said to establish that an official\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to meet [a] deadline\xe2\x80\x9d does not always deprive that official of \xe2\x80\x9cpower to act beyond it.\xe2\x80\x9d Regions Hospital v. Shalala, 522 U. S. 448, 459, n.\xc2\xa03 (1998). But the failure to comply with \xc2\xa73664(d)(5) does not deprive anyone of anything: The trial court never had the general authority to alter sentences once imposed, in the way that the administrative agencies in the cited cases were said to have general regulatory authority. The trial court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to add a restitution provision to an otherwise final sentence was conferred by the very provision that limited that authority. Section 3664(d)(5) did not take away anything that might persist in the absence of \xc2\xa73664(d)(5).[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even more perplexing is the Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that references to the authority of trial courts necessarily implicate questions of jurisdiction. Ante, at 14. To say that a court lacks authority to order belated restitution does not use \xe2\x80\x9cauthority\xe2\x80\x9d in a jurisdictional sense, see Arbaugh v. Y\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0H Corp., 546 U. S. 500, 511 (2006), but only in the same sense in which a court lacks \xe2\x80\x9cauthority\xe2\x80\x9d to impose a sentence above the statutory maximum. Such action is an error of law, reversible on appeal, but it is not jurisdictional. As in United Student Aid Funds, Inc. v. Espinosa, 559 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 9), compliance with \xc2\xa73664(d)(5) is \xe2\x80\x9cnot a limitation on the \xe2\x80\xa6 court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d but it is a statutory \xe2\x80\x9cprecondition to obtaining a [particular] order.\xe2\x80\x9d Here that condition was not satisfied.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the end, the Court does not appear to need \xc2\xa73664(d)(5) at all. It instead suggests that we abandon the bedrock rules that sentences once imposed are final, and that the only exceptions are ones Congress chooses to allow (and Congress has allowed various ones). The Court instead proposes a judicial power to alter sentences, apparently at any time. But if a trial court can \xe2\x80\x9cleave open, say, the amount of a fine,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12, why not, say, the number of years? Thus, after a defendant like Dolan has served his entire sentence\xe2\x80\x94and who knows how long after?\xe2\x80\x94a court might still order additional imprisonment, additional restitution, an additional fine, or an additional condition of supervised release. See ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court cites no authority in support of such \xe2\x80\x9cfill in th[e] blank\xe2\x80\x9d sentencing, other than two cases implicated in the Circuit split below. Ante, at 13. Prior to the enactment of \xc2\xa73664(d)(5), however, it was widely recognized that the requirement to impose restitution \xe2\x80\x9cwhen sentencing\xe2\x80\x9d meant that \xe2\x80\x9c[r]estitution must be determined at the time of sentencing,\xe2\x80\x9d and could not be left open after sentencing had concluded. Federal Judicial Center, J. Wood, Guideline Sentencing: An Outline of Appellate Case Law on Selected Issues, p. 300 (Sept. 2002) (emphasis added; citing United States v. Porter, 41 F.\xc2\xa03d 68, 71 (CA2 1994); United States v. Ramilo, 986 F.\xc2\xa02d 333, 335\xe2\x80\x93336 (CA9 1993); United States v. Prendergast, 979 F.\xc2\xa02d 1289, 1293 (CA8 1992); United States v. Sasnett, 925 F.\xc2\xa02d 392, 398\xe2\x80\x93399 (CA11 1991)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court finds Rule 35(a) inapplicable because the District Court was not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98correct[ing]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d a clear error in the sentence. Ante, at 14. True enough; but that is why the Government should lose. The limitation of Rule 35(a) to clear errors, corrected within 14 days of sentencing, does not leave trial courts free to make other changes to sentences whenever they choose. Rule 35(a) only makes sense against a background rule that trial courts cannot change sentences at will.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The same is true of \xc2\xa73552(b), which empowers a court that does not wish to delay sentencing but \xe2\x80\x9cdesires more information than is otherwise available to it\xe2\x80\x9d to impose a provisional sentence during a 120-day study period. That statute would be largely unnecessary if a trial court could do the same by order.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Addonizio, 442 U. S., at 189, we thought it noncontroversial that a sentence once imposed is final, subject to such exceptions as Congress has allowed. Contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, ante, at 13, Dolan invoked that principle both here and below. See, e.g., Brief for Petitioner 13, 15\xe2\x80\x9318, 20, 29, 33, and n.\xc2\xa014, 36, 48, and n.\xc2\xa019; Reply Brief for Petitioner 1, 5\xe2\x80\x938; Appellant\xe2\x80\x99s Opening Brief in No. 08\xe2\x80\x932104 (CA10), pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313 (citing United States v. Blackwell, 81 F.\xc2\xa03d 945, 949 (CA10 1996), for the proposition that a \xe2\x80\x9cdistrict court does not have inherent authority to modify a sentence\xe2\x80\x9d). That the Court finds it necessary to question that principle\xe2\x80\x94indeed, to accuse this dissent of \xe2\x80\x9ccreat[ing] th[e] rule,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x94highlights how misguided its decision is.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To counter the effects of its opinion and to restore some semblance of finality to sentencing, the Court advises defendants to seek mandamus\xe2\x80\x94a remedy we have described as \xe2\x80\x9cdrastic and extraordinary,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9creserved for really extraordinary causes,\xe2\x80\x9d and one of \xe2\x80\x9cthe most potent weapons in the judicial arsenal.\xe2\x80\x9d Cheney v. United States Dist. Court for D. C., 542 U. S. 367, 380 (2004) (internal quotation marks omitted). What an odd procedure the Court contemplates! A defendant, who should have received a harsher sentence, is to invoke the drastic and extraordinary remedy of mandamus, to make sure he gets it. That is not how sentencing errors are corrected: If the trial court fails to order the appropriate sentence, the Government must appeal to correct it. It did not do so here, and that ends the case. Greenlaw v. United States, 554 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 6).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s mandamus remedy only helps defendants who know they are in danger of an increased sentence. So the Court imposes another rule, namely that the trial court must explicitly \xe2\x80\x9cleave open\xe2\x80\x9d the precise sentence at the time of sentencing, ante, at 12, or must make clear, \xe2\x80\x9cprior to the deadline\xe2\x80\x99s expiration[,] that it would order restitution\xe2\x80\x9d at some indeterminate time, ante, at 1 (emphasis added). But what if the court does not make the crucial announcement at sentencing, or \xe2\x80\x9cprior to the deadline\xe2\x80\x99s expiration\xe2\x80\x9d? Are these judicially created deadlines to be taken more seriously than those imposed by Congress? Or are we just back at the beginning, asking what the \xe2\x80\x9cremedy\xe2\x80\x9d should be for failing to meet the relevant deadline?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion to require notice of intent to augment the sentence at some future date may be a good idea. But an even better one might be to set a particular date\xe2\x80\x94say, 90 days after sentencing\xe2\x80\x94on which the parties could base their expectations. That was Congress\xe2\x80\x99s choice, and it should be good enough for us.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court in this case failed to order mandatory restitution in sentencing Dolan. That was wrong. But two wrongs do not make a right, and that mistake gave the court no authority to amend Dolan\xe2\x80\x99s sentence later, beyond the 90 days allowed to add a sentencing term requiring restitution.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I am mindful of the fact that when a trial court blunders, the victims may suffer. Consequences like that are the unavoidable result of having a system of rules. If no one appeals a mistaken ruling on the amount of restitution (or whether restitution applies at all), finality will necessarily obstruct the victims\xe2\x80\x99 full recovery.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is up to Congress to balance the competing interests in recovery and finality. Where\xe2\x80\x94as here\xe2\x80\x94it has done so clearly, the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial inquiry is complete.\xe2\x80\x9d Barnhart v. Sigmon Coal Co., 534 U. S. 438, 462 (2002) (internal quotation marks omitted).
