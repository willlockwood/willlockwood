NATIONAL CABLE & TELECOMMUNICATIONS \nASSOCIATION et\xc2\xa0al. v. BRAND X INTERNET \nSERVICES et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

No. 04\xe2\x80\x93277.Argued March 29, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 27, 2005

\n

Consumers traditionally access the Internet through \xe2\x80\x9cdial-up\xe2\x80\x9d connections provided via local telephone lines. Internet service providers (ISPs), in turn, link those calls to the Internet network, not only by providing a physical connection, but also by offering consumers the ability to translate raw data into information they may both view on their own computers and transmit to others connected to the Internet. Technological limitations of local telephone wires, however, retard the speed at which Internet data may be transmitted through such \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowband\xe2\x80\x9d connections. \xe2\x80\x9cBroadband\xe2\x80\x9d Internet service, by contrast, transmits data at much higher speeds. There are two principal kinds of broadband service: cable modem service, which transmits data between the Internet and users\xe2\x80\x99 computers via the network of television cable lines owned by cable companies, and Digital Subscriber Line (DSL) service, which uses high-speed wires owned by local telephone companies. Other ways of transmitting high-speed Internet data, including terrestrial- and satellite-based wireless networks, are also emerging.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Communications Act of 1934, as amended by the Telecommunications Act of 1996, defines two categories of entities relevant here. \xe2\x80\x9cInformation service\xe2\x80\x9d providers\xe2\x80\x94those \xe2\x80\x9coffering \xe2\x80\xa6 a capability for [processing] information via telecommunications,\xe2\x80\x9d 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(20)\xe2\x80\x94are subject to mandatory regulation by the Federal Communications Commission as common carriers under Title II of the Act. Conversely, telecommunications carriers\xe2\x80\x94i.e., those \xe2\x80\x9coffering \xe2\x80\xa6 telecommunications for a fee directly to the public \xe2\x80\xa6 regardless of the facilities used,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(46)\xe2\x80\x94are not subject to mandatory Title II regulation. These two classifications originated in the late 1970\xe2\x80\x99s, as the Commission developed rules to regulate data-processing services offered over telephone wires. Regulated \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d under the 1996 Act is the analog to \xe2\x80\x9cbasic service\xe2\x80\x9d under the prior regime, the Computer II rules. Those rules defined such service as a \xe2\x80\x9cpure\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ctransparent\xe2\x80\x9d transmission capability over a communications path enabling the consumer to transmit an ordinary-language message to another point without computer processing or storage of the information, such as via a telephone or a facsimile. Under the 1996 Act, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]nformation service\xe2\x80\x9d is the analog to \xe2\x80\x9cenhanced\xe2\x80\x9d service, defined by the Computer II rules as computer-processing applications that act on the subscriber\xe2\x80\x99s information, such as voice and data storage services, as well as \xe2\x80\x9cprotocol conversion,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., the ability to communicate between networks that employ different data-transmission formats.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the Declaratory Ruling under review, the Commission classified broadband cable modem service as an \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service\xe2\x80\x9d but not a \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d under the 1996 Act, so that it is not subject to mandatory Title II common-carrier regulation. The Commission relied heavily on its Universal Service Report, which earlier classified \xe2\x80\x9cnon-facilities-based\xe2\x80\x9d ISPs\xe2\x80\x94those that do not own the transmission facilities they use to connect the end user to the Internet\xe2\x80\x94solely as information-service providers. Because Internet access is a capability for manipulating and storing information, the Commission concluded, it was an \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service.\xe2\x80\x9d However, the integrated nature of such access and the high-speed wire used to provide it led the Commission to conclude that cable companies providing it are not \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d providers. Adopting the Universal Service Report\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, the Commission held that cable companies offering broadband Internet access, like non-facilities-based ISPs, do not offer the end user telecommunications service, but merely use telecommunications to provide end users with cable modem service.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Numerous parties petitioned for review. By judicial lottery, the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit was selected as the venue for the challenge. That court granted the petitions in part, vacated the Declaratory Ruling in part, and remanded for further proceedings. In particular, the court held that the Commission could not permissibly construe the Communications Act to exempt cable companies providing cable modem service from mandatory Title II regulation. Rather than analyzing the permissibility of that construction under the deferential framework of Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837, however, the court grounded that holding in the stare decisis effect of its decision in AT&T Corp. v. Portland, 216 F.\xc2\xa03d 871, which had held that cable modem service is a \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that broadband cable modem companies are exempt from mandatory common-carrier regulation is a lawful construction of the Communications Act under Chevron and the Administrative Procedure Act. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9332.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s framework applies to the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Chevron governs this Court\xe2\x80\x99s review of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction. See, e.g., National Cable & Telecommunications Assn., Inc. v. Gulf Power Co., 534 U. S. 327, 333\xe2\x80\x93339. Chevron requires a federal court to defer to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction, even if it differs from what the court believes to be the best interpretation, if the particular statute is within the agency\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction to administer, the statute is ambiguous on the point at issue, and the agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction is reasonable. 467 U. S., at 843\xe2\x80\x93844, and n.\xc2\xa011, 865\xe2\x80\x93866. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authority to \xe2\x80\x9cexecute and enforce\xe2\x80\x9d the Communications Act, \xc2\xa7151, and to \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe such rules and regulations as may be necessary \xe2\x80\xa6 to carry out the [Act\xe2\x80\x99s] provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7201(b), give the Commission power to promulgate binding legal rules; the Commission issued the order under review in the exercise of that authority; and there is no dispute that the order is within the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit should have applied Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s framework, instead of following the contrary construction it adopted in Portland. A court\xe2\x80\x99s prior construction of a statute trumps an agency construction otherwise entitled to Chevron deference only if the prior court decision holds that its construction follows from the unambiguous terms of the statute and thus leaves no room for agency discretion. See Smiley, supra, at 740\xe2\x80\x93741. Because Portland held only that the best reading of \xc2\xa7153(46) was that cable modem service was \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service,\xe2\x80\x9d not that this was the only permissible reading or that the Communications Act unambiguously required it, the Ninth Circuit erred in refusing to apply Chevron. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa7153(46)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d definition is a permissible reading of the Communications Act at both steps of Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s test. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0For the Commission, the question whether cable companies providing cable modem service \xe2\x80\x9coffe[r]\xe2\x80\x9d telecommunications within \xc2\xa7153(46)\xe2\x80\x99s meaning turned on the nature of the functions offered the end user. Seen from the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s point of view, the Commission concluded, the cable wire is used to access the World Wide Web, newsgroups, etc., rather than \xe2\x80\x9ctransparently\xe2\x80\x9d to transmit and receive ordinary-language messages without computer processing or storage of the message. The integrated character of this offering led the Commission to conclude that cable companies do not make a stand-alone, transparent offering of telecommunications. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa7153(46) is permissible at Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s first step, which asks whether the statute\xe2\x80\x99s plain terms \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly addres[s] the precise question at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d 467 U. S., at 843. This conclusion follows both from the ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9coffering\xe2\x80\x9d and the Communications Act\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory history. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0Where a statute\xe2\x80\x99s plain terms admit of two or more reasonable ordinary usages, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s choice of one of them is entitled to deference. See, e.g., Verizon Communications Inc. v. FCC, 535 U. S. 467, 498. It is common usage to describe what a company \xe2\x80\x9coffers\xe2\x80\x9d to a consumer as what the consumer perceives to be the integrated finished product, even to the exclusion of discrete components that compose the product. What cable companies providing cable modem service \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d is finished Internet service, though they do so using the discrete components composing the end product, including data transmission. Such functionally integrated components need not be described as distinct \xe2\x80\x9cofferings.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s traditional distinction between basic and enhanced service also supports the conclusion that the Communications Act is ambiguous about whether cable companies \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d telecommunications with cable modem service. Congress passed the Act\xe2\x80\x99s definitions against the background of this regulatory history, and it may be assumed that the parallel terms \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service\xe2\x80\x9d substantially incorporated the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cbasic\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cenhanced\xe2\x80\x9d service. That history in at least two respects confirms that the term \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d is ambiguous. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, in the Computer II order establishing the terms \xe2\x80\x9cbasic\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cenhanced\xe2\x80\x9d services, the Commission defined those terms functionally, based on how the consumer interacts with the provided information, just as the Commission did in the order under review. Cable modem service is not \xe2\x80\x9ctransparent\xe2\x80\x9d in terms of its interaction with customer-supplied information; the transmission occurs only in connection with information processing. It was therefore consistent with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s terms for the Commission to assume that the parallel term \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7153(46) likewise describes a \xe2\x80\x9cpure\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ctransparent\xe2\x80\x9d communications path not necessarily separately present in an integrated information-processing service from the end-user\xe2\x80\x99s perspective. Second, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s application of the basic/enhanced service distinction to non-facilities-based ISPs also supports the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion. The Commission has historically not subjected non-facilities-based information-service providers to common carrier regulation. That history suggests, in turn, that the Act does not unambiguously classify nonfacilities based ISPs as \xe2\x80\x9cofferors\xe2\x80\x9d of telecommunications. If the Act does not unambiguously classify such providers as \xe2\x80\x9coffering telecommunications,\xe2\x80\x9d it also does not unambiguously so classify facilities-based information-service providers such as cable companies; the relevant definitions do not distinguish the two types of carriers. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s silence suggests, instead, that the Commission has the discretion to fill the statutory gap. Pp.\xc2\xa021\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is also permissible at Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s step two because it is \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable policy choice for the agency to make,\xe2\x80\x9d 467 U. S., at 845. Respondents argue unpersuasively that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction is unreasonable because it allows any communications provider to evade common-carrier regulation simply by bundling information service with telecommunications. That result does not follow from the interpretation adopted in the Declaratory Ruling. The Commission classified cable modem service solely as an information service because the telecommunications input used to provide cable modem service is not separable from the service\xe2\x80\x99s data-processing capabilities, but is part and parcel of that service and integral to its other capabilities, and therefore is not a telecommunications offering. This construction does not leave all information-service offerings unregulated under Title II. It is plain, for example, that a local telephone company cannot escape regulation by packaging its telephone service with voice mail because such packaging offers a transparent transmission path\xe2\x80\x94telephone service\xe2\x80\x94that transmits information independent of the information-storage capabilities voice mail provides. By contrast, the high-speed transmission used to provide cable modem service is a functionally integrated component of Internet service because it transmits data only in connection with the further processing of information and is necessary to provide such service. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction therefore was more limited than respondents assume.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 argument that cable modem service does, in fact, provide \xe2\x80\x9ctransparent\xe2\x80\x9d transmission from the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s perspective is also mistaken. Their characterization of the \xe2\x80\x9cinformation-service\xe2\x80\x9d offering of Internet access as consisting only of access to a cable company\xe2\x80\x99s e-mail service, its Web page, and the ability it provides to create a personal Web page conflicts with the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable understanding of the nature of Internet service. When an end user accesses a third party\xe2\x80\x99s Web site, the Commission concluded, he is equally using the information service provided by the cable company as when he accesses that company\xe2\x80\x99s own Web site, its e-mail service, or his personal webpage. As the Commission recognized, the service that Internet access providers offer the public is Internet access, not a transparent ability (from the end-user\xe2\x80\x99s perspective) to transmit information. Pp.\xc2\xa025\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0The Court rejects respondent MCI, Inc.\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of cable modem service is inconsistent with its treatment of DSL service and is therefore an arbitrary and capricious deviation from agency policy under the Administrative Procedure Act, see 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7706(2)(A). MCI points out that when local telephone companies began to offer Internet access through DSL technology, the Commission required them to make the telephone lines used to provide DSL available to competing ISPs on nondiscriminatory, common-carrier terms. Respondents claim that the Commission has not adequately explained its decision not to regulate cable companies similarly.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court thinks that the Commission has provided a reasoned explanation for this decision. The traditional reason for its Computer II common-carrier treatment of facilities-based carriers was that the telephone network was the primary, if not the exclusive, means through which information service providers could gain access to their customers. The Commission applied the same treatment to DSL service based on that history, rather than on an analysis of contemporaneous market conditions. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s Declaratory Ruling, by contrast, concluded that changed market conditions warrant different treatment of cable modem service. Unlike at the time of the DSL order, substitute forms of Internet transmission exist today, including wireline, cable, terrestrial wireless, and satellite. The Commission therefore concluded that broadband services should exist in a minimal regulatory environment that promotes investment and innovation in a competitive market. There is nothing arbitrary or capricious about applying a fresh analysis to the cable industry. Pp.\xc2\xa029\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Title II of the Communications Act of 1934, 48 Stat. 1064, as amended, 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7151 et seq., subjects all providers of \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications servic[e]\xe2\x80\x9d to mandatory common-carrier regulation, \xc2\xa7153(44). In the order under review, the Federal Communications Commission concluded that cable companies that sell broadband Internet service do not provide \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications servic[e]\xe2\x80\x9d as the Communications Act defines that term, and hence are exempt from mandatory common-carrier regulation under Title II. We must decide whether that conclusion is a lawful construction of the Communications Act under Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837 (1984), and the Administrative Procedure Act, 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7555 et seq. We hold that it is.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The traditional means by which consumers in the United States access the network of interconnected computers that make up the Internet is through \xe2\x80\x9cdial-up\xe2\x80\x9d connections provided over local telephone facilities. See 345 F.\xc2\xa03d 1120, 1123\xe2\x80\x931124 (CA9 2003) (cases below); In\xc2\xa0re Inquiry Concerning High-Speed Access to the Internet Over Cable and Other Facilities, 17 FCC Rcd. 4798, 4802\xe2\x80\x934803, \xc2\xb69 (2002) (hereinafter Declaratory Ruling). Using these connections, consumers access the Internet by making calls with computer modems through the telephone wires owned by local phone companies. See Verizon Communications Inc. v. FCC, 535 U. S. 467, 489\xe2\x80\x93490 (2002) (describing the physical structure of a local telephone exchange). Internet service providers (ISPs), in turn, link those calls to the Internet network, not only by providing a physical connection, but also by offering consumers the ability to translate raw Internet data into information they may both view on their personal computers and transmit to other computers connected to the Internet. See In\xc2\xa0re Federal-State Joint Board on Universal Service, 13 FCC Rcd. 11501, 11531, \xc2\xb663 (1998) (hereinafter Universal Service Report); P. Huber, M. Kellogg, & J. Thorne, Federal Telecommunications Law 988 (2d ed. 1999) (hereinafter Huber); 345 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1123\xe2\x80\x931124. Technological limitations of local telephone wires, however, retard the speed at which data from the Internet may be transmitted through end users\xe2\x80\x99 dial-up connections. Dial-up connections are therefore known as \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowband,\xe2\x80\x9d or slower speed, connections.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cBroadband\xe2\x80\x9d Internet service, by contrast, transmits data at much higher speeds. There are two principal kinds of broadband Internet service: cable modem service and Digital Subscriber Line (DSL) service. Cable modem service transmits data between the Internet and users\xe2\x80\x99 computers via the network of television cable lines owned by cable companies. See id., at 1124. DSL service provides high-speed access using the local telephone wires owned by local telephone companies. See WorldCom, Inc. v. FCC, 246 F.\xc2\xa03d 690, 692 (CADC 2001) (describing DSL technology). Cable companies and telephone companies can either provide Internet access directly to consumers, thus acting as ISPs themselves, or can lease their transmission facilities to independent ISPs that then use the facilities to provide consumers with Internet access. Other ways of transmitting high-speed Internet data into homes, including terrestrial- and satellite-based wireless networks, are also emerging. Declaratory Ruling 4802, \xc2\xb66.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At issue in these cases is the proper regulatory classification under the Communications Act of broadband cable Internet service. The Act, as amended by the Telecommunications Act of 1996, 110 Stat. 56, defines two categories of regulated entities relevant to these cases: telecommunications carriers and information-service providers. The Act regulates telecommunications carriers, but not information-service providers, as common carriers. Telecommunications carriers, for example, must charge just and reasonable, nondiscriminatory rates to their customers, 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7201\xe2\x80\x93209, design their systems so that other carriers can interconnect with their communications networks, \xc2\xa7251(a)(1), and contribute to the federal \xe2\x80\x9cuniversal service\xe2\x80\x9d fund, \xc2\xa7254(d). These provisions are mandatory, but the Commission must forbear from applying them if it determines that the public interest requires it. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7160(a), (b). Information-service providers, by contrast, are not subject to mandatory common-carrier regulation under Title II, though the Commission has jurisdiction to impose additional regulatory obligations under its Title I ancillary jurisdiction to regulate interstate and foreign communications, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7151\xe2\x80\x93161.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These two statutory classifications originated in the late 1970\xe2\x80\x99s, as the Commission developed rules to regulate data-processing services offered over telephone wires. That regime, the \xe2\x80\x9cComputer II\xe2\x80\x9d rules, distinguished between \xe2\x80\x9cbasic\xe2\x80\x9d service (like telephone service) and \xe2\x80\x9cenhanced\xe2\x80\x9d service (computer-processing service offered over telephone lines). In re Amendment of Section 64.702 of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s Rules and Regulations (Second Computer Inquiry), 77 F.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. 2d 384, 417\xe2\x80\x93423, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb686\xe2\x80\x93101 (1980) (hereinafter Computer II Order). The Computer II rules defined both basic and enhanced services by reference to how the consumer perceives the service being offered.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In particular, the Commission defined \xe2\x80\x9cbasic service\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9ca pure transmission capability over a communications path that is virtually transparent in terms of its interaction with customer supplied information.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 420, \xc2\xb696. By \xe2\x80\x9cpure\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ctransparent\xe2\x80\x9d transmission, the Commission meant a communications path that enabled the consumer to transmit an ordinary-language message to another point, with no computer processing or storage of the information, other than the processing or storage needed to convert the message into electronic form and then back into ordinary language for purposes of transmitting it\nover the network\xe2\x80\x94such as via a telephone or a facsimile. Id., at 419\xe2\x80\x93420, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb694\xe2\x80\x9395. Basic service was subject to common-carrier regulation. Id., at 428, \xc2\xb6114.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[E]nhanced service,\xe2\x80\x9d however, was service in which \xe2\x80\x9ccomputer processing applications [were] used to act on the content, code, protocol, and other aspects of the subscriber\xe2\x80\x99s information,\xe2\x80\x9d such as voice and data storage services, id., at 420\xe2\x80\x93421, \xc2\xb697, as well as \xe2\x80\x9cprotocol conversion\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., ability to communicate between networks that employ different data-transmission formats), id., at 421\xe2\x80\x93422, \xc2\xb699. By contrast to basic service, the Commission decided not to subject providers of enhanced service, even enhanced service offered via transmission wires, to Title II common-carrier regulation. Id., at 428\xe2\x80\x93432, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb6115\xe2\x80\x93123. The Commission explained that it was unwise to subject enhanced service to common-carrier regulation given the \xe2\x80\x9cfast-moving, competitive market\xe2\x80\x9d in which they were offered. Id., at 434, \xc2\xb6129.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The definitions of the terms \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service\xe2\x80\x9d established by the 1996 Act are similar to the Computer II basic- and enhanced-service classifications. \xe2\x80\x9cTelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the analog to basic service\xe2\x80\x94is \xe2\x80\x9cthe offering of telecommunications for a fee directly to the public \xe2\x80\xa6 regardless of the facilities used.\xe2\x80\x9d 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(46). \xe2\x80\x9cTelecommunications\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cthe transmission, between or among points specified by the user, of information of the user\xe2\x80\x99s choosing, without change in the form or content of the information as sent and received.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(43). \xe2\x80\x9cTelecommunications carrier[s]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94those subjected to mandatory Title II common-carrier regulation\xe2\x80\x94are defined as \xe2\x80\x9cprovider[s] of telecommunications services.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(44). And \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the analog to enhanced service\xe2\x80\x94is \xe2\x80\x9cthe offering of a capability for generating, acquiring, storing, transforming, processing, retrieving, utilizing, or making available information via telecommunications\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(20).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In September 2000, the Commission initiated a rulemaking proceeding to, among other things, apply these classifications to cable companies that offer broadband Internet service directly to consumers. In March 2002, that rulemaking culminated in the Declaratory Ruling under review in these cases. In the Declaratory Ruling, the Commission concluded that broadband Internet service provided by cable companies is an \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service\xe2\x80\x9d but not a \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d under the Act, and therefore not subject to mandatory Title II common-carrier regulation. In support of this conclusion, the Commission relied heavily on its Universal Service Report. See Declaratory Ruling 4821\xe2\x80\x934822, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb636\xe2\x80\x9337 (citing Universal Service Report or Report). The Universal Service Report classified \xe2\x80\x9cnon-facilities-based\xe2\x80\x9d ISPs\xe2\x80\x94those that do not own the transmission facilities they use to connect the end user to the Internet\xe2\x80\x94solely as information-service providers. See Universal Service Report 11533, \xc2\xb667. Unlike those ISPs, cable companies own the cable lines they use to provide Internet access. Nevertheless, in the Declaratory Ruling, the Commission found no basis in the statutory definitions for treating cable companies differently from non-facilities-based ISPs: Both offer \xe2\x80\x9ca single, integrated service that enables the subscriber to utilize Internet access service \xe2\x80\xa6 and to realize the benefits of a comprehensive service offering.\xe2\x80\x9d Declaratory Ruling 4823, \xc2\xb638. Because Internet access provides a capability for manipulating and storing information, the Commission concluded that it was an information service. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The integrated nature of Internet access and the high-speed wire used to provide Internet access led the Commission to conclude that cable companies providing Internet access are not telecommunications providers. This conclusion, the Commission reasoned, followed from the logic of the Universal Service Report. The Report had concluded that, though Internet service \xe2\x80\x9cinvolves data transport elements\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9can Internet access provider must enable the movement of information between customers\xe2\x80\x99 own computers and distant computers with which those customers seek to interact,\xe2\x80\x9d it also \xe2\x80\x9coffers end users information-service capabilities inextricably intertwined with data transport.\xe2\x80\x9d Universal Service Report 11539\xe2\x80\x9311540, \xc2\xb680. ISPs, therefore, were not \xe2\x80\x9coffering \xe2\x80\xa6 telecommunications \xe2\x80\xa6 directly to the public,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(46), and so were not properly classified as telecommunications carriers, see id., at 11540, \xc2\xb681. In other words, the Commission reasoned that consumers use their cable modems not to transmit information \xe2\x80\x9ctransparently,\xe2\x80\x9d such as by using a telephone, but instead to obtain Internet access.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission applied this same reasoning to cable companies offering broadband Internet access. Its logic was that, like non-facilities-based ISPs, cable companies do not \xe2\x80\x9coffe[r] telecommunications service to the end user, but rather \xe2\x80\xa6 merely us[e] telecommunications to provide end users with cable modem service.\xe2\x80\x9d Declaratory Ruling 4824, \xc2\xb641. Though the Commission declined to apply mandatory Title II common-carrier regulation to cable companies, it invited comment on whether under its Title I jurisdiction it should require cable companies to offer other ISPs access to their facilities on common-carrier terms. Id., at 4839, \xc2\xb672. Numerous parties petitioned for judicial review, challenging the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that cable modem service was not telecommunications service. By judicial lottery, the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit was selected as the venue for the challenge.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals granted the petitions in part, vacated the Declaratory Ruling in part, and remanded to the Commission for further proceedings. In particular, the Court of Appeals vacated the ruling to the extent it concluded that cable modem service was not \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d under the Communications Act. It held that the Commission could not permissibly construe the Communications Act to exempt cable companies providing Internet service from Title II regulation. See 345 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1132. Rather than analyzing the permissibility of that construction under the deferential framework of Chevron, 467 U. S. 837, however, the Court of Appeals grounded its holding in the stare decisis effect of AT&T Corp. v. Portland, 216 F.\xc2\xa03d 871 (CA9 2000). See 345 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1128\xe2\x80\x931132. Portland held that cable modem service was a \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service,\xe2\x80\x9d though the court in that case was not reviewing an administrative proceeding and the Commission was not a party to the case. See 216 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 877\xe2\x80\x93880. Nevertheless, Portland\xe2\x80\x99s holding, the Court of Appeals reasoned, overrode the contrary interpretation reached by the Commission in the Declaratory Ruling. See 345 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1130\xe2\x80\x931131.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to settle the important questions of federal law that these cases present. 543 U. S. __ (2004).

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We first consider whether we should apply Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s framework to the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the term \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service.\xe2\x80\x9d We conclude that we should. We also conclude that the Court of Appeals should have done the same, instead of following the contrary construction it adopted in Portland.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Chevron, this Court held that ambiguities in statutes within an agency\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction to administer are delegations of authority to the agency to fill the statutory gap in reasonable fashion. Filling these gaps, the Court explained, involves difficult policy choices that agencies are better equipped to make than courts. 467 U. S., at 865\xe2\x80\x93866. If a statute is ambiguous, and if the implementing agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction is reasonable, Chevron requires a federal court to accept the agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the statute, even if the agency\xe2\x80\x99s reading differs from what the court believes is the best statutory interpretation. Id., at 843\xe2\x80\x93844, and n. 11.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Chevron framework governs our review of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction. Congress has delegated to the Commission the authority to \xe2\x80\x9cexecute and enforce\xe2\x80\x9d the Communications Act, \xc2\xa7151, and to \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe such rules and regulations as may be necessary in the public interest to carry out the provisions\xe2\x80\x9d of the Act, \xc2\xa7201(b); AT&T Corp. v. Iowa Utilities Bd., 525 U. S. 366, 377\xe2\x80\x93378 (1999). These provisions give the Commission the authority to promulgate binding legal rules; the Commission issued the order under review in the exercise of that authority; and no one questions that the order is within the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction. See Household Credit Services, Inc. v. Pfennig, 541 U. S. 232, 238\xe2\x80\x93239 (2004); United States v. Mead Corp., 533 U. S. 218, 231\xe2\x80\x93234 (2001); Christensen v. Harris County, 529 U. S. 576, 586\xe2\x80\x93588 (2000). Hence, as we have in the past, we apply the Chevron framework to the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the Communications Act. See National Cable & Telecommunications Assn., Inc. v. Gulf Power Co., 534 U. S. 327, 333\xe2\x80\x93339 (2002); Verizon, 535 U. S., at 501\xe2\x80\x93502.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Some of the respondents dispute this conclusion, on the ground that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is inconsistent with its past practice. We reject this argument. Agency inconsistency is not a basis for declining to analyze the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation under the Chevron framework. Unexplained inconsistency is, at most, a reason for holding an interpretation to be an arbitrary and capricious change from agency practice under the Administrative Procedure Act. See Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Assn. of United States, Inc. v. State Farm Mut. Automobile Ins. Co., 463 U. S. 29, 46\xe2\x80\x9357 (1983). For if the agency adequately explains the reasons for a reversal of policy, \xe2\x80\x9cchange is not invalidating, since the whole point of Chevron is to leave the discretion provided by the ambiguities of a statute with the implementing agency.\xe2\x80\x9d Smiley v. Citibank (South Dakota), N.\xc2\xa0A., 517 U. S. 735, 742 (1996); see also Rust v. Sullivan, 500 U. S. 173, 186\xe2\x80\x93187 (1991); Barnhart v. Walton, 535 U. S. 212, 226 (2002) (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). \xe2\x80\x9cAn initial agency interpretation is not instantly carved in stone. On the contrary, the agency \xe2\x80\xa6 must consider varying interpretations and the wisdom of its policy on a continuing basis,\xe2\x80\x9d Chevron, supra, at 863\xe2\x80\x93864, for example, in response to changed factual circumstances, or a change in administrations, see State Farm, supra, at 59 (Rehnquist, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). That is no doubt why in Chevron itself, this Court deferred to an agency interpretation that was a recent reversal of agency policy. See 467 U. S., at 857\xe2\x80\x93858. We therefore have no difficulty concluding that Chevron applies.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals declined to apply Chevron because it thought the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the Communications Act foreclosed by the conflicting construction of the Act it had adopted in Portland, supra. See 345 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1127\xe2\x80\x931132. It based that holding on the assumption that Portland\xe2\x80\x99s construction overrode the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s, regardless of whether Portland had held the statute to be unambiguous. 345 F. 3d, at 1131. That reasoning was incorrect.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A court\xe2\x80\x99s prior judicial construction of a statute trumps an agency construction otherwise entitled to Chevron deference only if the prior court decision holds that its construction follows from the unambiguous terms of the statute and thus leaves no room for agency discretion. This principle follows from Chevron itself. Chevron established a \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption that Congress, when it left ambiguity in a statute meant for implementation by an agency, understood that the ambiguity would be resolved, first and foremost, by the agency, and desired the agency (rather than the courts) to possess whatever degree of discretion the ambiguity allows.\xe2\x80\x9d Smiley, supra, at 740\xe2\x80\x93741. Yet allowing a judicial precedent to foreclose an agency from interpreting an ambiguous statute, as the Court of Appeals assumed it could, would allow a court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation to override an agency\xe2\x80\x99s. Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s premise is that it is for agencies, not courts, to fill statutory gaps. See 467 U. S., at 843\xe2\x80\x93844, and n.\xc2\xa011. The better rule is to hold judicial interpretations contained in precedents to the same demanding Chevron step one standard that applies if the court is reviewing the agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction on a blank slate: Only a judicial precedent holding that the statute unambiguously forecloses the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, and therefore contains no gap for the agency to fill, displaces a conflicting agency construction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A contrary rule would produce anomalous results. It would mean that whether an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of an ambiguous statute is entitled to Chevron deference would turn on the order in which the interpretations issue: If the court\xe2\x80\x99s construction came first, its construction would prevail, whereas if the agency\xe2\x80\x99s came first, the agency\xe2\x80\x99s construction would command Chevron deference. Yet whether Congress has delegated to an agency the authority to interpret a statute does not depend on the order in which the judicial and administrative constructions occur. The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 rule, moreover, would \xe2\x80\x9clead to the ossification of large portions of our statutory law,\xe2\x80\x9d Mead, supra, at 247 (Scalia, J., dissenting), by precluding agencies from revising unwise judicial constructions of ambiguous statutes. Neither Chevron nor the doctrine of stare decisis requires these haphazard results.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent answers that allowing an agency to override what a court believes to be the best interpretation of a statute makes \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial decisions subject to reversal by Executive officers.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 13 (opinion of Scalia, J.). It does not. Since Chevron teaches that a court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion as to the best reading of an ambiguous statute an agency is charged with administering is not authoritative, the agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision to construe that statute differently from a court does not say that the court\xe2\x80\x99s holding was legally wrong. Instead, the agency may, consistent with the court\xe2\x80\x99s holding, choose a different construction, since the agency remains the authoritative interpreter (within the limits of reason) of such statutes. In all other respects, the court\xe2\x80\x99s prior ruling remains binding law (for example, as to agency interpretations to which Chevron is inapplicable). The precedent has not been \xe2\x80\x9creversed\xe2\x80\x9d by the agency, any more than a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a State\xe2\x80\x99s law can be said to have been \xe2\x80\x9creversed\xe2\x80\x9d by a state court that adopts a conflicting (yet authoritative) interpretation of state law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals derived a contrary rule from a mistaken reading of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions. It read Neal v. United States, 516 U. S. 284 (1996), to establish that a prior judicial construction of a statute categorically controls an agency\xe2\x80\x99s contrary construction. 345 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1131\xe2\x80\x931132; see also post, at 12, n.\xc2\xa011 (Scalia, J., dissenting). Neal established no such proposition. Neal declined to defer to a construction adopted by the United States Sentencing Commission that conflicted with one the Court previously had adopted in Chapman v. United States, 500 U. S. 453 (1991). Neal, supra, at 290\xe2\x80\x93295. Chapman, however, had held the relevant statute to be unambiguous. See 500 U. S., at 463 (declining to apply the rule of lenity given the statute\xe2\x80\x99s clear language). Thus, Neal established only that a precedent holding a statute to be unambiguous forecloses a contrary agency construction. That limited holding accorded with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior decisions, which had held that a court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a statute trumps an agency\xe2\x80\x99s under the doctrine of stare decisis only if the prior court holding \xe2\x80\x9cdetermined a statute\xe2\x80\x99s clear meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Maislin Industries, U. S., Inc. v. Primary Steel, Inc., 497 U. S. 116, 131 (1990) (emphasis added); see also Lechmere, Inc. v. NLRB, 502 U. S. 527, 536\xe2\x80\x93537 (1992). Those decisions allow a court\xe2\x80\x99s prior interpretation of a statute to override an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation only if the relevant court decision held the statute unambiguous.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Against this background, the Court of Appeals erred in refusing to apply Chevron to the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service,\xe2\x80\x9d 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(46). Its prior decision in Portland held only that the best reading of \xc2\xa7153(46) was that cable modem service was a \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service,\xe2\x80\x9d not that it was the only permissible reading of the statute. See 216 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 877\xe2\x80\x93880. Nothing in Portland held that the Communications Act unambiguously required treating cable Internet providers as telecommunications carriers. Instead, the court noted that it was \xe2\x80\x9cnot presented with a case involving potential deference to an administrative agency\xe2\x80\x99s statutory construction pursuant to the Chevron doctrine,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 876; and the court invoked no other rule of construction (such as the rule of lenity) requiring it to conclude that the statute was unambiguous to reach its judgment. Before a judicial construction of a statute, whether contained in a precedent or not, may trump an agency\xe2\x80\x99s, the court must hold that the statute unambiguously requires the court\xe2\x80\x99s construction. Portland did not do so.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the dissent points out, it is not logically necessary for us to reach the question whether the Court of Appeals misapplied Chevron for us to decide whether the Commission acted lawfully. See post, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317 (opinion of Scalia, J.). Nevertheless, it is no \xe2\x80\x9cgreat mystery\xe2\x80\x9d why we are reaching the point here. Ibid. There is genuine confusion in the lower courts over the interaction between the Chevron doctrine and stare decisis principles, as the petitioners informed us at the certiorari stage of this litigation. See Pet. for Cert. of Federal Communications Commission et\xc2\xa0al. in No. 04\xe2\x80\x93281, pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9323; Pet. for Cert. of National Cable & Telecomm. Assn. et\xc2\xa0al. in No. 04\xe2\x80\x93277, pp. 22\xe2\x80\x9329. The point has been briefed. See Brief for Federal Petitioners 38\xe2\x80\x9344; Brief for Cable-Industry Petitioners 30\xe2\x80\x9336. And not reaching the point could undermine the purpose of our grant of certiorari: to settle authoritatively whether the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s Declaratory Ruling is lawful. Were we to uphold the Declaratory Ruling without reaching the Chevron point, the Court of Appeals could once again strike down the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s rule based on its Portland decision. Portland (at least arguably) could compel the Court of Appeals once again to reverse the Commission despite our decision, since our conclusion that it is reasonable to read the Communications Act to classify cable modem service solely as an \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service\xe2\x80\x9d leaves untouched Portland\xe2\x80\x99s holding that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is not the best reading of the statute. We have before decided similar questions that were not, strictly speaking, necessary to our disposition. See, e.g., Agostini v. Felton, 521 U. S. 203, 237 (1997) (requiring the Courts of Appeals to adhere to our directly controlling precedents, even those that rest on reasons rejected in other decisions); Roper v. Simmons, 543 U. S. ___ , ___ (2005) (slip op., at 23\xe2\x80\x9324) (Scalia, J., dissenting) (criticizing this Court for not reaching the question whether the Missouri Supreme Court erred by failing to follow directly controlling Supreme Court precedent, though that conclusion was not necessary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision). It is prudent for us to do so once again today.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We next address whether the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service,\xe2\x80\x9d 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(46), is a permissible reading of the Communications Act under the Chevron framework. Chevron established a familiar two-step procedure for evaluating whether an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a statute is lawful. At the first step, we ask whether the statute\xe2\x80\x99s plain terms \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly addres[s] the precise question at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d 467 U. S., at 843. If the statute is ambiguous on the point, we defer at step two to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation so long as the construction is \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable policy choice for the agency to make.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 845. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is permissible at both steps.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We first set forth our understanding of the interpretation of the Communications Act that the Commission embraced. The issue before the Commission was whether cable companies providing cable modem service are providing a \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d in addition to an \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission first concluded that cable modem service is an \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service,\xe2\x80\x9d a conclusion unchallenged here. The Act defines \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe offering of a capability for generating, acquiring, storing, transforming, processing, retrieving, utilizing, or making available information via telecommunications\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(20). Cable modem service is an information service, the Commission reasoned, because it provides consumers with a comprehensive capability for manipulating information using the Internet via high-speed telecommunications. That service enables users, for example, to browse the World Wide Web, to transfer files from file archives available on the Internet via the \xe2\x80\x9cFile Transfer Protocol,\xe2\x80\x9d and to access e-mail and Usenet newsgroups. Declaratory Ruling 4821, \xc2\xb637; Universal Service Report 11537, \xc2\xb676. Like other forms of Internet service, cable modem service also gives users access to the Domain Name System (DNS). DNS, among other things, matches the Web page addresses that end users type into their browsers (or \xe2\x80\x9cclick\xe2\x80\x9d on) with the Internet Protocol (IP) addresses[Footnote 1] of the servers containing the Web pages the users wish to access. Declaratory Ruling 4821\xe2\x80\x934822, \xc2\xb637. All of these features, the Commission concluded, were part of the information service that cable companies provide consumers. Id., at 4821\xe2\x80\x934823, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb636\xe2\x80\x9338; see also Universal Service Report 11536\xe2\x80\x9311539, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb675\xe2\x80\x9379.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the same time, the Commission concluded that cable modem service was not \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cTelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cthe offering of telecommunications for a fee directly to the public.\xe2\x80\x9d 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(46). \xe2\x80\x9cTelecommunications,\xe2\x80\x9d in turn, is defined as \xe2\x80\x9cthe transmission, between or among points specified by the user, of information of the user\xe2\x80\x99s choosing, without change in the form or content of the information as sent and received.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(43). The Commission conceded that, like all information-service providers, cable companies use \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications\xe2\x80\x9d to provide consumers with Internet service; cable companies provide such service via the high-speed wire that transmits signals to and from an end user\xe2\x80\x99s computer. Declaratory Ruling 4823, \xc2\xb640. For the Commission, however, the question whether cable broadband Internet providers \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d telecommunications involved more than whether telecommunications was one necessary component of cable modem service. Instead, whether that service also includes a telecommunications \xe2\x80\x9coffering\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ctur[ned] on the nature of the functions the end user is offered,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 4822, \xc2\xb638 (emphasis added), for the statutory definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d does not \xe2\x80\x9cres[t] on the particular types of facilities used,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 4821, \xc2\xb635; see \xc2\xa7153(46) (definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d applies \xe2\x80\x9cregardless of the facilities used\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Seen from the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s point of view, the Commission concluded, cable modem service is not a telecommunications offering because the consumer uses the high-speed wire always in connection with the information-processing capabilities provided by Internet access, and because the transmission is a necessary component of Internet access: \xe2\x80\x9cAs provided to the end user the telecommunications is part and parcel of cable modem service and is integral to its other capabilities.\xe2\x80\x9d Declaratory Ruling 4823, \xc2\xb639. The wire is used, in other words, to access the World Wide Web, newsgroups, and so forth, rather than \xe2\x80\x9ctransparently\xe2\x80\x9d to transmit and receive ordinary-language messages without computer processing or storage of the message. See supra, at 4 (noting the Computer II notion of \xe2\x80\x9ctransparent\xe2\x80\x9d transmission). The integrated character of this offering led the Commission to conclude that cable modem service is not a \xe2\x80\x9cstand-alone,\xe2\x80\x9d transparent offering of telecommunications. Declaratory Ruling 4823\xe2\x80\x934825, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb641\xe2\x80\x9343.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This construction passes Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s first step. Respondents argue that it does not, on the ground that cable companies providing Internet service necessarily \xe2\x80\x9coffe[r]\xe2\x80\x9d the underlying telecommunications used to transmit that service. The word \xe2\x80\x9coffering\xe2\x80\x9d as used in \xc2\xa7153(46), however, does not unambiguously require that result. Instead, \xe2\x80\x9coffering\xe2\x80\x9d can reasonably be read to mean a \xe2\x80\x9cstand-alone\xe2\x80\x9d offering of telecommunications, i.e., an offered service that, from the user\xe2\x80\x99s perspective, transmits messages unadulterated by computer processing. That conclusion follows not only from the ordinary meaning of the word \xe2\x80\x9coffering,\xe2\x80\x9d but also from the regulatory history of the Communications Act.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Cable companies in the broadband Internet service business \xe2\x80\x9coffe[r]\xe2\x80\x9d consumers an information service in the form of Internet access and they do so \xe2\x80\x9cvia telecommunications,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(20), but it does not inexorably follow as a matter of ordinary language that they also \xe2\x80\x9coffe[r]\xe2\x80\x9d consumers the high-speed data transmission (telecommunications) that is an input used to provide this service, \xc2\xa7153(46). We have held that where a statute\xe2\x80\x99s plain terms admit of two or more reasonable ordinary usages, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s choice of one of them is entitled to deference. See Verizon, 535 U. S., at 498 (deferring to the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the term \xe2\x80\x9ccost\xe2\x80\x9d by reference to an alternative linguistic usage defined by what \xe2\x80\x9c[a] merchant who is asked about \xe2\x80\x98the cost of providing the goods\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d might \xe2\x80\x9creasonably\xe2\x80\x9d say); National Railroad Passenger Corporation v. Boston & Maine Corp., 503 U. S. 407, 418 (1992) (agency construction entitled to deference where there were \xe2\x80\x9calternative dictionary definitions of the word\xe2\x80\x9d at issue). The term \xe2\x80\x9coffe[r]\xe2\x80\x9d as used in the definition of telecommunications service, 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(46), is ambiguous in this way.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is common usage to describe what a company \xe2\x80\x9coffers\xe2\x80\x9d to a consumer as what the consumer perceives to be the integrated finished product, even to the exclusion of discrete components that compose the product, as the dissent concedes. See post, at 3 (opinion of Scalia, J.). One might well say that a car dealership \xe2\x80\x9coffers\xe2\x80\x9d cars, but does not \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d the integrated major inputs that make purchasing the car valuable, such as the engine or the chassis. It would, in fact, be odd to describe a car dealership as \xe2\x80\x9coffering\xe2\x80\x9d consumers the car\xe2\x80\x99s components in addition to the car itself. Even if it is linguistically permissible to say that the car dealership \xe2\x80\x9coffers\xe2\x80\x9d engines when it offers cars, that shows, at most, that the term \xe2\x80\x9coffer,\xe2\x80\x9d when applied to a commercial transaction, is ambiguous about whether it describes only the offered finished product, or the product\xe2\x80\x99s discrete components as well. It does not show that no other usage is permitted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question, then, is whether the transmission component of cable modem service is sufficiently integrated with the finished service to make it reasonable to describe the two as a single, integrated offering. See ibid. We think that they are sufficiently integrated, because \xe2\x80\x9c[a] consumer uses the high-speed wire always in connection with the information-processing capabilities provided by Internet access, and because the transmission is a necessary component of Internet access.\xe2\x80\x9d Supra, at 16. In the telecommunications context, it is at least reasonable to describe companies as not \xe2\x80\x9coffering\xe2\x80\x9d to consumers each discrete input that is necessary to providing, and is always used in connection with, a finished service. We think it no misuse of language, for example, to say that cable companies providing Internet service do not \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d consumers DNS, even though DNS is essential to providing Internet access. Declaratory Ruling 4810, n. 74, 4822\xe2\x80\x934823, \xc2\xb638. Likewise, a telephone company \xe2\x80\x9coffers\xe2\x80\x9d consumers a transparent transmission path that conveys an ordinary-language message, not necessarily the data transmission facilities that also \xe2\x80\x9ctransmi[t] \xe2\x80\xa6 information of the user\xe2\x80\x99s choosing,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(43), or other physical elements of the facilities used to provide telephone service, like the trunks and switches, or the copper in the wires. What cable companies providing cable modem service and telephone companies providing telephone service \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d is Internet service and telephone service respectively\xe2\x80\x94the finished services, though they do so using (or \xe2\x80\x9cvia\xe2\x80\x9d) the discrete components composing the end product, including data transmission. Such functionally integrated components need not be described as distinct \xe2\x80\x9cofferings.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In response, the dissent argues that the high-speed transmission component necessary to providing cable modem service is necessarily \xe2\x80\x9coffered\xe2\x80\x9d with Internet service because cable modem service is like the offering of pizza delivery service together with pizza, and the offering of puppies together with dog leashes. Post, at 3\xe2\x80\x934 (opinion of Scalia, J.). The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s appeal to these analogies only underscores that the term \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d is ambiguous in the way that we have described. The entire question is whether the products here are functionally integrated (like the components of a car) or functionally separate (like pets and leashes). That question turns not on the language of the Act, but on the factual particulars of how Internet technology works and how it is provided, questions Chevron leaves to the Commission to resolve in the first instance. As the Commission has candidly recognized, \xe2\x80\x9cthe question may not always be straightforward whether, on the one hand, an entity is providing a single information service with communications and computing components, or, on the other hand, is providing two distinct services, one of which is a telecommunications service.\xe2\x80\x9d Universal Service Report 11530, \xc2\xb660. Because the term \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d can sometimes refer to a single, finished product and sometimes to the \xe2\x80\x9cindividual components in a package being offered\xe2\x80\x9d (depending on whether the components \xe2\x80\x9cstill possess sufficient identity to be described as separate objects,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 3), the statute fails unambiguously to classify the telecommunications component of cable modem service as a distinct offering. This leaves federal telecommunications policy in this technical and complex area to be set by the Commission, not by warring\nanalogies.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We also do not share the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s certainty that cable modem service is so obviously like pizza delivery service and the combination of dog leashes and dogs that the Commission could not reasonably have thought otherwise. Post, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. For example, unlike the transmission component of Internet service, delivery service and dog leashes are not integral components of the finished products (pizzas and pet dogs). One can pick up a pizza rather than having it delivered, and one can own a dog without buying a leash. By contrast, the Commission reasonably concluded, a consumer cannot purchase Internet service without also purchasing a connection to the Internet and the transmission always occurs in connection with information processing. In any event, we doubt that a statute that, for example, subjected offerors of \xe2\x80\x9cdelivery\xe2\x80\x9d service (such as Federal Express and United Parcel Service) to common-carrier regulation would unambiguously require pizza-delivery companies to offer their delivery services on a common carrier basis.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s traditional distinction between basic and enhanced service, see supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, also supports the conclusion that the Communications Act is ambiguous about whether cable companies \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d telecommunications with cable modem service. Congress passed the definitions in the Communications Act against the background of this regulatory history, and we may assume that the parallel terms \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service\xe2\x80\x9d substantially incorporated their meaning, as the Commission has held. See, e.g., In\xc2\xa0re Federal-State Joint Board on Universal Service, 12 FCC Rcd. 8776, 9179\xe2\x80\x939180, \xc2\xb6788 (1997) (noting that the \xe2\x80\x9cdefinition of enhanced services is substantially similar to the definition of information services\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9call services previously considered \xe2\x80\x98enhanced services\xe2\x80\x99 are \xe2\x80\x98information services\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Commissioner v. Keystone Consol. Industries, Inc., 508 U. S. 152, 159 (1993) (noting presumption that Congress is aware of \xe2\x80\x9csettled judicial and administrative interpretation[s]\xe2\x80\x9d of terms when it enacts a statute). The regulatory history in at least two respects confirms that the term \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d is ambiguous.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, in the Computer II Order that established the terms \xe2\x80\x9cbasic\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cenhanced\xe2\x80\x9d services, the Commission defined those terms functionally, based on how the consumer interacts with the provided information, just as the Commission did in the order below. See supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. As we have explained, Internet service is not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98transparent in terms of its interaction with customer-supplied information,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Computer II Order 420, \xc2\xb696; the transmission occurs in connection with information processing. It was therefore consistent with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s terms for the Commission to assume that the parallel term \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d in 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(46) likewise describes a \xe2\x80\x9cpure\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ctransparent\xe2\x80\x9d communications path not necessarily separately present, from the end user\xe2\x80\x99s perspective, in an integrated information-service offering.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s application of the basic/enhanced service distinction to non-facilities-based ISPs also supports this conclusion. The Commission has long held that \xe2\x80\x9call those who provide some form of transmission services are not necessarily common carriers.\xe2\x80\x9d Computer II Order 431, \xc2\xb6122; see also id., at 435, \xc2\xb6132 (\xe2\x80\x9cacknowledg[ing] the existence of a communications component\xe2\x80\x9d in enhanced-service offerings). For example, the Commission did not subject to common-carrier regulation those service providers that offered enhanced services over telecommunications facilities, but that did not themselves own the underlying facilities\xe2\x80\x94so-called \xe2\x80\x9cnon-facilities-based\xe2\x80\x9d providers. See Universal Service Report 11530, \xc2\xb660. Examples of these services included database services in which a customer used telecommunications to access information, such as Dow Jones News and Lexis, as well as \xe2\x80\x9cvalue added networks,\xe2\x80\x9d which lease wires from common carriers and provide transmission as well as protocol-processing service over those wires. See In\xc2\xa0re Amendment to Sections 64.702 of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s Rules and Regulations (Third Computer Inquiry), 3 FCC Rcd. 1150, 1153, n. 23 (1988); supra, at 4 (explaining protocol conversion). These services \xe2\x80\x9ccombin[ed] communications and computing components,\xe2\x80\x9d yet the Commission held that they should \xe2\x80\x9calways be deemed enhanced\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore not subject to common-carrier regulation. Universal Service Report 11530, \xc2\xb660. Following this traditional distinction, the Commission in the Universal Service Report classified ISPs that leased rather than owned their transmission facilities as pure information-service providers. Id., at 11540, \xc2\xb681.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 statutory arguments conflict with this regulatory history. They claim that the Communications Act unambiguously classifies as telecommunications carriers all entities that use telecommunications inputs to provide information service. As respondent MCI concedes, this argument would subject to mandatory common-carrier regulation all information-service providers that use telecommunications as an input to provide information service to the public. Brief for Respondent MCI, Inc. 30. For example, it would subject to common-carrier regulation non-facilities-based ISPs that own no transmission facilities. See Universal Service Report 11532\xe2\x80\x9311533, \xc2\xb666. Those ISPs provide consumers with transmission facilities used to connect to the Internet, see supra, at 2, and so, under respondents\xe2\x80\x99 argument, necessarily \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d telecommunications to consumers. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 position that all such entities are necessarily \xe2\x80\x9coffering telecommunications\xe2\x80\x9d therefore entails mandatory common-carrier regulation of entities that the Commission never classified as \xe2\x80\x9cofferors\xe2\x80\x9d of basic transmission service, and therefore common carriers, under the Computer II regime.[Footnote 2] See Universal Service Report 11540, \xc2\xb681 (noting past Commission policy); Computer and Communications Industry Assn. v. FCC, 693 F.\xc2\xa02d 198, 209 (CADC 1982) (noting and upholding Commission\xe2\x80\x99s Computer II \xe2\x80\x9cfinding that enhanced services \xe2\x80\xa6 are not common carrier services within the scope of Title II\xe2\x80\x9d). We doubt that the parallel term \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d unambiguously worked this abrupt shift in Commission policy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 analogy between cable companies that provide cable modem service and facilities-based enhanced-service providers\xe2\x80\x94that is, enhanced-service providers who own the transmission facilities used to provide those services\xe2\x80\x94fares no better. Respondents stress that under the Computer II rules the Commission regulated such providers more heavily than non-facilities-based providers. The Commission required, for example, local telephone companies that provided enhanced services to offer their wires on a common-carrier basis to competing enhanced-service providers. See, e.g., In\xc2\xa0re Amendment of Sections 64.702 of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s Rules and Regulations (Third Computer Inquiry), 104 F.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0C. 2d 958, 964, \xc2\xb64 (1986) (hereinafter Computer III Order). Respondents argue that the Communications Act unambiguously requires the same treatment for cable companies because cable companies also own the facilities they use to provide cable modem service (and therefore information service).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We disagree. We think it improbable that the Communications Act unambiguously freezes in time the Computer II treatment of facilities-based information-service providers. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d says nothing about imposing more stringent regulatory duties on facilities-based information-service providers. The definition hinges solely on whether the entity \xe2\x80\x9coffer[s] telecommunications for a fee directly to the public,\xe2\x80\x9d\n47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(46), though the Act elsewhere subjects facilities-based carriers to stricter regulation, see \xc2\xa7251(c) (imposing various duties on facilities-based local telephone companies). In the Computer II rules, the Commission subjected facilities-based providers to common-carrier duties not because of the nature of the \xe2\x80\x9coffering\xe2\x80\x9d made by those carriers, but rather because of the concern that local telephone companies would abuse the monopoly power they possessed by virtue of the \xe2\x80\x9cbottleneck\xe2\x80\x9d local telephone facilities they owned. See Computer II Order 474\xe2\x80\x93475, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb6229, 231; Computer III Order 968\xe2\x80\x93969, \xc2\xb612; Verizon, 535 U. S., at 489\xe2\x80\x93490 (describing the naturally monopolistic physical structure of a local telephone exchange). The differential treatment of facilities-based carriers was therefore a function not of the definitions of \xe2\x80\x9cenhanced-service\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cbasic service,\xe2\x80\x9d but instead of a choice by the Commission to regulate more stringently, in its discretion, certain entities that provided enhanced service. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s definitions, however, parallel the definitions of enhanced and basic service, not the facilities-based grounds on which that policy choice was based, and the Commission remains free to impose special regulatory duties on facilities-based ISPs under its Title I ancillary jurisdiction. In fact, it has invited comment on whether it can and should do so. See supra, at 7.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, if the Act fails unambiguously to classify non-facilities-based information-service providers that use telecommunications inputs to provide an information service as \xe2\x80\x9coffer[ors]\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications,\xe2\x80\x9d then it also fails unambiguously to classify facilities-based information-service providers as telecommunications-service offerors; the relevant definitions do not distinguish facilities-based and non-facilities-based carriers. That silence suggests, instead, that the Commission has the discretion to fill the consequent statutory gap.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We also conclude that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction was \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable policy choice for the [Commission] to make\xe2\x80\x9d at Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s second step. 467 U. S., at 845.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents argue that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction is unreasonable because it allows any communications provider to \xe2\x80\x9cevade\xe2\x80\x9d common-carrier regulation by the expedient of bundling information service with telecommunications. Respondents argue that under the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction a telephone company could, for example, offer an information service like voice mail together with telephone service, thereby avoiding common-carrier regulation of its telephone service.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We need not decide whether a construction that resulted in these consequences would be unreasonable because we do not believe that these results follow from the construction the Commission adopted. As we understand the Declaratory Ruling, the Commission did not say that any telecommunications service that is priced or bundled with an information service is automatically unregulated under Title II. The Commission said that a telecommunications input used to provide an information service that is not \xe2\x80\x9cseparable from the data-processing capabilities of the service\xe2\x80\x9d and is instead \xe2\x80\x9cpart and parcel of [the information service] and is integral to [the information service\xe2\x80\x99s] other capabilities\xe2\x80\x9d is not a telecommunications offering. Declaratory Ruling 4823, \xc2\xb639; see supra, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This construction does not leave all information service offerings exempt from mandatory Title II regulation. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is plain,\xe2\x80\x9d for example, that a local telephone company \xe2\x80\x9ccannot escape Title II regulation of its residential local exchange service simply by packaging that service with voice mail.\xe2\x80\x9d Universal Service Report 11530, \xc2\xb660. That is because a telephone company that packages voice mail with telephone service offers a transparent transmission path\xe2\x80\x94telephone service\xe2\x80\x94that transmits information independent of the information-storage capabilities provided by voice mail. For instance, when a person makes a telephone call, his ability to convey and receive information using the call is only trivially affected by the additional voice-mail capability. Equally, were a telephone company to add a time-of-day announcement that played every time the user picked up his telephone, the \xe2\x80\x9ctransparent\xe2\x80\x9d information transmitted in the ensuing call would be only trivially dependent on the information service the announcement provides. By contrast, the high-speed transmission used to provide cable modem service is a functionally integrated component of that service because it transmits data only in connection with the further processing of information and is necessary to provide Internet service. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction therefore was more limited than respondents assume.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents answer that cable modem service does, in fact, provide \xe2\x80\x9ctransparent\xe2\x80\x9d transmission from the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s perspective, but this argument, too, is mistaken. Respondents characterize the \xe2\x80\x9cinformation-service\xe2\x80\x9d offering of Internet access as consisting only of access to a cable company\xe2\x80\x99s e-mail service, its Web page, and the ability it provides consumers to create a personal Web page. When a consumer goes beyond those offerings and accesses content provided by parties other than the cable company, respondents argue, the consumer uses \xe2\x80\x9cpure transmission\xe2\x80\x9d no less than a consumer who purchases phone service together with voice mail.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This argument, we believe, conflicts with the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the nature of cable modem service, an understanding we find to be reasonable. When an end user accesses a third-party\xe2\x80\x99s Web site, the Commission concluded, he is equally using the information service provided by the cable company that offers him Internet access as when he accesses the company\xe2\x80\x99s own Web site, its e-mail service, or his personal Web page. For example, as the Commission found below, part of the information service cable companies provide is access to DNS service. See supra, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. A user cannot reach a third-party\xe2\x80\x99s Web site without DNS, which (among other things) matches the Web site address the end user types into his browser (or \xe2\x80\x9cclicks\xe2\x80\x9d on with his mouse) with the IP address of the Web page\xe2\x80\x99s host server. See P. Albitz & C. Liu, DNS and BIND 10 (4th ed. 2001) (For an Internet user, \xe2\x80\x9cDNS is a must. \xe2\x80\xa6 [N]early all of the Internet\xe2\x80\x99s network services use DNS. That includes the World Wide Web, electronic mail, remote terminal access, and file transfer\xe2\x80\x9d). It is at least reasonable to think of DNS as a \xe2\x80\x9ccapability for \xe2\x80\xa6 acquiring \xe2\x80\xa6 retrieving, utilizing, or making available\xe2\x80\x9d Web site addresses and therefore part of the information service cable companies provide. 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(20).[Footnote 3] Similarly, the Internet service provided by cable companies facilitates access to third-party Web pages by offering consumers the ability to store, or \xe2\x80\x9ccache,\xe2\x80\x9d popular content on local computer servers. See Declaratory Ruling 4810, \xc2\xb617, and n. 76. Cacheing obviates the need for the end user to download anew information from third-party Web sites each time the consumer attempts to access them, thereby increasing the speed of information retrieval. In other words, subscribers can reach third-party Web sites via \xe2\x80\x9cthe World Wide Web, and browse their contents, [only] because their service provider offers the \xe2\x80\x98capability for \xe2\x80\xa6 acquiring, [storing] \xe2\x80\xa6 retrieving [and] utilizing \xe2\x80\xa6 information.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Universal Service Report 11538, \xc2\xb676 (quoting 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(20)). \xe2\x80\x9cThe service that Internet access providers offer to members of the public is Internet access,\xe2\x80\x9d Universal Service Report 11539, \xc2\xb679, not a transparent ability (from the end user\xe2\x80\x99s perspective) to transmit information. We therefore conclude that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction was reasonable.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent MCI, Inc., urges that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of cable modem service is inconsistent with its treatment of DSL service, see supra, at 3 (describing DSL service), and therefore is an arbitrary and capricious deviation from agency policy. See 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7706(2)(A). MCI points out that when local telephone companies began to offer Internet access through DSL technology in addition to telephone service, the Commission applied its Computer II facilities-based classification to them and required them to make the telephone lines used to transmit DSL service available to competing ISPs on nondiscriminatory, common-carrier terms. See supra, at 24 (describing Computer II facilities-based classification of enhanced-service providers); In\xc2\xa0re Deployment of Wireline Services Offering Advanced Telecommunications Capability, 13 FCC Rcd. 24011, 24030\xe2\x80\x9324031, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb636\xe2\x80\x9337 (1998) (hereinafter Wireline Order) (classifying DSL service as a telecommunications service). MCI claims that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to regulate cable companies similarly under Title II is inconsistent with its DSL policy.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude, however, that the Commission provided a reasoned explanation for treating cable modem service differently from DSL service. As we have already noted, see supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, the Commission is free within the limits of reasoned interpretation to change course if it adequately justifies the change.[Footnote 4] It has done so here. The traditional reason for its Computer II common-carrier treatment of facilities-based carriers (including DSL carriers), as the Commission explained, was \xe2\x80\x9cthat the telephone network [was] the primary, if not exclusive, means through which information service providers can gain access to their customers.\xe2\x80\x9d Declaratory Ruling 4825, \xc2\xb644 (emphasis in original; internal quotation marks omitted). The Commission applied the same treatment to DSL service based on that history, rather than on an analysis of contemporaneous market conditions. See Wireline Order 24031, \xc2\xb637 (noting DSL carriers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing obligation\xe2\x80\x9d to offer their transmission facilities to competing ISPs on nondiscriminatory terms).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission in the order under review, by contrast, concluded that changed market conditions warrant different treatment of facilities-based cable companies providing Internet access. Unlike at the time of Computer II, substitute forms of Internet transmission exist today: \xe2\x80\x9c[R]esidential high-speed access to the Internet is evolving over multiple electronic platforms, including wireline, cable, terrestrial wireless and satellite.\xe2\x80\x9d Declaratory Ruling 4802, \xc2\xb66; see also U. S. Telecom Assn. v. FCC, 290 F.\xc2\xa03d 415, 428 (CADC 2002) (noting Commission findings of \xe2\x80\x9crobust competition \xe2\x80\xa6 in the broadband market\xe2\x80\x9d). The Commission concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98broadband services should exist in a minimal regulatory environment that promotes investment and innovation in a competitive market.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Declaratory Ruling 4802, \xc2\xb65. This, the Commission reasoned, warranted treating cable companies unlike the facilities-based enhanced-service providers of the past. Id., at 4825, \xc2\xb644. We find nothing arbitrary about the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s providing a fresh analysis of the problem as applied to the cable industry, which it has never subjected to these rules. This is adequate rational justification for the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s conclusions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents argue, in effect, that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s justification for exempting cable modem service providers from common-carrier regulation applies with similar force to DSL providers. We need not address that argument. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s decision appears to be a first step in an effort to reshape the way the Commission regulates information-service providers; that may be why it has tentatively concluded that DSL service provided by facilities-based telephone companies should also be classified solely as an information service. See In\xc2\xa0re Appropriate Framework for Broadband Access to the Internet over Wireline Facilities, 17 FCC Rcd. 3019, 3030, \xc2\xb620 (2002). The Commission need not immediately apply the policy reasoning in the Declaratory Ruling to all types of information-service providers. It apparently has decided to revisit its longstanding Computer II classification of facilities-based information-service providers incrementally. Any inconsistency between the order under review and the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of DSL service can be adequately addressed when the Commission fully reconsiders its treatment of DSL service and when it decides whether, pursuant to its ancillary Title I jurisdiction, to require cable companies to allow independent ISPs access to their facilities. See supra, at 7, this page. We express no view on those matters. In particular, we express no view on how the Commission should, or lawfully may, classify DSL service.

\n

***

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The questions the Commission resolved in the order under review involve a \xe2\x80\x9csubject matter [that] is technical, complex, and dynamic.\xe2\x80\x9d Gulf Power, 534 U. S., at 339. The Commission is in a far better position to address these questions than we are. Nothing in the Communications Act or the Administrative Procedure Act makes unlawful the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s use of its expert policy judgment to resolve these difficult questions. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the cases are remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion because I believe that the Federal Communications Commission\xe2\x80\x99s decision falls within the scope of its statutorily delegated authority\xe2\x80\x94though perhaps just barely. He states that the Court held in Mead that \xe2\x80\x9csome unspecified degree of formal process\xe2\x80\x9d before the agency \xe2\x80\x9cwas required\xe2\x80\x9d for courts to accord the agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision deference under Chevron U. (formal process is \xe2\x80\x9cat least the only safe harbor\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia has correctly characterized the way in which he, in dissent, characterized the Court\xe2\x80\x99s Mead opinion. 533 U. S., at 245\xe2\x80\x93246. The Court said in Mead that such delegation \xe2\x80\x9cmay be shown in a variety of ways, as by an agency\xe2\x80\x99s power to engage in adjudication or notice-and-comment rulemaking, or by some other indication of a comparable congressional intent.\xe2\x80\x9d 533 U. And the Court repeated that it \xe2\x80\x9chas recognized a variety of indicators that Congress would expect Chevron deference.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 237 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is not surprising that the Court would hold that the existence of a formal rulemaking proceeding is neither a necessary nor a sufficient condition for according Chevron deference to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of a statute. \n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, while I believe Justice Scalia is right in emphasizing that Chevron deference may be appropriate in the absence of formal agency proceedings, Mead should not give him cause for concern.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, with whom Justice Souter and Justice Ginsburg join as to Part I, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Federal Communications Commission (FCC or Commission) has once again attempted to concoct \xe2\x80\x9ca whole new regime of regulation (or of free-market competition)\xe2\x80\x9d under the guise of statutory construction. MCI Telecommunications Corp. v. American Telephone & Telegraph Co., 512 U. S. 218, 234 (1994). Actually, in these cases, it might be more accurate to say the Commission has attempted to establish a whole new regime of non-regulation, which will make for more or less free-market competition, depending upon whose experts are believed. The important fact, however, is that the Commission has chosen to achieve this through an implausible reading of the statute, and has thus exceeded the authority given it by Congress.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first sentence of the FCC ruling under review reads as follows: \xe2\x80\x9cCable modem service provides high-speed access to the Internet, as well as many applications or functions that can be used with that access, over cable system facilities.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Inquiry Concerning High-Speed Access to the Internet Over Cable and Other Facilities, 17 FCC Rcd. 4798, 4799, \xc2\xb61 (2002) (hereinafter Declaratory Ruling) (emphasis added, footnote omitted). Does this mean that cable companies \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d high-speed access to the Internet? Surprisingly not, if the Commission and the Court are to be believed.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It happens that cable-modem service is popular precisely because of the high-speed access it provides, and that, once connected with the Internet, cable-modem subscribers often use Internet applications and functions from providers other than the cable company. Nevertheless, for purposes of classifying what the cable company does, the Commission (with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approval) puts all the emphasis on the rest of the package (the additional \xe2\x80\x9capplications or functions\xe2\x80\x9d). It does so by claiming that the cable company does not \xe2\x80\x9coffe[r]\xe2\x80\x9d its customers high-speed Internet access because it offers that access only in conjunction with particular applications and functions, rather than \xe2\x80\x9cseparate[ly],\xe2\x80\x9d as a \xe2\x80\x9cstand-alone offering.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 4802, \xc2\xb67, 4823, \xc2\xb640.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The focus on the term \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d appropriately derives from the statutory definitions at issue in these cases. Under the Telecommunications Act of 1996, 110 Stat. 56, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98information service\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d involves the capacity to generate, store, interact with, or otherwise manipulate \xe2\x80\x9cinformation via telecommunications.\xe2\x80\x9d 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(20). In turn, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98telecommunications\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is defined as \xe2\x80\x9cthe transmission, between or among points specified by the user, of information of the user\xe2\x80\x99s choosing, without change in the form or content of the information as sent and received.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(43). Finally, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98telecommunications service\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is defined as \xe2\x80\x9cthe offering of telecommunications for a fee directly to the public \xe2\x80\xa6 regardless of the facilities used.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(46). The question here is whether cable-modem-service providers \xe2\x80\x9coffe[r] \xe2\x80\xa6 telecommunications for a fee directly to the public.\xe2\x80\x9d If so, they are subject to Title II regulation as common carriers, like their chief competitors who provide Internet access through other technologies.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court concludes that the word \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d is ambiguous in the sense that it has \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98alternative dictionary definitions\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that might be relevant. Ante, at 18 (quoting National Railroad Passenger Corporation v. Boston & Maine Corp., 503 U. S. 407, 418 (1992)). It seems to me, however, that the analytic problem pertains not really to the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9coffer,\xe2\x80\x9d but to the identity of what is offered. The relevant question is whether the individual components in a package being offered still possess sufficient identity to be described as separate objects of the offer, or whether they have been so changed by their combination with the other components that it is no longer reasonable to describe them in that way.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, I agree (to adapt the Court\xe2\x80\x99s example, ante, at 18) that it would be odd to say that a car dealer is in the business of selling steel or carpets because the cars he sells include both steel frames and carpeting. Nor does the water company sell hydrogen, nor the pet store water (though dogs and cats are largely water at the molecular level). But what is sometimes true is not, as the Court seems to assume, always true. There are instances in which it is ridiculous to deny that one part of a joint offering is being offered merely because it is not offered on a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98stand-alone\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d basis, ante, at 17.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If, for example, I call up a pizzeria and ask whether they offer delivery, both common sense and common \xe2\x80\x9cusage,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 18, would prevent them from answering: \xe2\x80\x9cNo, we do not offer delivery\xe2\x80\x94but if you order a pizza from us, we\xe2\x80\x99ll bake it for you and then bring it to your house.\xe2\x80\x9d The logical response to this would be something on the order of, \xe2\x80\x9cso, you do offer delivery.\xe2\x80\x9d But our pizza-man may continue to deny the obvious and explain, paraphrasing the FCC and the Court: \xe2\x80\x9cNo, even though we bring the pizza to your house, we are not actually \xe2\x80\x98offering\xe2\x80\x99 you delivery, because the delivery that we provide to our end users is \xe2\x80\x98part and parcel\xe2\x80\x99 of our pizzeria-pizza-at-home service and is \xe2\x80\x98integral to its other capabilities.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Cf. Declaratory Ruling 4823, \xc2\xb639; ante, at 16, 26.[Footnote 1] Any reasonable customer would conclude at that point that his interlocutor was either crazy or following some too-clever-by-half legal advice.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, for the inputs of a finished service to qualify as the objects of an \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d (as that term is reasonably understood), it is perhaps a sufficient, but surely not a necessary, condition that the seller offer separately \xe2\x80\x9ceach discrete input that is necessary to providing \xe2\x80\xa6 a finished service,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 19. The pet store may have a policy of selling puppies only with leashes, but any customer will say that it does offer puppies\xe2\x80\x94because a leashed puppy is still a puppy, even though it is not offered on a \xe2\x80\x9cstand-alone\xe2\x80\x9d basis.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite the Court\xe2\x80\x99s mighty labors to prove otherwise, ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9329, the telecommunications component of cable-modem service retains such ample independent identity that it must be regarded as being on offer\xe2\x80\x94especially when seen from the perspective of the consumer or the end user, which the Court purports to find determinative, ante, at 18, 22, 27, 28. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s ruling began by noting that cable-modem service provides both \xe2\x80\x9chigh-speed access to the Internet\xe2\x80\x9d and other \xe2\x80\x9capplications and functions,\xe2\x80\x9d Declaratory Ruling 4799, \xc2\xb61, because that is exactly how any reasonable consumer would perceive it: as consisting of two separate things.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The consumer\xe2\x80\x99s view of the matter is best assessed by asking what other products cable-modem service substitutes for in the marketplace. Broadband Internet service provided by cable companies is one of the three most common forms of Internet service, the other two being dial-up access and broadband Digital Subscriber Line (DSL) service. Ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. In each of the other two, the physical transmission pathway to the Internet is sold\xe2\x80\x94indeed, is legally required to be sold\xe2\x80\x94separately from the Internet functionality. With dial-up access, the physical pathway comes from the telephone company and the Internet service provider (ISP) provides the functionality.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn the case of Internet access, the end user utilizes two different and distinct services. One is the transmission pathway, a telecommunications service that the end user purchases from the telephone company. The second is the Internet access service, which is an enhanced service provided by an ISP\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Th[e] functions [provided by the ISP] are separate from the transmission pathway over which that data travels. The pathway is a regulated telecommunications service; the enhanced service offered over it is not.\xe2\x80\x9d Oxman, The FCC and the Unregulation of the Internet, p.\xc2\xa013 (FCC, Office of Plans and Policy, Working Paper No. 31, July 1999), available at http://www.fcc.gov/\nBureaus/OPP/working_papers/oppwp31.pdf (as visited June 24, 2005, and available in the Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file).[Footnote 2]

\n

\nAs the Court acknowledges, ante, at 29, DSL service has been similar to dial-up service in the respect that the physical connection to the Internet must be offered separately from Internet functionality.[Footnote 3] Thus, customers shopping for dial-up or DSL service will not be able to use the Internet unless they get both someone to provide them with a physical connection and someone to provide them with applications and functions such as e-mail and Web access. It is therefore inevitable that customers will regard the competing cable-modem service as giving them both computing functionality and the physical pipe by which that functionality comes to their computer\xe2\x80\x94both the pizza and the delivery service that nondelivery pizzerias require to be purchased from the cab company.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since the delivery service provided by cable (the broad-band connection between the customer\xe2\x80\x99s computer and the cable company\xe2\x80\x99s computer-processing facilities) is downstream from the computer-processing facilities, there is no question that it merely serves as a conduit for the information services that have already been \xe2\x80\x9cassembled\xe2\x80\x9d by the cable company in its capacity as ISP. This is relevant because of the statutory distinction between an \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications.\xe2\x80\x9d The former involves the capability of getting, processing, and manipulating information. \xc2\xa7153(20). The latter, by contrast, involves no \xe2\x80\x9cchange in the form or content of the information as sent and received.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7153(43). When cable-company-assembled information enters the cable for delivery to the subscriber, the information service is already complete. The information has been (as the statute requires) generated, acquired, stored, transformed, processed, retrieved, utilized, or made available. All that remains is for the information in its final, unaltered form, to be delivered (via telecommunications) to the subscriber.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This reveals the insubstantiality of the fear invoked by both the Commission and the Court: the fear of what will happen to ISPs that do not provide the physical pathway to Internet access, yet still use telecommunications to acquire the pieces necessary to assemble the information that they pass back to their customers. According to this reductio, ante, at 22\xe2\x80\x9324, if cable-modem-service providers are deemed to provide \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service,\xe2\x80\x9d then so must all ISPs because they all \xe2\x80\x9cuse\xe2\x80\x9d telecommunications in providing Internet functionality (by connecting to other parts of the Internet, including Internet backbone providers, for example). In terms of the pizzeria analogy, this is equivalent to saying that, if the pizzeria \xe2\x80\x9coffers\xe2\x80\x9d delivery, all restaurants \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d delivery, because the ingredients of the food they serve their customers have come from other places; no matter how their customers get the food (whether by eating it at the restaurant, or by coming to pick it up themselves), they still consume a product for which delivery was a necessary \xe2\x80\x9cinput.\xe2\x80\x9d This is nonsense. Concluding that delivery of the finished pizza constitutes an \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d of delivery does not require the conclusion that the serving of prepared food includes an \xe2\x80\x9coffer\xe2\x80\x9d of delivery. And that analogy does not even do the point justice, since \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98telecommunications service\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is defined as \xe2\x80\x9cthe offering of telecommunications for a fee directly to the public.\xe2\x80\x9d 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(46) (emphasis added). The ISPs\xe2\x80\x99 use of telecommunications in their processing of information is not offered directly to the public.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cregulatory history\xe2\x80\x9d on which the Court depends so much, ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9325, provides another reason why common-carrier regulation of all ISPs is not a worry. Under its Computer Inquiry rules, which foreshadowed the definitions of \xe2\x80\x9cinformation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications\xe2\x80\x9d services, ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, the Commission forbore from regulating as common carriers \xe2\x80\x9cvalue-added networks\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94non-facilities-based providers who leased basic services from common carriers and bundled them with enhanced services; it said that they, unlike facilities-based providers, would be deemed to provide only enhanced services, ante, at 22.[Footnote 5] That same result can be achieved today under the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authority to forbear from imposing most Title II regulations. 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7160. In fact, the statutory criteria for forbearance\xe2\x80\x94which include what is \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary for the protection of consumers,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent with the public interest,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7160(a)(1), (2), (3)\xe2\x80\x94correspond well with the kinds of policy reasons the Commission has invoked to justify its peculiar construction of \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d to exclude cable-modem service.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court also puts great stock in its conclusion that cable-modem subscribers cannot avoid using information services provided by the cable company in its ISP capacity, even when they only click-through to other ISPs. Ante, at 27\xe2\x80\x9329. For, even if a cable-modem subscriber uses e-mail from another ISP, designates some page not provided by the cable company as his home page, and takes advantage of none of the other standard applications and functions provided by the cable company, he will still be using the cable company\xe2\x80\x99s Domain Name System (DNS) server and, when he goes to popular Web pages, perhaps versions of them that are stored in the cable company\xe2\x80\x99s cache. This argument suffers from at least two problems. First, in the context of telephone services, the Court recognizes a de minimis exception to contamination of a telecommunications service by an information service. Ante, at 26\xe2\x80\x9327. A similar exception would seem to apply to the functions in question here. DNS, in particular, is scarcely more than routing information, which is expressly excluded from the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cinformation service.\xe2\x80\x9d 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153(20).[Footnote 6] Second, it is apparently possible to sell a telecommunications service separately from, although in conjunction with, ISP-like services; that is precisely what happens in the DSL context, and the Commission does not contest that it could be done in the context of cable. The only impediment appears to be the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s failure to require from cable companies the unbundling that it required of facilities-based providers under its Computer Inquiry.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, I must note that, notwithstanding the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s self-congratulatory paean to its deregulatory largesse, e.g., Brief for Federal Petitioners 29\xe2\x80\x9332, it concluded the Declaratory Ruling by asking, as the Court paraphrases, \xe2\x80\x9cwhether under its Title I jurisdiction [the Commission] should require cable companies to offer other ISPs access to their facilities on common-carrier terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7; see also Reply Brief for Federal Petitioners 9; Tr. of Oral Arg. 17. In other words, what the Commission hath given, the Commission may well take away\xe2\x80\x94unless it doesn\xe2\x80\x99t. This is a wonderful illustration of how an experienced agency can (with some assistance from credulous courts) turn statutory constraints into bureaucratic discretions. The main source of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory authority over common carriers is Title II, but the Commission has rendered that inapplicable in this instance by concluding that the definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d is ambiguous and does not (in its current view) apply to cable-modem service. It contemplates, however, altering that (unnecessary) outcome, not by changing the law (i.e., its construction of the Title II definitions), but by reserving the right to change the facts. Under its undefined and sparingly used \xe2\x80\x9cancillary\xe2\x80\x9d powers, the Commission might conclude that it can order cable companies to \xe2\x80\x9cunbundle\xe2\x80\x9d the telecommunications component of cable-modem service.[Footnote 7] And presto, Title II will then apply to them, because they will finally be \xe2\x80\x9coffering\xe2\x80\x9d telecommunications service! Of course, the Commission will still have the statutory power to forbear from regulating them under \xc2\xa7160 (which it has already tentatively concluded it would do, Declaratory Ruling 4847\xe2\x80\x934848, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb694\xe2\x80\x9395). Such M\xc3\xb6bius-strip reasoning mocks the principle that the statute constrains the agency in any meaningful way.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After all is said and done, after all the regulatory cant has been translated, and the smoke of agency expertise blown away, it remains perfectly clear that someone who sells cable-modem service is \xe2\x80\x9coffering\xe2\x80\x9d telecommunications. For that simple reason set forth in the statute, I would affirm the Court of Appeals.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Part III\xe2\x80\x93B of its opinion, the Court continues the administrative-law improvisation project it began four years ago in United States v. Mead Corp., 533 U. S. 218 (2001). To the extent it set forth a comprehensible rule,[Footnote 8] Mead drastically limited the categories of agency action that would qualify for deference under Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837 (1984). For example, the position taken by an agency before the Supreme Court, with full approval of the agency head, would not qualify. Rather, some unspecified degree of formal process was required\xe2\x80\x94or was at least the only safe harbor. See Mead, supra, at 245\xe2\x80\x93246 (Scalia, J., dissenting).[Footnote 9]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This meant that many more issues appropriate for agency determination would reach the courts without benefit of an agency position entitled to Chevron deference, requiring the courts to rule on these issues de novo.[Footnote 10] As I pointed out in dissent, this in turn meant (under the law as it was understood until today)[Footnote 11] that many statutory ambiguities that might be resolved in varying fashions by successive agency administrations, would be resolved finally, conclusively, and forever, by federal judges\xe2\x80\x94producing an \xe2\x80\x9cossification of large portions of our statutory law,\xe2\x80\x9d 533 U. S., at 247. The Court today moves to solve this problem of its own creation by inventing yet another breathtaking novelty: judicial decisions subject to reversal by Executive officers.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Imagine the following sequence of events: FCC action is challenged as ultra vires under the governing statute; the litigation reaches all the way to the Supreme Court of the United States. The Solicitor General sets forth the FCC\xe2\x80\x99s official position (approved by the Commission) regarding interpretation of the statute. Applying Mead, however, the Court denies the agency position Chevron deference, finds that the best interpretation of the statute contradicts the agency\xe2\x80\x99s position, and holds the challenged agency action unlawful. The agency promptly conducts a rulemaking, and adopts a rule that comports with its earlier position\xe2\x80\x94in effect disagreeing with the Supreme Court concerning the best interpretation of the statute. According to today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, the agency is thereupon free to take the action that the Supreme Court found unlawful.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is not only bizarre. It is probably unconstitutional. As we held in Chicago & Southern Air Lines, Inc. v. Waterman S. S. Corp., 333 U. S. 103 (1948), Article III courts do not sit to render decisions that can be reversed or ignored by Executive officers. In that case, the Court of Appeals had determined it had jurisdiction to review an order of the Civil Aeronautics Board awarding an overseas air route. By statute such orders were subject to Presidential approval and the order in question had in fact been approved by the President. Id., at 110\xe2\x80\x93111. In order to avoid any conflict with the President\xe2\x80\x99s foreign-affairs powers, the Court of Appeals concluded that it would review the board\xe2\x80\x99s action \xe2\x80\x9cas a regulatory agent of Congress,\xe2\x80\x9d and the results of that review would remain subject to approval or disapproval by the President. Id., at 112\xe2\x80\x93113. As I noted in my Mead dissent, 533 U. S., at 248, the Court bristled at the suggestion: \xe2\x80\x9cJudgments within the powers vested in courts by the Judiciary Article of the Constitution may not lawfully be revised, overturned or refused faith and credit by another Department of Government.\xe2\x80\x9d Waterman, supra, at 113. That is what today\xe2\x80\x99s decision effectively allows. Even when the agency itself is party to the case in which the Court construes a statute, the agency will be able to disregard that construction and seek Chevron deference for its contrary construction the next time around.[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, like Mead itself, today\xe2\x80\x99s novelty in belated remediation of Mead creates many uncertainties to bedevil the lower courts. A court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is conclusive, the Court says, only if it holds that interpretation to be \xe2\x80\x9cthe only permissible reading of the statute,\xe2\x80\x9d and not if it merely holds it to be \xe2\x80\x9cthe best reading.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. Does this mean that in future statutory-construction cases involving agency-administered statutes courts must specify (presumably in dictum) which of the two they are holding? And what of the many cases decided in the past, before this dictum\xe2\x80\x99s requirement was established? Apparently, silence on the point means that the court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is subject to agency reversal: \xe2\x80\x9cBefore a judicial construction of a statute, whether contained in a precedent or not, may trump an agency\xe2\x80\x99s, the court must hold that the statute unambiguously requires the court\xe2\x80\x99s construction.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 13] Ibid. (I have not made, and as far as I know the Court has not made, any calculation of how many hundreds of past statutory decisions are now agency-reversible because of failure to include an \xe2\x80\x9cunambiguous\xe2\x80\x9d finding. I suspect the number is very large.) How much extra work will it entail for each court confronted with an agency-administered statute to determine whether it has reached, not only the right (\xe2\x80\x9cbest\xe2\x80\x9d) result, but \xe2\x80\x9cthe only permissible\xe2\x80\x9d result? Is the standard for \xe2\x80\x9cunambiguous\xe2\x80\x9d under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new agency-reversal rule the same as the standard for \xe2\x80\x9cunambiguous\xe2\x80\x9d under step one of Chevron? (If so, of course, every case that reaches step two of Chevron will be agency-reversible.) Does the \xe2\x80\x9cunambiguous\xe2\x80\x9d dictum produce stare decisis effect even when a court is affirming, rather than reversing, agency action\xe2\x80\x94so that in the future the agency must adhere to that affirmed interpretation? If so, does the victorious agency have the right to appeal a Court of Appeals judgment in its favor, on the ground that the text in question is in fact not (as the Court of Appeals held) unambiguous, so the agency should be able to change its view in the future?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is indeed a wonderful new world that the Court creates, one full of promise for administrative-law professors in need of tenure articles and, of course, for litigators.[Footnote 14] I would adhere to what has been the rule in the past: When a court interprets a statute without Chevron deference to agency views, its interpretation (whether or not asserted to rest upon an unambiguous text) is the law. I might add that it is a great mystery why any of this is relevant here. Whatever the stare decisis effect of AT&T Corp. v. Portland, 216 F.\xc2\xa03d 871 (CA9 2000), in the Ninth Circuit, it surely does not govern this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. And\xe2\x80\x94despite the Court\xe2\x80\x99s peculiar, self-abnegating suggestion to the contrary, ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x94the Ninth Circuit would already be obliged to abandon Portland\xe2\x80\x99s holding in the face of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xe2\x80\x9ctelecommunications service\xe2\x80\x9d is entitled to deference and is reasonable. It is a sadness that the Court should go so far out of its way to make bad law.

\n
