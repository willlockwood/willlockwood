\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

WALLACE v. KATO et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 05\xe2\x80\x931240.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 6, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 21, 2007

\n

In January 1994, Chicago police arrested petitioner, a minor, for murder. He was tried and convicted, but the charges were ultimately dropped in April 2002. In April 2003, he filed this suit under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 against the city and several of its officers, seeking damages for, inter alia, his unlawful arrest in violation of the Fourth Amendment. The District Court granted respondents summary judgment, and the Seventh Circuit affirmed, ruling that the \xc2\xa71983 suit was time barred because petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s cause of action accrued at the time of his arrest, not when his conviction was later set aside.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The statute of limitations upon a \xc2\xa71983 claim seeking damages for a false arrest in violation of the Fourth Amendment, where the arrest is followed by criminal proceedings, begins to run at the time the claimant becomes detained pursuant to legal process. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The statute of limitations in a \xc2\xa71983 suit is that provided by the State for personal-injury torts, e.g., Owens v. Okure, 488 U. S. 235, 249\xe2\x80\x93250; here, two years under Illinois law. For false imprisonment and its subspecies false arrest, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he \xe2\x80\xa6 cause[s] of action provid[ing] the closest analogy to claims of the type considered here,\xe2\x80\x9d Heck v. Humphrey, 512 U. S. 477, 484, the statute of limitations begins to run when the alleged false imprisonment ends, see, e.g., 4 Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7899, Comment c, that is, in the present context, when the victim becomes held pursuant to legal process, see, e.g., Heck, supra, at 484. Thus, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s false imprisonment did not end, as he contends, when he was released from custody after the State dropped the charges against him, but rather when he appeared before the examining magistrate and was bound over for trial. Since more than two years elapsed between that date and the filing of this suit\xe2\x80\x94even leaving out of the count the period before he reached his majority\xe2\x80\x94the action was time barred. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s contention that Heck compels the conclusion that his suit could not accrue until the State dropped its charges against him is rejected. The Heck Court held that \xe2\x80\x9cin order to recover damages for allegedly unconstitutional conviction or imprisonment, or for other harm caused by actions whose unlawfulness would render a conviction or sentence invalid, a \xc2\xa71983 plaintiff must prove that the conviction or sentence has been [set aside]. A claim for damages bearing that relationship to a conviction or sentence that has not been so invalidated is not cognizable under \xc2\xa71983.\xe2\x80\x9d 512 U. S., at 486\xe2\x80\x93487. Even assuming that the Heck deferred-accrual rule would be applied to the date petitioner was first held pursuant to legal process, there was in existence at that time no criminal conviction that the cause of action would impugn. What petitioner seeks is the adoption of a principle going well beyond Heck: that an action which would impugn an anticipated future conviction cannot be brought until that conviction occurs and is set aside. The impracticality of such a speculative rule is obvious.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The fact that \xc2\xa71983 actions sometimes accrue before the setting aside of\xe2\x80\x94indeed, even before the existence of\xe2\x80\x94the related criminal conviction raises the question whether, assuming the Heck bar takes effect when the later conviction is obtained, the statute of limitations on the once valid cause of action is tolled as long as the Heck bar subsists. However, this Court generally refers to state-law tolling rules, e.g., Hardin v. Straub, 490 U. S. 536, 538\xe2\x80\x93539, and is unaware of Illinois cases providing tolling in even remotely comparable circumstances. Moreover, a federal tolling rule to this effect would create a jurisprudential limbo in which it would not be known whether tolling is appropriate by reason of the Heck bar until it is established that the newly entered conviction would be impugned by the not-yet-filed, and thus utterly indeterminate, \xc2\xa71983 claim. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

440 F.\xc2\xa03d 421, affirmed.

ANDRE WALLACE, PETITIONER v. KRISTEN\nKATO et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[February 21, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner filed suit under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, seeking damages for an arrest that violated the Fourth Amendment. We decide whether his suit is timely.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On January 17, 1994, John Handy was shot to death in the city of Chicago. Sometime around 8 p.m. two days later, Chicago police officers located petitioner, then 15 years of age, and transported him to a police station for questioning. After interrogations that lasted into the early morning hours the next day, petitioner agreed to confess to Handy\xe2\x80\x99s murder. An assistant state\xe2\x80\x99s attorney prepared a statement to this effect, and petitioner signed it, at the same time waiving his Miranda rights.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Prior to trial in the Circuit Court of Cook County, petitioner unsuccessfully attempted to suppress his station house statements as the product of an unlawful arrest. He was convicted of first-degree murder and sentenced to 26 years in prison. On direct appeal, the Appellate Court of Illinois held that officers had arrested petitioner without probable cause, in violation of the Fourth Amendment. People v. Wallace, 299 Ill. App. 3d 9, 17\xe2\x80\x9318, 701 N.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 87, 94 (1998). According to that court (whose determination we are not reviewing here), even assuming petitioner willingly accompanied police to the station, his presence there \xe2\x80\x9cescalated to an involuntary seizure prior to his formal arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 18, 701 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 94. After another round of appeals, the Appellate Court concluded on August 31, 2001, that the effect of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s illegal arrest had not been sufficiently attenuated to render his statements admissible, see Brown v. Illinois, 422 U. S. 590 (1975), and remanded for a new trial. Judgt. order reported sub nom. People v. Wallace, 324 Ill. App. 3d 1139, 805 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 756 (2001). On April 10, 2002, prosecutors dropped the charges against petitioner.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On April 2, 2003, petitioner filed this \xc2\xa71983 suit against the city of Chicago and several Chicago police officers, seeking damages arising from, inter alia, his unlawful arrest.[Footnote 1] The District Court granted summary judgment to respondents and the Court of Appeals affirmed. According to the Seventh Circuit, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa71983 suit was time barred because his cause of action accrued at the time of his arrest, and not when his conviction was later set aside. Wallace v. Chicago, 440 F. 3d 421, 427 (2006). We granted certiorari, 547 U. S. ___ (2006).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 1983 provides a federal cause of action, but in several respects relevant here federal law looks to the law of the State in which the cause of action arose. This is so for the length of the statute of limitations: It is that which the State provides for personal-injury torts. Owens v. Okure, 488 U. S. 235, 249\xe2\x80\x93250 (1989); Wilson v. Garcia, 471 U. S. 261, 279\xe2\x80\x93280 (1985). The parties agree that under Illinois law, this period is two years. Ill. Comp. Stat., ch. 735, \xc2\xa75/13\xe2\x80\x93202 (West 2003). Thus, if the statute on petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s cause of action began to run at the time of his unlawful arrest, or even at the time he was ordered held by a magistrate, his \xc2\xa71983 suit was plainly dilatory, even according him tolling for the two-plus years of his minority, see \xc2\xa75/13\xe2\x80\x93211. But if, as the dissenting judge argued below, the commencement date for running of the statute is governed by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Heck v. Humphrey, 512 U. S. 477 (1994), that date may be the date on which petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction was vacated, in which case the \xc2\xa71983 suit would have been timely filed.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While we have never stated so expressly, the accrual date of a \xc2\xa71983 cause of action is a question of federal law that is not resolved by reference to state law. The parties agree, the Seventh Circuit in this case so held, see 440 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 424, and we are aware of no federal court of appeals holding to the contrary. Aspects of \xc2\xa71983 which are not governed by reference to state law are governed by federal rules conforming in general to common-law tort principles. See Heck, supra, at 483; Carey v. Piphus, 435 U. S. 247, 257\xe2\x80\x93258 (1978). Under those principles, it is \xe2\x80\x9cthe standard rule that [accrual occurs] when the plaintiff has \xe2\x80\x98a complete and present cause of action.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bay Area Laundry and Dry Cleaning Pension Trust Fund v. Ferbar Corp. of Cal., 522 U. S. 192, 201 (1997) (quoting Rawlings v. Ray, 312 U. S. 96, 98 (1941)), that is, when \xe2\x80\x9cthe plaintiff can file suit and obtain relief,\xe2\x80\x9d Bay Area Laundry, supra, at 201. There can be no dispute that petitioner could have filed suit as soon as the allegedly wrongful arrest occurred, subjecting him to the harm of involuntary detention, so the statute of limitations would normally commence to run from that date.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is, however, a refinement to be considered, arising from the common law\xe2\x80\x99s distinctive treatment of the torts of false arrest and false imprisonment, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he \xe2\x80\xa6 cause[s] of action [that] provid[e] the closest analogy to claims of the type considered here,\xe2\x80\x9d Heck, supra, at 484. See 1 D. Dobbs, Law of Torts \xc2\xa747, p.\xc2\xa088 (2001). False arrest and false imprisonment overlap; the former is a species of the latter. \xe2\x80\x9cEvery confinement of the person is an imprisonment, whether it be in a common prison or in a private house, or in the stocks, or even by forcibly detaining one in the public streets; and when a man is lawfully in a house, it is imprisonment to prevent him from leaving the room in which he is.\xe2\x80\x9d M. Newell, Law of Malicious Prosecution, False Imprisonment, and Abuse of Legal Process \xc2\xa72, p.\xc2\xa057 (1892) (footnotes omitted). See also 7 S. Speiser, C. Krause, & A. Gans, American Law of Torts \xc2\xa727:2, pp.\xc2\xa0940\xe2\x80\x93942 (1990). We shall thus refer to the two torts together as false imprisonment. That tort provides the proper analogy to the cause of action asserted against the present respondents for the following reason: The sort of unlawful detention remediable by the tort of false imprisonment is detention without legal process, see, e.g., W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, R. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on Law of Torts \xc2\xa711, p.\xc2\xa054, \xc2\xa7119, pp.\xc2\xa0885\xe2\x80\x93886 (5th ed. 1984); 7 Speiser, supra, \xc2\xa727:2, at 943\xe2\x80\x93944, and the allegations before us arise from respondents\xe2\x80\x99 detention of petitioner without legal process in January 1994. They did not have a warrant for his arrest.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The running of the statute of limitations on false imprisonment is subject to a distinctive rule\xe2\x80\x94dictated, perhaps, by the reality that the victim may not be able to sue while he is still imprisoned: \xe2\x80\x9cLimitations begin to run against an action for false imprisonment when the alleged false imprisonment ends.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 H. Wood, Limitation of Actions \xc2\xa7187d(4), p.\xc2\xa0878 (4th rev. ed. 1916); see also 4 Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7899, Comment c (1977); A. Underhill, Principles of Law of Torts 202 (1881). Thus, to determine the beginning of the limitations period in this case, we must determine when petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s false imprisonment came to an end.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reflective of the fact that false imprisonment consists of detention without legal process, a false imprisonment ends once the victim becomes held pursuant to such process\xe2\x80\x94when, for example, he is bound over by a magistrate or arraigned on charges. Dobbs, supra, \xc2\xa739, at 74, n.\xc2\xa02; Keeton, supra, \xc2\xa7119, at 888; H. Stephen, Actions for Malicious Prosecution 120\xe2\x80\x93123 (1888). Thereafter, unlawful detention forms part of the damages for the \xe2\x80\x9centirely distinct\xe2\x80\x9d tort of malicious prosecution, which remedies detention accompanied, not by absence of legal process, but by wrongful institution of legal process.[Footnote 2] Keeton, supra, \xc2\xa7119, at 885\xe2\x80\x93886; see 1 F. Harper, F. James, & O. Gray, Law of Torts \xc2\xa73.9, p.\xc2\xa03:36 (3d ed. 1996); 7 Speiser, supra, \xc2\xa727:2, at 943\xe2\x80\x93945. \xe2\x80\x9cIf there is a false arrest claim, damages for that claim cover the time of detention up until issuance of process or arraignment, but not more. From that point on, any damages recoverable must be based on a malicious prosecution claim and on the wrongful use of judicial process rather than detention itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Keeton, supra, \xc2\xa7119, at 888; see also Heck, supra, at 484; 8 Speiser, supra, \xc2\xa728:15, at 80. Thus, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s contention that his false imprisonment ended upon his release from custody, after the State dropped the charges against him, must be rejected. It ended much earlier, when legal process was initiated against him, and the statute would have begun to run from that date, but for its tolling by reason of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s minority.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner asserts that the date of his release from custody must be the relevant date in the circumstances of the present suit, since he is seeking damages up to that time. The theory of his complaint is that the initial Fourth Amendment violation set the wheels in motion for his subsequent conviction and detention: The unlawful arrest led to the coerced confession, which was introduced at his trial, producing his conviction and incarceration. As we have just explained, at common law damages for detention after issuance of process or arraignment would be attributable to a tort other than the unlawful arrest alleged in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s complaint\xe2\x80\x94and probably a tort chargeable to defendants other than the respondents here. Even assuming, however, that all damages for detention pursuant to legal process could be regarded as consequential damages attributable to the unlawful arrest, that would not alter the commencement date for the statute of limitations. \xe2\x80\x9cUnder the traditional rule of accrual \xe2\x80\xa6 the tort cause of action accrues, and the statute of limitations commences to run, when the wrongful act or omission results in damages. The cause of action accrues even though the full extent of the injury is not then known or predictable.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 C. Corman, Limitation of Actions \xc2\xa77.4.1, pp.\xc2\xa0526\xe2\x80\x93527 (1991) (footnotes omitted); see also 54 C.\xc2\xa0J.\xc2\xa0S., Limitations of Actions \xc2\xa7112, p.\xc2\xa0150 (2005). Were it otherwise, the statute would begin to run only after a plaintiff became satisfied that he had been harmed enough, placing the supposed statute of repose in the sole hands of the party seeking relief.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude that the statute of limitations on petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa71983 claim commenced to run when he appeared before the examining magistrate and was bound over for trial. Since more than two years elapsed between that date and the filing of this suit\xe2\x80\x94even leaving out of the count the period before he reached his majority\xe2\x80\x94the action was time barred.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This would end the matter, were it not for petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s contention that Heck v. Humphrey, 512 U. S., at 477, compels the conclusion that his suit could not accrue until the State dropped its charges against him. In Heck, a state prisoner filed suit under \xc2\xa71983 raising claims which, if true, would have established the invalidity of his outstanding conviction. We analogized his suit to one for malicious prosecution, an element of which is the favorable termination of criminal proceedings. Id., at 484. We said:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n order to recover damages for allegedly unconstitutional conviction or imprisonment, or for other harm caused by actions whose unlawfulness would render a conviction or sentence invalid, a \xc2\xa71983 plaintiff must prove that the conviction or sentence has been reversed on direct appeal, expunged by executive order, declared invalid by a state tribunal authorized to make such determination, or called into question by a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s issuance of a writ of habeas corpus, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. A claim for damages bearing that relationship to a conviction or sentence that has not been so invalidated is not cognizable under \xc2\xa71983.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 486\xe2\x80\x93487 (footnote omitted).

\n

\nWe rested this conclusion upon \xe2\x80\x9cthe hoary principle that civil tort actions are not appropriate vehicles for challenging the validity of outstanding criminal judgments.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 486. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Congress,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d we said, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98has determined that habeas corpus is the appropriate remedy for state prisoners attacking the validity of the fact or length of their confinement, and that specific determination must override the general terms of \xc2\xa71983.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 482 (quoting Preiser v. Rodriguez, 411 U. S. 475, 490 (1973)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the above excerpts show, the Heck rule for deferred accrual is called into play only when there exists \xe2\x80\x9ca conviction or sentence that has not been \xe2\x80\xa6 invalidated,\xe2\x80\x9d that is to say, an \xe2\x80\x9coutstanding criminal judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d It delays what would otherwise be the accrual date of a tort action until the setting aside of an extant conviction which success in that tort action would impugn. We assume that, for purposes of the present tort action, the Heck principle would be applied not to the date of accrual but to the date on which the statute of limitations began to run, that is, the date petitioner became held pursuant to legal process. See supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. Even at that later time, there was in existence no criminal conviction that the cause of action would impugn; indeed, there may not even have been an indictment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What petitioner seeks, in other words, is the adoption of a principle that goes well beyond Heck: that an action which would impugn an anticipated future conviction cannot be brought until that conviction occurs and is set aside. The impracticality of such a rule should be obvious. In an action for false arrest it would require the plaintiff (and if he brings suit promptly, the court) to speculate about whether a prosecution will be brought, whether it will result in conviction, and whether the pending civil action will impugn that verdict, see Heck, 512 U. S., at 487, n.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x94all this at a time when it can hardly be known what evidence the prosecution has in its possession. And what if the plaintiff (or the court) guesses wrong, and the anticipated future conviction never occurs, because of acquittal or dismissal? Does that event (instead of the Heck-required setting aside of the extant conviction) trigger accrual of the cause of action? Or what if prosecution never occurs\xe2\x80\x94what will the trigger be then?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are not disposed to embrace this bizarre extension of Heck. If a plaintiff files a false arrest claim before he has been convicted (or files any other claim related to rulings that will likely be made in a pending or anticipated criminal trial), it is within the power of the district court, and in accord with common practice, to stay the civil action until the criminal case or the likelihood of a criminal case is ended. See id., at 487\xe2\x80\x93488, n.\xc2\xa08 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cabstention may be an appropriate response to the parallel state-court proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d); Quackenbush v. Allstate Ins. Co., 517 U. S. 706, 730 (1996). If the plaintiff is ultimately convicted, and if the stayed civil suit would impugn that conviction, Heck will require dismissal; otherwise, the civil action will proceed, absent some other bar to suit. Edwards v. Balisok, 520 U. S. 641, 649 (1997); Heck, 512 U. S., at 487.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is, however, one complication that we must address here. It arises from the fact that \xc2\xa71983 actions, unlike the tort of malicious prosecution which Heck took as its model, see id., at 484, sometimes accrue before the setting aside of\xe2\x80\x94indeed, even before the existence of\xe2\x80\x94the related criminal conviction. That of course is the case here, and it raises the question whether, assuming that the Heck bar takes effect when the later conviction is obtained, the statute of limitations on the once valid cause of action is tolled as long as the Heck bar subsists. In the context of the present case: If petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction on April 19, 1996, caused the statute of limitations on his (possibly) impugning but yet-to-be-filed cause of action to be tolled until that conviction was set aside, his filing here would have been timely.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have generally referred to state law for tolling rules, just as we have for the length of statutes of limitation. Hardin v. Straub, 490 U. S. 536, 538\xe2\x80\x93539 (1989); Board of Regents of Univ. of State of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. Tomanio, 446 U. S. 478, 484\xe2\x80\x93486 (1980). Petitioner has not brought to our attention, nor are we aware of, Illinois cases providing tolling in even remotely comparable circumstances. (Indeed, petitioner did not even argue for such tolling below, though he supported its suggestion at oral argument.) Nor would we be inclined to adopt a federal tolling rule to this effect. Under such a regime, it would not be known whether tolling is appropriate by reason of the Heck bar until it is established that the newly entered conviction would be impugned by the not-yet-filed, and thus utterly indeterminate, \xc2\xa71983 claim.[Footnote 4] It would hardly be desirable to place the question of tolling vel non in this jurisprudential limbo, leaving it to be determined by those later events, and then pronouncing it retroactively. Defendants need to be on notice to preserve beyond the normal limitations period evidence that will be needed for their defense; and a statute that becomes retroactively extended, by the action of the plaintiff in crafting a conviction-impugning cause of action, is hardly a statute of repose.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer argues in dissent that equitable tolling should apply \xe2\x80\x9cso long as the issues that [a \xc2\xa71983] claim would raise are being pursued in state court.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 4. We know of no support (nor does the dissent suggest any) for the far-reaching proposition that equitable tolling is appropriate to avoid the risk of concurrent litigation. As best we can tell, the only rationale for such a rule is the concern that \xe2\x80\x9cpetitioner would have had to divide his attention between criminal and civil cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 1. But when has it been the law that a criminal defendant, or a potential criminal defendant, is absolved from all other responsibilities that the law would otherwise place upon him? If a defendant has a breach-of-contract claim against the prime contractor for his new home, is he entitled to tolling for that as well while his criminal case is pending? Equitable tolling is a rare remedy to be applied in unusual circumstances, not a cure-all for an entirely common state of affairs. Besides its never-heard-of-before quality, the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s proposal suffers from a more ironic flaw. Although the dissent criticizes us for having to develop a system of stays and dismissals, it should be obvious that the omnibus tolling solution will require the same. Despite the existence of the new tolling rule, some (if not most) plaintiffs will nevertheless file suit before or during state criminal proceedings. How does the dissent propose to handle such suits? Finally, the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s contention that law enforcement officers would prefer the possibility of a later \xc2\xa71983 suit to the more likely reality of an immediate filing, post at 5, is both implausible and contradicted by those who know best. As no fewer than 11 States have informed us in this litigation, \xe2\x80\x9cStates and municipalities have a strong interest in timely notice of alleged misconduct by their agents.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for State of Illinois et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 18.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that the statute of limitations upon a \xc2\xa71983 claim seeking damages for a false arrest in violation of the Fourth Amendment, where the arrest is followed by criminal proceedings, begins to run at the time the claimant becomes detained pursuant to legal process. Since in the present case this occurred (with appropriate tolling for the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s minority) more than two years before the complaint was filed, the suit was out of time. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.

\xc2\xa0We have never explored the contours of a Fourth Amendment malicious-prosecution suit under \xc2\xa71983, see Albright v. Oliver, 510 U. S. 266, 270\xe2\x80\x93271, 275 (1994) (plurality opinion), and we do not do so here. See generally 1 M. Schwartz, Section 1983 Litigation \xc2\xa73.18[C], pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x93605 to 3\xe2\x80\x93629 (4th ed. 2004) (noting a range of approaches in the lower courts). Assuming without deciding that such a claim is cognizable under \xc2\xa71983, petitioner has not made one. Petitioner did not include such a claim in his complaint. He in fact abandoned a state-law malicious-prosecution claim in the District Court, and stated, in his opposition to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 first motion for summary judgment, that \xe2\x80\x9cPlaintiff does not seek to raise \xe2\x80\xa6 a malicious prosecution claim under \xc2\xa71983,\xe2\x80\x9d Record, Doc. 17, p.\xc2\xa03, n.\xc2\xa05. In this Court, he has told us that respondents are \xe2\x80\x9cmistaken in characterizing petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s cause of action as involving \xe2\x80\x98unwarranted prosecution.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 12.

\nFootnote 3

\xc2\xa0This is not to say, of course, that petitioner could not have filed suit immediately upon his false arrest. While the statute of limitations did not begin to run until petitioner became detained pursuant to legal process, he was injured and suffered damages at the moment of his arrest, and was entitled to bring suit at that time. See Adler v. Beverly Hills Hospital, 594 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa02d 153, 156 (Tex. Civ. App. 1980) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe may concede that a person falsely imprisoned has the right to sue on the first day for his detention\xe2\x80\x9d).

\nFootnote 4

\xc2\xa0Had petitioner filed suit upon his arrest and had his suit then been dismissed under Heck, the statute of limitations, absent tolling, would have run by the time he obtained reversal of his conviction. If under those circumstances he were not allowed to refile his suit, Heck would produce immunity from \xc2\xa71983 liability, a result surely not intended. Because in the present case petitioner did not file his suit within the limitations period, we need not decide, had he done so, how much time he would have had to refile the suit once the Heck bar was removed.

\nFootnote 5

\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens reaches the same result by arguing that, under Stone v. Powell, 428 U. S. 465 (1976), the Heck bar can never come into play in a \xc2\xa71983 suit seeking damages for a Fourth Amendment violation, so that \xe2\x80\x9ca habeas remedy was never available to [petitioner] in the first place.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 3 (opinion concurring in judgment). This reads Stone to say more than it does. Under Stone, Fourth Amendment violations are generally not cognizable on federal habeas, but they are cognizable when the State has failed to provide the habeas petitioner \xe2\x80\x9can opportunity for full and fair litigation of a Fourth Amendment claim.\xe2\x80\x9d 428 U. S., at 482. Federal habeas petitioners have sometimes succeeded in arguing that Stone\xe2\x80\x99s general prohibition does not apply. See, e.g., Herrera v. LeMaster, 225 F.\xc2\xa03d 1176, 1178 (2000), aff\xe2\x80\x99d on this point, 301 F.\xc2\xa03d 1192, 1195, n. 4 (CA10 2002) (en banc); United States ex rel. Bostick v. Peters, 3 F.\xc2\xa03d 1023, 1029 (CA7 1993); Agee v. White, 809 F.\xc2\xa02d 1487, 1490 (CA11 1987); Doescher v. Estelle, 666 F.\xc2\xa02d 285, 287 (CA5 1982); Boyd v. Mintz, 631 F.\xc2\xa02d 247, 250\xe2\x80\x93251 (CA3 1980); see also 2 R. Hertz & J. Liebman, Federal Habeas Corpus Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa727.1\xe2\x80\x9327.3, pp. 1373\xe2\x80\x931389 (5th ed. 2005). At the time of a Fourth Amendment wrong, and at the time of conviction, it cannot be known whether a prospective \xc2\xa71983 plaintiff will receive a full and fair opportunity to litigate his Fourth Amendment claim. It thus remains the case that a conflict with the federal habeas statute is possible, that a Fourth Amendment claim can necessarily imply the invalidity of a conviction, and that if it does it must, under Heck, be dismissed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Insofar as Justice Stevens simply suggests that Heck has no bearing here because petitioner received a full and fair opportunity to litigate his Fourth Amendment claim in state court, the argument is equally untenable. At the time that petitioner became detained pursuant to legal process, it was impossible to predict whether this would be true. And even at the point when his limitations period ended, state proceedings on his conviction were ongoing; full and fair opportunity up to that point was not enough. Stone requires full and fair opportunity to litigate a Fourth Amendment claim \xe2\x80\x9cat trial and on direct review.\xe2\x80\x9d 428 U. S., at 494\xe2\x80\x93495, n. 37 (emphasis added).

ANDRE WALLACE, PETITIONER v. KRISTEN\nKATO et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[February 21, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Souter joins, concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While I do not disagree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion, I reach it by a more direct route. The alleged Fourth Amendment violation at issue in this case had two distinct consequences for petitioner: First, it provided him with a federal cause of action for damages under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, and second, it provided him with an objection to the admissibility of certain evidence in his state criminal trial. The crux of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument before this Court is that Heck v. Humphrey, 512 U. S. 477 (1994), provides the appropriate rule of accrual for his \xc2\xa71983 claim. As both he and the majority note, Heck held that

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cin order to recover damages for allegedly unconstitutional conviction or imprisonment, or for other harm caused by actions whose unlawfulness would render a conviction or sentence invalid, a \xc2\xa71983 plaintiff must prove that the conviction or sentence has been reversed on direct appeal, expunged by executive order, declared invalid by a state tribunal authorized to make such determination, or called into question by a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s issuance of a writ of habeas corpus, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. \xe2\x80\xa6 Thus, when a state prisoner seeks damages in a \xc2\xa71983 suit, the district court must consider whether a judgment in favor of the plaintiff would necessarily imply the invalidity of his conviction or sentence; if it would, the complaint must be dismissed unless the plaintiff can demonstrate that the conviction or sentence has already been invalidated.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 486\xe2\x80\x93487 (footnote omitted).

\n

\nRelying on this principle, petitioner contends that his federal cause of action did not accrue until after the criminal charges against him were dropped.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike the majority, my analysis would not depend on any common-law tort analogies.[Footnote 1] Instead, I would begin where all nine Justices began in Heck. That case, we unanimously agreed, required the Court to reconcile \xc2\xa71983 with the federal habeas corpus statute.[Footnote 2] In concluding that Heck\xe2\x80\x99s damages claim was not cognizable under \xc2\xa71983, we found that the writ of habeas corpus, and not \xc2\xa71983, affords the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98appropriate remedy for state prisoners attacking the validity of the fact or length of their confinement.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 482 (quoting Preiser v. Rodriguez, 411 U. S. 475, 490 (1973)). Given our holding in Stone v. Powell, 428 U. S. 465, 481\xe2\x80\x93482 (1976), however, that writ cannot provide a remedy for this petitioner. And because a habeas remedy was never available to him in the first place, Heck cannot postpone the accrual of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa71983 Fourth Amendment claim.[Footnote 3] So while it may well be appropriate to stay the trial of claims of this kind until after the completion of state proceedings, see, e.g., Quackenbush v. Allstate Ins. Co., 517 U. S. 706, 731 (1996); cf. Younger v. Harris, 401 U. S. 37 (1971), I am aware of no legal basis for holding that the cause of action has not accrued once the Fourth Amendment violation has been completed.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court regrettably lets the perfect become the enemy of the good. It eschews my reasoning because \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ederal habeas petitioners have sometimes succeeded in arguing that Stone\xe2\x80\x99s general prohibition does not apply.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11, n.\xc2\xa05 (emphasis added). However, in the vast run of cases, a State will provide a habeas petitioner with \xe2\x80\x9can opportunity for full and fair litigation of a Fourth Amendment claim,\xe2\x80\x9d Stone, 428 U. S., at 482, and Heck will not apply. It is always possible to find aberrant examples in the law, but we should not craft rules for the needle rather than the haystack in an area like this.

\nFootnote 1

\xc2\xa0See Heck, 512 U. S., at, 492 (Souter, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cCommon-law tort rules can provide a \xe2\x80\x98starting point for the inquiry under \xc2\xa71983,\xe2\x80\x99 Carey v. Piphus, 435 U. S. 247, 258 (1978), but . . . [a]t the same time, we have consistently refused to allow common-law analogies to displace statutory analysis, declining to import even well-settled common-law rules into \xc2\xa71983 \xe2\x80\x98if [the statute\xe2\x80\x99s] history or purpose counsel against applying [such rules] in \xc2\xa71983 actions.\xe2\x80\x99 Wyatt v. Cole, 504 U. S. 158, 164 (1992)\xe2\x80\x9d (brackets in original)).

\nFootnote 2

\xc2\xa0See id., at 480 (\xe2\x80\x9cThis case lies at the intersection of the two most fertile sources of federal-court prisoner litigation\xe2\x80\x94the Civil Rights Act of 1871, Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, as amended, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, and the federal habeas corpus statute, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 491 (Souter, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court begins its analysis as I would, by observing that \xe2\x80\x98this case lies at the intersection of the two most fertile sources of federal-court prisoner litigation\xe2\x80\x94the Civil Rights Act of 1871, . . . 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, and the federal habeas corpus statute, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 490 (Thomas, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court and Justice Souter correctly begin their analyses with the realization that \xe2\x80\x98this case lies at the intersection of . . . the Civil Rights Act of 1871, Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, as amended, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C \xc2\xa71983, and the federal habeas corpus statute, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\nFootnote 3

\xc2\xa0See Spencer v. Kemna, 523 U. S. 1, 21 (1998) (Souter, J., joined by O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, Ginsburg, and Breyer, JJ., concurring) (concluding that a plaintiff may bring \xc2\xa71983 claim if he could not bring the same claim under the habeas statute); ibid. (Ginsburg, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cI have come to agree with Justice Souter\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning: Individuals without recourse to the habeas statute because they are not \xe2\x80\x98in custody\xe2\x80\x99 . . . fit within \xc2\xa71983\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98broad reach\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 25, n.\xc2\xa08 (Stevens, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cGiven the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that petitioner does not have a remedy under the habeas statute, it is perfectly clear, as Justice Souter explains, that he may bring an action under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983\xe2\x80\x9d).

\nBREYER, J., DISSENTING
\nWALLACE V. KATO
\n549 U. S. ____ (2007)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 05-1240

ANDRE WALLACE, PETITIONER v. KRISTEN\nKATO et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[February 21, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Ginsburg joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that the accrual date of a 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 claim is not postponed by the presence of a possible bar to suit under Heck v. Humphrey, 512 U. S. 477 (1994). I also agree with the rest of the Court and with Justice Stevens that had petitioner timely filed his \xc2\xa71983 case, the Federal District Court might have found it appropriate to stay the trial of his claims until the completion of state proceedings. E.g., Quackenbush v. Allstate Ins. Co., 517 U. S. 706, 731 (1996). In the absence of a stay, a litigant like petitioner would have had to divide his attention between criminal and civil cases with attendant risks of loss of time and energy as well as of inconsistent findings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding, however, simply leads to the question of what is to happen when, for example, the possibility of a Heck problem prevents the court from considering the merits of a \xc2\xa71983 claim. And I disagree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s insistence upon a rule of law that would require immediate filing, followed by an uncertain system of stays, dismissals, and possible refiling. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, n.\xc2\xa04 (majority opinion); ante, at 3 (Stevens,\xc2\xa0J., concurring in judgment). I disagree because there is a well-established legal tool better able to deal with the problems presented by this type of suit.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Where a \xe2\x80\x9cplaintiff because of disability, irremediable lack of information, or other circumstances beyond his control just cannot reasonably be expected to sue in time,\xe2\x80\x9d courts have applied a doctrine of \xe2\x80\x9cequitable tolling.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller v. Runyon, 77 F.\xc2\xa03d 189, 191 (CA7 1996) (Posner, C.\xc2\xa0J.). The doctrine tolls the running of the limitations period until the disabling circumstance can be overcome. (This is why the limitations period does not run against a falsely arrested person until his false imprisonment ends. His action has certainly accrued because, as the majority recognizes, he can file his claim immediately if he is able to do so. Ante, at 4, 6, n.\xc2\xa03.) 77 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 191; see also Cada v. Baxter Healthcare Corp., 920 F.\xc2\xa02d 446, 450\xe2\x80\x93453 (CA7 1990).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In particular, equitable tolling could apply where a \xc2\xa71983 plaintiff reasonably claims that the unlawful behavior of which he complains was, or will be, necessary to a criminal conviction. It could toll the running of the limitations period: (1) from the time charges are brought until the time they are dismissed or the defendant is acquitted or convicted, and (2) thereafter during any period in which the criminal defendant challenges a conviction (on direct appeal, on state collateral challenge, or on federal habeas) and reasonably asserts the behavior underlying the \xc2\xa71983 action as a ground for overturning the conviction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I find it difficult to understand why the Court rejects the use of \xe2\x80\x9cequitable tolling\xe2\x80\x9d in regard to typical \xc2\xa71983 plaintiffs. Ante, at 10. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s alternative\xe2\x80\x94file all \xc2\xa71983 claims (including potentially Heck-barred claims) at once and then seek stays or be subject to dismissal and refiling\xe2\x80\x94suffers serious practical disadvantages. For one thing, that approach would force all potential criminal defendants to file all potential \xc2\xa71983 actions soon lest they lose those claims due to protracted criminal proceedings. For another, it would often require a federal court, seeking to determine whether to dismiss an action as Heck barred or to grant a stay, to consider issues likely being litigated in the criminal proceeding (Was the Constitution violated? Was the violation-related evidence necessary for conviction?). The federal court\xe2\x80\x99s decision as to whether a claim was Heck barred (say, whether the alleged constitutional violation was central to the state criminal conviction) might later bind a state court on conviction review. Because of this, even a claim without a likely Heck bar might linger on a federal docket because the federal court (or the plaintiff who has been forced to early file) wishes to avoid interfering with any state proceedings and therefore must postpone reaching, not only the merits of the \xc2\xa71983 claim, but the threshold Heck inquiry as well.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Principles of equitable tolling avoid these difficulties. Since equitable tolling obviates the need for immediate filing, it permits the criminal proceedings to winnow the constitutional wheat from chaff, and thereby increase the likelihood that the constitutionally meritless claims will never (in a \xc2\xa71983 action) see the light of day. See Allen v. McCurry, 449 U. S. 90, 95\xe2\x80\x9396 (1980) (federal court gives preclusive effect to constitutional determinations as to issues already litigated in state court). Moreover, an appropriate equitable tolling principle would apply not only to state criminal proceedings as here, but also to state appellate proceedings, state collateral attacks, and federal habeas proceedings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, \xc2\xa71983 ordinarily borrows its limitations principles from state law. 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71988(a). And I do not know whether or which States have comparable equitable tolling principles in place. If a given state court lacks the necessary tolling provision, however, \xc2\xa71983, in my view, permits the federal courts to devise and impose such principles. See Hardin v. Straub, 490 U. S. 536, 538\xe2\x80\x93540 (1989) (\xe2\x80\x9c[G]aps in federal civil rights acts should be filled by state law, as long as that law is not inconsistent with federal law\xe2\x80\x9d and its \xe2\x80\x9cchief goals of compensation and deterrence or its subsidiary goals of uniformity and federalism\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)); Heck v. Humphrey, 997 F.\xc2\xa02d 355, 358 (CA7 1993) (Posner, J.) (articulating why federal tolling regime may apply to \xc2\xa71983 claims), aff\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99d on other grounds, 512 U. S., at 489. Cf. Board of Regents of Univ. of State of N. Y. v. Tomanio, 446 U. S. 478, 490 (1980).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The use of equitable tolling in cases of potential temporal conflict between civil \xc2\xa71983 and related criminal proceedings is consistent with, indeed, it would further, \xc2\xa71983\xe2\x80\x99s basic purposes. It would provide for orderly adjudication, minimize the risk of inconsistent legal determinations, avoid clogging the courts with potentially unnecessary \xe2\x80\x9cprotective\xe2\x80\x9d filings, and, above all, assure a plaintiff who possesses a meritorious \xc2\xa71983 claim that his pursuit of criminal remedies designed to free him from unlawful confinement will not compromise his later ability to obtain civil \xc2\xa71983 redress as well.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is wrong in concluding that the principle I have described would \xe2\x80\x9cplace\xe2\x80\x9d the tolling \xe2\x80\x9cquestion\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cjurisprudential limbo.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. Under the approach I propose, supra, at 2, a potential \xc2\xa71983 plaintiff knows his claim is being tolled so long as the issues that claim would raise are being pursued in state court. Such a rule is prophylactic (it will sometimes toll claims that would not be barred by Heck), but under such an approach neither the plaintiff, nor the defendant, nor the federal court need speculate as to whether the claims are in any way barred until the state court has had the opportunity to consider the claims in the criminal context.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A tolling principle certainly seems to me to create greater order than the rule the majority sets out, whereby all criminal defendants must file their \xc2\xa71983 suits immediately, some will be stayed, some dismissed, and then some may be refiled and entitled to tolling, ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa04. The majority acknowledges that tolling may be necessary to protect the plaintiff who previously filed and was dismissed. Ibid. Why not simply apply that tolling principle across the board?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority is also wrong when it suggests that the proposed equitable tolling rule would create a significant problem of lack of notice. Ante, at 12. Because the rule would toll only while the potential \xc2\xa71983 plaintiff is challenging the alleged misconduct in a state court, the State itself would have notice of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claims. For similar reasons, the potential individual \xc2\xa71983 defendants, the state officers, would also likely have notice of the charge. But even if they do not, I believe that many would prefer to forgo immediate notice, for it comes with a pricetag attached\xe2\x80\x94the price consists of being immediately sued by the filing of a \xc2\xa71983 lawsuit, rife with stays and delays, which otherwise, in the course of time (as claims are winnowed in state court) might never have been filed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggested limitations system, like an equitable tolling rule, will produce some instances in which a plaintiff will file a \xc2\xa71983 lawsuit at an initially uncertain future date. Ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa04. And, under both approaches, in the many \xc2\xa71983 suits that do not involve any Heck bar, a defendant can and will file immediately and his suit would proceed (for there is no tolling unless the potential \xc2\xa71983 plaintiff is asserting in a conviction challenge that a constitutional violation did impugn his conviction). My problem with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach lies in its insistence that all potential plaintiffs (including those whose suits may be Heck barred) file immediately\xe2\x80\x94even though their suits cannot then proceed. With tolling, only rarely would a plaintiff choose to file a potentially Heck-barred \xc2\xa71983 suit while his criminal case is pending; and in those cases the district court could, if it wished, stay the action, or simply dismiss the suit without prejudice, secure in the knowledge that the suit could be timely filed at a later date.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to admit the equitable tolling possibility means that large numbers of defendants will be sued immediately by all potential \xc2\xa71983 plaintiffs with arguable Heck issues, no matter how meritless the claims; these suits may be endlessly stayed or dismissed and then, at some point in the future, some defendants will also be sued again. With equitable tolling, however, defendants will be sued once, in suits with constitutional claims that a state court has not already found meritless, at a time when the suit can be promptly litigated. Given the practical difficulties of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach, I would not rule out now, in advance, the use of an equitable tolling rule along the lines I have described.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because this matter has not been fully argued, I would vacate the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s determination and remand for consideration of the issues I here raise. For these reasons, I respectfully dissent.
