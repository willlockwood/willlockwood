BRUCE v. SAMUELS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93844.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 4, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 12, 2016

\n

The Prison Litigation Reform Act of 1995 provides that prisoners qualified to proceed in forma pauperis (IFP) must nonetheless pay an initial partial filing fee, set as \xe2\x80\x9c20 percent of the greater of\xe2\x80\x9d the average monthly deposits in the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s account or the average monthly balance of the account over the preceding six months.\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71915(b)(1). They must then pay the remainder of the fee in monthly installments of \xe2\x80\x9c20 percent of the preceding month\xe2\x80\x99s income credited to the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s account.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71915(b)(2). The initial partial fee is assessed on a per-case basis, i.e., each time the prisoner files a lawsuit. The initial payment may not be exacted if the prisoner has no means to pay it, \xc2\xa71915(b)(4), and no monthly installments are required unless the prisoner has more than $10 in his account, \xc2\xa71915(b)(2). In contest here is the calculation of subsequent monthly installment payments when more than one fee is owed.

\n

Petitioner Antoine Bruce, a federal inmate and a frequent litigant, argued that the monthly filing-fee payments do not become due until filing-fee obligations previously incurred in other cases are satisfied. The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit disagreed, holding that Bruce\xe2\x80\x99s monthly payments were due simultaneously with monthly payments in the earlier cases.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section 1915(b)(2) calls for simultaneous, not sequential, recoupment of multiple monthly installment payments. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Bruce and the Government present competing interpretations of the IFP statute, which does not explicitly address how multiple filing fees should be paid. In urging a per-prisoner approach under which he would pay 20 percent of his monthly income regardless of the number of cases he has filed, Bruce relies principally on the contrast between the singular \xe2\x80\x9cclerk\xe2\x80\x9d and the plural \xe2\x80\x9cfees\xe2\x80\x9d as those nouns appear in \xc2\xa71915(b)(2), which requires payments to be forwarded \xe2\x80\x9cto the clerk of the court .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. until the filing fees are paid.\xe2\x80\x9d Even when more than one filing fee is owed, Bruce contends, \xc2\xa71915(b)(2) instructs that only one clerk will receive payment each month. In contrast, the Government urges a per-case approach. Emphasizing that \xc2\xa71915 as a whole has a single-case focus, providing instructions for each case, the Government contends that it would be anomalous to treat paragraph (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s initial partial payment, admittedly directed at a single case, differently than paragraph (b)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent monthly payments. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Section 1915\xe2\x80\x99s text and context support the per-case approach. Just as \xc2\xa71915(b)(1) calls for assessment of \xe2\x80\x9can initial partial filing fee\xe2\x80\x9d each time a prisoner \xe2\x80\x9cbrings a civil action or files an appeal\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added), so its allied provision, \xc2\xa71915(b)(2), calls for monthly 20 percent payments simultaneously for each action pursued. Section 1915(b)(3), which imposes a ceiling on fees permitted \xe2\x80\x9cfor the commencement of a civil action or an appeal\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added), and \xc2\xa71915(b)(4), which protects the right to bring \xe2\x80\x9ca civil action or appea[l] a .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. judgment\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added), confirm that subsection (b) as a whole is written from the perspective of a single case. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

761 F.\xc2\xa03d 1, affirmed.

ANTOINE BRUCE, PETITIONER v. CHARLES E. SAMUELS, JR., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

[January 12, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case concerns the payment of filing fees for civil actions commenced by prisoners in federal courts. Until 1996, indigent prisoners, like other indigent persons, could file a civil action without paying any filing fee. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71915(a)(1). In the Prison Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PLRA),\n\n110Stat.\n1321\xe2\x80\x9366, Congress placed several limitations on prisoner litigation in federal courts. Among those limitations, Congress required prisoners qualified to proceed in forma pauperis nevertheless to pay an initial partial filing fee. That fee is statutorily set as \xe2\x80\x9c20 percent of the greater of\xe2\x80\x9d the average monthly deposits in the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s account or the average monthly balance of the account over the preceding six months. \xc2\xa71915(b)(1). Thereafter, to complete payment of the filing fee, prisoners must pay, in monthly installments, \xe2\x80\x9c20 percent of the preceding month\xe2\x80\x99s income credited to the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s account.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71915(b)(2). The initial partial filing fee may not be exacted if the prisoner has no means to pay it, \xc2\xa71915(b)(4), and no monthly installments are required unless the prisoner has more than $10 in his account, \xc2\xa71915(b)(2).

\n

It is undisputed that the initial partial filing fee is to be assessed on a per-case basis, i.e., each time the prisoner files a lawsuit. In contest here is the calculation of subsequent monthly installment payments. Petitioner Antoine Bruce urges a per-prisoner approach under which he would pay 20 percent of his monthly income regardless of the number of cases he has filed. The Government urges, and the court below followed, a per-case approach under which a prisoner would pay 20 percent of his monthly income for each case he has filed. Courts of Appeals have divided on which of these two approaches \xc2\xa71915(b)(2) orders.[1] To resolve the conflict, we granted certiorari. 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

We hold that monthly installment payments, like the initial partial payment, are to be assessed on a per-case basis. Nothing in \xc2\xa71915\xe2\x80\x99s current design supports treating a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s second or third action unlike his first lawsuit.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

In 1892, Congress enacted the in forma pauperis (IFP) statute, now codified at\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71915, \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure that indigent litigants have meaningful access to the federal courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Neitzke v. Williams,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319,\n324 (1989)\n. Reacting to \xe2\x80\x9ca sharp rise in prisoner litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d Woodford v. Ngo,\n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 81,\n84 (2006)\n, Congress in 1996 enacted the PLRA, which installed a variety of measures \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to filter out the bad claims [filed by prisoners] and facilitate consideration of the good,\xe2\x80\x9d Coleman v. Tollefson, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 3) (quoting Jones v. Bock,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 199,\n204 (2007)\n; alteration in original).

\n

Among those measures, Congress required prisoners to pay filing fees for the suits or appeals they launch. The provisions on fee payment, set forth in \xc2\xa71915(b), read:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(1) .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [I]f a prisoner brings a civil action or files an appeal in forma pauperis, the prisoner shall be required to pay the full amount of a filing fee. The court shall assess and, when funds exist, collect, as a partial payment of any court fees required by law, an initial partial filing fee of 20 percent of the greater of\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(A) the average monthly deposits to the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s account; or

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(B) the average monthly balance in the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s account for the 6-month period immediately preceding the filing of the complaint or notice of appeal.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(2) After payment of the initial partial filing fee, the prisoner shall be required to make monthly payments of 20 percent of the preceding month\xe2\x80\x99s income credited to the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s account. The agency having custody of the prisoner shall forward payments from the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s account to the clerk of the court each time the amount in the account exceeds $10 until the filing fees are paid.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The monthly installment scheme described in \xc2\xa71915(b)(2) also applies to costs awarded against prisoners when they are judgment losers. \xc2\xa71915(f)(2)(B).

\n

To further contain prisoner litigation, the PLRA introduced a three-strikes provision: Prisoners whose suits or appeals are dismissed three or more times as frivolous, malicious, or failing to state a claim on which relief may be granted are barred from proceeding IFP \xe2\x80\x9cunless the prisoner is under imminent danger of serious physical injury.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71915(g). In other words, for most three strikers, all future filing fees become payable in full upfront.

\n

Congress included in its 1996 overhaul of \xc2\xa71915 a safety-valve provision to ensure that the fee requirementswould not bar access to the courts: \xe2\x80\x9cIn no event shall a prisoner be prohibited from bringing a civil action or appealing a civil or criminal judgment for the reason that the prisoner has no assets and no means by which to pay the initial partial filing fee.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71915(b)(4).

\n

B

\n

Petitioner Antoine Bruce, a federal inmate serving a 15-year sentence, is a frequent litigant.[2] In the instant case, Bruce challenges his placement in a special management unit at the Federal Correctional Institution in Talladega, Alabama. Pinson v. Samuels, 761 F.\xc2\xa03d 1, 3\xe2\x80\x934 (CADC 2014).[3] Bruce had previously incurred filing-fee obligations in other cases and maintained that the monthly filing-fee payments for this case would not become due until those prior obligations were satisfied. Id., at 4, 7. The Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit, whose decision is before us for review, rejected Bruce\xe2\x80\x99s argument. Id., at 8\xe2\x80\x9310. Bruce must make monthly filing-fee payments in this case, the court held, simultaneously with such payments in earlier commenced cases. Id., at 8. We agree with the appeals court that \xc2\xa71915(b)(2) calls for simultaneous, not sequential, recoupment of multiple filing fees.

\n

II

\n

The IFP statute does not explicitly address whether multiple filing fees (after the initial partial payment) should be paid simultaneously or sequentially. Bruce and the Government present competing interpretations.

\n

A

\n

In support of the per-prisoner approach, Bruce relies principally on what he sees as a significant contrast between the singular \xe2\x80\x9cclerk\xe2\x80\x9d and the plural \xe2\x80\x9cfees\xe2\x80\x9d as those nouns appear in\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71915(b)(2). That provision requires payments to be forwarded \xe2\x80\x9cto the clerk of the court\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. until the filing fees are paid.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (empha-sis added). Even when more than one filing fee isowed, Bruce contends, the statute instructs that only one clerk will receive payment each month; in other words,fee payments are to be made sequentially rather than simultaneously.

\n

The initial partial payment, which is charged on a per-case basis, plus the three-strikes provision, Bruce urges, together suffice to satisfy the PLRA\xe2\x80\x99s purpose, which is to \xe2\x80\x9cforce prisoners to think twice about the case and not just file reflexively,\xe2\x80\x9d 141 Cong. Rec. 14572 (1995) (remarks of Sen. Kyl). The additional economic disincentive that the per-case approach would occasion, Bruce asserts, could excessively encumber access to federal courts.

\n

Furthermore, Bruce points out, the per-case approach breaks down when a prisoner incurs more than five obligations. Nothing will be left in the account to pay the sixth fee, Bruce observes. Necessarily, therefore, its payment will be entirely deferred. Why treat the second obligation unlike the sixth, Bruce asks. Isn\xe2\x80\x99t the statute sensibly read to render all monthly payments sequential? Bruce notes in this regard that, under the per-case approach, his ability to use his account to purchase amenities will be progressively curtailed; indeed, the account might be reduced to zero upon his filing or joining a fifth case.

\n

Finally, Bruce argues, administrative difficulties counsel against the per-case approach. Costs could dwarf the monetary yield if prisons, under a per-case regime, were obliged to send as many as five checks to five different courts each month. And the problems faced by state-prison officials\xe2\x80\x94who sometimes must choose which of several claims on a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s income (e.g., child-support, medical copayments) should take precedence\xe2\x80\x94would be exacerbated under a system demanding simultaneous payment of multiple litigation charges.

\n

B

\n

The Government emphasizes that \xc2\xa71915 as a whole has a single-case focus, providing instructions for each case. It would be anomalous, the Government urges, to treat paragraph (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s initial partial payment, which Bruce concedes is directed at a single case, differently than paragraph (b)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent monthly payments. The two paragraphs, the Government observes, are linked by paragraph (b)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s opening clause: \xe2\x80\x9cAfter payment of the initial partial filing fee.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The per-case approach, the Government adds, better comports with the purpose of the PLRA to deter frivolous suits. See Newlin v. Helman, 123 F.\xc2\xa03d 429, 436 (CA7 1997) (Easterbrook, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cOtherwise a prisoner could file multiple suits for the price of one, postponing payment of the fees for later-filed suits until after the end of imprisonment (and likely avoiding them altogether [because fees are often uncollectable on a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s release]).\xe2\x80\x9d), overruled in part on other grounds by Lee v. Clinton, 209 F.\xc2\xa03d 1025 (CA7 2000), and Walker v. O\xe2\x80\x99Brien, 216 F.\xc2\xa03d 626 (CA7 2000). The Government further observes that the generally small size of the initial partial fee\xe2\x80\x94here, $0.64, App. to Pet. for Cert. 21a\xe2\x80\x94provides scant disincentive, on its own, for multiple filings.

\n

Responding to Bruce\xe2\x80\x99s observation that, for a prisoner with more than five charges, even the per-case approach resorts to sequential payments, the Government agrees, but tells us that this scenario arises infrequently. \xe2\x80\x9c[M]ost prisoners,\xe2\x80\x9d the Government states, \xe2\x80\x9cwould accrue three strikes (and therefore be required to pay the full filing fees upfront) by the time they incurred the obligation for their sixth case.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 29.

\n

Finally, answering Bruce\xe2\x80\x99s concern that the per-case approach could leave a prisoner without money for amenities, the Government points out that prisons \xe2\x80\x9care constitutionally bound to provide inmates with adequate food, clothing, shelter, and medical care,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 48 (citing Farmer v. Brennan,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825,\n832 (1994)\n), and must furnish \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98paper and pen to draft legal documents\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98stamps to mail them,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 48 (quoting Bounds v. Smith,\n\n430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 817,\n824,\n825 (1977)\n). More-over, the Government notes, the Federal Bureau of Prisons (BOP) \xe2\x80\x9cgoes beyond those requirements,\xe2\x80\x9d providing inmates \xe2\x80\x9carticles necessary for maintaining personal hygiene,\xe2\x80\x9d and free postage \xe2\x80\x9cnot only for legal mailings but also to enable the inmate to maintain community ties.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 48, n.\xc2\xa021 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

III

\n

The Circuits following the per-case approach, we conclude, better comprehend the statute. Just as \xc2\xa71915(b)(1) calls for assessment of \xe2\x80\x9can initial partial filing fee\xe2\x80\x9d each time a prisoner \xe2\x80\x9cbrings a civil action or files an appeal\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added), so its allied provision, \xc2\xa71915(b)(2), triggered immediately after, calls for \xe2\x80\x9cmonthly payments of 20 percent of the preceding month\xe2\x80\x99s income\xe2\x80\x9d simultaneously for each action pursued. The other two paragraphs of \xc2\xa71915(b) confirm that the subsection as a whole is written from the perspective of a single case. See \xc2\xa71915(b)(3) (imposing a ceiling on fees permitted \xe2\x80\x9cfor the commencement of a civil action or an appeal\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); \xc2\xa71915(b)(4) (protecting the right to \xe2\x80\x9cbrin[g] a civil action or appea[l] a civil or criminal judgment\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). There is scant indication that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s perspective shifts partway through paragraph (2).[4]

\n

Bruce\xe2\x80\x99s extratextual points do not warrant a departure from the interpretation suggested by the text and context. The per-case approach more vigorously serves the statutory objective of containing prisoner litigation, while the safety-valve provision, see supra, at 4, ensures against denial of access to federal courts. Bruce\xe2\x80\x99s administrability concerns carry little weight given reports from several States that the per-case approach is unproblematic. See Brief for State of Michigan et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 18\xe2\x80\x9320.
