SOSSAMON v. TEXAS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x931438.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 2, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 20, 2011

\n

After this Court held that the Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993 was unconstitutional as applied to state and local governments because it exceeded Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power under \xc2\xa75 of the Fourteenth Amendment, see City of Boerne v. Flores, 521 U. S. 507, Congress passed the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act of 2000 (RLUIPA) pursuant to its Spending Clause and Commerce Clause authority. RLUIPA targets two areas of state and local action: land\xe2\x80\x93use regulation, RLUIPA \xc2\xa72, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc, and restrictions on the religious exercise of institutionalized persons, RLUIPA \xc2\xa73, \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931. It also provides an express private cause of action for \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief against a government,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(a), including, inter alia, States, their instrumentalities and officers, and persons acting under color of state law, \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x935(4)(A).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Sossamon, a Texas prison inmate, sued respondents, the State and prison officials, seeking injunctive and monetary relief under RLUIPA for prison policies that prevented inmates from attending religious services while on cell restriction for disciplinary infractions and that barred use of the prison chapel for religious worship. Granting respondents summary judgment, the District Court held that sovereign immunity barred Sossamon\xe2\x80\x99s claims for monetary relief. The Fifth Circuit affirmed, holding that the statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief against a government\xe2\x80\x9d did not unambiguously notify Texas that its acceptance of federal funds was conditioned on a waiver of sovereign immunity to claims for monetary relief.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0States, in accepting federal funding, do not consent to waive their sovereign immunity to private suits for money damages under RLUIPA. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Sovereign immunity principles enforce an important constitutional limitation on the power of the federal courts. See Pennhurst State School and Hospital v. Halderman, 465 U. S. 89, 98. This Court has consistently made clear that \xe2\x80\x9cfederal jurisdiction over suits against unconsenting States \xe2\x80\x98was not contemplated by the Constitution when establishing the judicial power of the United States.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Florida, 517 U. S. 44, 54. A State, however, may choose to waive its immunity. Clark v. Barnard, 108 U. S. 436, 447\xe2\x80\x93448. The \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98test for determining whether [it has done so] is a stringent one.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d College Savings Bank v. Florida Prepaid Postsecondary Ed. Expense Bd., 527 U. S. 666, 675. The State\xe2\x80\x99s consent to suit must be \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocally expressed\xe2\x80\x9d in the relevant statute\xe2\x80\x99s text. Pennhurst, supra, at 99. A waiver \xe2\x80\x9cwill be strictly construed, in terms of its scope, in favor of the sovereign.\xe2\x80\x9d Lane v. Pe\xc5\x84a, 518 U. S. 187, 192. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s authorization of \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief against a government,\xe2\x80\x9d is not an unequivocal expression of state consent. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cAppropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d is open-ended and ambiguous about the relief it includes. \xe2\x80\x9cAppropriate\xe2\x80\x9d is inherently context-dependent. And the context here\xe2\x80\x94where the defendant is a sovereign\xe2\x80\x94suggests, if anything, that monetary damages are not \xe2\x80\x9csuitable\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cproper.\xe2\x80\x9d See Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. South Carolina Ports Authority, 535 U. S. 743, 765. Further, where a statute is susceptible of multiple plausible interpretations, including one preserving immunity, this Court will not consider a State to have waived its sovereign immunity. Sossamon\xe2\x80\x99s and Texas\xe2\x80\x99 conflicting plausible arguments about whether immunity is preserved here demonstrate that \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d in RLUIPA is not so free from ambiguity that the Court may conclude that the States, by receiving federal funds, have unequivocally expressed intent to waive their immunity. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s use of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d in Franklin v. Gwinnett County Public Schools, 503 U. S. 60, and Barnes v. Gorman, 536 U. S. 181, does not compel a contrary conclusion. In those cases, where there was no express congressional intent to limit remedies available against municipal entities under an implied right of action, the Court presumed that compensatory damages were available. Franklin, supra, at 73. But that presumption is irrelevant to construing the scope of an express waiver of sovereign immunity, where the question is not whether Congress has given clear direction that it intends to exclude a damages remedy, but whether it has given clear direction that it intends to include a damages remedy. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Sossamon mistakenly contends that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enactment of RLUIPA \xc2\xa73 pursuant to the Spending Clause put the States on notice that they would be liable for damages because Spending Clause legislation operates as a contract and damages are always available for a breach of contract. While acknowledging the contract-law analogy, this Court has been clear \xe2\x80\x9cnot [to] imply \xe2\x80\xa6 that suits under Spending Clause legislation are suits in contract, or that contract-law principles apply to all issues that they raise,\xe2\x80\x9d Barnes, supra, at 188, n.\xc2\xa02, or to rely on that analogy to expand liability beyond what would exist under nonspending statutes, much less to extend monetary liability against the States. Applying ordinary contract principles here would also make little sense because contracts with a sovereign are unique: They do not traditionally confer a right of action for damages to enforce compliance. More fundamentally, Sossamon\xe2\x80\x99s implied-contract remedy cannot be squared with the rule that a sovereign immunity waiver must be expressly and unequivocally stated in the relevant statute\xe2\x80\x99s text. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Sossamon also errs in arguing that Texas was put on notice that it could be sued for damages under RLUIPA by \xc2\xa71003 of the Rehabilitation Act Amendments of 1986, which expressly waives state sovereign immunity for violations of \xe2\x80\x9csection 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972, the Age Discrimination Act of 1975, title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, or the provisions of any other Federal statute prohibiting discrimination by recipients of Federal financial assistance,\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000d\xe2\x80\x937. Even if such a residual clause could constitute an unequivocal textual waiver, RLUIPA \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x94which prohibits \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial burden[s]\xe2\x80\x9d on religious exercise\xe2\x80\x94is not unequivocally a \xe2\x80\x9cstatute prohibiting discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d within \xc2\xa71003\xe2\x80\x99s meaning. All the statutory provisions enumerated in \xc2\xa71003 explicitly prohibit discrimination; a State might reasonably conclude that the residual clause, strictly construed, covers only provisions using the term \xe2\x80\x9cdiscrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

560 F.\xc2\xa03d 316, affirmed.

HARVEY LEROY SOSSAMON, III, PETITIONER v. TEXAS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[April 20, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case presents the question whether the States, by accepting federal funds, consent to waive their sovereign immunity to suits for money damages under the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act of 2000 (RLUIPA), 114 Stat. 803, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc et seq. We hold that they do not. Sovereign immunity therefore bars this suit for damages against the State of Texas.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0RLUIPA is Congress\xe2\x80\x99 second attempt to accord heightened statutory protection to religious exercise in the wake of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Employment Division, Department of Human Resources of Oregon v. Smith, 494 U. S. 872 (1990). Congress first enacted the Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993 (RFRA), 107 Stat. 1488, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000bb et seq., with which it intended to \xe2\x80\x9crestore the compelling interest test as set forth in Sherbert v. Verner, 374 U. S. 398 (1963) and Wisconsin v. Yoder, 406 U. S. 205 (1972) \xe2\x80\xa6 in all cases where free exercise of religion is substantially burdened.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000bb(b)(1). See generally Gonzales v. O Centro Esp\xc3\xadrita Beneficente Uni\xc4\x83o do Vegetal, 546 U. S. 418, 424 (2006). We held RFRA unconstitutional as applied to state and local governments because it exceeded Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power under \xc2\xa75 of the Fourteenth Amendment. See City of Boerne v. Flores, 521 U. S. 507 (1997).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress responded by enacting RLUIPA pursuant to its Spending Clause and Commerce Clause authority. RLUIPA borrows important elements from RFRA\xe2\x80\x94which continues to apply to the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x94but RLUIPA is less sweeping in scope. See Cutter v. Wilkinson, 544 U. S. 709, 715 (2005). It targets two areas of state and local action: land-use regulation, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc (RLUIPA \xc2\xa72), and restrictions on the religious exercise of institutionalized persons, \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931 (RLUIPA \xc2\xa73).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 3 of RLUIPA provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o government shall impose a substantial burden on the religious exercise\xe2\x80\x9d of an institutionalized person unless, as in RFRA, the government demonstrates that the burden \xe2\x80\x9cis in furtherance of a compelling governmental interest\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cis the least restrictive means of furthering\xe2\x80\x9d that interest. \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931(a); cf. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72000bb\xe2\x80\x931(a), (b). As relevant here, \xc2\xa73 applies \xe2\x80\x9cin any case\xe2\x80\x9d in which \xe2\x80\x9cthe substantial burden is imposed in a program or activity that receives Federal financial assistance.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 1] \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931(b)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0RLUIPA also includes an express private cause of action that is taken from RFRA: \xe2\x80\x9cA person may assert a violation of [RLUIPA] as a claim or defense in a judicial proceeding and obtain appropriate relief against a government.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(a); cf. \xc2\xa72000bb\xe2\x80\x931(c). For purposes of this provision, \xe2\x80\x9cgovernment\xe2\x80\x9d includes, inter alia, States, counties, municipalities, their instrumentalities and officers, and persons acting under color of state law. \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x935(4)(A).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Harvey Leroy Sossamon III is an inmate in the Robertson Unit of the Texas Department of Criminal Justice, Correctional Institutions Division. In 2006, Sossamon sued the State of Texas and various prison officials in their official capacities under RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s private cause of action, seeking injunctive and monetary relief. Sossamon alleged that two prison policies violated RLUIPA: (1) a policy preventing inmates from attending religious services while on cell restriction for disciplinary infractions; and (2) a policy barring use of the prison chapel for religious worship. The District Court granted summary judgment in favor of respondents and held, as relevant here, that sovereign immunity barred Sossamon\xe2\x80\x99s claims for monetary relief.[Footnote 2] See 713 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 657, 662\xe2\x80\x93663 (WD Tex. 2007).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit affirmed. 560 F.\xc2\xa03d 316, 329 (2009). Acknowledging that Congress enacted RLUIPA pursuant to the Spending Clause, the court determined that Texas had not waived its sovereign immunity by accepting federal funds. The Court of Appeals strictly construed the text of RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s cause of action in favor of the State and concluded that the statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief against a government\xe2\x80\x9d did not \xe2\x80\x9cunambiguously notif[y]\xe2\x80\x9d Texas that its acceptance of funds was conditioned on a waiver of immunity from claims for money damages. Id., at 330\xe2\x80\x93331. We granted certiorari to resolve a division of authority among the courts of appeals on this question.[Footnote 3] 560 U. S. ___ (2010).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cDual sovereignty is a defining feature of our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional blueprint.\xe2\x80\x9d Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. South Carolina Ports Authority, 535 U. S. 743, 751 (2002). Upon ratification of the Constitution, the States entered the Union \xe2\x80\x9cwith their sovereignty intact.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Immunity from private suits has long been considered \xe2\x80\x9ccentral to sovereign dignity.\xe2\x80\x9d Alden v. Maine, 527 U. S. 706, 715 (1999). As was widely understood at the time the Constitution was drafted:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIt is inherent in the nature of sovereignty not to be amenable to the suit of an individual without its consent. This is the general sense, and the general practice of mankind; and the exemption, as one of the\nattributes of sovereignty, is now enjoyed by the\ngovernment of every State in the Union.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 81, p. 511 (Wright ed. 1961) (A. Hamilton).

\n

\nIndeed, when this Court threatened state immunity from private suits early in our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s history, the people responded swiftly to reiterate that fundamental principle. See Hans v. Louisiana, 134 U. S. 1, 11 (1890) (discussing Chisholm v. Georgia, 2 Dall. 419 (1793), and the Eleventh Amendment).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sovereign immunity principles enforce an important constitutional limitation on the power of the federal courts. See Pennhurst State School and Hospital v. Halderman, 465 U. S. 89, 98 (1984). For over a century now, this Court has consistently made clear that \xe2\x80\x9cfederal jurisdiction over suits against unconsenting States \xe2\x80\x98was not contemplated by the Constitution when establishing the judicial power of the United States.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Florida, 517 U. S. 44, 54 (1996) (quoting Hans, supra, at 15); see Seminole Tribe, supra, at 54\xe2\x80\x9355, n.\xc2\xa07 (collecting cases). A State, however, may choose to waive its immunity in federal court at its pleasure. Clark v. Barnard, 108 U. S. 436, 447\xe2\x80\x93448 (1883).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, \xe2\x80\x9cour test for determining whether a State has waived its immunity from federal-court jurisdiction is a stringent one.\xe2\x80\x9d College Savings Bank v. Florida Prepaid Postsecondary Ed. Expense Bd., 527 U. S. 666, 675 (1999) (internal quotation marks omitted). A State\xe2\x80\x99s consent to suit must be \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocally expressed\xe2\x80\x9d in the text of the relevant statute. Pennhurst State School and Hospital, supra, at 99; see Atascadero State Hospital v. Scanlon, 473 U. S. 234, 238, n.\xc2\xa01, 239\xe2\x80\x93240 (1985). Only by requiring this \xe2\x80\x9cclear declaration\xe2\x80\x9d by the State can we be \xe2\x80\x9ccertain that the State in fact consents to suit.\xe2\x80\x9d College Savings Bank, 527 U. S., at 680. Waiver may not be implied. Id., at 682.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, a waiver of sovereign immunity \xe2\x80\x9cwill be strictly construed, in terms of its scope, in favor of the sovereign.\xe2\x80\x9d Lane v. Pe\xc5\x84a, 518 U. S. 187, 192 (1996).[Footnote 4] So, for example, a State\xe2\x80\x99s consent to suit in its own courts is not a waiver of its immunity from suit in federal court. College Savings Bank, supra, at 676. Similarly, a waiver of sovereign immunity to other types of relief does not waive immunity to damages: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he waiver of sovereign immunity must extend unambiguously to such monetary claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Lane, supra, at 192; cf. United States v. Nordic Village, 503 U. S. 30, 34 (1992) (construing an ambiguous waiver of sovereign immunity to permit equitable but not monetary claims); Hoffman v. Connecticut Dept. of Income Maintenance, 492 U. S. 96, 101\xe2\x80\x93102 (1989) (construing a statute to authorize injunctive relief but not \xe2\x80\x9cmonetary recovery from the States\xe2\x80\x9d because intent to abrogate immunity to monetary recovery was not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unmistakably clear in the language of the statute\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Atascadero, supra, at 242)).

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s authorization of \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief against a government,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(a), is not the unequivocal expression of state consent that our precedents require. \xe2\x80\x9cAppropriate relief\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d does not so clearly and unambiguously waive sovereign immunity to private suits for damages that we can \xe2\x80\x9cbe certain that the State in fact consents\xe2\x80\x9d to such a suit. College Savings Bank, 527 U. S., at 680.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cAppropriate relief\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is open-ended and ambiguous about what types of relief it includes, as many lower courts have recognized. See, e.g., 560 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 330\xe2\x80\x93331.[Footnote 5] Far from clearly identifying money damages, the word \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d is inherently context-dependent. See Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 106 (1993) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cspecially suitable: fit, proper\xe2\x80\x9d). The context here\xe2\x80\x94where the defendant is a sovereign\xe2\x80\x94suggests, if anything, that monetary damages are not \xe2\x80\x9csuitable\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cproper.\xe2\x80\x9d See Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 535 U. S., at 765 (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]tate sovereign immunity serves the important function of shielding state treasuries \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, both the Court and dissent appeared to agree in West v. Gibson, 527 U. S. 212 (1999), that \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d relief, by itself, does not unambiguously include damages against a sovereign. The question was whether the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, which has authority to enforce Title VII of the Civil Rights Act against the Federal Government \xe2\x80\x9cthrough appropriate remedies,\xe2\x80\x9d could require the Federal Government to pay damages. 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x9316(b). The dissent argued that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate remedies\xe2\x80\x9d did not authorize damages \xe2\x80\x9cin express and unequivocal terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Gibson, 527 U. S., at 226 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). The Court apparently did not disagree but reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate remedies\xe2\x80\x9d had a flexible meaning that had expanded to include money damages after a related statute was amended to explicitly allow damages in actions under Title VII. See id., at 217\xe2\x80\x93218.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Further, where a statute is susceptible of multiple plausible interpretations, including one preserving immunity, we will not consider a State to have waived its sovereign immunity. See Dellmuth v. Muth, 491 U. S. 223, 232 (1989) (holding that \xe2\x80\x9ca permissible inference\xe2\x80\x9d is not the necessary \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocal declaration\xe2\x80\x9d that States were intended to be subject to damages actions); Nordic Village, supra, at 37 (holding that the existence of \xe2\x80\x9cplausible\xe2\x80\x9d interpretations that would not permit recovery \xe2\x80\x9cis enough to establish that a reading imposing monetary liability on the Government is not \xe2\x80\x98unambiguous\xe2\x80\x99 and therefore should not be adopted\xe2\x80\x9d). That is the case here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sossamon argues that, because RLUIPA expressly limits the United States to \xe2\x80\x9cinjunctive or declaratory relief\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to enforce the statute, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate re-lief\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in the private cause of action necessarily must be broader. 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(f). Texas responds that, because the State has no immunity defense to a suit brought by the Federal Government, Congress needed to exclude damages affirmatively in that context but not in the context of private suits. Further, the private cause of action provides that a person may assert a violation of the statute \xe2\x80\x9cas a claim or defense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(a) (emphasis added). Because an injunction or declaratory judgment is not \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for a successful defense, Texas explains, explicitly limiting the private cause of action to those forms of relief would make no sense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sossamon also emphasizes that the statute requires that it be \xe2\x80\x9cconstrued in favor of a broad protection of religious exercise.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x933(g). Texas responds that this provision is best read as addressing the substantive standards in the statute, not the scope of \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Texas also highlights Congress\xe2\x80\x99 choice of the word \xe2\x80\x9crelief,\xe2\x80\x9d which it argues primarily connotes equitable relief. See Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1295 (7th ed. 1999) (defining \xe2\x80\x9crelief\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he redress or benefit, esp. equitable in nature \xe2\x80\xa6 , that a party asks of a court\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These plausible arguments demonstrate that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in RLUIPA is not so free from ambiguity that we may conclude that the States, by receiving federal funds, have unequivocally expressed intent to waive their sovereign immunity to suits for damages. Strictly construing that phrase in favor of the sovereign\xe2\x80\x94as we must, see Lane, 518 U. S., at 192\xe2\x80\x94we conclude that it does not include suits for damages against a State.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s use of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in Franklin v. Gwinnett County Public Schools, 503 U. S. 60 (1992), and Barnes v. Gorman, 536 U. S. 181 (2002), does not compel a contrary conclusion. In those cases, the Court addressed what remedies are available against municipal entities under the implied right of action to enforce Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972, \xc2\xa7202 of the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990, and \xc2\xa7504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973. With no statutory text to interpret, the Court \xe2\x80\x9cpresume[d] the availability of all appropriate remedies unless Congress ha[d] expressly indicated otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Franklin, 503 U. S., at 66. The Court described the presumption as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he general rule\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cthe federal courts have the power to award any appropriate relief in a cognizable cause of action brought pursuant to a federal statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 70\xe2\x80\x9371 (emphasis added); see Barnes, supra, at 185 (quoting Franklin, supra, at 73). Finding no express congressional intent to limit the remedies available under the implied right of action, the Court held that compensatory damages were available. Franklin, supra, at 73.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The presumption in Franklin and Barnes is irrelevant to construing the scope of an express waiver of sovereign immunity. See Lane, supra, at 196 (\xe2\x80\x9c[R]eliance on Franklin \xe2\x80\xa6 is misplaced\xe2\x80\x9d in determining whether damages are available against the Federal Government). The question here is not whether Congress has given clear direction that it intends to exclude a damages remedy, see Franklin, supra, at 70\xe2\x80\x9371, but whether Congress has given clear direction that it intends to include a damages remedy. The text must \xe2\x80\x9cestablish unambiguously that the waiver extends to monetary claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Nordic Village, 503 U. S., at 34. In Franklin and Barnes, congressional silence had an entirely different implication than it does here. Whatever \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d might have meant in those cases does not translate to this context.[Footnote 6]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sossamon contends that, because Congress enacted \xc2\xa73 of RLUIPA pursuant to the Spending Clause, the States were necessarily on notice that they would be liable for damages. He argues that Spending Clause legislation operates as a contract and damages are always available relief for a breach of contract, whether the contract explicitly includes a damages remedy or not. Relying on Barnes and Franklin, he asserts that all recipients of federal funding are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98generally on notice that [they are] subject \xe2\x80\xa6 to those remedies traditionally available in suits for breach of contract,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d including compensatory damages. Brief for Petitioner 27 (quoting Barnes, 536 U. S., at 187).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have acknowledged the contract-law analogy, but we have been clear \xe2\x80\x9cnot [to] imply \xe2\x80\xa6 that suits under Spending Clause legislation are suits in contract, or that\ncontract-law principles apply to all issues that they raise.\xe2\x80\x9d Barnes, supra, at 189, n.\xc2\xa02. We have not relied on the Spending Clause contract analogy to expand liability beyond what would exist under non-spending statutes, much less to extend monetary liability against the States, as Sossamon would have us do. In fact, in Barnes and Franklin, the Court discussed the Spending Clause context only as a potential limitation on liability. See Barnes, supra, at 187\xe2\x80\x93188; Franklin, supra, at 74\xe2\x80\x9375.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, applying ordinary contract principles here would make little sense because contracts with a sovereign are unique. They do not traditionally confer a right of action for damages to enforce compliance: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The contracts between a Nation and an individual are only binding on the conscience of the sovereign and have no pretensions to compulsive force. They confer no right of action independent of the sovereign will.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lynch v. United States, 292 U. S. 571, 580\xe2\x80\x93581 (1934) (quoting The Federalist, No. 81, at 511 (A. Hamilton)).[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More fundamentally, Sossamon\xe2\x80\x99s implied-contract-remedies proposal cannot be squared with our longstanding rule that a waiver of sovereign immunity must be expressly and unequivocally stated in the text of the relevant statute. It would be bizarre to create an \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocal statement\xe2\x80\x9d rule and then find that every Spending Clause enactment, no matter what its text, satisfies that rule because it includes unexpressed, implied remedies against the States. The requirement of a clear statement in the text of the statute ensures that Congress has specifically considered state sovereign immunity and has intentionally legislated on the matter. Cf. Spector v. Norwegian Cruise Line Ltd., 545 U. S. 119, 139 (2005) (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]lear statement rules ensure Congress does not, by broad or general language, legislate on a sensitive topic inadvertently or without due deliberation\xe2\x80\x9d). Without such a clear statement from Congress and notice to the States, federal courts may not step in and abrogate state sovereign immunity.[Footnote 8]

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sossamon also argues that \xc2\xa71003 of the Rehabilitation Act Amendments of 1986, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000d\xe2\x80\x937, independently put the State on notice that it could be sued for damages under RLUIPA. That provision expressly waives state sovereign immunity for violations of \xe2\x80\x9csection 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972, the Age Discrimination Act of 1975, title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, or the provisions of any other Federal statute prohibiting discrimination by recipients of Federal financial assistance.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000d\xe2\x80\x937(a)(1) (emphasis added). Section 1003 makes \xe2\x80\x9cremedies (including remedies both at law and in equity) \xe2\x80\xa6 available for such a violation to the same extent as such remedies are available for such a violation in the suit against any public or private entity other than a State.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000d\xe2\x80\x937(a)(2). Sossamon contends that \xc2\xa73 of RLUIPA falls within the residual clause of \xc2\xa71003 and therefore \xc2\xa71003 waives Texas\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign immunity to RLUIPA suits for damages.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even assuming that a residual clause like the one in \xc2\xa71003 could constitute an unequivocal textual waiver, \xc2\xa73 is not unequivocally a \xe2\x80\x9cstatute prohibiting discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of \xc2\xa71003.[Footnote 9] The text of \xc2\xa73 does not prohibit \xe2\x80\x9cdiscrimination\xe2\x80\x9d; rather, it prohibits \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial burden[s]\xe2\x80\x9d on religious exercise. This distinction is especially conspicuous in light of \xc2\xa72 of RLUIPA, in which Congress expressly prohibited \xe2\x80\x9cland use regulation[s] that discriminat[e] \xe2\x80\xa6 on the basis of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc(b)(2). A waiver of sovereign immunity must be \xe2\x80\x9cstrictly construed, in terms of its scope, in favor of the sovereign.\xe2\x80\x9d Lane, 518 U. S., at 192. We cannot say that the residual clause clearly extends to \xc2\xa73; a State might reasonably conclude that the clause covers only provisions using the term \xe2\x80\x9cdiscrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The statutory provisions specifically listed in \xc2\xa71003 confirm that \xc2\xa73 does not unequivocally come within the scope of the residual clause. \xe2\x80\x9c[G]eneral words,\xe2\x80\x9d such as the residual clause here, \xe2\x80\x9care construed to embrace only objects similar in nature to those objects enumerated by the preceding specific words.\xe2\x80\x9d Washington State Dept. of Social and Health Servs. v. Guardianship Estate of Keffeler, 537 U. S. 371, 384 (2003) (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Jarecki v. G.\xc2\xa0D. Searle & Co., 367 U. S. 303, 307 (1961) (noting that this maxim \xe2\x80\x9cis often wisely applied where a word is capable of many meanings in order to avoid the giving of unintended breadth to the Acts of Congress\xe2\x80\x9d). Unlike \xc2\xa73, each of the statutes specifically enumerated in \xc2\xa71003 explicitly prohibits \xe2\x80\x9cdiscrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d See 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7794(a); 20 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71681(a); 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa76101, 6102; 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000d.[Footnote 10]

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude that States, in accepting federal funding, do not consent to waive their sovereign immunity to private suits for money damages under RLUIPA because no statute expressly and unequivocally includes such a waiver. The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit is affirmed.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor, with whom Justice Breyer joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court holds that the term \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d is too ambiguous to provide States with clear notice that they will be liable for monetary damages under the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act of 2000 (RLUIPA), 114 Stat. 803, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc et seq. I dis-agree. No one disputes that, in accepting federal funds, the States consent to suit for violations of RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s substantive provisions; the only question is what relief is available to plaintiffs asserting injury from such violations. That monetary damages are \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d is, in my view, self-evident. Under general remedies principles, the usual remedy for a violation of a legal right is damages. Consistent with these principles, our precedents make clear that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d includes monetary relief. By adopting a contrary reading of the term, the majority severely undermines the \xe2\x80\x9cbroad protection of religious exercise\xe2\x80\x9d Congress intended the statute to provide. \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x933(g). For these reasons, I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court acknowledges, the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9cStates may waive their sovereign immunity\xe2\x80\x9d is an \xe2\x80\x9cunremarkable\xe2\x80\x9d one. Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Florida, 517 U. S. 44, 65 (1996); see also Alden v. Maine, 527 U. S. 706, 737 (1999) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e have not questioned the general proposition that a State may waive its sovereign immunity and consent to suit\xe2\x80\x9d); Atascadero State Hospital v. Scanlon, 473 U. S. 234, 238 (1985) (noting the \xe2\x80\x9cwell-established\xe2\x80\x9d prin-ciple that \xe2\x80\x9cif a State waives its immunity and consents \nto suit in federal court, the Eleventh Amendment does \nnot bar the action\xe2\x80\x9d); Petty v. Tennessee-Missouri Bridge Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 359 U. S. 275, 276 (1959) (noting that a State may waive sovereign immunity \xe2\x80\x9cat its pleasure\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither the majority nor respondents (hereinafter Texas) dispute that, pursuant to its power under the Spending Clause, U. S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa01, Congress may secure a State\xe2\x80\x99s consent to suit as a condition of the State\xe2\x80\x99s receipt of federal funding.[Footnote 1] See College Savings Bank v. Florida Prepaid Postsecondary Ed. Expense Bd., 527 U. S. 666, 686 (1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress may, in the exercise of its spending power, condition its grant of funds to the States upon their taking certain actions that Congress could not require them to take, and \xe2\x80\xa6 acceptance of the funds entails an agreement to the actions\xe2\x80\x9d); Atascadero, 473 U. S., at 247 (suggesting that a federal statute can \xe2\x80\x9ccondition participation in the programs funded under the [statute] on a State\xe2\x80\x99s consent to waive its constitutional immunity\xe2\x80\x9d). As with all waivers of sovereign immunity, the question is whether the State has unequivocally consented to suit in federal court. See College Savings Bank, 527 U. S., at 680; Atascadero, 473 U. S., at 238, n.\xc2\xa01.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, in order to attach a waiver of sovereign immunity to federal funds, Congress \xe2\x80\x9cmust do so unambiguously,\xe2\x80\x9d so as to \xe2\x80\x9cenable the States to exercise their choice knowingly.\xe2\x80\x9d Pennhurst State School and Hospital v. Halderman, 451 U. S. 1, 17 (1981). In other words, the State must have notice of the condition it is accepting. See Arlington Central School Dist. Bd. of Ed. v. Murphy, 548 U. S. 291, 298 (2006) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]lear notice \xe2\x80\xa6 is required under the Spending Clause\xe2\x80\x9d). The reason for requiring notice is simple: \xe2\x80\x9cStates cannot knowingly accept conditions of which they are \xe2\x80\x98unaware\xe2\x80\x99 or which they are \xe2\x80\x98unable to ascertain.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 296 (quoting Pennhurst, 451 U. S., at 17). In assessing whether a federal statute provides clear notice of the conditions attached, \xe2\x80\x9cwe must view the [statute] from the perspective of a state official who is engaged in the process of deciding whether the State should accept [federal] funds and the obligations that go with those funds.\xe2\x80\x9d Arlington Central, 548 U. S., at 296.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is also no dispute that RLUIPA clearly conditions a State\xe2\x80\x99s receipt of federal funding on its consent to suit for violations of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s substantive provisions. The statute states that \xe2\x80\x9cprogram[s] or activit[ies] that receiv[e] Federal financial assistance\xe2\x80\x9d may not impose a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial burden on the religious exercise of a person residing in or confined to an institution.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931. When such a burden has been imposed, the victim \xe2\x80\x9cmay assert a violation of [RLUIPA] as a claim \xe2\x80\xa6 in a judicial proceeding and obtain appropriate relief against a government,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(a), which the statute defines, as relevant, as \xe2\x80\x9ca State, county, municipality, or other governmental entity created under the authority of a State,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x935(4)(A)(i). Accordingly, it is evident that Texas had notice that, in accepting federal funds, it waived its sovereign immunity to suit by institutionalized persons upon whom it has imposed an unlawful substantial burden. See Madison v. Virginia, 474 F.\xc2\xa03d 118, 130 (CA4 2006) (\xe2\x80\x9cOn its face, RLUIPA \xe2\x80\xa6 creates a private cause of action against the State, and Virginia cannot be heard to claim that it was unaware of this condition\xe2\x80\x9d (citations omitted)); Benning v. Georgia, 391 F.\xc2\xa03d 1299, 1305 (CA11 2004) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress unambiguously required states to waive their sovereign immunity from suits filed by prisoners to enforce RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court holds that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d does not provide state officials clear notice that monetary relief will be available against the States, meaning that they could not have waived their immunity with respect to that particular type of liability. This holding is contrary \nto general remedies principles and our precedents.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0RLUIPA straightforwardly provides a private right of action to \xe2\x80\x9cobtain appropriate relief against a government.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(a). Under \xe2\x80\x9cour traditional approach to deciding what remedies are available for violation of a federal right,\xe2\x80\x9d damages are the default\xe2\x80\x94and equitable relief the exception\xe2\x80\x94for \xe2\x80\x9cit is axiomatic that a court should determine the adequacy of a remedy in law before resorting to equitable relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Franklin v. Gwinnett County Public Schools, 503 U. S. 60, 75\xe2\x80\x9376 (1992); see also Weinberger v. Romero-Barcelo, 456 U. S. 305, 312 (1982) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court has repeatedly held that the basis for injunctive relief in the federal courts has always been \xe2\x80\xa6 the inadequacy of legal remedies\xe2\x80\x9d); Bivens v. Six Unknown Fed. Narcotics Agents, 403 U. S. 388, 395 (1971) (\xe2\x80\x9cHistorically, damages have been regarded as the ordinary remedy for an invasion of personal interests in liberty\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Monsanto Co. v. Geertson Seed Farms, 561 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 24) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn injunction is a drastic and extraordinary remedy, which should not be granted as a matter of course\xe2\x80\x9d). It is unsurprising, therefore, that on more than one occasion Congress has felt it necessary to clarify in the text of a statute that it meant the terms \xe2\x80\x9crelief\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d to exclude damages. See 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7702 (providing that, under the Administrative Procedure Act, \xe2\x80\x9crelief other than money damages\xe2\x80\x9d is available against a federal agency to remedy a \xe2\x80\x9clegal wrong\xe2\x80\x9d); see also 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76395(e)(1) (providing a cause of action for \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief,\xe2\x80\x9d but specifying that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in this subsection shall authorize any person to recover damages\xe2\x80\x9d); 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7797(b)(5) (similar).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If, despite the clarity of this background principle, state officials reading RLUIPA were somehow still uncertain as to whether the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d encompasses monetary damages, our precedents would relieve any doubt. In Franklin we made clear that, \xe2\x80\x9cabsent clear direction to the contrary by Congress,\xe2\x80\x9d federal statutes providing a private right of action authorize all \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief,\xe2\x80\x9d including damages, against violators of its substantive terms. 503 U. S., at 70\xe2\x80\x9371, 75\xe2\x80\x9376. We reiterated this principle in Barnes v. Gorman, 536 U. S. 181, 185, 187 (2002), affirming that \xe2\x80\x9cthe scope of \xe2\x80\x98appropriate relief\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d includes compensatory damages.[Footnote 2] The holdings in these cases are fully consistent with the general principle that monetary relief is available for violations of the substantive conditions Congress attaches, through Spending Clause legislation, to the acceptance of federal funding. See Davis v. Monroe County Bd. of Ed., 526 U. S. 629, 640 (1999) (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]ursuant to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 authority under the Spending Clause \xe2\x80\xa6 private damages actions are available\xe2\x80\x9d); Gebser v. Lago Vista Independent School Dist., 524 U. S. 274, 287 (1998) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen Congress attaches conditions to the award of federal funds under its spending power \xe2\x80\xa6 private actions holding the recipient liable in monetary damages\xe2\x80\x9d are permissible). It would be an odd derogation of the normal rules of statutory construction for state officials reading RLUIPA to assume that Congress drafted the statute in ignorance of these unambiguous precedents. See Merck & Co. v. Reynolds, 559 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 12) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe normally assume that, when Congress enacts statutes, it is aware of relevant judicial precedent\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 3]

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, it is difficult to understand the basis for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s position that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(a) fails to provide state officials with clear notice that waiving sovereign immunity to monetary relief is a condition of accepting federal funds. In arguing that \xe2\x80\x9ca waiver of sovereign immunity to other types of relief does not waive immunity to damages,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6 (emphasis added), the majority appears to accept that equitable relief is available to RLUIPA plaintiffs. See Madison, 474 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 131 (holding that a RLUIPA plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cclaims for equitable relief are not barred by the Eleventh Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. 560 F.\xc2\xa03d 316, 331, 336 (CA5 2009) (reversing the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of summary judgment to Texas on one of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s RLUIPA claims for declaratory and injunctive relief). The explanation for the majority\xe2\x80\x99s implicit acceptance of suits for injunctive and declaratory relief is obvious enough: It would be a particularly curious reading of the statute to conclude that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 express provision of a private right of action to seek \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d against \xe2\x80\x9ca State\xe2\x80\x9d nonetheless left plaintiffs suing for state violations of RLUIPA with no available relief.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is not apparent, however, why the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d is too ambiguous to secure a waiver of state sovereign immunity with respect to damages but is clear enough as to injunctive and other forms of equitable relief. The majority appears to believe that equitable relief is a \xe2\x80\x9csuitable\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d remedy for a state violation of RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s substantive provisions but monetary relief is not; therefore, a state official reading the \xe2\x80\x9copen-ended and ambiguous\xe2\x80\x9d phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d will be unaware that it includes damages but fully apprised that it makes equitable relief available. See ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. But sovereign immunity is not simply a defense against certain classes of remedies\xe2\x80\x94it is a defense against being sued at all. See, e.g., Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. South Carolina Ports Authority, 535 U. S. 743, 766 (2002). As a result, there is no inherent reason why the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d would provide adequate notice as to equitable remedies but not as to monetary ones. In fact, as discussed earlier, in light of general remedies principles the presumption arguably should be the reverse. See supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority suggests that equitable relief is the sole \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d for statutory violations \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the defendant is a sovereign.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. There can be little doubt, however, that the \xe2\x80\x9cappropriateness\xe2\x80\x9d of relief to be afforded a civil plaintiff is generally determined by the nature of the injury to his legal rights. See Franklin, 503 U. S., at 76 (concluding that monetary damages were \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d because equitable relief offered no redress for the injury suffered); see also Milliken v. Bradley, 433 U. S. 267, 280 (1977) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he nature of the \xe2\x80\xa6 remedy is to be determined by the nature and scope of the \xe2\x80\xa6 violation\xe2\x80\x9d); Bell v. Hood, 327 U. S. 678, 684 (1946) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]here federally protected rights have been invaded, it has been the rule from the beginning that courts will be alert to adjust their remedies so as to grant the necessary relief\xe2\x80\x9d). In support of its proposition the majority cites only to a case in which we expressly rejected the argument that state sovereign immunity operates differently according to what type of relief is sought. See Federal Maritime, 535 U. S., at 765 (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]overeign immunity applies regardless of whether a private plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s suit is for monetary damages or some other type of relief\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. id., at 769 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he primary function of sovereign immunity is not to protect state treasuries, but to afford the States the dignity and respect due sovereign entities\xe2\x80\x9d (citation omitted)). Nor is the basis for the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view apparent from the other cases that it cites.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s additional arguments in support of its holding also fail to persuade. The majority contends that the use of a \xe2\x80\x9ccontext-dependent\xe2\x80\x9d word like \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d necessarily renders the provision ambiguous. Ante, at 7. But the fact that the precise relief afforded by a court may vary depending on the particular injury to be addressed in a given case does not render \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(a) ambiguous; it simply means that Congress meant for that provision to be comprehensive. See Pennsylvania Dept. of Corrections \nv. Yeskey, 524 U. S. 206, 212 (1998) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he fact that \na statute can be applied in situations not expressly anti-cipated by Congress does not demonstrate ambiguity. It demonstrates breadth\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); cf. West v. Gibson, 527 U. S. 212, 217\xe2\x80\x93218 (1999) (holding that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate remedies\xe2\x80\x9d in 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x9316(b) includes remedies not expressly enumerated).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Next, the majority repeats Texas\xe2\x80\x99s dictionary-based contention that in using the word \xe2\x80\x9crelief\xe2\x80\x9d Congress meant to \xe2\x80\x9cconnot[e] equitable relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. This proposition suffers from three flaws. First, it is not established by the dictionary to which the majority cites. See Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1293 (7th ed. 1999) (\xe2\x80\x9crelief: \xe2\x80\xa6 Also termed remedy\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 1296 (\xe2\x80\x9cremedy: \xe2\x80\xa6 The means of enforcing a right or preventing or redressing a wrong; legal or equitable relief\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Second, it is inconsistent with our precedent. See Barnes, 536 U. S., at 185\xe2\x80\x93187 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d includes monetary and injunctive relief). Third, it is undermined by the fact that, on numerous occasions, Congress has deemed it necessary to specify that \xe2\x80\x9crelief\xe2\x80\x9d includes injunctive and other equitable relief. See 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7973i(e) (authorizing the Attorney General to \xe2\x80\x9ccommence a civil action \nfor appropriate relief, including permanent or tempo-\nrary injunction\xe2\x80\x9d); see also 2 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7437g(a)(6)(A); 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71324a(f)(2); 12 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71715z\xe2\x80\x934a(b); 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76309(a). If the term \xe2\x80\x9crelief\xe2\x80\x9d already connotes equitable relief\xe2\x80\x94and only equitable relief\xe2\x80\x94additional explication is redundant.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the majority asserts that because the parties to this case advance opposing \xe2\x80\x9cplausible arguments\xe2\x80\x9d regarding the correct interpretation of RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s text, we must conclude that the statute is ambiguous. Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. This view of how we adjudicate cases is incorrect as a descriptive matter. See, e.g., Carcieri v. Salazar, 555 U. S. 379, 390 (2009) (reviewing the parties\xe2\x80\x99 conflicting textual interpretations of a statute but concluding that it was unambiguous nonetheless). Moreover, I cannot agree with the majority that our capacity to interpret authoritatively the text of a federal statute is held hostage to the litigants\xe2\x80\x99 strategic arguments. If this were true, there would be few cases in which we would be able to decide that a statute was unambiguous.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that States accepting federal funds have not consented to suit for monetary relief cannot be reconciled with the fact that the availability of such relief is evident in light of RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s plain terms and the principles animating our relevant precedents. In so holding, the majority discovers ambiguity where none is to be found.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is another reason to question the soundness of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(a) severely undermines Congress\xe2\x80\x99 unmistakably stated intent in passing the statute: to afford \xe2\x80\x9cbroad protection of religious exercise, to the maximum extent permitted by the terms of [the statute] and the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x933(g). I find it improbable that, in light of this express statutory purpose and the history of \xe2\x80\x9clong-running congressional efforts to accord religious exercise heightened protection from government-imposed burdens,\xe2\x80\x9d Cutter v. Wilkinson, 544 U. S. 709, 714 (2005), state officials would read RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s relief provision in the same limited manner the majority does.[Footnote 5]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the majority acknowledges, RLUIPA was Congress\xe2\x80\x99 second attempt to guarantee by statute the \xe2\x80\x9cbroad protection\xe2\x80\x9d of religious exercise that we found to be unwarranted as a constitutional matter in Employment Div., Dept. of Human Resources of Ore. v. Smith, 494 U. S. 872 (1990). As we have previously recognized, in passing RLUIPA Congress was clearly concerned that state institutions regularly imposed \xe2\x80\x9cfrivolous or arbitrary barriers imped[ing] institutionalized persons\xe2\x80\x99 religious exercise.\xe2\x80\x9d Cutter, 544 U. S., at 716 (internal quotation marks omitted); see also 146 Cong. Rec. 16698, 16699 (2000) (joint statement of Sen. Hatch and Sen. Kennedy on RLUIPA) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhether from indifference, ignorance, bigotry, or lack of resources, some institutions restrict religious liberty in egregious and unnecessary ways\xe2\x80\x9d); ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cInstitutional residents\xe2\x80\x99 rights to practice their faith is at the mercy of those running the institution \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). It is difficult to believe that Congress would have devoted such care and effort to establishing significant statutory protections for religious exercise and specifically extended those protections to persons in state institutions, yet withheld from plaintiffs a crucial tool for securing the rights the statute guarantees.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By depriving prisoners of a damages remedy for violations of their statutory rights, the majority ensures that plaintiffs suing state defendants under RLUIPA will be forced to seek enforcement of those rights with one hand tied behind their backs. Most obviously, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s categorical denial of monetary relief means that a plaintiff who prevails on the merits of his claim that a State has substantially burdened his religious exercise will often be denied redress for the injury he has suffered, because in many instances \xe2\x80\x9cprospective relief accords \xe2\x80\xa6 no remedy at all.\xe2\x80\x9d Franklin, 503 U. S., at 76; see H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 102\xe2\x80\x9340, pt. 2, p.\xc2\xa025 (1991) (Report of Committee on the Judiciary on the Civil Rights Act of 1991) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe limitation of relief under Title VII to equitable remedies often means that victims \xe2\x80\xa6 may not recover for the very real effects of the [statutory violation]\xe2\x80\x9d). Injunctive relief from a federal court may address a violation going forward, but this \nfact will be of cold comfort to the victims of serious, non-recurring violations for which equitable relief may be inappropriate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, the unavailability of monetary relief will effectively shield unlawful policies and practices from judicial review in many cases. Under state law, discretion to transfer prisoners \xe2\x80\x9cin a wide variety of circumstances is vested in prison officials.\xe2\x80\x9d Meachum v. Fano, 427 U. S. 215, 227 (1976). A number of RLUIPA suits seeking injunctive relief have been dismissed as moot because the plaintiff was transferred from the institution where the alleged violation took place prior to adjudication on the merits. See, e.g., Colvin v. Caruso, 605 F.\xc2\xa03d 282, 287, 289 (CA6 2010); Simmons v. Herrera, No. C 09\xe2\x80\x930318 JSW (PR), 2010 WL 1233815, *3 (ND Cal., Mar. 26, 2010); see generally Brief for American Civil Liberties Union et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 8\xe2\x80\x9311. Absent a damages remedy, longstanding RLUIPA challenges may well be dismissed for lack of a case or controversy conferring Article III jurisdiction on the federal court. Cf. Moussazadeh v. Texas Dept. of Crim. Justice, Civ. Action No. G\xe2\x80\x9307\xe2\x80\x93574, 2009 WL 819497, *9 (SD Tex., Mar. 26, 2009) (dismissing as moot plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s RLUIPA claim because he had been transferred to a facility that provided kosher food), remanded, 364 Fed. Appx. 110 (CA5 2010); Opening Brief for Plaintiff-Appellant in Moussazadeh v. Texas Dept. of Crim. Justice, No. 09\xe2\x80\x9340400 (CA5), p.\xc2\xa011 (noting that transfer to a special facility took place 19 months after the plaintiff filed suit and just before discovery\xe2\x80\x94which had been stayed 12 months for negotiation\xe2\x80\x94was scheduled to recommence). Or, as happened in this case, officials may change the policy while litigation is pending. The fact of \xe2\x80\x9cvoluntary cessation\xe2\x80\x9d may allow some of these claims to go forward, but many will nonetheless be dismissed as moot (as happened in this case).[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, under the rule the majority announces, Congress can revise RLUIPA to provide specifically for monetary relief against the States, perhaps by inserting the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cincluding monetary relief\xe2\x80\x9d into the text of \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(a). But we have never demanded that a waiver be presented in a particular formulation to be effective; we only require that it be clear. See, e.g., Edelman v. Jordan, 415 U. S. 651, 673 (1974) (holding that waiver may be found in \xe2\x80\x9cexpress language\xe2\x80\x9d or by \xe2\x80\x9coverwhelming implications from the text\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). In holding to the contrary, the majority erects a formalistic barrier to the vindication of statutory rights deliberately provided for by Congress.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More problematically, because there is no apparent reason why the term \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d is sufficiently clear as to equitable relief but not as to monetary relief, we are left with the very real possibility that, in order to secure a waiver of immunity under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s new rule, Congress must now itemize in the statutory text every type of relief meant to be available against sovereign defendants. I, for one, do not relish the prospect of federal courts being presented with endless state challenges to all manner of federal statutes, on the ground that Congress failed to predict that a laundry list of terms must be included to waive sovereign immunity to all forms of relief. I would avoid the problems the majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision invites and hold instead that, as is the case here, when a general statutory term like \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relief\xe2\x80\x9d is used, clear no-tice has been provided and a State\xe2\x80\x99s acceptance of federal funds constitutes a waiver of sovereign immunity to all relief, equitable and monetary.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As explained above, nothing in our precedent demands the result the majority reaches today. The conclusion that RLUIPA fails to provide States with sufficient notice that they are liable for monetary relief cannot be squared with the straightforward terms of the statute and the general principles evident in our prior cases. For these reasons, and because the majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision significantly undermines Congress\xe2\x80\x99 ability to provide needed redress for violations of individuals\xe2\x80\x99 rights under federal law, I respectfully dissent.
