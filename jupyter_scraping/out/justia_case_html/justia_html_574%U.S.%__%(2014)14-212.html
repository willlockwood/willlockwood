SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

JEREMY CARROLL v. ANDREW CARMAN, et ux.

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93212.\xe2\x80\x83Decided November 10, 2014

\n

\n \n Per Curiam.

\n

\n \n On July 3, 2009, the Pennsylvania State Police Department received a report that a man named Michael Zita had stolen a car and two loaded handguns. The report also said that Zita might have fled to the home of Andrew and Karen Carman. The department sent Officers Jeremy Carroll and Brian Roberts to the Carmans\xe2\x80\x99 home to investigate. Neither officer had been to the home before. 749 F.\xc2\xa03d 192, 195 (CA3 2014).

\n

\n \n The officers arrived in separate patrol cars around 2:30 p.m. The Carmans\xe2\x80\x99 house sat on a corner lot\xe2\x80\x94the front of the house faced a main street while the left (as viewed from the front) faced a side street. The officers initially drove to the front of the house, but after discovering that parking was not available there, turned right onto the side street. As they did so, they saw several cars parked side-by-side in a gravel parking area on the left side of the Carmans\xe2\x80\x99 property. The officers parked in the \xe2\x80\x9cfirst available spot,\xe2\x80\x9d at \xe2\x80\x9cthe far rear of the property.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Tr. 70 (Apr. 8, 2013)).

\n

\n \n The officers exited their patrol cars. As they looked toward the house, the officers saw a small structure (either a carport or a shed) with its door open and a light on. Id., at 71. Thinking someone might be inside, Officer Carroll walked over, \xe2\x80\x9cpoked [his] head\xe2\x80\x9d in, and said \xe2\x80\x9cPennsylvania State Police.\xe2\x80\x9d 749 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 195 (quoting Tr. 71 (Apr. 8, 2013); alteration in original). No one was there, however, so the officers continued walking toward the house. As they approached, they saw a sliding glass door that opened onto a ground-level deck. Carroll thought the sliding glass door \xe2\x80\x9clooked like a customary entryway,\xe2\x80\x9d so he and Officer Roberts decided to knock on it. 749 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 195 (quoting Tr. 83 (Apr. 8, 2013)).

\n

\n \n As the officers stepped onto the deck, a man came outof the house and \xe2\x80\x9cbelligerent[ly] and aggressively approached\xe2\x80\x9d them. 749 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 195. The officers identified themselves, explained they were looking for Michael Zita, and asked the man for his name. The man refused to answer. Instead, he turned away from the officers and appeared to reach for his waist. Id., at 195\xe2\x80\x93196. Carroll grabbed the man\xe2\x80\x99s right arm to make sure he was not reaching for a weapon. The man twisted away from Carroll, lost his balance, and fell into the yard. Id., at 196.

\n

\n \n At that point, a woman came out of the house and asked what was happening. The officers again explained that they were looking for Zita. The woman then identified herself as Karen Carman, identified the man as her husband, Andrew Carman, and told the officers that Zita was not there. In response, the officers asked for permission to search the house for Zita. Karen Carman consented, and everyone went inside. Ibid.

\n

\n \n The officers searched the house, but did not find Zita. They then left. The Carmans were not charged with any crimes. Ibid.

\n

\n \n The Carmans later sued Officer Carroll in Federal District Court under\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. Among other things, they alleged that Carroll unlawfully entered their property in violation of the\n \nFourth Amendment when he went into their backyard and onto their deck without a warrant. 749 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 196.

\n

\n \n At trial, Carroll argued that his entry was lawful under the \xe2\x80\x9cknock and talk\xe2\x80\x9d exception to the warrant requirement. That exception, he contended, allows officers to knock on someone\xe2\x80\x99s door, so long as they stay \xe2\x80\x9con those portions of [the] property that the general public is allowed to go on.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 7 (Apr. 8, 2013). The Carmans responded that a normal visitor would have gone to their front door, rather than into their backyard or onto their deck. Thus, they argued, the \xe2\x80\x9cknock and talk\xe2\x80\x9d exception did not apply.

\n

\n \n At the close of Carroll\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief, the parties each moved for judgment as a matter of law. The District Court denied both motions, and sent the case to a jury. As relevant here, the District Court instructed the jury that the \xe2\x80\x9cknock and talk\xe2\x80\x9d exception \xe2\x80\x9callows officers without a warrant to knock on a resident\xe2\x80\x99s door or otherwise approach the residence seeking to speak to the inhabitants, just as any private citizen might.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 24 (Apr. 10, 2013). The District Court further explained that \xe2\x80\x9cofficers should restrict their movements to walkways, driveways, porches and places where visitors could be expected to go.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The jury then returned a verdict for Carroll.

\n

\n \n The Carmans appealed, and the Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit reversed in relevant part. The court held that Officer Carroll violated the\n \nFourth Amendment as a matter of law because the \xe2\x80\x9cknock and talk\xe2\x80\x9d exception \xe2\x80\x9crequires that police officers begin their encounter at the front door, where they have an implied invitation to go.\xe2\x80\x9d 749 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 199. The court also held that Carroll was not entitled to qualified immunity because his actions violated clearly established law. Ibid. The court therefore reversed the District Court and held that the Carmans were entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

\n

\n \n Carroll petitioned for certiorari. We grant the petition and reverse the Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s determination that Carroll was not entitled to qualified immunity.

\n

\n \n A government official sued under \xc2\xa71983 is entitled to qualified immunity unless the official violated a statutory or constitutional right that was clearly established at the time of the challenged conduct. See Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 3). A right is clearly established only if its contours are sufficiently clear that \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable official would understand that what he is doing violates that right.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Creighton,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635,\n640 (1987)\n. In other words, \xe2\x80\x9cexisting precedent must have placed the statutory or constitutional question beyond debate.\xe2\x80\x9d al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9). This doctrine \xe2\x80\x9cgives government officials breathing room to make reasonable but mistaken judgments,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprotects \xe2\x80\x98all but the plainly incompetent or those who knowingly violate the law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 12) (quoting Malley v. Briggs,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335,\n341 (1986)\n).

\n

\n \n Here the Third Circuit cited only a single case to support its decision that Carroll was not entitled to qualified immunity\xe2\x80\x94Estate of Smith v. Marasco, 318 F.\xc2\xa03d 497 (CA3 2003). Assuming for the sake of argument that a controlling circuit precedent could constitute clearly established federal law in these circumstances, see Reichle v. Howards, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 7), Marasco does not clearly establish that Carroll violated the Carmans\xe2\x80\x99\n \nFourth Amendment rights.

\n

\n \n In Marasco, two police officers went to Robert Smith\xe2\x80\x99s house and knocked on the front door. When Smith did not respond, the officers went into the backyard, and at least one entered the garage. 318 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 519. The court acknowledged that the officers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9centry into the curtilage after not receiving an answer at the front door might be reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 520. It held, however, that the District Court had not made the factual findings needed to decide that issue. Id., at 521. For example, the Third Circuit noted that the record \xe2\x80\x9cdid not discuss the layout of the property or the position of the officers on that prop-erty,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cthere [was] no indication of whether the officers followed a path or other apparently open route that would be suggestive of reasonableness.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The court therefore remanded the case for further proceedings.

\n

\n \n In concluding that Officer Carroll violated clearly established law in this case, the Third Circuit relied exclusively on Marasco\xe2\x80\x99s statement that \xe2\x80\x9centry into the curtilage after not receiving an answer at the front door might be reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 520; see 749 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 199 (quoting Marasco, supra, at 520). In the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, that statement clearly established that a \xe2\x80\x9cknock and talk\xe2\x80\x9d must begin at the front door. But that conclusion does not follow. Marasco held that an unsuccessful \xe2\x80\x9cknock and talk\xe2\x80\x9d at the front door does not automatically allow officers to go onto other parts of the property. It did not hold, however, that knocking on the front door is required before officers go onto other parts of the property that are open to visitors. Thus, Marasco simply did not answer the question whether a \xe2\x80\x9cknock and talk\xe2\x80\x9d must begin at the front door when visitors may also go to the back door. Indeed, the house at issue seems not to have even had a back door, let alone one that visitors could use. 318 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 521.

\n

\n \n Moreover, Marasco expressly stated that \xe2\x80\x9cthere [was] no indication of whether the officers followed a path or other apparently open route that would be suggestive of reasonableness.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That makes Marasco wholly different from this case, where the jury necessarily decided that Carroll \xe2\x80\x9crestrict[ed] [his] movements to walkways, driveways, porches and places where visitors could be expected to go.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 24 (Apr. 10, 2013).

\n

\n \n To the extent that Marasco says anything about this case, it arguably supports Carroll\xe2\x80\x99s view. In Marasco, the Third Circuit noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]fficers are allowed to knock on a residence\xe2\x80\x99s door or otherwise approach the residence seeking to speak to the inhabitants just as any private citizen may.\xe2\x80\x9d 318 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 519. The court also said that, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98when the police come on to private property .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and restrict their movements to places visitors could be expected to go (e.g., walkways, driveways, porches), observations made from such vantage points are not covered by the\n \nFourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting 1 W. LaFave, Search and Seizure \xc2\xa72.3(f\xc2\xa0) (3d ed. 1996 and Supp. 2003) (footnotes omitted)). Had Carroll read those statements before going to the Carmans\xe2\x80\x99 house, he may have concluded\xe2\x80\x94quite reasonably\xe2\x80\x94that he was allowed to knock on any door that was open to visitors.[1]*

\n

\n \n The Third Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision is even more perplexing in comparison to the decisions of other federal and state courts, which have rejected the rule the Third Circuit adopted here. For example, in United States v. Titemore, 437 F.\xc2\xa03d 251 (CA2 2006), a police officer approached a house that had two doors. The first was a traditional door that opened onto a driveway; the second was a sliding glass door that opened onto a small porch. The officer chose to knock on the latter. Id., at 253\xe2\x80\x93254. On appeal, the defendant argued that the officer had unlawfully entered his property without a warrant in violation of the\n \nFourth Amendment. Id., at 255\xe2\x80\x93256. But the Second Circuit rejected that argument. As the court explained, the sliding glass door was \xe2\x80\x9ca primary entrance visible to and used by the public.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 259. Thus, \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause [the officer] approached a principal entrance to the home using a route that other visitors could be expected to take,\xe2\x80\x9d the court held that he did not violate the\n \nFourth Amendment. Id., at 252.

\n

\n \n The Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in United States v. James, 40 F.\xc2\xa03d 850 (1994), vacated on other grounds,\n\n516 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1022 (1995)\n, provides another example. There, police officers approached a duplex with multiple entrances. Bypassing the front door, the officers \xe2\x80\x9cused a paved walkway along the side of the duplex leading to the rear side door.\xe2\x80\x9d 40 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 862. On appeal, the defendant argued that the officers violated his\n \nFourth Amendment rights when they went to the rear side door. The Seventh Circuit rejected that argument, explaining that the rear side door was \xe2\x80\x9caccessible to the general public\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwas commonly used for entering the duplex from the nearby alley.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In situations \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the back door of a residence is readily accessible to the general public,\xe2\x80\x9d the court held, \xe2\x80\x9cthe\n \nFourth Amendment is not implicated when police officers approach that door in the reasonable belief that it is a principal means of access to the dwelling.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. See also, e.g., United States v. Garcia, 997 F.\xc2\xa02d 1273, 1279\xe2\x80\x931280 (CA9 1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf the front and back of a residence are read-ily accessible from a public place, like the driveway and parking area here, the\n \nFourth Amendment is not implicated when officers go to the back door reasonably believing itis used as a principal entrance to the dwelling\xe2\x80\x9d); State v. Domicz, 188 N.\xc2\xa0J. 285, 302, 907 A.\xc2\xa02d 395, 405 (2006) (\xe2\x80\x9cwhen a law enforcement officer walks to a front or back door for the purpose of making contact with a resident and reasonably believes that the door is used by visitors, he is not unconstitutionally trespassing on to the property\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \n We do not decide today whether those cases were correctly decided or whether a police officer may conduct a \xe2\x80\x9cknock and talk\xe2\x80\x9d at any entrance that is open to visitors rather than only the front door. \xe2\x80\x9cBut whether or not the constitutional rule applied by the court below was correct, it was not \xe2\x80\x98beyond debate.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Stanton v. Sims, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (per curiam) (slip op., at 8) (quoting al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9)). The Third Circuit therefore erred when it held that Carroll was not entitled to qualified immunity.

\n

\n \n The petition for certiorari is granted. The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
