ILLINOIS v. CABALLES

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of illinois

\n

No. 03\xe2\x80\x93923.Argued November 10, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 24, 2005

\n

After an Illinois state trooper stopped respondent for speeding and radioed in, a second trooper, overhearing the transmission, drove to the scene with his narcotics-detection dog and walked the dog around respondent\xe2\x80\x99s car while the first trooper wrote respondent a warning ticket. When the dog alerted at respondent\xe2\x80\x99s trunk, the officers searched the trunk, found marijuana, and arrested respondent. At respondent\xe2\x80\x99s drug trial, the court denied his motion to suppress the seized evidence, holding, inter alia, that the dog\xe2\x80\x99s alerting provided sufficient probable cause to conduct the search. Respondent was convicted, but the Illinois Supreme Court reversed, finding that because there were no specific and articulable facts to suggest drug activity, use of the dog unjustifiably enlarged a routine traffic stop into a drug investigation.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A dog sniff conducted during a concededly lawful traffic stop that reveals no information other than the location of a substance that no individual has any right to possess does not violate the Fourth Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

207 Ill. 2d 504, 802 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 202, vacated and remanded.

\n

Ginsburg, J., filed a dissenting opinion, in which Souter, J., joined. Rehnquist, C.\xc2\xa0J., took no part in the decision of the case.

ILLINOIS, PETITIONER v. ROY I. CABALLES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of\nillinois

\n

[January 24, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Illinois State Trooper Daniel Gillette stopped respondent for speeding on an interstate highway. When Gillette radioed the police dispatcher to report the stop, a second trooper, Craig Graham, a member of the Illinois State Police Drug Interdiction Team, overheard the transmission and immediately headed for the scene with his narcotics-detection dog. When they arrived, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s car was on the shoulder of the road and respondent was in Gillette\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle. While Gillette was in the process of writing a warning ticket, Graham walked his dog around respondent\xe2\x80\x99s car. The dog alerted at the trunk. Based on that alert, the officers searched the trunk, found marijuana, and arrested respondent. The entire incident lasted less than 10 minutes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent was convicted of a narcotics offense and sentenced to 12 years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment and a $256,136 fine. The trial judge denied his motion to suppress the seized evidence and to quash his arrest. He held that the officers had not unnecessarily prolonged the stop and that the dog alert was sufficiently reliable to provide probable cause to conduct the search. Although the Appellate Court affirmed, the Illinois Supreme Court reversed, concluding that because the canine sniff was performed without any \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98specific and articulable facts\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to suggest drug activity, the use of the dog \xe2\x80\x9cunjustifiably enlarg[ed] the scope of a routine traffic stop into a drug investigation.\xe2\x80\x9d 207 Ill. 2d 504, 510, 802 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 202, 205 (2003).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question on which we granted certiorari, 541 U. S. 972 (2004), is narrow: \xe2\x80\x9cWhether the Fourth Amendment requires reasonable, articulable suspicion to justify using a drug-detection dog to sniff a vehicle during a legitimate traffic stop.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i. Thus, we proceed on the assumption that the officer conducting the dog sniff had no information about respondent except that he had been stopped for speeding; accordingly, we have omitted any reference to facts about respondent that might have triggered a modicum of suspicion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, the initial seizure of respondent when he was stopped on the highway was based on probable cause, and was concededly lawful. It is nevertheless clear that a seizure that is lawful at its inception can violate the Fourth Amendment if its manner of execution unreasonably infringes interests protected by the Constitution. United States v. Jacobsen, 466 U. S. 109, 124 (1984). A seizure that is justified solely by the interest in issuing a warning ticket to the driver can become unlawful if it is prolonged beyond the time reasonably required to complete that mission. In an earlier case involving a dog sniff that occurred during an unreasonably prolonged traffic stop, the Illinois Supreme Court held that use of the dog and the subsequent discovery of contraband were the product of an unconstitutional seizure. People v. Cox, 202 Ill. 2d 462, 782 N. E. 2d 275 (2002). We may assume that a similar result would be warranted in this case if the dog sniff had been conducted while respondent was being unlawfully detained.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the state-court proceedings, however, the judges carefully reviewed the details of Officer Gillette\xe2\x80\x99s conversations with respondent and the precise timing of his radio transmissions to the dispatcher to determine whether he had improperly extended the duration of the stop to enable the dog sniff to occur. We have not recounted those details because we accept the state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the duration of the stop in this case was entirely justified by the traffic offense and the ordinary inquiries incident to such a stop.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite this conclusion, the Illinois Supreme Court held that the initially lawful traffic stop became an unlawful seizure solely as a result of the canine sniff that occurred outside respondent\xe2\x80\x99s stopped car. That is, the court characterized the dog sniff as the cause rather than the consequence of a constitutional violation. In its view, the use of the dog converted the citizen-police encounter from a lawful traffic stop into a drug investigation, and because the shift in purpose was not supported by any reasonable suspicion that respondent possessed narcotics, it was unlawful. In our view, conducting a dog sniff would not change the character of a traffic stop that is lawful at its inception and otherwise executed in a reasonable manner, unless the dog sniff itself infringed respondent\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionally protected interest in privacy. Our cases hold that it did not.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Official conduct that does not \xe2\x80\x9ccompromise any legitimate interest in privacy\xe2\x80\x9d is not a search subject to the Fourth Amendment. Jacobsen, 466 U. S., at 123. We have held that any interest in possessing contraband cannot be deemed \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus, governmental conduct that only reveals the possession of contraband \xe2\x80\x9ccompromises no legitimate privacy interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This is because the expectation \xe2\x80\x9cthat certain facts will not come to the attention of the authorities\xe2\x80\x9d is not the same as an interest in \xe2\x80\x9cprivacy that society is prepared to consider reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 122 (punctuation omitted). In United States v. Place, 462 U. S. 696 (1983), we treated a canine sniff by a well-trained narcotics-detection dog as "sui generis" because it "discloses only the presence or absence of narcotics, a contraband item." Id., at 707; see also Indianapolis v. Edmond, 531 U. S. 32, 40 (2000). Respondent likewise concedes that \xe2\x80\x9cdrug sniffs are designed, and if properly conducted are generally likely, to reveal only the presence of contraband.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 17. Although respondent argues that the error rates, particularly the existence of false positives, call into question the premise that drug-detection dogs alert only to contraband, the record contains no evidence or findings that support his argument. Moreover, respondent does not suggest that an erroneous alert, in and of itself, reveals any legitimate private information, and, in this case, the trial judge found that the dog sniff was sufficiently reliable to establish probable cause to conduct a full-blown search of the trunk.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, the use of a well-trained narcotics-detection dog\xe2\x80\x94one that "does not expose noncontraband items that otherwise would remain hidden from public view," Place, 462 U. S., at 707\xe2\x80\x94during a lawful traffic stop, generally does not implicate legitimate privacy interests. In this case, the dog sniff was performed on the exterior of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s car while he was lawfully seized for a traffic violation. Any intrusion on respondent\xe2\x80\x99s privacy expectations does not rise to the level of a constitutionally cognizable infringement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This conclusion is entirely consistent with our recent decision that the use of a thermal-imaging device to detect the growth of marijuana in a home constituted an unlawful search. Kyllo v. United States, 533 U. S. 27 (2001). Critical to that decision was the fact that the device was capable of detecting lawful activity\xe2\x80\x94in that case, intimate details in a home, such as \xe2\x80\x9cat what hour each night the lady of the house takes her daily sauna and bath.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 38. The legitimate expectation that information about perfectly lawful activity will remain private is categorically distinguishable from respondent\xe2\x80\x99s hopes or expectations concerning the nondetection of contraband in the trunk of his car. A dog sniff conducted during a concededly lawful traffic stop that reveals no information other than the location of a substance that no individual has any right to possess does not violate the Fourth Amendment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Illinois Supreme Court is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

ILLINOIS, PETITIONER v. ROY I. CABALLES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of\nillinois

[January 24, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Souter, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I would hold that using the dog for the purposes of determining the presence of marijuana in the car\xe2\x80\x99s trunk was a search unauthorized as an incident of the speeding stop and unjustified on any other ground. I would accordingly affirm the judgment of the Supreme Court of Illinois, and I respectfully dissent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In United States v. Place, 462 U. S. 696 (1983), we categorized the sniff of the narcotics-seeking dog as \xe2\x80\x9csui generis\xe2\x80\x9d under the Fourth Amendment and held it was not a search. Id., at 707. The classification rests not only upon the limited nature of the intrusion, but on a further premise that experience has shown to be untenable, the assumption that trained sniffing dogs do not err. What we have learned about the fallibility of dogs in the years since Place was decided would itself be reason to call for reconsidering Place\xe2\x80\x99s decision against treating the intentional use of a trained dog as a search. The portent of this very case, however, adds insistence to the call, for an uncritical adherence to Place would render the Fourth Amendment indifferent to suspicionless and indiscriminate sweeps of cars in parking garages and pedestrians on sidewalks; if a sniff is not preceded by a seizure subject to Fourth Amendment notice, it escapes Fourth Amendment review entirely unless it is treated as a search. We should not wait for these developments to occur before rethinking Place\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, which invites such untoward consequences.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the heart both of Place and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion today is the proposition that sniffs by a trained dog are sui generis because a reaction by the dog in going alert is a response to nothing but the presence of contraband.[Footnote 2] See ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he sniff discloses only the presence or absence of narcotics, a contraband item\xe2\x80\x9d); ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x934 (assuming \xe2\x80\x9cthat a canine sniff by a well-trained narcotics dog will only reveal \xe2\x80\x98the presence or absence of narcotics, a contraband item\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Place, supra, at 707)). Hence, the argument goes, because the sniff can only reveal the presence of items devoid of any legal use, the sniff \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not implicate legitimate privacy interests\xe2\x80\x9d and is not to be treated as a search. Ante, at 4.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The infallible dog, however, is a creature of legal fiction. Although the Supreme Court of Illinois did not get into the sniffing averages of drug dogs, their supposed infallibility is belied by judicial opinions describing well-trained animals sniffing and alerting with less than perfect accuracy, whether owing to errors by their handlers, the limitations of the dogs themselves, or even the pervasive contamination of currency by cocaine. See, e.g., United States v. Kennedy, 131 F.\xc2\xa03d 1371, 1378 (CA10 1997) (describing a dog that had a 71% accuracy rate); United States v. Scarborough, 128 F.\xc2\xa03d 1373, 1378, n.\xc2\xa03 (CA10 1997) (describing a dog that erroneously alerted 4 times out of 19 while working for the postal service and 8% of the time over its entire career); United States v. Limares, 269 F.\xc2\xa03d 794, 797 (CA7 2001) (accepting as reliable a dog that gave false positives between 7 and 38% of the time); Laime v. State, 347 Ark. 142, 159, 60 S. W. 3d 464, 476 (2001) (speaking of a dog that made between 10 and 50 errors); United States v. $242,484.00, 351 F.\xc2\xa03d 499, 511 (CA11 2003) (noting that because as much as 80% of all currency in circulation contains drug residue, a dog alert \xe2\x80\x9cis of little value\xe2\x80\x9d), vacated on other grounds by rehearing en banc, 357 F.\xc2\xa03d 1225 (CA11 2004); United States v. Carr, 25 F.\xc2\xa03d 1194, 1214\xe2\x80\x931217 (CA3 1994) (Becker, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] substantial portion of United States currency \xe2\x80\xa6 is tainted with sufficient traces of controlled substances to cause a trained canine to alert to their presence\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, a study cited by Illinois in this case for the proposition that dog sniffs are \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally reliable\xe2\x80\x9d shows that dogs in artificial testing situations return false positives anywhere from 12.5 to 60% of the time, depending on the length of the search. See Reply Brief for Petitioner 13; K. Garner et\xc2\xa0al., Duty Cycle of the Detector Dog: A Baseline Study 12 (Apr. 2001) (prepared under Federal Aviation Administration grant by the Institute for Biological Detection Systems of Auburn University). In practical terms, the evidence is clear that the dog that alerts hundreds of times will be wrong dozens of times.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once the dog\xe2\x80\x99s fallibility is recognized, however, that ends the justification claimed in Place for treating the sniff as sui generis under the Fourth Amendment: the sniff alert does not necessarily signal hidden contraband, and opening the container or enclosed space whose emanations the dog has sensed will not necessarily reveal contraband or any other evidence of crime. This is not, of course, to deny that a dog\xe2\x80\x99s reaction may provide reasonable suspicion, or probable cause, to search the container or enclosure; the Fourth Amendment does not demand certainty of success to justify a search for evidence or contraband. The point is simply that the sniff and alert cannot claim the certainty that Place assumed, both in treating the deliberate use of sniffing dogs as sui generis and then taking that characterization as a reason to say they are not searches subject to Fourth Amendment scrutiny. And when that aura of uniqueness disappears, there is no basis in Place\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, and no good reason otherwise, to ignore the actual function that dog sniffs perform. They are conducted to obtain information about the contents of private spaces beyond anything that human senses could perceive, even when conventionally enhanced. The information is not provided by independent third parties beyond the reach of constitutional limitations, but gathered by the government\xe2\x80\x99s own officers in order to justify searches of the traditional sort, which may or may not reveal evidence of crime but will disclose anything meant to be kept private in the area searched. Thus in practice the government\xe2\x80\x99s use of a trained narcotics dog functions as a limited search to reveal undisclosed facts about private enclosures, to be used to justify a further and complete search of the enclosed area. And given the fallibility of the dog, the sniff is the first step in a process that may disclose \xe2\x80\x9cintimate details\xe2\x80\x9d without revealing contraband, just as a thermal-imaging device might do, as described in Kyllo v. United States, 533 U. S. 27 (2001).[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It makes sense, then, to treat a sniff as the search that it amounts to in practice, and to rely on the body of our Fourth Amendment cases, including Kyllo, in deciding whether such a search is reasonable. As a general proposition, using a dog to sniff for drugs is subject to the rule that the object of enforcing criminal laws does not, without more, justify suspicionless Fourth Amendment intrusions. See Indianapolis v. Edmond, 531 U. S. 32, 41\xe2\x80\x9342 (2000). Since the police claim to have had no particular suspicion that Caballes was violating any drug law,[Footnote 4] this sniff search must stand or fall on its being ancillary to the traffic stop that led up to it. It is true that the police had probable cause to stop the car for an offense committed in the officer\xe2\x80\x99s presence, which Caballes concedes could have justified his arrest. See Brief for Respondent 31. There is no occasion to consider authority incident to arrest, however, see Knowles v. Iowa, 525 U. S. 113 (1998), for the police did nothing more than detain Caballes long enough to check his record and write a ticket. As a consequence, the reasonableness of the search must be assessed in relation to the actual delay the police chose to impose, and as Justice Ginsburg points out in her opinion, post, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, the Fourth Amendment consequences of stopping for a traffic citation are settled law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Berkemer v. McCarty, 468 U. S. 420, 439\xe2\x80\x93440 (1984), followed in Knowles, supra, at 488, we held that the analogue of the common traffic stop was the limited detention for investigation authorized by Terry v. Ohio, 392 U. S. 1 (1968). While Terry authorized a restricted incidental search for weapons when reasonable suspicion warrants such a safety measure, id., at 25\xe2\x80\x9326, the Court took care to keep a Terry stop from automatically becoming a foot in the door for all investigatory purposes; the permissible intrusion was bounded by the justification for the detention, id., at 29\xe2\x80\x9330.[Footnote 5] Although facts disclosed by enquiry within this limit might give grounds to go further, the government could not otherwise take advantage of a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s immobility to search for evidence unrelated to the reason for the detention. That has to be the rule unless Terry is going to become an open-sesame for general searches, and that rule requires holding that the police do not have reasonable grounds to conduct sniff searches for drugs simply because they have stopped someone to receive a ticket for a highway offense. Since the police had no indication of illegal activity beyond the speed of the car in this case, the sniff search should be held unreasonable under the Fourth Amendment and its fruits should be suppressed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nothing in the case relied upon by the Court, United States v. Jacobsen, 466 U. S. 109 (1984), unsettled the limit of reasonable enquiry adopted in Terry. In Jacobsen, the Court found that no Fourth Amendment search occurred when federal agents analyzed powder they had already lawfully obtained. The Court noted that because the test could only reveal whether the powder was cocaine, the owner had no legitimate privacy interest at stake. 466 U. S., at 123. As already explained, however, the use of a sniffing dog in cases like this is significantly different and properly treated as a search that does indeed implicate Fourth Amendment protection.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Jacobsen, once the powder was analyzed, that was effectively the end of the matter: either the powder was cocaine, a fact the owner had no legitimate interest in concealing, or it was not cocaine, in which case the test revealed nothing about the powder or anything else that was not already legitimately obvious to the police. But in the case of the dog sniff, the dog does not smell the disclosed contraband; it smells a closed container. An affirmative reaction therefore does not identify a substance the police already legitimately possess, but informs the police instead merely of a reasonable chance of finding contraband they have yet to put their hands on. The police will then open the container and discover whatever lies within, be it marijuana or the owner\xe2\x80\x99s private papers. Thus, while Jacobsen could rely on the assumption that the enquiry in question would either show with certainty that a known substance was contraband or would reveal nothing more, both the certainty and the limit on disclosure that may follow are missing when the dog sniffs the car.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court today does not go so far as to say explicitly that sniff searches by dogs trained to sense contraband always get a free pass under the Fourth Amendment, since it reserves judgment on the constitutional significance of sniffs assumed to be more intrusive than a dog\xe2\x80\x99s walk around a stopped car, ante, at 4. For this reason, I do not take the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on Jacobsen as actually signaling recognition of a broad authority to conduct suspicionless sniffs for drugs in any parked car, about which Justice Ginsburg is rightly concerned, post, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, or on the person of any pedestrian minding his own business on a sidewalk. But the Court\xe2\x80\x99s stated reasoning provides no apparent stopping point short of such excesses. For the sake of providing a workable framework to analyze cases on facts like these, which are certain to come along, I would treat the dog sniff as the familiar search it is in fact, subject to scrutiny under the Fourth Amendment.[Footnote 7]

Ohio, 392 U. S. 1 (1968), the analysis I, too, adopt.

Unreasonable sniff searches for marijuana are not necessarily unreasonable sniff searches for destructive or deadly material if suicide bombs are a societal risk.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg, with whom Justice Souter joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Illinois State Police Trooper Daniel Gillette stopped Roy Caballes for driving 71 miles per hour in a zone with a posted speed limit of 65 miles per hour. Trooper Craig Graham of the Drug Interdiction Team heard on the radio that Trooper Gillette was making a traffic stop. Although Gillette requested no aid, Graham decided to come to the scene to conduct a dog sniff. Gillette informed Caballes that he was speeding and asked for the usual documents\xe2\x80\x94driver\xe2\x80\x99s license, car registration, and proof of insurance. Caballes promptly provided the requested documents but refused to consent to a search of his vehicle. After calling his dispatcher to check on the validity of Caballes\xe2\x80\x99 license and for outstanding warrants, Gillette returned to his vehicle to write Caballes a warning ticket. Interrupted by a radio call on an unrelated matter, Gillette was still writing the ticket when Trooper Graham arrived with his drug-detection dog. Graham walked the dog around the car, the dog alerted at Caballes\xe2\x80\x99 trunk, and, after opening the trunk, the troopers found marijuana. 207 Ill. 2d 504, 506\xe2\x80\x93507, 802 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 202, 203 (2003).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Supreme Court of Illinois held that the drug evidence should have been suppressed. Id., at 506, 802 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 202. Adhering to its decision in People v. Cox, 202 Ill. 2d 462, 782 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 275 (2002), the court employed a two-part test taken from Terry v. Ohio, 392 U. S. 1 (1968), to determine the overall reasonableness of the stop. 207 Ill. 2d, at 508, 802 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 204. The court asked first \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the officer\xe2\x80\x99s action was justified at its inception,\xe2\x80\x9d and second \xe2\x80\x9cwhether it was reasonably related in scope to the circumstances which justified the interference in the first place.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting People v. Brownlee, 186 Ill. 2d 501, 518\xe2\x80\x93519, 713 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 556, 565 (1999) (in turn quoting Terry, 392 U. S., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320)). \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is undisputed,\xe2\x80\x9d the court observed, \xe2\x80\x9cthat the traffic stop was properly initiated\xe2\x80\x9d; thus, the dispositive inquiry trained on the \xe2\x80\x9csecond part of the Terry test,\xe2\x80\x9d in which \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he State bears the burden of establishing that the conduct remained within the scope of the stop.\xe2\x80\x9d 207 Ill. 2d, at 509, 802 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 204.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court concluded that the State failed to offer sufficient justification for the canine sniff: \xe2\x80\x9cThe police did not detect the odor of marijuana in the car or note any other evidence suggesting the presence of illegal drugs.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Lacking \xe2\x80\x9cspecific and articulable facts\xe2\x80\x9d supporting the canine sniff, ibid. (quoting Cox, 202 Ill. 2d, at 470\xe2\x80\x93471, 782 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 281), the court ruled, \xe2\x80\x9cthe police impermissibly broadened the scope of the traffic stop in this case into a drug investigation.\xe2\x80\x9d 207 Ill. 2d, at 509, 802 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 204.[Footnote 1] I would affirm the Illinois Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment and hold that the drug sniff violated the Fourth Amendment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Terry v. Ohio, the Court upheld the stop and subsequent frisk of an individual based on an officer\xe2\x80\x99s observation of suspicious behavior and his reasonable belief that the suspect was armed. See 392 U. S., at 27\xe2\x80\x9328. In a Terry-type investigatory stop, \xe2\x80\x9cthe officer\xe2\x80\x99s action [must be] justified at its inception, and \xe2\x80\xa6 reasonably related in scope to the circumstances which justified the interference in the first place.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 20. In applying Terry, the Court has several times indicated that the limitation on \xe2\x80\x9cscope\xe2\x80\x9d is not confined to the duration of the seizure; it also encompasses the manner in which the seizure is conducted. See, e.g., Hiibel v. Sixth Judicial Dist. Court of Nev., Humboldt Cty., 542 U. S. ___, ___ (2004) (slip op., at 9) (an officer\xe2\x80\x99s request that an individual identify himself \xe2\x80\x9chas an immediate relation to the purpose, rationale, and practical demands of a Terry stop\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Hensley, 469 U. S. 221, 235 (1985) (examining, under Terry, both \xe2\x80\x9cthe length and intrusiveness of the stop and detention\xe2\x80\x9d); Florida v. Royer, 460 U. S. 491, 500 (1983) (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n investigative detention must be temporary and last no longer than is necessary to effectuate the purpose of the stop [and] \xe2\x80\xa6 the investigative methods employed should be the least intrusive means reasonably available to verify or dispel the officer\xe2\x80\x99s suspicion \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cA routine traffic stop,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court has observed, \xe2\x80\x9cis a relatively brief encounter and \xe2\x80\x98is more analogous to a so-called Terry stop \xe2\x80\xa6 than to a formal arrest.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Knowles v. Iowa, 525 U. S. 113, 117 (1998) (quoting Berkemer v. McCarty, 468 U. S. 420, 439 (1984)); see also ante, at 6 (Souter, J., dissenting) (The government may not \xe2\x80\x9ctake advantage of a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s immobility to search for evidence unrelated to the reason for the detention.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 2] I would apply Terry\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable-relation test, as the Illinois Supreme Court did, to determine whether the canine sniff impermissibly expanded the scope of the initially valid seizure of Caballes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is hardly dispositive that the dog sniff in this case may not have lengthened the duration of the stop. Cf. ante, at 2 (\xe2\x80\x9cA seizure \xe2\x80\xa6 can become unlawful if it is prolonged beyond the time reasonably required to complete [the initial] mission.\xe2\x80\x9d). Terry, it merits repetition, instructs that any investigation must be \xe2\x80\x9creasonably related in scope to the circumstances which justified the interference in the first place.\xe2\x80\x9d 392 U. S., at 20 (emphasis added). The unwarranted and nonconsensual expansion of the seizure here from a routine traffic stop to a drug investigation broadened the scope of the investigation in a manner that, in my judgment, runs afoul of the Fourth Amendment.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court rejects the Illinois Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment and, implicitly, the application of Terry to a traffic stop converted, by calling in a dog, to a drug search. The Court so rules, holding that a dog sniff does not render a seizure that is reasonable in time unreasonable in scope. Ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. Dog sniffs that detect only the possession of contraband may be employed without offense to the Fourth Amendment, the Court reasons, because they reveal no lawful activity and hence disturb no legitimate expectation of privacy. Ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, the Court diminishes the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s force by abandoning the second Terry inquiry (was the police action \xe2\x80\x9creasonably related in scope to the circumstances [justifiying] the [initial] interference\xe2\x80\x9d). 392 U. S., at 20. A drug-detection dog is an intimidating animal. Cf. United States v. Williams, 356 F.\xc2\xa03d 1268, 1276 (CA10 2004) (McKay, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cdrug dogs are not lap dogs\xe2\x80\x9d). Injecting such an animal into a routine traffic stop changes the character of the encounter between the police and the motorist. The stop becomes broader, more adversarial, and (in at least some cases) longer. Caballes\xe2\x80\x94who, as far as Troopers Gillette and Graham knew, was guilty solely of driving six miles per hour over the speed limit\xe2\x80\x94was exposed to the embarrassment and intimidation of being investigated, on a public thoroughfare, for drugs. Even if the drug sniff is not characterized as a Fourth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9csearch,\xe2\x80\x9d cf. Indianapolis v. Edmond, 531 U. S. 32, 40 (2000); United States v. Place, 462 U. S. 696, 707 (1983), the sniff surely broadened the scope of the traffic-violation-related seizure.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court has never removed police action from Fourth Amendment control on the ground that the action is well calculated to apprehend the guilty. See, e.g., United States v. Karo, 468 U. S. 705, 717 (1984) (Fourth Amendment warrant requirement applies to police monitoring of a beeper in a house even if \xe2\x80\x9cthe facts [justify] believing that a crime is being or will be committed and that monitoring the beeper wherever it goes is likely to produce evidence of criminal activity.\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Minnesota v. Carter, 525 U. S. 83, 110 (1998) (Ginsburg, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cFourth Amendment protection, reserved for the innocent only, would have little force in regulating police behavior toward either the innocent or the guilty.\xe2\x80\x9d). Under today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, every traffic stop could become an occasion to call in the dogs, to the distress and embarrassment of the law-abiding population.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Illinois Supreme Court, it seems to me, correctly apprehended the danger in allowing the police to search for contraband despite the absence of cause to suspect its presence. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, in contrast, clears the way for suspicionless, dog-accompanied drug sweeps of parked cars along sidewalks and in parking lots. Compare, e.g., United States v. Ludwig, 10 F.\xc2\xa03d 1523, 1526\xe2\x80\x931527 (CA10 1993) (upholding a search based on a canine drug sniff of a parked car in a motel parking lot conducted without particular suspicion), with United States v. Quinn, 815 F.\xc2\xa02d 153, 159 (CA1 1987) (officers must have reasonable suspicion that a car contains narcotics at the moment a dog sniff is performed), and Place, 462 U. S., at 706\xe2\x80\x93707 (Fourth Amendment not violated by a dog sniff of a piece of luggage that was seized, pre-sniff, based on suspicion of drugs). Nor would motorists have constitutional grounds for complaint should police with dogs, stationed at long traffic lights, circle cars waiting for the red signal to turn green.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision also undermines this Court\xe2\x80\x99s situation-sensitive balancing of Fourth Amendment interests in other contexts. For example, in Bond v. United States, 529 U. S. 334, 338\xe2\x80\x93339 (2000), the Court held that a bus passenger had an expectation of privacy in a bag placed in an overhead bin and that a police officer\xe2\x80\x99s physical manipulation of the bag constituted an illegal search. If canine drug sniffs are entirely exempt from Fourth Amendment inspection, a sniff could substitute for an officer\xe2\x80\x99s request to a bus passenger for permission to search his bag, with this significant difference: The passenger would not have the option to say \xe2\x80\x9cNo.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dog sniff in this case, it bears emphasis, was for drug detection only. A dog sniff for explosives, involving security interests not presented here, would be an entirely different matter. Detector dogs are ordinarily trained not as all-purpose sniffers, but for discrete purposes. For example, they may be trained for narcotics detection or for explosives detection or for agricultural products detection. See, e.g., U. S. Customs & Border Protection, Canine Enforcement Training Center, Training Program Course Descriptions, http://www.cbp.gov/xp/cgov/border_security/\ncanines/training_program.xml (all Internet materials as visited Dec. 16, 2004, and available in the Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file) (describing Customs training courses in narcotics detection); Transportation Security Administration,\nCanine and Explosives Program, http://www.tsa.gov/\npublic/display? theme=32 (describing Transportation Security Administration\xe2\x80\x99s explosives detection canine program); U. S. Dept. of Agriculture, Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service, USDA\xe2\x80\x99s Detector Dogs: Pro-\ntecting American Agriculture (Oct. 2001), available at http://www.aphis.usda.gov/oa/pubs/detdogs.pdf (describing USDA Beagle Brigade detector dogs trained to detect prohibited fruits, plants, and meat); see also Jennings, Origins and History of Security and Detector Dogs, in Canine Sports Medicine and Surgery 16, 18\xe2\x80\x9319 (M. Bloomberg, J. Dee, & R. Taylor eds. 1998) (describing narcotics detector dogs used by Border Patrol and Customs, and bomb detector dogs used by the Federal Aviation Administration and the Secret Service, but noting the possibility in some circumstances of cross training dogs for multiple tasks); S. Chapman, Police Dogs in North America 64, 70\xe2\x80\x9379 (1990) (describing narcotics- and explosives-detection dogs and noting the possibility of cross training). There is no indication in this case that the dog accompanying Trooper Graham was trained for anything other than drug detection. See 207 Ill. 2d, at 507, 802 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 203 (\xe2\x80\x9cTrooper Graham arrived with his drug-detection dog \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Petitioner 3 (\xe2\x80\x9cTrooper Graham arrived with a drug-detection dog \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has distinguished between the general interest in crime control and more immediate threats to public safety. In Michigan Dept. of State Police v. Sitz, 496 U. S. 444 (1990), this Court upheld the use of a sobriety traffic checkpoint. Balancing the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in preventing drunk driving, the extent to which that could be accomplished through the checkpoint program, and the degree of intrusion the stops involved, the Court determined that the State\xe2\x80\x99s checkpoint program was consistent with the Fourth Amendment. Id., at 455. Ten years after Sitz, in Indianapolis v. Edmond, 531 U. S. 32, this Court held that a drug interdiction checkpoint violated the Fourth Amendment. Despite the illegal narcotics traffic that the Nation is struggling to stem, the Court explained, a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral interest in crime control\xe2\x80\x9d did not justify the stops. Id., at 43\xe2\x80\x9344. The Court distinguished the sobriety checkpoints in Sitz on the ground that those checkpoints were designed to eliminate an \xe2\x80\x9cimmediate, vehicle-bound threat to life and limb.\xe2\x80\x9d 531 U. S., at 43.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The use of bomb-detection dogs to check vehicles for explosives without doubt has a closer kinship to the sobriety checkpoints in Sitz than to the drug checkpoints in Edmond. As the Court observed in Edmond: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Fourth Amendment would almost certainly permit an appropriately tailored roadblock set up to thwart an imminent terrorist attack \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 531 U. S., at 44. Even if the Court were to change course and characterize a dog sniff as an independent Fourth Amendment search, see ante, p. ___ (Souter, J., dissenting), the immediate, present danger of explosives would likely justify a bomb sniff under the special needs doctrine. See, e.g., ante, at 8, n.\xc2\xa07 (Souter, J., dissenting); Griffin v. Wisconsin, 483 U. S. 868, 873 (1987) (permitting exceptions to the warrant and probable-cause requirements for a search when \xe2\x80\x9cspecial needs, beyond the normal need for law enforcement,\xe2\x80\x9d make those requirements impracticable (quoting New Jersey v. T.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0O., 469 U. S. 325, 351 (1985) (Blackmun, J., concurring in judgment))).

\n

***

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated, I would hold that the police violated Caballes\xe2\x80\x99 Fourth Amendment rights when, without cause to suspect wrongdoing, they conducted a dog sniff of his vehicle. I would therefore affirm the judgment of the Illinois Supreme Court.
