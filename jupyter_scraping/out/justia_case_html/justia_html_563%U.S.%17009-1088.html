\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

CULLEN, ACTING WARDEN v. PINHOLSTER

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x931088.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 9, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 4, 2011

\n

A California jury convicted respondent Pinholster on two counts of first-degree murder. At the penalty phase before the same jury, the prosecution produced eight witnesses, who testified about Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s history of threatening and violent behavior. Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel, who unsuccessfully sought to exclude the aggravating evidence on the ground that the prosecution had not given Pinholster proper notice under California law, called only Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother. Counsel did not call a psychiatrist, though they had consulted with Dr. Stalberg, who had diagnosed Pinholster with antisocial personality disorder. The jury recommended the death penalty, and Pinholster was sentenced to death. Pinholster twice sought habeas relief in the California Supreme Court, alleging, inter alia, that his trial counsel had failed to adequately investigate and present mitigating evidence during the penalty phase. He introduced additional evidence to support his claim: school, medical, and legal records; and declarations from family members, one of his trial attorneys, and Dr. Woods, a psychiatrist who diagnosed him with bipolar mood disorder and seizure disorders, and who criticized Dr. Stalberg\xe2\x80\x99s report. Each time, the State Supreme Court unanimously and summarily denied the claim on the merits. Subsequently, a Federal District Court held an evidentiary hearing and granted Pinholster federal habeas relief under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. Affirming, the en banc Ninth Circuit considered the new evidence adduced in the District Court hearing and held that the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision \xe2\x80\x9cinvolved an unreasonable application of \xe2\x80\xa6 clearly established Federal law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72254(d)(1).

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Review under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) is limited to the record that was before the state court that adjudicated the claim on the merits. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0As amended by the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), \xc2\xa72254 sets several limits on a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s power to grant habeas relief to a state prisoner. As relevant here, a claim that has been \xe2\x80\x9cadjudicated on the merits in State court proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cshall not be granted \xe2\x80\xa6 unless the adjudication\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c(1) resulted in a decision that was contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c(2) resulted in a decision that was based on an unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented in the State court proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72254(d). This \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult to meet,\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington v. Richter, 562 U. S. ___, ___, and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98highly deferential standard\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 demands that state-court decisions be given the benefit of the doubt,\xe2\x80\x9d Woodford v. Visciotti, 537 U. S. 19, 24. Section 2254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s backward-looking language\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cresulted in\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinvolved\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94requires an examination of the state-court decision at the time it was made. It follows that the record under review is also limited to the record in existence at that same time\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the state-court record. This understanding is compelled by \xe2\x80\x9cthe broader context of the statute as a whole,\xe2\x80\x9d which demonstrates Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to channel prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 claims first to state courts. Robinson v. Shell Oil Co., 519 U. S. 337, 341. It is also consistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, which emphasize that \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) review focuses on what a state court knew and did. See, e.g., Lockyer v. Andrade, 538 U. S. 63, 71\xe2\x80\x9372. Moreover, it is consistent with Schriro v. Landrigan, 550 U. S. 465, 474, which explained that a federal habeas court is \xe2\x80\x9cnot required to hold an evidentiary hearing\xe2\x80\x9d when the state-court record \xe2\x80\x9cprecludes habeas relief\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa72254(d)\xe2\x80\x99s limitations. The Ninth Circuit wrongly interpreted Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 420, and Holland v. Jackson, 542 U. S. 649, as supporting the contrary view. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0This holding does not render superfluous \xc2\xa72254(e)(2)\xe2\x80\x94which limits the federal habeas courts\xe2\x80\x99 discretion to take new evidence in an evidentiary hearing. At a minimum, \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) still restricts their discretion in claims that were not adjudicated on the merits in state court. Although state prisoners may sometimes submit new evidence in federal court, AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statutory scheme is designed to strongly discourage them from doing so. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Remand for a properly limited review is inappropriate here, because the Ninth Circuit ruled, in the alternative, that Pinholster merited habeas relief on the state-court record alone. P.\xc2\xa014.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0On the record before the state court, Pinholster was not entitled to federal habeas relief. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x98\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0To satisfy \xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable application\xe2\x80\x9d prong, he must show that \xe2\x80\x9cthere was no reasonable basis\xe2\x80\x9d for the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s summary decision. Richter, supra, at ___. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668, provides the clearly established federal law here. To overcome the strong presumption that counsel has acted competently, id., at 690, a defendant must show that counsel failed to act \xe2\x80\x9creasonabl[y] considering all the circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 688, and must prove the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 694. Review here is thus \xe2\x80\x9cdoubly deferential,\xe2\x80\x9d Knowles v. Mirzayance, 556 U. S. ___, ___, requiring a \xe2\x80\x9chighly deferential\xe2\x80\x9d look at counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance, Strickland, supra, at 689, through \xc2\xa72254(d)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdeferential lens,\xe2\x80\x9d Mirzayance, supra, at ___, n.\xc2\xa02. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Pinholster has not shown that the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision that he could not demonstrate deficient performance by his trial counsel necessarily involved an unreasonable application of federal law. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9326.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0The state-court record supports the idea that his counsel acted strategically to get the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s aggravation witnesses excluded for lack of notice, and if that failed, to put on his mother as a mitigation witness. Billing records show that they spent time investigating mitigating evidence. The record also shows that they had an unsympathetic client who had boasted about his criminal history during the guilt phase, leaving them with limited mitigation strategies. In addition, when Dr. Stalberg concluded that Pinholster had no significant mental disorder or defect, he was aware of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s medical and social history. Given these impediments, it would have been a reasonable penalty-phase strategy to focus on evoking sympathy for Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother. Pinholster has responded with only a handful of post-hoc nondenials by one of his lawyers. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit misapplied Strickland when it drew from this Court\xe2\x80\x99s recent cases a \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional duty to investigate\xe2\x80\x9d and a principle that it was prima facie ineffective for counsel to abandon an investigation based on rudimentary knowledge of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s background. Beyond the general requirement of reasonableness, \xe2\x80\x9cspecific guidelines are not appropriate\xe2\x80\x9d under Strickland. 466 U. S., at 688. Nor did the Ninth Circuit properly apply the strong presumption of competence mandated by Strickland. Pp.\xc2\xa023\xe2\x80\x9326.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Even if his trial counsel had performed deficiently, Pinholster also has failed to show that the State Supreme Court must have unreasonably concluded that he was not prejudiced. Pp. 26\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0To determine \xe2\x80\x9cwhether there is a reasonable probability that, absent the errors, the sentencer \xe2\x80\xa6 would have concluded that\xe2\x80\x9d death was not warranted, Strickland, supra, at 695, the aggravating evidence is reweighed \xe2\x80\x9cagainst the totality of available mitigating evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d Wiggins v. Smith, 539 U. S. 510, 534. Here, the State presented extensive aggravating evidence at both the guilt and penalty phases. The mitigating evidence consisted primarily of the penalty-phase testimony of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother and guilt-phase testimony given by his brother. After considering the evidence, the jury returned a sentence of death, which the state trial court found supported overwhelmingly by the weight of the evidence. Pp.\xc2\xa026\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0There is no reasonable probability that the additional evidence presented at Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s state proceedings would have changed the verdict. The \xe2\x80\x9cnew\xe2\x80\x9d evidence largely duplicated the mitigation evidence of his mother and brother at trial. To the extent that there were new factual allegations or evidence, much of it is of questionable mitigating value. Dr. Woods\xe2\x80\x99 testimony would have opened the door to rebuttal by a state expert; and new evidence relating to Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s substance abuse, mental illness, and criminal problems could lead a jury to conclude that he was beyond rehabilitation. The remaining new material in the state habeas record is sparse. Given what little additional mitigating evidence Pinholster presented in state habeas, the Court cannot say that the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination was unreasonable. Pp.\xc2\xa029\xe2\x80\x9330.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(3)\xc2\xa0Because this Court did not apply AEDPA deference to the question of prejudice in Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362, and Rompilla v. Beard, 545 U. S. 374, those cases lack the important \xe2\x80\x9cdoubly deferential\xe2\x80\x9d standard of Strickland and AEDPA, and thus offer no guidance with respect to whether a state court has unreasonably determined that prejudice is lacking. Pp.\xc2\xa030\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

590 F.\xc2\xa03d 651, reversed.

VINCENT CULLEN, ACTING WARDEN, PETITIONER\nv. SCOTT LYNN PINHOLSTER

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[April 4, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Scott Lynn Pinholster and two accomplices broke into a house in the middle of the night and brutally beat and stabbed to death two men who happened to interrupt the burglary. A jury convicted Pinholster of first-degree murder, and he was sentenced to death.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the California Supreme Court twice unanimously denied Pinholster habeas relief, a Federal District Court held an evidentiary hearing and granted Pinholster habeas relief under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. The District Court concluded that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel had been constitutionally ineffective at the penalty phase of trial. Sitting en banc, the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed. Pinholster v. Ayers, 590 F.\xc2\xa03d 651 (2009). Considering the new evidence adduced in the District Court hearing, the Court of Appeals held that the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision \xe2\x80\x9cwas contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72254(d)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari and now reverse.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the evening of January 8, 1982, Pinholster solicited Art Corona and Paul David Brown to help him rob Michael Kumar, a local drug dealer. On the way, they stopped at Lisa Tapar\xe2\x80\x99s house, where Pinholster put his buck knife through her front door and scratched a swastika into her car after she refused to talk to him. The three men, who were all armed with buck knives, found no one at Kumar\xe2\x80\x99s house, broke in, and began ransacking the home. They came across only a small amount of marijuana before Kumar\xe2\x80\x99s friends, Thomas Johnson and Robert Beckett, arrived and shouted that they were calling the police.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pinholster and his accomplices tried to escape through the rear door, but Johnson blocked their path. Pinholster backed Johnson onto the patio, demanding drugs and money and repeatedly striking him in the chest. Johnson dropped his wallet on the ground and stopped resisting. Beckett then came around the corner, and Pinholster attacked him, too, stabbing him repeatedly in the chest. Pinholster forced Beckett to the ground, took both men\xe2\x80\x99s wallets, and began kicking Beckett in the head. Meanwhile, Brown stabbed Johnson in the chest, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98bury[ing] his knife to the hilt.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 35 Reporter\xe2\x80\x99s Tr. 4947 (hereinafter Tr.). Johnson and Beckett died of their wounds.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Corona drove the three men to Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s apartment. While in the car, Pinholster and Brown exulted, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98We got \xe2\x80\x99em, man, we got \xe2\x80\x99em good.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. At the apartment, Pinholster washed his knife, and the three split the proceeds of the robbery: $23 and one quarter-ounce of marijuana. Although Pinholster instructed Corona to \xe2\x80\x9clay low,\xe2\x80\x9d Corona turned himself in to the police two weeks later. Id., at 4955. Pinholster was arrested shortly thereafter and threatened to kill Corona if he did not keep quiet about the burglary and murders. Corona later became the State\xe2\x80\x99s primary witness. The prosecution brought numerous charges against Pinholster, including two counts of first-degree murder.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The California trial court appointed Harry Brainard and Wilbur Dettmar to defend Pinholster on charges of first-degree murder, robbery, and burglary. Before their appointment, Pinholster had rejected other attorneys and insisted on representing himself. During that time, the State had mailed Pinholster a letter in jail informing him that the prosecution planned to offer aggravating evidence during the penalty phase of trial to support a sentence of death.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The guilt phase of the trial began on February 28, 1984. Pinholster testified on his own behalf and presented an alibi defense. He claimed that he had broken into Kumar\xe2\x80\x99s house alone at around 8 p.m. on January 8, 1982, and had stolen marijuana but denied killing anyone. Pinholster asserted that later that night around 1 a.m., while he was elsewhere, Corona went to Kumar\xe2\x80\x99s house to steal more drugs and did not return for three hours. Pinholster told the jury that he was a \xe2\x80\x9cprofessional robber,\xe2\x80\x9d not a murderer. 43 id., at 6204. He boasted of committing hundreds of robberies over the previous six years but insisted that he always used a gun, never a knife. The jury convicted Pinholster on both counts of first-degree murder.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before the penalty phase, Brainard and Dettmar moved to exclude any aggravating evidence on the ground that the prosecution had failed to provide notice of the evidence to be introduced, as required by Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa7190.3 (West 2008). At a hearing on April 24, Dettmar argued that, in reliance on the lack of notice, he was \xe2\x80\x9cnot presently prepared to offer anything by way of mitigation.\xe2\x80\x9d 52 Tr. 7250. He acknowledged, however, that the prosecutor \xe2\x80\x9cpossibly ha[d] met the [notice] requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The trial court asked whether a continuance might be helpful, but Dettmar declined, explaining that he could not think of a mitigation witness other than Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother and that additional time would not \xe2\x80\x9cmake a great deal of difference.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7257\xe2\x80\x937258. Three days later, after hearing testimony, the court found that Pinholster had received notice while representing himself and denied the motion to exclude.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The penalty phase was held before the same jury that had convicted Pinholster. The prosecution produced eight witnesses, who testified about Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s history of threatening and violent behavior, including resisting arrest and assaulting police officers, involvement with juvenile gangs, and a substantial prison disciplinary record. Defense counsel called only Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother, Burnice Brashear. She gave an account of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s troubled childhood and adolescent years, discussed Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s siblings, and described Pinholster as \xe2\x80\x9ca perfect gentleman at home.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7405. Defense counsel did not call a psychiatrist, though they had consulted Dr. John Stalberg at least six weeks earlier. Dr. Stalberg noted Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpsychopathic personality traits,\xe2\x80\x9d diagnosed him with antisocial personality disorder, and concluded that he \xe2\x80\x9cwas not under the influence of extreme mental or emotional disturbance\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the murders. App. 131.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After 2 days of deliberation, the jury unanimously voted for death on each of the two murder counts. On mandatory appeal, the California Supreme Court affirmed the judgment. People v. Pinholster, 1 Cal.\xc2\xa04th 865, 824 P.\xc2\xa02d 571 (1992).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In August 1993, Pinholster filed his first state habeas petition. Represented by new counsel, Pinholster alleged, inter alia, ineffective assistance of counsel at the penalty phase of his trial. He alleged that Brainard and Dettmar had failed to adequately investigate and present mitigating evidence, including evidence of mental disorders. Pinholster supported this claim with school, medical, and legal records, as well as declarations from family members, Brainard, and Dr. George Woods, a psychiatrist who diagnosed Pinholster with bipolar mood disorder and seizure disorders. Dr. Woods criticized Dr. Stalberg\xe2\x80\x99s report as incompetent, unreliable, and inaccurate. The California Supreme Court unanimously and summarily[Footnote 1] denied Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s penalty-phase ineffective-assistance claim \xe2\x80\x9con the substantive ground that it is without merit.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 302.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pinholster filed a federal habeas petition in April 1997. He reiterated his previous allegations about penalty-phase ineffective assistance and also added new allegations that his trial counsel had failed to furnish Dr. Stalberg with adequate background materials. In support of the new allegations, Dr. Stalberg provided a declaration stating that in 1984, Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel had provided him with only some police reports and a 1978 probation report. Dr. Stalberg explained that, had he known about the material that had since been gathered by Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s habeas counsel, he would have conducted \xe2\x80\x9cfurther inquiry\xe2\x80\x9d before concluding that Pinholster suffered only from a personality disorder. App. to Brief in Opposition 219. He noted that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s school records showed evidence of \xe2\x80\x9csome degree of brain damage.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Dr. Stalberg did not, however, retract his earlier diagnosis. The parties stipulated that this declaration had never been submitted to the California Supreme Court, and the federal petition was held in abeyance to allow Pinholster to go back to state court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In August 1997, Pinholster filed his second state habeas petition, this time including Dr. Stalberg\xe2\x80\x99s declaration and requesting judicial notice of the documents previously submitted in support of his first state habeas petition. His allegations of penalty-phase ineffective assistance of counsel mirrored those in his federal habeas petition. The California Supreme Court again unanimously and summarily denied the petition \xe2\x80\x9con the substantive ground that it is without merit.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 2] App. to Pet. for Cert. 300.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Having presented Dr. Stalberg\xe2\x80\x99s declaration to the state court, Pinholster returned to the District Court. In November 1997, he filed an amended petition for a writ of habeas corpus. His allegations of penalty-phase ineffective assistance of counsel were identical to those in his second state habeas petition. Both parties moved for summary judgment and Pinholster also moved, in the alternative, for an evidentiary hearing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court concluded that the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), 110 Stat. 1214, did not apply and granted an evidentiary hearing. Before the hearing, the State deposed Dr. Stalberg, who stated that none of the new material he reviewed altered his original diagnosis. Dr. Stalberg disagreed with Dr. Woods\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion that Pinholster suffers from bipolar disorder. Pinholster did not call Dr. Stalberg to testify at the hearing. He presented two new medical experts: Dr. Sophia Vinogradov, a psychiatrist who diagnosed Pinholster with organic personality syndrome and ruled out antisocial personality disorder, and Dr. Donald Olson, a pediatric neurologist who suggested that Pinholster suffers from partial epilepsy and brain injury. The State called Dr. F. David Rudnick, a psychiatrist who, like Dr. Stalberg, diagnosed Pinholster with antisocial personality disorder and rejected any diagnosis of bipolar disorder.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court granted habeas relief. Applying pre-AEDPA standards, the court granted the habeas petition \xe2\x80\x9cfor inadequacy of counsel by failure to investigate and present mitigation evidence at the penalty hearing.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 262. After Woodford v. Garceau, 538 U. S. 202 (2003), clarified that AEDPA applies to cases like Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s, the court amended its order but did not alter its conclusion. Over a dissent, a panel of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed. Pinholster v. Ayers, 525 F.\xc2\xa03d 742 (2008).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On rehearing en banc, the Court of Appeals vacated the panel opinion and affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of habeas relief. The en banc court held that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s evidentiary hearing was not barred by 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(e)(2). The court then determined that new evidence from the hearing could be considered in assessing whether the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision \xe2\x80\x9cwas contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). See 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 666 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress did not intend to restrict the inquiry under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) only to the evidence introduced in the state habeas court\xe2\x80\x9d). Taking the District Court evidence into account, the en banc court determined that the California Supreme Court unreasonably applied Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668 (1984), in denying Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s claim of penalty-phase ineffective assistance of counsel.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Three judges dissented and rejected the majority\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the District Court hearing was not barred by \xc2\xa72254(e)(2). 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 689 (opinion of Kozinski, \nC.\xc2\xa0J.) (characterizing Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s efforts as \xe2\x80\x9chabeas-by-sandbagging\xe2\x80\x9d). Limiting its review to the state-court record, the dissent concluded that the California Supreme Court did not unreasonably apply Strickland. 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 691\xe2\x80\x93723.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to resolve two questions. 560 U. S. ___ (2010). First, whether review under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) permits consideration of evidence introduced in an evidentiary hearing before the federal habeas court. Second, whether the Court of Appeals properly granted Pinholster habeas relief on his claim of penalty-phase ineffective assistance of counsel.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We first consider the scope of the record for a \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) inquiry. The State argues that review is limited to the record that was before the state court that adjudicated the claim on the merits. Pinholster contends that evidence presented to the federal habeas court may also be considered. We agree with the State.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As amended by AEDPA, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254 sets several limits on the power of a federal court to grant an application for a writ of habeas corpus on behalf of a state prisoner. Section 2254(a) permits a federal court to entertain only those applications alleging that a person is in state custody \xe2\x80\x9cin violation of the Constitution or laws or treaties of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Sections 2254(b) and (c) provide that a federal court may not grant such applications unless, with certain exceptions, the applicant has exhausted state remedies.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If an application includes a claim that has been \xe2\x80\x9cadjudicated on the merits in State court proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72254(d), an additional restriction applies. Under \xc2\xa72254(d), that application \xe2\x80\x9cshall not be granted with respect to [such a] claim \xe2\x80\xa6 unless the adjudication of the claim\xe2\x80\x9d:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(1) resulted in a decision that was contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States; or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2) resulted in a decision that was based on an unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented in the State court proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nThis is a \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult to meet,\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington v. Richter, 562 U. S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 12), and \xe2\x80\x9chighly deferential standard for evaluating state-court rulings, which demands that state-court decisions be given the benefit of the doubt,\xe2\x80\x9d Woodford v. Visciotti, 537 U. S. 19, 24 (2002) (per curiam) (citation and internal quotation marks omitted). The petitioner carries the burden of proof. Id., at 25.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We now hold that review under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) is limited to the record that was before the state court that adjudicated the claim on the merits. Section 2254(d)(1) refers, in the past tense, to a state-court adjudication that \xe2\x80\x9cresulted in\xe2\x80\x9d a decision that was contrary to, or \xe2\x80\x9cinvolved\xe2\x80\x9d an unreasonable application of, established law. This backward-looking language requires an examination of the state-court decision at the time it was made. It follows that the record under review is limited to the record in existence at that same timei.e., the record before the state court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This understanding of the text is compelled by \xe2\x80\x9cthe broader context of the statute as a whole,\xe2\x80\x9d which demonstrates Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to channel prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 claims first to the state courts. Robinson v. Shell Oil Co., 519 U. S. 337, 341 (1997). \xe2\x80\x9cThe federal habeas scheme leaves primary responsibility with the state courts \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Visciotti, supra, at 27. Section 2254(b) requires that prisoners must ordinarily exhaust state remedies before filing for federal habeas relief. It would be contrary to that purpose to allow a petitioner to overcome an adverse state-court decision with new evidence introduced in a federal habeas court and reviewed by that court in the first instance effectively de novo.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Limiting \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) review to the state-court record is consistent with our precedents interpreting that statu-\ntory provision. Our cases emphasize that review under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) focuses on what a state court knew and did. State-court decisions are measured against this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents as of \xe2\x80\x9cthe time the state court renders its decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Lockyer v. Andrade, 538 U. S. 63, 71\xe2\x80\x9372 (2003). To determine whether a particular decision is \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to\xe2\x80\x9d then-established law, a federal court must consider whether the decision \xe2\x80\x9capplies a rule that contradicts [such] law\xe2\x80\x9d and how the decision \xe2\x80\x9cconfronts [the] set of facts\xe2\x80\x9d that were before the state court. Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362, 405, 406 (2000) (Terry Williams). If the state-court decision \xe2\x80\x9cidentifies the correct governing legal principle\xe2\x80\x9d in existence at the time, a federal court must assess whether the decision \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonably applies that principle to the facts of the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 413. It would be strange to ask federal courts to analyze whether a state court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication resulted in a decision that unreasonably applied federal law to facts not before the state court.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our recent decision in Schriro v. Landrigan, 550 U. S. 465 (2007), is consistent as well with our holding here. We explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause the deferential standards prescribed by \xc2\xa72254 control whether to grant habeas relief, a federal court must take into account those standards in deciding whether an evidentiary hearing is appropriate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 474. In practical effect, we went on to note, this means that when the state-court record \xe2\x80\x9cprecludes habeas relief\xe2\x80\x9d under the limitations of \xc2\xa72254(d), a district court is \xe2\x80\x9cnot required to hold an evidentiary hearing.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 474 (citing with approval the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s recognition that \xe2\x80\x9can evidentiary hearing is not required on issues that can be resolved by reference to the state court record\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals wrongly interpreted Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 420 (2000) (Michael Williams), as supporting the contrary view. The question there was whether the lower court had correctly determined that \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) barred the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s request for a federal evidentiary hearing.[Footnote 4] Michael Williams did not concern whether evidence introduced in such a hearing could be considered under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). In fact, only one claim at issue in that case was even subject to \xc2\xa72254(d); the rest had not been adjudicated on the merits in state-court proceedings. See id., at 429 (\xe2\x80\x9cPetitioner did not develop, or raise, his claims \xe2\x80\xa6 until he filed his federal habeas petition\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If anything, the decision in Michael Williams supports our holding. The lower court in that case had determined that the one claim subject to \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) did not satisfy that statutory requirement. In light of that ruling, this Court concluded that it was \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary to reach the question whether \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) would permit a [federal] hearing on th[at] claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 444. That conclusion is fully consistent with our holding that evidence later introduced in federal court is irrelevant to \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) review.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on Holland v. Jackson, 542 U. S. 649 (2004) (per curiam), was also mistaken. In Holland, we initially stated that \xe2\x80\x9cwhether a state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was unreasonable [under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1)] must be assessed in light of the record the court had before it.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 652. We then went on to assume for the sake of argument what some Courts of Appeals had heldthat \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), despite its mandatory language, simply does not apply when a federal habeas court has admitted new evidence that supports a claim previously adjudicated in state court.[Footnote 6] Id., at 653. There was no reason to decide that question because regardless, the hearing should have been barred by \xc2\xa72254(e)(2). Today, we reject that assumption and hold that evidence introduced in federal court\nhas no bearing on \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) review. If a claim has\nbeen adjudicated on the merits by a state court, a fed-\neral habeas petitioner must overcome the limitation of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) on the record that was before that state court.[Footnote 7]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s contention that our holding renders \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) superfluous is incorrect. Section 2254(e)(2) imposes a limitation on the discretion of federal habeas courts to take new evidence in an evidentiary hearing. See Landrigan, supra, at 473 (noting that district courts, under AEDPA, generally retain the discretion to grant an evidentiary hearing). Like \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), it carries out \xe2\x80\x9cAEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s goal of promoting comity, finality, and federalism by giving state courts the first opportunity to review [a] claim, and to correct any constitutional violation in the first instance.\xe2\x80\x9d Jimenez v. Quarterman, 555 U. S. 113, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 8) (internal quotation marks omitted).[Footnote 8]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 2254(e)(2) continues to have force where \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) does not bar federal habeas relief. For example, not all federal habeas claims by state prisoners fall within the scope of \xc2\xa72254(d), which applies only to claims \xe2\x80\x9cadjudicated on the merits in State court proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d At a minimum, therefore, \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) still restricts the discretion of federal habeas courts to consider new evidence when deciding claims that were not adjudicated on the merits in state court. See, e.g., Michael Williams, 529 U. S., at 427\xe2\x80\x93429.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although state prisoners may sometimes submit new evidence in federal court, AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statutory scheme is designed to strongly discourage them from doing so. Provisions like \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d)(1) and (e)(2) ensure that \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ederal courts sitting in habeas are not an alternative forum for trying facts and issues which a prisoner made insufficient effort to pursue in state proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 437; see also Richter, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 13) (\xe2\x80\x9cSection 2254(d) is part of the basic structure of federal habeas jurisdiction, designed to confirm that state courts are the principal forum for asserting constitutional challenges to state convictions\xe2\x80\x9d); Wainwright v. Sykes, 433 U. S. 72, 90 (1977) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he state trial on the merits [should be] the \xe2\x80\x98main event,\xe2\x80\x99 so to speak, rather than a \xe2\x80\x98tryout on the road\xe2\x80\x99 for what will later be the determinative federal habeas hearing\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 10]

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, we conclude that the Court of Appeals erred in considering the District Court evidence in its review under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). Although we might ordinarily remand for a properly limited review, the Court of Appeals also ruled, in the alternative, that Pinholster merited habeas relief even on the state-court record alone. 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 669. Remand is therefore inappropriate, and we turn next to a review of the state-court record.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 alternative holding was also erroneous. Pinholster has failed to demonstrate that the California Supreme Court unreasonably applied clearly established federal law to his penalty-phase ineffective-assistance claim on the state-court record. Section 2254(d) prohibits habeas relief.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 2254(d) applies to Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s claim because that claim was adjudicated on the merits in state-court proceedings. No party disputes that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s federal petition alleges an ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim that had been included in both of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s state habeas petitions. The California Supreme Court denied each of those petitions \xe2\x80\x9con the substantive ground that it is without merit.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 11]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 2254(d) applies even where there has been a summary denial. See Richter, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 8). In these circumstances, Pinholster can satisfy the \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable application\xe2\x80\x9d prong of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) only by showing that \xe2\x80\x9cthere was no reasonable basis\xe2\x80\x9d for the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 8). \xe2\x80\x9c[A] habeas court must determine what arguments or theories \xe2\x80\xa6 could have supporte[d] the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision; and then it must ask whether it is possible fairminded jurists could disagree that those arguments or theories are inconsistent with the holding in a prior decision of this Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 12). After a thorough review of the state-court record,[Footnote 12] we conclude that Pinholster has failed to meet that high threshold.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no dispute that the clearly established federal law here is Strickland v. Washington. In Strickland, this Court made clear that \xe2\x80\x9cthe purpose of the effective assistance guarantee of the Sixth Amendment is not to improve the quality of legal representation \xe2\x80\xa6 [but] simply to ensure that criminal defendants receive a fair trial.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 689. Thus, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he benchmark for judging any claim of ineffectiveness must be whether counsel\xe2\x80\x99s conduct so undermined the proper functioning of the adversarial process that the trial cannot be relied on as having produced a just result.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 686 (emphasis added). The Court acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here are countless ways to provide effective assistance in any given case,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ven the best criminal defense attorneys would not defend a particular client in the same way.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 689.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Recognizing the \xe2\x80\x9ctempt[ation] for a defendant to second-guess counsel\xe2\x80\x99s assistance after conviction or adverse sentence,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., the Court established that counsel should be \xe2\x80\x9cstrongly presumed to have rendered adequate assistance and made all significant decisions in the exercise of reasonable professional judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 690. To overcome that presumption, a defendant must show that counsel failed to act \xe2\x80\x9creasonabl[y] considering all the circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 688. The Court cautioned that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he availability of intrusive post-trial inquiry into attorney performance or of detailed guidelines for its evaluation would encourage the proliferation of ineffectiveness challenges.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 690.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court also required that defendants prove prejudice. Id., at 691\xe2\x80\x93692. \xe2\x80\x9cThe defendant must show that there is a reasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 694. \xe2\x80\x9cA reasonable probability is a probability sufficient to undermine confidence in the outcome.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That requires a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial,\xe2\x80\x9d not just \xe2\x80\x9cconceivable,\xe2\x80\x9d likelihood of a different result. Richter, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 22).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our review of the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is thus \xe2\x80\x9cdoubly deferential.\xe2\x80\x9d Knowles v. Mirzayance, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 11) (citing Yarborough v. Gentry, 540 U. S. 1, 5\xe2\x80\x936 (2003) (per curiam)). We take a \xe2\x80\x9chighly deferential\xe2\x80\x9d look at counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance, Strickland, supra, at 689, through the \xe2\x80\x9cdeferential lens of \xc2\xa72254(d),\xe2\x80\x9d Mirzayance, supra, at ___, n.\xc2\xa02 (slip op., at 9, n.\xc2\xa02). Pinholster must demonstrate that it was necessarily unreasonable for the California Supreme Court to conclude: (1) that he had not overcome the strong presumption of competence; and (2) that he had failed to undermine confidence in the jury\xe2\x80\x99s sentence of death.

\n

C

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pinholster has not shown that the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision that he could not demonstrate deficient performance by his trial counsel necessarily involved an unreasonable application of federal law. In arguing to the state court that his counsel performed deficiently, Pinholster contended that they should have pursued and presented additional evidence about: his family members and their criminal, mental, and substance abuse problems; his schooling; and his medical and mental health history, including his epileptic disorder. To support his allegation that his trial counsel had \xe2\x80\x9cno reasonable tactical basis\xe2\x80\x9d for the approach they took, Pinholster relied on statements his counsel made at trial. App. to Brief in Opposition 143. When arguing the motion to exclude the State\xe2\x80\x99s aggravating evidence at the penalty phase for failure to comply with Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa7190.3, Dettmar, one of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s counsel, contended that because the State did not provide notice, he \xe2\x80\x9c[was] not presently prepared to offer anything by way of mitigation,\xe2\x80\x9d 52 Tr. 7250. In response to the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry as to whether a continuance might be helpful, Dettmar noted that the only mitigation witness he could think of was Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother. Additional time, Dettmar stated, would not \xe2\x80\x9cmake a great deal of difference.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7257\xe2\x80\x937258.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin with the premise that \xe2\x80\x9cunder the circumstances, the challenged action[s] might be considered sound trial strategy.\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Strickland, supra, at 689 (internal quotation marks omitted). The Court of Appeals dissent described one possible strategy:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys] were fully aware that they would have to deal with mitigation sometime during the course of the trial, did spend considerable time and effort investigating avenues for mitigation[,] and made a reasoned professional judgment that the best way to serve their client would be to rely on the fact that they never got [the required \xc2\xa7190.3] notice and hope the judge would bar the state from putting on their aggravation witnesses.\xe2\x80\x9d 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 701\xe2\x80\x93702 (opinion of Kozinski, C. J.).

\n

\nFurther, if their motion was denied, counsel were prepared to present only Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother in the penalty phase to create sympathy not for Pinholster, but for his mother. After all, the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98family sympathy\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d mitigation defense was known to the defense bar in California at the time and had been used by other attorneys. Id., at 707. Rather than displaying neglect, we presume that Dettmar\xe2\x80\x99s arguments were part of this trial strategy. See Gentry, supra, at 8 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is a strong presumption that [counsel took certain actions] for tactical reasons rather than through sheer neglect\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Strickland, supra, at 690)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The state-court record supports the idea that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s counsel acted strategically to get the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s aggravation witnesses excluded for lack of notice, and if that failed, to put on Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother. Other statements made during the argument regarding the motion to exclude suggest that defense counsel were trying to take advantage of a legal technicality and were not truly surprised. Brainard and Dettmar acknowledged that the prosecutor had invited them on numerous occasions to review Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s state prison file but argued that such an invitation did not meet with the \xe2\x80\x9cstrict demands\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa7190.3. 52 Tr. 7260. Dettmar admitted that the prosecutor, \xe2\x80\x9cbeing as thorough as she is, possibly ha[d] met the requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7250. But if so, he wanted her \xe2\x80\x9cto make that representation to the court.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 13] Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Timesheets indicate that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel investigated mitigating evidence.[Footnote 14] Long before the guilty verdict, Dettmar talked with Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother and contacted a psychiatrist.[Footnote 15] On February 26, two months before the penalty phase started, he billed six hours for\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[p]reparation argument, death penalty phase.\xe2\x80\x9d See Clerk\xe2\x80\x99s Tr. 864. Brainard, who merely assisted Dettmar for the penalty phase, researched epilepsy and also interviewed Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother.[Footnote 16] We know that Brainard likely spent additional time, not reflected in these entries, preparing Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s brother, Terry, who provided some mitigation testimony about Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s background during the guilt phase. Infra, at 28.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The record also shows that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s counsel confronted a challenging penalty phase with an unsympathetic client, which limited their feasible mitigation strategies. By the end of the guilt phase, the jury had observed Pinholster \xe2\x80\x9cglor[y]\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9chis criminal disposition\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9chundreds of robberies.\xe2\x80\x9d Pinholster, 1 Cal.\xc2\xa04th, at 945, 907, 824 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 611, 584. During his cross-examination, Pinholster laughed or smirked when he told the jury that his \xe2\x80\x9coccupation\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9ca crook,\xe2\x80\x9d when he was asked whether he had threatened a potential witness, and when he described thwarting police efforts to recover a gun he had once used. 44 Tr. 6225. He bragged about being a \xe2\x80\x9cprofessional robber.\xe2\x80\x9d 43 id., at 6204. To sup-\nport his defense, Pinholster claimed that he used only gunsnot knivesto commit his crimes. But during cross-examination, Pinholster admitted that he had previously been convicted of using a knife in a kidnaping. Pinholster also said he was a white supremacist and that he frequently carved swastikas into other people\xe2\x80\x99s property as \xe2\x80\x9ca sideline to robbery.\xe2\x80\x9d 44 id., at 6246.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Trial counsel\xe2\x80\x99s psychiatric expert, Dr. Stalberg, had concluded that Pinholster showed no significant signs or symptoms of mental disorder or defect other than his \xe2\x80\x9cpsychopathic personality traits.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 131. Dr. Stalberg was aware of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s hyperactivity as a youngster, hospitalization at age 14 for incorrigibility, alleged epileptic disorder, and history of drug dependency. Nevertheless, Dr. Stalberg told counsel that Pinholster did not appear to suffer from brain damage, was not significantly intoxicated or impaired on the night in question, and did not have an impaired ability to appreciate the criminality of his conduct.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given these impediments, it would have been a reasonable penalty-phase strategy to focus on evoking sympathy for Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother. In fact, such a family sympathy defense is precisely how the State understood defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s strategy. The prosecutor carefully opened her cross-examination of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother with, \xe2\x80\x9cI hope you understand I don\xe2\x80\x99t enjoy cross-examining a mother of anybody.\xe2\x80\x9d 52 Tr. 7407. And in her closing argument, the prosecutor attempted to undercut defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s strategy by pointing out, \xe2\x80\x9cEven the most heinous person born, even Adolph Hitler[,] probably had a mother who loved him.\xe2\x80\x9d 53 id., at 7452.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s only response to this evidence is a series of declarations from Brainard submitted with Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s first state habeas petition, seven years after the trial. Brainard declares that he has \xe2\x80\x9cno recollection\xe2\x80\x9d of interviewing any family members (other than Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother) regarding penalty-phase testimony, of attempting to secure Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s school or medical records, or of interviewing any former teachers or counselors. Pet. for Writ of Habeas Corpus in No. S004616 (Cal.), Exh. 3. Brainard also declares that Dettmar was primarily responsible for mental health issues in the case, but he has \xe2\x80\x9cno recollection\xe2\x80\x9d of Dettmar ever having secured Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s medical records. Id., Exh. 2. Dettmar neither confirmed nor denied Brainard\xe2\x80\x99s statements, as he had died by the time of the first state habeas petition. 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 700 (Kozinski, C. J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, Brainard and Dettmar made statements suggesting that they were not surprised that the State intended to put on aggravating evidence, billing records show that they spent time investigating mitigating evidence, and the record demonstrates that they represented a psychotic client whose performance at trial hardly endeared him to the jury. Pinholster has responded to this evidence with only a handful of post-hoc nondenials by one of his lawyers. The California Supreme Court could have reasonably concluded that Pinholster had failed to rebut the presumption of competence mandated by Strickland\xe2\x80\x94here, that counsel had adequately performed at the penalty phase of trial.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals held that the California Supreme Court had unreasonably applied Strickland because Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys \xe2\x80\x9cw[ere] far more deficient than \xe2\x80\xa6 the attorneys in Terry Williams, Wiggins [v. Smith, 539 U. S. 510 (2003)], and Rompilla [v. Beard, 545 U. S. 374 (2005)], where in each case the Supreme Court upheld the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective assistance claim.\xe2\x80\x9d 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 671. The court drew from those cases a \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional duty to investigate,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 674, and the principle that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is prima facie ineffective assistance for counsel to \xe2\x80\x98abandon[ ] their investigation of [the] petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s background after having acquired only rudimentary knowledge of his history from a narrow set of sources,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (quoting Wiggins v. Smith, 539 U. S. 510, 524\xe2\x80\x93525 (2003)). The court explained that it could not \xe2\x80\x9clightly disregard\xe2\x80\x9d a failure to introduce evidence of \xe2\x80\x9cexcruciating life history\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cnightmarish childhood.\xe2\x80\x9d 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 684 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals misapplied Strickland and overlooked \xe2\x80\x9cthe constitutionally protected independence of counsel and \xe2\x80\xa6 the wide latitude counsel must have in making tactical decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 689. Beyond the general requirement of reasonableness, \xe2\x80\x9cspecific guidelines are not appropriate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 688. \xe2\x80\x9cNo particular set of detailed rules for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s conduct can satisfactorily take account of the variety of circumstances faced by defense counsel or the range of legitimate decisions \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 688\xe2\x80\x93689. Strickland itself rejected the notion that the same investigation will be required in every case. Id., at 691 (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ounsel has a duty to make reasonable investigations or to make a reasonable decision that makes particular investigations unnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). It is \xe2\x80\x9c[r]are\xe2\x80\x9d that constitutionally competent representation will require \xe2\x80\x9cany one technique or approach.\xe2\x80\x9d Richter, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 17). The Court of Appeals erred in attributing strict rules to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s recent case law.[Footnote 17]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor did the Court of Appeals properly apply the strong presumption of competence that Strickland mandates. The court dismissed the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s application of the presumption as \xe2\x80\x9cfabricat[ing] an excuse that the attorneys themselves could not conjure up.\xe2\x80\x9d 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 673. But Strickland specifically commands that a court \xe2\x80\x9cmust indulge [the] strong presumption\xe2\x80\x9d that counsel \xe2\x80\x9cmade all significant decisions in the exercise of reasonable professional judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 689\xe2\x80\x93690. The Court of Appeals was required not simply to \xe2\x80\x9cgive [the] attorneys the benefit of the doubt,\xe2\x80\x9d 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 673, but to affirmatively entertain the range of possible \xe2\x80\x9creasons Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s counsel may have had for proceeding as they did,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 692 (Kozinski, C. J., dissenting). See also Richter, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 20) (\xe2\x80\x9cStrickland \xe2\x80\xa6 calls for an inquiry into the objective reasonableness of counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance, not counsel\xe2\x80\x99s subjective state of mind\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor questions whether it would have been a reasonable professional judgment for Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel to adopt a family-sympathy mitigation defense. Post, at 27. She cites no evidence, however, that such an approach would have been inconsistent with the standard of professional competence in capital cases that prevailed in Los Angeles in 1984. Indeed, she does not contest that, at the time, the defense bar in California had been using that strategy. See supra, at 19; post, at 28, n.\xc2\xa021. Justice Sotomayor relies heavily on Wiggins, but in that case the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel specifically acknowledged a standard practice for capital cases in Maryland that was inconsistent with what he had done. 539 U. S., at 524.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At bottom, Justice Sotomayor\xe2\x80\x99s view is grounded in little more than her own sense of \xe2\x80\x9cprudence,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 26 (internal quotation marks omitted), and what appears to be her belief that the only reasonable mitigation strategy in capital cases is to \xe2\x80\x9chelp\xe2\x80\x9d the jury \xe2\x80\x9cunderstand\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant, post, at 35. According to Justice Sotomayor, that Pinholster was an unsympathetic client \xe2\x80\x9ccompound[ed], rather than excuse[d], counsel\xe2\x80\x99s deficiency\xe2\x80\x9d in pursuing further evidence \xe2\x80\x9cthat could explain why Pinholster was the way he was.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 30. But it certainly can be reasonable for attorneys to conclude that creating sympathy for the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s family is a better idea because the defendant himself is simply unsympathetic.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor\xe2\x80\x99s approach is flatly inconsistent with Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s recognition that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here are countless ways to provide effective assistance in any given case.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 689. There comes a point where a defense attorney will reasonably decide that another strategy is in order, thus \xe2\x80\x9cmak[ing] particular investigations unnecessary.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 691; cf. 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 692 (Kozinski, C. J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe current infatuation with \xe2\x80\x98humanizing\xe2\x80\x99 the defendant as the be-all and end-all of mitigation disregards the possibility that this may be the wrong tactic in some cases because experienced lawyers conclude that the jury simply won\xe2\x80\x99t buy it\xe2\x80\x9d). Those decisions are due \xe2\x80\x9ca heavy measure of deference.\xe2\x80\x9d Strickland, supra, at 691. The California Supreme Court could have reasonably concluded that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s counsel made such a reasoned decision in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have recently reiterated that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[s]urmounting Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s high bar is never an easy task.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Richter, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 15) (quoting Padilla v. Kentucky, 559 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 14)). The Strickland standard must be applied with \xe2\x80\x9cscrupulous care.\xe2\x80\x9d Richter, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 15). The Court of Appeals did not do so here.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if his trial counsel had performed deficiently, Pinholster also has failed to show that the California Supreme Court must have unreasonably concluded that Pinholster was not prejudiced. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he question is whether there is a reasonable probability that, absent the errors, the sentencer \xe2\x80\xa6 would have concluded that the balance of aggravating and mitigating circumstances did not warrant death.\xe2\x80\x9d Strickland, supra, at 695. We therefore \xe2\x80\x9creweigh the evidence in aggravation against the totality of available mitigating evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Wiggins, supra, at 534.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn first to the aggravating and mitigating evidence that the sentencing jury considered. See Strickland, supra, at 695 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] court hearing an ineffectiveness claim must consider the totality of the evidence before the judge or jury\xe2\x80\x9d). Here, the same jury heard both the guilt and penalty phases and was instructed to consider all the evidence presented. Cf. Visciotti, 537 U. S., at 25 (noting that the state habeas court had correctly considered mitigating evidence introduced during the guilt phase).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State presented extensive aggravating evidence. As we have already discussed, the jury watched Pinholster revel in his extensive criminal history. Supra, at 21. Then, during the penalty phase, the State presented evidence that Pinholster had threatened to kill the State\xe2\x80\x99s lead witness, assaulted a man with a straight razor, and kidnaped another person with a knife. The State showed that Pinholster had a history of violent outbursts, including striking and threatening a bailiff after a court proceeding at age 17, breaking his wife\xe2\x80\x99s jaw,[Footnote 18] resisting arrest by faking seizures, and assaulting and spitting on police officers. The jury also heard about Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s involvement in juvenile gangs and his substantial disciplinary record in both county and state jails, where he had threatened, assaulted, and thrown urine at guards, and fought with other inmates. While in jail, Pinholster had been segregated for a time due to his propensity for violence and placed on a \xe2\x80\x9cspecial disciplinary diet\xe2\x80\x9d reserved only for the most disruptive inmates. 52 Tr. 7305.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The mitigating evidence consisted primarily of the penalty-phase testimony of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother, Brashear, who gave a detailed account of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s troubled childhood and adolescence. Early childhood was quite difficult. The family \xe2\x80\x9cdidn\xe2\x80\x99t have lots of money.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7404. When he was very young, Pinholster suffered two serious head injuries, first at age 2 or 3 when he was run over by a car, and again at age 4 or 5 when he went through the windshield during a car accident. When he was 5, Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s stepfather moved in and was abusive, or nearly so.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pinholster always struggled in school. He was disruptive in kindergarten and was failing by first grade. He got in fights and would run out of the classroom. In third grade, Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s teacher suggested that he was more than just a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98disruptive child.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7394. Following tests at a clinic, Pinholster was sent to a school for educationally handicapped children where his performance improved.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At age 10, psychiatrists recommended that Pinholster be sent to a mental institution, although he did not go. Pinholster had continued to initiate fights with his brothers and to act like \xe2\x80\x9cRobin Hood\xe2\x80\x9d around the neighborhood, \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tealing from the rich and giving to the poor.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7395. Brashear had thought then that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]omething was not working right.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7396.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By age 10 or 11, Pinholster was living in boy\xe2\x80\x99s homes and juvenile halls. He spent six months when he was 12 in a state mental institution for emotionally handicapped children. By the time he was 18, Pinholster was in county jail, where he was beaten badly. Brashear suspected that the beating caused Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s epilepsy, for which he has been prescribed medication. After a stint in state prison, Pinholster returned home but acted \xe2\x80\x9cunusual\xe2\x80\x9d and had trouble readjusting to life. Id., at 7405.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s siblings were \xe2\x80\x9cbasically very good children,\xe2\x80\x9d although they would get into trouble. Id., at 7401. His brother, Terry, had been arrested for drunk driving and his sister, Tammy, for public intoxication. Tammy also was arrested for drug possession and was self-destructive and \xe2\x80\x9cwild.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s eldest brother, Alvin, died a fugitive from California authorities.[Footnote 19]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to Brashear\xe2\x80\x99s penalty-phase testimony, Pinholster had previously presented mitigating evidence during the guilt phase from his brother, Terry. Terry testified that Pinholster was \xe2\x80\x9cmore or less in institutions all his life,\xe2\x80\x9d suffered from epilepsy, and was \xe2\x80\x9cmore or less\xe2\x80\x9d drunk on the night of the murders. 42 id., at 6015, 6036.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After considering this aggravating and mitigating evidence, the jury returned a sentence of death. The state trial court found that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s determination was \xe2\x80\x9csupported overwhelmingly by the weight of the evidence\xe2\x80\x9d and added that \xe2\x80\x9cthe factors in aggravation beyond all reasonable doubt outweigh those in mitigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Clerk\xe2\x80\x99s Tr. 1184, 1186.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no reasonable probability that the additional evidence Pinholster presented in his state habeas proceedings would have changed the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict. The \xe2\x80\x9cnew\xe2\x80\x9d evidence largely duplicated the mitigation evidence at trial. School and medical records basically substantiate the testimony of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother and brother. Declarations from Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s siblings support his mother\xe2\x80\x99s testimony that his stepfather was abusive and explain that Pinholster was beaten with fists, belts, and even wooden boards.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To the extent the state habeas record includes new factual allegations or evidence, much of it is of questionable mitigating value. If Pinholster had called Dr. Woods to testify consistently with his psychiatric report, Pinholster would have opened the door to rebuttal by a state expert. See, e.g., Wong v. Belmontes, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (per curiam) (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9312) (taking into account that certain mitigating evidence would have exposed the petitioner to further aggravating evidence). The new evidence relating to Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s familytheir more serious substance abuse, mental illness, and criminal problems, see post, at 22is also by no means clearly mitigating, as the jury might have concluded that Pinholster was simply beyond rehabilitation. Cf. Atkins v. Virginia, 536 U. S. 304, 321 (2002) (recognizing that mitigating evidence can be a \xe2\x80\x9ctwo-edged sword\xe2\x80\x9d that juries might find to show future dangerousness).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The remaining new material in the state habeas record is sparse. We learn that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s brother Alvin died of suicide by drug overdose, and there are passing references to Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s own drug dependency. According to Dr. Stalberg, Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cschool records\xe2\x80\x9d apparently evidenced \xe2\x80\x9csome degree\xe2\x80\x9d of brain damage. App. to Brief in Opposition 219. Mostly, there are just a few new details about Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s childhood. Pinholster apparently looked like his biological father, whom his grandparents \xe2\x80\x9cloathed.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Writ of Habeas Corpus in No. S004616 (Cal.), Exh. 98, p. 1. Accordingly, whenever his grandparents \xe2\x80\x9cspanked or disciplined\xe2\x80\x9d the kids, Pinholster \xe2\x80\x9calways got the worst of it.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Pinholster was mostly unsupervised and \xe2\x80\x9cdidn\xe2\x80\x99t get much love,\xe2\x80\x9d because his mother and stepfather were always working and \xe2\x80\x9cwere more concerned with their own lives than the welfare of their kids.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 2. Neither parent seemed concerned about Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s schooling. Finally, Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s aunt once saw the children mixing flour and water to make something to eat, although \xe2\x80\x9c[m]ost meals consisted of canned spaghetti and foods of that ilk.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given what little additional mitigating evidence Pinholster presented in state habeas, we cannot say that the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination was unreasonable. Having already heard much of what is included in the state habeas record, the jury returned a sentence of death. Moreover, some of the new testimony would likely have undercut the mitigating value of the testimony by Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother. The new material is thus not so significant that, even assuming Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel performed deficiently, it was necessarily unreasonable for the California Supreme Court to conclude that Pinholster had failed to show a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d likelihood of a different sentence. Richter, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 22) (citing Strickland, 466 U. S., at 693).

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As with deficiency, the Court of Appeals found this case to be \xe2\x80\x9cmaterially indistinguishable\xe2\x80\x9d from Terry Williams and Rompilla v. Beard, 545 U. S. 374 (2005). 590 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 684. But this Court did not apply AEDPA deference to the question of prejudice in those cases; each of them lack the important \xe2\x80\x9cdoubly deferential\xe2\x80\x9d standard of Strickland and AEDPA. See Terry Williams, 529 U. S., at 395\xe2\x80\x93397 (reviewing a state-court decision that did not apply the correct legal standard); Rompilla, supra, at 390 (reviewing Strickland prejudice de novo because the state-court decision did not reach the question). Those cases therefore offer no guidance with respect to whether a state court has unreasonably determined that prejudice is lacking. We have said time and again that \xe2\x80\x9can unreasonable application of federal law is different from an incorrect application of federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Richter, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 11) (internal quotation marks omitted). Even if the Court of Appeals might have reached a different conclusion as an initial matter, it was not an unreasonable application of our precedent for the California Supreme Court to conclude that Pinholster did not establish prejudice.[Footnote 20]\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

If that requirement is not satisfied, the petitioner may establish the factual predicate for a claim in a federal-court hearing only if, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9cthe facts underlying the claim would be sufficient to establish by clear and convincing evidence that but for constitutional error, no reasonable factfinder would have found the applicant guilty of the underlying offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72254(e)(2)(B).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even when the petitioner does satisfy the diligence standard adopted in Williams v. Taylor, supra, a hearing should not be held in federal court unless the new evidence that the petitioner seeks to introduce was not and could not have been offered in the state-court proceeding. Section 2254(e)(2) bars a hearing in certain situations, but it does not mean that a hearing is allowed in all other situations. See Schriro v. Landrigan, 550 U. S. 465, 473\xe2\x80\x93474 (2007). The whole thrust of AEDPA is essentially to reserve federal habeas relief for those cases in which the state courts acted unreasonably. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d)(1), (2), (e)(1). Permitting a petitioner to obtain federal habeas relief on the basis of evidence that could have been but was not offered in state court would upset this scheme.

Ayers, 590 F. 3d 651, 688\xe2\x80\x93691 (CA9 2009) (opinion of Kozinski, J.), I would hold that the federal-court hearing should not have been held because respondent did not diligently present his new evidence to the California courts. And I join all but Part II of the opinion of the Court, as I agree that the decision of the state court represented a reasonable application of clearly established Supreme Court precedent in light of the state-court record.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Some habeas petitioners are unable to develop the fac-tual basis of their claims in state court through no fault of\xc2\xa0their own. Congress recognized as much when it enacted the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), 110 Stat. 1214, and permitted therein the introduction of new evidence in federal habeas proceed-ings in certain limited circumstances. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(e)(2). Under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s novel interpretation of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), however, federal courts must turn a blind eye to new evidence in deciding whether a petitioner has satisfied \xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s threshold obstacle to federal habeas relief\xe2\x80\x94even when it is clear that the petitioner would be entitled to relief in light of that evidence. In reading\nthe statute to \xe2\x80\x9ccompe[l]\xe2\x80\x9d this harsh result, ante, at 9,\nthe Court ignores a key textual difference between \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d)(1) and 2254(d)(2) and discards the previous understanding in our precedents that new evidence can, in fact, inform the \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) inquiry. I therefore dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s first holding.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I also disagree with the Court that, even if the \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) analysis is limited to the state-court record, respondent Scott Pinholster failed to demonstrate that the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision denying his ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim was an unreasonable application of Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668 (1984). There is no reason for the majority to decide whether the \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) analysis is limited to the state-court record because Pinholster satisfied \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) on either the state- or federal-court record.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court first holds that, in determining whether a state-court decision is an unreasonable application of Supreme Court precedent under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), \xe2\x80\x9creview \xe2\x80\xa6 is limited to the record that was before the state court that adjudicated the claim on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. New evidence adduced at a federal evidentiary hearing is now\xc2\xa0irrelevant to determining whether a petitioner has satisfied \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). This holding is unnecessary to pro-mote AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s purposes, and it is inconsistent with the provision\xe2\x80\x99s text, the structure of the statute, and our precedents.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To understand the significance of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s holding, it is important to view the issue in context. AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s entire structure\xe2\x80\x94which gives state courts the opportunity to decide factual and legal questions in the first instance\xe2\x80\x94ensures that evidentiary hearings in federal habeas proceedings are very rare. See N. King, F. Cheesman, & B. Ostrom, Final Technical Report: Habeas Litigation in U. S. District Courts 35\xe2\x80\x9336 (2007) (evidentiary hearings under AEDPA occur in 0.4 percent of noncapital cases and 9.5 percent of capital cases). Even absent the new restriction created by today\xe2\x80\x99s holding, AEDPA erects multiple hurdles to a state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s ability to introduce new evidence in a federal habeas proceeding.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nder the exhaustion requirement, a habeas petitioner challenging a state conviction must first attempt to present his claim in state court.\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington v. Richter, 562 U. S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 13); see also \xc2\xa72254(b)(1)(A). With certain narrow exceptions, federal courts cannot consider a claim at all, let alone accept new evidence relevant to the claim, if it has not been exhausted in state court.[Footnote 1] The exhaustion requirement thus reserves to state courts the first opportunity to resolve factual disputes relevant to a state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s claim. See O\xe2\x80\x99Sullivan v. Boerckel, 526 U. S. 838, 845 (1999).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the exhaustion requirement is \xe2\x80\x9ccomplement[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d by the standards set forth in \xc2\xa72254(d). Harrington, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 14). Under this provision, a federal court may not grant habeas relief on any \xe2\x80\x9cclaim that was adjudicated on the merits in State court proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d unless the adjudication

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(1) resulted in a decision that was contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States; or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2) resulted in a decision that was based on an unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented in the State court proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nThese standards \xe2\x80\x9ccontrol whether to grant habeas relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Schriro v. Landrigan, 550 U. S. 465, 474 (2007). Accordingly, we have said, if the factual allegations a petitioner seeks to prove at an evidentiary hearing would not satisfy these standards, there is no reason for a hearing. See id., at 481. In such a case, the district court may exercise its \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretion to deny an evidentiary hearing.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also infra, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. This approach makes eminent sense: If district courts held evidentiary hearings without first asking whether the evidence the petitioner seeks to present would satisfy AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s demanding standards, they would needlessly prolong federal habeas proceedings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, even when a petitioner seeks to introduce new evidence that would entitle him to relief, AEDPA prohibits him from doing so, except in a narrow range of cases, unless he \xe2\x80\x9cmade a reasonable attempt, in light of the information available at the time, to investigate and pursue claims in state court.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 420, 435 (2000) (Michael Williams). Thus, \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf the applicant has failed to develop the factual basis of a claim in State court proceedings, the court shall not hold an evidentiary hearing on the claim unless the applicant shows that\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(A) the claim relies on\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(i) a new rule of constitutional law, made retroactive to cases on collateral review by the Supreme Court, that was previously unavailable; or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(ii) a factual predicate that could not have been previously discovered through the exercise of due diligence; and

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(B) the facts underlying the claim would be sufficient to establish by clear and convincing evidence that but for constitutional error, no reasonable factfinder would have found the applicant guilty of the underlying offense.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nIn Michael Williams, we construed the opening clause of this provision\xe2\x80\x94which triggers the bar on evidentiary hearings\xe2\x80\x94to apply when \xe2\x80\x9cthere is lack of diligence, or some greater fault, attributable to the prisoner or the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 2] Id., at 432. AEDPA thus bars an evidentiary hearing for a nondiligent petitioner unless the petitioner can satisfy both \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(e)(2)(A) and (B), which few petitioners can. Section 2254(e)(2) in this way incentivizes state petitioners to develop the factual basis of their claims in state court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To the limited extent that federal evidentiary hearings are available under AEDPA, they ensure that petitioners who diligently developed the factual basis of their claims in state court, discovered new evidence after the state-court proceeding, and cannot return to state court retain the ability to access the Great Writ. See ante, at 2 (Alito, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). \xe2\x80\x9cWhen Congress codified new rules governing this previously judicially managed area of law, it did so without losing sight of the fact that the \xe2\x80\x98writ of habeas corpus plays a vital role in protecting constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Holland v. Florida, 560 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 16) (quoting Slack v. McDaniel, 529 U. S. 473, 483 (2000)). Allowing a petitioner to introduce new evidence at a hearing in the limited circumstance permitted by \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) does not upset the balance that Congress struck in AEDPA between the state and federal courts. By construing \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) to do the work of other provisions in AEDPA, the majority has subverted Congress\xe2\x80\x99 careful balance of responsibilities. It has also created unnecessarily a brand-new set of procedural complexities that lower courts will have to confront.[Footnote 3]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) finds no support in the provision\xe2\x80\x99s text or the statute\xe2\x80\x99s structure as a whole.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 2254(d)(1) requires district courts to ask whether a state-court adjudication on the merits \xe2\x80\x9cresulted in a decision that was contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Because this provision uses \xe2\x80\x9cbackward-looking language\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., past-tense verbs\xe2\x80\x94the majority believes that it limits review to the state-court record. Ante, at 9. But both \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d)(1) and 2254(d)(2) use \xe2\x80\x9cbackward-looking language,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xc2\xa72254(d)(2)\xe2\x80\x94unlike \xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x94expressly directs district courts to base their review on \xe2\x80\x9cthe evidence presented in the State court proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d If use of the past tense were sufficient to indicate Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to restrict analysis to the state-court record, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin light of the evidence presented in the State court proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa72254(d)(2) would be superfluous. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) fails to give meaning to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to include language referring to the evidence presented to the state court in \xc2\xa72254(d)(2). Cf. Bates v. United States, 522 U. S. 23, 29\xe2\x80\x9330 (1997) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhere Congress includes particular language in one section of a statute but omits it in another section of the same Act, it is generally presumed that Congress acts intentionally and purposely in the disparate inclusion or exclusion\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ignoring our usual \xe2\x80\x9creluctan[ce] to treat statutory terms as surplusage in any setting,\xe2\x80\x9d TRW Inc. v. Andrews, 534 U. S. 19, 31 (2001) (internal quotation marks omitted), the majority characterizes the phrase appearing in \xc2\xa72254(d)(2) as mere \xe2\x80\x9cclarifying language,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12, n.\xc2\xa07. It speculates that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he omission of clarifying language from \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) just as likely reflects Congress\xe2\x80\x99 belief that such language was unnecessary as it does anything else.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313, n.\xc2\xa07. The argument that this phrase is merely \xe2\x80\x9cclarifying\xe2\x80\x9d might have more force, however, had Congress included this phrase in \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) but not in \xc2\xa72254(d)(2). As between the two provisions, \xc2\xa72254(d)(2)\xe2\x80\x94which requires review of the state court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdetermination of the facts\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94more logically depends on the facts presented to the state court. Because this provision needs less clarification on this point than \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), it is all the more telling that Congress included this phrase in \xc2\xa72254(d)(2) but elected to exclude it from \xc2\xa72254(d)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike my colleagues in the majority, I refuse to assume that Congress simply engaged in sloppy drafting. The inclusion of this phrase in \xc2\xa72254(d)(2)\xe2\x80\x94coupled with its omission from \xc2\xa72254(d)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s partner provision, \xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x94provides strong reason to think that Congress did not intend for the \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) analysis to be limited categorically to \xe2\x80\x9cthe evidence presented in the State court proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98broader context of the statute as a whole,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9 (quoting Robinson v. Shell Oil Co., 519 U. S. 337, 341 (1997)), reinforces this conclusion. In particular, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to include in AEDPA a provision, \xc2\xa72254(e)(2), that permits federal evidentiary hearings in certain circumstances provides further evidence that Congress did not intend to limit the \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) inquiry to the state-court record in every case.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have long recognized that some diligent habeas petitioners are unable to develop all of the facts supporting their claims in state court.[Footnote 4] As discussed above, in enacting AEDPA, Congress generally barred evidentiary hearings for petitioners who did not \xe2\x80\x9cexercise diligence in pursuing their claims\xe2\x80\x9d in state court. Michael Williams, 529 U. S., at 436; see also \xc2\xa72254(e)(2). Importantly, it did not impose any express limit on evidentiary hearings for petitioners who had been diligent in state court. See id., at 436 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he statute does not equate prisoners who exercise diligence in pursuing their claims with those who do not\xe2\x80\x9d). For those petitioners, Congress left the decision to hold a hearing \xe2\x80\x9cto the sound discretion of district courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Landrigan, 550 U. S., at 473.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Faced with situations in which a diligent petitioner offers additional evidence in federal court, the courts of appeals have taken two approaches to applying \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). Some courts have held that when a federal court admits new evidence supporting a claim adjudicated on the merits in state court, \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) does not apply at all and the federal court may review the claim de novo. See ante, at 12; Holland v. Jackson, 542 U. S. 649, 653 (2004) (per curiam); see, e.g., Winston v. Kelly, 592 F.\xc2\xa03d 535, 555\xe2\x80\x93556 (CA4 2010). I agree with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s rejection of this approach. See ante, at 12. It would undermine the comity principles motivating AEDPA to decline to defer to a state-court adjudication of a claim\nbecause the state court, through no fault of its own,\nlacked all the relevant evidence.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Other courts of appeals, including the court below, have struck a more considered balance. These courts have held that \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) continues to apply but that new evidence properly presented in a federal hearing is relevant to the reasonableness of the state-court decision. See Pinholster v. Ayers, 590 F.\xc2\xa03d 651, 668 (CA9 2009) (en banc) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf the evidence is admissible under Michael Williams or \xc2\xa72254(e)(2), and if it does not render the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claims unexhausted \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, then it is properly considered in evaluating whether the legal conclusion reached by the state habeas court was a reasonable application of Supreme Court law\xe2\x80\x9d); accord, Wilson v. Mazzuca, 570 F.\xc2\xa03d 490, 500 (CA2 2009); Pecoraro v. Walls, 286 F.\xc2\xa03d 439, 443 (CA7 2002); Valdez v. Cockrell, 274 F.\xc2\xa03d 941, 952 (CA5 2001). This approach accommodates the competing goals, reflected in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d) and 2254(e)(2), of according deference to reasonable state-court decisions and preserving the opportunity for diligent petitioners to present evidence to the federal court when they were unable to do so in state court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority charts a third, novel course that, so far as I am aware, no court of appeals has adopted: \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) continues to apply when a petitioner has additional evidence that he was unable to present to the state court, but the district court cannot consider that evidence in deciding whether the petitioner has satisfied \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). The problem with this approach is its potential to bar federal habeas relief for diligent habeas petitioners who cannot present new evidence to a state court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Consider, for example, a petitioner who diligently attempted in state court to develop the factual basis of a claim that prosecutors withheld exculpatory witness statements in violation of Brady v. Maryland, 373 U. S. 83 (1963). The state court denied relief on the ground that the withheld evidence then known did not rise to the level of materiality required under Brady. Before the time for filing a federal habeas petition has expired, however, a state court orders the State to disclose additional documents the petitioner had timely requested under the State\xe2\x80\x99s public records Act. The disclosed documents reveal that the State withheld other exculpatory witness statements, but state law would not permit the petitioner to present the new evidence in a successive petition.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under our precedent, if the petitioner had not presented his Brady claim to the state court at all, his claim would be deemed defaulted and the petitioner could attempt to show cause and prejudice to overcome the default. See Michael Williams, 529 U. S., at 444; see also n. 1, supra. If, however, the new evidence merely bolsters a Brady claim that was adjudicated on the merits in state court, it is unclear how the petitioner can obtain federal habeas relief after today\xe2\x80\x99s holding. What may have been a reasonable decision on the state-court record may no longer be reasonable in light of the new evidence. See Kyles v. Whitley, 514 U. S. 419, 436 (1995) (materiality of Brady evidence is viewed \xe2\x80\x9ccollectively, not item by item\xe2\x80\x9d). Because the state court adjudicated the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Brady claim on the merits, \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) would still apply. Yet, under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), a federal court is now prohibited from considering the new evidence in determining the reasonableness of the state-court decision.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) thus suggests the anomalous result that petitioners with new claims based on newly obtained evidence can obtain federal habeas relief if they can show cause and prejudice for their default but petitioners with newly obtained evidence supporting a claim adjudicated on the merits in state court cannot obtain federal habeas relief if they cannot first satisfy \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) without the new evidence. That the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation leads to this anomaly is good reason to conclude that its interpretation is wrong. See Keeney v. Tamayo-Reyes, 504 U. S. 1, 7\xe2\x80\x938 (1992) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is \xe2\x80\xa6 irrational to distinguish between failing to properly assert a federal claim in state court and failing in state court to properly develop such a claim\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority responds to this anomaly by suggesting that my hypothetical petitioner \xe2\x80\x9cmay well [have] a new claim.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 7] Ante, at 14, n.\xc2\xa010. This suggestion is puzzling. New evidence does not usually give rise to a new claim; it merely provides additional proof of a claim already adjudicated on the merits.[Footnote 8] The majority presumably means to\xc2\xa0suggest that the petitioner might be able to obtain federal-court review of his new evidence if he can show cause and\xc2\xa0prejudice for his failure to present the \xe2\x80\x9cnew\xe2\x80\x9d claim to a state court. In that scenario, however, the federal court would review the purportedly \xe2\x80\x9cnew\xe2\x80\x9d claim de novo. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach thus threatens to replace deferential review of new evidence under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) with de novo review of new evidence in the form of \xe2\x80\x9cnew\xe2\x80\x9d claims.[Footnote 9] Because it is unlikely that Congress intended\nde novo review\xe2\x80\x94the result suggested by the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion\xe2\x80\x94it must have intended for district courts to\nconsider newly discovered evidence in conducting the \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) analysis.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) appears ultimately to rest on its understanding that state courts must have the first opportunity to adjudicate habeas petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims. See ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt would be contrary to [AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s exhaustion requirement] to allow a petitioner to overcome an adverse state-court decision with new evidence introduced in a federal habeas court and reviewed by that court in the first instance effectively de novo\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 10] Justice Breyer takes the same position. See ante, at 2 (opinion concurring in part and dissenting in part) (AEDPA is designed \xe2\x80\x9cto give the State a first opportunity to consider most matters\xe2\x80\x9d). I fully agree that habeas petitioners must attempt to present evidence to state courts in the first instance, as does Justice Alito, see ante, at 2. Where I disagree with the majority is in my understanding that \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) already accomplishes this result. By reading \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) to do the work of \xc2\xa72254(e)(2), the majority gives \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) an unnaturally cramped reading. As a result, the majority either has foreclosed habeas relief for diligent petitioners who, through no fault of their own, were unable to present exculpatory evidence to the state court that adjudicated their claims or has created a new set of procedural complexities for the lower courts to navigate to ensure the availability of the Great Writ for diligent petitioners.

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These considerations lead me to agree with the courts of appeals that have concluded that a federal court should assess the reasonableness of a state court\xe2\x80\x99s application of clearly established federal law under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) in light of evidence properly admitted in a federal evidentiary hearing. There is nothing \xe2\x80\x9cstrange\xe2\x80\x9d about this approach. Ante, at 10. Under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), federal courts routinely engage in analysis that the state court itself might never have conducted or did not conduct. For example, when a state court summarily denies a claim without explanation, as the California Supreme Court did here, district courts must deny habeas relief pursuant to \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) so long as \xe2\x80\x9cthere is any reasonable argument\xe2\x80\x9d supporting the denial of the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim. Harrington, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 16). We likewise ask whether a state-court decision unreasonably applied clearly established federal law when the state court issued a reasoned decision but failed to cite federal law altogether. See Early v. Packer, 537 U. S. 3, 8 (2002) (per curiam). Determining whether a state court could reasonably have denied a petitioner relief in light of newly discovered evidence is not so different than determining whether there is any reasonable basis for a state court\xe2\x80\x99s unreasoned decision.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Admittedly, the text of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), standing alone, does not compel either reading of that provision. But construing \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) to permit consideration of evidence properly introduced in federal court best accords with the text of \xc2\xa72254(d)(2) and AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s structure as a whole. By interpreting \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) to prevent nondiligent petitioners from gaming the system\xe2\x80\x94the very purpose of \xc2\xa72254(e)(2)\xe2\x80\x94the majority potentially has put habeas relief out of reach for diligent petitioners with meritorious claims based on new evidence.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority claims that its holding is \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent\xe2\x80\x9d with our case law. Ante, at 10. Quite the opposite is true: Our cases reflect our previous understanding that evidence properly admitted pursuant to \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) is relevant to the \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) analysis.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Landrigan, Justice Thomas, the author of today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, confirmed this understanding of the interplay between \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d)(1) and 2254(e)(2). As noted above, we admonished district courts to consider whether a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s allegations, if proved true, would satisfy \xc2\xa72254(d) in determining whether to grant a hearing. After highlighting the deference owed to state courts under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(d) and 2254(e)(1), we stated:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn deciding whether to grant an evidentiary hearing, a federal court must consider whether such a hearing could enable an applicant to prove the petition\xe2\x80\x99s factual allegations, which, if true, would entitle the\napplicant to federal habeas relief. Because the deferential standards prescribed by \xc2\xa72254 control whether to grant habeas relief, a federal court must take into account those standards in deciding whether an evidentiary hearing is appropriate.\xe2\x80\x9d 550 U. S., at 474 (citation omitted).

\n

\nBy instructing district courts to consider the \xc2\xa72254(d) standards in deciding whether to grant a hearing, we must have understood that the evidence admitted at a hearing could be considered in the \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) analysis. See Brief for American Civil Liberties Union as Amicus Curiae 9 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe whole point of Landrigan\xe2\x80\x99s admonition that the court must decide whether to hold a hearing with an eye on \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) is that some proffers of evidence will not justify federal fact-finding in view of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), but that other proffers of proof will\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 11]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Michael Williams, the warden argued that \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) bars an evidentiary hearing whenever a petitioner was unable to develop the factual record in state court, \xe2\x80\x9cwhether or not through his own fault or neglect.\xe2\x80\x9d 529 U. S., at 430. Under the warden\xe2\x80\x99s argument, a petitioner who did not develop the record in state court, whatever the reason, would be barred from presenting evidence to the federal court. In rejecting that argument, we\nobserved:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA prisoner who developed his claim in state court and can prove the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was \xe2\x80\x98contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States,\xe2\x80\x99 is not barred from obtaining relief by \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). If the opening clause of \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) covers a request for an evidentiary hearing on a claim which was pursued with diligence but remained undeveloped in state court because, for instance, the prosecution concealed the facts, a prisoner lacking clear and convincing evidence of innocence could be barred from a hearing on the claim even if he could satisfy \xc2\xa72254(d).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 434 (citation omitted; emphasis added).

\n

\nA petitioner in the latter situation would almost certainly be unable to \xe2\x80\x9csatisfy \xc2\xa72254(d)\xe2\x80\x9d without introducing the concealed facts in federal court. This passage thus reflects our understanding that, in some circumstances, a petitioner might need an evidentiary hearing in federal court to prove the facts necessary to satisfy \xc2\xa72254(d). To avoid foreclosing habeas relief for such petitioners, we concluded that \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) could not bear the warden\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9charsh reading,\xe2\x80\x9d which essentially would have held petitioners strictly at fault for their inability to develop the facts in state court. Ibid. The majority today gives an equally \xe2\x80\x9charsh reading\xe2\x80\x9d to \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) to achieve the result we rejected in Michael Williams.[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0None of the other cases cited by the majority supports its result. In Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362 (2000) (Terry Williams), we interpreted \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) to ask whether the state-court decision \xe2\x80\x9cidentifies the correct governing legal principle from this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions but unreasonably applies that principle to the facts of the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 413. However, we had no reason to decide whether the \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) inquiry was limited to the state-court record, as the District Court did not hold an evidentiary hearing in that case. See id., at 372.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Holland v. Jackson, we stated that \xe2\x80\x9cwe have made clear that whether a state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was unreasonable must be assessed in light of the record the court had before it.\xe2\x80\x9d 542 U. S., at 652. In the next sentence, however, we observed that the evidence at issue \xe2\x80\x9ccould have been the subject of an evidentiary hearing by the District Court, but only if respondent was not at fault in failing to develop that evidence in state court.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 652\xe2\x80\x93653. We proceeded to find that the evidence was not properly admitted under \xc2\xa72254(e)(2) before concluding that the Court of Appeals had erred in its \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) analysis. Id., at 653; see also Bradshaw v. Richey, 546 U. S. 74, 79 (2005) (per curiam).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, our cases reflect our recognition that it is sometimes appropriate to consider new evidence in deciding whether a petitioner can satisfy \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). In reading our precedent to require the opposite conclusion, the majority disregards the concerns that motivated our decision in Michael Williams: Some petitioners, even if diligent, may be unable to develop the factual record in state court through no fault of their own. We should not interpret \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) to foreclose these diligent petitioners from accessing the Great Writ when the state court will not consider the new evidence and could not reasonably have reached the same conclusion with the new evidence before it.

Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s stepfather beat him several times a week, including at least once with a two-by-four board. \xe2\x80\x9cThere was so much violence in [the] home\xe2\x80\x9d that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s brother \xe2\x80\x9cdreaded coming home each day.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ER\xe2\x80\x93313. Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s second habeas submission to the California Supreme Court included Stalberg\xe2\x80\x99s declaration. That court summarily denied Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s petition on the merits.

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pinholster returned to Federal District Court and filed an amended petition. After an evidentiary hearing, the District Court concluded that Pinholster had demonstrated deficient performance and prejudice under Strickland.[Footnote 19] The Ninth Circuit, sitting en banc, affirmed. 590 F.\xc2\xa03d 651.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the majority notes, Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s claim arises under Strickland v. Washington. \xe2\x80\x9cThe benchmark for judging any claim of ineffectiveness [under Strickland] must be whether counsel\xe2\x80\x99s conduct so undermined the proper functioning of the adversarial process that the trial cannot be relied on as having produced a just result.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 686. To satisfy this benchmark, a defendant must show both that \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance was deficient\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe deficient performance prejudiced the defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 687.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) applies, the question is whether \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fairminded jurists could disagree\xe2\x80\x99 on the correctness of the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 11) (quoting Yarborough v. Alvarado, 541 U. S. 652, 664 (2004)). When the state court rejected a Strickland claim on the pleadings assuming the allegations to be true, as here, see ante, at 16, n. 12, the federal court must ask whether \xe2\x80\x9cthere is any reasonable argument\xe2\x80\x9d supporting the state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s allegations did not state a claim, Harrington, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 16). This standard is \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult,\xe2\x80\x9d but not impossible, \xe2\x80\x9cto meet.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 12). This case is one in which fairminded jurists could not disagree that the state court erred.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under Strickland, \xe2\x80\x9cthe defendant must show that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s representation fell below an objective standard of reasonableness,\xe2\x80\x9d measured according to \xe2\x80\x9cprevailing professional norms.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 688. We \xe2\x80\x9cindulge a strong presumption that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s conduct falls within the wide range of reasonable professional assistance.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 689. When \xc2\xa72254(d) applies, federal-court review is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98doubly\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d deferential. Harrington, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 16) (quoting Knowles v. Mirzayance, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 11)). In the present AEDPA posture, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he question is whether there is any reasonable argument that counsel satisfied Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s deferential standard.\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 16). Here, there is none.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority surmises that counsel decided on a strategy \xe2\x80\x9cto get the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s aggravation witnesses excluded for lack of notice, and if that failed, to put on Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 19. This is the sort of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98post hoc rationalization\xe2\x80\x99 for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s decisionmaking that contradicts the available evidence of counsel\xe2\x80\x99s actions\xe2\x80\x9d that courts cannot indulge. Harrington, 562 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 20) (quoting Wiggins v. Smith, 539 U. S. 510, 526\xe2\x80\x93527 (2003)). The majority\xe2\x80\x99s explanation for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s conduct contradicts the best available evidence of counsel\xe2\x80\x99s actions: Dettmar\xe2\x80\x99s frank, contemporaneous statement to the trial judge that he \xe2\x80\x9chad not prepared any evidence by way of mitigation.\xe2\x80\x9d 52 Tr. 7250. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s conjecture that counsel had in fact prepared a mitigation defense, based primarily on isolated entries in counsel\xe2\x80\x99s billing records, requires it to assume that Dettmar was lying to the trial judge.[Footnote 20]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, even if Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s counsel had a strategic reason for their actions, that would not automatically render their actions reasonable. For example, had counsel decided their best option was to move to exclude the aggravating evidence, it would have been unreasonable to forgo a mitigation investigation on the hope that the motion would be granted. With a client\xe2\x80\x99s life at stake, it would \xe2\x80\x9cflou[t] prudence,\xe2\x80\x9d Rompilla v. Beard, 545 U. S. 374, 389 (2005), for an attorney to rely on the possibility that the court might preclude aggravating evidence pursuant to a \xe2\x80\x9clegal technicality\xe2\x80\x9d without any backup plan in place in case the court denied the motion, ante, at 19. No reasonable attorney would pursue such a risky strategy. I do not understand the majority to suggest otherwise.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead, I understand the majority\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s actions were reasonable to rest on its belief that they did have a backup plan: a family-sympathy defense. In reaching this conclusion, the majority commits the same Strickland error that we corrected, applying \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), in Wiggins: It holds a purportedly \xe2\x80\x9ctactical judgment\xe2\x80\x9d to be reasonable without assessing \xe2\x80\x9cthe adequacy of the investigatio[n] supporting [that] judgmen[t],\xe2\x80\x9d 539 U. S., at 521. As we stated in Strickland:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[S]trategic choices made after thorough investigation of law and facts relevant to plausible options are virtually unchallengeable; and strategic choices made after less than complete investigation are reasonable precisely to the extent that reasonable professional judgments support the limitations on investigation. In other words, counsel has a duty to make reasonable investigations or to make a reasonable decision that makes particular investigations unnecessary. In any ineffectiveness case, a particular decision not to investigate must be directly assessed for reasonableness in all the circumstances, applying a heavy measure of deference to counsel\xe2\x80\x99s judgments.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 690\xe2\x80\x93691.

\n

\nWe have repeatedly applied this principle since Strickland. See Sears v. Upton, 561 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (per curiam) (slip op., at 9); Porter v. McCollum, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (per curiam) (slip op., at 10); Wiggins, 539 U. S., at 527; Terry Williams, 529 U. S., at 396.[Footnote 21]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As these cases make clear, the prevailing professional norms at the time of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s trial required his attorneys to \xe2\x80\x9cconduct a thorough investigation of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s background,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (citing 1 ABA Standards for Criminal Justice 4\xe2\x80\x934.1, commentary, p.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9355 (2d ed. 1980) (hereinafter ABA Standards)), or \xe2\x80\x9cto make a reasonable decision that makes particular investigations unnecessary,\xe2\x80\x9d Strickland, 466 U. S., at 691.[Footnote 22] \xe2\x80\x9cIn judging the defense\xe2\x80\x99s investigation, as in applying Strickland generally, hindsight is discounted by pegging adequacy to \xe2\x80\x98counsel\xe2\x80\x99s perspective at the time\xe2\x80\x99 investigative decisions are made, and by giving a \xe2\x80\x98heavy measure of deference to counsel\xe2\x80\x99s judgments.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Rompilla, 545 U. S., at 381 (quoting Strickland, 466 U. S., at 689, 691; citation omitted). In some cases, \xe2\x80\x9creasonably diligent counsel may draw a line when they have good reason to think further investigation would be a waste.\xe2\x80\x9d Rompilla, 545 U. S., at 383; see, e.g., Bobby v. Van Hook, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (per curiam) (slip op., at 8); Burger v. Kemp, 483 U. S. 776, 794\xe2\x80\x93795 (1987). In other cases, however, Strickland requires further investigation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Wiggins is illustrative of the competence we have required of counsel in a capital case. There, counsel\xe2\x80\x99s in-vestigation was limited to three sources: psychological testing, a presentencing report, and Department of Social Services records. 539 U. S., at 523\xe2\x80\x93524. The records revealed that the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s mother was an alcoholic, that he displayed emotional difficulties in foster care, that he was frequently absent from school, and that on one occasion, his mother left him alone for days without food. Id., at 525. In these circumstances, we concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cany reasonably competent attorney would have realized that pursuing these leads was necessary to making an informed choice among possible defenses.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Accordingly, we held, the state court\xe2\x80\x99s assumption that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s investigation was adequate was an unreasonable application of Strickland. 539 U. S., at 528.[Footnote 23]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case is remarkably similar to Wiggins. As the majority reads the record, counsel\xe2\x80\x99s mitigation investigation consisted of talking to Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother, consulting with Dr. Stalberg, and researching epilepsy.[Footnote 24] Ante, at 20. What little information counsel gleaned from this \xe2\x80\x9crudimentary\xe2\x80\x9d investigation, Wiggins, 539 U. S., at 524, would have led any reasonable attorney \xe2\x80\x9cto investigate further,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 527. Counsel learned from Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mother that he attended a class for educationally handicapped children, that a psychologist had recommended placing him in a mental institution, and that he spent time in a state hospital for emotionally handicapped children. They knew that Pinholster had been diagnosed with epilepsy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[A]ny reasonably competent attorney would have realized that pursuing\xe2\x80\x9d the leads suggested by this information \xe2\x80\x9cwas necessary to making an informed choice among possible defenses.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 525; see also Penry v. Lynaugh, 492 U. S. 302, 319 (1989) (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]vidence about the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s background and character is relevant because of the belief, long held by this society, that defendants who commit criminal acts that are attributable to a disadvantaged background, or to emotional and mental problems, may be less culpable than defendants who have no such excuse\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Yet counsel made no effort to obtain the readily available evidence suggested by the information they learned, such as Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s schooling or medical records, or to contact\nPinholster\xe2\x80\x99s school authorities. They did not contact Dr. Dubin or the many other health-care providers who had treated Pinholster. Put simply, counsel \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to act while potentially powerful mitigating evidence stared them in the face.\xe2\x80\x9d Bobby, 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 8) (citing Wiggins, 539 U. S., at 525).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cimpediments\xe2\x80\x9d facing counsel, ante, at 21, did not justify their minimal investigation. It is true that Pinholster was \xe2\x80\x9can unsympathetic client.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But this fact compounds, rather than excuses, counsel\xe2\x80\x99s deficiency in ignoring the glaring avenues of investigation that could explain why Pinholster was the way he was. See Sears, 561 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 7) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis evidence might not have made Sears any more likable to the jury, but it might well have helped the jury understand Sears, and his horrendous acts\xe2\x80\x94especially in light of his purportedly stable upbringing\xe2\x80\x9d). Nor can Dr. Stalberg\xe2\x80\x99s two-page report, which was based on a very limited record and focused primarily on Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s mental state at the time of\nthe homicides, excuse counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to investigate the broader range of potential mitigating circumstances.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe record of the actual sentencing proceedings underscores the unreasonableness of counsel\xe2\x80\x99s conduct by\nsuggesting that their failure to investigate thoroughly re-sulted from inattention, not reasoned strategic judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Wiggins, 539 U. S., at 526. Dettmar told the trial judge that he was unprepared to present any mitigation evidence. The mitigation case that counsel eventually put on can be described, at best, as \xe2\x80\x9chalfhearted.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Counsel made no effort to bolster Brashear\xe2\x80\x99s self-interested testimony with school or medical records, as the prosecutor effectively emphasized in closing argument. And because they did not pursue obvious leads, they failed to recognize that Brashear\xe2\x80\x99s testimony painting Pinholster as the bad apple in a normal, nondeprived family was false.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In denying Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s claim, the California Supreme Court necessarily overlooked Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s clearly established admonition that \xe2\x80\x9cstrategic choices made after less than complete investigation are reasonable precisely to the extent that reasonable professional judgments support the limitations.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 690\xe2\x80\x93691. As in Wiggins, in light of the information available to Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s counsel, it is plain that \xe2\x80\x9creasonable professional judgments\xe2\x80\x9d could not have supported their woefully inadequate investigation.[Footnote 25] 466 U. S., at 691. Accordingly, the California Supreme Court could not reasonably have concluded that Pinholster had failed to allege that his counsel\xe2\x80\x99s investigation was inadequate under Strickland.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority also concludes that the California Supreme Court could reasonably have concluded that Pinholster did not state a claim of prejudice. This conclusion, in light of the overwhelming mitigating evidence that was not before the jury, is wrong. To establish prejudice, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he defendant must show that there is a reasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 694. When a habeas petitioner challenges a death sentence, \xe2\x80\x9cthe question is whether there is a reasonable probability that, absent the errors, the sentencer \xe2\x80\xa6 would have concluded that the balance of aggravating and mitigating circumstances did not warrant death.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 695. This inquiry requires evaluating \xe2\x80\x9cthe totality of the available mitigation evidence\xe2\x80\x94both that adduced at trial, and the evidence adduced in the habeas proceeding\xe2\x80\x94in reweighing it against the evidence in aggravation.\xe2\x80\x9d Terry Williams, 529 U. S., at 397\xe2\x80\x93398. The ultimate question in this case is whether, taking into account all the mitigating and aggravating evidence, \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a reasonable probability that at least one juror would have struck a different balance.\xe2\x80\x9d Wiggins, 539 U. S., at 537; see Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa7190.4(b) (West 2008) (requiring a unanimous jury verdict to impose a death sentence).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Like the majority, I first consider the aggravating and mitigating evidence presented at trial. By virtue of its verdict in the guilt phase, the jury had already concluded that Pinholster had stabbed and killed the victims. As the majority states, the jury saw Pinholster \xe2\x80\x9crevel\xe2\x80\x9d in his history of burglaries during the guilt phase. Ante, at 26. Notwithstanding Brashear\xe2\x80\x99s obvious self-interest, counsel failed to offer readily available, objective evidence that would have substantiated and expanded on her testimony. And Brashear\xe2\x80\x99s statement that Pinholster would not be alive much longer because he had \xe2\x80\x9ca chip in his head floating around,\xe2\x80\x9d 52 Tr. 7397, could only have undermined her credibility, as the prosecutor urged, see 53 id., at 7447 (\xe2\x80\x9cDoes she want you to believe sometime before he got to country jail some doctor looked in a crystal ball and\nsaid, \xe2\x80\x98In three years you are going to die\xe2\x80\x99? That\xe2\x80\x99s ridiculous\xe2\x80\x9d). The trial judge was thoroughly unimpressed with Brashear\xe2\x80\x99s testimony. See supra, at 20.

As in Sears, 561 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 3), the prosecutor used Brashear\xe2\x80\x99s testimony that Pinholster came from a good family against him. See 53 Tr. 7442.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, counsel presented little in the way of mitigating evidence, and the prosecutor effectively used their halfhearted attempt to present a mitigation case to advocate for the death penalty. The jury nonetheless took two days to reach a decision to impose a death sentence.

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dr. Woods concluded that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s aggressive conduct resulted from bipolar mood disorder. Just months before the murders, a doctor had recommended that Pinholster be sent to a psychiatric institute. Dr. Woods also explained that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s bizarre behavior before the murders reflected \xe2\x80\x9c[a]uditory hallucinations\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csevere psychosis.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ER\xe2\x80\x93169. The available records confirmed that Pinholster suffered from longstanding seizure disorders, which may have been caused by his childhood head injuries.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On this record, I do not see how it can be said that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he \xe2\x80\x98new\xe2\x80\x99 evidence largely duplicated the mitigation evidence at trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 29; see Arizona v. Fulminante, 499 U. S. 279, 298\xe2\x80\x93299 (1991) (evidence is not \xe2\x80\x9cmerely cumulative\xe2\x80\x9d if it corroborates other evidence that is \xe2\x80\x9cunbelievable\xe2\x80\x9d on its own). Brashear\xe2\x80\x99s self-interested testimony was not confirmed with objective evidence, as the prosecutor highlighted. The new evidence would have \xe2\x80\x9cdestroyed the [relatively] benign conception of [Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s] upbringing\xe2\x80\x9d presented by his mother. Rompilla, 545 U. S., at 391. The jury heard no testimony at all that Pinholster likely suffered from brain damage or bipolar mood disorder, and counsel offered no evidence to help the jury understand the likely effect of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s head injuries or his bizarre behavior on the night of the homicides. The jury heard no testimony recounting the substantial evidence of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s likely neurological problems. And it heard no medical evidence that Pinholster suffered from epilepsy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority responds that \xe2\x80\x9cmuch\xe2\x80\x9d of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s new mitigating evidence \xe2\x80\x9cis of questionable mitigating value.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 29. By presenting psychiatric testimony, it contends, \xe2\x80\x9cPinholster would have opened the door to rebuttal by a state expert.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But, because the California Supreme Court denied Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s petition on the pleadings, it had no reason to know what a state expert might have said. Moreover, given the record evidence, it is reasonably probable that at least one juror would have credited his expert. In any event, even if a rebuttal expert testified that Pinholster suffered from antisocial personality disorder, this would hardly have come as a surprise to the jury. See ante, at 22 (describing Pinholster as a \xe2\x80\x9cpsychotic client whose performance at trial hardly endeared him to the jury\xe2\x80\x9d). It is for this reason that it was especially important for counsel to present the available evidence to help the jury understand Pinholster. See Sears, 561 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 6\xe2\x80\x937).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Had counsel conducted an adequate investigation, the judge and jury would have heard credible evidence showing that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s criminal acts and aggressive tendencies were \xe2\x80\x9cattributable to a disadvantaged background, or to emotional and mental problems.\xe2\x80\x9d Penry, 492 U. S., at 319 (internal quotation marks omitted). They would have learned that Pinholster had the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98kind of troubled history we have declared relevant to assessing a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s moral culpability.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Porter, 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 12) (quoting Wiggins, 539 U. S., at 535). Applying Strickland, we have repeatedly found \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable probability,\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 694, that the sentencer would have reached a different result had counsel presented similar evidence. See, e.g., Porter, 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313) (evidence of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s childhood history of physical abuse, brain abnormality, limited schooling, and heroic military service); Rompilla, 545 U. S., at 392 (evidence of severe abuse and neglect as a child, as well as brain\ndamage); Wiggins, 539 U. S., at 535 (evidence of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csevere privation and abuse\xe2\x80\x9d as a child, homelessness, and \xe2\x80\x9cdiminished mental capacities\xe2\x80\x9d); Terry\nWilliams, 529 U. S., at 398 (evidence of childhood\nmistreatment and neglect, head injuries, possible organic mental impairments, and borderline mental retardation).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority does not dispute the similarity between this case and the cited cases. However, it criticizes the Court of Appeals for relying on Rompilla and Terry Williams on the ground that we reviewed the prejudice question de novo in those cases. See ante, at 31. I do not read Terry Williams to review the prejudice question de novo.[Footnote 27] More fundamentally, however, I cannot agree with the premise that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hose cases \xe2\x80\xa6 offer no guidance with respect to whether a state court has unreasonably determined that prejudice is lacking.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 31 (emphasis deleted). In each of these cases, we did not purport to create new law; we simply applied the same clearly established precedent, Strickland, to a different set of facts. Because these cases illuminate the kinds of mitigation evidence that suffice to establish prejudice under Strickland, they provide useful, but not dispositive, guidance for courts to consider when determining whether a state court has unreasonably applied Strickland.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In many cases, a state court presented with additional mitigation evidence will reasonably conclude that there is no \xe2\x80\x9creasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different.\xe2\x80\x9d Strickland, 466 U. S., at 694. This is not such a case. Admittedly, Pinholster unjustifiably stabbed and killed two people, and his history of violent outbursts and burglaries surely did not endear him to the jury. But the homicides did not appear premeditated. And the State\xe2\x80\x99s aggravation case was no stronger than in Rompilla and Terry Williams. See 545 U. S., at 378, 383 (the defendant committed murder by torture and had a significant history of violent felonies, including a rape); 529 U. S., at 418 (Rehnquist, C. J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (the defendant had a lifetime of crime, and after the murder he \xe2\x80\x9csavagely beat an elderly woman,\xe2\x80\x9d set a home on fire, and stabbed a man (internal quotation marks omitted)). Even on the trial record, it took the jury two days to decide on a penalty. The contrast between the \xe2\x80\x9cnot persuasive\xe2\x80\x9d mitigation case put on by Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s counsel, 54 Tr. 7514, and the substantial mitigation evidence at their fingertips was stark. Given these considerations, it is not a foregone conclusion, as the majority deems it, that a juror familiar with his troubled background and psychiatric issues would have reached the same conclusion regarding Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s culpability. Fairminded jurists could not doubt that, on the record before the California Supreme Court, \xe2\x80\x9cthere [was] a reasonable probability that at least one juror would have struck a different balance.\xe2\x80\x9d Wiggins, 539 U. S., at 537.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The state-court record on its own was more than adequate to support the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion that the California Supreme Court could not reasonably have rejected Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s Strickland claim. The additional evidence presented in the federal evidentiary hearing only confirms that conclusion.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the hearing, Pinholster offered many of the same documents that were before the state habeas court. He also offered his trial attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 billing records, which were before the state habeas court as part of the trial record. Of the seven lay witnesses who testified at the hearing, six had previously executed declarations in support of Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s state-court petition. (The seventh, Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s uncle, provided testimony cumulative of other testimony.)

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Two experts testified on Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s behalf; neither had presented declarations to the state habeas court. The first was Dr. Donald Olson, assistant professor of neurology and neurological sciences and director of the Pediatric Epilepsy Program at Stanford University Medical Center. It appears that Pinholster retained Dr. Olson to rebut the testimony of the expert disclosed by the State in the federal proceeding. See Decl. of Michael D. Abzug in Support of Stipulated Ex Parte Application to Continue Evidentiary Hearing and Discovery Cut-Off and to Substitute Counsel in Pinholster v. Calderon, No. CV 95\xe2\x80\x936240\xe2\x80\x93GLT (CD Cal.), p.\xc2\xa02. Relying in part on Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s abnormal EEG, Dr. Olson opined that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s childhood accidents \xe2\x80\x9clikely result[ed] in brain injury\xe2\x80\x9d and that these injuries \xe2\x80\x9cconferred a risk of epilepsy.\xe2\x80\x9d Record ER\xe2\x80\x93699 to ER\xe2\x80\x93700. He concluded that it was reasonably probable that Pinholster had suffered from partial epilepsy since at least 1968 and had suffered from brain injury since at least 1964. Id., at ER\xe2\x80\x93701.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s second expert was Dr. Sophia Vinogradov, associate professor of psychiatry at the University of California, San Francisco. Dr. Vinogradov\xe2\x80\x99s testimony was based on essentially the same facts as Dr. Woods\xe2\x80\x99 and Dr. Stalberg\xe2\x80\x99s state-court declarations. She highlighted Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s childhood head traumas, history of epilepsy, abusive and neglected upbringing, history of substance abuse, and bizarre behavior on the night of the homicides. She opined that his aggressive behavior resulted from childhood head traumas:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cAll data indicates that there were severe effects of the two serious head injuries sustained at age 2 and age 3, with evidence for behavioral changes related to dysfunction of frontal cortex: severe attentional and learning problems in childhood, hyperactivity, aggressivity, impulsivity, social-emotional impairment, seizure disorder, and explosive dyscontrol.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ER\xe2\x80\x93731.

\n

\nShe also opined that, right before the homicides, Pinholster was in an \xe2\x80\x9capparently hallucinatory state [that] was likely the result of his intoxication with multiple substances.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ER\xe2\x80\x93707

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State presented two experts: Dr. Stalberg, the psychiatrist who had examined Pinholster in the middle of trial,[Footnote 28] and Dr. David Rudnick. Although Dr. Stalberg maintained that Pinholster suffered from antisocial personality disorder, which was his original diagnosis in the middle of trial, he again emphasized that there was \xe2\x80\x9cvoluminous\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccompelling\xe2\x80\x9d mitigation evidence that had not previously been made available to him or presented to the jury. Id., at ER\xe2\x80\x93926, ER\xe2\x80\x93953. He stated that conversations with Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s family revealed that he and his siblings were \xe2\x80\x9craised like animals, wild animals,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ER\xe2\x80\x93948, and he opined that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s upbringing was a risk factor for antisocial personality disorder. See ibid. (Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s upbringing \xe2\x80\x9cwould speak volumes, looking at it from a mitigation point of view\xe2\x80\x9d). And he agreed that the mitigation evidence presented at trial was \xe2\x80\x9cprofoundly misleading.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ER\xe2\x80\x93966. Dr. Rudnick testified that Pinholster suffered from antisocial personality disorder.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State also introduced into evidence the 1978 probation report that Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s counsel had in their possession at the time of his trial. The report demonstrated that counsel were aware that Pinholster was in classes for educationally handicapped children, that he was committed to a state hospital for emotionally handicapped children, and that he suffered two \xe2\x80\x9csevere head injuries.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at SER\xe2\x80\x93243.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Much of the evidence presented at the federal hearing was duplicative of the evidence submitted to the California Supreme Court. The additional evidence presented at the hearing only confirmed that the California Supreme Court could not reasonably have rejected Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s claim.[Footnote 29]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For example, the probation report presented by the State confirmed that counsel had in their possession information that would have led any reasonable attorney \xe2\x80\x9cto investigate further.\xe2\x80\x9d Wiggins, 539 U. S., at 527. Counsel nevertheless took no action to investigate these leads.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s experts opined that his childhood head traumas likely resulted in brain injury and conferred a risk of epilepsy. Although the State presented testimony that Pinholster had antisocial personality disorder, it was not clear error for the District Court to conclude that jurors could have credited Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s experts. Even the State\xe2\x80\x99s own expert, Dr. Stalberg, testified to the \xe2\x80\x9cvoluminous\xe2\x80\x9d mitigation evidence in Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s case. Record ER\xe2\x80\x93926.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the evidence confirmed what was already apparent from the state-court record: Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s counsel failed to conduct an adequate mitigation investigation, and there was a reasonable probability that at least one juror confronted with the \xe2\x80\x9cvoluminous\xe2\x80\x9d mitigating evidence counsel should have discovered would have voted to spare Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s life. Ibid. Accordingly, whether on the basis of the state- or federal-court record, the courts below correctly concluded that Pinholster had shown that the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision reflected an unreasonable application of Strickland.[Footnote 30]

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I cannot agree with either aspect of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling. I fear the consequences of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s novel interpretation of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) for diligent state habeas petitioners with compelling evidence supporting their claims who were unable, through no fault of their own, to present that evidence to the state court that adjudicated their claims. And the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the California Supreme Court reasonably denied Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim overlooks counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to investigate obvious avenues of mitigation and the contrast between the woefully inadequate mitigation case they presented and the evidence they should and would have discovered. I respectfully dissent.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join Parts I and II of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. I do not join Part III, for I would send this case back to the Court of Appeals so that it can apply the legal standards that Part II announces to the complex facts of this case. Compare ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9331 (majority opinion), with post, at 17\xe2\x80\x9342 (Sotomayor, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Like the Court, I believe that its understanding of 28\xc2\xa0U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) does not leave AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s hearing section, \xc2\xa72254(e), without work to do. An offender who believes he is entitled to habeas relief must first present a claim (including his evidence) to the state courts. If the state courts reject the claim, then a federal habeas court may review that rejection on the basis of the materials considered by the state court. If the federal habeas court finds that the state-court decision fails (d)\xe2\x80\x99s test (or if (d) does not apply), then an (e) hearing may be needed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For example, if the state-court rejection assumed the habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s facts (deciding that, even if those facts were true, federal law was not violated), then (after finding the state court wrong on a (d) ground) an (e) hearing might be needed to determine whether the facts alleged were indeed true. Or if the state-court rejection rested on a state ground, which a federal habeas court found inadequate, then an (e) hearing might be needed to consider the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s (now unblocked) substantive federal claim. Or if the state-court rejection rested on only one of several related federal grounds (e.g., that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s assistance was not \xe2\x80\x9cinadequate\xe2\x80\x9d), then, if the federal court found that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in respect to the ground it decided violated (d), an (e) hearing might be needed to consider other related parts of the whole constitutional claim (e.g., whether the counsel\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinadequate\xe2\x80\x9d assistance was also prejudicial). There may be other situations in which an (e) hearing is needed as well.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, however, we cannot say whether an (e) hearing is needed until we know whether the state court, in rejecting Pinholster\xe2\x80\x99s claim on the basis presented to that state court, violated (d). (In my view, the lower courts\xe2\x80\x99 analysis in respect to this matter is inadequate.)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no role in (d) analysis for a habeas petitioner to introduce evidence that was not first presented to the state courts. But that does not mean that Pinholster is without recourse to present new evidence. He can always return to state court presenting new evidence not previously presented. If the state court again denies relief, he might be able to return to federal court to make claims related to the latest rejection, subject to AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s limitations on successive petitions. See \xc2\xa72244.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I am not trying to predict the future course of these proceedings. I point out only that, in my view, AEDPA is not designed to take necessary remedies from a habeas petitioner but to give the State a first opportunity to consider most matters and to insist that federal courts properly respect state-court determinations.
