\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

WALKER, WARDEN, et\xc2\xa0al. v. MARTIN

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x93996.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 29, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 23, 2011

\n

While most States set determinate time limits for collateral relief applications, California courts \xe2\x80\x9cappl[y] a general \xe2\x80\x98reasonableness\xe2\x80\x99 standard\xe2\x80\x9d to judge whether a habeas petition is timely filed, Carey v. Saffold, 536 U. S. 214, 222. Under that standard, \xe2\x80\x9ca [habeas] petition should be filed as promptly as the circumstances allow \xe2\x80\xa6 ,\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Clark, 5 Cal. 4th 750, 765, n.\xc2\xa05, 855 P.\xc2\xa02d 729, 738, n.\xc2\xa05. Three decisions, Clark, In\xc2\xa0re Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th 770, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d 311, and In\xc2\xa0re Gallego, 18 Cal. 4th 825, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d 290, describe California\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness requirement. A prisoner must seek habeas relief without \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial delay,\xe2\x80\x9d e.g., Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th, at 780, 959 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 317, as \xe2\x80\x9cmeasured from the time the petitioner or counsel knew, or reasonably should have known, of the information offered in support of the claim and the legal basis of the claim,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 787, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 322. All California courts have \xe2\x80\x9coriginal jurisdiction in habeas corpus proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Cal. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0VI, \xc2\xa710. Because a habeas petitioner may skip over the lower courts and file directly in the California Supreme Court, that court rules on a staggering number of habeas petitions each year. A summary denial citing Clark and Robbins means that the petition is rejected as untimely. California courts, however, have discretion to bypass a timeliness issue and, instead, summarily reject the petition for want of merit.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Martin was convicted of murder and robbery, and was sentenced to life in prison without parole. After the California Supreme Court denied Martin\xe2\x80\x99s first state habeas petition, he filed a federal habeas petition. The District Court ordered a stay to permit Martin to return to state court to raise ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claims he had not previously aired. Martin raised those claims in his second habeas petition in the California Supreme Court, but gave no reason for his failure to assert the additional claims until nearly five years after his sentence and conviction became final. The California Supreme Court denied the petition, citing Clark and Robbins. Having exhausted his state-court remedies, Martin filed an amended federal habeas petition. The District Court dismissed his belatedly asserted claims as untimely under California law. The Ninth Circuit vacated that order and directed the District Court to determine the \xe2\x80\x9cadequacy\xe2\x80\x9d of the State\xe2\x80\x99s time bar. Again rejecting Martin\xe2\x80\x99s petition, the District Court found California\xe2\x80\x99s bar an adequate state ground for denying Martin\xe2\x80\x99s new pleas. Concluding that the time bar was not firmly defined or consistently applied, the Ninth Circuit remanded for a determination of the merits of Martin\xe2\x80\x99s claims.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0California\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness requirement qualifies as an independent state ground adequate to bar habeas corpus relief in federal court. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Absent showings of \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprejudice,\xe2\x80\x9d see Wainwright v. Sykes, 433 U. S. 72, 84\xe2\x80\x9385, federal habeas relief will be unavailable when (1) \xe2\x80\x9ca state court [has] declined to address a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s federal claims because the prisoner had failed to meet a state procedural requirement,\xe2\x80\x9d and (2) \xe2\x80\x9cthe state judgment rests on independent and adequate state procedural grounds,\xe2\x80\x9d Coleman v. Thompson, 501 U. S. 722, 729\xe2\x80\x93730. P.\xc2\xa07.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0A \xe2\x80\x9crule can be \xe2\x80\x98firmly established\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98regularly followed,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore adequate, \xe2\x80\x9ceven if the appropriate exercise of discretion may permit consideration of a federal claim in some cases but not others.\xe2\x80\x9d Beard v. Kindler, 558 U. S. ___, ___. California\xe2\x80\x99s time rule, although discretionary, meets this \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly established\xe2\x80\x9d criterion. The California Supreme Court framed the requirement in a trilogy of cases, instructing habeas petitioners to \xe2\x80\x9calleg[e] with specificity\xe2\x80\x9d the absence of substantial delay, good cause for delay, or eligibility for one of four exceptions to the time bar. Gallego, 18 Cal. 4th, at 838, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 299. And California\xe2\x80\x99s case law made it plain that Martin\xe2\x80\x99s nearly five-year delay was \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., id., at 829\xe2\x80\x93831, 838, and n. 13, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 293\xe2\x80\x93294, 299, and n.\xc2\xa013. The Court finds unpersuasive Martin\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the terms \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time\xe2\x80\x9d period and \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial delay\xe2\x80\x9d make California\xe2\x80\x99s rule too vague to be regarded as \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly established.\xe2\x80\x9d While indeterminate language is typical of discretionary rules, application of those rules in particular circumstances can supply the requisite clarity. Congressional statutes and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions have employed time limitations that are not stated in precise, numerical terms. For example, current federal habeas prescriptions limit the time for filing a petition to one year. The clock runs from \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the [supporting] facts \xe2\x80\xa6 could have been discovered through \xe2\x80\xa6 due diligence.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255(f)(4). Although \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98due diligence\xe2\x80\x99 is an inexact measure of how much delay is too much,\xe2\x80\x9d Johnson v. United States, 544 U. S. 295, 309, n. 7, \xe2\x80\x9cuse of an imprecise standard is no justification for depriving [a rule\xe2\x80\x99s] language of any meaning,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Nor is California\xe2\x80\x99s time rule vulnerable on the ground that it is not regularly followed. Each year, the California State Supreme Court summarily denies hundreds of habeas petitions by citing Clark and Robbins. Contrary to Martin\xe2\x80\x99s argument, California\xe2\x80\x99s time bar is not infirm simply because a court may opt to bypass the Clark/Robbins assessment and summarily dismiss a petition on the merits, if that is the easier path. Nor should a discretionary rule be disregarded automatically upon a showing that outcomes under the rule vary from case to case. Discretion enables a court to home in on case-specific considerations and to avoid the harsh results that may attend consistent application of an unyielding rule. A state ground may be found inadequate when a court has exercised its discretion in a surprising or unfair manner, but Martin makes no such contention here. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0This decision leaves unaltered the Court\xe2\x80\x99s repeated recognition that federal courts must carefully examine state procedural requirements to ensure that they do not operate to discriminate against claims of federal rights. See, e.g., Brown v. Western R. Co. of Ala., 338 U. S. 294, 298\xe2\x80\x93299. On the record here, however, there is no basis for concluding that California\xe2\x80\x99s rule operates in such a discriminatory manner. P.\xc2\xa013.

\n

357 Fed. Appx. 793, reversed.

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 09-996

JAMES WALKER, WARDEN, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS\nv. CHARLES W. MARTIN

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[February 23, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns California\xe2\x80\x99s time limitation on applications for postconviction (habeas corpus) relief. The question presented: Does California\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness requirement qualify as an independent state ground adequate to bar habeas corpus relief in federal court?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0California does not employ fixed statutory deadlines to determine the timeliness of a state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s petition for habeas corpus. Instead, California directs petitioners to file known claims \xe2\x80\x9cas promptly as the circumstances allow.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Clark, 5 Cal. 4th 750, 765, n.\xc2\xa05, 855 P.\xc2\xa02d 729, 738, n.\xc2\xa05 (1993). Petitioners are further instructed to state when they first learned of the asserted claims and to explain why they did not seek postconviction relief sooner. In\xc2\xa0re Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th 770, 780, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d 311, 317\xe2\x80\x93318 (1998). Claims substantially delayed without justification may be denied as untimely. Ibid.; Clark, 5 Cal. 4th, at 765, n. 5, 855 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 738, n.\xc2\xa05.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0California courts signal that a habeas petition is denied as untimely by citing the controlling decisions, i.e., Clark and Robbins. A spare order denying a petition without explanation or citation ordinarily ranks as a disposition on the merits. Tr. of Oral Arg. 7; see Harrington v. Richter, ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. California courts may elect to pretermit the question whether a petition is timely and simply deny the petition, thereby signaling that the petition lacks merit.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner below, respondent here, Charles W. Martin, presented the claims at issue\xe2\x80\x94all alleging ineffective assistance of counsel\xe2\x80\x94in a habeas petition filed in the California Supreme Court nearly five years after his conviction became final. He stated no reason for the long delay. Citing Clark and Robbins, the court denied Martin\xe2\x80\x99s petition. In turn, the U. S. District Court for the Eastern District of California dismissed Martin\xe2\x80\x99s federal habeas petition raising the same ineffective assistance claims. Denial of Martin\xe2\x80\x99s state-court petition as untimely, the District Court held, rested on an adequate and independent state ground, i.e., Martin\xe2\x80\x99s failure to seek relief in state court \xe2\x80\x9cwithout substantial delay.\xe2\x80\x9d See Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th, at 787, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 322.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The U. S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Contrasting the precision of \xe2\x80\x9cfixed statutory deadlines\xe2\x80\x9d with California\xe2\x80\x99s proscription of \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial delay,\xe2\x80\x9d the appeals court held that California\xe2\x80\x99s standard lacked the clarity and certainty necessary to constitute an adequate state bar. 357 Fed. Appx. 793, 794 (2009) (relying on Townsend v. Knowles, 562 F.\xc2\xa03d 1200 (CA9 2009)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a recent decision, Beard v. Kindler, 558 U. S. ___ (2009), this Court clarified that a state procedural bar may count as an adequate and independent ground for denying a federal habeas petition even if the state court had discretion to reach the merits despite the default. Guided by that decision, we hold that California is not put to the choice of imposing a specific deadline for habeas petitions (which would almost certainly rule out Martin\xe2\x80\x99s nearly five-year delay) or preserving the flexibility of current practice, \xe2\x80\x9cbut only at the cost of undermining the finality of state court judgments.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 7). In so ruling, we stress that Martin has not alleged that California\xe2\x80\x99s time bar, either by design or in operation, discriminates against federal claims or claimants.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While most States set determinate time limits for collateral relief applications, in California, neither statute nor rule of court does so. Instead, California courts \xe2\x80\x9cappl[y] a general \xe2\x80\x98reasonableness\xe2\x80\x99 standard\xe2\x80\x9d to judge whether a habeas petition is timely filed. Carey v. Saffold, 536 U. S. 214, 222 (2002). The basic instruction provided by the California Supreme Court is simply that \xe2\x80\x9ca [habeas] petition should be filed as promptly as the circumstances allow \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Clark, 5 Cal. 4th, at 765, n.\xc2\xa05, 855 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 738, n.\xc2\xa05.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Three leading decisions describe California\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness requirement: Robbins, Clark, and In\xc2\xa0re Gallego, 18 Cal. 4th 825, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d 290 (1998). A prisoner must seek habeas relief without \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial delay,\xe2\x80\x9d Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th, at 780, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 317; Gallego, 18 Cal. 4th, at 833, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 296; Clark, 5 Cal. 4th, at 783, 855 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 750, as \xe2\x80\x9cmeasured from the time the petitioner or counsel knew, or reasonably should have known, of the information offered in support of the claim and the legal basis for the claim,\xe2\x80\x9d Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th, at 787, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 322. Petitioners in noncapital cases have \xe2\x80\x9cthe burden of establishing (i) absence of substantial delay, (ii) good cause for the delay, or (iii) that the claim falls within an exception to the bar of untimeliness.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 780, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 317.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0California\xe2\x80\x99s collateral review regime differs from that of other States in a second notable respect: All California courts \xe2\x80\x9chave original jurisdiction in habeas corpus proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d Cal. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0VI, \xc2\xa710, thus \xe2\x80\x9cno appeal lies from the denial of a petition for writ of habeas corpus,\xe2\x80\x9d Clark, 5 Cal. 4th, at 767, n.\xc2\xa07, 855 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 740, n. 7. \xe2\x80\x9c[A] prisoner whose petition has been denied by the superior court can obtain review of his claims only by the filing of a new petition in the Court of Appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The new petition, however, must be confined to claims raised in the initial petition. See In\xc2\xa0re Martinez, 46 Cal. 4th 945, 956, 209 P.\xc2\xa03d 908, 915 (2009).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because a habeas petitioner may skip over the lower courts and file directly in the California Supreme Court, In\xc2\xa0re Kler, 188 Cal. App. 4th 1399, 1403, 115 Cal. Rptr. 3d 889, 891\xe2\x80\x93892 (2010), that court rules on a staggering number of habeas petitions each year.[Footnote 2] The court issues generally unelaborated \xe2\x80\x9csummary denials\xe2\x80\x9d of petitions that \xe2\x80\x9cd[o] not state a prima facie case for relief\xe2\x80\x9d or that contain \xe2\x80\x9cclaims [that] are all procedurally barred.\xe2\x80\x9d People v. Romero, 8 Cal. 4th 728, 737, 883 P.\xc2\xa02d 388, 391 (1994) (internal quotation marks omitted). A summary denial citing Clark and Robbins means that the petition is rejected as untimely. See, e.g., Brief for Habeas Corpus Resource Center as Amicus Curiae 20, and n. 23. California courts have discretion, however, to bypass a timeliness issue and, instead, summarily reject the petition for want of merit. See Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th, at 778, n.\xc2\xa01, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 316, n.\xc2\xa01. See also Saffold, 536 U. S., at 225\xe2\x80\x93226.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In December 1986, Charles Martin participated in a robbery and murder in California. Martin fled the State, but eight years later he was extradited to California to stand trial. Convicted in state court of murder and robbery, Martin was sentenced to life in prison without the possibility of parole. In 1997, the California Court of Appeal affirmed his conviction and sentence, and the California Supreme Court denied review.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Martin initiated his first round of state habeas proceedings in 1998, and the next year, the California Supreme Court denied his petition. He then filed a habeas petition in the appropriate U. S. District Court. Finding that Martin\xe2\x80\x99s federal petition included ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claims he had not aired in state court, the District Court stayed the federal proceedings pending Martin\xe2\x80\x99s return to state court to exhaust his remedies there.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In March 2002, Martin filed his second habeas petition in the California Supreme Court, raising the federal ineffective assistance claims his earlier filing omitted. He gave no reason for his failure to assert the additional claims until nearly five years after his sentence and conviction became final. Tr. of Oral Arg. 36, 39. In September 2002, the California Supreme Court denied Martin\xe2\x80\x99s petition in an order typical of that court\xe2\x80\x99s summary dispositions for failure to file \xe2\x80\x9cas promptly as the circumstances allow.\xe2\x80\x9d Clark, 5 Cal. 4th, at 765, n.\xc2\xa05, 855 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 738, n.\xc2\xa05. The order read in its entirety: \xe2\x80\x9cPetition for writ of habeas corpus is DENIED. (See In\xc2\xa0re Clark (1993) 5 Cal. 4th 750, In\xc2\xa0re Robbins (1998) 18 Cal. 4th 770, 780.).\xe2\x80\x9d See App. to Pet. for Cert. 60.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Having exhausted state postconviction remedies, Martin returned to federal court and filed an amended petition. Based upon the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s time-bar disposition, the District Court dismissed Martin\xe2\x80\x99s belatedly asserted claims as procedurally precluded. Id., at 27, 57. The Ninth Circuit vacated the dismissal order and remanded the case, directing the District Court to determine the \xe2\x80\x9cadequacy\xe2\x80\x9d of the State\xe2\x80\x99s time bar. Martin v. Hubbard, 192 Fed. Appx. 616, 618 (2006). The District Court again rejected Martin\xe2\x80\x99s petition, stating that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he California timeliness bar as set forth in \xe2\x80\xa6 Clark/Robbins is clearly defined, well established and consistently applied.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 4.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit again disagreed. Controlled by its prior decision in Townsend, 562 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1207\xe2\x80\x931208, the Court of Appeals held that California\xe2\x80\x99s time bar \xe2\x80\x9chas yet to be firmly defined\xe2\x80\x9d and was not shown by the State to be \xe2\x80\x9cconsistently applied.\xe2\x80\x9d 357 Fed. Appx., at 794. The remand order directed the District Court to determine the merits of the claims Martin asserted in his second petition to the California Supreme Court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 561 U. S. ___ (2010), to determine the \xe2\x80\x9cadequacy\xe2\x80\x9d of California\xe2\x80\x99s practice under which a prisoner may be barred from collaterally attacking his conviction when he has \xe2\x80\x9csubstantially delayed\xe2\x80\x9d filing his habeas petition. Martin does not here dispute that the time limitation is an \xe2\x80\x9cindependent\xe2\x80\x9d state ground. See Brief in Opposition 5\xe2\x80\x936. See also Bennett v. Mueller, 322 F.\xc2\xa03d 573, 582\xe2\x80\x93583 (CA9 2003). Nor does he contend that he established \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprejudice,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., cause for the delay in asserting his claims and actual prejudice resulting from the State\xe2\x80\x99s alleged violation of his constitutional rights. See Wainwright v. Sykes, 433 U. S. 72, 87\xe2\x80\x9391 (1977).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cA federal habeas court will not review a claim rejected by a state court \xe2\x80\x98if the decision of [the state] court rests on a state law ground that is independent of the federal question and adequate to support the judgment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Kind-ler, 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 1) (quoting Coleman v. Thompson, 501 U. S. 722, 729 (1991)). The state-law ground may be a substantive rule dispositive of the case, or a procedural barrier to adjudication of the claim on the merits. See Sykes, 433 U. S., at 81\xe2\x80\x9382, 90.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ordinarily, a state prisoner seeking federal habeas relief must first \xe2\x80\x9cexhaus[t] the remedies available in the courts of the State,\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(b)(1)(A), thereby affording those courts \xe2\x80\x9cthe first opportunity to address and correct alleged violations of [the] prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s federal rights,\xe2\x80\x9d Coleman, 501 U. S., at 731. The adequate and independent state ground doctrine furthers that objective, for without it, \xe2\x80\x9chabeas petitioners would be able to avoid the exhaustion requirement by defaulting their federal claims in state court.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 732. Accordingly, absent showings of \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprejudice,\xe2\x80\x9d see Sykes, 433 U. S., at 84\xe2\x80\x9385, federal habeas relief will be unavailable when (1) \xe2\x80\x9ca state court [has] declined to address a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s federal claims because the prisoner had failed to meet a state procedural requirement,\xe2\x80\x9d and (2) \xe2\x80\x9cthe state judgment rests on independent and adequate state procedural grounds.\xe2\x80\x9d Coleman, 501 U. S., at 729\xe2\x80\x93730.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To qualify as an \xe2\x80\x9cadequate\xe2\x80\x9d procedural ground, a state rule must be \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly established and regularly followed.\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Kindler, 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 7) (internal quotation marks omitted).[Footnote 4] \xe2\x80\x9c[A] discretionary state procedural rule,\xe2\x80\x9d we held in Kindler, \xe2\x80\x9ccan serve as an adequate ground to bar federal habeas review.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. A \xe2\x80\x9crule can be \xe2\x80\x98firmly established\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98regularly followed,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Kindler observed, \xe2\x80\x9ceven if the appropriate exercise of discretion may permit consideration of a federal claim in some cases but not others.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0California\xe2\x80\x99s time rule, although discretionary, meets the \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly established\xe2\x80\x9d criterion, as Kindler comprehended that requirement. The California Supreme Court, as earlier noted, framed the timeliness requirement for habeas petitioners in a trilogy of cases. See supra, at 3. Those decisions instruct habeas petitioners to \xe2\x80\x9calleg[e] with specificity\xe2\x80\x9d the absence of substantial delay, good cause for delay, or eligibility for one of four exceptions to the time bar. Gallego, 18 Cal. 4th, at 838, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 299; see Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th, at 780, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 317.[Footnote 5] And California\xe2\x80\x99s case law made it altogether plain that Martin\xe2\x80\x99s delay of nearly five years ranked as \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial.\xe2\x80\x9d See Gallego, 18 Cal. 4th, at 829\xe2\x80\x93831, 838, and n. 13, 959 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 293\xe2\x80\x93294, 299, and n. 13 (delay of four years barred claim); In\xc2\xa0re Tsaturyan, No. B156012, 2002 WL 1614107, *3 (Cal. App., July 23, 2002) (delay of 16 months barred claim). See also In\xc2\xa0re Miller, No. B186447, 2006 WL 1980385, *2\xe2\x80\x933 (Cal. App., July 17, 2006) (delay of two years and six months barred claim).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Martin nevertheless urges that California\xe2\x80\x99s rule is too vague to be regarded as \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly established.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[R]eason-able time\xe2\x80\x9d period and \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial delay,\xe2\x80\x9d he maintains, are \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningless terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 48 (internal quotation marks omitted). We disagree. Indeterminate language is typical of discretionary rules. Application of those rules in particular circumstances, however, can supply the requisite clarity.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congressional statutes and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions, we note, have employed time limitations that are not stated in precise, numerical terms. Former Federal Habeas Corpus Rule 9(a), for example, set no fixed time limit on submission of habeas petitions. The Rule permitted dismissal of a state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s petition when it appeared that delay in commencing litigation \xe2\x80\x9cprejudiced [the State] in its ability to respond.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254 Rule 9(a) (1994 ed.). To stave off dismissal, the petitioner had to show that he could not earlier have known, \xe2\x80\x9cby the exercise of reasonable diligence,\xe2\x80\x9d the grounds on which he based the petition. Ibid. In Rhines v. Weber, 544 U. S. 269 (2005), we instructed district courts, when employing stay and abeyance procedure, see supra, at 5, n.\xc2\xa03, to \xe2\x80\x9cplace reasonable time limits on a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s trip to state court and back.\xe2\x80\x9d 544 U. S., at 278.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Current federal habeas prescriptions limit the time for filing a petition to one year. The clock runs from \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the [supporting] facts \xe2\x80\xa6 could have been discovered through the exercise of due diligence.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255(f)(4) (2006 ed., Supp. III) (applicable to federal prisoners); see \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D) (2006 ed.) (similar provision applicable to state prisoners). \xe2\x80\x9c[D]ue diligence,\xe2\x80\x9d we have observed, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0is an inexact measure of how much delay is too much.\xe2\x80\x9d Johnson v. United States, 544 U. S. 295, 309, n. 7 (2005) (internal quotation marks omitted). But \xe2\x80\x9cuse of an imprecise standard,\xe2\x80\x9d we immediately added, \xe2\x80\x9cis no justification for depriving [a rule\xe2\x80\x99s] language of any meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t would seem particularly strange to disregard state procedural rules that are substantially similar to those to which we give full force in our own courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Kind- ler, 558 U. S., at __ (slip op., at 8).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor is California\xe2\x80\x99s time rule vulnerable on the ground that it is not regularly followed. Each year, the California Supreme Court summarily denies hundreds of habeas petitions by citing Clark and Robbins. Brief for Appellant in No. 08\xe2\x80\x9315752 (CA9), pp. 31\xe2\x80\x9332. On the same day the court denied Martin\xe2\x80\x99s petition, it issued 21 other Clark/Robbins summary denials. See Brief for Habeas Corpus Resource Center as Amicus Curiae 20. In reasoned opinions, too, California courts regularly invoke Clark, Robbins, and Gallego to determine whether a habeas petition is time barred.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Martin argued below that California\xe2\x80\x99s time bar is not regularly followed in this sense: Use of summary denials makes it \xe2\x80\x9cimpossible to tell\xe2\x80\x9d why the California Supreme Court \xe2\x80\x9cdecides some delayed petitions on the merits and rejects others as untimely.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellant in No. 08\xe2\x80\x9315752 (CA9), pp. 37\xe2\x80\x9338. We see no reason to reject California\xe2\x80\x99s time bar simply because a court may opt to bypass the Clark/Robbins assessment and summarily dismiss a petition on the merits, if that is the easier path. See, e.g., Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668, 697 (1984) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] court need not determine whether counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance was deficient \xe2\x80\xa6 [i]f it is easier to dispose of an ineffectiveness claim on the ground of lack of sufficient prejudice \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Ruhrgas AG v. Marathon Oil Co., 526 U. S. 574, 585 (1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is hardly novel for a federal court to choose among threshold grounds for denying audience to a case on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit concluded that California\xe2\x80\x99s time bar is not consistently applied because outcomes under the rule vary from case to case. See 357 Fed. Appx., at 794. For example, in People v. Fairbanks, No. C047810, 2006 WL 950267, *2\xe2\x80\x93*3 (Cal. App., Apr. 11, 2006), a one-year delay was found substantial, while in In\xc2\xa0re Little, No. D047468, 2008 WL 142832, *4, n.\xc2\xa06 (Cal. App., Jan. 16, 2008), a delay of 14 months was determined to be\ninsubstantial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A discretionary rule ought not be disregarded automatically upon a showing of seeming inconsistencies.[Footnote 7] Discretion enables a court to home in on case-specific considerations and to avoid the harsh results that sometimes\nattend consistent application of an unyielding rule. See Prihoda v. McCaughtry, 910 F.\xc2\xa02d 1379, 1385 (CA7 1990) (\xe2\x80\x9cUncertainty is not enough to disqualify a state\xe2\x80\x99s\nprocedural ground as one \xe2\x80\x98adequate\xe2\x80\x99 under federal law. If it were, states would be induced to make their rules\ndraconian \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A state ground, no doubt, may be found inadequate when \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretion has been exercised to impose novel and unforeseeable requirements without fair or substantial support in prior state law \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 16B C.\xc2\xa0Wright, A. Miller, & E. Cooper, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa74026, p. 386 (2d ed. 1996) (hereinafter Wright & Miller); see Prihoda, 910 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1383 (state ground \xe2\x80\x9capplied infrequently, unexpectedly, or freakishly\xe2\x80\x9d may \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[e] against the federal rights asserted\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore rank as \xe2\x80\x9cinadequate\xe2\x80\x9d). Martin does not contend, however, that in his case, the California Supreme Court exercised its discretion in a surprising or unfair manner.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[S]ound procedure often requires discretion to exact or excuse compliance with strict rules,\xe2\x80\x9d 16B Wright & Miller \xc2\xa74028, p.\xc2\xa0403, and we have no cause to discourage standards allowing courts to exercise such discretion. As this Court observed in Kindler, if forced to choose between mandatory rules certain to be found \xe2\x80\x9cadequate,\xe2\x80\x9d or more supple prescriptions that federal courts may disregard as \xe2\x80\x9cinadequate,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cmany States [might] opt for mandatory rules to avoid the high costs that come with plenary federal review.\xe2\x80\x9d 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 7). \xe2\x80\x9cTh[at] result would be particularly unfortunate for [habeas petitioners], who would lose the opportunity to argue that a procedural default should be excused through the exercise of judicial discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 8).[Footnote 8]

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, trained on California\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness rule for habeas petitions, leaves unaltered this Court\xe2\x80\x99s repeated recognition that federal courts must carefully examine state procedural requirements to ensure that they do not operate to discriminate against claims of federal rights. See Brown v. Western R. Co. of Ala., 338 U. S. 294, 298\xe2\x80\x93299 (1949); Davis v. Wechsler, 263 U. S. 22, 24\xe2\x80\x9325 (1923); 16B Wright & Miller \xc2\xa74026, p.\xc2\xa0386 (noting \xe2\x80\x9crisk that discretionary procedural sanctions may be invoked more harshly against disfavored federal rights, \xe2\x80\xa6 deny[ing] [litigants] a fair opportunity to present federal claims\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Kindler, 558 U. S., at ___ (Kennedy, J., concurring) (slip op., at 3) (a state procedural ground would be inadequate if the challenger shows a \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose or pattern to evade constitutional guarantees\xe2\x80\x9d). On the record before us, however, there is no basis for concluding that California\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness rule operates to the particular disadvantage of petitioners asserting federal rights.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated, we find no inadequacy in California\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness rule generally or as applied in Martin\xe2\x80\x99s case. The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is therefore
