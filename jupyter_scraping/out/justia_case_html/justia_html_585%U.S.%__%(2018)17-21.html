SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

\n

Lozman v. City of Riviera Beach, Florida

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 17\xe2\x80\x9321.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 27, 2018\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 18, 2018

\n

After petitioner Lozman towed his floating home into a slip in a marina owned by the city of Riviera Beach, he became an outspoken critic of the City\xe2\x80\x99s plan to use its eminent domain power to seize waterfront homes for private development and often made critical comments about officials during the public-comment period of city council meetings. He also filed a lawsuit alleging that the City Council\xe2\x80\x99s approval of an agreement with developers violated Florida\xe2\x80\x99s open-meetings laws. In June 2006 the Council held a closed-door session, in part to discuss Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s lawsuit. He alleges that the meeting\xe2\x80\x99s transcript shows that councilmembers devised an official plan to intimidate him, and that many of his subsequent disputes with city officials and employees were part of the City\xe2\x80\x99s retaliation plan. Five months after the closed-door meeting, the Council held a public meeting. During the public-comment session, Lozman began to speak about the arrests of officials from other jurisdictions. When he refused a councilmember\xe2\x80\x99s request to stop making his remarks, the councilmember told the police officer in attendance to \xe2\x80\x9ccarry him out.\xe2\x80\x9d The officer handcuffed Lozman and ushered him out of the meeting. The City contends that he was arrested for violating the City Council\xe2\x80\x99s rules of procedure by discussing issues unrelated to the City and then refusing to leave the podium. Lozman claims that his arrest was to retaliate for his lawsuit and his prior public criticisms of city officials. The State\xe2\x80\x99s attorney determined that there was probable cause for his arrest, but decided to dismiss the charges.

\n

Lozman then filed suit under\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, alleging a number of incidents that, under his theory, showed the City\xe2\x80\x99s purpose was to harass him, including by initiating an admiralty lawsuit against his floating home, see Lozman v. Riviera Beach,\n\n568 U.\xc2\xa0S. 115. The jury returned a verdict for the City on all of the claims. The District Court instructed the jury that, for Lozman to prevail on his claim of a retaliatory arrest at the city council meeting, he had to prove that the arresting officer was motivated by impermissible animus against Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s protected speech and that the officer lacked probable cause to make the arrest. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed, concluding that any error the District Court made when it instructed the jury to consider the officer\xe2\x80\x99s retaliatory animus was harmless because the jury necessarily determined that the arrest was supported by probable cause when it found for the City on Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s other claims. The existence of probable cause, the court ruled, defeated a\n \nFirst Amendment claim for retaliatory arrest.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The existence of probable cause does not bar Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFirst Amendment retaliation claim under the circumstances of this case. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The issue here is narrow. Lozman concedes that there was probable cause for his arrest. Nonetheless, he claims, the arrest violated the\n \nFirst Amendment because it was ordered in retaliation for his earlier, protected speech: his open-meetings lawsuit and his prior public criticisms of city officials. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0In a \xc2\xa71983 case, a city or other local governmental entity cannot be subject to liability unless the harm was caused in the implementation of \xe2\x80\x9cofficial municipal policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Monell v. New York City Dept. of Social Servs.,\n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 658, 691. The Court assumes that Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s arrest was taken pursuant to an official city policy.

\n

Two major precedents bear on the issue whether the conceded existence of probable cause for the arrest bars recovery regardless of any intent or purpose to retaliate for past speech. Lozman argues that the controlling rule is found in Mt. Healthy City Bd. of Ed. v. Doyle,\n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 274, a civil case in which a city board of education decided not to rehire an untenured teacher after a series of incidents, including a telephone call to a local radio station. The phone call was protected speech, but, the Court held, there was no liability unless the alleged constitutional violation was a but-for cause of the employment termination. Id., at 285\xe2\x80\x93287. The City counters that the applicable precedent is Hartman v. Moore,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 250, where the Court held that a plaintiff alleging a retaliatory prosecution must show the absence of probable cause for the underlying criminal charge, id., at 265\xe2\x80\x93266. If there was probable cause, the case ends. If the plaintiff proves the absence of probable cause, then the Mt. Healthy test governs. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Whether Hartman or Mt. Healthy governs here is a determination that must await a different case. For Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s claim is far afield from the typical retaliatory arrest claim, and the difficulties that might arise if Mt. Healthy is applied to the mine run of arrests made by police officers are not present here. Lozman alleges that the City itself retaliated against him pursuant to an \xe2\x80\x9cofficial municipal policy\xe2\x80\x9d of intimidation. Monell, supra, at 691. The fact that he must prove the existence and enforcement of an official policy motivated by retaliation separates his claim from the typical retaliatory arrest claim. An official retaliatory policy can be long term and pervasive, unlike an ad\xc2\xa0hoc, on-the-spot decision by an individual officer. And it can be difficult to dislodge. A citizen can seek to have an individual officer disciplined or removed from service, but there may be little practical recourse when the government itself orchestrates the retaliation. Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s allegations, if proved, also alleviate the problems that the City says will result from applying Mt. Healthy in retaliatory arrest cases, for it is unlikely that the connection between the alleged animus and injury in a case like this will be \xe2\x80\x9cweakened .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by [an official\xe2\x80\x99s] legitimate consideration of speech,\xe2\x80\x9d Reichle v. Howards,\n\n566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 658, 668, and there is little risk of a flood of retaliatory arrest suits against high-level policymakers. Because Lozman alleges that the City deprived him of the right to petition, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98one of the most precious of the liberties safeguarded by the Bill of Rights,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d BE&K Constr. Co. v. NLRB,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 516, 524, his speech is high in the hierarchy of\n \nFirst Amendment values. On these facts, Mt. Healthy provides the correct standard for assessing a retaliatory arrest claim. On remand, the Eleventh Circuit may consider any arguments in support of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment that have been preserved by the City, including whether a reasonable juror could find that the City formed a retaliatory policy to intimidate Lozman during its closed-door session, whether a reasonable juror could find that the arrest constituted an official act by the City, and whether, under Mt. Healthy, the City has proved that it would have arrested Lozman regardless of any retaliatory animus. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

681 Fed. Appx. 746, vacated and remanded.

\n

FANE LOZMAN, PETITIONER v. CITY OF RIVIERA BEACH, FLORIDA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[June 18, 2018]

\n

\n

Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case requires the Court to address the intersection of principles that define when arrests are lawful and principles that prohibit the government from retaliating against a person for having exercised the right to free speech. An arrest deprives a person of essential liberties, but if there is probable cause to believe the person has committed a criminal offense there is often no recourse for the deprivation. See, e.g., Devenpeck v. Alford,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 146, 153 (2004). At the same time, the\n \nFirst Amendment prohibits government officials from retaliating against individuals for engaging in protected speech. Crawford-El v. Britton,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 574, 592 (1998).

\n

The petitioner in this case alleges that high-level city policymakers adopted a plan to retaliate against him for protected speech and then ordered his arrest when he attempted to make remarks during the public-comment portion of a city council meeting. The petitioner now concedes there was probable cause for the arrest. The question is whether the presence of probable cause bars petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s retaliatory arrest claim under these circumstances.

\n

I

\n

The city of Riviera Beach is on the Atlantic coast of Florida, about 75 miles north of Miami. The petitioner here is Fane Lozman. In 2006 Lozman towed his floating home into a slip in the City-owned marina, where he became a resident. Thus began his contentious relationship with the City\xe2\x80\x99s elected officials.

\n

Soon after his arrival Lozman became an outspoken critic of the City\xe2\x80\x99s plan to use its eminent domain power to seize homes along the waterfront for private development. Lozman often spoke during the public-comment period at city council meetings and criticized councilmembers, the mayor, and other public employees. He also filed a lawsuit alleging that the Council\xe2\x80\x99s approval of an agreement with developers violated Florida\xe2\x80\x99s open-meetings laws.

\n

In June 2006 the Council held a closed-door session, in part to discuss the open-meetings lawsuit that Lozman recently had filed. According to the transcript of the meeting, Councilmember Elizabeth Wade suggested that the City use its resources to \xe2\x80\x9cintimidate\xe2\x80\x9d Lozman and others who had filed lawsuits against the City. App. 176. Later in the meeting a different councilmember asked whether there was \xe2\x80\x9ca consensus of what Ms. Wade is saying,\xe2\x80\x9d and others responded in the affirmative. Id., at 181\xe2\x80\x93182. Lozman alleges that these remarks formed an official plan to intimidate him. The City, on the other hand, maintains that the only consensus reached during the meeting was to invest the money and resources necessary to prevail in the litigation against it.

\n

In all events, Lozman became embroiled in a number of disputes with city officials and employees over the ensuing years, many of which Lozman says were part of the City\xe2\x80\x99s plan of retaliation. The dispute that led to this litigation took place in 2006. In November of that year, five months after the closed-door meeting where the \xe2\x80\x9cintimidate\xe2\x80\x9d com- ment was made, the City Council held a public meeting. The agenda included a public-comment session in which citizens could address the Council for a few minutes. As he had done on earlier occasions and would do more than 200 times over the coming years, see Tr. in No. 9:08\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9380134 (SD Fla.), Doc. 785, p.\xc2\xa061, Lozman stepped up to the podium to give remarks. He began to discuss the recent arrest of a former county official. Councilmember Wade interrupted Lozman, directing him to stop making those remarks. Lozman continued speaking, this time about the arrest of a former official from the city of West Palm Beach. Wade then called for the assistance of the police officer in attendance. The officer approached Lozman and asked him to leave the podium. Lozman refused. So Wade told the officer to \xe2\x80\x9ccarry him out.\xe2\x80\x9d The officer handcuffed Lozman and ushered him out of the meeting. The incident was recorded on video. See Record, Def. Exh. 505, Doc. 687, available at https://www.supremecourt.gov/media/ video/mp4files/Lozman_v_RivieraBeach.mp4. According to the City, Lozman was arrested because he violated the City Council\xe2\x80\x99s rules of procedure by discussing issues unrelated to the City and then refused to leave the po- dium. According to Lozman, the arrest was to retaliate for his open-meetings lawsuit against the City and his prior public criticisms of city officials.

\n

Under arrest, Lozman was escorted to police headquarters. He was charged with disorderly conduct and resisting arrest without violence and then released. Later, the State\xe2\x80\x99s attorney determined there was probable cause to arrest Lozman for those offenses but decided to dismiss the charges.

\n

Lozman filed this lawsuit under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. The complaint described a number of alleged incidents that, under Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s theory, showed the City\xe2\x80\x99s purpose to harass him in different ways. These ranged from a city employee telling Lozman that his dog needed a muzzle to the City\xe2\x80\x99s initiation of an admiralty lawsuit against Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s floating home\xe2\x80\x94the latter resulting in an earlier decision by this Court. See Lozman v. Riviera Beach,\n\n568 U.\xc2\xa0S. 115 (2013). The evidence and arguments presented by both parties with respect to all the matters alleged in Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s suit consumed 19 days of trial before a jury. The jury returned a verdict for the City on all of the claims.

\n

Before this Court, Lozman seeks a reversal only as to the City\xe2\x80\x99s alleged retaliatory arrest at the November 2006 city council meeting. The District Court instructed the jury that, for Lozman to prevail on this claim, he had to prove that the arresting officer was himself motivated by impermissible animus against Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s protected speech and that the officer lacked probable cause to make the arrest. The District Court determined that the evidence was insufficient as a matter of law to support probable cause for the offenses charged at the time of the arrest (disorderly conduct and resisting arrest without violence). But the District Court concluded that there may have been probable cause to arrest Lozman for violating a Florida statute that prohibits interruptions or disturbances in schools, churches, or other public assemblies. Fla. Stat. \xc2\xa7871.01 (2017). (The City had brought this statute to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s attention during the course of the litigation.) The District Court allowed the jury to decide whether there was probable cause to arrest for the public-disturbance offense.

\n

Judgment having been entered for the City after the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict, Lozman appealed. The Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed. 681 Fed. Appx. 746 (2017). As relevant here, the Court of Appeals assumed that the District Court erred when it instructed the jury that the officer, rather than the City, must have harbored the retaliatory animus. But the Court of Appeals held that any error was harmless because the jury necessarily determined that the arrest was supported by probable cause when it found for the City on some of Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s other claims\xe2\x80\x94specifically, his claims that the arrest violated the\n \nFourth Amendment and state law. Id., at 751\xe2\x80\x93752. And, under precedents which the Court of Appeals deemed controlling, the existence of probable cause defeated a\n \nFirst Amendment claim for retaliatory arrest. See id., at 752 (citing Dahl v. Holley, 312 F.\xc2\xa03d 1228, 1236 (CA11 2002)).

\n

This Court granted certiorari, 583 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017), on the issue whether the existence of probable cause defeats a\n \nFirst Amendment claim for retaliatory arrest under \xc2\xa71983. The Court considered this issue once before, see Reichle v. Howards,\n\n566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 658, 663 (2012), but resolved the case on different grounds.

\n

II

\n

The issue before the Court is a narrow one. In this Court Lozman does not challenge the constitutionality of Florida\xe2\x80\x99s statute criminalizing disturbances at public assemblies. He does not argue that the statute is overly broad, e.g., Terminiello v. Chicago,\n\n337 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1949); Watchtower Bible & Tract Soc. of N.\xc2\xa0Y., Inc. v. Village of Stratton,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 150 (2002); or that it impermissibly targets speech based on its content or viewpoint, e.g., Texas v. Johnson,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 397 (1989); Cohen v. California,\n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 15 (1971); or that it was enforced in a way that curtailed Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s right to peaceful assembly, e.g., Brown v. Louisiana,\n\n383 U.\xc2\xa0S. 131 (1966). Lozman, furthermore, does not challenge the validity of the City Council\xe2\x80\x99s asserted limitations on the subjects speakers may discuss during the public-comment portion of city council meetings (although he continues to dispute whether those limitations in fact existed).

\n

Instead Lozman challenges only the lawfulness of his arrest, and even that challenge is a limited one. There is no contention that the City ordered Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s arrest to discriminate against him based on protected classifications, or that the City denied Lozman his equal protection rights by placing him in a \xe2\x80\x9cclass of one.\xe2\x80\x9d See Village of Willowbrook v. Olech,\n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 562 (2000) (per curiam). Lozman, moreover, now concedes that there was probable cause for the arrest. Although Lozman does not indicate what facts he believes support this concession, it appears that the existence of probable cause must be based on the assumption that Lozman failed to depart the podium after receiving a lawful order to leave.

\n

Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s claim is that, notwithstanding the presence of probable cause, his arrest at the city council meeting violated the\n \nFirst Amendment because the arrest was ordered in retaliation for his earlier, protected speech: his open-meetings lawsuit and his prior public criticisms of city officials. The question this Court is asked to consider is whether the existence of probable cause bars that\n \nFirst Amendment retaliation claim.

\n

III

\n

It is well established that in a \xc2\xa71983 case a city or other local governmental entity cannot be subject to liability at all unless the harm was caused in the implementation of \xe2\x80\x9cofficial municipal policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Monell v. New York City Dept. of Social Servs.,\n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 658, 691 (1978); see Los Angeles County v. Humphries,\n\n562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29, 36 (2010). Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa71983 damages claim is against only the City itself, based on the acts of its officers and employees\xe2\x80\x94here, the members of the City Council. Lozman says that the City, through its city councilmembers, formed an official policy to retaliate against him and ordered his arrest. The Court assumes in the discussion to follow that the arrest was taken pursuant to an official city policy, but whether there was such a policy and what its content may have been are issues not decided here.

\n

This brings the discussion to the issue the parties deem central to the case: whether the conceded existence of probable cause for the arrest bars recovery regardless of any intent or purpose to retaliate for past speech. Two major precedents could bear on this point, and the parties disagree on which should be applicable here. The first is this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Mt. Healthy City Bd. of Ed. v. Doyle,\n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 274 (1977). See also Board of Comm\xe2\x80\x99rs, Wabaunsee Cty. v. Umbehr,\n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668 (1996). Lozman urges that the rule of Mt. Healthy should control and that under it he is entitled to recover. The second is this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Hartman v. Moore,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 250 (2006), which the City cites for the proposition that once there is probable cause there can be no further claim that the arrest was retaliation for protected speech.

\n

Mt. Healthy arose in a civil, not criminal, context. A city board of education decided not to rehire an untenured school teacher after a series of incidents indicating unprofessional demeanor. 429 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 281\xe2\x80\x93283. One of the incidents was a telephone call the teacher made to a local radio station to report on a new school policy. Id., at 282. Because the board of education did not suggest that the teacher violated any established policy in making the call, this Court accepted a finding by the District Court that the call was protected speech. Id., at 284. The Court went on to hold, however, that since the other incidents, standing alone, would have justified the dismissal, relief could not be granted if the board could show that the discharge would have been ordered even without reference to the protected speech. Id., at 285\xe2\x80\x93287. In terms of precepts in the law of torts, the Court held that even if retaliation might have been a substantial motive for the board\xe2\x80\x99s action, still there was no liability unless the alleged constitutional violation was a but-for cause of the employment termination. Ibid.; see also Umbehr, supra, at 675.

\n

The City resists the applicability of the Mt. Healthy test as the sole determinant here. It contends that, where there was probable cause for the arrest, the applicable precedent is Hartman\xe2\x80\x94a case that was in the criminal sphere and that turned on the existence of probable cause.

\n

The background in Hartman was that a company and its chief executive, William Moore, had engaged in an extensive lobbying and governmental relations campaign opposing a particular postal service policy. 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 252\xe2\x80\x93253. Moore and the company were later prosecuted for violating federal statutes in the course of that lobbying. Id., at 253\xe2\x80\x93254. After being acquitted, Moore filed suit against five postal inspectors, alleging that they had violated his\n \nFirst Amendment rights when they instigated his prosecution in retaliation for his criticisms of the Postal Service. Id., at 254. This Court held that a plaintiff alleging a retaliatory prosecution must show the absence of probable cause for the underlying criminal charge. Id., at 265\xe2\x80\x93266. If there was probable cause, the case ends. If the plaintiff proves the absence of probable cause, then the Mt. Healthy test governs: The plaintiff must show that the retaliation was a substantial or motivating factor behind the prosecution, and, if that showing is made, the defendant can prevail only by showing that the prosecution would have been initiated without respect to retaliation. See 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 265\xe2\x80\x93266.

\n

The Court in Hartman deemed it necessary to inquire as to the existence of probable cause because proving the link between the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s retaliatory animus and the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury in retaliatory prosecution cases \xe2\x80\x9cis usually more complex than it is in other retaliation cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 261. An action for retaliatory prosecution \xe2\x80\x9cwill not be brought against the prosecutor, who is absolutely immune from liability for the decision to prosecute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 261\xe2\x80\x93262. Instead, the plaintiff must sue some other government official and prove that the official \xe2\x80\x9cinduced the prosecutor to bring charges that would not have been initiated without his urging.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 262. Noting that inquiries with respect to probable cause are commonplace in criminal cases, the Court determined that requiring plaintiffs in retaliatory prosecution cases to prove the lack of probable cause would help \xe2\x80\x9cbridge the gap between the nonprosecuting government agent\xe2\x80\x99s motive and the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 263.

\n

The City\xe2\x80\x99s argument here is that, just as probable cause is a bar in retaliatory prosecution cases, so too should it be a bar in this case, involving a retaliatory arrest. There is undoubted force in the City\xe2\x80\x99s position. Reichle, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 667\xe2\x80\x93668. There are on average about 29,000 arrests per day in this country. Dept. of Justice\xe2\x80\x93FBI, Uniform Crime Report, Crime in the United States, 2016 (Fall 2017). In deciding whether to arrest, police officers often make split-second judgments. The content of the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s speech might be a consideration in circumstances where the officer must decide whether the suspect is ready to cooperate, or, on the other hand, whether he may present a continuing threat to interests that the law must protect. See, e.g., District of Columbia v. Wesby, 583 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2018) (slip op., at 10) (\xe2\x80\x9csuspect\xe2\x80\x99s untruthful and evasive answers to police questioning could support probable cause\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

For these reasons retaliatory arrest claims, much like retaliatory prosecution claims, can \xe2\x80\x9cpresent a tenuous causal connection between the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s alleged animus and the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Reichle, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 668. That means it can be difficult to discern whether an arrest was caused by the officer\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate or illegitimate consideration of speech. Ibid. And the complexity of proving (or disproving) causation in these cases creates a risk that the courts will be flooded with dubious retaliatory arrest suits. See Brief for District of Columbia et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 5\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

At the same time, there are substantial arguments that Hartman\xe2\x80\x99s framework is inapt in retaliatory arrest cases, and that Mt. Healthy should apply without a threshold inquiry into probable cause. For one thing, the causation problem in retaliatory arrest cases is not the same as the problem identified in Hartman. Hartman relied in part on the fact that, in retaliatory prosecution cases, the causal connection between the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s animus and the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s decision to prosecute is weakened by the \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption of regularity accorded to prosecutorial decisionmaking.\xe2\x80\x9d 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 263. That presumption does not apply in this context. See Reichle, supra, at 669. In addition, there is a risk that some police officers may exploit the arrest power as a means of suppressing speech. See Brief for Institute for Free Speech as Amicus Curiae.

\n

IV

\n

The parties\xe2\x80\x99 arguments raise difficult questions about the scope of\n \nFirst Amendment protections when speech is made in connection with, or contemporaneously to, criminal activity. But whether in a retaliatory arrest case the Hartman approach should apply, thus barring a suit where probable cause exists, or, on the other hand, the inquiry should be governed only by Mt. Healthy is a determination that must await a different case. For Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s claim is far afield from the typical retaliatory arrest claim, and the difficulties that might arise if Mt. Healthy is applied to the mine run of arrests made by police offi- cers are not present here.

\n

Here Lozman does not sue the officer who made the arrest. Indeed, Lozman likely could not have maintained a retaliation claim against the arresting officer in these circumstances, because the officer appears to have acted in good faith, and there is no showing that the officer had any knowledge of Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s prior speech or any motive to arrest him for his earlier expressive activities.

\n

Instead Lozman alleges more governmental action than simply an arrest. His claim is that the City itself retali- ated against him pursuant to an \xe2\x80\x9cofficial municipal policy\xe2\x80\x9d of intimidation. Monell, 436 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 691. In particular, he alleges that the City, through its legislators, formed a premeditated plan to intimidate him in retaliation for his criticisms of city officials and his open-meetings lawsuit. And he asserts that the City itself, through the same high officers, executed that plan by ordering his arrest at the November 2006 city council meeting.

\n

The fact that Lozman must prove the existence and enforcement of an official policy motivated by retaliation separates Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s claim from the typical retaliatory arrest claim. An official retaliatory policy is a particularly troubling and potent form of retaliation, for a policy can be long term and pervasive, unlike an ad\xc2\xa0hoc, on-the-spot decision by an individual officer. An official policy also can be difficult to dislodge. A citizen who suffers retaliation by an individual officer can seek to have the officer disciplined or removed from service, but there may be little practical recourse when the government itself orchestrates the retaliation. For these reasons, when retaliation against protected speech is elevated to the level of official policy, there is a compelling need for adequate avenues of redress.

\n

In addition, Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s allegations, if proved, alleviate the problems that the City says will result from applying Mt. Healthy in retaliatory arrest cases. The causation problem in arrest cases is not of the same difficulty where, as is alleged here, the official policy is retaliation for prior, protected speech bearing little relation to the criminal offense for which the arrest is made. In determining whether there was probable cause to arrest Lozman for disrupting a public assembly, it is difficult to see why a city official could have legitimately considered that Lozman had, months earlier, criticized city officials or filed a lawsuit against the City. So in a case like this one it is unlikely that the connection between the alleged animus and injury will be \xe2\x80\x9cweakened .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by [an official\xe2\x80\x99s] legitimate consideration of speech.\xe2\x80\x9d Reichle, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 668. This unique class of retaliatory arrest claims, moreover, will require objective evidence of a policy motivated by retaliation to survive summary judgment. Lozman, for instance, cites a transcript of a closed-door city council meeting and a video recording of his arrest. There is thus little risk of a flood of retaliatory arrest suits against high-level policymakers.

\n

As a final matter, it must be underscored that this Court has recognized the \xe2\x80\x9cright to petition as one of the most precious of the liberties safeguarded by the Bill of Rights.\xe2\x80\x9d BE&K Constr. Co. v. NLRB,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 516, 524 (2002) (internal quotation marks omitted). Lozman alleges the City deprived him of this liberty by retaliating against him for his lawsuit against the City and his criticisms of public officials. Thus, Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s speech is high in the hierarchy of\n \nFirst Amendment values. See Connick v. Myers,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 138, 145 (1983).

\n

For these reasons, Lozman need not prove the absence of probable cause to maintain a claim of retaliatory arrest against the City. On facts like these, Mt. Healthy provides the correct standard for assessing a retaliatory arrest claim. The Court need not, and does not, address the elements required to prove a retaliatory arrest claim in other contexts.

\n

This is not to say, of course, that Lozman is ultimately entitled to relief or even a new trial. On remand, the Court of Appeals, applying Mt. Healthy and other relevant precedents, may consider any arguments in support of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment that have been preserved by the City. Among other matters, the Court of Appeals may wish to consider (1) whether any reasonable juror could find that the City actually formed a retaliatory policy to intimidate Lozman during its June 2006 closed-door session; (2) whether any reasonable juror could find that the November 2006 arrest constituted an official act by the City; and (3) whether, under Mt. Healthy, the City has proved that it would have arrested Lozman regardless of any retaliatory animus\xe2\x80\x94for example, if Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s conduct during prior city council meetings had also violated valid rules as to proper subjects of discussion, thus explaining his arrest here.

\n

For these reasons, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

FANE LOZMAN, PETITIONER v. CITY OF RIVIERA BEACH, FLORIDA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[June 18, 2018]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, dissenting.

\n

We granted certiorari to decide \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the existence of probable cause defeats a\n \nFirst Amendment claim for retaliatory arrest under [42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C.] \xc2\xa71983.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. Instead of resolving that question, the Court decides that probable cause should not defeat a \xe2\x80\x9cunique class of retaliatory arrest claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. To fall within this unique class, a claim must involve objective evidence, of an official municipal policy of retaliation, formed well before the arrest, in response to highly protected speech, that has little relation to the offense of arrest. See ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. No one briefed, argued, or even hinted at the rule that the Court announces today. Instead of dreaming up our own rule, I would have answered the question presented and held that plaintiffs must plead and prove a lack of prob- able cause as an element of a\n \nFirst Amendment retaliatory-arrest claim. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

The petition for certiorari asked us to resolve whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe existence of probable cause defeat[s] a\n \nFirst Amendment retaliatory-arrest claim as a matter of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i. That question has divided the federal courts for decades. See id., at 10\xe2\x80\x9313. We granted certiorari to consider it six years ago in Reichle v. Howards,\n\n566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 658, 663 (2012). But we did not resolve it then because the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s second question presented\xe2\x80\x94whether qualified immunity applied\xe2\x80\x94fully resolved the case. Ibid. Since Reichle, the split in the federal courts has widened. See Pet. for Cert. 12\xe2\x80\x9313. In this case, we again granted certiorari, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017), this time only on the question of probable cause, see Pet. for Cert. i.

\n

Yet the Court chooses not to resolve that question, leaving in place the decades-long disagreement among the federal courts. The parties concentrated all their arguments on this question in their briefs and at oral argument. Neither party suggested that there was something special about Fane Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s claim that would justify a narrower rule. See, e.g., Tr. of Oral Arg. 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (refusing to take the \xe2\x80\x9cfallback position\xe2\x80\x9d that this \xe2\x80\x9cis some special kind of case\xe2\x80\x9d). Yet the Court does that work for them by defining a \xe2\x80\x9cunique class of retaliatory arrest claims\xe2\x80\x9d that do not require plaintiffs to plead and prove a lack of probable cause. Ante, at 12.

\n

By my count, the Court has identified five conditions that are necessary to trigger its new rule. First, there must be \xe2\x80\x9can \xe2\x80\x98official municipal policy\xe2\x80\x99 of intimidation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11 (quoting Monell v. New York City Dept. of Social Servs.,\n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 658, 691 (1978)). Second, the policy must be \xe2\x80\x9cpremeditated\xe2\x80\x9d and formed well before the arrest\xe2\x80\x94here, for example, the policy was formed \xe2\x80\x9cmonths earlier.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11.[1] Third, there must be \xe2\x80\x9cobjective evidence\xe2\x80\x9d of such a policy. Ante, at 12. Fourth, there must be \xe2\x80\x9clittle relation\xe2\x80\x9d between the \xe2\x80\x9cprotected speech\xe2\x80\x9d that prompted the retaliatory policy and \xe2\x80\x9cthe criminal offense for which the arrest is made.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. Finally, the protected speech that provoked the retaliatory policy must be \xe2\x80\x9chigh in the hierarchy of\n \nFirst Amendment values.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. Where all these features are present, the Court explains, there is not the same \xe2\x80\x9ccausation problem\xe2\x80\x9d that exists for other retaliatory-arrest claims. Ante, at 11.

\n

I find it hard to believe that there will be many cases where this rule will even arguably apply, and even harder to believe that the plaintiffs in those cases will actually prove all five requirements. Not even Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s case is a good fit, as the Court admits when it discusses the relevant considerations for remand. See ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. In my view, we should not have gone out of our way to fashion a complicated rule with no apparent applicability to this case or any other.

\n

II

\n

Turning to the question presented, I would hold that plaintiffs bringing a\n \nFirst Amendment retaliatory-arrest claim must plead and prove an absence of probable cause.[2] This Court has \xe2\x80\x9crepeatedly noted that\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 creates \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9ca species of tort liability.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Memphis Commu- nity School Dist. v. Stachura,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 299, 305 (1986) (footnote omitted). Accordingly, we \xe2\x80\x9cdefin[e] the contours and prerequisites of a \xc2\xa71983 claim\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9clook[ing] first to the common law of torts.\xe2\x80\x9d Manuel v. Joliet, 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2017) (slip op., at 12); see, e.g., Heck v. Humphrey,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477, 484 (1994) (analogizing to the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-law cause of action for malicious prosecution\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 491 (Thomas, J., concurring) (emphasizing that the decision was \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with the state of the common law at the time \xc2\xa71983 was enacted\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

When \xc2\xa71983 was enacted, there was no common-law tort for retaliatory arrest in violation of the freedom of speech. See Hartman v. Moore,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 250, 259 (2006). I would therefore look to the common-law torts that \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[e] the closest analogy\xe2\x80\x9d to this claim. Heck, supra, at 484. The closest analogs here are the three arrest-based torts under the common law: false imprisonment, malicious prosecution, and malicious arrest. In defining the elements of these three torts, 19th-century courts emphasized the importance of probable cause.

\n

Consider first the tort of false imprisonment. Common-law courts stressed the need to shape this tort with an \xe2\x80\x9cindulgence\xe2\x80\x9d for peace officers, who are \xe2\x80\x9cspecially charged with a duty in the enforcement of the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d T. Cooley, Law of Torts 175 (1880) (Cooley); see, e.g., Hogg v. Ward, 3 H.\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0N. 417, 423, 157 Eng. Rep. 533, 536 (Ex. 1858) (opinion of Watson, B.) (stressing \xe2\x80\x9cthe utmost importance that the police throughout the country should be supported in the execution of their duty\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, private citizens were always liable for false imprisonment if the arrestee had not actually committed a felony, but constables were \xe2\x80\x9cexcused\xe2\x80\x9d if they had \xe2\x80\x9cmade [the arrest] on reasonable grounds of belief\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., probable cause. Cooley 175; accord, 2 C. Addison, Law of Torts \xc2\xa7803, p. 18 (1876); 1 F. Hilliard, The Law of Torts or Private Wrongs \xc2\xa718, pp.\xc2\xa0207\xe2\x80\x93208, and n.\xc2\xa0(a) (1866). As Lord Mansfield explained, it was \xe2\x80\x9cof great consequence to the police\xe2\x80\x9d that probable cause shield officers from false-imprisonment claims, as \xe2\x80\x9cit would be a terrible thing\xe2\x80\x9d if the threat of liability dissuaded them from performing their official duties. Ledwith v. Catchpole, 2 Cald. 291, 295 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1783). This concern outweighed \xe2\x80\x9cthe mischief and inconvenience to the public\xe2\x80\x9d from the reality that \xe2\x80\x9c[m]any an innocent man has and may be taken up upon suspicion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Many State Supreme Courts agreed with Lord Mansfield\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning. See, e.g., Burns v. Erben, 40 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 463, 469 (1869) (opinion of Woodruff, J.) (quoting Ledwith); Brockway v. Crawford, 48 N.\xc2\xa0C. 433, 437 (1856) (\xe2\x80\x9c[The] exempt[ion] for responsibility\xe2\x80\x9d for arrests based on probable cause \xe2\x80\x9cencourages .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a sharp look-out for the apprehension of felons\xe2\x80\x9d). As one court put it, \xe2\x80\x9cHow, in the great cities of this land, could police power be exercised, if every peace officer is liable to civil action for false imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d whenever \xe2\x80\x9cpersons arrested upon probable cause shall afterwards be found innocent?\xe2\x80\x9d Hawley v. Butler, 54 Barb. 490, 496 (N.\xc2\xa0Y. Sup. 1868).

\n

Courts also stressed the importance of probable cause when defining the torts of malicious prosecution and malicious arrest. See, e.g., Ahern v. Collins, 39 Mo. 145, 150 (1866) (holding that \xe2\x80\x9cmalice and want of probable cause are necessary ingredients of both\xe2\x80\x9d). For the tort of malicious prosecution, courts emphasized the \xe2\x80\x9cnecessity\xe2\x80\x9d of both the \xe2\x80\x9callegation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cproof\xe2\x80\x9d of probable cause, in light of the public interest \xe2\x80\x9cthat criminals should be brought to justice.\xe2\x80\x9d Hogg v. Pinckney, 16 S.\xc2\xa0C. 387, 393 (1882); see also Chrisman v. Carney, 33 Ark. 316, 326 (1878) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe existence of probable cause is of itself alone a complete defense\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. The interest which society has in the enforcement of the criminal laws requires this rule\xe2\x80\x9d). Similarly, if the element of probable cause were not \xe2\x80\x9cstrictly guarded,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cill consequences would ensue to the public, for no one would willingly undertake to vindicate a breach of the public law and discharge his duty to society, with the prospect of an annoying suit staring him in the face.\xe2\x80\x9d Ventress v. Rosser, 73 Ga. 534, 541 (1884); accord, Cardival v. Smith, 109 Mass. 158 (1872). The element of probable cause also played an evidentiary role for both torts. Lack of probable cause provided \xe2\x80\x9cevidence of malice, though inconclusive,\xe2\x80\x9d Herman v. Brookerhoff, 8 Watts 240, 241 (Pa. 1839), because \xe2\x80\x9c[m]alice may be inferred from a total want of probable cause,\xe2\x80\x9d Ventress, supra, at 541; accord, Ahern, supra, at 150.

\n

In sum, when \xc2\xa71983 was enacted, the common law recognized probable cause as an important element for ensuring that arrest-based torts did not unduly interfere with the objectives of law enforcement. Common-law courts were wary of \xe2\x80\x9cthrow[ing] down the bars which protect public officers from suits for acts done within the scope of their duty and authority, by recognizing the right of every one who chooses to imagine or assert that he is aggrieved by their doings, to make use of an allegation that they were malicious in motive to harass them with suits on that ground.\xe2\x80\x9d Chelsey v. King, 74 Me. 164, 175\xe2\x80\x93176 (1882).

\n

Applying that principle here, it follows that plaintiffs bringing a\n \nFirst Amendment retaliatory-arrest claim under \xc2\xa71983 should have to plead and prove a lack of probable cause. I see no justification for deviating from the historical practice simply because an arrest claim is framed in terms of the\n \nFirst Amendment. Even under a\n \nFirst Amendment theory, \xe2\x80\x9cthe significance of probable cause or the lack of it looms large.\xe2\x80\x9d Hartman, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 265. The presence of probable cause will tend to disprove that the arrest was done out of retaliation for the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s speech, and the absence of probable cause will tend to prove the opposite. See id., at 261. Because \xe2\x80\x9c[p]robable cause or its absence will be at least an evidentiary issue in practically all such cases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause showing [its] absence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. will have high probative force, and can be made mandatory with little or no added cost,\xe2\x80\x9d the absence of probable cause should be an \xe2\x80\x9celement\xe2\x80\x9d of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s case. Id., at 265\xe2\x80\x93266; see also id., at 264, n. 10 (refusing to carve out an exception for unusual cases).

\n

Moreover, as with the traditional arrest-based torts, police officers need the safe harbor of probable cause in the\n \nFirst Amendment context to be able to do their jobs effectively. Police officers almost always exchange words with suspects before arresting them. And often a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cspeech provides evidence of a crime or suggests a potential threat.\xe2\x80\x9d Reichle, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 668. If probable cause were not required, the threat of liability might deter an officer from arresting a suspected criminal who, for example, has a political bumper sticker on his car, cf. Kilpatrick v. United States, 432 Fed. Appx. 937 (CA11 2011); is participating in a politically tinged protest, Morse v. San Francisco Bay Area Rapid Transit Dist., 2014 WL 572352 (ND Cal., Feb. 11, 2014); or confronts and criticizes the officer during the arrest of a third party, Holland v. San Francisco, 2013 WL 968295 (ND Cal., Mar. 12, 2013). Allowing plaintiffs to bring a retaliatory-arrest claim in such circumstances, without pleading and proving a lack of probable cause, would permit plaintiffs to harass offi- cers with the kind of suits that common-law courts deemed intolerable.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Because we should have answered the question presented and held that probable cause necessarily defeats\n \nFirst Amendment retaliatory-arrest claims, I respectfully dissent.
