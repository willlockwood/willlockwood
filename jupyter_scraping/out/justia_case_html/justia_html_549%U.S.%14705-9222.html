\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari in this case, 547 U. S. ___ (2006), to determine whether our decision in Blakely v. Washington, 542 U. S. 296 (2004), announced a new rule and, if so, whether it applies retroactively on collateral review. We do not answer these questions, however, because petitioner\xe2\x80\x94a state prisoner seeking postconviction relief from the federal courts\xe2\x80\x94failed to comply with the gatekeeping requirements of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(b). That failure deprived the District Court of jurisdiction to hear his claims. Accordingly, we vacate the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand with instructions to direct the District Court to dismiss petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s habeas corpus application for lack of jurisdiction.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On October 31, 1994, a Washington jury convicted petitioner Lonnie Burton of rape, robbery, and burglary. App. 3\xe2\x80\x934. The state trial court initially entered judgment and sentence on December 19, 1994 (1994 judgment). In that judgment, the court sentenced Burton to a total of 562 months in prison. State v. Burton, No. 35747\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93I etc., 1997 WL 306429, *12 (Wash. App., June 9, 1997). The trial court rested the 562-month sentence on two alternative grounds under Washington\xe2\x80\x99s determinate sentencing scheme. First, it sentenced Burton to within-guidelines sentences for each offense\xe2\x80\x94153 months for robbery, 105 months for burglary, and 304 months for rape\xe2\x80\x94and directed that the sentences be served consecutively, for a total term of 562 months. Id., at *13. Under Washington\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmultiple offense policy,\xe2\x80\x9d imposition of consecutive sentences constitutes an \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional\xe2\x80\x9d sentence, Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa79.94A.120(18), 9.94A.400(1)(a) (2000),[Footnote 1] but the trial court justified such a sentence on the ground that running the three terms concurrently would result in a sentence \xe2\x80\x9cclearly too lenient\xe2\x80\x9d in light of the purposes of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing scheme. See \xc2\xa79.94A.390(2)(i).[Footnote 2] The second basis on which the court calculated a 562-month term was by running the sentences concurrently but imposing an exceptional sentence of 562 months solely for the rape conviction\xe2\x80\x94again on the ground that the total sentence would otherwise be \xe2\x80\x9cclearly too lenient.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Burton, 1997 WL 306429, at *13.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After an unrelated prior conviction was overturned, Burton requested resentencing. Accordingly, over a year after the 1994 judgment, the trial court entered an amended judgment and sentence (1996 judgment), which, after recalculating Burton\xe2\x80\x99s offender scores, imposed a new sentence that relied solely on an exceptional 562-month sentence for the rape conviction, run concurrently with the other two terms. Ibid.; App. 45. On direct review, the Washington Court of Appeals upheld Burton\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, State v. Burton, supra, a decision the Washington Supreme Court declined to review, State v. Burton, 133 Wash. 2d 1025, 950 P.\xc2\xa02d 475 (1997), cert. denied, 523 U. S. 1082 (1998). The State Court of Appeals remanded for resentencing, however, because the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive reliance on the exceptional rape sentence decreased Burton\xe2\x80\x99s potential early release credits, raising vindictiveness concerns. State v. Burton, 1997 WL 306429, at *14.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In response, on March 16, 1998, the trial court entered a second amended judgment and sentence (1998 judgment). App. 3. In this judgment, the trial court recited the jury\xe2\x80\x99s 1994 guilty verdicts, id., at 3\xe2\x80\x934, and again imposed a 562-month sentence, reverting to its original basis for doing so\xe2\x80\x94running the three within-guidelines sentences consecutively. Id., at 7, 29\xe2\x80\x9332. Burton sought review of this sentence, but the Washington courts eventually rejected his challenges both on direct review and in state postconviction proceedings. Id., at 43\xe2\x80\x9355; App. to Brief for Petitioner 1a\xe2\x80\x934a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On December 28, 1998, while state review of his sentence was still pending, Burton filed a petition under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254 for a writ of habeas corpus in the United States District Court for the Western District of Washington (1998 petition). App. 34. The standard form he filled out warned applicants that they must \xe2\x80\x9cordinarily first exhaust \xe2\x80\xa6 available state court remedies as to each ground on which\xe2\x80\x9d they sought \xe2\x80\x9caction by the federal court,\xe2\x80\x9d or run the risk of being \xe2\x80\x9cbarred from presenting additional grounds at a later date.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 37\xe2\x80\x9338. Burton nonetheless challenged his custody only by disputing the constitutionality of his three convictions, not by pressing any sentencing claims. Where the form requested the \xe2\x80\x9c[d]ate of judgment of conviction,\xe2\x80\x9d Burton listed \xe2\x80\x9cDec. 16, 1994,\xe2\x80\x9d corresponding roughly to the date of the 1994 judgment. Id., at 34. The form asked whether the applicant had \xe2\x80\x9cany petition or appeal now pending in any court, either state or federal, as to the judgment under attack,\xe2\x80\x9d to which Burton answered \xe2\x80\x9cYes,\xe2\x80\x9d explaining that \xe2\x80\x9c[the] sentence I received at resentencing is on direct appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 40 (emphasis added). The District Court denied relief, id., at 42, and the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed, Burton v. Walter, 21 Fed. Appx. 632 (2001), cert. denied, 535 U. S. 1060 (2002).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Over three years subsequent to filing the 1998 petition, after the Washington courts had rejected his sentencing challenges, Burton filed another federal habeas petition (2002 petition), again in the Western District of Washington. This time, Burton claimed to be contesting the 1998 judgment, and challenged only the constitutionality of his sentence. In particular, he alleged that it violated our decision in Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466 (2000), to the extent the sentencing court departed from a standard sentence based on its own factual determinations. The District Court again denied the petition, App. 77, and the Ninth Circuit again affirmed, Burton v. Waddington, 142 Fed. Appx. 297 (2005). Both courts rejected the State\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the District Court lacked jurisdiction to entertain the petition because Burton had not obtained an order from the Court of Appeals authorizing him to file a \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d habeas petition, as required by the habeas gatekeeping provisions, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(b)(3). On the merits, the Ninth Circuit rejected Burton\xe2\x80\x99s Apprendi claim and agreed with the State that Burton could not benefit from Blakely v. Washington, 542 U. S. 296, because that decision announced a new rule that did not apply retroactively to Burton\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. 142 Fed. Appx., at 299.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is this petition, the 2002 petition, that is before us today. We conclude, though, that because the 2002 petition is a \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d petition that Burton did not seek or obtain authorization to file in the District Court, the District Court never had jurisdiction to consider it in the first place.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) established a stringent set of procedures that a prisoner \xe2\x80\x9cin custody pursuant to the judgment of a State court,\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(a), must follow if he wishes to file a \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d habeas corpus application challenging that custody, \xc2\xa72244(b)(1). In pertinent part, before filing the application in the district court, a prisoner \xe2\x80\x9cshall move in the appropriate court of appeals for an order authorizing the district court to consider the application.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(b)(3)(A). A three-judge panel of the court of appeals may authorize the filing of the second or successive application only if it presents a claim not previously raised that satisfies one of the two grounds articulated in \xc2\xa72244(b)(2). \xc2\xa72244(b)(3)(C); Gonzalez v. Crosby, 545 U. S. 524, 529\xe2\x80\x93530 (2005); see also Felker v. Turpin, 518 U. S. 651, 656\xe2\x80\x93657, 664 (1996).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Burton\xe2\x80\x99s 2002 petition was a \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d habeas application for which he did not seek, much less obtain, authorization to file. When Burton filed his first petition, the 1998 petition, he was being held in custody pursuant to the 1998 judgment, which had been entered some nine months earlier. When he filed his second petition, the 2002 petition, he was still being held in custody pursuant to the same 1998 judgment. In short, Burton twice brought claims contesting the same custody imposed by the same judgment of a state court. As a result, under AEDPA, he was required to receive authorization from the Court of Appeals before filing his second challenge. Because he did not do so, the District Court was without jurisdiction to entertain it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit determined that the 2002 petition was not \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d because, under McCleskey v. Zant, 499 U. S. 467 (1991), Burton had a \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate excuse for failing to raise\xe2\x80\x9d his sentencing challenges in the 1998 petition. 142 Fed. Appx., at 299 (quoting McCleskey, supra, at 490; internal quotation marks omitted). Specifically, the Ninth Circuit reasoned that because Burton had not exhausted his sentencing claims in state court when he filed the 1998 petition, \xe2\x80\x9cthey were not ripe for federal habeas review\xe2\x80\x9d at that time. 142 Fed. Appx., at 298.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We assume for purposes of this case, without deciding, that the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate excuse\xe2\x80\x9d approach to determining whether a petition is \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d is correct. That court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling that Burton had a \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate excuse,\xe2\x80\x9d however, is inconsistent with the precise practice we have explained governs in circumstances such as Burton\xe2\x80\x99s. The plurality opinion in Rose v. Lundy, 455 U. S. 509, 520\xe2\x80\x93522 (1982), stated that district courts should dismiss \xe2\x80\x9cmixed petitions\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94those with exhausted and unexhausted claims\xe2\x80\x94and that petitioners with such petitions have two options. They may withdraw a mixed petition, exhaust the remaining claims, and return to district court with a fully exhausted petition. We have held that in such circumstances the later filed petition would not be \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive.\xe2\x80\x9d Slack v. McDaniel, 529 U. S. 473, 485\xe2\x80\x93486 (2000).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Alternatively, prisoners filing mixed petitions may proceed with only the exhausted claims, but doing so risks subjecting later petitions that raise new claims to rigorous procedural obstacles. Lundy, supra, at 520\xe2\x80\x93521 (plurality opinion); see also Slack, supra, at 486\xe2\x80\x93487. As noted, the form Burton used in filing his first petition warned of just that consequence. App. 37\xe2\x80\x9338, supra, at 3. There is no basis in our cases for supposing, as the Ninth Circuit did, that a petitioner with unexhausted claims who chooses the second of these options\xe2\x80\x94who elects to proceed to adjudication of his exhausted claims\xe2\x80\x94may later assert that a subsequent petition is not \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d precisely because his new claims were unexhausted at the time he filed his first petition. This reasoning conflicts with both Lundy and \xc2\xa72244(b) and would allow prisoners to file separate habeas petitions in the not uncommon situation where a conviction is upheld but a sentence is reversed. Such a result would be inconsistent with both the exhaustion requirement, with its purpose of reducing \xe2\x80\x9cpiecemeal litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d Duncan v. Walker, 533 U. S. 167, 180 (2001), and AEDPA, with its goal of \xe2\x80\x9cstreamlining federal habeas proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Rhines v. Weber, 544 U. S. 269, 277 (2005).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Burton directs us to two decisions, Stewart v. Martinez-Villareal, 523 U. S. 637 (1998), and Slack, supra, in which we have not regarded subsequent petitions to be \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive.\xe2\x80\x9d But these cases are readily distinguishable. In Martinez-Villareal, we held that the claim of a capital prisoner that he was insane and therefore could not be put to death was necessarily unripe until the State issued a warrant for his execution, and so the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent request for consideration of that previously unripe claim was not \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of \xc2\xa72244(b). 523 U. S., at 644\xe2\x80\x93645. But unlike Burton, the prisoner there had attempted to bring this claim in his initial habeas petition, prompting us to look to Lundy in concluding that the claim \xe2\x80\x9cshould be treated in the same manner as the claim of a petitioner who returns to a federal habeas court after exhausting state remedies,\xe2\x80\x9d that is, characterizing it as not \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive.\xe2\x80\x9d Martinez-Villareal, 523 U. S., at 644. Indeed, we expressly declined to address the situation where a petitioner fails to raise the claim in the initial petition. See id., at 645, n. In this case, Burton did not raise the relevant claims in his 1998 petition. Without more, therefore, our holding in Martinez-Villareal does not support the conclusion that Burton\xe2\x80\x99s 2002 petition was not \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Slack is equally unhelpful to Burton; that decision merely confirmed that when a \xe2\x80\x9cfirst\xe2\x80\x9d petition is dismissed because it contains unexhausted claims, a prisoner returning later with a fully exhausted petition would not confront the \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d bar. 529 U. S., at 485\xe2\x80\x93486. We held that a \xe2\x80\x9cpetition filed after a mixed petition has been dismissed under Rose v. Lundy before the district court adjudicated any claims is to be treated as \xe2\x80\x98any other first petition\xe2\x80\x99 and is not a second or successive petition.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 487 (emphasis added). See also id., at 478 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] habeas petition which is filed after an initial petition was dismissed without adjudication on the merits for failure to exhaust state remedies is not a \xe2\x80\x98second or successive\xe2\x80\x99 petition\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Burton\xe2\x80\x99s case is quite different\xe2\x80\x94his first petition was not subject to dismissal as containing unexhausted claims, and in fact was adjudicated on the merits.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moving beyond the ground relied upon by the Ninth Circuit, Burton argues that his 1998 and 2002 petitions challenged different judgments. He notes that his 1998 petition identified the pertinent judgment as the 1994 judgment, App. 34, while the 2002 petition challenged the sentence imposed in the 1998 judgment. The 1998 judgment, however, had been entered nine months before Burton filed his first petition. That judgment, the same one challenged in the subsequent 2002 petition, was the judgment pursuant to which Burton was being detained. Unlike In\xc2\xa0re Taylor, 171 F.\xc2\xa03d 185 (CA4 1999), cited by Burton, there was no new judgment intervening between the two habeas petitions. In his 1998 petition, Burton specifically described his unexhausted sentencing claims as claims \xe2\x80\x9cas to the judgment under attack,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 40, belying any notion that those claims arose from a judgment distinct from the one challenged in 1998.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Burton finally contends that had he not filed the 1998 petition when he did, and instead waited until state review of his sentencing claims was complete, he risked losing the opportunity to challenge his conviction in federal court due to AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s 1-year statute of limitations. See \xc2\xa72244(d)(1). But this argument misreads AEDPA, which states that the limitations period applicable to \xe2\x80\x9ca person in custody pursuant to the judgment of a State court\xe2\x80\x9d shall run from, as relevant here, \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the judgment became final by the conclusion of direct review or the expiration of the time for seeking such review.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(A). \xe2\x80\x9cFinal judgment in a criminal case means sentence. The sentence is the judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Berman v. United States, 302 U. S. 211, 212 (1937). Accordingly, Burton\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period did not begin until both his conviction and sentence \xe2\x80\x9cbecame final by the conclusion of direct review or the expiration of the time for seeking such review\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94which occurred well after Burton filed his 1998 petition.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Burton argues in rebuttal that this reasoning would necessarily mean the District Court lacked jurisdiction to consider the 1998 petition, but he is mistaken. Section 2254(a) states that a district court \xe2\x80\x9cshall entertain\xe2\x80\x9d a habeas petition \xe2\x80\x9cin behalf of a person in custody pursuant to the judgment of a State court.\xe2\x80\x9d When he filed the 1998 petition, Burton assuredly was \xe2\x80\x9cin custody pursuant to the judgment of a State court\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94even if, at that point, the 1998 judgment was not final for purposes of triggering AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The long and short of it is that Burton neither sought nor received authorization from the Court of Appeals before filing his 2002 petition, a \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d petition challenging his custody, and so the District Court was without jurisdiction to entertain it. The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is therefore vacated, and the case is remanded with instructions to direct the District Court to dismiss the habeas petition for lack of jurisdiction.

\n

It is so ordered.
