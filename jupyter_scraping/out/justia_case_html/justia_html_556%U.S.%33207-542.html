ARIZONA v. GANT

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of arizona

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93542.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 7, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 21, 2009

\n

Respondent Gant was arrested for driving on a suspended license, handcuffed, and locked in a patrol car before officers searched his car and found cocaine in a jacket pocket. The Arizona trial court denied his motion to suppress the evidence, and he was convicted of drug offenses. Reversing, the State Supreme Court distinguished New York v. Belton, 453 U. S. 454\xe2\x80\x94which held that police may search the passenger compartment of a vehicle and any containers therein as a contemporaneous incident of a recent occupant\xe2\x80\x99s lawful arrest\xe2\x80\x94on the ground that it concerned the scope of a search incident to arrest but did not answer the question whether officers may conduct such a search once the scene has been secured. Because Chimel v. California, 395 U. S. 752, requires that a search incident to arrest be justified by either the interest in officer safety or the interest in preserving evidence and the circumstances of Gant\xe2\x80\x99s arrest implicated neither of those interests, the State Supreme Court found the search unreasonable.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Police may search the passenger compartment of a vehicle incident to a recent occupant\xe2\x80\x99s arrest only if it is reasonable to believe that the arrestee might access the vehicle at the time of the search or that the vehicle contains evidence of the offense of arrest. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Warrantless searches \xe2\x80\x9care per se unreasonable,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9csubject only to a few specifically established and well-delineated exceptions.\xe2\x80\x9d Katz v. United States, 389 U. S. 347, 357. The exception for a search incident to a lawful arrest applies only to \xe2\x80\x9cthe area from within which [an arrestee] might gain possession of a weapon or destructible evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Chimel, 395 U. S., at 763. This Court applied that exception to the automobile context in Belton, the holding of which rested in large part on the assumption that articles inside a vehicle\xe2\x80\x99s passenger compartment are \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally \xe2\x80\xa6 within \xe2\x80\x98the area into which an arrestee might reach.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 453 U. S., at 460. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0This Court rejects a broad reading of Belton that would permit a vehicle search incident to a recent occupant\xe2\x80\x99s arrest even if there were no possibility the arrestee could gain access to the vehicle at the time of the search. The safety and evidentiary justifications underlying Chimel\xe2\x80\x99s exception authorize a vehicle search only when there is a reasonable possibility of such access. Although it does not follow from Chimel, circumstances unique to the automobile context also justify a search incident to a lawful arrest when it is \xe2\x80\x9creasonable to believe evidence relevant to the crime of arrest might be found in the vehicle.\xe2\x80\x9d Thornton v. United States, 541 U. S. 615, 632 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment). Neither Chimel\xe2\x80\x99s reaching-distance rule nor Thornton\xe2\x80\x99s allowance for evidentiary searches authorized the search in this case. In contrast to Belton, which involved a single officer confronted with four unsecured arrestees, five officers handcuffed and secured Gant and the two other suspects in separate patrol cars before the search began. Gant clearly could not have accessed his car at the time of the search. An evidentiary basis for the search was also lacking. Belton and Thornton were both arrested for drug offenses, but Gant was arrested for driving with a suspended license\xe2\x80\x94an offense for which police could not reasonably expect to find evidence in Gant\xe2\x80\x99s car. Cf. Knowles v. Iowa, 525 U. S. 113, 118. The search in this case was therefore unreasonable. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0This Court is unpersuaded by the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument that its expansive reading of Belton correctly balances law enforcement interests with an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s limited privacy interest in his vehicle. The State seriously undervalues the privacy interests at stake, and it exaggerates both the clarity provided by a broad reading of Belton and its importance to law enforcement interests. A narrow reading of Belton and Thornton, together with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s other Fourth Amendment decisions, e.g., Michigan v. Long, 463 U. S. 103, and United States v. Ross, 456 U. S. 798, permit an officer to search a vehicle when safety or evidentiary concerns demand. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Stare decisis does not require adherence to a broad reading of Belton. The experience of the 28 years since Belton has shown that the generalization underpinning the broad reading of that decision is unfounded, and blind adherence to its faulty assumption would authorize myriad unconstitutional searches. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9318.

ARIZONA, PETITIONER v. RODNEY JOSEPH GANT

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of\narizona

\n

[April 21, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After Rodney Gant was arrested for driving with a suspended license, handcuffed, and locked in the back of a patrol car, police officers searched his car and discovered cocaine in the pocket of a jacket on the backseat. Because Gant could not have accessed his car to retrieve weapons or evidence at the time of the search, the Arizona Supreme Court held that the search-incident-to-arrest exception to the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s warrant requirement, as defined in Chimel v. California, 395 U. S. 752 (1969), and applied to vehicle searches in New York v. Belton, 453 U. S. 454 (1981), did not justify the search in this case. We agree with that conclusion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under Chimel, police may search incident to arrest only the space within an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98immediate control,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d meaning \xe2\x80\x9cthe area from within which he might gain possession of a weapon or destructible evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d 395 U. S., at 763. The safety and evidentiary justifications underlying Chimel\xe2\x80\x99s reaching-distance rule determine Belton\xe2\x80\x99s scope. Accordingly, we hold that Belton does not authorize a vehicle search incident to a recent occupant\xe2\x80\x99s arrest after the arrestee has been secured and cannot access the interior of the vehicle. Consistent with the holding in Thornton v. United States, 541 U. S. 615 (2004), and following the suggestion in Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s opinion concurring in the judgment in that case, id., at 632, we also conclude that circumstances unique to the automobile context justify a search incident to arrest when it is reasonable to believe that evidence of the offense of arrest might be found in the vehicle.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On August 25, 1999, acting on an anonymous tip that the residence at 2524 North Walnut Avenue was being used to sell drugs, Tucson police officers Griffith and Reed knocked on the front door and asked to speak to the owner. Gant answered the door and, after identifying himself, stated that he expected the owner to return later. The officers left the residence and conducted a records check, which revealed that Gant\xe2\x80\x99s driver\xe2\x80\x99s license had been suspended and there was an outstanding warrant for his arrest for driving with a suspended license.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When the officers returned to the house that evening, they found a man near the back of the house and a woman in a car parked in front of it. After a third officer arrived, they arrested the man for providing a false name and the woman for possessing drug paraphernalia. Both arrestees were handcuffed and secured in separate patrol cars when Gant arrived. The officers recognized his car as it entered the driveway, and Officer Griffith confirmed that Gant was the driver by shining a flashlight into the car as it drove by him. Gant parked at the end of the driveway, got out of his car, and shut the door. Griffith, who was about 30 feet away, called to Gant, and they approached each other, meeting 10-to-12 feet from Gant\xe2\x80\x99s car. Griffith immediately arrested Gant and handcuffed him.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the other arrestees were secured in the only patrol cars at the scene, Griffith called for backup. When two more officers arrived, they locked Gant in the backseat of their vehicle. After Gant had been handcuffed and placed in the back of a patrol car, two officers searched his car: One of them found a gun, and the other discovered a bag of cocaine in the pocket of a jacket on the backseat.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Gant was charged with two offenses\xe2\x80\x94possession of a narcotic drug for sale and possession of drug paraphernalia (i.e., the plastic bag in which the cocaine was found). He moved to suppress the evidence seized from his car on the ground that the warrantless search violated the Fourth Amendment. Among other things, Gant argued that Belton did not authorize the search of his vehicle because he posed no threat to the officers after he was handcuffed in the patrol car and because he was arrested for a traffic offense for which no evidence could be found in his vehicle. When asked at the suppression hearing why the search was conducted, Officer Griffith responded: \xe2\x80\x9cBecause the law says we can do it.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 75.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial court rejected the State\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the officers had probable cause to search Gant\xe2\x80\x99s car for contraband when the search began, id., at 18, 30, but it denied the motion to suppress. Relying on the fact that the police saw Gant commit the crime of driving without a license and apprehended him only shortly after he exited his car, the court held that the search was permissible as a search incident to arrest. Id., at 37. A jury found Gant guilty on both drug counts, and he was sentenced to a 3-year term of imprisonment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After protracted state-court proceedings, the Arizona Supreme Court concluded that the search of Gant\xe2\x80\x99s car was unreasonable within the meaning of the Fourth Amendment. The court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion discussed at length our decision in Belton, which held that police may search the passenger compartment of a vehicle and any containers therein as a contemporaneous incident of an arrest of the vehicle\xe2\x80\x99s recent occupant. 216 Ariz. 1, 3\xe2\x80\x934, 162 P.\xc2\xa03d 640, 642\xe2\x80\x93643 (2007) (citing 453 U. S., at 460). The court distinguished Belton as a case concerning the permissible scope of a vehicle search incident to arrest and concluded that it did not answer \xe2\x80\x9cthe threshold question whether the police may conduct a search incident to arrest at all once the scene is secure.\xe2\x80\x9d 216 Ariz., at 4, 162 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 643. Relying on our earlier decision in Chimel, the court observed that the search-incident-to-arrest exception to the warrant requirement is justified by interests in officer safety and evidence preservation. 216 Ariz., at 4, 162 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 643. When \xe2\x80\x9cthe justifications underlying Chimel no longer exist because the scene is secure and the arrestee is handcuffed, secured in the back of a patrol car, and under the supervision of an officer,\xe2\x80\x9d the court concluded, a \xe2\x80\x9cwarrantless search of the arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s car cannot be justified as necessary to protect the officers at the scene or prevent the destruction of evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 5, 162 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 644. Accordingly, the court held that the search of Gant\xe2\x80\x99s car was unreasonable.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissenting justices would have upheld the search of Gant\xe2\x80\x99s car based on their view that \xe2\x80\x9cthe validity of a Belton search \xe2\x80\xa6 clearly does not depend on the presence of the Chimel rationales in a particular case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 8, 162 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 647. Although they disagreed with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view of Belton, the dissenting justices acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he bright-line rule embraced in Belton has long been criticized and probably merits reconsideration.\xe2\x80\x9d 216 Ariz., at 10, 162 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 649. They thus \xe2\x80\x9cadd[ed their] voice[s] to the others that have urged the Supreme Court to revisit Belton.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 11, 163 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 650.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The chorus that has called for us to revisit Belton includes courts, scholars, and Members of this Court who have questioned that decision\xe2\x80\x99s clarity and its fidelity to Fourth Amendment principles. We therefore granted the State\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari. 552 U. S. ___ (2008).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consistent with our precedent, our analysis begins, as it should in every case addressing the reasonableness of a warrantless search, with the basic rule that \xe2\x80\x9csearches conducted outside the judicial process, without prior approval by judge or magistrate, are per se unreasonable under the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x94subject only to a few specifically established and well-delineated exceptions.\xe2\x80\x9d Katz v. United States, 389 U. S. 347, 357 (1967) (footnote omitted). Among the exceptions to the warrant requirement is a search incident to a lawful arrest. See Weeks v. United States, 232 U. S. 383, 392 (1914). The exception derives from interests in officer safety and evidence preservation that are typically implicated in arrest situations. See United States v. Robinson, 414 U. S. 218, 230\xe2\x80\x93234 (1973); Chimel, 395 U. S., at 763.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Chimel, we held that a search incident to arrest may only include \xe2\x80\x9cthe arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s person and the area \xe2\x80\x98within his immediate control\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94construing that phrase to mean the area from within which he might gain possession of a weapon or destructible evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That limitation, which continues to define the boundaries of the exception, ensures that the scope of a search incident to arrest is commensurate with its purposes of protecting arresting officers and safeguarding any evidence of the offense of arrest that an arrestee might conceal or destroy. See ibid. (noting that searches incident to arrest are reasonable \xe2\x80\x9cin order to remove any weapons [the arrestee] might seek to use\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cin order to prevent [the] concealment or destruction\xe2\x80\x9d of evidence (emphasis added)). If there is no possibility that an arrestee could reach into the area that law enforcement officers seek to search, both justifications for the search-incident-to-arrest exception are absent and the rule does not apply. E.g., Preston v. United States, 376 U. S. 364, 367\xe2\x80\x93368 (1964). \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Belton, we considered Chimel\xe2\x80\x99s application to the automobile context. A lone police officer in that case stopped a speeding car in which Belton was one of four occupants. While asking for the driver\xe2\x80\x99s license and registration, the officer smelled burnt marijuana and observed an envelope on the car floor marked \xe2\x80\x9cSupergold\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a name he associated with marijuana. Thus having probable cause to believe the occupants had committed a drug offense, the officer ordered them out of the vehicle, placed them under arrest, and patted them down. Without handcuffing the arrestees,[Footnote 1] the officer \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98split them up into four separate areas of the Thruway \xe2\x80\xa6 so they would not be in physical touching area of each other\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and searched the vehicle, including the pocket of a jacket on the backseat, in which he found cocaine. 453 U. S., at 456.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The New York Court of Appeals found the search unconstitutional, concluding that after the occupants were arrested the vehicle and its contents were \xe2\x80\x9csafely within the exclusive custody and control of the police.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Belton, 50 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 2d 447, 452, 407 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 420, 423 (1980). The State asked this Court to consider whether the exception recognized in Chimel permits an officer to search \xe2\x80\x9ca jacket found inside an automobile while the automobile\xe2\x80\x99s four occupants, all under arrest, are standing unsecured around the vehicle.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief in No. 80\xe2\x80\x93328, p.\xc2\xa0i. We granted certiorari because \xe2\x80\x9ccourts ha[d] found no workable definition of \xe2\x80\x98the area within the immediate control of the arrestee\xe2\x80\x99 when that area arguably includes the interior of an automobile.\xe2\x80\x9d 453 U. S., at 460.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In its brief, the State argued that the Court of Appeals erred in concluding that the jacket was under the officer\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive control. Focusing on the number of arrestees and their proximity to the vehicle, the State asserted that it was reasonable for the officer to believe the arrestees could have accessed the vehicle and its contents, making the search permissible under Chimel. Brief in No. 80\xe2\x80\x93328, at\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938. The United States, as amicus curiae in support of the State, argued for a more permissive standard, but it maintained that any search incident to arrest must be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98substantially contemporaneous\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d with the arrest\xe2\x80\x94a requirement it deemed \xe2\x80\x9csatisfied if the search occurs during the period in which the arrest is being consummated and before the situation has so stabilized that it could be said that the arrest was completed.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae in New York v. Belton, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1980, No. 80\xe2\x80\x93328, p. 14. There was no suggestion by the parties or amici that Chimel authorizes a vehicle search incident to arrest when there is no realistic possibility that an arrestee could access his vehicle.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After considering these arguments, we held that when an officer lawfully arrests \xe2\x80\x9cthe occupant of an automobile, he may, as a contemporaneous incident of that arrest, search the passenger compartment of the automobile\xe2\x80\x9d and any containers therein. Belton, 453 U. S., at 460 (footnote omitted). That holding was based in large part on our assumption \xe2\x80\x9cthat articles inside the relatively narrow compass of the passenger compartment of an automobile are in fact generally, even if not inevitably, within \xe2\x80\x98the area into which an arrestee might reach.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Arizona Supreme Court read our decision in Belton as merely delineating \xe2\x80\x9cthe proper scope of a search of the interior of an automobile\xe2\x80\x9d incident to an arrest, id., at 459. That is, when the passenger compartment is within an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s reaching distance, Belton supplies the generalization that the entire compartment and any containers therein may be reached. On that view of Belton, the state court concluded that the search of Gant\xe2\x80\x99s car was unreasonable because Gant clearly could not have accessed his car at the time of the search. It also found that no other exception to the warrant requirement applied in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Gant now urges us to adopt the reading of Belton followed by the Arizona Supreme Court.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite the textual and evidentiary support for the Arizona Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of Belton, our opinion has been widely understood to allow a vehicle search incident to the arrest of a recent occupant even if there is no possibility the arrestee could gain access to the vehicle at the time of the search. This reading may be attributable to Justice Brennan\xe2\x80\x99s dissent in Belton, in which he characterized the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding as resting on the \xe2\x80\x9cfiction \xe2\x80\xa6 that the interior of a car is always within the immediate control of an arrestee who has recently been in the car.\xe2\x80\x9d 453 U. S., at 466. Under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach, he argued, \xe2\x80\x9cthe result would presumably be the same even if [the officer] had handcuffed Belton and his companions in the patrol car\xe2\x80\x9d before conducting the search. Id., at 468.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since we decided Belton, Courts of Appeals have given different answers to the question whether a vehicle must be within an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s reach to justify a vehicle search incident to arrest,[Footnote 2] but Justice Brennan\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion has predominated. As Justice O\xe2\x80\x99Connor observed, \xe2\x80\x9clower court decisions seem now to treat the ability to search a vehicle incident to the arrest of a recent occupant as a police entitlement rather than as an exception justified by the twin rationales of Chimel.\xe2\x80\x9d Thornton, 541 U. S., at 624 (opinion concurring in part). Justice Scalia has similarly noted that, although it is improbable that an arrestee could gain access to weapons stored in his vehicle after he has been handcuffed and secured in the backseat of a patrol car, cases allowing a search in \xe2\x80\x9cthis precise factual scenario \xe2\x80\xa6 are legion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 628 (opinion concurring in judgment) (collecting cases).[Footnote 3] Indeed, some courts have upheld searches under Belton \xe2\x80\x9ceven when \xe2\x80\xa6 the handcuffed arrestee has already left the scene.\xe2\x80\x9d 541 U. S., at 628 (same).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under this broad reading of Belton, a vehicle search would be authorized incident to every arrest of a recent occupant notwithstanding that in most cases the vehicle\xe2\x80\x99s passenger compartment will not be within the arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s reach at the time of the search. To read Belton as authorizing a vehicle search incident to every recent occupant\xe2\x80\x99s arrest would thus untether the rule from the justifications underlying the Chimel exception\xe2\x80\x94a result clearly incompatible with our statement in Belton that it \xe2\x80\x9cin no way alters the fundamental principles established in the Chimel case regarding the basic scope of searches incident to lawful custodial arrests.\xe2\x80\x9d 453 U. S., at 460, n.\xc2\xa03. Accordingly, we reject this reading of Belton and hold that the Chimel rationale authorizes police to search a vehicle incident to a recent occupant\xe2\x80\x99s arrest only when the arrestee is unsecured and within reaching distance of the passenger compartment at the time of the search.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although it does not follow from Chimel, we also conclude that circumstances unique to the vehicle context justify a search incident to a lawful arrest when it is \xe2\x80\x9creasonable to believe evidence relevant to the crime of arrest might be found in the vehicle.\xe2\x80\x9d Thornton, 541 U. S., at 632 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment). In many cases, as when a recent occupant is arrested for a traffic violation, there will be no reasonable basis to believe the vehicle contains relevant evidence. See, e.g., Atwater v. Lago Vista, 532 U. S. 318, 324 (2001); Knowles v. Iowa, 525 U. S. 113, 118 (1998). But in others, including Belton and Thornton, the offense of arrest will supply a basis for searching the passenger compartment of an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle and any containers therein.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither the possibility of access nor the likelihood of discovering offense-related evidence authorized the search in this case. Unlike in Belton, which involved a single officer confronted with four unsecured arrestees, the five officers in this case outnumbered the three arrestees, all of whom had been handcuffed and secured in separate patrol cars before the officers searched Gant\xe2\x80\x99s car. Under those circumstances, Gant clearly was not within reaching distance of his car at the time of the search. An evidentiary basis for the search was also lacking in this case. Whereas Belton and Thornton were arrested for drug offenses, Gant was arrested for driving with a suspended license\xe2\x80\x94an offense for which police could not expect to find evidence in the passenger compartment of Gant\xe2\x80\x99s car. Cf. Knowles, 525 U. S., at 118. Because police could not reasonably have believed either that Gant could have accessed his car at the time of the search or that evidence of the offense for which he was arrested might have been found therein, the search in this case was unreasonable.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State does not seriously disagree with the Arizona Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Gant could not have accessed his vehicle at the time of the search, but it nevertheless asks us to uphold the search of his vehicle under the broad reading of Belton discussed above. The State argues that Belton searches are reasonable regardless of the possibility of access in a given case because that expansive rule correctly balances law enforcement interests, including the interest in a bright-line rule, with an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s limited privacy interest in his vehicle.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For several reasons, we reject the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument. First, the State seriously undervalues the privacy interests at stake. Although we have recognized that a motorist\xe2\x80\x99s privacy interest in his vehicle is less substantial than in his home, see New York v. Class, 475 U. S. 106, 112\xe2\x80\x93113 (1986), the former interest is nevertheless important and deserving of constitutional protection, see Knowles, 525 U. S., at 117. It is particularly significant that Belton searches authorize police officers to search not just the passenger compartment but every purse, briefcase, or other container within that space. A rule that gives police the power to conduct such a search whenever an individual is caught committing a traffic offense, when there is no basis for believing evidence of the offense might be found in the vehicle, creates a serious and recurring threat to the privacy of countless individuals. Indeed, the character of that threat implicates the central concern underlying the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x94the concern about giving police officers unbridled discretion to rummage at will among a person\xe2\x80\x99s private effects.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the same time as it undervalues these privacy concerns, the State exaggerates the clarity that its reading of Belton provides. Courts that have read Belton expansively are at odds regarding how close in time to the arrest and how proximate to the arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle an officer\xe2\x80\x99s first contact with the arrestee must be to bring the encounter within Belton\xe2\x80\x99s purview[Footnote 6] and whether a search is reasonable when it commences or continues after the arrestee has been removed from the scene.[Footnote 7] The rule has thus generated a great deal of uncertainty, particularly for a rule touted as providing a \xe2\x80\x9cbright line.\xe2\x80\x9d See 3 LaFave, \xc2\xa77.1(c), at 514\xe2\x80\x93524.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Contrary to the State\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, a broad reading of Belton is also unnecessary to protect law enforcement safety and evidentiary interests. Under our view, Belton and Thornton permit an officer to conduct a vehicle search when an arrestee is within reaching distance of the vehicle or it is reasonable to believe the vehicle contains evidence of the offense of arrest. Other established exceptions to the warrant requirement authorize a vehicle search under additional circumstances when safety or evidentiary concerns demand. For instance, Michigan v. Long, 463 U. S. 1032 (1983), permits an officer to search a vehicle\xe2\x80\x99s passenger compartment when he has reasonable suspicion that an individual, whether or not the arrestee, is \xe2\x80\x9cdangerous\xe2\x80\x9d and might access the vehicle to \xe2\x80\x9cgain immediate control of weapons.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1049 (citing Terry v. Ohio, 392 U. S. 1, 21 (1968)). If there is probable cause to believe a vehicle contains evidence of criminal activity, United States v. Ross, 456 U. S. 798, 820\xe2\x80\x93821 (1982), authorizes a search of any area of the vehicle in which the evidence might be found. Unlike the searches permitted by Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s opinion concurring in the judgment in Thornton, which we conclude today are reasonable for purposes of the Fourth Amendment, Ross allows searches for evidence relevant to offenses other than the offense of arrest, and the scope of the search authorized is broader. Finally, there may be still other circumstances in which safety or evidentiary interests would justify a search. Cf. Maryland v. Buie, 494 U. S. 325, 334 (1990) (holding that, incident to arrest, an officer may conduct a limited protective sweep of those areas of a house in which he reasonably suspects a dangerous person may be hiding).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These exceptions together ensure that officers may search a vehicle when genuine safety or evidentiary concerns encountered during the arrest of a vehicle\xe2\x80\x99s recent occupant justify a search. Construing Belton broadly to allow vehicle searches incident to any arrest would serve no purpose except to provide a police entitlement, and it is anathema to the Fourth Amendment to permit a warrantless search on that basis. For these reasons, we are unpersuaded by the State\xe2\x80\x99s arguments that a broad reading of Belton would meaningfully further law enforcement interests and justify a substantial intrusion on individuals\xe2\x80\x99 privacy.[Footnote 8]

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our dissenting colleagues argue that the doctrine of stare decisis requires adherence to a broad reading of Belton even though the justifications for searching a vehicle incident to arrest are in most cases absent.[Footnote 9] The doctrine of stare decisis is of course \xe2\x80\x9cessential to the respect accorded to the judgments of the Court and to the stability of the law,\xe2\x80\x9d but it does not compel us to follow a past decision when its rationale no longer withstands \xe2\x80\x9ccareful analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d Lawrence v. Texas, 539 U. S. 558, 577 (2003).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have never relied on stare decisis to justify the continuance of an unconstitutional police practice. And we would be particularly loath to uphold an unconstitutional result in a case that is so easily distinguished from the decisions that arguably compel it. The safety and evidentiary interests that supported the search in Belton simply are not present in this case. Indeed, it is hard to imagine two cases that are factually more distinct, as Belton involved one officer confronted by four unsecured arrestees suspected of committing a drug offense and this case involves several officers confronted with a securely detained arrestee apprehended for driving with a suspended license. This case is also distinguishable from Thornton, in which the petitioner was arrested for a drug offense. It is thus unsurprising that Members of this Court who concurred in the judgments in Belton and Thornton also concur in the decision in this case.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not agree with the contention in Justice Alito\xe2\x80\x99s dissent (hereinafter dissent) that consideration of police reliance interests requires a different result. Although it appears that the State\xe2\x80\x99s reading of Belton has been widely taught in police academies and that law enforcement officers have relied on the rule in conducting vehicle searches during the past 28 years,[Footnote 11] many of these searches were not justified by the reasons underlying the Chimel exception. Countless individuals guilty of nothing more serious than a traffic violation have had their constitutional right to the security of their private effects violated as a result. The fact that the law enforcement community may view the State\xe2\x80\x99s version of the Belton rule as an entitlement does not establish the sort of reliance interest that could outweigh the countervailing interest that all individuals share in having their constitutional rights fully protected. If it is clear that a practice is unlawful, individuals\xe2\x80\x99 interest in its discontinuance clearly outweighs any law enforcement \xe2\x80\x9centitlement\xe2\x80\x9d to its persistence. Cf. Mincey v. Arizona, 437 U. S. 385, 393 (1978) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he mere fact that law enforcement may be made more efficient can never by itself justify disregard of the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d). The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reference in this regard to the reliance interests cited in Dickerson v. United States, 530 U. S. 428 (2000), is misplaced. See post, at 5. In observing that \xe2\x80\x9cMiranda has become embedded in routine police practice to the point where the warnings have become part of our national culture,\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U. S., at 443, the Court was referring not to police reliance on a rule requiring them to provide warnings but to the broader societal reliance on that individual right.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent also ignores the checkered history of the search-incident-to-arrest exception. Police authority to search the place in which a lawful arrest is made was broadly asserted in Marron v. United States, 275 U. S. 192 (1927), and limited a few years later in Go-Bart Importing Co. v. United States, 282 U. S. 344 (1931), and United States v. Lefkowitz, 285 U. S. 452 (1932). The limiting views expressed in Go-Bart and Lefokwitz were in turn abandoned in Harris v. United States, 331 U. S. 145 (1947), which upheld a search of a four-room apartment incident to the occupant\xe2\x80\x99s arrest. Only a year later the Court in Trupiano v. United States, 334 U. S. 699, 708 (1948), retreated from that holding, noting that the search-incident-to-arrest exception is \xe2\x80\x9ca strictly limited\xe2\x80\x9d one that must be justified by \xe2\x80\x9csomething more in the way of necessity than merely a lawful arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d And just two years after that, in United States v. Rabinowitz, 339 U. S. 56 (1950), the Court again reversed course and upheld the search of an entire apartment. Finally, our opinion in Chimel overruled Rabinowitz and what remained of Harris and established the present boundaries of the search-incident-to-arrest exception. Notably, none of the dissenters in Chimel or the cases that preceded it argued that law enforcement reliance interests outweighed the interest in protecting individual constitutional rights so as to warrant fidelity to an unjustifiable rule.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The experience of the 28 years since we decided Belton has shown that the generalization underpinning the broad reading of that decision is unfounded. We now know that articles inside the passenger compartment are rarely \xe2\x80\x9cwithin \xe2\x80\x98the area into which an arrestee might reach,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 453 U. S., at 460, and blind adherence to Belton\xe2\x80\x99s faulty assumption would authorize myriad unconstitutional searches. The doctrine of stare decisis does not require us to approve routine constitutional violations.

\n

VI

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Police may search a vehicle incident to a recent occupant\xe2\x80\x99s arrest only if the arrestee is within reaching distance of the passenger compartment at the time of the search or it is reasonable to believe the vehicle contains evidence of the offense of arrest. When these justifications are absent, a search of an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle will be unreasonable unless police obtain a warrant or show that another exception to the warrant requirement applies. The Arizona Supreme Court correctly held that this case involved an unreasonable search. Accordingly, the judgment of the State Supreme Court is affirmed.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To determine what is an \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d search within the meaning of the Fourth Amendment, we look first to the historical practices the Framers sought to preserve; if those provide inadequate guidance, we apply traditional standards of reasonableness. See Virginia v. Moore, 553 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 3\xe2\x80\x936). Since the historical scope of officers\xe2\x80\x99 authority to search vehicles incident to arrest is uncertain, see Thornton v. United States, 541 U. S. 615, 629\xe2\x80\x93631 (2004) (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment), traditional standards of reasonableness govern. It is abundantly clear that those standards do not justify what I take to be the rule set forth in New York v. Belton, 453 U. S. 454 (1981), and Thornton: that arresting officers may always search an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle in order to protect themselves from hidden weapons. When an arrest is made in connection with a roadside stop, police virtually always have a less intrusive and more effective means of ensuring their safety\xe2\x80\x94and a means that is virtually always employed: ordering the arrestee away from the vehicle, patting him down in the open, handcuffing him, and placing him in the squad car.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Law enforcement officers face a risk of being shot whenever they pull a car over. But that risk is at its height at the time of the initial confrontation; and it is not at all reduced by allowing a search of the stopped vehicle after the driver has been arrested and placed in the squad car. I observed in Thornton that the government had failed to provide a single instance in which a formerly restrained arrestee escaped to retrieve a weapon from his own vehicle, 541 U. S., at 626; Arizona and its amici have not remedied that significant deficiency in the present case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It must be borne in mind that we are speaking here only of a rule automatically permitting a search when the driver or an occupant is arrested. Where no arrest is made, we have held that officers may search the car if they reasonably believe \xe2\x80\x9cthe suspect is dangerous and \xe2\x80\xa6 may gain immediate control of weapons.\xe2\x80\x9d Michigan v. Long, 463 U. S. 1032, 1049 (1983). In the no-arrest case, the possibility of access to weapons in the vehicle always exists, since the driver or passenger will be allowed to return to the vehicle when the interrogation is completed. The rule of Michigan v. Long is not at issue here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens acknowledges that an officer-safety rationale cannot justify all vehicle searches incident to arrest, but asserts that that is not the rule Belton and Thornton adopted. (As described above, I read those cases differently). Justice Stevens would therefore retain the application of Chimel v. California, 395 U. S. 752 (1969), in the car-search context but would apply in the future what he believes our cases held in the past: that officers making a roadside stop may search the vehicle so long as the \xe2\x80\x9carrestee is within reaching distance of the passenger compartment at the time of the search.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18. I believe that this standard fails to provide the needed guidance to arresting officers and also leaves much room for manipulation, inviting officers to leave the scene unsecured (at least where dangerous suspects are not involved) in order to conduct a vehicle search. In my view we should simply abandon the Belton-Thornton charade of officer safety and overrule those cases. I would hold that a vehicle search incident to arrest is ipso facto \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d only when the object of the search is evidence of the crime for which the arrest was made, or of another crime that the officer has probable cause to believe occurred. Because respondent was arrested for driving without a license (a crime for which no evidence could be expected to be found in the vehicle), I would hold in the present case that the search was unlawful.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito insists that the Court must demand a good reason for abandoning prior precedent. That is true enough, but it seems to me ample reason that the precedent was badly reasoned and produces erroneous (in this case unconstitutional) results. See Payne v. Tennessee, 501 U. S. 808, 827 (1991). We should recognize Belton\xe2\x80\x99s fanciful reliance upon officer safety for what it was: \xe2\x80\x9ca return to the broader sort of [evidence-gathering] search incident to arrest that we allowed before Chimel.\xe2\x80\x9d Thornton, supra, at 631 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment; citations omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito argues that there is no reason to adopt a rule limiting automobile-arrest searches to those cases where the search\xe2\x80\x99s object is evidence of the crime of arrest. Post, at 10 (dissenting opinion). I disagree. This formulation of officers\xe2\x80\x99 authority both preserves the outcomes of our prior cases and tethers the scope and rationale of the doctrine to the triggering event. Belton, by contrast, allowed searches precisely when its exigency-based rationale was least applicable: The fact of the arrest in the automobile context makes searches on exigency grounds less reasonable, not more. I also disagree with Justice Alito\xe2\x80\x99s conclusory assertion that this standard will be difficult to administer in practice, post, at 7; the ease of its application in this case would suggest otherwise.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No other Justice, however, shares my view that application of Chimel in this context should be entirely abandoned. It seems to me unacceptable for the Court to come forth with a 4-to-1-to-4 opinion that leaves the governing rule uncertain. I am therefore confronted with the choice of either leaving the current understanding of Belton and Thornton in effect, or acceding to what seems to me the artificial narrowing of those cases adopted by Justice Stevens. The latter, as I have said, does not provide the degree of certainty I think desirable in this field; but the former opens the field to what I think are plainly unconstitutional searches\xe2\x80\x94which is the greater evil. I therefore join the opinion of the Court.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito, with whom The Chief Justice and Justice Kennedy join, and with whom Justice Breyer joins except as to Part II\xe2\x80\x93E, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Twenty-eight years ago, in New York v. Belton, 453 U. S. 454, 460 (1981), this Court held that \xe2\x80\x9cwhen a policeman has made a lawful custodial arrest of the occupant of an automobile, he may, as a contemporaneous incident of that arrest, search the passenger compartment of that automobile.\xe2\x80\x9d (Footnote omitted.) Five years ago, in Thornton v. United States, 541 U. S. 615 (2004)\xe2\x80\x94a case involving a situation not materially distinguishable from the situation here\xe2\x80\x94the Court not only reaffirmed but extended the holding of Belton, making it applicable to recent occupants. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision effectively overrules those important decisions, even though respondent Gant has not asked us to do so.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To take the place of the overruled precedents, the Court adopts a new two-part rule under which a police officer who arrests a vehicle occupant or recent occupant may search the passenger compartment if (1) the arrestee is within reaching distance of the vehicle at the time of the search or (2) the officer has reason to believe that the vehicle contains evidence of the offense of arrest. Ante, at 18. The first part of this new rule may endanger arresting officers and is truly endorsed by only four Justices; Justice Scalia joins solely for the purpose of avoiding a \xe2\x80\x9c4-to-1-to 4 opinion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4 (concurring opinion). The second part of the new rule is taken from Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s separate opinion in Thornton without any independent explanation of its origin or justification and is virtually certain to confuse law enforcement officers and judges for some time to come. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision will cause the suppression of evidence gathered in many searches carried out in good-faith reliance on well-settled case law, and although the Court purports to base its analysis on the landmark decision in Chimel v. California, 395 U. S. 752 (1969), the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning undermines Chimel. I would follow Belton, and I therefore respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the Court refuses to acknowledge that it is overruling Belton and Thornton, there can be no doubt that it does so.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Belton, an officer on the New York Thruway removed the occupants from a car and placed them under arrest but did not handcuff them. See 453 U. S., at 456; Brief for Petitioner in New York v. Belton, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1980, No. 80\xe2\x80\x93328, p.\xc2\xa03. The officer then searched a jacket on the car\xe2\x80\x99s back seat and found drugs. 453 U. S., at 455. By a divided vote, the New York Court of Appeals held that the search of the jacket violated Chimel, in which this Court held that an arresting officer may search the area within an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s immediate control. See State v. Belton, 50 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 2d 447, 407 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 420 (1980). The justices of the New York Court of Appeals disagreed on the factual question whether the Belton arrestees could have gained access to the car. The majority thought that they could not have done so, id., at 452, n.\xc2\xa02, 407 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 423, n.\xc2\xa02, but the dissent thought that this was a real possibility. Id., at 453, 407 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 424 (opinion of Gabrielli, J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Viewing this disagreement about the application of the Chimel rule as illustrative of a persistent and important problem, the Belton Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[a] single familiar standard\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98essential to guide police officers\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d who make roadside arrests. 453 U. S., at 458 (quoting Dunaway v. New York, 442 U. S. 200, 213\xe2\x80\x93214 (1979)). The Court acknowledged that articles in the passenger compartment of a car are not always within an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s reach, but \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n order to establish the workable rule this category of cases requires,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court adopted a rule that categorically permits the search of a car\xe2\x80\x99s passenger compartment incident to the lawful arrest of an occupant. 453 U. S., at 460.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The precise holding in Belton could not be clearer. The Court stated unequivocally: \xe2\x80\x9c[W]e hold that when a policeman has made a lawful custodial arrest of the occupant of an automobile, he may, as a contemporaneous incident of that arrest, search the passenger compartment of that automobile.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (footnote omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite this explicit statement, the opinion of the Court in the present case curiously suggests that Belton may reasonably be read as adopting a holding that is narrower than the one explicitly set out in the Belton opinion, namely, that an officer arresting a vehicle occupant may search the passenger compartment \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the passenger compartment is within an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s reaching distance.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938 (emphasis in original). According to the Court, the broader reading of Belton that has gained wide acceptance \xe2\x80\x9cmay be attributable to Justice Brennan\xe2\x80\x99s dissent.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, however, Justice Brennan\xe2\x80\x99s Belton dissent did not mischaracterize the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in that case or cause that holding to be misinterpreted. As noted, the Belton Court explicitly stated precisely what it held. In Thornton, the Court recognized the scope of Belton\xe2\x80\x99s holding. See 541 U. S., at 620. So did Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s separate opinion. See id., at 625 (opinion concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn [Belton] we set forth a bright-line rule for arrests of automobile occupants, holding that \xe2\x80\xa6 a search of the whole [passenger] compartment is justified in every case\xe2\x80\x9d). So does Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in the present case. See ante, at 1 (Belton and Thornton held that \xe2\x80\x9carresting officers may always search an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle in order to protect themselves from hidden weapons\xe2\x80\x9d). This \xe2\x80\x9cbright-line rule\xe2\x80\x9d has now been interred.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the Court has substantially overruled Belton and Thornton, the Court must explain why its departure from the usual rule of stare decisis is justified. I recognize that stare decisis is not an \xe2\x80\x9cinexorable command,\xe2\x80\x9d Payne v. Tennessee, 501 U. S. 808, 828 (1991), and applies less rigidly in constitutional cases, Glidden Co. v. Zdanok, 370 U. S. 530, 543 (1962) (plurality opinion). But the Court has said that a constitutional precedent should be followed unless there is a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98special justification\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for its abandonment. Dickerson v. United States, 530 U. S. 428, 443 (2000). Relevant factors identified in prior cases include whether the precedent has engendered reliance, id., at 442, whether there has been an important change in circumstances in the outside world, Randall v. Sorrell, 548 U. S. 230, 244 (2006) (plurality opinion); Burnet v. Coronado Oil & Gas Co., 285 U. S. 393, 412 (1932) (Brandeis, J., dissenting), whether the precedent has proved to be unworkable, Vieth v. Jubelirer, 541 U. S. 267, 306 (2004) (plurality opinion) (citing Payne, supra, at 827), whether the precedent has been undermined by later decisions, see, e.g., Patterson v. McLean Credit Union, 491 U. S. 164, 173\xe2\x80\x93174 (1989), and whether the decision was badly reasoned. Vieth, supra, at 306 (plurality opinion). These factors weigh in favor of retaining the rule established in Belton.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reliance. While reliance is most important in \xe2\x80\x9ccases involving property and contract rights,\xe2\x80\x9d Payne, supra, at 828, the Court has recognized that reliance by law enforcement officers is also entitled to weight. In Dickerson, the Court held that principles of stare decisis \xe2\x80\x9cweigh[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d heavily against overruling Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436 (1966), because the Miranda rule had become \xe2\x80\x9cembedded in routine police practice.\xe2\x80\x9d 530 U. S., at 443.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If there was reliance in Dickerson, there certainly is substantial reliance here. The Belton rule has been taught to police officers for more than a quarter century. Many searches\xe2\x80\x94almost certainly including more than a few that figure in cases now on appeal\xe2\x80\x94were conducted in scrupulous reliance on that precedent. It is likely that, on the very day when this opinion is announced, numerous vehicle searches will be conducted in good faith by police officers who were taught the Belton rule.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The opinion of the Court recognizes that \xe2\x80\x9cBelton has been widely taught in police academies and that law enforcement officers have relied on the rule in conducting vehicle searches during the past 28 years.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. But for the Court, this seemingly counts for nothing. The Court states that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e have never relied on stare decisis to justify the continuance of an unconstitutional police practice,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15, but of course the Court routinely relies on decisions sustaining the constitutionality of police practices without doing what the Court has done here\xe2\x80\x94sua sponte considering whether those decisions should be overruled. And the Court cites no authority for the proposition that stare decisis may be disregarded or provides only lesser protection when the precedent that is challenged is one that sustained the constitutionality of a law enforcement practice.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court also errs in arguing that the reliance interest that was given heavy weight in Dickerson was not \xe2\x80\x9cpolice reliance on a rule requiring them to provide warnings but to the broader societal reliance on that individual right.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17. The Dickerson opinion makes no reference to \xe2\x80\x9csocietal reliance,\xe2\x80\x9d and petitioner in that case contended that there had been reliance on Miranda because, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or nearly thirty-five years, Miranda\xe2\x80\x99s requirements ha[d] shaped law enforcement training [and] police conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d See Brief for Petitioner in Dickerson v. United States, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1999, No. 99\xe2\x80\x935525, p. 33.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Changed circumstances. Abandonment of the Belton rule cannot be justified on the ground that the dangers surrounding the arrest of a vehicle occupant are different today than they were 28 years ago. The Court claims that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e now know that articles inside the passenger compartment are rarely \xe2\x80\x98within \xe2\x80\x9cthe area into which an arrestee might reach,\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318, but surely it was well known in 1981 that a person who is taken from a vehicle, handcuffed, and placed in the back of a patrol car is unlikely to make it back into his own car to retrieve a weapon or destroy evidence.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Workability. The Belton rule has not proved to be unworkable. On the contrary, the rule was adopted for the express purpose of providing a test that would be relatively easy for police officers and judges to apply. The Court correctly notes that even the Belton rule is not perfectly clear in all situations. Specifically, it is sometimes debatable whether a search is or is not contemporaneous with an arrest, ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937, but that problem is small in comparison with the problems that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new two-part rule will produce.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first part of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new rule\xe2\x80\x94which permits the search of a vehicle\xe2\x80\x99s passenger compartment if it is within an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s reach at the time of the search\xe2\x80\x94reintroduces the same sort of case-by-case, fact-specific decisionmaking that the Belton rule was adopted to avoid. As the situation in Belton illustrated, there are cases in which it is unclear whether an arrestee could retrieve a weapon or evidence in the passenger compartment of a car.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even more serious problems will also result from the second part of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new rule, which requires officers making roadside arrests to determine whether there is reason to believe that the vehicle contains evidence of the crime of arrest. What this rule permits in a variety of situations is entirely unclear.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consistency with later cases. The Belton bright-line rule has not been undermined by subsequent cases. On the contrary, that rule was reaffirmed and extended just five years ago in Thornton.

\n

E

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bad reasoning. The Court is harshly critical of Belton\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, but the problem that the Court perceives cannot be remedied simply by overruling Belton. Belton represented only a modest\xe2\x80\x94and quite defensible\xe2\x80\x94extension of Chimel, as I understand that decision.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Prior to Chimel, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents permitted an arresting officer to search the area within an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpossession\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccontrol\xe2\x80\x9d for the purpose of gathering evidence. See 395 U. S., at 759\xe2\x80\x93760. Based on this \xe2\x80\x9cabstract doctrine,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 760, n.\xc2\xa04, the Court had sustained searches that extended far beyond an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s grabbing area. See United States v. Rabinowitz, 339 U. S. 56 (1950) (search of entire office); Harris v. United States, 331 U. S. 145 (1947) (search of entire apartment).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Chimel Court, in an opinion written by Justice Stewart, overruled these cases. Concluding that there are only two justifications for a warrantless search incident to arrest\xe2\x80\x94officer safety and the preservation of evidence\xe2\x80\x94the Court stated that such a search must be confined to \xe2\x80\x9cthe arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s person\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe area from within which he might gain possession of a weapon or destructible evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d 395 U. S., at 762\xe2\x80\x93763.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unfortunately, Chimel did not say whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe area from within which [an arrestee] might gain possession of a weapon or destructible evidence\xe2\x80\x9d is to be measured at the time of the arrest or at the time of the search, but unless the Chimel rule was meant to be a specialty rule, applicable to only a few unusual cases, the Court must have intended for this area to be measured at the time of arrest.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is so because the Court can hardly have failed to appreciate the following two facts. First, in the great majority of cases, an officer making an arrest is able to handcuff the arrestee and remove him to a secure place before conducting a search incident to the arrest. See ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa04 (stating that it is \xe2\x80\x9cthe rare case\xe2\x80\x9d in which an arresting officer cannot secure an arrestee before conducting a search). Second, because it is safer for an arresting officer to secure an arrestee before searching, it is likely that this is what arresting officers do in the great majority of cases. (And it appears, not surprisingly, that this is in fact the prevailing practice.[Footnote 1]) Thus, if the area within an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s reach were assessed, not at the time of arrest, but at the time of the search, the Chimel rule would rarely come into play.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, if the applicability of the Chimel rule turned on whether an arresting officer chooses to secure an arrestee prior to conducting a search, rather than searching first and securing the arrestee later, the rule would \xe2\x80\x9ccreate a perverse incentive for an arresting officer to prolong the period during which the arrestee is kept in an area where he could pose a danger to the officer.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Abdul-Saboor, 85 F.\xc2\xa03d 664, 669 (CADC 1996). If this is the law, the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit observed, \xe2\x80\x9cthe law would truly be, as Mr. Bumble said, \xe2\x80\x98a ass.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. See also United States v. Tejada, 524 F.\xc2\xa03d 809, 812 (CA7 2008) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f the police could lawfully have searched the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s grabbing radius at the moment of arrest, he has no legitimate complaint if, the better to protect themselves from him, they first put him outside that radius\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I do not think that this is what the Chimel Court intended. Handcuffs were in use in 1969. The ability of arresting officers to secure arrestees before conducting a search\xe2\x80\x94and their incentive to do so\xe2\x80\x94are facts that can hardly have escaped the Court\xe2\x80\x99s attention. I therefore believe that the Chimel Court intended that its new rule apply in cases in which the arrestee is handcuffed before the search is conducted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Belton Court, in my view, proceeded on the basis of this interpretation of Chimel. Again speaking through Justice Stewart, the Belton Court reasoned that articles in the passenger compartment of a car are \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally, even if not inevitably\xe2\x80\x9d within an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s reach. 453 U. S., at 460. This is undoubtedly true at the time of the arrest of a person who is seated in a car but plainly not true when the person has been removed from the car and placed in handcuffs. Accordingly, the Belton Court must have proceeded on the assumption that the Chimel rule was to be applied at the time of arrest. And that is why the Belton Court was able to say that its decision \xe2\x80\x9cin no way alter[ed] the fundamental principles established in the Chimel case regarding the basic scope of searches incident to lawful custodial arrests.\xe2\x80\x9d 453 U. S., at 460, n.\xc2\xa03. Viewing Chimel as having focused on the time of arrest, Belton\xe2\x80\x99s only new step was to eliminate the need to decide on a case-by-case basis whether a particular person seated in a car actually could have reached the part of the passenger compartment where a weapon or evidence was hidden. For this reason, if we are going to reexamine Belton, we should also reexamine the reasoning in Chimel on which Belton rests.

\n

F

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court, however, does not reexamine Chimel and thus leaves the law relating to searches incident to arrest in a confused and unstable state. The first part of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new two-part rule\xe2\x80\x94which permits an arresting officer to search the area within an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s reach at\nthe time of the search\xe2\x80\x94applies, at least for now, only to vehicle occupants and recent occupants, but there is no logical reason why the same rule should not apply to all arrestees.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second part of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new rule, which the Court takes uncritically from Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s separate opinion in Thornton, raises doctrinal and practical problems that the Court makes no effort to address. Why, for example, is the standard for this type of evidence-gathering search \xe2\x80\x9creason to believe\xe2\x80\x9d rather than probable cause? And why is this type of search restricted to evidence of the offense of arrest? It is true that an arrestee\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle is probably more likely to contain evidence of the crime of arrest than of some other crime, but if reason-to-believe is the governing standard for an evidence-gathering search incident to arrest, it is not easy to see why an officer should not be able to search when the officer has reason to believe that the vehicle in question possesses evidence of a crime other than the crime of arrest.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor is it easy to see why an evidence-gathering search incident to arrest should be restricted to the passenger compartment. The Belton rule was limited in this way because the passenger compartment was considered to be the area that vehicle occupants can generally reach, 453 U. S., at 460, but since the second part of the new rule is not based on officer safety or the preservation of evidence, the ground for this limitation is obscure.[Footnote 2]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent in this case has not asked us to overrule Belton, much less Chimel. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument rests entirely on an interpretation of Belton that is plainly incorrect, an interpretation that disregards Belton\xe2\x80\x99s explicit delineation of its holding. I would therefore leave any reexamination of our prior precedents for another day, if such a reexamination is to be undertaken at all. In this case, I would simply apply Belton and reverse the judgment below.
