\n\t\t\t\tMARX v. GENERAL REVENUE CORP.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x931175.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 7, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 26, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Marx filed suit, alleging that General Revenue Corporation (GRC) violated the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) by harassing and falsely threatening her in order to collect on a debt. The District Court ruled against Marx and awarded GRC costs pursuant to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure (FRCP) 54(d)(1), which gives district courts discretion to award costs to prevailing defendants \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nless a federal statute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. provides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Marx sought to vacate the award, arguing that the court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion under Rule 54(d)(1) was displaced by \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3), which provides, in pertinent part, that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]n a finding by the court that an action under this section was brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment, the court may award to the defendant attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees reasonable in relation to the work expended and costs.\xe2\x80\x9d The District Court rejected Marx\xe2\x80\x99s argument. The Tenth Circuit affirmed, in pertinent part, agreeing that costs are allowed under the Rule and concluding that nothing in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text, history, or purpose indicates that it was meant to displace the Rule.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Section \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) is not contrary to, and, thus, does not displace a district court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award costs under, Rule 54(d)(1). Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9316.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Rule 54(d)(1) gives courts discretion to award costs to prevailing parties, but this discretion can be displaced by a federal statute or FRCP that \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., is \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary\xe2\x80\x9d to Rule 54(d)(1). Contrary to the argument of Marx and the United States, as amicus, language of the original 1937 version of the Rule does not suggest that any \xe2\x80\x9cexpress provision\xe2\x80\x9d for costs should displace Rule 54(d)(1), regardless of whether it is contrary to the Rule. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x937.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Section 1692k(a)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s language and context demonstrate that the provision is not contrary to Rule 54(d)(1). Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0GRC argues that since \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) does not address whether costs may be awarded in an FDCPA case brought in good faith, it does not set forth a standard that is contrary to the Rule and therefore does not displace the presumption that a court has discretion to award costs. Marx and the United States concede that the statute does not expressly limit a court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award costs under the Rule, but argue that it does so by negative implication. They claim that unless \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) sets forth the exclusive basis on which to award costs, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cand costs\xe2\x80\x9d would be superfluous with Rule 54(d)(1). And the United States also argues that \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s more specific cost statute displaces Rule 54(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s more general rule. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0The argument of Marx and the United States depends critically on whether \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s allowance of costs creates a negative implication that costs are unavailable in any other circumstances. The expressio unius canon that they invoke does not apply \xe2\x80\x9cunless it is fair to suppose that Congress considered the unnamed possibility and meant to say no to it,\xe2\x80\x9d Barnhart v. Peabody Coal Co., \n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 149\n, and can be overcome by \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary indications that adopting a particular rule or statute was probably not meant to signal any exclusion,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Vonn, \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 55\n. Here, context indicates that Congress did not intend \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) to foreclose courts from awarding costs under the Rule. First, under the American Rule, each litigant generally pays his own attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, but the Court has long recognized that federal courts have inherent power to award attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees in a narrow set of circumstances, e.g., when a party brings an action in bad faith. The statute is thus best read as codifying a court\xe2\x80\x99s pre-existing authority to award both attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs. Next, \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s second sentence must be understood in light of its first, which provides an award of attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs, but to prevailing plaintiffs. By adding \xe2\x80\x9cand costs\xe2\x80\x9d to the second sentence, Congress foreclosed the argument that defendants can only recover attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees when plaintiffs bring an action in bad faith and removed any doubt that defendants may recover costs as well as attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees in such cases. Finally, \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s language sharply contrasts with that of other statutes in which Congress has placed conditions on awarding costs to prevailing defendants. See, e.g., \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71928. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(3)\xc2\xa0Even assuming that their surplusage argument is correct, the canon against surplusage is not absolute. First, the canon \xe2\x80\x9cassists only where a competing interpretation gives effect to every clause and word of a statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Microsoft Corp. v. i4i Ltd. Partnership, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Here, no interpretation of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) gives effect to every word. Second, redundancy is not unusual in statutes addressing costs. See, e.g., \n\n12 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72607(d)(5). Finally, the canon is strongest when an interpretation would render superfluous another part of the same statutory scheme. Because \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) is not part of Rule 54(d)(1), the force of this canon is diminished. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(4)\xc2\xa0Lastly, contrary to the United States\xe2\x80\x99 claim that specific cost-shifting standards displace general ones, the context of the statute indicates that Congress was simply confirming the background presumption that courts may award to defendants attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs when the plaintiff brings an action in bad faith. Because Marx did not bring this suit in bad faith, the specific provision is not applicable. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t668 F.\xc2\xa03d 1174, affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tOLIVEA MARX, PETITIONER v. GENERAL REVENUE CORPORATION\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[February 26, 2013]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFederal Rule of Civil Procedure 54(d)(1) gives district courts discretion to award costs to prevailing defendants \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nless a federal statute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. provides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA), \n\n91Stat.\n881, \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3), provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]n a finding by the court that an action under this section was brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment, the court may award to the defendant attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees reasonable in relation to the work expended and costs.\xe2\x80\x9d This case presents the question whether \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise\xe2\x80\x9d than Rule 54(d)(1). We conclude that \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) does not \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[e] otherwise,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus a district court may award costs to prevailing defendants in FDCPA cases without finding that the plaintiff brought the case in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Olivea Marx defaulted on a student loan guaranteed by EdFund, a division of the California Student Aid Commission. In September 2008, EdFund hired respondent General Revenue Corporation (GRC) to collect the debt. One month later, Marx filed an FDCPA enforcement action against GRC.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Marx alleged that GRC had violated the FDCPA by harassing her with phone calls several times a day and falsely threatening to garnish up to 50% of her wages and to take the money she owed directly from her bank account. Shortly after the complaint was filed, GRC made an offer of judgment under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 68 to pay Marx $1,500, plus reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs, to settle any claims she had against it. Marx did not respond to the offer. She subsequently amended her complaint to add a claim that GRC unlawfully sent a fax to her workplace that requested information about her employment status.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFollowing a 1-day bench trial, the District Court found that Marx had failed to prove any violation of the FDCPA. As the prevailing party, GRC submitted a bill of costs seeking $7,779.16 in witness fees, witness travel expenses, and deposition transcript fees. The court disallowed several items of costs and, pursuant to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 54(d)(1), ordered Marx to pay GRC $4,543.03. Marx filed a motion to vacate the award of costs, arguing that the court lacked authority to award costs under Rules 54(d)(1) and 68(d) because \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) sets forth the exclusive basis for awarding costs in FDCPA cases.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Section 1692k(a)(3) provides, in relevant part: \xe2\x80\x9cOn a finding by the court that an action under this section was brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment, the court may award to the defendant attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees reasonable in relation to the work expended and costs.\xe2\x80\x9d Marx argued that because the court had not found that she brought the case in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment, GRC was not entitled to costs. The Dis- trict Court rejected Marx\xe2\x80\x99s argument, concluding that \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) does not displace a court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award costs under Rule 54(d)(1) and that costs should also be awarded under Rule 68(d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Tenth Circuit affirmed but agreed only with part of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning. In particular, the court disagreed that costs were allowed under Rule 68(d). 668 F.\xc2\xa03d 1174, 1182 (2011). It explained that \xe2\x80\x9cRule 68 applies only where the district court enters judgment in favor of a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x9d for less than the amount of the settlement offer and not where the plaintiff loses outright. Ibid. (citing Delta Air Lines, Inc. v. August, \n\n450 U.\xc2\xa0S. 346, \n352 (1981)\n). Because the District Court had not entered judgment in favor of Marx, the court concluded that costs were not allowed under Rule 68(d). 668 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1182. Nevertheless, the court found that costs were allowed under Rule 54(d)(1), which grants district courts discretion to award costs to prevailing parties unless a federal statute or the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure provide otherwise. Id., at 1178, 1182. After describing the \xe2\x80\x9cvenerable\xe2\x80\x9d presumption that prevailing parties are entitled to costs, id., at 1179, the court concluded that nothing in the text, history, or purpose of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) indicated that it was meant to displace Rule 54(d)(1), id., at 1178\xe2\x80\x931182. Judge Lucero dissented, arguing that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he only sensible reading of [\xc2\xa71692k(a)(3)] is that the district court may only award costs to a defendant\xe2\x80\x9d upon finding that the action was brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment and that to read it otherwise rendered the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cand costs\xe2\x80\x9d superfluous. Id., at 1187 (emphasis in original).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), to resolve a conflict among the Circuits regarding whether a prevailing defendant in an FDCPA case may be awarded costs where the lawsuit was not brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment. Compare 668 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1182 (case below), with Rouse v. Law Offices of Rory Clark, 603 F.\xc2\xa03d 699, 701 (CA9 2010). We now affirm the judgment of the Tenth Circuit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs in all statutory construction cases, we \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98assum[e] that the ordinary meaning of [the statutory] language accurately expresses the legislative purpose.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hardt v. Re- liance Standard Life Ins. Co., 560 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 8) (quoting Gross v. FBL Financial Services, Inc., \n\n557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 167, \n175 (2009)\n (alteration in original)). In this case, we must construe both Rule 54(d)(1) and \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) and assess the relationship between them.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRule 54(d)(1) is straightforward. It provides, in relevant part: \xe2\x80\x9cUnless a federal statute, these rules, or a court or- der provides otherwise, costs\xe2\x80\x94other than attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees\xe2\x80\x94should be allowed to the prevailing party.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs the Tenth Circuit correctly recognized, Rule 54(d)(1) codifies a venerable presumption that prevailing parties are entitled to costs.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Notwithstanding this presumption, the word \xe2\x80\x9cshould\xe2\x80\x9d makes clear that the decision whether to award costs ultimately lies within the sound discretion of the district court. See Taniguchi v. Kan Pacific Saipan, Ltd., 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 4) (\xe2\x80\x9cFederal Rule of Civil Procedure 54(d) gives courts the discretion to award costs to prevailing parties\xe2\x80\x9d). Rule 54(d)(1) also makes clear, however, that this discretion can be displaced by a federal statute or a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure that \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA statute \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise\xe2\x80\x9d than Rule 54(d)(1) if it is \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary\xe2\x80\x9d to the Rule. See 10 J. Moore, Moore\xe2\x80\x99s Federal Practice \xc2\xa754.101[1][c], p. 54\xe2\x80\x93159 (3d ed. 2012) (hereinafter 10 Moore\xe2\x80\x99s). Because the Rule grants district courts discretion to award costs, a statute is contrary to the Rule if it limits that discretion. A statute may limit a court\xe2\x80\x99s dis- cretion in several ways, and it need not expressly state that it is displacing Rule 54(d)(1) to do so. For instance, a statute providing that \xe2\x80\x9cplaintiffs shall not be liable for costs\xe2\x80\x9d is contrary to Rule 54(d)(1) because it precludes a court from awarding costs to prevailing defendants. See, e.g., \n\n7 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa718(d)(1) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe petitioner shall not be liable for costs in the district court\xe2\x80\x9d). Similarly, a statute providing that plaintiffs may recover costs only under certain conditions is contrary to Rule 54(d) because it precludes a court from awarding costs to prevailing plaintiffs when those conditions have not been satisfied. See, e.g., \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71928 (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]o costs shall be included in such judgment, unless the proper disclaimer has been filed in the United States Patent and Trademark Office\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tImportantly, not all statutes that provide for costs are contrary to Rule 54(d)(1). A statute providing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe court may award costs to the prevailing party,\xe2\x80\x9d for example, is not contrary to the Rule because it does not limit a court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion. See 10 Moore\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa754.101[1][c], at 54\xe2\x80\x93159 (\xe2\x80\x9cA number of statutes state simply that the court may award costs in its discretion. Such a provision is not con- trary to Rule 54(d)(1) and does not displace the court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion under the Rule\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMarx and the United States as amicus curiae suggest that any statute that specifically provides for costs displaces Rule 54(d)(1), regardless of whether it is contrary to the Rule. Brief for Petitioner 17; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 11\xe2\x80\x9312 (hereinafter Brief for United States). The United States relies on the original 1937 version of Rule 54(d)(1), which provided, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Except when express provision therefor is made either in a statute of the United States or in these rules, costs shall be allowed as of course to the prevailing party unless the court otherwise directs.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 12 (quoting Rule). Though the Rules Committee updated the language of Rule 54(d)(1) in 2007, the change was \xe2\x80\x9cstylistic only.\xe2\x80\x9d Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 734 (2006 ed., Supp. V). Accordingly, the United States asserts that any \xe2\x80\x9cexpress provision\xe2\x80\x9d for costs should displace Rule 54(d)(1).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe are not persuaded, however, that the original version of Rule 54(d) should be interpreted as Marx and the United States suggest. The original language was meant to ensure that Rule 54(d) did not displace existing costs provisions that were contrary to the Rule. Under the prior language, statutes that simply permitted a court to award costs did not displace the Rule. See 6 J. Moore, Moore\xe2\x80\x99s Federal Practice \xc2\xa754.71[1], p. 54\xe2\x80\x93304 (2d ed. 1996) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen permissive language is used [in a statute regarding costs] the district court may, pursuant to Rule 54(d), exercise a sound discretion relative to the allowance of costs\xe2\x80\x9d). Rather, statutes had to set forth a standard for awarding costs that was different from Rule 54(d)(1) in order to displace the Rule. See Friedman v. Ganassi, 853 F.\xc2\xa02d 207, 210 (CA3 1988) (holding that \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa777k(e) is not an \xe2\x80\x9cexpress provision\xe2\x80\x9d under Rule 54(d) because it does not provide an \xe2\x80\x9calternative standard\xe2\x80\x9d for awarding taxable costs). The original version of Rule 54(d) is consistent with our conclusion that a statute must be contrary to Rule 54(d)(1) in order to displace it.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe now turn to whether \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) is contrary to Rule 54(d)(1). The language of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) and the context surrounding it persuade us that it is not.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe second sentence of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) provides: \xe2\x80\x9cOn a finding by the court that an action under this section was brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment, the court may award to the defendant attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees reasonable in relation to the work expended and costs.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGRC contends that the statute does not address whether costs may be awarded in this case\xe2\x80\x94where the plaintiff brought the case in good faith\xe2\x80\x94and thus it does not set forth a standard for awarding costs that is contrary to Rule 54(d)(1). In its view, Congress intended \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) to deter plaintiffs from bringing nuisance lawsuits. It, therefore, expressly provided that when plaintiffs bring an action in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment, the court may award attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs to the defendant. The statute does address this type of case\xe2\x80\x94i.e., cases in which the plaintiff brings the action in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment. But it is silent where bad faith and purpose of harassment are absent, and silence does not displace the background rule that a court has discretion to award costs.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMarx and the United States take the contrary view. They concede that the language does not expressly limit a court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award costs under Rule 54(d)(1), Brief for Petitioner 10; Brief for United States 19, but argue that it does so by negative implication. Invoking the expressio unius canon of statutory construction, they contend that by specifying that a court may award attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs when an action is brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment, Congress intended to preclude a court from awarding fees and costs when bad faith and purpose of harassment are absent. They further argue that unless \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) sets forth the exclusive basis on which a court may award costs, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cand costs\xe2\x80\x9d would be superfluous. According to this argument, Congress would have had no reason to specify that a court may award costs when a plaintiff brings an action in bad faith if it could have nevertheless awarded costs under Rule 54(d)(1). Finally, the United States argues that \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) is a more specific cost statute that displaces Rule 54(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s more general rule.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe context surrounding \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) persuades us that GRC\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is correct.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe argument of Marx and the United States depends critically on whether \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s allowance of costs creates a negative implication that costs are unavailable in any other circumstances. The force of any negative implication, however, depends on context. We have long held that the expressio unius canon does not apply \xe2\x80\x9cunless it is fair to suppose that Congress considered the unnamed possibility and meant to say no to it,\xe2\x80\x9d Barnhart v. Peabody Coal Co., \n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 149, \n168 (2003)\n, and that the canon can be overcome by \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary indications that adopting a particular rule or statute was probably not meant to signal any exclusion,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Vonn, \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 55, \n65 (2002)\n. In this case, context persuades us that Congress did not intend \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) to foreclose courts from awarding costs under Rule 54(d)(1).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, the background presumptions governing attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs are a highly relevant contextual feature. As already explained, under Rule 54(d)(1) a prevailing party is entitled to recover costs from the losing party unless a federal statute, the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, or a court order \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d The opposite presumption exists with respect to attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. Under the \xe2\x80\x9cbedrock principle known as the \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cAmerican Rule,\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach litigant pays his own attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, win or lose, unless a statute or contract provides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Hardt, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9) (quoting Ruckelshaus v. Sierra Club, \n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 680, \n683 (1983)\n). Notwithstanding the American Rule, however, we have long recognized that federal courts have inherent power to award attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees in a narrow set of circumstances, including when a party brings an action in bad faith. See Chambers v. NASCO, Inc., \n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 32\n\xe2\x80\x9346 (1991) (explaining that a court has inherent power to award attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees to a party whose litigation efforts directly benefit others, to sanction the willful disobedience of a court order, and to sanction a party who has acted in bad faith, vexatiously, wantonly, or for oppressive reasons); Alyeska Pipeline Service Co. v. Wilderness Society, \n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 240\n\xe2\x80\x93259 (1975) (same).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is undisputed that \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) leaves the background rules for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees intact. The statute provides that when the plaintiff brings an action in bad faith, the court may award attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees to the defendant. But, as noted, a court has inherent power to award fees based on a litigant\xe2\x80\x99s bad faith even without \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3). See Chambers, supra, at 45\xe2\x80\x9346. Because \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) codifies the background rule for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, it is dubious to infer congressional intent to override the background rule with respect to costs. The statute is best read as codifying a court\xe2\x80\x99s pre-existing authority to award both attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNext, the second sentence of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) must be understood in light of the sentence that precedes it.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The first sentence of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) provides that defendants who violate the FDCPA are liable for the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs. The second sentence of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) similarly provides that plaintiffs who bring an action in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment may be liable for the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf Congress had excluded \xe2\x80\x9cand costs\xe2\x80\x9d in the second sen- tence, plaintiffs might have argued that the expression of costs in the first sentence and the exclusion of costs in the second meant that defendants could only recover attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees when plaintiffs bring an action in bad faith. By adding \xe2\x80\x9cand costs\xe2\x80\x9d to the second sentence, Congress foreclosed that argument, thereby removing any doubt that defendants may recover costs as well as attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees when plaintiffs bring suits in bad faith. See Ali v. Federal Bureau of Prisons, \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 214, \n226 (2008)\n (explaining that a phrase is not superfluous if used to \xe2\x80\x9cremove .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. doubt\xe2\x80\x9d about an issue); Fort Stewart Schools v. FLRA, \n\n495 U.\xc2\xa0S. 641, \n646 (1990)\n (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9ctechnically unnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d examples may have been \xe2\x80\x9cinserted out of an abundance of caution\xe2\x80\x9d). The fact that there might have been a negative implication that costs are precluded, depending on whether Congress included or excluded the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cand costs,\xe2\x80\x9d weighs against giving effect to any implied limitation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the language in \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) sharply contrasts with other statutes in which Congress has placed conditions on awarding costs to prevailing defendants. See, e.g., \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71928 (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]o costs shall be included in such judgment, unless the proper disclaimer has been filed in the United States Patent and Trademark Office prior to the commencement of the action\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71988(b) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n any action brought against a judicial officer .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. such officer shall not be held liable for any costs .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. unless such action was clearly in excess of such officer\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough Congress need not use explicit language to limit a court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion under Rule 54(d)(1), its use of explicit language in other statutes cautions against inferring a limitation in \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3). These statutes confirm that Congress knows how to limit a court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion under Rule 54(d)(1) when it so desires. See Small v. United States, \n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385, \n398 (2005)\n (Thomas, J., dissent- ing) (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress\xe2\x80\x99 explicit use of [language] in other provisions shows that it specifies such restrictions when it wants to do so\xe2\x80\x9d). Had Congress intended the second sentence of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) to displace Rule 54(d)(1), it could have easily done so by using the word \xe2\x80\x9conly\xe2\x80\x9d before setting forth the condition \xe2\x80\x9c[o]n a finding by the court that an action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs the above discussion suggests, we also are not persuaded by Marx\xe2\x80\x99s objection that our interpretation renders the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cand costs\xe2\x80\x9d superfluous. As noted, supra, at 11, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cand costs\xe2\x80\x9d would not be superfluous if Congress included it to remove doubt that defendants may recover costs when plaintiffs bring suits in bad faith. But even assuming that our interpretation renders the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cand costs\xe2\x80\x9d superfluous, that would not alter our conclusion. The canon against surplusage is not an absolute rule, see Arlington Central School Dist. Bd. of Ed. v. Murphy, \n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 291\n, n.\xc2\xa01 (2006) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhile it is generally presumed that statutes do not contain surplusage, instances of surplusage are not unknown\xe2\x80\x9d); Connecticut Nat. Bank v. Germain, \n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 249, \n253 (1992)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cRedundancies across statutes are not unusual events in drafting .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d), and it has considerably less force in this case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, the canon against surplusage \xe2\x80\x9cassists only where a competing interpretation gives effect to every clause and word of a statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Microsoft Corp. v. i4i Ltd. Partnership, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 12) (internal quotation marks omitted). But, in this case, no interpretation of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) gives effect to every word. Both Marx and the United States admit that a court has inherent power to award attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees to a defendant when the plaintiff brings an action in bad faith. Because there was, consequently, no need for Congress to specify that courts have this power, \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) is superfluous insofar as it addresses attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. In light of this redundancy, we are not overly concerned that the reference to costs may be redundant as well.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, redundancy is \xe2\x80\x9chardly unusual\xe2\x80\x9d in statutes addressing costs. See id., at ___ (slip op., at 13). Numerous statutes overlap with Rule 54(d)(1). See, e.g., \n\n12 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72607(d)(5) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he court may award to the prevailing party the court costs of the action\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa75565(b) (2006 ed., Supp. V) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe [Consumer Financial Protection] Bureau .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may recover its costs in connection with prosecuting such action if [it] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is the prevailing party in the action\xe2\x80\x9d); \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76104(d) (2006 ed.) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe court .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may award costs of suit and reasonable fees for attorneys and expert witnesses to the prevailing party\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa77706(f)(4) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn the case of any successful action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the court, in its discretion, may award the costs of the action\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa77805(b)(3) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he court may award to the prevailing party costs\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa78131(2) (2006 ed., Supp. V) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe court may also, in its discretion, award costs and attorneys fees to the prevailing party\xe2\x80\x9d); \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7431(c) (2006 ed.) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe court .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may, in its discretion .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. allow a reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fee to be paid by the defendant, and costs of the action\xe2\x80\x9d); \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73612(p) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he court .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in its discretion, may allow the prevailing party .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fee and costs\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa73613(c)(2) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he court, in its discretion, may allow the prevailing party .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fee and costs\xe2\x80\x9d); \n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7551(f)(2) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he court may award .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. other litigation costs reasonably incurred\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the canon against surplusage is strongest when an interpretation would render superfluous another part of the same statutory scheme. Cf. United States v. Jica- rilla Apache Nation, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 22) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98As our cases have noted in the past, we are hesitant to adopt an interpretation of a congressional enactment which renders superfluous another portion of that same law\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Mackey v. Lanier Collection Agency & Service, Inc., \n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825, \n837 (1988)\n)). Because \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) is not part of Rule 54(d)(1), the force of this canon is diminished.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLastly, the United States contends that \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) \xe2\x80\x9cestablishes explicit cost-shifting standards that displace Rule 54(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s more general default standard.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 17; see also EC Term of Years Trust v. United States, \n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429, \n433 (2007)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[A] precisely drawn, detailed statute pre-empts more general remedies\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Brown v. GSA, \n\n425 U.\xc2\xa0S. 820, \n834 (1976)\n)). Were we to accept the argument that \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) has a negative implication, this argument might be persuasive. But the context of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) indicates that Congress was simply confirming the background rule that courts may award to defendants attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs when the plaintiff brings an action in bad faith. The statute speaks to one type of case\xe2\x80\x94the case of the bad-faith and harassing plaintiff. Because Marx did not bring this suit in bad faith, this case does not \xe2\x80\x9cfal[l] within the ambit of the more specific provision.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 13; see also RadLAX Gateway Hotel, LLC v. Amalgamated Bank, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 9) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen the conduct at issue falls within the scope of both provisions, the specific presumptively governs .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis in original)).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Accordingly, this canon is inapplicable.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause we conclude that the second sentence of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) is not contrary to Rule 54(d)(1), and, thus, does not displace a district court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award costs under the Rule, we need not address GRC\xe2\x80\x99s alternative argument that costs were required under Rule 68.\n\t\t\t

If the patient understands the doctor to mean \xe2\x80\x9conce a day, each morning,\xe2\x80\x9d we agree that such advice would \xe2\x80\x9cprovide otherwise,\xe2\x80\x9d because the doctor\xe2\x80\x99s order would be \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary\xe2\x80\x9d to the label\xe2\x80\x99s instruction. For the reasons set forth in Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, however, we are not convinced that \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) is \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary\xe2\x80\x9d to Rule 54(d)(1).\n\t\n
\n\n5\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0It is undisputed that GRC is not entitled to costs under \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) because the District Court did not find that Marx brought this action in bad faith. But Rule 54(d)(1) independently authorizes district courts to award costs to prevailing parties. The question in this case is not whether costs are allowed under \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) but whether \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) precludes an award of costs under Rule 54(d)(1).\n\t\n
\n\n6\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Indeed, had Congress intended \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) to foreclose a court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award costs, it could not have chosen a more circuitous way to do so. The statute sets forth the circumstances in which a court \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d award costs. But under Marx\xe2\x80\x99s and the United States\xe2\x80\x99 view, the only consequence of the statute is to set forth the circumstances in which it may not award costs.\n\t\n
\n\n7\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Section 1692k(a) provides:\n\xe2\x80\x9cExcept as otherwise provided by this section, any debt collector who fails to comply with any provision of this subchapter with respect to any person is liable to such person in an amount equal to the sum of\xe2\x80\x94\n\xe2\x80\x9c(1) any actual damages sustained by such person as a result of such failure;\n\xe2\x80\x9c(2)(A) in the case of any action by an individual, such additional damages as the court may allow, but not exceeding $1,000; or\n\xe2\x80\x9c(B) in the case of a class action, (i) such amount for each named plaintiff as could be recovered under subparagraph (A), and (ii) such amount as the court may allow for all other class members, without regard to a minimum individual recovery, not to exceed the lesser of $500,000 or 1 per centum of the net worth of the debt collector; and\n\xe2\x80\x9c(3) in the case of any successful action to enforce the foregoing liability, the costs of the action, together with a reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fee as determined by the court. On a finding by the court that an action under this section was brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment, the court may award to the defendant attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees reasonable in relation to the work expended and costs.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\n
\n\n8\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Marx also suggests that \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) is similar to the PipelineSafety Act, \n, which provides: \xe2\x80\x9cThe court may award costs to a prevailing defendant when the action is unreasonable, frivolous, or meritless.\xe2\x80\x9d We have never had occasion to interpret \xc2\xa760121(b) and its interaction with Rule 54(d)(1).\n\t\n
\n\n9\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Marx, the United States, and GRC also spar over the purpose of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3). Brief for Petitioner 14\xe2\x80\x9316; Brief for United States 21\xe2\x80\x9328; Reply Brief 11\xe2\x80\x9314; Brief for Respondent 30\xe2\x80\x9343. Marx and the United States contend that Congress intended to limit a court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award costs to prevailing defendants because FDCPA plaintiffs are often poor and may be deterred from challenging unlawful debt collection practices by the possibility of being held liable for the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s costs. This purposive argument cannot overcome the language and context of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3), but even if it could, we find it unpersuasive. Rule 54(d)(1) does not require courts to award costs to prevailing defendants. District courts may appropriately consider an FDCPA plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s indigency in deciding whether to award costs. See Badillo v. Central Steel & Wire Co., 717 F.\xc2\xa02d 1160, 1165 (CA7 1983) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is within the discretion of the district court to consider a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s indigency in denying costs under Rule 54(d)\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFederal Rule of Civil Procedure 54(d)(1) is a default standard that grants district courts discretion to award litigation costs to a prevailing party. This default, how- ever, gives way when a federal statute includes a costs provision that \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA), \n\n91Stat.\n874, \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71692 et\xc2\xa0seq., contains a costs provision, \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3), and it \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d That is apparent from the statute\xe2\x80\x99s plain language, which limits a court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award costs to prevailing defendants to cases \xe2\x80\x9cbrought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment.\xe2\x80\x9d In reaching the opposite conclusion, the Court ignores the plain meaning of both the FDCPA and Rule 54(d)(1) and renders the statutory language at issue in this case meaningless. I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority correctly recognizes, see ante, at 4, the fundamental principle of statutory construction that we begin \xe2\x80\x9cwith the language of the statute itself.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Ron Pair Enterprises, Inc., \n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 235, \n241 (1989)\n; Ingalls Shipbuilding, Inc. v. Director, Office of Workers\xe2\x80\x99 Compensation Programs, \n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 248, \n255 (1997)\n; Caminetti v. United States, \n\n242 U.\xc2\xa0S. 470, \n485 (1917)\n. We presume that Congress \xe2\x80\x9cmeans in a statute what it says there,\xe2\x80\x9d Connecticut Nat. Bank v. Germain, \n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 249, \n254 (1992)\n, and \xe2\x80\x9cwhere .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language is plain, the sole function of the courts is to enforce it according to its terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Ron Pair, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 241 (internal quotation marks omitted). This basic tenet is the appropriate starting point for interpreting both Rule 54(d)(1) and \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3). After invoking this principle, however, the majority casts it aside entirely in interpreting the statute and the Rule.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRule 54(d)(1) states, as relevant here, that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nless a federal statute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. provides otherwise, costs\xe2\x80\x94other than attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees\xe2\x80\x94should be allowed to the prevailing party.\xe2\x80\x9d The first question is what it means for a statute to \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[e] otherwise\xe2\x80\x9d than Rule 54(d)(1).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise\xe2\x80\x9d is not defined in the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, we look to its ordinary meaning. Asgrow Seed Co. v. Winterboer, \n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 179, \n187 (1995)\n. In common usage, to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide otherwise\xe2\x80\x9d means to \xe2\x80\x9cmake a .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. stipulation\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cdifferen[t].\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 1598, 1827 (2002) (Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cprovide\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise,\xe2\x80\x9d respectively); see Random House Dictionary of the English Language 1372, 1556 (2d ed. 1987) (Random House) (\xe2\x80\x9cto arrange for or stipulate\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n another manner\xe2\x80\x9d); 10 Oxford English Dictionary 984 (2d ed. 1989) (Oxford Dictionary); 12 id., at 713 (\xe2\x80\x9cto stipulate\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n another way .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. ; in a different manner\xe2\x80\x9d). This reading of the plain text is confirmed by the original 1937 codification of the Rule, which made clear that any \xe2\x80\x9cexpress provision\xe2\x80\x9d relating to costs in a statute is sufficient to displace the default.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAccordingly, to displace Rule 54(d)(1), a federal statute need only address costs in a way different from, but not necessarily inconsistent with, the default.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The reason is straightforward. If Congress has enacted a provision with respect to costs in a statute, there is no longer any need for the default, so it gives way. This design of the Rule is sensible, because many statutes contain specific costs provisions. 10 Moore\xe2\x80\x99s Federal Practice \xc2\xa754.101[1][c], p. 54\xe2\x80\x93160 (3d ed. 2012) (noting that such statutes \xe2\x80\x9care far too numerous to list comprehensively\xe2\x80\x9d). Rule 54(d)(1) is therefore consistent with the canon of statutory interpretation that \xe2\x80\x9ca precisely drawn, detailed statute pre-empts more general remedies.\xe2\x80\x9d Hinck v. United States, \n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 501, \n506 (2007)\n; Crawford Fitting Co. v. J. T. Gibbons, Inc., \n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437, \n445 (1987)\n; United States v. Erika, Inc., \n\n456 U.\xc2\xa0S. 201, \n208 (1982)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile purporting to interpret the \xe2\x80\x9cordinary meaning\xe2\x80\x9d of Rule 54(d)(1), ante, at 4, the majority immediately abandons the ordinary meaning. The majority concludes that a statute provides otherwise for purposes of Rule 54(d)(1) only if it is \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary\xe2\x80\x9d to the default. Ante, at 5. But the majority does not cite even a single dictionary definition in support of that reading, despite the oft-cited principle that a definition widely reflected in dictionaries generally governs over other possible meanings.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Lacking any dictionary support for its interpretation, the majority relies instead upon a treatise published nearly 60 years after the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s adoption. See ante, 6\xe2\x80\x937 (citing 6 J. Moore, Moore\xe2\x80\x99s Federal Practice, p. 54\xe2\x80\x93304 (2d ed. 1996)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cOtherwise\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cdifferent.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third 1518; see supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s preferred term of art, \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary,\xe2\x80\x9d sets a higher bar; it signifies \xe2\x80\x9cthe opposite,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ca proposition, fact, or condition incompatible with an- other.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third 495 (emphasis added). See also American Heritage Dictionary of the English Language 399 (5th ed. 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cOpposed, as in character or purpose\xe2\x80\x9d); 3 Oxford Dictionary 844 (\xe2\x80\x9cOpposed in nature or tendency; diametrically different, extremely unlike\xe2\x80\x9d); Random House 442 (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]pposite in nature or character; diametrically or mutually opposed\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading does not square with the everyday meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Consider, for example, a medication labeled with the instruction, \xe2\x80\x9ctake twice a day unless otherwise directed.\xe2\x80\x9d If a doctor advises her patient to take the medicine \xe2\x80\x9cin the morning,\xe2\x80\x9d the patient would understand her to mean that he should take the medicine once a day, each morning. Although the instruction to take the medication in the morning is not incompatible with taking it twice a day\xe2\x80\x94it could be taken in the evening as well\xe2\x80\x94an ordinary English speaker would interpret \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise\xe2\x80\x9d to mean that the doctor\xe2\x80\x99s more specific instructions entirely supersede what is printed on the bottle. Rule 54(d)(1) is just the same: Its default is supplanted whenever Congress provides more specific instructions, not only when they are diametrically opposed to it.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, the straightforward question in this case is whether \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) implements a \xe2\x80\x9cdifferent\xe2\x80\x9d standard with respect to costs than Rule 54(d)(1), and so \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d As relevant, \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) states: \xe2\x80\x9cOn a finding by the court that an action under this section was brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment, the court may award to the defendant attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees reasonable in relation to the work expended and costs.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is readily apparent that this provision is different from the default of Rule 54(d)(1). In \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3), Congress described with specificity a single circumstance in which costs may be awarded. Far from merely restating a district court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award costs, this provision imposes a prerequisite to the exercise of that discretion: a finding by the court that an action was brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause the text is plain, there is no need to proceed any further. Even so, relevant canons of statutory interpretation lend added support to reading \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) as having a negative implication. That reading accords with the expressio unius, exclusio alterius canon, which instructs that when Congress includes one possibility in a statute, it excludes another by implication. See Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Echazabal, \n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 73\n\xe2\x80\x9381 (2002). This rule reinforces what the text makes clear. By limiting a court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award costs to cases brought in bad faith or for the purpose of harassment, Congress foreclosed the award of costs in other circumstances.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the statute is also strongly favored by the rule that statutes should be read to avoid superfluity. Under this \xe2\x80\x9cmost basic of interpretative canons, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[a] statute should be constructed so that effect is given to all of its provisions, so that no part will be inoperative or superfluous, void or insignificant.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Corley v. United States, \n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 303, \n314 (2009)\n (quoting Hibbs v. Winn, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 88, \n101 (2004)\n). Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s reading, as it mostly acknowledges, renders the entire sentence meaningless because it reiterates powers that federal courts already possess with respect to both costs and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. See Brief for Respondent 22\xe2\x80\x9324.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority rejects this argument, citing the rule that this canon \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98assists only where a competing interpretation gives effect to every clause and word of a statute.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13 (quoting Microsoft Corp. v. i4i Ltd. Partnership, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 12)). In its view, neither of the available interpretations can eliminate superfluity because the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees provision is redundant under any reading. Ante, at 13. But the canon against super- fluity surely counsels against an interpretation that renders the entire provision at issue superfluous when a competing interpretation would at least render part of the provision meaningful. Nor does the majority\xe2\x80\x99s observation that redundancy is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98hardly unusual,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 14, in provisions relating to costs make the canon inapplicable. While Congress sometimes drafts redundant language with respect to costs, Congress did not do so in \xc2\xa71692(a)(3).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Instead, it drafted specific language that permits a court to award costs only on the satisfaction of a condition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tInterpreting \xc2\xa71692k(1)(3) as having a negative implication is consistent with our construction of another statute that includes similar language. In Cooper Industries, Inc. v. Aviall Services, Inc., \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 157, \n166 (2004)\n (opinion for the Court by Thomas, J.), we considered a provision in the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act of 1980, \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa79613(f)(1) that provided: \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person may seek contribution .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. during .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any civil action under section 9606 of this title\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added). We rejected the argument that the word \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d indicated that \xe2\x80\x9cduring a civil action\xe2\x80\x9d was one but not the exclusive circumstance in which the right of contribution was available. 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 166. We instead adopted the natural reading of the text, holding that a party\xe2\x80\x99s ability to seek contribution was limited by the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cduring any civil action\xe2\x80\x9d and that contribution was only available while a lawsuit is pending. Ibid. The same logic applies here, because \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) imposes a closely analogous condition on a court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award costs.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t The first sentence of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) underscores that Congress implemented a different rule than Rule 54(d)(1). That sentence provides that a debt collector who violates the FDCPA is \xe2\x80\x9cliable to\xe2\x80\x9d a prevailing plaintiff for \xe2\x80\x9cthe costs of the action, together with a reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fee as determined by the court.\xe2\x80\x9d This sentence makes a losing defendant always liable for the \xe2\x80\x9ccosts of the action,\xe2\x80\x9d which is a clear departure from the Rule 54(d)(1) discretionary default. Cf. Taniguchi v. Kan Pacific Saipan, Ltd., 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 4). Because Congress deviated from Rule 54(d)(1) in the first sentence of \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3), the most reasonable reading is that the sentence that immediately follows, which states the rule for prevailing defendants, takes a similar path.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority believes that its reading of the costs provision follows from the first sentence as well, but for a different reason. Ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. It suggests that if Congress had not included costs in the second sentence, a plaintiff might have been able to argue that the inclusion of costs in the first sentence and the exclusion of costs in the second indicated that defendants could recover only fees when an action is brought in bad faith. The majority then speculates that Congress included costs in the second sentence to foreclose that argument.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe text of the previous sentence makes plain, however, that the second sentence departs from the Rule 54(d)(1) default, and the majority offers no evidence in support of its supposition that Congress intended a different meaning.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Moreover, I see no basis for invoking potential confusion or indulging in speculation to explain away the words Congress chose. Ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. Some Members of the majority have expressed doubt about the relevance of legislative history, claiming that relying upon it is analogous to \xe2\x80\x9centering a crowded cocktail party and looking .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for one\xe2\x80\x99s friends.\xe2\x80\x9d Conroy v. Aniskoff, \n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 511, \n519 (1993)\n (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment). But speculating whole cloth about congressional intent, as the majority does, is surely more problematic. The majority is saved the trouble of having to look for its friends at the party; it simply invites them.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tReduced to its essence, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s analysis turns on reading \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) in the context of what it calls the \xe2\x80\x9cvenerable presumption\xe2\x80\x9d that prevailing parties are entitled to costs. See ante, at 4. Even if it were appropriate to consider a background presumption rather than reading the plain text at issue, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of the presumption is at best incomplete.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that the presumption regarding costs is a \xe2\x80\x9cvenerable\xe2\x80\x9d one in American law is an overstatement. Ibid. It is true, as the majority points out, that prior to the federal rules, \xe2\x80\x9cprevailing parties were entitled to costs as of right in actions at law while courts had discretion to award costs in equity proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4, n.\xc2\xa03; see Wright & Miller \xc2\xa72665, at 199. But the doctrine governing costs at law carved out an important exception for statutory provisions that set forth a differ- ent rule.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Where there was such a statute, courts would simply apply it. In its assessment of the background principles underlying its approach, the majority glosses over the longstanding expectation that Congress often enacts different rules with respect to costs, and when it does, these rules govern.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, Rule 54(d)(1) embraces this long-recognized exception because it specifies that a statute can displace its default rule. To repeat, Rule 54(d)(1) merely enacts a default standard that applies unless, among other things, a statute or rule \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Here, for the reasons explained, Congress enacted exactly such a statute. That is clear from the text, because \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) conditions the award of costs on the satisfaction of a condition, and because the previous sentence of the same provision breaks from the default.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe plain text of Rule 54(d)(1) and \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) dictates the result in this case. Accordingly, I would reverse the Tenth Circuit and hold that \xc2\xa71692k(a)(3) \xe2\x80\x9cprovides otherwise\xe2\x80\x9d than Rule 54(d)(1), such that a district court cannot award costs to a prevailing defendant in an FDCPA action except upon a showing that the action was brought in bad faith and for the purpose of harassment. I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t
