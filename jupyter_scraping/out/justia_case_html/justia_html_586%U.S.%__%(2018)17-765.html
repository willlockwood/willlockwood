United States v. Stitt

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 17\xe2\x80\x93765.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 9, 2018\xe2\x80\x94Decided December 10, 2018[1]

\n

Respondents Victor J. Stitt and Jason Daniel Sims were each convicted in federal court of unlawfully possessing a firearm, in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7922(g)(1). The sentencing judge in each case imposed the mandatory minimum 15-year prison term that the Armed Career Criminal Act requires for \xc2\xa7922(g)(1) offenders who have at least three previous convictions for certain \xe2\x80\x9cviolent\xe2\x80\x9d or drug-related felonies, \xc2\xa7924(e)(1). The Act defines \xe2\x80\x9cviolent felony\xe2\x80\x9d to mean, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9cany crime punishable by imprisonment for a term exceeding one year .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is burglary.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(B). Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 prior convictions were for violations of state burglary statutes\xe2\x80\x94a Tennessee statute in Stitt\xe2\x80\x99s case and an Arkansas statute in Sims\xe2\x80\x99 case\xe2\x80\x94that prohibit burglary of a structure or vehicle that has been adapted or is customarily used for overnight accommodation. In both cases, the District Courts found that the state statutory crimes fell within the scope of the federal Act\xe2\x80\x99s term \xe2\x80\x9cburglary.\xe2\x80\x9d The relevant Court of Appeals in each case disagreed, vacated the sentence, and remanded for resentencing.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0The term \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d in the Armed Career Criminal Act includes burglary of a structure or vehicle that has been adapted or is customarily used for overnight accommodation. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0In deciding whether an offense qualifies as a violent felony under the Act, the categorical approach first adopted in Taylor v. United States,\n\n495 U.\xc2\xa0S. 575, requires courts to evaluate a prior state conviction by reference to the elements of the state offense, rather than to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s behavior on a particular occasion. A prior state conviction does not qualify as generic burglary under the Act where \xe2\x80\x9cthe elements of [the relevant state statute] are broader than those of generic burglary.\xe2\x80\x9d Mathis v. United States, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Taylor, which specifically considered the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d and defined the elements of generic burglary as \xe2\x80\x9can unlawful or unprivileged entry into, or remaining in, a building or other structure, with intent to commit a crime,\xe2\x80\x9d 495 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 598, governs and determines the outcome here. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The state statutes at issue here fall within the scope of Taylor\xe2\x80\x99s definition of generic burglary. Congress intended that definition to reflect \xe2\x80\x9cthe generic sense in which the term [was] used in the criminal codes of most States\xe2\x80\x9d when the Act was passed. 495 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 598. And at that time, a majority of state burglary statutes covered vehicles adapted or customarily used for lodging. Congress also viewed burglary as an inherently dangerous crime that \xe2\x80\x9ccreates the possibility of a violent confrontation\xe2\x80\x9d between the offender and an occupant or someone who comes to investigate. Id., at 588. An offender who breaks into a mobile home, an RV, a camping tent, or another structure or vehicle that is adapted or customarily used for lodging creates a similar or greater risk of violent confrontation. Although the risk of violence is diminished if the vehicle is only used for lodging part of the time, the Court finds no reason to believe that Congress intended to make a part-time/full-time distinction. Respondents also argue that the vehicles covered here are analogous to the nontypical structures and vehicles that Taylor, Mathis, and other cases described as falling outside the scope of generic burglary, but none of those prior cases presented the question whether generic burglary includes structures or vehicles that are adapted or customarily used for overnight use. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0Sims\xe2\x80\x99 case is remanded for further proceedings. His argument that Arkansas\xe2\x80\x99 residential burglary statute is too broad to count as generic burglary because it also covers burglary of \xe2\x80\x9ca vehicle .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [w]here any person lives,\xe2\x80\x9d Ark. Code Ann. \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x9339\xe2\x80\x93101(1)(A), rests in part upon state law, and the lower courts have not considered it. Those courts remain free to determine whether Sims properly presented that argument and, if so, to decide the merits. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

No. 17\xe2\x80\x93765, 860 F.\xc2\xa03d 854, reversed; No. 17\xe2\x80\x93766, 854 F.\xc2\xa03d 1037, vacated and remanded.

Breyer, J., delivered the opinion for a unanimous Court.

\n

Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The Armed Career Criminal Act requires a federal sentencing judge to impose upon certain persons convicted of unlawfully possessing a firearm a 15-year minimum prison term. The judge is to impose that special sentence if the offender also has three prior convictions for certain violent or drug-related crimes.\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(e). Those prior convictions include convictions for \xe2\x80\x9cburglary.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(B)(ii). And the question here is whether the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d includes burglary of a structure or vehicle that has been adapted or is customarily used for overnight accommodation. We hold that it does.

\n

I

\n

The consolidated cases before us involve two defendants, each of whom was convicted in a federal court of unlaw- fully possessing a firearm in violation of \xc2\xa7922(g)(1). The maximum punishment for this offense is typically 10 years in prison. \xc2\xa7924(a)(2). Each offender, however, had prior state burglary convictions sufficient, at least potentially, to require the sentencing judge to impose a mandatory 15-year minimum prison term under the Armed Career Criminal Act. That Act, as we have just said, requires an enhanced sentence for offenders who have at least three previous convictions for certain \xe2\x80\x9cviolent\xe2\x80\x9d or drug-related felonies. \xc2\xa7924(e)(1). Those prior felonies include \xe2\x80\x9cany crime\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cpunishable by imprisonment for a term exceeding one year\xe2\x80\x9d and that also

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(i) has as an element the use, attempted use, or threatened use of physical force against the person of another; or

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(ii) is burglary, arson, or extortion, involves use of explosives, or otherwise involves conduct that presents a serious potential risk of physical injury to another.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(B) (emphasis added).

\n

The question here concerns the scope of the statutory word \xe2\x80\x9cburglary.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The relevant prior convictions of one of the unlawful firearms offenders, Victor J. Stitt, were for violations of a Tennessee statute that defines \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ggravated burglary\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cburglary of a habitation.\xe2\x80\x9d Tenn. Code Ann. \xc2\xa739\xe2\x80\x9314\xe2\x80\x93403(a) (1997). It further defines \xe2\x80\x9c[h]abitation\xe2\x80\x9d to include: (1) \xe2\x80\x9cany structure, including .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. mobile homes, trailers, and tents, which is designed or adapted for the overnight accommodation of persons,\xe2\x80\x9d and (2) any \xe2\x80\x9cself-propelled vehicle that is designed or adapted for the overnight accommodation of persons and is actually occupied at the time of initial entry by the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa739\xe2\x80\x9314\xe2\x80\x93401(1)(A), (B) (emphasis added).

\n

The relevant prior convictions of the other unlawful firearms offender, Jason Daniel Sims, were for violations of an Arkansas statute that prohibits burglary of a \xe2\x80\x9cresidential occupiable structure.\xe2\x80\x9d Ark. Code Ann. \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x9339\xe2\x80\x93201(a)(1) (Michie 1997). The statute defines \xe2\x80\x9c[r]esidential occupiable structure\xe2\x80\x9d to include:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9ca vehicle, building, or other structure:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(A)[w]here any person lives; or

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(B)[w]hich is customarily used for overnight accommodation of persons whether or not a person is actually present.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x9339\xe2\x80\x93101(1) (emphasis added).

\n

In both cases, the District Courts found that the state statutory crimes fell within the scope of the word \xe2\x80\x9cbur- glary\xe2\x80\x9d in the Armed Career Criminal Act and consequently imposed that statute\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory sentence enhancement. In both cases, the relevant Federal Court of Appeals held that the statutory crimes did not fall within the scope of the word \xe2\x80\x9cburglary,\xe2\x80\x9d vacated the sentence, and remanded for resentencing. See 860 F.\xc2\xa03d 854 (CA6 2017) (en banc) (reversing panel decision to the contrary); 854 F.\xc2\xa03d 1037 (CA8 2017).

\n

The Government asked us to grant certiorari to consider the question \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether burglary of a nonpermanent or mobile structure that is adapted or used for overnight accommodation can qualify as \xe2\x80\x98burglary\xe2\x80\x99 under the Armed Career Criminal Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. in No. 17\xe2\x80\x93765, p. i; Pet. for Cert. in No. 17\xe2\x80\x93766, p. i. And, in light of uncertainty about the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d in the lower courts, we granted the Government\xe2\x80\x99s request. Compare 860 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 862\xe2\x80\x93863; 854 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1040; United States v. White, 836 F.\xc2\xa03d 437, 446 (CA4 2016); United States v. Grisel, 488 F.\xc2\xa03d 844 (CA9 2007) (en banc), with Smith v. United States, 877 F.\xc2\xa03d 720, 724 (CA7 2017), cert. pending, No. 17\xe2\x80\x937517; United States v. Spring, 80 F.\xc2\xa03d 1450, 1462 (CA10 1996).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

The word \xe2\x80\x9cburglary,\xe2\x80\x9d like the word \xe2\x80\x9ccrime\xe2\x80\x9d itself, is ambiguous. It might refer to a kind of crime, a generic crime, as set forth in a statute (\xe2\x80\x9ca burglary consists of behavior that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d), or it might refer to the way in which an individual offender acted on a particular occasion (\xe2\x80\x9con January 25, Jones committed a burglary on Oak Street in South San Francisco\xe2\x80\x9d). We have held that the words in the Armed Career Criminal Act do the first. Accordingly, we have held that the Act requires us to evaluate a prior state conviction \xe2\x80\x9cin terms of how the law defines the offense and not in terms of how an individual offender might have committed it on a particular occasion.\xe2\x80\x9d Begay v. United States,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 137, 141 (2008). A prior state conviction, we have said, does not qualify as generic burglary under the Act where \xe2\x80\x9cthe elements of [the relevant state statute] are broader than those of generic burglary.\xe2\x80\x9d Mathis v. United States, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 19). The case in which we first adopted this \xe2\x80\x9ccategorical approach\xe2\x80\x9d is Taylor v. United States,\n\n495 U.\xc2\xa0S. 575 (1990). That case, which specifically considered the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cburglary,\xe2\x80\x9d governs here and determines the outcome.

\n

In Taylor, we did more than hold that the word \xe2\x80\x9cbur- glary\xe2\x80\x9d refers to a kind of generic crime rather than to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s behavior on a particular occasion. We also explained, after examining the Act\xe2\x80\x99s history and purpose, that Congress intended a \xe2\x80\x9cuniform definition of burglary [to] be applied to all cases in which the Government seeks\xe2\x80\x9d an enhanced sentence under the Act. Id., at 580\xe2\x80\x93592. We held that this uniform definition includes \xe2\x80\x9cat least the \xe2\x80\x98classic\xe2\x80\x99 common-law definition,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, breaking and entering a dwelling at night with intent to commit a fel- ony. Id., at 593. But we added that it must include more. The classic definition, by excluding all places other than dwellings, we said, has \xe2\x80\x9clittle relevance to modern law enforcement concerns.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Perhaps for that reason, by the time the Act was passed in 1986, most States had expanded the meaning of burglary to include \xe2\x80\x9cstructures other than dwellings.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (citing W. LaFave & A. Scott, Substantive Criminal Law \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa78.13(a)\xe2\x80\x93(f) (1986)).

\n

In addition, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose, revealed by its language, ruled out limiting the scope of \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d to especially serious burglaries, e.g., those having elements that created a particularly serious risk of physical harm. If that had been Congress\xe2\x80\x99s intent, adding the word \xe2\x80\x9cbur- glary\xe2\x80\x9d would have been unnecessary, since the (now-invalid) residual clause \xe2\x80\x9calready include[d] any crime that \xe2\x80\x98involves conduct that presents a serious potential risk of physical injury to another.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Taylor, 495 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 597 (quoting\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(e)(2)(B)(ii)); see Johnson v. United States, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2015) (slip op., at 5\xe2\x80\x9310) (holding residual clause unconstitutionally vague). We concluded that the Act\xe2\x80\x99s term \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d must include \xe2\x80\x9cordinary,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9crun-of-the-mill\xe2\x80\x9d burglaries as well as aggravated ones. Taylor, 495 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 597. And we defined the elements of generic \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9can unlawful or unprivileged entry into, or remaining in, a building or other structure, with intent to commit a crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 598.

\n

B

\n

The relevant language of the Tennessee and Arkansas statutes falls within the scope of generic burglary\xe2\x80\x99s definition as set forth in Taylor. For one thing, we made clear in Taylor that Congress intended the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cbur- glary\xe2\x80\x9d to reflect \xe2\x80\x9cthe generic sense in which the term [was] used in the criminal codes of most States\xe2\x80\x9d at the time the Act was passed. Ibid. In 1986, a majority of state bur- glary statutes covered vehicles adapted or customarily used for lodging\xe2\x80\x94either explicitly or by defining \xe2\x80\x9cbuilding\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cstructure\xe2\x80\x9d to include those vehicles. See, e.g., N.\xc2\xa0H. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7635:1 (1974) (prohibiting burglary of an \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ccupied structure,\xe2\x80\x9d defined to include \xe2\x80\x9cany structure, vehicle, boat or place adapted for overnight accommodation of persons\xe2\x80\x9d); Ore. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7164.205, 164.215, 164.225 (1985) (prohibiting burglary of a \xe2\x80\x9cbuilding,\xe2\x80\x9d defined to include \xe2\x80\x9cany booth, vehicle, boat, aircraft or other structure adapted for overnight accommodation of persons\xe2\x80\x9d); see also ALI, Model Penal Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7220.0(1), 221.1(1) (1980) (defining \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98occupied structure\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of burglary as \xe2\x80\x9cany structure, vehicle or place adapted for overnight accommodation of persons, or for carrying on business therein, whether or not a person is actually present\xe2\x80\x9d); Appendix, infra (collecting burglary statutes from 1986 or earlier that covered either vehicles adapted or customarily used for overnight accommodation or a broader class of vehicles).

\n

For another thing, Congress, as we said in Taylor, viewed burglary as an inherently dangerous crime because burglary \xe2\x80\x9ccreates the possibility of a violent confrontation between the offender and an occupant, caretaker, or some other person who comes to investigate.\xe2\x80\x9d 495 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 588; see also James v. United States,\n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 192, 203 (2007). An offender who breaks into a mobile home, an RV, a camping tent, a vehicle, or another structure that is adapted for or customarily used for lodging runs a similar or greater risk of violent confrontation. See Spring, 80 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1462 (noting the greater risk of confrontation in a mobile home or camper, where \xe2\x80\x9cit is more difficult for the burglar to enter or escape unnoticed\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Although, as respondents point out, the risk of violence is diminished if, for example, a vehicle is only used for lodging part of the time, we have no reason to believe that Congress intended to make a part-time/full-time distinction. After all, a burglary is no less a burglary because it took place at a summer home during the winter, or a commercial building during a holiday. Cf. Model Penal Code \xc2\xa7221.1, Comment 3(b), p. 72 (burglary should cover places with the \xe2\x80\x9capparent potential for regular occupancy\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Respondents make several additional arguments. Respondent Stitt argues that the Tennessee statute is too broad even under the Government\xe2\x80\x99s definition of generic burglary. That is so, Stitt contends, because the statute covers the burglary of a \xe2\x80\x9cstructure appurtenant to or connected with\xe2\x80\x9d a covered structure or vehicle, a provision that Stitt reads to include the burglary of even ordinary vehicles that are plugged in or otherwise appurtenant to covered structures. Tenn. Code Ann. \xc2\xa739\xe2\x80\x9314\xe2\x80\x93401(1)(C). Stitt\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, however, ignores that the \xe2\x80\x9cappurtenant to\xe2\x80\x9d provision extends only to \xe2\x80\x9cstructure[s],\xe2\x80\x9d not to the separate statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cvehicle[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We therefore disagree with Stitt\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the \xe2\x80\x9cappurtenant to\xe2\x80\x9d provision sweeps more broadly than generic burglary, as defined in Taylor, 495 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 598.

\n

Respondents also point out that in Taylor, Mathis, and other cases, we said that burglary of certain nontypical structures and vehicles fell outside the scope of the federal Act\xe2\x80\x99s statutory word \xe2\x80\x9cburglary.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Taylor, 495 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 599 (noting that some States \xe2\x80\x9cdefine burglary more broadly\xe2\x80\x9d than generic burglary by, for example, \xe2\x80\x9cincluding places, such as automobiles and vending machines, other than buildings\xe2\x80\x9d). And they argue that the vehicles covered here are analogous to the nontypical structures and vehicles to which the Court referred in those cases. Our examination of those cases, however, convinces us that we did not decide in either case the question now before us.

\n

In Taylor, for example, we referred to a Missouri breaking and entering statute that among other things criminalized breaking and entering \xe2\x80\x9cany boat or vessel, or railroad car.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (citing Mo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7560.070 (1969); emphasis added). We did say that that particular provision was beyond the scope of the federal Act. But the statute used the word \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d; it referred to ordinary boats and vessels often at sea (and railroad cars often filled with cargo, not people), nowhere restricting its coverage, as here, to vehicles or structures customarily used or adapted for overnight accommodation. The statutes before us, by using these latter words, more clearly focus upon circumstances where burglary is likely to present a serious risk of violence.

\n

In Mathis, we considered an Iowa statute that covered \xe2\x80\x9cany building, structure, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. land, water or air vehicle, or similar place adapted for overnight accommodation of persons [or used] for the storage or safekeeping of anything of value.\xe2\x80\x9d Iowa Code \xc2\xa7702.12 (2013). Courts have construed that statute to cover ordinary vehicles because they can be used for storage or safekeeping. See State v. Buss, 325 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 384 (Iowa 1982); Weaver v. Iowa, 949 F.\xc2\xa02d 1049 (CA8 1991). That is presumably why, as we wrote in our opinion, \xe2\x80\x9call parties agree[d]\xe2\x80\x9d that Iowa\xe2\x80\x99s burglary statute \xe2\x80\x9ccovers more conduct than generic burglary does.\xe2\x80\x9d Mathis, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 5). The question before us was whether federal generic \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d includes within its scope a burglary statute that lists multiple, alternative means of satisfying one element, some of which fall within Taylor\xe2\x80\x99s generic definition and some of which fall outside it. We held, in light of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement that the Iowa statute covered some \xe2\x80\x9coutside\xe2\x80\x9d behavior (i.e., ordinary vehicles), that the statute did not count as a generic burglary statute. But for present purposes, what matters is that the Court in Mathis did not decide the question now before us\xe2\x80\x94that is, whether coverage of vehicles designed or adapted for overnight use takes the statute outside the generic burglary definition. We now decide that latter question, and, for the reasons we have stated, we hold that it does not.

\n

III

\n

Respondent Sims argues that Arkansas\xe2\x80\x99 residential burglary statute is too broad to count as generic burglary for a different reason, namely, because it also covers burglary of \xe2\x80\x9ca vehicle .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [i]n which any person lives.\xe2\x80\x9d See supra, at 3. Sims adds that these words might cover a car in which a homeless person occasionally sleeps. Sims\xe2\x80\x99 argument rests in part upon state law, and the lower courts have not considered it. As \xe2\x80\x9cwe are a court of review, not of first view,\xe2\x80\x9d Cutter v. Wilkinson,\n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 709, 718, n.\xc2\xa07 (2005), we remand the Arkansas case to the lower courts for further proceedings. Those courts remain free to determine whether Sims properly presented the argument and to decide the merits, if appropriate.

\n

We reverse the judgment of the Sixth Circuit Court of Appeals. We vacate the judgment of the Eighth Circuit Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
