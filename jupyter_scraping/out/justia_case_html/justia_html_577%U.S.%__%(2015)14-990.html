SHAPIRO et\xc2\xa0al. v. McMANUS, CHAIRMAN, MARYLAND STATE BOARD OF ELECTIONS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fourth circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93990.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 4, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided December 8, 2015

\n

Since 1976, federal law has mandated that a \xe2\x80\x9cdistrict court of three judges shall be convened .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. when an action is filed challenging the constitutionality of the apportionment of congressional districts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72284(a), and has provided that \xe2\x80\x9cthe judge [presented with a request for a three-judge court] shall, unless he determines that three judges are not required, immediately notify the chief judge of the circuit, who shall designate two other judges\xe2\x80\x9d to serve, \xc2\xa72284(b)(1).

\n

Petitioners requested that a three-judge court be convened to consider their claim that Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s 2011 congressional redistricting plan burdens their\n \nFirst Amendment right of political association. Concluding that no relief could be granted for this claim, the District Judge dismissed the action instead of notifying the Chief Judge of the Circuit to convene a three-judge court. The Fourth Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section 2284 entitles petitioners to make their case before a three-judge court. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Section 2284(a)\xe2\x80\x99s prescription could not be clearer. Because the present suit is indisputably \xe2\x80\x9can action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. challenging the constitutionality of the apportionment of congressional districts,\xe2\x80\x9d the District Judge was required to refer the case to a three-judge court. Section 2284(a) admits of no exception, and \xe2\x80\x9cthe mandatory \xe2\x80\x98shall\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. normally creates an obligation impervious to judicial discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d Lexecon Inc. v. Milberg Weiss Bershad Hynes & Lerach,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 26\n. The subsequent provision of \xc2\xa72284(b)(1), that the district judge shall commence the process for appointment of a three-judge panel \xe2\x80\x9cunless he determines that three judges are not required,\xe2\x80\x9d should be read not as a grant of discretion to the district judge to ignore \xc2\xa72284(a), but as a compatible administrative detail requiring district judges to \xe2\x80\x9cdetermin[e]\xe2\x80\x9d only whether the \xe2\x80\x9crequest for three judges\xe2\x80\x9d is made in a case covered by \xc2\xa72284(a). This conclusion is bolstered by \xc2\xa72284(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s explicit command that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] single judge shall not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. enter judgment on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 alternative argument, that the District Judge should have dismissed petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claim as \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutionally insubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d under Goosby v. Osser,\n\n409 U.\xc2\xa0S. 512\n, is unpersuasive. This Court has long distinguished between failing to raise a substantial federal question for jurisdictional purposes\xe2\x80\x94what Goosby addressed\xe2\x80\x94and failing to state a claim for relief on the merits\xe2\x80\x94what the District Judge found here; only \xe2\x80\x9cwholly insubstantial and frivolous\xe2\x80\x9d claims implicate the former, Bell v. Hood,\n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678\n\xe2\x80\x93683. Absent such obvious frivolity, \xe2\x80\x9cthe failure to state a proper cause of action calls for a judgment on the merits and not for a dismissal for want of jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 682. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 plea for relief, which was based on a legal theory put forward in Justice Kennedy\xe2\x80\x99s concurrence in Vieth v. Jubelirer,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267\n, and uncontradicted in subsequent majority opinions, easily clears Goosby\xe2\x80\x99s low bar. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

584 Fed. Appx. 140, reversed and remanded.

STEPHEN M. SHAPIRO, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v.DAVID J. McMANUS, JR., CHAIRMAN, MARYLAND STATE BOARD OF ELECTIONS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fourth circuit

\n

[December 8, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

We consider under what circumstances, if any, a district judge is free to \xe2\x80\x9cdetermin[e] that three judges are not required\xe2\x80\x9d for an action \xe2\x80\x9cchallenging the constitutionality of the apportionment of congressional districts.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72284(a), (b)(1).

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Rare today, three-judge district courts were more common in the decades before 1976, when they were required for various adjudications, including the grant of an \xe2\x80\x9cinterlocutory or permanent injunction restraining the enforcement, operation or execution of any State statute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. upon the ground of the unconstitutionality of such statute.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72281 (1970 ed.), repealed, Pub. L. 94\xe2\x80\x93381, \xc2\xa71,\n\n90Stat.\n1119. See Currie, The Three-Judge District Courtin Constitutional Litigation, 32 U. Chi. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 3\xe2\x80\x9312 (1964). Decisions of three-judge courts could, then as now, be appealed as of right directly to this Court.\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71253.

\n

In 1976, Congress substantially curtailed the circumstances under which a three-judge court is required. It was no longer required for the grant of an injunction against state statutes, see Pub. L. 94\xe2\x80\x93381, \xc2\xa71,\n\n90Stat.\n1119 (repealing\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72281), but was mandated for \xe2\x80\x9can action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. challenging the constitutionality of the apportionment of congressional districts or the apportionment of any statewide legislative body.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa73, now codified at\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72284(a).

\n

Simultaneously, Congress amended the procedures governing three-judge district courts. The prior statute had provided: \xe2\x80\x9cThe district judge to whom the application for injunction or other relief is presented shall constitute one member of [the three-judge] court. On the filing of the application, he shall immediately notify the chief judge of the circuit, who shall designate two other judges\xe2\x80\x9d to serve.\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72284(1) (1970 ed.). The amended statute provides: \xe2\x80\x9cUpon the filing of a request for three judges, the judge to whom the request is presented shall, unless he determines that three judges are not required, immediately notify the chief judge of the circuit, who shall designate two other judges\xe2\x80\x9d to serve.\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72284(b)(1) (2012 ed.) (emphasis added). The dispute here concerns the scope of the italicized text.

\n

B

\n

In response to the 2010 Census, Maryland enacted a statute in October 2011 establishing\xe2\x80\x94or, more pejora-tively, gerrymandering\xe2\x80\x94the districts for the State\xe2\x80\x99s eight congressional seats. Dissatisfied with the crazy-quilt results, see App. to Pet. for Cert. 23a, petitioners, a bipartisan group of citizens, filed suit pro\xc2\xa0se in Federal District Court. Their amended complaint alleges, inter alia, that Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s redistricting plan burdens their\n \nFirst Amendment right of political association. Petitioners also requested that a three-judge court be convened to hear the case.

\n

The District Judge, however, thought the claim \xe2\x80\x9cnot one for which relief can be granted.\xe2\x80\x9d Benisek v. Mack, 11 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa03d 516, 526 (Md. 2014). \xe2\x80\x9c[N]othing about the congressional districts at issue in this case affects in any proscribed way [petitioners\xe2\x80\x99] ability to participate in the political debate in any of the Maryland congressional districts in which they might find themselves. They are free to join preexisting political committees, form new ones, or use whatever other means are at their disposal to influence the opinions of their congressional representatives.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (brackets, ellipsis, and internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

For that reason, instead of notifying the Chief Judge of the Circuit of the need for a three-judge court, the District Judge dismissed the action. The Fourth Circuit summar-ily affirmed in an unpublished disposition. Benisek v. Mack, 584 Fed. Appx. 140 (CA4 2014). Seeking review in this Court, petitioners pointed out that at least two other Circuits consider it reversible error for a district judge to dismiss a case under \xc2\xa72284 for failure to state a claim for relief rather than refer it for transfer to a three-judge court. See LaRouche v. Fowler, 152 F.\xc2\xa03d 974, 981\xe2\x80\x93983 (CADC 1998); LULAC v. Texas, 113 F.\xc2\xa03d 53, 55\xe2\x80\x9356 (CA5 1997) (per curiam). We granted certiorari. Shapiro v. Mack, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

II

\n

Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 sole contention is that the District Judge had no authority to dismiss the case rather than initiate the procedures to convene a three-judge court. Not so, argue respondents; the 1976 addition to \xc2\xa72284(b)(1) of the clause \xe2\x80\x9cunless he determines that three judges are not required\xe2\x80\x9d is precisely such a grant of authority. Moreover, say respondents, Congress declined to specify a standard to constrain the exercise of this authority. Choosing, as the District Judge did, the familiar standard for dismissal under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6) best serves the purposes of a three-judge court, which (in respondents\xe2\x80\x99 view) is to protect States from \xe2\x80\x9chasty, imprudent invalidation\xe2\x80\x9d of their statutes by rogue district judges acting alone. Brief for Respondents 27.

\n

Whatever the purposes of a three-judge court may be, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 argument needlessly produces a contradiction in the statutory text. That text\xe2\x80\x99s initial prescription could not be clearer: \xe2\x80\x9cA district court of three judges shall be convened .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. when an action is filed challenging the constitutionality of the apportionment of congressional districts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72284(a) (emphasis added). Nobody disputes that the present suit is \xe2\x80\x9can action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. challenging the constitutionality of the apportionment of congressional districts.\xe2\x80\x9d It follows that the district judge was required to refer the case to a three-judge court, for \xc2\xa72284(a) admits of no exception, and \xe2\x80\x9cthe mandatory \xe2\x80\x98shall\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. normally creates an obligation impervious to judicial discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d Lexecon Inc. v. Milberg Weiss Bershad Hynes & Lerach,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 26,\n35 (1998)\n; see also National Assn. of Home Builders v. Defenders of Wildlife,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 644\n\xe2\x80\x93662 (2007) (same).

\n

The subsequent provision of \xc2\xa72284(b)(1), that the district judge shall commence the process for appointment of a three-judge panel \xe2\x80\x9cunless he determines that three judges are not required,\xe2\x80\x9d need not and therefore should not be read as a grant of discretion to the district judge to ignore \xc2\xa72284(a). It is not even framed as a proviso, or an exception from that provision, but rather as an administrative detail that is entirely compatible with \xc2\xa72284(a). The old \xc2\xa72284(1) triggered the district judge\xe2\x80\x99s duty to refer the matter for the convening of a three-judge court \xe2\x80\x9c[o]n the filing of the application\xe2\x80\x9d to enjoin an unconstitutional state law. By contrast, the current \xc2\xa72284(b)(1) triggers the district judge\xe2\x80\x99s duty \xe2\x80\x9c[u]pon the filing of a request for three judges\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added). But of course a party may\xe2\x80\x94whether in good faith or bad, through ignorance or hope or malice\xe2\x80\x94file a request for a three-judge court even if the case does not merit one under \xc2\xa72284(a). Section 2284(b)(1) merely clarifies that a district judge need not unthinkingly initiate the procedures to convene a three-judge court without first examining the allegations in the complaint. In short, all the district judge must \xe2\x80\x9cdetermin[e]\xe2\x80\x9d is whether the \xe2\x80\x9crequest for three judges\xe2\x80\x9d is made in a case covered by \xc2\xa72284(a)\xe2\x80\x94no more, no less.

\n

That conclusion is bolstered by \xc2\xa72284(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s explicit command that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] single judge shall not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. enter judgment on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d It would be an odd interpretation that allowed a district judge to do under \xc2\xa72284(b)(1) what he is forbidden to do under \xc2\xa72284(b)(3). More likely that Congress intended a three-judge court, and not a single district judge, to enter all final judgments in cases satisfying the criteria of \xc2\xa72284(a).

\n

III

\n

Respondents argue in the alternative that a district judge is not required to refer a case for the convening of a three-judge court if the constitutional claim is (as they assert petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claim to be) \xe2\x80\x9cinsubstantial.\xe2\x80\x9d In Goosby v. Osser,\n\n409 U.\xc2\xa0S. 512 (1973)\n, we stated that the filing of a \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutionally insubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d claim did not trigger the three-judge-court requirement under the pre-1976 statu-tory regime. Id., at 518. Goosby rested not on an interpretation of statutory text, but on the familiar proposition that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n the absence of diversity of citizenship, it is essential to jurisdiction that a substantial federal question should be presented.\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte Poresky,\n\n290 U.\xc2\xa0S. 30,\n31 (1933)\n (per curiam) (emphasis added). Absent a substantial federal question, even a single-judge district court lacks jurisdiction, and \xe2\x80\x9c[a] three-judge court is not required where the district court itself lacks jurisdiction of the complaint or the complaint is not justiciable in the federal courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzalez v. Automatic Employees Credit Union,\n\n419 U.\xc2\xa0S. 90,\n100 (1974)\n.

\n

In the present case, however, the District Judge dismissed petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 complaint not because he thought he lacked jurisdiction, but because he concluded that the allegations failed to state a claim for relief on the merits, citing Ashcroft v. Iqbal,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 662 (2009)\n, and Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly,\n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 544 (2007)\n. See 11 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa03d, at 520. That was in accord with Fourth Circuit precedent, which holds that where the \xe2\x80\x9cpleadings do not state a claim, then by definition they are insubstantial and so properly are subject to dismissal by the district court without convening a three-judge court.\xe2\x80\x9d Duckworth v. State Admin. Bd. of Election Laws, 332 F.\xc2\xa03d 769, 772\xe2\x80\x93773 (CA4 2003) (emphasis added).

\n

We think this standard both too demanding and inconsistent with our precedents. \xe2\x80\x9c[C]onstitutional claims will not lightly be found insubstantial for purposes of\xe2\x80\x9d the three-judge-court statute. Washington v. Confederated Tribes of Colville Reservation,\n\n447 U.\xc2\xa0S. 134\n\xe2\x80\x93148 (1980). We have long distinguished between failing to raise a substantial federal question for jurisdictional purposes\xe2\x80\x94which is what Goosby addressed\xe2\x80\x94and failing to state a claim for relief on the merits; only \xe2\x80\x9cwholly insubstantial and frivolous\xe2\x80\x9d claims implicate the former. Bell v. Hood,\n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678\n\xe2\x80\x93683 (1946); see also Hannis Distilling Co. v. Mayor and City Council of Baltimore,\n\n216 U.\xc2\xa0S. 285,\n288 (1910)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cobviously frivolous or plainly insubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d); Bailey v. Patterson,\n\n369 U.\xc2\xa0S. 31,\n33 (1962)\n (per curiam) (\xe2\x80\x9cwholly insubstantial,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9clegally speaking non-existent,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cessentially fictitious\xe2\x80\x9d); Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83,\n89 (1998)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cfrivolous or immaterial\xe2\x80\x9d). Absent such frivolity, \xe2\x80\x9cthe failure to state a proper cause of action calls for a judgment on the merits and not for a dismissal for want of jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Bell, supra, at 682. Consistent with this principle, Goosby clarified that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[c]onstitutional insubstantiality\xe2\x80\x99 for this purpose has been equated with such concepts as \xe2\x80\x98essen-tially fictitious,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98wholly insubstantial,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98obviously frivolous,\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98obviously without merit.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 409 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 518 (citations omitted). And the adverbs were no mere throwaways; \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he limiting words \xe2\x80\x98wholly\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98obviously\xe2\x80\x99 have cogent legal significance.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Without expressing any view on the merits of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claim, we believe it easily clears Goosby\xe2\x80\x99s low bar; after all, the amended complaint specifically challenges Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s apportionment \xe2\x80\x9calong the lines suggested by Justice Kennedy in his concurrence in Vieth [v. Jubelirer,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267 (2004)\n].\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Brief in Opposition 44. Although the Vieth plurality thought all political gerrymandering claims nonjusticiable, Justice Kennedy, concurring in the judgment, surmised that if \xe2\x80\x9ca State did impose burdens and restrictions on groups or persons by reason of their views, there would likely be a\n \nFirst Amendment violation, unless the State shows some compelling interest. .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Where it is alleged that a gerrymander had the purpose and effect of imposing burdens on a disfavored party and its voters, the\n \nFirst Amendment may offer a sounder and more prudential basis for intervention than does the Equal Protection Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Vieth v. Jubelirer,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267,\n315 (2004)\n. Whatever \xe2\x80\x9cwholly insubstantial,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cobviously frivolous,\xe2\x80\x9d etc., mean, at a minimum they cannot include a plea for relief based on a legal theory put forward by a Justice of this Court and uncontradicted by the majority in any of our cases. Accordingly, the District Judge should not have dismissed the claim as \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutionally insubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d under Goosby. Perhaps petitioners will ultimately fail on the merits of their suit, but \xc2\xa72284 entitles them to make their case before a three-judge district court.

\n

\n

The judgment of the Fourth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.
