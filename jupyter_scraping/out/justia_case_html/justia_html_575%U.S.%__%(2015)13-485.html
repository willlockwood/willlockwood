Syllabus

\n

comptroller of the treasury of maryland v. wynne et ux.

\n

certiorari to the court of appeals of maryland

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93485.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 12, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 18, 2015

\n

Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s personal income tax on state residents consists of a \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x9d income tax, Md. Tax-Gen. Code Ann. \xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93105(a), and a \xe2\x80\x9ccounty\xe2\x80\x9d income tax, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93103, 10\xe2\x80\x93106. Residents who pay income tax to another jurisdiction for income earned in that other jurisdiction are allowed a credit against the \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x9d tax but not the \xe2\x80\x9ccounty\xe2\x80\x9d tax. \xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93703. Nonresidents who earn income from sources within Maryland must pay the \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x9d income tax, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93105(d), 10\xe2\x80\x93210, and nonresidents not subject to the county tax must pay a \xe2\x80\x9cspecial nonresident tax\xe2\x80\x9d in lieu of the \xe2\x80\x9ccounty\xe2\x80\x9d tax, \xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93106.1.

\n

Respondents, Maryland residents, earned pass-through income from a Subchapter S corporation that earned income in several States. Respondents claimed an income tax credit on their 2006 Maryland income tax return for taxes paid to other States. The Mary-land State Comptroller of the Treasury, petitioner here, allowed respondents a credit against their \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x9d income tax but not against their \xe2\x80\x9ccounty\xe2\x80\x9d income tax and assessed a tax deficiency. That decision was affirmed by the Hearings and Appeals Section of the Comptroller\xe2\x80\x99s Office and by the Maryland Tax Court, but the Circuit Court for Howard County reversed on the ground that Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax system violated the Commerce Clause of the Federal Constitution. The Court of Appeals of Maryland affirmed and held that the tax unconstitutionally discriminated against interstate commerce.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s personal income tax scheme violates the dormant Commerce Clause. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9328.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The Commerce Clause, which grants Congress power to \xe2\x80\x9cregulate Commerce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0among the several States,\xe2\x80\x9d Art I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa03, also has \xe2\x80\x9ca further, negative command, known as the dormant Commerce Clause,\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Jefferson Lines, Inc., 514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 175, 179, which precludes States from \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[ing] between transactions on the basis of some interstate element,\xe2\x80\x9d Boston Stock Exchange v. State Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 318\n, n.\xc2\xa012. Thus, inter alia, a State \xe2\x80\x9cmay not tax a transaction or incident more heavily when it crosses state lines than when it occurs entirely within the State,\xe2\x80\x9d Armco Inc. v. Hardesty,\n\n467 U. S. 638\n, or \xe2\x80\x9cimpose a tax which discriminates against interstate commerce either by providing a direct commercial advantage to local business, or by subjecting interstate commerce to the burden of \xe2\x80\x98multiple taxation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Northwestern States Portland Cement Co. v. Minnesota,\n\n358 U. S. 450\n. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The result in this case is all but dictated by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s dormant Commerce Clause cases, particularly J. D. Adams Mfg. Co. v. Storen,\n\n304 U.\xc2\xa0S. 307\n, Gwin, White & Prince, Inc. v. Henneford,\n\n305 U.\xc2\xa0S. 434\n, and Central Greyhound Lines, Inc. v. Mealey,\n\n334 U.\xc2\xa0S. 653\n, which all invalidated state tax schemes that might lead to double taxation of out-of-state income and that discriminated in favor of intrastate over interstate economic activity. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0This conclusion is not affected by the fact that these three cases involved a tax on gross receipts rather than net income, and a tax on corporations rather than individuals. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions have previously rejected the formal distinction between gross receipts and net income taxes. And there is no reason the dormant Commerce Clause should treat individuals less favorably than corporations; in addition, the taxes invalidated in J.\xc2\xa0D. Adams and Gwin, White applied to the income of both individuals and corporations. Nor does the right of the individual to vote in political elections justify disparate treatment of corporate and personal income. Thus the Court has previously entertained and even sustained dormant Commerce Clause challenges by individual residents of the State that imposed the alleged burden on interstate commerce. See Department of Revenue of Ky. v. Davis,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 328\n; Granholm v. Heald,\n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 460,\n469 (2005)\n. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(d) Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme is not immune from dormant Commerce Clause scrutiny simply because Maryland has the jurisdictional power under the Due Process Clause to impose the tax. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hile a state may, consistent with the Due Process Clause, have the authority to tax a particular taxpayer, imposition of the tax may nonetheless violate the Commerce Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Quill Corp. v. North Dakota,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 298\n. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

(e)\xc2\xa0Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s income tax scheme discriminates against interstate commerce. The \xe2\x80\x9cinternal consistency\xe2\x80\x9d test, which helps courts identify tax schemes that discriminate against interstate commerce, as-sumes that every State has the same tax structure. Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s income tax scheme fails the internal consistency test because if every State adopted Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax structure, interstate commerce would be taxed at a higher rate than intrastate commerce. Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme is inherently discriminatory and operates as a tariff, which is fatal because tariffs are \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he paradigmatic example of a law discriminating against interstate commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d West Lynn Creamery, Inc. v. Healy,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 186\n. Petitioner emphasizes that by offering residents who earn income in interstate commerce a credit against the \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x9d portion of the income tax, Maryland actually receives less tax revenue from residents who earn income from interstate commerce rather than intrastate commerce, but this argument is a red herring. The critical point is that the total tax burden on interstate commerce is higher. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9326.

\n

431 Md. 147, 64 A. 3d 453, affirmed.

COMPTROLLER OF THE TREASURY OF MARYLAND,PETITIONER v. BRIAN WYNNE et\xc2\xa0ux.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the court of appeals of maryland

\n

[May 18, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case involves the constitutionality of an unusual feature of Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s personal income tax scheme. Like many other States, Maryland taxes the income its residents earn both within and outside the State, as well as the income that nonresidents earn from sources within Maryland. But unlike most other States, Maryland does not offer its residents a full credit against the income taxes that they pay to other States. The effect of this scheme is that some of the income earned by Maryland residents outside the State is taxed twice. Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s scheme creates an incentive for taxpayers to opt for intrastate rather than interstate economic activity.

\n

We have long held that States cannot subject corporate income to tax schemes similar to Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s, and we see no reason why income earned by individuals should be treated less favorably. Maryland admits that its law has the same economic effect as a state tariff, the quintessential evil targeted by the dormant Commerce Clause. We therefore affirm the decision of Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s highest court and hold that this feature of the State\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme vio-lates the Federal Constitution.

\n

I

\n

Maryland, like most States, raises revenue in part by levying a personal income tax. The income tax that Maryland imposes upon its own residents has two parts: a \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x9d income tax, which is set at a graduated rate, Md. Tax-Gen. Code Ann. \xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93105(a) (Supp. 2014), and a so-called \xe2\x80\x9ccounty\xe2\x80\x9d income tax, which is set at a rate that varies by county but is capped at 3.2%, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93103, 10\xe2\x80\x93106 (2010). Despite the names that Maryland has assigned to these taxes, both are State taxes, and both are collected by the State\xe2\x80\x99s Comptroller of the Treasury. Frey v. Comptroller of Treasury, 422 Md. 111, 125, 141\xe2\x80\x93142, 29 A. 3d 475, 483, 492 (2011). Of course, some Maryland residents earn income in other States, and some of those States also tax this income. If Maryland residents pay income tax to another jurisdiction for income earned there, Maryland allows them a credit against the \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x9d tax but not the \xe2\x80\x9ccounty\xe2\x80\x9d tax. \xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93703; 431 Md. 147, 156\xe2\x80\x93157, 64 A. 3d 453, 458 (2013) (case below). As a result, part of the income that a Maryland resident earns outside the State may be taxed twice.

\n

Maryland also taxes the income of nonresidents. This tax has two parts. First, nonresidents must pay the \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x9d income tax on all the income that they earn from sources within Maryland. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93105(d) (Supp. 2014), 10\xe2\x80\x93210 (2010). Second, nonresidents not subject to the county tax must pay a \xe2\x80\x9cspecial nonresident tax\xe2\x80\x9d in lieu of the \xe2\x80\x9ccounty\xe2\x80\x9d tax. \xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93106.1; Frey, supra, at 125\xe2\x80\x93126, 29 A. 3d, at 483. The \xe2\x80\x9cspecial nonresident tax\xe2\x80\x9d is levied on income earned from sources within Maryland, and its rate is \xe2\x80\x9cequal to the lowest county income tax rate set by any Maryland county.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93106.1. Maryland does not tax the income that nonresidents earn from sources outside Maryland. See \xc2\xa710\xe2\x80\x93210.

\n

Respondents Brian and Karen Wynne are Maryland residents. In 2006, the relevant tax year, Brian Wynne owned stock in Maxim Healthcare Services, Inc., a Subchapter S corporation.[1] That year, Maxim earned income in States other than Maryland, and it filed state income tax returns in 39 States. The Wynnes earned income passed through to them from Maxim. On their 2006 Mary-land tax return, the Wynnes claimed an income tax credit for income taxes paid to other States.

\n

Petitioner, the Maryland State Comptroller of the Treasury, denied this claim and assessed a tax deficiency. In accordance with Maryland law, the Comptroller allowed the Wynnes a credit against their Maryland \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x9d income tax but not against their \xe2\x80\x9ccounty\xe2\x80\x9d income tax. The Hearings and Appeals Section of the Comptroller\xe2\x80\x99s Office slightly modified the assessment but otherwise affirmed. The Maryland Tax Court also affirmed, but the Circuit Court for Howard County reversed on the ground that Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax system violated the Commerce Clause.

\n

The Court of Appeals of Maryland affirmed. 431 Md. 147, 64 A. 3d 453. That court evaluated the tax under the four-part test of Complete Auto Transit, Inc. v. Brady, 430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 274 (1977), which asks whether a \xe2\x80\x9ctax is applied to an activity with a substantial nexus with the taxing State, is fairly apportioned, does not discriminate against interstate commerce, and is fairly related to the services provided by the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 279. The Court of Appeals held that the tax failed both the fair apportionment and nondiscrimination parts of the Complete Auto test. With respect to fair apportionment, the court first held that the tax failed the \xe2\x80\x9cinternal consistency\xe2\x80\x9d test because if every State adopted Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme, interstate commerce would be taxed at a higher rate than intrastate commerce. It then held that the tax failed the \xe2\x80\x9cexternal consistency\xe2\x80\x9d test because it created a risk of multiple taxation. With respect to nondiscrimination, the court held that the tax discriminated against interstate commerce because it denied residents a credit on income taxes paid to other States and so taxed income earned interstate at a rate higher than income earned intrastate. The court thus concluded that Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme was unconstitutional insofar as it denied the Wynnes a credit against the \xe2\x80\x9ccounty\xe2\x80\x9d tax for income taxes they paid to other States. Two judges dissented and argued that the tax did not violate the Commerce Clause. The Court of Appeals later issued a brief clarification that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] state may avoid discrimination against interstate commerce by providing a tax credit, or some other method of apportionment, to avoid discriminating against interstate commerce in violation of the dormant Commerce Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d 431 Md., at 189, 64 A. 3d at 478.

\n

We granted certiorari. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

The Commerce Clause grants Congress power to \xe2\x80\x9cregulate Commerce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. among the several States.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa08, cl.\xc2\xa03. These \xe2\x80\x9cfew simple words .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. reflected a central concern of the Framers that was an immediate reason for calling the Constitutional Convention: the conviction that in order to succeed, the new Union would have to avoid the tendencies toward economic Balkanization that had plagued relations among the Colonies and later among the States under the Articles of Confederation.\xe2\x80\x9d Hughes v. Oklahoma,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 322\n\xe2\x80\x93326 (1979). Although the Clause is framed as a positive grant of power to Congress, \xe2\x80\x9cwe have consistently held this language to contain a further, negative command, known as the dormant Commerce Clause, prohibiting certain state taxation even when Congress has failed to legislate on the subject.\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Jefferson Lines, Inc.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 175,\n179 (1995)\n.

\n

This interpretation of the Commerce Clause has been disputed. See Camps Newfound/Owatonna, Inc. v. Town of Harrison,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564\n\xe2\x80\x93620 (1997) (Thomas, J., dissenting); Tyler Pipe Industries, Inc. v. Washington State Dept. of Revenue,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 232\n\xe2\x80\x93265 (1987) (Scalia, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part); License Cases, 5 How. 504, 578\xe2\x80\x93579 (1847) (Taney, C. J.). But it also has deep roots. See, e.g., Case of the State Freight Tax, 15 Wall. 232, 279\xe2\x80\x93280 (1873); Cooley v. Board of Wardens of Port of Philadelphia ex rel. Soc. for Relief of Distressed Pilots, 12 How. 299, 318\xe2\x80\x93319 (1852); Gibbons v. Ogden, 9 Wheat. 1, 209 (1824) (Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J.). By prohibiting States from discriminating against or imposing excessive burdens on interstate commerce without congressional approval, it strikes at one of the chief evils that led to the adoption of the Constitution, namely, state tariffs and other laws that burdened interstate commerce. Fulton Corp. v. Faulkner,\n\n516 U.\xc2\xa0S. 325\n\xe2\x80\x93331 (1996); Hughes, supra, at 325; Welton v. Missouri,\n\n91 U.\xc2\xa0S. 275,\n280 (1876)\n; see also The Federalist Nos. 7, 11 (A. Hamilton), and 42 (J. Madison).

\n

Under our precedents, the dormant Commerce Clause precludes States from \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[ing] between transactions on the basis of some interstate element.\xe2\x80\x9d Boston Stock Exchange v. State Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 318\n, n.\xc2\xa012 (1977). This means, among other things, that a State \xe2\x80\x9cmay not tax a transaction or incident more heavily when it crosses state lines than when it occurs entirely within the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Armco Inc. v. Hardesty,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 638,\n642 (1984)\n. \xe2\x80\x9cNor may a State impose a tax which discriminates against interstate commerce either by providing a direct commercial advantage to local business, or by subjecting interstate commerce to the burden of \xe2\x80\x98multiple taxation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Northwestern States Portland Cement Co. v. Minnesota,\n\n358 U.\xc2\xa0S. 450,\n458 (1959)\n (citations omitted).

\n

B

\n

Our existing dormant Commerce Clause cases all but dictate the result reached in this case by Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s highest court. Three cases involving the taxation of the income of domestic corporations are particularly instructive.

\n

In J. D. Adams Mfg. Co. v. Storen,\n\n304 U.\xc2\xa0S. 307 (1938)\n, Indiana taxed the income of every Indiana resident (including individuals) and the income that every nonresident derived from sources within Indiana. Id., at 308. The State levied the tax on income earned by the plaintiff Indiana corporation on sales made out of the State. Id., at 309. Holding that this scheme violated the dormant Commerce Clause, we explained that the \xe2\x80\x9cvice of the statute\xe2\x80\x9d was that it taxed, \xe2\x80\x9cwithout apportionment, receipts derived from activities in interstate commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 311. If these receipts were also taxed by the States in which the sales occurred, we warned, interstate commerce would be subjected \xe2\x80\x9cto the risk of a double tax burden to which intrastate commerce is not exposed, and which the commerce clause forbids.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The next year, in Gwin, White & Prince, Inc. v. Henneford,\n\n305 U.\xc2\xa0S. 434 (1939)\n, we reached a similar result. In that case, the State of Washington taxed all the income of persons doing business in the State. Id., at 435. Washington levied that tax on income that the plaintiff Washington corporation earned in shipping fruit from Washington to other States and foreign countries. Id., at 436\xe2\x80\x93437. This tax, we wrote, \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates against interstate commerce, since it imposes upon it, merely because interstate commerce is being done, the risk of a multiple burden to which local commerce is not exposed.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 439.

\n

In the third of these cases involving the taxation of a domestic corporation, Central Greyhound Lines, Inc. v. Mealey,\n\n334 U.\xc2\xa0S. 653 (1948)\n, New York sought to tax the portion of a domiciliary bus company\xe2\x80\x99s gross receipts that were derived from services provided in neighboring States. Id., at 660; see also id., at 665 (Murphy, J., dissenting) (stating that the plaintiff was a New York corporation). Noting that these other States might also attempt to tax this portion of the company\xe2\x80\x99s gross receipts, the Court held that the New York scheme violated the dormant Commerce Clause because it imposed an \xe2\x80\x9cunfair burden\xe2\x80\x9d on interstate commerce. Id., at 662 (majority opinion).

\n

In all three of these cases, the Court struck down a state tax scheme that might have resulted in the double taxation of income earned out of the State and that discriminated in favor of intrastate over interstate economic activity. As we will explain, see Part II\xe2\x80\x93F, infra, Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme is unconstitutional for similar reasons.

\n

C

\n

The principal dissent distinguishes these cases on the sole ground that they involved a tax on gross receipts rather than net income. We see no reason why the distinction between gross receipts and net income should matter, particularly in light of the admonition that we must consider \xe2\x80\x9cnot the formal language of the tax statute but rather its practical effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Complete Auto, 430 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 279. The principal dissent claims, post, at 13 (opinion of Ginsburg, J.), that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Court, historically, has taken the position that the difference between taxes on net income and taxes on gross receipts from interstate commerce warrants different results.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 C. Trost & P. Hartman, Federal Limitations on State and Local Taxation 2d \xc2\xa710:1, p. 251 (2003) (emphasis added) (hereinafter Trost). But this historical point is irrelevant. As the principal dissent seems to acknowledge, our cases rejected this formal distinction some time ago. And the distinction between gross receipts and net income taxes was not the basis for our decisions in J.\xc2\xa0D. Adams, Gwin, White, and Central Greyhound, which turned instead on the threat of multiple taxation.

\n

The discarded distinction between taxes on gross receipts and net income was based on the notion, endorsed in some early cases, that a tax on gross receipts is an impermissible \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and immediate burden\xe2\x80\x9d on interstate commerce, whereas a tax on net income is merely an \xe2\x80\x9cindirect and incidental\xe2\x80\x9d burden. United States Glue Co. v. Town of Oak Creek,\n\n247 U.\xc2\xa0S. 321\n\xe2\x80\x93329 (1918); see also Shaffer v. Carter,\n\n252 U.\xc2\xa0S. 37,\n57 (1920)\n. This arid distinction between direct and indirect burdens allowed \xe2\x80\x9cvery little coherent, trustworthy guidance as to tax valid-ity.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Trost \xc2\xa79:1, at 212. And so, beginning with Justice Stone\xe2\x80\x99s seminal opinion in Western Live Stock v. Bureau of Revenue,\n\n303 U.\xc2\xa0S. 250 (1938)\n, and continuing through cases like J.\xc2\xa0D. Adams and Gwin, White, the direct-indirect burdens test was replaced with a more practical approach that looked to the economic impact of the tax. These cases worked \xe2\x80\x9ca substantial judicial reinterpretation of the power of the States to levy taxes on gross income from interstate commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Trost \xc2\xa72:20, at 175.

\n

After a temporary reversion to our earlier formalism, see Spector Motor Service, Inc. v. O\xe2\x80\x99Connor,\n\n340 U.\xc2\xa0S. 602\n (1951), \xe2\x80\x9cthe gross receipts judicial pendulum has swung in a wide arc, recently reaching the place where taxation of gross receipts from interstate commerce is placed on an equal footing with receipts from local business, in Complete Auto Transit Inc. v. Brady,\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Trost \xc2\xa79:1, at 212. And we have now squarely rejected the argument that the Commerce Clause distinguishes between taxes on net and gross income. See Jefferson Lines, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 190 (explaining that the Court in Central Greyhound \xe2\x80\x9cunderstood the gross receipts tax to be simply a variety of tax on income\xe2\x80\x9d); Moorman Mfg. Co. v. Bair,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267,\n280 (1978)\n (rejecting a suggestion that the Commerce Clause distinguishes between gross receipts taxes and net income taxes); id., at 281 (Brennan, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cI agree with the Court that, for purposes of constitutional review, there is no distinction between a corporate income tax and a gross-receipts tax\xe2\x80\x9d); Complete Auto, supra, at 280 (upholding a gross receipts tax and rejecting the notion that the Commerce Clause places \xe2\x80\x9ca blanket prohibition against any state taxation imposed directly on an interstate transaction\xe2\x80\x9d).[2]

\n

For its part, petitioner distinguishes J. D. Adams, Gwin, White, and Central Greyhound on the ground that they concerned the taxation of corporations, not individuals. But it is hard to see why the dormant Commerce Clause should treat individuals less favorably than corporations. See Camps Newfound, 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 574 (\xe2\x80\x9cA tax on real estate, like any other tax, may impermissibly burden interstate commerce\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). In addition, the distinction between individuals and corporations cannot stand because the taxes invalidated in J.\xc2\xa0D.\xc2\xa0Adams and Gwin, White applied to the income of both individuals and corporations. See Ind. Stat. Ann., ch. 26, \xc2\xa764\xe2\x80\x932602 (Burns 1933) (tax in J.\xc2\xa0D. Adams); 1935 Wash. Sess. Laws ch. 180, Tit. II, \xc2\xa74(e), pp. 710\xe2\x80\x93711 (tax in Gwin, White).

\n

Attempting to explain why the dormant Commerce Clause should provide less protection for natural persons than for corporations, petitioner and the Solicitor General argue that States should have a free hand to tax their residents\xe2\x80\x99 out-of-state income because States provide their residents with many services. As the Solicitor General puts it, individuals \xe2\x80\x9creap the benefits of local roads, local police and fire protection, local public schools, [and] local health and welfare benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 30.

\n

This argument fails because corporations also benefit heavily from state and local services. Trucks hauling a corporation\xe2\x80\x99s supplies and goods, and vehicles transporting its employees, use local roads. Corporations call upon local police and fire departments to protect their facilities. Corporations rely on local schools to educate prospective employees, and the availability of good schools and other government services are features that may aid a corporation in attracting and retaining employees. Thus, disparate treatment of corporate and personal income cannot be justified based on the state services enjoyed by these two groups of taxpayers.

\n

The sole remaining attribute that, in the view of petitioner, distinguishes a corporation from an individual for present purposes is the right of the individual to vote. The principal dissent also emphasizes that residents can vote to change Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s discriminatory tax law. Post, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. The argument is that this Court need not be concerned about state laws that burden the interstate activities of individuals because those individuals can lobby and vote against legislators who support such measures. But if a State\xe2\x80\x99s tax unconstitutionally discriminates against interstate commerce, it is invalid regardless of whether the plaintiff is a resident voter or nonresident of the State. This Court has thus entertained and even sustained dormant Commerce Clause challenges by individual residents of the State that imposed the alleged burden on interstate commerce, Department of Revenue of Ky. v. Davis,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 328,\n336 (2008)\n; Granholm v. Heald,\n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 460,\n469 (2005)\n, and we have also sustained such a challenge to a tax whose burden was borne by in-state consumers, Bacchus Imports, Ltd. v. Dias,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 263,\n272 (1984)\n.[3]

\n

The principal dissent and Justice Scalia respond to these holdings by relying on dictum in Goldberg v. Sweet,\n\n488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 252,\n266 (1989)\n, that it is not the purpose of the dormant Commerce Clause \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to protect state residents from their own state taxes.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 3 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting); post, at 5 (Scalia, J., dissenting). But we repudiated that dictum in West Lynn Creamery, Inc. v. Healy,\n\n512 U. S. 186 (1994)\n, where we stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tate taxes are ordinarily paid by in-state businesses and consumers, yet if they discriminate against out-of-state products, they are unconstitutional.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 203. And, of course, the dictum must bow to the holdings of our many cases entertaining Commerce Clause challenges brought by residents. We find the dissents\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on Goldberg\xe2\x80\x99s dictum particularly inappropriate since they do not find themselves similarly bound by the rule of that case, which applied the internal consistency test to determine whether the tax at issue violated the dormant Commerce Clause. 488 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 261.

\n

In addition, the notion that the victims of such discrimination have a complete remedy at the polls is fanciful. It is likely that only a distinct minority of a State\xe2\x80\x99s residents earns income out of State. Schemes that discriminate against income earned in other States may be attractiveto legislators and a majority of their constituents for precisely this reason. It is even more farfetched to suggest that natural persons with out-of-state income are better able to influence state lawmakers than large corporations headquartered in the State. In short, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument would leave no security where the majority of voters prefer protectionism at the expense of the few who earn income interstate.

\n

It would be particularly incongruous in the present case to disregard our prior decisions regarding the taxation of corporate income because the income at issue here is a type of corporate income, namely, the income of a Subchapter S corporation. Only small businesses may incorporate under Subchapter S, and thus acceptance of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s submission would provide greater protection for income earned by large Subchapter C corporations than small businesses incorporated under Subchapter S.

\n

D

\n

In attempting to justify Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s unusual tax scheme, the principal dissent argues that the Commerce Clause imposes no limit on Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s ability to tax the income of its residents, no matter where that income is earned. It argues that Maryland has the sovereign power to tax all of the income of its residents, wherever earned, and it there-fore reasons that the dormant Commerce Clause cannot constrain Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s ability to expose its residents (and nonresidents) to the threat of double taxation.

\n

This argument confuses what a State may do without violating the Due Process Clause of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment with what it may do without violating the Commerce Clause. The Due Process Clause allows a State to tax \xe2\x80\x9call the income of its residents, even income earned outside the taxing jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Chickasaw Nation,\n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 450\n\xe2\x80\x93463 (1995). But \xe2\x80\x9cwhile a State may, consistent with the Due Process Clause, have the authority to tax a particular taxpayer, imposition of the tax may nonetheless violate the Commerce Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Quill Corp. v. North Dakota,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 298,\n305 (1992)\n (rejecting a due process challenge to a tax before sustaining a Commerce Clause challenge to that tax).

\n

Our decision in Camps Newfound illustrates the point. There, we held that the Commerce Clause prohibited Maine from granting more favorable tax treatment to charities that operated principally for the benefit of Maine residents. 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 580\xe2\x80\x93583. Because the plaintiff charity in that case was a Maine nonprofit corporation, there is no question that Maine had the raw jurisdictional power to tax the charity. See Chickasaw Nation, supra, at 462\xe2\x80\x93463. Nonetheless, the tax failed scrutiny under the Commerce Clause. Camps Newfound, supra, at 580\xe2\x80\x93581. Similarly, Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s raw power to tax its residents\xe2\x80\x99 out-of-state income does not insulate its tax scheme from scrutiny under the dormant Commerce Clause.

\n

Although the principal dissent claims the mantle of precedent, it is unable to identify a single case that endorses its essential premise, namely, that the Commerce Clause places no constraint on a State\xe2\x80\x99s power to tax the income of its residents wherever earned. This is unsurprising. As cases like Quill Corp. and Camps Newfound recognize, the fact that a State has the jurisdictional power to impose a tax says nothing about whether that tax violates the Commerce Clause. See also, e.g., Barclays Bank PLC v. Franchise Tax Bd. of Cal.,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 298 (1994)\n (separately addressing due process and Commerce Clause challenges to a tax); Moorman,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267\n (same); Standard Pressed Steel Co. v. Department of Revenue of Wash.,\n\n419 U.\xc2\xa0S. 560 (1975)\n (same); Lawrence v. State Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Miss.,\n\n286 U.\xc2\xa0S. 276 (1932)\n (separately addressing due process and equal protection challengesto a tax); Travis v. Yale & Towne Mfg. Co.,\n\n252 U.\xc2\xa0S. 60 (1920)\n (separately addressing due process and privileges-and-immunities challenges to a tax).

\n

One good reason why we have never accepted the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s logic is that it would lead to plainly untenable results. Imagine that Maryland taxed the income that its residents earned in other States but exempted income earned out of State from any business that primarily served Maryland residents. Such a tax would violate the dormant Commerce Clause, see Camps Newfound, supra, and it cannot be saved by the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s admonition that Maryland has the power to tax all the income of its residents. There is no principled difference between that hypothetical Commerce Clause challenge and this one.

\n

The principal dissent, if accepted, would work a sea change in our Commerce Clause jurisprudence. Legion are the cases in which we have considered and even upheld dormant Commerce Clause challenges brought by residents to taxes that the State had the jurisdictional power to impose. See, e.g., Davis,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 328\n; Camps Newfound,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564\n; Fulton Corp.,\n\n516 U.\xc2\xa0S. 325\n; Bacchus Imports,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 263\n; Central Greyhound,\n\n334 U.\xc2\xa0S. 653\n; Gwin, White,\n\n305 U.\xc2\xa0S. 434\n; J.\xc2\xa0D. Adams,\n\n304 U.\xc2\xa0S. 307\n. If the principal dissent were to prevail, all of these cases would be thrown into doubt. After all, in those cases, as here, the State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to tax in a way that allegedly discriminates against interstate commerce could be justified by the argument that a State may tax its residents without any Commerce Clause constraints.

\n

E

\n

While the principal dissent claims that we are departing from principles that have been accepted for \xe2\x80\x9ca century\xe2\x80\x9d and have been \xe2\x80\x9crepeatedly acknowledged by this Court,\xe2\x80\x9d see post, at 1, 2, 19, when it comes to providing supporting authority for this assertion, it cites exactly two Commerce Clause decisions that are supposedly inconsistent with our decision today. One is a summary affirmance, West Publishing Co. v. McColgan,\n\n328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 823 (1946)\n, and neither actually supports the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s argument.

\n

In the first of these cases, Shaffer v. Carter,\n\n252 U.\xc2\xa0S. 37\n, a resident of Illinois who earned income from oil in Oklahoma unsuccessfully argued that his Oklahoma income tax assessment violated several provisions of the Federal Constitution. His main argument was based on due process, but he also raised a dormant Commerce Clause challenge. Although the principal dissent relies on Shaffer for the proposition that a State may tax the income of its residents wherever earned, Shaffer did not reject the Commerce Clause challenge on that basis.

\n

The dormant Commerce Clause challenge in Shaffer was nothing like the Wynnes\xe2\x80\x99 challenge here. The tax-payer in Shaffer argued that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the tax is considered an excise tax on business, rather than an income tax proper,\xe2\x80\x9d it unconstitutionally burdened interstate commerce. Brief for Appellant, O.\xc2\xa0T.\xc2\xa01919, No. 531, p. 166. The taxpayer did not argue that this burden occurred because he was subject to double taxation; instead, he argued that the tax was an impermissible direct \xe2\x80\x9ctax on interstate business.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That argument was based on the notion that States may not impose a tax \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly\xe2\x80\x9d on interstate commerce. See supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. After assuming that the taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s business was engaged in interstate commerce, we held that \xe2\x80\x9cit is sufficient to say that the tax is imposed not upon the gross receipts,\xc2\xa0but only upon the net proceeds, and is plainly sustainable, even if it includes net gains from interstate commerce. [United States Glue Co. v. Town of Oak Creek],\n\n247 U.\xc2\xa0S. 321\n.\xe2\x80\x9d Shaffer, supra, at 57 (citation omitted).

\n

Shaffer thus did not adjudicate anything like the double taxation argument that was accepted in later cases and is before us today. And the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that Shaffer allows States to levy discriminatory net income taxes is refuted by a case decided that same day. In Travis, a Connecticut corporation challenged New York\xe2\x80\x99s net income tax, which allowed residents, but not nonresidents, certain tax exemptions. The Court first rejected the taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s due process argument as \xe2\x80\x9csettled by our decision in Shaffer.\xe2\x80\x9d 252 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 75. But that due process inquiry was not the end of the matter: the Court then separately considered\xe2\x80\x94and sustained\xe2\x80\x94the argument that the net income tax\xe2\x80\x99s disparate treatment of residents and nonresidents violated the Privileges and Immunities Clause. Id., at 79\xe2\x80\x9380.

\n

The second case on which the principal dissent relies, West Publishing, is a summary affirmance and thus has \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderably less precedential value than an opinion on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois Bd. of Elections v. Socialist Workers Party,\n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 173\n\xe2\x80\x93181 (1979). A summary affirmance \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is not to be read as a renunciation by this Court of doctrines previously announced in our opinions after full argument.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Mandel v. Bradley,\n\n432 U.\xc2\xa0S. 173,\n176 (1977)\n (per curiam) (quoting Fusari v. Steinberg,\n\n419 U.\xc2\xa0S. 379,\n392 (1975)\n (Burger, C.\xc2\xa0J., concurring)). The principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the state-court decision below in that case is particularly inappropriate because \xe2\x80\x9ca summary affirmance is an affirmance of the judgment only,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe rationale of the affirmance may not be gleaned solely from the opinion below.\xe2\x80\x9d 432 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 176.

\n

Moreover, we do not disagree with the result of West Publishing. The tax in that case was levied only on \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the net income of every corporation derived from sources within this State,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and thus was an internally consistent and nondiscriminatory tax scheme. See West Publishing Co. v. McColgan, 27 Cal. 2d 705, 707, n., 166 P.\xc2\xa02d 861, 862, n. (1946) (emphasis added). Moreover, even if we did disagree with the result, the citation in our summary affirmance to United States Glue Co. suggests that our decision was based on the since-discarded distinction between net income and gross receipts taxes. West Publishing did not\xe2\x80\x94indeed, it could not\xe2\x80\x94repudiate the double taxation cases upon which we rely.

\n

The principal dissent also finds it significant that, when States first enacted modern income taxes in the early 1900\xe2\x80\x99s, some States had tax schemes similar to Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s. This practice, however, was by no means universal. A great many States\xe2\x80\x94such as Alabama, Colorado, Georgia, Kentucky, and Maryland\xe2\x80\x94had early income tax schemes that allowed their residents a credit against taxes paid to other States. See Ala. Code, Tit. 51, ch. 17, \xc2\xa7390 (1940); Colo. Stat. Ann., ch. 84A, \xc2\xa738 (Cum. Supp. 1951); Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa792\xe2\x80\x933111 (1974); Carroll\xe2\x80\x99s Ky. Stat. Ann., ch. 108, Art. XX, \xc2\xa74281b\xe2\x80\x9315 (Baldwin rev. 1936); Md. Ann. Code, Art. 81, ch. 277, \xc2\xa7231 (1939). Other States also adopted internally consistent tax schemes. For example, Massachusetts and Utah taxed only the income of residents, not nonresidents. See Mass. Gen. Laws, ch. 62 (1932); Utah Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa780\xe2\x80\x9314\xe2\x80\x931 et\xc2\xa0seq. (1933).

\n

In any event, it is hardly surprising that these early state ventures into the taxation of income included some protectionist regimes that favored the local economy over interstate commerce. What is much more significant is that over the next century, as our Commerce Clause juris-prudence developed, the States have almost entirely abandoned that approach, perhaps in recognition of their doubtful constitutionality. Today, the near-universal state practice is to provide credits against personal income taxes for such taxes paid to other States. See 2 J. Hellerstein & W. Hellerstein, State Taxation, \xc2\xb620.10, pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x93163 to 20\xe2\x80\x93164 (3d ed. 2003).[4]

F

\n

1

\n

As previously noted, the tax schemes held to be unconstitutional in J.\xc2\xa0D. Adams, Gwin, White, and Central Greyhound, had the potential to result in the discriminatory double taxation of income earned out of state and created a powerful incentive to engage in intrastate rather than interstate economic activity. Although we did not use the term in those cases, we held that those schemes could be cured by taxes that satisfy what we have subsequently labeled the \xe2\x80\x9cinternal consistency\xe2\x80\x9d test. See Jefferson Lines, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 185 (citing Gwin, White as a case requiring internal consistency); see also 1 Trost \xc2\xa72:19, at 122\xe2\x80\x93123, and n.\xc2\xa0160 (explaining that the internal consistency test has its origins in Western Live Stock, J.\xc2\xa0D. Adams, and Gwin, White). This test, which helps courts identify tax schemes that discriminate against interstate commerce, \xe2\x80\x9clooks to the structure of the tax at issue to see whether its identical application by every State in the Union would place interstate commerce at a disadvantage as compared with commerce intrastate.\xe2\x80\x9d 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 185. See also, e.g., Tyler Pipe, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 246\xe2\x80\x93248; Armco, 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 644\xe2\x80\x93645; Container Corp. of America v. Franchise Tax Bd.,\n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 159,\n169 (1983)\n.

\n

By hypothetically assuming that every State has the same tax structure, the internal consistency test allows courts to isolate the effect of a defendant State\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme. This is a virtue of the test because it allows courts to distinguish between (1)\xc2\xa0tax schemes that inherently discriminate against interstate commerce without regard to the tax policies of other States, and (2)\xc2\xa0tax schemes that create disparate incentives to engage in interstate commerce (and sometimes result in double taxation) only as a result of the interaction of two different but nondiscriminatory and internally consistent schemes. See Armco, supra, at 645\xe2\x80\x93646; Moorman, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 277, n.\xc2\xa012; Brief for Tax Economists as Amici Curiae 23\xe2\x80\x9324 (hereinafter Brief for Tax Economists); Brief for Michael S. Knoll & Ruth Mason as Amici Curiae 18\xe2\x80\x9323 (hereinafter Brief for Knoll & Mason). The first category of taxes is typically unconstitutional; the second is not.[5] See Armco, supra, at 644\xe2\x80\x93646; Moorman, supra, at 277, and n.\xc2\xa012. Tax schemes that fail the internal consistency test will fall into the first category, not the second: \xe2\x80\x9c[A]ny cross-border tax disadvantage that remains after application of the [test] cannot be due to tax disparities\xe2\x80\x9d[6] but is instead attributable to the taxing State\xe2\x80\x99s discriminatory policies alone.

\n

Neither petitioner nor the principal dissent questions the economic bona fides of the internal consistency test. And despite its professed adherence to precedent, the principal dissent ignores the numerous cases in which we have applied the internal consistency test in the past. The internal consistency test was formally introduced more than three decades ago, see Container Corp., supra, and it has been invoked in no fewer than seven cases, invalidating the tax in three of those cases. See American Trucking Assns., Inc. v. Michigan Pub. Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429 (2005)\n;[7] Jefferson Lines, Inc.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 175\n; Goldberg, 488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 252; American Trucking Assns., Inc. v. Scheiner,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 266 (1987)\n; Tyler Pipe,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 232\n; Armco,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 638\n; Container Corp., supra.

\n

2

\n

Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s income tax scheme fails the internal consistency test.[8] A simple example illustrates the point. Assume that every State imposed the following taxes, which are similar to Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccounty\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cspecial nonresident\xe2\x80\x9d taxes: (1) a 1.25% tax on income that residents earn in State, (2)\xc2\xa0a 1.25% tax on income that residents earn in other jurisdictions, and (3)\xc2\xa0a 1.25% tax on income that nonresidents earn in State. Assume further that two taxpayers, April and Bob, both live in State A, but that April earns her income in State A whereas Bob earns his income in State B. In this circumstance, Bob will pay more income tax than April solely because he earns income interstate. Specifically, April will have to pay a 1.25% tax only once, to State A. But Bob will have to pay a 1.25% tax twice: once to State A, where he resides, and once to State B, where he earns the income.

\n

Critically\xe2\x80\x94and this dispels a central argument made by petitioner and the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x94the Maryland scheme\xe2\x80\x99s discriminatory treatment of interstate commerce is not simply the result of its interaction with the taxing schemes of other States. Instead, the internal consistency test reveals what the undisputed economic analysis shows: Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme is inherently discriminatory and operates as a tariff. See Brief for Tax Economists 4, 9; Brief for Knoll & Mason 2. This identity between Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax and a tariff is fatal because tariffs are \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he paradigmatic example of a law discriminating against interstate commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d West Lynn, 512 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 193. Indeed, when asked about the foregoing analysis made by amici Tax Economists and Knoll & Mason, counsel for Maryland responded, \xe2\x80\x9cI don\xe2\x80\x99t dispute the mathematics. They lose me when they switch from tariffs to income taxes.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 9. But Maryland has offered no reason why our analysis should change because we deal with an income tax rather than a formal tariff, and we see none. After all, \xe2\x80\x9ctariffs against the products of other States are so patently unconstitutional that our cases reveal not a single attempt by any State to enact one. Instead, the cases are filled with state laws that aspire to reap some of the benefits of tariffs by other means.\xe2\x80\x9d West Lynn, supra, at 193.

\n

None of our dissenting colleagues dispute this economic analysis. The principal dissent focuses instead on a supposed \xe2\x80\x9coddity\xe2\x80\x9d with our analysis: The principal dissent can envision other tax schemes that result in double taxation but do not violate the internal consistency test. This would happen, the principal dissent points out, if State A taxed only based on residence and State B taxed only based on source. Post, at 17 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting); see also post, at 7 (Scalia, J., dissenting). Our prior decisions have already considered and rejected this precise argument\xe2\x80\x94and for good reason. For example, in Armco, we struck down an internally inconsistent tax that posed a risk of double taxation even though we recognized that there might be other permissible arrangements that would result in double taxation. Such schemes would be constitutional, we explained, because \xe2\x80\x9csuch a result would not arise from impermissible discrimination against interstate commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 645. The principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s protest that our distinction is \xe2\x80\x9centirely circular,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318, n.\xc2\xa010, misunderstands the critical distinction, recognized in cases like Armco, between discriminatory tax schemes and double taxation that results only from the interaction of two different but nondiscriminatory tax schemes. See also Moorman, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 277, n.\xc2\xa012 (distinguishing \xe2\x80\x9cthe potential consequences of the use of different formulas by the two States,\xe2\x80\x9d which is not prohibited by the Commerce Clause, from discrimination that \xe2\x80\x9cinhere[s] in either State\xe2\x80\x99s formula,\xe2\x80\x9d which is prohibited).

\n

Petitioner and the Solicitor General argue that Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax is neutral, not discriminatory, because the same tax applies to all three categories of income. Specifically, they point out that the same tax is levied on (1)\xc2\xa0residents who earn income in State, (2)\xc2\xa0residents who earn income out of State, and (3)\xc2\xa0nonresidents who earn income in State. But the fact that the tax might have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the advantage of appearing nondiscriminatory\xe2\x80\x99 does not save it from invalidation.\xe2\x80\x9d Tyler Pipe, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 248 (quoting General Motors Corp. v. Washington,\n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 436,\n460 (1964)\n (Goldberg, J., dissenting)). See also American Trucking Assns., Inc. v. Scheiner, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S. at, 281 (dormant Commerce Clause applies to state taxes even when they \xe2\x80\x9cdo not allocate tax burdens between insiders and outsiders in a manner that is facially discriminatory\xe2\x80\x9d); Maine v. Taylor,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 131,\n138 (1986)\n (a state law may discriminate against interstate commerce \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98either on its face or in practical effect\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Hughes, 441 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 336)). In this case, the internal consistency test and economic analysis\xe2\x80\x94indeed, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s own concession\xe2\x80\x94confirm that the tax scheme operates as a tariff and discriminates against interstate commerce, and so the scheme is invalid.

\n

Petitioner and the principal dissent, post, at 6, also note that by offering residents who earn income in interstate commerce a credit against the \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x9d portion of the income tax, Maryland actually receives less tax revenue from residents who earn income from interstate commerce rather than intrastate commerce. This argument is a red herring. The critical point is that the total tax burden on interstate commerce is higher, not that Maryland may receive more or less tax revenue from a particular tax-payer. See Armco, supra, at 642\xe2\x80\x93645. Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax un-constitutionally discriminates against interstate commerce, and it is thus invalid regardless of how much a particular taxpayer must pay to the taxing State.

\n

Once again, a simple hypothetical illustrates the point. Assume that State A imposes a 5% tax on the income that its residents earn in-state but a 10% tax on income they earn in other jurisdictions. Assume also that State A happens to grant a credit against income taxes paid to other States. Such a scheme discriminates against interstate commerce because it taxes income earned interstate at a higher rate than income earned intrastate. This is so despite the fact that, in certain circumstances, a resident of State A who earns income interstate may pay less tax to State A than a neighbor who earns income intrastate. For example, if Bob lives in State A but earns his income in State B, which has a 6% income tax rate, Bob would pay a total tax of 10% on his income, though 6% would go to State B and (because of the credit) only 4% would go to State A. Bob would thus pay less to State A than his neighbor, April, who lives in State A and earns all of her income there, because April would pay a 5% tax to State A. But Bob\xe2\x80\x99s tax burden to State A is irrelevant; his total tax burden is what matters.

\n

The principal dissent is left with two arguments against the internal consistency test. These arguments are inconsistent with each other and with our precedents.

\n

First, the principal dissent claims that the analysis outlined above requires a State taxing based on residence to \xe2\x80\x9crecede\xe2\x80\x9d to a State taxing based on source. Post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932. We establish no such rule of priority. To be sure, Maryland could remedy the infirmity in its tax scheme by offering, as most States do, a credit against income taxes paid to other States. See Tyler Pipe, supra, at 245\xe2\x80\x93246, and n.\xc2\xa013. If it did, Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme would survive the internal consistency test and would not be inherently discriminatory. Tweak our first hypothetical, supra, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322, and assume that all States impose a 1.25% tax on all three categories of income but also allow a credit against income taxes that residents pay to other jurisdictions. In that circumstance, April (who lives and works in State A) and Bob (who lives in State A but works in State B) would pay the same tax. Specifically, April would pay a 1.25% tax only once (to State A), and Bob would pay a 1.25% tax only once (to State B, because State A would give him a credit against the tax he paid to State B).

\n

But while Maryland could cure the problem with its current system by granting a credit for taxes paid to other States, we do not foreclose the possibility that it could comply with the Commerce Clause in some other way. See Brief for Tax Economists 32; Brief for Knoll & Mason 28\xe2\x80\x9330. Of course, we do not decide the constitutionality of a hypothetical tax scheme that Maryland might adopt because such a scheme is not before us. That Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s existing tax unconstitutionally discriminates against interstate commerce is enough to decide this case.

\n

Second, the principal dissent finds a \xe2\x80\x9cdeep flaw\xe2\x80\x9d with the possibility that \xe2\x80\x9cMaryland could eliminate the inconsistency [with its tax scheme] by terminating the special nonresident tax\xe2\x80\x94a measure that would not help the Wynnes at all.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 16. This second objection refutes the first. By positing that Maryland could remedy the unconstitutionality of its tax scheme by eliminating the special nonresident tax, the principal dissent accepts that Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s desire to tax based on residence need not \xe2\x80\x9crecede\xe2\x80\x9d to another State\xe2\x80\x99s desire to tax based on source.

\n

Moreover, the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s supposed flaw is simply a truism about every case under the dormant Commerce Clause (not to mention the Equal Protection Clause): Whenever government impermissibly treats like cases differently, it can cure the violation by either \xe2\x80\x9cleveling up\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cleveling down.\xe2\x80\x9d Whenever a State impermissibly taxes interstate commerce at a higher rate than intrastate commerce, that infirmity could be cured by lowering the higher rate, raising the lower rate, or a combination of the two. For this reason, we have concluded that \xe2\x80\x9ca State found to have imposed an impermissibly discriminatory tax retains flexibility in responding to this determination.\xe2\x80\x9d McKesson Corp. v. Division of Alcoholic Beverages and Tobacco, Fla. Dept. of Business Regulation,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 18\n\xe2\x80\x9340 (1990). See also Associated Industries of Mo. v. Lohman,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 641,\n656 (1994)\n; Fulton Corp., 516 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 346\xe2\x80\x93347. If every claim that suffers from this \xe2\x80\x9cflaw\xe2\x80\x9d cannot succeed, no dormant Commerce Clause or equal protection claim could ever succeed.

\n

G

\n

Justice Scalia would uphold the constitutionality of the Maryland tax scheme because the dormant Commerce Clause, in his words, is \xe2\x80\x9ca judicial fraud.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 2. That was not the view of the Court in Gibbons v. Ogden, 9 Wheat, at 209, where Chief Justice Marshall wrote that there was \xe2\x80\x9cgreat force\xe2\x80\x9d in the argument that the Commerce Clause by itself limits the power of the States to enact laws regulating interstate commerce. Since that time, this supposedly fraudulent doctrine has been applied in dozens of our opinions, joined by dozens of Justices. Perhaps for this reason, petitioner in this case, while challenging the interpretation and application of that doctrine by the court below, did not ask us to reconsider the doctrine\xe2\x80\x99s validity.

\n

Justice Scalia does not dispute the fact that State tariffs were among the principal problems that led to the adoption of the Constitution. See post, at 3. Nor does he dispute the fact that the Maryland tax scheme is tantamount to a tariff on work done out of State. He argues, however, that the Constitution addresses the problem of state tariffs by prohibiting States from imposing \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Imposts or Duties on Imports or Exports.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Art. I, \xc2\xa710, cl.\xc2\xa02). But he does not explain why, under his interpretation of the Constitution, the Import-Export Clause would not lead to the same result that we reach under the dormant Commerce Clause. Our cases have noted the close relationship between the two provisions. See, e.g., State Tonnage Tax Cases, 12 Wall. 204, 214 (1871).

\n

Justice Thomas also refuses to accept the dormant Commerce Clause doctrine, and he suggests that the Constitution was ratified on the understanding that it would not prevent a State from doing what Maryland has done here. He notes that some States imposed income taxes at the time of the adoption of the Constitution, and he observes that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no indication that those early state income tax schemes provided credits for income taxes paid elsewhere.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 2 (dissenting opinion). \xe2\x80\x9cIt seems highly implausible,\xe2\x80\x9d he writes, \xe2\x80\x9cthat those who ratified the Commerce Clause understood it to conflict with the income tax laws of their States and nonetheless adopted it without a word of concern.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This argument is plainly unsound.

\n

First, because of the difficulty of interstate travel, the number of individuals who earned income out of State in 1787 was surely very small. (We are unaware of records showing, for example, that it was common in 1787 for workers to commute to Manhattan from New Jersey by rowboat or from Connecticut by stagecoach.)

\n

Second, Justice Thomas has not shown that the small number of individuals who earned income out of State were taxed twice on that income. A number of Founding-era income tax schemes appear to have taxed only the income of residents, not nonresidents. For example, in his report to Congress on direct taxes, Oliver Wolcott, Jr., Secretary of Treasury, describes Delaware\xe2\x80\x99s income tax as being imposed only on \xe2\x80\x9cthe inhabitants of this State,\xe2\x80\x9d and he makes no mention of the taxation of nonresidents\xe2\x80\x99 income. Report to 4th Cong., 2d Sess. (1796), concerning Direct Taxes, in 1 American State Papers, Finance 429 (1832). Justice Thomas likewise understands that the Massachusetts and Delaware income taxes were imposed only on residents. Post, at 2, n. These tax schemes, of course, pass the internal consistency test. Moreover, the difficulty of administering an income tax on nonresidents would have diminished the likelihood of double taxation. See R. Blakey, State Income Taxation 1 (1930).

\n

Third, even if some persons were taxed twice, it is unlikely that this was a matter of such common knowledge that it must have been known by the delegates to the State ratifying conventions who voted to adopt theConstitution.

\n

\n

For these reasons, the judgment of the Court of Appeals of Maryland is affirmed.

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg, with whom Justice Scalia and Justice Kagan join, dissenting.

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision veers from a principle of interstate and international taxation repeatedly acknowledged by this Court: A nation or State \xe2\x80\x9cmay tax all the income of its residents, even income earned outside the taxing jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Chickasaw Nation,\n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 450\n\xe2\x80\x93463 (1995). In accord with this principle, the Court has regularly rejected claims that taxes on a resident\xe2\x80\x99s out-of-state income violate the Due Process Clause for lack of a sufficient \xe2\x80\x9cconnection\xe2\x80\x9d to the taxing State. Quill Corp. v. North Dakota,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 298,\n306 (1992)\n (internal quotation marks omitted); see, e.g., Lawrence v. State Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Miss.,\n\n286 U.\xc2\xa0S. 276,\n281 (1932)\n. But under dormant Commerce Clause jurisprudence, the Court decides, a State is not really empowered to tax a resident\xe2\x80\x99s income from whatever source derived. In taxing personal income, the Court holds, source-based authority, i.e., authority to tax commerce conducted within a State\xe2\x80\x99s territory, boxes in the taxing authority of a taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s domicile.

\n

As I see it, nothing in the Constitution or in prior decisions of this Court dictates that one of two States, the domiciliary State or the source State, must recede simply because both have lawful tax regimes reaching the same income. See Moorman Mfg. Co. v. Bair,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267\n, n.\xc2\xa012 (1978) (finding no \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[ion] against interstate commerce\xe2\x80\x9d where alleged taxation disparities were \xe2\x80\x9cthe consequence of the combined effect\xe2\x80\x9d of two otherwise lawful income-tax schemes). True, Maryland elected to deny a credit for income taxes paid to other States in computing a resident\xe2\x80\x99s county tax liability. It is equally true, however, that the other States that taxed the Wynnes\xe2\x80\x99 income elected not to offer them a credit for their Maryland county income taxes. In this situation, the Constitution does not prefer one lawful basis for state taxation of a person\xe2\x80\x99s income over the other. Nor doesit require one State, in this case Maryland, to limit its residence-based taxation, should the State also choose to exercise, to the full extent, its source-based authority. States often offer their residents credits for income taxes paid to other States, as Maryland does for state income tax purposes. States do so, however, as a matter of tax \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy,\xe2\x80\x9d Chickasaw Nation, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 463, n.\xc2\xa012 (internal quotation marks omitted), not because the Constitution compels that course.

\n

I

\n

For at least a century, \xe2\x80\x9cdomicile\xe2\x80\x9d has been recognized as a secure ground for taxation of residents\xe2\x80\x99 worldwide income. Lawrence, 286 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 279. \xe2\x80\x9cEnjoyment of the privileges of residence within [a] state, and the attendant right to invoke the protection of its laws,\xe2\x80\x9d this Court has explained, \xe2\x80\x9care inseparable from the responsibility for sharing the costs of government.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cA tax measured by the net income of residents is an equitable method of distributing the burdens of government among those who are privileged to enjoy its benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d New York ex rel. Cohn v. Graves,\n\n300 U.\xc2\xa0S. 308,\n313 (1937)\n.

\n

More is given to the residents of a State than to those who reside elsewhere, therefore more may be demanded of them. With this Court\xe2\x80\x99s approbation, States have long favored their residents over nonresidents in the provision of local services. See Reeves, Inc. v. Stake,\n\n447 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429,\n442 (1980)\n (such favoritism does not violate the Commerce Clause). See also Martinez v. Bynum,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 321 (1983)\n (upholding residency requirements for free primary and secondary schooling). The cost of services residents enjoy is substantial. According to the State\xe2\x80\x99s Comptroller, for example, in 2012 Maryland and its local governments spent over $11 billion to fund public schools, $4 billion for state health programs, and $1.1 billion for the State\xe2\x80\x99s food supplemental program\xe2\x80\x94all programs available to residents only. Brief for Petitioner 20\xe2\x80\x9323. See also Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 18 (Howard County\xe2\x80\x94where the Wynnes lived in 2006\xe2\x80\x94budgeted more than $903 million for education in fiscal year 2014). Excluding nonresidents from these services, this Court has observed, is rational for it is residents \xe2\x80\x9cwho fund the state treasury and whom the State was created to serve.\xe2\x80\x9d Reeves, 447 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 442. A taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s home State, then, can hardly be faulted for making support of local government activities an obligation of every resident, regardless of any obligations residents may have to other States.[1]

\n

Residents, moreover, possess political means, not shared by outsiders, to ensure that the power to tax their income is not abused. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is not,\xe2\x80\x9d this Court has observed, \xe2\x80\x9ca purpose of the Commerce Clause to protect state residents from their own state taxes.\xe2\x80\x9d Goldberg v. Sweet,\n\n488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 252,\n266 (1989)\n. The reason is evident. Residents are \xe2\x80\x9cinsider[s] who presumably [are] able to complain about and change the tax through the [State\xe2\x80\x99s] political process.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Nonresidents, by contrast, are not similarly positioned to \xe2\x80\x9ceffec[t] legislative change.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As Chief Justice Marshall, developer of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s Commerce Clause jurisprudence, reasoned: \xe2\x80\x9cIn imposing a tax the legislature acts upon its constituents. This is in general a sufficient security against erroneous and oppressive taxation.\xe2\x80\x9d McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 428 (1819). The \xe2\x80\x9cpeople of a State\xe2\x80\x9d can thus \xe2\x80\x9cres[t] confidently on the interest of the legislator, and on the influence of the constituents over their representative, to guard them against .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. abuse\xe2\x80\x9d of the \xe2\x80\x9cright of taxing themselves and their property.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[2]

\n

I hardly maintain, as the majority insistently asserts I do, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Commerce Clause places no constraint on a State\xe2\x80\x99s power to tax\xe2\x80\x9d its residents. Ante, at 13. See also ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9315. This Court has not shied away from striking down or closely scrutinizing state efforts to tax residents at a higher rate for out-of-state activities than for in-state activities (or to exempt from taxation only in-state activities). See, e.g., Department of Revenue of Ky. v. Davis,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 328,\n336 (2008)\n; Camps Newfound/ Owatonna, Inc. v. Town of Harrison,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564\n(1997); Fulton Corp. v. Faulkner,\n\n516 U.\xc2\xa0S. 325 (1996)\n; Bacchus Imports, Ltd. v. Dias,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 263,\n272 (1984)\n. See also ante, at 11, and n.\xc2\xa03, 14\xe2\x80\x9315 (mistakenly charging that under my analysis \xe2\x80\x9call of these cases would be thrown into doubt\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9c[P]olitical processes\xe2\x80\x9d are ill equipped to guard against such facially discriminatory taxes because the effect of a tax of this sort is to \xe2\x80\x9cmollif[y]\xe2\x80\x9d some of the \xe2\x80\x9cin-state interests [that] would otherwise lobby against\xe2\x80\x9d it. West Lynn Creamery, Inc. v. Healy,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 186,\n200 (1994)\n. By contrast, the Court has generally upheld \xe2\x80\x9cevenhanded tax[es] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in spite of any adverse effects oninterstate commerce, in part because \xe2\x80\x98[t]he existence of major in-state interests adversely affected .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is a powerful safeguard against legislative abuse.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (citing, inter alia, Goldberg, 488 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 266). That justification applies with full force to the \xe2\x80\x9cevenhanded tax\xe2\x80\x9d challenged here, which taxes residents\xe2\x80\x99 income at the same rate whether earned in-state or out-of-state.[3]

\n

These rationales for a State taxing its residents\xe2\x80\x99 worldwide income are not diminished by another State\xe2\x80\x99s independent interest in \xe2\x80\x9crequiring contributions from [nonresidents] who realize current pecuniary benefits under the protection of the [State\xe2\x80\x99s] government.\xe2\x80\x9d Shaffer v. Carter,\n\n252 U.\xc2\xa0S. 37,\n51 (1920)\n. A taxpayer living in one State and working in another gains protection and benefits from both\xe2\x80\x94and so can be called upon to share in the costs of both States\xe2\x80\x99 governments.

\n

States deciding whether to tax residents\xe2\x80\x99 entire worldwide income must choose between legitimate but competing tax policy objectives. A State might prioritize obtaining equal contributions from those who benefit from the State\xe2\x80\x99s protection in roughly similar ways. Or a State might prioritize ensuring that its taxpayers are not subject to double taxation. A State cannot, however, accomplish both objectives at once.

\n

To illustrate, consider the Wynnes. Under the tax scheme in place in 2006, other Howard County residents who earned their income in-state but who otherwise had the same tax profile as the Wynnes (e.g., $2.67 million in taxable net income) owed the same amount of taxes to Maryland as the Wynnes. See App. to Pet. for Cert. A\xe2\x80\x9356. The scheme thus ensured that all residents with similar access to the State\xe2\x80\x99s protection and benefits and similar ability to pay made equal contributions to the State to defray the costs of those benefits. Maryland could not achieve that objective, however, without exposing the Wynnes to a risk of double taxation. Conversely, the Court prioritizes reducing the risk that the Wynnes\xe2\x80\x99 income will be taxed twice by two different States. But that choice comes at a cost: The Wynnes enjoyed equal access to the State\xe2\x80\x99s services but will have paid $25,000 less to cover the costs of those services than similarly situated neighbors who earned their income entirely within the State. See Pet. for Cert. 15.

\n

States confront the same trade-off when deciding whether to tax nonresidents\xe2\x80\x99 entire in-state income. A State can require all residents and nonresidents who work within the State under its protection to contribute equally to the cost of that protection. Or the State can seek to avoid exposing its workers to any risk of double taxation. But it cannot achieve both objectives.

\n

For at least a century, responsibility for striking the right balance between these two policy objectives has belonged to the States (and Congress), not this Court. Some States have chosen the same balance the Court embraces today. See ante, at 17. But since almost the dawn of the modern era of state income taxation, other States have taken the same approach as Maryland does now, taxing residents\xe2\x80\x99 entire income, wherever earned, while at the same time taxing nonresidents\xe2\x80\x99 entire in-state income. And recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]rotection, benefit, and power over [a taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s income] are not confined to either\xe2\x80\x9d the State of residence or the State in which income is earned, this Court has long afforded States that flexibility. Curry v. McCanless,\n\n307 U.\xc2\xa0S. 357,\n368 (1939)\n. This history of States imposing and this Court upholding income tax schemes materially identical to the one the Court confronts here should be the beginning and end of this case.

\n

The modern era of state income taxation dates from a Wisconsin tax enacted in 1911. See 1911 Wis. Laws ch. 658; R. Blakey, State Income Taxation 1 (1930). From close to the start of this modern era, States have taxed residents and nonresidents in ways materially indistinguishable from the way Maryland does now. In 1915, for example, Oklahoma began taxing residents\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9centire net income .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. arising or accruing from all sources,\xe2\x80\x9d while at the same time taxing nonresidents\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9centire net income from [sources] in th[e] State.\xe2\x80\x9d 1915 Okla. Sess. Laws ch.\xc2\xa0164, \xc2\xa71, pp. 232\xe2\x80\x93233 (emphasis added). Like Maryland today, Oklahoma provided no credit to either residents or nonresidents for taxes paid elsewhere. See id., ch.\xc2\xa0164, \xc2\xa71 et\xc2\xa0seq., at 232\xe2\x80\x93237. In 1917, neighboring Missouri adopted a similar scheme: Residents owed taxes on their \xe2\x80\x9centire net income .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. from all sources\xe2\x80\x9d and nonresidents owed taxes on their \xe2\x80\x9centire net income .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. from all sources within th[e] state.\xe2\x80\x9d 1917 Mo. Laws \xc2\xa71(a), pp. 524\xe2\x80\x93525 (emphasis added). Missouri too provided neither residents nor nonresidents a credit for taxes paid to other jurisdic-tions. See id., \xc2\xa71 et\xc2\xa0seq., at 524\xe2\x80\x93538. Thus, much like Maryland today, these early income tax adopters simultaneously taxed residents on all income, wherever earned, and nonresidents on all income earned within the State.[4]

\n

Almost immediately, this Court began issuing what became a long series of decisions, repeatedly upholding States\xe2\x80\x99 authority to tax both residents\xe2\x80\x99 worldwide income and nonresidents\xe2\x80\x99 in-state income. E.g., Maguire v. Trefry,\n\n253 U.\xc2\xa0S. 12,\n17 (1920)\n (resident income tax); Shaffer, 252 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 52\xe2\x80\x9353, 57 (nonresident income tax). See also State Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Utah v. Aldrich,\n\n316 U.\xc2\xa0S. 174,\n178 (1942)\n; Curry, 307 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 368; Guaranty Trust Co. v. Virginia,\n\n305 U.\xc2\xa0S. 19,\n23 (1938)\n; Graves, 300 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 313; Lawrence, 286 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 281. By the end of the 20th cen-tury, it was \xe2\x80\x9ca well-established principle of interstate and international taxation\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9csovereigns have authority to tax all income of their residents, including income earned outside their borders,\xe2\x80\x9d Chickasaw Nation, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 462, 463, n.\xc2\xa012, and that sovereigns generally may also tax nonresidents on \xe2\x80\x9cincome earned within the [sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s] jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 463, n.\xc2\xa011.

\n

Far from suggesting that States must choose between taxing residents or nonresidents, this Court specifically affirmed that the exact same \xe2\x80\x9cincome may be taxed [si-multaneously] both by the state where it is earned and by the state of the recipient\xe2\x80\x99s domicile.\xe2\x80\x9d Curry, 307 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 368 (emphasis added). See also Aldrich, 316 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 176\xe2\x80\x93178, 181 (rejecting \xe2\x80\x9ca rule of immunity from taxation by more than one state,\xe2\x80\x9d including with respect to income taxation (internal quotation marks omitted)). In Lawrence, for example, this Court dealt with a Mississippi tax \xe2\x80\x9cscheme\xe2\x80\x9d with the same structure Maryland has today: Mississippi taxed residents on all income, wherever earned, and nonresidents on income earned within the State, without providing either set of taxpayers a credit for taxes paid elsewhere. See 286 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 278\xe2\x80\x93279; Miss. Code Ann. \xc2\xa75033(a), (b)(9) (1930). Lawrence upheld a Mississippi tax on net income earned by one of its residents on the construction of public highways in Tennessee. See 286 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 279\xe2\x80\x93281. The Court did so fully aware that both Mississippi and Tennessee were effectively imposing \xe2\x80\x9can income tax upon the same occupation.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief in Lawrence v. State Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Miss., O.\xc2\xa0T. 1931, No. 580, p.\xc2\xa032. See also Curry, 307 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 363, n.\xc2\xa01, 368 (discussing Lawrence).

\n

Likewise, in Guaranty Trust, both New York and Vir-ginia had taxed income of a New York trust that had been distributed to a Virginia resident. 305 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 21\xe2\x80\x9322. The resident sought to block Virginia\xe2\x80\x99s tax in order to avoid \xe2\x80\x9cdouble taxation\xe2\x80\x9d of the \xe2\x80\x9cidentical income.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 22. Rejecting that challenge, the Court once again reiterated that \xe2\x80\x9ctwo States\xe2\x80\x9d may simultaneously tax the \xe2\x80\x9csame income.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The majority deems these cases irrelevant because they involved challenges brought under the Due Process Clause, not the Commerce Clause. See ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9315. These cases are significant, however, not because the constraints imposed by the two Clauses are identical. Obviously, they are not. See Quill Corp., 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 305. What the sheer volume and consistency of this precedent confirms, rather, is the degree to which this Court has\xe2\x80\x94until now\xe2\x80\x94endorsed the \xe2\x80\x9cwell-established principle of interstate and international taxation\xe2\x80\x9d that a State may tax its residents\xe2\x80\x99 worldwide income, without restriction arising from the source-based taxes imposed by other States and regardless of whether the State also chooses to impose source-based taxes of its own. Chickasaw Nation, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 462.[5]

\n

In any event, it is incorrect that support for this principle is limited to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause cases. In Shaffer, for example, this Court rejected both a Due Process Clause challenge and a dormant Commerce Clause challenge to an income tax \xe2\x80\x9cscheme\xe2\x80\x9d (the Oklahoma statute described above) with the very features the majority latches onto today: Oklahoma taxed residents on all worldwide income and nonresidents on all in-state income, without providing a credit for taxes paid elsewhere to either residents or nonresidents. 252 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 52\xe2\x80\x9353 (Due Process challenge); id., at 57 (dormant Commerce Clause challenge). See also supra, at 7. The specific tax challenged in Shaffer\xe2\x80\x94a tax on a nonresident\xe2\x80\x99s in-state income\xe2\x80\x94exposed taxpayers to the same risk of double taxation as the Maryland tax challenged in this case. The majority labors mightily to distinguish Shaffer, but it does not dispute the one thing that ought to give it pause: Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision overrules Shaffer\xe2\x80\x99s dormant Commerce Clause holding. See ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. I would not discard our precedents so lightly. Just as the tax in Shaffer encountered no constitutional shoals, so Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s scheme should survive the Court\xe2\x80\x99s inspection.

\n

This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in West Publishing Co. v. McColgan,\n\n328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 823 (1946)\n, reinforces that conclusion. In West Publishing, the Court summarily affirmed a decision of the California Supreme Court that denied a dormant Commerce Clause challenge based on the principles today\xe2\x80\x99s majority disrespects:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here [is no] merit to the contention that [California\xe2\x80\x99s tax] discriminates against interstate commerce on the ground that it subjects part of plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s income to double taxation, given the taxability of plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s entire net income in the state of its domicile. Taxation in one state is not an immunization against taxation in other states. Taxation by states in which a corporation carries on business activities is justified by the advantages that attend the pursuit of such activities. Income may be taxed both by the state where it is earned and by the state of the recipient\xe2\x80\x99s domicile. Protection, benefit and power over the subject matter are not confined to either state.\xe2\x80\x9d 27 Cal. 2d 705, 710\xe2\x80\x93711, 166 P.\xc2\xa02d 861, 864 (1946) (citations and internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

In treating the matter summarily, the Court rejected an argument strikingly similar to the one the majority now embraces: that California\xe2\x80\x99s tax violated the dormant Commerce Clause because it subjected \xe2\x80\x9cinterstate commerce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to the risk of a double tax burden.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellant Opposing Motion to Dismiss or Affirm in West Publishing Co. v. McColgan, O. T. 1945, No. 1255, pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9321 (quoting J. D. Adams Mfg. Co. v. Storen,\n\n304 U.\xc2\xa0S. 307,\n311 (1938)\n).

\n

The long history just recounted counsels in favor of respecting States\xe2\x80\x99 authority to tax without discount its residents\xe2\x80\x99 worldwide income. As Justice Holmes stated over a century ago, in regard to a \xe2\x80\x9cmode of taxation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of long standing, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the fact that the system has been in force for a very long time is of itself a strong reason .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for leaving any improvement that may be desired to the legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d Paddell v. City of New York,\n\n211 U.\xc2\xa0S. 446,\n448 (1908)\n. Only recently, this Court followed that sound advice in resisting a dormant Commerce Clause challenge to a taxing practice with a pedigree as enduring as the practice in this case. See Department of Revenue of Ky. v. Davis,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 328\n\xe2\x80\x93357 (2008) (quoting Padell, 211 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 448). Surely that advice merits application here, where the challenged tax draws support from both historical practice and numerous decisions of this Court.

\n

The majority rejects Justice Holmes\xe2\x80\x99 counsel, observing that most States, over time, have chosen not to exercise plenary authority to tax residents\xe2\x80\x99 worldwide income. See ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. The Court, however, learns the wrong lesson from the \xe2\x80\x9cindependent policy decision[s]\xe2\x80\x9d States have made. Chickasaw, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 463, n.\xc2\xa012 (emphasis added; internal quotation marks omitted). This history demonstrates not that States \xe2\x80\x9cdoub[t]\xe2\x80\x9d their \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutiona[l]\xe2\x80\x9d authority to tax residents\xe2\x80\x99 income, wherever earned, as the majority speculates, ante, at 18, but that the very political processes the Court disregards as \xe2\x80\x9cfanciful,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12, have in fact worked to produce policies the Court ranks as responsible\xe2\x80\x94all the more reason to resist this Court\xe2\x80\x99s heavy-handed supervision.

\n

The Court also attempts to deflect the force of this his-tory and precedent by relying on a \xe2\x80\x9ctrilogy\xe2\x80\x9d of decisions it finds \xe2\x80\x9cparticularly instructive.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937 (citing Central Greyhound Lines, Inc. v. Mealey,\n\n334 U. S. 653 (1948)\n; Gwin, White & Prince, Inc. v. Henneford,\n\n305 U. S. 434 (1939)\n; J. D. Adams Mfg.,\n\n304 U. S. 307\n). As the majority acknowledges, however, those three decisions involved gross receipts taxes, not income taxes. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x939. True, this Court has recently pointed to similarities between these two forms of taxation. See ante, at 9. But it is an indulgence in wishful thinking to say that this Court has previously \xe2\x80\x9crejected the argument that the Commerce Clause distinguishes between\xe2\x80\x9d these taxes. Ante, at 9. For decades\xe2\x80\x94including the years when the majority\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctrilogy\xe2\x80\x9d was decided\xe2\x80\x94the Court has routinely maintained that \xe2\x80\x9cthe difference between taxes on net income and taxes on gross receipts from interstate commerce warrants different results\xe2\x80\x9d under the Commerce Clause. C. Trost & P. Hartman, Federal Limitations on State and Local Taxation 2d \xc2\xa710:1 (2003).

\n

In Shaffer, for example, the Court rejected the taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s dormant Commerce Clause challenge because \xe2\x80\x9cthe tax [was] imposed not upon gross receipts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. but only upon the net proceeds.\xe2\x80\x9d 252 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 57. Just three years before deciding J. D. Adams, the Court emphasized \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest and substantial\xe2\x80\x9d differences between the two types of taxes, calling the burden imposed by a gross receipts tax \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and immediate,\xe2\x80\x9d in contrast to the \xe2\x80\x9cindirect and incidental\xe2\x80\x9d burden imposed by an income tax. Stewart Dry Goods Co. v. Lewis,\n\n294 U.\xc2\xa0S. 550,\n558 (1935)\n (quoting United States Glue Co. v. Town of Oak Creek,\n\n247 U.\xc2\xa0S. 321,\n328 (1918)\n). And the Gwin, White opinion observed that invalidating the gross receipts tax at issue \xe2\x80\x9cleft to the states wide scope for taxation of those engaged in interstate commerce, extending to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. net income derived from it.\xe2\x80\x9d 305 U. S., at 441 (emphasis added).

\n

The majority asserts that this Court \xe2\x80\x9crejected\xe2\x80\x9d this distinction in Moorman Mfg. See ante, at 9. That decision in fact described gross receipts taxes as \xe2\x80\x9cmore burdensome\xe2\x80\x9d than income taxes\xe2\x80\x94twice. 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 280, 281. In particular, Moorman upheld a state income tax because an earlier decision had upheld a similar but \xe2\x80\x9cinherently more burdensome\xe2\x80\x9d gross receipts tax. Id., at 281. To say that the constitutionality of an income tax follows a\xc2\xa0fortiori from the constitutionality of a similar but \xe2\x80\x9cmore burdensome\xe2\x80\x9d gross receipts tax is to affirm, not reject, a distinction between the two.

\n

The Justices participating in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctrilogy,\xe2\x80\x9d in short, would scarcely expect to see the three decisions invoked to invalidate a tax on net income.

\n

II

\n

Abandoning principles and precedent sustaining simultaneous residence- and source-based income taxation, the Court offers two reasons for striking down Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s county income tax: (1) the tax creates a risk of double taxation, ante, at 7, 18; and (2) the Court deems Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s income tax \xe2\x80\x9cscheme\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cinherently discriminatory\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94by which the Court means, the scheme fails the so-called \xe2\x80\x9cinternal consistency\xe2\x80\x9d test, ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322. The first objection is overwhelmed by the history, recounted above, of States imposing and this Court upholding income taxes that carried a similar risk of double taxation. See supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x9312. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the internal consistency test is no more compelling.

\n

This Court has not rigidly required States to maintain internally consistent tax regimes. Before today, for two decades, the Court has not insisted that a tax under review pass the internal consistency test, see Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Jefferson Lines, Inc.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 175,\n185 (1995)\n, and has not struck down a state tax for failing the test in nearly 30 years, see American Trucking Assns., Inc. v. Scheiner,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 266\n\xe2\x80\x93287 (1987) (ATA I); Tyler Pipe Industries, Inc. v. Washington State Dept. of Revenue,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 232\n\xe2\x80\x93248 (1987). Moreover, the Court has rejected challenges to taxes that flunk the test. The Oklahoma tax \xe2\x80\x9cscheme\xe2\x80\x9d upheld under the dormant Commerce Clause in Shaffer, for example, is materially indistinguishable from\xe2\x80\x94therefore as internally inconsistent as\xe2\x80\x94Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s scheme. 252 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 57. And more recently, in American Trucking Assns., Inc. v. Michigan Pub. Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, the Court upheld a \xe2\x80\x9cconcede[dly]\xe2\x80\x9d internally inconsistent state tax.\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429,\n438 (2005)\n (ATA II). The Court did so, satisfied that there was a sufficiently close connection between the tax at issue and the local conduct that triggered the tax. See ibid.[6]

\n

The logic of ATA II, counsel for the Wynnes appeared to recognize, see Tr. of Oral Arg. 46\xe2\x80\x9347, would permit a State to impose a head tax\xe2\x80\x94i.e., a flat charge imposed on every resident in the State\xe2\x80\x94even if that tax were part of an internally inconsistent tax scheme. Such a tax would rest on purely local conduct: the taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s residence in the taxing State. And the taxes paid would defray costsclosely connected to that local conduct\xe2\x80\x94the services used by the taxpayer while living in the State.

\n

I see no reason why the Constitution requires us to disarm States from using a progressive tax, rather than a flat toll, to cover the costs of local services all residents enjoy. A head tax and a residence-based income tax differ, do they not, only in that the latter is measured by each taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s ability to pay. Like the head tax, however, a residence-based income tax is triggered by the purely local conduct of residing in the State. And also like the head tax, a residence-based income tax covers costs closely connected to that residence: It finances services used by those living in the State. If a head tax qualifies for ATA II\xe2\x80\x99s reprieve from internal consistency, then so too must a residence-based income tax.

\n

The majority asserts that because Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme is internally inconsistent, it \xe2\x80\x9coperates as a tariff,\xe2\x80\x9d making it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98patently unconstitutional.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 22. This is a curious claim. The defining characteristic of a tariff is that it taxes interstate activity at a higher rate than it taxes the same activity conducted within the State. See West Lynn Creamery, 512 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 193. Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s resident income tax does the exact opposite: It taxes the income of its residents at precisely the same rate, whether the income is earned in-state or out-of-state.[7]

\n

There is, moreover, a deep flaw in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s chosen test. The Court characterizes internal consistency as a \xe2\x80\x9ccure,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 18, 25\xe2\x80\x9326, but the test is scarcely that, at least for the double taxation the Court believes to justify its intervention. According to the Court, Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax \xe2\x80\x9cscheme\xe2\x80\x9d is internally inconsistent because Maryland simultaneously imposes two taxes: the county income tax and the special nonresident tax. See ante, at 7, 21\xe2\x80\x9322, and n.\xc2\xa08. But only one of these taxes\xe2\x80\x94the county income tax\xe2\x80\x94actually falls on the Wynnes. Because it is the interaction between these two taxes that renders Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme internally inconsistent, Maryland could eliminate the inconsistency by terminating the special nonresident tax\xe2\x80\x94a measure that would not help the Wynnes at all.[8] Maryland could, in other words, bring itself into compliance with the test at the heart of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis without removing the double tax burden the test is pur-portedly designed to \xe2\x80\x9ccure.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

To illustrate this oddity, consider the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csimple example\xe2\x80\x9d of April (who lives and works in State A) and Bob (who lives in State A, but works in State B). Ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322, 25. Both States fail the internal consistency test because they impose (1) a 1.25% tax on income that residents earn in-state, (2)\xc2\xa0a 1.25% tax on income that residents earn in other jurisdictions, and (3)\xc2\xa0a 1.25% tax on income that nonresidents earn in-state. According to the Court, these tax schemes are troubling because \xe2\x80\x9cBob will pay more income tax than April solely because he earns income interstate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 22.

\n

Each State, however, need not pursue the same approach to make their tax schemes internally consistent.[9] See ante, at 25\xe2\x80\x9326. State A might choose to tax residents\xe2\x80\x99 worldwide income only, which it could do by eliminating tax #3 (on nonresidents\xe2\x80\x99 in-state income). State B might instead choose exclusively to tax income earned within the State by deleting tax #2 (on residents\xe2\x80\x99 out-of-state income). Each State\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme would then be internally consistent. But the tax burden on April and Bob would remain unchanged: Just as under the original schemes, April would have to pay a 1.25% tax only once, to State A, and Bob would have to pay a 1.25% tax twice: once to State A, where he resides, and once to State B, where he earns the income. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccure,\xe2\x80\x9d in other words, is no match for the perceived disease.[10]

\n

The Court asserts that this flaw is just a \xe2\x80\x9ctruism\xe2\x80\x9d of every discrimination case, whether brought under the dormant Commerce Clause or the Equal Protection Clause. Ante, at 26. That is simply incorrect. As the Court acknowledges, a government that impermissibly \xe2\x80\x9ctreats like cases differently\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., discriminates) can ordinarily cure the violation either by \xe2\x80\x9cleveling up\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cleveling down.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). Consider another April and Bob example. If Bob must pay a 10% tax and April must pay a 5% tax, that discrimination can be eliminated either by requiring both to pay the 10% tax (\xe2\x80\x9cleveling up\xe2\x80\x9d) or by requiring both to pay the 5% tax (\xe2\x80\x9cleveling down\xe2\x80\x9d). True, \xe2\x80\x9cleveling up\xe2\x80\x9d leaves Bob\xe2\x80\x99s tax bill unchanged. \xe2\x80\x9cLeveling up\xe2\x80\x9d nonetheless benefits Bob because it eliminates the unfairness of being treated differently. And if, as is often true in dormant Commerce Clause cases, April and Bob compete in the same market, then \xe2\x80\x9cleveling up\xe2\x80\x9d provides the concrete benefit of placing a new burden on Bob\xe2\x80\x99s competitors.

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s rule does not work this way. As just explained, Maryland can \xe2\x80\x9ccure\xe2\x80\x9d what the majority deems discrimination without lowering the Wynnes\xe2\x80\x99 taxes or increasing the tax burden on any of the Wynnes\xe2\x80\x99 neighbors\xe2\x80\x94by terminating the special nonresident tax. See supra, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317. The State can, in other words, satisfy the majority not by lowering Bob\xe2\x80\x99s taxes or by raising April\xe2\x80\x99s taxes, but by eliminating the taxes imposed on yet a third taxpayer (say, Cathy). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s internal consis-tency test thus scarcely resembles \xe2\x80\x9cordinary\xe2\x80\x9d anti-discrimination law. Whatever virtue the internal consistency test has in other contexts, this shortcoming makes it a poor excuse for jettisoning taxation principles as entrenched as those here.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

This case is, at bottom, about policy choices: Should States prioritize ensuring that all who live or work within the State shoulder their fair share of the costs of government? Or must States prioritize avoidance of double taxation? As I have demonstrated, supra, at 16\xe2\x80\x9319, achieving even the latter goal is beyond this Court\xe2\x80\x99s competence. Resolving the competing tax policy considerations this case implicates is something the Court is even less well equipped to do. For a century, we have recognized that state legislatures and the Congress are constitutionally assigned and institutionally better equipped to balance such issues. I would reverse, so that we may leave that task where it belongs.

COMPTROLLER OF THE TREASURY OF MARYLAND, PETITIONER v. BRIAN WYNNE et\xc2\xa0ux.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the court of appeals of maryland

\n

[May 18, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Scalia, with whom Justice Thomas joins as to Parts I and II, dissenting.

\n

The Court holds unconstitutional Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to give its residents full credits against income taxes paid to other States. It does this by invoking the negative Commerce Clause, a judge-invented rule under which judges may set aside state laws that they think impose too much of a burden upon interstate commerce. I join the principal dissent, which demonstrates the incompatibility of this decision with our prior negative Commerce Clause cases. Post, at 2\xe2\x80\x9314 (opinion of Ginsburg, J.). Incompatibility, however, is not the test for me\xe2\x80\x94though what is incompatible with our cases a\xc2\xa0fortiori fails my test as well, as discussed briefly in Part III below. The principal purpose of my writing separately is to point out how wrong our negative Commerce Clause jurisprudence is in the first place, and how well today\xe2\x80\x99s decision illustrates its error.

\n

I

\n

The fundamental problem with our negative Commerce Clause cases is that the Constitution does not contain a negative Commerce Clause. It contains only a Commerce Clause. Unlike the negative Commerce Clause adopted by the judges, the real Commerce Clause adopted by the People merely empowers Congress to \xe2\x80\x9cregulate Commerce with foreign Nations, and among the several States, and with the Indian Tribes.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa03. The Clause says nothing about prohibiting state laws that burden commerce. Much less does it say anything about authorizing judges to set aside state laws they believe burden commerce. The clearest sign that the negative Commerce Clause is a judicial fraud is the utterly illogical holding that congressional consent enables States to enact laws that would otherwise constitute impermissible burdens upon interstate commerce. See Prudential Ins. Co. v. Benjamin,\n\n328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 408\n\xe2\x80\x93427 (1946). How could congressional consent lift a constitutional prohibition? See License Cases, 5 How. 504, 580 (1847) (opinion of Taney, C.\xc2\xa0J.).

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to justify this judicial economic veto come to naught. The Court claims that the doctrine \xe2\x80\x9chas deep roots.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. So it does, like many weeds. But age alone does not make up for brazen invention. And the doctrine in any event is not quite as old as the Court makes it seem. The idea that the Commerce Clause of its own force limits state power \xe2\x80\x9cfinds no expression\xe2\x80\x9d in discussions surrounding the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s ratification. F. Frankfurter, The Commerce Clause Under Marshall, Taney and Waite 13 (1937). For years after the adoption of the Constitution, States continually made regulations that burdened interstate commerce (like pilotage laws and quarantine laws) without provoking any doubts about their constitutionality. License Cases, supra, at 580\xe2\x80\x93581. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s earliest allusions to a negative Commerce Clause came only in dicta\xe2\x80\x94ambiguous dicta, at that\xe2\x80\x94and were vigorously contested at the time. See, e.g., id., at 581\xe2\x80\x93582. Our first clear holding setting aside a state law under the negative Commerce Clause came after the Civil War, more than 80 years after the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s adoption. Case of the State Freight Tax, 15 Wall. 232 (1873). Since then, we have tended to revamp the doctrine every couple of decades upon finding existing decisions unworkable or unsatisfactory. See Quill Corp. v. North Dakota,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 298,\n309 (1992)\n. The negative Commerce Clause applied today has little in common with the negative Commerce Clause of the 19th century, except perhaps for incoherence.

\n

The Court adds that \xe2\x80\x9ctariffs and other laws that burdened interstate commerce\xe2\x80\x9d were among \xe2\x80\x9cthe chief evils that led to the adoption of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. This line of reasoning forgets that interpretation requires heeding more than the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s purposes; it requires heeding the means the Constitution uses to achieve those purposes. The Constitution addresses the evils of local impediments to commerce by prohibiting States from imposing certain especially burdensome taxes\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cImposts or Duties on Imports or Exports\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cDut[ies] of Tonnage\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94without congressional consent. Art. I, \xc2\xa710, cls. 2\xe2\x80\x933. It also addresses these evils by giving Congress a com-merce power under which it may prohibit other burdensome taxes and laws. As the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s text shows, however, it does not address these evils by empowering the judiciary to set aside state taxes and laws that it deems too burdensome. By arrogating this power anyway, our negative Commerce Clause cases have disrupted the balance the Constitution strikes between the goal of protecting commerce and competing goals like preserving local autonomy and promoting democratic responsibility.

\n

II

\n

The failings of negative Commerce Clause doctrine go beyond its lack of a constitutional foundation, as today\xe2\x80\x99s decision well illustrates.

\n

\n

1.\xe2\x80\x82One glaring defect of the negative Commerce Clause is its lack of governing principle. Neither the Constitution nor our legal traditions offer guidance about how to sepa-rate improper state interference with commerce from permissible state taxation or regulation of commerce. So we must make the rules up as we go along. That is how we ended up with the bestiary of ad\xc2\xa0hoc tests and ad\xc2\xa0hoc exceptions that we apply nowadays, including the substantial nexus test, the fair apportionment test, and the fair relation test, Complete Auto Transit, Inc. v. Brady,\n\n430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 274,\n279 (1977)\n, the interest-on-state-bonds exception, Department of Revenue of Ky. v. Davis,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 328\n\xe2\x80\x93356 (2008), and the sales-taxes-on-mail-orders exception, Quill Corp., supra, at 314\xe2\x80\x93319.

\n

The internal consistency rule invoked by the Court nicely showcases our ad hocery. Under this rule, a tax violates the Constitution if its hypothetical adoption by all States would interfere with interstate commerce. Ante, at 19. How did this exercise in counterfactuals find its way into our basic charter? The test, it is true, bears some resemblance to Kant\xe2\x80\x99s first formulation of the categorical imperative: \xe2\x80\x9cAct only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law\xe2\x80\x9d without contradiction. Grounding for the Metaphysics of Morals 30 (J. Ellington transl. 3d ed. 1993). It bears no resemblance, however, to anything in the text or structure of the Constitution. Nor can one discern an obligation of internal consistency from our legal traditions, which show that States have been imposing internally inconsistent taxes for quite a while\xe2\x80\x94until recently with our approval. See, e.g., General Motors Corp. v. Washington,\n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 436 (1964)\n (upholding internally inconsistent business activities tax); Hinson v. Lott, 8 Wall. 148 (1869) (upholding internally inconsistent liquor tax). No, the only justification for the test seems to be that this Court disapproves of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98cross-border tax disadvantage[s]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d when created by internally inconsistent taxes, but is willing to tolerate them when created by \xe2\x80\x9cthe interaction of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. internally consistent schemes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 19. \xe2\x80\x9cWhatever it is we are expounding in this area, it is not a Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d American Trucking Assns., Inc. v. Smith,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 167,\n203 (1990)\n (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

\n

2.\xe2\x80\x82Another conspicuous feature of the negative Commerce Clause is its instability. Because no principle anchors our development of this doctrine\xe2\x80\x94and because the line between wise regulation and burdensome interference changes from age to economic age\xe2\x80\x94one can never tell when the Court will make up a new rule or throw away an old one. \xe2\x80\x9cChange is almost [the doctrine\xe2\x80\x99s] natural state, as it is the natural state of legislation in a constantly changing national economy.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision continues in this proud tradition. Consider a few ways in which it contradicts earlier decisions:

\n

In an earlier case, the Court conceded that a trucking tax \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ed] the \xe2\x80\x98internal consistency\xe2\x80\x99 test,\xe2\x80\x9d but upheld the tax anyway. American Trucking Assns., Inc. v. Michigan Pub. Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429,\n437 (2005)\n. Now, the Court proclaims that an income tax \xe2\x80\x9cfails the internal consistency test,\xe2\x80\x9d and for that reason strikes it down. Ante, at 21.

\n

In an earlier case, the Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is not a purpose of the Commerce Clause to protect state residents from their own state taxes\xe2\x80\x9d and that residents could \xe2\x80\x9ccomplain about and change the tax through the [State\xe2\x80\x99s] political process.\xe2\x80\x9d Goldberg v. Sweet,\n\n488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 252,\n266 (1989)\n. Now, the Court concludes that the negative Commerce Clause operates \xe2\x80\x9cregardless of whether the plaintiff is a resident .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or nonresident\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe notion that [residents] have a complete remedy at the polls is fanciful.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11, 12.

\n

In an earlier case, the Court said that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he difference in effect between a tax measured by gross receipts and one measured by net income .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is manifest and substantial.\xe2\x80\x9d United States Glue Co. v. Town of Oak Creek,\n\n247 U.\xc2\xa0S. 321,\n328 (1918)\n. Now, the Court says that the \xe2\x80\x9cformal distinction\xe2\x80\x9d between taxes on net and gross income \xe2\x80\x9cshould [not] matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7.

\n

In an earlier case, the Court upheld a tax despite its economic similarity to the gross-receipts tax struck down in Central Greyhound Lines, Inc. v. Mealey,\n\n334 U.\xc2\xa0S. 653 (1948)\n. Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Jefferson Lines, Inc.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 175\n\xe2\x80\x93191 (1995). The Court explained that \xe2\x80\x9ceconomic equivalence alone has .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not been (and should not be) the touchstone of Commerce Clause jurisprudence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 196\xe2\x80\x93197, n.\xc2\xa07. Now, the Court strikes down a tax in part because of its economic similarity to the gross-receipts tax struck down in Central Greyhound. Ante, at 7. The Court explains that \xe2\x80\x9cwe must consider \xe2\x80\x98not the formal language of the tax statute but rather its practical effect.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

So much for internal consistency.

\n

\n

3.\xe2\x80\x82A final defect of our Synthetic Commerce Clause cases is their incompatibility with the judicial role. The doctrine does not call upon us to perform a conventional judicial function, like interpreting a legal text, discerning a legal tradition, or even applying a stable body of precedents. It instead requires us to balance the needs of commerce against the needs of state governments. That is a task for legislators, not judges.

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s enterprise of eliminating double taxation puts this problem prominently on display. The one sure way to eliminate all double taxation is to prescribe uniform national tax rules\xe2\x80\x94for example, to allow taxation of income only where earned. But a program of prescribing a national tax code plainly exceeds the judicial competence. (It may even exceed the legislative competence to come up with a uniform code that accounts for the many political and economic differences among the States.) As an alternative, we could consider whether a State\xe2\x80\x99s taxes in practice overlap too much with the taxes of other States. But any such approach would drive us \xe2\x80\x9cto the perplexing inquiry, so unfit for the judicial department, what degree of taxation is the legitimate use, and what degree may amount to an abuse of power.\xe2\x80\x9d McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 430 (1819). The Court today chooses a third approach, prohibiting States from imposing internally inconsistent taxes. Ante, at 19. But that rule avoids double taxation only in the hypothetical world where all States adopt the same internally consistent tax, not in the real world where different States might adopt different internally consistent taxes. For example, if Maryland imposes its income tax on people who live in Maryland regardless of where they work (one internally consistent scheme), while Virginia imposes its income tax on people who work in Virginia regardless of where they live (an-other internally consistent scheme), Marylanders who work in Virginia still face double taxation. Post, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. Then again, it is only fitting that the Imaginary Commerce Clause would lead to imaginary benefits.

\n

III

\n

For reasons of stare decisis, I will vote to set aside a tax under the negative Commerce Clause if (but only if) it discriminates on its face against interstate commerce or cannot be distinguished from a tax this Court has already held unconstitutional. American Trucking Assns., 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 439 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment). The income tax before us does not discriminate on its face against interstate commerce; a resident pays no less to Maryland when he works in Maryland than when he works elsewhere. Neither is the tax before us indistinguishable from one that we have previously held unconstitutional. To the contrary, as the principal dissent establishes, our prior cases validate this tax.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to give residents full tax credits against income taxes paid to other States has its disadvantages. It threatens double taxation and encourages residents to work in Maryland. But Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s law also has its advantages. It allows the State to collect equal revenue from taxpayers with equal incomes, avoids the administrative burdens of verifying tax payments to other States, and ensures that every resident pays the State at least some income tax. Nothing in the Constitution precludes Maryland from deciding that the benefits of its tax scheme are worth the costs.

\n

I respectfully dissent.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cI continue to adhere to my view that the negative Commerce Clause has no basis in the text of the Constitution, makes little sense, and has proved virtually unworkable in application, and, consequently, cannot serve as a basis for striking down a state statute.\xe2\x80\x9d McBurney v. Young, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (Thomas, J., concurring) (slip op., at 1) (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted); accord, e.g., Camps Newfound/Owatonna, Inc. v. Town of Harrison,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564\n\xe2\x80\x93612 (1997) (Thomas, J., dissenting). For that reason, I would uphold Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme.

\n

In reaching the contrary conclusion, the Court proves just how far our negative Commerce Clause jurisprudence has departed from the actual Commerce Clause. According to the majority, a state income tax that fails to provide residents with \xe2\x80\x9ca full credit against the income taxes that they pay to other States\xe2\x80\x9d violates the Commerce Clause. Ante, at 1. That news would have come as a surprise to those who penned and ratified the Constitution. As this Court observed some time ago, \xe2\x80\x9cIncome taxes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. were imposed by several of the States at or shortly after the adoption of the Federal Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Shaffer v. Carter, \n\n252 U.\xc2\xa0S. 37,\n51 (1920)\n.[1]* There is no indication that those early state income tax schemes provided credits for income taxes paid elsewhere. Thus, under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, all of those state laws would have contravened the newly ratified Commerce Clause.

\n

It seems highly implausible that those who ratified the Commerce Clause understood it to conflict with the income tax laws of their States and nonetheless adopted it without a word of concern. That silence is particularly deafening given the importance of such taxes for raising revenues at the time. See Kinsman, The Income Tax in the Commonwealths of the United States 7, in 4 Publications of the American Economic Assn. (1903) (noting, for example, that \xe2\x80\x9cConnecticut from her earliest history had followed the plan of taxing incomes rather than property\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he total assessed value of [taxable] incomes in Connecticut in the year 1795 was a little over $300,000\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

In other areas of constitutional analysis, we would have considered these laws to be powerful evidence of the original understanding of the Constitution. We have, for ex-ample, relied on the practices of the First Congress to guide our interpretation of provisions defining congressional power. See, e.g., Golan v. Holder, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 16) (Copyright Clause); McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 401\xe2\x80\x93402 (1819) (Necessary and Proper Clause). We have likewise treated \xe2\x80\x9cactions taken by the First Congress a[s] presumptively consistent with the Bill of Rights,\xe2\x80\x9d Town of Greece v. Galloway, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (Alito, J., concurring) (slip op., at 12). See, e.g., id., at ___ \xe2\x80\x93 ___ (majority opinion) (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x938); Carroll v. United States,\n\n267 U.\xc2\xa0S. 132\n\xe2\x80\x93152 (1925). And we have looked to founding-era state laws to guide our understanding of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s meaning. See, e.g., District of Columbia v. Heller,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 570\n\xe2\x80\x93602 (2008) (\n \nSecond Amendment); Atwater v. Lago Vista,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 318\n\xe2\x80\x93340 (2001) (\n \nFourth Amendment); Roth v. United States,\n\n354 U.\xc2\xa0S. 476\n\xe2\x80\x93483 (1957) (\n \nFirst Amendment); Kilbourn v. Thompson,\n\n103 U.\xc2\xa0S. 168\n\xe2\x80\x93203 (1881) (Speech and Debate Clause); see also Calder v. Bull, 3 Dall. 386, 396\xe2\x80\x93397 (1798) (opinion of Paterson, J.) (Ex Post Facto Clause).

\n

Even if one assumed that the negative Commerce Clause existed, I see no reason why it would be subject to a different mode of constitutional interpretation. The majority quibbles that I fail to \xe2\x80\x9csho[w] that the small number of individuals who earned income out of State were taxed twice on that income,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 28, but given the deference we owe to the duly enacted laws of a State\xe2\x80\x94particularly those concerning the paradigmatically sovereign activity of taxation\xe2\x80\x94the burden of proof falls on those who would wield the Federal Constitution to foreclose that exercise of sovereign power.

\n

I am doubtful that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s application of one of our many negative Commerce Clause tests is correct under our precedents, see ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x937 (Scalia, J., dissenting); post, at 10\xe2\x80\x9319 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting), but I am certain that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s result is incorrect under our Constitution. As was well said in another area of constitutional law: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f there is any inconsistency between [our] tests and the historic practice .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , the inconsistency calls into question the validity of the test, not the historic practice.\xe2\x80\x9d Town of Greece, supra, at ___ (Alito, J., concurring) (slip op., at 12).

\n
