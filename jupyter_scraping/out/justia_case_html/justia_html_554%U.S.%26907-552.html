SPRINT COMMUNICATIONS CO., L. P., et\xc2\xa0al. v. APCC SERVICES, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93552.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 21, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 23, 2008

\n

A payphone customer making a long-distance call with an access code or 1\xe2\x80\x93800 number issued by a long-distance carrier pays the carrier (which completes the call). The carrier then compensates the payphone operator (which connects the call to the carrier in the first place). The payphone operator can sue the long-distance carrier for any compensation that the carrier fails to pay for these \xe2\x80\x9cdial-around\xe2\x80\x9d calls. Many payphone operators assign their dial-around claims to billing and collection firms (aggregators) so that, in effect, these aggregators can bring suit on their behalf. A group of aggregators (respondents here) were assigned legal title to the claims of approximately 1,400 payphone operators. The aggregators separately agreed to remit all proceeds to those operators, who would then pay the aggregators for their services. After entering into these agreements, the aggregators filed federal-court lawsuits seeking compensation from petitioner long-distance carriers. The District Court refused to dismiss the claims, finding that the aggregators had standing, and the D.C. Circuit ultimately affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0An assignee of a legal claim for money owed has standing to pursue that claim in federal court, even when the assignee has promised to remit the proceeds of the litigation to the assignor. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0History and precedent show that, for centuries, courts have found ways to allow assignees to bring suit; where assignment is at issue, courts\xe2\x80\x94both before and after the founding\xe2\x80\x94have always permitted the party with legal title alone to bring suit; and there is a strong tradition specifically of suits by assignees for collection. And while precedents of this Court, Waite v. Santa Cruz, 184 U. S. 302, Spiller v. Atchison, T. & S. F. R. Co., 253 U. S. 117, and Titus v. Wallick, 306 U. S. 282, do not conclusively resolve the standing question here, they offer powerful support for the proposition that suits by assignees for collection have long been seen as \xe2\x80\x9camenable\xe2\x80\x9d to resolution by the judicial process, Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U. S. 83, 102. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Petitioners offer no convincing reason to depart from the historical tradition of suits by assignees, including assignees for collection. In any event, the aggregators satisfy the Article III standing requirements articulated in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s more modern decisions. Petitioners argue that the aggregators have not themselves suffered an injury and that assignments for collection do not transfer the payphone operators\xe2\x80\x99 injuries. But the operators assigned their claims lock, stock, and barrel, and precedent makes clear that an assignee can sue based on his assignor\xe2\x80\x99s injuries. Vermont Agency of Natural Resources v. United States ex rel. Stevens, 529 U. S. 765. In arguing that the aggregators cannot satisfy the redressability requirement because they will remit their recovery to the payphone operators, petitioners misconstrue the nature of the redressability inquiry, which focuses on whether the injury that a plaintiff alleges is likely to be redressed through the litigation\xe2\x80\x94not on what the plaintiff ultimately intends to do with the money recovered. See, e.g., id., at 771. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claim that the assignments constitute nothing more than a contract for legal services is overstated. There is an important distinction between simply hiring a lawyer and assigning a claim to a lawyer. The latter confers a property right (which creditors might attach); the former does not. Finally, as a practical matter, it would be particularly unwise to abandon history and precedent in resolving the question here, for any such ruling could be overcome by, e.g., rewriting the agreement to give the aggregator a tiny portion of the assigned claim itself, perhaps only a dollar or two. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9320

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 reasons for denying prudential standing\xe2\x80\x94that the aggregators are seeking redress for third parties; that the litigation represents an effort by the aggregators and payphone operators to circumvent Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23\xe2\x80\x99s class-action requirements; and that practical problems could arise because the aggregators are suing, e.g., payphone operators may not comply with discovery requests or honor judgments\xe2\x80\x94are unpersuasive. And because there are no allegations that the assignments were made in bad faith and because the assignments were made for ordinary business purposes, any other prudential questions need not be considered here. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9323.

SPRINT COMMUNICATIONS COMPANY, L.\xc2\xa0P., et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. APCC SERVICES, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

[June 23, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question before us is whether an assignee of a legal claim for money owed has standing to pursue that claim in federal court, even when the assignee has promised to remit the proceeds of the litigation to the assignor. Because history and precedent make clear that such an assignee has long been permitted to bring suit, we conclude that the assignee does have standing.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When a payphone customer makes a long-distance call with an access code or 1\xe2\x80\x93800 number issued by a long-distance communications carrier, the customer pays the carrier (which completes that call), but not the payphone operator (which connects that call to the carrier in the first place). In these circumstances, the long-distance carrier is required to compensate the payphone operator for the customer\xe2\x80\x99s call. See 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7226; 47 CFR \xc2\xa764.1300 (2007). The payphone operator can sue the long-distance carrier in court for any compensation that the carrier fails to pay for these \xe2\x80\x9cdial-around\xe2\x80\x9d calls. And many have done so. See Global Crossing Telecommunications, Inc. v. Metrophones Telecommunications, Inc., 550 U. S. ___ (2007) (finding that the Communications Act of 1934 authorizes such suits).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because litigation is expensive, because the evidentiary demands of a single suit are often great, and because the resulting monetary recovery is often small, many payphone operators assign their dial-around claims to billing and collection firms called \xe2\x80\x9caggregators\xe2\x80\x9d so that, in effect, these aggregators can bring suit on their behalf. See Brief for Respondents 3. Typically, an individual aggregator collects claims from different payphone operators; the aggregator promises to remit to the relevant payphone operator (i.e., the assignor of the claim) any dial-around compensation that is recovered; the aggregator then pursues the claims in court or through settlement negotiations; and the aggregator is paid a fee for this service.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The present litigation involves a group of aggregators who have taken claim assignments from approximately 1,400 payphone operators. Each payphone operator signed an Assignment and Power of Attorney Agreement (Agreement) in which the payphone operator \xe2\x80\x9cassigns, transfers and sets over to [the aggregator] for purposes of collection all rights, title and interest of the [payphone operator] in the [payphone operator\xe2\x80\x99s] claims, demands or causes of action for \xe2\x80\x98Dial-Around Compensation\xe2\x80\x99 . . . due the [payphone operator] for periods since October 1, 1997.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 114a. The Agreement also \xe2\x80\x9cappoints\xe2\x80\x9d the aggregator as the payphone operator\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctrue and lawful attorney-in-fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Agreement provides that the aggregator will litigate \xe2\x80\x9cin the [payphone operator\xe2\x80\x99s] interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 115a. And the Agreement further stipulates that the assignment of the claims \xe2\x80\x9cmay not be revoked without the written consent of the [aggregator].\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The aggregator and payphone operator then separately agreed that the aggregator would remit all proceeds to the payphone operator and that the payphone operator would pay the aggregator for its services (typically via a quarterly charge).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After signing the agreements, the aggregators (respondents here) filed lawsuits in federal court seeking dial-around compensation from Sprint, AT&T, and other long-distance carriers (petitioners here). AT&T moved to dismiss the claims, arguing that the aggregators lack standing to sue under Article III of the Constitution. The District Court initially agreed to dismiss, APCC Servs., Inc. v. AT&T Corp., 254 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 135, 140\xe2\x80\x93141 (DC 2003), but changed its mind in light of a \xe2\x80\x9clong line of cases and legal treatises that recognize a well-established principle that assignees for collection purposes are entitled to bring suit where [as here] the assignments transfer absolute title to the claims.\xe2\x80\x9d APCC Servs., Inc. v. AT&T Corp., 281 F. Supp. 2d 41, 45 (DC 2003). After consolidating similar cases, a divided panel of the Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit agreed that the aggregators have standing to sue, but held that the relevant statutes do not create a private right of action. APCC Servs., Inc. v. Sprint Communications Co., 418 F.\xc2\xa03d 1238 (2005) (per curiam). This Court granted the aggregators\xe2\x80\x99 petition for certiorari on the latter statutory question, vacated the judgment, and remanded the case for reconsideration in light of Global Crossing, supra. APPC Services, Inc. v. Sprint Communications Co. 550 U. S. ___ (2007). On remand, the Court of Appeals affirmed the orders of the District Court allowing the litigation to go forward. 489 F.\xc2\xa03d 1249, 1250 (2007) (per curiam). The long-distance carriers then asked us to consider the standing question. We granted certiorari, and we now affirm.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin with the most basic doctrinal principles: Article III, \xc2\xa72, of the Constitution restricts the federal \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial Power\xe2\x80\x9d to the resolution of \xe2\x80\x9cCases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cControversies.\xe2\x80\x9d That case-or-controversy requirement is satisfied only where a plaintiff has standing. See, e.g., DaimlerChrysler Corp. v. Cuno, 547 U. S. 332 (2006). And in order to have Article III standing, a plaintiff must adequately establish: (1) an injury in fact (i.e., a \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete and particularized\xe2\x80\x9d invasion of a \xe2\x80\x9clegally protected interest\xe2\x80\x9d); (2) causation (i.e., a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fairly \xe2\x80\xa6 trace[able]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d connection between the alleged injury in fact and the alleged conduct of the defendant); and (3) redressability (i.e., it is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98likely\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and not \xe2\x80\x9cmerely \xe2\x80\x98speculative\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury will be remedied by the relief plaintiff seeks in bringing suit). Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U. S. 555, 560\xe2\x80\x93561 (1992) (calling these the \xe2\x80\x9cirreducible constitutional minimum\xe2\x80\x9d requirements).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In some sense, the aggregators clearly meet these requirements. They base their suit upon a concrete and particularized \xe2\x80\x9cinjury in fact,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, the carriers\xe2\x80\x99 failure to pay dial-around compensation. The carriers \xe2\x80\x9ccaused\xe2\x80\x9d that injury. And the litigation will \xe2\x80\x9credress\xe2\x80\x9d that injury\xe2\x80\x94if the suits are successful, the long-distance carriers will pay what they owe. The long-distance carriers argue, however, that the aggregators lack standing because it was the payphone operators (who are not plaintiffs), not the aggregators (who are plaintiffs), who were \xe2\x80\x9cinjured in fact\xe2\x80\x9d and that it is the payphone operators, not the aggregators, whose injuries a legal victory will truly \xe2\x80\x9credress\xe2\x80\x9d: The aggregators, after all, will remit all litigation proceeds to the payphone operators. Brief for Petitioners 18. Thus, the question before us is whether, under these circumstances, an assignee has standing to pursue the assignor\xe2\x80\x99s claims for money owed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have often said that history and tradition offer a meaningful guide to the types of cases that Article III empowers federal courts to consider. See, e.g., Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U. S. 83, 102 (1998) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe have always taken [the case-or-controversy requirement] to mean cases and controversies of the sort traditionally amenable to, and resolved by, the judicial process\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); GTE Sylvania, Inc. v. Consumers Union of United States, Inc., 445 U. S. 375, 382 (1980) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe purpose of the case-or-controversy requirement is to limit the business of federal courts to questions presented in an adversary context and in a form historically viewed as capable of resolution through the judicial process\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added and internal quotation marks omitted)); cf. Coleman v. Miller, 307 U. S. 433, 460 (1939) (opinion of Frankfurter, J.) (in crafting Article III, \xe2\x80\x9cthe framers \xe2\x80\xa6 gave merely the outlines of what were to them the familiar operations of the English judicial system and its manifestations on this side of the ocean before the Union\xe2\x80\x9d). Consequently, we here have carefully examined how courts have historically treated suits by assignors and assignees. And we have discovered that history and precedent are clear on the question before us: Assignees of a claim, including assignees for collection, have long been permitted to bring suit. A clear historical answer at least demands reasons for change. We can find no such reasons here, and accordingly we conclude that the aggregators have standing.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We must begin with a minor concession. Prior to the 17th century, English law would not have authorized a suit like this one. But that is because, with only limited exceptions, English courts refused to recognize assignments at all. See, e.g., Lampet\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 10 Co. Rep. 46b, 48a, 77 Eng. Rep. 994, 997 (K. B. 1612) (stating that \xe2\x80\x9cno possibility, right, title, nor thing in action, shall be granted or assigned to strangers\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)); Penson & Highbed\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 4 Leo. 99, 74 Eng. Rep. 756 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1590) (refusing to recognize the right of an assignee of a right in contract); see also 9 J. Murray, Corbin on Contracts \xc2\xa747.3, p. 134 (rev. ed. 2007) (noting that the King was excepted from the basic rule and could, as a result, always receive assignments).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Courts then strictly adhered to the rule that a \xe2\x80\x9cchose in action\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94an interest in property not immediately reducible to possession (which, over time, came to include a financial interest such as a debt, a legal claim for money, or a contractual right)\xe2\x80\x94simply \xe2\x80\x9ccould not be transferred to another person by the strict rules of the ancient common law.\xe2\x80\x9d See 2 W. Blackstone, Commentaries *442. To permit transfer, the courts feared, would lead to the \xe2\x80\x9cmultiplying of contentions and suits,\xe2\x80\x9d Lampet\xe2\x80\x99s Case, supra, at 48a, 77 Eng. Rep., at 997, and would also promote \xe2\x80\x9cmaintenance,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., officious intermeddling with litigation, see Holdsworth, History of the Treatment of Choses in Action by the Common Law, 33 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 997, 1006\xe2\x80\x931009 (1920).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the 17th century began, however, strict anti-assignment rules seemed inconsistent with growing commercial needs. And as English commerce and trade expanded, courts began to liberalize the rules that prevented assignments of choses in action. See 9 Corbin, supra, \xc2\xa747.3, at 134 (suggesting that the \xe2\x80\x9cpragmatic necessities of trade\xe2\x80\x9d induced \xe2\x80\x9cevolution of the common law\xe2\x80\x9d); Holdsworth, supra, at 1021\xe2\x80\x931022 (the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon law\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cinduced\xe2\x80\x9d to change because of \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderations of mercantile convenience or necessity\xe2\x80\x9d); J. Ames, Lectures on Legal History 214 (1913) (noting that the \xe2\x80\x9cobjection of maintenance\xe2\x80\x9d yielded to \xe2\x80\x9cthe modern commercial spirit\xe2\x80\x9d). By the beginning of the 18th century, courts routinely recognized assignments of equitable (but not legal) interests in a chose in action: Courts of equity permitted suits by an assignee who had equitable (but not legal) title. And courts of law effectively allowed suits either by the assignee (who had equitable, but not legal title) or the assignor (who had legal, but not equitable title).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be more specific, courts of equity would simply permit an assignee with a beneficial interest in a chose in action to sue in his own name. They might, however, require the assignee to bring in the assignor as a party to the action so as to bind him to whatever judgment was reached. See, e.g., Warmstrey v. Tanfield, 1 Ch. Rep. 29, 21 Eng. Rep. 498 (1628\xe2\x80\x931629); Fashion v. Atwood, 2 Ch. Cas. 36, 22 Eng. Rep. 835 (1688); Peters v. Soame, 2 Vern. 428, 428\xe2\x80\x93429, 23 Eng. Rep. 874 (Ch. 1701); Squib v. Wyn, 1 P. Wms. 378, 381, 24 Eng. Rep. 432, 433 (Ch. 1717); Lord Carteret v. Paschal, 3 P. Wms. 197, 199, 24 Eng. Rep. 1028, 1029 (Ch. 1733); Row v. Dawson, 1 Ves. sen. 331, 332\xe2\x80\x93333, 27 Eng. Rep. 1064, 1064\xe2\x80\x931065 (Ch. 1749). See also M. Smith, Law of Assignment: The Creation and Transfer of Choses in Action 131 (2007) (by the beginning of the 18th century, \xe2\x80\x9cit became settled that equity would recognize the validity of the assignment of both debts and of other things regarded by the common law as choses in action\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Courts of law, meanwhile, would permit the assignee with an equitable interest to bring suit, but nonetheless required the assignee to obtain a \xe2\x80\x9cpower of attorney\xe2\x80\x9d from the holder of the legal title, namely, the assignor, and further required the assignee to bring suit in the name of that assignor. See, e.g., Cook, Alienability of Choses in Action, 29 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 816, 822 (1916) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ommon law lawyers were able, through the device of the \xe2\x80\x98power of attorney\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 to enable the assignee to obtain relief in common law proceedings by suing in the name of the assignor\xe2\x80\x9d); 29 R. Lord, Williston on Contracts \xc2\xa774.2, pp. 214\xe2\x80\x93215 (4th ed. 2003). Compare, e.g., Barrow v. Gray, Cro. Eliz. 551, 78 Eng. Rep. 797 (Q. B. 1653), and South & Marsh\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 3 Leo. 234, 74 Eng. Rep. 654 (Exch. 1686) (limiting the use of a power of attorney to cases in which the assignor owed the assignee a debt), with Holdsworth, supra, at 1021 (noting that English courts abandoned that limitation by the end of the 18th century). At the same time, courts of law would permit an assignor to sue even when he had transferred away his beneficial interest. And they permitted the assignor to sue in such circumstances precisely because the assignor retained legal title. See, e.g., Winch v. Keeley, 1 T. R. 619, 99 Eng. Rep. 1284 (K. B. 1787) (allowing the bankrupt assignor of a chose in action to sue a debtor for the benefit of the assignee because the assignor possessed legal, though not equitable, title).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The upshot is that by the time Blackstone published volume II of his Commentaries in 1766, he could dismiss the \xe2\x80\x9cancient common law\xe2\x80\x9d prohibition on assigning choses in action as a \xe2\x80\x9cnicety \xe2\x80\xa6 now disregarded.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Blackstone, supra, at *442.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Legal practice in the United States largely mirrored that in England. In the latter half of the 18th century and throughout the 19th century, American courts regularly \xe2\x80\x9cexercised their powers in favor of the assignee,\xe2\x80\x9d both at law and in equity. 9 Corbin on Contracts \xc2\xa747.3, at 137. See, e.g., McCullum v. Coxe, 1 Dall. 139 (Pa. 1785) (protecting assignee of a debt against a collusive settlement by the assignor); Dennie v. Chapman, 1 Root 113, 115 (Conn. Super. 1789) (assignee of a nonnegotiable note can bring suit \xe2\x80\x9cin the name of the original promisee or his administrator\xe2\x80\x9d); Andrews v. Beecker, 1 Johns. Cas. 411, 411\xe2\x80\x93412, n. (N. Y. Sup. 1800) (\xe2\x80\x9cCourts of law \xe2\x80\xa6 are, in justice, bound to protect the rights of the assignees, as much as a court of equity, though they may still require the action to be brought in the name of the assignor\xe2\x80\x9d); Riddle & Co. v. Mandeville, 5 Cranch 322 (1809) (assignees of promissory notes entitled to bring suit in equity). Indeed, \xc2\xa711 of the Judiciary Act of 1789 specifically authorized federal courts to take \xe2\x80\x9ccognizance of any suit to recover the contents of any promissory note or other chose in action in favour of an assignee\xe2\x80\x9d so long as federal jurisdiction would lie if the assignor himself had brought suit. 1 Stat. 79.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, in 1816, Justice Story, writing for a unanimous Court, summarized the practice in American courts as follows: \xe2\x80\x9cCourts of law, following in this respect the rules of equity, now take notice of assignments of choses in action, and exert themselves to afford them every support and protection.\xe2\x80\x9d Welch v. Mandeville, 1 Wheat. 233, 236. He added that courts of equity have \xe2\x80\x9cdisregarded the rigid strictness of the common law, and protected the rights of the assignee of choses in action,\xe2\x80\x9d and noted that courts of common law \xe2\x80\x9cnow consider an assignment of a chose in action as substantially valid, only preserving, in certain cases, the form of an action commenced in the name of the assignor.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 237, n.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It bears noting, however, that at the time of the founding (and in some States well before then) the law did permit the assignment of legal title to at least some choses in action. In such cases, the assignee could bring suit on the assigned claim in his own name, in a court of law. See, e.g., 3 Va. Stat. at Large 378, Ch. XXXIV (W. Hening ed. 1823) (reprinted 1969) (Act of Oct. 1705) (permitting any person to \xe2\x80\x9cassign or transfer any bond or bill for debt over to any other person\xe2\x80\x9d and providing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe asignee or assignees, his and their executors and administrators by virtue of such assignment shall and may have lawfull power to commence and prosecute any suit at law in his or their own name or names\xe2\x80\x9d); Act of May 28, 1715, Ch. XXVIII, Gen. Laws of Penn. 60 (J. Dunlop 2d ed. 1849) (permitting the assignment of \xe2\x80\x9cbonds, specialties, and notes\xe2\x80\x9d and authorizing \xe2\x80\x9cthe person or persons, to whom the said bonds, specialties or notes, are \xe2\x80\xa6 assigned\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9ccommence and prosecute his, her, or their actions at law\xe2\x80\x9d); Patent Act of 1793, ch. 11, \xc2\xa74, 1 Stat. 322 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t shall be lawful for any inventor, his executor or administrator to assign the title and interest in the said invention, at anytime, and the assignee \xe2\x80\xa6 shall thereafter stand in\nthe place of the original inventor, both as to right and responsibility\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By the 19th century, courts began to consider the specific question presented here: whether an assignee of a legal claim for money could sue when that assignee had promised to give all litigation proceeds back to the assignor. During that century American law at the state level became less formalistic through the merger of law and equity, through statutes more generously permitting an assignor to pass legal title to an assignee, and through the adoption of rules that permitted any \xe2\x80\x9creal party in interest\xe2\x80\x9d to bring suit. See 6A C. Wright, A. Miller, & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa71541, pp. 320\xe2\x80\x93321 (2d ed. 1990) (hereinafter Wright & Miller); see also 9 Corbin, supra, \xc2\xa747.3, at 137. The courts recognized that pre-existing law permitted an assignor to bring suit on a claim even though the assignor retained nothing more than naked legal title. Since the law increasingly permitted the transfer of legal title to an assignee, courts agreed that assignor and assignee should be treated alike in this respect. And rather than abolish the assignor\xe2\x80\x99s well-established right to sue on the basis of naked legal title alone, many courts instead extended the same right to an assignee. See, e.g., Clark & Hutchins, The Real Party in Interest, 34 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 259, 264\xe2\x80\x93265 (1925) (noting that the changes in the law permitted both the assignee with \xe2\x80\x9cnaked legal title\xe2\x80\x9d and the assignee with an equitable interest in a claim to bring suit).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, during the 19th century, most state courts entertained suits virtually identical to the litigation before us: suits by individuals who were assignees for collection only, i.e., assignees who brought suit to collect money owed to their assignors but who promised to turn over to those assignors the proceeds secured through litigation. See, e.g., Webb & Hepp v. Morgan, McClung & Co., 14 Mo. 428, 431 (1851) (holding that the assignees of a promissory note for collection only can bring suit, even though they lack a beneficial interest in the note, because the assignment \xe2\x80\x9ccreates in them such legal interest, that they thereby become the persons to sue\xe2\x80\x9d); Meeker v. Claghorn, 44 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 349, 350, 353 (1871) (allowing suit by the assignee of a cause of action even though the assignors \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98expected to receive the amount recovered in the action,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d because the assignee, as \xe2\x80\x9clegal holder of the claim,\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cthe real party in interest\xe2\x80\x9d); Searing v. Berry, 58 Iowa 20, 23, 24, 11 N.\xc2\xa0W. 708, 709 (1882) (where legal title to a judgment was assigned \xe2\x80\x9cmerely for the purpose of enabling plaintiff to enforce the collection\xe2\x80\x9d and the assignor in fact retained the beneficial interest, the plaintiff-assignee could \xe2\x80\x9cprosecute this suit to enforce the collection of the judgment\xe2\x80\x9d); Grant v. Heverin, 77 Cal. 263, 265, 19 P. 493 (1888) (holding that the assignee of a bond could bring suit, even though he lacked a beneficial interest in the bond, and adopting the rule that an assignee with legal title to an assigned claim can bring suit even where the assignee must \xe2\x80\x9caccount to the assignor\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9ca part of the proceeds\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cis to account for the whole proceeds\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); McDaniel v. Pressler, 3 Wash. 636, 638, 637, 29 P. 209, 210 (1892) (holding that the assignee of promissory notes was the real party in interest, even though the assignment was \xe2\x80\x9cfor the purpose of collection\xe2\x80\x9d and the assignee had \xe2\x80\x9cno interest other than that of the legal holder of said notes\xe2\x80\x9d); Wines v. Rio Grande W.\xc2\xa0R. Co., 9 Utah 228, 235, 33 P. 1042, 1044, 1045 (1893) (holding that an assignee could bring suit based on causes of action assigned to him \xe2\x80\x9csimply to enable him to sue\xe2\x80\x9d and who \xe2\x80\x9cwould turn over to the assignors all that was recovered in the action, after deducting [the assignors\xe2\x80\x99] proportion of the expenses of the suit\xe2\x80\x9d); Gomer v. Stockdale, 5 Colo. App. 489, 492, 39 P. 355, 357, 356 (1895) (permitting suit by a party who was assigned legal title to contractual rights, where the assignor retained the beneficial interest, noting that the doctrine that \xe2\x80\x9cprevails in Colorado\xe2\x80\x9d is that the assignee may bring suit in his own name \xe2\x80\x9calthough there may be annexed to the transfer the condition that when the sum is collected the whole or some part of it must be paid over to the assignor\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Appendix, infra (collecting cases from numerous other States approving of suits by assignees for collection).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, the dissent rightly notes, some States during this period of time refused to recognize assignee-for-collection suits, or otherwise equivocated on the matter. See post, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. But so many States allowed these suits that by 1876, the distinguished procedure and equity scholar John Norton Pomeroy declared it \xe2\x80\x9csettled by a great preponderance of authority, although there is some conflict\xe2\x80\x9d that an assignee is \xe2\x80\x9centitled to sue in his own name\xe2\x80\x9d whenever the assignment vests \xe2\x80\x9clegal title\xe2\x80\x9d in the assignee, and notwithstanding \xe2\x80\x9cany contemporaneous, collateral agreement by virtue of which he is to receive a part only of the proceeds \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0or even is to thus account [to the assignor] for the whole proceeds.\xe2\x80\x9d Remedies and Remedial Rights \xc2\xa7132, p. 159 (internal quotation marks omitted and emphasis added). Other contemporary scholars reached the same basic conclusion. See, e.g., P. Bliss, A Treatise upon the Law of Pleading \xc2\xa751, p. 69 (2d ed. 1887) (stating that \xe2\x80\x9c[m]ost of the courts have held that where negotiable paper has been indorsed, or other choses in action have been assigned, it does not concern the defendant for what purpose the transfer has been made\xe2\x80\x9d and giving examples of States permitting assignees to bring suit even where they lacked a beneficial interest in the assigned claims (emphasis added)). See also Clark & Hutchins, supra, at 264 (\xe2\x80\x9cmany, probably most, American jurisdictions\xe2\x80\x9d have held that \xe2\x80\x9can assignee who has no beneficial interest, like an assignee for collection only, may prosecute an action in his own name\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Even Michael Ferguson\xe2\x80\x99s California Law Review Comment\xe2\x80\x94which the dissent cites as support for its argument about \xe2\x80\x9cthe divergent practice\xe2\x80\x9d among the courts, post, at 14\xe2\x80\x94recognizes that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] majority of courts has held that an assignee for collection only is a real party in interest\xe2\x80\x9d entitled to bring suit. See Comment, The Real Party in Interest Rule Revitalized: Recognizing Defendant\xe2\x80\x99s Interest in the Determination of Proper Parties Plaintiff, 55 Cal. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1452, 1475 (1967) (emphasis added); see also id., at 1476, n. 118 (noting that even \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he few courts that have wavered on the question have always ended up in the camp of the majority\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0During this period, a number of federal courts similarly indicated approval of suits by assignees for collection only. See, e.g., Bradford v. Jenks, 3 F. Cas. 1132, 1134 (No. 1,769) (CC Ill. 1840) (stating that the plaintiff, the receiver of a bank, could bring suit in federal court to collect on a note owed to that bank if he sued as the bank\xe2\x80\x99s assignee, not its receiver, but ultimately holding that the plaintiff could not sue as an assignee because there was no diversity jurisdiction); Orr v. Lacy, 18 F. Cas. 834 (No. 10,589) (CC Mich. 1847) (affirming judgment for the plaintiff, the endorsee of a bill of exchange, on the ground that, as endorsee, he had the \xe2\x80\x9clegal right\xe2\x80\x9d to bring suit notwithstanding the fact that the proceeds of the litigation would be turned over to the endorser); Murdock v. The Emma Graham, 17 F. Cas. 1012, 1013 (No. 9,940) (DC SD Ohio 1878) (permitting the assignee of a claim for injury to a \xe2\x80\x9cfloat or barge\xe2\x80\x9d to bring suit when, \xe2\x80\x9cunder the assignment,\xe2\x80\x9d the assignor\xe2\x80\x99s creditors would benefit from the litigation); The Rupert City, 213 F. 263, 266\xe2\x80\x93267 (WD Wash. 1914) (assignees of claims for collection only could bring suit in maritime law because \xe2\x80\x9can assignment for collection \xe2\x80\xa6 vest[s] such an interest in [an] assignee as to entitle him to sue\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even this Court long ago indicated that assignees for collection only can properly bring suit. For example, in Waite v. Santa Cruz, 184 U. S. 302 (1902), the plaintiff sued to collect on a number of municipal bonds and coupons whose \xe2\x80\x9clegal title\xe2\x80\x9d had been vested in him but which were transferred to him \xe2\x80\x9cfor collection only.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 324. The Court, in a unanimous decision, ultimately held that the federal courts could not hear his suit because the amount-in-controversy requirement of diversity jurisdiction would not have been satisfied if the bondholders and coupon holders had sued individually. See id., at 328\xe2\x80\x93329. However, before reaching this holding, the Court expressly stated that the suit could properly be brought in federal court \xe2\x80\x9cif the only objection to the jurisdiction of the Circuit Court is that the plaintiff was invested with the legal title to the bonds and coupons simply for purposes of collection.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 325.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Next, in Spiller v. Atchison, T. & S. F. R. Co., 253 U. S. 117 (1920), a large number of cattle shippers assigned to Spiller (the secretary of a Cattle Raiser\xe2\x80\x99s Association) their individual reparation claims against railroads they said had charged them excessive rates. The Federal Court of Appeals held that Spiller could not bring suit because, in effect, he was an assignee for collection only and would be passing back to the cattle shippers any money he recovered from the litigation. In a unanimous decision, this Court reversed. The Court wrote that the cattle shippers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cassignments were absolute in form\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cplainly\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cvest[ed] the legal title in Spiller.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 134. The Court conceded that the assignments did not pass \xe2\x80\x9cbeneficial or equitable title\xe2\x80\x9d to Spiller. Ibid. But the Court then said that \xe2\x80\x9cthis was not necessary to support the right of the assignee to claim an award of reparation and enable him to recover it by action at law brought in his own name but for the benefit of the equitable owners of the claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court thereby held that Spiller\xe2\x80\x99s legal title alone was sufficient to allow him to bring suit in federal court on the aggregated claims of his assignors.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, in Titus v. Wallick, 306 U. S. 282 (1939), this Court unanimously held that (under New York law) a plaintiff, an assignee for collection, had \xe2\x80\x9cdominion over the claim for purposes of suit\xe2\x80\x9d because the assignment purported to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98sell, assign, transfer and set over\xe2\x80\x99 the chose in action\xe2\x80\x9d to the assignee. Id., at 289. More importantly for present purposes, the Court said that the assignment\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9clegal effect was not curtailed by the recital that the assignment was for purposes of suit and that its proceeds were to be turned over or accounted for to another.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be clear, we do not suggest that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions in Waite, Spiller, and Titus conclusively resolve the standing question before us. We cite them because they offer additional and powerful support for the proposition that suits by assignees for collection have long been seen as \xe2\x80\x9camenable\xe2\x80\x9d to resolution by the judicial process. Steel Co., 523 U. S., at 102.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, we note that there is also considerable, more recent authority showing that an assignee for collection may properly sue on the assigned claim in federal court. See, e.g., 6A Wright & Miller \xc2\xa71545, at 346\xe2\x80\x93348 (noting that an assignee with legal title is considered to be a real party in interest and that as a result \xe2\x80\x9cfederal courts have held that an assignee for purposes of collection who holds legal title to the debt according to the governing substantive law is the real party in interest even though the assignee must account to the assignor for whatever is recovered in the action\xe2\x80\x9d); 6 Am. Jur. 2d, Assignments \xc2\xa7184, pp. 262\xe2\x80\x93263 (1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn assignee for collection or security only is within the meaning of the real party in interest statutes and entitled to sue in his or her own name on an assigned account or chose in action, although he or she must account to the assignor for the proceeds of the action, even when the assignment is without consideration\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)). See also Rosenblum v. Dingfelder, 111 F.\xc2\xa02d 406, 407 (CA2 1940); Staggers v. Otto Gerdau Co., 359 F.\xc2\xa02d 292, 294 (CA2 1966); Dixie Portland Flour Mills, Inc. v. Dixie Feed & Seed Co., 382 F.\xc2\xa02d 830, 833 (CA6 1967); Klamath-Lake Pharmaceutical Assn. v. Klamath Medical Serv. Bur., 701 F.\xc2\xa02d 1276, 1282 (CA9 1983).

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The history and precedents that we have summarized make clear that courts have long found ways to allow assignees to bring suit; that where assignment is at issue, courts\xe2\x80\x94both before and after the founding\xe2\x80\x94 have always permitted the party with legal title alone to bring suit; and that there is a strong tradition specifically of suits by assignees for collection. We find this history and precedent \xe2\x80\x9cwell nigh conclusive\xe2\x80\x9d in respect to the issue before us: Lawsuits by assignees, including assignees for collection only, are \xe2\x80\x9ccases and controversies of the sort traditionally amenable to, and resolved by, the judicial process.\xe2\x80\x9d Vermont Agency of Natural Resources v. United States ex rel. Stevens, 529 U. S. 765, 777\xe2\x80\x93778 (2000) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners have not offered any convincing reason why we should depart from the historical tradition of suits by assignees, including assignees for collection. In any event, we find that the assignees before us satisfy the Article III standing requirements articulated in more modern decisions of this Court.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners argue, for example, that the aggregators have not themselves suffered any injury in fact and that the assignments for collection \xe2\x80\x9cdo not suffice to transfer the payphone operators\xe2\x80\x99 injuries.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 18. It is, of course, true that the aggregators did not originally suffer any injury caused by the long-distance carriers; the payphone operators did. But the payphone operators assigned their claims to the aggregators lock, stock, and barrel. See APPC Servs., 418 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1243 (there is \xe2\x80\x9cno reason to believe the assignment is anything less than a complete transfer to the aggregator\xe2\x80\x9d of the injury and resulting claim); see also App. to Pet. for Cert. 114a (Agreement provides that each payphone operator \xe2\x80\x9cassigns, transfers and sets over\xe2\x80\x9d to the aggregator \xe2\x80\x9call rights, title and interest\xe2\x80\x9d in dial-around compensation claims). And within the past decade we have expressly held that an assignee can sue based on his assignor\xe2\x80\x99s injuries. In Vermont Agency, supra, we considered whether a qui tam relator possesses Article III standing to bring suit under the False Claims Act, which authorizes a private party to bring suit to remedy an injury (fraud) that the United States, not the private party, suffered. We held that such a relator does possess standing. And we said that is because the Act \xe2\x80\x9ceffect[s] a partial assignment of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s damages claim\xe2\x80\x9d and that assignment of the \xe2\x80\x9cUnited States\xe2\x80\x99 injury in fact suffices to confer standing on [the relator].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 773, 774. Indeed, in Vermont Agency we stated quite unequivocally that \xe2\x80\x9cthe assignee of a claim has standing to assert the injury in fact suffered by the assignor.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 773.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners next argue that the aggregators cannot satisfy the redressability requirement of standing because, if successful in this litigation, the aggregators will simply remit the litigation proceeds to the payphone operators. But petitioners misconstrue the nature of our redressability inquiry. That inquiry focuses, as it should, on whether the injury that a plaintiff alleges is likely to be redressed through the litigation\xe2\x80\x94not on what the plaintiff ultimately intends to do with the money he recovers. See, e.g., id., at 771 (to demonstrate redressability, the plaintiff must show a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial likelihood that the requested relief will remedy the alleged injury in fact\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted and emphasis added)); Lujan, 504 U. S., at 561 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t must be likely . . . that the injury will be redressed by a favorable decision\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted and emphasis added)). Here, a legal victory would unquestionably redress the injuries for which the aggregators bring suit. The aggregators\xe2\x80\x99 injuries relate to the failure to receive the required dial-around compensation. And if the aggregators prevail in this litigation, the long-distance carriers would write a check to the aggregators for the amount of dial-around compensation owed. What does it matter what the aggregators do with the money afterward? The injuries would be redressed whether the aggregators remit the litigation proceeds to the payphone operators, donate them to charity, or use them to build new corporate headquarters. Moreover, the statements our prior cases made about the need to show redress of the injury are consistent with what numerous authorities have long held in the assignment context, namely, that an assignee for collection may properly bring suit to redress the injury originally suffered by his assignor. Petitioners might disagree with those authorities. But petitioners have not provided us with a good reason to reconsider them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent argues that our redressability analysis \xe2\x80\x9ccould not be more wrong,\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e have never approved federal-court jurisdiction over a claim where the entire relief requested will run to a party not before the court. Never.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 5 (opinion of Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J.). But federal courts routinely entertain suits which will result in relief for parties that are not themselves directly bringing suit. Trustees bring suits to benefit their trusts; guardians ad\xc2\xa0litem bring suits to benefit their wards; receivers bring suit to benefit their receiverships; assignees in bankruptcy bring suit to benefit bankrupt estates; executors bring suit to benefit testator estates; and so forth. The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view of redressability, if taken seriously, would work a sea change in the law. Moreover, to the extent that trustees, guardians ad\xc2\xa0litem, and the like have some sort of \xe2\x80\x9cobligation\xe2\x80\x9d to the parties whose interests they vindicate through litigation, see post, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, n.\xc2\xa02, the same is true in respect to the aggregators here. The aggregators have a contractual obligation to litigate \xe2\x80\x9cin the [payphone operator\xe2\x80\x99s] interest.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 115a. (And if the aggregators somehow violate that contractual obligation, say, by agreeing to settle the claims against the long-distance providers in exchange for a kickback from those providers, each payphone operator would be able to bring suit for breach of contract.)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners also make a further conceptual argument. They point to cases in which this Court has said that a party must possess a \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal stake\xe2\x80\x9d in a case in order to have standing under Article III. See Baker v. Carr, 369 U. S. 186, 204 (1962). And petitioners add that, because the aggregators will not actually benefit from a victory in this case, they lack a \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal stake\xe2\x80\x9d in the litigation\xe2\x80\x99s outcome. The problem with this argument is that the general \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal stake\xe2\x80\x9d requirement and the more specific standing requirements (injury in fact, redressability, and causation) are flip sides of the same coin. They are simply different descriptions of the same judicial effort to assure, in every case or controversy, \xe2\x80\x9cthat concrete adverseness which sharpens the presentation of issues upon which the court so largely depends for illumination.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. See also Massachusetts v. EPA, 549 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 13) (\xe2\x80\x9cAt bottom, the gist of the question of standing is whether petitioners have such a personal stake in the outcome of the controversy as to assure that concrete adverseness\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Courts, during the past two centuries, appear to have found that \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete adverseness\xe2\x80\x9d where an assignee for collection brings a lawsuit. And petitioners have provided us with no grounds for reaching a contrary conclusion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners make a purely functional argument, as well. Read as a whole, they say, the assignments in this litigation constitute nothing more than a contract for legal services. We think this argument is overstated. There is an important distinction between simply hiring a lawyer and assigning a claim to a lawyer (on the lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s promise to remit litigation proceeds). The latter confers a property right (which creditors might attach); the former does not.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, we note, as a practical matter, that it would be particularly unwise for us to abandon history and precedent in resolving the question before us. Were we to agree with petitioners that the aggregators lack standing, our holding could easily be overcome. For example, the Agreement could be rewritten to give the aggregator a tiny portion of the assigned claim itself, perhaps only a dollar or two. Or the payphone operators might assign all of their claims to a \xe2\x80\x9cDial-Around Compensation Trust\xe2\x80\x9d and then pay a trustee (perhaps the aggregator) to bring suit on behalf of the trust. Accordingly, the far more sensible course is to abide by the history and tradition of assignee suits and find that the aggregators possess Article III standing.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners argue that, even if the aggregators have standing under Article III, we should nonetheless deny them standing for a number of prudential reasons. See Elk Grove Unified School Dist. v. Newdow, 542 U. S. 1, 11 (2004) (prudential standing doctrine \xe2\x80\x9cembodies judicially self-imposed limits on the exercise of federal jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, petitioners invoke certain prudential limitations that we have imposed in prior cases where a plaintiff has sought to assert the legal claims of third parties. See, e.g., Warth v. Seldin, 422 U. S. 490, 501 (1975) (expressing a \xe2\x80\x9creluctance to exert judicial power when the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim to relief rests on the legal rights of third parties\xe2\x80\x9d); Arlington Heights v. Metropolitan Housing Development Corp., 429 U. S. 252, 263 (1977) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn the ordinary case, a party is denied standing to assert the rights of third persons\xe2\x80\x9d); Secretary of State of Md. v. Joseph H. Munson Co., 467 U. S. 947, 955 (1984) (a plaintiff ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98cannot rest his claim to relief on the legal rights or interests of third parties\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These third-party cases, however, are not on point. They concern plaintiffs who seek to assert not their own legal rights, but the legal rights of others. See, e.g., Warth, supra, at 499 (plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally must assert his own legal rights and interests, and cannot rest his claim to relief on the legal rights or interests of third parties\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); see also Kowalski v. Tesmer, 543 U. S. 125 (2004) (lawyers lack standing to assert the constitutional rights of defendants deprived of appointed counsel on appeal); Powers v. Ohio, 499 U. S. 400 (1991) (permitting a criminal defendant to assert rights of juror discriminated against because of race); Craig v. Boren, 429 U. S. 190 (1976) (permitting beer vendors to assert rights of prospective male customers aged 18 to 21 who, unlike females of the same ages, were barred from purchasing beer). Here, the aggregators are suing based on injuries originally suffered by third parties. But the payphone operators assigned to the aggregators all \xe2\x80\x9crights, title and interest\xe2\x80\x9d in claims based on those injuries. Thus, in the litigation before us, the aggregators assert what are, due to that transfer, legal rights of their own. The aggregators, in other words, are asserting first-party, not third-party, legal rights. Moreover, we add that none of the third-party cases cited by petitioners involve assignments or purport to overturn the longstanding doctrine permitting an assignee to bring suit on an assigned claim.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, petitioners suggest that the litigation here simply represents an effort by the aggregators and the payphone operators to circumvent Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23\xe2\x80\x99s class-action requirements. But we do not understand how \xe2\x80\x9ccircumvention\xe2\x80\x9d of Rule 23 could constitute a basis for denying standing here. For one thing, class actions are permissive, not mandatory. More importantly, class actions constitute but one of several methods for bringing about aggregation of claims, i.e., they are but one of several methods by which multiple similarly situated parties get similar claims resolved at one time and in one federal forum. See Rule 20(a) (permitting joinder of multiple plaintiffs); Rule 42 (permitting consolidation of related cases filed in the same district court); 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71407 (authorizing consolidation of pretrial proceedings for related cases filed in multiple federal districts); \xc2\xa71404 (making it possible for related cases pending in different federal courts to be transferred and consolidated in one district court); D. Herr, Annotated Manual for Complex Litigation \xc2\xa720.12, p. 279 (4th ed. 2007) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[r]elated cases pending in different federal courts may be consolidated in a single district\xe2\x80\x9d by transfer under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71404(a)); J. Tidmarsh & R. Trangsrud, Complex Litigation and the Adversary System 473\xe2\x80\x93524 (1998) (section on \xe2\x80\x9cTransfer Devices that Aggregate Cases in a Single Venue\xe2\x80\x9d). Because the federal system permits aggregation by other means, we do not think that the payphone operators should be denied standing simply because they chose one aggregation method over another.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners also point to various practical problems that could arise because the aggregators, rather than the payphone operators, are suing. In particular, they say that the payphone operators may not comply with discovery requests served on them, that the payphone operators may not honor judgments reached in this case, and that petitioners may not be able to bring, in this litigation, counterclaims against the payphone operators. See Brief for Petitioners 46\xe2\x80\x9348. Even assuming all that is so, courts have long permitted assignee lawsuits notwithstanding the fact that such problems could arise. Regardless, courts are not helpless in the face of such problems. For example, a district court can, if appropriate, compel a party to collect and to produce whatever discovery-related information is necessary. See Fed. Rules Civ. Proc. 26(b)(1), 30\xe2\x80\x9331, 33\xe2\x80\x9336. That court might grant a motion to join the payphone operators to the case as \xe2\x80\x9crequired\xe2\x80\x9d parties. See Rule 19. Or the court might allow the carriers to file a third-party complaint against the payphone operators. See Rule 14(a). And the carriers could always ask the Federal Communications Commission to find administrative solutions to any remaining practical problems. Cf. 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7276(b)(1)(A) (authorizing the FCC to \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe regulations\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9censure that all payphone service providers are fairly compensated for each and every completed [dial-around] call\xe2\x80\x9d). We do not say that the litigation before us calls for the use of any such procedural device. We mention them only to explain the lack of any obvious need for the remedy that the carriers here propose, namely, denial of standing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, we note that in this litigation, there has been no allegation that the assignments were made in bad faith. We note, as well, that the assignments were made for ordinary business purposes. Were this not so, additional prudential questions might perhaps arise. But these questions are not before us, and we need not consider them here.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority concludes that a private litigant may sue in federal court despite having to \xe2\x80\x9cpass back \xe2\x80\xa6 all proceeds of the litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 9, thus depriving that party of any stake in the outcome of the litigation. The majority reaches this conclusion, in flat contravention of our cases interpreting the case-or-controversy requirement of Article III, by reference to a historical tradition that is, at best, equivocal. That history does not contradict what common sense should tell us: There is a legal difference between something and nothing. Respondents have nothing to gain from their lawsuit. Under settled principles of standing, that fact requires dismissal of their complaint.[Footnote 1]

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Article III of the Constitution confines the judicial power of the federal courts to actual \xe2\x80\x9cCases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cControversies.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72. As we have recently reaffirmed, \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o principle is more fundamental to the judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s proper role in our system of government than the constitutional limitation of federal-court jurisdiction to actual cases or controversies.\xe2\x80\x9d DaimlerChrysler Corp. v. Cuno, 547 U. S. 332, 341 (2006) (quoting Raines v. Byrd, 521 U. S. 811, 818 (1997); internal quotation marks omitted). Unlike the political branches, directly elected by the people, the courts derive their authority under Article III, including the power of judicial review, from \xe2\x80\x9cthe necessity \xe2\x80\xa6 of carrying out the judicial function of deciding cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Cuno, supra, at 340. That is why Article III courts \xe2\x80\x9cmay exercise power only \xe2\x80\xa6 \xe2\x80\x98as a necessity,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that is, only when they are sure they have an actual case before them. Allen v. Wright, 468 U. S. 737, 752 (1984) (quoting Chicago & Grand Trunk R. Co. v. Wellman, 143 U. S. 339, 345 (1892)). \xe2\x80\x9cIf a dispute is not a proper case or controversy, the courts have no business deciding it, or expounding the law in the course of doing so.\xe2\x80\x9d Cuno, supra, at 341.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given the importance of assuring a court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction before deciding the merits of a case, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e have always insisted on strict compliance with th[e] jurisdictional standing requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d Raines, supra, at 819. And until today, it has always been clear that a party lacking a direct, personal stake in the litigation could not invoke the power of the federal courts. See Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U. S. 555, 573 (1992) (plaintiff must demonstrate a \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete private interest in the outcome of [the] suit\xe2\x80\x9d); Lance v. Coffman, 549 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (per curiam) (slip op., at 3) (plaintiff must seek relief that \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly and tangibly benefits him\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Lujan, supra, at 574; emphasis added; internal quotation marks omitted)); Larson v. Valente, 456 U. S. 228, 244, n. 15 (1982) (Article III requires a litigant to show that a favorable decision \xe2\x80\x9cwill relieve a discrete injury to himself\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); Warth v. Seldin, 422 U. S. 490, 499 (1975) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Art. III judicial power exists only to redress or otherwise to protect against injury to the complaining party\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In recent years, we have elaborated the standing requirements of Article III in terms of a three-part test\xe2\x80\x94whether the plaintiff can demonstrate an injury in fact that is fairly traceable to the challenged actions of the defendant and likely to be redressed by a favorable judicial decision. See Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U. S. 83, 102\xe2\x80\x93103 (1998). But regardless of how the test is articulated, \xe2\x80\x9cthe point has always been the same: whether a plaintiff \xe2\x80\x98personally would benefit in a tangible way from the court\xe2\x80\x99s intervention.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 103, n.\xc2\xa05 (quoting Warth, supra, at 508; emphasis added). An assignee who has acquired the bare legal right to prosecute a claim but no right to the substantive recovery cannot show that he has a personal stake in the litigation. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision today is unprecedented. Vermont Agency of Natural Resources v. United States ex rel. Stevens, 529 U. S. 765 (2000), does not support it. Vermont Agency, in recognizing that a qui tam relator as assignee of the United States had standing to sue, did not dispense with the essential requirement of Article III standing that the plaintiff have a \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete private interest in the outcome of [the] suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 772 (quoting Lujan, supra, at 573; internal quotation marks omitted). In Vermont Agency, the qui tam relator\xe2\x80\x99s bounty was sufficient to establish standing because it represented a \xe2\x80\x9cpartial assignment of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s damages claim,\xe2\x80\x9d encompassing both a legal right to assert the claim and a stake in the recovery. 529 U. S., at 773. Thus, it was clear that the False Claims Act gave the \xe2\x80\x9crelator himself an interest in the lawsuit,\xe2\x80\x9d in addition to \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to retain a fee out of the recovery.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 772.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, respondents are authorized to bring suit on behalf of the payphone operators, but they have no claim to the recovery. Indeed, their take is not tied to the recovery in any way. Respondents receive their compensation based on the number of payphones and telephone lines operated by their clients, see App. 198, not based on the measure of damages ultimately awarded by a court or paid by petitioners as part of a settlement. Respondents received the assignments only as a result of their willingness to assume the obligation of remitting any recovery to the assignors, the payphone operators. That is, after all, the entire point of the arrangement. The payphone operators assigned their claims to respondents \xe2\x80\x9cfor purposes of collection,\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 114a; respondents never had any share in the amount collected. The absence of any right to the substantive recovery means that respondents cannot benefit from the judgment they seek and thus lack Article III standing. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen you got nothing, you got nothing to lose.\xe2\x80\x9d Bob Dylan, Like A Rolling Stone, on Highway 61 Revisited (Columbia Records 1965).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, respondents doubtless have more than just a passing interest in the litigation. As collection agencies, respondents must demonstrate that they are willing to make good on their threat to pursue their clients\xe2\x80\x99 claims in litigation. Even so, \xe2\x80\x9can interest that is merely a \xe2\x80\x98byproduct\xe2\x80\x99 of the suit itself cannot give rise to a cognizable injury in fact for Article III standing purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Vermont Agency, supra, at 773. The benefit respondents would receive\xe2\x80\x94the general business goodwill that would result from a successful verdict, the ability to collect dial-around compensation for their clients more effectively\xe2\x80\x94is nothing more than a byproduct of the current litigation. Such an interest cannot support their standing to sue in federal court. Cf. Steel Co., supra, at 107 (the costs of investigating and prosecuting a substantive claim do not give rise to standing to assert the claim); Diamond v. Charles, 476 U. S. 54, 70 (1986) (an interest in recovering attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees does not confer standing to litigate the underlying claim).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The undeniable consequence of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is that a plaintiff need no longer demonstrate a personal stake in the outcome of the litigation. Instead, the majority has replaced the personal stake requirement with a completely impersonal one. The right to sue is now the exact opposite of a personal claim\xe2\x80\x94it is a marketable commodity. By severing the right to recover from the right to prosecute a claim, the Court empowers anyone to bring suit on any claim, whether it be the first assignee, the second, the third, or so on. But, as we have said in another context, standing is not \xe2\x80\x9ccommutative.\xe2\x80\x9d Cuno, 547 U. S., at 352. Legal claims, at least those brought in federal court, are not fungible commodities.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The source of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s mistake is easy to identify. The Court goes awry when it asserts that the standing inquiry focuses on whether the injury is likely to be redressed, not whether the complaining party\xe2\x80\x99s injury is likely to be redressed. See ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. That could not be more wrong. We have never approved federal-court jurisdiction over a claim where the entire relief requested will run to a party not before the court. Never. The Court commits this mistake by treating the elements of standing as separate strands rather than as interlocking and related elements meant to ensure a personal stake. Our cases do not condone this approach.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court expressly rejected such an argument in Vermont Agency, where the relator argued that he was \xe2\x80\x9csuing to remedy an injury in fact suffered by the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 529 U. S., at 771. We dismissed the argument out of hand, noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Art. III judicial power exists only to redress or otherwise to protect against injury to the complaining party.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 771\xe2\x80\x93772 (quoting Warth, 422 U. S., at 499; emphasis in Vermont Agency; internal quotation marks omitted). Although the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis in that section of the opinion concerned the right of the relator to assert the United States\xe2\x80\x99 injury, the Court treated it as axiomatic that any \xe2\x80\x9credress\xe2\x80\x9d must also redound to the benefit of the relator.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Steel Co., the Court similarly rejected a basis for standing that turned on relief sought\xe2\x80\x94the imposition of civil penalties\xe2\x80\x94that was \xe2\x80\x9cpayable to the United States Treasury,\xe2\x80\x9d but not to the plaintiff. 523 U. S., at 106. We observed that the plaintiff sought \xe2\x80\x9cnot remediation of its own injury,\xe2\x80\x9d but merely the \xe2\x80\x9cvindication of the rule of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). Importantly, the Court recognized that \xe2\x80\x9c[r]elief that does not remedy the injury suffered cannot bootstrap a plaintiff into federal court; that is the very essence of the redressability requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 107. Again, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on the party\xe2\x80\x99s injury makes clear that the basis for rejecting standing in Steel Co. was the fact that the remedy sought would not benefit the party before the Court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s view of the Article III redressability requirement is also incompatible with what we said in Raines, 521 U. S. 811. In that case, we held that individual Members of Congress lacked standing to contest the constitutionality of the Line Item Veto Act. We observed that the Congressmen \xe2\x80\x9cdo not claim that they have been deprived of something to which they personally are entitled.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 821. Rather, the Members sought to enforce a right that ran to their office, not to their person. \xe2\x80\x9cIf one of the Members were to retire tomorrow, he would no longer have a claim; the claim would be possessed by his successor instead. The claimed injury thus runs (in a sense) with the Member\xe2\x80\x99s seat, a seat which the Member holds \xe2\x80\xa6 as trustee for his constituents, not as a prerogative of personal power.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We therefore held that the individual Members did \xe2\x80\x9cnot have a sufficient \xe2\x80\x98personal stake\xe2\x80\x99 in th[e] dispute\xe2\x80\x9d to maintain their challenge. Id., at 830. See also Warth, supra, at 506 (denying standing where \xe2\x80\x9cthe record is devoid of any indication\xe2\x80\x9d that the requested \xe2\x80\x9crelief would benefit petitioners\xe2\x80\x9d); Simon v. Eastern Ky. Welfare Rights Organization, 426 U. S. 26, 39, 42 (1976) (denying standing to plaintiffs who did not \xe2\x80\x9cstand to profit in some personal interest\xe2\x80\x9d because it was \xe2\x80\x9cpurely speculative\xe2\x80\x9d whether the relief sought \xe2\x80\x9cwould result in these respondents\xe2\x80\x99 receiving the hospital services they desire\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority finds that respondents have a sufficient stake in this litigation because the substantive recovery will initially go to them, and \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hat does it matter what the aggregators do with the money afterward?\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s assertion implies, incorrectly, that respondents have, or ever had, a choice of what to do with the recovery. It may be true that a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s independent decision to pledge his recovery to another, as in respondents\xe2\x80\x99 hypothetical of an \xe2\x80\x9coriginal owner of a claim who signs a collateral agreement with a charity obligating herself to donate every penny she recovers in [the] litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 21, would not divest the plaintiff of Article III standing. But respondents never had the right to direct the disposition of the recovery; they have only the right to sue. The hypothetical plaintiff who chooses to pledge her recovery to charity, by contrast, will secure a personal benefit from the recovery. Unlike respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims, the hypothetical plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s pre-existing claim is not tied in any way to her separate agreement to direct her recovery to charity. She has more than the right to sue; she has the right to exercise her independent authority to direct the proceeds as she sees fit. In that situation, the Article III requirement that a plaintiff demonstrate a personal stake in the outcome of the litigation is satisfied.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court believes that these standing principles, embodying a \xe2\x80\x9ccore component derived directly from the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d Allen, 468 U. S., at 751, that is of \xe2\x80\x9cparticular importance in ensuring that the Federal Judiciary respects the proper\xe2\x80\x94and properly limited\xe2\x80\x94role of the courts in a democratic society,\xe2\x80\x9d and that is \xe2\x80\x9ccrucial in maintaining the tripartite allocation of power set forth in the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d Cuno, 547 U. S., at 341 (internal quotation marks omitted), should yield \xe2\x80\x9cas a practical matter\xe2\x80\x9d to the prospect that a contrary \xe2\x80\x9cholding could easily be overcome,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 20. The Court chooses to elevate expediency above the strictures imposed by the Constitution. That is a tradeoff the Constitution does not allow. Cf. Raines, supra, at 820 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e must put aside the natural urge to proceed directly to the merits of this important dispute and to \xe2\x80\x98settle\xe2\x80\x99 it for the sake of convenience and efficiency\xe2\x80\x9d). Perhaps it is true that a \xe2\x80\x9cdollar or two,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 20, would give respondents a sufficient stake in the litigation. Article III is worth a dollar. And in any case, the ease with which respondents can comply with the requirements of Article III is not a reason to abandon our precedents; it is a reason to adhere to them.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given all this, it is understandable that the majority opts to minimize its reliance on modern standing principles and to retreat to a broad, generalized reading of the historical tradition of assignments. But that history does not support the majority\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first problem lies in identifying the relevant tradition. Much of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s historical analysis focuses on the generic (and undisputed) point that common law and equity courts eventually permitted assignees to sue on their assigned claims. See ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x9310. I would treat that point as settled as much by stare decisis, see Vermont Agency, 529 U. S., at 773, as by the historic practice of the King\xe2\x80\x99s Bench and Chancery. But the general history of assignments says nothing about the particular aspect of suits brought on assigned claims that is relevant to this case: whether an assignee who has acquired the legal right to sue, but no right to any substantive recovery, can maintain an action in court. On that precise question, the historical sources are either nonexistent or equivocal.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0None of the English common-law sources on which the majority relies establishes that assignments of this sort would be permitted either at law or in equity. As the majority\xe2\x80\x99s discussion makes clear, both systems permitted suits brought on assignments\xe2\x80\x94either in equity by an assignee having a beneficial interest in the litigation, or at law by an assignee who had a power of attorney and sued in the name of the assignor. See ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x938. But at all times, suits based on assignments remained subject to the prohibition on champerty and maintenance. See 7 W. Holdsworth, History of English Law 535\xe2\x80\x93536 (1926).[Footnote 3] By the 18th century, an assignment no longer constituted maintenance per se, see id., at 536, but it appears to have been an open question whether an assignment of the \xe2\x80\x9c[b]are [r]igh[t] to [l]itigate\xe2\x80\x9d would fail as \xe2\x80\x9c[s]avouring\xe2\x80\x9d of champerty and maintenance, see M. Smith, Law of Assignment: The Creation and Transfer of Choses in Action 318, 321 (2007). In order to sustain an assignment of the right to sue, the assignment had to include the transfer of a property interest to which the right of action was incident or subsidiary. Id., at 321\xe2\x80\x93322; see also Prosser v. Edmonds, 1 Y. & C. Exch. 481, 160 Eng. Rep. 196 (1835); Dickinson v. Burrell, 35 Beav. 257, 55 Eng. Rep. 894 (1866); 2 J. Story, Commentaries on Equity Jurisprudence \xc2\xa71040h, pp. 234\xe2\x80\x93235 (8th ed. 1861); R. Megarry & P. Baker, Snell\xe2\x80\x99s Principles of Equity 82 (25th ed. 1960).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0American courts as well understood the common-law rule to require a transfer of interest to the assignee\xe2\x80\x94over and above the \xe2\x80\x9cnaked right to bring a suit\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that gave the assignee a \xe2\x80\x9cvaluable right of property.\xe2\x80\x9d Traer v. Clews, 115 U. S. 528, 541 (1885). A New York court, surveying the English sources, concluded that \xe2\x80\x9can assignment to the plaintiff of the assignor\xe2\x80\x99s right to maintain and prosecute an action for the specific performance of defendants\xe2\x80\x99 agreement, amounts to nothing more than an assertion that the assignor has undertaken to assign to the plaintiff a bare right to litigate for the former\xe2\x80\x99s benefit exclusively.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams v. Boyle, 1 Misc. 364, 367, 20 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0S. 720, 722 (Ct. Common Pleas 1892). To secure standing in a court of equity, the court held, \xe2\x80\x9cit must appear that the assignee\xe2\x80\x99s successful prosecution of the action is susceptible of personal enjoyment by him\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0So while there is no doubt that at common law, courts of law and equity sought ways of protecting the rights of assignees, they did not do so to the exclusion of the age-long objection to maintenance, which could be found when the assignee lacked a sufficient interest in the subject matter of the litigation. During the common-law period at least, it remained an open question whether an assignee for collection, who by agreement took nothing from the suit, had a sufficient interest in the assigned debt to support his right to sue.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, the assignments at issue here purport to give respondents \xe2\x80\x9call rights, title and interest\xe2\x80\x9d in the payphone operators\xe2\x80\x99 claims for dial-around compensation. App. to Pet. for Cert. 114a. But when severed from the right to retain any of the substantive recovery, it is not clear that common-law courts of law or equity would have treated the assigned right to litigate as incidental or subsidiary to the interest represented by the claim itself. Cf. 7 Holdsworth, supra, at 538 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t was not till certain classes of rights \xe2\x80\xa6 became more freely assignable in equity, that it became necessary to distinguish between the cases in which assignment was permitted and cases in which it was not; and it is for this reason that we find very little clear authority on these questions till quite modern times\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I do not take the majority\xe2\x80\x99s point to be that the common-law tradition supplies the answer to this question. As the majority concedes, it was not until the 19th century that \xe2\x80\x9ccourts began to consider the specific question presented here.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. But even granting this starting point, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s recitation of the 19th-century tradition fails to account for the deep divergence in practice regarding the right of assignees with no stake in the substantive recovery to maintain an action in court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority concedes that \xe2\x80\x9csome States during this period of time refused to recognize assignee-for-collection suits,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12, but that refusal was substantially more widespread than the majority acknowledges. See Robbins v. Deverill, 20 Wis. 142 (1865); Bostwick v. Bryant, 113 Ind. 448, 16 N. E. 378 (1888); Moses v. Ingram, 99 Ala. 483, 12 So. 374 (1893); Brown v. Ginn, 66 Ohio St. 316, 64 N. E. 123 (1902); Coombs v. Harford, 99 Me. 426, 59 A. 529 (1904); Martin v. Mask, 158 N. C. 436, 74 S. E. 343 (1912). These courts concluded that assignees having no legal or beneficial interest to vindicate could not sue on the assigned claims.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Several more States, including some enlisted by the majority, only eventually recognized the right of assignees for collection to sue after taking inconsistent positions on the issue. In fact, the rule regarding assignees for collection only was so unsettled that the Kansas Supreme Court reversed itself twice in the span of 19 years. Compare Krapp v. Eldridge, 33 Kan. 106, 5 P. 372 (1885) (assignees for collection only may sue as the real party in interest), with Stewart v. Price, 64 Kan. 191, 67 P. 553 (1902) (assignees for collection only may not sue), with Manley v. Park, 68 Kan. 400, 75 P. 557 (1904) (assignees for collection only may sue again). During this period, many other courts reversed course on the flinty problem posed by assignees for collection only. See Hoagland v. Van Etten, 23 Neb. 462, 36 N.\xc2\xa0W. 755 (1888), overruled by Archer v. Musick, 147 Neb. 1018, 25 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 908 (1947); State ex rel. Freebourn v. Merchant\xe2\x80\x99s Credit Serv., Inc., 104 Mont. 76, 66 P.\xc2\xa02d 337 (1937), overruled by Rae v. Cameron, 112 Mont. 159, 114 P.\xc2\xa02d 1060 (1941).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s survey of 19th-century judicial practice thus ignores a substantial contrary tradition during this period. That tradition makes clear that state courts did not regularly \xe2\x80\x9centertai[n] suits virtually identical to the litigation before us.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. In reality, all that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s cases show is that the question whether assignees for collection could maintain an action in court was hotly contested\xe2\x80\x94a live issue that spawned much litigation and diverse published decisions. The confusion was much remarked on by courts of this period, even those that ultimately sided with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the prevailing practice. See, e.g., Gomer v. Stockdale, 5 Colo. App. 489, 492, 39 P. 355, 356 (1895) (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is much controversy in the various states respecting that almost universal code provision, that a suit must be prosecuted in the name of the real party in interest\xe2\x80\x9d); Compton v. Atwell, 207 F. 2d 139, 140\xe2\x80\x93141 (CADC 1953) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hether an assignee for collection only is the real party in interest \xe2\x80\xa6 has produced a variance of judicial opinion\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9chas so divided other courts\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Commentators have also called attention to the divergent practice. As the majority notes, John Norton Pomeroy observed that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is some conflict\xe2\x80\x9d on the question whether an assignee for collection obligated to \xe2\x80\x9caccount for the whole proceeds \xe2\x80\xa6 is entitled to sue in his own name.\xe2\x80\x9d Remedies and Remedial Rights \xc2\xa7132, p. 159 (1876) (internal quotation marks omitted). See also M. Ferguson, Comment, The Real Party in Interest Rule Revitalized: Recognizing Defendant\xe2\x80\x99s Interest in the Determination of Proper Parties Plaintiff, 55 Cal. L. Rev. 1452, 1475 (1967) (\xe2\x80\x9cNowhere do the courts manifest more confusion than in deciding whether an assignee for collection only is a real party in interest\xe2\x80\x9d); Note, 51 Mich. L. Rev. 587, 588 (1953) (observing that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is, however, little agreement among the courts as to the meaning and purpose of [real party in interest] provisions\xe2\x80\x9d and noting that they have been construed \xe2\x80\x9cto prevent the owner of the bare legal title to a chose in action from suing\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, notable legal commentators of the period argued against permitting suits by assignees for collection. See, e.g., 1 J. Kerr, Law of Pleading and Practice \xc2\xa7586, pp. 791\xe2\x80\x93792 (1919) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he party in whom the legal interest is vested is not always the real party in interest. \xe2\x80\x98The \xe2\x80\x98real party in interest\xe2\x80\x99 is the party who would be benefited or injured by the judgment in the cause\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. The rule should be restricted to parties whose interests are in issue, and are to be affected by the decree\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This unsettled and conflicting state of affairs is understandable given the transformation in the understanding of the common-law prohibition on suits by assignees with no beneficial interest. The immediate cause for this transformation was the merger of law and equity, and the creation of real party in interest provisions intended to reconcile the two forms of actions. Allen v. Brown, 44 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 228, 231 (1870) (noting that New York code provision allowing assignees to sue as the real party in interest \xe2\x80\x9cabolishe[d] the distinction between actions at law and suits in equity\xe2\x80\x9d); see ante, at 10. The fusion of law and equity forced courts to confront the novel question of what to do with assignees for collection only, who could not sue at law in their own name, and who could not recover on a bill in equity for the lack of any beneficial interest to enforce. Were such assignees, under the new system, real parties in interest who could bring suit? It is not surprising that courts took conflicting positions on this question, a question for which the historical tradition did not provide an answer. Given this, it is difficult to characterize a practice as showing what sort of cases and controversies were \xe2\x80\x9ctraditionally amenable to \xe2\x80\xa6 the judicial process,\xe2\x80\x9d Steel Co., 523 U. S., at 102 (emphasis added), when the practice was a self-conscious break in tradition.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Vermont Agency, by contrast, the Court relied on a long and unbroken tradition of informer statutes that reached back to the 14th century and prevailed up to the \xe2\x80\x9cperiod immediately before and after the framing of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d 529 U. S., at 776. The Court noted that the American Colonies \xe2\x80\x9cpass[ed] several informer statutes expressly authorizing qui tam suits,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the First Congress itself \xe2\x80\x9cenacted a considerable number of informer statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This tradition provided relevant evidence of what the Framers in 1787 would have understood the terms \xe2\x80\x9ccase\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversy\xe2\x80\x9d to mean. See Coleman v. Miller, 307 U. S. 433, 460 (1939) (opinion of Frankfurter, J.) (the Article III \xe2\x80\x9c[j]udicial power could come into play only in matters that were the traditional concern of the courts at Westminster and only if they arose in ways that to the expert feel of lawyers constituted \xe2\x80\x98Cases\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98Controversies\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is certainly no comparable tradition here. The belated innovations of the mid- to late-19th-century courts come too late to provide insight into the meaning of Article III. Although we have sometimes looked to cases postdating the founding era as evidence of common-law traditions, we have never done so when the courts self-consciously confronted novel questions arising from a break in the received tradition, or where the practice of later courts was so divergent. A belated and equivocal tradition cannot fill in for the fundamental requirements of Article III where, as here, those requirements are so plainly lacking.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor do our own cases establish that we \xe2\x80\x9clong ago indicated that assignees for collection only can properly bring suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. (If the majority truly believed that, one would expect the cases to be placed front and center in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, rather than as an afterthought.) None addressed the requirements of Article III, and so none constitutes binding precedent. See Steel Co., supra, at 91 (\xe2\x80\x9c[D]rive-by jurisdictional rulings of this sort \xe2\x80\xa6 have no precedential effect\xe2\x80\x9d); Lewis v. Casey, 518 U. S. 343, 352, n. 2 (1996) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e have repeatedly held that the existence of unaddressed jurisdictional defects has no precedential effect\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Waite v. Santa Cruz, 184 U. S. 302 (1902), we addressed the then-existing statutory provision that barred jurisdiction over suits \xe2\x80\x9cimproperly or collusively made or joined \xe2\x80\xa6 for the purpose of creating a case cognizable or removable under this act.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 325. We held that a plaintiff who took legal title of multiple bonds \xe2\x80\x9cfor purposes of collection\xe2\x80\x9d could not satisfy the statute when the bonds individually did not meet the amount in controversy requirement. Ibid. The Court did not say that the \xe2\x80\x9csuit could properly be brought in federal court,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 14, if the only objection was the limitation placed on the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s assignment; instead, the Court remarked that such a limited assignment would not violate the statutory prohibition on suits that are \xe2\x80\x9cimproperly or collusively made or joined,\xe2\x80\x9d Waite, supra, at 325.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Spiller v. Atchison, T. & S. F. R. Co., 253 U. S. 117 (1920), the plaintiff, secretary of the Cattle Raisers\xe2\x80\x99 Association, sued to enforce an order of reparations issued by the Interstate Commerce Commission, which found that the defendant railroads had charged excessive shipping rates to the members of the association. The question before the Court was the validity of the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling that the assignments to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x94which reserved a beneficial interest in the assignors, the individual members of the association\xe2\x80\x94did not vest legal title in the secretary \xe2\x80\x9cso as [to] authorize the commission to make the award of damages in his name.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 134. We concluded that the agency was authorized to issue the reparations order in the name of the plaintiff because the assignments were \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute in form.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We then concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cbeneficial or equitable title\xe2\x80\x9d was not necessary for the plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cto claim an award of reparation\xe2\x80\x9d and enforce that award in his own name in court. Ibid. In other words, the Court addressed merely the question whether it was appropriate for a federal agency (not bound by the constraints of Article III) to enter an award in the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s name. In no way did the Court endorse the right of an assignee for collection to sue as an initial matter in federal court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor did the Court address Article III standing requirements in Titus v. Wallick, 306 U. S. 282 (1939). There, we found that an assignment \xe2\x80\x9cfor purposes of suit,\xe2\x80\x9d where the assignee had an obligation to account for the proceeds (in part) to another, did not render the assignment invalid under New York state law. Id., at 289. Thus, we held that the Ohio courts had failed to give full faith and credit to an earlier, valid New York court judgment. Id., at 292. If we had been presented with the Article III question, we would likely have found it significant that the plaintiff-assignee stood to take the balance of any recovery after the proceeds were used to discharge the debts of the assignor (plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s brother) and the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s wife. Id., at 286. But in any event, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the assignment was valid under New York law, where the restrictions of Article III do not operate, does not support the view that suits by assignees for collection are permissible in federal courts.

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When we have looked to history to confirm our own Article III jurisdiction, we have relied on a firmly entrenched historical tradition that served to confirm the application of modern standing principles. See Vermont Agency, 529 U. S., at 774\xe2\x80\x93778. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision today illustrates the converse approach. It relies on an equivocal and contradictory tradition to override the clear application of the case-or-controversy requirement that would otherwise bar respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suit.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But perhaps we should heed the counsels of hope rather than despair. The majority, after all, purports to comply with our Article III precedents, see ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9318, so those precedents at least live to give meaning to \xe2\x80\x9cthe judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s proper role in our system of government\xe2\x80\x9d another day. Raines, 521 U. S., at 818. What is more, the majority expressly and repeatedly grounds its finding of standing on its conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9chistory and precedent are clear\xe2\x80\x9d that these types of suits \xe2\x80\x9chave long been permitted,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 5, and that there is \xe2\x80\x9ca strong tradition\xe2\x80\x9d of such suits \xe2\x80\x9cduring the past two centuries,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 16, 19. This conclusion is, for the reasons we have set forth, achingly wrong\xe2\x80\x94but at least the articulated test is clear and daunting.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, there is the majority\xe2\x80\x99s point that all this fuss could have been avoided for a dollar, see ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x94a price, by this point, that most readers would probably be happy to contribute. The price will be higher in future standing cases. And when it is\xe2\x80\x94when standing really matters\xe2\x80\x94it would be surprising if the Court were to look to a case in which it did not.
