RICE, WARDEN, et\xc2\xa0al. v. COLLINS

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x9352.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 5, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 18, 2006

\n

After the prosecutor struck a young, African-American woman, Juror 16, from the panel at respondent Collins\xe2\x80\x99 state-court drug trial, Collins objected that the strike was made on account of Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s race. As race-neutral explanations for the strike, the prosecutor said that Juror 16 had rolled her eyes in response to a question from the court; that she was young and might be too tolerant of a drug crime; and that she was single and lacked ties to the community. In rejecting Collins\xe2\x80\x99 challenge, the trial court declared that it did not observe the complained-of demeanor by Juror 16, but noted that she was youthful, as was a white male juror also dismissed by peremptory challenge, and stated it would give the prosecutor \xe2\x80\x9cthe benefit of the doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d The prosecutor had also referred to Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s gender in explaining the strike, but the trial court disallowed any reliance on that ground. The California Court of Appeal upheld the conviction and the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling on the peremptory challenge, finding that the prosecutor permissibly excluded Juror 16 based on her youth. Even if youth was not a legitimate reason to exercise a peremptory challenge, said the court, Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor supported the strike; nothing in the record suggested the trial court failed to conduct a searching inquiry of the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for striking her. The California Supreme Court denied review. The Federal District Court dismissed Collins\xe2\x80\x99 habeas petition with prejudice, but the Ninth Circuit reversed and remanded, concluding that, under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), the State Court of Appeal\xe2\x80\x99s affirmance was based on an unreasonable factual determination in light of the evidence presented at trial.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to use a set of debatable inferences to set aside the state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion does not satisfy AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s requirements for granting habeas relief. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Under Batson v. Kentucky, 476 U. S. 79, 98, a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to a peremptory strike allegedly based on race requires, inter alia, that the trial court determine whether the defendant has carried his burden of proving purposeful discrimination. This involves evaluating \xe2\x80\x9cthe persuasiveness of the [prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s proffered] justification\xe2\x80\x9d for the strike, but \xe2\x80\x9cthe ultimate burden of persuasion regarding racial motivation rests with, and never shifts from, the opponent of the strike.\xe2\x80\x9d Purkett v. Elem, 514 U. S. 765, 768. Because, under AEDPA, a federal habeas court must find the state-court conclusion \xe2\x80\x9can unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented in the State court proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(2), a federal court can only grant Collins\xe2\x80\x99 petition if it was unreasonable to credit the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s race-neutral explanations for the Batson challenge. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Though the Ninth Circuit recited the proper standard of review, it improperly substituted its evaluation of the record for that of the state trial court, which, under \xc2\xa72254(d)(2), did not make an unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented. Noting that the trial court had not witnessed Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s purported eye rolling, the Ninth Circuit concluded that no reasonable factfinder could have accepted the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s rendition of the alleged incident because the prosecutor had completely undermined her own credibility based on three considerations: her erroneous statement that another prospective African-American juror, Juror 19, was \xe2\x80\x9cyoung\xe2\x80\x9d when, in fact, she was a grandmother; the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s improper attempt to use gender as a basis for exclusion; and the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 skepticism toward the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s explanation that she struck Juror 16 in part because of her youth and lack of ties to the community. As to the first reason, because the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s reference to Juror 19\xe2\x80\x99s youth occurred during a discussion of three prospective jurors, two of whom were, indeed, young, it is quite plausible that the prosecutor simply misspoke. It is a tenuous inference to say that an accidental reference with respect to one juror undermines the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s credibility with respect to another. Second, the Ninth Circuit assigned the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s reference to Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s gender more weight than it can bear, given that the prosecutor provided a number of other permissible and plausible race-neutral reasons for excluding her. Collins provides no argument why this matter demonstrates that a reasonable factfinder must conclude the prosecutor lied about the eye rolling and struck Juror 16 based on her race. Finally, even if the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s concerns about Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s youth and lack of community ties were overly cautious, her wariness could be seen as race neutral, for she used a peremptory strike on a white male juror, Juror 6, with the same characteristics. Viewing the foregoing concerns together, the most generous reading would suggest only that the trial court had reason to question the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s credibility regarding Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s alleged improper demeanor. That does not, however, compel the conclusion that the trial court had no permissible alternative but to reject the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s race-neutral justifications and conclude Collins had shown a Batson violation. Reasonable minds reviewing the record might disagree about the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s credibility, but on habeas review that does not suffice to supersede the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility determination. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

365 F.\xc2\xa03d 667, reversed and remanded.

\n

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nRICE V. COLLINS
\n546 U. S. ____ (2006)

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 04-52

\n

BERTRAM RICE, WARDEN, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v.\nSTEVEN MARTELL COLLINS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[January 18, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Concerned that, in this habeas corpus case, a federal court set aside reasonable state-court determinations of fact in favor of its own debatable interpretation of the record, we granted certiorari. Our review confirms that the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit erred, misapplying settled rules that limit its role and authority.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After a 4-day trial in the Superior Court of California for the County of Los Angeles, a jury convicted Steven Martell Collins on one count of possessing cocaine with intent to distribute. The conviction was all the more serious because it subjected him to California\xe2\x80\x99s three strikes rule for sentencing. The question at issue in this federal habeas corpus action, however, is the California courts\xe2\x80\x99 rejection of Collins\xe2\x80\x99 argument that the prosecutor struck a young, African-American woman, Juror 16, from the panel on account of her race. A second African-American juror was also the subject of a peremptory strike, and although Collins challenged that strike in the trial court, on appeal he objected only to the excusal of Juror 16.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even prior to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Batson v. Kentucky, 476 U. S. 79 (1986), California courts barred peremptory challenges to jurors based on race. People v. Wheeler, 22 Cal. 3d 258, 583 P.\xc2\xa02d 748 (1978). Although our recent decision in Johnson v. California, 545 U. S. ___ (2005), disapproved of the manner in which Wheeler and Batson were implemented in some California cases, the state courts in this case used the correct analytical framework in considering and ruling upon the objection to the prosecutorial strike.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As race-neutral explanations for striking Juror 16, the prosecutor said that Juror 16 had rolled her eyes in response to a question from the court; that Juror 16 was young and might be too tolerant of a drug crime; and that Juror 16 was single and lacked ties to the community. A further, more troubling part of the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s unorganized explanation was her reference to Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s gender. The trial court, correctly, disallowed any reliance on that ground. The trial court, furthermore, which had the benefit of observing the prosecutor firsthand over the course of the proceedings, rejected Collins\xe2\x80\x99 challenge.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWith regard to 016, the court, frankly, did not observe the demeanor of Ms. 016 that was complained of by the District Attorney; however, Ms. 016 was a youthful person, as was [a white male juror the prosecutor also dismissed by peremptory challenge]. And one or more prospective jurors also. The Court is prepared to give the District Attorney the benefit of the doubt as to Ms. 016.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 App. 14\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The California Court of Appeal upheld the conviction and the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling on the peremptory challenge. People v. Collins, No. B106939 (Dec. 12, 1997), App. to Pet. for Cert. 112\xe2\x80\x93117. In its view, youth was a legitimate reason to exercise a peremptory challenge; and, even if it were not, Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor also supported the strike. Id., at 116. According to its review of the record, nothing suggested the trial court failed to conduct a searching inquiry of the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for striking Juror 16. Id., at 116\xe2\x80\x93117. The appeals court thus upheld the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate conclusion to credit the prosecutor. Ibid. Without comment, the Supreme Court of California denied Collins\xe2\x80\x99 petition for review. Id., at 96.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Collins sought collateral relief on this claim in federal court. The United States District Court for the Central District of California dismissed with prejudice Collins\xe2\x80\x99 petition for a writ of habeas corpus. Id., at 91. A divided panel of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed and remanded with instructions to grant the petition. 348 F.\xc2\xa03d 1082 (2003), amended and superseded by 365 F.\xc2\xa03d 667 (2004). Noting that the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) governed Collins\xe2\x80\x99 petition, the panel majority concluded that it was an unreasonable factual determination to credit the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s race-neutral reasons for striking Juror 16. Id., at 679. Judge Hall dissented, id., at 687\xe2\x80\x93691; and later, over the dissent of five judges, the Court of Appeals declined to rehear the case en banc, id., at 670\xe2\x80\x93673. Though it recited the proper standard of review, the panel majority improperly substituted its evaluation of the record for that of the state trial court. We granted the petition for certiorari, 545 U. S. ___ (2005), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A defendant\xe2\x80\x99s Batson challenge to a peremptory strike requires a three-step inquiry. First, the trial court must determine whether the defendant has made a prima facie showing that the prosecutor exercised a peremptory challenge on the basis of race. 476 U. S., at 96\xe2\x80\x9397. Second, if the showing is made, the burden shifts to the prosecutor to present a race-neutral explanation for striking the juror in question. Id., at 97\xe2\x80\x9398. Although the prosecutor must present a comprehensible reason, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he second step of this process does not demand an explanation that is persuasive, or even plausible\xe2\x80\x9d; so long as the reason is not inherently discriminatory, it suffices. Purkett v. Elem, 514 U. S. 765, 767\xe2\x80\x93768 (1995) (per curiam). Third, the court must then determine whether the defendant has carried his burden of proving purposeful discrimination. Batson, supra, at 98; Miller-El v. Dretke, 545 U. S. ___, ___ (2005) (slip op., at 18). This final step involves evaluating \xe2\x80\x9cthe persuasiveness of the justification\xe2\x80\x9d proffered by the prosecutor, but \xe2\x80\x9cthe ultimate burden of persuasion regarding racial motivation rests with, and never shifts from, the opponent of the strike.\xe2\x80\x9d Purkett, supra, at 768.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On direct appeal in federal court, the credibility findings a trial court makes in a Batson inquiry are reviewed for clear error. Hernandez v. New York, 500 U. S. 352, 364\xe2\x80\x93366 (1991) (plurality opinion) (holding that evaluation of a prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s credibility \xe2\x80\x9clies \xe2\x80\x98peculiarly within a trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s province\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Under AEDPA, however, a federal habeas court must find the state-court conclusion \xe2\x80\x9can unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented in the State court proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(2). Thus, a federal habeas court can only grant Collins\xe2\x80\x99 petition if it was unreasonable to credit the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s race-neutral explanations for the Batson challenge. State-court factual findings, moreover, are presumed correct; the petitioner has the burden of rebutting the presumption by \xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72254(e)(1). See Miller-El, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 6). Although the Ninth Circuit assumed \xc2\xa72254(e)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s presumption applied in this case, 365 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 677, the parties disagree about whether and when it does. We need not address that question. Even assuming, arguendo, that only \xc2\xa72254(d)(2) applied in this proceeding, the state-court decision was not an unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented in the state court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the California Court of Appeal accepted the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility finding, the panel majority inquired whether the appellate court made an unreasonable factual determination. See id., at 682. The panel majority\xe2\x80\x99s analysis and conclusions, however, depended entirely on its view of the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility holding. The panel majority found no error in the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s proceedings or rulings in the first two steps of the Batson inquiry. 365 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 677\xe2\x80\x93678. It disagreed, however, with the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusions on the third step, holding that it was unreasonable to accept the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s explanation that Juror 16 was excused on account of her youth and her demeanor. Id., at 678\xe2\x80\x93687. We conclude the Ninth Circuit erred, for the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility determination was not unreasonable.

\n

\n\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Noting that the trial court had not witnessed Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s purported eye rolling, the panel majority concluded that no reasonable factfinder could have accepted the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s rendition of the alleged incident because the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s conduct completely undermined her credibility. Id., at 683. Having before it only the trial court record, the Court of Appeals majority drew this conclusion based on three considerations: first, the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous statement concerning another prospective African-American juror\xe2\x80\x99s age; second, the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s improper attempt to use gender as a basis for exclusion; and third, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s skepticism toward the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s explanation that she struck Juror 16 in part because of her youth and lack of ties to the community. Id., at 683\xe2\x80\x93684.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first reason the panel majority noted for rejecting the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility finding pertained not to Juror 16, the subject of Collins\xe2\x80\x99 claim on appeal, but to another prospective African-American juror, Juror 19. The prosecutor referred to Juror 19 as \xe2\x80\x9cyoung\xe2\x80\x9d even though she was a grandmother. This reference to youth took place during a discussion about three prospective jurors, Jurors 6, 16, and 19. Jurors 6 and 16 were both young. As Judge Hall observed, it is quite plausible that the prosecutor simply misspoke with respect to a juror\xe2\x80\x99s numerical designation, an error defense counsel may also have committed. Id., at 688; 2 App. 9. It is a tenuous inference to say that an accidental reference with respect to one juror, Juror 19, undermines the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s credibility with respect to Juror 16. Seizing on what can plausibly be viewed as an innocent transposition makes little headway toward the conclusion that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s explanation was clearly not credible.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the panel majority concluded that the trial court should have questioned the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s credibility because of her \xe2\x80\x9cattempt to use gender as a race-neutral basis for excluding Jurors 016 and 019.\xe2\x80\x9d 365 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 684. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s trial occurred in August 1996, over two years after our decision in J.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa0B. v. Alabama ex rel. T.\xc2\xa0B., 511 U. S. 127 (1994), made clear that discrimination in jury selection on the basis of gender violates the Equal Protection Clause. Although the record contains a somewhat confusing colloquy on this point, it can be read as indicating that one of the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s aims in striking Juror 16 was achieving gender balance on the jury. Concerned about the constitutionality of such a strike, the trial court made clear that it would not accept gender as a race-neutral explanation. The panel majority assigned the gender justification more weight than it can bear. The prosecutor provided a number of other permissible and plausible race-neutral reasons, and Collins provides no argument why this portion of the colloquy demonstrates that a reasonable factfinder must conclude the prosecutor lied about the eye rolling and struck Juror 16 based on her race.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the panel majority believed to be unsupportable the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s stated concern that Juror 16 might, as a young and single citizen with no ties to the community, be too tolerant of the crime with which respondent was charged. 365 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 680\xe2\x80\x93682, 684. This was so, the majority concluded, because during voir dire Juror 16 replied affirmatively when asked if she believed the crime with which respondent was charged should be illegal and disclaimed any other reason she could not be impartial. Id., at 680. That the prosecutor claimed to hold such concerns despite Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s voir dire averments does not establish that she offered a pretext. It is not unreasonable to believe the prosecutor remained worried that a young person with few ties to the community might be less willing than an older, more permanent resident to impose a lengthy sentence for possessing a small amount of a controlled substance. Accord, id., at 690 (Hall, J., dissenting). Even if the prosecutor was overly cautious in this regard, her wariness of the young and the rootless could be seen as race neutral, for she used a peremptory strike on a white male juror, Juror 6, with the same characteristics. 2\xc2\xa0App. 5, 14.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Viewing the panel majority\xe2\x80\x99s concerns together, the most generous reading would suggest only that the trial court had reason to question the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s credibility regarding Juror 16\xe2\x80\x99s alleged improper demeanor. That does not, however, compel the conclusion that the trial court had no permissible alternative but to reject the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s race-neutral justifications and conclude Collins had shown a Batson violation. Reasonable minds reviewing the record might disagree about the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s credibility, but on habeas review that does not suffice to supersede the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility determination.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The panel majority did not stop at the conclusion that the trial court rendered an unreasonable factual determination in light of the evidence presented. It further concluded that the state courts had unreasonably applied clearly established federal law as determined by this Court. 365 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 679; 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). The question whether a state court errs in determining the facts is a different question from whether it errs in applying the law. In this case there is no demonstration that either the trial court or the California Court of Appeal acted contrary to clearly established federal law in recognizing and applying Batson\xe2\x80\x99s burden-framework. See 2\xc2\xa0App. 14\xe2\x80\x9315; App. to Pet. for Cert. 114\xe2\x80\x93116. The only question, as we have noted, is whether the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s factual determination at Batson\xe2\x80\x99s third step was unreasonable. For the reasons discussed above, we conclude it was not.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The panel majority\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to use a set of debatable inferences to set aside the conclusion reached by the state court does not satisfy AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s requirements for granting a writ of habeas corpus. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Souter joins, concurring.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Twenty years ago Justice Thurgood Marshall warned that the test of Batson v. Kentucky, 476 U. S. 79 (1986), would fail to ferret out unconstitutional discrimination in the selection of jurors. Id., at 102\xe2\x80\x93103 (concurring opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe decision today will not end the racial discrimination that peremptories inject into the jury-selection process\xe2\x80\x9d). In my view, history has proved Justice Marshall right. See Miller-El v. Dretke, 545 U. S. ___, ___ (2005) (slip op., at 1) (Breyer, J., concurring). And today\xe2\x80\x99s case, like Miller-El, helps to illustrate Batson\xe2\x80\x99s fundamental failings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For one thing, the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s inability in this case to provide a clear explanation of why she exercised her peremptory challenges may well reflect the more general fact that the exercise of a peremptory challenge can rest upon instinct not reason. Insofar as Batson asks prosecutors to explain the unexplainable, how can it succeed? Miller-El, 545 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x933) (Breyer, J., concurring).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For another thing, the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s uncertainty about the legal validity of the exercise of peremptory challenges in this case may reflect the more general fact that, sometimes, no one, not even the lawyer herself, can be certain whether a decision to exercise a peremptory challenge rests upon an impermissible racial, religious, gender-based, or ethnic stereotype. Ibid. See also Batson, supra, at 106 (Marshall, J., concurring) (noting unconscious internalization of racial stereotypes). How can trial judges second-guess an instinctive judgment the underlying basis for which may be a form of stereotyping invisible even to the prosecutor? Miller-El, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 2) (Breyer, J., concurring).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the case before us makes clear that ordinary mechanisms of judicial review cannot assure Batson\xe2\x80\x99s effectiveness. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The reasons are structural. The trial judge is best placed to consider the factors that underlie credibility: demeanor, context, and atmosphere. And the trial judge is best placed to determine whether, in a borderline case, a prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s hesitation or contradiction reflect (a) deception, or (b) the difficulty of providing a rational reason for an instinctive decision. Appellate judges cannot on the basis of a cold record easily second-guess a trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision about likely motivation. These circumstances mean that appellate courts will, and must, grant the trial courts considerable leeway in applying Batson. See Hernandez v. New York, 500 U. S. 352 (1991). As the present case illustrates, considerations of federalism require federal habeas courts to show yet further deference to state-court judgments. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(2) (state-court factual determination must stand unless \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The upshot is an unresolvable tension between, on the one hand, what Blackstone called an inherently \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98arbitrary and capricious\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d peremptory challenge system, Miller-El, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 7) (Breyer, J., concurring) (quoting 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 346 (1769)), and, on the other hand, the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s nondiscrimination command. Given this constitutional tension, we may have to choose. Miller-El, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 8) (Breyer, J., concurring); Swain v. Alabama, 380 U. S. 202, 244 (1965) (Goldberg, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cWere it necessary to make an absolute choice between the right of a defendant to have a jury chosen in conformity with the requirements of the Fourteenth Amendment and the right to challenge peremptorily, the Constitution compels a choice of the former\xe2\x80\x9d); Batson, supra, at 107 (Marshall, J., concurring) (same).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I have argued that legal life without peremptories is no longer unthinkable. Miller-El, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 6\xe2\x80\x937) (concurring opinion) (citing, inter alia, the experience of England). I continue to believe that we should reconsider Batson\xe2\x80\x99s test and the peremptory challenge system as a whole. Nonetheless, because the Court correctly applies the present legal framework, I concur in its opinion.
