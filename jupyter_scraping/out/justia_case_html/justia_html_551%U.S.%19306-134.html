PERMANENT MISSION OF INDIA TO THE UNITED NATIONS et\xc2\xa0al. v. CITY OF NEW YORK

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x93134.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 24, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 14, 2007

\n

Under New York law, real property owned by a foreign government is exempt from taxation when used exclusively for diplomatic offices or quarters for ambassadors or ministers plenipotentiary to the United Nations. For years, respondent (City) has levied property taxes against petitioner foreign governments for that portion of their diplomatic office buildings used to house lower level employees and their families. Petitioners have refused to pay the taxes. By operation of state law, the unpaid taxes converted into tax liens held by the City against the properties. The City filed a state-court suit seeking declaratory judgments to establish the liens\xe2\x80\x99 validity, but petitioners removed the cases to federal court, where they argued that they were immune under the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 (FSIA), which is \xe2\x80\x9cthe sole basis for obtaining jurisdiction over a foreign state in federal court,\xe2\x80\x9d Argentine Republic v. Amerada Hess Shipping Corp., 488 U. S. 428, 439. The District Court disagreed, relying on an FSIA exception withdrawing a foreign state\xe2\x80\x99s immunity from jurisdiction where \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property situated in the United States are in issue.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71605(a)(4). The Second Circuit affirmed, holding that the \xe2\x80\x9cimmovable property\xe2\x80\x9d exception applied, and thus the District Court had jurisdiction over the City\xe2\x80\x99s suits.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The FSIA does not immunize a foreign government from a lawsuit to declare the validity of tax liens on property held by the sovereign for the purpose of housing its employees. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Under the FSIA, a foreign state is presumptively immune from suit unless a specific exception applies. In determining the immovable property exception\xe2\x80\x99s scope, the Court begins, as always, with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text. Contrary to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position, \xc2\xa71605(a)(4) does not expressly limit itself to cases in which the specific right at issue is title, ownership, or possession, or specifically exclude cases in which a lien\xe2\x80\x99s validity is at issue. Rather, it focuses more broadly on \xe2\x80\x9crights in\xe2\x80\x9d property. At the time of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s adoption, \xe2\x80\x9clien\xe2\x80\x9d was defined as a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge or security or incumbrance upon property,\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1072, and \xe2\x80\x9cincumbrance\xe2\x80\x9d was defined as \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny right to, or interest in, land which may subsist in another to the diminution of its value,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 908. New York law defines \xe2\x80\x9ctax lien\xe2\x80\x9d in accordance with these general definitions. A lien\xe2\x80\x99s practical effects bear out the definitions of liens as interests in property. Because a lien on real property runs with the land and is enforceable against subsequent purchasers, a tax lien inhibits a quintessential property ownership right\xe2\x80\x94the right to convey. It is thus plain that a suit to establish a tax lien\xe2\x80\x99s validity implicates \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading is supported by two of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s related purposes. First, Congress intended the FSIA to adopt the restrictive theory of sovereign immunity, which recognizes immunity \xe2\x80\x9cwith regard to sovereign or public acts (jure imperii) of a state, but not \xe2\x80\xa6 private acts (jure gestionis).\xe2\x80\x9d Alfred Dunhill of London, Inc. v. Republic of Cuba, 425 U. S. 682, 711. Property ownership is not an inherently sovereign function. The FSIA was also meant to codify the real property exception recognized by international practice at the time of its enactment. That practice supports the City\xe2\x80\x99s view that petitioners are not immune, as does the contemporaneous restatement of foreign relations law. The Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations, on which both parties rely, does not unambiguously support either party, and, in any event, does nothing to deter this Court from its interpretation. Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

446 F.\xc2\xa03d 365, affirmed and remanded.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nPERMANENT MISSION OF INDIA TO UNITED NATIONS V.CITY OF NEW YORK

THE PERMANENT MISSION OF INDIA TO THE\nUNITED NATIONS, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v.\nCITY OF NEW YORK, NEW YORK

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the second circuit

\n

[June 14, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 (FSIA), 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71602 et seq., governs federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 jurisdiction in lawsuits against foreign sovereigns. Today, we must decide whether the FSIA provides immunity to a foreign sovereign from a lawsuit to declare the validity of tax liens on property held by the sovereign for the purpose of housing its employees. We hold that the FSIA does not immunize a foreign sovereign from such a suit.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Permanent Mission of India to the United Nations is located in a 26-floor building in New York City that is owned by the Government of India. Several floors are used for diplomatic offices, but approximately 20 floors contain residential units for diplomatic employees of the mission and their families. The employees\xe2\x80\x94all of whom are below the rank of Head of Mission or Ambassador\xe2\x80\x94are Indian citizens who receive housing from the mission rent free.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, the Ministry for Foreign Affairs of the People\xe2\x80\x99s Republic of Mongolia is housed in a six-story building in New York City that is owned by the Mongolian Government. Like the Permanent Mission of India, certain floors of the Ministry Building include residences for lower level employees of the Ministry and their families.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under New York law, real property owned by a foreign government is exempt from taxation if it is \xe2\x80\x9cused exclusively\xe2\x80\x9d for diplomatic offices or for the quarters of a diplomat \xe2\x80\x9cwith the rank of ambassador or minister plenipotentiary\xe2\x80\x9d to the United Nations. N.\xc2\xa0Y. Real Prop. Tax Law Ann. \xc2\xa7418 (West 2000). But \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f a portion only of any lot or building \xe2\x80\xa6 is used exclusively for the purposes herein described, then such portion only shall be exempt and the remainder shall be subject to taxation\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For several years, the City of New York (City) has levied property taxes against petitioners for the portions of their buildings used to house lower level employees. Petitioners, however, refused to pay the taxes. By operation of New York law, the unpaid taxes eventually converted into tax liens held by the City against the two properties. As of February 1, 2003, the Indian Mission owed about $16.4 million in unpaid property taxes and interest, and the Mongolian Ministry owed about $2.1 million.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On April 2, 2003, the City filed complaints in state court seeking declaratory judgments to establish the validity of the tax liens.[Footnote 1] Petitioners removed their cases to federal court, pursuant to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71441(d), which provides for removal by a foreign state or its instrumentality. Once there, petitioners argued that they were immune from the suits under the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s general rule of immunity for foreign governments. \xc2\xa71604. The District Court disagreed, relying on the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cimmovable property\xe2\x80\x9d exception, which provides that a foreign state shall not be immune from jurisdiction in any case in which \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property situated in the United States are in issue.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71605(a)(4).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reviewing the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision under the collateral order doctrine, a unanimous panel of the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit affirmed. 446 F.\xc2\xa03d 365 (2006). The Court of Appeals held that the text and purpose of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s immovable property exception confirmed that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 personal property tax obligations involved \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property.\xe2\x80\x9d It therefore held that the District Court had jurisdiction to consider the City\xe2\x80\x99s suits. We granted certiorari, 549 U. S. ___ (2007), and now affirm.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he FSIA provides the sole basis for obtaining jurisdiction over a foreign state in federal court.\xe2\x80\x9d Argentine Republic v. Amerada Hess Shipping Corp., 488 U. S. 428, 439 (1989). Under the FSIA, a foreign state is presumptively immune from suit unless a specific exception applies. \xc2\xa71604; Saudi Arabia v. Nelson, 507 U. S. 349, 355 (1993). At issue here is the scope of the exception where \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property situated in the United States are in issue.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71605(a)(4). Petitioners contend that the language \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property\xe2\x80\x9d limits the reach of the exception to actions contesting ownership or possession. The City argues that the exception encompasses additional rights in immovable property, including tax liens. Each party claims international practice at the time of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s adoption supports its view. We agree with the City.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin, as always, with the text of the statute. Limtiaco v. Camacho, 549 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 5). The FSIA provides: \xe2\x80\x9cA foreign state shall not be immune from the jurisdiction of courts of the United States \xe2\x80\xa6 in any case \xe2\x80\xa6 in which \xe2\x80\xa6 rights in immovable property situated in the United States are in issue.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71605(a)(4). Contrary to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position, \xc2\xa71605(a)(4) does not expressly limit itself to cases in which the specific right at issue is title, ownership, or possession. Neither does it specifically exclude cases in which the validity of a lien is at issue. Rather, the exception focuses more broadly on \xe2\x80\x9crights in\xe2\x80\x9d property. Accordingly, we must determine whether an action seeking a declaration of the validity of a tax lien places \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property \xe2\x80\xa6 in issue.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the time of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s adoption in 1976, a \xe2\x80\x9clien\xe2\x80\x9d was defined as \xe2\x80\x9c[a] charge or security or incumbrance upon property.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1072 (4th ed. 1951). \xe2\x80\x9cIncumbrance,\xe2\x80\x9d in turn, was defined as \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny right to, or interest in, land which may subsist in another to the diminution of its value . . . .\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 908; see also id., at 941 (8th ed. 2004) (defining \xe2\x80\x9clien\xe2\x80\x9d as a \xe2\x80\x9clegal right or interest that a creditor has in another\xe2\x80\x99s property\xe2\x80\x9d). New York law defines \xe2\x80\x9ctax lien\xe2\x80\x9d in accordance with these general definitions. See N.\xc2\xa0Y. Real Prop. Tax Law Ann. \xc2\xa7102(21) (West Supp. 2007) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Tax lien\xe2\x80\x99 means an unpaid tax \xe2\x80\xa6 which is an encumbrance of real property\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). This Court, interpreting the Bankruptcy Code, has also recognized that a lienholder has a property interest, albeit a \xe2\x80\x9cnonpossessory\xe2\x80\x9d interest. United States v. Security Industrial Bank, 459 U. S. 70, 76 (1982).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The practical effects of a lien bear out these definitions of liens as interests in property. A lien on real property runs with the land and is enforceable against subsequent purchasers. See 5 Restatement of Property \xc2\xa7540 (1944). As such, \xe2\x80\x9ca lien has an immediate adverse effect upon the amount which [could be] receive[d] on a sale,\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6 constitut[ing] a direct interference with the property\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Republic of Argentina v. New York, 25 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 2d 252, 262, 250 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 698, 702 (1969). A tax lien thus inhibits one of the quintessential rights of property ownership\xe2\x80\x94the right to convey. It is therefore plain that a suit to establish the validity of a lien implicates \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our reading of the text is supported by two well-recognized and related purposes of the FSIA: adoption of the restrictive view of sovereign immunity and codification of international law at the time of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment. Until the middle of the last century, the United States followed \xe2\x80\x9cthe classical or virtually absolute theory of sovereign immunity,\xe2\x80\x9d under which \xe2\x80\x9ca sovereign cannot, without his consent, be made a respondent in the courts of another sovereign.\xe2\x80\x9d Letter from Jack B. Tate, Acting Legal Adviser, U. S. Dept. of State, to Acting U. S. Attorney General Phillip B. Perlman (May 19, 1952) (Tate Letter), reprinted in 26 Dept. of State Bull. 984 (1952), and in Alfred Dunhill of London, Inc. v. Republic of Cuba, 425 U. S. 682, 711, 712 (1976) (App. 2 to opinion of the Court). The Tate Letter announced the United States\xe2\x80\x99 decision to join the majority of other countries by adopting the \xe2\x80\x9crestrictive theory\xe2\x80\x9d of sovereign immunity, under which \xe2\x80\x9cthe immunity of the sovereign is recognized with regard to sovereign or public acts (jure imperii) of a state, but not with respect to private acts (jure gestionis).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 711. In enacting the FSIA, Congress intended to codify the restrictive theory\xe2\x80\x99s limitation of immunity to sover-\neign acts. Republic of Argentina v. Weltover, Inc., 504 U. S. 607, 612 (1992); Asociacion de Reclamantes v.\nUnited Mexican States, 735 F.\xc2\xa02d 1517, 1520 (CADC 1984) (Scalia, J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a threshold matter, property ownership is not an inherently sovereign function. See Schooner Exchange v. McFaddon, 7 Cranch 116, 145 (1812) (\xe2\x80\x9cA prince, by acquiring private property in a foreign country, may possibly be considered as subjecting that property to the territorial jurisdiction, he may be considered as so far laying down the prince, and assuming the character of a private individual\xe2\x80\x9d). In addition, the FSIA was also meant \xe2\x80\x9cto codify \xe2\x80\xa6 the pre-existing real property exception to sovereign immunity recognized by international practice.\xe2\x80\x9d Reclamantes, supra, at 1521 (Scalia, J.). Therefore, it is useful to note that international practice at the time of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment also supports the City\xe2\x80\x99s view that these sovereigns are not immune. The most recent restatement of foreign relations law at the time of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment states that a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s immunity does not extend to \xe2\x80\x9can action to obtain possession of or establish a property interest in immovable property located in the territory of the state exercising jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Second) of Foreign Relations Law of the United States \xc2\xa768(b), p.\xc2\xa0205 (1965). As stated above, because an action seeking the declaration of the validity of a tax lien on property is a suit to establish an interest in such property, such an action would be allowed under this rule.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners respond to this conclusion by citing the second sentence of Comment d to \xc2\xa768, which states that the rule \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not preclude immunity with respect to a claim arising out of a foreign state\xe2\x80\x99s ownership or possession of immovable property but not contesting such ownership or the right to possession.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 207. According to petitioners, that sentence limits the exception to cases contesting ownership or possession. When read in context, however, the comment supports the City. Petitioners ignore the first sentence of the comment, which reemphasizes that immunity does not extend to cases involving the possession of or \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in\xe2\x80\x9d the property. Ibid. And the illustrations following the comment make clear that it refers only to claims incidental to property ownership, such as actions involving an \xe2\x80\x9cinjury suffered in a fall\xe2\x80\x9d on the property, for which immunity would apply. Id., at 208. By contrast, for an eminent-domain proceeding, the foreign sovereign could not claim immunity. Ibid. Like the eminent-domain proceeding, the City\xe2\x80\x99s lawsuits here directly implicate rights in property.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, both parties rely on various international agreements, primarily the Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations, Apr. 18, 1961, [1972] 23 U. S.\xc2\xa0T. 3227, T.\xc2\xa0I.\xc2\xa0A.\xc2\xa0S. No. 7502, to identify pre-FSIA international practice. Petitioners point to the Vienna Convention\xe2\x80\x99s analogous withholding of immunity for \xe2\x80\x9ca real action relating to private immovable property situated in the territory of the receiving State, unless [the diplomatic agent] holds it on behalf of the sending State for the purposes of the mission.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3240, Art. 31(1)(a). Petitioners contend that this language indicates they are entitled to immunity for two reasons. First, petitioners argue that \xe2\x80\x9creal action[s]\xe2\x80\x9d do not include actions for performance of obligations \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98deriving from ownership or possession of immovable property.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 28 (quoting E. Denza, Diplomatic Law: A Commentary on the Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations 238 (2d ed. 1998); emphasis deleted). Second, petitioners assert that the property here is held \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98on behalf of the sending State for purposes of the Mission.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 28.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But as the City shows, it is far from apparent that the term \xe2\x80\x9creal action\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a term derived from the civil law\xe2\x80\x94is as limited as petitioners suggest. See Chateau Lafayette Apartments, Inc. v. Meadow Brook Nat. Bank, 416 F.\xc2\xa02d 301, 304, n.\xc2\xa07 (CA5 1969). Moreover, the exception for property held \xe2\x80\x9con behalf of the sending State\xe2\x80\x9d concerns only the case\xe2\x80\x94not at issue here\xe2\x80\x94where local law requires an agent to hold in his own name property used for the purposes of a mission. 1957 Y.\xc2\xa0B. Int\xe2\x80\x99l L. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n 94\xe2\x80\x9395 (402d Meeting, May 22, 1957); see also Deputy Registrar Case, 94 I.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R. 308, 313 (D.\xc2\xa0Ct. The Hague 1980). Other tribunals construing Article 31 have also held that it does not extend immunity to staff housing. See id., at 312; cf. Intpro Properties (U.\xc2\xa0K.) Ltd. v. Sauvel, [1983] 1 Q.\xc2\xa0B. 1019, 1032\xe2\x80\x931033.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the Vienna Convention does not unambiguously support either party on the jurisdictional question.[Footnote 2] In any event, nothing in the Vienna Convention deters us from our interpretation of the FSIA. Under the language of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s exception for immovable property, petitioners are not immune from the City\xe2\x80\x99s suits.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the statutory text and the acknowledged purposes of the FSIA make it clear that a suit to establish the validity of a tax lien places \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property \xe2\x80\xa6 in issue,\xe2\x80\x9d we affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\nFootnote 1

\xc2\xa0The City concedes that even if a court of competent jurisdiction declares the liens valid, petitioners are immune from foreclosure proceedings. See Brief for Respondent 40 (noting that there is no FSIA immunity exception for enforcement actions). The City claims, however, that the declarations of validity are necessary for three reasons. First, once a court has declared property tax liens valid, foreign sovereigns traditionally concede and pay. Second, if the foreign sovereign fails to pay in the face of a valid court judgment, that country\xe2\x80\x99s foreign aid may be reduced by the United States by 110% of the outstanding debt. See Foreign Operations, Export Financing, and Related Programs Appropriations Act, 2006, \xc2\xa7543(a), 119 Stat. 2214 (hereinafter Foreign Operations); Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2005, \xc2\xa7543(a), 118 Stat. 3011 (hereinafter Consolidated Appropriations). Third, the liens would be enforceable against subsequent purchasers. 5 Restatement of Property \xc2\xa7540 (1944).

\nFootnote 2

\xc2\xa0The City offers several other arguments against immunity based on the Vienna Convention, but those arguments ultimately go to the merits of the case, i.e., whether petitioners are actually responsible for paying the taxes. Because the only question before us is one of jurisdiction, and because the text and historical context of the FSIA demonstrate that petitioners are not immune from the City\xe2\x80\x99s suits, we leave these merits-related arguments to the lower courts.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Breyer joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Diplomatic channels provide the normal method of resolving disputes between local governmental entities and foreign sovereigns. See Schooner Exchange v. McFaddon, 7 Cranch 116, 146 (1812). Following well-established international practice, American courts throughout our history have consistently endorsed the general rule that foreign sovereigns enjoy immunity from suit in our courts. See Verlinden B. V. v. Central Bank of Nigeria, 461 U. S. 480, 486 (1983); Nevada v. Hall, 440 U. S. 410, 417 (1979). The fact that the immunity is the product of comity concerns rather than a want of juridical power, see Verlinden B. V., 461 U. S., at 486, does not detract from the important role that it performs in ordering our affairs.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 (FSIA) both codified and modified that basic rule. The statute confirms that sovereigns are generally immune from suit in our courts, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71604, but identifies seven specific exceptions through which courts may accept jurisdiction, \xc2\xa71605(a). None of those exceptions pertains, or indeed makes any reference, to actions brought to establish a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s tax liabilities. Because this is such an action, I think it is barred by the general rule codified in the FSIA.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true that the FSIA contains an exception for suits to resolve disputes over \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71605(a)(4), and New York City law provides that unpaid real estate taxes create a lien that constitutes an interest in such property, N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0C. Admin. Code \xc2\xa711\xe2\x80\x93301 (Cum. Supp. 2006). It follows that a literal application of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s text provides a basis for applying the exception to this case. See ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. Given the breadth and vintage of the background general rule, however, it seems to me highly unlikely that the drafters of the FSIA intended to abrogate sovereign immunity in suits over property interests whose primary function is to provide a remedy against delinquent taxpayers.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s logic, since \xe2\x80\x9ca suit to establish the validity of a lien implicates \xe2\x80\x98rights in immovable property,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 5, whenever state or municipal law recognizes a lien against a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s real property, the foreign government may be haled into federal court to litigate the validity of that lien. Such a broad exception to sovereign immunity threatens, as they say, to swallow the rule. Under the municipal law of New York City, for example, liens are available against real property, among other things, to compel landowners to pay for pest control, emergency repairs, and sidewalk upkeep. See N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0C. Admin. Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa717\xe2\x80\x93145, 17\xe2\x80\x93147, 17\xe2\x80\x93151(b) (2000); see also M. Mitzner, Liens and Encumbrances, in Real Estate Titles 299, 311\xe2\x80\x93314 (J. Pedowitz ed. 1984). A whole host of routine civil controversies, from sidewalk slip-and-falls to landlord-tenant disputes, could be converted into property liens under local law, and then used\xe2\x80\x94as the tax lien was in this case\xe2\x80\x94to pierce a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s traditional and statutory immunity. In order to reclaim immunity, foreign governments might argue in those cases\xe2\x80\x94just as the Governments of India and the People\xe2\x80\x99s Republic of Mongolia tried to argue here\xe2\x80\x94that slip-and-fall claims, even once they are transformed into property liens, do not implicate \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property.\xe2\x80\x9d But the burden of answering such complaints and making such arguments is itself an imposition that foreign sovereigns should not have to bear.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The force of the arguments of the Solicitor General as amicus curiae supporting petitioners buttresses my conviction that a narrow reading of the statutory exception is more faithful to congressional intent than a reading that enables a dispute over taxes to be classified as a dispute over \xe2\x80\x9crights in immovable property.\xe2\x80\x9d It is true that insofar as the FSIA transferred the responsibility for making immunity decisions from the State Department to the Judiciary, Verlinden B. V., 461 U. S., at 487\xe2\x80\x93488, the views of the Executive are not entitled to any special deference on this issue. But we have recognized that well-reasoned opinions of the Executive Branch about matters within its expertise may have the \xe2\x80\x9cpower to persuade, if lacking power to control.\xe2\x80\x9d Skidmore v. Swift & Co., 323 U. S. 134, 140 (1944).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And I am persuaded. At bottom, this case is not about the validity of the city\xe2\x80\x99s title to immovable property, or even the validity of its automatic prejudgment lien. Rather, it is a dispute over a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s tax liability. If Congress had intended the statute to waive sovereign immunity in tax litigation, I think it would have said so.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, I respectfully dissent.
