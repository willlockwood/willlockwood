\nSYLLABUS
\nOCTOBER TERM, 2005
\nVOLVO TRUCKS NORTH AMERICA, INC. V. REEDER-SIMCO GMC, INC.
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

VOLVO TRUCKS NORTH AMERICA, INC. v. REEDER-SIMCO GMC, INC.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x93905.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 31, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 10, 2006

\n

Reeder-Simco GMC, Inc. (Reeder), an authorized dealer of heavy-duty trucks manufactured by Volvo Trucks North America, Inc. (Volvo), generally sold those trucks through an industry-wide competitive bidding process, whereby the retail customer describes its specific product requirements and invites bids from dealers it selects based on such factors as an existing relationship, geography, and reputation. Once a Volvo dealer receives the customer\xe2\x80\x99s specifications, it requests from Volvo a discount or \xe2\x80\x9cconcession\xe2\x80\x9d off the wholesale price. Volvo decides on a case-by-case basis whether to offer a concession. The dealer then uses its Volvo discount in preparing its bid; it purchases trucks from Volvo only if and when the retail customer accepts its bid. Reeder was one of many regional Volvo dealers. Although nothing prohibits a Volvo dealer from bidding outside its territory, Reeder rarely bid against another Volvo dealer. In the atypical case in which a retail customer solicited a bid from more than one Volvo dealer, Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s stated policy was to provide the same price concession to each dealer. In 1997, after Volvo announced plans to enlarge the size of its dealers\xe2\x80\x99 markets and to reduce by almost half the number of its dealers, Reeder learned that Volvo had given another dealer a price concession greater than the discounts Reeder typically received.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reeder, suspecting it was one of the dealers Volvo sought to eliminate, filed this suit under, inter alia, \xc2\xa72 of the Clayton Act, as amended by the Robinson-Patman Act, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa713, alleging that its sales and profits declined because Volvo offered other dealers more favorable price concessions. At trial, Reeder presented evidence of two instances when it bid against another Volvo dealer for a particular sale. In the first, although Volvo initially offered Reeder a lower concession, Volvo ultimately matched the concession offered to the competing dealer. Neither dealer won the bid. In the second, Volvo initially offered the two dealers the same concession, but increased the other dealer\xe2\x80\x99s discount after it, rather than Reeder, was selected. Reeder dominantly relied on comparisons between concessions it received on four occasions when it bid successfully against non-Volvo dealers (and thus purchased Volvo trucks), with more favorable concessions other successful Volvo dealers received in bidding processes in which Reeder did not participate. Reeder also compared concessions Volvo offered it on several occasions when it bid unsuccessfully against non-Volvo dealers (and therefore did not purchase Volvo trucks), with more favorable concessions accorded other Volvo dealers who gained contracts on which Reeder did not bid. Reeder did not look for instances in which it received a larger concession than another Volvo dealer, but acknowledged it was \xe2\x80\x9cquite possible\xe2\x80\x9d that such instances occurred. Nor did Reeder offer any statistical analysis revealing whether it was disfavored on average as compared to other dealers. The jury found a reasonable possibility that discriminatory pricing may have harmed competition between Reeder and other Volvo dealers, that Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s discriminatory pricing injured Reeder, and that Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s damages from Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s Robinson-Patman violation exceeded $1.3 million. The District Court awarded treble damages on the Robinson-Patman Act claim, and entered judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Affirming, the Eighth Circuit, among other things, noted the threshold requirement that Reeder show it was a \xe2\x80\x9cpurchaser\xe2\x80\x9d within the Act\xe2\x80\x99s meaning; rejected Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s contention that competitive bidding situations do not give rise to Robinson-Patman claims; held that the four instances in which Reeder purchased trucks following successful bids rendered it a purchaser under the Act; determined that a jury could reasonably decide Reeder was in actual competition with favored dealers at the time price differentials were imposed; and held that the jury could properly find Reeder had proved competitive injury based on evidence that (1) Volvo intended to reduce the number of its dealers, (2) Reeder lost one contract for which it competed with another Volvo dealer, (3) Reeder would have earned more profits, had it received the concessions given other dealers, and (4) Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s sales declined over time.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A manufacturer may not be held liable for secondary-line price discrimination under the Robinson-Patman Act in the absence of a showing that the manufacturer discriminated between dealers competing to resell its product to the same retail customer. The Act does not reach the case Reeder presents. It centrally addresses price discrimination in cases involving competition between different purchasers for resale of the purchased product. Competition of that character ordinarily is not involved when a product subject to special order is sold through a customer-specific competitive bidding process. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Section 2 was enacted to curb financially powerful corporations\xe2\x80\x99 use of localized price-cutting tactics that gravely impaired other sellers\xe2\x80\x99 competitive position. FTC v. Anheuser-Busch, Inc., 363 U. S. 536, 543, and n.\xc2\xa06. Augmenting \xc2\xa72, the Robinson-Patman Act targeted the perceived harm to competition occasioned by the advent of large chain stores able to obtain lower prices for goods than smaller buyers could demand. Robinson-Patman does not ban all price differences charged to different purchasers of similar commodities, but proscribes only \xe2\x80\x9cprice discrimination [that] threatens to injure competition,\xe2\x80\x9d Brooke Group Ltd. v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., 509 U. S. 209, 220. Of the three categories of competitive injury that may give rise to a Robinson-Patman claim, secondary-line cases, like this one, involve price discrimination that injures competition among the discriminating seller\xe2\x80\x99s customers (here, Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s dealerships). Reeder has satisfied the Act\xe2\x80\x99s first two requirements for establishing secondary-line injury: (1) The relevant Volvo truck sales were made in interstate commerce, and (2) the trucks were of \xe2\x80\x9clike grade and quality,\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x99 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa713(a). Because Reeder has not identified any differentially-priced transaction in which it was both a \xe2\x80\x9cpurchaser\xe2\x80\x9d under the Act and \xe2\x80\x9cin actual competition\xe2\x80\x9d with a favored purchaser for the same customer, see e.g., FTC v. Sun Oil Co., 371 U. S. 505, 518\xe2\x80\x93519, Volvo and amicus United States maintain that Reeder cannot satisfy the Act\xe2\x80\x99s third and fourth requirements\xe2\x80\x94that (3) Volvo \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate[d] in price between\xe2\x80\x9d Reeder and another purchaser of Volvo trucks, and (4) \xe2\x80\x9cthe effect of such discrimination may be . . . to injure, destroy, or prevent competition\xe2\x80\x9d to the advantage of a favored purchaser, i.e., one who \xe2\x80\x9creceive[d] the benefit of such discrimination,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Absent actual competition with a favored Volvo dealer, Reeder cannot establish the competitive injury the Act requires. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The injury to competition targeted by the Robinson-Patman Act is not established by the selective comparisons Reeder presented at trial: (1) comparisons of concessions Reeder received for four successful bids against non-Volvo dealers, with larger concessions other successful Volvo dealers received for different sales on which Reeder did not bid (purchase-to-purchase comparisons); (2) comparisons of concessions offered to Reeder in connection with several unsuccessful bids against non-Volvo dealers, with greater concessions accorded other Volvo dealers who competed successfully for different sales on which Reeder did not bid (offer-to-purchase comparisons); and (3) comparisons of two occasions on which Reeder bid against another Volvo dealer (head-to-head comparisons). Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Because the purchase-to-purchase and offer-to-purchase comparisons fail to show that Volvo sold at a lower price to Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitors,\xe2\x80\x9d those comparisons do not support an inference of competitive injury. See Falls City Industries, Inc. v. Vanco Beverage, Inc., 460 U. S. 428, 435. Both types of comparisons fall short because in none of the discrete instances on which Reeder relied did it compete with beneficiaries of the alleged discrimination for the same customer. Nor did Reeder even attempt to show that the compared dealers were consistently favored over it. Reeder simply paired occasions on which it competed with non-Volvo dealers for a sale to Customer A with instances in which other Volvo dealers competed with non-Volvo dealers for a sale to Customer B. The compared incidents were tied to no systematic study and were separated in time by as many as seven months. This Court declines to permit an inference of competitive injury from evidence of such a mix-and-match, manipulable quality. No similar risk of manipulation occurs in cases kin to the chain-store paradigm. Here, there is no discrete \xe2\x80\x9cfavored\xe2\x80\x9d dealer comparable to a chain store or a large independent department store\xe2\x80\x94at least, Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s evidence is insufficient to support an inference that such a dealer exists. For all that appears, Reeder, on occasion, might have gotten a better deal vis-\xc3\x80-vis one or more of the dealers in its comparisons. While Reeder may have competed with other Volvo dealers for the opportunity to bid on potential sales in a broad geographic area, competition at that initial stage is based on a variety of factors, including the existence vel non of a relationship between the potential bidder and the customer, geography, and reputation. Once the customer has chosen the particular dealers from which it will solicit bids, the relevant market becomes limited to the needs and demands of the particular end user, with only a handful of dealers competing for the sale. Volvo dealers\xe2\x80\x99 bidding for sales in the same geographic area does not import that they in fact competed for the same customer-tailored sales. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Nor is a Robinson-Patman violation established by Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s evidence of two instances in which it competed head to head with another Volvo dealer. When multiple dealers bid for the business of the same customer, only one dealer will win the business and thereafter purchase the supplier\xe2\x80\x99s product to fulfill its contractual commitment. Even assuming the Act applies to head-to-head transactions, Reeder did not establish that it was disfavored vis-\xc3\x80-vis other Volvo dealers in the rare instances in which they competed for the same sale\xe2\x80\x94let alone that the alleged discrimination was substantial. Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s evidence showed loss of only one sale to another Volvo dealer, a sale of 12 trucks that would have generated $30,000 in gross profits for Reeder. Per its policy, Volvo initially offered Reeder and the other dealer the same concession, but ultimately granted a larger concession to the other dealer after it had won the bid. In the only other instance of head-to-head competition, Volvo increased Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s initial discount to match the discount offered the other competing Volvo dealer, but neither dealer won the bid. If price discrimination between two purchasers existed at all, it was not of such magnitude as to affect substantially competition between Reeder and the \xe2\x80\x9cfavored\xe2\x80\x9d Volvo dealer. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0The Robinson-Patman Act signals no large departure from antitrust law\xe2\x80\x99s primary concern, interbrand competition. Even if the Act\xe2\x80\x99s text could be construed as Reeder urges and the Eighth Circuit held, this Court would resist interpretation geared more to the protection of existing competitors than to the stimulation of competition. There is no evidence here that any favored purchaser possesses market power, the allegedly favored purchasers are dealers with little resemblance to large independent department stores or chain operations, and the supplier\xe2\x80\x99s selective price discounting fosters competition among suppliers of different brands. By declining to extend Robinson-Patman\xe2\x80\x99s governance to such cases, the Court continues to construe the Act consistently with antitrust law\xe2\x80\x99s broader policies. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

374 F.\xc2\xa03d 701, reversed and remanded.

\n

\nOPINION OF THE COURT

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 04-905
\n

VOLVO TRUCKS NORTH AMERICA, INC., PETI-\nTIONER v. REEDER-SIMCO GMC, INC.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

[January 10, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns specially ordered products\xe2\x80\x94heavy-duty trucks supplied by Volvo Trucks North America, Inc. (Volvo), and sold by franchised dealers through a competitive bidding process. In this process, the retail customer states its specifications and invites bids, generally from dealers franchised by different manufacturers. Only when a Volvo dealer\xe2\x80\x99s bid proves successful does the dealer arrange to purchase the trucks, which Volvo then builds to meet the customer\xe2\x80\x99s specifications.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reeder-Simco GMC, Inc. (Reeder), a Volvo dealer located in Fort Smith, Arkansas, commenced suit against Volvo alleging that Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s sales and profits declined because Volvo offered other dealers more favorable price concessions than those offered to Reeder. Reeder sought redress for its alleged losses under \xc2\xa72 of the Clayton Act, 38 Stat. 730, as amended by the Robinson-Patman Price Discrimination Act, 49 Stat. 1526, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa713 (Robinson-Patman Act or Act), and the Arkansas Franchise Practices Act, Ark. Code Ann. \xc2\xa74\xe2\x80\x9372\xe2\x80\x93201 et seq. (2001). Reeder prevailed at trial and on appeal on both claims. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted review on the federal claim to resolve the question whether a manufacturer offering its dealers different wholesale prices may be held liable for price discrimination proscribed by Robinson-Patman, absent a showing that the manufacturer discriminated between dealers contemporaneously competing to resell to the same retail customer. While state law designed to protect franchisees may provide, and in this case has provided, a remedy for the dealer exposed to conduct of the kind Reeder alleged, the Robinson-Patman Act, we hold, does not reach the case Reeder presents. The Act centrally addresses price discrimination in cases involving competition between different purchasers for resale of the purchased product. Competition of that character ordinarily is not involved when a product subject to special order is sold through a customer-specific competitive bidding process.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Volvo manufactures heavy-duty trucks. Reeder sells new and used trucks, including heavy-duty trucks. 374 F.\xc2\xa03d 701, 704 (CA8 2004). Reeder became an authorized dealer of Volvo trucks in 1995, pursuant to a five-year franchise agreement that provided for automatic one-year extensions if Reeder met sales objectives set by Volvo. Ibid. Reeder generally sold Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s trucks through a competitive bidding process. Ibid. In this process, the retail customer describes its specific product requirements and invites bids from several dealers it selects. The customer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdecision to request a bid from a particular dealer or to allow a particular dealer to bid is controlled by such factors as an existing relationship, geography, reputation, and cold calling or other marketing strategies initiated by individual dealers.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 719 (Hansen, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once a Volvo dealer receives the customer\xe2\x80\x99s specifications, it turns to Volvo and requests a discount or \xe2\x80\x9cconcession\xe2\x80\x9d off the wholesale price (set at 80% of the published retail price). Id., at 704. It is common practice in the industry for manufacturers to offer customer-specific discounts to their dealers. Ibid.; App. 334, 337. Volvo decides on a case-by-case basis whether to offer a discount and, if so, what the discount rate will be, taking account of such factors as industry-wide demand and whether the retail customer has, historically, purchased a different brand of trucks. App. 348\xe2\x80\x93349, 333\xe2\x80\x93334.[Footnote 1] The dealer then uses the discount offered by Volvo in preparing its bid; it purchases trucks from Volvo only if and when the retail customer accepts its bid. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reeder was one of many Volvo dealers, each assigned by Volvo to a geographic territory. Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s territory encompassed ten counties in Arkansas and two in Oklahoma. 374 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 709. Although nothing prohibits a Volvo dealer from bidding outside its territory, ibid., Reeder rarely bid against another Volvo dealer, see id., at 705; 5 App. in No. 02\xe2\x80\x932462 (CA8), pp. 1621\xe2\x80\x931622 (hereinafter C.\xc2\xa0A. App.). In the atypical event that the same retail customer solicited a bid from more than one Volvo dealer, Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s stated policy was to provide the same price concession to each dealer competing head to head for the same sale. 4 C.\xc2\xa0A. App. 1161\xe2\x80\x931162; 5 id., at 1619, 1621.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1997, Volvo announced a program it called \xe2\x80\x9cVolvo Vision,\xe2\x80\x9d in which the company addressed problems it faced in the market for heavy trucks, among them, the company\xe2\x80\x99s assessment that it had too many dealers. Volvo projected enlarging the size of its dealers\xe2\x80\x99 markets and reducing the number of dealers from 146 to 75. 374 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 705. Coincidentally, Reeder learned that Volvo had given another dealer a price concession greater than the concessions Reeder typically received, and \xe2\x80\x9cReeder came to suspect it was one of the dealers Volvo sought to eliminate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Reeder filed suit against Volvo in February 2000, alleging losses attributable to Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s violation of the Arkansas Franchise Practices Act and the Robinson-Patman Act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At trial, Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s vice-president, William E. Heck, acknowledged that Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s policy was to offer equal concessions to Volvo dealers bidding against one another for a particular contract, but he contended that the policy \xe2\x80\x9cwas not executed.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 C.\xc2\xa0A. App. 1162. Reeder presented evidence concerning two instances over the five-year course of its authorized dealership when Reeder bid against other Volvo dealers for a particular sale. 374 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 705, 708\xe2\x80\x93709. One of the two instances involved Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s bid on a sale to Tommy Davidson Trucking. 4 C.\xc2\xa0A. App. 1267\xe2\x80\x931268. Volvo initially offered Reeder a concession of 17%, which Volvo, unprompted, increased to 18.1% and then, one week later, to 18.9%, to match the concession Volvo had offered to another of its dealers. 5 id., at 1268\xe2\x80\x931272. Neither dealer won the bid. Id., at 1272. The other instance involved Hiland Dairy, which solicited bids from both Reeder and Southwest Missouri Truck Center. Id., at 1626\xe2\x80\x931627. Per its written policy, Volvo offered the two dealers the same concession, and Hiland selected Southwest Missouri, a dealer from which Hiland had previously purchased trucks. Ibid. After selecting Southwest Missouri, Hiland insisted on the price Southwest Missouri had bid prior to a general increase in Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s prices; Volvo obliged by increasing the size of the discount. Id., at 1627. See also id., at 1483\xe2\x80\x931488; 374 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 720 (Hansen, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reeder dominantly relied on comparisons between concessions Volvo offered when Reeder bid against non-Volvo dealers, with concessions accorded to other Volvo dealers similarly bidding against non-Volvo dealers for other sales. Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s evidence compared concessions Reeder received on four occasions when it bid successfully against non-Volvo dealers (and thus purchased Volvo trucks), with more favorable concessions other successful Volvo dealers received in connection with bidding processes in which Reeder did not participate. Id., at 705\xe2\x80\x93706. Reeder also compared concessions offered by Volvo on several occasions when Reeder bid unsuccessfully against non-Volvo dealers (and therefore did not purchase Volvo trucks), with more favorable concessions received by other Volvo dealers who gained contracts on which Reeder did not bid. Id., at 706\xe2\x80\x93707.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s vice-president, Heck, testified that Reeder did not look for instances in which it received a larger concession than another Volvo dealer, although he acknowledged it was \xe2\x80\x9cquite possible\xe2\x80\x9d that such instances occurred. 5 C.\xc2\xa0A. App. 1462. Nor did Reeder endeavor to determine by any statistical analysis whether Reeder was disfavored on average as compared to another dealer or set of dealers. Id., at 1462\xe2\x80\x931464.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury found that there was a reasonable possibility that discriminatory pricing may have harmed competition between Reeder and other Volvo truck dealers, and that Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s discriminatory pricing injured Reeder. App. 480\xe2\x80\x93486. It further found that Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s damages from Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s Robinson-Patman Act violation exceeded $1.3 million. Id., at 486.[Footnote 2] The District Court summarily denied Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s motion for judgment as a matter of law and the company\xe2\x80\x99s alternative motion for new trial or remittitur, awarded treble damages on the Robinson-Patman Act claim, and entered judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A divided Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit affirmed. The appeals court noted that, \xe2\x80\x9cas a threshold matter[,] Reeder had to show [that] it was a \xe2\x80\x98purchaser\xe2\x80\x99 within the meaning of the [Act],\xe2\x80\x9d 374 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 708, i.e., that \xe2\x80\x9cthere were actual sales at two different prices[,] \xe2\x80\xa6 a sale to [Reeder] and a sale to another Volvo dealer,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 707\xe2\x80\x93708. Rejecting Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s contention that competitive bidding situations do not give rise to claims under the Robinson-Patman Act, id., at 708\xe2\x80\x93709, the Court of Appeals observed that Reeder was \xe2\x80\x9cmore than an unsuccessful bidder,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 709. The four instances in which Reeder \xe2\x80\x9cactually purchased Volvo trucks following successful bids on contracts,\xe2\x80\x9d the court concluded, sufficed to render Reeder a purchaser within the meaning of the Act. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals next determined that a jury could reasonably decide that Reeder was \xe2\x80\x9cin actual competition\xe2\x80\x9d with favored dealers. Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]s of the time the price differential was imposed,\xe2\x80\x9d the court reasoned, \xe2\x80\x9cthe favored and disfavored purchasers competed at the same functional level . . . and within the same geographic market.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Best Brands Beverage, Inc. v. Falstaff Brewing Corp., 842 F.\xc2\xa02d 578, 585 (CA2 1987)). The court further held that the jury could properly find from the evidence that Reeder had proved competitive injury from price discrimination. Specifically, the court pointed to evidence showing that (1) Volvo intended to reduce the number of its dealers; (2) Reeder lost the Hiland Dairy contract, for which it competed head to head with another Volvo dealer; (3) Reeder would have earned more profits, had it received the concessions other dealers received; and (4) Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s sales had declined over a period of time. 374 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 711\xe2\x80\x93712. The court also affirmed the award of treble damages to Reeder. Id., at 712\xe2\x80\x93714.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Hansen dissented as to the Robinson-Patman Act claim. \xe2\x80\x9cTraditional [Robinson-Patman Act] cases,\xe2\x80\x9d he observed, \xe2\x80\x9cinvolve sellers and purchasers that carry inventory or deal in fungible goods.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 718. The majority, Judge Hansen commented, \xe2\x80\x9cattempt[ed] to fit a square peg into a round hole,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., when it extended the Act\xe2\x80\x99s reach to the marketplace for heavy-duty trucks, where \xe2\x80\x9cspecial-order products are sold to individual, pre-identified customers only after competitive bidding,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. There may be competition among dealers for the opportunity to bid on potential sales, he noted, but \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nce bidding begins, . . . the relevant market becomes limited to the needs and demands of a particular end user, with only a handful of dealers competing for the ultimate sale.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 719. Violation of the Act, in Judge Hansen\xe2\x80\x99s view, could not be predicated on the instances Reeder identified in which it was a purchaser, for \xe2\x80\x9cthere was no actual competition between\xe2\x80\x9d Reeder and another Volvo dealer at the time of Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s purchases. Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cWithout proof of actual competition\xe2\x80\x9d for the same customer when the requisite purchases were made, he concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cReeder cannot demonstrate a reasonable possibility of competitive injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 544 U. S. ___ (2005), to resolve this question: May a manufacturer be held liable for secondary-line price discrimination under the Robinson-Patman Act in the absence of a showing that the manufacturer discriminated between dealers competing to resell its product to the same retail customer? Satisfied that the Court of Appeals erred in answering that question in the affirmative, we reverse the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 2, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen originally enacted as part of the Clayton Act in 1914, was born of a desire by Congress to curb the use by financially powerful corporations of localized price-cutting tactics which had gravely impaired the competitive position of other sellers.\xe2\x80\x9d FTC v. Anheuser-Busch, Inc., 363 U. S. 536, 543, and n. 6 (1960) (citing H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 627, 63d Cong., 2d Sess., 8 (1914); S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 698, 63d Cong., 2d Sess., 2\xe2\x80\x934 (1914)). Augmenting that provision in 1936 with the Robinson-Patman Act, Congress sought to target the perceived harm to competition occasioned by powerful buyers, rather than sellers; specifically, Congress responded to the advent of large chain stores, enterprises with the clout to obtain lower prices for goods than smaller buyers could demand. See 14 H. Hovenkamp, Antitrust Law \xc2\xb62302, p. 11 (2d ed. 2006) (hereinafter Hovenkamp); P. Areeda & L. Kaplow, Antitrust Analysis \xc2\xb6602, pp. 908\xe2\x80\x93909 (5th ed. 1997) (hereinafter Areeda). The Act provides, in relevant part:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIt shall be unlawful for any person engaged in commerce \xe2\x80\xa6 to discriminate in price between different purchasers of commodities of like grade and quality, \xe2\x80\xa6 where the effect of such discrimination may be substantially to lessen competition or tend to create a monopoly in any line of commerce, or to injure, destroy, or prevent competition with any person who either grants or knowingly receives the benefit of such discrimination, or with customers of either of them\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa713(a).

\n

\nPursuant to \xc2\xa74 of the Clayton Act, a private plaintiff may recover threefold for actual injury sustained as a result of a violation of the Robinson-Patman Act. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa715(a); J. Truett Payne Co. v. Chrysler Motors Corp., 451 U. S. 557, 562 (1981).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Mindful of the purposes of the Act and of the antitrust laws generally, we have explained that Robinson-Patman does not \xe2\x80\x9cban all price differences charged to different purchasers of commodities of like grade and quality,\xe2\x80\x9d Brooke Group Ltd. v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., 509 U. S. 209, 220 (1993) (internal quotation marks omitted); rather, the Act proscribes \xe2\x80\x9cprice discrimination only to the extent that it threatens to injure competition,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Our decisions describe three categories of competitive injury that may give rise to a Robinson-Patman Act claim: primary-line, secondary-line, and tertiary-line. Primary-line cases entail conduct\xe2\x80\x94most conspicuously, predatory pricing\xe2\x80\x94that injures competition at the level of the discriminating seller and its direct competitors. See, e.g., id., at 220\xe2\x80\x93222; see also Hovenkamp \xc2\xb62301a, pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x936. Secondary-line cases, of which this is one, involve price discrimination that injures competition among the discriminating seller\xe2\x80\x99s customers (here, Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s dealerships); cases in this category typically refer to \xe2\x80\x9cfavored\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdisfavored\xe2\x80\x9d purchasers. See ibid.; Texaco Inc. v. Hasbrouck, 496 U. S. 543, 558, n. 15 (1990). Tertiary-line cases involve injury to competition at the level of the purchaser\xe2\x80\x99s customers. See Areeda \xc2\xb6601e, p. 907.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To establish the secondary-line injury of which it complains, Reeder had to show that (1) the relevant Volvo truck sales were made in interstate commerce; (2) the trucks were of \xe2\x80\x9clike grade and quality\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x99; (3) Volvo \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate[d] in price between\xe2\x80\x9d Reeder and another purchaser of Volvo trucks; and (4) \xe2\x80\x9cthe effect of such discrimination may be . . . to injure, destroy, or prevent competition\xe2\x80\x9d to the advantage of a favored purchaser, i.e., one who \xe2\x80\x9creceive[d] the benefit of such discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa713(a). It is undisputed that Reeder has satisfied the first and second requirements. Volvo and the United States, as amicus curiae, maintain that Reeder cannot satisfy the third and fourth requirements, because Reeder has not identified any differentially-priced transaction in which it was both a \xe2\x80\x9cpurchaser\xe2\x80\x9d under the Act and \xe2\x80\x9cin actual competition\xe2\x80\x9d with a favored purchaser for the same customer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A hallmark of the requisite competitive injury, our decisions indicate, is the diversion of sales or profits from a disfavored purchaser to a favored purchaser. FTC v. Sun Oil Co., 371 U. S. 505, 518\xe2\x80\x93519 (1963) (evidence showed patronage shifted from disfavored dealers to favored dealers); Falls City Industries, Inc. v. Vanco Beverage, Inc., 460 U. S. 428, 437\xe2\x80\x93438, and n. 8 (1983) (complaint \xe2\x80\x9csupported by direct evidence of diverted sales\xe2\x80\x9d). We have also recognized that a permissible inference of competitive injury may arise from evidence that a favored competitor received a significant price reduction over a substantial period of time. See FTC v. Morton Salt Co., 334 U. S. 37, 49\xe2\x80\x9351 (1948); Falls City Industries, 460 U. S., at 435. Absent actual competition with a favored Volvo dealer, however, Reeder cannot establish the competitive injury required under the Act.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The evidence Reeder offered at trial falls into three categories: (1) comparisons of concessions Reeder received for four successful bids against non-Volvo dealers, with larger concessions other successful Volvo dealers received for different sales on which Reeder did not bid (purchase-to-purchase comparisons); (2) comparisons of concessions offered to Reeder in connection with several unsuccessful bids against non-Volvo dealers, with greater concessions accorded other Volvo dealers who competed successfully for different sales on which Reeder did not bid (offer-to-purchase comparisons); and (3) evidence of two occasions on which Reeder bid against another Volvo dealer (head-to-head comparisons). The Court of Appeals concluded that Reeder demonstrated competitive injury under the Act because Reeder competed with favored purchasers \xe2\x80\x9cat the same functional level . . . and within the same geographic market.\xe2\x80\x9d 374 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 709 (quoting Best Brands, 842 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 585). As we see it, however, selective comparisons of the kind Reeder presented do not show the injury to competition targeted by the Robinson-Patman Act.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both the purchase-to-purchase and the offer-to-purchase comparisons fall short, for in none of the discrete instances on which Reeder relied did Reeder compete with beneficiaries of the alleged discrimination for the same customer. Nor did Reeder even attempt to show that the compared dealers were consistently favored vis-\xc3\x80-vis Reeder. Reeder simply paired occasions on which it competed with non-Volvo dealers for a sale to Customer A with instances in which other Volvo dealers competed with non-Volvo dealers for a sale to Customer B. The compared incidents were tied to no systematic study and were separated in time by as many as seven months. See 374 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 706, 710.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We decline to permit an inference of competitive injury from evidence of such a mix-and-match, manipulable quality. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 34\xe2\x80\x9335, 55. No similar risk of manipulation occurs in cases kin to the chain-store paradigm. Here, there is no discrete \xe2\x80\x9cfavored\xe2\x80\x9d dealer comparable to a chain store or a large independent department store\xe2\x80\x94at least, Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s evidence is insufficient to support an inference of such a dealer or set of dealers. For all we know, Reeder, on occasion, might have gotten a better deal vis-\xc3\x80-vis one or more of the dealers in its comparisons. See supra, at 5.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reeder may have competed with other Volvo dealers for the opportunity to bid on potential sales in a broad geographic area. At that initial stage, however, competition is not affected by differential pricing; a dealer in the competitive bidding process here at issue approaches Volvo for a price concession only after it has been selected by a retail customer to submit a bid. Competition for an opportunity to bid, we earlier observed, is based on a variety of factors, including the existence vel non of a relationship between the potential bidder and the customer, geography, and reputation. See supra, at 2.[Footnote 3] We reiterate in this regard an observation made by Judge Hansen, dissenting from the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s Robinson-Patman holding: Once a retail customer has chosen the particular dealers from which it will solicit bids, \xe2\x80\x9cthe relevant market becomes limited to the needs and demands of a particular end user, with only a handful of dealers competing for the ultimate sale.\xe2\x80\x9d 374 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 719. That Volvo dealers may bid for sales in the same geographic area does not import that they in fact competed for the same customer-tailored sales. In sum, the purchase-to-purchase and offer-to-purchase comparisons fail to show that Volvo sold at a lower price to Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitors,\xe2\x80\x9d hence those comparisons do not support an inference of competitive injury. See Falls City Industries, 460 U. S., at 435 (inference of competitive injury under Morton Salt arises from \xe2\x80\x9cproof of a substantial price discrimination between competing purchasers over time\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reeder did offer evidence of two instances in which it competed head to head with another Volvo dealer. See supra, at 4. When multiple dealers bid for the business of the same customer, only one dealer will win the business and thereafter purchase the supplier\xe2\x80\x99s product to fulfill its contractual commitment. Because Robinson-Patman \xe2\x80\x9cprohibits only discrimination \xe2\x80\x98between different purchasers,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 26 (quoting 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa713(a); emphasis added), Volvo and the United States argue, the Act does not reach markets characterized by competitive bidding and special-order sales, as opposed to sales from inventory. See Brief for Petitioner 27; Brief for United\xc2\xa0States as Amicus Curiae 9, 17\xe2\x80\x9320. We need not decide that question today. Assuming the Act applies to the head-to-head transactions, Reeder did not establish that it was disfavored vis-\xc3\x80-vis other Volvo dealers in the rare instances in which they competed for the same sale\xe2\x80\x94let alone that the alleged discrimination was substantial. See 1 ABA Section of Antitrust Law, Antitrust Law Developments 478\xe2\x80\x93479 (5th ed. 2002) (\xe2\x80\x9cNo inference of injury to competition is permitted when the discrimination is not substantial.\xe2\x80\x9d (collecting cases)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s evidence showed loss of only one sale to another Volvo dealer, a sale of 12 trucks that would have generated $30,000 in gross profits for Reeder. 374 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 705. Per its policy, Volvo initially offered Reeder and the other dealer the same concession. Volvo ultimately granted a larger concession to the other dealer, but only after it had won the bid. In the only other instance of head-to-head competition Reeder identified, Volvo increased Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s initial 17% discount to 18.9%, to match the discount offered to the other competing Volvo dealer; neither dealer won the bid. See supra, at 4. In short, if price discrimination between two purchasers existed at all, it was not of such magnitude as to affect substantially competition between Reeder and the \xe2\x80\x9cfavored\xe2\x80\x9d Volvo dealer.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Interbrand competition, our opinions affirm, is the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary concern of antitrust law.\xe2\x80\x9d Continental T. V., Inc. v. GTE Sylvania, Inc., 433 U. S. 36, 51\xe2\x80\x9352, n.\xc2\xa019 (1977). The Robinson-Patman Act signals no large departure from that main concern. Even if the Act\xe2\x80\x99s text could be construed in the manner urged by Reeder and embraced by the Court of Appeals, we would resist interpretation geared more to the protection of existing competitors than to the stimulation of competition.[Footnote 4] In the case before us, there is no evidence that any favored purchaser possesses market power, the allegedly favored purchasers are dealers with little resemblance to large independent department stores or chain operations, and the supplier\xe2\x80\x99s selective price discounting fosters competition among suppliers of different brands. See id., at 51\xe2\x80\x9352 (observing that the market impact of a vertical practice, such as a change in a supplier\xe2\x80\x99s distribution system, may be a \xe2\x80\x9csimultaneous reduction of intrabrand competition and stimulation of interbrand competition\xe2\x80\x9d). By declining to extend Robinson-Patman\xe2\x80\x99s governance to such cases, we continue to construe the Act \xe2\x80\x9cconsistently with broader policies of the antitrust laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Brooke Group, 509 U. S., at 220 (quoting Great Atlantic & Pacific Tea Co. v. FTC, 440 U. S. 69, 80, n.\xc2\xa013 (1979)); see Automatic Canteen Co. of America v. FTC, 346 U. S. 61, 63 (1953) (cautioning against Robinson-Patman constructions that \xe2\x80\x9cextend beyond the prohibitions of the Act and, in doing so, help give rise to a price uniformity and rigidity in open conflict with the purposes of other antitrust legislation\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 5]

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated, the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n546 U. S. ____ (2006)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 04-905
\n
\n

VOLVO TRUCKS NORTH AMERICA, INC., PETI-\nTIONER v. REEDER-SIMCO GMC, INC.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

[January 10, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Thomas joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Franchised dealers who sell Volvo trucks, like those who sell automobiles, farm equipment, washing machines, and a variety of other expensive items, routinely engage in negotiations with prospective purchasers. Sometimes the prospect is simultaneously negotiating with two Volvo dealers, sometimes with a Volvo dealer and a dealer representing another manufacturer, and still other times a satisfied customer who is generally familiar with the options available in a competitive market may negotiate with only one dealer at a time. Until today, the Robinson-Patman Act\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition of price discrimination[Footnote 1] would have protected the dealer\xe2\x80\x99s ability to negotiate in all those situations. Today, however, by adopting a novel, transaction-specific concept of competition, the Court eliminates that statutory protection in all but those rare situations in which a prospective purchaser is negotiating with two Volvo dealers at the same time.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Setting aside for the moment the fact that the case involves goods specially ordered for particular customers rather than goods stocked in inventory, the case is a rather ordinary Robinson-Patman suit. Respondent Reeder alleged a violation of the Act; the parties submitted a good deal of conflicting evidence to the jury; the trial judge properly instructed the jurors on the elements of price discrimination, competitive injury, and damages; and the jury returned a verdict resolving all issues in Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s favor. The Court of Appeals found no error in either the instructions or the sufficiency of the evidence. 374 F.\xc2\xa03d 701 (CA8 2004).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Two issues of fact bear particular mention.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, Volvo does not challenge the jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding of price discrimination. Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s theory of the case was that Volvo sought to cut back its number of dealers and deemed Reeder expendable. To avoid possible violations of franchise agreements and state laws, Volvo chose to accomplish this goal by offering Reeder worse prices than other regional dealers.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reeder introduced substantial evidence of this theory. It showed that Volvo had an explicit business strategy, known as the \xe2\x80\x9cVolvo Vision,\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cfewer dealers, larger markets.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 34. It showed that Volvo could afford to lose sales as it squeezed dealers out, since the boom years of the late 1990\xe2\x80\x99s left Volvo with about as many orders as it could fill. Id., at 256\xe2\x80\x93257. And it showed that Volvo frequently gave worse prices to it than to other regional dealers. On at least four occasions, Volvo sold trucks to Reeder at significantly higher prices than to other dealers buying similar trucks around the same time.[Footnote 2] To give one example, in the spring of 1998 Volvo sold 20 trucks to Reeder at a 9% concession, but sold similar trucks to a Texas dealer at a 12.3% concession. Id., at 132\xe2\x80\x93134. This left Reeder paying $2,606 more per truck. Id., at 134. Although the Court chides Reeder for failing to perform statistical analyses, see ante, at 5, 11, the jury clearly had a sufficient basis for finding price discrimination. It could infer that Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s pricing policies were comparable to a secret catalog listing one set of low prices for its \xe2\x80\x9cA\xe2\x80\x9d dealers and a higher set for its \xe2\x80\x9cB\xe2\x80\x9d dealers like Reeder, with an exception providing for the same prices where an \xe2\x80\x9cA\xe2\x80\x9d dealer and a \xe2\x80\x9cB\xe2\x80\x9d dealer were engaged in negotiations with the same customer at the same time.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the jury found that the favored dealers at issue in these comparisons were competitive players in the same geographic market as Reeder. This conclusion is implicit in the jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding of competitive injury, since the jury instruction on that element required Reeder to prove

\n

\xe2\x80\x9ca substantial difference in price in sales by defendant to plaintiff and other competing Volvo dealers over a significant period of time. This requires plaintiff to show that it and the other Volvo dealer(s) were retail dealers within the same geographic market and that the effect of the price differential was to allow the other Volvo dealer(s) to draw sales or profits away from plaintiff.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 480, Instruction No. 18.

\n

\nVolvo does not dispute that the evidence was sufficient to support the jury finding that Reeder and the favored dealers operated in the same geographic market.[Footnote 3] Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s restraint is wise, as Reeder offered evidence that truck buyers are unsurprisingly mobile, that it delivered trucks to purchasers throughout the region, and that customers would sometimes solicit bids from more than one regional Volvo dealer.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For decades, juries have routinely inferred the requisite injury to competition under the Robinson-Patman Act from the fact that a manufacturer sells goods to one retailer at a higher price than to its competitors. This rule dates back to the following discussion of competitive injury in Justice Black\xe2\x80\x99s opinion for the Court in FTC v. Morton Salt Co., 334 U. S. 37 (1948):

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIt is argued that the findings fail to show that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s discriminatory discounts had in fact caused injury to competition. There are specific findings that such injuries had resulted from respondent\xe2\x80\x99s discounts, although the statute does not require the Commission to find that injury has actually resulted. The statute requires no more than that the effect of the prohibited price discriminations \xe2\x80\x98may be substantially to lessen competition \xe2\x80\xa6 or to injure, destroy, or prevent competition.\xe2\x80\x99 After a careful consideration of this provision of the Robinson-Patman Act, we have said that \xe2\x80\x98the statute does not require that the discriminations must in fact have harmed competition, but only that there is a reasonable possibility that they \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d have such an effect.\xe2\x80\x99 Corn Products Co. v. Federal Trade Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 324 U. S. 726, 742. Here the Commission found what would appear to be obvious, that the competitive opportunities of certain merchants were injured when they had to pay respondent substantially more for their goods than their competitors had to pay. The findings are adequate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 45\xe2\x80\x9347 (footnote omitted).

\n

\nWe have treated as competitors those who sell \xe2\x80\x9cin a single, interstate retail market.\xe2\x80\x9d Falls City Industries, Inc. v. Vanco Beverage, Inc., 460 U. S. 428, 436 (1983); cf. Tampa Elec. Co. v. Nashville Coal Co., 365 U. S. 320, 327 (1961). Under this approach\xe2\x80\x94uncontroversial until today\xe2\x80\x94Reeder would readily prevail. There is ample evidence that Volvo charged Reeder higher prices than it charged to competing dealers in the same market over a period of many months. That those higher prices impaired Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s ability to compete with those dealers is just as obvious as the injury to competition described by the Court in Morton Salt.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Volvo nonetheless argues that no competitive injury could have occurred because it never discriminated against Reeder when Reeder and another Volvo dealer were seeking concessions with regard to the same ultimate customer. In Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s view, each transaction was a separate market, one defined by the customer and those dealers whom it had asked for bids. For each specific customer who has solicited bids, Reeder\xe2\x80\x99s only \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitors\xe2\x80\x9d were the other dealers making bids. Accordingly, if none of these other dealers were Volvo dealers, then Reeder suffered no competitive harm (relative to other Volvo dealers) when Volvo gave it a discriminatorily high price.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike the Court, I cannot accept Volvo\xe2\x80\x99s vision. Nothing in the statute or in our precedent suggests that \xe2\x80\x9ccompetition\xe2\x80\x9d is evaluated by a transaction-specific inquiry, and such an approach makes little sense. It requires us to ignore the fact that competition among truck dealers is a continuing war waged over time rather than a series of wholly discrete events. Each time Reeder managed to resell trucks it had purchased at discriminatorily high prices, it was forced either to accept lower profit margins than were available to favored Volvo dealers or to pass on the higher costs to its customers (who then might well go to a different dealer the next time). And we have long indicated that lost profits relative to a competitor are a proper basis for permitting the Morton Salt inference. See, e.g., Falls City Industries, 460 U. S., at 435 (noting that to overcome the Morton Salt inference, a defendant needs \xe2\x80\x9cevidence breaking the causal connection between a price differential and lost sales or profits\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). By ignoring these commonsense points, the Court gives short shrift to the Robinson-Patman Act\xe2\x80\x99s prophylactic intent. See 15 U. S. C. \xc2\xa713(a) (barring price discrimination where \xe2\x80\x9cthe effect of such discrimination may be substantially to lessen competition\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); see also, e.g., Morton Salt, 334 U. S., at 46.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court appears to hold that, absent head-to-head bidding with a favored dealer, a dealer in a competitive bidding market can suffer no competitive injury.[Footnote 4] It is unclear whether that holding is limited to franchised dealers who do not maintain inventories, or excludes virtually all franchisees from the effective protection of the Act. In either event, it is not faithful to the statutory text.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court recognizes, the Robinson-Patman Act was primarily intended to protect small retailers from the vigorous competition afforded by chainstores and other large volume purchasers. Whether that statutory mission represented sound economic policy is not merely the subject of serious debate, but may well merit Judge Bork\xe2\x80\x99s characterization as \xe2\x80\x9cwholly mistaken economic theory.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 5] I do not suggest that disagreement with the policy of the Act has played a conscious role in my colleagues\xe2\x80\x99 unprecedented decision today. I cannot avoid, however, identifying the irony in a decision refusing to adhere to the text of the Act in a case in which the jury credited evidence that discriminatory prices were employed as means of escaping contractual commitments and eliminating specifically targeted firms from a competitive market. The exceptional quality of this case provides strong reason to enforce the Act\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition against discrimination even if Judge Bork\xe2\x80\x99s evaluation (with which I happen to agree) is completely accurate.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, I respectfully dissent.
