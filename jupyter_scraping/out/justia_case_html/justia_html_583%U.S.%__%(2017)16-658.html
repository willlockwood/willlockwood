HAMER v. NEIGHBORHOOD HOUSING SERVICES OF CHICAGO et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x93658.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 10, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided November 8, 2017

\n

An appeal filing deadline prescribed by statute is considered \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d meaning that late filing of the appeal notice necessitates dismissal of the appeal. See Bowles v. Russell,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 205\n\xe2\x80\x93213. In contrast, a time limit prescribed only in a court-made rule is not jurisdictional. It is a mandatory claim-processing rule that may be waived or forfeited. Ibid. This Court and other forums have sometimes overlooked this critical distinction. See Reed Elsevier, Inc. v. Muchnick,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 154\n.

\n

Petitioner Charmaine Hamer filed an employment discrimination suit against respondents. The District Court granted respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion for summary judgment, entering final judgment on September 14, 2015. Before October 14, the date Hamer\xe2\x80\x99s notice of appeal was due, her attorneys filed a motion to withdraw as counsel and a motion for an extension of the appeal filing deadline to give Hamer time to secure new counsel. The District Court granted both motions, extending the deadline to December 14, a two-month extension, even though the governing Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure, Rule 4(a)(5)(C), confines such extensions to 30 days. Concluding that Rule 4(a)(5)(C)\xe2\x80\x99s time prescription is jurisdictional, the Court of Appeals dismissed Hamer\xe2\x80\x99s appeal.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals erred in treating as jurisdictional Rule 4(a)(5)(C)\xe2\x80\x99s limitation on extensions of time to file a notice of appeal. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The 1948 version of\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107 allowed extensions of time to file a notice of appeal, not exceeding 30 days, \xe2\x80\x9cupon a showing of excusable neglect based on failure of a party to learn of the entry of the judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d but the statute said nothing about extensions when the judgment loser did receive notice of the entry of judgment. In 1991, the statute was amended, broadening the class of prospective appellants who could gain extensions to include all who showed \xe2\x80\x9cexcusable neglect or good cause\xe2\x80\x9d and reducing the time prescription for appellants who lacked notice of the entry of judgment from 30 to 14 days. \xc2\xa72107(c). For other cases, the statute does not say how long an extension may run. Rule 4(a)(5)(C), however, does prescribe a limit: \xe2\x80\x9cNo extension [of time for filing a notice of appeal] may exceed 30 days after the prescribed time [for filing a notice of appeal] or 14 days after the date [of] the order granting the [extension] motion\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , whichever is later.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent shapes a rule of decision that is both clear and easy to apply: If a time prescription governing the transfer of adjudicatory authority from one Article III court to another appears in a statute, the limitation is jurisdictional; otherwise, the time specification fits within the claim-processing category.

\n

In concluding otherwise, the Court of Appeals relied on Bowles. There, Bowles filed a notice of appeal outside a limitation set by Congress in \xc2\xa72107(c). This Court held that, as a result, the Court of Appeals lacked jurisdiction over his tardy appeal. 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 213. In conflating Rule 4(a)(5)(C) with \xc2\xa72107(c) here, the Seventh Circuit failed to grasp the distinction between jurisdictional appeal filing deadlines and deadlines stated only in mandatory claim-processing rules. It therefore misapplied Bowles. Bowles\xe2\x80\x99s statement that \xe2\x80\x9cthe taking of an appeal within the prescribed time is \xe2\x80\x98mandatory and jurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 209, is a characterization left over from days when the Court was \xe2\x80\x9cless than meticulous\xe2\x80\x9d in using the term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d Kontrick v. Ryan,\n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 443\n. The statement was correct in Bowles, where the time prescription was imposed by Congress, but it would be incorrect here, where only Rule 4(a)(5)(C) limits the length of the extension. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

835 F.\xc2\xa03d 761, vacated and remanded.

CHARMAINE HAMER, PETITIONER v. NEIGH-BORHOOD HOUSING SERVICES OFCHICAGO, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[November 8, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case presents a question of time, specifically, time to file a notice of appeal from a district court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. In Bowles v. Russell,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 205\n\xe2\x80\x93213 (2007), this Court clarified that an appeal filing deadline prescribed by statute will be regarded as \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d meaning that late filing of the appeal notice necessitates dismissal of the appeal. But a time limit prescribed only in a court-made rule, Bowles acknowledged, is not jurisdictional; it is, instead, a mandatory claim-processing rule subject to forfeiture if not properly raised by the appellee. Ibid.; Kontrick v. Ryan,\n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 443,\n456 (2004)\n. Because the Court of Appeals held jurisdictional a time limit specified in a rule, not in a statute, 835 F.\xc2\xa03d 761, 763 (CA7 2016), we vacate that court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment dismissing the appeal.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cOnly Congress may determine a lower federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Kontrick, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 452 (citing U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. III, \xc2\xa71); Owen Equipment & Erection Co. v. Kroger,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 365,\n370 (1978)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is axiomatic that the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure do not create or withdraw federal jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, a provision governing the time to appeal in a civil action qualifies as jurisdictional only if Congress sets the time. See Bowles, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 211\xe2\x80\x93212 (noting \xe2\x80\x9cthe jurisdictional distinction between court-promulgated rules and limits enacted by Congress\xe2\x80\x9d); Sibbach v. Wilson & Co.,\n\n312 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n10 (1941)\n (noting \xe2\x80\x9cthe inability of a court, by rule, to extend or restrict the jurisdiction conferred by a statute\xe2\x80\x9d). A time limit not prescribed by Congress ranks as a mandatory claim-processing rule, serving \xe2\x80\x9cto promote the orderly progress of litigation by requiring that the parties take certain procedural steps at certain specified times.\xe2\x80\x9d Henderson v. Shinseki,\n\n562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 428,\n435 (2011)\n.

\n

This Court and other forums have sometimes overlooked this distinction, \xe2\x80\x9cmischaracteriz[ing] claim-processing rules or elements of a cause of action as jurisdictional limitations, particularly when that characterization was not central to the case, and thus did not require close analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d Reed Elsevier, Inc. v. Muchnick,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 154,\n161 (2010)\n. But prevailing precedent makes the distinction critical. Failure to comply with a jurisdictional time prescription, we have maintained, deprives a court of adjudicatory authority over the case, necessitating dismissal\xe2\x80\x94a \xe2\x80\x9cdrastic\xe2\x80\x9d result. Shinseki, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 435; Bowles, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 213 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen an \xe2\x80\x98appeal has not been prosecuted .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. within the time limited by the acts of Congress, it must be dismissed for want of jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting United States v. Curry, 6 How. 106, 113 (1848))). The jurisdictional defect is not subject to waiver or forfeiture[1] and may be raised at any time in the court of first instance and on direct appeal. Kontrick, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 455.[2] In contrast to the ordinary operation of our adversarial system, courts are obliged to notice jurisdictional issues and raise them on their own initiative. Shinseki, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 434.

\n

Mandatory claim-processing rules are less stern. If properly invoked, mandatory claim-processing rules must be enforced, but they may be waived or forfeited. Manrique v. United States, 581 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2017) (slip op., at 4). \xe2\x80\x9c[C]laim-processing rules .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [ensure] relief to a party properly raising them, but do not compel the same result if the party forfeits them.\xe2\x80\x9d Eberhart v. United States,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 12,\n19 (2005)\n (per curiam).[3]

\n

B

\n

Petitioner Charmaine Hamer filed a complaint against respondents Neighborhood Housing Services of Chicago and Fannie Mae alleging employment discrimination in violation of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e et\xc2\xa0seq., and the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967,\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7621 et\xc2\xa0seq. The District Court granted respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion for summary judgment on September 10, 2015, and entered final judgment on September 14, 2015. In the absence of a time extension, Hamer\xe2\x80\x99s notice of appeal would have been due by October 14, 2015. Fed. Rule App. Proc. 4(a)(1)(A).

\n

On October 8, 2015, before the October 14 deadline for filing Hamer\xe2\x80\x99s notice of appeal, her attorneys made two motions.[4] First, they sought to withdraw as counsel because of their disagreement with Hamer on pursuit of an appeal. Second, they sought a two-month extension of the notice of appeal filing date, so that Hamer would have adequate time to engage new counsel for her appeal. App. to Pet. for Cert. 57\xe2\x80\x9359. The District Court granted both motions on the same day and ordered extension of the deadline for Hamer\xe2\x80\x99s notice of appeal from October 14 to December 14, 2015. Id., at 60. Respondents did not move for reconsideration or otherwise raise any objection to the length of the extension.

\n

In the docketing statement respondents filed in the Court of Appeals, they stated: \xe2\x80\x9cThe United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit has jurisdiction over this appeal under\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71291, in that on December 11, 2015, [Hamer] filed a timely Notice of Appeal from a final judgment of the United States District Court for the Northern District of Illinois that disposed of all of [Hamer\xe2\x80\x99s] claims against [respondents].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 63. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 statement later reiterated: \xe2\x80\x9cOn December 11, 2015, [Hamer] timely filed a Notice of Appeal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 64. Nevertheless, the Court of Appeals, on its own ini-tiative, questioned the timeliness of the appeal and instructed respondents to brief the issue. 835 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 762. Respondents did so and, for the first time, asserted that the appeal was untimely, citing the relevant Rule confining extensions to 30 days. Id., at 762\xe2\x80\x93763 (citing Fed. Rule App. Proc. 4(a)(5)(C)). Concluding that it lacked jurisdiction to reach the merits, the Court of Appeals dismissed Hamer\xe2\x80\x99s appeal. 835 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 763.[5] We granted certio-rari. 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

Section 2107 of Title 28 of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Code, as enacted in 1948, allowed extensions of the time to file a notice of appeal, not exceeding 30 days, \xe2\x80\x9cupon a showing of excus-able neglect based on failure of a party to learn of the entry of the judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of June 25, 1948, \xc2\xa72107,\n\n62Stat.\n963.[6] Nothing in the statute provided for extension of the time to file a notice of appeal when, as in this case, the judgment loser did receive notice of the entry of judgment. In 1991, Congress broadened the class of persons who could gain extensions to include all prospective appellants who showed \xe2\x80\x9cexcusable neglect or good cause.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa712,\n\n105Stat.\n1627. In addition, Congress retained a time prescription covering appellants who lacked notice of the entry of judgment: \xe2\x80\x9c[A] party entitled to notice of the entry of a judgment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [who] did not receive such notice from the clerk or any party within 21 days of [the judgment\xe2\x80\x99s] entry\xe2\x80\x9d qualifies for a 14-day extension,[7] if \xe2\x80\x9cno party would be prejudiced [thereby].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72107(c). In full, \xc2\xa72107(c) now provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(c) The district court may, upon motion filed not later than 30 days after the expiration of the time otherwise set for bringing appeal, extend the time for appeal upon a showing of excusable neglect or good cause. In addition, if the district court finds\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(1) that a party entitled to notice of the entry of a judgment or order did not receive such notice from the clerk or any party within 21 days of its entry, and

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(2) that no party would be prejudiced,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cthe district court may, upon motion filed within 180 days after entry of the judgment or order or within 14 days after receipt of such notice, whichever is earlier, reopen the time for appeal for a period of 14 days from the date of entry of the order reopening the time for appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

In short, current \xc2\xa72107(c), like the provision as initially enacted, specifies the length of an extension for cases in which the appellant lacked notice of the entry of judgment.[8] For other cases, the statute does not say how long an extension may run.

\n

But Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 4(a)(5)(C) does prescribe a limit: \xe2\x80\x9cNo extension [of time for filing a notice of appeal] may exceed 30 days after the prescribed time [for filing a notice of appeal] or 14 days after the date [of] the order granting the [extension] motion .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , whichever is later.\xe2\x80\x9d Unlike \xc2\xa72107(c), we note, Rule 4(a)(5)(C) limits extensions of time to file a notice of appeal in all circumstances, not just in cases in which the prospective appellant lacked notice of the entry of judgment.

\n

B

\n

Although Rule 4(a)(5)(C)\xe2\x80\x99s limit on extensions of time appears nowhere in the text of \xc2\xa72107(c), respondents now contend that Rule 4(a)(5)(C) has a \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory basis\xe2\x80\x9d because \xc2\xa72107(c) once limited extensions (to the extent it did authorize them) to 30 days. Brief for Respondents 17. No matter, respondents submit, that Congress struck the 30-day limit in 1991 and replaced it with a 14-day limit governing, as the 30-day limit did, only lack-of-notice cases; deleting the 30-day prescription, respondents conjecture, was \xe2\x80\x9cprobably inadverten[t].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1. In support of their argument that Congress accidentally failed to impose an all-purpose limit on extensions, respondents observe that the 1991 statute identifies Congress\xe2\x80\x99 aim as the enactment of \xe2\x80\x9ccertain technical corrections in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. provisions of law relating to the courts.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n105Stat.\n1623. They also note the caption of the relevant section of the amending statute: \xe2\x80\x9cConformity with Rules of Appellate Procedure.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1627. Because striking the 30-day limit from \xc2\xa72107 made the statute less like Rule 4(a)(5)(C), respondents reason, Congress likely erased the relevant paragraph absentmindedly. Hence, respondents conclude, \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no reason to interpret the 1991 amendment as stripping Rule 4(a)(5)(C) of its jurisdictional significance.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 2.

\n

Overlooked by respondents, pre-1991 \xc2\xa72107 never spoke to extensions for reasons other than lack of notice. In any event, we resist speculating whether Congress acted inadvertently. See Henson v. Santander Consumer USA Inc., 582 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2017) (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e will not presume with [respondents] that any result consistent with their account of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s overarching goal must be the law but will presume more modestly instead \xe2\x80\x98that [the] legislature says .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. what it means and means .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. what it says.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Dodd v. United States,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 353,\n357 (2005)\n)); Magwood v. Patterson,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 320\n, 334 (2010) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe cannot replace the actual text with speculation as to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent.\xe2\x80\x9d). The rule of decision our precedent shapes is both clear and easy to apply: If a time prescription governing the transfer of adjudicatory authority from one Article III court to another appears in a statute, the limitation is jurisdictional, supra, at 2; otherwise, the time specification fits within the claim-processing category, ibid.[9]

\n

In dismissing Hamer\xe2\x80\x99s appeal for want of jurisdiction, the Court of Appeals relied heavily on our decision in Bowles. We therefore reiterate what that precedent conveys. There, petitioner Keith Bowles did not receive timely notice of the entry of a postjudgment order and consequently failed to file a timely notice of appeal. Bowles v. Russell, 432 F.\xc2\xa03d 668, 670 (CA6 2005). When Bowles learned of the postjudgment order, he moved for an extension under Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 4(a)(6), which implements \xc2\xa72107(c)\xe2\x80\x99s authorization of extensions in lack-of-notice cases. Ibid. The District Court granted Bowles\xe2\x80\x99s motion, but inexplicably provided a 17-day extension, rather than the 14-day extension authorized by \xc2\xa72107(c). Bowles, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 207. Bowles filed his notice of appeal within the 17 days allowed by the District Court but outside the 14 days allowed by \xc2\xa72107(c). Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cBecause Congress specifically limited the amount of time by which district courts can extend the notice-of-appeal period in \xc2\xa72107(c),\xe2\x80\x9d we explained, the Court of Appeals lacked jurisdiction over Bowles\xe2\x80\x99s tardy appeal. Id., at 213.

\n

Quoting Bowles at length, the Court of Appeals in this case reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9c[l]ike Rule 4(a)(6), Rule 4(a)(5)(C) is the vehicle by which \xc2\xa72107(c) is employed and it limits a district court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to extend the notice of appeal filing deadline to no more than an additional 30 days.\xe2\x80\x9d 835 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 763. In conflating Rule 4(a)(5)(C) with \xc2\xa72107(c), the Court of Appeals failed to grasp the distinction our decisions delineate between jurisdictional appeal filing deadlines and mandatory claim-processing rules, and therefore misapplied Bowles.

\n

Several Courts of Appeals,[10] including the Court of Appeals in Hamer\xe2\x80\x99s case, have tripped over our statement in Bowles that \xe2\x80\x9cthe taking of an appeal within the prescribed time is \xe2\x80\x98mandatory and jurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 209 (quoting Griggs v. Provident Consumer Discount Co.,\n\n459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 56,\n61 (1982)\n (per curiam)). The \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory and jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d formulation is a characterization left over from days when we were \xe2\x80\x9cless than meticulous\xe2\x80\x9d in our use of the term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Kontrick, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 454.[11] The statement was correct as applied in Bowles because, as the Court there explained, the time prescription at issue in Bowles was imposed by Congress. 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 209\xe2\x80\x93213. But \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory and jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d is erroneous and confounding terminology where, as here, the relevant time prescription is absent from the U.\xc2\xa0S. Code. Because Rule 4(a)(5)(C), not \xc2\xa72107, limits the length of the extension granted here, the time prescription is not jurisdictional. See Youkelsone v. FDIC, 660 F.\xc2\xa03d 473, 475 (CADC 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cRule 4(a)(5)(C)\xe2\x80\x99s thirty-day limit on the length of any extension ultimately granted appears nowhere in the U.\xc2\xa0S. Code.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

For the reasons stated, the Court of Appeals erroneously treated as jurisdictional Rule 4(a)(5)(C)\xe2\x80\x99s 30-day limitation on extensions of time to file a notice of appeal. We therefore vacate that court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion. We note, in this regard, that our decision does not reach issues raised by Hamer, but left unaddressed by the Court of Appeals, including: (1) whether respondents\xe2\x80\x99 failure to raise any objection in the District Court to the overlong time extension, by itself, effected a forfeiture, see Brief for Petitioner 21\xe2\x80\x9322; (2) whether respondents could gain review of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s time extension only by filing their own appeal notice, see id., at 23\xe2\x80\x9327; and (3) whether equitable considerations may occasion an exception to Rule 4(a)(5)(C)\xe2\x80\x99s time constraint, see id., at 29\xe2\x80\x9343.

\n

It is so ordered.
