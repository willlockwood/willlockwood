\n\t\t\t\tSEBELIUS, SECRETARY OF HEALTH AND HUMAN SERVICES v. AUBURN REGIONAL MEDICAL CENTER et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x931231.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 4, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 22, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe reimbursement amount health care providers receive for inpatient services rendered to Medicare beneficiaries is adjusted upward for hospitals that serve a disproportionate share of low-income patients. The adjustment amount is determined in part by the percentage of a hospital\xe2\x80\x99s patients who are eligible for Supplemental Security Income (SSI), called the SSI fraction. Each year, the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) calculates the SSI fraction for an eligible hospital and submits that number to the hospital\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfiscal intermediary,\xe2\x80\x9d a Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) contractor. The intermediary computes the reimbursement amount due and then sends the hospital a Notice of Program Reimbursement (NPR). A provider dissatisfied with the determination has a right to appeal to the Provider Reimbursement Review Board (PRRB or Board) within 180 days of receiving the NPR. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3). By regulation, the Secretary of HHS authorized the PRRB to extend the 180-day limit, for good cause, up to three years. See 42 CFR 405.1841(b) (2007).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Baystate Medical Center\xe2\x80\x94not a party here\xe2\x80\x94timely appealed its SSI fraction calculation for each year from 1993 through 1996. The PRRB found that errors in CMS\xe2\x80\x99s methodology resulted in a systematic undercalculation of the disproportionate share adjustment and corresponding underpayments to providers. In March 2006, the Board\xe2\x80\x99s Baystate decision was made public. Within 180 days, respondent hospitals filed a complaint with the Board, challenging their adjustments for 1987 through 1994. Acknowledging that their challenges were more than a decade out of time, they urged that equitable tolling of the limitations period was in order due to CMS\xe2\x80\x99s failure to tell them about the computation error. The PRRB held that it lacked jurisdiction, reasoning that it had no equitable powers save those legislation or regulation might confer. On judicial review, the District Court dismissed the hospitals\xe2\x80\x99 claims. The D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit reversed. The presumption that statutory limitations periods are generally subject to equitable tolling, the court concluded, applied to the 180-day time limit because nothing in \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3) indicated that Congress intended to disallow such tolling.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t1.\xc2\xa0The 180-day limitation in \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3) is not \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Unless Congress has \xe2\x80\x9cclearly state[d]\xe2\x80\x9d that a statutory limitation is jurisdictional, the restriction should be treated \xe2\x80\x9cas nonjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., \n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 500\n\xe2\x80\x93516. \xe2\x80\x9c[C]ontext, including this Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretations of similar provisions in many years past,\xe2\x80\x9d is probative of whether Congress intended a particular provision to rank as jurisdictional. Reed Elsevier, Inc. v. Muchnick, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. If \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3) were jurisdictional, the 180-day time limit could not be enlarged by agency or court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 1395oo(a)(3) hardly reveals a design to preclude any regulatory extension. The provision instructs that a provider \xe2\x80\x9cmay obtain a hearing\xe2\x80\x9d by filing \xe2\x80\x9ca request .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. within 180 days after notice of the intermediary\xe2\x80\x99s final determination.\xe2\x80\x9d It \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not speak in jurisdictional terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Zipes v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., \n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385\n. This Court has repeatedly held that filing deadlines ordinarily are not jurisdictional; indeed, they have been described as \xe2\x80\x9cquintessential claim-processing rules.\xe2\x80\x9d Henderson v. Shinseki, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Court-appointed amicus urges that \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3) should be classified as a jurisdictional requirement based on its proximity to \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71395oo(a)(1) and (a)(2), both jurisdictional requirements, amicus asserts. But a requirement that would otherwise be nonjurisdictional does not become jurisdictional simply because it is in a section of a statute that also contains jurisdictional provisions. Gonzalez v. Thaler, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Amicus also urges that the Medicare Act\xe2\x80\x99s express grant of authority for the Secretary to extend the time for beneficiary appeals implies the absence of such leeway for \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s provider appeals. In support, amicus relies on the general rule that Congress\xe2\x80\x99s use of \xe2\x80\x9ccertain language in one part of the statute and different language in another\xe2\x80\x9d can indicate that \xe2\x80\x9cdifferent meanings were intended,\xe2\x80\x9d Sosa v. Alvarez-Machain, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 692\n, n.\xc2\xa09. But that interpretive guide, like other canons of construction, is \xe2\x80\x9cno more than [a] rul[e] of thumb\xe2\x80\x9d that can tip the scales when a statute could be read in multiple ways. Connecticut Nat. Bank v. Germain, \n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 249\n. Here, \xc2\xa71395oo(a)\xe2\x80\x99s limitation is most sensibly characterized as nonjurisdictional. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t2.\xc2\xa0The Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulation is a permissible interpretation of \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3). Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Congress vested in the Secretary large rulemaking authority to administer Medicare. A court lacks authority to undermine the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regime unless her regulation is \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary, capricious, or manifestly contrary to the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837\n. Here, the regulation survives inspection under that deferential standard. The Secretary brought to bear practical experience in superintending the huge program generally, and the PRRB in particular, in maintaining a three-year outer limit for intermediary determination challenges. A court must uphold her judgment as long as it is a permissible construction of the statute, even if the court would have interpreted the statute differently absent agency regulation. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0A presumption of equitable tolling generally applies to suits against the United States, Irwin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, \n\n498 U.S. 89\n\xe2\x80\x9396, but application of this presumption is not in order for \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3). This Court has never applied Irwin\xe2\x80\x99s presumption to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s internal appeal deadline. The presumption was adopted in part on the premise that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]uch a principle is likely to be a realistic assessment of legislative intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Irwin, 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 95. That premise is inapt in the context of providers\xe2\x80\x99 administrative appeals under the Medicare Act. For nearly 40 years the Secretary has prohibited the Board from extending the 180-day deadline, except as provided by regulation. In the six times \xc2\xa71395oo has been amended since 1974, Congress has left untouched the 180-day provision and the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s rulemaking authority. Furthermore, the statutory scheme, which applies to sophisticated institutional providers, is not designed to be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unusually protective\xe2\x80\x99 of claimants.\xe2\x80\x9d Bowen v. City of New York, \n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 467\n. Nor is the scheme one \xe2\x80\x9cin which laymen, unassisted by trained lawyers, initiate the process.\xe2\x80\x9d Zipes, 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 397.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe hospitals ultimately argue that the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulations fail to adhere to \xe2\x80\x9cfundamentals of fair play.\xe2\x80\x9d FCC v. Pottsville Broadcasting Co., \n\n309 U.\xc2\xa0S. 134\n. They point to 42 CFR \xc2\xa7405.1885(b)(3), which permits reopening of an intermediary\xe2\x80\x99s reimbursement determination \xe2\x80\x9cat any time\xe2\x80\x9d if the determination was procured by fraud or fault of the provider. But this Court has explained that giving intermediaries more time to discover overpayments than providers have to discover underpayments may be justified by the \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative realities\xe2\x80\x9d of the system: a few dozen fiscal intermediaries are charged with issuing tens of thousands of NPRs, while each provider can concentrate on a single NPR, its own. Your Home Visiting Nurse Services, Inc. v. Shalala, \n\n525 U.\xc2\xa0S. 449\n. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t642 F.\xc2\xa03d 1145, reversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case concerns the time within which health care providers may file an administrative appeal from the initial determination of the reimbursement due them for inpatient services rendered to Medicare beneficiaries. Government contractors, called fiscal intermediaries, receive cost reports annually from care providers and notify them of the reimbursement amount for which they qualify. A provider dissatisfied with the fiscal intermediary\xe2\x80\x99s determination may appeal to an administrative body named the Provider Reimbursement Review Board (PRRB or Board). The governing statute, \xc2\xa7602(h)(l)(D), \n\n97Stat.\n165, \n\n42 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3), sets a 180-day limit for filing appeals from the fiscal intermediary to the PRRB. By a regulation promulgated in 1974, the Secretary of the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) authorized the Board to extend the 180-day limitation, for good cause, up to three years.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe providers in this case are hospitals who appealed to the PRRB more than ten years after expiration of the 180-day statutory deadline. They assert that the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s failure to disclose information that made the fiscal intermediary\xe2\x80\x99s reimbursement calculation incorrect prevented them from earlier appealing to the Board. Three positions have been briefed and argued regarding the time for providers\xe2\x80\x99 appeals to the PRRB. First, a Court-appointed amicus curiae has urged that the 180-day limitation is \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore cannot be enlarged at all by agency or court. Second, the Government maintains that the Secretary has the prerogative to set an outer limit of three years for appeals to the Board. And third, the hos- pitals argue that the doctrine of equitable tolling applies, stopping the 180-day clock during the time the Secretary concealed the information that made the fiscal interme- diary\xe2\x80\x99s reimbursement determinations incorrect.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe hold that the statutory 180-day limitation is not \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the Secretary reasonably construed the statute to permit a regulation extending the time for a provider\xe2\x80\x99s appeal to the PRRB to three years. We further hold that the presumption in favor of equitable tolling does not apply to administrative appeals of the kind here at issue.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Medicare program covers certain inpatient services that hospitals provide to Medicare beneficiaries. Providers are reimbursed at a fixed amount per patient, regardless of the actual operating costs they incur in rendering these services. But the total reimbursement amount is adjusted upward for hospitals that serve a disproportionate share of low-income patients. This adjustment is made because hospitals with an unusually high percentage of low-income patients generally have higher per-patient costs; such hospitals, Congress therefore found, should receive higher reimbursement rates. See H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 99\xe2\x80\x93241, pt. 1, p.\xc2\xa016 (1985). The amount of the disproportionate share adjustment is determined in part by the percentage of the patients served by the hospital who are eligible for Supplemental Security Income (SSI) payments, a percentage commonly called the SSI fraction. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71395ww(d) (2006 ed. and Supp. V).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt the end of each year, providers participating in Medicare submit cost reports to contractors acting on behalf of HHS known as fiscal intermediaries. Also at year end, the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) cal- culates the SSI fraction for each eligible hospital and submits that number to the intermediary for that hospital. Using these numbers to determine the total payment due, the intermediary issues a Notice of Program Reimbursement (NPR) informing the provider how much it will be paid for the year.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf a provider is dissatisfied with the intermediary\xe2\x80\x99s re- imbursement determination, the statute gives it the right to file a request for a hearing before the PRRB within 180 days of receiving the NPR. \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3) (2006 ed.) In 1974, the Secretary promulgated a regulation, after notice and comment rulemaking, permitting the Board to extend the 180-day time limit upon a showing of good cause; the regulation further provides that \xe2\x80\x9cno such extension shall be granted by the Board if such request is filed more than 3 years after the date the notice of the intermediary\xe2\x80\x99s determination is mailed to the provider.\xe2\x80\x9d 39 Fed. Reg. 34517 (1974) (codified in 42 CFR \xc2\xa7405.1841(b) (2007)).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor many years, CMS released only the results of its SSI fraction calculations and not the underlying data.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The Baystate Medical Center\xe2\x80\x94a hospital not party to this case\xe2\x80\x94timely appealed the calculation of its SSI fraction for each year from 1993 through 1996. Eventually, the PRRB determined that CMS had omitted several categories of SSI data from its calculations and was using a flawed process to determine the number of low-income beneficiaries treated by hospitals. These errors caused a systematic undercalculation of the disproportionate share adjustment, resulting in underpayments to the providers. Baystate Medical Center v. Leavitt, 545 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 20, 26\xe2\x80\x9330 (DC 2008); see id., at 57\xe2\x80\x9358 (concluding that CMS failed to use \xe2\x80\x9cthe best available data\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe methodological errors revealed by the Board\xe2\x80\x99s Bay- state decision would have yielded similarly reduced payments to all providers for which CMS had calculated an SSI fraction. In March 2006, the Board\xe2\x80\x99s decision in the Baystate case was made public. Within 180 days, the hospitals in this case filed a complaint with the Board seeking to challenge their disproportionate share adjustments for the years 1987 through 1994. The hospitals acknowledged that their challenges, unlike Baystate\xe2\x80\x99s timely contest, were more than a decade out of time. But equitable tolling of the limitations period was in order, they urged, due to CMS\xe2\x80\x99s failure to inform the hospitals that their SSI fractions had been based on faulty data.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe PRRB held that it lacked jurisdiction over the hospitals\xe2\x80\x99 complaint, reasoning that it had no equitable powers save those legislation or regulation might confer, and that the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulation permitted it to excuse late appeals only for good cause, with three years as the outer limit. On judicial review, the District Court dismissed the hospitals\xe2\x80\x99 claims for relief, holding that nothing in the statute suggests that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress intended to authorize equitable tolling.\xe2\x80\x9d 686 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 55, 70 (DC 2010).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals reversed. 642 F.\xc2\xa03d 1145 (CADC 2011). It relied on the presumption that statutory limitations periods are generally subject to equitable tolling and reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the same rebuttable presumption of equitable tolling applicable to suits against private defendants should also apply to suits against the United States.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1148 (quoting Irwin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, \n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 89\n\xe2\x80\x9396 (1990)). The presumption applies to the 180-day time limit for provider appeals from re- imbursement determinations, the Court of Appeals held, finding nothing in the statutory provision for PRRB review indicating that Congress intended to disallow equi- table tolling. 642 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1149\xe2\x80\x931151.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), to resolve a conflict among the Courts of Appeals over whether the 180-day time limit in \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3) constricts the Board\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction. Compare 642 F.\xc2\xa03d 1145 (case below); Western Medical Enterprises, Inc. v. Heckler, 783 F.\xc2\xa02d 1376, 1379\xe2\x80\x931380 (CA9 1986) (180-day limit is not jurisdictional and the Secretary may extend it for good cause), with Alacare Home Health Servs., Inc. v. Sullivan, 891 F.\xc2\xa02d 850, 855\xe2\x80\x93856 (CA11 1990) (statute of limitations is jurisdictional and the Secretary lacked authority to promulgate good-cause exception); St. Joseph\xe2\x80\x99s Hospital of Kansas City v. Heckler, 786 F.\xc2\xa02d 848, 852\xe2\x80\x93853 (CA8 1986) (same). Beyond the jurisdictional inquiry,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t the Secretary asked us to determine whether the Court of Appeals erred in concluding that equitable tolling applies to providers\xe2\x80\x99 Medicare reimbursement appeals to the PRRB, notwithstanding the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulation barring such appeals after three years.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCharacterizing a rule as jurisdictional renders it unique in our adversarial system. Objections to a tribunal\xe2\x80\x99s ju- risdiction can be raised at any time, even by a party that once conceded the tribunal\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction over the controversy. Tardy jurisdictional objections can therefore result in a waste of adjudicatory resources and can disturbingly disarm litigants. See Henderson v. Shinseki, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 5); Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., \n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 500, \n514 (2006)\n. With these untoward consequences in mind, \xe2\x80\x9cwe have tried in recent cases to bring some discipline to the use\xe2\x80\x9d of the term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Henderson, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 5); see also Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83, \n90 (1998)\n (jurisdiction has been a \xe2\x80\x9cword of many, too many, meanings\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo ward off profligate use of the term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d we have adopted a \xe2\x80\x9creadily administrable bright line\xe2\x80\x9d for determining whether to classify a statutory limitation as jurisdictional. Arbaugh, 546 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 516. We inquire whether Congress has \xe2\x80\x9cclearly state[d]\xe2\x80\x9d that the rule is jurisdictional; absent such a clear statement, we have cautioned, \xe2\x80\x9ccourts should treat the restriction as nonjurisdictional in character.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 515\xe2\x80\x93516; see also Gonzalez v. Thaler, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 6); Henderson, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 6). This is not to say that Congress must incant magic words in order to speak clearly. We consider \xe2\x80\x9ccontext, including this Court\xe2\x80\x99s inter- pretations of similar provisions in many years past,\xe2\x80\x9d as probative of whether Congress intended a particular provision to rank as jurisdictional. Reed Elsevier, Inc. v. Muchnick, \n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 154, \n168 (2010)\n; see also John R. Sand & Gravel Co. v. United States, \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 130\n\xe2\x80\x93134 (2008).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe reiterate what it would mean were we to type the gov- erning statute, \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3), \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Under no circumstance could providers engage PRRB re- view more than 180 days after notice of the fiscal inter- mediary\xe2\x80\x99s final determination. Not only could there be no equitable tolling. The Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulation providing for a good-cause extension, see supra, at 3, would fall as well.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe language Congress used hardly reveals a design to preclude any regulatory extension. Section 1395oo(a)(3) instructs that a provider of services \xe2\x80\x9cmay obtain a hearing\xe2\x80\x9d by the Board regarding its reimbursement amount if \xe2\x80\x9csuch provider files a request for a hearing within 180 days after notice of the intermediary\xe2\x80\x99s final determination.\xe2\x80\x9d This provision \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not speak in jurisdictional terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Zipes v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., \n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385, \n394 (1982)\n. Indeed, it is less \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d in tone than the provision we held to be nonjurisdictional in Henderson. There, the statute provided that a veteran seeking Veterans Court review of the Department of Veterans Affairs\xe2\x80\x99 determination of disability benefits \xe2\x80\x9cshall file a notice of appeal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. within 120 days.\xe2\x80\x9d 562 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9) (quoting \n\n38 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77266(a) (emphasis added)). Section 1395oo(a)(3), by contrast, contains neither the mandatory word \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d nor the appellation \xe2\x80\x9cnotice of appeal,\xe2\x80\x9d words with jurisdictional import in the context of \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107\xe2\x80\x99s limitations on the time for appeal from a district court to a court of appeals. See Bowles v. Russell, \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 205, \n214 (2007)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tKey to our decision, we have repeatedly held that filing deadlines ordinarily are not jurisdictional; indeed, we have described them as \xe2\x80\x9cquintessential claim-processing rules.\xe2\x80\x9d Henderson, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 6); see also Scarborough v. Principi, \n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 401, \n414 (2004)\n (filing deadline for fee applications under Equal Access to Justice Act); Kontrick v. Ryan, \n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 443, \n454 (2004)\n (filing deadlines for objecting to debtor\xe2\x80\x99s discharge in bank- ruptcy); Honda v. Clark, \n\n386 U.\xc2\xa0S. 484, \n498 (1967)\n (filing deadline for claims under the Trading with the Enemy Act). This case is scarcely the exceptional one in which a \xe2\x80\x9ccen- tury\xe2\x80\x99s worth of precedent and practice in American courts\xe2\x80\x9d rank a time limit as jurisdictional. Bowles, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 209, n.\xc2\xa02; cf. Kontrick, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 454 (a time limitation may be emphatic, yet not jurisdictional).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAmicus urges that the three requirements in \xc2\xa71395oo(a) are specifications that together define the limits of the PRRB\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction. Subsection (a)(1) specifies the claims providers may bring to the Board, and subsection (a)(2) sets forth an amount-in-controversy requirement. These are jurisdictional requirements, amicus asserts, so we should read the third specification, subsection (a)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s 180-day limitation, as also setting a jurisdictional requirement.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLast Term, we rejected a similar proximity-based argument. A requirement we would otherwise classify as nonjurisdictional, we held, does not become jurisdictional simply because it is placed in a section of a statute that also contains jurisdictional provisions. Gonzalez, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 11); see Weinberger v. Salfi, \n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 749\n\xe2\x80\x93764 (1975) (statutory provision at issue contained three requirements for judicial review, only one of which was jurisdictional).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAmicus also argues that the 180-day time limit for pro- vider appeals to the PRRB should be viewed as jurisdictional because Congress could have expressly made the provision nonjurisdictional, and indeed did so for other time limits in the Medicare Act. Amicus notes particularly that when Medicare beneficiaries request the Secretary to reconsider a benefits determination, the statute gives them a time limit of 180 days or \xe2\x80\x9csuch additional time as the Secretary may allow.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71395ff(b)(1)(D)(i); see also \xc2\xa71395ff(b)(1)(D)(ii) (permitting Medicare beneficiary to request a hearing by the Secretary within \xe2\x80\x9ctime limits\xe2\x80\x9d the Secretary \xe2\x80\x9cshall establish in regulations\xe2\x80\x9d). We have recognized, as a general rule, that Congress\xe2\x80\x99s use of \xe2\x80\x9ccertain language in one part of the statute and different language in another\xe2\x80\x9d can indicate that \xe2\x80\x9cdifferent meanings were intended.\xe2\x80\x9d Sosa v. Alvarez-Machain, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 692, \n711, n. \n9 (2004)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). Amicus notes this general rule in urging that an express grant of authority for the Secretary to extend the time for beneficiary appeals implies the absence of such leeway for provider appeals.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut the interpretive guide just identified, like other canons of construction, is \xe2\x80\x9cno more than [a] rul[e] of thumb\xe2\x80\x9d that can tip the scales when a statute could be read in multiple ways. Connecticut Nat. Bank v. Germain, \n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 249, \n253 (1992)\n. For the reasons earlier stated, see supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x938, we are persuaded that the time limi- tation in \xc2\xa71395oo(a) is most sensibly characterized as a nonjurisdictional prescription. The limitation therefore does not bar the modest extension contained in the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe turn now to the question whether \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s 180-day time limit for a provider to appeal to the PRRB is subject to equitable tolling.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress vested in the Secretary large rulemaking authority to administer the Medicare program. The PRRB may adopt rules and procedures only if \xe2\x80\x9cnot inconsistent\xe2\x80\x9d with the Medicare Act or \xe2\x80\x9cregulations of the Secretary.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71395oo(e). Concerning the 180-day period for an appeal to the Board from an intermediary\xe2\x80\x99s reimbursement determination, the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulation im- plementing \xc2\xa71395oo, adopted after notice and comment, speaks in no uncertain terms:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cA request for a Board hearing filed after [the 180-day time limit] shall be dismissed by the Board, except that for good cause shown, the time limit may be extended. However, no such extension shall be granted by the Board if such request is filed more than 3 years after the date the notice of the intermediary\xe2\x80\x99s determination is mailed to the provider.\xe2\x80\x9d 42 CFR \xc2\xa7405.1841(b) (2007).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Secretary allowed only a distinctly limited extension of time to appeal to the PRRB, cognizant that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Board is burdened by an immense caseload,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural rules requiring timely filings are indispens- able devices for keeping the machinery of the reimbursement appeals process running smoothly.\xe2\x80\x9d High Country Home Health, Inc. v. Thompson, 359 F.\xc2\xa03d 1307, 1310 (CA10 2004). Imposing equitable tolling to permit appeals barred by the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulation would essentially gut the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that an appeal to the Board \xe2\x80\x9cshall be dismissed\xe2\x80\x9d if filed more than 180 days after the NPR, unless the provider shows \xe2\x80\x9cgood cause\xe2\x80\x9d and requests an extension no later than three years after the NPR. A court lacks authority to undermine the regime established by the Secretary unless her regulation is \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary, capricious, or manifestly contrary to the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837, \n844 (1984)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulation, we are satisfied, survives inspection under that deferential standard. As HHS has explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is in the interest of providers and the pro- gram that, at some point, intermediary determinations and the resulting amount of program payment due the provider or the program become no longer open to correction.\xe2\x80\x9d CMS, Medicare: Provider Reimbursement Manual, pt. 1, ch. 29, \xc2\xa72930, p. 29\xe2\x80\x9373 (rev. no. 372, 2011); cf. Taylor v. Freeland & Kronz, \n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 638, \n644 (1992)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cDeadlines may lead to unwelcome results, but they prompt parties to act and produce finality.\xe2\x80\x9d). The Secretary brought to bear practical experience in superintending the huge pro- gram generally, and the PRRB in particular, in maintaining three years as the outer limit. A court must uphold the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s judgment as long as it is a permissible construction of the statute, even if it differs from how the court would have interpreted the statute in the absence of an agency regulation. National Cable & Telecommunications Assn. v. Brand X Internet Services, \n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 967, \n980 (2005)\n; see also Chevron, 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 843, n. 11.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRejecting the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s position, the Court of Appeals relied principally on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Irwin, 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 95\xe2\x80\x9396. Irwin concerned the then 30-day time period for filing suit against a federal agency under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x9316(c) (1988 ed.). We held in Irwin that \xe2\x80\x9cthe same rebut- table presumption of equitable tolling applicable to suits against private defendants should also apply to suits against the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 95\xe2\x80\x9396. Irwin itself, and equitable-tolling cases we have considered both pre- and post-Irwin, have generally involved time limits for filing suit in federal court. See, e.g., Holland v. Flor- ida, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2010) (one-year limitation for filing application for writ of habeas corpus); Rotella v. Wood, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549 (2000)\n (four-year period for filing civil RICO suit); United States v. Beggerly, \n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 38 (1998)\n (12-year period to bring suit under Quiet Title Act); Lampf, Pleva, Lipkind, Prupis & Petigrow v. Gilbertson, \n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 350 (1991)\n (one- and three-year periods for commencing civil action under \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934); Honda v. Clark, \n\n386 U.\xc2\xa0S. 484 (1967)\n (60-day period for filing suit under Trading with the Enemy Act); Kendall v. United States, \n\n107 U.\xc2\xa0S. 123 (1883)\n (six-year period for filing suit in Court of Claims). Courts in those cases rendered in the first instance the decision whether equity required tolling.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case is of a different order. We have never applied the Irwin presumption to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s internal appeal dead- line, here the time a provider has to appeal an inter- mediary\xe2\x80\x99s reimbursement determination to the PRRB. Cf. United States v. Brockamp, \n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 347, \n350 (1997)\n (assuming, arguendo, that Irwin presumption applied to time limit for filing an administrative claim for a tax re- fund, but concluding based on statutory text, structure, and purpose that there was \xe2\x80\x9cgood reason to believe that Congress did not want the equitable tolling doctrine to apply\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe presumption of equitable tolling was adopted in part on the premise that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]uch a principle is likely to be a realistic assessment of legislative intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Irwin, 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 95. But that premise is inapt in the context of providers\xe2\x80\x99 administrative appeals under the Medicare Act. The Act, until 1972, provided no avenue for providers to obtain administrative or judicial review. When Congress first directed the Secretary to establish the PRRB, Congress simultaneously imposed the 180-day deadline, with no statutory exceptions. For nearly 40 years the Secre- tary has prohibited the Board from extending that deadline, except as provided by regulation. And until the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in this case, no court had ever read equitable tolling into \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3) or the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s implementation of that provision. Congress amended \xc2\xa71395oo six times since 1974, each time leaving untouched the 180-day administrative appeal provision and the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s rulemaking authority. At no time did Congress express disapproval of the three-year outer time limit set by the Secretary for an extension upon a showing of good cause. See Commodity Futures Trading Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Schor, \n\n478 U.\xc2\xa0S. 833, \n846 (1986)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen Congress revisits a statute giving rise to a longstanding administrative interpretation without pertinent change, the congressional failure to revise or repeal the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is persuasive evidence that the interpretation is the one in- tended by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe note, furthermore, that unlike the remedial statutes at issue in many of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s equitable-tolling decisions, see Irwin, 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 91; Bowen v. City of New York, \n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 467, \n480 (1986)\n; Zipes, 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 398, the statu- tory scheme before us is not designed to be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unusually protective\xe2\x80\x99 of claimants.\xe2\x80\x9d Bowen, 476 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 480. Nor is it one \xe2\x80\x9cin which laymen, unassisted by trained lawyers, initiate the process.\xe2\x80\x9d Zipes, 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 397 (internal quotation marks omitted). The Medicare payment system in question applies to \xe2\x80\x9csophisticated\xe2\x80\x9d institutional pro- viders assisted by legal counsel, and \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally capable of identifying an underpayment in [their] own NPR within the 180-day time period specified in \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3).\xe2\x80\x9d Your Home Visiting Nurse Services, Inc. v. Shalala, \n\n525 U.\xc2\xa0S. 449, \n456 (1999)\n. As repeat players who elect to participate in the Medicare system, providers can hardly claim lack of notice of the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulations.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe hospitals ultimately argue that the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulations fail to adhere to the \xe2\x80\x9cfundamentals of fair play.\xe2\x80\x9d FCC v. Pottsville Broadcasting Co., \n\n309 U.\xc2\xa0S. 134, \n143 (1940)\n. They point, particularly, to 42 CFR \xc2\xa7405.1885(b)(3) (2012), which permits reopening of an intermediary\xe2\x80\x99s reimbursement determination \xe2\x80\x9cat any time if it is established that such determination .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was procured by fraud or similar fault of any party to the determination.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe considered a similar alleged inequity in Your Home and explained that it was justified by the \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative realities\xe2\x80\x9d of the provider reimbursement appeal system. 525 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 455. There are only a few dozen fiscal intermediaries and they are charged with issuing tens of thousands of NPRs, while each provider can concentrate on a single NPR, its own. Id., at 456. The Secretary, Your Home concluded, could reasonably believe that this asymmetry justifies giving the intermediaries more time to discover overpayments than the providers have to discover underpayments. Moreover, the fraud exception allowing indefinite reopening does apply to an intermediary if it \xe2\x80\x9cprocured\xe2\x80\x9d a Board decision by \xe2\x80\x9cfraud or similar fault.\xe2\x80\x9d Although an intermediary is not a party to its own determination, it does rank as a party in proceedings before the Board. 42 CFR \xc2\xa7405.1843(a).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe hold, in sum, that the 180-day statutory deadline for administrative appeals to the PRRB, contained in \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3), is not \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Therefore the Secretary lawfully exercised her rulemaking authority in providing for a three-year \xe2\x80\x9cgood cause\xe2\x80\x9d extension. We further hold that the equitable tolling presumption our Irwin decision approved for suits brought in court does not similarly apply to administrative appeals of the kind here considered, and that the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulation, 42 CFR \xc2\xa7405.1841(b), is a permissible interpretation of the statute.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit is therefore reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Sotomayor, concurring.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court holds that the presumption in favor of equitable tolling that we adopted in Irwin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, \n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 89\n\xe2\x80\x9396 (1990), does not apply to \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71395oo(a)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s nonjurisdictional 180-day deadline for health care providers to file administrative appeals with the Provider Reimbursement Review Board (PRRB), and that the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulation limiting \xe2\x80\x9cgood cause\xe2\x80\x9d extensions of that deadline to three years is a permissible interpretation of the statute. I agree with those holdings and join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in full. I write separately to note that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in this case does not establish that equitable tolling principles are irrelevant to internal administrative deadlines in all, or even most, contexts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court is correct that our equitable tolling cases have typically involved deadlines to bring suit in federal court. Ante, at 12. But we have never suggested that the presumption in favor of equitable tolling is generally inapplicable to administrative deadlines. Cf. Henderson v. Shinseki, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, n. 4 (2011) (slip op., at 12, n.\xc2\xa04) (noting that the Government did not dispute whether the statutory filing deadline in the Article I Veterans Court was subject to equitable tolling if the deadline was nonjurisdictional); United States v. Brockamp, \n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 347\n\xe2\x80\x93353 (1997) (assuming without deciding that the Irwin presumption applied to administrative tax refund claims but finding based on statutory text, structure, and the underlying subject matter that tolling was unavail- able); see also ante, at 12 (discussing Brockamp). And we have previously applied the Irwin presumption outside the context of filing deadlines in Article III courts. See Young v. United States, \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 43\n\xe2\x80\x9353 (2002) (applying the presumption to a limitations period in bankruptcy proceedings); cf. Zipes v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., \n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385\n\xe2\x80\x93398 (1982) (holding that the statutory time limit for filing charges with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission was \xe2\x80\x9cnot a jurisdictional prerequisite to suit in federal court, but a requirement that, like a statute of limitations, is subject to waiver, estoppel, and equitable tolling\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn administrative settings other than the one presented here, I believe the \xe2\x80\x9cbackground principle\xe2\x80\x9d that limita- tions periods \xe2\x80\x9care customarily subject to equitable tolling,\xe2\x80\x9d Young, 535 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 49\xe2\x80\x9350 (internal quotation marks omitted), may limit an agency\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to make filing deadlines absolute. The Court quite properly observes that the question whether equitable tolling is available turns on congressional intent. See ante, at 13. \xe2\x80\x9c[A] realistic assessment\xe2\x80\x9d of that intent, Irwin, 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 95, may vary by context.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, given the nature of the statutory scheme, which \xe2\x80\x9capplies to \xe2\x80\x98sophisticated\xe2\x80\x99 institutional providers\xe2\x80\x9d who are \xe2\x80\x9crepeat players\xe2\x80\x9d in the Medicare system, and the statute\xe2\x80\x99s history, I agree that it would distort congres- sional intent to presume that the PRRB\xe2\x80\x99s administrative deadline should be subject to equitable tolling. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9314 (quoting Your Home Visiting Nurse Services, Inc. v. Shalala, \n\n525 U.\xc2\xa0S. 449, \n456 (1999)\n). By contrast, with respect to remedial statutes designed to protect the rights of unsophisticated claimants, see ante, at 13, agencies (and reviewing courts) may best honor congressional intent by presuming that statutory deadlines for admin- istrative appeals are subject to equitable tolling, just as courts presume comparable judicial deadlines under such statutes may be tolled. Because claimants must generally pursue administrative relief before seeking judicial review, see\xc2\xa0Woodford v. Ngo, \n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 81\n\xe2\x80\x9391 (2006), a contrary approach could have odd practical consequences and would attribute a strange intent to Congress: to protect a claimant\xe2\x80\x99s ability to seek judicial review of an agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision by making equitable tolling available, while leaving to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s discretion whether the same claimant may invoke equitable tolling in order to seek an administrative remedy in the first place.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven in cases where the governing statute clearly delegates to an agency the discretion to adopt rules that limit the scope of equitable exceptions to administrative deadlines, I believe \xe2\x80\x9ccases may arise where the equities in favor of tolling the limitations period are \xe2\x80\x98so great that deference to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s judgment is inappropriate.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bowen v. City of New York, \n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 467, \n480 (1986)\n (quoting Mathews v. Eldridge, \n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319, \n330 (1976)\n). In particular, efforts by an agency to enforce tight filing deadlines in cases where there are credible allegations that filing delay was due to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s own misfea- sance may not survive deferential review. While equitable tolling extends to circumstances outside both parties\xe2\x80\x99 control, the related doctrines of equitable estoppel and fraudulent concealment may bar a defendant from enforcing a statute of limitation when its own deception prevented a reasonably diligent plaintiff from bringing a timely claim. See United States v. Beggerly, \n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 38\n\xe2\x80\x9350 (1998) (Stevens, J., concurring) (noting that these doctrines are distinct); see generally 2 C. Corman, Limitation of Actions \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa79.1, 9.7 (1991) (describing the doctrines). In Bowen, we applied the basic principle underlying these doctrines to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, as we concluded that a 60-day deadline to seek judicial review of the administrative denial of disability benefits should be tolled because the Social Security Administration\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98secretive conduct pre- vent[ed] plaintiffs from knowing of a violation of rights.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 476 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 481 (quoting New York v. Heckler, 742 F.\xc2\xa02d 729, 738 (CA2 1984)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile the providers in this case allege that the agency\xe2\x80\x99s failure to disclose information about how it calculated the Supplemental Security Income fraction prevented them from bringing timely challenges to reimbursement de- terminations, I am satisfied that the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s 3-year good-cause exception is a reasonable accommodation of the competing interests in administrative efficiency and fairness. We would face a different case if the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s regulation did not recognize an exception for good cause or defined good cause so narrowly as to exclude cases of fraudulent concealment and equitable estoppel. See\xc2\xa0ante, at 3, n.\xc2\xa02 (explaining that the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s amended regulation limiting the scope of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98good cause,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 73 Fed. Reg. 30250 (2008) (codified in 42 CFR \xc2\xa7405.1836(b) (2012)), is not before us).\n\t\t\t
