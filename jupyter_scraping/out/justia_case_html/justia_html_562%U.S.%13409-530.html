\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

NATIONAL AERONAUTICS AND SPACE ADMINISTRATION et\xc2\xa0al. v. NELSON et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x93530.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 5, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 19, 2011

\n

The National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) has a workforce of both federal civil servants and Government contract employees. Respondents are contract employees at NASA\xe2\x80\x99s Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), which is operated by the California Institute of Technology (Cal Tech). Respondents were not subject to Government background checks at the time they were hired, but that changed when the President ordered the adoption of uniform identification standards for both federal civil servants and contractor employees. The Department of Commerce mandated that contract employees with long-term access to federal facilities complete a standard background check, typically the National Agency Check with Inquiries (NACI), by October 2007. NASA modified its contract with Cal Tech to reflect the new requirement, and JPL announced that employees who did not complete the NACI process in time would be denied access to JPL and face termination by Cal Tech.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The NACI process, long used for prospective civil servants, begins with the employee filling out a standard form (here, Standard Form 85, the Questionnaire for Non-Sensitive Positions (SF\xe2\x80\x9385)). SF\xe2\x80\x9385 asks whether an employee has \xe2\x80\x9cused, possessed, supplied, or manufactured illegal drugs\xe2\x80\x9d in the last year. If so, the employee must provide details, including information about \xe2\x80\x9ctreatment or counseling received.\xe2\x80\x9d The employee must also sign a release authorizing the Government to obtain personal information from schools, employers, and others during its investigation. Once SF\xe2\x80\x9385 is completed, the Government sends the employee\xe2\x80\x99s references a questionnaire (Form 42) that asks open-ended questions about whether they have \xe2\x80\x9cany reason to question\xe2\x80\x9d the employee\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9chonesty or trustworthiness,\xe2\x80\x9d or have \xe2\x80\x9cadverse information\xe2\x80\x9d concerning a variety of other matters. All SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42 responses are subject to the protections of the Privacy Act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With the deadline for completing the NACI process drawing near, respondents brought suit, claiming, as relevant here, that the background-check process violates a constitutional right to informational privacy. The District Court declined to issue a preliminary injunction, but the Ninth Circuit reversed. It held that SF\xe2\x80\x9385\xe2\x80\x99s inquiries into recent drug involvement furthered the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in combating illegal-drug use, but that the drug \xe2\x80\x9ctreatment or counseling\xe2\x80\x9d question furthered no legitimate interest and was thus likely to be held unconstitutional. It also held that Form 42\xe2\x80\x99s open-ended questions were not narrowly tailored to meet the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interests in verifying contractors\xe2\x80\x99 identities and ensuring JPL\xe2\x80\x99s security, and thus also likely violated respondents\xe2\x80\x99 informational-privacy rights.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0In two cases decided over 30 years ago, this Court referred broadly to a constitutional privacy \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in avoiding disclosure of personal matters.\xe2\x80\x9d Whalen v. Roe, 429 U. S. 589, 599\xe2\x80\x93600; Nixon v. Administrator of General Services, 433 U. S. 425, 457. In Whalen, the Court upheld a New York law permitting the collection of names and addresses of persons prescribed dangerous drugs, finding that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csecurity provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d which protected against \xe2\x80\x9cpublic disclosure\xe2\x80\x9d of patient information, 462 U. S., at 600\xe2\x80\x93601, were sufficient to protect a privacy interest \xe2\x80\x9carguably . . . root[ed] in the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 605. In Nixon, the Court upheld a law requiring the former President to turn over his presidential papers and tape recordings for archival review and screening, concluding that the federal Act at issue, like the statute in Whalen, had protections against \xe2\x80\x9cundue dissemination of private materials.\xe2\x80\x9d 433 U. S. at 458. Since Nixon, the Court has said little else on the subject of a constitutional right to informational privacy. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Assuming, without deciding, that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s challenged inquiries implicate a privacy interest of constitutional significance, that interest, whatever its scope, does not prevent the Government from asking reasonable questions of the sort included on SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42 in an employment background investigation that is subject to the Privacy Act\xe2\x80\x99s safeguards against public disclosure. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9324.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The forms are reasonable in light of the Government interests at stake. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0Judicial review of the forms must take into account the context in which the Government\xe2\x80\x99s challenged inquiries arise. When the Government acts in its capacity \xe2\x80\x9cas proprietor\xe2\x80\x9d and manager of its \xe2\x80\x9cinternal operation,\xe2\x80\x9d Cafeteria & Restaurant Workers v. McElroy, 367 U. S. 886, 896, it has a much freer hand than when it regulates as to citizens generally. The questions respondents challenge are part of a standard background check of the sort used by millions of private employers. The Government has been conducting employment investigations since the Republic\xe2\x80\x99s earliest days, and the President has had statutory authority to assess an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s fitness for the civil service since 1871. Standard background investigations similar to those at issue became mandatory for federal civil-service candidates in 1953, and the investigations challenged here arose from a decision to extend that requirement to federal contract employees. This history shows that the Government has an interest in conducting basic background checks in order to ensure the security of its facilities and to employ a competent, reliable workforce to carry out the people\xe2\x80\x99s business. The interest is not diminished by the fact that respondents are contract employees. There are no meaningful distinctions in the duties of NASA\xe2\x80\x99s civil-service and contractor employees, especially at JPL, where contract employees do work that is critical to NASA\xe2\x80\x99s mission and that is funded with a multibillion dollar taxpayer investment. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0The challenged questions on SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42 are reasonable, employment-related inquiries that further the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interests in managing its internal operations. SF\xe2\x80\x9385\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctreatment or counseling\xe2\x80\x9d question is a followup question to a reasonable inquiry about illegal-drug use. In context, the drug-treatment inquiry is also a reasonable, employment-related inquiry. The Government, recognizing that illegal-drug use is both a criminal and medical issue, seeks to separate out those drug users who are taking steps to address and overcome their problems. Thus, it uses responses to the drug-treatment question as a mitigating factor in its contractor credentialing decisions. The Court rejects the argument that the Government has a constitutional burden to demonstrate that its employment background questions are \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d or the least restrictive means of furthering its interests. So exacting a standard runs directly contrary to Whalen. See 429 U. S., at 596\xe2\x80\x93597. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(3)\xc2\xa0Like SF\xe2\x80\x9385\xe2\x80\x99s drug-treatment question, Form 42\xe2\x80\x99s open-ended questions are reasonably aimed at identifying capable employees who will faithfully conduct the Government\xe2\x80\x99s business. Asking an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s designated references broad questions about job suitability is an appropriate tool for separating strong candidates from weak ones. The reasonableness of such questions is illustrated by their pervasiveness in the public and private sectors. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0In addition to being reasonable in light of the Government interests at stake, SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42 are also subject to substantial protections against disclosure to the public. Whalen and Nixon recognized that a \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory or regulatory duty to avoid unwarranted disclosures\xe2\x80\x9d generally allays privacy concerns created by government \xe2\x80\x9caccumulation\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal information\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cpublic purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Whalen, supra, at 605. Respondents attack only the Government\xe2\x80\x99s collection of information, and here, as in Whalen and Nixon, the information collected is shielded by statute from unwarranted disclosure. The Privacy Act\xe2\x80\x94which allows the Government to maintain only those records \xe2\x80\x9crelevant and necessary to accomplish\xe2\x80\x9d a purpose authorized by law, 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7552a(e)(1); requires written consent before the Government may disclose an individual\xe2\x80\x99s records, \xc2\xa7552a(b); and imposes criminal liability for willful violations of its nondisclosure obligations, \xc2\xa7552a(i)(1)\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cevidence[s] a proper concern\xe2\x80\x9d for individual privacy. Whalen, supra, at 605; Nixon, supra, at 458\xe2\x80\x93459. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim that the statutory exceptions to the Privacy Act\xe2\x80\x99s disclosure bar, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7552a(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(12), leave its protections too porous to supply a meaningful check against unwarranted disclosures. But that argument rests on an incorrect reading of Whalen, Nixon, and the Privacy Act. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

530 F.\xc2\xa03d 865, reversed and remanded.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In two cases decided more than 30 years ago, this Court referred broadly to a constitutional privacy \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in avoiding disclosure of personal matters.\xe2\x80\x9d Whalen v. Roe, 429 U. S. 589, 599\xe2\x80\x93600 (1977); Nixon v. Administrator of General Services, 433 U. S. 425, 457 (1977). Respondents in this case, federal contract employees at a Government laboratory, claim that two parts of a standard employment background investigation violate their rights under Whalen and Nixon. Respondents challenge a section of a form questionnaire that asks employees about treatment or counseling for recent illegal-drug use. They also object to certain open-ended questions on a form sent to employees\xe2\x80\x99 designated references.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We assume, without deciding, that the Constitution protects a privacy right of the sort mentioned in Whalen and Nixon. We hold, however, that the challenged portions of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s background check do not violate this right in the present case. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s interests as employer and proprietor in managing its internal operations, combined with the protections against public dissemination provided by the Privacy Act of 1974, 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7552a, satisfy any \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in avoiding disclosure\xe2\x80\x9d that may \xe2\x80\x9carguably ha[ve] its roots in the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Whalen, supra, at 599, 605.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) is an independent federal agency charged with planning and conducting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cspace activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Pub. L. 111\xe2\x80\x93314, \xc2\xa73, 124 Stat. 3333, 51 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa720112(a)(1). NASA\xe2\x80\x99s workforce numbers in the tens of thousands of employees. While many of these workers are federal civil servants, a substantial majority are employed directly by Government contractors. Contract employees play an important role in NASA\xe2\x80\x99s mission, and their duties are functionally equivalent to those performed by civil servants.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One NASA facility, the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) in Pasadena, California, is staffed exclusively by contract employees. NASA owns JPL, but the California Institute of Technology (Cal Tech) operates the facility under a Government contract. JPL is the lead NASA center for deep-space robotics and communications. Most of this country\xe2\x80\x99s unmanned space missions\xe2\x80\x94from the Explorer 1 satellite in 1958 to the Mars Rovers of today\xe2\x80\x94have been developed and run by JPL. JPL scientists contribute to NASA earth-observation and technology-development projects. Many JPL employees also engage in pure scientific research on topics like \xe2\x80\x9cthe star formation history of the universe\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe fundamental properties of quantum fluids.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 64\xe2\x80\x9365, 68.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Twenty-eight JPL employees are respondents here. Many of them have worked at the lab for decades, and none has ever been the subject of a Government background investigation. At the time when respondents were hired, background checks were standard only for federal civil servants. See Exec. Order No. 10450, 3 CFR 936 (1949\xe2\x80\x931953 Comp.). In some instances, individual contracts required background checks for the employees of federal contractors, but no blanket policy was in place.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government has recently taken steps to eliminate this two-track approach to background investigations. In\xc2\xa02004, a recommendation by the 9/11 Commission prompted the President to order new, uniform identification standards for \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ederal employees,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9ccontractor employees.\xe2\x80\x9d Homeland Security Presidential Directive/HSPD\xe2\x80\x9312\xe2\x80\x94Policy for a Common Identification Standard for Federal Employees and Contractors, Public Papers of the President, George W. Bush, Vol. 2, Aug. 27, p.\xc2\xa01765 (2007) (hereinafter HSPD\xe2\x80\x9312), App. 127. The Department of Commerce implemented this directive by mandating that contract employees with long-term access to federal facilities complete a standard background check, typically the National Agency Check with Inquiries (NACI). National Inst. of Standards and Technology, Personal Identity Verification of Federal Employees & Contractors, pp. iii\xe2\x80\x93vi, 1\xe2\x80\x938, 6 (FIPS PUB 201\xe2\x80\x931, Mar. 2006) (hereinafter FIPS PUB 201\xe2\x80\x931), App. 131\xe2\x80\x93150,\n144\xe2\x80\x93145.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An October 2007 deadline was set for completion of these investigations. Memorandum from Joshua B. Bolten, Director, OMB, to the Heads of all Departments and Agencies (Aug. 5, 2005), App. 112. In January 2007, NASA modified its contract with Cal Tech to reflect the new background-check requirement. JPL management informed employees that anyone failing to complete the NACI process by October 2007 would be denied access to JPL and would face termination by Cal Tech.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The NACI process has long been the standard background investigation for prospective civil servants. The process begins when the applicant or employee fills out a form questionnaire. Employees who work in \xe2\x80\x9cnon-sensitive\xe2\x80\x9d positions (as all respondents here do) complete Standard Form 85 (SF\xe2\x80\x9385). Office of Personnel Management (OPM), Standard Form 85, Questionnaire for Non-Sensitive Positions, App. 88\xe2\x80\x9395.[Footnote 2]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Most of the questions on SF\xe2\x80\x9385 seek basic biographical information: name, address, prior residences, education, employment history, and personal and professional references. The form also asks about citizenship, selective-service registration, and military service. The last question asks whether the employee has \xe2\x80\x9cused, possessed, supplied, or manufactured illegal drugs\xe2\x80\x9d in the last year. Id., at 94. If the answer is yes, the employee must provide details, including information about \xe2\x80\x9cany treatment or counseling received.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. A \xe2\x80\x9ctruthful response,\xe2\x80\x9d the form notes, cannot be used as evidence against the employee in a criminal proceeding. Ibid. The employee must certify that all responses on the form are true and must sign a release authorizing the Government to obtain personal information from schools, employers, and others during its investigation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once a completed SF\xe2\x80\x9385 is on file, the \xe2\x80\x9cagency check\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinquiries\xe2\x80\x9d begin. 75 Fed. Reg. 5359 (2010). The Government runs the information provided by the employee through FBI and other federal-agency databases. It also sends out form questionnaires to the former employers, schools, landlords, and references listed on SF\xe2\x80\x9385. The particular form at issue in this case\xe2\x80\x94the Investigative Request for Personal Information, Form 42\xe2\x80\x94goes to the employee\xe2\x80\x99s former landlords and references. Ibid.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Form 42 is a two-page document that takes about five minutes to complete. See ibid. It explains to the reference that \xe2\x80\x9c[y]our name has been provided by\xe2\x80\x9d a particular employee or applicant to help the Government determine that person\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csuitability for employment or a security clearance.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 96\xe2\x80\x9397. After several preliminary questions about the extent of the reference\xe2\x80\x99s associations with the employee, the form asks if the reference has \xe2\x80\x9cany reason to question\xe2\x80\x9d the employee\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9chonesty or trustworthiness.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 97. It also asks if the reference knows of any \xe2\x80\x9cadverse information\xe2\x80\x9d concerning the employee\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cviolations of the law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfinancial integrity,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cabuse of alcohol and/or drugs,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cmental or emotional stability,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral behavior or conduct,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cother matters.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. If \xe2\x80\x9cyes\xe2\x80\x9d is checked for any of these categories, the form calls for an explanation in the space below. That space is also available for providing \xe2\x80\x9cadditional information\xe2\x80\x9d (\xe2\x80\x9cderogatory\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cfavorable\xe2\x80\x9d) that may bear on \xe2\x80\x9csuitability for government employment or a security clearance.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0All responses to SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42 are subject to the protections of the Privacy Act. The Act authorizes the Government to keep records pertaining to an individual only when they are \xe2\x80\x9crelevant and necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to an end \xe2\x80\x9crequired to be accomplished\xe2\x80\x9d by law. 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7552a(e)(1). Individuals are permitted to access their records and request amendments to them. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7552a(d)(1),(2). Subject to certain exceptions, the Government may not disclose records pertaining to an individual without that individual\xe2\x80\x99s written consent. \xc2\xa7552a(b).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0About two months before the October 2007 deadline for completing the NACI, respondents brought this suit, claiming, as relevant here, that the background-check process violates a constitutional right to informational privacy. App. 82 (Complaint for Injunctive and Declaratory Relief).[Footnote 4] The District Court denied respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion for a preliminary injunction, but the Ninth Circuit granted an injunction pending appeal, 506 F.\xc2\xa03d 713 (2007), and later reversed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order. The court held that portions of both SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42 are likely unconstitutional and should be preliminarily enjoined. 512 F.\xc2\xa03d 1134, vacated and superseded, 530 F.\xc2\xa03d 865 (2008).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Turning first to SF\xe2\x80\x9385, the Court of Appeals noted respondents\xe2\x80\x99 concession \xe2\x80\x9cthat most of the questions\xe2\x80\x9d on the form are \xe2\x80\x9cunproblematic\xe2\x80\x9d and do not \xe2\x80\x9cimplicate the constitutional right to informational privacy.\xe2\x80\x9d 530 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 878. But the court determined that the \xe2\x80\x9cgroup of questions concerning illegal drugs\xe2\x80\x9d required closer scrutiny. Ibid. Applying Circuit precedent, the court upheld SF\xe2\x80\x9385\xe2\x80\x99s inquiries into recent involvement with drugs as \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary to further the government\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate interest\xe2\x80\x9d in combating illegal-drug use. Id., at 879. The court went on to hold, however, that the portion of the form requiring disclosure of drug \xe2\x80\x9ctreatment or counseling\xe2\x80\x9d furthered no legitimate interest and was thus likely to be held unconstitutional. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Form 42, in the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 estimation, was even \xe2\x80\x9cmore problematic.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The form\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9copen-ended and highly private\xe2\x80\x9d questions, the court concluded, were not \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowly tailored\xe2\x80\x9d to meet the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interests in verifying contractors\xe2\x80\x99 identities and \xe2\x80\x9censuring the security of the JPL.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 881, 880. As a result, the court held, these \xe2\x80\x9copen-ended\xe2\x80\x9d questions, like the drug-treatment question on SF\xe2\x80\x9385, likely violate respondents\xe2\x80\x99 informational-privacy rights.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Over the dissents of five judges, the Ninth Circuit\ndenied rehearing en banc. 568 F.\xc2\xa03d 1028 (2009). We granted certiorari. 559 U. S. ___ (2010).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As noted, respondents contend that portions of SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42 violate their \xe2\x80\x9cright to informational privacy.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 15. This Court considered a similar claim in Whalen, 429 U. S. 589, which concerned New York\xe2\x80\x99s practice of collecting \xe2\x80\x9cthe names and addresses of all persons\xe2\x80\x9d prescribed dangerous drugs with both \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate and illegitimate uses.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 591. In discussing that claim, the Court said that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he cases sometimes characterized as protecting \xe2\x80\x98privacy\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d actually involved \xe2\x80\x9cat least two different kinds of interests\xe2\x80\x9d: one, an \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in avoiding disclosure of personal matters\xe2\x80\x9d;[Footnote 6] the other, an interest in \xe2\x80\x9cmaking certain kinds of important decisions\xe2\x80\x9d free from government interference.[Footnote 7] The patients who brought suit in Whalen argued that New York\xe2\x80\x99s statute \xe2\x80\x9cthreaten[ed] to impair\xe2\x80\x9d both their \xe2\x80\x9cnondisclosure\xe2\x80\x9d interests and their interests in making healthcare decisions independently. Id., at 600. The Court, however, upheld the statute as a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable exercise of New York\xe2\x80\x99s broad police powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 598.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whalen acknowledged that the disclosure of \xe2\x80\x9cprivate information\xe2\x80\x9d to the State was an \xe2\x80\x9cunpleasant invasion of privacy,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 602, but the Court pointed out that the New York statute contained \xe2\x80\x9csecurity provisions\xe2\x80\x9d that protected against \xe2\x80\x9cpublic disclosure\xe2\x80\x9d of patients\xe2\x80\x99 information, id., at 600\xe2\x80\x93601. This sort of \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory or regulatory duty to avoid unwarranted disclosures\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9caccumulated private data\xe2\x80\x9d was sufficient, in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, to protect a privacy interest that \xe2\x80\x9carguably ha[d] its roots in the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 605\xe2\x80\x93606. The Court thus concluded that the statute did not violate \xe2\x80\x9cany right or liberty protected by the Fourteenth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 606.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Four months later, the Court referred again to a constitutional \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in avoiding disclosure.\xe2\x80\x9d Nixon, 433 U. S., at 457 (internal quotation marks omitted). Former President Nixon brought a challenge to the Presidential Recordings and Materials Preservation Act, 88 Stat. 1695, note following 44 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72111, a statute that required him to turn over his presidential papers and tape recordings for archival review and screening. 433 U. S., at 455\xe2\x80\x93465. In a section of the opinion entitled \xe2\x80\x9cPrivacy,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court addressed a combination of claims that the review required by this Act violated the former President\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cFourth and Fifth Amendmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d rights. Id., at 455, and n.\xc2\xa018, 458\xe2\x80\x93459. The Court rejected those challenges after concluding that the Act at issue, like the statute in Whalen, contained protections against \xe2\x80\x9cundue dissemination of private materials.\xe2\x80\x9d 433 U. S., at 458. Indeed, the Court observed that the former President\xe2\x80\x99s claim was \xe2\x80\x9cweaker\xe2\x80\x9d than the one \xe2\x80\x9cfound wanting . . . in Whalen,\xe2\x80\x9d as the Government was required to return immediately all \xe2\x80\x9cpurely private papers and recordings\xe2\x80\x9d identified by the archivists. Id., at 458\xe2\x80\x93459. Citing Fourth Amendment precedent, the Court also stated that the public interest in preserving presidential papers outweighed any \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate expectation of privacy\xe2\x80\x9d that the former President may have enjoyed. Id., at 458 (citing Katz v. United States, 389 U. S. 347 (1967); Camara v. Municipal Court of City and County of San Francisco, 387 U. S. 523 (1967); and Terry v. Ohio, 392 U. S. 1 (1968)).[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court announced the decision in Nixon in the waning days of October Term 1976. Since then, the Court has said little else on the subject of an \xe2\x80\x9cindividual interest in avoiding disclosure of personal matters.\xe2\x80\x9d Whalen, supra, at 599; Nixon, supra, at 457. A few opinions have mentioned the concept in passing and in other contexts. See Department of Justice v. Reporters Comm. for Freedom of Press, 489 U. S. 749, 762\xe2\x80\x93763 (1989); New York v. Ferber, 458 U. S. 747, 759, n.\xc2\xa010 (1982). But no other decision has squarely addressed a constitutional right to informational privacy.[Footnote 9]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As was our approach in Whalen, we will assume for present purposes that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s challenged inquiries implicate a privacy interest of constitutional significance. 429 U. S., at 599, 605.[Footnote 10] We hold, however, that, whatever the scope of this interest, it does not prevent the Government from asking reasonable questions of the sort included on SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42 in an employment background investigation that is subject to the Privacy Act\xe2\x80\x99s safeguards against public disclosure.

\n

A

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As an initial matter, judicial review of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s challenged inquiries must take into account the context in which they arise. When the Government asks respondents and their references to fill out SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42, it does not exercise its sovereign power \xe2\x80\x9cto regulate or license.\xe2\x80\x9d Cafeteria & Restaurant Workers v. McElroy, 367 U. S. 886, 896 (1961). Rather, the Government conducts the challenged background checks in its capacity \xe2\x80\x9cas proprietor\xe2\x80\x9d and manager of its \xe2\x80\x9cinternal operation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Time and again our cases have recognized that the Government has a much freer hand in dealing \xe2\x80\x9cwith citizen employees than it does when it brings its sovereign power to bear on citizens at large.\xe2\x80\x9d Engquist v. Oregon Dept. of Agriculture, 553 U. S. 591, 598 (2008); Waters v. Churchill, 511 U. S. 661, 674 (1994) (plurality opinion). This distinction is grounded on the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-sense realization\xe2\x80\x9d that if every \xe2\x80\x9cemployment decision became a constitutional matter,\xe2\x80\x9d the Government could not function. See Connick v. Myers, 461 U. S. 138, 143 (1983); see also Bishop v. Wood, 426 U. S. 341, 350 (1976) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Due Process Clause . . . is not a guarantee against incorrect or ill-advised personnel decisions\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An assessment of the constitutionality of the challenged portions of SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42 must account for this distinction. The questions challenged by respondents are part of a standard employment background check of the sort used by millions of private employers. See Brief for Consumer Data Indus. Assn. et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 2 (hereinafter CDIA Brief) (\xe2\x80\x9c[M]ore than 88% of U. S.\ncompanies \xe2\x80\xa6 perform background checks on their employees\xe2\x80\x9d). The Government itself has been conducting employment investigations since the earliest days of the Republic. L. White, The Federalists: A Study in Administrative History 262\xe2\x80\x93263 (1948); see OPM, Biography of An Ideal: History of the Federal Civil Service 8 (2002) (noting that President Washington \xe2\x80\x9cset a high standard\xe2\x80\x9d for federal office and finalized appointments only after \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigating [candidates\xe2\x80\x99] capabilities and reputations\xe2\x80\x9d). Since 1871, the President has enjoyed statutory authority to \xe2\x80\x9cascertain the fitness of applicants\xe2\x80\x9d for the civil service \xe2\x80\x9cas to age, health, character, knowledge and ability for the employment sought,\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Mar. 3, 1871, Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71753, as amended, 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73301(2), and that Act appears to have been regarded as a codification of established practice.[Footnote 11] Standard background investigations similar to those at issue here became mandatory for all candidates for the federal civil service in 1953. Exec. Order No.\xc2\xa010450, 3 CFR 936. And the particular investigations challenged in this case arose from a decision to extend that requirement to federal contract employees requiring long-term access to federal facilities. See HSPD\xe2\x80\x9312, at 1765, App. 127; FIPS PUB 201\xe2\x80\x931, at iii\xe2\x80\x93vi, 1\xe2\x80\x938, App. 131\xe2\x80\x93150.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As this long history suggests, the Government has an interest in conducting basic employment background checks. Reasonable investigations of applicants and employees aid the Government in ensuring the security of its facilities and in employing a competent, reliable workforce. See Engquist, supra, at 598\xe2\x80\x93599. Courts must keep those interests in mind when asked to go line-by-line through the Government\xe2\x80\x99s employment forms and to scrutinize the choice and wording of the questions they contain.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents argue that, because they are contract employees and not civil servants, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s broad authority in managing its affairs should apply with diminished force. But the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest as \xe2\x80\x9cproprietor\xe2\x80\x9d in managing its operations, Cafeteria & Restaurant Workers, supra, at 896, does not turn on such formalities. See Board of Comm\xe2\x80\x99rs, Wabaunsee Cty. v. Umbehr, 518 U. S. 668, 678, 679 (1996) (formal distinctions such as whether a \xe2\x80\x9cservice provider\xe2\x80\x9d has a \xe2\x80\x9ccontract of employment or a contract for services\xe2\x80\x9d with the government is a \xe2\x80\x9cvery poor proxy\xe2\x80\x9d for constitutional interests at stake). The fact that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 direct employment relationship is with Cal Tech\xe2\x80\x94which operates JPL under a Government contract\xe2\x80\x94says very little about the interests at stake in this case. The record shows that, as a \xe2\x80\x9cpractical matter,\xe2\x80\x9d there are no \xe2\x80\x9c[r]elevant distinctions\xe2\x80\x9d between the duties performed by NASA\xe2\x80\x99s civil-service workforce and its contractor workforce. App. 221. The two classes of employees perform \xe2\x80\x9cfunctionally equivalent duties,\xe2\x80\x9d and the extent of employees\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9caccess to NASA \xe2\x80\xa6 facilities\xe2\x80\x9d turns not on formal status but on the nature of \xe2\x80\x9cthe jobs they perform.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At JPL, in particular, the work that contract employees perform is critical to NASA\xe2\x80\x99s mission. Respondents in this case include \xe2\x80\x9cthe lead trouble-shooter for \xe2\x80\xa6 th[e] $568 [million]\xe2\x80\x9d Kepler space observatory, 7 Record 396; the leader of the program that \xe2\x80\x9ctests \xe2\x80\xa6 all new technology that NASA will use in space,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 60; and one of the lead \xe2\x80\x9ctrajectory designers for \xe2\x80\xa6 the Galileo Project and the Apollo Moon landings,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 62. This is important work, and all of it is funded with a multibillion dollar investment from the American taxpayer. See NASA, Jet Propulsion Laboratory Annual Report 09, p.\xc2\xa035 (2010), online at http://www.jpl.nasa.gov/annualreport/2009-report.pdf. The\nGovernment has a strong interest in conducting basic background checks into the contract employees minding the store at JPL.[Footnote 12]

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With these interests in view, we conclude that the challenged portions of both SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42 consist of reasonable, employment-related inquiries that further the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interests in managing its internal operations. See Engquist, 553 U. S., at 598\xe2\x80\x93599; Whalen, 429 U. S., at 597\xe2\x80\x93598. As to SF\xe2\x80\x9385, the only part of the form challenged here is its request for information about \xe2\x80\x9cany treatment or counseling received\xe2\x80\x9d for illegal-drug use within the previous year. The \xe2\x80\x9ctreatment or counseling\xe2\x80\x9d question, however, must be considered in context. It is a followup to SF\xe2\x80\x9385\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry into whether the employee has \xe2\x80\x9cused, possessed, supplied, or manufactured illegal drugs\xe2\x80\x9d during the past year. The Government has good reason to ask employees about their recent illegal-drug use. Like any employer, the Government is entitled to have its projects staffed by reliable, law-abiding persons who will \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98efficiently and effectively\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d discharge their duties. See Engquist, supra, at 598\xe2\x80\x93599. Questions about illegal-drug use are a useful way of figuring out which persons have these characteristics. See, e.g., Breen & Matusitz, An Updated Examination of the Effects of Illegal Drug Use in the Workplace, 19 J. Human Behavior in the Social Environment, 434 (2009) (illicit drug use negatively correlated with workplace productivity).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In context, the follow-up question on \xe2\x80\x9ctreatment or counseling\xe2\x80\x9d for recent illegal-drug use is also a reasonable, employment-related inquiry. The Government, recognizing that illegal-drug use is both a criminal and a medical issue, seeks to separate out those illegal-drug users who are taking steps to address and overcome their problems. The Government thus uses responses to the \xe2\x80\x9ctreatment or counseling\xe2\x80\x9d question as a mitigating factor in determining whether to grant contract employees long-term access to federal facilities.[Footnote 13]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is a reasonable, and indeed a humane, approach, and respondents do not dispute the legitimacy of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s decision to use drug treatment as a mitigating factor in its contractor credentialing decisions. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 argument is that, if drug treatment is only used to mitigate, then the Government should change the mandatory phrasing of SF\xe2\x80\x9385\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cInclude [in your answer] any treatment or counseling received\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94so as to make a response optional. App. 94. As it stands, the mandatory \xe2\x80\x9ctreatment or counseling\xe2\x80\x9d question is unconstitutional, in respondents\xe2\x80\x99 view, because it is \xe2\x80\x9cmore intrusive than necessary to satisfy the government\xe2\x80\x99s objective.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 26; 530 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 879 (holding that \xe2\x80\x9ctreatment or counseling\xe2\x80\x9d question should be enjoined because the form \xe2\x80\x9cappears to compel disclosure\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We reject the argument that the Government, when it requests job-related personal information in an employment background check, has a constitutional burden to demonstrate that its questions are \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d or the least restrictive means of furthering its interests. So exacting a standard runs directly contrary to Whalen. The patients in Whalen, much like respondents here, argued that New York\xe2\x80\x99s statute was unconstitutional because the State could not \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrate the necessity\xe2\x80\x9d of its program. 429 U. S., at 596. The Court quickly rejected that argument, concluding that New York\xe2\x80\x99s collection of patients\xe2\x80\x99 prescription information could \xe2\x80\x9cnot be held unconstitutional simply because\xe2\x80\x9d a court viewed it as \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary, in whole or in part.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 596\xe2\x80\x93597.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That analysis applies with even greater force where the Government acts, not as a regulator, but as the manager of its internal affairs. See Engquist, supra, at 598\xe2\x80\x93599. SF\xe2\x80\x9385\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctreatment or counseling\xe2\x80\x9d question reasonably seeks to identify a subset of acknowledged drug users who are attempting to overcome their problems. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s considered position is that phrasing the question in more permissive terms would result in a lower response rate, and the question\xe2\x80\x99s effectiveness in identifying illegal-drug users who are suitable for employment would be \xe2\x80\x9cmaterially reduced.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioners 19. That is a reasonable position, falling within the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98wide latitude\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d granted the Government in its dealings with employees. See Engquist, supra, at 600.

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals also held that the broad, \xe2\x80\x9copen-ended questions\xe2\x80\x9d on Form 42 likely violate respondents\xe2\x80\x99 informational-privacy rights. Form 42 asks applicants\xe2\x80\x99 designated references and landlords for \xe2\x80\x9cinformation\xe2\x80\x9d bearing on \xe2\x80\x9csuitability for government employment or a security clearance.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 97. In a series of questions, the Government asks if the reference has any \xe2\x80\x9cadverse information\xe2\x80\x9d about the applicant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9chonesty or trustworthiness,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cviolations of the law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfinancial integrity,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cabuse of alcohol and/or drugs,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cmental or emotional stability,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral behavior or conduct,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cother matters.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These open-ended inquiries, like the drug-treatment question on SF\xe2\x80\x9385, are reasonably aimed at identifying capable employees who will faithfully conduct the Government\xe2\x80\x99s business. See Engquist, supra, at 598\xe2\x80\x93599. Asking an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s designated references broad, open-ended questions about job suitability is an appropriate tool for separating strong candidates from weak ones. It would be a truly daunting task to catalog all the reasons why a person might not be suitable for a particular job, and references do not have all day to answer a laundry list of specific questions. See CDIA Brief 6\xe2\x80\x937 (references \xe2\x80\x9ctypically have limited time to answer questions from potential employers,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9copen-ended questions\xe2\x80\x9d yield more relevant information than narrow inquiries). Form 42, by contrast, takes just five minutes to complete. 75 Fed. Reg. 5359.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The reasonableness of such open-ended questions is illustrated by their pervasiveness in the public and private sectors. Form 42 alone is sent out by the Government over 1.8 million times annually. Ibid. In addition, the use of open-ended questions in employment background checks appears to be equally commonplace in the private sector. See, e.g., S. Bock et\xc2\xa0al., Mandated Benefits 2008 Compliance Guide, Exh. 20.1, A Sample Policy on Reference Checks on Job Applicants (\xe2\x80\x9cFollowing are the guidelines for conducting a telephone reference check: \xe2\x80\xa6 Ask open-ended questions, then wait for the respondent to answer\xe2\x80\x9d); M. Zweig, Human Resources Management 87 (1991) (\xe2\x80\x9cAlso ask, \xe2\x80\x98Is there anything else I need to know about [candidate\xe2\x80\x99s name]?\xe2\x80\x99 This kind of open-ended question may turn up all kinds of information you wouldn\xe2\x80\x99t have gotten any other way\xe2\x80\x9d). The use of similar open-ended questions by the Government is reasonable and furthers its interests in managing its operations.

\n

B

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Not only are SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42 reasonable in light of the Government interests at stake, they are also subject to substantial protections against disclosure to the public. Both Whalen and Nixon recognized that government \xe2\x80\x9caccumulation\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal information\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cpublic purposes\xe2\x80\x9d may pose a threat to privacy. Whalen, 429 U. S., at 605; see Nixon 433 U. S., at 457\xe2\x80\x93458, 462. But both decisions also stated that a \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory or regulatory duty to avoid unwarranted disclosures\xe2\x80\x9d generally allays these privacy concerns. Whalen, supra, at 605; Nixon, supra, at 458\xe2\x80\x93459. The Court in Whalen, relying on New York\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csecurity provisions\xe2\x80\x9d prohibiting public disclosure, turned aside a challenge to the collection of patients\xe2\x80\x99 prescription information. 429 U. S., at 594, and n.\xc2\xa012, 600\xe2\x80\x93601, 605. In Nixon, the Court rejected what it regarded as an even \xe2\x80\x9cweaker\xe2\x80\x9d claim by the former President because the Presidential Recordings and Materials Preservation Act \xe2\x80\x9c[n]ot only . . . mandate[d] regulations\xe2\x80\x9d against \xe2\x80\x9cundue dissemination,\xe2\x80\x9d but also required immediate return of any \xe2\x80\x9cpurely private\xe2\x80\x9d materials flagged by the Government\xe2\x80\x99s archivists. 433 U. S., at 458\xe2\x80\x93459.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents in this case, like the patients in Whalen and former President Nixon, attack only the Government\xe2\x80\x99s collection of information on SF\xe2\x80\x9385 and Form 42. And here, no less than in Whalen and Nixon, the information collected is shielded by statute from \xe2\x80\x9cunwarranted disclosur[e].\xe2\x80\x9d See Whalen, supra, at 605. The Privacy Act, which covers all information collected during the background-check process, allows the Government to maintain records \xe2\x80\x9cabout an individual\xe2\x80\x9d only to the extent the records are \xe2\x80\x9crelevant and necessary to accomplish\xe2\x80\x9d a purpose authorized by law. 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7552a(e)(1). The Act requires written consent before the Government may disclose records pertaining to any individual. \xc2\xa7552a(b). And the Act imposes criminal liability for willful violations of its nondisclosure obligations. \xc2\xa7552a(i)(1). These requirements, as we have noted, give \xe2\x80\x9cforceful recognition\xe2\x80\x9d to a Government employee\xe2\x80\x99s interest in maintaining the \xe2\x80\x9cconfidentiality of sensitive information . . . in his personnel files.\xe2\x80\x9d Detroit Edison Co. v. NLRB, 440 U. S. 301, 318, n.\xc2\xa016 (1979). Like the protections against disclosure in Whalen and Nixon, they \xe2\x80\x9cevidence a proper concern\xe2\x80\x9d for individual privacy. Whalen, supra, at 605; Nixon, supra, at 458\xe2\x80\x93459.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notwithstanding these safeguards, respondents argue that statutory exceptions to the Privacy Act\xe2\x80\x99s disclosure bar, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7552a(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(12), leave its protections too porous to supply a meaningful check against \xe2\x80\x9cunwarranted disclosures,\xe2\x80\x9d Whalen, supra, at 605. Respondents point in particular to what they describe as a \xe2\x80\x9cbroad\xe2\x80\x9d exception\nfor \xe2\x80\x9croutine use[s],\xe2\x80\x9d defined as uses that are \xe2\x80\x9ccompatible with the purpose for which the record was collected.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7552a(b)(3), (a)(7).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on these exceptions rests on an incorrect reading of both our precedents and the terms of the Privacy Act. As to our cases, the Court in Whalen and Nixon referred approvingly to statutory or regulatory protections against \xe2\x80\x9cunwarranted disclosures\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cundue dissemination\xe2\x80\x9d of personal information collected by the Government. Whalen, supra, at 605; Nixon, supra, at 458. Neither case suggested that an ironclad disclosure bar is needed to satisfy privacy interests that may be \xe2\x80\x9croot[ed] in the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Whalen, supra, at 605. In Whalen, the New York statute prohibiting \xe2\x80\x9c[p]ublic disclosure of the identity of patients\xe2\x80\x9d was itself subject to several exceptions. 429 U. S., at 594\xe2\x80\x93595, and n.\xc2\xa012. In Nixon, the protections against \xe2\x80\x9cundue dissemination\xe2\x80\x9d mentioned in the opinion were not even before the Court, but were to be included in forthcoming regulations \xe2\x80\x9cmandate[d]\xe2\x80\x9d by the challenged Act. 433 U. S., at 458; see id., at 437\xe2\x80\x93439 (explaining that the Court was limiting its review to the Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfacial validity\xe2\x80\x9d and was not considering the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s forthcoming regulations). Thus, the mere fact that the Privacy Act\xe2\x80\x99s nondisclosure requirement is subject to exceptions does not show that the statute provides insufficient protection against public disclosure.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does the substance of the \xe2\x80\x9croutine use\xe2\x80\x9d exception relied on by respondents create any undue risk of public dissemination. None of the authorized \xe2\x80\x9croutine use[s]\xe2\x80\x9d of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 background-check information allows for release to the public. 71 Fed. Reg. 45859\xe2\x80\x9345860, 45862 (2006); 60 Fed. Reg. 63084 (1995), as amended, 75 Fed. Reg. 28307 (2010). Rather, the established \xe2\x80\x9croutine use[s]\xe2\x80\x9d consist of limited, reasonable steps designed to complete the background-check process in an efficient and orderly manner. See Whalen, supra, at 602 (approving disclosures to authorized New York Department of Health employees that were not \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningfully distinguishable\xe2\x80\x9d from routine disclosures \xe2\x80\x9cassociated with many facets of health care\xe2\x80\x9d). One routine use, for example, involves a limited disclosure to persons filling out Form 42 so that designated references can \xe2\x80\x9cidentify the individual\xe2\x80\x9d at issue and can understand the \xe2\x80\x9cnature and purpose of the investigation.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 89. Authorized JPL employees also review each completed SF\xe2\x80\x9385 to verify that all requested information has been provided. Id., at 211. These designated JPL employees may not \xe2\x80\x9cdisclose any information contained in the form to anyone else,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and Cal Tech is not given access to adverse information uncovered during the Government\xe2\x80\x99s background check, id., at 207\xe2\x80\x93208. The \xe2\x80\x9cremote possibility\xe2\x80\x9d of public disclosure created by these narrow \xe2\x80\x9croutine use[s]\xe2\x80\x9d does not undermine the Privacy Act\xe2\x80\x99s substantial protections. See Whalen, 429 U. S., at 601\xe2\x80\x93602 (\xe2\x80\x9cremote possibility\xe2\x80\x9d that statutory security provisions will \xe2\x80\x9cprovide inadequate protection against unwarranted disclosures\xe2\x80\x9d not a sufficient basis for striking down statute).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Citing past violations of the Privacy Act,[Footnote 14] respondents note that it is possible that their personal information could be disclosed as a result of a similar breach. But data breaches are a possibility any time the Government stores information. As the Court recognized in Whalen, the mere possibility that security measures will fail provides no \xe2\x80\x9cproper ground\xe2\x80\x9d for a broad-based attack on government information-collection practices. Ibid. Respondents also cite a portion of SF\xe2\x80\x9385 that warns of possible disclosure \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o the news media or the general public.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 89. By its terms, this exception allows public disclosure only where release is \xe2\x80\x9cin the public interest\xe2\x80\x9d and would not result in \xe2\x80\x9can unwarranted invasion of personal privacy.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Respondents have not cited any example of such a disclosure, nor have they identified any plausible scenario in which their information might be unduly disclosed under this exception.[Footnote 15]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of the protection provided by the Privacy Act\xe2\x80\x99s nondisclosure requirement, and because the challenged portions of the forms consist of reasonable inquiries in an employment background check, we conclude that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s inquiries do not violate a constitutional right to informational privacy. Whalen, supra, at 605.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court, of course, that background checks of employees of government contractors do not offend the Constitution. But rather than reach this conclusion on the basis of the never-explained assumption that the Constitution requires courts to \xe2\x80\x9cbalance\xe2\x80\x9d the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interests in data collection against its contractor employees\xe2\x80\x99 interest in privacy, I reach it on simpler grounds. Like many other desirable things not included \nin the Constitution, \xe2\x80\x9cinformational privacy\xe2\x80\x9d seems like a good idea\xe2\x80\x94wherefore the People have enacted laws at the federal level and in the states restricting the government\xe2\x80\x99s collection and use of information. But it is up to the People to enact those laws, to shape them, and, when they think it appropriate, to repeal them. A federal constitutional right to \xe2\x80\x9cinformational privacy\xe2\x80\x9d does not exist.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before addressing the constitutional issues, however, I must observe a remarkable and telling fact about this case, unique in my tenure on this Court: Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 brief, in arguing that the Federal Government violated the Constitution, does not once identify which provision of \nthe Constitution that might be. The Table of Authorities contains citations of cases from federal and state courts, federal and state statutes, Rules of Evidence from four states, two Executive Orders, a House Report, and even more exotic sources of law, such as two reports of the Government Accountability Office and an EEOC document concerning \xe2\x80\x9cEnforcement Guidance.\xe2\x80\x9d And yet it contains not a single citation of the sole document we are called upon to construe: the Constitution of the United States. The body of the brief includes a single, fleeting reference to the Due Process Clause, buried in a citation of the assuredly inapposite Lawrence v. Texas, 539 U. S. 558 (2003), Brief for Respondents 42; but no further attempt is made to argue that NASA\xe2\x80\x99s actions deprived respondents of liberty without due process of law. And this legal strategy was not limited to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 filing in this Court; in the Ninth Circuit respondents asserted in a footnote that \xe2\x80\x9ccourts have grounded the right to informational privacy in various provisions of the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellants in No. 07\xe2\x80\x9356424, p.\xc2\xa025, n.\xc2\xa018, but declined to identify which ones applied here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To tell the truth, I found this approach refreshingly honest. One who asks us to invent a constitutional right out of whole cloth should spare himself and us the pretense of tying it to some words of the Constitution. Regrettably, this Lincolnesque honesty evaporated at oral argument, when counsel asserted, apparently for the first time in this litigation, that the right to informational privacy emerged from the Due Process Clause of the Fifth Amendment. Tr. of Oral Arg. 28\xe2\x80\x9329. That counsel invoked the infinitely plastic concept of \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d due process does not make this constitutional theory any less invented.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case is easily resolved on the simple ground that the Due Process Clause does not \xe2\x80\x9cguarante[e] certain (unspecified) liberties\xe2\x80\x9d; rather, it \xe2\x80\x9cmerely guarantees certain procedures as a prerequisite to deprivation of liberty.\xe2\x80\x9d Albright v. Oliver, 510 U. S. 266, 275 (1994) (Scalia, J., concurring). Respondents make no claim that the State has deprived them of liberty without the requisite procedures, and their due process claim therefore must fail. Even under the formula we have adopted for identifying liberties entitled to protection under the faux \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d component of the Due Process Clause\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Due Process Clause specially protects those fundamental rights and liberties which are, objectively, deeply rooted in this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s history and tradition,\xe2\x80\x9d Washington v. Glucksberg, 521 U. S. 702, 720\xe2\x80\x93721 (1997) (internal quotation marks omitted)\xe2\x80\x94respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim would fail. Respondents do not even attempt to argue that the claim at issue in this case passes that test, perhaps recognizing the farcical nature of a contention that a right deeply rooted in our history and tradition bars the Government from ensuring that the Hubble Telescope is not used by recovering drug addicts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The absurdity of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 position in this case should not, however, obscure the broader point: Our due process precedents, even our \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive due process\xe2\x80\x9d precedents, do not support any right to informational privacy. First, we have held that the government\xe2\x80\x99s act of defamation does not deprive a person \xe2\x80\x9cof any \xe2\x80\x98liberty\xe2\x80\x99 protected by the procedural guarantees of the Fourteenth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Paul v. Davis, 424 U. S. 693, 709 (1976). We reasoned that stigma, standing alone, does not \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly alte[r]\xe2\x80\x9d a person\xe2\x80\x99s legal status so as to \xe2\x80\x9cjustif[y] the invocation of procedural safeguards.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 708\xe2\x80\x93709. If outright defamation does not qualify, it is unimaginable that the mere disclosure of private information does.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, respondents challenge the Government\xe2\x80\x99s collection of their private information. But the Government\xe2\x80\x99s collection of private information is regulated by the Fourth Amendment, and \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here a particular Amendment provides an explicit textual source of constitutional protection against a particular sort of government behavior, that Amendment, not the more generalized notion of substantive due process, must be the guide for analyzing these claims.\xe2\x80\x9d County of Sacramento v. Lewis, 523 U. S. 833, 842 (1998) (internal quotation marks omitted; alteration in original). Here, the Ninth Circuit rejected respondents\xe2\x80\x99 Fourth Amendment argument, correctly holding that the Form 42 inquiries to third parties were not Fourth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9csearches\xe2\x80\x9d under United States v. Miller, 425 U. S. 435 (1976), and that the Fourth Amendment does not prohibit the Government from asking questions about private information. 530 F.\xc2\xa03d 865, 876\xe2\x80\x93877 (2008). That should have been the end of the matter. Courts should not use the Due Process Clause as putty to fill up gaps they deem unsightly in the protections provided by other constitutional provisions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, I would simply hold that there is no constitutional right to \xe2\x80\x9cinformational privacy.\xe2\x80\x9d Besides being consistent with constitutional text and tradition, this view has the attractive benefit of resolving this case without resort to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s exegesis on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate interest in identifying contractor drug abusers and the comfortingly narrow scope of NASA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9croutine use\xe2\x80\x9d regulations. I shall not fill the U. S. Reports with further explanation of the incoherence of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive due process\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine in its many manifestations, since the Court does not play the substantive-due-process card. Instead, it states that it will \xe2\x80\x9cassume, without deciding\xe2\x80\x9d that there exists a right to informational privacy, ante, \nat 1.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s sole justification for its decision to \xe2\x80\x9cassume, without deciding\xe2\x80\x9d is that the Court made the same mistake before\xe2\x80\x94in two 33-year-old cases, Whalen v. Roe, 429 U. S. 589 (1977), and Nixon v. Administrator of General Services, 433 U. S. 425 (1977).* Ante, at 11. But stare decisis is simply irrelevant when the pertinent precedent assumed, without deciding, the existence of a constitutional right. \xe2\x80\x9cStare decisis reflects a policy judgment that in most matters it is more important that the applicable rule of law be settled than that it be settled right.\xe2\x80\x9d State Oil Co. v. Khan, 522 U. S. 3, 20 (1997) (internal quotation marks omitted). \xe2\x80\x9cIt is the preferred course because it promotes the evenhanded, predictable, and consistent development of legal principles.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). Here, however, there is no applicable rule of law that is settled. To the contrary, Whalen and Nixon created an uncertainty that the text of the Constitution did not contain and that today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion perpetuates.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A further reason Whalen and Nixon are not entitled to stare decisis effect is that neither opinion supplied any coherent reason why a constitutional right to informational privacy might exist. As supporting authority, Whalen cited Stanley v. Georgia, 394 U. S. 557 (1969), a First Amendment case protecting private possession of obscenity; the deservedly infamous dictum in Griswold v. Connecticut, 381 U. S. 479 (1965), concerning the \xe2\x80\x9cpenumbra\xe2\x80\x9d of the First Amendment; and three concurring or dissenting opinions, none of which remotely intimated that there might be such a thing as a substantive due process right to informational privacy. 429 U. S., at 599, n.\xc2\xa025. Nixon provided even less support. After citing the observation in Whalen that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne element of privacy has been characterized as the individual interest in avoiding disclosure of personal matters,\xe2\x80\x9d Nixon, supra, at 457 (quoting Whalen, supra, at 599; internal quotation marks omitted), it proceeded to conduct a straightforward Fourth Amendment analysis. It \xe2\x80\x9cassume[d]\xe2\x80\x9d that there was a \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate expectation of privacy\xe2\x80\x9d in the materials, and rejected the appellant\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the statute at issue was \xe2\x80\x9cprecisely the kind of abuse that the Fourth Amendment was intended to prevent.\xe2\x80\x9d Nixon, supra, at 457\xe2\x80\x93458, 460. It is unfathomable why these cases\xe2\x80\x99 passing, barely explained reference to a right separate from the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x94an unenumerated right that they held to be not applicable\xe2\x80\x94should be afforded stare decisis weight.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At this point the reader may be wondering: \xe2\x80\x9cWhat, after all, is the harm in being \xe2\x80\x98minimalist\xe2\x80\x99 and simply refusing to say that violation of a constitutional right of informational privacy can never exist? The outcome in this case is the same, so long as the Court holds that any such hypothetical right was not violated.\xe2\x80\x9d Well, there is harm. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s never-say-never disposition does damage for several reasons.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01. It is in an important sense not actually minimalist. By substituting for one real constitutional question (whether there exists a constitutional right to informational privacy) a different constitutional question (whether NASA\xe2\x80\x99s background checks would contravene a right to informational privacy if such a right existed), the Court gets to pontificate upon a matter that is none of its \nbusiness: the appropriate balance between security and privacy. If I am correct that there exists no right to in-formational privacy, all that discussion is an exercise in judicial maximalism. Better simply to state and apply \nthe law forthrightly than to hold our view of the law in pectore, so that we can inquire into matters beyond our charter, and probably beyond our ken.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If, on the other hand, the Court believes that there is a constitutional right to informational privacy, then I fail to see the minimalist virtues in delivering a lengthy opinion analyzing that right while coyly noting that the right is \xe2\x80\x9cassumed\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cdecided.\xe2\x80\x9d Thirty-three years have passed since the Court first suggested that the right may, or may not, exist. It is past time for the Court to abandon this Alfred Hitchcock line of our jurisprudence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02. It harms our image, if not our self-respect, because it makes no sense. The Court decides that the Government did not violate the right to informational privacy without deciding whether there is a right to informational privacy, and without even describing what hypothetical standard should be used to assess whether the hypothetical right has been violated. As I explained last Term in objecting to another of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s never-say-never dispositions:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[The Court] cannot decide that [respondents\xe2\x80\x99] claim fails without first deciding what a valid claim would consist of\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [A]greeing to or crafting a hypothetical standard for a hypothetical constitutional right is sufficiently unappealing \xe2\x80\xa6 that [the Court] might as well acknowledge the right as well. Or [it] could avoid the need to agree with or craft a hypothetical standard by denying the right. But embracing a standard while being coy about the right is, well, odd; and deciding this case while addressing neither the standard nor the right is quite impossible.\xe2\x80\x9d Stop the Beach Renourishment, Inc. v. Florida Dept. of Environmental Protection, 560 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (plurality opinion) (joined by Alito, J.) (slip op., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313).

\n

\nWhatever the virtues of judicial minimalism, it cannot justify judicial incoherence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court defends its approach by observing that \n\xe2\x80\x9cwe have only the \xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98scarce and open-ended\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d guideposts of substantive due process to show us the way.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11, n.\xc2\xa010. I would have thought that this doctrinal obscurity should lead us to provide more clarity for lower courts; surely one vague opinion should not provide an excuse for another.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court observes that I have joined other opinions that have assumed the existence of constitutional rights. Ibid. It is of course acceptable to reserve difficult constitutional questions, so long as answering those questions is unnecessary to coherent resolution of the issue presented in the case. So in Cruzan v. Director, Mo. Dept. of Health, 497 U. S. 261, 279\xe2\x80\x93280 (1990), we declined to decide whether a competent person had a constitutional right to refuse lifesaving hydration, because\xe2\x80\x94under a constitutional standard we laid out in detail\xe2\x80\x94such a right did not exist for an incompetent person. In Herrera v. Collins, 506 U. S. 390, 417\xe2\x80\x93418 (1993), we declined to decide whether it would be unconstitutional to execute an innocent person, because Herrera had not shown that he was innocent. In New York State Club Assn., Inc. v. City of New York, 487 U. S. 1, 10\xe2\x80\x9315 (1988), we declined to decide whether there was a constitutional right of private association for certain clubs, because the plaintiff had brought a facial challenge, which would fail if the statute was valid in many of its applications, making it unnecessary to decide whether an as-applied challenge as to some clubs could succeed. Here, however, the Court actually applies a constitutional informational privacy standard without giving a clue as to the rule of law it is applying.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03. It provides no guidance whatsoever for lower courts. Consider the sheer multiplicity of unweighted, relevant factors alluded to in today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is relevant that the Government is acting \xe2\x80\x9cin its capacity \xe2\x80\x98as proprietor\xe2\x80\x99 and manager of its \xe2\x80\x98internal operation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. Of course, given that we are told neither what the appropriate standard should be when the Government is acting as regulator nor what the appropriate standard should be when it is acting as proprietor, it is not clear what effect this fact has on the analysis; but at least we know that it is something.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0History and tradition have some role to play, ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314, but how much is uncertain. The Court points out that the Federal Government has been conducting investigations of candidates for employment since the earliest days; but on the other hand it acknowledges that extension of those investigations to employees of contractors is of very recent vintage.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The contract employees are doing important work. They are not mere janitors and maintenance men; they are working on a $568 million observatory. Ante, at 15. Can it possibly be that the outcome of today\xe2\x80\x99s case would be different for background checks of lower-level employees? In the spirit of minimalism we are never told.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Questions about drug treatment are (hypothetically) constitutional because they are \xe2\x80\x9creasonable,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cuseful,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9chumane.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317 (internal quotation marks omitted). And questions to third parties are constitutional because they are \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpervasiv[e].\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319. Any or all of these adjectives may be the hypothetical standard by which violation of the hypothetical constitutional right to \xe2\x80\x9cinformational privacy\xe2\x80\x9d is evaluated.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court notes that a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98statutory or regulatory duty to avoid unwarranted disclosures\xe2\x80\x99 generally allays these privacy concerns,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 20 (emphasis added), but it gives no indication of what the exceptions to this general rule might be. It then discusses the provisions of the Privacy Act in detail, placing considerable emphasis on the limitations imposed by NASA\xe2\x80\x99s routine-use regulations. Ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9323. From the length of the discussion, I would bet that the Privacy Act is necessary to today\xe2\x80\x99s holding, but how much of it is necessary is a mystery.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa04. It will dramatically increase the number of lawsuits claiming violations of the right to informational privacy. Rare will be the claim that is supported by none of the factors deemed relevant in today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. Moreover, the utter silliness of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 position in this case leaves plenty of room for the possible success of future claims that are meritless, but slightly less absurd. Respondents claim that even though they are Government contractor employees, and even though they are working with highly expensive scientific equipment, and even though the Government is seeking only information about drug treatment and information from third parties that is standard in background checks, and even though the Government is liable for damages if that information is ever revealed, and even though NASA\xe2\x80\x99s Privacy Act regulations are very protective of private information, NASA\xe2\x80\x99s background checks are unconstitutional. Ridiculous. In carefully citing all of these factors as the basis for its decision, the Court makes the distinguishing of this case simple as pie.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In future cases filed under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 in those circuits that recognize (rather than merely hypothesize) a constitutional right to \xe2\x80\x9cinformational privacy,\xe2\x80\x9d lawyers will always (and I mean always) find some way around today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion: perhaps the plaintiff will be a receptionist or a janitor, or the protections against disclosure will be less robust. And oh yes, the fact that a losing defendant will be liable not only for damages but also for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees under \xc2\xa71988 will greatly encourage lawyers to sue, and defendants\xe2\x80\x94for whom no safe harbor can be found in the many words of today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion\xe2\x80\x94to settle. This plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim has failed today, but the Court makes a generous gift to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s bar.

\n

*\xc2\xa0Contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s protestation, ante, at 11, n.\xc2\xa010, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to address whether there is a right to informational privacy cannot be blamed upon the Government\xe2\x80\x99s concession that such a right exists, and indeed the Government\xe2\x80\x99s startling assertion that Whalen and Nixon (which decided nothing on the constitutional point, and have not been so much as cited in our later opinions) were \xe2\x80\x9cseminal\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94seminal!\xe2\x80\x94decisions. Reply Brief for Petitioner 22. We are not bound by a litigant\xe2\x80\x99s concession on an issue of law. See, e.g., Grove City College v. Bell, 465 U. S. 555, 562, n.\xc2\xa010 (1984). And it should not be thought that the concession by the United States is an entirely self-denying act. To be sure, it subjects the Executive Branch to constitutional limitations on the collection and use of information; but the Privacy Act, 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7552a (2006 ed. and Supp. III), already contains extensive limitations not likely to be surpassed by constitutional improvisation. And because Congress\xe2\x80\x99s power under \xc2\xa75 of the Fourteenth Amendment extends to the full scope of the Due Process Clause, see City of Boerne v. Flores, 521 U. S. 507 (1997), the United States \nhas an incentive to give that Clause a broad reading, thus expanding the scope of federal legislation that it justifies. Federal laws prevent-ing state disregard of \xe2\x80\x9cinformational privacy\xe2\x80\x9d may be a twinkle in the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x99s eye.
