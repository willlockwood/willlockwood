BOWLES v. RUSSELL, WARDEN

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x935306.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 26, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 14, 2007

\n

Having failed to file a timely notice of appeal from the Federal District Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of habeas relief, petitioner Bowles moved to reopen the filing period pursuant to Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 4(a)(6), which allows a district court to grant a 14-day extension under certain conditions, see 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107(c). The District Court granted Bowles\xe2\x80\x99 motion but inexplicably gave him 17 days to file his notice of appeal. He filed within the 17 days allowed by the District Court, but after the 14-day period allowed by Rule 4(a)(6) and \xc2\xa72107(c). The Sixth Circuit held that the notice was untimely and that it therefore lacked jurisdiction to hear the case under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Bowles\xe2\x80\x99 untimely notice of appeal\xe2\x80\x94though filed in reliance upon the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order\xe2\x80\x94deprived the Sixth Circuit of jurisdiction. Pp. 2\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The taking of an appeal in a civil case within the time prescribed by statute is \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory and jurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Griggs v. Provident Consumer Discount Co., 459 U. S. 56, 61 (per curiam). There is a significant distinction between time limitations set forth in a statute such as \xc2\xa72107, which limit a court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction, see, e.g., Kontrick v. Ryan, 540 U. S. 443, 453, and those based on court rules, which do not, see, e.g., id., at 454. Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., 546 U. S. 500, 505, and Scarborough v. Principi, 541 U. S. 401, 314, distinguished. Because Congress decides, within constitutional bounds, whether federal courts can hear cases at all, it can also determine when, and under what conditions, federal courts can hear them. See United States v. Curry, 6 How. 106, 113. And when an \xe2\x80\x9cappeal has not been prosecuted in the manner directed, within the time limited by the acts of Congress, it must be dismissed for want of jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 113. The resolution of this case follows naturally from this reasoning. Because Congress specifically limited the amount of time by which district courts can extend the notice-of-appeal period in \xc2\xa72107(c), Bowles\xe2\x80\x99 failure to file in accordance with the statute deprived the Court of Appeals of jurisdiction. And because Bowles\xe2\x80\x99 error is one of jurisdictional magnitude, he cannot rely on forfeiture or waiver to excuse his lack of compliance. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Bowles\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on the \xe2\x80\x9cunique circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine, rooted in Harris Truck Lines, Inc. v. Cherry Meat Packers, Inc., 371 U. S. 215 (per curiam) and applied in Thompson v. INS, 375 U. S. 384 (per curiam), is rejected. Because this Court has no authority to create equitable exceptions to jurisdictional requirements, use of the doctrine is illegitimate. Harris Truck Lines and Thompson are overruled to the extent they purport to authorize an exception to a jurisdictional rule. Pp. 8\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

432 F.\xc2\xa03d 668, affirmed.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nBOWLES V. RUSSELL
\n551 U. S. ____ (2007)

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 06-5306

KEITH BOWLES, PETITIONER v. HARRY RUSSELL, WARDEN

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[June 14, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, a District Court purported to extend a party\xe2\x80\x99s time for filing an appeal beyond the period allowed by statute. We must decide whether the Court of Appeals had jurisdiction to entertain an appeal filed after the statutory period but within the period allowed by the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order. We have long and repeatedly held that the time limits for filing a notice of appeal are jurisdictional in nature. Accordingly, we hold that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s untimely notice\xe2\x80\x94even though filed in reliance upon a District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order\xe2\x80\x94deprived the Court of Appeals of jurisdiction.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1999, an Ohio jury convicted petitioner Keith Bowles of murder for his involvement in the beating death of Ollie Gipson. The jury sentenced Bowles to 15 years to life imprisonment. Bowles unsuccessfully challenged his conviction and sentence on direct appeal.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bowles then filed a federal habeas corpus application on September 5, 2002. On September 9, 2003, the District Court denied Bowles habeas relief. After the entry of final judgment, Bowles had 30 days to file a notice of appeal. Fed. Rule App. Proc.\xc2\xa04(a)(1)(A); 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107(a). He failed to do so. On December 12, 2003, Bowles moved to reopen the period during which he could file his notice of appeal pursuant to Rule 4(a)(6), which allows district courts to extend the filing period for 14 days from the day the district court grants the order to reopen, provided certain conditions are met. See \xc2\xa72107(c).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On February 10, 2004, the District Court granted Bowles\xe2\x80\x99 motion. But rather than extending the time period by 14 days, as Rule 4(a)(6) and \xc2\xa72107(c) allow, the District Court inexplicably gave Bowles 17 days\xe2\x80\x94until February 27\xe2\x80\x94to file his notice of appeal. Bowles filed his notice on February 26\xe2\x80\x94within the 17 days allowed by the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order, but after the 14-day period allowed by Rule 4(a)(6) and \xc2\xa72107(c).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On appeal, respondent Russell argued that Bowles\xe2\x80\x99 notice was untimely and that the Court of Appeals therefore lacked jurisdiction to hear the case. The Court of Appeals agreed. It first recognized that this Court has consistently held the requirement of filing a timely notice of appeal is \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory and jurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d 432 F.\xc2\xa03d 668, 673 (CA6 2005) (citing Browder v. Director, Dept. of Corrections of Ill., 434 U. S. 257, 264 (1978)). The court also noted that courts of appeals have uniformly held that Rule 4(a)(6)\xe2\x80\x99s 180-day period for filing a motion to reopen is also mandatory and not susceptible to equitable modification. 432 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 673 (collecting cases). Concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe fourteen-day period in Rule 4(a)(6) should be treated as strictly as the 180-day period in that same Rule,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 676, the Court of Appeals held that it was without jurisdiction. We granted certiorari, 549 U. S. ___ (2006), and now affirm.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0According to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107(a), parties must file notices of appeal within 30 days of the entry of the judgment being appealed. District courts have limited authority to grant an extension of the 30-day time period. Relevant to this case, if certain conditions are met, district courts have the statutory authority to grant motions to reopen the time for filing an appeal for 14 additional days. \xc2\xa72107(c). Rule 4 of the Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure carries \xc2\xa72107 into practice. In accord with \xc2\xa72107(c), Rule 4(a)(6) describes the district court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to reopen and extend the time for filing a notice of appeal after the lapse of the usual 30 days:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(6)\xc2\xa0Reopening the Time to File an Appeal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe district court may reopen the time to file an appeal for a period of 14 days after the date when its order to reopen is entered, but only if all the following conditions are satisfied:

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(A)\xe2\x80\x82the motion is filed within 180 days after the judgment or order is entered or within 7 days after the moving party receives notice of the entry, whichever is earlier;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(B)\xe2\x80\x82the court finds that the moving party was entitled to notice of the entry of the judgment or order sought to be appealed but did not receive the notice from the district court or any party within 21 days after entry; and

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(C)\xe2\x80\x82the court finds that no party would be prejudiced.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.)[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is undisputed that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order in this case purported to reopen the filing period for more than 14 days. Thus, the question before us is whether the Court of Appeals lacked jurisdiction to entertain an appeal filed outside the 14-day window allowed by \xc2\xa72107(c) but within the longer period granted by the District Court.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has long held that the taking of an appeal within the prescribed time is \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory and jurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Griggs v. Provident Consumer Discount Co., 459 U. S. 56, 61 (1982) (per curiam) (internal quotation marks omitted);[Footnote 2] accord, Hohn v. United States, 524 U. S. 236, 247 (1998); Torres v. Oakland Scavenger Co., 487 U. S. 312, 314\xe2\x80\x93315 (1988); Browder, supra, at 264. Indeed, even prior to the creation of the circuit courts of appeals, this Court regarded statutory limitations on the timing of appeals as limitations on its own jurisdiction. See Scarborough v. Pargoud, 108 U. S. 567, 568 (1883) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he writ of error in this case was not brought within the time limited by law, and we have consequently no jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Curry, 6 How. 106, 113 (1848) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]s this appeal has not been prosecuted in the manner directed, within the time limited by the acts of Congress, it must be dismissed for want of jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d). Reflecting the consistency of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s holdings, the courts of appeals routinely and uniformly dismiss untimely appeals for lack of jurisdiction. See, e.g., Atkins v. Medical Dept. of Augusta Cty. Jail, No. 06\xe2\x80\x937792, 2007 WL 1048810 (CA4, Apr. 4, 2007) (per curiam) (unpublished); see also 15A C. Wright, A. Miller, & E. Cooper, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa73901, p. 6 (2d ed. 1992) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe rule is well settled that failure to file a timely notice of appeal defeats the jurisdiction of a court of appeals\xe2\x80\x9d). In fact, the author of today\xe2\x80\x99s dissent recently reiterated that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he accepted fact is that some time limits are jurisdictional even though expressed in a separate statutory section from jurisdictional grants, see, e.g., \xe2\x80\xa6 \xc2\xa72107 (providing that notice of appeal in civil cases must be filed \xe2\x80\x98within thirty days after the entry of such judgment\xe2\x80\x99).\xe2\x80\x9d Barnhart v. Peabody Coal Co., 537 U. S. 149, 160, n.\xc2\xa06 (2003) (majority opinion of Souter,\xc2\xa0J., joined by Stevens, Ginsburg, and Breyer, JJ., inter alios) (citation omitted).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although several of our recent decisions have undertaken to clarify the distinction between claims-processing rules and jurisdictional rules, none of them calls into question our longstanding treatment of statutory time limits for taking an appeal as jurisdictional. Indeed, those decisions have also recognized the jurisdictional significance of the fact that a time limitation is set forth in a statute. In Kontrick v. Ryan, 540 U. S. 443 (2004), we held that failure to comply with the time requirement in Federal Rule of Bankruptcy Procedure 4004 did not affect a court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction. Critical to our analysis was the fact that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o statute \xe2\x80\xa6 specifies a time limit for filing a complaint objecting to the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s discharge.\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U. S., at 448. Rather, the filing deadlines in the Bankruptcy Rules are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98procedural rules adopted by the Court for the orderly transaction of its business\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98not jurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 454 (quoting Schacht v. United States, 398 U. S. 58, 64 (1970)). Because \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nly Congress may determine a lower federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U. S., at 452 (citing U. S. Const.,\xc2\xa0Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa71), it was improper for courts to use \xe2\x80\x9cthe term \xe2\x80\x98jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x99 to describe emphatic time prescriptions in rules of court,\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U. S., at 454. See also Eberhart v. United States, 546 U. S. 12 (2005) (per curiam). As a point of contrast, we noted that \xc2\xa72107 contains the type of statutory time constraints that would limit a court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction. 540 U. S., at 453, and n.\xc2\xa08.[Footnote 3] Nor do Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., 546 U. S. 500 (2006), or Scarborough v. Principi, 541 U. S. 401 (2004), aid petitioner. In Arbaugh, the statutory limitation was an employee-numerosity requirement, not a time limit. 546 U. S., at 505. Scarborough, which addressed the availability of attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees under the Equal Access to Justice Act, concerned \xe2\x80\x9ca mode of relief \xe2\x80\xa6 ancillary to the judgment of a court\xe2\x80\x9d that already had plenary jurisdiction. 541 U. S., at 413.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of its certiorari jurisdiction also demonstrates the jurisdictional distinction between court-promulgated rules and limits enacted by Congress. According to our Rules, a petition for a writ of certiorari must be filed within 90 days of the entry of the judgment sought to be reviewed. See this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 13.1. That 90-day period applies to both civil and criminal cases. But the 90-day period for civil cases derives from both this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 13.1 and 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72101(c). We have repeatedly held that this statute-based filing period for civil cases is jurisdictional. See, e.g., Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. NRA Political Victory Fund, 513 U. S. 88, 90 (1994). Indeed, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 13.2 cites \xc2\xa72101(c) in directing the Clerk not to file any petition \xe2\x80\x9cthat is jurisdictionally out of time.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) On the other hand, we have treated the rule-based time limit for criminal cases differently, stating that it may be waived because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he procedural rules adopted by the Court for the orderly transaction of its business are not jurisdictional and can be relaxed by the Court in the exercise of its discretion\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Schacht, supra, at 64.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Jurisdictional treatment of statutory time limits makes good sense. Within constitutional bounds, Congress decides what cases the federal courts have jurisdiction to consider. Because Congress decides whether federal courts can hear cases at all, it can also determine when, and under what conditions, federal courts can hear them. See Curry, 6 How., at 113. Put another way, the notion of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98subject-matter\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d jurisdiction obviously extends to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98classes of cases \xe2\x80\xa6 falling within a court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudicatory authority,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Eberhart, supra, at 16 (quoting Kontrick, supra, at 455), but it is no less \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d when Congress forbids federal courts from adjudicating an otherwise legitimate \xe2\x80\x9cclass of cases\xe2\x80\x9d after a certain period has elapsed from final judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The resolution of this case follows naturally from this reasoning. Like the initial 30-day period for filing a notice of appeal, the limit on how long a district court may reopen that period is set forth in a statute, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107(c). Because Congress specifically limited the amount of time by which district courts can extend the notice-of-appeal period in \xc2\xa72107(c), that limitation is more than a simple \xe2\x80\x9cclaim-processing rule.\xe2\x80\x9d As we have long held, when an \xe2\x80\x9cappeal has not been prosecuted in the manner directed, within the time limited by the acts of Congress, it must be dismissed for want of jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Curry, supra, at 113. Bowles\xe2\x80\x99 failure to file his notice of appeal in accordance with the statute therefore deprived the Court of Appeals of jurisdiction. And because Bowles\xe2\x80\x99 error is one of jurisdictional magnitude, he cannot rely on forfeiture or waiver to excuse his lack of compliance with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s time limitations. See Arbaugh, supra, at 513\xe2\x80\x93514.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bowles contends that we should excuse his untimely filing because he satisfies the \xe2\x80\x9cunique circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine, which has its roots in Harris Truck Lines, Inc. v. Cherry Meat Packers, Inc., 371 U. S. 215 (1962) (per curiam). There, pursuant to then-Rule 73(a) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, a District Court entertained a timely motion to extend the time for filing a notice of appeal. The District Court found the moving party had established a showing of \xe2\x80\x9cexcusable neglect,\xe2\x80\x9d as required by the Rule, and granted the motion. The Court of Appeals reversed the finding of excusable neglect and, accordingly, held that the District Court lacked jurisdiction to grant the extension. Harris Truck Lines, Inc. v. Cherry Meat Packers, Inc., 303 F.\xc2\xa02d 609, 611\xe2\x80\x93612 (CA7 1962). This Court reversed, noting \xe2\x80\x9cthe obvious great hardship to a party who relies upon the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s finding of \xe2\x80\x98excusable neglect.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 371 U. S., at 217.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today we make clear that the timely filing of a notice of appeal in a civil case is a jurisdictional requirement. Because this Court has no authority to create equitable exceptions to jurisdictional requirements, use of the \xe2\x80\x9cunique circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine is illegitimate. Given that this Court has applied Harris Truck Lines only once in the last half century, Thompson v. INS, 375 U. S. 384 (1964) (per curiam), several courts have rightly questioned its continuing validity. See, e.g., Panhorst v. United States, 241 F. 3d 367, 371 (CA4 2001) (doubting \xe2\x80\x9cthe continued viability of the unique circumstances doctrine\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Houston v. Lack, 487 U. S. 266, 282 (1988) (Scalia, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cOur later cases \xe2\x80\xa6 effectively repudiate the Harris Truck Lines approach \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Osterneck v. Ernst & Whinney, 489 U. S. 169, 170 (1989) (referring to \xe2\x80\x9cthe so-called \xe2\x80\x98unique circumstances\xe2\x80\x99 exception\xe2\x80\x9d to the timely appeal requirement). We see no compelling reason to resurrect the doctrine from its 40-year slumber. Accordingly, we reject Bowles\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on the doctrine, and we overrule Harris Truck Lines and Thompson to the extent they purport to authorize an exception to a jurisdictional rule.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If rigorous rules like the one applied today are thought to be inequitable, Congress may authorize courts to promulgate rules that excuse compliance with the statutory time limits. Even narrow rules to this effect would give rise to litigation testing their reach and would no doubt detract from the clarity of the rule. However, congressionally authorized rulemaking would likely lead to less litigation than court-created exceptions without authorization. And in all events, for the reasons discussed above, we lack present authority to make the exception petitioner seeks.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals correctly held that it lacked jurisdiction to consider Bowles\xe2\x80\x99 appeal. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court told petitioner Keith Bowles that his notice of appeal was due on February 27, 2004. He filed a notice of appeal on February 26, only to be told that he was too late because his deadline had actually been February 24. It is intolerable for the judicial system to treat people this way, and there is not even a technical justification for condoning this bait and switch. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d we have warned several times in the last decade, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is a word of many, too many, meanings.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U. S. 83, 90 (1998) (quoting United States v. Vanness, 85 F.\xc2\xa03d 661, 663, n.\xc2\xa02 (CADC 1996)); Kontrick v. Ryan, 540 U. S. 443, 454 (2004) (quoting Steel Co.); Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., 546 U. S. 500, 510 (2006) (quoting Steel Co.); Rockwell Int\xe2\x80\x99l Corp. v. United States, 549 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 9) (quoting Steel Co.). This variety of meaning has insidiously tempted courts, this one included, to engage in \xe2\x80\x9cless than meticulous,\xe2\x80\x9d Kontrick, supra, at 454, sometimes even \xe2\x80\x9cprofligate \xe2\x80\xa6 use of the term,\xe2\x80\x9d Arbaugh, supra, at 510.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In recent years, however, we have tried to clean up our language, and until today we have been avoiding the erroneous jurisdictional conclusions that flow from indiscriminate use of the ambiguous word. Thus, although we used to call the sort of time limit at issue here \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory and jurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Robinson, 361 U. S. 220, 229 (1960), we have recently and repeatedly corrected that designation as a misuse of the \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d label. Arbaugh, supra, at 510 (citing Robinson as an example of improper use of the term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d); Eberhart v. United States, 546 U. S. 12, 17\xe2\x80\x9318 (2005) (per curiam) (same); Kontrick, supra, at 454 (same).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But one would never guess this from reading the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in this case, which suddenly restores Robinson\xe2\x80\x99s indiscriminate use of the \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory and jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d label to good law in the face of three unanimous repudiations of Robinson\xe2\x80\x99s error. See ante, at 4. This is puzzling, the more so because our recent (and, I repeat, unanimous) efforts to confine jurisdictional rulings to jurisdiction proper were obviously sound, and the majority makes no attempt to show they were not.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The stakes are high in treating time limits as jurisdictional. While a mandatory but nonjurisdictional limit is enforceable at the insistence of a party claiming its benefit or by a judge concerned with moving the docket, it may be waived or mitigated in exercising reasonable equitable discretion. But if a limit is taken to be jurisdictional, waiver becomes impossible, meritorious excuse irrelevant (unless the statute so provides), and sU. S.onte consideration in the courts of appeals mandatory, see Arbaugh, supra, at 514.[Footnote 2] As the Court recognizes, ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, this is no way to regard time limits set out in a court rule rather than a statute, see Kontrick, supra, at 452 (\xe2\x80\x9cOnly Congress may determine a lower federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d). But neither is jurisdictional treatment automatic when a time limit is statutory, as it is in this case. Generally speaking, limits on the reach of federal statutes, even nontemporal ones, are only jurisdictional if Congress says so: \xe2\x80\x9cwhen Congress does not rank a statutory limitation on coverage as jurisdictional, courts should treat the restriction as nonjurisdictional in character.\xe2\x80\x9d Arbaugh, 546 U. S., at 516. Thus, we have held \xe2\x80\x9cthat time prescriptions, however emphatic, \xe2\x80\x98are not properly typed \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 510 (quoting Scarborough v. Principi, 541 U. S. 401, 414 (2004)), absent some jurisdictional designation by Congress. Congress put no jurisdictional tag on the time limit here.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The doctrinal underpinning of this recently repeated view was set out in Kontrick: \xe2\x80\x9cthe label \xe2\x80\x98jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x99 [is appropriate] not for claim-processing rules, but only for prescriptions delineating the classes of cases (subject-matter jurisdiction) and the persons (personal jurisdiction) falling within a court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudicatory authority.\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U. S., at 455. A filing deadline is the paradigm of a claim-processing rule, not of a delineation of cases that federal courts may hear, and so it falls outside the class of limitations on subject matter jurisdiction unless Congress says otherwise.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The time limit at issue here, far from defining the set of cases that may be adjudicated, is much more like a statute of limitations, which provides an affirmative defense, see Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 8(c), and is not jurisdictional, Day v. McDonough, 547 U. S. 198, 205 (2006). Statutes of limitations may thus be waived, id., at 207\xe2\x80\x93208, or excused by rules, such as equitable tolling, that alleviate hardship and unfairness, see Irwin v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 498 U. S. 89, 95\xe2\x80\x9396 (1990).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consistent with the traditional view of statutes of limitations, and the carefully limited concept of jurisdiction explained in Arbaugh, Eberhart, and Kontrick, an exception to the time limit in 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107(c) should be available when there is a good justification for one, for reasons we recognized years ago. In Harris Truck Lines, Inc. v. Cherry Meat Packers, Inc., 371 U. S. 215, 217 (1962) (per curiam), and Thompson v. INS, 375 U. S. 384, 387 (1964) (per curiam), we found that \xe2\x80\x9cunique circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d excused failures to comply with the time limit. In fact, much like this case, Harris and Thompson involved district court errors that misled litigants into believing they had more time to file notices of appeal than a statute actually provided. Thus, even back when we thoughtlessly called time limits jurisdictional, we did not actually treat them as beyond exemption to the point of shrugging at the inequity of penalizing a party for relying on what a federal judge had said to him. Since we did not dishonor reasonable reliance on a judge\xe2\x80\x99s official word back in the days when we uncritically had a jurisdictional reason to be unfair, it is unsupportable to dishonor it now, after repeatedly disavowing any such jurisdictional justification that would apply to the 14-day time limit of \xc2\xa72107(c).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority avoids clashing with Harris and Thompson by overruling them on the ground of their \xe2\x80\x9cslumber,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9, and inconsistency with a time-limit-as-jurisdictional rule.[Footnote 5] But eliminating those precedents underscores what has become the principal question of this case: why does today\xe2\x80\x99s majority refuse to come to terms with the steady stream of unanimous statements from this Court in the past four years, culminating in Arbaugh\xe2\x80\x99s summary a year ago? The majority begs this question by refusing to confront what we have said: \xe2\x80\x9cin recent decisions, we have clarified that time prescriptions, however emphatic, \xe2\x80\x98are not properly typed \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Arbaugh, 546 U. S., at 510 (quoting Scarborough, 541 U. S., at 414). This statement of the Court, and those preceding it for which it stands as a summation, cannot be dismissed as \xe2\x80\x9csome dicta,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 4, n.\xc2\xa02, and cannot be ignored on the ground that some of them were made in cases where the challenged restriction was not a time limit, see ante, at 6. By its refusal to come to grips with our considered statements of law the majority leaves the Court incoherent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In ruling that Bowles cannot depend on the word of a District Court Judge, the Court demonstrates that no one may depend on the recent, repeated, and unanimous statements of all participating Justices of this Court. Yet more incongruously, all of these pronouncements by the Court, along with two of our cases,[Footnote 6] are jettisoned in a ruling for which the leading justification is stare decisis, see ante, at 4 (\xe2\x80\x9cThis Court has long held \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have the authority to recognize an equitable exception to the 14-day limit, and we should do that here, as it certainly seems reasonable to rely on an order from a federal judge.[Footnote 7] Bowles, though, does not have to convince us as a matter of first impression that his reliance was justified, for we only have to look as far as Thompson to know that he ought to prevail. There, the would-be appellant, Thompson, had filed post-trial motions 12 days after the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s final order. Although the rules said they should have been filed within 10, Fed. Rules Civ. Proc. 52(b) and 59(b) (1964), the trial court nonetheless had \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically declared that the \xe2\x80\x98motion for a new trial\xe2\x80\x99 was made \xe2\x80\x98in ample time.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Thompson, 375 U. S., at 385. Thompson relied on that statement in filing a notice of appeal within 60 days of the denial of the post-trial motions but not within 60 days of entry of the original judgment. Only timely post-trial motions affected the 60-day time limit for filing a notice of appeal, Rule 73(a) (1964), so the Court of Appeals held the appeal untimely. We vacated\xc2\xa0because Thompson \xe2\x80\x9crelied on the statement of the District Court and filed the appeal within the assumedly new deadline but beyond the old deadline.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 387.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thompson should control. In that case, and this one, the untimely filing of a notice of appeal resulted from reliance on an error by a district court, an error that caused no evident prejudice to the other party. Actually, there is one difference between Thompson and this case: Thompson filed his post-trial motions late and the District Court was mistaken when it said they were timely; here, the District Court made the error out of the blue, not on top of any mistake by Bowles, who then filed his notice of appeal by the specific date the District Court had declared timely. If anything, this distinction ought to work in Bowles\xe2\x80\x99s favor. Why should we have rewarded Thompson, who introduced the error, but now punish Bowles, who merely trusted the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s statement?[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under Thompson, it would be no answer to say that Bowles\xe2\x80\x99s trust was unreasonable because the 14-day limit was clear and counsel should have checked the judge\xe2\x80\x99s arithmetic. The 10-day limit on post-trial motions was no less pellucid in Thompson, which came out the other way. And what is more, counsel here could not have uncovered the court\xe2\x80\x99s error simply by counting off the days on a calendar. Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 4(a)(6) allows a party to file a notice of appeal within 14 days of \xe2\x80\x9cthe date when [the district court\xe2\x80\x99s] order to reopen is entered.\xe2\x80\x9d See also 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107(c)(2) (allowing reopening for \xe2\x80\x9c14 days from the date of entry\xe2\x80\x9d). The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order was dated February 10, 2004, which reveals the date the judge signed it but not necessarily the date on which the order was entered. Bowles\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer therefore could not tell from reading the order, which he received by mail, whether it was entered the day it was signed. Nor is the possibility of delayed entry merely theoretical: the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s original judgment in this case, dated July 10, 2003, was not entered until July 28. See App. 11 (District Court docket). According to Bowles\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer, electronic access to the docket was unavailable at the time, so to learn when the order was actually entered he would have had to call or go to the courthouse and check. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 56\xe2\x80\x9357. Surely this is more than equity demands, and unless every statement by a federal court is to be tagged with the warning \xe2\x80\x9cBeware of the Judge,\xe2\x80\x9d Bowles\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer had no obligation to go behind the terms of the order he received.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I have to admit that Bowles\xe2\x80\x99s counsel probably did not think the order might have been entered on a different day from the day it was signed. He probably just trusted that the date given was correct, and there was nothing unreasonable in so trusting. The other side let the order pass without objection, either not caring enough to make a fuss or not even noticing the discrepancy; the mistake of a few days was probably not enough to ring the alarm bell to send either lawyer to his copy of the federal rules and then off to the courthouse to check the docket.[Footnote 9] This would be a different case if the year were wrong on the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order, or if opposing counsel had flagged the error. But on the actual facts, it was reasonable to rely on a facially plausible date provided by a federal judge.
