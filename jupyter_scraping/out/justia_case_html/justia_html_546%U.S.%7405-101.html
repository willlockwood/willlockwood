MARGARET BRADSHAW, WARDEN v. KENNETH T.\nRICHEY

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united\nstates court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 05\xe2\x80\x93101.\xe2\x80\x83Decided November 28, 2005

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1987, respondent Kenneth T. Richey was tried in Ohio for aggravated murder committed in the course of a felony. Evidence showed that respondent set fire to the apartment of his neighbor, Hope Collins, in an attempt to kill his ex-girlfriend and her new boyfriend, who were spending the night together in the apartment below. The intended victims escaped unharmed, but Hope Collins\xe2\x80\x99 2-year-old daughter Cynthia died in the fire. At trial, the State presented evidence of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s intent to kill his ex-girlfriend and her boyfriend, but not of specific intent to kill Cynthia Collins. The State also offered expert forensic evidence to show that the fire had been started deliberately. Respondent did not contest this forensic evidence at trial because his retained arson expert had reported that the State\xe2\x80\x99s evidence conclusively established arson. Respondent was convicted of aggravated felony murder on a theory of transferred intent and sentenced to death. His conviction and sentence were affirmed on direct appeal, where he was represented by new counsel.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent sought postconviction relief in state court. The state trial court denied his request for an evidentiary hearing and denied relief on all claims, and the state appellate court affirmed. Respondent then sought federal habeas relief. The District Court permitted discovery on certain issues, but ultimately denied all of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s claims. The Sixth Circuit reversed, holding that respondent was entitled to habeas relief on two alternative grounds. First, that transferred intent was not a permissible theory for aggravated felony murder under Ohio law, and that the evidence of direct intent was constitutionally insufficient to support conviction. Second, that the performance of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel had been constitutionally deficient under Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668 (1984), in his retaining and mishandling of his arson expert and in his inadequate treatment of the State\xe2\x80\x99s expert testimony.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We now grant the State\xe2\x80\x99s petition for writ of certiorari and vacate the judgment below.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Circuit erred in holding that the doctrine of transferred intent was inapplicable to aggravated felony murder for the version of Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa72903.01(B) (Anderson 1982) under which respondent was convicted. See Richey v. Mitchell, 395 F.\xc2\xa03d 660, 675 (2005). The Ohio Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of that section, as announced in its review of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s case, directly contradicts the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe fact that the intended victims escaped harm, and that an innocent child, Cynthia Collins, was killed instead, does not alter Richey\xe2\x80\x99s legal and moral responsibility. \xe2\x80\x98The doctrine of transferred intent is firmly rooted in Ohio law.\xe2\x80\x99 Very simply, \xe2\x80\x98the culpability of a scheme designed to implement the calculated decision to kill is not altered by the fact that the scheme is directed at someone other than the actual victim.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Richey, 64 Ohio St.\xc2\xa03d 353, 364, 595 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 915, 925 (1992) (citations omitted).

\n

\nThis statement was dictum, since the only sufficiency-of-evidence claim raised by respondent pertained to his setting of the fire. Nonetheless, its explanation of Ohio law was perfectly clear and unambiguous. We have repeatedly held that a state court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of state law, including one announced on direct appeal of the challenged conviction, binds a federal court sitting in habeas corpus. Estelle v. McGuire, 502 U. S. 62, 67\xe2\x80\x9368 (1991); Mullaney v. Wilbur, 421 U. S. 684, 691 (1975).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Circuit held that the Ohio Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion should not be read to endorse transferred intent in respondent\xe2\x80\x99s case because such a construction would likely constitute \xe2\x80\x9can unforeseeable and retroactive judicial expansion of narrow and precise statutory language,\xe2\x80\x9d Bouie v. City of Columbia, 378 U. S. 347, 352 (1964), in violation of the Due Process Clause. 395 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 677 (citing United States v. Lanier, 520 U. S. 259 (1997); Bouie, 378 U. S., at 352). It is doubtful whether this principle of fair notice has any application to a case of transferred intent, where the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s contemplated conduct was exactly what the relevant statute forbade, see id., at 351. And it is further doubtful whether the doctrine of constitutional doubt permits such a flatly countertextual interpretation of what the Ohio Supreme Court said, see Salinas v. United States, 522 U. S. 52, 59\xe2\x80\x9360 (1997). But assuming all that, Ohio law at the time of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s offense provided fully adequate notice of the applicability of transferred intent. The relevant mens rea provision in \xc2\xa72903.01(D) required only that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o person shall be convicted of aggravated murder unless he is specifically found to have intended to cause the death of another.\xe2\x80\x9d Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa72903.01(D) (Anderson 1982) (emphasis added). Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s intention to kill his ex-girlfriend and her boyfriend plainly came within this provision. There was no reason to read \xe2\x80\x9canother\xe2\x80\x9d (countertextually) as meaning only \xe2\x80\x9cthe actual victim,\xe2\x80\x9d since the doctrine of transferred intent was \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly rooted in Ohio law.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Sowell, 39 Ohio St.\xc2\xa03d 322, 332, 530 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 1294, 1305 (1988) (citing Wareham v. State, 25 Ohio St. 601 (1874)). Respondent could not plausibly claim unfair surprise that the doctrine applied in his case. See Lanier, supra, at 269\xe2\x80\x93270 (requiring, as adequate notice for due process purposes, only \xe2\x80\x9creasonable warning,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than fundamentally similar prior cases).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The foregoing provision was in effect at the time of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s crime in 1986. The Sixth Circuit reasoned, however, that the following subsequent clause in the version of \xc2\xa72903.01(D) that existed in 1986 foreclosed transferred intent in this case:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf a jury in an aggravated murder case is instructed that a person who commits or attempts to commit any offense listed in division (B) of this section may be inferred, because he engaged in a common design with others to commit the offense by force or violence or because the offense and the manner of its commission would be likely to produce death, to have intended to cause the death of any person who is killed during the commission of \xe2\x80\xa6 the offense, the jury also shall be instructed that \xe2\x80\xa6 it is to consider all evidence introduced by the prosecution to indicate the person\xe2\x80\x99s intent and by the person to indicate his lack of intent in determining whether the person specifically intended to cause the death of the person killed\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa72903.01(D) (Anderson 1982) (emphases added).

\n

\nContrary to the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reading, see 395 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 673, this clause by its terms did not apply to every case in which the defendant was charged with aggravated felony murder, but rather only to those in which intent to kill was sought to be proved from the inherent dangerousness of the relevant felony. See State v. Phillips, 74 Ohio St. 3d 72, 100, 656 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 643, 668 (1995) (\xe2\x80\x9cR.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72903.01(D) does not apply in this case because the trial court never instructed that the jury could infer purpose to kill from the commission of an underlying felony \xe2\x80\x98in a manner likely to produce death\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Here, however, intent to kill was proved directly. It was not inferred from the dangerousness of the arson; it was shown to be the purpose of the arson.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Circuit also argued that dicta in a case decided by an intermediate Ohio appellate court, prior to the Ohio Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion here, rejected transferred intent for respondent\xe2\x80\x99s crime, and thus rendered its application in respondent\xe2\x80\x99s case unforeseeable and retroactive. 395 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 675\xe2\x80\x93676 (citing State v. Mullins, 76 Ohio App.\xc2\xa03d 633, 602 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 769 (1992)). But that case was decided long after the 1986 offense for which respondent was convicted, and thus has no bearing on whether the law at the time of the charged conduct was clear enough to provide fair notice. Lanier, supra; see also Marks v. United States, 430 U. S. 188, 196 (1977).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the Sixth Circuit disregarded the Ohio Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s authoritative interpretation of Ohio law, its ruling on sufficiency of the evidence was erroneous.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Circuit also held that respondent was entitled to relief on the ground that the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 denial of his Strickland claim was unreasonable. 395 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 688. As petitioner contends, the Sixth Circuit erred in its adjudication of this claim by relying on evidence that was not properly presented to the state habeas courts without first determining (1) whether respondent was at fault for failing to develop the factual bases for his claims in state court, see Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 420, 430\xe2\x80\x93432 (2000), or (2) whether respondent satisfied the criteria established by 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(e)(2). See Holland v. Jackson, 452 U. S. 649, (2004) (per curiam). Similarly, the Sixth Circuit erred by disregarding the state habeas courts\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion that the forensic expert whom respondent\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel hired was a \xe2\x80\x9cproperly qualified expert,\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 347a, without analyzing whether the state court\xe2\x80\x99s factual finding had been rebutted by clear and convincing evidence. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(e)(1). Compare App. to Pet. for Cert., 347a, with 395 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 683. In addition, as petitioner contends, the Sixth Circuit erred in relying on certain grounds that were apparent from the trial record but not raised on direct appeal\xe2\x80\x94namely that trial counsel (1) inadequately cross-examined experts called by the State, (2) erred by prematurely placing the forensic expert counsel had hired on the witness list, and (3) failed to present competing scientific evidence against the State\xe2\x80\x99s forensic experts\xe2\x80\x94without first determining whether respondent\xe2\x80\x99s procedural default of these subclaims could be excused by a showing of cause and prejudice or by the need to avoid a miscarriage of justice. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 340a\xe2\x80\x93341a, 351a\xe2\x80\x93354a (state courts\xe2\x80\x99 holding that these subclaims should have been raised on direct appeal); id., at 109a\xe2\x80\x93110a (District Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that this default was not excusable under Coleman v. Thompson, 501 U. S. 722, 749\xe2\x80\x93750 (1991)). Respondent, however, contends that the State failed to preserve its objection to the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on evidence not presented in state court by failing to raise this argument properly before the Sixth Circuit. See Brief in Opposition 24\xe2\x80\x9326. Because the relevant errors had not yet occurred, the Sixth Circuit has had no opportunity to address the argument that the State failed to preserve its Holland argument. It is better situated to address this argument in the first instance.

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the foregoing reasons, the judgment of the Sixth Circuit is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
