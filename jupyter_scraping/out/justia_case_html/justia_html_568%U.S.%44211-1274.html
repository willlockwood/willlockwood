\n\t\t\t\tGABELLI et\xc2\xa0al. v. SECURITIES AND EXCHANGE COMMISSION\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x931274.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 8, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 27, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Investment Advisers Act makes it illegal for investment advisers to defraud their clients, 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa780b\xe2\x80\x936(1), (2), and authorizes the Securities and Exchange Commission to bring enforcement actions against investment advisers who violate the Act, or against individuals who aid and abet such violations, \xc2\xa780b\xe2\x80\x939(d). If the SEC seeks civil penalties as part of those actions, it must file suit \xe2\x80\x9cwithin five years from the date when the claim first accrued,\xe2\x80\x9d pursuant to a general statute of limitations that governs many penalty provisions throughout the U.\xc2\xa0S. Code, \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72462.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2008, the SEC sought civil penalties from petitioners Alpert and Gabelli. The complaint alleged that they aided and abetted investment adviser fraud from 1999 until 2002. Petitioners moved to dismiss, arguing in part that the civil penalty claim was untimely. Invoking the five-year statute of limitations in \xc2\xa72462, they pointed out that the complaint alleged illegal activity up until August 2002 but was not filed until April 2008. The District Court agreed and dismissed the civil penalty claim as time barred. The Second Circuit reversed, accepting the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s argument that because the underlying violations sounded in fraud, the \xe2\x80\x9cdiscovery rule\xe2\x80\x9d applied, meaning that the statute of limitations did not begin to run until the SEC discovered or reasonably could have discovered the fraud.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The five-year clock in \xc2\xa72462 begins to tick when the fraud occurs, not when it is discovered. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0This is the most natural reading of the statute. \xe2\x80\x9cIn common parlance a right accrues when it comes into existence.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Lindsay, \n\n346 U.\xc2\xa0S. 568\n. The \xe2\x80\x9cstandard rule\xe2\x80\x9d is that a claim accrues \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the plaintiff has \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9ca complete and present cause of action.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Wallace v. Kato, \n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 384\n. Such an understanding appears in cases and dictionaries from the 19th century, when the predecessor to \xc2\xa72462 was enacted. And this reading sets a fixed date when exposure to the specified Government enforcement efforts ends, advancing \xe2\x80\x9cthe basic policies of all limitations provisions: repose, elimination of stale claims, and certainty about a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s opportunity for recovery and a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s potential liabilities.\xe2\x80\x9d Rotella v. Wood, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549\n. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The Government nonetheless argues that the discovery rule should apply here. That doctrine is an \xe2\x80\x9cexception\xe2\x80\x9d to the standard rule, and delays accrual \xe2\x80\x9cuntil a plaintiff has \xe2\x80\x98discovered\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d his cause of action. Merck & Co. v. Reynolds, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. It arose from the recognition that \xe2\x80\x9csomething different was needed in the case of fraud, where a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s deceptive conduct may prevent a plaintiff from even knowing that he or she has been defrauded.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Thus \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a plaintiff has been injured by fraud and \xe2\x80\x98remains in ignorance of it without any fault or want of diligence or care on his part, the bar of the statute does not begin to run until the fraud is discovered.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Holmberg v. Armbrecht, \n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 392\n. This Court, however, has never applied the discovery rule in this context, where the plaintiff is not a defrauded victim seeking recompense, but is instead the Government bringing an enforcement action for civil penalties. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s case is not saved by Exploration Co. v. United States, \n\n247 U.\xc2\xa0S. 435\n. There, the discovery rule was applied in favor of the Government, but the Government was itself a victim; it had been defrauded and was suing to recover its loss. It was not bringing an enforcement action for penalties.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere are good reasons why the fraud discovery rule has not been extended to Government civil penalty enforcement actions. The discovery rule exists in part to preserve the claims of parties who have no reason to suspect fraud. The Government is a different kind of plaintiff. The SEC\xe2\x80\x99s very purpose, for example, is to root out fraud, and it has many legal tools at hand to aid in that pursuit. The Government in these types of cases also seeks a different type of relief. The discovery rule helps to ensure that the injured receive recompense, but civil penalties go beyond compensation, are intended to punish, and label defendants wrongdoers. Emphasizing the importance of time limits on penalty actions, Chief Justice Marshall admonished that it \xe2\x80\x9cwould be utterly repugnant to the genius of our laws\xe2\x80\x9d if actions for penalties could \xe2\x80\x9cbe brought at any distance of time.\xe2\x80\x9d Adams v. Woods, 2 Cranch 336, 342. Yet grafting the discovery rule onto \xc2\xa72462 would raise similar concerns. It would leave defendants exposed to Government enforcement action not only for five years after their misdeeds, but for an additional uncertain period into the future. And repose would hinge on speculation about what the Government knew, when it knew it, and when it should have known it. Deciding when the Government knew or reasonably should have known of a fraud would also present particular challenges for the courts, such as determining who the relevant actor is in assessing Government knowledge, whether and how to consider agency priorities and resource constraints in deciding when the Government reasonably should have known of a fraud, and so on. Applying a discovery rule to Government penalty actions is far more challenging than applying the rule to suits by defrauded victims, and the Court declines to do so. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t653 F.\xc2\xa03d 49, reversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tMARC J. GABELLI and BRUCE ALPERT, PETITIONERS v. SECURITIES AND EXCHANGE COMMISSION\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[February 27, 2013]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Investment Advisers Act makes it illegal for investment advisers to defraud their clients, and authorizes the Securities and Exchange Commission to seek civil penalties from advisers who do so. Under the general statute of limitations for civil penalty actions, the SEC has five years to seek such penalties. The question is whether the five-year clock begins to tick when the fraud is complete or when the fraud is discovered.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder the Investment Advisers Act of 1940, it is unlawful for an investment adviser \xe2\x80\x9cto employ any device, scheme, or artifice to defraud any client or prospective client\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cto engage in any transaction, practice, or course of business which operates as a fraud or deceit upon any client or prospective client.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n54Stat.\n852, as amended, 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa780b\xe2\x80\x936(1), (2). The Securities and Exchange Commission is authorized to bring enforcement actions against investment advisers who violate the Act, or individuals who aid and abet such violations. \xc2\xa780b\xe2\x80\x939(d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs part of such enforcement actions, the SEC may seek civil penalties, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa780b\xe2\x80\x939(e), (f) (2006 ed. and Supp. V), in which case a five-year statute of limitations applies:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cExcept as otherwise provided by Act of Congress, an action, suit or proceeding for the enforcement of any civil fine, penalty, or forfeiture, pecuniary or otherwise, shall not be entertained unless commenced within five years from the date when the claim first accrued if, within the same period, the offender or the property is found within the United States in order that proper service may be made thereon.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72462.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThis statute of limitations is not specific to the Investment Advisers Act, or even to securities law; it governs many penalty provisions throughout the U.\xc2\xa0S. Code. Its origins date back to at least 1839, and it took on its current form in 1948. See Act of Feb. 28, 1839, ch. 36, \xc2\xa74, \n\n5Stat.\n322.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGabelli Funds, LLC, is an investment adviser to a mutual fund formerly known as Gabelli Global Growth Fund (GGGF). Petitioner Bruce Alpert is Gabelli Funds\xe2\x80\x99 chief operating officer, and petitioner Marc Gabelli used to be GGGF\xe2\x80\x99s portfolio manager.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2008, the SEC brought a civil enforcement action against Alpert and Gabelli. According to the complaint, from 1999 until 2002 Alpert and Gabelli allowed one GGGF investor\xe2\x80\x94Headstart Advisers, Ltd.\xe2\x80\x94to engage in \xe2\x80\x9cmarket timing\xe2\x80\x9d in the fund.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs this Court has explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[m]arket timing is a trading strategy that exploits time delay in mutual funds\xe2\x80\x99 daily valuation system.\xe2\x80\x9d Janus Capital Group, Inc. v. First Derivative Traders, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, n. 1 (2011) (slip op., at 2, n.\xc2\xa01). Mutual funds are typically valued once a day, at the close of the New York Stock Exchange. Because funds often hold securities traded on different exchanges around the world, their reported valuation may be based on stale information. If a mutual fund\xe2\x80\x99s reported valuation is artificially low compared to its real value, market timers will buy that day and sell the next to realize quick profits. Market timing is not illegal but can harm long-term investors in a fund. See id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___, and n.\xc2\xa01 (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x933, and n.\xc2\xa01).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe SEC\xe2\x80\x99s complaint alleged that Alpert and Gabelli permitted Headstart to engage in market timing in exchange for Headstart\xe2\x80\x99s investment in a hedge fund run by Gabelli. According to the SEC, petitioners did not disclose Headstart\xe2\x80\x99s market timing or the quid pro quo agreement, and instead banned others from engaging in market timing and made statements indicating that the practice would not be tolerated. The complaint stated that during the relevant period, Headstart earned rates of return of up to 185%, while \xe2\x80\x9cthe rate of return for long-term investors in GGGF was no more than negative 24.1 percent.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 73.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe SEC alleged that Alpert and Gabelli aided and abetted violations of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa780b\xe2\x80\x936(1) and (2), and it sought civil penalties under \xc2\xa780b\xe2\x80\x939. Petitioners moved to dismiss, arguing in part that the claim for civil penalties was untimely. They invoked the five-year statute of limitations in \xc2\xa72462, pointing out that the complaint alleged market timing up until August 2002 but was not filed until April 2008. The District Court agreed and dismissed the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s civil penalty claim as time barred.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Second Circuit reversed. It acknowledged that \xc2\xa72462 required an action for civil penalties to be brought within five years \xe2\x80\x9cfrom the date when the claim first accrued,\xe2\x80\x9d but accepted the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s argument that because the underlying violations sounded in fraud, the \xe2\x80\x9cdiscovery rule\xe2\x80\x9d applied to the statute of limitations. As explained by the Second Circuit, \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nder the discovery rule, the statute of limitations for a particular claim does not accrue until that claim is discovered, or could have been discovered with reasonable diligence, by the plaintiff.\xe2\x80\x9d 653 F.\xc2\xa03d 49, 59 (2011). The court concluded that while \xe2\x80\x9cthis rule does not govern the accrual of most claims,\xe2\x80\x9d it does govern the claims at issue here. Ibid. As the court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cfor claims that sound in fraud a discovery rule is read into the relevant statute of limitation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 60.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari. 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case centers around the meaning of \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72462: \xe2\x80\x9can action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for the enforcement of any civil fine, penalty, or forfeiture .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall not be entertained unless commenced within five years from the date when the claim first accrued.\xe2\x80\x9d Petitioners argue that a claim based on fraud accrues\xe2\x80\x94and the five-year clock begins to tick\xe2\x80\x94when a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s allegedly fraudulent conduct occurs.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat is the most natural reading of the statute. \xe2\x80\x9cIn common parlance a right accrues when it comes into existence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Lindsay, \n\n346 U.\xc2\xa0S. 568, \n569 (1954)\n. Thus the \xe2\x80\x9cstandard rule\xe2\x80\x9d is that a claim accrues \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the plaintiff has a complete and present cause of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Wallace v. Kato, \n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 384, \n388 (2007)\n (internal quotation marks omitted); see also, e.g., Bay Area Laundry and Dry Cleaning Pension Trust Fund v. Ferbar Corp. of Cal., \n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 192, \n201 (1997)\n; Clark v. Iowa City, 20 Wall. 583, 589 (1875). That rule has governed since the 1830s when the predecessor to \xc2\xa72462 was enacted. See, e.g., Bank of United States v. Daniel, 12 Pet. 32, 56 (1838); Evans v. Gee, 11 Pet. 80, 84 (1837). And that definition appears in dictionaries from the 19th century up until today. See, e.g., 1 A. Burrill, A Law Dictionary and Glossary 17 (1850) (\xe2\x80\x9can action accrues when the plaintiff has a right to commence it\xe2\x80\x9d); Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 23 (9th ed. 2009) (defining \xe2\x80\x9caccrue\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o come into existence as an enforceable claim or right\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis reading sets a fixed date when exposure to the specified Government enforcement efforts ends, advancing \xe2\x80\x9cthe basic policies of all limitations provisions: repose, elimination of stale claims, and certainty about a plain- tiff\xe2\x80\x99s opportunity for recovery and a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s potential liabilities.\xe2\x80\x9d Rotella v. Wood, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549, \n555 (2000)\n. Statutes of limitations are intended to \xe2\x80\x9cpromote justice by preventing surprises through the revival of claims that have been allowed to slumber until evidence has been lost, memories have faded, and witnesses have disappeared.\xe2\x80\x9d Railroad Telegraphers v. Railway Express Agency, Inc., \n\n321 U.\xc2\xa0S. 342\n\xe2\x80\x93349 (1944). They provide \xe2\x80\x9csecurity and stability to human affairs.\xe2\x80\x9d Wood v. Carpenter, \n\n101 U.\xc2\xa0S. 135, \n139 (1879)\n. We have deemed them \xe2\x80\x9cvital to the welfare of society,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and concluded that \xe2\x80\x9ceven wrongdoers are entitled to assume that their sins may be forgot- ten,\xe2\x80\x9d Wilson v. Garcia, \n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 261, \n271 (1985)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNotwithstanding these considerations, the Government argues that the discovery rule should apply instead. Under this rule, accrual is delayed \xe2\x80\x9cuntil the plaintiff has \xe2\x80\x98discovered\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d his cause of action. Merck & Co. v. Reynolds, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 8). The doctrine arose in 18th-century fraud cases as an \xe2\x80\x9cexception\xe2\x80\x9d to the standard rule, based on the recognition that \xe2\x80\x9csomething different was needed in the case of fraud, where a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s deceptive conduct may prevent a plaintiff from even knowing that he or she has been defrauded.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This Court has held that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a plaintiff has been injured by fraud and \xe2\x80\x98remains in ignorance of it without any fault or want of diligence or care on his part, the bar of the statute does not begin to run until the fraud is discovered.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Holmberg v. Armbrecht, \n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 392, \n397 (1946)\n (quoting Bailey v. Glover, 21 Wall. 342, 348 (1875)). And we have explained that \xe2\x80\x9cfraud is deemed to be discovered when, in the exercise of reasonable diligence, it could have been discovered.\xe2\x80\x9d Merck & Co., supra, at ___ (slip op., at 9) (internal quotation marks and alterations omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut we have never applied the discovery rule in this context, where the plaintiff is not a defrauded victim seeking recompense, but is instead the Government bringing an enforcement action for civil penalties. Despite the discovery rule\xe2\x80\x99s centuries-old roots, the Government cites no lower court case before 2008 employing a fraud-based discovery rule in a Government enforcement action for civil penalties. See Brief for Respondent 23 (citing SEC v. Tambone, 550 F.\xc2\xa03d 106, 148\xe2\x80\x93149 (CA1 2008); SEC v. Koenig, 557 F.\xc2\xa03d 736, 739 (CA7 2009)). When pressed at oral argument, the Government conceded that it was aware of no such case. Tr. of Oral Arg. 25. The Government was also unable to point to any example from the first 160 years after enactment of this statute of limitations where it had even asserted that the fraud discovery rule applied in such a context. Id., at 26\xe2\x80\x9327 (citing only United States v. Maillard, 26 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 1140, 1142 (No. 15,709) (SDNY 1871), a \xe2\x80\x9cfraudulent concealment\xe2\x80\x9d case, see n.\xc2\xa02, supra).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tInstead the Government relies heavily on Exploration Co. v. United States, \n\n247 U.\xc2\xa0S. 435 (1918)\n, in an attempt to show that the discovery rule should benefit the Government to the same extent as private parties. See, e.g., Brief for Respondent 10\xe2\x80\x9311, 16, 17, 33\xe2\x80\x9334, 41\xe2\x80\x9345. In that case, a company had fraudulently procured land from the United States, and the United States sued to undo the trans- action. The company raised the statute of limitations as a defense, but this Court allowed the case to proceed, concluding that the rule \xe2\x80\x9cthat statutes of limitations upon suits to set aside fraudulent transactions shall not begin to run until the discovery of the fraud\xe2\x80\x9d applied \xe2\x80\x9cin favor of the Government as well as a private individual.\xe2\x80\x9d Exploration Co., supra, at 449. But in Exploration Co., the Government was itself a victim; it had been defrauded and was suing to recover its loss. The Government was not bringing an enforcement action for penalties. Exploration Co. cannot save the Government\xe2\x80\x99s case here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere are good reasons why the fraud discovery rule has not been extended to Government enforcement actions for civil penalties. The discovery rule exists in part to preserve the claims of victims who do not know they are injured and who reasonably do not inquire as to any injury. Usually when a private party is injured, he is imme- diately aware of that injury and put on notice that his time to sue is running. But when the injury is self-concealing, private parties may be unaware that they have been harmed. Most of us do not live in a state of constant investigation; absent any reason to think we have been injured, we do not typically spend our days looking for evidence that we were lied to or defrauded. And the law does not require that we do so. Instead, courts have developed the discovery rule, providing that the statute of limitations in fraud cases should typically begin to run only when the injury is or reasonably could have been discovered.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe same conclusion does not follow for the Government in the context of enforcement actions for civil penalties. The SEC, for example, is not like an individual victim who relies on apparent injury to learn of a wrong. Rather, a central \xe2\x80\x9cmission\xe2\x80\x9d of the Commission is to \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigat[e] potential violations of the federal securities laws.\xe2\x80\x9d SEC, Enforcement Manual 1 (2012). Unlike the private party who has no reason to suspect fraud, the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s very purpose is to root it out, and it has many legal tools at hand to aid in that pursuit. It can demand that securities brokers and dealers submit detailed trading information. Id., at 44. It can require investment advisers to turn over their comprehensive books and records at any time. \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa780b\xe2\x80\x934 (2006 ed. and Supp. V). And even without fil- ing suit, it can subpoena any documents and witnesses it deems relevant or material to an investigation. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa777s(c), 78u(b), 80a\xe2\x80\x9341(b), 80b\xe2\x80\x939(b) (2006 ed.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe SEC is also authorized to pay monetary awards to whistleblowers, who provide information relating to violations of the securities laws. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x936 (2006 ed., Supp. V). In addition, the SEC may offer \xe2\x80\x9ccooperation agreements\xe2\x80\x9d to violators to procure information about others in exchange for more lenient treatment. See Enforcement Manual, at 119\xe2\x80\x93137. Charged with this mission and armed with these weapons, the SEC as enforcer is a far cry from the defrauded victim the discovery rule evolved to protect.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn a civil penalty action, the Government is not only a different kind of plaintiff, it seeks a different kind of relief. The discovery rule helps to ensure that the injured receive recompense. But this case involves penalties, which go beyond compensation, are intended to punish, and label defendants wrongdoers. See Meeker v. Lehigh Valley R. Co., \n\n236 U.\xc2\xa0S. 412, \n423 (1915)\n (a penalty covered by the predecessor to \xc2\xa72462 is \xe2\x80\x9csomething imposed in a punitive way for an infraction of a public law\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Tull v. United States, \n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 412, \n422 (1987)\n (penalties are \xe2\x80\x9cintended to punish culpable individuals,\xe2\x80\x9d not \xe2\x80\x9cto extract compensation or restore the status quo\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChief Justice Marshall used particularly forceful language in emphasizing the importance of time limits on penalty actions, stating that it \xe2\x80\x9cwould be utterly repugnant to the genius of our laws\xe2\x80\x9d if actions for penalties could \xe2\x80\x9cbe brought at any distance of time.\xe2\x80\x9d Adams v. Woods, 2 Cranch 336, 342 (1805). \n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tYet grafting the discovery rule onto \xc2\xa72462 would raise similar concerns. It would leave defendants exposed to Government enforcement action not only for five years after their misdeeds, but for an additional uncertain period into the future. Repose would hinge on speculation about what the Government knew, when it knew it, and when it should have known it. See Rotella, 528 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 554 (disapproving a rule that would have \xe2\x80\x9cextended the limitations period to many decades\xe2\x80\x9d because such a rule was \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond any limit that Congress could have contemplated\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwould have thwarted the basic objective of repose underlying the very notion of a limitations period\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDetermining when the Government, as opposed to an individual, knew or reasonably should have known of a fraud presents particular challenges for the courts. Agencies often have hundreds of employees, dozens of offices, and several levels of leadership. In such a case, when does \xe2\x80\x9cthe Government\xe2\x80\x9d know of a violation? Who is the relevant actor? Different agencies often have overlapping responsibilities; is the knowledge of one attributed to all?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn determining what a plaintiff should have known, we ask what facts \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonably diligent plaintiff would have discovered.\xe2\x80\x9d Merck & Co., 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8). It is unclear whether and how courts should consider agency priorities and resource constraints in applying that test to Government enforcement actions. See 3M Co. v. Browner, 17 F.\xc2\xa03d 1453, 1461 (CADC 1994) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn agency may experience problems in detecting statutory violations because its enforcement effort is not sufficiently funded; or because the agency has not devoted an adequate number of trained personnel to the task; or because the agency\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement program is ill-designed or inefficient; or because the nature of the statute makes it difficult to uncover violations; or because of some combination of these factors and others\xe2\x80\x9d). And in the midst of any inquiry as to what it knew when, the Government can be expected to assert various privileges, such as law enforcement, attorney-client, work product, or deliberative process, further complicating judicial attempts to apply the discovery rule. See, e.g., App. in No. 10\xe2\x80\x933581 (CA2), p. 147 (Government invoking such privileges in this case, in response to a request for documents relating to the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s investigation of Headstart); see also Rotella, supra, at 559 (rejecting a rule in part due to \xe2\x80\x9cthe controversy inherent in divining when a plaintiff should have discovered\xe2\x80\x9d a wrong).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo be sure, Congress has expressly required such inquiries in some statutes. But in many of those instances, the Government is itself an injured victim looking for recompense, not a prosecutor seeking penalties. See, e.g., 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72415, 2416(c) (Government suits for money dam- ages founded on contracts or torts). Moreover, statutes applying a discovery rule in the context of Govern- ment suits often couple that rule with an absolute provision for repose, which a judicially imposed discovery rule would lack. See, e.g., \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7335b(b)(3) (limiting certain Government civil penalty actions to \xe2\x80\x9c6 years after the date when facts material to the act are known or reasonably should have been known by the Secretary but in no event more than 10 years after the date the act took place\xe2\x80\x9d). And several statutes applying a discovery rule to the Government make some effort to identify the official whose knowledge is relevant. See \n\n31 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73731(b)(2) (relevant knowledge is that of \xe2\x80\x9cthe official of the United States charged with responsibility to act in the circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApplying a discovery rule to Government penalty actions is far more challenging than applying the rule to suits by defrauded victims, and we have no mandate from Congress to undertake that challenge here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs we held long ago, the cases in which \xe2\x80\x9ca statute of limitation may be suspended by causes not mentioned in the statute itself .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are very limited in character, and are to be admitted with great caution; otherwise the court would make the law instead of administering it.\xe2\x80\x9d Amy v. Watertown (No. 2), \n\n130 U.\xc2\xa0S. 320, \n324 (1889)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). Given the lack of textual, historical, or equitable reasons to graft a discovery rule onto the statute of limitations of \xc2\xa72462, we decline to do so.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t
