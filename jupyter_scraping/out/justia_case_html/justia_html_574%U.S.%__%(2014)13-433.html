Syllabus

\n

integrity staffing solutions, INC. v. busk et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93433.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 8, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided December 9, 2014

\n

Petitioner Integrity Staffing Solutions, Inc., required its hourly warehouse workers, who retrieved products from warehouse shelves and packaged them for delivery to Amazon.com customers, to undergo a security screening before leaving the warehouse each day. Respondents, former employees, sued the company alleging, as relevant here, that they were entitled to compensation under the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (FLSA) for the roughly 25 minutes each day that they spent waiting to undergo and undergoing those screenings. They also alleged that the company could have reduced that time to a de minimis amount by adding screeners or staggering shift terminations and that the screenings were conducted to prevent employee theft and, thus, for the sole benefit of the employers and their customers.

\n

\n \n \n \n The District Court dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim, holding that the screenings were not integral and indispensable to the employees\xe2\x80\x99 principal activities but were instead postliminary and noncompensable. The U.\xc2\xa0S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed in relevant part, asserting that postshift activities that would ordinarily be classified as noncompensable postliminary activities are compensable as integral and indispensable to an employee\xe2\x80\x99s principal activities if the postshift activities are necessary to the principal work and performed for the employer\xe2\x80\x99s benefit.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The time that respondents spent waiting to undergo and undergoing security screenings is not compensable under the FLSA. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n \n (a)\xc2\xa0Congress passed the Portal-to-Portal Act to respond to an economic emergency created by the broad judicial interpretation given to the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s undefined terms \xe2\x80\x9cwork\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cworkweek.\xe2\x80\x9d See 29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7251(a); Tennessee Coal, Iron & R. Co. v. Muscoda Local No. 123,\n\n321 U.\xc2\xa0S. 590\n. The Portal-to-Portal Act exempted employers from FLSA liability for claims based on \xe2\x80\x9cactivities which are preliminary to or postliminary to\xe2\x80\x9d the performance of the principal activities that an employee is employed to perform. \xc2\xa7254(a)(2). Under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, the term \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal activities\xe2\x80\x9d includes all activities which are an \xe2\x80\x9cintegral and indispensable part of the principal activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Steiner v. Mitchell,\n\n350 U.\xc2\xa0S. 247\n\xe2\x80\x93253. An activity is \xe2\x80\x9cintegral and indispensable\xe2\x80\x9d if it is an intrinsic element of the employee\xe2\x80\x99s principal activities and one with which the employee cannot dispense if he is to perform his principal activities. This Court has identified several activities that satisfy this test\xe2\x80\x94see, e.g., id., at 249, 251; Mitchell v. King Packing Co.,\n\n350 U.\xc2\xa0S. 260\n\xe2\x80\x94and Department of Labor regulations are consistent with this approach, see 29 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7790.8(c), 790.7(g). Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n \n (b)\xc2\xa0The security screenings at issue are noncompensable postliminary activities. To begin with, the screenings were not the principal activities the employees were employed to perform\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the workers were employed not to undergo security screenings but to retrieve products from warehouse shelves and package them for shipment. Nor were they \xe2\x80\x9cintegral and indispensable\xe2\x80\x9d to those activities. This view is consistent with a 1951 Department of Labor opinion letter, which found noncompensable under the Portal-to-Portal Act both a preshift screening conducted for employee safety and a postshift search conducted to prevent employee theft. The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s test, which focused on whether the particular activity was required by the employer rather than whether it was tied to the productive work that the employee was employed to perform, would sweep into \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal activities\xe2\x80\x9d the very activities that the Portal-to-Portal Act was designed to exclude from compensation. See, e.g., IBP, supra, at 41. Finally, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim that the screenings are compensable because Integrity Staffing could have reduced the time to a de minimis amount is properly presented at the bargaining table, not to a court in an FLSA claim. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

713 F. 3d 525, reversed.

\n

INTEGRITY STAFFING SOLUTIONS, INC., PETITIONER v. JESSE BUSK et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[December 9, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \n Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n \n The employer in this case required its employees, warehouse workers who retrieved inventory and packaged itfor shipment, to undergo an antitheft security screen-ing before leaving the warehouse each day. The question presented is whether the employees\xe2\x80\x99 time spent waiting to undergo and undergoing those security screenings is compensable under the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (FLSA),\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7201 et\xc2\xa0seq., as amended by the Portal-to-Portal Act of 1947, \xc2\xa7251 et\xc2\xa0seq. We hold that the time is not compensable. We therefore reverse the judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit.

\n

I

\n

\n \n Petitioner Integrity Staffing Solutions, Inc., provides warehouse staffing to Amazon.com throughout the United States. Respondents Jesse Busk and Laurie Castro worked as hourly employees of Integrity Staffing at warehouses in Las Vegas and Fenley, Nevada, respectively. As warehouse employees, they retrieved products from the shelves and packaged those products for delivery to Amazon customers.

\n

\n \n Integrity Staffing required its employees to undergo a security screening before leaving the warehouse at the end of each day. During this screening, employees removed items such as wallets, keys, and belts from their persons and passed through metal detectors.

\n

\n \n In 2010, Busk and Castro filed a putative class action against Integrity Staffing on behalf of similarly situated employees in the Nevada warehouses for alleged violations of the FLSA and Nevada labor laws. As relevant here, the employees alleged that they were entitled to compensation under the FLSA for the time spent waiting to undergo and actually undergoing the security screenings. They alleged that such time amounted to roughly 25 minutes each day and that it could have been reduced to a de minimis amount by adding more security screeners or by staggering the termination of shifts so that employees could flow through the checkpoint more quickly. They also alleged that the screenings were conducted \xe2\x80\x9cto prevent employee theft\xe2\x80\x9d and thus occurred \xe2\x80\x9csolely for the benefit of the employers and their customers.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 19, 21.

\n

\nThe District Court dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim, holding that the time spent waiting for and undergoing the security screenings was not compensable under the FLSA. It explained that, because the screenings occurred after the regular work shift, the employees could state a claim for compensation only if the screenings were an integral and indispensable part of the principal activities they were employed to perform. The District Court held that these screenings were not integral and indispensable but instead fell into a noncompensable category of postliminary activities.

\n

\n \n The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed in relevant part. 713 F.\xc2\xa03d 525 (2013). The Court of Appeals asserted that postshift activities that would ordinarily be classified as noncompensable postliminary activities are nevertheless compensable as integral and indispensable to an employee\xe2\x80\x99s principal activities if those postshift activities are necessary to the principal work performed and done for the benefit of the employer. Id., at 530. Accepting as true the allegation that Integrity Staffing required the security screenings to prevent employee theft, the Court of Appeals concluded that the screenings were \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d to the employees\xe2\x80\x99 primary work as warehouse employees and done for Integrity Staffing\xe2\x80\x99s benefit. Id., at 531.

\n

\n \n We granted certiorari, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n \n Enacted in 1938, the FLSA established a minimum wage and overtime compensation for each hour worked in excess of 40 hours in each workweek. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa76(a)(1), 7(a)(3),\n\n52Stat.\n1062\xe2\x80\x931063. An employer who violated these provisions could be held civilly liable for backpay, liquidated damages, and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. \xc2\xa716, id., at 1069.

\n

\n \n But the FLSA did not define \xe2\x80\x9cwork\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cworkweek,\xe2\x80\x9d and this Court interpreted those terms broadly. It defined \xe2\x80\x9cwork\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cphysical or mental exertion (whether burdensome or not) controlled or required by the employer and pursued necessarily and primarily for the benefit of the employer and his business.\xe2\x80\x9d Tennessee Coal, Iron & R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Muscoda Local No. 123,\n\n321 U.\xc2\xa0S. 590,\n598 (1944)\n. Similarly, it defined \xe2\x80\x9cthe statutory workweek\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cinclud[e] all time during which an employee is necessarily required to be on the employer\xe2\x80\x99s premises, on duty or at a prescribed workplace.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Mt. Clemens Pottery Co.,\n\n328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 680\n\xe2\x80\x93691 (1946). Applying these expansive definitions, the Court found compensable the time spent traveling between mine portals and underground work areas, Tennessee Coal, supra, at 598, and the time spent walking from timeclocks to work benches, Anderson, supra, at 691\xe2\x80\x93692.

\n

\n \n These decisions provoked a flood of litigation. In the six months following this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Anderson, unions and employees filed more than 1,500 lawsuits under the FLSA. S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 37, 80th Cong., 1st Sess., pp. 2\xe2\x80\x933 (1947). These suits sought nearly $6 billion in back pay and liquidated damages for various preshift and postshift activities. Ibid.\xc2\xa0

\n

\n \n Congress responded swiftly. It found that the FLSAhad \xe2\x80\x9cbeen interpreted judicially in disregard of long-established customs, practices, and contracts between employers and employees, thereby creating wholly unexpected liabilities, immense in amount and retroactive in operation, upon employers.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7251(a). Declaring the situation to be an \xe2\x80\x9cemergency,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress found that, if such interpretations \xe2\x80\x9cwere permitted to stand, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the payment of such liabilities would bring about financial ruin of many employers\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cemployees would receive windfall payments .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for activities performed by them without any expectation of reward beyond that included in their agreed rates of pay.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7251(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b).

\n

\n \n Congress met this emergency with the Portal-to-Portal Act. The Portal-to-Portal Act exempted employers from liability for future claims based on two categories of work-related activities as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(a) Except as provided in subsection (b) [which covers work compensable by contract or custom], no employer shall be subject to any liability or punishment under the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938, as amended, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. on account of the failure of such employer .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.to pay an employee overtime compensation, for or on account of any of the following activities of such employee engaged in on or after the date of the enactment of this Act\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(1) walking, riding, or traveling to and from the ac-tual place of performance of the principal activity or ac-tivities which such employee is employed to perform, and

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(2) activities which are preliminary to or postliminary to said principal activity or activities,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cwhich occur either prior to the time on any particular workday at which such employee commences, or subsequent to the time on any particular workday at which he ceases, such principal activity or activities.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa74,\n\n61Stat.\n86\xe2\x80\x9387 (codified at\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7254(a)).

\n

At issue here is the exemption for \xe2\x80\x9cactivities which are preliminary to or postliminary to said principal activity or activities.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

B

\n

\n \n This Court has consistently interpreted \xe2\x80\x9cthe term \xe2\x80\x98principal activity or activities\xe2\x80\x99 [to] embrac[e] all activities which are an \xe2\x80\x98integral and indispensable part of the principal activities.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d IBP, Inc. v. Alvarez,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 21\n\xe2\x80\x9330 (2005) (quoting Steiner v. Mitchell,\n\n350 U.\xc2\xa0S. 247\n\xe2\x80\x93253 (1956)). Our prior opinions used those words in their ordinary sense. The word \xe2\x80\x9cintegral\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9c[b]elonging to or making up an integral whole; constituent, component; spec[ifically] necessary to the completeness or integrity of the whole; forming an intrinsic portion or element, as distinguished from an adjunct or appendage.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 Oxford English Dictionary 366 (1933) (OED); accord, Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 20 (Brief for United States); see also Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 1290 (2d ed. 1954) (Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Second) (\xe2\x80\x9c[e]ssential to completeness; constituent, as a part\xe2\x80\x9d). And, when used to describe a duty, \xe2\x80\x9cindispensable\xe2\x80\x9d means a duty \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hat cannot be dispensed with, remitted, set aside, disregarded, or neglected.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 OED 219; accord, Brief for United States 19; see also Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Second 1267 (\xe2\x80\x9c[n]ot capable of being dispensed with, set aside, neglected, or pronounced nonobligatory\xe2\x80\x9d). An activity is therefore integral and indispensable to the principal activities that an employee is employed to perform if it is an intrinsic element of those activities and one with which the employee cannot dispense if he is to perform his principal activities. As we describe below, this definition, as applied in these circumstances, is consistent with the Department of Labor\xe2\x80\x99s regulations.

\n

\n \n Our precedents have identified several activities that satisfy this test. For example, we have held compensable the time battery-plant employees spent showering and changing clothes because the chemicals in the plant were \xe2\x80\x9ctoxic to human beings\xe2\x80\x9d and the employer conceded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe clothes-changing and showering activities of the employees [were] indispensable to the performance of their productive work and integrally related thereto.\xe2\x80\x9d Steiner, supra, at 249, 251. And we have held compensable the time meatpacker employees spent sharpening their knives because dull knives would \xe2\x80\x9cslow down production\xe2\x80\x9d on the assembly line, \xe2\x80\x9caffect the appearance of the meat as well as the quality of the hides,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccause waste,\xe2\x80\x9d and lead to \xe2\x80\x9caccidents.\xe2\x80\x9d Mitchell v. King Packing Co.,\n\n350 U.\xc2\xa0S. 260,\n262 (1956)\n. By contrast, we have held noncompensable the time poultry-plant employees spent waiting to don protective gear because such waiting was \xe2\x80\x9ctwo steps removed from the productive activity on the assembly line.\xe2\x80\x9d IBP, supra, at 42.

\n

\n \n The Department of Labor\xe2\x80\x99s regulations are consistent with this approach. See 29 CFR \xc2\xa7790.8(b) (2013) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe term \xe2\x80\x98principal activities\xe2\x80\x99 includes all activities which are an integral part of a principal activity\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7790.8(c) (\xe2\x80\x9cAmong the activities included as an integral part of a principal activity are those closely related activities which are indispensable to its performance\xe2\x80\x9d). As an illustration, those regulations explain that the time spent by an employee in a chemical plant changing clothes would be compensable if he \xe2\x80\x9cc[ould not] perform his principal activities without putting on certain clothes\xe2\x80\x9d but would not be compensable if \xe2\x80\x9cchanging clothes [were] merely a convenience to the employee and not directly related to his principal activities.\xe2\x80\x9d See \xc2\xa7790.8(c). As the regulations explain, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen performed under the conditions normally present,\xe2\x80\x9d activities including \xe2\x80\x9cchecking in and out and waiting in line to do so, changing clothes, washing up or showering, and waiting in line to receive pay checks\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98preliminary\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98postliminary\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d activities. \xc2\xa7790.7(g).

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n \n The security screenings at issue here are noncompensable postliminary activities. To begin with, the screenings were not the \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal activity or activities which [the] employee is employed to perform.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7254(a)(1). Integrity Staffing did not employ its workers to undergo security screenings, but to retrieve products from warehouse shelves and package those products for shipment to Amazon customers.

\n

\n \n The security screenings also were not \xe2\x80\x9cintegral and indispensable\xe2\x80\x9d to the employees\xe2\x80\x99 duties as warehouse workers. As explained above, an activity is not integral and indispensable to an employee\xe2\x80\x99s principal activities unless it is an intrinsic element of those activities and one with which the employee cannot dispense if he is to perform those activities. The screenings were not an intrinsic element of retrieving products from warehouse shelves or packaging them for shipment. And Integrity Staffing could have eliminated the screenings altogether without impairing the employees\xe2\x80\x99 ability to complete their work.

\n

\n \n The Solicitor General, adopting the position of the Department of Labor, agrees that these screenings were noncompensable postliminary activities. See Brief for United States 10. That view is fully consistent with an Opinion Letter the Department issued in 1951. The letter found noncompensable a preshift security search of employees in a rocket-powder plant \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98for matches, spark producing devices such as cigarette lighters, and other items which have a direct bearing on the safety of the employees,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d as well as a postshift security search of the employees done \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98for the purpose of preventing theft.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Opinion Letter from Dept. of Labor, Wage and Hour Div., to Dept. of Army, Office of Chief of Ordnance (Apr. 18, 1951), pp. 1\xe2\x80\x932 (available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). The Department drew no distinction between the searches conducted for the safety of the employees and those conducted for the purpose of preventing theft\xe2\x80\x94neither were compensable under the Portal-to-Portal Act.

\n

B

\n

\n \n The Court of Appeals erred by focusing on whether an employer required a particular activity. The integral and indispensable test is tied to the productive work that the employee is employed to perform. See, e.g., IBP, 546 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 42; Mitchell, supra, at 262; Steiner, 350 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 249\xe2\x80\x93251; see also 29 CFR \xc2\xa7790.8(a) (explaining that the term \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal activities\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cconsidered sufficiently broad to embrace within its terms such activities as are indispensable to the performance of productive work\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted; emphasis added)); \xc2\xa7790.8(c) (\xe2\x80\x9cAmong the activities included as an integral part of a principal activity are those closely related activities which are indispensable to its performance\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\n \n If the test could be satisfied merely by the fact that an employer required an activity, it would sweep into \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal activities\xe2\x80\x9d the very activities that the Portal-to-Portal Act was designed to address. The employer in Anderson, for instance, required its employees to walk \xe2\x80\x9cfrom a timeclock near the factory gate to a workstation\xe2\x80\x9d so that they could \xe2\x80\x9cbegin their work,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cbut it is indisputable that the Portal-to-Portal Act evinces Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to repudiate Anderson\xe2\x80\x99s holding that such walking time was compensable under the FLSA.\xe2\x80\x9d IBP, supra, at 41. A test that turns on whether the activity is for the benefit of the employer is similarly overbroad.

\n

\n \n Finally, we reject the employees\xe2\x80\x99 argument that time spent waiting to undergo the security screenings is compensable under the FLSA because Integrity Staffing could have reduced that time to a de minimis amount. The fact that an employer could conceivably reduce the time spent by employees on any preliminary or postliminary activity does not change the nature of the activity or its relationship to the principal activities that an employee is employed to perform. These arguments are properly presented to the employer at the bargaining table, see\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7254(b)(1), not to a court in an FLSA claim.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n \n We hold that an activity is integral and indispensable to the principal activities that an employee is employed to perform\xe2\x80\x94and thus compensable under the FLSA\xe2\x80\x94if it is an intrinsic element of those activities and one with which the employee cannot dispense if he is to perform his principal activities. Because the employees\xe2\x80\x99 time spent waiting to undergo and undergoing Integrity Staffing\xe2\x80\x99s security screenings does not meet these criteria, we reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

It is so ordered.

INTEGRITY STAFFING SOLUTIONS, INC., PETITIONER v. JESSE BUSK et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[December 9, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \n Justice Sotomayor, with whom Justice Kagan joins, concurring.

\n

\n \n I concur in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, and write separately only to explain my understanding of the standards the Court applies.

\n

\n \n The Court reaches two critical conclusions. First, the Court confirms that compensable \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98principal\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d activities \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98includ[e] . . . those closely related activities which are indispensable to [a principal activity\xe2\x80\x99s] performance,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6 (quoting 29 CFR \xc2\xa7790.8(c)(2013)), and holds that the required security screenings here were not \xe2\x80\x9cintegral and indispensable\xe2\x80\x9d to another principal activity the employees were employed to perform, ante, at 7. I agree. As both Department of Labor regulations and our precedent make clear, an activity is \xe2\x80\x9cindispensable\xe2\x80\x9d to another, principal activity only when an employee could not dispense with it without impairing his ability to perform the principal activity safely and effectively. Thus, although a battery plant worker might, for example, perform his principal activities without donning proper protective gear, he could not do so safely, see Steiner v. Mitchell,\n\n350 U. S. 247\n\xe2\x80\x93253 (1956); likewise, a butcher might be able to cut meat without having sharpened his knives, but he could not do so effectively, see Mitchell v. King Packing Co.,\n\n350 U. S. 260\n\xe2\x80\x93263 (1956); accord, 29 CFR \xc2\xa7790.8(c). Here, by contrast, the security screenings were not \xe2\x80\x9cintegral and indispensable\xe2\x80\x9d to the employees\xe2\x80\x99 other principal activities in this sense. The screenings may, as the Ninth Circuit observed below, have been in some way related to the work that the employees performed in the warehouse, see 713 F. 3d 525, 531 (2013), but the employees could skip the screenings altogether without the safety or effectiveness of their principal activities being substantially impaired, see ante, at 7.

\n

\n \n Second, the Court holds also that the screenings were not themselves \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98principal . . . activities\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the employees were \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98employed to perform.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting\n\n29 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7254(a)(1)). On this point, I understand the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis to turn on its conclusion that undergoing security screenings was not itself work of consequence that the employees performed for their employer. See ante, at 7. Again, I agree. As the statute\xe2\x80\x99s use of the words \xe2\x80\x9cpreliminary\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpostliminary\xe2\x80\x9d suggests, \xc2\xa7254(a)(2), and as our precedents make clear, the Portal-to-Portal Act of 1947 is primarily concerned with defining the beginning and end of the workday. See IBP, Inc. v. Alvarez,\n\n546 U. S. 21\n,34\xe2\x80\x9337 (2005). It distinguishes between activities that are essentially part of the ingress and egress process, on the one hand, and activities that constitute the actual \xe2\x80\x9cwork of consequence performed for an employer,\xe2\x80\x9d on the other hand. 29 CFR \xc2\xa7790.8(a); see also ibid. (clarifying that a principal activity need not predominate over other activities, and that an employee could be employed to perform multiple principal activities). The security screenings at issue here fall on the \xe2\x80\x9cpreliminary . . . or postliminary\xe2\x80\x9d side of this line.\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7254(a)(2). The searches were part of the process by which the employees egressed their place of work, akin to checking in and out and waiting in line to do so\xe2\x80\x94activities that Congress clearly deemed to be preliminary or postlimininary. See S. Rep. No. 48, 80th Cong., 1st Sess., 47 (1947); 29 CFR \xc2\xa7790.7(g). Indeed, as the Court observes, the Department of Labor reached the very same conclusion regarding similar security screenings shortly after the Portal-to-Portal Act was adopted, see ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, and we owe deference to that determination, see Christensen v. Harris County,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 576,\n587 (2000)\n.
