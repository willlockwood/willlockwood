WILLIAMS v. PENNSYLVANIA

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of pennsylvania

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x935040.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 29, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 9, 2016

\n

Petitioner Williams was convicted of the 1984 murder of Amos Norwood and sentenced to death. During the trial, the then-district attorney of Philadelphia, Ronald Castille, approved the trial prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s request to seek the death penalty against Williams. Over the next 26 years, Williams\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and sentence were upheld on direct appeal, state postconviction review, and federal habeas review. In 2012, Williams filed a successive petition pursuant to Pennsylvania\xe2\x80\x99s Post Conviction Relief Act (PCRA), arguing that the prosecutor had obtained false testimony from his codefendant and suppressed material, exculpatory evidence in violation of Brady v. Maryland,\n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83\n. Finding that the trial prosecutor had committed Brady violations, the PCRA court stayed Williams\xe2\x80\x99s execution and ordered a new sentencing hearing. The Commonwealth asked the Pennsylvania Supreme Court, whose chief justice was former District Attorney Castille, to vacate the stay. Williams filed a response, along with a motion asking Chief Justice Castille to recuse himself or, if he declined to do so, to refer the motion to the full court for decision. Without explanation, the chief justice denied Williams\xe2\x80\x99s motion for recusal and the request for its referral. He then joined the State Supreme Court opinion vacating the PCRA court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of penalty-phase relief and reinstating Williams\xe2\x80\x99s death sentence. Two weeks later, Chief Justice Castille retired from the bench.

\n

Held: \xc2\xa0

\n

1.\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s denial of the recusal motion and his subsequent judicial participation violated the Due Process Clause of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s due process precedents do not set forth a specific test governing recusal when a judge had prior involvement in a case as a prosecutor; but the principles on which these precedents rest dictate the rule that must control in the circumstances here: Under the Due Process Clause there is an impermissible risk of actual bias when a judge earlier had significant, personal involvement as a prosecutor in a critical decision regarding the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s case. The Court applies an objective standard that requires recusal when the likelihood of bias on the part of the judge \xe2\x80\x9cis too high to be constitutionally tolerable.\xe2\x80\x9d Caperton v. A. T. Massey Coal Co.,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 868\n. A constitutionally intolerable probability of bias exists when the same person serves as both accuser and adjudicator in a case. See In re Murchison,\n\n349 U.\xc2\xa0S. 133\n\xe2\x80\x93137. No attorney is more integral to the accusatory process than a prosecutor who participates in a major adversary decision. As a result, a serious question arises as to whether a judge who has served as an advocate for the State in the very case the court is now asked to adjudicate would be influenced by an improper, if inadvertent, motive to validate and preserve the result obtained through the adversary process. In these circumstances, neither the involvement of multiple actors in the case nor the passage of time relieves the former prosecutor of the duty to withdraw in order to ensure the neutrality of the judicial process in determining the consequences his or her own earlier, critical decision may have set in motion. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Because Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s authorization to seek the death penalty against Williams amounts to significant, personal involvement in a critical trial decision, his failure to recuse from Williams\xe2\x80\x99s case presented an unconstitutional risk of bias. The decision to pursue the death penalty is a critical choice in the adversary process, and Chief Justice Castille had a significant role in this decision. Without his express authorization, the Commonwealth would not have been able to pursue a death sentence against Williams. Given the importance of this decision and the profound consequences it carries, a responsible prosecutor would deem it to be a most significant exercise of his or her official discretion. The fact that many jurisdictions, including Pennsylvania, have statutes and professional codes of conduct that already require recusal under the circumstances of this case suggests that today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will not occasion a significant change in recusal practice. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0An unconstitutional failure to recuse constitutes structural error that is \xe2\x80\x9cnot amenable\xe2\x80\x9d to harmless-error review, regardless of whether the judge\xe2\x80\x99s vote was dispositive, Puckett v. United States,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 129\n. Because an appellate panel\xe2\x80\x99s deliberations are generally confidential, it is neither possible nor productive to inquire whether the jurist in question might have influenced the views of his or her colleagues during the decisionmaking process. Indeed, one purpose of judicial confidentiality is to ensure that jurists can reexamine old ideas and suggest new ones, while both seeking to persuade and being open to persuasion by their colleagues. It does not matter whether the disqualified judge\xe2\x80\x99s vote was necessary to the disposition of the case. The fact that the interested judge\xe2\x80\x99s vote was not dispositive may mean only that the judge was successful in persuading most members of the court to accept his or her position\xe2\x80\x94an outcome that does not lessen the unfairness to the affected party. A multimember court must not have its guarantee of neutrality undermined, for the appearance of bias demeans the reputation and integrity not just of one jurist, but of the larger institution of which he or she is a part. Because Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s participation in Williams\xe2\x80\x99s case was an error that affected the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s whole adjudicatory framework below, Williams must be granted an opportunity to present his claims to a court unburdened by any \xe2\x80\x9cpossible temptation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not to hold the balance nice, clear and true between the State and the accused,\xe2\x80\x9d Tumey v. Ohio,\n\n273 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510\n. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.

TERRANCE WILLIAMS, PETITIONER v. PENNSYLVANIA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of pennsylvania, eastern district

\n

[June 9, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

In this case, the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania vacated the decision of a postconviction court, which had granted relief to a prisoner convicted of first-degree murder and sentenced to death. One of the justices on the State Supreme Court had been the district attorney who gave his official approval to seek the death penalty in the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s case. The justice in question denied the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s motion for recusal and participated in the decision to deny relief. The question presented is whether the justice\xe2\x80\x99s denial of the recusal motion and his subsequent judicial participation violated the Due Process Clause of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment.

\n

This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents set forth an objective standard that requires recusal when the likelihood of bias on the part of the judge \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is too high to be constitutionally tolerable.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Caperton v. A. T. Massey Coal Co.,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 868,\n872 (2009)\n (quoting Withrow v. Larkin,\n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35,\n47 (1975)\n). Applying this standard, the Court concludes that due process compelled the justice\xe2\x80\x99s recusal.

\n

I

\n

Petitioner is Terrance Williams. In 1984, soon after Williams turned 18, he murdered 56-year-old Amos Norwood in Philadelphia. At trial, the Commonwealth presented evidence that Williams and a friend, Marc Draper, had been standing on a street corner when Norwood drove by. Williams and Draper requested a ride home from Norwood, who agreed. Draper then gave Norwood false directions that led him to drive toward a cemetery. Williams and Draper ordered Norwood out of the car and into the cemetery. There, the two men tied Norwood in his own clothes and beat him to death. Testifying for the Commonwealth, Draper suggested that robbery was the motive for the crime. Williams took the stand in his own defense, stating that he was not involved in the crime and did not know the victim.

\n

During the trial, the prosecutor requested permission from her supervisors in the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office to seek the death penalty against Williams. To support the request, she prepared a memorandum setting forth the details of the crime, information supporting two statutory aggravating factors, and facts in mitigation. After reviewing the memorandum, the then-district attorney of Philadelphia, Ronald Castille, wrote this note at the bottom of the document: \xe2\x80\x9cApproved to proceed on the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 426a.

\n

During the penalty phase of the trial, the prosecutor argued that Williams deserved a death sentence because he killed Norwood \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98for no other reason but that a kind man offered him a ride home.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 7. The jurors found two aggravating circumstances: that the murder was committed during the course of a robbery and that Williams had a significant history of violent felony convictions. That criminal history included a previous conviction for a murder he had committed at age 17. The jury found no mitigating circumstances and sentenced Williams to death. Over a period of 26 years, Williams\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and sentence were upheld on direct appeal, state postconviction review, and federal habeas review.

\n

In 2012, Williams filed a successive petition pursuant to Pennsylvania\xe2\x80\x99s Post Conviction Relief Act (PCRA), 42 Pa. Cons. Stat. \xc2\xa79541 et seq. (2007). The petition was based on new information from Draper, who until then had refused to speak with Williams\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys. Draper told Williams\xe2\x80\x99s counsel that he had informed the Commonwealth before trial that Williams had been in a sexual relationship with Norwood and that the relationship was the real motive for Norwood\xe2\x80\x99s murder. According to Draper, the Commonwealth had instructed him to give false testimony that Williams killed Norwood to rob him. Draper also admitted he had received an undisclosed benefit in exchange for his testimony: the trial prosecutor had promised to write a letter to the state parole board on his behalf. At trial, the prosecutor had elicited testimony from Draper indicating that his only agreement with the prosecution was to plead guilty in exchange for truthful testimony. No mention was made of the additional promise to write the parole board.

\n

The Philadelphia Court of Common Pleas, identified in the proceedings below as the PCRA court, held an evidentiary hearing on Williams\xe2\x80\x99s claims. Williams alleged in his petition that the prosecutor had procured false testimony from Draper and suppressed evidence regarding Norwood\xe2\x80\x99s sexual relationship with Williams. At the hearing, both Draper and the trial prosecutor testified regarding these allegations. The PCRA court ordered the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office to produce the previously undisclosed files of the prosecutor and police. These documents included the trial prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing memorandum, bearing then-District Attorney Castille\xe2\x80\x99s authorization to pursue the death penalty. Based on the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s files and the evidentiary hearing, the PCRA court found that the trial prosecutor had suppressed material, exculpatory evidence in violation of Brady v. Maryland,\n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83 (1963)\n, and engaged in \xe2\x80\x9cprosecutorial gamesmanship.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 168a. The court stayed Williams\xe2\x80\x99s execution and ordered a new sentencing hearing.

\n

Seeking to vacate the stay of execution, the Commonwealth submitted an emergency application to the Pennsylvania Supreme Court. By this time, almost three decades had passed since Williams\xe2\x80\x99s prosecution. Castille had been elected to a seat on the State Supreme Court and was serving as its chief justice. Williams filed a response to the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s application. The disclosure of the trial prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing memorandum in the PCRA proceedings had alerted Williams to Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s involvement in the decision to seek a death sentence in his case. For this reason, Williams also filed a motion asking Chief Justice Castille to recuse himself or, if he declined to do so, to refer the recusal motion to the full court for decision. The Commonwealth opposed Williams\xe2\x80\x99s recusal motion. Without explanation, Chief Justice Castille denied the motion for recusal and the request for its referral. Two days later, the Pennsylvania Supreme Court denied the application to vacate the stay and ordered full briefing on the issues raised in the appeal. The State Supreme Court then vacated the PCRA court\xe2\x80\x99s order granting penalty-phase relief and reinstated Williams\xe2\x80\x99s death sentence. Chief Justice Castille and Justices Baer and Stevens joined the majority opinion written by Justice Eakin. Justices Saylor and Todd concurred in the result without issuing a separate opinion. See ___ Pa. ___, ___, 105 A. 3d 1234, 1245 (2014).

\n

Chief Justice Castille authored a concurrence. He lamented that the PCRA court had \xe2\x80\x9clost sight of its role as a neutral judicial officer\xe2\x80\x9d and had stayed Williams\xe2\x80\x99s execution \xe2\x80\x9cfor no valid reason.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___, 105 A. 3d, at 1245. \xe2\x80\x9c[B]efore condemning officers of the court,\xe2\x80\x9d the chief justice stated, \xe2\x80\x9cthe tribunal should be aware of the substantive status of Brady law,\xe2\x80\x9d which he believed the PCRA court had misapplied. Id., at ___, 105 A. 3d, at 1246. In addition, Chief Justice Castille denounced what he perceived as the \xe2\x80\x9cobstructionist anti-death penalty agenda\xe2\x80\x9d of Williams\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys from the Federal Community Defender Office. Ibid. PCRA courts \xe2\x80\x9cthroughout Pennsylvania need to be vigilant and circumspect when it comes to the activities of this particular advocacy group,\xe2\x80\x9d he wrote, lest Defender Office lawyers turn postconviction proceedings \xe2\x80\x9cinto a circus where [they] are the ringmasters, with their parrots and puppets as a sideshow.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___, 105 A. 3d, at 1247.

\n

Two weeks after the Pennsylvania Supreme Court decided Williams\xe2\x80\x99s case, Chief Justice Castille retired from the bench. This Court granted Williams\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari. 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

Williams contends that Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s decision as district attorney to seek a death sentence against him barred the chief justice from later adjudicating Williams\xe2\x80\x99s petition to overturn that sentence. Chief Justice Castille, Williams argues, violated the Due Process Clause of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment by acting as both accuser and judge in his case.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s due process precedents do not set forth a specific test governing recusal when, as here, a judge had prior involvement in a case as a prosecutor. For the reasons explained below, however, the principles on which these precedents rest dictate the rule that must control in the circumstances here. The Court now holds that under the Due Process Clause there is an impermissible risk of actual bias when a judge earlier had significant, personal involvement as a prosecutor in a critical decision regarding the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s case.

\n

Due process guarantees \xe2\x80\x9can absence of actual bias\xe2\x80\x9d on the part of a judge. In re Murchison,\n\n349 U.\xc2\xa0S. 133,\n136 (1955)\n. Bias is easy to attribute to others and difficult to discern in oneself. To establish an enforceable and work-able framework, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents apply an objective standard that, in the usual case, avoids having to determine whether actual bias is present. The Court asks not whether a judge harbors an actual, subjective bias, but instead whether, as an objective matter, \xe2\x80\x9cthe average judge in his position is \xe2\x80\x98likely\xe2\x80\x99 to be neutral, or whether there is an unconstitutional \xe2\x80\x98potential for bias.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Caperton, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 881. Of particular relevance to the instant case, the Court has determined that an unconstitutional potential for bias exists when the same person serves as both accuser and adjudicator in a case. See Murchison, 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 136\xe2\x80\x93137. This objective risk of bias is reflected in the due process maxim that \xe2\x80\x9cno man can be a judge in his own case and no man is permitted to try cases where he has an interest in the outcome.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 136.

\n

The due process guarantee that \xe2\x80\x9cno man can be a judge in his own case\xe2\x80\x9d would have little substance if it did not disqualify a former prosecutor from sitting in judgment of a prosecution in which he or she had made a critical decision. This conclusion follows from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis in In\xc2\xa0re Murchison. That case involved a \xe2\x80\x9cone-man judge-grand jury\xe2\x80\x9d proceeding, conducted pursuant to state law, in which the judge called witnesses to testify about suspected crimes. Id., at 134. During the course of the examinations, the judge became convinced that two witnesses were obstructing the proceeding. He charged one witness with perjury and then, a few weeks later, tried and convicted him in open court. The judge charged the other witness with contempt and, a few days later, tried and convicted him as well. This Court overturned the convictions on the ground that the judge\xe2\x80\x99s dual position as accuser and decisionmaker in the contempt trials violated due process: \xe2\x80\x9cHaving been a part of [the accusatory] process a judge cannot be, in the very nature of things, wholly disinterested in the conviction or acquittal of those accused.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 137.

\n

No attorney is more integral to the accusatory process than a prosecutor who participates in a major adversary decision. When a judge has served as an advocate for the State in the very case the court is now asked to adjudicate, a serious question arises as to whether the judge, even with the most diligent effort, could set aside any personal interest in the outcome. There is, furthermore, a risk that the judge \xe2\x80\x9cwould be so psychologically wedded\xe2\x80\x9d to his or her previous position as a prosecutor that the judge \xe2\x80\x9cwould consciously or unconsciously avoid the appearance of having erred or changed position.\xe2\x80\x9d Withrow, 421 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 57. In addition, the judge\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cown personal knowledge and impression\xe2\x80\x9d of the case, acquired through his or her role in the prosecution, may carry far more weight with the judge than the parties\xe2\x80\x99 arguments to the court. Murchison, supra, at 138; see also Caperton, supra, at 881.

\n

Pennsylvania argues that Murchison does not lead to the rule that due process requires disqualification of a judge who, in an earlier role as a prosecutor, had significant involvement in making a critical decision in the case. The facts of Murchison, it should be acknowledged, differ in many respects from a case like this one. In Murchison, over the course of several weeks, a single official (the so-called judge-grand jury) conducted an investigation into suspected crimes; made the decision to charge witnesses for obstruction of that investigation; heard evidence on the charges he had lodged; issued judgments of conviction; and imposed sentence. See 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 135 (petitioners objected to \xe2\x80\x9ctrial before the judge who was at the same time the complainant, indicter and prosecutor\xe2\x80\x9d). By contrast, a judge who had an earlier involvement in a prosecution might have been just one of several prosecutors working on the case at each stage of the proceedings; the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s immediate role might have been limited to a particular aspect of the prosecution; and decades might have passed before the former prosecutor, now a judge, is called upon to adjudicate a claim in the case.

\n

These factual differences notwithstanding, the constitutional principles explained in Murchison are fully applicable where a judge had a direct, personal role in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s prosecution. The involvement of other actors and the passage of time are consequences of a complex criminal justice system, in which a single case may be litigated through multiple proceedings taking place over a period of years. This context only heightens the need for objective rules preventing the operation of bias that otherwise might be obscured. Within a large, impersonal system, an individual prosecutor might still have an influence that, while not so visible as the one-man grand jury in Murchison, is nevertheless significant. A prosecutor may bear responsibility for any number of critical decisions, including what charges to bring, whether to extend a plea bargain, and which witnesses to call. Even if decades intervene before the former prosecutor revisits the matter as a jurist, the case may implicate the effects and continuing force of his or her original decision. In these circumstances, there remains a serious risk that a judge would be influenced by an improper, if inadvertent, motive to validate and preserve the result obtained through the adversary process. The involvement of multiple actors and the passage of time do not relieve the former prosecutor of the duty to withdraw in order to ensure the neutral-ity of the judicial process in determining the consequences that his or her own earlier, critical decision may have set in motion.

\n

B

\n

This leads to the question whether Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s authorization to seek the death penalty against Williams amounts to significant, personal involvement in a critical trial decision. The Court now concludes that it was a significant, personal involvement; and, as a result, Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s failure to recuse from Williams\xe2\x80\x99s case presented an unconstitutional risk of bias.

\n

As an initial matter, there can be no doubt that the decision to pursue the death penalty is a critical choice in the adversary process. Indeed, after a defendant is charged with a death-eligible crime, whether to ask a jury to end the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s life is one of the most serious discretionary decisions a prosecutor can be called upon to make.

\n

Nor is there any doubt that Chief Justice Castille had a significant role in this decision. Without his express authorization, the Commonwealth would not have been able to pursue a death sentence against Williams. The importance of this decision and the profound consequences it carries make it evident that a responsible prosecutor would deem it to be a most significant exercise of his or her official discretion and professional judgment.

\n

Pennsylvania nonetheless contends that Chief Justice Castille in fact did not have significant involvement in the decision to seek a death sentence against Williams. The chief justice, the Commonwealth points out, was the head of a large district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office in a city that saw many capital murder trials. Tr. of Oral Arg. 36. According to Pennsylvania, his approval of the trial prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s request to pursue capital punishment in Williams\xe2\x80\x99s case amounted to a brief administrative act limited to \xe2\x80\x9cthe time it takes to read a one-and-a-half-page memo.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In this Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, that characterization cannot be credited. The Court will not assume that then-District Attorney Castille treated so major a decision as a perfunctory task requiring little time, judgment, or reflection on his part.

\n

Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s own comments while running for judicial office refute the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s claim that he played a mere ministerial role in capital sentencing decisions. During the chief justice\xe2\x80\x99s election campaign, multiple news outlets reported his statement that he \xe2\x80\x9csent 45 people to death rows\xe2\x80\x9d as district attorney. Seelye, Castille Keeps His Cool in Court Run, Philadelphia Inquirer, Apr. 30, 1993, p. B1; see also, e.g., Brennan, State Voters Must Choose Next Supreme Court Member, Legal Intelligencer, Oct. 28, 1993, pp. 1, 12. Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s willingness to take personal responsibility for the death sentences obtained during his tenure as district attorney indicate that, in his own view, he played a meaningful role in those sentencing decisions and considered his involvement to be an important duty of his office.

\n

Although not necessary to the disposition of this case, the PCRA court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling underscores the risk of permitting a former prosecutor to be a judge in what had been his or her own case. The PCRA court determined that the trial prosecutor\xe2\x80\x94Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s former subordinate in the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office\xe2\x80\x94had engaged in multiple, intentional Brady violations during Williams\xe2\x80\x99s prosecution. App. 131\xe2\x80\x93145, 150\xe2\x80\x93154. While there is no indication that Chief Justice Castille was aware of the alleged prosecutorial misconduct, it would be difficult for a judge in his position not to view the PCRA court\xe2\x80\x99s findings as a criticism of his former office and, to some extent, of his own leadership and supervision as district attorney.

\n

The potential conflict of interest posed by the PCRA court\xe2\x80\x99s findings illustrates the utility of statutes and professional codes of conduct that \xe2\x80\x9cprovide more protection than due process requires.\xe2\x80\x9d Caperton, 556 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 890. It is important to note that due process \xe2\x80\x9cdemarks only the outer boundaries of judicial disqualifications.\xe2\x80\x9d Aetna Life Ins. Co. v. Lavoie,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 813\n, 828 (1986). Most questions of recusal are addressed by more stringent and detailed ethical rules, which in many jurisdictions already require disqualification under the circumstances of this case. See Brief for American Bar Association as Amicus Curiae 5, 11\xe2\x80\x9314; see also ABA Model Code of Judicial Conduct Rules 2.11(A)(1), (A)(6)(b) (2011) (no judge may participate \xe2\x80\x9cin any proceeding in which the judge\xe2\x80\x99s impartiality might reasonably be questioned,\xe2\x80\x9d including where the judge \xe2\x80\x9cserved in governmental employment, and in such capacity participated personally and substantially as a lawyer or public official concerning the proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d); ABA Center for Professional Responsibility Policy Implementation Comm., Comparison of ABA Model Judicial Code and State Variations (Dec. 14, 2015), available at http://www.americanbar.org/content/dam/aba/administrative/professional_responsibility/2_11.authcheckdam.pdf (as last visited June 7, 2016) (28 States have adopted language similar to ABA Model Judicial Code Rule 2.11);\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7455(b)(3) (recusal required where judge \xe2\x80\x9chas served in governmental employment and in such capacity participated as counsel, adviser or material witness concerning the proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d). At the time Williams filed his recusal motion with the Pennsylvania Supreme Court, for example, Pennsylvania\xe2\x80\x99s Code of Judicial Conduct disqualified judges from any proceeding in which \xe2\x80\x9cthey served as a lawyer in the matter in controversy, or a lawyer with whom they previously practiced law served during such association as a lawyer concerning the matter.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Pa. Code of Judicial Conduct, Canon 3C (1974, as amended). The fact that most jurisdictions have these rules in place suggests that today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will not occasion a significant change in recusal practice.

\n

Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s significant, personal involvement in a critical decision in Williams\xe2\x80\x99s case gave rise to an unacceptable risk of actual bias. This risk so endangered the appearance of neutrality that his participation in the case \xe2\x80\x9cmust be forbidden if the guarantee of due process is to be adequately implemented.\xe2\x80\x9d Withrow, 421 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 47.

\n

III

\n

Having determined that Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s participation violated due process, the Court must resolve whether Williams is entitled to relief. In past cases, the Court has not had to decide the question whether a due process violation arising from a jurist\xe2\x80\x99s failure to recuse amounts to harmless error if the jurist is on a multimember court and the jurist\xe2\x80\x99s vote was not decisive. See Lavoie, supra, at 827\xe2\x80\x93828 (addressing \xe2\x80\x9cthe question whether a decision of a multimember tribunal must be vacated because of the participation of one member who had an interest in the outcome of the case,\xe2\x80\x9d where that member\xe2\x80\x99s vote was outcome determinative). For the reasons discussed below, the Court holds that an unconstitutional failure to recuse constitutes structural error even if the judge in question did not cast a deciding vote.

\n

The Court has little trouble concluding that a due process violation arising from the participation of an inter-ested judge is a defect \xe2\x80\x9cnot amenable\xe2\x80\x9d to harmless-error review, regardless of whether the judge\xe2\x80\x99s vote was dispositive. Puckett v. United States,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 129,\n141 (2009)\n (emphasis deleted). The deliberations of an appellate panel, as a general rule, are confidential. As a result, it is neither possible nor productive to inquire whether the jurist in question might have influenced the views of his or her colleagues during the decisionmaking process. Indeed, one purpose of judicial confidentiality is to assure jurists that they can reexamine old ideas and suggest new ones, while both seeking to persuade and being open to persuasion by their colleagues. As Justice Brennan wrote in his Lavoie concurrence,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe description of an opinion as being \xe2\x80\x98for the court\xe2\x80\x99 connotes more than merely that the opinion has been joined by a majority of the participating judges. It reflects the fact that these judges have exchanged ideas and arguments in deciding the case. It reflects the collective process of deliberation which shapes the court\xe2\x80\x99s perceptions of which issues must be addressed and, more importantly, how they must be addressed. And, while the influence of any single participant in this process can never be measured with precision, experience teaches us that each member\xe2\x80\x99s involvement plays a part in shaping the court\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate disposition.\xe2\x80\x9d 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 831.

\n

These considerations illustrate, moreover, that it does not matter whether the disqualified judge\xe2\x80\x99s vote was necessary to the disposition of the case. The fact that the interested judge\xe2\x80\x99s vote was not dispositive may mean only that the judge was successful in persuading most members of the court to accept his or her position. That outcome does not lessen the unfairness to the affected party. See id., at 831\xe2\x80\x93832 (Blackmun, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

A multimember court must not have its guarantee of neutrality undermined, for the appearance of bias demeans the reputation and integrity not just of one jurist, but of the larger institution of which he or she is a part. An insistence on the appearance of neutrality is not some artificial attempt to mask imperfection in the judicial process, but rather an essential means of ensuring the reality of a fair adjudication. Both the appearance and reality of impartial justice are necessary to the public legitimacy of judicial pronouncements and thus to the rule of law itself. When the objective risk of actual bias on the part of a judge rises to an unconstitutional level, the failure to recuse cannot be deemed harmless.

\n

The Commonwealth points out that ordering a rehearing before the Pennsylvania Supreme Court may not provide complete relief to Williams because judges who were exposed to a disqualified judge may still be influenced by their colleague\xe2\x80\x99s views when they rehear the case. Brief for Respondent 51, 62. An inability to guarantee complete relief for a constitutional violation, however, does not justify withholding a remedy altogether. Allowing an appellate panel to reconsider a case without the participation of the interested member will permit judges to probe lines of analysis or engage in discussions they may have felt constrained to avoid in their first deliberations.

\n

Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s participation in Williams\xe2\x80\x99s case was an error that affected the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s whole adjudicatory framework below. Williams must be granted an opportunity to present his claims to a court unburdened by any \xe2\x80\x9cpossible temptation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not to hold the balance nice, clear and true between the State and the accused.\xe2\x80\x9d Tumey v. Ohio,\n\n273 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510,\n532 (1927)\n.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Where a judge has had an earlier significant, personal involvement as a prosecutor in a critical decision in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s case, the risk of actual bias in the judicial proceeding rises to an unconstitutional level. Due process entitles Terrance Williams to \xe2\x80\x9ca proceeding in which he may present his case with assurance\xe2\x80\x9d that no member of the court is \xe2\x80\x9cpredisposed to find against him.\xe2\x80\x9d Marshall v. Jerrico, Inc.,\n\n446 U.\xc2\xa0S. 238,\n242 (1980)\n.

\n

The judgment of the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

TERRANCE WILLIAMS, PETITIONER v. PENNSYLVANIA

\n

\n

[June 9, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, dissenting.

\n

The Court concludes that it violates the Due Process Clause for the chief justice of the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania, a former district attorney who was not the trial prosecutor in petitioner Terrance Williams\xe2\x80\x99 case, to review Williams\xe2\x80\x99 fourth petition for state postconviction review. Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939, 14. That conclusion is flawed. The specter of bias alone in a judicial proceeding is not a deprivation of due process. Rather than constitutionalize every judicial disqualification rule, the Court has left such rules to legislatures, bar associations, and the judgment of individual adjudicators. Williams, moreover, is not a criminal defendant. His complaint is instead that the due process protections in his state postconviction proceedings\xe2\x80\x94an altogether new civil matter, not a continuation of his criminal trial\xe2\x80\x94were lacking. Ruling in Williams\xe2\x80\x99 favor, the Court ignores this posture and our precedents commanding less of state postconviction proceedings than of criminal prosecutions involving defendants whose convictions are not yet final. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

A reader of the majority opinion might mistakenly think that the prosecution against Williams is ongoing, for the majority makes no mention of the fact that Williams\xe2\x80\x99 sentence has been final for more than 25 years. Because the postconviction posture of this case is of crucial importance in considering the question presented, I begin with the protracted procedural history of Williams\xe2\x80\x99 repeated attempts to collaterally attack his sentence.

\n

A

\n

Thirty-two years ago, Williams and his accomplice beat their victim to death with a tire iron and a socket wrench. Commonwealth v. Williams, 524 Pa. 218, 222\xe2\x80\x93224, 570 A.\xc2\xa02d 75, 77\xe2\x80\x9378 (1990) (Williams I\xc2\xa0). Williams later returned to the scene of the crime, a cemetery, soaked the victim\xe2\x80\x99s body in gasoline, and set it on fire. Id., at 224, 570 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 78. After the trial against Williams commenced, both the Chief of the Homicide Unit and the District Attorney, Ronald Castille, approved the trial prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s decision to seek the death penalty by signing a piece of paper. See App. 426. That was Castille\xe2\x80\x99s only involvement in Williams\xe2\x80\x99 criminal case. Thereafter, a Pennsylvania jury convicted Williams of first-degree murder, and he was sentenced to death. Williams I, 524 Pa., at 221\xe2\x80\x93222, 570 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 77. The Supreme Court of Pennsylvania affirmed his conviction and sentence. Id., at 235, 570 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 84.

\n

Five years later, Williams filed his first petition for state postconviction relief. Commonwealth v. Williams, 581 Pa. 57, 65, 863 A.\xc2\xa02d 505, 509 (2004) (Williams II\xc2\xa0). The postconviction court denied the petition. Id., at 65, 863 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 510. Williams appealed, raising 23 alleged errors. Ibid. The Supreme Court of Pennsylvania, which included Castille in his new capacity as a justice of that court, affirmed the denial of relief. Id., at 88, 863 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 523. The court rejected some claims on procedural grounds and denied the remaining claims on the merits. Id., at 68\xe2\x80\x9388, 863 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 511\xe2\x80\x93523. The court\xe2\x80\x99s lengthy opinion did not mention the possibility of Castille\xe2\x80\x99s bias, and Williams apparently never asked for his recusal.

\n

Then in 2005, Williams filed two more petitions for state postconviction relief. Both petitions were dismissed as untimely, and the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania affirmed. Commonwealth v. Williams, 589 Pa. 355, 909 A. 2d 297 (2006) (\xc2\xa0per curiam) (Williams III\xc2\xa0); Commonwealth v. Williams, 599 Pa. 495, 962 A. 2d 609 (2009) (\xc2\xa0per curiam) (Williams IV\xc2\xa0). Castille also presumably participated in those proceedings, but, again, Williams apparently did not ask for him to recuse.[1]

\n

Williams then made a fourth attempt to vacate his sentence in state court in 2012. ___ Pa. ___, ___, 105 A.\xc2\xa03d 1234, 1237 (2014) (Williams VI\xc2\xa0). Williams alleged that the prosecution violated Brady v. Maryland,\n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83 (1963)\n, by failing to disclose exculpatory evidence. The allegedly exculpatory evidence was information about Williams\xe2\x80\x99 motive. According to Williams, the prosecution should have disclosed to his counsel that it knew that Williams and the victim had previously engaged in a sexual relationship when Williams was a minor. Williams VI, ___ Pa., at ___, 105 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 1237.[2] The state postconviction court agreed and vacated his sentence. Id., at ___, 105 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 1239.

\n

The Commonwealth appealed to the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania. Only then\xe2\x80\x94the fourth time that Williams appeared before Castille\xe2\x80\x94did Williams ask him to recuse. App. 181. Castille denied the recusal motion and declined to refer it to the full court. Id., at 171. Shortly thereafter, the court vacated the postconviction court\xe2\x80\x99s order and reinstated Williams\xe2\x80\x99 sentence. The court first noted that Williams\xe2\x80\x99 fourth petition \xe2\x80\x9cwas filed over 20 years after [Williams\xe2\x80\x99] judgment of sentence became final\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwas untimely on its face.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams VI, ___ Pa., at ___, 105 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 1239. The court rejected the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that an exception to Pennsylvania\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness rule applied and reached \xe2\x80\x9cthe inescapable conclusion that [Williams] is not entitled to relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___, 105 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 1239\xe2\x80\x931241; see also id., at ___, 105 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 1245(Castille, J., concurring) (writing separately \xe2\x80\x9cto address the important responsibilities of the [state postconvic-tion] trial courts in serial capital [state postconviction] matters\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Finally, Williams filed an application for reargument. App. 9. The court denied the application without Castille\xe2\x80\x99s participation. Id., at 8. Castille had retired from the bench nearly two months before the court ruled.

\n

B

\n

As this procedural history illustrates, the question presented is hardly what the majority makes it out to be. The majority incorrectly refers to the case before us and Williams\xe2\x80\x99 criminal case (that ended in 1990) as a decades-long \xe2\x80\x9csingle case\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmatter.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8; see also ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x939. The majority frames the issue as follows: whether the Due Process Clause permits Castille to \xe2\x80\x9cac[t] as both accuser and judge in [Williams\xe2\x80\x99] case.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. The majority answers: \xe2\x80\x9cWhen a judge has served as an advocate for the State in the very case the court is now asked to adjudicate, a serious question arises as to whether the judge, even with the most diligent effort, could set aside any personal interest in the outcome.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7 (emphasis added). Accordingly, the majority holds that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here a judge has had an earlier significant, personal involvement as a prosecutor in a critical decision in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s case, the risk of actual bias in the judicial proceeding rises to an unconstitutional level.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14 (emphasis added). That is all wrong.

\n

There has been, however, no \xe2\x80\x9csingle case\xe2\x80\x9d in which Castille acted as both prosecutor and adjudicator. Castille was still serving in the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office when Williams\xe2\x80\x99 criminal proceedings ended and his sentence of death became final. Williams\xe2\x80\x99 filing of a petition for state postconviction relief did not continue (or resurrect) that already final criminal proceeding. A postconviction proceeding \xe2\x80\x9cis not part of the criminal proceeding itself\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9cis in fact considered to be civil in nature,\xe2\x80\x9d Pennsylvania v. Finley,\n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551\n\xe2\x80\x93557 (1987), and brings with it fewer procedural protections. See, e.g., District Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office for Third Judicial Dist. v. Osborne,\n\n557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 52,\n68 (2009)\n.

\n

Williams\xe2\x80\x99 case therefore presents a much different question from that posited by the majority. It is more accurately characterized as whether a judge may review a petition for postconviction relief when that judge previ-ously served as district attorney while the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s criminal case was pending. For the reasons that follow, that different question merits a different answer.

\n

II

\n

The \xe2\x80\x9csettled usages and modes of proceeding existing in the common and statute law of England before the emigration of our ancestors\xe2\x80\x9d are the touchstone of due process. Tumey v. Ohio,\n\n273 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510,\n523 (1927)\n; see also Murray\xe2\x80\x99s Lessee v. Hoboken Land & Improvement Co., 18 How. 272, 277 (1856). What due process requires of the judicial proceedings in the Pennsylvania postconviction courts, therefore, is guided by the historical treatment of judicial disqualification. And here, neither historical practice nor this Court\xe2\x80\x99s case law constitutionalizing that practice requires a former prosecutor to recuse from a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s postconviction proceedings.

\n

A

\n

At common law, a fair tribunal meant that \xe2\x80\x9cno man shall be a judge in his own case.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 E. Coke, Institutes of the Laws of England \xc2\xa7212, *141a (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]liquis non debet esse judex in propi\xc3\xa2 caus\xc3\xa2\xe2\x80\x9d). That common-law conception of a fair tribunal was a narrow one. A judge could not decide a case in which he had a direct and personal financial stake. For example, a judge could not reap the fine paid by a defendant. See, e.g., Dr. Bonham\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 8 Co. Rep. 107a, 114a, 118a, 77 Eng. Rep. 638, 647, 652 (C. P. 1610) (opining that a panel of adjudicators could not all at once serve as \xe2\x80\x9cjudges to give sentence or judgment; ministers to make summons; and parties to have the moiety of the forfeiture\xe2\x80\x9d). Nor could he adjudicate a case in which he was a party. See, e.g., Earl of Derby\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 12 Co. Rep. 114, 77 Eng. Rep. 1390 (K. B. 1614). But mere bias\xe2\x80\x94without any financial stake in a case\xe2\x80\x94was not grounds for disqualification. The biases of judges \xe2\x80\x9ccannot be challenged,\xe2\x80\x9d according to Blackstone, \xe2\x80\x9c[f\xc2\xa0]or the law will not suppose a possibility of bias or favour in a judge, who is already sworn to administer impartial justice, and whose author-ity greatly depends upon that presumption and idea.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England, 361 (1768) (Blackstone); see also, e.g., Brookes v. Earl of Rivers, Hardres 503, 145 Eng. Rep. 569 (Exch. 1668) (deciding that a judge\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfavour shall not be presumed\xe2\x80\x9d merely because his brother-in-law was involved).

\n

The early American conception of judicial disqualification was in keeping with the \xe2\x80\x9cclear and simple\xe2\x80\x9d common-law rule\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ca judge was disqualified for direct pecuniary interest and for nothing else.\xe2\x80\x9d Frank, Disqualification of Judges, 56 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 605, 609 (1947) (Frank); see also R. Flamm, Judicial Disqualification: Recusal and Disqualification of Judges \xc2\xa71.4, p. 7 (2d ed. 2007). Most jurisdictions required judges to recuse when they stood to profit from their involvement or, more broadly, when their property was involved. See Moses v. Julian, 45 N. H. 52, 55\xe2\x80\x9356 (1863); see also, e.g., Jim v. State, 3 Mo. 147, 155 (1832) (deciding that a judge was unlawfully interested in a criminal case in which his slave was the defendant). But the judge\xe2\x80\x99s pecuniary interest had to be directly implicated in the case. See, e.g., Davis v. State, 44 Tex. 523, 524 (1876) (deciding that a judge, who was the victim of a theft, was not disqualified in the prosecution of the theft); see also T. Cooley, Constitutional Limitations 594 (7th ed. 1903) (rejecting a financial stake \xe2\x80\x9cso remote, trifling, and insignificant that it may fairly be supposed to be incapable of affecting the judgment\xe2\x80\x9d); Moses, supra, at 57 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] creditor, lessee, or debtor, may be judge in the case of his debtor, landlord, or creditor, except in cases where the amountof the party\xe2\x80\x99s property involved in the suit is so great that his ability to meet his engagements with the judge may depend upon the success of his suit\xe2\x80\x9d); Inhabitants of Readington Twp. Hunterdon County v. Dilley, 24 N. J. L. 209, 212\xe2\x80\x93213 (N. J. 1853) (deciding that a judge, who had previously been paid to survey the roadway at issue in the case, was not disqualified).

\n

Shortly after the founding, American notions of judicial disqualification expanded in important respects. Of particular relevance here, the National and State Legislatures enacted statutes and constitutional provisions that diverged from the common law by requiring disqualification when the judge had served as counsel for one of the parties. The first federal recusal statute, for example, required disqualification not only when the judge was \xe2\x80\x9cconcerned in interest,\xe2\x80\x9d but also when he \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] been of counsel for either party.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of May 8, 1792, \xc2\xa711,\n\n1Stat.\n278\xe2\x80\x93279. Many States followed suit by enacting similar disqualification statutes or constitutional provisions expanding the common-law rule. See, e.g., Wilks v. State, 27 Tex. App. 381, 385, 11 S.\xc2\xa0W. 415, 416 (1889); Fechheimer v. Washington, 77 Ind. 366, 368 (1881) (\xc2\xa0per curiam); Sjoberg v. Nordin, 26 Minn. 501, 503, 5 N.\xc2\xa0W. 677, 678 (1880); Whipple v. Saginaw Circuit Court Judge, 26 Mich. 342, 343 (1873); Mathis v. State, 50 Tenn. 127, 128 (1871); but see Owings v. Gibson, 9 Ky. 515, 517\xe2\x80\x93518 (1820) (deciding that it was for the judge to choose whether he could fairly adjudicate a case in which he had served as a lawyer for the plaintiff in the same action). Courts applied this expanded view of disqualification not only in cases involving judges who had previously served as counsel for private parties but also for those who previously served as former attorneys general or district attorneys. See, e.g., Terry v. State, 24 S. W. 510, 510\xe2\x80\x93511 (Tex. Crim. App. 1893); Mathis, supra, at 128.

\n

This expansion was modest: disqualification was required only when the newly appointed judge had served as counsel in the same case. In Carr v. Fife,\n\n156 U.\xc2\xa0S. 494 (1895)\n, for example, this Court rejected the argument that a judge was required to recuse because he had previously served as counsel for some of the defendants in another matter. Id., at 497\xe2\x80\x93498. The Court left it to the judge \xe2\x80\x9cto decide for himself whether it was improper for him to sit in trial of the suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 498. Likewise, in Taylor v. Williams, 26 Tex. 583 (1863), the Supreme Court of Texas acknowledged that a judge was not, \xe2\x80\x9cby the common law, disqualified from sitting in a cause in which he had been of counsel\xe2\x80\x9d and concluded \xe2\x80\x9cthat the fact that the presiding judge had been of counsel in the case did not necessarily render him interested in it.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 585\xe2\x80\x93586. A fortiori, the Texas court held, a judge was not \xe2\x80\x9cinterested\xe2\x80\x9d in a case \xe2\x80\x9cmerely from his having been of counsel in another cause involving the same title.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 586 (emphasis added); see also The Richmond, 9 F. 863, 864 (CCED La. 1881) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe decisions, so far as I have been able to find, are unanimous that \xe2\x80\x98of counsel\xe2\x80\x99 means \xe2\x80\x98of counsel for a party in that cause and in that controversy,\xe2\x80\x99 and if either the cause or controversy is not identical the disqualification does not exist\xe2\x80\x9d); Wolfe v. Hines, 93 Ga. 329, 20 S.\xc2\xa0E. 322 (1894) (same); Cleghorn v. Cleghorn, 66 Cal. 309, 5 P. 516 (1885) (same).

\n

This limitation\xe2\x80\x94that the same person must act as counsel and adjudicator in the same case\xe2\x80\x94makes good sense. At least one of the State\xe2\x80\x99s highest courts feared that any broader rule would wreak havoc: \xe2\x80\x9cIf the circumstance of the judge having been of counsel, for some parties in some case involving some of the issues which had been theretofore tried[,] disqualified him from acting in every case in which any of those parties, or those issues should be subsequently involved, the most eminent members of the bar, would, by reason of their extensive professional relations and their large experience be rendered ineligible, or useless as judges.\xe2\x80\x9d Blackburn v. Craufurd, 22 Md. 447, 459 (1864). Indeed, any broader rule would be at odds with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s historical practice. Past Justices have decided cases involving their former clients in the private sector or their former offices in the public sector. See Frank 622\xe2\x80\x93625. The examples are legion; chief among them is Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137 (1803), in which then\xe2\x80\x93Secretary of State John Marshall sealed but failed to deliver William Marbury\xe2\x80\x99s commission and then, as newly appointed Chief Justice, Marshall decided whether mandamus was an available remedy to require James Madison to finish the job. See Paulsen, Marbury\xe2\x80\x99s Wrongness, 20 Constitutional Commentary 343, 350 (2003).

\n

Over the next century, this Court entered the fray of judicial disqualifications only a handful of times. Drawing from longstanding historical practice, the Court announced that the Due Process Clause compels judges to disqualify in the narrow circumstances described below. But time and again, the Court cautioned that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll questions of judicial qualification may not involve constitu-tional validity.\xe2\x80\x9d Tumey, 273 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 523. And \xe2\x80\x9cmatters of kinship, personal bias, state policy, remoteness of interest would seem generally to be matters merely of legisla-tive discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also Aetna Life Ins. Co. v.Lavoie,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 813,\n828 (1986)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Due Process Clause demarks only the outer boundaries of judicial disqualifications\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

First, in Tumey, the Court held that due process would not tolerate an adjudicator who would profit from the case if he convicted the defendant. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding paralleled the common-law rule: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t certainly violates the\n \nFourteenth Amendment, and deprives a defendant in a criminal case of due process of law, to subject his liberty or property to the judgment of a court, the judge of which has a direct, personal, substantial pecuniary interest in reaching a conclusion against him in his case.\xe2\x80\x9d 273 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 523 (emphasis added); see also Ward v. Monroeville,\n\n409 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57,\n59,\n61 (1972)\n (deciding that a mayor could not adjudicate traffic violations if revenue from convictions constituted a substantial portion of the municipality\xe2\x80\x99s revenue). Later, applying Tumey\xe2\x80\x99s rule in Aetna Life Ins., the Court held that a judge who decided a case involving an insurance company had a \xe2\x80\x9cdirect, personal, substantial, and pecuniary\xe2\x80\x9d interest because he had brought a similar case against an insurer and his opinion for the court \xe2\x80\x9chad the clear and immediate effect of enhancing both the legal status and the settlement value of his own case.\xe2\x80\x9d 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 824 (alterations and internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Second, in In re Murchison,\n\n349 U.\xc2\xa0S. 133 (1955)\n, the Court adopted a constitutional rule resembling the historical practice for disqualification of former counsel. Id., at 139. There, state law empowered a trial judge to sit as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98one man judge-grand jury,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d meaning that he could \xe2\x80\x9ccompel witnesses to appear before him in secret to testify about suspected crimes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 133. During those secret proceedings, the trial judge suspected that one of the witnesses, Lee Roy Murchison, had committed perjury, and he charged another, John White, with contempt after he refused to answer the judge\xe2\x80\x99s questions without counsel present. See id., at 134\xe2\x80\x93135. The judge then tried both men in open court and convicted and sentenced them based, in part, on his interrogation of them in the secret proceedings. See id., at 135, 138\xe2\x80\x93139. The defendants appealed, arguing that the \xe2\x80\x9ctrial before the judge who was at the same time the complainant, indicter and prosecutor, constituted a denial of fair and impartial trial required by\xe2\x80\x9d due process. Id., at 135. This Court agreed: \xe2\x80\x9cIt would be very strange if our system of law permitted a judge to act as a grand jury and then try the very persons accused as a result of his investigations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 137. Broadly speaking, Murchison\xe2\x80\x99s rule constitutionalizes the early American statutes requiring disqualification when a single person acts as both counsel and judge in a single civil or criminal proceeding.[3]

\n

Both Tumey and Murchison arguably reflect historical understandings of judicial disqualification. Traditionally, judges disqualified themselves when they had a direct and substantial pecuniary interest or when they served as counsel in the same case.

\n

B

\n

Those same historical understandings of judicial disqualification resolve Williams\xe2\x80\x99 case. Castille did not serve as both prosecutor and judge in the case before us. Even assuming Castille\xe2\x80\x99s supervisory role as district attorney was tantamount to serving as \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel\xe2\x80\x9d in Williams\xe2\x80\x99 criminal case, that case ended nearly five years before Castille joined the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania. Castille then participated in a separate proceeding by reviewing Williams\xe2\x80\x99 petition for postconviction relief.

\n

As discussed above, see Part I\xe2\x80\x93B, supra, this postconviction proceeding is not an extension of Williams\xe2\x80\x99 criminal case but is instead a new civil proceeding. See Finley, 481 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 556\xe2\x80\x93557. Our case law bears out the many distinctions between the two proceedings. In his criminal case, Williams was presumed innocent, Coffin v. United States,\n\n156 U.\xc2\xa0S. 432,\n453 (1895)\n, and the Constitution guaranteed him counsel, Gideon v. Wainwright,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335\n\xe2\x80\x93345 (1963); Powell v. Alabama,\n\n287 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45\n\xe2\x80\x9369 (1932), a public trial by a jury of his peers, Duncan v. Louisiana,\n\n391 U.\xc2\xa0S. 145,\n149 (1968)\n, and empowered him to confront the witnesses against him, Crawford v. Washington,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 36,\n68 (2004)\n, as well as all the other requirements of a criminal proceeding. But in postconviction proceedings, \xe2\x80\x9cthe presumption of innocence [has] disappear[ed].\xe2\x80\x9d Herrera v. Collins,\n\n506 U.\xc2\xa0S. 390,\n399 (1993)\n. The postconviction petitioner has no constitutional right to counsel. Finley, supra, at 555\xe2\x80\x93557; see also Johnson v. Avery,\n\n393 U.\xc2\xa0S. 483,\n488 (1969)\n. Nor has this Court ever held that he has a right to demand that his postconviction court consider a freestanding claim of actual innocence, Herrera, supra, at 417\xe2\x80\x93419, or to demand the State to turn over exculpatory evidence, Osborne, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 68\xe2\x80\x9370; see also Wright v. West,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 277,\n293 (1992)\n (plurality opinion) (cataloguing differences between direct and collateral review and concluding that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hese differences simply reflect the fact that habeas review entails significant costs\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). And, under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, his due process rights are \xe2\x80\x9cnot parallel to a trial right, but rather must be analyzed in light of the fact that he has already been found guilty at a fair trial, and has only a limited interest in postconviction relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Osborne, supra, at 69.

\n

Because Castille did not act as both counsel and judge in the same case, Castille\xe2\x80\x99s participation in the postconviction proceedings did not violate the Due Process Clause. Castille might have been \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal[ly] involve[d] in a critical trial decision,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9, but that \xe2\x80\x9ctrial\xe2\x80\x9d was Williams\xe2\x80\x99 criminal trial, not the postconviction proceedings before us now. Perhaps Castille\xe2\x80\x99s participation in Williams\xe2\x80\x99 postconviction proceeding was unwise, but it was within the bounds of historical practice. That should end this case, for it \xe2\x80\x9cis not for Members of this Court to decide from time to time whether a process approved by the legal traditions of our people is \xe2\x80\x98due\xe2\x80\x99 process.\xe2\x80\x9d Pacific Mut. Life Ins. Co. v. Haslip,\n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n28 (1991)\n (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

C

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s holding departs both from common-law practice and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior precedents by ignoring the critical distinction between criminal and postconviction proceedings. Chief Justice Castille had no \xe2\x80\x9cdirect, personal, substantial pecuniary interest\xe2\x80\x9d in the adjudication of Williams\xe2\x80\x99 fourth postconviction petition. Tumey, 273 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 523. And although the majority invokes Murchison, ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x938, it wrongly relies on that decision too. In Murchison, the judge acted as both the accuser and judge in the same proceeding. 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 137\xe2\x80\x93139. But here, Castille did not. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, supra.

\n

The perceived bias that the majority fears is instead outside the bounds of the historical expectations of judicial recusal. Perceived bias (without more) was not recognized as a constitutionally compelled ground for disqualification until the Court\xe2\x80\x99s recent decision in Caperton v. A. T. Massey Coal Co.,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 868 (2009)\n. In Caperton, the Court decided that due process demanded disqualification when \xe2\x80\x9cextreme facts\xe2\x80\x9d proved \xe2\x80\x9cthe probability of actual bias.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 886\xe2\x80\x93887. Caperton, of course, elicited more questions than answers. Id., at 893\xe2\x80\x93898 (Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting). And its conclusion that bias alone could be grounds for disqualification as a constitutional matter \xe2\x80\x9crepresents a complete departure from common law principles.\xe2\x80\x9d Frank 618\xe2\x80\x93619; see Blackstone 361 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he law will not suppose a possibility of bias or favor in a judge\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The Court, therefore, should not so readily extend Caperton\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprobability of actual bias\xe2\x80\x9d rule to state postconviction proceedings. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents demand far less \xe2\x80\x9cprocess\xe2\x80\x9d in postconviction proceedings than in a criminal prosecution. See Osborne, supra, at 69; see also Cafeteria & Restaurant Workers v. McElroy,\n\n367 U.\xc2\xa0S. 886,\n895 (1961)\n (concluding that the Due Process Clause does not demand \xe2\x80\x9cinflexible procedures universally applicable to every imaginable situation\xe2\x80\x9d). If a state habeas petitioner is not entitled to counsel as a constitutional matter in state postconviction proceedings, Finley, supra, at 555\xe2\x80\x93557, it is not unreasonable to think that he is likewise not entitled to demand, as a constitutional matter, that a state postconviction court consider his case anew because a judge, who had no direct and substantial pecuniary interest and had not served as counsel in this case, failed to recuse himself.

\n

The bias that the majority fears is a problem for the state legislature to resolve, not the Federal Constitution. See, e.g., Aetna Life Ins., 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 821 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe need not decide whether allegations of bias or prejudice by a judge of the type we have here would ever be sufficient under the Due Process Clause to force recusal\xe2\x80\x9d). And, indeed, it appears that Pennsylvania has set its own standard by requiring a judge to disqualify if he \xe2\x80\x9cserved in governmental employment, and in such capacity participated personally and substantially as a lawyer or public official concerning the proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d in its Code of Judicial Conduct. See Pa. Code of Judicial Conduct Rule 2.11(A)(6)(b) (West 2016). Officials in Pennsylvania are fully capable of deciding when their judges have \xe2\x80\x9cparticipated personally and substantially\xe2\x80\x9d in a manner that would require disqualification without this Court\xe2\x80\x99s intervention. Due process requires no more, especially in state postconviction review where the States \xe2\x80\x9cha[ve] more flexibility in deciding what procedures are needed.\xe2\x80\x9d Osborne, supra, at 69.

\n

III

\n

Even if I were to assume that an error occurred in Williams\xe2\x80\x99 state postconviction proceedings, the question remains whether there is anything left for the Pennsylvania courts to remedy. There is not.

\n

The majority remands the case to \xe2\x80\x9c[a]llo[w] an appellate panel to reconsider a case without the participation of the interested member,\xe2\x80\x9d which it declares \xe2\x80\x9cwill permit judges to probe lines of analysis or engage in discussions they may have felt constrained to avoid in their first deliberations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. The majority neglects to mention that the Supreme Court of Pennsylvania might have done just that. It entertained Williams\xe2\x80\x99 motion for reargument without Castille, who had retired months before the court denied the motion. The Supreme Court of Pennsylvania is free to decide on remand that it cured any alleged deprivation of due process in Williams\xe2\x80\x99 postconviction proceeding by considering his motion for reargument without Castille\xe2\x80\x99s participation.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

This is not a case about the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98accused.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14 (quoting Tumey, supra, at 532). It is a case about the due process rights of the already convicted. Whatever those rights might be, they do not include policing alleged violations of state codes of judicial ethics in postconviction proceedings. The Due Process Clause does not require any and all conceivable procedural protections that Members of this Court think \xe2\x80\x9cWestern liberal democratic government ought to guarantee to its citizens.\xe2\x80\x9d Monaghan, Our Perfect Constitution, 56 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0U. L. Rev. 353, 358 (1981) (emphasis deleted). I respectfully dissent.

TERRANCE WILLIAMS, PETITIONER v. PENNSYLVANIA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of pennsylvania, eastern district

\n

[June 9, 2016]

\n

\n

Chief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Alito joins, dissenting.

\n

In 1986, Ronald Castille, then District Attorney of Philadelphia, authorized a prosecutor in his office to seek the death penalty against Terrance Williams. Almost 30 years later, as Chief Justice of the Pennsylvania Supreme Court, he participated in deciding whether Williams\xe2\x80\x99s fifth habeas petition\xe2\x80\x94which raised a claim unconnected to the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s decision to seek the death penalty\xe2\x80\x94could be heard on the merits or was instead untimely. This Court now holds that because Chief Justice Castille made a \xe2\x80\x9ccritical\xe2\x80\x9d decision as a prosecutor in Williams\xe2\x80\x99s case, there is a risk that he \xe2\x80\x9cwould be so psychologically wedded\xe2\x80\x9d to his previous decision that it would violate the Due Process Clause for him to decide the distinct issues raised in the habeas petition. Ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937 (internal quotation marks omitted). According to the Court, that conclusion follows from the maxim that \xe2\x80\x9cno man can be a judge in his own case.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

The majority opinion rests on proverb rather than precedent. This Court has held that there is \xe2\x80\x9ca presumption of honesty and integrity in those serving as adjudicators.\xe2\x80\x9d Withrow v. Larkin,\n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35,\n47 (1975)\n. To overcome that presumption, the majority relies on In re Murchison,\n\n349 U.\xc2\xa0S. 133 (1955)\n. We concluded there that the Due Process Clause is violated when a judge adjudicates the same question\xe2\x80\x94based on the same facts\xe2\x80\x94that he had already considered as a grand juror in the same case. Here, however, Williams does not allege that Chief Justice Castille had any previous knowledge of the contested facts at issue in the habeas petition, or that he had previously made any decision on the questions raised by that petition. I would accordingly hold that the Due Process Clause did not require Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s recusal.

\n

I

\n

In 1986, petitioner Terrance Williams stood trial for the murder of Amos Norwood. Prosecutors believed that Williams and his friend Marc Draper had asked Norwood for a ride, directed him to a cemetery, and then beat him to death with a tire iron after robbing him. Andrea Foulkes, the Philadelphia Assistant District Attorney prosecut-ing the case, prepared a one-and-a-half page memo for her superiors\xe2\x80\x94Homicide Unit Chief Mark Gottlieb and District Attorney Ronald Castille\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9crequest[ing] that we actively seek the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 424a. The memo briefly described the facts of the case and Williams\xe2\x80\x99s prior felonies, including a previous murder conviction. Gottlieb read the memo and then passed it to Castille with a note recommending the death penalty. Id., at 426a. Castille wrote at the bottom of the memo, \xe2\x80\x9cApproved to proceed on the death penalty,\xe2\x80\x9d and signed his name. Ibid.

\n

At trial, Williams testified that he had never met Norwood and that someone else must have murdered him. After hearing extensive evidence linking Williams to the crime, the jury convicted him of murder and sentenced him to death. 524 Pa. 218, 227, 570 A.\xc2\xa02d 75, 79\xe2\x80\x9380 (1990).

\n

In 1995, Williams filed a habeas petition in Pennsylvania state court, alleging that his trial counsel had been ineffective for failing to present mitigating evidence of his childhood sexual abuse, among other claims. At a hearing related to that petition, Williams acknowledged that he knew Norwood and claimed that Norwood had sexually abused him. ___ Pa. ___, ___, 105 A. 3d 1234, 1240 (2014). The petition was denied. Williams filed two more state habeas petitions, which were both dismissed as untimely, and a federal habeas petition, which was also denied. See Williams v. Beard, 637 F.\xc2\xa03d 195, 238 (CA3 2011).

\n

This case arises out of Williams\xe2\x80\x99s fifth habeas petition, which he filed in state court in 2012. In that petition, Williams argued that he was entitled to a new sentencing proceeding because the prosecution at trial had failed to turn over certain evidence suggesting that \xe2\x80\x9cNorwood was sexually involved with boys around [Williams\xe2\x80\x99s] age at the time of his murder.\xe2\x80\x9d Crim. No. CP\xe2\x80\x9351\xe2\x80\x93CR\xe2\x80\x930823621\xe2\x80\x931984 (Phila. Ct. Common Pleas, Nov. 27, 2012), App. 80a.

\n

It is undisputed that Williams\xe2\x80\x99s fifth habeas petition is untimely under Pennsylvania law. In order to overcome that time bar, Pennsylvania law required Williams to show that \xe2\x80\x9c(1) the failure to previously raise [his] claim was the result of interference by government officials and (2) the information on which he relies could not have been obtained earlier with the exercise of due diligence.\xe2\x80\x9d ___ Pa., at ___, 105 A. 3d, at 1240. The state habeas court held that Williams met that burden because \xe2\x80\x9cthe government withheld multiple statements from [Williams\xe2\x80\x99s] trial counsel, all of which strengthened the inference that Amos Norwood was sexually inappropriate with a number of teenage boys,\xe2\x80\x9d and Williams was unable to access those statements until an evidentiary proceeding ordered by the court. App. 95a.

\n

The Commonwealth appealed to the Pennsylvania Supreme Court, and Williams filed a motion requesting that Chief Justice Castille recuse himself on the ground that he had \xe2\x80\x9cpersonally authorized his Office to seek the death penalty\xe2\x80\x9d nearly 30 years earlier. Id., at 181a (emphasis deleted). Chief Justice Castille summarily denied the recusal motion, and the six-member Pennsylvania Supreme Court proceeded to hear the case. The court unanimously reinstated Williams\xe2\x80\x99s sentence.

\n

According to the Pennsylvania Supreme Court, Williams failed to make the threshold showing necessary to overcome the time bar because there was \xe2\x80\x9cabundant evidence\xe2\x80\x9d that Williams \xe2\x80\x9cknew of Norwood\xe2\x80\x99s homosexuality and conduct with teenage boys well before trial, sufficient to present [Norwood] as unsympathetic before the jury.\xe2\x80\x9d ___ Pa., at ___, 105 A. 3d, at 1241. The court pointed out that Williams was, of course, personally aware of Norwood\xe2\x80\x99s abuse and could have raised the issue at trial, but instead chose to disclaim having ever met Norwood. The court also noted that Williams had raised similar claims of abuse in his first state habeas proceeding. Ibid. Chief Justice Castille concurred separately, criticizing the lower court for failing to dismiss Williams\xe2\x80\x99s petition as \xe2\x80\x9ctime-barred and frivolous.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___, 105 A. 3d, at 1245.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

In the context of a criminal proceeding, the Due Process Clause requires States to adopt those practices that are fundamental to principles of liberty and justice, and which inhere \xe2\x80\x9cin the very idea of free government\xe2\x80\x9d and are \xe2\x80\x9cthe inalienable right of a citizen of such a government.\xe2\x80\x9d Twining v. New Jersey,\n\n211 U.\xc2\xa0S. 78,\n106 (1908)\n. A fair trial and appeal is one such right. See Lisenba v. California,\n\n314 U.\xc2\xa0S. 219,\n236 (1941)\n; Aetna Life Ins. Co. v. Lavoie,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 813,\n825 (1986)\n. In ensuring that right, \xe2\x80\x9cit is normally within the power of the State to regulate procedures under which its laws are carried out,\xe2\x80\x9d unless a procedure \xe2\x80\x9coffends some principle of justice so rooted in the traditions and conscience of our people as to be ranked as fundamental.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 821 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

It is clear that a judge with \xe2\x80\x9ca direct, personal, substantial, pecuniary interest\xe2\x80\x9d in a case may not preside over that case. Tumey v. Ohio,\n\n273 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510,\n523 (1927)\n. We have also held that a judge may not oversee a criminal contempt proceeding where the judge has previously served as grand juror in the same case, or where the party charged with contempt has conducted \xe2\x80\x9can insulting attack upon the integrity of the judge carrying such potential for bias as to require disqualification.\xe2\x80\x9d Mayberry v. Pennsylvania,\n\n400 U.\xc2\xa0S. 455\n\xe2\x80\x93466 (1971) (internal quotation marks omitted); see Murchison, 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 139.

\n

Prior to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Caperton v. A. T. Massey Coal Co.,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 868 (2009)\n, we had declined to require judicial recusal under the Due Process Clause beyond those defined situations. In Caperton, however, the Court adopted a new standard that requires recusal \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the probability of actual bias on the part of the judge or decisionmaker is too high to be constitutionally tolerable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 872 (internal quotation marks omitted). The Court framed the inquiry as \xe2\x80\x9cwhether, under a realistic appraisal of psychological tendencies and human weakness, the interest poses such a risk of actual bias or prejudgment that the practice must be forbidden if the guarantee of due process is to be adequately implemented.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 883\xe2\x80\x93884 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

B

\n

According to the majority, the Due Process Clause required Chief Justice Castille\xe2\x80\x99s recusal because he had \xe2\x80\x9csignificant, personal involvement in a critical trial decision\xe2\x80\x9d in Williams\xe2\x80\x99s case. Ante, at 9. Otherwise, the majority explains, there is \xe2\x80\x9can unacceptable risk of actual bias.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. In the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his conclusion follows from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis in In re Murchison.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. But Murchison does not support the majority\xe2\x80\x99s new rule\xe2\x80\x94far from it.

\n

Murchison involved a peculiar Michigan law that authorized the same person to sit as both judge and \xe2\x80\x9cone-man grand jury\xe2\x80\x9d in the same case. 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 133 (internal quotation marks omitted). Pursuant to that law, a Michigan judge\xe2\x80\x94serving as grand jury\xe2\x80\x94heard testimony from two witnesses in a corruption case. The testimony \xe2\x80\x9cpersuaded\xe2\x80\x9d the judge that one of the witnesses \xe2\x80\x9chad committed perjury\xe2\x80\x9d; the second witness refused to answer questions. Id., at 134\xe2\x80\x93135. The judge accordingly charged the witnesses with criminal contempt, presided over the trial, and convicted them. Ibid. We reversed, holding that the trial had violated the Due Process Clause. Id., at 139.

\n

The Court today, acknowledging that Murchison \xe2\x80\x9cdiffer[s] in many respects from a case like this one,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7, earns full marks for understatement. The Court in fact fails to recognize the differences that are critical.

\n

First, Murchison found a due process violation because the judge (sitting as grand jury) accused the witnesses of contempt, and then (sitting as judge) presided over their trial on that charge. As a result, the judge had made up his mind about the only issue in the case before the trial had even begun. We held that such prejudgment violated the Due Process Clause. 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 137.

\n

Second, Murchison expressed concern that the judge\xe2\x80\x99s recollection of the testimony he had heard as grand juror was \xe2\x80\x9clikely to weigh far more heavily with him than any testimony given\xe2\x80\x9d at trial. Id., at 138. For that reason, the Court found that the judge was at risk of calling \xe2\x80\x9con his own personal knowledge and impression of what had occurred in the grand jury room,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than the evidence presented to him by the parties. Ibid.

\n

Neither of those due process concerns is present here. Chief Justice Castille was involved in the decision to seek the death penalty, and perhaps it would be reasonable under Murchison to require him to recuse himself from any challenge casting doubt on that recommendation. But that is not this case.

\n

This case is about whether Williams may overcome the procedural bar on filing an untimely habeas petition, which required him to show that the government interfered with his ability to raise his habeas claim, and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe information on which he relies could not have been obtained earlier with the exercise of due diligence.\xe2\x80\x9d ___ Pa., at ___, 105 A. 3d, at 1240. Even if Williams were to overcome the timeliness bar, moreover, the only claim he sought to raise on the merits was that the prosecution had failed to turn over certain evidence at trial. The problem in Murchison was that the judge, having been \xe2\x80\x9cpart of the accusatory process\xe2\x80\x9d regarding the guilt or innocence of the defendants, could not then be \xe2\x80\x9cwholly disinterested\xe2\x80\x9d when called upon to decide that very same issue. 349 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 137. In this case, in contrast, neither the procedural question nor Williams\xe2\x80\x99s merits claim in any way concerns the pretrial decision to seek the death penalty.

\n

It is abundantly clear that, unlike in Murchison, Chief Justice Castille had not made up his mind about either the contested evidence or the legal issues under review in Williams\xe2\x80\x99s fifth habeas petition. How could he have? Neither the contested evidence nor the legal issues were ever before him as prosecutor. The one-and-a-half page memo prepared by Assistant District Attorney Foulkes in 1986 did not discuss the evidence that Williams claims was withheld by the prosecution at trial. It also did not discuss Williams\xe2\x80\x99s allegation that Norwood sexually abused young men. It certainly did not discuss whether Williams could have obtained that evidence of abuse ear-lier through the exercise of due diligence.

\n

Williams does not assert that Chief Justice Castille had any prior knowledge of the alleged failure of the prosecution to turn over such evidence, and he does not argue that Chief Justice Castille had previously made any decision with respect to that evidence in his role as prosecutor. Even assuming that Chief Justice Castille remembered the contents of the memo almost 30 years later\xe2\x80\x94which is doubtful\xe2\x80\x94the memo could not have given Chief Justice Castille any special \xe2\x80\x9cimpression\xe2\x80\x9d of facts or issues not raised in that memo. Id., at 138.

\n

The majority attempts to justify its rule based on the \xe2\x80\x9crisk\xe2\x80\x9d that a judge \xe2\x80\x9cwould be so psychologically wedded to his or her previous position as a prosecutor that the judge would consciously or unconsciously avoid the appearance of having erred or changed position.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7 (internal quotation marks omitted). But as a matter of simple logic, nothing about how Chief Justice Castille might rule on Williams\xe2\x80\x99s fifth habeas petition would suggest that the judge had erred or changed his position on the distinct question whether to seek the death penalty prior to trial. In sum, there was not such an \xe2\x80\x9cobjective risk of actual bias,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 13, that it was fundamentally unfair for Chief Justice Castille to participate in the decision of an issue having nothing to do with his prior participation in the case.

The Due Process Clause did not prohibit Chief Justice Castille from hearing Williams\xe2\x80\x99s case. That does not mean, however, that it was appropriate for him to do so. Williams cites a number of state court decisions and ethics opinions that prohibit a prosecutor from later serving as judge in a case that he has prosecuted. Because the Due Process Clause does not mandate recusal in cases such as this, it is up to state authorities\xe2\x80\x94not this Court\xe2\x80\x94to determine whether recusal should be required.

\n

I would affirm the judgment of the Pennsylvania Supreme Court, and respectfully dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s contrary conclusion.
