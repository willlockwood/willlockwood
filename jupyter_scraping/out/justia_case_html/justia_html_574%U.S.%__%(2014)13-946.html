SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

RAUL LOPEZ, WARDEN v. MARVIN VERNIS SMITH

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93946\xe2\x80\x83Decided October 6, 2014

\n

\n

\n \n Per Curiam.

\n

\n \n When a state prisoner seeks federal habeas relief on the ground that a state court, in adjudicating a claim on the merits, misapplied federal law, a federal court may grant relief only if the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). We have emphasized, time and again, that the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA),\n\n110Stat.\n1214, prohibits the federal courts of appeals from rely-ing on their own precedent to conclude that a particular constitutional principle is \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g.,Marshall v. Rodgers, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. __, __ (2013) (per curiam) (slip op. at 6). Because the Ninth Circuit failed to comply with this rule, we reverse its decision granting habeas relief to respondent Marvin Smith.

\n

I

\n

\n \n Respondent was arrested for the murder of his wife, Minnie Smith. On December 15, 2005, Mrs. Smith was found dead in the home she shared with respondent, and it was determined that she was killed by a massive blow to the head from a fireplace log roller. The home appeared to have been ransacked, and valuable jewelry was missing.

\n

\n \n The State charged respondent with first-degree murder and offered substantial incriminating evidence at trial. The prosecution presented evidence that respondent \xe2\x80\x9cwas unfaithful to his wife for many years, that his wife was threatening to divorce him, and that he told one of his former employees .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the \xe2\x80\x98only way\xe2\x80\x99 he or his wife would get out of their marriage was \xe2\x80\x98to die,\xe2\x80\x99 because he was \xe2\x80\x98not going to give [Mrs. Smith] half of what [he] got so some other man can live off of it.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 731 F.\xc2\xa03d 859, 862\xe2\x80\x93863 (CA9 2013) (second alteration in original). Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s DNA was also found on the murder weapon, pieces of duct tape found near the body, and a burned matchstick that was found in the bedroom and that may have been used to inflict burns on the body. See id., at 863; see also People v. Smith, 2010 WL 4975500, *1\xe2\x80\x93*2 (Cal. App., Dec. 8, 2010). The missing jewelry was discovered in the trunk of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s car, wrapped in duct tape from the same roll that had provided the pieces found near the body. See 731 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 863. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s DNA was found on the duct tape in his trunk. See Smith, 2010 WL 4975500, at *2. In addition, a criminologist testified that the ransacking of the Smiths\xe2\x80\x99 home appeared to have been staged. See 731 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 863.

\n

\n \n Respondent defended in part on the basis that he could not have delivered the fatal blow due to rotator cuff surgery several weeks before the murder. See ibid. (He mounted this defense despite the fact that police had observed him wielding a 6-foot-long 2 by 4 to pry something out of a concrete slab at a construction site the week after the murder. See Smith, 2010 WL 4975500, at *1.) The defense also suggested that one of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s former employees had committed the crime to obtain money to pay a debt he owed respondent. See 731 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 863.

\n

\n \n At the close of evidence, the prosecution requested an aiding-and-abetting instruction, and the trial court agreed to give such an instruction. During closing argument, the prosecutor contended that respondent was physically able to wield the log roller that had killed Mrs. Smith, but he also informed the jury that, even if respondent had not delivered the fatal blow, he could still be convicted on an aiding-and-abetting theory. See id., at 864. The jury convicted respondent of first-degree murder without specifying which theory of guilt it adopted.

\n

\n \n After a series of state-court proceedings not relevant here, the California Court of Appeal affirmed respondent\xe2\x80\x99s conviction. The state court rejected respondent\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that he had inadequate notice of the possibility of con-viction on an aiding-and-abetting theory. The court ex-plained that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98an accusatory pleading charging a defendant with murder need not specify the theory of murder on which the prosecution intends to rely,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and noted that the \xe2\x80\x9cinformation charged defendant with murder in compliance with the governing statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Smith, 2010 WL 4975500, at *6\xe2\x80\x93*7. Furthermore, the court held that \xe2\x80\x9ceven if this case required greater specificity concerning the basis of defendant\xe2\x80\x99s liability, the evidence presented at his preliminary examination provided it.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at *7. The upshot was that \xe2\x80\x9cthe information and preliminary examination testimony adequately notified defendant he could be prosecuted for murder as an aider and abettor.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at *8. The California Supreme Court denied respondent\xe2\x80\x99s petition for review.

\n

\n \n Respondent filed a petition for habeas relief with the United States District Court for the Central District of California. The Magistrate Judge recommended granting relief, and the District Court summarily adopted the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s recommendation.

\n

\n \n The Ninth Circuit affirmed. The court acknowledged that the \xe2\x80\x9cinformation charging [respondent] with first-degree murder was initially sufficient to put him on notice that he could be convicted either as a principal or as an aider-and-abettor,\xe2\x80\x9d because under California law \xe2\x80\x9caiding and abetting a crime is the same substantive offense as perpetrating the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d 731 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 868. But the Ninth Circuit nevertheless concluded that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment and due process right to notice had been violated because it believed the prosecution (until it requested the aiding-and-abetting jury instruction) had tried the case only on the theory that respondent himself had delivered the fatal blow. See id., at 869.

\n

\n \n The Ninth Circuit did not purport to identify any case in which we have found notice constitutionally inadequate because, although the defendant was initially adequately apprised of the offense against him, the prosecutor focused at trial on one potential theory of liability at the expense of another. Rather, it found the instant case to be \xe2\x80\x9cindistinguishable from\xe2\x80\x9d the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s own decision in Sheppard v. Rees, 909 F.\xc2\xa02d 1234 (1989), which the court thought \xe2\x80\x9cfaithfully applied the principles enunciated by the Supreme Court.\xe2\x80\x9d 731 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 868. The court also rejected, as an \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable determination of the facts,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(2), the California Court of Appeal\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that preliminary examination testimony and the jury instructions conference put respondent on notice of the possibility of conviction on an aiding-and-abetting theory. See id., at 871\xe2\x80\x93872.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n \n The Ninth Circuit held, and respondent does not dispute, that respondent initially received adequate notice of the possibility of conviction on an aiding-and-abetting theory. The question is therefore whether habeas relief is warranted because the State principally relied at trial on the theory that respondent himself delivered the fatal blow.

\n

\n \n Assuming, arguendo, that a defendant is entitled to notice of the possibility of conviction on an aiding-and-abetting theory, the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s grant of habeas relief may be affirmed only if this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases clearly establish that a defendant, once adequately apprised of such a possibility, can nevertheless be deprived of adequate notice by a prosecutorial decision to focus on another theory of liability at trial. The Ninth Circuit pointed to no case of ours holding as much. Instead, the Court of Appeals cited three older cases that stand for nothing more than the general proposition that a defendant must have adequate notice of the charges against him. See 731 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 866\xe2\x80\x93867 (citing Russell v. United States,\n\n369 U.\xc2\xa0S. 749\n\xe2\x80\x93764 (1962); In\xc2\xa0re Oliver,\n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 257\n\xe2\x80\x93274 (1948); Cole v. Arkansas,\n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 196,\n201 (1948)\n).

\n

\n \n This proposition is far too abstract to establish clearly the specific rule respondent needs. We have before cautioned the lower courts\xe2\x80\x94and the Ninth Circuit in particular\xe2\x80\x94against \xe2\x80\x9cframing our precedents at such a high level of generality.\xe2\x80\x9d Nevada v. Jackson, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (per curiam) (slip op., at 7). None of our decisions that the Ninth Circuit cited addresses, even remotely, the specific question presented by this case. See Russell, supra, at 752 (indictment for \xe2\x80\x9crefus[ing] to answer any question pertinent to [a] question under [congressional] inquiry,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n2 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7192, failed to \xe2\x80\x9cidentify the subject under congressional subcommittee inquiry\xe2\x80\x9d); In\xc2\xa0re Oliver, supra, at 259 (instantaneous indictment, conviction, and sentence by judge acting as grand jury with no prior notice of charge to defendant); Cole, supra, at 197 (affirmance of criminal convictions \xe2\x80\x9cunder a .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. statute for violation of which [defendants] had not been charged\xe2\x80\x9d).[1]

\n

\n \n Because our case law does not clearly establish the legal proposition needed to grant respondent habeas relief, the Ninth Circuit was forced to rely heavily on its own decision in Sheppard, supra. Of course, AEDPA permits habeas relief only if a state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law\xe2\x80\x9d as determined by this Court, not by the courts of appeals.\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). The Ninth Circuit attempted to evade this barrier by holding that Sheppard \xe2\x80\x9cfaithfully applied the principles enunciated by the Supreme Court in Cole, Oliver, and Russell.\xe2\x80\x9d 731 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 868. But Circuit precedent cannot \xe2\x80\x9crefine or sharpen a general principle of Supreme Court jurisprudence into a specific legal rule that this Court has not announced.\xe2\x80\x9d Marshall, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at __ (slip op., at 6). Sheppard is irrelevant to the question presented by this case: whether our case law clearly establishes that a prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s focus on one theory of liability at trial can render earlier notice of another theory of liability inadequate.

\n

B

\n

\n \n The Ninth Circuit also disagreed with what it termed the state court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdetermination of the facts\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94principally, the state court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that preliminary examination testimony and the prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 request for an aiding-and-abetting jury instruction shortly before closing arguments adequately put respondent on notice of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s aiding-and-abetting theory. 731 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 871 (internal quotation marks omitted). The Ninth Circuit therefore granted relief under \xc2\xa72254(d)(2), which permits habeas relief where the state-court \xe2\x80\x9cdecision .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was based on an unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented in the State court proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d That holding cannot be sustained.

\n

\n \n In purporting to reject the state court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdetermination of the facts,\xe2\x80\x9d the Ninth Circuit focused on preliminary examination testimony by an investigator about conversations between respondent and his cellmate. According to the investigator, the cellmate stated that respondent told him that respondent \xe2\x80\x9chad to get rid of his wife because she was standing in the way of his future plans; that she was threatening to divorce him and he wasn\xe2\x80\x99t going to give up half of his property\xe2\x80\x9d; that respondent made his house look like the site of a home invasion robbery; and that, when he left for work the morning of the murder, he left the window open and did not set the alarm. Smith, 2010 WL 4975500, at *7. The investigator also testified that the cellmate did not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98know any of the details of the homicide itself and how it was carried out,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and that respondent \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98never told [the cellmate] specifically who\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d committed the homicide. Ibid. The California Court of Appeal held that these statements, taken together, suggested that respondent was involved in planning and facilitating the crime but that the fatal blow might have been delivered by an accomplice. Ibid. Thus, the California court believed that even assuming that the information by itself was not sufficient, this testimony naturally lent itself to conviction on an aiding-and-abetting theory and so gave respondent even greater notice of such a possibility. Ibid.

\n

\nThe Ninth Circuit also focused on the jury instructions conference, which occurred after the defense rested but before the parties\xe2\x80\x99 closing arguments. During that conference, prosecutors requested an aiding-and-abetting instruction, which further provided notice to respondent. The California Court of Appeal concluded that this case is distinguishable from Sheppard v. Rees, 909 F.\xc2\xa02d 1234, because, unlike that case, the conference here did not occur immediately before closing arguments. The Ninth Circuit disagreed, holding that because \xe2\x80\x9cdefense counsel had only the lunch recess to formulate a response\xe2\x80\x9d to the aiding-and-abetting instruction, this case \xe2\x80\x9cis indistinguishable from Sheppard,\xe2\x80\x9d where the prosecution also \xe2\x80\x9crequested the new instruction the same day as closing.\xe2\x80\x9d 731 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 868, 870.

\n

\n \n Although the Ninth Circuit claimed its disagreement with the state court was factual in nature, in reality its grant of relief was based on a legal conclusion about the adequacy of the notice provided. The Ninth Circuit believed that the events detailed above, even when taken together with the information filed against respondent, failed to measure up to the standard of notice applicable in cases like this. That ranked as a legal determination governed by \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), not one of fact governed by \xc2\xa72254(d)(2). But, as we have explained, the Ninth Circuit cited only its own precedent for establishing the appropriate standard. Absent a decision of ours clearly establishing the relevant standard, the Ninth Circuit had nothing against which it could assess, and deem lacking, the notice afforded respondent by the information and proceedings. It therefore had no basis to reject the state court\xe2\x80\x99s assessment that respondent was adequately apprised of the pos-sibility of conviction on an aiding-and-abetting theory.[2]

\n

\n \n The petition for a writ of certiorari is granted. The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.
