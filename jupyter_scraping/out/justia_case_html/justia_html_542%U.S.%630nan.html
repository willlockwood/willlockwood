UNITED STATES v. PATANE

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x931183. Argued December 9, 2003\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 28, 2004

\n

After Officer Fox began to investigate respondent\xe2\x80\x99s apparent violation of a temporary restraining order, a federal agent told Fox\xe2\x80\x99s colleague, Detective Benner, that respondent, a convicted felon, illegally possessed a pistol. Officer Fox and Detective Benner proceeded to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s home, where Fox arrested him for violating the restraining order. Benner attempted to advise respondent of his rights under Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436, but respondent interrupted, asserting that he knew his rights. Benner then asked about the pistol and retrieved and seized it. Respondent was indicted for possession of a firearm by a convicted felon, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7922(g)(1). The District Court granted his motion to suppress the pistol, reasoning that the officers lacked probable cause to arrest him, and declining to rule on his alternative argument that the gun should be suppressed as the fruit of an unwarned statement. The Tenth Circuit reversed the probable-cause ruling, but affirmed the suppression order on respondent\xe2\x80\x99s alternative theory. Rejecting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Oregon v. Elstad, 470 U. S. 298, and Michigan v. Tucker, 417 U. S. 433, foreclosed application of the fruit of the poisonous tree doctrine of Wong Sun v. United States, 371 U. S. 471, 488, to the present context, the appeals court reasoned that Oregon and Tucker, which were based on the view that Miranda announced a prophylactic rule, were incompatible with Dickerson v. United States, 530 U. S. 428, 444, in which this Court held that Miranda announced a constitutional rule. The appeals court thus equated Dickerson\xe2\x80\x99s ruling with the proposition that a failure to warn pursuant to Miranda is itself a violation of the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s Fifth Amendment rights.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is reversed, and the case is remanded.

\n

304 F.\xc2\xa03d 1013, reversed and remanded.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas, joined by The Chief Justice and Justice Scalia, concluded that a failure to give a suspect Miranda warnings does not require suppression of the physical fruits of the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s unwarned but voluntary statements. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Miranda rule is a prophylactic employed to protect against violations of the Self-Incrimination Clause, U. S.Const., Amdt. 5. That Clause\xe2\x80\x99s core protection is a prohibition on compelling a criminal defendant to testify against himself at trial. See, e.g., Chavez v. Martinez, 538 U. S. 760, 764\xe2\x80\x93768. It cannot be violated by the introduction of nontestimonial evidence obtained as a result of voluntary statements. See, e.g., United States v. Hubbell, 530 U. S. 27, 34. The Court has recognized and applied several prophylactic rules designed to protect the core privilege against self-incrimination. For example, the Miranda rule creates a presumption of coercion in custodial interrogations, in the absence of specific warnings, that is generally irrebuttable for purposes of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief. E.g., 384 U. S., at 467. But because such prophylactic rules necessarily sweep beyond the Self-Incrimination Clause\xe2\x80\x99s actual protections, see, e.g., Withrow v. Williams, 507 U. S. 680, 690\xe2\x80\x93691, any further extension of one of them must be justified by its necessity for the protection of the actual right against compelled self-incrimination, e.g., Chavez, supra, at 778. Thus, uncompelled statements taken without Miranda warnings can be used to impeach a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s testimony at trial, see Elstad, supra, at 307\xe2\x80\x93308, though the fruits of actually compelled testimony cannot, see New Jersey v. Portash, 440 U. S. 450, 458\xe2\x80\x93459. A blanket rule requiring suppression of statements noncompliant with the Miranda rule could not be justified by reference to the \xe2\x80\x9cFifth Amendment goal of assuring trustworthy evidence\xe2\x80\x9d or by any deterrence rationale, e.g., Elstad, 470 U. S., at 308, and would therefore fail the Court\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that the closest possible fit be maintained between the Self-Incrimination Clause and any rule designed to protect it. Furthermore, the Clause contains its own exclusionary rule that automatically protects those subjected to coercive police interrogations from the use of their involuntary statements (or evidence derived from their statements) in any subsequent criminal trial. E.g., id., at 307\xe2\x80\x93308. This explicit textual protection supports a strong presumption against expanding the Miranda rule any further. Cf. Graham v. Connor, 490 U. S. 386. Finally, nothing in Dickerson calls into question the Court\xe2\x80\x99s continued insistence on its close-fit requirement. Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0That a mere failure to give Miranda warnings does not, by itself, violate a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights or even the Miranda rule was evident in many of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s pre-Dickerson cases, see, e.g., Elstad, supra, at 308, and the Court has adhered to that view since Dickerson, see Chavez, supra, at 772\xe2\x80\x93773. This follows from the nature of the \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental trial right\xe2\x80\x9d protected by the Self-Incrimination Clause, e.g., Withrow, supra, at 691, which the Miranda rule, in turn, protects. Thus, the police do not violate a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights (or the Miranda rule) by negligent or even deliberate failures to provide full Miranda warnings. Potential violations occur, if at all, only upon the admission of unwarned statements into evidence. And, at that point, the exclusion of such statements is a complete and sufficient remedy for any perceived Miranda violation. Chavez, supra, at 790. Unlike actual violations of the Self-Incrimination Clause, there is, with respect to mere failures to warn, nothing to deter and therefore no reason to apply Wong Sun\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfruit of the poisonous tree\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine. It is not for this Court to impose its preferred police practices on either federal or state officials. Pp. 8\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The Tenth Circuit erred in ruling that the taking of unwarned statements violates a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights. Dickerson\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of Miranda as a constitutional rule does not lessen the need to maintain the close-fit requirement. There is no such fit here. Introduction of the nontestimonial fruit of a voluntary statement, such as respondent\xe2\x80\x99s pistol, does not implicate the Clause. It presents no risk that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s coerced statements (however defined) will be used against him at a criminal trial. In any case, the exclusion of unwarned statements is a complete and sufficient remedy for any perceived Miranda violation. E.g., Chavez, supra, at 790. Similarly, because police cannot violate the Clause by taking unwarned though voluntary statements, an exclusionary rule cannot be justified by reference to a deterrence effect on law enforcement, as the court below believed. The word \xe2\x80\x9cwitness\xe2\x80\x9d in the constitutional text limits the Self-Incrimination Clause\xe2\x80\x99s scope to testimonial evidence. Hubbell, supra, at 34\xe2\x80\x9335. And although the Court requires the exclusion of the physical fruit of actually coerced statements, statements taken without sufficient Miranda warnings are presumed to have been coerced only for certain purposes and then only when necessary to protect the privilege against self-incrimination. This Court declines to extend that presumption further. Pp. 10\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy, joined by Justice O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, concluded that it is unnecessary to decide whether the detective\xe2\x80\x99s failure to give Patane full Miranda warnings should be characterized as a violation of the Miranda rule itself, or whether there is anything to deter so long as the unwarned statements are not later introduced at trial. In Oregon v. Elstad, 470 U. S. 298, New York v. Quarles, 467 U. S. 649, and Harris v. New York, 401 U. S. 222, evidence obtained following unwarned interrogations was held admissible based in large part on the Court\xe2\x80\x99s recognition that the concerns underlying the Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436, rule must be accommodated to other objectives of the criminal justice system. Here, it is sufficient to note that the Government presents an even stronger case for admitting the evidence obtained as the result of Patane\xe2\x80\x99s unwarned statement than was presented in Elstad and Michigan v. Tucker, 417 U. S. 433. Admission of nontestimonial physical fruits (the pistol here) does not run the risk of admitting into trial an accused\xe2\x80\x99s coerced incriminating statements against himself. In light of reliable physical evidence\xe2\x80\x99s important probative value, it is doubtful that exclusion can be justified by a deterrence rationale sensitive to both law enforcement interests and a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s rights during an in-custody interrogation. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x932.

\nOPINION OF THOMAS, J.
\nUNITED STATES V. PATANE
\n542 U. S. ____ (2004)

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 02-1183

UNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. SAMUEL\nFRANCIS PATANE

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

[June 28, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas announced the judgment of the Court and delivered an opinion, in which The Chief Justice and Justice Scalia join.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case we must decide whether a failure to give a suspect the warnings prescribed by Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436 (1966), requires suppression of the physical fruits of the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s unwarned but voluntary statements. The Court has previously addressed this question but has not reached a definitive conclusion. See Massachusetts v. White, 439 U. S. 280 (1978) (per curiam) (dividing evenly on the question); see also Patterson v. United States, 485 U. S. 922 (1988) (White, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari). Although we believe that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions in Oregon v. Elstad, 470 U. S. 298 (1985), and Michigan v. Tucker, 417 U. S. 433 (1974), are instructive, the Courts of Appeals have split on the question after our decision in Dickerson v. United States, 530 U. S. 428 (2000). See, e.g., United States v. Villalba-Alvarado, 345 F.\xc2\xa03d 1007 (CA8 2003) (holding admissible the physical fruits of a Miranda violation); United States v. Sterling, 283 F.\xc2\xa03d 216 (CA4 2002) (same); United States v. DeSumma, 272 F.\xc2\xa03d 176 (CA3 2001) (same); United States v. Faulkingham, 295 F.\xc2\xa03d 85 (CA1 2002) (holding admissible the physical fruits of a negligent Miranda violation). Because the Miranda rule protects against violations of the Self-Incrimination Clause, which, in turn, is not implicated by the introduction at trial of physical evidence resulting from voluntary statements, we answer the question presented in the negative.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In June 2001, respondent, Samuel Francis Patane, was arrested for harassing his ex-girlfriend, Linda O\xe2\x80\x99Donnell. He was released on bond, subject to a temporary restraining order that prohibited him from contacting O\xe2\x80\x99Donnell. Respondent apparently violated the restraining order by attempting to telephone O\xe2\x80\x99Donnell. On June 6, 2001, Officer Tracy Fox of the Colorado Springs Police Department began to investigate the matter. On the same day, a county probation officer informed an agent of the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, and Firearms (ATF), that respondent, a convicted felon, illegally possessed a .40 Glock pistol. The ATF relayed this information to Detective Josh Benner, who worked closely with the ATF. Together, Detective Benner and Officer Fox proceeded to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s residence.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After reaching the residence and inquiring into respondent\xe2\x80\x99s attempts to contact O\xe2\x80\x99Donnell, Officer Fox arrested respondent for violating the restraining order. Detective Benner attempted to advise respondent of his Miranda rights but got no further than the right to remain silent. At that point, respondent interrupted, asserting that he knew his rights, and neither officer attempted to complete the warning.[Footnote 1] App. 40.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Detective Benner then asked respondent about the Glock. Respondent was initially reluctant to discuss the matter, stating: \xe2\x80\x9cI am not sure I should tell you anything about the Glock because I don\xe2\x80\x99t want you to take it away from me.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 41. Detective Benner persisted, and respondent told him that the pistol was in his bedroom. Respondent then gave Detective Benner permission to retrieve the pistol. Detective Benner found the pistol and seized it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A grand jury indicted respondent for possession of a firearm by a convicted felon, in violation of 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7922(g)(1). The District Court granted respondent\xe2\x80\x99s motion to suppress the firearm, reasoning that the officers lacked probable cause to arrest respondent for violating the restraining order. It therefore declined to rule on respondent\xe2\x80\x99s alternative argument that the gun should be suppressed as the fruit of an unwarned statement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals reversed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling with respect to probable cause but affirmed the suppression order on respondent\xe2\x80\x99s alternative theory. The court rejected the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions in Elstad, supra, and Tucker, supra, foreclosed application of the fruit of the poisonous tree doctrine of Wong Sun v. United States, 371 U. S. 471 (1963), to the present context. 304 F.\xc2\xa03d 1013, 1019 (CA10 2002). These holdings were, the Court of Appeals reasoned, based on the view that Miranda announced a prophylactic rule, a position that it found to be incompatible with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Dickerson, supra, at 444 (\xe2\x80\x9cMiranda announced a constitutional rule that Congress may not supersede legislatively\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 2] The Court of Appeals thus equated Dickerson\xe2\x80\x99s announcement that Miranda is a constitutional rule with the proposition that a failure to warn pursuant to Miranda is itself a violation of the Constitution (and, more particularly, of the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s Fifth Amendment rights). Based on its understanding of Dickerson, the Court of Appeals rejected the post-Dickerson views of the Third and Fourth Circuits that the fruits doctrine does not apply to Miranda violations. 304 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1023\xe2\x80\x931027 (discussing United States v. Sterling, 283 F.\xc2\xa03d 216 (CA4 2002), and United States v. DeSumma, 272 F.\xc2\xa03d 176 (CA3 2001)). It also disagreed with the First Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that suppression is not generally required in the case of negligent failures to warn, 304 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1027\xe2\x80\x931029 (discussing United States v. Faulkingham, 295 F.\xc2\xa03d 85 (CA1 2002)), explaining that \xe2\x80\x9c[d]eterrence is necessary not merely to deter intentional wrongdoing, but also to ensure that officers diligently (non-negligently) protect\xe2\x80\x94and properly are trained to protect\xe2\x80\x94the constitutional rights of citizens,\xe2\x80\x9d 304 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1028\xe2\x80\x931029. We granted certiorari. 538 U. S. 976 (2003).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As we explain below, the Miranda rule is a prophylactic employed to protect against violations of the Self-Incrimination Clause. The Self-Incrimination Clause, however, is not implicated by the admission into evidence of the physical fruit of a voluntary statement. Accordingly, there is no justification for extending the Miranda rule to this context. And just as the Self-Incrimination Clause primarily focuses on the criminal trial, so too does the Miranda rule. The Miranda rule is not a code of police conduct, and police do not violate the Constitution (or\neven the Miranda rule, for that matter) by mere failures to warn. For this reason, the exclusionary rule articu-\nlated in cases such as Wong Sun does not apply. Accordingly, we reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Self-Incrimination Clause provides: \xe2\x80\x9cNo person \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself.\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Amdt. 5. We need not decide here the precise boundaries of the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s protection. For present purposes, it suffices to note that the core protection afforded by the Self-Incrimination Clause is a prohibition on compelling a criminal defendant to testify against himself at trial. See, e.g., Chavez v. Martinez, 538 U. S. 760, 764\xe2\x80\x93768 (2003) (plurality opinion); id., at 777\xe2\x80\x93779 (Souter, J., concurring in judgment); 8 J.\xc2\xa0Wigmore, Evidence \xc2\xa72263, p.\xc2\xa0378 (J. McNaughton rev. ed. 1961) (explaining that the Clause \xe2\x80\x9cwas directed at the employment of legal process to extract from the person\xe2\x80\x99s own lips an admission of guilt, which would thus take the place of other evidence\xe2\x80\x9d); see also United States v. Hubbell, 530 U. S. 27, 49\xe2\x80\x9356 (2000) (Thomas, J., concurring) (explaining that the privilege might extend to bar the compelled production of any incriminating evidence, testimonial or otherwise). The Clause cannot be violated by the introduction of nontestimonial evidence obtained as a result of voluntary statements. See, e.g., id., at 34 (noting that the word \xe2\x80\x9cwitness\xe2\x80\x9d in the Self-Incrimination Clause \xe2\x80\x9climits the relevant category of compelled incriminating communications to those that are \xe2\x80\x98testimonial\xe2\x80\x99 in character\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 35 (discussing why compelled blood samples do not violate the Clause; cataloging other examples and citing cases); Elstad, 470 U. S., at 304 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Fifth Amendment, of course, is not concerned with nontestimonial evidence\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 306\xe2\x80\x93307 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Fifth Amendment prohibits use by the prosecution in its case in chief only of compelled testimony\xe2\x80\x9d); Withrow v. Williams, 507 U. S. 680, 705 (1993) (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (describing \xe2\x80\x9ctrue Fifth Amendment claims [as] the extraction and use of compelled testimony\xe2\x80\x9d); New York v. Quarles, 467 U. S. 649, 665\xe2\x80\x93672, and n.\xc2\xa04 (1984) (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part) (explaining that the physical fruit of a Miranda violation need not be suppressed for these reasons).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, the Court has recognized and applied several prophylactic rules designed to protect the core privilege against self-incrimination. See, e.g., Chavez, supra, at 770\xe2\x80\x93772 (plurality opinion). For example, although the text of the Self-Incrimination Clause at least suggests that \xe2\x80\x9cits coverage [is limited to] compelled testimony that is used against the defendant in the trial itself,\xe2\x80\x9d Hubbell, supra, at 37, potential suspects may, at times, assert the privilege in proceedings in which answers might be used to incriminate them in a subsequent criminal case. See, e.g., United States v. Balsys, 524 U. S. 666, 671\xe2\x80\x93672 (1998); Minnesota v. Murphy, 465 U. S. 420, 426 (1984); cf. Kastigar v. United States, 406 U. S. 441 (1972) (holding that the Government may compel grand jury testimony from witnesses over Fifth Amendment objections if the witnesses receive \xe2\x80\x9cuse and derivative use immunity\xe2\x80\x9d); Uniformed Sanitation Men Assn., Inc. v. Commissioner of Sanitation of City of New York, 392 U. S. 280, 284 (1968) (allowing the Government to use economic compulsion to secure statements but only if the Government grants appropriate immunity). We have explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he natural concern which underlies [these] decisions is that an inability to protect the right at one stage of a proceeding may make its invocation useless at a later stage.\xe2\x80\x9d Tucker, 417 U. S., at 440\xe2\x80\x93441.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, in Miranda, the Court concluded that the possibility of coercion inherent in custodial interrogations unacceptably raises the risk that a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s privilege against self-incrimination might be violated. See Dickerson, 530 U. S., at 434\xe2\x80\x93435; Miranda, 384 U. S., at 467. To protect against this danger, the Miranda rule creates a presumption of coercion, in the absence of specific warnings, that is generally irrebuttable for purposes of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But because these prophylactic rules (including the Miranda rule) necessarily sweep beyond the actual protections of the Self-Incrimination Clause, see, e.g., Withrow, supra, at 690\xe2\x80\x93691; Elstad, supra, at 306, any further extension of these rules must be justified by its necessity for the protection of the actual right against compelled self-incrimination, Chavez, supra, at 778 (opinion of Souter, J.) (requiring a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98powerful showing\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d before \xe2\x80\x9cexpand[ing] \xe2\x80\xa6 the privilege against compelled self-incrimination\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, at times the Court has declined to extend Miranda even where it has perceived a need to protect the privilege against self-incrimination. See, e.g., Quarles, supra, at 657 (concluding \xe2\x80\x9cthat the need for answers to questions in a situation posing a threat to the public safety outweighs the need for the prophylactic rule protecting the Fifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s privilege against self-incrimination\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is for these reasons that statements taken without Miranda warnings (though not actually compelled) can be used to impeach a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s testimony at trial, see Elstad, supra, at 307\xe2\x80\x93308; Harris v. New York, 401 U. S. 222 (1971), though the fruits of actually compelled testimony cannot, see New Jersey v. Portash, 440 U. S. 450, 458\xe2\x80\x93459 (1979). More generally, the Miranda rule \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not require that the statements [taken without complying with the rule] and their fruits be discarded as inherently tainted,\xe2\x80\x9d Elstad, 470 U. S., at 307. Such a blanket suppression rule could not be justified by reference to the \xe2\x80\x9cFifth Amendment goal of assuring trustworthy evidence\xe2\x80\x9d or by any deterrence rationale, id., at 308; see Tucker, supra, at 446\xe2\x80\x93449; Harris, supra, at 225\xe2\x80\x93226, and n.\xc2\xa02, and would therefore fail our close-fit requirement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, the Self-Incrimination Clause contains its own exclusionary rule. It provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o person \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself.\xe2\x80\x9d Amdt. 5. Unlike the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s bar on unreasonable searches, the Self-Incrimination Clause is self-executing. We have repeatedly explained \xe2\x80\x9cthat those subjected to coercive police interrogations have an automatic protection from the use of their involuntary statements (or evidence derived from their statements) in any subsequent criminal trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Chavez, 538 U. S., at 769 (plurality opinion) (citing, for example, Elstad, supra, at 307\xe2\x80\x93308). This explicit textual protection supports a strong presumption against expanding the Miranda rule any further. Cf. Graham v. Connor, 490 U. S. 386 (1989).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, nothing in Dickerson, including its characterization of Miranda as announcing a constitutional rule, 530 U. S., at 444, changes any of these observations. Indeed, in Dickerson, the Court specifically noted that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csubsequent cases have reduced the impact of the Miranda rule on legitimate law enforcement while reaffirming [Miranda]\xe2\x80\x99s core ruling that unwarned statements may not be used as evidence in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 443\xe2\x80\x93444. This description of Miranda, especially the emphasis on the use of \xe2\x80\x9cunwarned statements \xe2\x80\xa6 in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case in chief,\xe2\x80\x9d makes clear our continued focus on the protections of the Self-Incrimination Clause. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on our Miranda precedents, including both Tucker and Elstad, see, e.g., Dickerson, supra, at 438, 441, further demonstrates the continuing validity of those decisions. In short, nothing in Dickerson calls into question our continued insistence that the closest possible fit be maintained between the Self-Incrimination Clause and any rule designed to protect it.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our cases also make clear the related point that a mere failure to give Miranda warnings does not, by itself, violate a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights or even the Miranda rule. So much was evident in many of our pre-Dickerson cases, and we have adhered to this view since Dickerson. See Chavez, supra, at 772\xe2\x80\x93773 (plurality opinion) (holding that a failure to read Miranda warnings did not violate the respondent\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights); 538 U. S., at 789. (Kennedy, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (agreeing \xe2\x80\x9cthat failure to give a Miranda warning does not, without more, establish a completed violation when the unwarned interrogation ensues\xe2\x80\x9d); Elstad, supra, at 308; Quarles, 467 U. S., at 654; cf. Chavez, supra, at 777\xe2\x80\x93779 (opinion of Souter, J.). This, of course, follows from the nature of the right protected by the Self-Incrimination Clause, which the Miranda rule, in turn, protects. It is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a fundamental trial right.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Withrow, 507 U. S., at 691 (quoting United States v. Verdugo-Urquidez, 494 U. S. 259, 264 (1990)). See also Chavez, supra, at 766\xe2\x80\x93768 (plurality opinion); id., at 790 (Kennedy, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe identification of a Miranda violation and its consequences, then, ought to be determined at trial\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It follows that police do not violate a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights (or the Miranda rule) by negligent or even deliberate failures to provide the suspect with the full panoply of warnings prescribed by Miranda. Potential violations occur, if at all, only upon the admission of unwarned statements into evidence at trial. And, at that point, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he exclusion of unwarned statements \xe2\x80\xa6 is a complete and sufficient remedy\xe2\x80\x9d for any perceived Miranda violation. Chavez, supra, at 790.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, unlike unreasonable searches under the Fourth Amendment or actual violations of the Due Process Clause or the Self-Incrimination Clause, there is, with respect to mere failures to warn, nothing to deter. There is therefore no reason to apply the \xe2\x80\x9cfruit of the poisonous tree\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine of Wong Sun, 371 U. S., at 488.[Footnote 4] See also Nix v. Williams, 467 U. S. 431, 441 (1984) (discussing the exclusionary rule in the Sixth Amendment context and noting that it applies to \xe2\x80\x9cillegally obtained evidence [and] other incriminating evidence derived from [it]\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). It is not for this Court to impose its preferred police practices on either federal law enforcement officials or their state counterparts.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the present case, the Court of Appeals, relying on Dickerson, wholly adopted the position that the taking of unwarned statements violates a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights. 304 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1028\xe2\x80\x931029.[Footnote 5] And, of course, if this were so, a strong deterrence-based argument could be made for suppression of the fruits. See, e.g., Nix, supra, at 441\xe2\x80\x93444; Wong Sun, supra, at 484\xe2\x80\x93486; cf. Nardone v. United States, 308 U. S. 338, 341 (1939).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But Dickerson\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of Miranda as a constitutional rule does not lessen the need to maintain the closest possible fit between the Self-Incrimination Clause and any judge-made rule designed to protect it. And there is no such fit here. Introduction of the nontestimonial fruit of a voluntary statement, such as respondent\xe2\x80\x99s Glock, does not implicate the Self-Incrimination Clause. The admission of such fruit presents no risk that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s coerced statements (however defined) will be used against him at a criminal trial. In any case, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he exclusion of unwarned statements \xe2\x80\xa6 is a complete and sufficient remedy\xe2\x80\x9d for any perceived Miranda violation. Chavez, 538 U. S., at 790 (Kennedy, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). See also H. Friendly, Benchmarks 280\xe2\x80\x93281 (1967). There is simply no need to extend (and therefore no justification for extending) the prophylactic rule of Miranda to this context.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, because police cannot violate the Self-Incrimination Clause by taking unwarned though voluntary statements, an exclusionary rule cannot be justified by reference to a deterrence effect on law enforcement, as the Court of Appeals believed, 304 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1028\xe2\x80\x931029. Our decision not to apply Wong Sun to mere failures to give Miranda warnings was sound at the time Tucker and Elstad were decided, and we decline to apply Wong Sun to such failures now.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals ascribed significance to the fact that, in this case, there might be \xe2\x80\x9clittle [practical] difference between [respondent\xe2\x80\x99s] confessional statement\xe2\x80\x9d and the actual physical evidence. 304 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1027. The distinction, the court said, \xe2\x80\x9cappears to make little sense as a matter of policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But, putting policy aside, we have held that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he word \xe2\x80\x98witness\xe2\x80\x99 in the constitutional text limits the\xe2\x80\x9d scope of the Self-Incrimination Clause to testimonial evidence. Hubbell, 530 U. S., at 34\xe2\x80\x9335. The Constitution itself makes the distinction.[Footnote 6] And although it is true that the Court requires the exclusion of the physical fruit of actually coerced statements, it must be remembered that statements taken without sufficient Miranda warnings are presumed to have been coerced only for certain purposes and then only when necessary to protect the privilege against self-incrimination. See Part II, supra. For the reasons discussed above, we decline to extend that presumption further.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, we reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Souter, with whom Justice Stevens and Justice Ginsburg join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority repeatedly says that the Fifth Amendment does not address the admissibility of nontestimonial evidence, an overstatement that is beside the point. The issue actually presented today is whether courts should apply the fruit of the poisonous tree doctrine lest we create an incentive for the police to omit Miranda warnings, see Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436 (1966), before custodial interrogation.[Footnote 1] In closing their eyes to the consequences of giving an evidentiary advantage to those who ignore Miranda, the majority adds an important inducement for interrogators to ignore the rule in that case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Miranda rested on insight into the inherently coercive character of custodial interrogation and the inherently difficult exercise of assessing the voluntariness of any confession resulting from it. Unless the police give the prescribed warnings meant to counter the coercive atmosphere, a custodial confession is inadmissible, there being no need for the previous time-consuming and difficult enquiry into voluntariness. That inducement to forestall involuntary statements and troublesome issues of fact can only atrophy if we turn around and recognize an evidentiary benefit when an unwarned statement leads investigators to tangible evidence. There is, of course, a price for excluding evidence, but the Fifth Amendment is worth a price, and in the absence of a very good reason, the logic of Miranda should be followed: a Miranda violation raises a presumption of coercion, Oregon v. Elstad, 470 U. S. 298, 306\xe2\x80\x93307, and n.\xc2\xa01 (1985), and the Fifth Amendment privilege against compelled self-incrimination extends to the exclusion of derivative evidence, see United States v. Hubbell, 530 U. S. 27, 37\xe2\x80\x9338 (2000) (recognizing \xe2\x80\x9cthe Fifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection against the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s use of incriminating information derived directly or indirectly from \xe2\x80\xa6 [actually] compelled testimony\xe2\x80\x9d); Kastigar v. United States, 406 U. S. 441, 453 (1972). That should be the end of this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The fact that the books contain some exceptions to the Miranda exclusionary rule carries no weight here. In Harris v. New York, 401 U. S. 222 (1971), it was respect for the integrity of the judicial process that justified the admission of unwarned statements as impeachment evidence. But Patane\xe2\x80\x99s suppression motion can hardly be described as seeking to \xe2\x80\x9cpervert\xe2\x80\x9d Miranda \xe2\x80\x9cinto a license to use perjury\xe2\x80\x9d or otherwise handicap the \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional truth-testing devices of the adversary process.\xe2\x80\x9d 401 U. S., at 225\xe2\x80\x93226. Nor is there any suggestion that the officers\xe2\x80\x99 failure to warn Patane was justified or mitigated by a public emergency or other exigent circumstance, as in New York v. Quarles, 467 U. S. 649 (1984). And of course the premise of Oregon v. Elstad, supra, is not on point; although a failure to give Miranda warnings before one individual statement does not necessarily bar the admission of a subsequent statement given after adequate warnings, 470 U. S. 298; cf. Missouri v. Seibert, ante, at ___ (slip op., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313) (plurality opinion), that rule obviously does not apply to physical evidence seized once and for all.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no way to read this case except as an unjustifiable invitation to law enforcement officers to flout Miranda when there may be physical evidence to be gained. The incentive is an odd one, coming from the Court on the same day it decides Missouri v. Seibert, ante. I respectfully dissent.

\xc2\xa0In so saying, we are taking the legal issue as it comes to us, even though the facts give off the scent of a made-up case. If there was a Miranda failure, the most immediate reason was that Patane told the police to stop giving the warnings because he already knew his rights. There could easily be an analogy in this case to the bumbling mistake the police committed in Oregon v. Elstad, 470 U. S. 298 (1985). See Missouri v. Seibert, ante, at ___ (plurality opinion) (slip op., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313).

\nFootnote 2

\xc2\xa0To the extent that Michigan v. Tucker, 417 U. S. 433 (1974) (admitting the testimony of a witness who was discovered because of an unwarned custodial interrogation), created another exception to Miranda, it is off the point here. In Tucker, we explicitly declined to lay down a broad rule about the fruits of unwarned statements. Instead, we \xe2\x80\x9cplace[d] our holding on a narrower ground,\xe2\x80\x9d relying principally on the fact that the interrogation occurred before Miranda was decided and was conducted in good faith according to constitutional standards governing at that time. 417 U. S., at 447\xe2\x80\x93448 (citing Escobedo v. Illinois, 378 U. S. 478 (1964)).
