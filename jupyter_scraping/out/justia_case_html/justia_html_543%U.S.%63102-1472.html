\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

CHEROKEE NATION OF OKLAHOMA et al. v. LEAVITT, SECRETARY OF HEALTH AND \nHUMAN SERVICES, et al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x931472.Argued November 9, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 1, 2005

\n

The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (Act) authorizes the Government and Indian tribes to enter into contracts in which tribes promise to supply federally funded services that a Government agency normally would provide, 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7450(f); and requires the Government to pay, inter alia, a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontract support costs,\xe2\x80\x9d which are \xe2\x80\x9creasonable costs\xe2\x80\x9d that a federal agency would not have incurred, but which the tribe would incur in managing the program, \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(a)(2). Here, each Tribe agreed to supply health services normally provided by the Department of Health and Human Services\xe2\x80\x99 Indian Health Service, and the contracts included an annual funding agreement with a Government promise to pay contract support costs. In each instance, the Government refused to pay the full amount promised because Congress had not appropriated sufficient funds. In the first case, the Tribes submitted administrative payment claims under the Contract Disputes Act of 1978, which the Department of the Interior (the appropriations manager) denied. They then brought a breach-of-contract action. The District Court found against them, and the Tenth Circuit affirmed. In the second case, the Cherokee Nation submitted claims to the Department of the Interior, which the Board of Contract Appeals ordered paid. The Federal Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Government is legally bound to pay the \xe2\x80\x9ccontract support costs\xe2\x80\x9d at issue. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Government argues that it is legally bound by its promises to pay the relevant costs only if Congress appropriated sufficient funds, which the Government contends Congress did not do in this instance. It does not deny that it promised, but failed, to pay the costs; that, were these ordinary procurement contracts, its promises to pay would be legally binding; that each year Congress appropriated more than the amounts at issue; that those appropriations Acts had no relevant statutory restrictions; that where Congress makes such appropriations, a clear inference arises that it does not intend to impose legally binding restrictions; and that as long as Congress has appropriated sufficient legally unrestricted funds to pay contracts, as it did here, the Government normally cannot back out of a promise to pay on grounds of insufficient appropriations. Thus, in order to show that its promises were not legally binding, the Government must show something special about the promises at issue. It fails to do so here. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Act does not support the Government\xe2\x80\x99s initial argument that, because the Act creates a special contract with a unique nature differentiating it from standard Government procurement contracts, a tribe should bear the risk that a lump-sum appropriation will be insufficient to pay its contract. In general, the Act\xe2\x80\x99s language runs counter to this view, strongly suggesting instead that Congress, in respect to a promise\xe2\x80\x99s binding nature, meant to treat alike promises made under the Act and ordinary contractual promises. The Act uses \xe2\x80\x9ccontract\xe2\x80\x9d 426 times to describe the nature of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s promise, and \xe2\x80\x9ccontract\xe2\x80\x9d normally refers to \xe2\x80\x9ca promise \xe2\x80\xa6 for the breach of which the law gives a remedy, or the performance of which the law \xe2\x80\xa6 recognizes as a duty,\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Second) of Contracts \xc2\xa71. Payment of contract support costs is described in a provision containing a sample \xe2\x80\x9cContract,\xe2\x80\x9d 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7450l(c), and contractors are entitled to \xe2\x80\x9cmoney damages\xe2\x80\x9d under the Contract Disputes Act if the Government refuses to pay, \xc2\xa7450m\xe2\x80\x931(a). Nor do the Act\xe2\x80\x99s general purposes support any special treatment. The Government points to the statement that tribes need not spend funds \xe2\x80\x9cin excess of the amount of funds awarded,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7450l(c), but that kind of statement often appears in procurement contracts; and the statement that \xe2\x80\x9cno [self-determination] contract \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be construed to be a procurement contract,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7450b(j), in context, seems designed to relieve tribes and the Government of technical burdens that may accompany procurement, not to weaken a contract\xe2\x80\x99s binding nature. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Neither of the phrases in an Act proviso renders the Government\xe2\x80\x99s promise nonbinding. One phrase\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthe Secretary is not required to reduce funding for programs, projects, or activities serving a tribe to make funds available to another tribe,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(b)\xe2\x80\x94did not make the Government\xe2\x80\x99s promise nonbinding, since the relevant appropriations contained unrestricted funds sufficient to pay the claims at issue. When this happens in an ordinary procurement contract case, the Government admits that the contractor is entitled to payment even if the agency has allocated the funds to another purpose. That the Government used the unrestricted funds to satisfy important needs\xe2\x80\x94e.g., the cost of running the Indian Health Service\xe2\x80\x94does not matter, for there is nothing special in the Act\xe2\x80\x99s language or the contracts to convince the Court that anything but the ordinary rule applies here. The other proviso phrase\xe2\x80\x94which subjects the Government\xe2\x80\x99s provision of funds under the Act \xe2\x80\x9cto the availability of appropriations,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.\xe2\x80\x94also fails to help the Government. Congress appropriated adequate unrestricted funds here, and the Government provides no convincing argument for a special, rather than ordinary, interpretation of the phrase. Legislative history shows only that Executive Branch officials wanted discretionary authority to allocate a lump-sum appropriation too small to pay for all contracts, not that Congress granted such authority. And other statutory provisions, e.g., \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(c)(2), to which the Government points, do not provide sufficient support. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Finally, the Government points to \xc2\xa7314 of the later-enacted 1999 Appropriations Act, which states that amounts \xe2\x80\x9cearmarked in committee reports for the \xe2\x80\xa6 Indian Health Service \xe2\x80\xa6 [for] payments to tribes \xe2\x80\xa6 for contract support costs \xe2\x80\xa6 are the total amounts available for fiscal years 1994 through 1998 for such purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court rejects the Government\xe2\x80\x99s claims that this statute merely clarifies earlier ambiguous appropriations language that was wrongly read as unrestricted. Earlier appropriations statutes were not ambiguous, and restrictive language in Committee Reports is not legally binding. Because no other restrictive language exists, the earlier statutes unambiguously provided unrestricted lump-sum appropriations. Nor should \xc2\xa7314 be interpreted to retroactively bar payment of claims arising under 1994 through 1997 contracts. That would raise serious constitutional issues by undoing binding governmental contractual obligations. Thus, the Court adopts the interpretation that Congress intended to forbid the Indian Health Service to use unspent appropriated funds to pay unpaid contract support costs. So interpreted, \xc2\xa7314 does not bar recovery here. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x931472, 311 F.\xc2\xa03d 1054, reversed; No. 03\xe2\x80\x93853, 334 F.\xc2\xa03d 1075, affirmed; and both cases remanded.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States and two Indian Tribes have entered into agreements in which the Government promises to pay certain \xe2\x80\x9ccontract support costs\xe2\x80\x9d that the Tribes incurred during fiscal years 1994 through 1997. The question before us is whether the Government\xe2\x80\x99s promises are legally binding. We conclude that they are.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Indian Self-Determination and Education Assistance Act (Act), 88 Stat. 2203, as amended, 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7450 et seq., authorizes the Government and Indian tribes to enter into contracts in which the tribes promise to supply federally funded services, for example tribal health services, that a Government agency would otherwise provide. See \xc2\xa7450f(a); see also \xc2\xa7450a(b). The Act specifies that the Government must pay a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s costs, including administrative expenses. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(a)(1) and (2). Administrative expenses include (1) the amount that the agency would have spent \xe2\x80\x9cfor the operation of the progra[m]\xe2\x80\x9d had the agency itself managed the program, \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(a)(1), and (2) \xe2\x80\x9ccontract support costs,\xe2\x80\x9d the costs at issue here. \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(a)(2).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Act defines \xe2\x80\x9ccontract support costs\xe2\x80\x9d as other \xe2\x80\x9creasonable costs\xe2\x80\x9d that a federal agency would not have incurred, but which nonetheless \xe2\x80\x9ca tribal organization\xe2\x80\x9d acting \xe2\x80\x9cas a contractor\xe2\x80\x9d would incur \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure compliance with the terms of the contract and prudent management.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cContract support costs\xe2\x80\x9d can include indirect administrative costs, such as special auditing or other financial management costs, \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(a)(3)(A)(ii); they can include direct costs, such as workers\xe2\x80\x99 compensation insurance, \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(a)(3)(A)(i); and they can include certain startup costs, \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(a)(5). Most contract support costs are indirect costs \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally calculated by applying an \xe2\x80\x98indirect cost rate\xe2\x80\x99 to the amount of funds otherwise payable to the Tribe.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Federal Parties 7; see 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7450b(f)\xe2\x80\x93(g).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first case before us concerns Shoshone-Pauite contracts for fiscal years 1996 and 1997 and a Cherokee Nation contract for 1997. The second case concerns Cherokee Nation contracts for fiscal years 1994, 1995, and 1996. In each contract, the Tribe agreed to supply health services that a Government agency, the Indian Health Service, would otherwise have provided. See, e.g., App. 88\xe2\x80\x9392 (Shoshone-Paiute Tribal Health Compact), 173\xe2\x80\x93175 (Compact between the United States and the Cherokee Nation). Each contract included an \xe2\x80\x9cAnnual Funding Agreement\xe2\x80\x9d with a Government promise to pay contract support costs. See, e.g., id., at 104\xe2\x80\x93128, 253\xe2\x80\x93264. In each instance, the Government refused to pay the full amount promised because, the Government says, Congress did not appropriate sufficient funds.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both cases began as administrative proceedings. In the first case, the Tribes submitted claims seeking payment under the Contract Disputes Act of 1978, 92 Stat. 2383, 41 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7601 et seq., and the Act, 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7450m\xe2\x80\x931(a), (d), 458cc(h), from the Department of Interior (which manages the relevant appropriations). See, e.g., App. 150\xe2\x80\x93151, 201\xe2\x80\x93203. The Department denied their claim; they then brought a breach-of-contract action in the Federal District Court for the Eastern District of Oklahoma seeking $3.5 million (Shoshone-Paiute) and $3.4 million (Cherokee Nation). See Cherokee Nation of Okla. v. Thompson, 311 F.\xc2\xa03d 1054, 1059 (CA10 2002). The District Court found against the Tribes. Cherokee Nation of Okla. v. United States, 190 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1248 (ED Okla. 2001). And the Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit affirmed. 311 F.\xc2\xa03d 1054.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the second case, the Cherokee Nation submitted claims to the Department of Interior. See App. 229\xe2\x80\x93230. A contracting officer denied the claims; the Board of Contract Appeals reversed this ruling, ordering the Government to pay $8.5 million in damages. Appeals of the Cherokee Nation of Okla., 99\xe2\x80\x932 B.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xb6\xc2\xa030,462, p. 150488 (1999); App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 03\xe2\x80\x93853, pp. 38a\xe2\x80\x9340a. The Government sought judicial review in the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit. The Federal Circuit affirmed the Board\xe2\x80\x99s determination for the Tribe. Thompson v. Cherokee Nation of Okla., 334 F.\xc2\xa03d 1075 (2003).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of the identical nature of the claims in the two cases and the opposite results that the two Courts of Appeals have reached, we granted certiorari. We now affirm the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in favor of the Cherokee Nation, and we reverse the Tenth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in favor of the Government.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government does not deny that it promised to pay the relevant contract support costs. Nor does it deny that it failed to pay. Its sole defense consists of the argument that it is legally bound by its promises if, and only if, Congress appropriated sufficient funds, and that, in this instance, Congress failed to do so.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government in effect concedes yet more. It does not deny that, were these contracts ordinary procurement contracts, its promises to pay would be legally binding. The Tribes point out that each year Congress appropriated far more than the amounts here at issue (between $1.277 billion and $1.419 billion) for the Indian Health Service \xe2\x80\x9cto carry out,\xe2\x80\x9d inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Indian Self-Determination Act.\xe2\x80\x9d See 107 Stat. 1408 (1993); 108 Stat. 2527\xe2\x80\x932528 (1994); 110 Stat. 1321\xe2\x80\x93189 (1996); id., at 3009\xe2\x80\x93212 to 3009\xe2\x80\x93213 (1996). These appropriations Acts contained no relevant statutory restriction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Tribes (and their amici) add, first, that this Court has said that it is

\n

\xe2\x80\x9ca fundamental principle of appropriations law that where Congress merely appropriates lump-sum amounts without statutorily restricting what can be done with those funds, a clear inference arises that it does not intend to impose legally binding restrictions, and indicia in committee reports and other legislative history as to how the funds should or are expected to be spent do not establish any legal requirements on the agency.\xe2\x80\x9d Lincoln v. Vigil, 508 U. S. 182, 192 (1993) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\nSee also International Union, United Auto., Aerospace & Agricultural Implement Workers of America v. Donovan, 746 F.\xc2\xa02d 855, 860\xe2\x80\x93861 (CADC 1984) (Scalia, J.); Blackhawk Heating & Plumbing Co. v. United States, 224 Ct. Cl. 111, 135, and n.\xc2\xa09, 622 F.\xc2\xa02d 539, 552, and n.\xc2\xa09 (1980).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Tribes and their amici add, second, that as long as Congress has appropriated sufficient legally unrestricted funds to pay the contracts at issue, the Government normally cannot back out of a promise to pay on grounds of \xe2\x80\x9cinsufficient appropriations,\xe2\x80\x9d even if the contract uses language such as \xe2\x80\x9csubject to the availability of appropriations,\xe2\x80\x9d and even if an agency\xe2\x80\x99s total lump-sum appropriation is insufficient to pay all the contracts the agency has made. See Ferris v. United States, 27 Ct. Cl. 542, 546 (1892) (\xe2\x80\x9cA contractor who is one of several persons to be paid out of an appropriation is not chargeable with knowledge of its administration, nor can his legal rights be affected or impaired by its maladministration or by its diversion, whether legal or illegal, to other objects\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Blackhawk, supra, at 135, and n.\xc2\xa09, 622 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 552, and n.\xc2\xa09.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As we have said, the Government denies none of this. Thus, if it is nonetheless to demonstrate that its promises were not legally binding, it must show something special about the promises here at issue. That is precisely what the Government here tries, but fails, to do.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government initially argues that the Act creates a special kind of \xe2\x80\x9cself-determination contrac[t]\xe2\x80\x9d with a \xe2\x80\x9cunique, government-to-government nature\xe2\x80\x9d that differentiates it from \xe2\x80\x9cstandard government procurement contracts.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Federal Parties 4. Because a tribe does not bargain with the Government at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length, id., at 24, the law should charge it with knowledge that the Government has entered into other, similar contracts with other tribes; the tribe should bear the risk that a total lump-sum appropriation (though sufficient to cover its own contracts) will not prove sufficient to pay all similar contracts. Id., at 23\xe2\x80\x9325. Because such a tribe has elected to \xe2\x80\x9cste[p] into the shoes of a federal agency,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 25, the law should treat it like an agency; and an agency enjoys no legal entitlement to receive promised amounts from Congress, id., at 24\xe2\x80\x9325. Rather, a tribe should receive only the portion of the total lump-sum appropriation allocated to it, not the entire sum to which a private contractor might well be entitled. Id., at 24.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government finds support for this special treatment of its promises made pursuant to the Act by pointing to a statutory provision stating that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98no [self-determination] contract \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be construed to be a procurement contract,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 23 (quoting 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7450b(j) (alterations in original)). It finds supplementary support in another provision that says that a tribe need not deliver services \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in excess of the amount of funds awarded,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Federal Parties 24 (quoting 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7450l(c); citing \xc2\xa7458aaa\xe2\x80\x937(k)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These statutory provisions, in our view, fall well short of providing the support the Government needs. In general, the Act\xe2\x80\x99s language runs counter to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view. That language strongly suggests that Congress, in respect to the binding nature of a promise, meant to treat alike promises made under the Act and ordinary contractual promises (say, those made in procurement contracts). The Act, for example, uses the word \xe2\x80\x9ccontract\xe2\x80\x9d 426 times to describe the nature of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s promise; and the word \xe2\x80\x9ccontract\xe2\x80\x9d normally refers to \xe2\x80\x9ca promise or a set of promises for the breach of which the law gives a remedy, or the performance of which the law in some way recognizes as a duty,\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Second) of Contracts \xc2\xa71 (1981). The Act also describes payment of contract support costs in a provision setting forth a sample \xe2\x80\x9cContract.\xe2\x80\x9d 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7450l(c) (Model Agreement \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71(a)(1), 1(b)(4)). Further, the Act says that if the Government refuses to pay, then contractors are entitled to \xe2\x80\x9cmoney damages\xe2\x80\x9d in accordance with the Contract Disputes Act. 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7450m\xe2\x80\x931(a); see also \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7450m\xe2\x80\x931(d), 458cc(h).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither do the Act\xe2\x80\x99s general purposes support any special treatment. The Act seeks greater tribal self-reliance brought about through more \xe2\x80\x9ceffective and meaningful participation by the Indian people\xe2\x80\x9d in, and less \xe2\x80\x9cFederal domination\xe2\x80\x9d of, \xe2\x80\x9cprograms for, and services to, Indians.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7450a(b). The Act also reflects a congressional concern with Government\xe2\x80\x99s past failure adequately to reimburse tribes\xe2\x80\x99 indirect administrative costs and a congressional decision to require payment of those costs in the future. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(g); see also \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(a), (d)(2).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The specific statutory language to which the Government points\xe2\x80\x94stating that tribes need not spend funds \xe2\x80\x9cin excess of the amount of funds awarded,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7450l(c) (Model Agreement \xc2\xa71(b)(5))\xe2\x80\x94does not help the Government. Cf. Brief for Federal Parties 18. This kind of statement often appears in ordinary procurement contracts. See, e.g., 48 CFR \xc2\xa752.232\xe2\x80\x9320(d)(2) (2004) (sample \xe2\x80\x9cLimitation of Cost\xe2\x80\x9d clause); see generally W. Keyes, Government Contracts Under the Federal Acquisition Regulation \xc2\xa732.38, p. 724 (3d ed. 2003). Nor can the Government find adequate support in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s statement that \xe2\x80\x9cno [self-determination] contract \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be construed to be a procurement contract.\xe2\x80\x9d 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7450b(j). In context, that statement seems designed to relieve tribes and the Government of the technical burdens that often accompany procurement, not to weaken a contract\xe2\x80\x99s binding nature. Cf. 41 CFR \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x934.6001 (1976) (applying procurement rules to tribal contracts); S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 100\xe2\x80\x93274, p.\xc2\xa07 (1987) (noting that application of procurement rules to contracts with tribes \xe2\x80\x9cresulted in excessive paperwork and unduly burdensome reporting requirements\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 18\xe2\x80\x9319 (describing decision not to apply procurement rules to tribal contracts as intended to \xe2\x80\x9cgreatly reduc[e]\xe2\x80\x9d the federal bureaucracy associated with them). Finally, we have found no indication that Congress believed or accepted the Government\xe2\x80\x99s current claim that, because of mutual self-awareness among tribal contractors, tribes, not the Government, should bear the risk that an unrestricted lump-sum appropriation would prove insufficient to pay all contractors. Compare Brief for Federal Parties 23\xe2\x80\x9324 with Ferris, 27 Ct. Cl., at 546.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government next points to an Act proviso, which states:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cNotwithstanding any other provision in this subchapter, the provision of funds under this subchapter is [1] subject to the availability of appropriations and the Secretary [2] is not required to reduce funding for programs, projects, or activities serving a tribe to make funds available to another tribe or tribal organization under the subchapter.\xe2\x80\x9d 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(b) (emphasis and bracketed numbers added).

\n

\nThe Government believes that the two italicized phrases, taken separately or together, render its promises nonbinding.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin with phrase (2). This phrase, says the Government, makes nonbinding a promise to pay one tribe\xe2\x80\x99s costs where doing so would require funds that the Government would otherwise devote to \xe2\x80\x9cprograms, projects, or activities serving \xe2\x80\xa6 another tribe,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. See Brief for Federal Parties 27\xe2\x80\x9336. This argument is inadequate, however, for at the least it runs up against the fact\xe2\x80\x94found by the Federal Circuit, see 334 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1093\xe2\x80\x931094, and nowhere here denied\xe2\x80\x94that the relevant congressional appropriations contained other unrestricted funds, small in amount but sufficient to pay the claims at issue. And as we have said, supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, the Government itself tells us that, in the case of ordinary contracts, say, procurement contracts,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cif the amount of an unrestricted appropriation is sufficient to fund the contract, the contractor is entitled to payment even if the agency has allocated the funds to another purpose or assumes other obligations that exhaust the funds.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Federal Parties 23 (emphasis added).

\n

\nSee, e.g., Lincoln, 508 U. S., at 192; Blackhawk, 224 Ct. Cl., at 135, and n.\xc2\xa09, 622 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 552, and n.\xc2\xa09; Ferris, supra, at 546.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government argues that these other funds, though legally unrestricted (as far as the appropriations statutes\xe2\x80\x99 language is concerned) were nonetheless unavailable to pay \xe2\x80\x9ccontract support costs\xe2\x80\x9d because the Government had to use those funds to satisfy a critically important need, namely, to pay the costs of \xe2\x80\x9cinherent federal functions,\xe2\x80\x9d such as the cost of running the Indian Health Service\xe2\x80\x99s central Washington office. Brief for Federal Parties 9\xe2\x80\x9310, 27\xe2\x80\x9334. This argument cannot help the Government, however, for it amounts to no more than a claim that the agency has allocated the funds to another purpose, albeit potentially a very important purpose. If an important alternative need for funds cannot rescue the Government from the binding effect of its promises where ordinary procurement contracts are at issue, it cannot rescue the Government here, for we can find nothing special in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language or in the contracts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s best effort to find something special in the statutory language is unpersuasive. The Government points to language that forbids the Government to enter into a contract with a tribe in which it promises to pay the tribe for performing federal functions. See 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7458aaa6\xe2\x80\x93(c)(1)(A)(ii); see also \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7450f(a)(2)(E), 450j\xe2\x80\x931(a)(1), 450l(c) (Model Agreement \xc2\xa71(a)(2)). Language of this kind, however, which forbids the Government to contract for certain kinds of services, says nothing about the source of funds used to pay for the supply of contractually legitimate activities (and that is what is at issue here).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We recognize that agencies may sometimes find that they must spend unrestricted appropriated funds to satisfy needs they believe more important than fulfilling a contractual obligation. But the law normally expects the Government to avoid such situations, for example, by refraining from making less essential contractual commitments; or by asking Congress in advance to protect funds needed for more essential purposes with statutory earmarks; or by seeking added funding from Congress; or, if necessary, by using unrestricted funds for the more essential purpose while leaving the contractor free to pursue appropriate legal remedies arising because the Government broke its contractual promise. See New York Airways, Inc. v. United States, 177 Ct. Cl. 800, 808\xe2\x80\x93811, 369 F.\xc2\xa02d 743, 747\xe2\x80\x93748 (1966); 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71341(a)(1)(A) and (B) (Anti-Deficiency Act); 41 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7601 et seq. (Contract Disputes Act); 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71304 (Judgment Fund); see generally 2 General Accounting Office, Principles of Federal Appropriations Law 6\xe2\x80\x9317 to 6\xe2\x80\x9319 (2d ed. 1992) (GAO Redbook). The Government, without denying that this is so as a general matter of procurement law, says nothing to convince us that a different legal rule should apply here.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Phrase (1) of the proviso says that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s provision of funds under the Act is \xe2\x80\x9csubject to the availability of appropriations.\xe2\x80\x9d 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(b). This language does not help the Government either. Language of this kind is often used with respect to Government contracts. See, e.g., 22 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72716(a)(1); 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76249(b)(4); \xc2\xa712206(d)(1). This kind of language normally makes clear that an agency and a contracting party can negotiate a contract prior to the beginning of a fiscal year but that the contract will not become binding unless and until Congress appropriates funds for that year. See, e.g., Blackhawk, supra, at 133\xe2\x80\x93138, 622 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 551\xe2\x80\x93553; see generally 1 GAO Redbook 4\xe2\x80\x936 (3d ed. 2004); 2 id., at 6\xe2\x80\x936 to 6\xe2\x80\x938, 6\xe2\x80\x9317 to 6\xe2\x80\x9319 (2d ed. 1992). It also makes clear that a Government contracting officer lacks any special statutory authority needed to bind the Government without regard to the availability of appropriations. See Ferris, 27 Ct. Cl., at 546; New York Airways, supra, at 808\xe2\x80\x93811, 369 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 748\xe2\x80\x93749; Dougherty v. United States, 18 Ct. Cl. 496, 503 (1883); 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71341(a)(1)(A) and (B) (providing that without some such special authority,\na contracting officer cannot bind the Government in\nthe absence of an appropriation). Since Congress appropriated adequate unrestricted funds here, phrase (1), if interpreted as ordinarily understood, would not help the Government.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government again argues for a special interpretation. It says the language amounts to \xe2\x80\x9can affirmative grant of authority to the Secretary to adjust funding levels based on appropriations.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Federal Parties 41 (emphasis in original). In so arguing, the Government in effect claims (on the basis of this language) to have the legal right to disregard its contractual promises if, for example, it reasonably finds other, more important uses for an otherwise adequate lump-sum appropriation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In our view, however, the Government must again shoulder the burden of explaining why, in the context of Government contracts, we should not give this kind of statutory language its ordinary contract-related interpretation, at least in the absence of a showing that Congress meant the contrary. We believe it important to provide a uniform interpretation of similar language used in comparable statutes, lest legal uncertainty undermine contractors\xe2\x80\x99 confidence that they will be paid, and in turn increase the cost to the Government of purchasing goods and services. See, e.g., Franconia Associates v. United States, 536 U. S. 129, 142 (2002); United States v. Winstar Corp., 518 U. S. 839, 884\xe2\x80\x93885, and n.\xc2\xa029 (1996) (plurality opinion); id., at 913 (Breyer, J., concurring); Lynch v. United States, 292 U. S. 571, 580 (1934). The Government, in our view, has provided no convincing argument for a special, rather than ordinary, interpretation here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government refers to legislative history, see Brief for Federal Parties 41\xe2\x80\x9342 (citing, e.g., S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 100\xe2\x80\x93274, at 48, 57), but that history shows only that Executive Branch officials would have liked to exercise discretionary authority to allocate a lump-sum appropriation too small to pay for all the contracts that the Government had entered into; the history does not show that Congress granted such authority. Nor can we find sufficient support in the other statutory provisions to which the Government points. See 25 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7450j\xe2\x80\x931(c)(2) (requiring the Government to report underpayments of promised contract support costs); 107 Stat. 1408 (Appropriations Act for fiscal year (FY) 1994) (providing that $7.5 million for contract support costs in \xe2\x80\x9cinitial and expanded\xe2\x80\x9d contracts \xe2\x80\x9cshall remain available\xe2\x80\x9d until expended); 108 Stat. 2528 (1994) (same for FY 1995); 110 Stat. 1321\xe2\x80\x93189 (same for FY 1996); id., at 3009\xe2\x80\x93213 (same for FY 1997). We cannot adopt the Government\xe2\x80\x99s special interpretation of phrase (1) of the proviso.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the Government points to a later-enacted statute, \xc2\xa7314 of the 1999 Appropriations Act, which says:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cNotwithstanding any other provision of law \xe2\x80\xa6 [the] amounts appropriated to or earmarked in committee reports for the \xe2\x80\xa6 Indian Health Service \xe2\x80\xa6 [for] payments to tribes \xe2\x80\xa6 for contract support costs \xe2\x80\xa6 are the total amounts available for fiscal years 1994 through 1998 for such purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d 112 Stat. 2681\xe2\x80\x93288 (emphasis added).

\n

\nSee Brief for Federal Parties 45\xe2\x80\x9350. The Government adds that congressional Committee Reports \xe2\x80\x9cearmarked,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., restricted, appropriations available to pay \xe2\x80\x9ccontract support costs\xe2\x80\x9d in each of fiscal years 1994 through 1997. Id., at 48. And those amounts have long since been spent. See id., at 12. Since those amounts \xe2\x80\x9care the total amounts available for\xe2\x80\x9d payment of \xe2\x80\x9ccontract support costs,\xe2\x80\x9d the Government says, it is unlawful to pay the Tribes\xe2\x80\x99 claims. Id., at 45\xe2\x80\x9348.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The language in question is open to the interpretation that it retroactively bars payment of claims arising under 1994 through 1997 contracts. It is also open to another interpretation. Just prior to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enactment of \xc2\xa7314, the Interior Department\xe2\x80\x99s Board of Contract Appeals considered a case similar to the present ones and held that the Government was legally bound to pay amounts it had promised in similar contracts. In re Alamo Navajo School Bd., Inc. and Miccosukee Corp., 98\xe2\x80\x932 B.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xb6\xc2\xa029,831, p. 147680, and \xc2\xb6\xc2\xa029,832, p. 147699 (1997). The Indian Health Service contemporaneously issued a draft document that suggested the use of unspent funds appropriated in prior years to pay unpaid \xe2\x80\x9ccontract support costs.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 206\xe2\x80\x93209. Indeed, the document referred to use of unobligated funds from years including 1994 through 1997 to pay \xe2\x80\x9ccontract support cost\xe2\x80\x9d debts. Id., at 206\xe2\x80\x93207. Section 314\xe2\x80\x99s language may be read as simply forbidding the Service to use those left-over funds for that purpose.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the basis of language alone we would find either interpretation reasonable. But there are other considerations. The first interpretation would undo a binding governmental contractual promise. A statute that retroactively repudiates the Government\xe2\x80\x99s contractual obligation may violate the Constitution. See, e.g., Winstar, supra, at 875\xe2\x80\x93876 (plurality opinion); Perry v. United States, 294 U. S. 330, 350\xe2\x80\x93351 (1935); Lynch, supra, at 579\xe2\x80\x93580; United States v. Klein, 13 Wall. 128, 144\xe2\x80\x93147 (1872); see also, e.g., Winstar, supra, at 884\xe2\x80\x93885, and n.\xc2\xa029 (plurality opinion) (describing practical disadvantages flowing from governmental repudiation); Lynch, supra, at 580 (same). And such an interpretation is disfavored. See Clark v. Martinez, 543 U. S. ___, ___ (2005) (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310); Zadvydas v. Davis, 533 U. S. 678, 689 (2001); Edward J. DeBartolo Corp. v. Florida Gulf Coast Building & Constr. Trades Council, 485 U. S. 568, 575 (1988). This consideration tips the balance against the retroactive interpretation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government, itself not relying on either interpretation, offers us a third. It says that the statute simply clarifies earlier ambiguous appropriations language that was wrongly read as unrestricted. Brief for Federal Parties 48 (citing Red Lion Broadcasting Co. v. FCC, 395 U. S. 367, 380\xe2\x80\x93381 (1969)). The earlier appropriations statutes, however, were not ambiguous. The relevant case law makes clear that restrictive language contained in Committee Reports is not legally binding. See, e.g., Lincoln, 508 U. S., at 192; International Union, 746 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 860\xe2\x80\x93861; Blackhawk, 224 Ct. Cl., at 135, and n.\xc2\xa09, 622 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 552, and n.\xc2\xa09. No other restrictive language exists. The earlier appropriations statutes unambiguously provided unrestricted lump-sum appropriations. We therefore cannot accept the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa7314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hence we, like the Federal Circuit, are left with the second interpretation, which we adopt, concluding that Congress intended it in the circumstances. See Zadvydas, supra, at 689; cf. 334 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1092. So interpreted, the provision does not bar recovery here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, we affirm the judgment of the Federal Circuit; we reverse the judgment of the Tenth Circuit; and we remand the cases for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
