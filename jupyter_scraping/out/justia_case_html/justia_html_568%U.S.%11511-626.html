\n\t\t\t\tLOZMAN v. CITY OF RIVIERA BEACH, FLORIDA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x93626.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 1, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 15, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s floating home was a house-like plywood structure with empty bilge space underneath the main floor to keep it afloat. He had it towed several times before deciding on a marina owned by the city of Riviera Beach (City). After various disputes with Lozman and unsuccessful efforts to evict him from the marina, the City brought a federal admiralty lawsuit in rem against the floating home, seeking a lien for dockage fees and damages for trespass. Lozman moved to dismiss the suit for lack of admiralty jurisdiction. The District Court found the floating home to be a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d under the Rules of Construction Act, which defines a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d as including \xe2\x80\x9cevery description of watercraft or other artificial contrivance used, or capable of being used, as a means of transportation on water,\xe2\x80\x9d 1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73, concluded that admiralty jurisdiction was proper, and awarded the City dockage fees and nominal damages. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed, agreeing that the home was a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d since it was \xe2\x80\x9ccapable\xe2\x80\x9d of movement over water despite petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s subjective intent to remain moored indefinitely.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t1.\xc2\xa0This case is not moot. The District Court ordered the floating home sold, and the City purchased the home at auction and had it destroyed. Before the sale, the court ordered the City to post a bond to ensure Lozman could obtain monetary relief if he prevailed. P.\xc2\xa03.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t2.\xc2\xa0Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s floating home is not a \xc2\xa73 \xe2\x80\x9cvessel.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The Eleventh Circuit found the home \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of being used .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as a means of transportation on water\xe2\x80\x9d because it could float and proceed under tow and its shore connections did not render it incapable of transportation. This interpretation is too broad. The definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctransportation,\xe2\x80\x9d the conveyance of persons or things from one place to another, must be applied in a practical way. Stewart v. Dutra Constr. Co., \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 481\n. Consequently, a structure does not fall within the scope of the statutory phrase unless a reasonable observer, looking to the home\xe2\x80\x99s physical characteristics and activities, would consider it designed to a practical degree for carrying people or things over water. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0But for the fact that it floats, nothing about Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s home suggests that it was designed to any practical degree to transport persons or things over water. It had no steering mechanism, had an unraked hull and rectangular bottom 10 inches below the water, and had no capacity to generate or store electricity. It also lacked self-propulsion, differing significantly from an ordinary houseboat. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0This view of the statute is consistent with its text, precedent, and relevant purposes. The statute\xe2\x80\x99s language, read naturally, lends itself to that interpretation: The term \xe2\x80\x9ccontrivance\xe2\x80\x9d refers to something \xe2\x80\x9cemployed in contriving to effect a purpose\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9ccraft\xe2\x80\x9d explains that purpose as \xe2\x80\x9cwater carriage and transport\xe2\x80\x9d; the addition of \xe2\x80\x9cwater\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9ccraft\xe2\x80\x9d emphasizes the point; and the words, \xe2\x80\x9cused, or capable of being used, as a means of transportation on water,\xe2\x80\x9d drive the point home. Both Evansville & Bowling Green Packet Co. v. Chero Cola Bottling Co., \n\n271 U.\xc2\xa0S. 19\n, and Stewart, supra, support this conclusion. Evansville involved a wharfboat floated next to a dock, used to transfer cargo, and towed to harbor each winter; and Stewart involved a dredge used to remove silt from the ocean floor, which carried a captain and crew and could be navigated only by manipulating anchors and cables or by being towed. Water transportation was not the primary purpose of either structure; neither was in motion at relevant times; and both were sometimes attached to the ocean bottom or to land. However, Stewart\xe2\x80\x99s dredge, which was regularly, but not primarily, used to transport workers and equipment over water, fell within the statutory definition while Evansville\xe2\x80\x99s wharfboat, which was not designed to, and did not, serve a transportation function, did not. Lower court cases, on balance, also tend to support this conclusion. Further, the purposes of major federal maritime statutes\xe2\x80\x94e.g., admiralty provisions provide special attachment procedures lest a vessel avoid liability by sailing away, recognize that sailors face special perils at sea, and encourage shipowners to engage in port-related commerce\xe2\x80\x94reveal little reason to classify floating homes as \xe2\x80\x9cvessels.\xe2\x80\x9d Finally, this conclusion is consistent with state laws in States where floating home owners have congregated in communities. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0Several important arguments made by the City and its amici are unavailing. They argue that a purpose-based test may introduce a subjective element into \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d determinations. But the Court has considered only objective evidence, looking to the views of a reasonable observer and the physical attributes and behavior of the structure. They also argue against using criteria that are too abstract, complex, or open-ended. While this Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach is neither perfectly precise nor always determinative, it is workable and consistent and should offer guidance in a significant number of borderline cases. And contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, the Court sees nothing to be gained by a remand. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(e)\xc2\xa0The City\xe2\x80\x99s additional argument that Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s floating home was actually used for transportation over water is similarly unpersuasive. P.\xc2\xa014.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Rules of Construction Act defines a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d as in- cluding \xe2\x80\x9cevery description of watercraft or other artificial contrivance used, or capable of being used, as a means of transportation on water.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73. The question before us is whether petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s floating home (which is not self-propelled) falls within the terms of that definition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn answering that question we focus primarily upon the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of being used.\xe2\x80\x9d This term encompasses \xe2\x80\x9cpractical\xe2\x80\x9d possibilities, not \xe2\x80\x9cmerely .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. theoretical\xe2\x80\x9d ones. Stewart v. Dutra Constr. Co., \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 481, \n496 (2005)\n. We believe that a reasonable observer, looking to the home\xe2\x80\x99s physical characteristics and activities, would not consider it to be designed to any practical degree for carrying people or things on water. And we consequently conclude that the floating home is not a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2002 Fane Lozman, petitioner, bought a 60-foot by 12-foot floating home. App. 37, 71. The home consisted of a house-like plywood structure with French doors on three sides. Id., at 38, 44. It contained a sitting room, bedroom, closet, bathroom, and kitchen, along with a stairway leading to a second level with office space. Id., at 45\xe2\x80\x9366. An empty bilge space underneath the main floor kept it afloat. Id., at 38. (See Appendix, infra, for a photograph.) After buying the floating home, Lozman had it towed about 200 miles to North Bay Village, Florida, where he moored it and then twice more had it towed between nearby marinas. In 2006 Lozman had the home towed a further 70 miles to a marina owned by the city of Riviera Beach (City), respondent, where he kept it docked. Brief for Respondent 5.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter various disputes with Lozman and unsuccessful efforts to evict him from the marina, the City brought this federal admiralty lawsuit in rem against the floating home. It sought a maritime lien for dockage fees and damages for trespass. See Federal Maritime Lien Act, \n\n46 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa731342 (authorizing federal maritime lien against vessel to collect debts owed for the provision of \xe2\x80\x9cnecessaries to a vessel\xe2\x80\x9d); \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71333(1) (civil admiralty jurisdiction). See also Leon v. Galceran, 11 Wall. 185 (1871); The Rock Island Bridge, 6 Wall. 213, 215 (1867).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLozman, acting pro se, asked the District Court to dismiss the suit on the ground that the court lacked admi- ralty jurisdiction. See 2 Record, Doc. 64. After summary judgment proceedings, the court found that the floating home was a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d and concluded that admiralty jurisdiction was consequently proper. Pet. for Cert. 42a. The judge then conducted a bench trial on the merits and awarded the City $3,039.88 for dockage along with $1 in nominal damages for trespass. Id., at 49a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn appeal the Eleventh Circuit affirmed. Riviera Beach v. That Certain Unnamed Gray, Two-Story Vessel Approximately Fifty-Seven Feet in Length, 649 F.\xc2\xa03d 1259 (2011). It agreed with the District Court that the home was a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel.\xe2\x80\x9d In its view, the home was \xe2\x80\x9ccapable\xe2\x80\x9d of movement over water and the owner\xe2\x80\x99s subjective intent to remain moored \xe2\x80\x9cindefinitely\xe2\x80\x9d at a dock could not show the con- trary. Id., at 1267\xe2\x80\x931269.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLozman sought certiorari. In light of uncertainty among the Circuits about application of the term \xe2\x80\x9ccapable\xe2\x80\x9d we granted his petition. Compare De La Rosa v. St. Charles Gaming Co., 474 F.\xc2\xa03d 185, 187 (CA5 2006) (structure is not a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d where \xe2\x80\x9cphysically,\xe2\x80\x9d but only \xe2\x80\x9ctheoretical[ly],\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of sailing,\xe2\x80\x9d and owner intends to moor it indef- initely as floating casino), with Board of Comm\xe2\x80\x99rs of Or- leans Levee Dist. v. M/V Belle of Orleans, 535 F.\xc2\xa03d 1299, 1311\xe2\x80\x931312 (CA11 2008) (structure is a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d where capable of moving over water under tow, \xe2\x80\x9calbeit to her detriment,\xe2\x80\x9d despite intent to moor indefinitely). See also 649 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1267 (rejecting views of Circuits that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98focus on the intent of the shipowner\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt the outset we consider one threshold matter. The District Court ordered the floating home sold to satisfy the City\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. The City bought the home at public auction and subsequently had it destroyed. And, after the parties filed their merits briefs, we ordered further briefing on the question of mootness in light of the home\xe2\x80\x99s destruction. 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012). The parties now have pointed out that, prior to the home\xe2\x80\x99s sale, the District Court ordered the City to post a $25,000 bond \xe2\x80\x9cto secure Mr. Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s value in the vessel.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Record, Doc. 20, p. 2. The bond ensures that Lozman can obtain monetary relief if he ultimately prevails. We consequently agree with the parties that the case is not moot.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe focus primarily upon the statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of being used .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as a means of transportation on water.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73. The Court of Appeals found that the home was \xe2\x80\x9ccapable\xe2\x80\x9d of transportation because it could float, it could proceed under tow, and its shore connections (power cable, water hose, rope lines) did not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98rende[r]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98practically incapable of transportation or movement.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 649 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1266 (quoting Belle of Orleans, supra, at 1312, in turn quoting Stewart, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 494). At least for argument\xe2\x80\x99s sake we agree with the Court of Appeals about the last-mentioned point, namely that Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s shore connections did not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98render\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the home \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98practically incapable of transportation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d But unlike the Eleventh Circuit, we do not find these considerations (even when combined with the home\xe2\x80\x99s other characteristics) sufficient to show that Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s home was a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals recognized that it had applied the term \xe2\x80\x9ccapable\xe2\x80\x9d broadly. 649 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1266. Indeed, it pointed with approval to language in an earlier case, Burks v. American River Transp. Co., 679 F.\xc2\xa02d 69 (1982), in which the Fifth Circuit said:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98No doubt the three men in a tub would also fit within our definition, and one probably could make a convincing case for Jonah inside the whale.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 649 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1269 (brackets omitted) (quoting Burks, supra, at 75).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tBut the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is too broad. Not every floating structure is a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel.\xe2\x80\x9d To state the obvious, a wooden washtub, a plastic dishpan, a swimming platform on pontoons, a large fishing net, a door taken off its hinges, or Pinocchio (when inside the whale) are not \xe2\x80\x9cvessels,\xe2\x80\x9d even if they are \xe2\x80\x9cartificial contrivance[s]\xe2\x80\x9d capable of floating, moving under tow, and incidentally carrying even a fair-sized item or two when they do so. Rather, the statute applies to an \xe2\x80\x9cartificial contrivance .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. capable of being used .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as a means of transportation on water.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73 (emphasis added). \xe2\x80\x9c[T]ransportation\xe2\x80\x9d involves the \xe2\x80\x9cconveyance (of things or persons) from one place to another.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 Oxford English Dictionary 424 (2d ed. 1989) (OED). Accord, N. Webster, An American Dictionary of the English Language 1406 (C. Goodrich & N. Porter eds. 1873) (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]he act of transporting, carrying, or conveying from one place to another\xe2\x80\x9d). And we must apply this definition in a \xe2\x80\x9cpractical,\xe2\x80\x9d not a \xe2\x80\x9ctheoretical,\xe2\x80\x9d way. Stewart, supra, at 496. Consequently, in our view a structure does not fall within the scope of this statutory phrase unless a reasonable observer, looking to the home\xe2\x80\x99s phys- ical characteristics and activities, would consider it designed to a practical degree for carrying people or things over water.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThough our criterion is general, the facts of this case illustrate more specifically what we have in mind. But for the fact that it floats, nothing about Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s home suggests that it was designed to any practical degree to transport persons or things over water. It had no rudder or other steering mechanism. 649 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1269. Its hull was unraked, ibid., and it had a rectangular bottom 10 inches below the water. Brief for Petitioner 27; App. 37. It had no special capacity to generate or store electricity but could obtain that utility only through ongoing connections with the land. Id., at 40. Its small rooms looked like ordinary nonmaritime living quarters. And those inside those rooms looked out upon the world, not through watertight portholes, but through French doors or ordinary windows. Id., at 44\xe2\x80\x9366.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough lack of self-propulsion is not dispositive, e.g., The Robert W. Parsons, \n\n191 U.\xc2\xa0S. 17, \n31 (1903)\n, it may be a relevant physical characteristic. And Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s home differs significantly from an ordinary houseboat in that it has no ability to propel itself. Cf. 33 CFR \xc2\xa7173.3 (2012) (\xe2\x80\x9cHouseboat means a motorized vessel .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. designed primarily for multi-purpose accommodation spaces with low freeboard and little or no foredeck or cockpit\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s home was able to travel over water only by being towed. Prior to its arrest, that home\xe2\x80\x99s travel by tow over water took place on only four occasions over a period of seven years. Supra, at 2. And when the home was towed a significant distance in 2006, the towing company had a second boat follow behind to prevent the home from swinging dangerously from side to side. App. 104.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe home has no other feature that might suggest a design to transport over water anything other than its own furnishings and related personal effects. In a word, we can find nothing about the home that could lead a reasonable observer to consider it designed to a practical degree for \xe2\x80\x9ctransportation on water.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur view of the statute is consistent with its text, precedent, and relevant purposes. For one thing, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language, read naturally, lends itself to that interpretation. We concede that the statute uses the word \xe2\x80\x9cevery,\xe2\x80\x9d referring to \xe2\x80\x9cevery description of watercraft or other artificial contrivance.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73 (emphasis added). But the term \xe2\x80\x9ccontrivance\xe2\x80\x9d refers to \xe2\x80\x9csomething contrived for, or employed in contriving to effect a purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 OED 850 (def. 7). The term \xe2\x80\x9ccraft\xe2\x80\x9d explains that purpose as \xe2\x80\x9cwater carriage and transport.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1104 (def. V(9)(b)) (de- fining \xe2\x80\x9ccraft\xe2\x80\x9d as a \xe2\x80\x9cvesse[l] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for\xe2\x80\x9d that purpose). The ad-dition of the word \xe2\x80\x9cwater\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9ccraft,\xe2\x80\x9d yielding the term \xe2\x80\x9cwatercraft,\xe2\x80\x9d emphasizes the point. And the next few words, \xe2\x80\x9cused, or capable of being used, as a means of transportation on water,\xe2\x80\x9d drive the point home.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor another thing, the bulk of precedent supports our conclusion. In Evansville & Bowling Green Packet Co. v. Chero Cola Bottling Co., \n\n271 U.\xc2\xa0S. 19 (1926)\n, the Court held that a wharfboat was not a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel.\xe2\x80\x9d The wharfboat floated next to a dock; it was used to transfer cargo from ship to dock and ship to ship; and it was connected to the dock with cables, utility lines, and a ramp. Id., at 21. At the same time, it was capable of being towed. And it was towed each winter to a harbor to avoid river ice. Id., at 20\xe2\x80\x9321. The Court reasoned that, despite the annual movement under tow, the wharfboat \xe2\x80\x9cwas not used to carry freight from one place to another,\xe2\x80\x9d nor did it \xe2\x80\x9cencounter perils of navigation to which craft used for transportation are exposed.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 22. (See Appendix, infra, for photograph of a period wharfboat).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning in Stewart also supports our conclusion. We there considered the application of the statutory definition to a dredge. 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 494. The dredge was \xe2\x80\x9ca massive floating platform\xe2\x80\x9d from which a suspended clamshell bucket would \xe2\x80\x9cremov[e] silt from the ocean floor,\xe2\x80\x9d depositing it \xe2\x80\x9conto one of two scows\xe2\x80\x9d floating alongside the dredge. Id., at 484. Like more traditional \xe2\x80\x9cseagoing vessels,\xe2\x80\x9d the dredge had, e.g., \xe2\x80\x9ca captain and crew, navigational lights, ballast tanks, and a crew dining area.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Unlike more ordinary vessels, it could navigate only by \xe2\x80\x9cmanipulating its anchors and cables\xe2\x80\x9d or by being towed. Ibid. Nonetheless it did move. In fact it moved over water \xe2\x80\x9cevery couple of hours.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 485.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe held that the dredge was a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel.\xe2\x80\x9d We wrote that \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x99s definition \xe2\x80\x9cmerely codified the meaning that the term \xe2\x80\x98vessel\xe2\x80\x99 had acquired in general maritime law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 490. We added that the question of the \xe2\x80\x9cwatercraft\xe2\x80\x99s use \xe2\x80\x98as a means of transportation on water\xe2\x80\x99 is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. practical,\xe2\x80\x9d and not \xe2\x80\x9cmerely .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. theoretical.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 496. And we pointed to cases holding that dredges ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9cserved a waterborne transportation function,\xe2\x80\x9d namely that \xe2\x80\x9cin performing their work they carried machinery, equipment, and crew over water.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 491\xe2\x80\x93492 (citing, e.g., Butler v. Ellis, 45 F.\xc2\xa02d 951, 955 (CA4 1930)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs the Court of Appeals pointed out, in Stewart we also wrote that \xc2\xa73 \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not require that a watercraft be used primarily for that [transportation] purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 495; that a \xe2\x80\x9cwatercraft need not be in motion to qualify as a vessel,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; and that a structure may qualify as a vessel even if attached\xe2\x80\x94but not \xe2\x80\x9cpermanently\xe2\x80\x9d attached\xe2\x80\x94to the land or ocean floor. Id., at 493\xe2\x80\x93494. We did not take these statements, however, as implying a universal set of sufficient conditions for application of the definition. Rather, they say, and they mean, that the statutory definition may (or may not) apply\xe2\x80\x94not that it automatically must apply\xe2\x80\x94where a structure has some other primary purpose, where it is stationary at relevant times, and where it is attached\xe2\x80\x94but not permanently attached\xe2\x80\x94to land.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter all, a washtub is normally not a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d though it does not have water transportation as its primary purpose, it may be stationary much of the time, and it might be attached\xe2\x80\x94but not permanently attached\xe2\x80\x94to land. More to the point, water transportation was not the primary purpose of either Stewart\xe2\x80\x99s dredge or Evansville\xe2\x80\x99s wharfboat; neither structure was \xe2\x80\x9cin motion\xe2\x80\x9d at relevant times; and both were sometimes attached (though not permanently attached) to the ocean bottom or to land. Nonetheless Stewart\xe2\x80\x99s dredge fell within the statute\xe2\x80\x99s definition while Evansville\xe2\x80\x99s wharfboat did not.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe basic difference, we believe, is that the dredge was regularly, but not primarily, used (and designed in part to be used) to transport workers and equipment over water while the wharfboat was not designed (to any practical degree) to serve a transportation function and did not do so. Compare Cope v. Vallette Dry Dock Co., \n\n119 U.\xc2\xa0S. 625 (1887)\n (floating drydock not a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d because permanently fixed to wharf), with Jerome B. Grubart, Inc. v. Great Lakes Dredge & Dock Co., \n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 527, \n535 (1995)\n (barge sometimes attached to river bottom to use as a work platform remains a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d when \xe2\x80\x9cat other times it was used for transportation\xe2\x80\x9d). See also ibid. (citing Great Lakes Dredge & Dock Co. v. Chicago, 3 F.\xc2\xa03d 225, 229 (CA7 1993) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] craft is a \xe2\x80\x98vessel\xe2\x80\x99 if its purpose is to some reasonable degree \xe2\x80\x98the transportation of passengers, cargo, or equipment from place to place across navigable waters\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d)); Cope, supra, at 630 (describing \xe2\x80\x9chopper-barge,\xe2\x80\x9d as potentially a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d because it is a \xe2\x80\x9cnavigable structure[,] used for the purpose of transportation\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. 1 Benedict on Admiralty \xc2\xa7164, p. 10\xe2\x80\x936 (7th rev. ed. 2012) (maritime jurisdiction proper if \xe2\x80\x9cthe craft is a navigable structure intended for maritime transportation\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLower court cases also tend, on balance, to support our conclusion. See, e.g., Bernard v. Binnings Constr. Co., 741 F.\xc2\xa02d 824, 828, n.\xc2\xa013, 832, n. 25 (CA5 1984) (work punt lacking features objectively indicating a transportation function not a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel,\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cour decisions make clear that the mere capacity to float or move across navigable waters does not necessarily make a structure a vessel\xe2\x80\x9d); Ruddiman v. A Scow Platform, 38 F. 158 (SDNY 1889) (scow, though \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of being towed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. though not without some difficulty, from its clumsy structure\xe2\x80\x9d just a floating box, not a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel,\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cit was not designed or used for the purpose of navigation,\xe2\x80\x9d not engaged \xe2\x80\x9cin the transportation of persons or cargo,\xe2\x80\x9d and had \xe2\x80\x9cno motive power, no rudder, no sails\xe2\x80\x9d). See also 1 T. Schoenbaum, Admi- ralty and Maritime Law \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x936, p. 155 (5th ed. 2011) (courts have found that \xe2\x80\x9cfloating dry-dock[s],\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfloating platforms, barges, or rafts used for construction or repair of piers, docks, bridges, pipelines and other\xe2\x80\x9d similar facilities are not \xe2\x80\x9cvessels\xe2\x80\x9d); E. Benedict, American Admiralty \xc2\xa7215, p.\xc2\xa0116 (3d rev. ed. 1898) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98machine adapted to transportation over rivers, seas, and oceans\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe recognize that some lower court opinions can be read as endorsing the \xe2\x80\x9canything that floats\xe2\x80\x9d approach. See Miami River Boat Yard, Inc. v. 60\xe2\x80\x99 Houseboat, 390 F.\xc2\xa02d 596, 597 (CA5 1968) (so-called \xe2\x80\x9chouseboat\xe2\x80\x9d lacking self-propulsion); Sea Village Marina, LLC v. A 1980 Carlcraft Houseboat, No. 09\xe2\x80\x933292, 2009 WL 3379923, *5\xe2\x80\x93*6 (D NJ, Oct. 19, 2009) (following Miami River Boat Yard); Hudson Harbor 79th Street Boat Basin, Inc. v. Sea Casa, 469 F. Supp. 987, 989 (SDNY 1979) (same). Cf. Holmes v. Atlantic Sounding Co., 437 F.\xc2\xa03d 441 (CA5 2006) (floating dormitory); Summerlin v. Massman Constr. Co., 199 F.\xc2\xa02d 715 (CA4 1952) (derrick anchored in the river engaged in building a bridge is a vessel). For the reasons we have stated, we find such an approach inappropriate and inconsistent with our precedents.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFurther, our examination of the purposes of major federal maritime statutes reveals little reason to classify floating homes as \xe2\x80\x9cvessels.\xe2\x80\x9d Admiralty law, for example, provides special attachment procedures lest a vessel avoid liability by sailing away. 46 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa731341\xe2\x80\x9331343 (2006 ed. and Supp. IV). Liability statutes such as the Jones Act recognize that sailors face the special \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98perils of the sea.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Chandris, Inc. v. Latsis, \n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 347, \n354, \n373 (1995)\n (referring to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98vessel[s] in navigation\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Certain admiralty tort doctrines can encourage shipowners to engage in port-related commerce. E.g., \n\n46 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa730505; Executive Jet Aviation, Inc. v. Cleveland, \n\n409 U.\xc2\xa0S. 249\n\xe2\x80\x93270 (1972). And maritime safety statutes subject vessels to U.\xc2\xa0S. Coast Guard inspections. E.g., \n\n46 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73301.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLozman, however, cannot easily escape liability by sailing away in his home. He faces no special sea dangers. He does not significantly engage in port-related commerce. And the Solicitor General tells us that to adopt a version of the \xe2\x80\x9canything that floats\xe2\x80\x9d test would place unneces- sary and undesirable inspection burdens upon the Coast Guard. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 29, n.\xc2\xa011.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, our conclusion is consistent with state laws in States where floating home owners have congregated in communities. See Brief for Seattle Floating Homes As- sociation et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 1. A Washington State environmental statute, for example, defines a floating home (for regulatory purposes) as \xe2\x80\x9ca single-family dwelling unit constructed on a float, that is moored, anchored, or otherwise secured in waters, and is not a vessel, even though it may be capable of being towed.\xe2\x80\x9d Wash. Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa790.58.270(5)(b)(ii) (Supp. 2012). A California statute defines a floating home (for tax purposes) as \xe2\x80\x9ca floating structure\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned and built to be used, or is modified to be used, as a stationary waterborne residential dwelling,\xe2\x80\x9d and which (unlike a typical houseboat), has no independent power generation, and is dependent on shore utilities. Cal. Health & Safety Code Ann. \xc2\xa718075.55(d) (West 2006). These States, we are told, treat structures that meet their \xe2\x80\x9cfloating home\xe2\x80\x9d definitions like ordinary land-based homes rather than like vessels. Brief for Seattle Floating Homes Association 2. Consistency of interpretation of related state and federal laws is a virtue in that it helps to create simplicity making the law easier to understand and to follow for lawyers and for nonlawyers alike. And that consideration here supports our conclusion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tD\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe City and supporting amici make several important arguments that warrant our response. First, they ar- gue against use of any purpose-based test lest we introduce into \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d determinations a subjective element\xe2\x80\x94namely, the owner\xe2\x80\x99s intent. That element, they say, is often \xe2\x80\x9cunverifiable\xe2\x80\x9d and too easily manipulated. Its introduction would \xe2\x80\x9cfoment unpredictability and invite gamesmanship.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 33.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe agree with the City about the need to eliminate the consideration of evidence of subjective intent. But we cannot agree that the need requires abandonment of all criteria based on \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Cf. Stewart, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 495 (discussing transportation purpose). Indeed, it is difficult, if not impossible, to determine the use of a human \xe2\x80\x9ccontrivance\xe2\x80\x9d without some consideration of human purposes. At the same time, we have sought to avoid subjective elements, such as owner\xe2\x80\x99s intent, by permitting consideration only of objective evidence of a waterborne transportation purpose. That is why we have referred to the views of a reasonable observer. Supra, at 1. And it is why we have looked to the physical attributes and behavior of the structure, as objective manifestations of any relevant purpose, and not to the subjective intent of the owner. Supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. We note that various admiralty treatises refer to the use of purpose-based tests without any suggestion that administration of those tests has introduced too much subjectivity into the vessel-determination process. 1 Benedict on Admiralty \xc2\xa7164; 1 Admiralty and Maritime Law \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x936.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, the City, with support of amici, argues against the use of criteria that are too abstract, complex, or open-ended. Brief for Respondent 28\xe2\x80\x9329. A court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction, e.g., admiralty jurisdiction, may turn on application of the term \xe2\x80\x9cvessel.\xe2\x80\x9d And jurisdictional tests, often applied at the outset of a case, should be \xe2\x80\x9cas simple as possible.\xe2\x80\x9d Hertz Corp. v. Friend, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 1).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe agree with the last-mentioned sentiment. And we also understand that our approach is neither perfectly pre-cise nor always determinative. Satisfaction of a design-based or purpose-related criterion, for example, is not always sufficient for application of the statutory word \xe2\x80\x9cvessel.\xe2\x80\x9d A craft whose physical characteristics and activities objectively evidence a waterborne transportation purpose or function may still be rendered a nonvessel by later physical alterations. For example, an owner might take a structure that is otherwise a vessel (even the Queen Mary) and connect it permanently to the land for use, say, as a hotel. See Stewart, supra, at 493\xe2\x80\x93494. Further, changes over time may produce a new form, i.e., a newly designed structure\xe2\x80\x94in which case it may be the new de-sign that is relevant. See Kathriner v. Unisea, Inc., 975 F.\xc2\xa02d 657, 660 (CA9 1992) (floating processing plant was no longer a vessel where a \xe2\x80\x9clarge opening [had been] cut into her hull\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor is satisfaction of the criterion always a necessary condition, see Part IV, infra. It is conceivable that an owner might actually use a floating structure not designed to any practical degree for transportation as, say, a ferry boat, regularly transporting goods and persons over water.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNonetheless, we believe the criterion we have used, taken together with our example of its application here, should offer guidance in a significant number of borderline cases where \xe2\x80\x9ccapacity\xe2\x80\x9d to transport over water is in doubt. Moreover, borderline cases will always exist; they require a method for resolution; we believe the method we have used is workable; and, unlike, say, an \xe2\x80\x9canything that floats\xe2\x80\x9d test, it is consistent with statutory text, purpose, and precedent. Nor do we believe that the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approach would prove any more workable. For example, the dissent suggests a relevant distinction between an own- er\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cclothes and personal effects\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clarge appliances (like an oven or a refrigerator).\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 8 (opinion of Sotomayor, J.). But a transportation function need not turn on the size of the items in question, and we believe the line between items being transported from place to place (e.g., cargo) and items that are mere appurtenances is the one more likely to be relevant. Cf. Benedict, American Admiralty \xc2\xa7222, at 121 (\xe2\x80\x9cA ship is usually described as consisting of the ship, her tackle, apparel, and furniture .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the dissent and the Solicitor General (as amicus for Lozman) argue that a remand is warranted for further factfinding. See post, at 10\xe2\x80\x9312; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 29\xe2\x80\x9331. But neither the City nor Lozman makes such a request. Brief for Respondent 18, 49, 52. And the only potentially relevant factual dispute the dis- sent points to is that the home suffered serious damage during a tow. Post, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. But this would add support to our ultimate conclusion that this floating home was not a vessel. We consequently see nothing to be gained by a remand.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough we have focused on the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of be- ing used\xe2\x80\x9d for transportation over water, the statute also includes as a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d a structure that is actually \xe2\x80\x9cused\xe2\x80\x9d for that transportation. \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73 (emphasis added). And the City argues that, irrespective of its design, Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s floating home was actually so used. Brief for Respondent 32. We are not persuaded by its argument.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe are willing to assume for argument\xe2\x80\x99s sake that sometimes it is possible actually to use for water transportation a structure that is in no practical way designed for that purpose. See supra, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. But even so, the City cannot show the actual use for which it argues. Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s floating home moved only under tow. Before its arrest, it moved significant distances only twice in seven years. And when it moved, it carried, not passengers or cargo, but at the very most (giving the benefit of any factual ambiguity to the City) only its own furnishings, its owner\xe2\x80\x99s personal effects, and personnel present to assure the home\xe2\x80\x99s safety. 649 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1268; Brief for Respondent 32; Tr. of Oral Arg. 37\xe2\x80\x9338. This is far too little actual \xe2\x80\x9cuse\xe2\x80\x9d to bring the floating home within the terms of the statute. See Evansville, 271 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 20\xe2\x80\x9321 (wharfboat not a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel\xe2\x80\x9d even though \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach winter\xe2\x80\x9d it \xe2\x80\x9cwas towed to [a] harbor to protect it from ice\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Roper v. United States, \n\n368 U.\xc2\xa0S. 20, \n23 (1961)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cUnlike a barge, the S.\xc2\xa0S. Harry Lane was not moved in order to transport commodities from one location to another\xe2\x80\x9d). See also supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI agree with much of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning. Our precedents fully support the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning that the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s test is overinclusive; that the subjective intentions of a watercraft\xe2\x80\x99s owner or designer play no role in the vessel analysis of \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73; and that an ob- jective assessment of a watercraft\xe2\x80\x99s purpose or function governs whether that structure is a vessel. The Court, however, creates a novel and unnecessary \xe2\x80\x9creasonable observer\xe2\x80\x9d reformulation of these principles and errs in its determination, under this new standard, that the craft before us is not a vessel. Given the underdeveloped rec- ord below, we should remand. Therefore, I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe relevant statute, \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73, \xe2\x80\x9csweeps broadly.\xe2\x80\x9d Stewart v. Dutra Constr. Co., \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 481, \n494 (2005)\n. It provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he word \xe2\x80\x98vessel\xe2\x80\x99 includes every description of watercraft or other artificial contrivance used, or capable of being used, as a means of transportation on water.\xe2\x80\x9d This broad phrasing flows from admiralty law\xe2\x80\x99s long recognition that vessels come in many shapes and sizes. See E. Benedict, American Admiralty \xc2\xa7218, p. 121 (1870 ed.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[V]essel, is a general word, many times used for any kind of navigation\xe2\x80\x9d); M. Cohen, Admiralty Jurisdiction, Law, and Practice 232 (1883) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he term \xe2\x80\x98ves- sel\xe2\x80\x99 shall be understood to comprehend every description of vessel navigating on any sea or channel, lake or river .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur test for vessel status has remained the same for decades: \xe2\x80\x9cUnder \xc2\xa73, a \xe2\x80\x98vessel\xe2\x80\x99 is any watercraft practically capable of maritime transportation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Stewart, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 497; see also Evansville & Bowling Green Packet Co. v. Chero Cola Bottling Co., \n\n271 U. S. 19, \n22 (1926)\n; Cope v. Vallette Dry Dock Co., \n\n119 U. S. 625, \n627 (1887)\n. At its core, vessel status has always rested upon the objective physical characteristics of a vessel (such as its structure, shape, and materials of construction), as well as its usage history. But over time, several important principles have guided both this Court and the lower courts in determining what kinds of watercraft fall properly within the scope of admiralty jurisdiction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsider the most basic of requirements. For a watercraft to be \xe2\x80\x9cpractically capable\xe2\x80\x9d of maritime transportation, it must first be \xe2\x80\x9ccapable\xe2\x80\x9d of such transportation. Only those structures that can simultaneously float and carry people or things over water are even presumptively within \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x99s reach. Stopping here, as the Eleventh Circuit essentially did, results in an overinclusive test. Section 3, after all, does not drag every bit of floating and towable flotsam and jetsam into admiralty jurisdiction. Rather, the terms \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of being used\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpractical\xe2\x80\x9d have real significance in our maritime jurisprudence.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[A] water craft is not \xe2\x80\x98capable of being used\xe2\x80\x99 for maritime transport in any meaningful sense if it has been permanently moored.\xe2\x80\x9d Stewart, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 494. So, to take an obvious example, a floating bridge over water does not constitute a vessel; such mooring is clearly permanent. Cf. The Rock Island Bridge, 6 Wall. 213, 216 (1867). Less dramatically, a watercraft whose objective physical connections to land \xe2\x80\x9cevidence a permanent location\xe2\x80\x9d does not fall within \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x99s ambit. See, e.g., Evansville, 271 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 22 (\xe2\x80\x9c[The wharfboat] served at Evansville as an office, warehouse and wharf, and was not taken from place to place. The connections with the water, electric light and telephone systems of the city evidence a permanent location\xe2\x80\x9d); Dunklin v. Louisiana Riverboat Gaming Partnership, No. 00\xe2\x80\x9331455, 2001 WL 650209, *1, n. 1 (CA5, May 22, 2001) (per\xc2\xa0curiam) (describing a fully functional ca- sino boat placed \xe2\x80\x9cin an enclosed pond in a cofferdam\xe2\x80\x9d). Put plainly, structures \xe2\x80\x9cpermanently affixed to shore or resting on the ocean floor,\xe2\x80\x9d Stewart, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 493\xe2\x80\x93494, have never been treated as vessels for the purposes of \xc2\xa73.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur precedents have also excluded from vessel status those watercraft \xe2\x80\x9crendered practically incapable of transportation or movement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 494. Take the easiest case, a vessel whose physical characteristics have been so altered as to make waterborne transportation a practical impossibility. Ibid. (explaining that a \xe2\x80\x9cfloating processing plant was no longer a vessel where a \xe2\x80\x98large opening [had been] cut into her hull,\xe2\x80\x99 rendering her incapable of moving over the water\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Kathriner v. UNISEA, Inc., 975 F.\xc2\xa02d 657, 660 (CA9 1992)). The longstanding admiralty exception for \xe2\x80\x9cdead ships,\xe2\x80\x9d those watercraft that \xe2\x80\x9crequire a major overhaul\xe2\x80\x9d for their \xe2\x80\x9creactivation,\xe2\x80\x9d also falls into this category. See Roper v. United States, \n\n368 U. S. 20, \n21 (1961)\n (finding that a liberty ship \xe2\x80\x9cdeactivated from service and \xe2\x80\x98mothballed\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d is not a \xe2\x80\x9cvessel in navigation\xe2\x80\x9d); see generally Rutherglen, Dead Ships, 30 J. Maritime L. & Comm. 677 (1999).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Likewise, ships that \xe2\x80\x9chave been withdrawn from the water for extended periods of time\xe2\x80\x9d in order to facilitate repairs and reconstruction may lose their status as vessels until they are rendered capable of maritime transport. Stewart, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 496. Cf. West v. United States, \n\n361 U.\xc2\xa0S. 118, \n120, \n122 (1959)\n (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Mary Austin was withdrawn from any operation whatever while in storage with the \xe2\x80\x98moth-ball fleet\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Mary Austin, as anyone could see, was not in maritime service. She was undergoing major repairs and complete renovation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, our maritime jurisprudence excludes from ves- sel status those floating structures that, based on their physical characteristics, do not \xe2\x80\x9ctransport people, freight, or cargo from place to place\xe2\x80\x9d as one of their purposes. Stewart, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 493. \xe2\x80\x9cPurpose,\xe2\x80\x9d in this context, is determined solely by an objective inquiry into a craft\xe2\x80\x99s function. \xe2\x80\x9c[N]either size, form, equipment nor means of propulsion are determinative factors upon the question of [vessel status],\xe2\x80\x9d though all may be considered. The Robert W. Parsons, \n\n191 U. S. 17, \n30 (1903)\n. Moreover, in assessing a particular structure\xe2\x80\x99s function, we have consis- tently examined its past and present activities. Stewart, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 495; Cope, 119 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 627. Of course, a seaborne craft is not excluded from vessel status simply because its \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose\xe2\x80\x9d is not maritime transport. Stewart, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 497. We held as much in Stewart when we concluded that a dredge was a vessel notwithstanding that its \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cdredging rather than transportation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 486, 495. So long as one purpose of a craft is transportation, whether of cargo or people or both, \xc2\xa73\xe2\x80\x99s practical capability requirement is satisfied.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCertainly, difficult and marginal cases will arise. Fortunately, courts do not consider each floating structure anew. So, for example, when we were confronted in Stewart with the question whether a dredge is a \xc2\xa73 vessel, we did not commence with a clean slate; we instead sought guidance from previous cases that had confronted similar structures. See id., at 490, and n.\xc2\xa05; see also Norton v. Warner Co., \n\n321 U.\xc2\xa0S. 565\n\xe2\x80\x93572 (1944) (likewise surveying earlier cases).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, our precedents offer substantial guidance for how objectively to determine whether a watercraft is practically capable of maritime transport and thus qualifies as a \xc2\xa73 vessel. First, the capacity to float and carry things or people is an obvious prerequisite to vessel status. Second, structures or ships that are permanently moored or fixed in place are not \xc2\xa73 vessels. Likewise, structures that are practically incapable of maritime transport are not vessels, whether they are ships that have been altered so that they may no longer be put to sea, dead ships, or ships removed from navigation for extended periods of time. Third, those watercraft whose physical characteristics and usage history reveal no maritime transport purpose or use are not \xc2\xa73 vessels.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority does not appear to disavow the legal principles described above. The majority apparently accepts that permanent mooring suffices to take a ship out of vessel status, ante, at 8, 12,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] craft whose physical characteristics and activities objectively evidence a waterborne transportation purpose or function may still be rendered a nonvessel by later physical alterations,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t No one argues that Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft was permanently moored, see App. 32 (describing the \xe2\x80\x9cdeteriorated\xe2\x80\x9d ropes holding the craft in place), or that it had un- dergone physical alterations sufficient to take it out of vessel status, see Tr. of Oral Arg. 13 (Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s counsel arguing that the craft was never a vessel in the first place). Our precedents make clear that the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9canything that floats\xe2\x80\x9d test is overinclusive and ignores that purpose is a crucial factor in determining whether a particular craft is or is not a vessel. Accord- ingly, the majority is correct that determining whether Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft is a vessel hinges on whether that craft had any maritime transportation purpose or function.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority errs, though, in concluding that the purpose component of the \xc2\xa73 test is whether \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable observer, looking to the [craft]\xe2\x80\x99s physical characteristics and activities, would not consider it to be designed to any practical degree for carrying people or things on water.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1. This phrasing has never appeared in any of our cases and the majority\xe2\x80\x99s use of it, despite its seemingly objective gloss, effectively (and erroneously) introduces a subjective component into the vessel-status inquiry.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor one thing, in applying this test the majority points to some characteristics of Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft that have no relationship to maritime transport, such as the style of the craft\xe2\x80\x99s rooms or that \xe2\x80\x9cthose inside those rooms looked out upon the world, not through water-tight portholes, but through French doors or ordinary windows.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. The majority never explains why it believes these particular esthetic elements are important for determining vessel status. In fact, they are not. Section 3 is focused on whether a structure is \xe2\x80\x9cused, or capable of being used, as a means of transportation on water.\xe2\x80\x9d By importing windows, doors, room style, and other esthetic criteria into the \xc2\xa73 analysis, the majority gives our vessel test an \xe2\x80\x9cI know it when I see it\xe2\x80\x9d flavor. Jacobellis v. Ohio, \n\n378 U.\xc2\xa0S. 184, \n197 (1964)\n (Stewart, J., concurring). But that has never been nor should it be the test: A badly designed and unattractive vessel is different from a structure that lacks any \xe2\x80\x9cpractical capacity\xe2\x80\x9d for maritime transport. In the major- ity\xe2\x80\x99s eyes, the two appear to be one and the same.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of the craft\xe2\x80\x99s past voyages is also strange. The majority notes that Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft could be and was, in fact, towed over long distances, including over 200 miles at one point. Ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x936. But the major- ity determines that, given the design of Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft, this is \xe2\x80\x9cfar too little actual \xe2\x80\x98use\xe2\x80\x99 to bring the floating home within the terms of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. This is because \xe2\x80\x9cwhen it moved, it carried, not passengers or cargo, but at the very most (giving the benefit of any factual ambiguity to the City) only its own furnishings, its owner\xe2\x80\x99s personal effects, and personnel present to assure the home\xe2\x80\x99s safety.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI find this analysis confusing. The majority accepts that the record indicates that Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft traveled hundreds of miles while \xe2\x80\x9ccarrying people or things.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1. But then, in the same breath, the majority concludes that a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable observer\xe2\x80\x9d would nonetheless conclude that the craft was not \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to any practical degree for carrying people or things on water.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The majority fails to explain how a craft that apparently did carry people and things over water for long distances was not \xe2\x80\x9cpractically capable\xe2\x80\x9d of maritime transport. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis is not to say that a structure capable of such feats is necessarily a vessel. A craft like Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s might not be a vessel, for example, if it could only carry its owner\xe2\x80\x99s clothes and personal effects, or if it is only capable of transporting itself and its appurtenances. Jerome B. Gru- bart, Inc. v. Great Lakes Dredge & Dock Co., \n\n513 U. S. 527, \n535 (1995)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[M]aritime law .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. ordinarily treats an \xe2\x80\x98appurtenance\xe2\x80\x99 attached to a vessel in navigable waters as part of the vessel itself\xe2\x80\x9d). But if such a craft can carry large appliances (like an oven or a refrigerator) and all of the other things we might find in a normal home in addition to the occupants of that home, as the existing record suggests Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft may have done, then it would seem to be much more like a mobile home (and therefore a vessel) than a firmly rooted residence. The simple truth is that we know very little about the craft\xe2\x80\x99s capabilities and what did or did not happen on its various trips. By fo- cusing on the little we do know for certain about this craft (i.e., its windows, doors, and the style of its rooms) in determining whether it is a vessel, the majority renders the \xc2\xa73 inquiry opaque and unpredictable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, the little we do know about Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft suggests only that it was an unusual structure. A sur- veyor was unable to find any comparable craft for sale in the State of Florida. App. 43. Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s home was neither obviously a houseboat, as the majority describes such ships, ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, nor clearly a floating home, ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. See App. 13, 31, 79 (sale, lease, and surveying documents describing Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft as a \xe2\x80\x9chouseboat\xe2\x80\x9d). The only clear difference that the majority identifies between these two kinds of structures is that the former are self-propelled, while the latter are not. Ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. But even the majority recognizes that self-propulsion has never been a prerequisite for vessel status. Ante, at 5 (citing The Robert W. Parsons, 191 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 31); see Norton, 321 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 571. Consequently, it is unclear why Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft is a floating home, why all floating homes are not vessels,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t or why Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft is not a vessel. If windows, doors, and other esthetic attributes are what take Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft out of vessel status, then the major- ity\xe2\x80\x99s test is completely malleable. If it is the craft\xe2\x80\x99s lack of self-propulsion, then the majority\xe2\x80\x99s test is unfaithful to our longstanding precedents. See The Robert W. Parsons, 191 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 30\xe2\x80\x9331. If it is something else, then that something is not apparent from the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWorse still, in straining to find that Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft was a floating home and therefore not a vessel, the major- ity calls into question the conclusions of numerous lower courts that have found houseboats that lacked self-propulsion to be \xc2\xa73 vessels. See ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (citing Miami River Boat Yard, Inc. v. 60\xe2\x80\x99 Houseboat, 390 F. 2d 596, 597 (CA5 1968); Sea Village Marina, LLC v. A 1980 Carlcraft Houseboat, No. 09\xe2\x80\x933292, 2009 WL 3379923, *5\xe2\x80\x93*6 (D NJ, Oct. 19, 2009); Hudson Harbor 79th Street Boat Basin, Inc. v. Sea Casa, 469 F. Supp. 987, 989 (SDNY 1979)). The majority incorrectly suggests that these cases applied an \xe2\x80\x9c \xe2\x80\x98anything that floats\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d test. Ante, at 9. These cases suggest something different. Many of these decisions in assessing the crafts before them looked carefully at these crafts\xe2\x80\x99 structure and function, and determined that these ships had capabilities similar to other long-established vessels, suggesting a significant maritime transportation function. See Miami River Boat Yard, 390 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 597 (likening houseboat at issue to a \xe2\x80\x9cbarg[e]\xe2\x80\x9d); Sea Village Marina, 2009 WL 3379923, *7 (\xe2\x80\x9cAccording to the available evidence, [the houseboats in question] float and can be towed to a new marina without substantial effort .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d); Hudson Harbor, 469 F.\xc2\xa0Supp., at 989 (houseboat \xe2\x80\x9cwas capable of being used at least to the extent that a \xe2\x80\x98dumb barge\xe2\x80\x99 is capable of being used\xe2\x80\x9d and comparable to a \xe2\x80\x9cyach[t]\xe2\x80\x9d). \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is not clear that Lozman\xe2\x80\x99s craft is a \xc2\xa73 vessel. It is clear, however, that we are not in a good position to make such a determination based on the limited record we pos- sess.
