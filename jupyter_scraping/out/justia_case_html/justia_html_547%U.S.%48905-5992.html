\nOCTOBER TERM, 2005
\nZEDNER V. UNITED STATES
\n

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

ZEDNER v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 05\xe2\x80\x935992.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 18, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 5, 2006

\n

The Speedy Trial Act of 1974 (Act) generally requires a federal criminal trial to begin within 70 days after a defendant is charged or makes an initial appearance. 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73161(c)(1). Recognizing that criminal cases vary widely and that there are valid reasons for greater delay in particular cases, the Act includes a long and detailed list of periods of delay that are excluded in computing the time within which trial must start. Section 3161(h)(8) permits a district court to grant a continuance and exclude the resulting delay if it makes on-the-record findings that the ends of justice served by granting the continuance outweigh the public\xe2\x80\x99s and defendant\xe2\x80\x99s interests in a speedy trial. To promote compliance without needlessly subverting important criminal prosecutions, the Act provides that, if the trial does not begin on time and the defendant moves, before the trial\xe2\x80\x99s start or entry of a guilty plea, to dismiss, the district court must dismiss the charges, though it may choose whether to do so with or without prejudice.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In April 1996, petitioner was indicted on charges arising from his attempt to open accounts using counterfeit United States bonds. The District Court granted two \xe2\x80\x9cends-of-justice\xe2\x80\x9d continuances, see \xc2\xa73161(h)(8). When, at a November 8 status conference, petitioner requested another delay to January 1997, the court suggested that petitioner waive the application of the Act \xe2\x80\x9cfor all time,\xe2\x80\x9d and produced a preprinted waiver form for petitioner to sign. At a January 31, 1997, status conference, the court granted petitioner another continuance so that he could attempt to authenticate the bonds, but made no mention of the Act and no findings to support excluding the 91 days between January 31 and petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s next court appearance on May 2 (1997 continuance). Four years later, petitioner filed a motion to dismiss the indictment for failure to comply with the Act, which the District Court denied based on the waiver \xe2\x80\x9cfor all time.\xe2\x80\x9d In a 2003 trial, petitioner was convicted. The Second Circuit affirmed. Acknowledging that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of rights under the Act may be ineffective because of the public interest served by compliance with the Act, the court found an exception for situations when the defendant causes or contributes to the delay. It also suggested that the District Court could have properly excluded the 91-day period based on the ends of justice, given the case\xe2\x80\x99s complexity and the defense\xe2\x80\x99s request for additional time to prepare.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Because a defendant may not prospectively waive the application of the Act, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s waiver \xe2\x80\x9cfor all time\xe2\x80\x9d was ineffective. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Act comprehensively regulates the time within which a trial must begin. Section 3161(h), which details numerous categories of delay that are not counted in applying the Act\xe2\x80\x99s deadlines, conspicuously has no provision excluding periods of delay during which a defendant waives the Act\xe2\x80\x99s application. It is apparent from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s terms that this was a considered omission. Instead of allowing defendants to opt out, the Act demands that continuances fit within one of \xc2\xa73161(h)\xe2\x80\x99s specific exclusions. In deciding whether to grant an ends-of-justice continuance, a court must consider a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s need for \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time to obtain counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuity of counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ceffective preparation\xe2\x80\x9d of counsel. \xc2\xa73161(h)(8)(B)(iv). If a defendant could simply waive the Act\xe2\x80\x99s application in order to secure more time, no defendant would ever need to put such considerations before the court under the rubric of an ends-of-justice exclusion. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s purposes also cut against exclusion on the grounds of mere consent or waiver. Were the Act solely designed to protect a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to a speedy trial, such an application might make sense, but the Act was also designed with the public interest firmly in mind. This interpretation is entirely in accord with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0This Court rejects the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on \xc2\xa73162(a)(2), which provides that a defendant whose trial does not begin on time is deemed to have waived the right to move for dismissal if that motion is not filed prior to trial or entry of a guilty plea. That section makes no mention of prospective waivers, and there is no reason to think that Congress wanted to treat prospective and retrospective waivers similarly. Allowing prospective waivers would seriously undermine the Act because, in many cases, the prosecution, defense, and court would all like to opt out, to the detriment of the public interest. Section 3162(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s retrospective waiver does not pose a comparable danger. Because the prosecution and court cannot know until the trial starts or the guilty plea is entered whether the defendant will forgo moving to dismiss, they retain a strong incentive to make sure the trial begins on time. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Petitioner is not estopped from challenging the excludability under the Act of the 1997 continuance. Factors that \xe2\x80\x9ctypically inform the decision whether to apply the [estoppel] doctrine in a particular case\xe2\x80\x9d include (1) whether \xe2\x80\x9ca party\xe2\x80\x99s later position [is] clearly inconsistent with its earlier position\xe2\x80\x9d; (2) \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the party has succeeded in persuading a court to accept that \xe2\x80\xa6 earlier position\xe2\x80\x9d; and (3) \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the party seeking to assert an inconsistent position would derive an unfair advantage or impose an unfair detriment on the opposing party if not estopped.\xe2\x80\x9d New Hampshire v. Maine, 532 U. S. 742, 750\xe2\x80\x93751. None of the three possible \xe2\x80\x9cpositions\xe2\x80\x9d taken by petitioner gives rise to an estoppel. First, recognizing an estoppel based on petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s promise not to move for dismissal under \xc2\xa73162(a)(2) would entirely swallow the Act\xe2\x80\x99s no-waiver policy. Second, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s (mistaken) agreement that waivers are enforceable does not provide a ground for estoppel because petitioner did not \xe2\x80\x9csuccee[d] in persuading\xe2\x80\x9d the District Court to accept the validity of prospective waivers. On the contrary, the District Court requested the waiver and produced the form for petitioner to sign. Even if the other factors favor estoppel, they do not predominate. Finally, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s representation at the January 31 status conference that a continuance was needed to gather evidence of the bonds\xe2\x80\x99 authenticity does not support estoppel because that position was not \xe2\x80\x9cclearly inconsistent\xe2\x80\x9d with the position that he now takes in seeking dismissal, i.e., that delay from that continuance was not excluded under the Act. Nothing in the discussion at the conference suggests that the question presented by the continuance request was viewed as anything other than a case-management question laying entirely within the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0When a district court makes no findings on the record to support a \xc2\xa73161(h)(8) continuance, harmless-error review is not appropriate. The Government argues that an express finding need not be entered contemporaneously and could be supplied on remand. But the Act requires express findings, see \xc2\xa73161(h)(8)(A), and at the very least implies that those findings must be put on the record by the time the district court rules on the motion to dismiss. Because the District Court made no such express findings, the 1997 continuance is not excluded from the speedy trial clock. This error is not subject to harmless-error review. Harmless-error review under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 52(a) presumptively applies to \xe2\x80\x9call errors where a proper objection is made,\xe2\x80\x9d Neder v. United States, 527 U. S. 1, 7, but strong support for an implied repeal of Rule 52(a) in this context is provided by the Act\xe2\x80\x99s unequivocal provisions, which specify that a trial \xe2\x80\x9cshall commence\xe2\x80\x9d within 70 days, \xc2\xa73161(c)(1) (emphasis added), and that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o \xe2\x80\xa6 period of delay\xe2\x80\x9d from an ends-of-justice continuance \xe2\x80\x9cshall be excludable\xe2\x80\x9d from the time period unless the court sets forth its reasoning, \xc2\xa73161(h)(8)(A) (emphasis added). Applying harmless-error review would also tend to undermine the detailed requirements of the provisions regulating ends-of-justice continuances. Pp. 15\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa04.\xc2\xa0Because the 91-day continuance, which was not excluded from the speedy trial clock, exceeded the maximum 70-day delay, the Act was violated, and there is no need to address whether other periods of delay were not excludable. The District Court may determine in the first instance whether the dismissal in this case should be with or without prejudice. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

401 F.\xc2\xa03d 36, reversed and remanded.

JACOB ZEDNER, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the second circuit

\n

[June 5, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case requires us to consider the application of the doctrines of waiver, judicial estoppel, and harmless error to a violation of the Speedy Trial Act of 1974 (Act), 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73161\xe2\x80\x933174. The Act generally requires a federal criminal trial to begin within 70 days after a defendant is charged or makes an initial appearance, \xc2\xa73161(c)(1), but the Act contains a detailed scheme under which certain specified periods of delay are not counted. In this case, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s trial did not begin within 70 days of indictment. Indeed, his trial did not commence until more than seven years after the filing of the indictment, but petitioner, at the suggestion of the trial judge, signed a blanket, prospective waiver of his rights under the Act. We address the following questions: whether this waiver was effective; whether petitioner is judicially estopped from challenging the validity of the waiver; and whether the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s failure to make the findings required to exclude a period of delay under a particular provision of the Act, \xc2\xa73161(h)(8), was harmless error.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In March 1996, petitioner attempted to open accounts at seven financial institutions using counterfeit $10 million United States bonds. The quality of the counterfeiting was, to put it mildly, not expert. One bond purported to be issued by the \xe2\x80\x9cMinistry of Finance of U. S.\xc2\xa0A.\xe2\x80\x9d 401 F.\xc2\xa03d 36, 39 (CA2 2005) (internal quotation marks omitted). Others contained misspelled words such as \xe2\x80\x9cThunted States\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cOnited States\xe2\x80\x9d (United States), \xe2\x80\x9cDhtladelphla\xe2\x80\x9d (Philadelphia), \xe2\x80\x9cCgicago\xe2\x80\x9d (Chicago), and \xe2\x80\x9cforevev\xe2\x80\x9d (forever). Id., at 39, n.\xc2\xa01 (internal quotation marks omitted). After petitioner presented these bonds, the Secret Service was contacted, and petitioner was arrested. Following arraignment on a criminal complaint, he was released on bond.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On April 4, 1996, a grand jury in the Eastern District of New York indicted petitioner on seven counts of attempting to defraud a financial institution, in violation of 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71344, and one count of knowingly possessing counterfeit obligations of the United States, in violation of \xc2\xa7472. On June 26, the District Court, citing the complexity of the case, granted what is termed an \xe2\x80\x9cends-of-justice\xe2\x80\x9d continuance, see \xc2\xa73161(h)(8)(B)(ii), until September 6. On September 6, the District Court granted another continuance, this time until November 8.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the November 8 status conference, petitioner requested, without opposition from the Government, a further adjournment to January 1997. Concerned about the difficulty of fitting petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s trial into its heavily scheduled calendar and the prospect that petitioner might \xe2\x80\x9conly waive [the Act] for so long as it is convenient for [him] to waive,\xe2\x80\x9d the District Court instructed petitioner as follows: \xe2\x80\x9cI think if I\xe2\x80\x99m going to give you that long an adjournment, I will have to take a waiver for all time.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 71. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel responded that the defense would \xe2\x80\x9cwaive for all time. That will not be a problem. That will not be an issue in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 72.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court then addressed petitioner directly and appears to have attempted to explain the operation of a provision of the Act, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73162(a)(2), under which a defendant whose trial does not begin on time is deemed to have waived the right to move for dismissal of the information or indictment if he or she does not file that motion prior to trial or entry of a guilty plea. The District Court reasoned: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f you can waive [the Act] by inaction, i.\xc2\xa0e., not raising the motion to dismiss, you can waive affirmatively, knowledgeably, intelligently your right to do so, your right to a speedy trial and your right to make a motion to dismiss for the speedy trial.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 73. The court told petitioner that it was \xe2\x80\x9cprepared to start \xe2\x80\xa6 trial right away,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., but that if a continuance was granted, petitioner might have to wait some time for trial because the court had a \xe2\x80\x9cfairly big cas[e] \xe2\x80\xa6 which [wa]s set to take eight months for trial.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f that [trial] starts before you start,\xe2\x80\x9d the court warned, \xe2\x80\x9cyou may have to wait until that is done.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 74.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court then produced a preprinted form\xe2\x80\x94apparently of its own devising\xe2\x80\x94captioned \xe2\x80\x9cWaiver of Speedy Trial Rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 79. The court led petitioner and his counsel through the form, and both signed it. Among other things, the form stated: \xe2\x80\x9cI wish to waive my rights to a speedy trial \xe2\x80\xa6 under the Speedy Trial Act of 1974 (18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73161 et seq.), under the Rules of this Circuit and under the Speedy Trial Plan adopted by this Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The form also stated: \xe2\x80\x9cI have been advised and fully understand that \xe2\x80\xa6 I also waive any and all rights to make a motion to dismiss the indictment \xe2\x80\xa6 against me for failure of the Court to give me a speedy trial and that I waive all of such rights to a speedy trial and to make such a motion or motions for all time.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. After the form was signed, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel requested that a further status conference be scheduled for January 31, 1997, and the court agreed. Id., at 77.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the January 31 status conference, petitioner sought yet another continuance \xe2\x80\x9cto tap \xe2\x80\xa6 the proper channels to authenticate [the] bonds.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 81. Petitioner and the Government emphasized that this request raised no issue under the Act because petitioner had \xe2\x80\x9cwaived for all time,\xe2\x80\x9d though the Government suggested that it \xe2\x80\x9cwould like to try the case sometime in 1997.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. After a brief discussion between the court and petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel about the need to investigate the authenticity of what seemed such obviously fake bonds, the court offered to set trial for May 5, 1997. Id., at 86. The court admonished petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel to \xe2\x80\x9c[g]et to work\xe2\x80\x9d and noted: \xe2\x80\x9cThis [case] is a year old. That\xe2\x80\x99s enough for a criminal case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 86, 85. Nevertheless, apparently satisfied with petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s waiver \xe2\x80\x9cfor all time,\xe2\x80\x9d the District Court made no mention of the Act and did not make any findings to support exclusion of the 91 days between January 31 and petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s next court appearance on May 2, 1997 (1997 continuance).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The four years that followed saw a variety of proceedings in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s case, but no trial. See 401 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 40\xe2\x80\x9341. Counsel sought to be relieved because petitioner insisted that he argue that the bonds were genuine, and the court ultimately granted counsel\xe2\x80\x99s request to withdraw. At the court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, petitioner was examined by a psychiatrist, who determined that petitioner was competent to stand trial. Petitioner then asked to proceed pro se and sought to serve subpoenas on, among others, the President, the Chairman of the Federal Reserve Board, the Attorney General, the Secretary of State, the late Chinese leader Chiang Kai-shek, and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The Treasury Department of Treasury International Corporation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 40; App. 129. After a year of quashed subpoenas, the District Court set the case for trial, only to conclude on the morning of jury selection that it had to inquire once again into petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s competency. The court dismissed the jury panel, found petitioner incompetent, and committed him to the custody of the Attorney General for hospitalization and treatment. On interlocutory appeal, however, the Court of Appeals vacated that order and remanded for further hearings. In July and August 2000, the District Court held those hearings and received further briefing on the competency issue.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On March 7, 2001, while the competency issue remained under submission, petitioner moved to dismiss the indictment for failure to comply with the Act. The District Court denied the motion on the ground that petitioner had waived his Speedy Trial Act rights \xe2\x80\x9cfor all time,\xe2\x80\x9d mentioning in passing that the case was complex. Id., at 128\xe2\x80\x93129. In the same order, the court found petitioner incompetent. Id., at 135. That latter determination was upheld on interlocutory appeal, and petitioner was committed for evaluation. After several months of hospitalization, petitioner was found to be delusional but competent to stand trial, and he was released.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, on April 7, 2003, more than seven years after petitioner was indicted, his trial began. The jury found petitioner guilty on six counts of attempting to defraud a financial institution,[Footnote 1] and the court sentenced him to 63 months of imprisonment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals affirmed the judgment of conviction.[Footnote 2] Acknowledging that \xe2\x80\x9ca defendant\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of rights under the Speedy Trial Act may be ineffective\xe2\x80\x9d because of the public interest served by compliance with the Act, the Court of Appeals found an exception for situations \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98when defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct causes or contributes to a period of delay.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 401 F. 3d, at 43\xe2\x80\x9344 (quoting United States v. Gambino, 59 F.\xc2\xa03d 353, 360 (CA2 1995)). \xe2\x80\x9c[D]oubt[ing] that the public interest in expeditious prosecution would be served by a rule that allows defendants to request a delay and then protest the grant of their request,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals held that petitioner would not be heard to complain of the 91-day delay in early 1997. 401 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 45. The Court of Appeals went on to suggest that there \xe2\x80\x9ccan be no doubt that the district court could have properly excluded this period of time based on the ends of justice\xe2\x80\x9d in light of the complexity of the case and defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s request for additional time to prepare. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to resolve the disagreement among the Courts of Appeals on the standard for analyzing whether a defendant has made an effective waiver of rights under the Act. 546 U. S. ___ (2006).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As noted above, the Speedy Trial Act generally requires a trial to begin within 70 days of the filing of an information or indictment or the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s initial appearance, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73161(c)(1), but the Act recognizes that criminal cases vary widely and that there are valid reasons for greater delay in particular cases. To provide the necessary flexibility, the Act includes a long and detailed list of periods of delay that are excluded in computing the time within which trial must start. See \xc2\xa73161(h). For example, the Act excludes \xe2\x80\x9cdelay resulting from other proceedings concerning the defendant,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73161(h)(1), \xe2\x80\x9cdelay resulting from the absence or unavailability of the defendant or an essential witness,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73161(h)(3), \xe2\x80\x9cdelay resulting from the fact that the defendant is mentally incompetent or physically unable to stand trial,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73161(h)(4), and \xe2\x80\x9c[a] reasonable period of delay when the defendant is joined for trial with a codefendant as to whom the time for trial has not run and no motion for severance has been granted,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73161(h)(7).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Much of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s flexibility is furnished by \xc2\xa73161(h)(8), which governs ends-of-justice continuances, and which we set out in relevant part in the margin.[Footnote 3] This provision permits a district court to grant a continuance and to exclude the resulting delay if the court, after considering certain factors, makes on-the-record findings that the ends of justice served by granting the continuance outweigh the public\xe2\x80\x99s and defendant\xe2\x80\x99s interests in a speedy trial. This provision gives the district court discretion\xe2\x80\x94within limits and subject to specific procedures\xe2\x80\x94to accommodate limited delays for case-specific needs.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To promote compliance with its requirements, the Act contains enforcement and sanctions provisions. If a trial does not begin on time, the defendant may move, before the start of trial or the entry of a guilty plea, to dismiss the charges, and if a meritorious and timely motion to dismiss is filed, the district court must dismiss the charges, though it may choose whether to dismiss with or without prejudice. In making that choice, the court must take into account, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9cthe seriousness of the offense; the facts and circumstances of the case which led to the dismissal; and the impact of a reprosecution on the administration of [the Act] and on the administration of justice.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73162(a)(2).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This scheme is designed to promote compliance with the Act without needlessly subverting important criminal prosecutions. The more severe sanction (dismissal with prejudice) is available for use where appropriate, and the knowledge that a violation could potentially result in the imposition of this sanction gives the prosecution a powerful incentive to be careful about compliance. The less severe sanction (dismissal without prejudice) lets the court avoid unduly impairing the enforcement of federal criminal laws\xe2\x80\x94though even this sanction imposes some costs on the prosecution and the court, which further encourages compliance. When an indictment is dismissed without prejudice, the prosecutor may of course seek\xe2\x80\x94and in the great majority of cases will be able to obtain\xe2\x80\x94a new indictment, for even if \xe2\x80\x9cthe period prescribed by the applicable statute of limitations has expired, a new indictment may be returned \xe2\x80\xa6 within six calendar months of the date of the dismissal.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73288.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With this background in mind, we turn to the questions presented by the unusual procedures followed in this case.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner contends, and the Government does not seriously dispute, that a defendant may not prospectively waive the application of the Act.[Footnote 4] We agree.

\n

A

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As our discussion above suggests, the Speedy Trial Act comprehensively regulates the time within which a trial must begin. Section 3161(h) specifies in detail numerous categories of delay that are not counted in applying the Act\xe2\x80\x99s deadlines. Conspicuously, \xc2\xa73161(h) has no provision excluding periods of delay during which a defendant waives the application of the Act, and it is apparent from the terms of the Act that this omission was a considered one. Instead of simply allowing defendants to opt out of the Act, the Act demands that defense continuance requests fit within one of the specific exclusions set out in subsection (h). Subsection (h)(8), which permits ends-of-justice continuances, was plainly meant to cover many of these requests. Among the factors that a district court must consider in deciding whether to grant an ends-of-justice continuance are a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s need for \xe2\x80\x9creasonable time to obtain counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuity of counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ceffective preparation\xe2\x80\x9d of counsel. \xc2\xa73161(h)(8)(B)(iv). If a defendant could simply waive the application of the Act whenever he or she wanted more time, no defendant would ever need to put such considerations before the court under the rubric of an ends-of-justice exclusion.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The purposes of the Act also cut against exclusion on the grounds of mere consent or waiver. If the Act were designed solely to protect a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to a speedy trial, it would make sense to allow a defendant to waive the application of the Act. But the Act was designed with the public interest firmly in mind. See, e.g., 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73161(h)(8)(A) (to exclude delay resulting from a continuance\xe2\x80\x94even one \xe2\x80\x9cgranted \xe2\x80\xa6 at the request of the defendant\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the district court must find \xe2\x80\x9cthat the ends of justice served \xe2\x80\xa6 outweigh the best interest of the public and the defendant in a speedy trial\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). That public interest cannot be served, the Act recognizes, if defendants may opt out of the Act entirely.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This interpretation is entirely in accord with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history. As both the 1974 House and Senate Reports illustrate, the Act was designed not just to benefit defendants but also to serve the public interest by, among other things, reducing defendants\xe2\x80\x99 opportunity to commit crimes while on pretrial release and preventing extended pretrial delay from impairing the deterrent effect of punishment. See S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 93\xe2\x80\x931021, pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x938 (citing \xe2\x80\x9cbail problems,\xe2\x80\x9d offenses committed during pretrial release, and the \xe2\x80\x9cseriously undermined \xe2\x80\xa6 deterrent value of the criminal process\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe debilitating effect[s] of court delay upon our criminal justice system\xe2\x80\x9d); H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 93\xe2\x80\x931508, p.\xc2\xa08 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe purpose of this bill is to assist in reducing crime and the danger of recidivism by requiring speedy trials \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). The Senate Report accompanying the 1979 amendments to the Act put an even finer point on it: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Act seeks to protect and promote speedy trial interests that go beyond the rights of the defendant; although the Sixth Amendment recognizes a societal interest in prompt dispositions, it primarily safeguards the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s speedy trial right\xe2\x80\x94which may or may not be in accord with society\xe2\x80\x99s.\xe2\x80\x9d S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 96\xe2\x80\x93212, p.\xc2\xa029; see also id., at 6; H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 96\xe2\x80\x93390, p.\xc2\xa03 (1979). Because defendants may be content to remain on pretrial release, and indeed may welcome delay, it is unsurprising that Congress refrained from empowering defendants to make prospective waivers of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s application. See S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 96\xe2\x80\x93212, at 29 (\xe2\x80\x9cBecause of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on that societal right, a defendant ought not be permitted to waive rights that are not his or hers alone to relinquish\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court reasoned that 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73162(a)(2) supports the conclusion that a defendant may prospectively waive the strictures of the Act. This provision states that \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ailure of the defendant to move for dismissal prior to trial or entry of a plea of guilty or nolo contendere shall constitute a waiver of the right to dismissal under this section.\xe2\x80\x9d Because this provision in effect allows a defendant to waive a completed violation of the Act (by declining to move to dismiss before the start of trial or the entry of a guilty plea), it follows, so the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning went, that a defendant should be allowed to make a prospective waiver. We disagree.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is significant that \xc2\xa73162(a)(2) makes no mention of prospective waivers, and there is no reason to think that Congress wanted to treat prospective and retrospective waivers similarly. Allowing prospective waivers would seriously undermine the Act because there are many cases\xe2\x80\x94like the case at hand\xe2\x80\x94in which the prosecution, the defense, and the court would all be happy to opt out of the Act, to the detriment of the public interest. The sort of retrospective waiver allowed by \xc2\xa73161(a)(2) does not pose a comparable danger because the prosecution and the court cannot know until the trial actually starts or the guilty plea is actually entered whether the defendant will forgo moving to dismiss. As a consequence, the prosecution and the court retain a strong incentive to make sure that the trial begins on time.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead of granting broad opt-out rights, \xc2\xa73162(a)(2) serves two unrelated purposes. First, \xc2\xa73162(a)(2) assigns the role of spotting violations of the Act to defendants\xe2\x80\x94for the obvious reason that they have the greatest incentive to perform this task.[Footnote 5] Second, by requiring that a defendant move before the trial starts or a guilty plea is entered, \xc2\xa73162(a)(2) both limits the effects of a dismissal with-\nout prejudice (by ensuring that an expensive and\ntime-consuming trial will not be mooted by a late-filed motion under the Act) and prevents undue defense gamesmanship.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, we reject the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on \xc2\xa73162(a)(2) and conclude a defendant may not prospectively waive the application of the Act. It follows that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s waiver \xe2\x80\x9cfor all time\xe2\x80\x9d was ineffective. We therefore turn to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s alternative grounds in support of the result below.

\n

IV

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government contends that because \xe2\x80\x9cpetitioner\xe2\x80\x99s express waiver induced the district court to grant a continuance without making an express ends-of-justice finding \xe2\x80\xa6, basic principles of judicial estoppel preclude petitioner from enjoying the benefit of the continuance, but then challenging the lack of a finding.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 10. In this case, however, we see no basis for applying the doctrine of judicial estoppel.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As this Court has explained:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[W]here a party assumes a certain position in a legal proceeding, and succeeds in maintaining that position, he may not thereafter, simply because his interests have changed, assume a contrary position, especially if it be to the prejudice of the party who has acquiesced in the position formerly taken by him.\xe2\x80\x99 Davis v. Wakelee, 156 U. S. 680, 689 (1895). This rule, known as judicial estoppel, \xe2\x80\x98generally prevents a party from prevailing in one phase of a case on an argument and then relying on a contradictory argument to prevail in another phase.\xe2\x80\x99 Pegram v. Herdrich, 530 U. S. 211, 227, n.\xc2\xa08 (2000).\xe2\x80\x9d New Hampshire v. Maine, 532 U. S. 742, 749 (2001).

\n

\nAlthough this estoppel doctrine is equitable and thus cannot be reduced to a precise formula or test,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cseveral factors typically inform the decision whether to apply the doctrine in a particular case: First, a party\xe2\x80\x99s later position must be clearly inconsistent with its earlier position. Second, courts regularly inquire whether the party has succeeded in persuading a court to accept that party\xe2\x80\x99s earlier position \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. A third consideration is whether the party seeking to assert an inconsistent position would derive an unfair advantage or impose an unfair detriment on the opposing party if not estopped.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 750\xe2\x80\x93751 (citations and internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In applying this doctrine to the present case, we must first identify the \xe2\x80\x9cposition\xe2\x80\x9d of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s that the Government seeks to enforce. There are three possibilities: (1) petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s promise not to move for dismissal under \xc2\xa73162(a)(2), (2) petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s (implied) position that waivers of the Act are enforceable, and (3) petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim that counsel needed additional time to research the authenticity of the bonds. None of these gives rise to an estoppel.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, we are unwilling to recognize an estoppel based on petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s promise not to move for dismissal because doing so would entirely swallow the Act\xe2\x80\x99s no-waiver policy. We see little difference between granting a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s request for a continuance in exchange for a promise not to move for dismissal and permitting a prospective waiver, and as we hold above, prospective waivers are inconsistent with the Act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s (mistaken) agreement that Speedy Trial Act waivers are valid also does not provide a ground for estoppel. Petitioner did not \xe2\x80\x9csuccee[d] in persuading\xe2\x80\x9d the District Court to accept the proposition that prospective waivers of Speedy Trial Act rights are valid. On the contrary, it was the District Court that requested the waiver and produced the form for petitioner to sign. And while the other relevant factors (clear inconsistency and unfair advantage or detriment) might in isolation support the Government, we think they do not predominate where, as here, the Government itself accepted the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation without objection.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s representation to the District Court at the January 31 status conference that a continuance was needed to gather evidence of the bonds\xe2\x80\x99 authenticity does not support the Government\xe2\x80\x99s estoppel argument because the position that petitioner took then was not \xe2\x80\x9cclearly inconsistent\xe2\x80\x9d with the position that he now takes in seeking dismissal of the indictment. This would be a different case if petitioner had succeeded in persuading the District Court at the January 31 status conference that the factual predicate for a statutorily authorized exclusion of delay could be established\xe2\x80\x94for example, if defense counsel had obtained a continuance only by falsely representing that he was in the midst of working with an expert who might authenticate the bonds. In fact, however, the discussion at the January 31 status conference did not focus on the requirements of the Act. Rather, the court and the parties proceeded on the assumption that the court\xe2\x80\x99s waiver form was valid and that the Act could simply be disregarded. Nothing in the discussion at the conference suggests that the question presented by the defense continuance request was viewed as anything other than a case-management question that lay entirely within the scope of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion. Under these circumstances, the best understanding of the position taken by petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s attorney at the January 31 status conference is that granting the requested continuance would represent a sound exercise of the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s discretion in managing its calendar. This position was not \xe2\x80\x9cclearly inconsistent\xe2\x80\x9d with petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s later position that the continuance was not permissible under the terms of the Act. Accordingly, we hold that petitioner is not estopped from challenging the excludability under the Act of the 1997 continuance.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While conceding that the District Court \xe2\x80\x9cnever made an express finding on the record\xe2\x80\x9d about the ends-of-justice balance, Brief for United States 30, the Government argues that such an express finding did not need to be entered contemporaneously\xe2\x80\x94and could be supplied on remand\xe2\x80\x94because, given the circumstances in 1997, the ends-of-justice balance in fact supported the 1997 continuance. We reject this argument. In the first place, the Act requires express findings, and in the second place, it does not permit those findings to be made on remand as the Government proposes.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Act requires that when a district court grants an ends-of-justice continuance, it must \xe2\x80\x9cse[t] forth, in the record of the case, either orally or in writing, its reasons\xe2\x80\x9d for finding that the ends of justice are served and they outweigh other interests. 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73161(h)(8)(A). Although the Act is clear that the findings must be made, if only in the judge\xe2\x80\x99s mind, before granting the continuance (the continuance can only be \xe2\x80\x9cgranted \xe2\x80\xa6 on the basis of [the court\xe2\x80\x99s] findings\xe2\x80\x9d), the Act is ambiguous on precisely when those findings must be \xe2\x80\x9cse[t] forth, in the record of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d However this ambiguity is resolved, at the very least the Act implies that those findings must be put on the record by the time a district court rules on a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss under \xc2\xa73162(a)(2).[Footnote 7] In ruling on a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss, the court must tally the unexcluded days. This, in turn, requires identifying the excluded days. But \xc2\xa73161(h)(8)(A) is explicit that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o \xe2\x80\xa6 period of delay resulting from a continuance granted by the court in accordance with this paragraph shall be excludable \xe2\x80\xa6 unless the court sets forth \xe2\x80\xa6 its reasons for [its] finding[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, without on-the-record findings, there can be no exclusion under \xc2\xa73161(h)(8). Here, the District Court set forth no such findings at the January 31 status conference, and \xc2\xa73161(h)(8)(A) is not satisfied by the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s passing reference to the case\xe2\x80\x99s complexity in its ruling on petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss. Therefore, the 1997 continuance is not excluded from the speedy trial clock.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government suggests that this error, stemming as it does from the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s technical failure to make an express finding, may be regarded as harmless. Brief for United States 31, n.\xc2\xa08. Harmless-error review under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 52(a) presumptively applies to \xe2\x80\x9call errors where a proper objection is made,\xe2\x80\x9d Neder v. United States, 527 U. S. 1, 7 (1999), and we have required \xe2\x80\x9cstrong support\xe2\x80\x9d to find an implied repeal of Rule 52, United States v. Vonn, 535 U. S. 55, 65 (2002). We conclude, however, that the provisions of the Act provide such support here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The relevant provisions of the Act are unequivocal. If a defendant pleads not guilty, the trial \xe2\x80\x9cshall commence\xe2\x80\x9d within 70 days \xe2\x80\x9cfrom the filing date (and making public) of the information or indictment\xe2\x80\x9d or from the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s initial appearance, whichever is later. \xc2\xa73161(c)(1) (emphasis added). Delay resulting from an ends-of-justice continuance is excluded from this time period, but \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o such period of delay \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be excludable under this subsection unless the court sets forth, in the record of the case, either orally or in writing, its reasons for finding that the ends of justice served by the granting of such continuance outweigh the best interests of the public and the defendant in a speedy trial.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73161(h)(8)(A) (emphasis added). When a trial is not commenced within the prescribed period of time, \xe2\x80\x9cthe information or indictment shall be dismissed on motion of the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73162(a)(2) (emphasis added). A straightforward reading of these provisions leads to the conclusion that if a judge fails to make the requisite findings regarding the need for an ends-of-justice continuance, the delay resulting from the continuance must be counted, and if as a result the trial does not begin on time, the indictment or information must be dismissed. The argument that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to make the prescribed findings may be excused as harmless error is hard to square with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s categorical terms. See Alabama v. Bozeman, 533 U. S. 146, 153\xe2\x80\x93154 (2001) (no \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98harmless\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98technical\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d violations of the Interstate Agreement on Detainers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cantishuttling\xe2\x80\x9d provision in light of its \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute language\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying the harmless-error rule would also tend to undermine the detailed requirements of the provisions regulating ends-of-justice continuances. The exclusion of delay resulting from an ends-of-justice continuance is the most open-ended type of exclusion recognized under the Act and, in allowing district courts to grant such continuances, Congress clearly meant to give district judges a measure of flexibility in accommodating unusual, complex, and difficult cases. But it is equally clear that Congress, knowing that the many sound grounds for granting ends-of-justice continuances could not be rigidly structured, saw a danger that such continuances could get out of hand and subvert the Act\xe2\x80\x99s detailed scheme. The strategy of \xc2\xa73161(h)(8), then, is to counteract substantive open-endedness with procedural strictness. This provision demands on-the-record findings and specifies in some detail certain factors that a judge must consider in making those findings. Excusing the failure to make these findings as harmless error would be inconsistent with the strategy embodied in \xc2\xa73161(h). Such an approach would almost always lead to a finding of harmless error because the simple failure to make a record of this sort is unlikely to affect the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s rights. We thus conclude that when a district court makes no findings on the record in support of an \xc2\xa73161(h)(8) continuance, harmless-error review is not appropriate.

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that that the 91-day continuance granted on January 31 was not excluded from petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s speedy trial clock. Because this continuance by itself exceeded the maximum 70-day delay provided in \xc2\xa73161(c)(1), the Act was violated, and we need not address whether any other periods of delay during petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s case were not excludable. The sanction for a violation of the Act is dismissal, but we leave it to the District Court to determine in the first instance whether dismissal should be with or without prejudice. See \xc2\xa73162(a)(2). The judgment of the Court of Appeals is therefore reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I concur in the opinion of the Court with the exception of its discussion of legislative history in Part III\xe2\x80\x93A\xe2\x80\x932. For reasons I have expressed elsewhere, I believe that the only language that constitutes \xe2\x80\x9ca Law\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the Bicameralism and Presentment Clause of Article I, \xc2\xa77, and hence the only language adopted in a fashion that entitles it to our attention, is the text of the enacted statute. See, e.g., Conroy v. Aniskoff, 507 U. S. 511, 518\xe2\x80\x93528 (1993) (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment). Here, the Court looks to legislative history even though the remainder of its opinion amply establishes that the Speedy Trial Act is unambiguous. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s language rejects the possibility of a prospective waiver, and even expresses the very point that the Court relies on legislative history to support\xe2\x80\x94that the Act protects the interests of the public as well as those of the defendant. See ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (citing 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73161(h)(8)(A)). Use of legislative history in this context thus conflicts not just with my own views but with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s repeated statements that when the language of the statute is plain, legislative history is irrelevant. See, e.g., United States v. Gonzales, 520 U. S. 1, 6 (1997). \xe2\x80\x9cWe have stated time and again that courts must presume that a legislature says in a statute what it means and means in a statute what it says there. When the words of a statute are unambiguous, then, this first canon is also the last: the judicial inquiry is complete.\xe2\x80\x9d Connecticut Nat. Bank v. Germain, 503 U. S. 249, 253\xe2\x80\x93254 (1992) (citations and internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It may seem that there is no harm in using committee reports and other such sources when they are merely in accord with the plain meaning of the Act. But this sort of intellectual piling-on has addictive consequences. To begin with, it accustoms us to believing that what is said by a single person in a floor debate or by a committee report represents the view of Congress as a whole\xe2\x80\x94so that we sometimes even will say (when referring to a floor statement and committee report) that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has expressed\xe2\x80\x9d thus-and-so. See, e.g., Conroy, supra, at 516\xe2\x80\x93517. There is no basis either in law or in reality for this naive belief. Moreover, if legislative history is relevant when it confirms the plain meaning of the statutory text, it should also be relevant when it contradicts the plain meaning, thus rendering what is plain ambiguous. Because the use of legislative history is illegitimate and ill advised in the interpretation of any statute\xe2\x80\x94and especially a statute that is clear on its face\xe2\x80\x94I do not join this portion of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.
