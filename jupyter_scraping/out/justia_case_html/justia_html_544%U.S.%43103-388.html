\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

BATES et al. v. DOW AGROSCIENCES LLC

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 03\xe2\x80\x93388.Argued January 10, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 27, 2005

\n

Petitioner Texas peanut farmers allege that their crops were severely damaged by the application of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s (Dow) \xe2\x80\x9cStrongarm\xe2\x80\x9d pesticide, which the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) registered pursuant to its authority under the Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA). Petitioners gave Dow notice of their intent to sue, claiming that Strongarm\xe2\x80\x99s label recommended its use in all peanut-growing areas when Dow knew or should have known that it would stunt the growth of peanuts in their soil, which had pH levels of at least 7.0. In response, Dow sought a declaratory judgment in the Federal District Court, asserting that FIFRA pre-empted petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims. Petitioners counterclaimed, raising several state-law claims sounding in strict liability, negligence, fraud, and breach of express warranty. The District Court rejected one claim on state-law grounds and found the others barred by FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision, 7 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7136v(b). Affirming, the Fifth Circuit held that \xc2\xa7136v(b) expressly pre-empted the state-law claims because a judgment against Dow would induce it to alter its product label.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Under FIFRA, which was comprehensively amended in 1972, a manufacturer must obtain permission to market a pesticide by submitting a proposed label and supporting data to EPA, which will register the pesticide if it is efficacious, it will not cause unreasonable adverse effects on humans and the environment, and its label complies with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s misbranding prohibition. A pesticide is \xe2\x80\x9cmisbranded\xe2\x80\x9d if its label, for example, contains a statement that is \xe2\x80\x9cfalse or misleading,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7136(q)(1)(A), or lacks adequate instructions or warnings, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7136(q)(1)(F), (G). A State may regulate the sale and use of federally registered pesticides to the extent that regulation does not permit any sales or uses prohibited by FIFRA, \xc2\xa7136v(a), but \xe2\x80\x9c[s]uch State shall not impose or continue in effect any requirements for labeling or packaging in addition to or different from those required under [FIFRA],\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7136v(b). Though tort litigation against pesticide manufacturers was a common feature of the legal landscape in 1972, after this Court held in Cipollone v. Liggett Group, Inc., 505 U. S. 504, that the term \xe2\x80\x9crequirement\xe2\x80\x9d in the Public Health Cigarette Smoking Act of 1969 included common-law duties, and therefore pre-empted certain tort claims against cigarette companies, courts began holding that \xc2\xa7136v(b) pre-empted claims such as petitioners\xe2\x80\x99. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision applies only to state-law \xe2\x80\x9crequirements for labeling or packaging.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7136v(b). While the Fifth Circuit was correct that \xe2\x80\x9crequirements\xe2\x80\x9d embraces both positive enactments and common-law duties, it erred in supposing that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 defective design, defective manufacture, negligent testing, and breach of express warranty claims were premised on requirements for labeling or packaging. None of the common-law rules upon which these claims are based requires that manufacturers label or package their products in any particular way. The Fifth Circuit reached a contrary conclusion by reasoning that a finding of liability on these claims would induce Dow to alter its label. This was error because the prohibitions of \xc2\xa7136v(b) apply only to \xe2\x80\x9crequirements.\xe2\x80\x9d A requirement is a rule of law that must be obeyed; an event, such as a jury verdict, that merely motives an optional decision is not a requirement. The proper inquiry calls for an examination of the elements of the common-law duty at issue, not for speculation as to whether a jury verdict will prompt the manufacturer to change its label. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 fraud and negligent-failure-to-warn claims, by contrast, are based on common-law rules that qualify as \xe2\x80\x9crequirements for labeling or packaging,\xe2\x80\x9d since these rules set a standard for a product\xe2\x80\x99s labeling that Dow is alleged to have violated. While these common-law rules are subject to \xc2\xa7136v(b), it does not automatically follow that they are pre-empted. Unlike the pre-emption clause in Cipollone, \xc2\xa7136v(b) prohibits only state-law labeling requirements that are \xe2\x80\x9cin addition to or different from\xe2\x80\x9d FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s labeling requirements. Thus, \xc2\xa7136v(b) pre-empts any statutory or common-law rule that would impose a labeling requirement that diverges from those set out in FIFRA and its implementing regulations. It does not pre-empt a state-law requirement that is equivalent to, and fully consistent with, FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s labeling standards. This \xe2\x80\x9cparallel requirements\xe2\x80\x9d reading of \xc2\xa7136v(b) finds strong support in Medtronic, Inc. v. Lohr, 518 U. S. 470. Thus, although FIFRA does not provide a federal remedy to those injured as a result of a manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s violation of FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s labeling requirements, nothing in \xc2\xa7136v(b) precludes States from providing such a remedy. Dow\xe2\x80\x99s contrary reading of \xc2\xa7136v(b) fails to make sense of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin addition to or different from.\xe2\x80\x9d Even if Dow offered a plausible alternative reading of \xc2\xa7136v(b), this Court would have a duty to accept the reading disfavoring pre-emption. See New York State Conference of Blue Cross & Blue Shield Plans v. Travelers Ins. Co., 514 U. S. 645, 655. The long history of tort litigation against manufacturers of poisonous substances adds force to the presumption against pre-emption, for Congress surely would have expressed its intention more clearly if it had meant to deprive injured parties of a long available form of compensation. Moreover, this history emphasizes the importance of providing an incentive to manufacturers to use the utmost care in distributing inherently dangerous items. Finally, the policy objections raised against this Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa7136v(b) are unpersuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa04.\xc2\xa0Under the \xe2\x80\x9cparallel requirements\xe2\x80\x9d reading of \xc2\xa7136v(b), a state-law labeling requirement must be equivalent to its federal counterpart to avoid pre-emption. State law need not, however, explicitly incorporate FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s standards as an element of a cause of action. Because this Court has not received sufficient briefing on whether the Texas law governing petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 fraud and failure-to-warn claims is equivalent to FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s misbranding standards and any relevant regulations, it is up to the Fifth Circuit to resolve the issue in the first instance. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

DENNIS BATES, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. DOW\nAGROSCIENCES LLC

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[April 27, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners are 29 Texas peanut farmers who allege that in the 2000 growing season their crops were severely damaged by the application of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s newly marketed pesticide named \xe2\x80\x9cStrongarm.\xe2\x80\x9d The question presented is whether the Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA), 7 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7136 et seq. (2000 ed. and Supp. II), pre-empts their state-law claims for damages.

\n

I

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pursuant to its authority under FIFRA, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) conditionally registered Strongarm on March 8, 2000, thereby granting respondent (Dow) permission to sell this pesticide\xe2\x80\x94a weed killer[Footnote 1]\xe2\x80\x94in the United States. Dow obtained this registration in time to market Strongarm to Texas farmers, who normally plant their peanut crops around May 1. According to petitioners\xe2\x80\x94whose version of the facts we assume to be true at this stage\xe2\x80\x94Dow knew, or should have known, that Strongarm would stunt the growth of peanuts in soils with pH levels of 7.0 or greater.[Footnote 2] Nevertheless, Strongarm\xe2\x80\x99s label stated, \xe2\x80\x9cUse of Strongarm is recommended in all areas where peanuts are grown,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 108, and Dow\xe2\x80\x99s agents made equivalent representations in their sales pitches to petitioners. When petitioners applied Strongarm on their farms\xe2\x80\x94whose soils have pH levels of 7.2 or higher, as is typical in western Texas\xe2\x80\x94the pesticide severely damaged their peanut crops while failing to control the growth of weeds. The farmers reported these problems to Dow, which sent its experts to inspect the crops.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Meanwhile, Dow reregistered its Strongarm label with EPA prior to the 2001 growing season. EPA approved a \xe2\x80\x9csupplemental\xe2\x80\x9d label that was for \xe2\x80\x9c[d]istribution and [u]se [o]nly in the states of New Mexico, Oklahoma and Texas,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 179, the three States in which peanut farmers experienced crop damage. This new label contained the following warning: \xe2\x80\x9cDo not apply Strongarm to soils with a pH of 7.2 or greater.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 181.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After unsuccessful negotiations with Dow, petitioners gave Dow notice of their intent to bring suit as required by the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices-Consumer Protection Act[Footnote 3] (hereinafter Texas DTPA). In response, Dow filed a declaratory judgment action in Federal District Court, asserting that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims were expressly or impliedly pre-empted by FIFRA. Petitioners, in turn, brought counterclaims, including tort claims sounding in strict liability and negligence. They also alleged fraud, breach of warranty, and violation of the Texas DTPA. The District Court granted Dow\xe2\x80\x99s motion for summary judgment, rejecting one claim on state-law grounds and dismissing the remainder as expressly pre-empted by 7 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7136v(b), which provides that States \xe2\x80\x9cshall not impose or continue in effect any requirements for labeling or packaging in addition to or different from those required under this subchapter.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals affirmed. It read \xc2\xa7136v(b) to pre-empt any state-law claim in which \xe2\x80\x9ca judgment against Dow would induce it to alter its product label.\xe2\x80\x9d 332 F.\xc2\xa03d 323, 331 (CA5 2003). The court held that because petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 fraud, warranty, and deceptive trade practices claims focused on oral statements by Dow\xe2\x80\x99s agents that did not differ from statements made on the product\xe2\x80\x99s label, success on those claims would give Dow a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong incentive\xe2\x80\x9d to change its label. Those claims were thus pre-empted. Id., at 331\xe2\x80\x93332. The court also found that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 strict liability claim alleging defective design was essentially a \xe2\x80\x9cdisguised\xe2\x80\x9d failure-to-warn claim and therefore pre-empted. Id., at 332. It reasoned: \xe2\x80\x9cOne cannot escape the heart of the farmers\xe2\x80\x99 grievance: Strongarm is dangerous to peanut crops in soil with a pH level over 7.0, and that was not disclosed to them\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. It is inescapable that success on this claim would again necessarily induce Dow to alter the Strongarm label.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 332\xe2\x80\x93333. The court employed similar reasoning to find the negligent testing and negligent manufacture claims pre-empted as well. Id., at 333.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This decision was consistent with those of a majority of the Courts of Appeals,[Footnote 4] as well of several state high courts,[Footnote 5] but conflicted with the decisions of other courts[Footnote 6] and with the views of the EPA set forth in an amicus curiae brief filed with the California Supreme Court in 2000.[Footnote 7] We granted certiorari to resolve this conflict. 542 U. S. ___ (2004).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Prior to 1910 the States provided the primary and possibly the exclusive source of regulatory control over the distribution of poisonous substances. Both the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s first effort at regulation in this area, the Insecticide Act of 1910, 36 Stat. 331, and FIFRA as originally enacted in 1947, ch. 125, 61 Stat. 163, primarily dealt with licensing and labeling. Under the original version of FIFRA, all pesticides sold in interstate commerce had to be registered with the Secretary of Agriculture. The Secretary would register a pesticide if it complied with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s labeling standards and was determined to be efficacious and safe.[Footnote 8] In 1970, EPA assumed responsibility for this registration process.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1972, spurred by growing environmental and safety concerns, Congress adopted the extensive amendments[Footnote 9] that \xe2\x80\x9ctransformed FIFRA from a labeling law into a comprehensive regulatory statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Ruckelshaus v. Monsanto Co., 467 U. S. 986, 991 (1984). \xe2\x80\x9cAs amended, FIFRA regulated the use, as well as the sale and labeling, of pesticides; regulated pesticides produced and sold in both intrastate and interstate commerce; provided for review, cancellation, and suspension of registration; and gave EPA greater enforcement authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 991\xe2\x80\x93992. The 1972 amendments also imposed a new criterion for registration\xe2\x80\x94environmental safety. Id., at 992. See generally 4 F. Grad, Treatise on Environmental Law \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa78.02\xe2\x80\x938.03 (2004) (tracing FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s statutory evolution).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under FIFRA as it currently stands, a manufacturer seeking to register a pesticide must submit a proposed label to EPA as well as certain supporting data. 7 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7136a(c)(1)(C), (F). The agency will register the pesticide if it determines that the pesticide is efficacious (with the caveat discussed below), \xc2\xa7136a(c)(5)(A); that it will not cause unreasonable adverse effects on humans and the environment, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7136a(c)(5)(C), (D); \xc2\xa7136(bb); and that its label complies with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on misbranding, \xc2\xa7136a(c)(5)(B); 40 CFR \xc2\xa7152.112(f) (2004). A pesticide is \xe2\x80\x9cmisbranded\xe2\x80\x9d if its label contains a statement that is \xe2\x80\x9cfalse or misleading in any particular,\xe2\x80\x9d including a false or misleading statement concerning the efficacy of the pesticide. \xc2\xa7136(q)(1)(A); 40 CFR \xc2\xa7156.10(a)(5)(ii). A pesticide is also misbranded if its label does not contain adequate instructions for use, or if its label omits necessary warnings or cautionary statements. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7136(q)(1)(F), (G).[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because it is unlawful under the statute to sell a pesticide that is registered but nevertheless misbranded, manufacturers have a continuing obligation to adhere to FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s labeling requirements. \xc2\xa7136j(a)(1)(E); see also \xc2\xa7136a(f)(2) (registration is prima facie evidence that the pesticide and its labeling comply with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s requirements, but registration does not provide a defense to the violation of the statute); \xc2\xa7136a(f)(1) (a manufacturer may seek approval to amend its label). Additionally, manufacturers have a duty to report incidents involving a pesticide\xe2\x80\x99s toxic effects that may not be adequately reflected in its label\xe2\x80\x99s warnings, 40 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7159.184(a), (b) (2004), and EPA may institute cancellation proceedings, 7 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7136d(b), and take other enforcement action if it determines that a registered pesticide is misbranded.[Footnote 11]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 136v, which was added in the 1972 amendments, addresses the States\xe2\x80\x99 continuing role in pesticide regulation. As currently codified, \xc2\xa7136v provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(a) In general

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cA State may regulate the sale or use of any federally registered pesticide or device in the State, but only if and to the extent the regulation does not permit any sale or use prohibited by this subchapter.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c(b) Uniformity

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cSuch State shall not impose or continue in effect any requirements for labeling or packaging in addition to or different from those required under this subchapter.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c(c) Additional uses

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(1) A State may provide registration for additional uses of federally registered pesticides formulated for distribution and use within that State to meet special local needs in accord with the purposes of this subchapter and if registration for such use has not previously been denied, disapproved, or canceled by the Administrator. Such registration shall be deemed registration under section 136a of this title for all purposes of this subchapter, but shall authorize distribution and use only within such State\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1978, Congress once again amended FIFRA, 92 Stat. 819, this time in response to EPA\xe2\x80\x99s concern that its evaluation of pesticide efficacy during the registration process diverted too many resources from its task of assessing the environmental and health dangers posed by pesticides. Congress addressed this problem by authorizing EPA to waive data requirements pertaining to efficacy, thus permitting the agency to register a pesticide without confirming the efficacy claims made on its label. \xc2\xa7136a(c)(5). In 1979, EPA invoked this grant of permission and issued a general waiver of efficacy review, with only limited qualifications not applicable here. See 44 Fed. Reg. 27932 (1979); 40 CFR \xc2\xa7158.640(b) (2004). In a notice published years later in 1996, EPA confirmed that it had \xe2\x80\x9cstopped evaluating pesticide efficacy for routine label approvals almost two decades ago,\xe2\x80\x9d Pesticide Registration Notice 96\xe2\x80\x934, p.\xc2\xa03 (June 3, 1996), available at www.epa.gov/opppmsd1/PR_Notices/pr96-4.html, App. 232, and clarified that \xe2\x80\x9cEPA\xe2\x80\x99s approval of a pesticide label does not reflect any determination on the part of EPA that the pesticide will be efficacious or will not damage crops or cause other property damage.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 5, App. 235. The notice also referred to an earlier statement in which EPA observed that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98pesticide producers are aware that they are potentially subject to damage suits by the user community if their products prove ineffective in actual use.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 5, App. 230 (quoting 47 Fed. Reg. 40661 (col. 2) (1982)). This general waiver was in place at the time of Strongarm\xe2\x80\x99s registration; thus, the EPA never passed on the accuracy of the statement in Strongarm\xe2\x80\x99s original label recommending the product\xe2\x80\x99s use \xe2\x80\x9cin all areas where peanuts are grown.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the modern version of FIFRA was enacted over three decades ago, this Court has never addressed whether that statute pre-empts tort and other common-law claims arising under state law. Courts entertained tort litigation against pesticide manufacturers since well before the passage of FIFRA in 1947,[Footnote 12] and such litigation was a common feature of the legal landscape at the time of the 1972 amendments.[Footnote 13] Indeed, for at least a decade after those amendments, arguments that such tort suits were pre-empted by \xc2\xa7136v(b) either were not advanced or were unsuccessful. See, e.g., Ferebee v. Chevron Chemical Co., 736 F.\xc2\xa02d 1529 (CADC 1984). It was only after 1992 when we held in Cipollone v. Liggett Group, Inc., 505 U. S. 504, that the term \xe2\x80\x9crequirement or prohibition\xe2\x80\x9d in the Public Health Cigarette Smoking Act of 1969 included common-law duties, and therefore pre-empted certain tort claims against cigarette companies, that a groundswell of federal and state decisions emerged holding that \xc2\xa7136v(b) pre-empted claims like those advanced in this litigation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has addressed FIFRA pre-emption in a different context. In Wisconsin Public Intervenor v. Mortier, 501 U. S. 597 (1991), we considered a claim that \xc2\xa7136v(b) pre-empted a small town\xe2\x80\x99s ordinance requiring a special permit for the aerial application of pesticides. Although the ordinance imposed restrictions not required by FIFRA or any EPA regulation, we unanimously rejected the pre-emption claim. In our opinion we noted that FIFRA was not \xe2\x80\x9ca sufficiently comprehensive statute to justify an inference that Congress had occupied the field to the exclusion of the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 607. \xe2\x80\x9cTo the contrary, the statute leaves ample room for States and localities to supplement federal efforts even absent the express regulatory authorization of \xc2\xa7136v(a).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 613.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a part of their supplementary role, States have ample authority to review pesticide labels to ensure that they comply with both federal and state labeling requirements.[Footnote 14] Nothing in the text of FIFRA would prevent a State from making the violation of a federal labeling or packaging requirement a state offense, thereby imposing its own sanctions on pesticide manufacturers who violate federal law. The imposition of state sanctions for violating state rules that merely duplicate federal requirements is equally consistent with the text of \xc2\xa7136v.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Against this background, we consider whether petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims[Footnote 15] are pre-empted by \xc2\xa7136v(b), which, again, reads as follows: \xe2\x80\x9cSuch State shall not impose or continue in effect any requirements for labeling or packaging in addition to or different from those required under this subchapter.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The introductory words of \xc2\xa7136v(b)\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cSuch State\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94appear to limit the coverage of that subsection to the States that are described in the preceding subsection (a). Texas is such a State because it regulates the sale and use of federally registered pesticides and does not permit any sales or uses prohibited by FIFRA. It is therefore beyond dispute that subsection (b) is applicable to this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The prohibitions in \xc2\xa7136v(b) apply only to \xe2\x80\x9crequirements.\xe2\x80\x9d An occurrence that merely motivates an optional decision does not qualify as a requirement. The Court of Appeals was therefore quite wrong when it assumed that any event, such as a jury verdict, that might \xe2\x80\x9cinduce\xe2\x80\x9d a pesticide manufacturer to change its label should be viewed as a requirement. The Court of Appeals did, however, correctly hold that the term \xe2\x80\x9crequirements\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7136v(b) reaches beyond positive enactments, such as statutes and regulations, to embrace common-law duties. Our decision in Cipollone supports this conclusion. See 505 U. S., at 521 (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe phrase \xe2\x80\x98[n]o requirement or prohibition\xe2\x80\x99 sweeps broadly and suggests no distinction between positive enactments and common law; to the contrary, those words easily encompass obligations that take the form of common-law rules\xe2\x80\x9d); see also id., at 548\xe2\x80\x93549 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part). While the use of \xe2\x80\x9crequirements\xe2\x80\x9d in a pre-emption clause may not invariably carry this meaning, we think this is the best reading of \xc2\xa7136v(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That \xc2\xa7136v(b) may pre-empt judge-made rules, as well as statutes and regulations, says nothing about the scope of that pre-emption. For a particular state rule to be pre-empted, it must satisfy two conditions. First, it must be a requirement \xe2\x80\x9cfor labeling or packaging\xe2\x80\x9d; rules governing the design of a product, for example, are not pre-empted. Second, it must impose a labeling or packaging requirement that is \xe2\x80\x9cin addition to or different from those required under this subchapter.\xe2\x80\x9d A state regulation requiring the word \xe2\x80\x9cpoison\xe2\x80\x9d to appear in red letters, for instance, would not be pre-empted if an EPA regulation imposed the same requirement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is perfectly clear that many of the common-law rules upon which petitioners rely do not satisfy the first condition. Rules that require manufacturers to design reasonably safe products, to use due care in conducting appropriate testing of their products, to market products free of manufacturing defects, and to honor their express warranties or other contractual commitments plainly do not qualify as requirements for \xe2\x80\x9clabeling or packaging.\xe2\x80\x9d None of these common-law rules requires that manufacturers label or package their products in any particular way. Thus, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims for defective design, defective manufacture, negligent testing, and breach of express warranty are not pre-empted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, Dow\xe2\x80\x99s express warranty was located on Strongarm\xe2\x80\x99s label.[Footnote 16] But a cause of action on an express warranty asks only that a manufacturer make good on the contractual commitment that it voluntarily undertook by placing that warranty on its product.[Footnote 17] Because this common-law rule does not require the manufacturer to make an express warranty, or in the event that the manufacturer elects to do so, to say anything in particular in that warranty, the rule does not impose a requirement \xe2\x80\x9cfor labeling or packaging.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 525\xe2\x80\x93526 (plurality opinion).[Footnote 18]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In arriving at a different conclusion, the court below reasoned that a finding of liability on these claims would \xe2\x80\x9cinduce Dow to alter [its] label.\xe2\x80\x9d 332 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 332.[Footnote 19] This effects-based test finds no support in the text of \xc2\xa7136v(b), which speaks only of \xe2\x80\x9crequirements.\xe2\x80\x9d A requirement is a rule of law that must be obeyed; an event, such as a jury verdict, that merely motivates an optional decision is not a requirement. The proper inquiry calls for an examination of the elements of the common-law duty at issue, see Cipollone, 505 U. S., at 524; it does not call for speculation as to whether a jury verdict will prompt the manufacturer to take any particular action (a question, in any event, that will depend on a variety of cost/benefit calculations best left to the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s accountants).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The inducement test is unquestionably overbroad because it would impeach many \xe2\x80\x9cgenuine\xe2\x80\x9d design defect claims that Dow concedes are not pre-empted. A design defect claim, if successful, would surely induce a manufacturer to alter its label to reflect a change in the list of ingredients or a change in the instructions for use necessitated by the improvement in the product\xe2\x80\x99s design. Moreover, the inducement test is not entirely consistent with \xc2\xa7136v(a), which confirms the State\xe2\x80\x99s broad authority to regulate the sale and use of pesticides.[Footnote 20] Under \xc2\xa7136v(a), a state agency may ban the sale of a pesticide if it finds, for instance, that one of the pesticide\xe2\x80\x99s label-approved uses is unsafe. This ban might well induce the manufacturer to change its label to warn against this questioned use. Under the inducement test, however, such a restriction would anomalously qualify as a \xe2\x80\x9clabeling\xe2\x80\x9d requirement. It is highly unlikely that Congress endeavored to draw a line between the type of indirect pressure caused by a State\xe2\x80\x99s power to impose sales and use restrictions and the even more attenuated pressure exerted by common-law suits. The inducement test is not supported by either the text or the structure of the statute.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike their other claims, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 fraud and negligent-failure-to-warn claims are premised on common-law rules that qualify as \xe2\x80\x9crequirements for labeling or packaging.\xe2\x80\x9d These rules set a standard for a product\xe2\x80\x99s labeling that the Strongarm label is alleged to have violated by containing false statements and inadequate warnings. While the courts of appeal have rightly found guidance in Cipollone\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9crequirements,\xe2\x80\x9d some of those courts too quickly concluded that failure-to-warn claims were pre-empted under FIFRA, as they were in Cipollone, without paying attention to the rather obvious textual differences between the two pre-emption clauses.[Footnote 21]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike the pre-emption clause at issue in Cipollone,[Footnote 22] \xc2\xa7136v(b) prohibits only state-law labeling and packaging requirements that are \xe2\x80\x9cin addition to or different from\xe2\x80\x9d the labeling and packaging requirements under FIFRA. Thus, a state-law labeling requirement is not pre-empted by \xc2\xa7136v(b) if it is equivalent to, and fully consistent with, FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s misbranding provisions. Petitioners argue that their claims based on fraud and failure-to-warn are not pre-empted because these common-law duties are equivalent to FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s requirements that a pesticide label not contain \xe2\x80\x9cfalse or misleading\xe2\x80\x9d statements, \xc2\xa7136(q)(1)(A), or inadequate instructions or warnings. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7136(q)(1)(F), (G). We agree with petitioners insofar as we hold that state law need not explicitly incorporate FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s standards as an element of a cause of action in order to survive pre-emption. As we will discuss below, however, we leave it to the Court of Appeals to decide in the first instance whether these particular common-law duties are equivalent to FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s misbranding standards.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cparallel requirements\xe2\x80\x9d reading of \xc2\xa7136v(b) that we adopt today finds strong support in Medtronic, Inc. v. Lohr, 518 U. S. 470 (1996). In addressing a similarly worded pre-emption provision in a statute regulating medical devices, we found that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in [21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.] \xc2\xa7360k denies Florida the right to provide a traditional damages remedy for violations of common-law duties when those duties parallel federal requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 495.[Footnote 23] As Justice O\xe2\x80\x99Connor explained in her separate opinion, a state cause of action that seeks to enforce a federal requirement \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not impose a requirement that is \xe2\x80\x98different from, or in addition to,\xe2\x80\x99 requirements under federal law. To be sure, the threat of a damages remedy will give manufacturers an additional cause to comply, but the requirements imposed on them under state and federal law do not differ. Section 360k does not preclude States from imposing different or additional remedies, but only different or additional requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 513 (opinion concurring in part and dissenting in part). Accordingly, although FIFRA does not provide a federal remedy to farmers and others who are injured as a result of a manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s violation of FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s labeling requirements, nothing in \xc2\xa7136v(b) precludes States from providing such a remedy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dow, joined by the United States as amicus curiae, argues that the \xe2\x80\x9cparallel requirements\xe2\x80\x9d reading of \xc2\xa7136v(b) would \xe2\x80\x9cgive juries in 50 States the authority to give content to FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s misbranding prohibition, establishing a crazy-quilt of anti-misbranding requirements different from the one defined by FIFRA itself and intended by Congress to be interpreted authoritatively by EPA.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 16; see also Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 25\xe2\x80\x9327. In our view, however, the clear text of \xc2\xa7136v(b) and the authority of Medtronic cannot be so easily avoided. Conspicuously absent from the submissions by Dow and the United States is any plausible alternative interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cin addition to or different from\xe2\x80\x9d that would give that phrase meaning. Instead, they appear to favor reading those words out of the statute, which would leave the following: \xe2\x80\x9cSuch State shall not impose or continue in effect any requirements for labeling or packaging.\xe2\x80\x9d This amputated version of \xc2\xa7136v(b) would no doubt have clearly and succinctly commanded the pre-emption of all state requirements concerning labeling. That Congress added the remainder of the provision is evidence of its intent to draw a distinction between state labeling requirements that are pre-empted and those that are not.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if Dow had offered us a plausible alternative reading of \xc2\xa7136v(b)\xe2\x80\x94indeed, even if its alternative were just as plausible as our reading of that text\xe2\x80\x94we would nevertheless have a duty to accept the reading that disfavors pre-emption. \xe2\x80\x9c[B]ecause the States are independent sovereigns in our federal system, we have long presumed that Congress does not cavalierly pre-empt state-law causes of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Medtronic, 518 U. S., at 485. In areas of traditional state regulation, we assume that a federal statute has not supplanted state law unless Congress has made such an intention \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98clear and manifest.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d New York State Conference of Blue Cross & Blue Shield Plans v. Travelers Ins. Co., 514 U. S. 645, 655 (1995) (quoting Rice v. Santa\xc2\xa0Fe Elevator Corp., 331 U. S. 218, 230 (1947)); see also Medtronic, 518 U. S., at 485. Our reading is at once the only one that makes sense of each phrase in \xc2\xa7136v(b) and the one favored by our canons of interpretation. The notion that FIFRA contains a nonambiguous command to pre-empt the types of tort claims that parallel FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s misbranding requirements is particularly dubious given that just five years ago the United States advocated the interpretation that we adopt today.[Footnote 24]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The long history of tort litigation against manufacturers of poisonous substances adds force to the basic presumption against pre-emption. If Congress had intended to deprive injured parties of a long available form of compensation, it surely would have expressed that intent more clearly. See Silkwood v. Kerr-McGee Corp., 464 U. S. 238, 251 (1984).[Footnote 25] Moreover, this history emphasizes the importance of providing an incentive to manufacturers to use the utmost care in the business of distributing inherently dangerous items. See Mortier, 501 U. S., at 613 (stating that the 1972 amendments\xe2\x80\x99 goal was to \xe2\x80\x9cstrengthen existing labeling requirements and ensure that these requirements were followed in practice\xe2\x80\x9d). Particularly given that Congress amended FIFRA to allow EPA to waive efficacy review of newly registered pesticides (and in the course of those amendments made technical changes to \xc2\xa7136v(b)), it seems unlikely that Congress considered a relatively obscure provision like \xc2\xa7136v(b) to give pesticide manufacturers virtual immunity from certain forms of tort liability. Overenforcement of FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s misbranding prohibition creates a risk of imposing unnecessary financial burdens on manufacturers; under-enforcement creates not only financial risks for consumers, but risks that affect their safety and the environment as well.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, we find the policy objections raised against our reading of \xc2\xa7136v(b) to be unpersuasive. Dow and the United States greatly overstate the degree of uniformity and centralization that characterizes FIFRA. In fact, the statute authorizes a relatively decentralized scheme that preserves a broad role for state regulation. See id., at 613. Most significantly, States may ban or restrict the uses of pesticides that EPA has approved, \xc2\xa7136v(a); they may also register, subject to certain restrictions, pesticides for uses beyond those approved by EPA, \xc2\xa7136v(c). See also \xc2\xa7136w\xe2\x80\x931 (authorizing EPA to grant States primary enforcement responsibility for use violations). A literal reading of \xc2\xa7136v(b) is fully consistent with the concurrent authority of the Federal and State Governments in this sphere.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Private remedies that enforce federal misbranding requirements would seem to aid, rather than hinder, the functioning of FIFRA. Unlike the cigarette labeling law at issue in Cipollone, which prescribed certain immutable warning statements, FIFRA contemplates that pesticide labels will evolve over time, as manufacturers gain more information about their products\xe2\x80\x99 performance in diverse settings. As one court explained, tort suits can serve as a catalyst in this process:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cBy encouraging plaintiffs to bring suit for injuries not previously recognized as traceable to pesticides such as [the pesticide there at issue], a state tort action of the kind under review may aid in the exposure of new dangers associated with pesticides. Successful actions of this sort may lead manufacturers to petition EPA to allow more detailed labelling of their products; alternatively, EPA itself may decide that revised labels are required in light of the new information that has been brought to its attention through common law suits. In addition, the specter of damage actions may provide manufacturers with added dynamic incentives to continue to keep abreast of all possible injuries stemming from use of their product so as to forestall such actions through product improvement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ferebee, 736 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1541\xe2\x80\x931542.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dow and the United States exaggerate the disruptive effects of using common-law suits to enforce the prohibition on misbranding. FIFRA has prohibited inaccurate representations and inadequate warnings since its enactment in 1947, while tort suits alleging failure-to-warn claims were common well before that date and continued beyond the 1972 amendments. We have been pointed to no evidence that such tort suits led to a \xe2\x80\x9ccrazy-quilt\xe2\x80\x9d of FIFRA standards or otherwise created any real hardship for manufacturers or for EPA. Indeed, for much of this period EPA appears to have welcomed these tort suits. While it is true that properly instructed juries might on occasion reach contrary conclusions on a similar issue of misbranding, there is no reason to think such occurrences would be frequent or that they would result in difficulties beyond those regularly experienced by manufacturers of other products that everyday bear the risk of conflicting jury verdicts. Moreover, it bears noting that lay juries are in no sense anathema to FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s scheme: In criminal prosecutions for violation of FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s provisions, see \xc2\xa7136l(b), juries necessarily pass on allegations of misbranding.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, under our interpretation, \xc2\xa7136v(b) retains a narrow, but still important, role. In the main, it pre-empts competing state labeling standards\xe2\x80\x94imagine 50 different labeling regimes prescribing the color, font size, and wording of warnings\xe2\x80\x94that would create significant inefficiencies for manufacturers.[Footnote 26] The provision also pre-empts any statutory or common-law rule that would impose a labeling requirement that diverges from those set out in FIFRA and its implementing regulations. It does not, however, pre-empt any state rules that are fully consistent with federal requirements.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Having settled on our interpretation of \xc2\xa7136v(b), it still remains to be decided whether that provision pre-empts petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 fraud and failure-to-warn claims. Because we have not received sufficient briefing on this issue,[Footnote 27] which involves questions of Texas law, we remand it to the Court of Appeals. We emphasize that a state-law labeling requirement must in fact be equivalent to a requirement under FIFRA in order to survive pre-emption. For example, were the Court of Appeals to determine that the element of falsity in Texas\xe2\x80\x99 common-law definition of fraud imposed a broader obligation than FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that labels not contain \xe2\x80\x9cfalse or misleading statements,\xe2\x80\x9d that state-law cause of action would be pre-empted by \xc2\xa7136v(b) to the extent of that difference. State-law requirements must also be measured against any relevant EPA regulations that give content to FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s misbranding standards. For example, a failure-to-warn claim alleging that a given pesticide\xe2\x80\x99s label should have stated \xe2\x80\x9cDANGER\xe2\x80\x9d instead of the more subdued \xe2\x80\x9cCAUTION\xe2\x80\x9d would be pre-empted because it is inconsistent with 40 CFR \xc2\xa7156.64 (2004), which specifically assigns these warnings to particular classes of pesticides based on their toxicity.[Footnote 28]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In undertaking a pre-emption analysis at the pleadings stage of a case, a court should bear in mind the concept of equivalence. To survive pre-emption, the state-law requirement need not be phrased in the identical language as its corresponding FIFRA requirement; indeed, it would be surprising if a common-law requirement used the same phraseology as FIFRA. If a case proceeds to trial, the court\xe2\x80\x99s jury instructions must ensure that nominally equivalent labeling requirements are genuinely equivalent. If a defendant so requests, a court should instruct the jury on the relevant FIFRA misbranding standards, as well as any regulations that add content to those standards. For a manufacturer should not be held liable under a state labeling requirement subject to \xc2\xa7136v(b) unless the manufacturer is also liable for misbranding as defined by FIFRA.

\n

Ackerly). By contrast, the lengthy legislative history is barren of any indication that Congress meant to abrogate most of the common-law duties long owed by pesticide manufacturers.

\nFootnote 27

\xc2\xa0Dow does not seem to argue that, by their terms, Texas\xe2\x80\x99s fraud and failure-to-warn causes of action are not equivalent to FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s misbranding standards. Nor has Dow identified any EPA regulations that further refine those general standards in any way that is relevant to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 allegations. Rather, Dow has chosen to mount a broader attack on the \xe2\x80\x9cparallel requirements\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation, thus seeming to argue for the pre-emption of even a state-law cause of action that expressly incorporates FIFRA\'s misbranding provisions. See Brief for Respondent 38, n. 25. Since Dow did not have the benefit of our construction of \xc2\xa7136v(b), Dow should be allowed to address these matters on remand.

The EPA enjoys similar authority here. See 7 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7136w(a)(1). As suggested by Medtronic, the federal agency charged with administering the statute is often better able than are courts to determine the extent to which state liability rules mirror or distort federal requirements. Thus, the EPA may prove better able than are courts to determine whether general state tort liability rules simply help to expose \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98new dangers associated with pesticides,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 18 (quoting Ferebee v. Chevron Chemical Co., 736 F.\xc2\xa02d 1529, 1541 (CADC 1984)), or instead bring about a counterproductive \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98crazy-quilt of anti-misbranding requirements,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15 (quoting Brief for Respondent 16). And, within appropriate legal and administrative constraints, it can act accordingly. Cf. Hillsborough County v. Automated Medical Laboratories, Inc., 471 U. S. 707, 721 (1985) (agencies can monitor the dynamic between federal and local requirements and promulgate regulations pre-empting local legislation that interferes with federal goals). Emphasizing the importance of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s role in overseeing FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s future implementation, I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Scalia joins, concurring in the judgment in part and dissenting in part.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that the term \xe2\x80\x9crequirements\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa724(b) of the Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA), 7 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7136v(b), includes common-law duties for labeling or packaging. Ante, at 10. I also agree that state-law damages claims may not impose requirements \xe2\x80\x9cin addition to or different from\xe2\x80\x9d FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s. Ante, at 19\xe2\x80\x9321. While States are free to impose liability predicated on a violation of the federal standards set forth in FIFRA and in any accompanying regulations promulgated by the Environmental Protection Agency, they may not impose liability for labeling requirements predicated on distinct state standards of care. Section 136v(b) permits States to add remedies\xe2\x80\x94not to alter or augment the substantive rules governing liability for labeling. See Medtronic, Inc. v. Lohr, 518 U. S. 470, 513 (1996) (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). Because the parties have not argued that Dow violated FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s labeling standards,* the majority properly remands for the District Court to consider whether Texas law mirrors the federal standards.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0However, the majority omits a step in its reasoning that should be made explicit: A state-law cause of action, even if not specific to labeling, nevertheless imposes a labeling requirement \xe2\x80\x9cin addition to or different from\xe2\x80\x9d FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s when it attaches liability to statements on the label that do not produce liability under FIFRA. The state-law cause of action then adds some supplemental requirement of truthfulness to FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that labeling statements not be \xe2\x80\x9cfalse or misleading.\xe2\x80\x9d 7 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7136(q)(1)(A). That is why the fraud claims here are properly remanded to determine whether the state and federal standards for liability-incurring statements are, in their application to this case, the same. See ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under that reasoning, the majority mistreats two sets of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims. First, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 breach-of-warranty claims should be remanded for pre-emption analysis, contrary to the majority\xe2\x80\x99s disposition, see ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. To the extent that Texas\xe2\x80\x99 law of warranty imposes liability for statements on the label where FIFRA would not, Texas\xe2\x80\x99 law is pre-empted. See Cipollone v. Liggett Group, Inc., 505 U. S. 504, 551 (1992) (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part). Second, the majority holds that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claim under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices-Consumer Protection Act (DTPA) is not pre-empted to the extent it is a breach-of-warranty claim. Ante, at 12, n.\xc2\xa018. However, the DTPA claim is also (and, in fact, perhaps exclusively) a claim for false or misleading representations on the label. App. 185\xe2\x80\x93186. Therefore, all aspects of the DTPA claim should be remanded. The DTPA claim, like petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 fraud claims, should be pre-empted insofar as it imposes liability for label content where FIFRA would not.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I also note that, despite the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reference to a failure-to-warn claim, ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, n.\xc2\xa015, petitioners have not advanced an actual failure-to-warn claim. Instead, the Court of Appeals treated petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims for negligent testing and defective design and manufacture as \xe2\x80\x9cdisguised claim[s] for failure to warn.\xe2\x80\x9d 332 F.\xc2\xa03d 323, 332\xe2\x80\x93333 (CA5 2003). If petitioners offer no evidence on remand that Dow erred in the testing, design, or manufacture of Strongarm, these claims will fail on the merits. On that point, I take the majority to agree. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, n.\xc2\xa015.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We need go no further to resolve this case. The ordinary meaning of \xc2\xa7136v(b)\xe2\x80\x99s terms makes plain that some of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 state-law causes of action may be pre-empted. Yet the majority advances several arguments designed to tip the scales in favor of the States and against the Federal Government. These arguments, in addition to being unnecessary, are unpersuasive. For instance, the majority states that the presumption against pre-emption requires choosing the interpretation of \xc2\xa7136v(b) that disfavors pre-emption. Ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317. That presumption does not apply, however, when Congress has included within a statute an express pre-emption provision. See Cipollone v. Liggett Group, Inc., supra, at 545\xe2\x80\x93546 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part); Nelson, Preemption, 86 Va. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 225, 291\xe2\x80\x93292, 298\xe2\x80\x93303 (2000). Section 136v(b) is an explicit statement that FIFRA pre-empts some state-law claims. Thus, our task is to determine which state-law claims \xc2\xa7136v(b) pre-empts, without slanting the inquiry in favor of either the Federal Government or the States.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The history of tort litigation against manufacturers is also irrelevant. Ante, at 17. We cannot know, without looking to the text of \xc2\xa7136v(b), whether FIFRA preserved that tradition or displaced it. The majority notes that Congress must have intended to preserve common-law suits, because the legislative history does not indicate that Congress meant to abrogate such suits. Ante, at 19\xe2\x80\x9320, n. 26; see also Small v. United States, ante, at __ (Thomas, J., dissenting) (criticizing novel practice of relying on silence in the legislative history); Koons Buick Pontiac GMC, Inc. v. Nigh, 543 U. S. ___, ___ (2004) (slip op., at 5) (Scalia, J., dissenting) (same). For the Court, then, enacting a pre-emption provision is not enough: either Congress must speak with added specificity in the statute (to avoid the presumption against pre-emption) or some individual Members of Congress or congressional committees must display their preference for pre-emption in the legislative record (to avoid a new canon of congressional silence). But the Court does not believe its own test, for it agrees that \xc2\xa7136v(b) stands to abrogate many common-law causes of action. On remand, for example, petitioners may be unable to pursue a traditional common-law suit under Texas\xe2\x80\x99 law of fraud. Finally, while allowing additional state-law remedies likely aids in enforcing FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s misbranding requirements, ante, at 18, it is for Congress, not this Court, to strike a balance between state tort suits and federal regulation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because we need only determine the ordinary meaning of \xc2\xa7136v(b), the majority rightly declines to address respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims are subject to other types of pre-emption. Brief for Respondent 36\xe2\x80\x9337. For instance, the majority does not ask whether FIFRA\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory scheme is \xe2\x80\x9cso pervasive,\xe2\x80\x9d and the federal interest in labeling \xe2\x80\x9cso dominant,\xe2\x80\x9d that there is no room for States to provide additional remedies. Rice v. Santa Fe Elevator Corp., 331 U. S. 218, 230 (1947). Nor does the majority ask whether enforcement of state-law labeling claims would \xe2\x80\x9cstan[d] as an obstacle to the accomplishment and execution of the full purposes and objectives of Congress\xe2\x80\x9d in enacting FIFRA. Hines v. Davidowitz, 312 U. S. 52, 67 (1941).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision thus comports with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s increasing reluctance to expand federal statutes beyond their terms through doctrines of implied pre-emption. See Camps Newfound/Owatonna, Inc. v. Town of Harrison, 520 U. S. 564, 617 (1997) (Thomas, J., dissenting). This reluctance reflects that pre-emption analysis is not \xe2\x80\x9c[a] freewheeling judicial inquiry into whether a state statute is in tension with federal objectives,\xe2\x80\x9d Gade v. National Solid Wastes Management Assn., 505 U. S. 88, 111 (1992) (Kennedy, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment), but an inquiry into whether the ordinary meanings of state and federal law conflict.
