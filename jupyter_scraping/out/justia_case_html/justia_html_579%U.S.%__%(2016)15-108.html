COMMONWEALTH OF PUERTO RICO v. SANCHEZ VALLE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of puerto rico

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93108.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 13, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 9, 2016

\n

Respondents Luis S\xc3\xa1nchez Valle and Jaime G\xc3\xb3mez V\xc3\xa1zquez each sold a gun to an undercover police officer. Puerto Rican prosecutors indicted them for illegally selling firearms in violation of the Puerto Rico Arms Act of 2000. While those charges were pending, federal grand juries also indicted them, based on the same transactions, for violations of analogous U.\xc2\xa0S. gun trafficking statutes. Both defendants pleaded guilty to the federal charges and moved to dismiss the pending Commonwealth charges on double jeopardy grounds. The trial court in each case dismissed the charges, rejecting prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 arguments that Puerto Rico and the United States are separate sovereigns for double jeopardy purposes and so could bring successive prosecutions against each defendant. The Puerto Rico Court of Appeals consolidated the cases and reversed. The Supreme Court of Puerto Rico granted review and held, in line with the trial court, that Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s gun sale prosecutions violated the Double Jeopardy Clause.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Double Jeopardy Clause bars Puerto Rico and the United States from successively prosecuting a single person for the same conduct under equivalent criminal laws. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Ordinarily, a person cannot be prosecuted twice for the same offense. But under the dual-sovereignty doctrine, the Double Jeopardy Clause does not bar successive prosecutions if they are brought by separate sovereigns. See, e.g., United States v. Lanza,\n\n260 U.\xc2\xa0S. 377\n. Yet \xe2\x80\x9csovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d in this context does not bear its ordinary meaning. This Court does not examine the extent of control that one prosecuting entity wields over the other, the degree to which an entity exercises self-governance, or a government\xe2\x80\x99s more particular ability to enact and enforce its own criminal laws. Rather, the test hinges on a single criterion: the \xe2\x80\x9cultimate source\xe2\x80\x9d of the power undergird-ing the respective prosecutions. United States v. Wheeler,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 313\n. If two entities derive their power to punish from independent sources, then they may bring successive prosecutions. Conversely, if those entities draw their power from the same ultimate source, then they may not.

\n

Under that approach, the States are separate sovereigns from the Federal Government and from one another. Because States rely on \xe2\x80\x9cauthority originally belonging to them before admission to the Union and preserved to them by the\n \nTenth Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d state prosecutions have their roots in an \xe2\x80\x9cinherent sovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d unconnected to the U.\xc2\xa0S. Congress. Heath v. Alabama,\n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 82\n. For similar reasons, Indian tribes also count as separate sovereigns. A tribe\xe2\x80\x99s power to punish pre-existed the Union, and so a tribal prosecution, like a State\xe2\x80\x99s, is \xe2\x80\x9cattributable in no way to any delegation . . . of federal authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Wheeler, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 328. Conversely, a municipality cannot count as a sovereign distinct from a State, because it receives its power, in the first instance, from the State. See, e.g., Waller v. Florida,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 387\n. And most pertinent here, this Court concluded in the early 20th century that U.\xc2\xa0S. territories\xe2\x80\x94including an earlier incarnation of Puerto Rico itself\xe2\x80\x94are not sovereigns distinct from the United States. Grafton v. United States,\n\n206 U. S. 333\n. The Court reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9cthe territorial and federal laws [were] creations emanating from the same sovereignty,\xe2\x80\x9d Puerto Rico v. Shell Co. (P. R.), Ltd.,\n\n302 U.\xc2\xa0S. 253\n, and so federal and territorial prosecutors do not derive their powers from independent sources of authority. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The Grafton and Shell Co. decisions, in and of themselves, do not control here. In the mid-20th century, Puerto Rico became a new kind of political entity, still closely associated with the United States but governed in accordance with, and exercising self-rule through, a popularly-ratified constitution. The magnitude of that change requires consideration of the dual-sovereignty question anew. Yet the result reached, given the historical test applied, ends up the same. Going back as far as the doctrine demands\xe2\x80\x94to the \xe2\x80\x9cultimate source\xe2\x80\x9d of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutorial power\xe2\x80\x94reveals, once again, the U.\xc2\xa0S. Congress. Wheeler, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0In 1950, Congress enacted Public Law 600, which authorized the people of Puerto Rico to organize a government pursuant to a constitution of their own adoption. The Puerto Rican people capitalized on that opportunity, calling a constitutional convention and overwhelmingly approving the charter it drafted. Once Congress approved that proposal\xe2\x80\x94subject to several important conditions accepted by the convention\xe2\x80\x94the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico, a new political entity, came into being.

\n

Those constitutional developments were of great significance\xe2\x80\x94and, indeed, made Puerto Rico \xe2\x80\x9csovereign\xe2\x80\x9d in one commonly understood sense of that term. At that point, Congress granted Puerto Rico a degree of autonomy comparable to that possessed by the States. If the dual-sovereignty doctrine hinged on measuring an entity\xe2\x80\x99s self-governance, the emergence of the Commonwealth would have resulted as well in the capacity to bring the kind of successive prosecutions attempted here. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0But the dual-sovereignty test focuses not on the fact of self-rule, but on where it first came from. And in identifying a prosecuting entity\xe2\x80\x99s wellspring of authority, the Court has insisted on going all the way back\xe2\x80\x94beyond the immediate, or even an intermediate, locus of power to what is termed the \xe2\x80\x9cultimate source.\xe2\x80\x9d On this settled approach, Puerto Rico cannot benefit from the dual-sovereignty doctrine. True enough, that the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s power to enact and enforce criminal law now proceeds, just as petitioner says, from the Puerto Rico Constitution as \xe2\x80\x9cordain[ed] and establish[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cthe people.\xe2\x80\x9d P.\xc2\xa0R. Const., Preamble. But back of the Puerto Rican people and their Constitution, the \xe2\x80\x9cultimate\xe2\x80\x9d source of prosecutorial power remains the U. S. Congress. Congress, in Public Law 600, authorized Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s constitution-making process in the first instance, and Congress, in later legislation, both amended the draft charter and gave it the indispensable stamp of approval. Put simply, Congress conferred the authority to create the Puerto Rico Constitution, which in turn confers the authority to bring criminal charges. That makes Congress the original source of power for Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutors\xe2\x80\x94as it is for the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s. The island\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution, significant though it is, does not break the chain. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

Affirmed.

COMMONWEALTH OF PUERTO RICO, PETITIONER v. LUIS M. SANCHEZ VALLE, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of puerto rico

\n

[June 9, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The Double Jeopardy Clause of the\n \nFifth Amendment prohibits more than one prosecution for the \xe2\x80\x9csame offence.\xe2\x80\x9d But under what is known as the dual-sovereignty doctrine, a single act gives rise to distinct offenses\xe2\x80\x94and thus may subject a person to successive prosecutions\xe2\x80\x94if it violates the laws of separate sovereigns. To determine whether two prosecuting authorities are different sovereigns for double jeopardy purposes, this Court asks a narrow, historically focused question. The inquiry does not turn, as the term \xe2\x80\x9csovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d sometimes suggests, on the degree to which the second entity is autonomous from the first or sets its own political course. Rather, the issue is only whether the prosecutorial powers of the two jurisdictions have independent origins\xe2\x80\x94or, said conversely, whether those powers derive from the same \xe2\x80\x9cultimate source.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Wheeler,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 313,\n320 (1978)\n.

\n

In this case, we must decide if, under that test, Puerto Rico and the United States may successively prosecute a single defendant for the same criminal conduct. We hold they may not, because the oldest roots of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s power to prosecute lie in federal soil.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Puerto Rico became a territory of the United States in 1898, as a result of the Spanish-American War. The treaty concluding that conflict ceded the island, then a Spanish colony, to the United States, and tasked Congress with determining \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he civil rights and political status\xe2\x80\x9d of its inhabitants. Treaty of Paris, Art.\xc2\xa09, Dec. 10, 1898,\n\n30Stat.\n1759. In the ensuing hundred-plus years, the United States and Puerto Rico have forged a unique political relationship, built on the island\xe2\x80\x99s evolution into a constitutional democracy exercising local self-rule.

\n

Acting pursuant to the U. S. Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Territory Clause, Congress initially established a \xe2\x80\x9ccivil government\xe2\x80\x9d for Puerto Rico possessing significant authority over internal affairs. Organic Act of 1900, ch. 191,\n\n31Stat.\n77; see U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0IV, \xc2\xa73, cl.\xc2\xa02 (granting Congress the \xe2\x80\x9cPower to dispose of and make all needful Rules and Regulations respecting the Territory or other Property belonging to the United States\xe2\x80\x9d). The U.\xc2\xa0S. President, with the advice and consent of the Senate, appointed the governor, supreme court, and upper house of the legislature; the Puerto Rican people elected the lower house themselves. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa717\xe2\x80\x9335,\n\n31Stat.\n81\xe2\x80\x9385. Federal statutes generally applied (as they still do) in Puerto Rico, but the newly constituted legislature could enact local laws in much the same way as the then-45 States. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa714\xe2\x80\x9315, 32, id., at 80, 83\xe2\x80\x9384; Puerto Rico v. Shell Co. (P. R.), Ltd.,\n\n302 U.\xc2\xa0S. 253,\n261 (1937)\n.

\n

Over time, Congress granted Puerto Rico additional autonomy. A federal statute passed in 1917, in addition to giving the island\xe2\x80\x99s inhabitants U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship, replaced the upper house of the legislature with a popularly elected senate. See Organic Act of Puerto Rico, ch. 145, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa75, 26,\n\n39Stat.\n953,\n958. And in 1947, an amendment to that law empowered the Puerto Rican people to elect their own governor, a right never before accorded in a U.\xc2\xa0S. territory. See Act of Aug. 5, 1947, ch. 490, \xc2\xa71,\n\n61Stat.\n770.

\n

Three years later, Congress enabled Puerto Rico to embark on the project of constitutional self-governance. Public Law 600, \xe2\x80\x9crecognizing the principle of government by consent,\xe2\x80\x9d authorized the island\xe2\x80\x99s people to \xe2\x80\x9corganize a government pursuant to a constitution of their own adoption.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of July 3, 1950, \xc2\xa71,\n\n64Stat.\n319. Describing itself as \xe2\x80\x9cin the nature of a compact,\xe2\x80\x9d the statute submitted its own terms to an up-or-down referendum of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s voters. Ibid. According to those terms, the eventual constitution had to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide a republican form of government\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinclude a bill of rights\xe2\x80\x9d; all else would be hashed out in a constitutional convention. \xc2\xa72,\n\n64Stat.\n319. The people of Puerto Rico would be the first to decide, in still another referendum, whether to adopt that convention\xe2\x80\x99s proposed charter. See \xc2\xa73,\n\n64Stat.\n319. But Congress would cast the dispositive vote: The constitution, Public Law 600 declared, would become effective only \xe2\x80\x9c[u]pon approval by the Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Thus began two years of constitution-making for the island. The Puerto Rican people first voted to accept Public Law 600, thereby triggering a constitutional convention. And once that body completed its work, the island\xe2\x80\x99s voters ratified the draft constitution. Congress then took its turn on the document: Before giving its approval, Congress removed a provision recognizing various social welfare rights (including entitlements to food, housing, medical care, and employment); added a sentence prohibiting certain constitutional amendments, including any that would restore the welfare-rights section; and inserted language guaranteeing children\xe2\x80\x99s freedom to attend private schools. See Act of July 3, 1952, 66 Stat. 327; Draft Constitution of the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico (1952), in Documents on the Constitutional Relationship of Puerto Rico and the United States 199 (M. Ramirez Lavandero ed., 3d ed. 1988). Finally, the constitution became law, in the manner Congress had specified, when the convention formally accepted those conditions and the governor \xe2\x80\x9cissue[d] a proclamation to that effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Ch. 567,\n\n66Stat.\n328.

\n

The Puerto Rico Constitution created a new political entity, the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x94or, in Spanish, Estado Libre Asociado de Puerto Rico. See P.\xc2\xa0R. Const., Art. I, \xc2\xa71. Like the U.\xc2\xa0S. Constitution, it divides political power into three branches\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9clegislative, judicial and executive.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. I, \xc2\xa72. And again resonant of American founding principles, the Puerto Rico Constitution describes that tripartite government as \xe2\x80\x9crepublican in form\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csubordinate to the sovereignty of the people of Puerto Rico.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s power, the Constitution proclaims, \xe2\x80\x9cemanates from the people and shall be exercised in accordance with their will, within the terms of the compact agreed upon between the people of Puerto Rico and the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. I, \xc2\xa71.

\n

B

\n

We now leave the lofty sphere of constitutionalism for the grittier precincts of criminal law. Respondents Luis S\xc3\xa1nchez Valle and Jaime G\xc3\xb3mez V\xc3\xa1zquez (on separate occasions) each sold a gun to an undercover police officer. Commonwealth prosecutors indicted them for, among other things, selling a firearm without a permit in violation of the Puerto Rico Arms Act of 2000. See 25 Laws P.\xc2\xa0R. Ann. \xc2\xa7458 (2008). While those charges were pending, federal grand juries indicted S\xc3\xa1nchez Valle and G\xc3\xb3mez V\xc3\xa1zquez, based on the same transactions, for violations of analogous U.\xc2\xa0S. gun trafficking statutes. See 18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7922(a)(1)(A), 923(a), 924(a)(1)(D), 924(a)(2). Both defendants pleaded guilty to those federal charges.

\n

Following their pleas, S\xc3\xa1nchez Valle and G\xc3\xb3mez V\xc3\xa1zquez moved to dismiss the pending Commonwealth charges on double jeopardy grounds. The prosecutors in both cases opposed those motions, arguing that Puerto Rico and the United States are different sovereigns for double jeopardy purposes, and so could bring successive prosecutions against each of the two defendants. The trial courts rejected that view and dismissed the charges. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 307a\xe2\x80\x93352a. But the Puerto Rico Court of Appeals, after consolidating the two cases, reversed those decisions. See id., at 243a\xe2\x80\x93306a.

\n

The Supreme Court of Puerto Rico granted review and held that Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s gun sale prosecutions violated the Double Jeopardy Clause. See id., at 1a\xe2\x80\x9370a. The majority reasoned that, under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s dual-sovereignty doctrine, \xe2\x80\x9cwhat is crucial\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he ultimate source\xe2\x80\x9d of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s power to prosecute. Id., at 19a; see id., at 20a (\xe2\x80\x9cThe use of the word \xe2\x80\x98sovereignty\xe2\x80\x99 in other contexts and for other purposes is irrelevant\xe2\x80\x9d). Because that power originally \xe2\x80\x9cderived from the United States Congress\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the same source on which federal prosecutors rely\xe2\x80\x94the Commonwealth could not retry S\xc3\xa1nchez Valle and G\xc3\xb3mez V\xc3\xa1zquez for unlawfully selling firearms. Id., at 66a. Three justices disagreed, believing that the Commonwealth and the United States are separate sovereigns. See id., at 71a\xe2\x80\x93242a.

\n

We granted certiorari, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015), to determine whether the Double Jeopardy Clause bars the Federal Government and Puerto Rico from successively prosecuting a defendant on like charges for the same conduct. We hold that it does, and so affirm.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

This case involves the dual-sovereignty carve-out from the Double Jeopardy Clause. The ordinary rule under that Clause is that a person cannot be prosecuted twice for the same offense. See U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 5 (\xe2\x80\x9cnor shall any person be subject for the same offence to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb\xe2\x80\x9d).[1] But two prosecutions, this Court has long held, are not for the same offense if brought by different sovereigns\xe2\x80\x94even when those actions target the identical criminal conduct through equivalent criminal laws. See, e.g., United States v. Lanza,\n\n260 U.\xc2\xa0S. 377,\n382 (1922)\n. As we have put the point: \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen the same act transgresses the laws of two sovereigns, it cannot be truly averred that the offender has been twice punished for the same offence; but only that by one act he has committed two offences.\xe2\x80\x9d Heath v. Alabama,\n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 82,\n88 (1985)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). The Double Jeopardy Clause thus drops out of the picture when the \xe2\x80\x9centities that seek successively to prosecute a defendant for the same course of conduct [are] separate sovereigns.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Truth be told, however, \xe2\x80\x9csovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d in this context does not bear its ordinary meaning. For whatever reason, the test we have devised to decide whether two governments are distinct for double jeopardy purposes overtly disregards common indicia of sovereignty. Under that standard, we do not examine the \xe2\x80\x9cextent of control\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cone prosecuting authority [wields] over the other.\xe2\x80\x9d Wheeler, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320. The degree to which an entity exercises self-governance\xe2\x80\x94whether autonomously managing its own affairs or continually submitting to outside direction\xe2\x80\x94plays no role in the analysis. See Shell Co., 302 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 261\xe2\x80\x93262, 264\xe2\x80\x93266. Nor do we care about a government\xe2\x80\x99s more particular ability to enact and enforce its own criminal laws. See Waller v. Florida,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 387\n\xe2\x80\x93395 (1970). In short, the inquiry (despite its label) does not probe whether a government possesses the usual attributes, or acts in the common manner, of a sovereign entity.[2]

\n

Rather, as Puerto Rico itself acknowledges, our test hinges on a single criterion: the \xe2\x80\x9cultimate source\xe2\x80\x9d of the power undergirding the respective prosecutions. Wheeler, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320; see Brief for Petitioner 26. Whether two prosecuting entities are dual sovereigns in the double jeopardy context, we have stated, depends on \xe2\x80\x9cwhether [they] draw their authority to punish the offender from distinct sources of power.\xe2\x80\x9d Heath, 474 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 88. The inquiry is thus historical, not functional\xe2\x80\x94looking at the deepest wellsprings, not the current exercise, of prosecutorial authority. If two entities derive their power to punish from wholly independent sources (imagine here a pair of parallel lines), then they may bring successive prosecutions. Conversely, if those entities draw their power from the same ultimate source (imagine now two lines emerging from a common point, even if later diverging), then they may not.[3]

\n

Under that approach, the States are separate sovereigns from the Federal Government (and from one another). See Abbate v. United States,\n\n359 U.\xc2\xa0S. 187,\n195 (1959)\n; Bartkus v. Illinois,\n\n359 U.\xc2\xa0S. 121\n\xe2\x80\x93137 (1959); Heath, 474 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 88. The States\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cpowers to undertake criminal prosecutions,\xe2\x80\x9d we have explained, do not \xe2\x80\x9cderive[\xc2\xa0] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. from the Federal Government.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 89. Instead, the States rely on \xe2\x80\x9cauthority originally belonging to them before admission to the Union and preserved to them by the\n \nTenth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 10 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are reserved to the States\xe2\x80\x9d); Blatchford v. Native Village of Noatak,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 775,\n779 (1991)\n (noting that the States \xe2\x80\x9centered the [Union] with their sovereignty intact\xe2\x80\x9d). Said otherwise: Prior to forming the Union, the States possessed \xe2\x80\x9cseparate and independent sources of power and authority,\xe2\x80\x9d which they continue to draw upon in enacting and enforcing criminal laws. Heath, 474 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 89. State prosecutions therefore have their most ancient roots in an \xe2\x80\x9cinherent sovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d unconnected to, and indeed pre-existing, the U.\xc2\xa0S. Congress. Ibid.[4]

\n

For similar reasons, Indian tribes also count as separate sovereigns under the Double Jeopardy Clause. Originally, this Court has noted, \xe2\x80\x9cthe tribes were self-governing sovereign political communities,\xe2\x80\x9d possessing (among other capacities) the \xe2\x80\x9cinherent power to prescribe laws for their members and to punish infractions of those laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Wheeler, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 322\xe2\x80\x93323. After the formation of the United States, the tribes became \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic dependent nations,\xe2\x80\x9d subject to plenary control by Congress\xe2\x80\x94so hardly \xe2\x80\x9csovereign\xe2\x80\x9d in one common sense. United States v.Lara,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193,\n204 (2004)\n (quoting Cherokee Nation v. Georgia, 5 Pet. 1, 17 (1831)); see Santa Clara Pueblo v. Martinez,\n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 49,\n56 (1978)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress has plenary authority to limit, modify or eliminate the [tribes\xe2\x80\x99] powers of local self-government\xe2\x80\x9d). But unless and until Congress withdraws a tribal power\xe2\x80\x94including the power to prosecute\xe2\x80\x94the Indian community retains that authority in its earliest form. See Wheeler, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 323. The \xe2\x80\x9cultimate source\xe2\x80\x9d of a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpower to punish tribal offenders\xe2\x80\x9d thus lies in its \xe2\x80\x9cprimeval\xe2\x80\x9d or, at any rate, \xe2\x80\x9cpre-existing\xe2\x80\x9d sovereignty: A tribal prosecution, like a State\xe2\x80\x99s, is \xe2\x80\x9cattributable in no way to any delegation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of federal authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 320, 322, 328; Santa Clara Pueblo, 436 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 56. And that alone is what matters for the double jeopardy inquiry.

\n

Conversely, this Court has held that a municipality cannot qualify as a sovereign distinct from a State\xe2\x80\x94no matter how much autonomy over criminal punishment the city maintains. See Waller, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 395. Florida law, we recognized in our pivotal case on the subject, treated a municipality as a \xe2\x80\x9cseparate sovereign entit[y]\xe2\x80\x9d for all relevant real-world purposes: The city possessed broad home-rule authority, including the power to enact criminal ordinances and prosecute offenses. Id., at 391. But that functional control was not enough to escape the double jeopardy bar; indeed, it was wholly beside the point. The crucial legal inquiry was backward-looking: Did the city and State ultimately \xe2\x80\x9cderive their powers to prosecute from independent sources of authority\xe2\x80\x9d? Heath, 474 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 90 (describing Waller\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning). Because the municipality, in the first instance, had received its power from the State, those two entities could not bring successive prosecutions for a like offense.

\n

And most pertinent here, this Court concluded in the early decades of the last century that U.\xc2\xa0S. territories\xe2\x80\x94including an earlier incarnation of Puerto Rico itself\xe2\x80\x94are not sovereigns distinct from the United States. In Grafton v. United States,\n\n206 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333,\n355 (1907)\n, we held that the Philippine Islands (then a U.\xc2\xa0S. territory, also acquired in the Spanish-American War) could not prosecute a defendant for murder after a federal tribunal had acquitted him of the same crime. We reasoned that whereas \xe2\x80\x9ca State does not derive its powers from the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d a territory does: The Philippine courts \xe2\x80\x9cexert[ed] all their powers by authority of\xe2\x80\x9d the Federal Government. Id., at 354. And then, in Shell Co., we stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he situation [in Puerto Rico] was, in all essentials, the same.\xe2\x80\x9d 302 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 265. Commenting on a Puerto Rican statute that overlapped with a federal law, we explained that this \xe2\x80\x9clegislative duplication [gave] rise to no danger of a second prosecution\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cthe territorial and federal laws [were] creations emanating from the same sovereignty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 264; see also Heath, 474 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 90 (notingthat federal and territorial prosecutors \xe2\x80\x9cd[o] not derive their powers to prosecute from independent sources of authority\xe2\x80\x9d).[5]

\n

B

\n

With that background established, we turn to the question presented: Do the prosecutorial powers belonging to Puerto Rico and the Federal Government derive from wholly independent sources? See Brief for Petitioner 26\xe2\x80\x9328 (agreeing with that framing of the issue). If so, the criminal charges at issue here can go forward; but if not, not. In addressing that inquiry, we do not view our decisions in Grafton and Shell Co. as, in and of themselves, controlling. Following 1952, Puerto Rico became a new kind of political entity, still closely associated with the United States but governed in accordance with, and exercising self-rule through, a popularly ratified constitution. The magnitude of that change requires us to consider the dual-sovereignty question anew. And yet the result we reach, given the legal test we apply, ends up the same. Puerto Rico today has a distinctive, indeed exceptional, status as a self-governing Commonwealth. But our approach is historical. And if we go back as far as our doctrine demands\xe2\x80\x94to the \xe2\x80\x9cultimate source\xe2\x80\x9d of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutorial power, Wheeler, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320\xe2\x80\x94we once again discover the U.\xc2\xa0S. Congress.

\n

Recall here the events of the mid-20th century\xe2\x80\x94when Puerto Rico, just as petitioner contends, underwent a profound change in its political system. See Brief for Petitioner 1\xe2\x80\x932 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he people of Puerto Rico[\xc2\xa0] engaged in an exercise of popular sovereignty .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by adopting their own Constitution establishing their own government to enact their own laws\xe2\x80\x9d); supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. At that time, Congress enacted Public Law 600 to authorize Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s adoption of a constitution, designed to replace the federal statute that then structured the island\xe2\x80\x99s governance. The people of Puerto Rico capitalized on that opportunity, calling a constitutional convention and overwhelmingly approving the charter it drafted. Once Congress approved that proposal\xe2\x80\x94subject to several important conditions accepted by the convention\xe2\x80\x94the Commonwealth, a new political entity, came into being.

\n

Those constitutional developments were of great significance\xe2\x80\x94and, indeed, made Puerto Rico \xe2\x80\x9csovereign\xe2\x80\x9d in one commonly understood sense of that term. As this Court has recognized, Congress in 1952 \xe2\x80\x9crelinquished its control over [the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s] local affairs[,] grant[ing]Puerto Rico a measure of autonomy comparable to that possessed by the States.\xe2\x80\x9d Examining Bd. of Engineers, Architects and Surveyors v. Flores de Otero,\n\n426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 572,\n597 (1976)\n; see id., at 594 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he purpose of Congress in the 1950 and 1952 legislation was to accord to Puerto Rico the degree of autonomy and independence normally associ-ated with States of the Union\xe2\x80\x9d); Rodriguez v. Popular Demo-cratic Party,\n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n8 (1982)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cPuerto Rico, like a state, is an autonomous political entity, sovereign over matters not ruled by the [Federal] Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). That newfound authority, including over local criminal laws, brought mutual benefit to the Puerto Rican people and the entire United States. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 3. And if our double jeopardy decisions hinged on measuring an entity\xe2\x80\x99s self-governance, the emergence of the Commonwealth would have resulted as well in the capacity to bring the kind of successive prosecutions attempted here.

\n

But as already explained, the dual-sovereignty test we have adopted focuses on a different question: not on the fact of self-rule, but on where it came from. See supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. We do not care, for example, that the States pres-ently exercise autonomous control over criminal law andother local affairs; instead, we treat them as separate sovereigns because they possessed such control as an original matter, rather than deriving it from the Federal Government. See supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. And in identifying a prosecuting entity\xe2\x80\x99s wellspring of authority, we have insisted on going all the way back\xe2\x80\x94beyond the immediate, or even an intermediate, locus of power to what we have termed the \xe2\x80\x9cultimate source.\xe2\x80\x9d Wheeler, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320. That is why we have emphasized the \xe2\x80\x9cinherent,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cprimeval,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpre-existing\xe2\x80\x9d capacities of the tribes and States\xe2\x80\x94the power they enjoyed prior to the Union\xe2\x80\x99s formation. Id., at 322\xe2\x80\x93323, 328; Heath, 474 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 90; Santa Clara Pueblo, 436 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 56; see supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x9310. And it is why cities fail our test even when they enact and enforce their own criminal laws under their own, popu-larly ratified charters: Because a State must initially authorize any such charter, the State is the furthest-back source of prosecutorial power. See Waller, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 391\xe2\x80\x93394; supra, at 10.

\n

On this settled approach, Puerto Rico cannot benefit from our dual-sovereignty doctrine. For starters, no one argues that when the United States gained possession of Puerto Rico, its people possessed independent prosecuto-rial power, in the way that the States or tribes did upon becoming part of this country. Puerto Rico was until then a colony \xe2\x80\x9cunder Spanish sovereignty.\xe2\x80\x9d Treaty of Paris, Art.\xc2\xa02,\n\n30Stat.\n1755. And local prosecutors in the ensuing decades, as petitioner itself acknowledges, exercised only such power as was \xe2\x80\x9cdelegated by Congress\xe2\x80\x9d through fed-eral statutes. Brief for Petitioner 28; see Shell Co., 302 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 264\xe2\x80\x93265; supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. Their authority derived from, rather than pre-existed association with, the Federal Government.

\n

And contrary to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim, Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s transformative constitutional moment does not lead to a different conclusion. True enough, that the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s power to enact and enforce criminal law now proceeds, just as petitioner says, from the Puerto Rico Constitution as \xe2\x80\x9cordain[ed] and establish[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cthe people.\xe2\x80\x9d P.\xc2\xa0R. Const., Preamble; see Brief for Petitioner 28\xe2\x80\x9330. But that makes the Puerto Rican populace only the most immediate source of such authority\xe2\x80\x94and that is not what our dual-sovereignty decisions make relevant. Back of the Puerto Rican people and their Constitution, the \xe2\x80\x9cultimate\xe2\x80\x9d source of prosecutorial power remains the U.\xc2\xa0S. Congress, just as back of a city\xe2\x80\x99s charter lies a state government. Wheeler, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 320. Congress, in Public Law 600, authorized Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s constitution-making process in the first instance; the people of a territory could not legally have initiated that process on their own. See, e.g., Simms v. Simms,\n\n175 U.\xc2\xa0S. 162,\n168 (1899)\n. And Congress, in later legislation, both amended the draft charter and gave it the indispensable stamp of approval; popular ratification, however meaningful, could not have turned the convention\xe2\x80\x99s handiwork into law.[6] Put simply, Congress conferred the authority to create the Puerto Rico Constitution, which in turn confers the authority to bring criminal charges. That makes Congress the original source of power for Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutors\xe2\x80\x94as it is for the Fed-eral Government\xe2\x80\x99s. The island\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution, significant though it is, does not break the chain.

\n

Petitioner urges, in support of its different view, that Congress itself recognized the new Constitution as \xe2\x80\x9ca democratic manifestation of the [people\xe2\x80\x99s] will,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 2\xe2\x80\x94but far from disputing that point, we readily acknowledge it to be so. As petitioner notes, Public Law 600 affirmed the \xe2\x80\x9cprinciple of government by consent\xe2\x80\x9d and offered the Puerto Rican public a \xe2\x80\x9ccompact,\xe2\x80\x9d under which they could \xe2\x80\x9corganize a government pursuant to a constitution of their own adoption.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71,\n\n64Stat.\n319; see Brief for Petitioner 2, 29; supra, at 3. And the Constitution that Congress approved, as petitioner again underscores, declares that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e, the people\xe2\x80\x9d of Puerto Rico, \xe2\x80\x9ccreate\xe2\x80\x9d the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x94a new political entity, \xe2\x80\x9crepublican in form,\xe2\x80\x9d in which the people\xe2\x80\x99s will is \xe2\x80\x9csovereign[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d over the government. P.\xc2\xa0R. Const., Preamble and Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71\xe2\x80\x932; see Brief for Petitioner 2, 29\xe2\x80\x9330; supra, at 4. With thatconsented-to language, Congress \xe2\x80\x9callow[ed] the people ofPuerto Rico,\xe2\x80\x9d in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s words, to begin a new chapter of democratic self-governance. Reply Brief 20.

\n

All that separates our view from petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s is what that congressional recognition means for Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s ability to bring successive prosecutions. We agree that Congress has broad latitude to develop innovative approaches to territorial governance, see U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. IV, \xc2\xa73, cl.\xc2\xa02; that Congress may thus enable a territory\xe2\x80\x99s people to make large-scale choices about their own political institutions; and that Congress did exactly that in enacting Public Law 600 and approving the Puerto Rico Constitution\xe2\x80\x94prime examples of what Felix Frankfurter once termed \xe2\x80\x9cinventive statesmanship\xe2\x80\x9d respecting the island. Memorandum for the Secretary of War, in Hearings on S. 4604 before the Senate Committee on Pacific Islands and Porto Rico, 63d Cong., 2d Sess., 22 (1914); see Reply Brief 18\xe2\x80\x9320. But one power Congress does not have, just in the nature of things: It has no capacity, no magic wand or airbrush, to erase or otherwise rewrite its own foundational role in conferring political authority. Or otherwise said, the delegator cannot make itself any less so\xe2\x80\x94no matter how much authority it opts to hand over. And our dual-sovereignty test makes this historical fact dispositive: If an entity\xe2\x80\x99s authority to enact and enforce criminal law ultimately comes from Congress, then it cannot follow a federal prosecution with its own. That is true of Puerto Rico, because Congress authorized and approved its Constitution, from which prosecutorial power now flows. So the Double Jeopardy Clause bars both Puerto Rico and the United States from prosecuting a single person for the same conduct under equivalent criminal laws.

\n

III

\n

Puerto Rico boasts \xe2\x80\x9ca relationship to the United States that has no parallel in our history.\xe2\x80\x9d Examining Bd., 426 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 596. And since the events of the early 1950\xe2\x80\x99s, an integral aspect of that association has been the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s wide-ranging self-rule, exercised under its own Constitution. As a result of that charter, Puerto Rico today can avail itself of a wide variety of futures. But for purposes of the Double Jeopardy Clause, the future is not what matters\xe2\x80\x94and there is no getting away from the past. Because the ultimate source of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s prosecutorial power is the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x94because when we trace that authority all the way back, we arrive at the doorstep of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Capitol\xe2\x80\x94the Commonwealth and the United States are not separate sovereigns. That means the two governments cannot \xe2\x80\x9ctwice put\xe2\x80\x9d respondents S\xc3\xa1nchez Valle and G\xc3\xb3mez V\xc3\xa1zquez \xe2\x80\x9cin jeopardy\xe2\x80\x9d for the \xe2\x80\x9csame offence.\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Amdt. 5. We accordingly affirm the judgment of the Supreme Court of Puerto Rico.

It is so ordered.

Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Sotomayor joins, dissenting.

\n

I agree with the Court that this case poses a special, not a general, question about Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty. It asks whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe prosecutorial powers belonging to Puerto Rico and the Federal Government derive from wholly independent sources.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. I do not agree, how-ever, with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s answer to that question. I do not believe that \xe2\x80\x9cif we go back [through history] as far as our doctrine demands\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., \xe2\x80\x9call the way back\xe2\x80\x9d to the \xe2\x80\x9cfurthest-back source of prosecutorial power\xe2\x80\x9d), we will \xe2\x80\x9cdiscover\xe2\x80\x9d that Puerto Rico and the Federal Government share the same source of power, namely, \xe2\x80\x9cthe U.\xc2\xa0S. Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313, 14. My reasons for disagreeing with the major-ity are in part conceptual and in part historical.

\n

I

\n

Conceptually speaking, the Court does not mean liter-ally that to find the \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d of an entity\xe2\x80\x99s criminal law, we must seek the \xe2\x80\x9cfurthest-back source of . . . power.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14 (emphasis added). We do not trace Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s source of power back to Spain or to Rome or to Justinian, nor do we trace the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s source of power back to the English Parliament or to William the Conqueror or to King Arthur. Rather the Court\xe2\x80\x99s statement means that we should trace the source of power back to a time when a previously nonexistent entity, or a previously dependent entity, became independent\xe2\x80\x94at least, sufficiently independent to be considered \xe2\x80\x9csovereign\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of the Double Jeopardy Clause.

\n

As so viewed, this approach explains the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions fairly well. The Federal Government became an independent entity when the Constitution first took effect. That document gave to the Federal Government the authority to enact criminal laws. And the Congress that the document created is consequently the source of those laws. The original 13 States, once dependents of Britain, became independent entities perhaps at the time of the Declaration of Independence, perhaps at the signing of the Treaty of Paris, perhaps with the creation of the Articles of Confederation. (I need not be precise.) See G. Wood, Creation of the American Republic 1776\xe2\x80\x931787, p. 354 (1969) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe problem of sovereignty was not solved by the Declaration of Independence. It continued to be the most important theoretical question of politics throughout the following decade\xe2\x80\x9d). And an independent colony\xe2\x80\x99s legislation-creating system is consequently the source of those original State\xe2\x80\x99s criminal laws.

\n

But the \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d question becomes more difficult with respect to other entities because Congress had an active role to play with respect to their creation (and thus congressional activity appears to be highly relevant to the double jeopardy question). Consider the Philippines. No one could doubt the Philippines\xe2\x80\x99 current possession of sovereign authority to enact criminal laws. Yet if we trace that power back through history, we must find the \xe2\x80\x9cfurthest-back\xe2\x80\x9d source of the islands\xe2\x80\x99 lawmaking authority, not in any longstanding independent Philippine institutions (for until 1946 the Philippines was dependent, not independent), but in a decision by Congress and the President (as well as by the Philippines) to change the Philippines\xe2\x80\x99 status to one of independence. In 1934 Congress authorized the President to \xe2\x80\x9cwithdraw and surrender all right of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. sovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d over the Philippines.\n\n48Stat.\n463, codified at\n\n22 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71394. That authorization culminated in the Treaty of Manila, signed in 1946 and approved by Congress that same year, which formally recognized the Philippines as an independent, self-governing nation-state. See\n\n61Stat.\n1174. In any obvious sense of the term, then, the \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d of the Philippines\xe2\x80\x99 independence (and its ability to enact and enforce its own criminal laws) was the U.\xc2\xa0S. Congress.

\n

The same is true for most of the States. In the usual course, a U.\xc2\xa0S. Territory becomes a State within our Union at the invitation of Congress. In fact, the parallels between admission of new States and the creation of the Commonwealth in this case are significant. Congress passes a law allowing \xe2\x80\x9cthe inhabitants of the territory .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to form for themselves a constitution and state government, and to assume such name as they shall deem proper.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Apr. 16, 1818, ch. 67,\n\n3Stat.\n428\xe2\x80\x93429 (Illinois); see also Act of June 20, 1910, ch. 310,\n\n36Stat.\n557 (New Mexico) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he qualified electors of the Territory .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are hereby authorized to vote for and choose delegates to form a constitutional convention for said Territory for the purpose of framing a constitution for the proposed State of New Mexico\xe2\x80\x9d). And after the Territory develops and proposes a constitution, Congress and the President review and approve it before allowing the Territory to become a full-fledged State. See, e.g., Res. 1,\n\n3Stat.\n536 (Illinois); Pub. Res. 8,\n\n37Stat.\n39 (New Mexico); Presidential Proclamation No. 62,\n\n37Stat.\n1723 (\xe2\x80\x9cI WILLIAM HOWARD TAFT, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. declare and proclaim the fact that the fundamental conditions imposed by Congress on the State of New Mexico to entitle that State to admission have been ratified and accepted\xe2\x80\x9d). The Federal Government thus is in an important sense the \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d of these States\xe2\x80\x99 legislative powers.

\n

One might argue, as this Court has argued, that the source of new States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign authority to enact criminal laws lies in the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s equal-footing doctrine\xe2\x80\x94the doctrine under which the Constitution treats new States the same as it does the original 13. See ante, at 9, n.\xc2\xa04. It is difficult, however, to characterize a constitutional insistence upon equality of the States as (in any here relevant sense) the \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d of those States\xe2\x80\x99 independent legislative powers. For one thing, the equal-footing doctrine is a requirement imposed by the U.\xc2\xa0S. Constitution. See Coyle v. Smith,\n\n221 U.\xc2\xa0S. 559\n\xe2\x80\x93567 (1911). For that reason, the Constitution is ultimately the source of even these new States\xe2\x80\x99 equal powers (just as it is the source of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 powers). This is not to suggest that we are not a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98union of States [alike] in power, dignity and authority.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9, n.\xc2\xa04 (quoting Coyle, supra, at 567). Of course I recognize that we are. It is merely to ask: without the Constitution (i.e., a federal \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d), what claim would new States have to a lawmaking power equal to that of their \xe2\x80\x9cearliest counterparts\xe2\x80\x9d? Ante, at 9, n.\xc2\xa04.

\n

For another thing, the equal-footing doctrine means that, going forward, new States must enjoy the same rights and obligations as the original States\xe2\x80\x94they are, for example, equally restricted by the\n \nFirst Amendment and equally \xe2\x80\x9ccompetent to exert that residuum of sovereignty not delegated to the United States by the Constitution itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Coyle, supra, at 567. But this current and future equality does not destroy the fact that there is a federal \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d from which those rights and obligations spring: the Congress which agreed to admit those new States into the Union in accordance with the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s terms. See, e.g., \n\n37Stat.\n39 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Territor[y] of New Mexico [is] hereby admitted into the Union upon an equal footing with the original States\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In respect to the Indian tribes, too, congressional action is relevant to the double jeopardy analysis. This Court has explained that the tribes possess an independent authority to enact criminal laws by tracing the source of power back to a time of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98primeval\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d tribal existence when \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the tribes were self-governing sovereign political communities.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (quoting United States v. Wheeler,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 313\n\xe2\x80\x93323 (1978)). But as the Court today recognizes, this prelapsarian independence must be read in light of congressional action\xe2\x80\x94or, as it were, inaction. That is because\xe2\x80\x94whatever a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s history\xe2\x80\x94Congress maintains \xe2\x80\x9cplenary authority to limit, modify or eliminate the [tribes\xe2\x80\x99] powers of local self-government,\xe2\x80\x9d Santa Clara Pueblo v. Martinez,\n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 49,\n56 (1978)\n, and thus the tribes remain sovereign for purposes of the Double Jeopardy Clause only \xe2\x80\x9cuntil\xe2\x80\x9d Congress chooses to withdraw that power, ante, at 10. In this sense, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 pattern of inaction (i.e., its choice to refrain from withdrawing dual sovereignty) amounts to an implicit decision to grant such sovereignty to the tribes. Is not Congress then, in this way, the \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d of the Indian tribes\xe2\x80\x99 criminal-enforcement power?

\n

These examples illustrate the complexity of the question before us. I do not believe, as the majority seems to believe, that the double jeopardy question can be answered simply by tracing Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s current legislative powers back to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enactment of Public Law 600 and calling the Congress that enacted that law the \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d of the island\xe2\x80\x99s criminal-enforcement authority. That is because\xe2\x80\x94as with the Philippines, new States, and the In-dian tribes\xe2\x80\x94congressional activity and other historic circumstances can combine to establish a new source of power. We therefore must consider Public Law 600 in the broader context of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s history. Only through that lens can we decide whether the Commonwealth, between the years 1950 and 1952, gained sufficient sovereign authority to become the \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d of power behind its own criminal laws.

\n

II

\n

The Treaty of Paris, signed with Spain in 1898, said that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he civil rights and political status\xe2\x80\x9d of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinhabitants .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be determined by the Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. 9,\n\n30Stat.\n1759. In my view, Congress, in enacting the Puerto Rican Federal Relations Act (i.e., Public Law 600), determined that the \xe2\x80\x9cpolitical status\xe2\x80\x9d of Puerto Rico would for double jeopardy purposes subsequently encompass the sovereign authority to enact and enforce\xe2\x80\x94pursuant to its own powers\xe2\x80\x94its own criminal laws. Several considerations support this conclusion.

\n

First, the timing of Public Law 600\xe2\x80\x99s enactment suggests that Congress intended it to work a significant change in the nature of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s political status. Prior to 1950 Puerto Rico was initially subject to the Foraker Act, which provided the Federal Government with virtually complete control of the island\xe2\x80\x99s affairs. In 1917 Puerto Rico became subject to the Jones Act, which provided for United States citizenship and permitted Puerto Ricans to elect local legislators but required submission of local laws to Congress for approval. In 1945 the United States, when signing the United Nations Charter, promised change. It told the world that it would \xe2\x80\x9cdevelop self-government\xe2\x80\x9d in its Territories. Art. 73(b),\n\n59Stat.\n1048, June 26, 1945, T.\xc2\xa0S. No. 993 (U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter). And contemporary observers referred to Public Law 600 as taking a significant step in the direction of change by granting Puerto Rico a special status carrying with it considerable autonomy. See, e.g., Magruder, The Commonwealth Status of Puerto Rico, 15 U. Pitt. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 14\xe2\x80\x9316 (1953); see also L. Kalman, Abe Fortas: A Biography 170\xe2\x80\x93171 (1990) (\xe2\x80\x9c[After the 1950 \xe2\x80\x98compact,\xe2\x80\x99] Puerto Rico was self-ruling, according to [Fortas], although the federal government retained the same power it would have over states in a union\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Second, Public Law 600 uses language that says or implies a significant shift in the legitimacy-conferring source of many local laws. The Act points out that the United States \xe2\x80\x9chas progressively recognized the right of self-government of the people of Puerto Rico.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n64Stat.\n319. It \xe2\x80\x9c[f\xc2\xa0]ully recogniz[es] the principle of government by consent.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n48 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7731b. It describes itself as being \xe2\x80\x9cin the nature of a compact so that the people of Puerto Rico may organize a government pursuant to a constitution of their own adoption.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. It specifies that the island\xe2\x80\x99s new constitution must \xe2\x80\x9cprovide a republican form of government,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7731c; and this Court has characterized that form of government as including \xe2\x80\x9cthe right of the people to choose their own officers for governmental administration, and pass their own laws in virtue of the legislative power reposed in representative bodies, whose legitimate acts may be said to be those of the people themselves,\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Duncan,\n\n139 U.\xc2\xa0S. 449,\n461 (1891)\n.

\n

Third, Public Law 600 created a constitution-writing process that led Puerto Rico to convene a constitutional convention and to write a constitution that, in assuring Puerto Rico independent authority to enact many local laws, specifies that the legitimacy-conferring source of much local lawmaking shall henceforth be the \xe2\x80\x9cpeople of Puerto Rico.\xe2\x80\x9d The constitution begins by stating:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWe, the people of Puerto Rico, in order to organize ourselves politically on a fully democratic basis, to promote the general welfare, and to secure for ourselves and our posterity the complete enjoyment of human rights, placing our trust in Almighty God, do ordain and establish this Constitution for the commonwealth .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.

\n

Fourth, both Puerto Rico and the United States ratified Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution. Puerto Rico did so initially through a referendum held soon after the constitution was written and then by a second referendum held after the convention revised the constitution in minor ways (ways that Congress insisted upon, but which are not relevant here). See\n\n66Stat.\n327; see also ante, at 3 (describing these revisions). And, as I have just said, the convention, having the last word, made the minor amendments and Puerto Rico ratified the constitution through a second referendum.

\n

Fifth, all three branches of the Federal Government subsequently recognized that Public Law 600, the Puerto Rican Constitution, and related congressional actions granted Puerto Rico considerable autonomy in local matters, sometimes akin to that of a State. See, e.g., S.\xc2\xa0Rep. Seventh, insofar as Public Law 600 (and related events) grants Puerto Rico local legislative autonomy, it is particularly likely to have done so in respect to local criminal law. \n

This history of statutes, language, organic acts, traditions, statements, and other actions, taken by all three branches of the Federal Government and by Puerto Rico, convinces me that the United States has entered into a compact one of the terms of which is that the \xe2\x80\x9csource\xe2\x80\x9d of Puerto Rico\xe2\x80\x99s criminal law ceased to be the U.\xc2\xa0S.
