\nASHCROFT V. AL-KIDD
\n

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

ASHCROFT v. al-KIDD

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 10\xe2\x80\x9398.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 2, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 31, 2011

\n

Respondent al-Kidd alleges that, after the September 11th terrorist attacks, then-Attorney General Ashcroft authorized federal officials to detain terrorism suspects using the federal material-witness statute, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73144. He claims that this pretextual detention policy led to his material-witness arrest as he was boarding a plane to Saudi Arabia. To secure the warrant, federal officials had told a Magistrate Judge that information \xe2\x80\x9ccrucial\xe2\x80\x9d to Sami Omar al-Hussayen\xe2\x80\x99s prosecution would be lost if al-Kidd boarded his flight. Prosecutors never called al-Kidd as a witness, and (as he alleges) never meant to do so. Al-Kidd filed suit pursuant to Bivens v. Six Unknown Fed. Narcotics Agents, 403 U. S. 388, challenging the constitutionality of Ashcroft\xe2\x80\x99s alleged policy. The District Court denied Ashcroft\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss on absolute and qualified immunity grounds. The Ninth Circuit affirmed, holding that the Fourth Amendment prohibits pretextual arrests absent probable cause of criminal wrongdoing, and that Ashcroft could not claim qualified or absolute immunity.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The objectively reasonable arrest and detention of a material witness pursuant to a validly obtained warrant cannot be challenged as unconstitutional on the basis of allegations that the arresting authority had an improper motive. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Qualified immunity shields a government official from money damages unless (1) the official violated a statutory or constitutional right, and (2) that right was \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the challenged conduct. Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U. S. 800, 818. Where, as here, a court considers both prongs of this inquiry, this Court has the discretion to correct the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s errors at each step. P.\xc2\xa03.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Whether a detention is reasonable under the Fourth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cis predominantly an objective inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d Indianapolis v. Edmond, 531 U. S. 32, 47. Courts ask whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe circumstances, viewed objectively, justify [the challenged] action.\xe2\x80\x9d Scott v. United States, 436 U. S. 128, 138. Except for cases that involve special-needs, e.g., Vernonia School Dist. 47J v. Acton, 515 U. S. 646, 653, or administrative searches, e.g., Michigan v. Clifford, 464 U. S. 287, 294, this Court has almost uniformly rejected invitations to probe subjective intent. The Court of Appeals was mistaken in believing that Edmond established that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98programmatic purpose\xe2\x80\x99 is relevant to Fourth Amendment analysis of programs of seizures without probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d 580 F.\xc2\xa03d 949, 968. It was not the absence of probable cause that triggered Edmond\xe2\x80\x99s invalidating-purpose inquiry, but the checkpoints\xe2\x80\x99 failure to be based on \xe2\x80\x9cindividualized suspicion.\xe2\x80\x9d 531 U. S., at 47. Here a neutral Magistrate Judge issued a warrant authorizing al-Kidd\xe2\x80\x99s arrest, and the affidavit accompanying the warrant application gave individualized reasons to believe that he was a material witness who would soon disappear. A warrant based on individualized suspicion grants more protection than existed in most of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases eschewing inquiries into intent, e.g., Whren v. United States, 517 U. S. 806, 813, and Terry v. Ohio, 392 U. S. 1, 21\xe2\x80\x9322. Al-Kidd\xe2\x80\x99s contrary, narrow reading of those cases is rejected. Because he concedes that individualized suspicion supported the issuance of the material-witness arrest warrant; and does not assert that his arrest would have been unconstitutional absent the alleged pretext; there is no Fourth Amendment violation here. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Ashcroft did not violate clearly established law and thus is entitled to qualified immunity. A Government official\xe2\x80\x99s conduct violates clearly established law when, at the time of the challenged conduct, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he contours of [a] right [are] sufficiently clear\xe2\x80\x9d that every \xe2\x80\x9creasonable official would have understood that what he is doing violates that right.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Creighton, 483 U. S. 635, 640. Here, the asserted constitutional right falls far short of that threshold. At the time of al-Kidd\xe2\x80\x99s arrest, not a single judicial opinion had held that pretext could render an objectively reasonable arrest pursuant to a material-witness warrant unconstitutional. The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on a District Court\xe2\x80\x99s footnoted dictum, irrelevant cases from this Court, and the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s broad purposes and history is rejected. Because Ashcroft did not violate clearly established law, the question whether he enjoys absolute immunity need not be addressed. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

580 F.\xc2\xa03d 949, reversed and remanded.

\n

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nASHCROFT V. AL-KIDD
\n563 U. S. ____ (2011)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 10-98
\n
\n

JOHN D. ASHCROFT, PETITIONER v. ABDULLAH\nal-KIDD

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[May 31, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We decide whether a former Attorney General enjoys immunity from suit for allegedly authorizing federal prosecutors to obtain valid material-witness warrants for detention of terrorism suspects whom they would otherwise lack probable cause to arrest.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The federal material-witness statute authorizes judges to \xe2\x80\x9corder the arrest of [a] person\xe2\x80\x9d whose testimony \xe2\x80\x9cis material in a criminal proceeding \xe2\x80\xa6 if it is shown that it may become impracticable to secure the presence of the person by subpoena.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73144. Material witnesses enjoy the same constitutional right to pretrial release as other federal detainees, and federal law requires release if their testimony \xe2\x80\x9ccan adequately be secured by deposition, and if further detention is not nec-essary to prevent a failure of justice.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because this case arises from a motion to dismiss, we accept as true the factual allegations in Abdullah al-Kidd\xe2\x80\x99s complaint. The complaint alleges that, in the aftermath of the September 11th terrorist attacks, then-Attorney General John Ashcroft authorized federal prosecutors and law enforcement officials to use the material-witness statute to detain individuals with suspected ties to terrorist organizations. It is alleged that federal officials had no intention of calling most of these individuals as witnesses, and that they were detained, at Ashcroft\xe2\x80\x99s direction, because federal officials suspected them of supporting terrorism but lacked sufficient evidence to charge them with a crime.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is alleged that this pretextual detention policy led\nto the material-witness arrest of al-Kidd, a native-born United States citizen. FBI agents apprehended him in March 2003 as he checked in for a flight to Saudi Arabia. Two days earlier, federal officials had informed a Magistrate Judge that, if al-Kidd boarded his flight, they believed information \xe2\x80\x9ccrucial\xe2\x80\x9d to the prosecution of Sami Omar al-Hussayen would be lost. App. 64. Al-Kidd remained in federal custody for 16 days and on supervised release until al-Hussayen\xe2\x80\x99s trial concluded 14 months later. Prosecutors never called him as a witness.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In March 2005, al-Kidd filed this Bivens action, see Bivens v. Six Unknown Fed. Narcotics Agents, 403 U. S. 388 (1971) to challenge the constitutionality of Ashcroft\xe2\x80\x99s alleged policy; he also asserted several other claims not relevant here against Ashcroft and others. Ashcroft filed a motion to dismiss based on absolute and qualified immunity, which the District Court denied. A divided panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed, holding that the Fourth Amendment prohibits pretextual arrests absent probable cause of criminal wrongdoing, and that Ashcroft could not claim qualified or absolute immunity. See 580 F.\xc2\xa03d 949 (2009).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Bea dissented, 580 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 981, and eight judges dissented from the denial of rehearing en banc, see 598 F.\xc2\xa03d 1129, 1137, 1142 (CA9 2010). We granted certiorari, see 562 U. S. ___ (2010).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Qualified immunity shields federal and state officials from money damages unless a plaintiff pleads facts showing (1) that the official violated a statutory or constitutional right, and (2) that the right was \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the challenged conduct. Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U. S. 800, 818 (1982). We recently reaffirmed that lower courts have discretion to decide which of the two prongs of qualified-immunity analysis to tackle first. See Pearson v. Callahan, 555 U. S. 223, 236 (2009).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Courts should think carefully before expending \xe2\x80\x9cscarce judicial resources\xe2\x80\x9d to resolve difficult and novel questions of constitutional or statutory interpretation that will \xe2\x80\x9chave no effect on the outcome of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 236\xe2\x80\x93237; see id., at 237\xe2\x80\x93242. When, however, a Court of Appeals does address both prongs of qualified-immunity analysis, we have discretion to correct its errors at each step. Although not necessary to reverse an erroneous judgment, doing so ensures that courts do not insulate constitutional decisions at the frontiers of the law from our review or inadvertently undermine the values qualified immunity seeks to promote. The former occurs when the constitutional-law question is wrongly decided; the latter when what is not clearly established is held to be so. In this case, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 analysis at both steps of the qualified-immunity inquiry needs correction.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fourth Amendment protects \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures.\xe2\x80\x9d An arrest, of course, qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9cseizure\xe2\x80\x9d of a \xe2\x80\x9cperson\xe2\x80\x9d under this provision, Dunaway v. New York, 442 U. S. 200, 207\xe2\x80\x93208 (1979), and so must be reasonable under the circumstances. Al-Kidd does not assert that Government officials would have acted unreasonably if they had used a material-witness warrant to arrest him for the purpose of securing his testimony for trial. See Brief for Respondent 16\xe2\x80\x9317; Tr. of Oral Arg. 20\xe2\x80\x9322. He contests, however (and the Court of Appeals here rejected), the reasonableness of using the warrant to detain him as a suspected criminal.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fourth Amendment reasonableness \xe2\x80\x9cis predominantly an objective inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d Edmond, supra, at 47. We ask whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe circumstances, viewed objectively, justify [the challenged] action.\xe2\x80\x9d Scott v. United States, 436 U. S. 128, 138 (1978). If so, that action was reasonable \xe2\x80\x9cwhatever the subjective intent\xe2\x80\x9d motivating the relevant officials. Whren v. United States, 517 U. S. 806, 814 (1996). This approach recognizes that the Fourth Amendment regulates conduct rather than thoughts, Bond v. United States, 529 U. S. 334, 338, n.\xc2\xa02 (2000); and it promotes evenhanded, uniform enforcement of the law, Devenpeck v. Alford, 543 U. S. 146, 153\xe2\x80\x93154 (2004).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Two \xe2\x80\x9climited exception[s]\xe2\x80\x9d to this rule are our special-needs and administrative-search cases, where \xe2\x80\x9cactual motivations\xe2\x80\x9d do matter. United States v. Knights, 534 U. S. 112, 122 (2001) (internal quotation marks omitted). A judicial warrant and probable cause are not needed where the search or seizure is justified by \xe2\x80\x9cspecial needs, beyond the normal need for law enforcement,\xe2\x80\x9d such as the need to deter drug use in public schools, Vernonia School Dist. 47J v. Acton, 515 U. S. 646, 653 (1995) (internal quotation marks omitted), or the need to assure that railroad employees engaged in train operations are not under the influence of drugs or alcohol, Skinner v. Railway Labor Executives\xe2\x80\x99 Assn., 489 U. S. 602 (1989); and where the search or seizure is in execution of an administrative warrant authorizing, for example, an inspection of fire-damaged premises to determine the cause, Michigan v. Clifford, 464 U. S. 287, 294 (1984) (plurality opinion), or an inspection of residential premises to assure compliance with a housing code, Camara v. Municipal Court of City and County of San Francisco, 387 U. S. 523, 535\xe2\x80\x93538 (1967). But those exceptions do not apply where the officer\xe2\x80\x99s purpose is not to attend to the special needs or to the investigation for which the administrative inspection is justified. See Whren, supra, at 811\xe2\x80\x93812. The Government seeks to justify the present arrest on the basis of a properly issued judicial warrant\xe2\x80\x94so that the special-needs and administrative-inspection cases cannot be the basis for a purpose inquiry here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Apart from those cases, we have almost uniformly rejected invitations to probe subjective intent. See Brigham City v. Stuart, 547 U. S. 398, 404 (2006). There is one category of exception, upon which the Court of Appeals principally relied. In Edmond, 531 U. S. 32, we held that the Fourth Amendment could not condone suspicionless vehicle checkpoints set up for the purpose of detecting illegal narcotics. Although we had previously approved vehicle checkpoints set up for the purpose of keeping off the road unlicensed drivers, Delaware v. Prouse, 440 U. S. 648, 663 (1979), or alcohol-impaired drivers, Michigan Dept. of State Police v. Sitz, 496 U. S. 444 (1990); and for the purpose of interdicting those who illegally cross the border, United States v. Martinez-Fuerte, 428 U. S. 543 (1976); we found the drug-detection purpose in Edmond invalidating because it was \xe2\x80\x9cultimately indistinguishable from the general interest in crime control,\xe2\x80\x9d 531 U. S., at 44. In the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 view, Edmond established that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98programmatic purpose\xe2\x80\x99 is relevant to Fourth Amendment analysis of programs of seizures without probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d 580 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 968.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 That was mistaken. It was not the absence of probable cause that triggered the invalidating-purpose inquiry in Edmond. To the contrary, Edmond explicitly said that it would approve checkpoint stops for \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral crime control purposes\xe2\x80\x9d that were based upon merely \xe2\x80\x9csome quantum of individualized suspicion.\xe2\x80\x9d 531 U. S., at 47. Purpose was relevant in Edmond because \xe2\x80\x9cprogrammatic purposes may be relevant to the validity of Fourth Amendment intrusions undertaken pursuant to a general scheme without individualized suspicion,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 45\xe2\x80\x9346 (emphasis added).[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Needless to say, warrantless, \xe2\x80\x9csuspicionless intrusions pursuant to a general scheme,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 47, are far removed from the facts of this case. A warrant issued by a neutral Magistrate Judge authorized al-Kidd\xe2\x80\x99s arrest. The affidavit accompanying the warrant application (as al-Kidd concedes) gave individualized reasons to believe that he was a material witness and that he would soon disappear. The existence of a judicial warrant based on individualized suspicion takes this case outside the domain of not only our special-needs and administrative-search cases, but of Edmond as well.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A warrant based on individualized suspicion[Footnote 2] in fact grants more protection against the malevolent and the incompetent than existed in most of our cases eschewing inquiries into intent. In Whren, 517 U. S., at 813, and Devenpeck, 543 U. S., at 153, we declined to probe the motives behind seizures supported by probable cause but lacking a warrant approved by a detached magistrate. Terry v. Ohio, 392 U. S. 1, 21\xe2\x80\x9322 (1968), and Knights, 534 U. S., at 121\xe2\x80\x93122, applied an objective standard to warrantless searches justified by a lesser showing of reasonable suspicion. We review even some suspicionless searches for objective reasonableness. See Bond, 529 U. S., at 335\xe2\x80\x93336, 338, n.\xc2\xa02. If concerns about improper motives and pretext do not justify subjective inquiries in those less protective contexts, we see no reason to adopt that inquiry here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Al-Kidd would read our cases more narrowly. He asserts that Whren establishes that we ignore subjective intent only when there exists \xe2\x80\x9cprobable cause to believe that a violation of law has occurred,\xe2\x80\x9d 517 U. S., at 811\xe2\x80\x94which was not the case here. That is a distortion of Whren. Our unanimous opinion held that we would not look\nbehind an objectively reasonable traffic stop to determine whether racial profiling or a desire to investigate other potential crimes was the real motive. See id., at 810, 813. In the course of our analysis, we dismissed Whren\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on our inventory-search and administrative-inspection cases by explaining that those cases do not \xe2\x80\x9cendors[e] the principle that ulterior motives can invalidate police conduct that is justifiable on the basis of probable cause to believe that a violation of law has occurred,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 811 But to say that ulterior motives do not in-validate a search that is legitimate because of probable cause to believe a crime has occurred is not to say that it does invalidate all searches that are legitimate for other reasons.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[O]nly an undiscerning reader,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., would think otherwise. We referred to probable cause to believe that a violation of law had occurred because that was the legitimating factor in the case at hand. But the analysis of\nour opinion swept broadly to reject inquiries into motive generally. See id., at 812\xe2\x80\x93815. We remarked that our special-needs and administrative-inspection cases are un-usual in their concern for pretext, and do nothing more than \xe2\x80\x9cexplain that the exemption from the need for probable cause (and warrant), which is accorded to searches made for the purpose of inventory or administrative regulation, is not accorded to searches that are not made for those purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 811\xe2\x80\x93812. And our opinion emphasized that we had at that time (prior to Edmond) rejected every request to examine subjective intent outside the narrow context of special needs and administrative inspections. See 517 U. S., at 812. Thus, al-Kidd\xe2\x80\x99s approach adds an \xe2\x80\x9conly\xe2\x80\x9d to a sentence plucked from the Whren opinion, and then elevates that sentence (as so revised) over the remainder of the opinion, and over the consistent holdings of our other cases.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because al-Kidd concedes that individualized suspicion supported the issuance of the material-witness arrest warrant; and does not assert that his arrest would have been unconstitutional absent the alleged pretextual use of the warrant; we find no Fourth Amendment violation.[Footnote 3] Efficient[Footnote 4] and evenhanded application of the law demands that we look to whether the arrest is objectively justified, rather than to the motive of the arresting officer.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A Government official\xe2\x80\x99s conduct violates clearly established law when, at the time of the challenged conduct, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he contours of [a] right [are] sufficiently clear\xe2\x80\x9d that every \xe2\x80\x9creasonable official would have understood that what he is doing violates that right.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Creighton, 483 U. S. 635, 640 (1987). We do not require a case directly on point, but existing precedent must have placed the statutory or constitutional question beyond debate. See ibid.; Malley v. Briggs, 475 U. S. 335, 341 (1986). The constitutional question in this case falls far short of that threshold.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the time of al-Kidd\xe2\x80\x99s arrest, not a single judicial opinion had held that pretext could render an objectively reasonable arrest pursuant to a material-witness warrant unconstitutional. A district-court opinion had suggested, in a footnoted dictum devoid of supporting citation, that using such a warrant for preventive detention of suspects \xe2\x80\x9cis an illegitimate use of the statute\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94implying (we accept for the sake of argument) that the detention would therefore be unconstitutional. United States v. Awadallah, 202 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 55, 77, n.\xc2\xa028 (SDNY 2002). The Court of Appeals thought nothing could \xe2\x80\x9chave given John Ashcroft fair[er] warning\xe2\x80\x9d that his conduct violated the Fourth Amendment, because the footnoted dictum \xe2\x80\x9ccall[ed] out Ashcroft by name\xe2\x80\x9d! 580 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 972\xe2\x80\x93973 (internal quotation marks omitted; emphasis added). We will indulge the assumption (though it does not seem to us realistic) that Justice Department lawyers bring to the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s personal attention all district judges\xe2\x80\x99 footnoted speculations that boldly \xe2\x80\x9ccall him out by name.\xe2\x80\x9d On that assumption, would it prove that for him (and for him only?) it became clearly established that pretextual use of the material-witness statute rendered the arrest unconstitutional? An extraordinary proposition. Even a district judge\xe2\x80\x99s ipse dixit of a holding is not \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolling authority\xe2\x80\x9d in any jurisdiction, much less in the entire United States; and his ipse dixit of a footnoted dictum falls far short of what is necessary absent controlling authority: a robust \xe2\x80\x9cconsensus of cases of persuasive authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Wilson v. Layne, 526 U. S. 603, 617 (1999).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 other cases \xe2\x80\x9cclearly establishing\xe2\x80\x9d the constitutional violation are, of course, those we rejected as irrelevant in our discussion of whether there was any constitutional violation at all. And the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 reference to those cases here makes the same error of assuming that purpose is only disregarded when there is probable cause to suspect a violation of law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals also found clearly established law lurking in the broad \xe2\x80\x9chistory and purposes of the Fourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d 580 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 971. We have repeatedly told courts\xe2\x80\x94and the Ninth Circuit in particular, see Brosseau v. Haugen, 543 U. S. 194, 198\xe2\x80\x93199 (2004) (per curiam)\xe2\x80\x94not to define clearly established law at a high level of generality. See also, e.g., Wilson, supra, at 615; Anderson, supra, at 639\xe2\x80\x93640; cf. Sawyer v. Smith, 497 U. S. 227, 236 (1990). The general proposition, for example, that an unreasonable search or seizure violates the Fourth Amendment is of little help in determining whether the violative nature of particular conduct is clearly established. See Saucier v. Katz, 533 U. S. 194, 201\xe2\x80\x93202 (2001); Wilson, supra, at 615.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The same is true of the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 broad historical assertions. The Fourth Amendment was a response to the English Crown\xe2\x80\x99s use of general warrants, which often allowed royal officials to search and seize whatever and whomever they pleased while investigating crimes or affronts to the Crown. See Stanford v. Texas, 379 U. S. 476, 481\xe2\x80\x93485 (1965). According to the Court of Appeals, Ashcroft should have seen that a pretextual warrant similarly \xe2\x80\x9cgut[s] the substantive protections of the Fourth Amendmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d and allows the State \xe2\x80\x9cto arrest upon the executive\xe2\x80\x99s mere suspicion.\xe2\x80\x9d 580 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 972.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ashcroft must be forgiven for missing the parallel, which escapes us as well. The principal evil of the general warrant was addressed by the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s particularity requirement, Stanford, supra, at 485, which Ashcroft\xe2\x80\x99s alleged policy made no effort to evade. The warrant authorizing al-Kidd\xe2\x80\x99s arrest named al-Kidd and only al-Kidd. It might be argued, perhaps, that when, in response to the English abuses, the Fourth Amendment said that warrants could only issue \xe2\x80\x9con probable cause\xe2\x80\x9d it meant only probable cause to suspect a violation of law, and not probable cause to believe that the individual named in the warrant was a material witness. But that would make all arrests pursuant to material-witness warrants unconstitutional, whether pretextual or not\xe2\x80\x94and that is not the position taken by al-Kidd in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While featuring a District Court\xe2\x80\x99s footnoted dictum, the Court of Appeals made no mention of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s affirmation in Edmond of the \xe2\x80\x9cpredominan[t]\xe2\x80\x9d rule that reasonableness is an objective inquiry, 531 U. S., at 47. Nor did it mention Whren\xe2\x80\x99s and Knights\xe2\x80\x99 statements that subjective intent mattered in a very limited subset of our Fourth Amendment cases; or Terry\xe2\x80\x99s objective evaluation of investigatory searches premised on reasonable suspicion rather than probable cause; or Bond\xe2\x80\x99s objective evaluation of a suspicionless investigatory search. The Court of Appeals seems to have cherry-picked the aspects of our opinions that gave colorable support to the proposition that the unconstitutionality of the action here was clearly established.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Qualified immunity gives government officials breathing room to make reasonable but mistaken judgments about open legal questions. When properly applied, it protects \xe2\x80\x9call but the plainly incompetent or those who knowingly violate the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Malley, 475 U. S., at 341. Ashcroft deserves neither label, not least because eight Court of Appeals judges agreed with his judgment in a case of first impression. See Wilson, supra, at 618. He deserves qualified immunity even assuming\xe2\x80\x94contrafactually\xe2\x80\x94that his alleged detention policy violated the Fourth Amendment.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that an objectively reasonable arrest and detention of a material witness pursuant to a validly obtained warrant cannot be challenged as unconstitutional on the basis of allegations that the arresting authority had an improper motive. Because Ashcroft did not violate clearly established law, we need not address the more difficult question whether he enjoys absolute immunity. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join the opinion of the Court in full. In holding that the Attorney General could be liable for damages based on an unprecedented constitutional rule, the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit disregarded the purposes of the doc-trine of qualified immunity. This concurring opinion makes two additional observations.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding is limited to the arguments presented by the parties and leaves unresolved whether the Government\xe2\x80\x99s use of the Material Witness Statute in this case was lawful. See ante, at 8 (noting that al-Kidd \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not assert that his arrest would have been unconstitutional absent the alleged pretextual use of the warrant\xe2\x80\x9d). Under the statute, a Magistrate Judge may issue a warrant to arrest someone as a material witness upon a showing by affidavit that \xe2\x80\x9cthe testimony of a person is material in a criminal proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthat it may become impracticable to secure the presence of the person by subpoena.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73144. The scope of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s lawful \nauthorization is uncertain. For example, a law-abiding citizen might observe a crime during the days or weeks before a scheduled flight abroad. It is unclear whether those facts alone might allow police to obtain a material witness warrant on the ground that it \xe2\x80\x9cmay become impracticable\xe2\x80\x9d to secure the person\xe2\x80\x99s presence by subpoena. Ibid. The question becomes more difficult if one further assumes the traveler would be willing to testify if asked; and more difficult still if one supposes that authorities delay obtaining or executing the warrant until the traveler has arrived at the airport. These possibilities resemble the facts in this case. See ante, at 2.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In considering these issues, it is important to bear in mind that the Material Witness Statute might not provide for the issuance of warrants within the meaning of the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Warrant Clause. The typical arrest warrant is based on probable cause that the arrestee has committed a crime; but that is not the standard for the issuance of warrants under the Material Witness Statute. See ante, at 11 (reserving the possibility that probable cause for purposes of the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Warrant Clause means \xe2\x80\x9conly probable cause to suspect a violation of law\xe2\x80\x9d). If material witness warrants do not qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cWarrants\xe2\x80\x9d under the Fourth Amendment, then material witness arrests might still be governed by the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s separate reasonableness requirement for seizures of the person. See United States v. Watson, 423 U. S. 411 (1976). Given the difficulty of these issues, the Court is correct to address only the legal theory put before it, without further exploring when material witness arrests might be consistent with statutory and constitutional requirements.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The fact that the Attorney General holds a high office in the Government must inform what law is clearly established for the purposes of this case. Mitchell v. Forsyth, 472 U. S. 511, 525 (1985). Some federal officers perform their functions in a single jurisdiction, say within the con-fines of one State or one federal judicial district. They \xe2\x80\x9creasonably can anticipate when their conduct may give rise to liability for damages\xe2\x80\x9d and so are expected to adjust their behavior in accordance with local precedent. Davis v. Scherer, 468 U. S. 183, 195 (1984); see also Anderson v. Creighton, 483 U. S. 635, 639\xe2\x80\x93640 (1987). In contrast the Attorney General occupies a national office and so sets policies implemented in many jurisdictions throughout the country. The official with responsibilities in many jurisdictions may face ambiguous and sometimes inconsistent sources of decisional law. While it may be clear that one Court of Appeals has approved a certain course of conduct, other Courts of Appeals may have disapproved it, or at least reserved the issue.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When faced with inconsistent legal rules in different jurisdictions, national officeholders should be given some deference for qualified immunity purposes, at least if they implement policies consistent with the governing law of the jurisdiction where the action is taken. As we have explained, qualified immunity is lost when plaintiffs point either to \xe2\x80\x9ccases of controlling authority in their jurisdiction at the time of the incident\xe2\x80\x9d or to \xe2\x80\x9ca consensus of cases of persuasive authority such that a reasonable officer could not have believed that his actions were lawful.\xe2\x80\x9d Wilson v. Layne, 526 U. S. 603, 617 (1999); see also ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. These standards ensure the officer has \xe2\x80\x9cfair and clear warning\xe2\x80\x9d of what the Constitution requires. United States v. Lanier, 520 U. S. 259, 271 (1997).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A national officeholder intent on retaining qualified immunity need not abide by the most stringent standard adopted anywhere in the United States. And the national officeholder need not guess at when a relatively small set of appellate precedents have established a binding legal rule. If national officeholders were subject to personal liability whenever they confronted disagreement among appellate courts, those officers would be deterred from full use of their legal authority. The consequences of that deterrence must counsel caution by the Judicial Branch, particularly in the area of national security. See Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 21). Furthermore, too expansive a view of \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established law\xe2\x80\x9d would risk giving local judicial determinations the effect of rules with de facto national significance, contrary to the normal process of ordered appellate review.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The proceedings in this case illustrate these concerns. The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit appears to have reasoned that a Federal District Court sitting in New York had authority to establish a legal rule binding on \nthe Attorney General and, therefore, on federal law-enforcement operations conducted nationwide. See 580 F.\xc2\xa03d 949, 972\xe2\x80\x93973 (2009). Indeed, this case involves a material witness warrant issued in Boise, Idaho, and an arrest near Washington, D.\xc2\xa0C. Of course, district court decisions are not precedential to this extent. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. But nationwide security operations should not have to grind to a halt even when an appellate court finds those operations unconstitutional. The doctrine of qualified immunity does not so constrain national officeholders entrusted with urgent responsibilities.

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that the term \xe2\x80\x9cindividualized suspicion\xe2\x80\x9d is more commonly associated with \xe2\x80\x9cknow[ing] something about [a] crime\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cthrowing \xe2\x80\xa6 a surprise birthday party\xe2\x80\x9d than with criminal suspects, ante, at 6, n.\xc2\xa02 (internal quotation marks omitted), is hardly credible. The import of the term in legal argot is not genuinely debatable. When the evening news reports that a murder \xe2\x80\x9csuspect\xe2\x80\x9d is on the loose, the viewer is meant to be on the lookout for the perpetrator, not the witness. Ashcroft understood the term as lawyers commonly do: He spoke of detaining material witnesses as a means to \xe2\x80\x9ctak[e] suspected terrorists off the street.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 41 (internal quotation marks omitted).
