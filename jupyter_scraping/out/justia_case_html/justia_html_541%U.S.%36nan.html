CRAWFORD v. WASHINGTON

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of washington

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x939410. Argued November 10, 2003\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 8, 2004

\n

Petitioner was tried for assault and attempted murder. The State sought to introduce a recorded statement that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s wife Sylvia had made during police interrogation, as evidence that the stabbing was not in self-defense. Sylvia did not testify at trial because of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s marital privilege. Petitioner argued that admitting the evidence would violate his Sixth Amendment right to be \xe2\x80\x9cconfronted with the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d Under Ohio v. Roberts, 448 U. S. 56, that right does not bar admission of an unavailable witness\xe2\x80\x99s statement against a criminal defendant if the statement bears \xe2\x80\x9cadequate \xe2\x80\x98indicia of reliability,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d a test met when the evidence either falls within a \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly rooted hearsay exception\xe2\x80\x9d or bears \xe2\x80\x9cparticularized guarantees of trustworthiness.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 66. The trial court admitted the statement on the latter ground. The State Supreme Court upheld the conviction, deeming the statement reliable because it was nearly identical to, i.e., interlocked with, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s own statement to the police, in that both were ambiguous as to whether the victim had drawn a weapon before petitioner assaulted him.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The State\xe2\x80\x99s use of Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s statement violated the Confrontation Clause because, where testimonial statements are at issue, the only indicium of reliability sufficient to satisfy constitutional demands is confrontation. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9333.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Confrontation Clause\xe2\x80\x99s text does not alone resolve this case, so this Court turns to the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s historical background. That history supports two principles. First, the principal evil at which the Clause was directed was the civil-law mode of criminal procedure, particularly the use of ex parte examinations as evidence against the accused. The Clause\xe2\x80\x99s primary object is testimonial hearsay, and interrogations by law enforcement officers fall squarely within that class. Second, the Framers would not have allowed admission of testimonial statements of a witness who did not appear at trial unless he was unavailable to testify and the defendant had had a prior opportunity for cross-examination. English authorities and early state cases indicate that this was the common law at the time of the founding. And the \xe2\x80\x9cright \xe2\x80\xa6 to be confronted with the witnesses against him,\xe2\x80\x9d Amdt. 6, is most naturally read as a reference to the common-law right of confrontation, admitting only those exceptions established at the time of the founding. See Mattox v. United States, 156 U. S. 237, 243. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions have generally remained faithful to the Confrontation Clause\xe2\x80\x99s original meaning. See, e.g., Mattox, supra. Pp.\xc2\xa021\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0However, the same cannot be said of the rationales of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s more recent decisions. See Roberts, supra, at 66. The Roberts test departs from historical principles because it admits statements consisting of ex parte testimony upon a mere reliability finding. Pp. 24\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0The Confrontation Clause commands that reliability be assessed in a particular manner: by testing in the crucible of cross-examination. Roberts allows a jury to hear evidence, untested by the adversary process, based on a mere judicial determination of reliability, thus replacing the constitutionally prescribed method of assessing reliability with a wholly foreign one. Pp. 25\xe2\x80\x9327.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(e)\xc2\xa0Roberts\xe2\x80\x99 framework is unpredictable. Whether a statement is deemed reliable depends on which factors a judge considers and how much weight he accords each of them. However, the unpardonable vice of the Roberts test is its demonstrated capacity to admit core testimonial statements that the Confrontation Clause plainly meant to exclude. Pp. 27\xe2\x80\x9330.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(f)\xc2\xa0The instant case is a self-contained demonstration of Roberts\xe2\x80\x99 unpredictable and inconsistent application. It also reveals Roberts\xe2\x80\x99 failure to interpret the Constitution in a way that secures its intended constraint on judicial discretion. The Constitution prescribes the procedure for determining the reliability of testimony in criminal trials, and this Court, no less than the state courts, lacks authority to replace it with one of its own devising. Pp.\xc2\xa030\xe2\x80\x9332.

\n

147 Wash. 2d 424, 54 P.\xc2\xa03d 656, reversed and remanded.

MICHAEL D. CRAWFORD, PETITIONER v.\nWASHINGTON

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of washington

\n

[March 8, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Michael Crawford stabbed a man who allegedly tried to rape his wife, Sylvia. At his trial, the State played for the jury Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s tape-recorded statement to the police describing the stabbing, even though he had no opportunity for cross-examination. The Washington Supreme Court upheld petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction after determining that Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s statement was reliable. The question presented is whether this procedure complied with the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee that, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right \xe2\x80\xa6 to be confronted with the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On August 5, 1999, Kenneth Lee was stabbed at his apartment. Police arrested petitioner later that night. After giving petitioner and his wife Miranda warnings, detectives interrogated each of them twice. Petitioner eventually confessed that he and Sylvia had gone in search of Lee because he was upset over an earlier incident in which Lee had tried to rape her. The two had found Lee at his apartment, and a fight ensued in which Lee was stabbed in the torso and petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s hand was cut.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner gave the following account of the fight:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Okay. Did you ever see anything in [Lee\xe2\x80\x99s] hands?

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cA.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I think so, but I\xe2\x80\x99m not positive.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Okay, when you think so, what do you mean by that?

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cA.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I coulda swore I seen him goin\xe2\x80\x99 for somethin\xe2\x80\x99 before, right before everything happened. He was like reachin\xe2\x80\x99, fiddlin\xe2\x80\x99 around down here and stuff \xe2\x80\xa6 and I just \xe2\x80\xa6 I don\xe2\x80\x99t know, I think, this is just a possibility, but I think, I think that he pulled somethin\xe2\x80\x99 out and I grabbed for it and that\xe2\x80\x99s how I got cut \xe2\x80\xa6 but I\xe2\x80\x99m not positive. I, I, my mind goes blank when things like this happen. I mean, I just, I remember things wrong, I remember things that just doesn\xe2\x80\x99t, don\xe2\x80\x99t make sense to me later.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 155 (punctuation added).

\n

\nSylvia generally corroborated petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s story about the events leading up to the fight, but her account of the fight itself was arguably different\xe2\x80\x94particularly with respect to whether Lee had drawn a weapon before petitioner assaulted him:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Did Kenny do anything to fight back from this assault?

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cA.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(pausing) I know he reached into his pocket \xe2\x80\xa6 or somethin\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 I don\xe2\x80\x99t know what.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After he was stabbed?

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cA.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0He saw Michael coming up. He lifted his hand \xe2\x80\xa6 his chest open, he might [have] went to go strike his hand out or something and then (inaudible).

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Okay, you, you gotta speak up.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cA.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Okay, he lifted his hand over his head maybe to strike Michael\xe2\x80\x99s hand down or something and then he put his hands in his \xe2\x80\xa6 put his right hand in his right pocket \xe2\x80\xa6 took a step back \xe2\x80\xa6 Michael proceeded to stab him \xe2\x80\xa6 then his hands were like \xe2\x80\xa6 how do you explain this \xe2\x80\xa6 open arms \xe2\x80\xa6 with his hands open and he fell down \xe2\x80\xa6 and we ran (describing subject holding hands open, palms toward assailant).

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Okay, when he\xe2\x80\x99s standing there with his open hands, you\xe2\x80\x99re talking about Kenny, correct?

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cA.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Yeah, after, after the fact, yes.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Did you see anything in his hands at that point?

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cA.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(pausing) um um (no).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 137 (punctuation added).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State charged petitioner with assault and attempted murder. At trial, he claimed self-defense. Sylvia did not testify because of the state marital privilege, which generally bars a spouse from testifying without the other spouse\xe2\x80\x99s consent. See Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa75.60.060(1) (1994). In Washington, this privilege does not extend to a spouse\xe2\x80\x99s out-of-court statements admissible under a hearsay exception, see State v. Burden, 120 Wash. 2d 371, 377, 841 P.\xc2\xa02d 758, 761 (1992), so the State sought to introduce Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s tape-recorded statements to the police as evidence that the stabbing was not in self-defense. Noting that Sylvia had admitted she led petitioner to Lee\xe2\x80\x99s apartment and thus had facilitated the assault, the State invoked the hearsay exception for statements against penal interest, Wash. Rule Evid. 804(b)(3) (2003).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner countered that, state law notwithstanding, admitting the evidence would violate his federal constitutional right to be \xe2\x80\x9cconfronted with the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d Amdt. 6. According to our description of that right in Ohio v. Roberts, 448 U. S. 56 (1980), it does not bar admission of an unavailable witness\xe2\x80\x99s statement against a criminal defendant if the statement bears \xe2\x80\x9cadequate \xe2\x80\x98indicia of reliability.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 66. To meet that test, evidence must either fall within a \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly rooted hearsay exception\xe2\x80\x9d or bear \xe2\x80\x9cparticularized guarantees of trustworthiness.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The trial court here admitted the statement on the latter ground, offering several reasons why it was trustworthy: Sylvia was not shifting blame but rather corroborating her husband\xe2\x80\x99s story that he acted in self-defense or \xe2\x80\x9cjustified reprisal\xe2\x80\x9d; she had direct knowledge as an eyewitness; she was describing recent events; and she was being questioned by a \xe2\x80\x9cneutral\xe2\x80\x9d law enforcement officer. App. 76\xe2\x80\x9377. The prosecution played the tape for the jury and relied on it in closing, arguing that it was \xe2\x80\x9cdamning evidence\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ccompletely refutes [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] claim of self-defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 468 (Oct. 21, 1999). The jury convicted petitioner of assault.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Washington Court of Appeals reversed. It applied a nine-factor test to determine whether Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s statement bore particularized guarantees of trustworthiness, and noted several reasons why it did not: The statement contradicted one she had previously given; it was made in response to specific questions; and at one point she admitted she had shut her eyes during the stabbing. The court considered and rejected the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s statement was reliable because it coincided with petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s to such a degree that the two \xe2\x80\x9cinterlocked.\xe2\x80\x9d The court determined that, although the two statements agreed about the events leading up to the stabbing, they differed on the issue crucial to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s self-defense claim: \xe2\x80\x9c[Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] version asserts that Lee may have had something in his hand when he stabbed him; but Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s version has Lee grabbing for something only after he has been stabbed.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 32.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Washington Supreme Court reinstated the conviction, unanimously concluding that, although Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s statement did not fall under a firmly rooted hearsay exception, it bore guarantees of trustworthiness: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[W]hen a codefendant\xe2\x80\x99s confession is virtually identical [to, i.e., interlocks with,] that of a defendant, it may be deemed reliable.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 147 Wash. 2d 424, 437, 54 P.\xc2\xa03d 656, 663 (2002) (quoting State v. Rice, 120 Wash. 2d 549, 570, 844 P.\xc2\xa02d 416, 427 (1993)). The court explained:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cAlthough the Court of Appeals concluded that the statements were contradictory, upon closer inspection they appear to overlap\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[B]oth of the Crawfords\xe2\x80\x99 statements indicate that Lee was possibly grabbing for a weapon, but they are equally unsure when this event may have taken place. They are also equally unsure how Michael received the cut on his hand, leading the court to question when, if ever, Lee possessed a weapon. In this respect they overlap.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[N]either Michael nor Sylvia clearly stated that Lee had a weapon in hand from which Michael was simply defending himself. And it is this omission by both that interlocks the statements and makes Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s statement reliable.\xe2\x80\x9d 147 Wash. 2d, at 438\xe2\x80\x93\n439, 54 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 664 (internal quotation marks omitted).[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to determine whether the State\xe2\x80\x99s use of Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s statement violated the Confrontation Clause. 539 U. S. 914 (2003).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Confrontation Clause provides that, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right \xe2\x80\xa6 to be confronted with the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d We have held that this bedrock procedural guarantee applies to both federal and state prosecutions. Pointer v. Texas, 380 U. S. 400, 406 (1965). As noted above, Roberts says that an unavailable witness\xe2\x80\x99s out-of-court statement may be admitted so long as it has adequate indicia of reliability\xe2\x80\x94i.e., falls within a \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly rooted hearsay exception\xe2\x80\x9d or bears \xe2\x80\x9cparticularized guarantees of trustworthiness.\xe2\x80\x9d 448 U. S., at 66. Petitioner argues that this test strays from the original meaning of the Confrontation Clause and urges us to reconsider it.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s text does not alone resolve this case. One could plausibly read \xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses against\xe2\x80\x9d a defendant to mean those who actually testify at trial, cf. Woodsides v. State, 3 Miss. 655, 664\xe2\x80\x93665 (1837), those whose statements are offered at trial, see 3 J. Wigmore, Evidence \xc2\xa71397, p. 104 (2d ed. 1923) (hereinafter Wigmore), or something in-between, see infra, at\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9316. We must therefore turn to the historical background of the Clause to understand its meaning.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The right to confront one\xe2\x80\x99s accusers is a concept that dates back to Roman times. See Coy v. Iowa, 487 U. S. 1012, 1015 (1988); Herrmann & Speer, Facing the Accuser: Ancient and Medieval Precursors of the Confrontation Clause, 34 Va. J. Int\xe2\x80\x99l L. 481 (1994). The founding generation\xe2\x80\x99s immediate source of the concept, however, was the common law. English common law has long differed from continental civil law in regard to the manner in which witnesses give testimony in criminal trials. The common-law tradition is one of live testimony in court subject to adversarial testing, while the civil law condones examination in private by judicial officers. See 3 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 373\xe2\x80\x93374 (1768).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nonetheless, England at times adopted elements of the civil-law practice. Justices of the peace or other officials examined suspects and witnesses before trial. These examinations were sometimes read in court in lieu of live testimony, a practice that \xe2\x80\x9coccasioned frequent demands by the prisoner to have his \xe2\x80\x98accusers,\xe2\x80\x99 i.e. the witnesses against him, brought before him face to face.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 J. Stephen, History of the Criminal Law of England 326 (1883). In some cases, these demands were refused. See 9 W. Holdsworth, History of English Law 216\xe2\x80\x93217, 228 (3d ed. 1944); e.g., Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 2 How. St. Tr. 1, 15\xe2\x80\x9316, 24 (1603); Throckmorton\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 1 How. St. Tr. 869, 875\xe2\x80\x93876 (1554); cf. Lilburn\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 3 How. St. Tr. 1315, 1318\xe2\x80\x931322, 1329 (Star Chamber 1637).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pretrial examinations became routine under two statutes passed during the reign of Queen Mary in the 16th century, 1 & 2 Phil. & M., c. 13 (1554), and 2 & 3 id., c. 10 (1555). These Marian bail and committal statutes required justices of the peace to examine suspects and witnesses in felony cases and to certify the results to the court. It is doubtful that the original purpose of the examinations was to produce evidence admissible at trial. See J. Langbein, Prosecuting Crime in the Renaissance 21\xe2\x80\x9334 (1974). Whatever the original purpose, however, they came to be used as evidence in some cases, see 2 M. Hale, Pleas of the Crown 284 (1736), resulting in an adoption of continental procedure. See 4 Holdsworth, supra, at 528\xe2\x80\x93530.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The most notorious instances of civil-law examination occurred in the great political trials of the 16th and 17th centuries. One such was the 1603 trial of Sir Walter Raleigh for treason. Lord Cobham, Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s alleged accomplice, had implicated him in an examination before the Privy Council and in a letter. At Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s trial, these were read to the jury. Raleigh argued that Cobham had lied to save himself: \xe2\x80\x9cCobham is absolutely in the King\xe2\x80\x99s mercy; to excuse me cannot avail him; by accusing me he may hope for favour.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 D. Jardine, Criminal Trials 435 (1832). Suspecting that Cobham would recant, Raleigh demanded that the judges call him to appear, arguing that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Proof of the Common Law is by witness and jury: let Cobham be here, let him speak it. Call my accuser before my face \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 How. St. Tr., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. The judges refused, id., at 24, and, despite Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s protestations that he was being tried \xe2\x80\x9cby the Spanish Inquisition,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 15, the jury convicted, and Raleigh was sentenced to death.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One of Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s trial judges later lamented that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the justice of England has never been so degraded and injured as by the condemnation of Sir Walter Raleigh.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Jardine, supra, at 520. Through a series of statutory and judicial reforms, English law developed a right of confrontation that limited these abuses. For example, treason statutes required witnesses to confront the accused \xe2\x80\x9cface to face\xe2\x80\x9d at his arraignment. E.g., 13 Car. 2, c. 1, \xc2\xa75 (1661); see 1 Hale, supra, at 306. Courts, meanwhile, developed relatively strict rules of unavailability, admitting examinations only if the witness was demonstrably unable to testify in person. See Lord Morley\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 6 How. St. Tr. 769, 770\xe2\x80\x93771 (H. L. 1666); 2 Hale, supra, at 284; 1 Stephen, supra, at 358. Several authorities also stated that a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s confession could be admitted only against himself, and not against others he implicated. See 2 W. Hawkins, Pleas of the Crown c. 46, \xc2\xa73, pp. 603\xe2\x80\x93604 (T. Leach 6th ed. 1787); 1 Hale, supra, at 585, n. (k); 1 G. Gilbert, Evidence 216 (C. Lofft ed. 1791); cf. Tong\xe2\x80\x99s Case, Kel. J. 17, 18, 84 Eng. Rep. 1061, 1062 (1662) (treason). But see King v. Westbeer, 1 Leach 12, 168 Eng. Rep. 108, 109 (1739).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One recurring question was whether the admissibility of an unavailable witness\xe2\x80\x99s pretrial examination depended on whether the defendant had had an opportunity to cross-examine him. In 1696, the Court of King\xe2\x80\x99s Bench answered this question in the affirmative, in the widely reported misdemeanor libel case of King v. Paine, 5 Mod. 163, 87 Eng. Rep. 584. The court ruled that, even though a witness was dead, his examination was not admissible where \xe2\x80\x9cthe defendant not being present when [it was] taken before the mayor \xe2\x80\xa6 had lost the benefit of a cross-examination.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 165, 87 Eng. Rep., at 585. The question was also debated at length during the infamous proceedings against Sir John Fenwick on a bill of attainder. Fenwick\xe2\x80\x99s counsel objected to admitting the examination of a witness who had been spirited away, on the ground that Fenwick had had no opportunity to cross-examine. See Fenwick\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 13 How. St. Tr. 537, 591\xe2\x80\x93592 (H. C. 1696) (Powys) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]hat which they would offer is something that Mr. Goodman hath sworn when he was examined \xe2\x80\xa6 ; sir J. F. not being present or privy, and no opportunity given to cross-examine the person; and I conceive that cannot be offered as evidence \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 592 (Shower) (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]o deposition of a person can be read, though beyond sea, unless in cases where the party it is to be read against was privy to the examination, and might have cross-examined him \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [O]ur constitution is, that the person shall see his accuser\xe2\x80\x9d). The examination was nonetheless admitted on a closely divided vote after several of those present opined that the common-law rules of procedure did not apply to parliamentary attainder proceedings\xe2\x80\x94one speaker even admitting that the evidence would normally be inadmissible. See id., at 603\xe2\x80\x93604 (Williamson); id., at 604\xe2\x80\x93605 (Chancellor of the Exchequer); id., at 607; 3 Wigmore \xc2\xa71364, at 22\xe2\x80\x9323, n. 54. Fenwick was condemned, but the proceedings \xe2\x80\x9cmust have burned into the general consciousness the vital importance of the rule securing the right of cross-examination.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa71364, at 22; cf. Carmell v. Texas, 529 U. S. 513, 526\xe2\x80\x93530 (2000).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Paine had settled the rule requiring a prior opportunity for cross-examination as a matter of common law, but some doubts remained over whether the Marian statutes prescribed an exception to it in felony cases. The statutes did not identify the circumstances under which examinations were admissible, see 1 & 2 Phil. & M., c. 13 (1554); 2 & 3 id., c. 10 (1555), and some inferred that no prior opportunity for cross-examination was required. See Westbeer, supra, at 12, 168 Eng. Rep., at 109; compare Fenwick\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 13 How. St. Tr., at 596 (Sloane), with id., at 602 (Musgrave). Many who expressed this view acknowledged that it meant the statutes were in derogation of the common law. See King v. Eriswell, 3 T. R. 707, 710, 100 Eng. Rep. 815, 817 (K. B. 1790) (Grose, J.) (dicta); id., at 722\xe2\x80\x93723, 100 Eng. Rep., at 823\xe2\x80\x93824 (Kenyon, C. J.) (same); compare 1 Gilbert, Evidence, at 215 (admissible only \xe2\x80\x9cby Force \xe2\x80\x98of the Statute\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d), with id., at 65. Nevertheless, by 1791 (the year the Sixth Amendment was ratified), courts were applying the cross-examination rule even to examinations by justices of the peace in felony cases. See King v. Dingler, 2 Leach 561, 562\xe2\x80\x93563, 168 Eng. Rep. 383, 383\xe2\x80\x93384 (1791); King v. Woodcock, 1 Leach 500, 502\xe2\x80\x93504, 168 Eng. Rep. 352, 353 (1789); cf. King v. Radbourne, 1 Leach 457, 459\xe2\x80\x93461, 168 Eng. Rep. 330, 331\xe2\x80\x93332 (1787); 3 Wigmore \xc2\xa71364, at 23. Early 19th-century treatises confirm that requirement. See 1 T. Starkie, Evidence 95 (1826); 2 id., at 484\xe2\x80\x93492; T. Peake, Evidence 63\xe2\x80\x9364 (3d ed. 1808). When Parliament amended the statutes in 1848 to make the requirement explicit, see 11 & 12 Vict., c. 42, \xc2\xa717, the change merely \xe2\x80\x9cintroduced in terms\xe2\x80\x9d what was already afforded the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cby the equitable construction of the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Queen v. Beeston, 29 Eng. L. & Eq. R. 527, 529 (Ct.\xc2\xa0Crim.\xc2\xa0App. 1854) (Jervis, C.\xc2\xa0J.).[Footnote 2]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Controversial examination practices were also used in the Colonies. Early in the 18th century, for example, the Virginia Council protested against the Governor for having \xe2\x80\x9cprivately issued several commissions to examine witnesses against particular men ex parte,\xe2\x80\x9d complaining that \xe2\x80\x9cthe person accused is not admitted to be confronted with, or defend himself against his defamers.\xe2\x80\x9d A Memorial Concerning the Maladministrations of His Excellency Francis Nicholson, reprinted in 9 English Historical Documents 253, 257 (D. Douglas ed. 1955). A decade before the Revolution, England gave jurisdiction over Stamp Act offenses to the admiralty courts, which followed civil-law rather than common-law procedures and thus routinely took testimony by deposition or private judicial examination. See 5 Geo. 3, c. 12, \xc2\xa757 (1765); Pollitt, The Right of Confrontation: Its History and Modern Dress, 8 J. Pub. L. 381, 396\xe2\x80\x93397 (1959). Colonial representatives protested that the Act subverted their rights \xe2\x80\x9cby extending the jurisdiction of the courts of admiralty beyond its ancient limits.\xe2\x80\x9d Resolutions of the Stamp Act Congress \xc2\xa78th (Oct. 19, 1765), reprinted in Sources of Our Liberties 270, 271 (R. Perry & J. Cooper eds. 1959). John Adams, defending a merchant in a high-profile admiralty case, argued: \xe2\x80\x9cExaminations of witnesses upon Interrogatories, are only by the Civil Law. Interrogatories are unknown at common Law, and Englishmen and common Lawyers have an aversion to them if not an Abhorrence of them.\xe2\x80\x9d Draft of Argument in Sewall v. Hancock (1768\xe2\x80\x931769), in 2 Legal Papers of John Adams 194, 207 (K. Wroth & H. Zobel eds. 1965).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Many declarations of rights adopted around the time of the Revolution guaranteed a right of confrontation. See Virginia Declaration of Rights \xc2\xa78 (1776); Pennsylvania Declaration of Rights \xc2\xa7IX (1776); Delaware Declaration of Rights \xc2\xa714 (1776); Maryland Declaration of Rights \xc2\xa7XIX (1776); North Carolina Declaration of Rights \xc2\xa7VII (1776); Vermont Declaration of Rights Ch. I, \xc2\xa7X (1777); Massachusetts Declaration of Rights \xc2\xa7XII (1780); New Hampshire Bill of Rights \xc2\xa7XV (1783), all reprinted in 1 B. Schwartz, The Bill of Rights: A Documentary History 235, 265, 278, 282, 287, 323, 342, 377 (1971). The proposed Federal Constitution, however, did not. At the Massachusetts ratifying convention, Abraham Holmes objected to this omission precisely on the ground that it would lead to civil-law practices: \xe2\x80\x9cThe mode of trial is altogether indetermined; \xe2\x80\xa6 whether [the defendant] is to be allowed to confront the witnesses, and have the advantage of cross-examination, we are not yet told\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [W]e shall find Congress possessed of powers enabling them to institute judicatories little less inauspicious than a certain tribunal in Spain, \xe2\x80\xa6 the Inquisition.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Debates on the Federal Constitution 110\xe2\x80\x93111 (J. Elliot 2d ed. 1863). Similarly, a prominent Antifederalist writing under the pseudonym Federal Farmer criticized the use of \xe2\x80\x9cwritten evidence\xe2\x80\x9d while objecting to the omission of a vicinage right: \xe2\x80\x9cNothing can be more essential than the cross examining [of] witnesses, and generally before the triers of the facts in question\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [W]ritten evidence \xe2\x80\xa6 [is] almost useless; it must be frequently taken ex parte, and but very seldom leads to the proper discovery of truth.\xe2\x80\x9d R. Lee, Letter IV by the Federal Farmer (Oct. 15, 1787), reprinted in 1 Schwartz, supra, at 469, 473. The First Congress responded by including the Confrontation Clause in the proposal that became the Sixth Amendment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Early state decisions shed light upon the original understanding of the common-law right. State v. Webb, 2 N.\xc2\xa0C. 103 (1794) (per curiam), decided a mere three years after the adoption of the Sixth Amendment, held that depositions could be read against an accused only if they were taken in his presence. Rejecting a broader reading of the English authorities, the court held: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is a rule of the common law, founded on natural justice, that no man shall be prejudiced by evidence which he had not the liberty to cross examine.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 104.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, in State v. Campbell, 1 S.\xc2\xa0C. 124 (1844), South Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s highest law court excluded a deposition taken by a coroner in the absence of the accused. It held: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f we are to decide the question by the established rules of the common law, there could not be a dissenting voice. For, notwithstanding the death of the witness, and whatever the respectability of the court taking the depositions, the solemnity of the occasion and the weight of the testimony, such depositions are ex parte, and, therefore, utterly incompetent.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 125. The court said that one of the \xe2\x80\x9cindispensable conditions\xe2\x80\x9d implicitly guaranteed by the State Constitution was that \xe2\x80\x9cprosecutions be carried on to the conviction of the accused, by witnesses confronted by him, and subjected to his personal examination.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Many other decisions are to the same effect. Some early cases went so far as to hold that prior testimony was inadmissible in criminal cases even if the accused had a previous opportunity to cross-examine. See Finn v. Commonwealth, 26 Va. 701, 708 (1827); State v. Atkins, 1 Tenn. 229 (1807) (per curiam). Most courts rejected that view, but only after reaffirming that admissibility depended on a prior opportunity for cross-examination. See United States v. Macomb, 26 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 1132, 1133 (No. 15,702) (CC Ill. 1851); State v. Houser, 26 Mo. 431, 435\xe2\x80\x93436 (1858); Kendrick v. State, 29 Tenn. 479, 485\xe2\x80\x93488 (1850); Bostick v. State, 22 Tenn. 344, 345\xe2\x80\x93346 (1842); Commonwealth v. Richards, 35 Mass. 434, 437 (1837); State v. Hill, 2 Hill 607, 608\xe2\x80\x93610 (S.\xc2\xa0C. 1835); Johnston v. State, 10 Tenn. 58, 59 (1821). Nineteenth-century treatises confirm the rule. See 1 J. Bishop, Criminal Procedure \xc2\xa71093, p. 689 (2d ed. 1872); T. Cooley, Constitutional Limitations *318.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This history supports two inferences about the meaning of the Sixth Amendment.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the principal evil at which the Confrontation Clause was directed was the civil-law mode of criminal procedure, and particularly its use of ex parte examinations as evidence against the accused. It was these practices that the Crown deployed in notorious treason cases like Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s; that the Marian statutes invited; that English law\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of a right to confrontation was meant to prohibit; and that the founding-era rhetoric decried. The Sixth Amendment must be interpreted with this focus in mind.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, we once again reject the view that the Confrontation Clause applies of its own force only to in-court testimony, and that its application to out-of-court statements introduced at trial depends upon \xe2\x80\x9cthe law of Evidence for the time being.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 Wigmore \xc2\xa71397, at 101; accord, Dutton v. Evans, 400 U. S. 74, 94 (1970) (Harlan, J., concurring in result). Leaving the regulation of out-of-court statements to the law of evidence would render the Confrontation Clause powerless to prevent even the most flagrant inquisitorial practices. Raleigh was, after all, perfectly free to confront those who read Cobham\xe2\x80\x99s confession in court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This focus also suggests that not all hearsay implicates the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s core concerns. An off-hand, overheard remark might be unreliable evidence and thus a good candidate for exclusion under hearsay rules, but it bears little resemblance to the civil-law abuses the Confrontation Clause targeted. On the other hand, ex parte examinations might sometimes be admissible under modern hearsay rules, but the Framers certainly would not have condoned them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The text of the Confrontation Clause reflects this focus. It applies to \xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses\xe2\x80\x9d against the accused\xe2\x80\x94in other words, those who \xe2\x80\x9cbear testimony.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 N. Webster, An American Dictionary of the English Language (1828). \xe2\x80\x9cTestimony,\xe2\x80\x9d in turn, is typically \xe2\x80\x9c[a] solemn declaration or affirmation made for the purpose of establishing or proving some fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. An accuser who makes a formal statement to government officers bears testimony in a sense that a person who makes a casual remark to an acquaintance does not. The constitutional text, like the history underlying the common-law right of confrontation, thus reflects an especially acute concern with a specific type of out-of-court statement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Various formulations of this core class of \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial\xe2\x80\x9d statements exist: \xe2\x80\x9cex parte in-court testimony or its functional equivalent\xe2\x80\x94that is, material such as affidavits, custodial examinations, prior testimony that the defendant was unable to cross-examine, or similar pretrial statements that declarants would reasonably expect to be used prosecutorially,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 23; \xe2\x80\x9cextrajudicial statements \xe2\x80\xa6 contained in formalized testimonial materials, such as affidavits, depositions, prior testimony, or confessions,\xe2\x80\x9d White v. Illinois, 502 U. S. 346, 365 (1992) (Thomas, J., joined by Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment); \xe2\x80\x9cstatements that were made under circumstances which would lead an objective witness reasonably to believe that the statement would be available for use at a later trial,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 3. These formulations all share a common nucleus and then define the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s coverage at various levels of abstraction around it. Regardless of the precise articulation, some statements qualify under any definition\xe2\x80\x94for example, ex parte testimony at a preliminary hearing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Statements taken by police officers in the course of interrogations are also testimonial under even a narrow standard. Police interrogations bear a striking resemblance to examinations by justices of the peace in England. The statements are not sworn testimony, but the absence of oath was not dispositive. Cobham\xe2\x80\x99s examination was unsworn, see 1 Jardine, Criminal Trials, at 430, yet Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s trial has long been thought a paradigmatic confrontation violation, see, e.g., Campbell, 1 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 130. Under the Marian statutes, witnesses were typically put on oath, but suspects were not. See 2 Hale, Pleas of the Crown, at 52. Yet Hawkins and others went out of their way to caution that such unsworn confessions were not admissible against anyone but the confessor. See supra, at 8.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That interrogators are police officers rather than magistrates does not change the picture either. Justices of the peace conducting examinations under the Marian statutes were not magistrates as we understand that office today, but had an essentially investigative and prosecutorial function. See 1 Stephen, Criminal Law of England, at 221; Langbein, Prosecuting Crime in the Renaissance, at 34\xe2\x80\x9345. England did not have a professional police force until the 19th century, see 1 Stephen, supra, at 194\xe2\x80\x93200, so it is not surprising that other government officers performed the investigative functions now associated primarily with the police. The involvement of government officers in the production of testimonial evidence presents the same risk, whether the officers are police or justices of the peace.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, even if the Sixth Amendment is not solely concerned with testimonial hearsay, that is its primary object, and interrogations by law enforcement officers fall squarely within that class.[Footnote 4]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The historical record also supports a second proposition: that the Framers would not have allowed admission of testimonial statements of a witness who did not appear at trial unless he was unavailable to testify, and the defendant had had a prior opportunity for cross-examination. The text of the Sixth Amendment does not suggest any open-ended exceptions from the confrontation requirement to be developed by the courts. Rather, the \xe2\x80\x9cright \xe2\x80\xa6 to be confronted with the witnesses against him,\xe2\x80\x9d Amdt. 6, is most naturally read as a reference to the right of confrontation at common law, admitting only those exceptions established at the time of the founding. See Mattox v. United States, 156 U. S. 237, 243 (1895); cf. Houser, 26 Mo., at 433\xe2\x80\x93435. As the English authorities above reveal, the common law in 1791 conditioned admissibility of an absent witness\xe2\x80\x99s examination on unavailability and a prior opportunity to cross-examine. The Sixth Amendment therefore incorporates those limitations. The numerous early state decisions applying the same test confirm that these principles were received as part of the common law in this country.[Footnote 5]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not read the historical sources to say that a prior opportunity to cross-examine was merely a sufficient, rather than a necessary, condition for admissibility of testimonial statements. They suggest that this requirement was dispositive, and not merely one of several ways to establish reliability. This is not to deny, as The Chief Justice notes, that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here were always exceptions to the general rule of exclusion\xe2\x80\x9d of hearsay evidence. Post, at 5. Several had become well established by 1791. See 3 Wigmore \xc2\xa71397, at 101; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 13, n. 5. But there is scant evidence that exceptions were invoked to admit testimonial statements against the accused in a criminal case.[Footnote 6] Most of the hearsay exceptions covered statements that by their nature were not testimonial\xe2\x80\x94for example, business records or statements in furtherance of a conspiracy. We do not infer from these that the Framers thought exceptions would apply even to prior testimony. Cf. Lilly v. Virginia, 527 U. S. 116, 134 (1999) (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]ccomplices\xe2\x80\x99 confessions that inculpate a criminal defendant are not within a firmly rooted exception to the hearsay rule\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 7]

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our case law has been largely consistent with these two principles. Our leading early decision, for example, involved a deceased witness\xe2\x80\x99s prior trial testimony. Mattox v. United States, 156 U. S. 237 (1895). In allowing the statement to be admitted, we relied on the fact that the defendant had had, at the first trial, an adequate opportunity to confront the witness: \xe2\x80\x9cThe substance of the constitutional protection is preserved to the prisoner in the advantage he has once had of seeing the witness face to face, and of subjecting him to the ordeal of a cross-examination.\xc2\xa0 This, the law says, he shall under no circumstances be deprived of \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 244.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our later cases conform to Mattox\xe2\x80\x99s holding that prior trial or preliminary hearing testimony is admissible only if the defendant had an adequate opportunity to cross-examine. See Mancusi v. Stubbs, 408 U. S. 204, 213\xe2\x80\x93216 (1972); California v. Green, 399 U. S. 149, 165\xe2\x80\x93168 (1970); Pointer v. Texas, 380 U. S., at 406\xe2\x80\x93408; cf. Kirby v. United States, 174 U. S. 47, 55\xe2\x80\x9361 (1899). Even where the defendant had such an opportunity, we excluded the testimony where the government had not established unavailability of the witness. See Barber v. Page, 390 U. S. 719, 722\xe2\x80\x93725 (1968); cf. Motes v. United States, 178 U. S. 458, 470\xe2\x80\x93471 (1900). We similarly excluded accomplice confessions where the defendant had no opportunity to cross-examine. See Roberts v. Russell, 392 U. S. 293, 294\xe2\x80\x93295 (1968) (per curiam); Bruton v. United States, 391 U. S. 123, 126\xe2\x80\x93128 (1968); Douglas v. Alabama, 380 U. S. 415, 418\xe2\x80\x93420 (1965). In contrast, we considered reliability factors beyond prior opportunity for cross-examination when the hearsay statement at issue was not testimonial. See Dutton v. Evans, 400 U. S., at 87\xe2\x80\x9389 (plurality opinion).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even our recent cases, in their outcomes, hew closely to the traditional line. Ohio v. Roberts, 448 U. S., at 67\xe2\x80\x9370, admitted testimony from a preliminary hearing at which the defendant had examined the witness. Lilly v. Virginia, supra, excluded testimonial statements that the defendant had had no opportunity to test by cross-examination. And Bourjaily v. United States, 483 U. S. 171, 181\xe2\x80\x93184 (1987), admitted statements made unwittingly to an FBI informant after applying a more general test that did not make prior cross-examination an indispensable requirement.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lee v. Illinois, 476 U. S. 530 (1986), on which the State relies, is not to the contrary. There, we rejected the State\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to admit an accomplice confession. The State had argued that the confession was admissible because it \xe2\x80\x9cinterlocked\xe2\x80\x9d with the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s. We dealt with the argument by rejecting its premise, holding that \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the discrepancies between the statements are not insignificant, the codefendant\xe2\x80\x99s confession may not be admitted.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 545. Respondent argues that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he logical inference of this statement is that when the discrepancies between the statements are insignificant, then the codefendant\xe2\x80\x99s statement may be admitted.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 6. But this is merely a possible inference, not an inevitable one, and we do not draw it here. If Lee had meant authoritatively to announce an exception\xe2\x80\x94previously unknown to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisprudence\xe2\x80\x94for interlocking confessions, it would not have done so in such an oblique manner. Our only precedent on interlocking confessions had addressed the entirely different question whether a limiting instruction cured prejudice to codefendants from admitting a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own confession against him in a joint trial. See Parker v. Randolph, 442 U. S. 62, 69\xe2\x80\x9376 (1979) (plurality opinion), abrogated by Cruz v. New York, 481 U. S. 186 (1987).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our cases have thus remained faithful to the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 understanding: Testimonial statements of witnesses absent from trial have been admitted only where the declarant is unavailable, and only where the defendant has had a prior opportunity to cross-examine.[Footnote 9]

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the results of our decisions have generally been faithful to the original meaning of the Confrontation Clause, the same cannot be said of our rationales. Roberts conditions the admissibility of all hearsay evidence on whether it falls under a \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly rooted hearsay exception\xe2\x80\x9d or bears \xe2\x80\x9cparticularized guarantees of trustworthiness.\xe2\x80\x9d 448 U. S., at 66. This test departs from the historical principles identified above in two respects. First, it is too broad: It applies the same mode of analysis whether or not the hearsay consists of ex parte testimony. This often results in close constitutional scrutiny in cases that are far removed from the core concerns of the Clause. At the same time, however, the test is too narrow: It admits statements that do consist of ex parte testimony upon a mere finding of reliability. This malleable standard often fails to protect against paradigmatic confrontation violations.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Members of this Court and academics have suggested that we revise our doctrine to reflect more accurately the original understanding of the Clause. See, e.g., Lilly, 527 U. S., at 140\xe2\x80\x93143 (Breyer, J., concurring); White, 502 U. S., at 366 (Thomas, J., joined by Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment); A. Amar, The Constitution and Criminal Procedure 125\xe2\x80\x93131 (1997); Friedman, Confrontation: The Search for Basic Principles, 86 Geo. L.\xc2\xa0J. 1011 (1998). They offer two proposals: First, that we apply the Confrontation Clause only to testimonial statements, leaving the remainder to regulation by hearsay law\xe2\x80\x94thus eliminating the overbreadth referred to above. Second, that we impose an absolute bar to statements that are testimonial, absent a prior opportunity to cross-examine\xe2\x80\x94thus eliminating the excessive narrowness referred to above.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In White, we considered the first proposal and rejected it. 502 U. S., at 352\xe2\x80\x93353. Although our analysis in this case casts doubt on that holding, we need not definitively resolve whether it survives our decision today, because Sylvia Crawford\xe2\x80\x99s statement is testimonial under any definition. This case does, however, squarely implicate the second proposal.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Where testimonial statements are involved, we do not think the Framers meant to leave the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection to the vagaries of the rules of evidence, much less to amorphous notions of \xe2\x80\x9creliability.\xe2\x80\x9d Certainly none of the authorities discussed above acknowledges any general reliability exception to the common-law rule. Admitting statements deemed reliable by a judge is fundamentally at odds with the right of confrontation. To be sure, the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate goal is to ensure reliability of evidence, but it is a procedural rather than a substantive guarantee. It commands, not that evidence be reliable, but that reliability be assessed in a particular manner: by testing in the crucible of cross-examination. The Clause thus reflects a judgment, not only about the desirability of reliable evidence (a point on which there could be little dissent), but about how reliability can best be determined. Cf. 3 Blackstone, Commentaries, at 373 (\xe2\x80\x9cThis open examination of witnesses . . . is much more conducive to the clearing up of truth\xe2\x80\x9d); M. Hale, History and Analysis of the Common Law of England 258 (1713) (adversarial testing \xe2\x80\x9cbeats and bolts out the Truth much better\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Roberts test allows a jury to hear evidence, untested by the adversary process, based on a mere judicial determination of reliability. It thus replaces the constitutionally prescribed method of assessing reliability with a wholly foreign one. In this respect, it is very different from exceptions to the Confrontation Clause that make no claim to be a surrogate means of assessing reliability. For example, the rule of forfeiture by wrongdoing (which we accept) extinguishes confrontation claims on essentially equitable grounds; it does not purport to be an alternative means of determining reliability. See Reynolds v. United States, 98 U. S. 145, 158\xe2\x80\x93159 (1879).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Raleigh trial itself involved the very sorts of reliability determinations that Roberts authorizes. In the face of Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s repeated demands for confrontation, the prosecution responded with many of the arguments a court applying Roberts might invoke today: that Cobham\xe2\x80\x99s statements were self-inculpatory, 2 How. St. Tr., at 19, that they were not made in the heat of passion, id., at 14, and that they were not \xe2\x80\x9cextracted from [him] upon any hopes or promise of Pardon,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 29. It is not plausible that the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 only objection to the trial was that Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s judges did not properly weigh these factors before sentencing him to death. Rather, the problem was that the judges refused to allow Raleigh to confront Cobham in court, where he could cross-examine him and try to expose his accusation as a lie.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dispensing with confrontation because testimony is obviously reliable is akin to dispensing with jury trial because a defendant is obviously guilty. This is not what the Sixth Amendment prescribes.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The legacy of Roberts in other courts vindicates the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 wisdom in rejecting a general reliability exception. The framework is so unpredictable that it fails to provide meaningful protection from even core confrontation violations.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reliability is an amorphous, if not entirely subjective, concept. There are countless factors bearing on whether a statement is reliable; the nine-factor balancing test applied by the Court of Appeals below is representative. See, e.g., People v. Farrell, 34 P.\xc2\xa03d 401, 406\xe2\x80\x93407 (Colo. 2001) (eight-factor test). Whether a statement is deemed reliable depends heavily on which factors the judge considers and how much weight he accords each of them. Some courts wind up attaching the same significance to opposite facts. For example, the Colorado Supreme Court held a statement more reliable because its inculpation of the defendant was \xe2\x80\x9cdetailed,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 407, while the Fourth Circuit found a statement more reliable because the portion implicating another was \xe2\x80\x9cfleeting,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Photogrammetric Data Servs., Inc., 259 F.\xc2\xa03d 229, 245 (2001). The Virginia Court of Appeals found a statement more reliable because the witness was in custody and charged with a crime (thus making the statement more obviously against her penal interest), see Nowlin v. Commonwealth, 40 Va. App. 327, 335\xe2\x80\x93338, 579 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 367, 371\xe2\x80\x93372 (2003), while the Wisconsin Court of Appeals found a statement more reliable because the witness was not in custody and not a suspect, see State v. Bintz, 2002 WI App. 204, \xc2\xb6 ;13, 257 Wis. 2d 177, 187, 650 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 913, 918. Finally, the Colorado Supreme Court in one case found a statement more reliable because it was given \xe2\x80\x9cimmediately after\xe2\x80\x9d the events at issue, Farrell, supra, at 407, while that same court, in another case, found a statement more reliable because two years had elapsed, Stevens v. People, 29 P.\xc2\xa03d 305, 316 (2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The unpardonable vice of the Roberts test, however, is not its unpredictability, but its demonstrated capacity to admit core testimonial statements that the Confrontation Clause plainly meant to exclude. Despite the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s speculation in Lilly, 527 U. S., at 137, that it was \xe2\x80\x9chighly unlikely\xe2\x80\x9d that accomplice confessions implicating the accused could survive Roberts, courts continue routinely to admit them. See Photogrammetric Data Servs., supra, at 245\xe2\x80\x93246; Farrell, supra, at 406\xe2\x80\x93408; Stevens, supra, at 314\xe2\x80\x93318; Taylor v. Commonwealth, 63 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 151, 166\xe2\x80\x93168 (Ky. 2001); State v. Hawkins, No. 2001\xe2\x80\x93P\xe2\x80\x930060, 2002 WL 31895118, \xc2\xb6 ;\xc2\xb6 ;34\xe2\x80\x9337, *6 (Ohio App., Dec. 31, 2002); Bintz, supra, \xc2\xb6 ;\xc2\xb6 ;7\xe2\x80\x9314, 257 Wis. 2d, at 183\xe2\x80\x93188, 650 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d, at 916\xe2\x80\x93918; People v. Lawrence, 55 P.\xc2\xa03d 155, 160\xe2\x80\x93161 (Colo. App. 2001); State v. Jones, 171 Ore. App. 375, 387\xe2\x80\x93391, 15 P.\xc2\xa03d 616, 623\xe2\x80\x93625 (2000); State v. Marshall, 136 Ohio App. 3d 742, 747\xe2\x80\x93748, 737 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 1005, 1009 (2000); People v. Schutte, 240 Mich. App. 713, 718\xe2\x80\x93721, 613 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 370, 376\xe2\x80\x93377 (2000); People v. Thomas, 313 Ill. App. 3d 998, 1005\xe2\x80\x931007, 730 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 618, 625\xe2\x80\x93626 (2000); cf. Nowlin, supra, at 335\xe2\x80\x93338, 579 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 371\xe2\x80\x93372 (witness confessed to a related crime); People v. Campbell, 309 Ill. App. 3d 423, 431\xe2\x80\x93432, 721 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 1225, 1230 (1999) (same). One recent study found that, after Lilly, appellate courts admitted accomplice statements to the authorities in 25 out of 70 cases\xe2\x80\x94more than one-third of the time. Kirst, Appellate Court Answers to the Confrontation Questions in Lilly v. Virginia, 53 Syracuse L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 87, 105 (2003). Courts have invoked Roberts to admit other sorts of plainly testimonial statements despite the absence of any opportunity to cross-examine. See United States v. Aguilar, 295 F.\xc2\xa03d 1018, 1021\xe2\x80\x931023 (CA9 2002) (plea allocution showing existence of a conspiracy); United States v. Centracchio, 265 F.\xc2\xa03d 518, 527\xe2\x80\x93530 (CA7 2001) (same); United States v. Dolah, 245 F.\xc2\xa03d 98, 104\xe2\x80\x93105 (CA2 2001) (same); United States v. Petrillo, 237 F.\xc2\xa03d 119, 122\xe2\x80\x93123 (CA2 2000) (same); United States v. Moskowitz, 215 F.\xc2\xa03d 265, 268\xe2\x80\x93269 (CA2 2000) (same); United States v. Gallego, 191 F.\xc2\xa03d 156, 166\xe2\x80\x93168 (CA2 1999) (same); United States v. Papajohn, 212 F.\xc2\xa03d 1112, 1118\xe2\x80\x931120 (CA8 2000) (grand jury testimony); United States v. Thomas, 30 Fed. Appx. 277, 279 (CA4 2002) (same); Bintz, supra, \xc2\xb6 ;\xc2\xb6 ;15\xe2\x80\x9322, 257 Wis. 2d, at 188\xe2\x80\x93191, 650 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d, at 918\xe2\x80\x93920 (prior trial testimony); State v. McNeill, 140 N.\xc2\xa0C. App. 450, 457\xe2\x80\x93460, 537 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 518, 523\xe2\x80\x93524 (2000) (same).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To add insult to injury, some of the courts that admit untested testimonial statements find reliability in the very factors that make the statements testimonial. As noted earlier, one court relied on the fact that the witness\xe2\x80\x99s statement was made to police while in custody on pending charges\xe2\x80\x94the theory being that this made the statement more clearly against penal interest and thus more reliable. Nowlin, supra, at 335\xe2\x80\x93338, 579 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 371\xe2\x80\x93372. Other courts routinely rely on the fact that a prior statement is given under oath in judicial proceedings. E.g., Gallego, supra, at 168 (plea allocution); Papajohn, supra, at 1120 (grand jury testimony). That inculpating statements are given in a testimonial setting is not an antidote to the confrontation problem, but rather the trigger that makes the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s demands most urgent. It is not enough to point out that most of the usual safeguards of the adversary process attend the statement, when the single safeguard missing is the one the Confrontation Clause demands.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Roberts\xe2\x80\x99 failings were on full display in the proceedings below. Sylvia Crawford made her statement while in police custody, herself a potential suspect in the case. Indeed, she had been told that whether she would be released \xe2\x80\x9cdepend[ed] on how the investigation continues.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 81. In response to often leading questions from police detectives, she implicated her husband in Lee\xe2\x80\x99s stabbing and at least arguably undermined his self-defense claim. Despite all this, the trial court admitted her statement, listing several reasons why it was reliable. In its opinion reversing, the Court of Appeals listed several other reasons why the statement was not reliable. Finally, the State Supreme Court relied exclusively on the interlocking character of the statement and disregarded every other factor the lower courts had considered. The case is thus a self-contained demonstration of Roberts\xe2\x80\x99 unpredictable and inconsistent application.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Each of the courts also made assumptions that cross-examination might well have undermined. The trial court, for example, stated that Sylvia Crawford\xe2\x80\x99s statement was reliable because she was an eyewitness with direct knowledge of the events. But Sylvia at one point told the police that she had \xe2\x80\x9cshut [her] eyes and \xe2\x80\xa6 didn\xe2\x80\x99t really watch\xe2\x80\x9d part of the fight, and that she was \xe2\x80\x9cin shock.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 134. The trial court also buttressed its reliability finding by claiming that Sylvia was \xe2\x80\x9cbeing questioned by law enforcement, and, thus, the [questioner] is \xe2\x80\xa6 neutral to her and not someone who would be inclined to advance her interests and shade her version of the truth unfavorably toward the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 77. The Framers would be astounded to learn that ex parte testimony could be admitted against a criminal defendant because it was elicited by \xe2\x80\x9cneutral\xe2\x80\x9d government officers. But even if the court\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of the officer\xe2\x80\x99s motives was accurate, it says nothing about Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s perception of her situation. Only cross-examination could reveal that.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State Supreme Court gave dispositive weight to the interlocking nature of the two statements\xe2\x80\x94that they were both ambiguous as to when and whether Lee had a weapon. The court\xe2\x80\x99s claim that the two statements were equally ambiguous is hard to accept. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s statement is ambiguous only in the sense that he had lingering doubts about his recollection: \xe2\x80\x9cA. I coulda swore I seen him goin\xe2\x80\x99 for somethin\xe2\x80\x99 before, right before everything happened\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [B]ut I\xe2\x80\x99m not positive.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 155. Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s statement, on the other hand, is truly inscrutable, since the key timing detail was simply assumed in the leading question she was asked: \xe2\x80\x9cQ. Did Kenny do anything to fight back from this assault?\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 137. Moreover, Sylvia specifically said Lee had nothing in his hands after he was stabbed, while petitioner was not asked about that.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The prosecutor obviously did not share the court\xe2\x80\x99s view that Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s statement was ambiguous\xe2\x80\x94he called it \xe2\x80\x9cdamning evidence\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ccompletely refutes [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] claim of self-defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 468 (Oct. 21, 1999). We have no way of knowing whether the jury agreed with the prosecutor or the court. Far from obviating the need for cross-examination, the \xe2\x80\x9cinterlocking\xe2\x80\x9d ambiguity of the two statements made it all the more imperative that they be tested to tease out the truth.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We readily concede that we could resolve this case by simply reweighing the \xe2\x80\x9creliability factors\xe2\x80\x9d under Roberts and finding that Sylvia Crawford\xe2\x80\x99s statement falls short. But we view this as one of those rare cases in which the result below is so improbable that it reveals a fundamental failure on our part to interpret the Constitution in a way that secures its intended constraint on judicial discretion. Moreover, to reverse the Washington Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision after conducting our own reliability analysis would perpetuate, not avoid, what the Sixth Amendment condemns. The Constitution prescribes a procedure for determining the reliability of testimony in criminal trials, and we, no less than the state courts, lack authority to replace it with one of our own devising.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have no doubt that the courts below were acting in utmost good faith when they found reliability. The Framers, however, would not have been content to indulge this assumption. They knew that judges, like other government officers, could not always be trusted to safeguard the rights of the people; the likes of the dread Lord Jeffreys were not yet too distant a memory. They were loath to leave too much discretion in judicial hands. Cf. U. S. Const., Amdt. 6 (criminal jury trial); Amdt. 7 (civil jury trial); Ring v. Arizona, 536 U. S. 584, 611\xe2\x80\x93612 (2002) (Scalia, J., concurring). By replacing categorical constitutional guarantees with open-ended balancing tests, we do violence to their design. Vague standards are manipulable, and, while that might be a small concern in run-of-the-mill assault prosecutions like this one, the Framers had an eye toward politically charged cases like Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x94great state trials where the impartiality of even those at the highest levels of the judiciary might not be so clear. It is difficult to imagine Roberts\xe2\x80\x99 providing any meaningful protection in those circumstances.

\n

* * *

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Where nontestimonial hearsay is at issue, it is wholly consistent with the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 design to afford the States flexibility in their development of hearsay law\xe2\x80\x94as does Roberts, and as would an approach that exempted such statements from Confrontation Clause scrutiny altogether. Where testimonial evidence is at issue, however, the Sixth Amendment demands what the common law required: unavailability and a prior opportunity for cross-examination. We leave for another day any effort to spell out a comprehensive definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 10] Whatever else the term covers, it applies at a minimum to prior testimony at a preliminary hearing, before a grand jury, or at a former trial; and to police interrogations. These are the modern practices with closest kinship to the abuses at which the Confrontation Clause was directed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the State admitted Sylvia\xe2\x80\x99s testimonial statement against petitioner, despite the fact that he had no opportunity to cross-examine her. That alone is sufficient to make out a violation of the Sixth Amendment. Roberts notwithstanding, we decline to mine the record in search of indicia of reliability. Where testimonial statements are at issue, the only indicium of reliability sufficient to satisfy constitutional demands is the one the Constitution actually prescribes: confrontation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Washington Supreme Court is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

S. 237 (1895), Kirby v. United States, 174 U. S. 47 (1899), and United States v. Burr, 25 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 187 (No. 14,694) (CC Va. 1807) (Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J.). Post, at 4\xe2\x80\x936. But nothing in these cases contradicts our holding in any way. Mattox and Kirby allowed or excluded evidence depending on whether the defendant had had an opportunity for cross-examination. Mattox, supra, at 242\xe2\x80\x93244; Kirby, supra, at 55\xe2\x80\x9361. That the two cases did not extrapolate a more general class of evidence to which that criterion applied does not prevent us from doing so now. As to Burr, we disagree with The Chief Justice\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the case. Although Chief Justice Marshall made one passing reference to the Confrontation Clause, the case was fundamentally about the hearsay rules governing statements in furtherance of a conspiracy. The \xe2\x80\x9cprinciple so truly important\xe2\x80\x9d on which \xe2\x80\x9cinroad[s]\xe2\x80\x9d had been introduced was the \xe2\x80\x9crule of evidence which rejects mere hearsay testimony.\xe2\x80\x9d See 25 F.\xc2\xa0Cas., at 193. Nothing in the opinion concedes exceptions to the Confrontation Clause\xe2\x80\x99s exclusion of testimonial statements as we use the term. The Chief Justice fails to identify a single case (aside from one minor, arguable exception, see supra, at 22, n.\xc2\xa08), where we have admitted testimonial statements based on indicia of reliability other than a prior opportunity for cross-examination. If nothing else, the test we announce is an empirically accurate explanation of the results our cases have reached.

\n

\n\nFinally, we reiterate that, when the declarant appears for cross-examination at trial, the Confrontation Clause places no constraints at all on the use of his prior testimonial statements. See California v. Green, 399 U. S. 149, 162 (1970). It is therefore irrelevant that the reliability of some out-of-court statements \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98cannot be replicated, even if the declarant testifies to the same matters in court.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6 (quoting United States v. Inadi, 475 U. S. 387, 395 (1986)). The Clause does not bar admission of a statement so long as the declarant is present at trial to defend or explain it. (The Clause also does not bar the use of testimonial statements for purposes other than establishing the truth of the matter asserted. See Tennessee v. Street, 471 U. S. 409, 414 (1985).)

But it can hardly be any worse than\nthe status quo. See supra, at 27\xe2\x80\x9330, and cases cited. The difference\nis that the Roberts test is inherently, and therefore permanently,\nunpredictable.

MICHAEL D. CRAWFORD, PETITIONER v.\nWASHINGTON

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of washington

\n

[March 8, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Rehnquist, with whom Justice O\xe2\x80\x99Connor joins, concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to overrule Ohio v. Roberts, 448 U. S. 56 (1980). I believe that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s adoption of a new interpretation of the Confrontation Clause is not backed by sufficiently persuasive reasoning to overrule long-established precedent. Its decision casts a mantle of uncertainty over future criminal trials in both federal and state courts, and is by no means necessary to decide the present case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s distinction between testimonial and nontestimonial statements, contrary to its claim, is no better rooted in history than our current doctrine. Under the common law, although the courts were far from consistent, out-of-court statements made by someone other than the accused and not taken under oath, unlike ex parte depositions or affidavits, were generally not considered substantive evidence upon which a conviction could be based.[Footnote 1] See, e.g., King v. Brasier, 1 Leach 199, 200, 168 Eng. Rep. 202 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1779); see also J. Langbein, Origins of Adversary Criminal Trial 235\xe2\x80\x93242 (2003); G. Gilbert, Evidence 152 (3d ed 1769).[Footnote 2] Testimonial statements such as accusatory statements to police officers likely would have been disapproved of in the 18th century, not necessarily because they resembled ex parte affidavits or depositions as the Court reasons, but more likely than not because they were not made under oath.[Footnote 3] See King v. Woodcock, 1 Leach 500, 503, 168 Eng. Rep. 352, 353 (1789) (noting that a statement taken by a justice of the peace may not be admitted into evidence unless taken under oath). Without an oath, one usually did not get to the second step of whether confrontation was required.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, while I agree that the Framers were mainly concerned about sworn affidavits and depositions, it does not follow that they were similarly concerned about the Court\xe2\x80\x99s broader category of testimonial statements. See 1 N. Webster, An American Dictionary of the English Language (1828) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cTestimony\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[a] solemn declaration or affirmation made for the purpose of establishing or proving some fact. Such affirmation in judicial proceedings, may be verbal or written, but must be under oath\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). As far as I can tell, unsworn testimonial statements were treated no differently at common law than were nontestimonial statements, and it seems to me any classification of statements as testimonial beyond that of sworn affidavits and depositions will be somewhat arbitrary, merely a proxy for what the Framers might have intended had such evidence been liberally admitted as substantive evidence like it is today.[Footnote 4]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I therefore see no reason why the distinction the Court draws is preferable to our precedent. Starting with Chief Justice Marshall\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation as a Circuit Justice in 1807, 16 years after the ratification of the Sixth Amendment, United States v. Burr, 25 F. Cas. 187, 193 (No.\xc2\xa014,694) (CC Va. 1807), continuing with our cases in the late 19th century, Mattox v. United States, 156 U. S. 237, 243\xe2\x80\x93244 (1895); Kirby v. United States, 174 U. S. 47, 54\xe2\x80\x9357 (1899), and through today, e.g., White v. Illinois, 502 U. S. 346, 352\xe2\x80\x93353 (1992), we have never drawn a distinction between testimonial and nontestimonial statements. And for that matter, neither has any other court of which I am aware. I see little value in trading our precedent for an imprecise approximation at this late date.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I am also not convinced that the Confrontation Clause categorically requires the exclusion of testimonial statements. Although many States had their own Confrontation Clauses, they were of recent vintage and were not interpreted with any regularity before 1791. State cases that recently followed the ratification of the Sixth Amendment were not uniform; the Court itself cites state cases from the early 19th century that took a more stringent view of the right to confrontation than does the Court, prohibiting former testimony even if the witness was subjected to cross-examination. See ante, at 13 (citing Finn v. Commonwealth, 26 Va. 701, 708 (1827); State v. Atkins, 1 Tenn. 229 (1807) (per curiam)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor was the English law at the time of the framing entirely consistent in its treatment of testimonial evidence. Generally ex parte affidavits and depositions were excluded as the Court notes, but even that proposition was not universal. See King v. Eriswell, 3 T.\xc2\xa0R. 707, 100 Eng. Rep. 815 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1790) (affirming by an equally divided court the admission of an ex parte examination because the declarant was unavailable to testify); King v. Westbeer, 1 Leach 12, 13, 168 Eng. Rep. 108, 109 (1739) (noting the admission of an ex parte affidavit); see also 1 M. Hale, Pleas of the Crown 585\xe2\x80\x93586 (1736) (noting that statements of \xe2\x80\x9caccusers and witnesses\xe2\x80\x9d which were taken under oath could be admitted into evidence if the declarant was \xe2\x80\x9cdead or not able to travel\xe2\x80\x9d). Wigmore notes that sworn examinations of witnesses before justices of the peace in certain cases would not have been excluded until the end of the 1700\xe2\x80\x99s, 5 Wigmore \xc2\xa71364, at 26\xe2\x80\x9327, and sworn statements of witnesses before coroners became excluded only by statute in the 1800\xe2\x80\x99s, see ibid.; id., \xc2\xa71374, at 59. With respect to unsworn testimonial statements, there is no indication that once the hearsay rule was developed courts ever excluded these statements if they otherwise fell within a firmly rooted exception. See, e.g., Eriswell, supra, at 715\xe2\x80\x93719 (Buller, J.), 720 (Ashhurst, J.), 100 Eng. Rep., at 819\xe2\x80\x93822 (concluding that an ex parte examination was admissible as an exception to the hearsay rule because it was a declaration by a party of his state and condition). Dying declarations are one example. See, e.g., Woodcock, supra, at 502\xe2\x80\x93504, 168 Eng. Rep., at 353\xe2\x80\x93354; King v. Reason, 16 How. St. Tr. 1, 22\xe2\x80\x9323 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1722).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Between 1700 and 1800 the rules regarding the admissibility of out-of-court statements were still being developed. See n.\xc2\xa01, supra. There were always exceptions to the general rule of exclusion, and it is not clear to me that the Framers categorically wanted to eliminate further ones. It is one thing to trace the right of confrontation back to the Roman Empire; it is quite another to conclude that such a right absolutely excludes a large category of evidence. It is an odd conclusion indeed to think that the Framers created a cut-and-dried rule with respect to the admissibility of testimonial statements when the law during their own time was not fully settled.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To find exceptions to exclusion under the Clause is not to denigrate it as the Court suggests. Chief Justice Marshall stated of the Confrontation Clause: \xe2\x80\x9cI know of no principle in the preservation of which all are more concerned. I know none, by undermining which, life, liberty and property, might be more endangered. It is therefore incumbent on courts to be watchful of every inroad on a principle so truly important.\xe2\x80\x9d Burr, 25 F. Cas., at 193. Yet, he recognized that such a right was not absolute, acknowledging that exceptions to the exclusionary component of the hearsay rule, which he considered as an \xe2\x80\x9cinroad\xe2\x80\x9d on the right to confrontation, had been introduced. See ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Exceptions to confrontation have always been derived from the experience that some out-of-court statements are just as reliable as cross-examined in-court testimony due to the circumstances under which they were made. We have recognized, for example, that co-conspirator statements simply \xe2\x80\x9ccannot be replicated, even if the declarant testifies to the same matters in court.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Inadi, 475 U. S. 387, 395 (1986). Because the statements are made while the declarant and the accused are partners in an illegal enterprise, the statements are unlikely to be false and their admission \xe2\x80\x9cactually furthers the \xe2\x80\x98Confrontation Clause\xe2\x80\x99s very mission\xe2\x80\x99 which is to \xe2\x80\x98advance the accuracy of the truth-determining process in criminal trials.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 396 (quoting Tennessee v. Street, 471 U. S. 409, 415 (1985) (some internal quotation marks omitted)). Similar reasons justify the introduction of spontaneous declarations, see White, 502 U. S., at 356, statements made in the course of procuring medical services, see ibid., dying declarations, see Kirby, supra, at 61, and countless other hearsay exceptions. That a statement might be testimonial does nothing to undermine the wisdom of one of these exceptions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, cross-examination is a tool used to flesh out the truth, not an empty procedure. See Kentucky v. Stincer, 482 U. S. 730, 737 (1987) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe right to cross-examination, protected by the Confrontation Clause, thus is essentially a \xe2\x80\x98functional\xe2\x80\x99 right designed to promote reliability in the truth-finding functions of a criminal trial\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Maryland v. Craig, 497 U. S. 836, 845 (1990) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe central concern of the Confrontation Clause is to ensure the reliability of the evidence against a criminal defendant by subjecting it to rigorous testing in the context of an adversary proceeding before the trier of fact\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9c[I]n a given instance [cross-examination may] be superfluous; it may be sufficiently clear, in that instance, that the statement offered is free enough from the risk of inaccuracy and untrustworthiness, so that the test of cross-examination would be a work of supererogation.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 Wigmore \xc2\xa71420, at 251. In such a case, as we noted over 100 years ago, \xe2\x80\x9cThe law in its wisdom declares that the rights of the public shall not be wholly sacrificed in order that an incidental benefit may be preserved to the accused.\xe2\x80\x9d Mattox, 156 U. S., at 243; see also Salinger v. United States, 272 U. S. 542, 548 (1926). By creating an immutable category of excluded evidence, the Court adds little to a trial\xe2\x80\x99s truth-finding function and ignores this longstanding guidance.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In choosing the path it does, the Court of course overrules Ohio v. Roberts, 448 U. S. 56 (1980), a case decided nearly a quarter of a century ago. Stare decisis is not an inexorable command in the area of constitutional law, see Payne v. Tennessee, 501 U. S. 808, 828 (1991), but by and large, it \xe2\x80\x9cis the preferred course because it promotes the evenhanded, predictable, and consistent development of legal principles, fosters reliance on judicial decisions, and contributes to the actual and perceived integrity of the judicial process,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 827. And in making this appraisal, doubt that the new rule is indeed the \xe2\x80\x9cright\xe2\x80\x9d one should surely be weighed in the balance. Though there are no vested interests involved, unresolved questions for the future of everyday criminal trials throughout the country surely counsel the same sort of caution. The Court grandly declares that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e leave for another day any effort to spell out a comprehensive definition of \xe2\x80\x98testimonial,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 33. But the thousands of federal prosecutors and the tens of thousands of state prosecutors need answers as to what beyond the specific kinds of \xe2\x80\x9ctestimony\xe2\x80\x9d the Court lists, see ibid., is covered by the new rule. They need them now, not months or years from now. Rules of criminal evidence are applied every day in courts throughout the country, and parties should not be left in the dark in this manner.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To its credit, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of \xe2\x80\x9ctestimony\xe2\x80\x9d excludes at least some hearsay exceptions, such as business records and official records. See ante, at 20. To hold otherwise would require numerous additional witnesses without any apparent gain in the truth-seeking process. Likewise to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s credit is its implicit recognition that the mistaken application of its new rule by courts which guess wrong as to the scope of the rule is subject to harmless-error analysis. See ante, at 5, n.\xc2\xa01.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But these are palliatives to what I believe is a mistaken change of course. It is a change of course not in the least necessary to reverse the judgment of the Supreme Court of Washington in this case. The result the Court reaches follows inexorably from Roberts and its progeny without any need for overruling that line of cases. In Idaho v. Wright, 497 U. S. 805, 820\xe2\x80\x93824 (1990), we held that an out-of-court statement was not admissible simply because the truthfulness of that statement was corroborated by other evidence at trial. As the Court notes, ante, at 31, the Supreme Court of Washington gave decisive weight to the \xe2\x80\x9cinterlocking nature of the two statements.\xe2\x80\x9d No re-weighing of the \xe2\x80\x9creliability factors,\xe2\x80\x9d which is hypothesized by the Court, ante, at 31, is required to reverse the judgment here. A citation to Idaho v. Wright, supra, would suffice. For the reasons stated, I believe that this would be a far preferable course for the Court to take here.
