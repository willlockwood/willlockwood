Syllabus

BETHUNE-HILL et\xc2\xa0al. v. VIRGINIA STATE BOARD OF ELECTIONS et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for the eastern district of virgina

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93680.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 5, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 1, 2017

\n

After the 2010 census, the Virginia State Legislature drew new lines for 12 state legislative districts, with a goal of ensuring that each district would have a black voting-age population (BVAP) of at least 55%. Certain voters filed suit, claiming that the new districts violated the\n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Equal Protection Clause. State legislative officials (State) intervened to defend the plan. A three-judge District Court rejected the challenges. As to 11 of the districts, the court concluded that the voters had not shown, as this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent requires, \xe2\x80\x9cthat race was the predominant factor motivating the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s decision to place a significant number of voters within or without a particular district,\xe2\x80\x9d Miller v. Johnson,\n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 900\n. In so doing, the court held that race predominates only where there is an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98actual conflict between traditional redistricting criteria and race.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 141 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d 505, 524. It thus confined the predominance analysis to the portions of the new lines that appeared to deviate from traditional criteria. As to the remaining district, District 75, the court found that race did predominate, but that the lines were constitutional because the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s use of race was narrowly tailored to a compelling state interest. In particular, the court found the legislature had good reasons to believe that a 55% racial target was necessary in District 75 to avoid diminishing the ability of black voters to elect their preferred candidates, which at the time would have violated \xc2\xa75 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965, see Alabama Legislative Black Caucus v. Alabama, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0The District Court employed an incorrect legal standard in determining that race did not predominate in 11 of the 12 districts. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The Equal Protection Clause prohibits a State, without sufficient justification, from \xe2\x80\x9cseparat[ing] its citizens into different voting districts on the basis of race.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 911. Courts must \xe2\x80\x9cexercise extraordinary caution in adjudicating claims\xe2\x80\x9d of racial gerrymandering, id., at 916, since a legislature is always \xe2\x80\x9caware of race when it draws district lines, just as it is aware of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. other demographic factors,\xe2\x80\x9d Shaw v. Reno,\n\n509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 630\n (Shaw I). A plaintiff alleging racial gerrymandering thus bears the burden \xe2\x80\x9cto show, either through circumstantial evidence of a district\xe2\x80\x99s shape and demographics or more direct evidence going to legislative purpose, that race was the predominant factor motivating the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s [districting] decision,\xe2\x80\x9d which requires proving \xe2\x80\x9cthat the legislature subordinated traditional race-neutral districting principles .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to racial considerations.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller, supra, at 916. Here, the District Court misapplied controlling law in two principal ways. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0First, the District Court misunderstood relevant precedents when it required the challengers to establish, as a prerequisite to showing racial predominance, an actual conflict between the enacted plan and traditional redistricting principles. This Court has made clear that parties may show predominance \xe2\x80\x9ceither through circumstantial evidence of a district\xe2\x80\x99s shape and demographics or more direct evidence going to legislative purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d Miller, supra, at 916, and that race may predominate even when a plan respects traditional principles, Shaw v. Hunt,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 899\n (Shaw II).

\n

The State\xe2\x80\x99s theory in this case is irreconcilable with Miller and Shaw II. The State insists, e.g., that the harm from racial gerrymandering lies not in racial line-drawing per se but in grouping voters of the same race together when they otherwise lack shared interests. But \xe2\x80\x9cthe constitutional violation\xe2\x80\x9d in racial gerrymandering cases stems from the \xe2\x80\x9cracial purpose of state action, not its stark manifestation.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller, supra, at 913. The State also contends that race does not have a prohibited effect on a district\xe2\x80\x99s lines if the legislature could have drawn the same lines in accordance with traditional criteria. The proper inquiry, however, concerns the actual considerations that provided the essential basis for the lines drawn, not post hoc justifications that the legislature could have used but did not. A legislature could construct a plethora of potential maps that look consistent with traditional, race-neutral principles, but if race is the overriding reason for choosing one map over others, race still may predominate. A conflict or inconsistency may be persuasive circumstantial evidence tending to show racial predomination, but no rule requires challengers to present this kind of evidence in every case. As a practical matter, this kind of evidence may be necessary in many or even most cases. But there may be cases where challengers can establish racial predominance without evidence of an actual conflict. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The District Court also erred in considering the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s racial motive only to the extent that the challengers identified deviations from traditional redistricting criteria attributable to race and not to some other factor. Racial gerrymandering claims proceed \xe2\x80\x9cdistrict-by-district,\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama, supra, at ___, and courts should not divorce any portion of a district\xe2\x80\x99s lines\xe2\x80\x94whatever their relationship to traditional principles\xe2\x80\x94from the rest of the district. Courts may consider evidence pertaining to an area that is larger or smaller than the district at issue. But the ultimate object of the inquiry is the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s predominant motive for the district\xe2\x80\x99s design as a whole, and any explanation for a particular portion of the lines must take account of the districtwide context. A holistic analysis is necessary to give the proper weight to districtwide evidence, such as stark splits in the racial composition of populations moved into and out of a district, or the use of a racial target. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0The District Court is best positioned to determine on remand the extent to which, under the proper standard, race directed the shape of these 11 districts, and if race did predominate, whether strict scrutiny is satisfied. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment regarding District 75 is consistent with the basic narrow tailoring analysis explained in Alabama. Where a challenger succeeds in establishing racial predominance, the burden shifts to the State to \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrate that its districting legislation is narrowly tailored to achieve a compelling interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller, supra, at 920. Here, it is assumed that the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in complying with the Voting Rights Act was a compelling interest. When a State justifies the predominant use of race in redistricting on the basis of the need to comply with the Voting Rights Act, \xe2\x80\x9cthe narrow tailoring requirement insists only that the legislature have a \xe2\x80\x98strong basis in evidence\xe2\x80\x99 in support of the (race-based) choice that it has made.\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___. The State must show not that its action was actually necessary to avoid a statutory violation, but only that the legislature had \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98good reasons to believe\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d its use of race was needed in order to satisfy the Voting Rights Act. Ibid. There was no error in the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the legislature had sufficient grounds to determine that the race-based calculus it employed in District 75 was necessary to avoid violating \xc2\xa75. Under the facts found by that court, the legislature performed the kind of functional analysis of District 75 necessary under \xc2\xa75, and the result reflected the good-faith efforts of legislators to achieve an informed bipartisan consensus. In contesting the sufficiency of that evidence and the evidence justifying the 55% BVAP floor, the challengers ask too much from state officials charged with the sensitive duty of reapportioning legislative districts. As to the claim that the BVAP floor is akin to the \xe2\x80\x9cmechanically numerical view\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa75 rejected in Alabama, supra, at ___, the record here supports the State\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that this was an instance where a 55% BVAP was necessary for black voters to have a functional working majority. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

141 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d 505, affirmed in part, vacated in part, and remanded.

\n

Alito, J., filed an opinion concurring in part and concurring in the judgment. Thomas, J., filed an opinion concurring in the judgment in part and dissenting in part.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93680

GOLDEN BETHUNE-HILL, et\xc2\xa0al., APPELLANTS v. VIRGINIA STATE BOARD OF ELECTIONS, et\xc2\xa0al.

on appeal from the united states district court for the eastern district of virginia

[March 1, 2017]

Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

This case addresses whether the Virginia state legislature\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of race in drawing new lines for 12 state legislative districts violated the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. After the 2010 census, some redistricting was required to ensure proper numerical apportionment for the Virginia House of Delegates. It is undisputed that the boundary lines for the 12 districts at issue were drawn with a goal of ensuring that each district would have a black voting-age population (BVAP) of at least 55%.

Certain voters challenged the new districts as unconstitutional racial gerrymanders. The United States District Court for the Eastern District of Virginia, constituted as a three-judge district court, rejected the challenges as to each of the 12 districts. As to 11 of the districts, the District Court concluded that the voters had not shown, as this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent requires, \xe2\x80\x9cthat race was the predominant factor motivating the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s decision to place a significant number of voters within or without a particular district.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller v. Johnson,515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 900 (1995). The District Court held that race predominates only where there is an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98actual conflict between traditional redistricting criteria and race,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 141 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d 505, 524 (ED Va. 2015), so it confined the predominance analysis to the portions of the new lines that appeared to deviate from traditional criteria, and found no violation. As to the remaining district, District 75, the District Court found that race did predominate. It concluded, however, that the lines were constitutional because the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s use of race was narrowly tailored to a compelling state interest. In particular, the District Court determined that the legislature had \xe2\x80\x9cgood reasons to believe\xe2\x80\x9d that a 55% racial target was necessary in District 75 to avoid diminishing the ability of black voters to elect their preferred candidates, which at the time would have violated \xc2\xa75 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965. Alabama Legislative Black Caucus v. Alabama, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 22) (internal quotation marks omitted and emphasis deleted).

On appeal to this Court, the challengers contend that the District Court employed an incorrect legal standard for racial predominance and that the legislature lacked good reasons for its use of race in District 75. This Court now affirms as to District 75 and vacates and remands as to the remaining 11 districts.

I

After the 2010 census, the Virginia General Assembly set out to redraw the legislative districts for the State Senate and House of Delegates in time for the 2011 elections. In February 2011, the House Committee on Privileges and Elections adopted a resolution establishing criteria to guide the redistricting process. Among those criteria were traditional redistricting factors such as compactness, contiguity of territory, and respect for communities of interest. But above those traditional objectives, the committee gave priority to two other goals. First, in accordance with the principle of one person, one vote, the committee resolved that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he population of each district shall be as nearly equal to the population of every other district as practicable,\xe2\x80\x9d with any deviations falling \xe2\x80\x9cwithin plus-or-minus one percent.\xe2\x80\x9d 141 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d, at 518. Second, the committee resolved that the new map must comply with the \xe2\x80\x9cprotections against .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. unwarranted retrogression\xe2\x80\x9d contained in \xc2\xa75 of the Voting Rights Act. Ibid. At the time, \xc2\xa75 required covered jurisdictions, including Virginia, to preclear any change to a voting standard, practice, or procedure by showing federal authorities that the change would not have the purpose or effect of \xe2\x80\x9cdiminishing the ability of [members of a minority group] to elect their preferred candidates of choice.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa75,120Stat.580\xe2\x80\x93581,52 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa710304(b). After the redistricting process here was completed, this Court held that the coverage formula in \xc2\xa74(b) of the Voting Rights Act no longer may be used to require preclearance under \xc2\xa75. See Shelby County v. Holder, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 24).

The committee\xe2\x80\x99s criteria presented potential problems for 12 House districts. Under \xc2\xa75 as Congress amended it in 2005, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] plan leads to impermissible retrogression when, compared to the plan currently in effect (typically called a \xe2\x80\x98benchmark plan\xe2\x80\x99), the new plan diminishes the number of districts in which minority groups can \xe2\x80\x98elect their preferred candidates of choice\xe2\x80\x99 (often called \xe2\x80\x98ability-to-elect\xe2\x80\x99 districts).\xe2\x80\x9d Harris v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2016) (slip op., at 5\xe2\x80\x936) (quoting52 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa710304(b)). The parties agree that the 12 districts at issue here, where minorities had constituted a majority of the voting-age population for many past elections, qualified as \xe2\x80\x9cability-to-elect\xe2\x80\x9d districts. Most of the districts were underpopulated, however, so any new plan required moving significant numbers of new voters into these districts in order to comply with the principle of one person, one vote. Under the benchmark plan, the districts had BVAPs ranging from 62.7% down to 46.3%. Three districts had BVAPs below 55%.

Seeking to maintain minority voters\xe2\x80\x99 ability to elect their preferred candidates in these districts while complying with the one-person, one-vote criterion, legislators concluded that each of the 12 districts \xe2\x80\x9cneeded to contain a BVAP of at least 55%.\xe2\x80\x9d 141 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d, at 519. At trial, the parties disputed whether the 55% figure \xe2\x80\x9cwas an aspiration or a target or a rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But they did not dispute \xe2\x80\x9cthe most important question\xe2\x80\x94whether [the 55%] figure was used in drawing the Challenged Districts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The parties agreed, and the District Court found, \xe2\x80\x9cthat the 55% BVAP figure was used in structuring the districts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In the enacted plan all 12 districts contained a BVAP greater than 55%.

Who first suggested the 55% BVAP criterion and how the legislators agreed upon it was less clear from the evidence. See id., at 521 (describing the \xe2\x80\x9c[t]estimony on this question\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9ca muddle\xe2\x80\x9d). In the end, the District Court found that the 55% criterion emerged from discussions among certain members of the House Black Caucus and the leader of the redistricting effort in the House, Delegate Chris Jones, \xe2\x80\x9cbased largely on concerns pertaining to the re-election of Delegate Tyler in [District] 75.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 522. The 55% figure \xe2\x80\x9cwas then applied across the board to all twelve\xe2\x80\x9d districts. Ibid.

In April 2011, the General Assembly passed Delegate Jones\xe2\x80\x99 plan with broad support from both parties and members of the Black Caucus. One of only two dissenting members of the Black Caucus was Delegate Tyler of District 75, who objected solely on the ground that the 55.4% BVAP in her district was too low. In June 2011, the U.\xc2\xa0S. Department of Justice precleared the plan.

Three years later, before this suit was filed, a separate District Court struck down Virginia\xe2\x80\x99s third federal congressional district (not at issue here), based in part on the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s use of a 55% BVAP threshold. See Page v. Virginia State Bd. of Elections, 58 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d 533, 553 (ED Va. 2014), vacated and remanded sub\xc2\xa0nom. Cantor v. Personhuballah, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015), judgt. entered sub\xc2\xa0nom. Page v. Virginia State Bd. of Elections, 2015 WL 3604029 (June 5, 2015), appeal dism\xe2\x80\x99d sub\xc2\xa0nom. Wittman v. Personhuballah, 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016). After that decision, 12 voters registered in the 12 districts here at issue filed this action challenging the district lines under the Equal Protection Clause. Because the claims \xe2\x80\x9cchalleng[ed] the constitutionality of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the apportionment of [a] statewide legislative body,\xe2\x80\x9d the case was heard by a three-judge District Court.28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72284(a). The Virginia House of Delegates and its Speaker, William Howell (together referred to hereinafter as the State), intervened and assumed responsibility for defending the plan, both before the District Court and now before this Court.

After a 4-day bench trial, a divided District Court ruled for the State. With respect to each challenged district, the court first assessed whether \xe2\x80\x9cracial considerations predominated over\xe2\x80\x94or \xe2\x80\x98subordinated\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94traditional redistricting criteria.\xe2\x80\x9d 141 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d, at 523. An essential premise of the majority opinion was that race does not predominate unless there is an \xe2\x80\x9cactual conflict between traditional redistricting criteria and race that leads to the subordination of the former.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 524. To implement that standard, moreover, the court limited its inquiry into racial motive to those portions of the district lines that appeared to deviate from traditional criteria. The court thus \xe2\x80\x9cexamine[d] those aspects of the [district] that appear[ed] to constitute \xe2\x80\x98deviations\xe2\x80\x99 from neutral criteria\xe2\x80\x9d to ascertain whether the deviations were attributable to race or to other considerations, \xe2\x80\x9csuch as protection of incumbents.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 533\xe2\x80\x93534. Only if the court found a deviation attributable to race did it proceed to \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine whether racial considerations qualitatively subordinated all other non-racial districting criteria.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Under that analysis, the court found that race did not predominate in 11 of the 12 districts.

When it turned to District 75, the District Court found that race did predominate. The court reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]chieving a 55% BVAP floor required \xe2\x80\x98drastic maneuvering\xe2\x80\x99 that is reflected on the face of the district.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 557. Applying strict scrutiny, the court held that compliance with \xc2\xa75 was a compelling state interest and that the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of race in District 75 was narrowly tailored. As to narrow tailoring, the court explained that the State had \xe2\x80\x9ca strong basis in evidence\xe2\x80\x9d to believe that its actions were \xe2\x80\x9creasonably necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to avoid retrogression. Id., at 548. In particular, the court found that Delegate Jones had considered \xe2\x80\x9cprecisely the kinds of evidence that legislators are encouraged to use\xe2\x80\x9d in achieving compliance with \xc2\xa75, including turnout rates, the district\xe2\x80\x99s large disenfranchised prison population, and voting patterns in the contested 2005 primary and general elections. Id., at 558.

Judge Keenan dissented as to all 12 districts. She concluded that the majority applied an incorrect understanding of racial predominance and that Delegate Jones\xe2\x80\x99 analysis of District 75 was too \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral and conclusory.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 578. This appeal followed, and probable jurisdiction was noted. 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016); see28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71253.

II

Against the factual and procedural background set out above, it is now appropriate to consider the controlling legal principles in this case. The Equal Protection Clause prohibits a State, without sufficient justification, from \xe2\x80\x9cseparat[ing] its citizens into different voting districts on the basis of race.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 911. The harms that flow from racial sorting \xe2\x80\x9cinclude being personally subjected to a racial classification as well as being represented by a legislator who believes his primary obligation is to represent only the members of a particular racial group.\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 6) (alterations, citation, and internal quotation marks omitted). At the same time, courts must \xe2\x80\x9cexercise extraordinary caution in adjudicating claims that a State has drawn district lines on the basis of race.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 916. \xe2\x80\x9cElectoral districting is a most difficult subject for legislatures,\xe2\x80\x9d requiring a delicate balancing of competing considerations. Id., at 915. And \xe2\x80\x9credistricting differs from other kinds of state decisionmaking in that the legislature always is aware of race when it draws district lines, just as it is aware of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a variety of other demographic factors.\xe2\x80\x9d Shaw v. Reno,509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 630,646 (1993) (Shaw I).

In light of these considerations, this Court has held that a plaintiff alleging racial gerrymandering bears the burden \xe2\x80\x9cto show, either through circumstantial evidence of a district\xe2\x80\x99s shape and demographics or more direct evidence going to legislative purpose, that race was the predominant factor motivating the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s decision to place a significant number of voters within or without a particular district.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S. at 916. To satisfy this burden, the plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cmust prove that the legislature subordinated traditional race-neutral districting principles .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to racial considerations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The challengers contend that, in finding that race did not predominate in 11 of the 12 districts, the District Court misapplied controlling law in two principal ways. This Court considers them in turn.

A

The challengers first argue that the District Court misunderstood the relevant precedents when it required the challengers to establish, as a prerequisite to showing racial predominance, an actual conflict between the enacted plan and traditional redistricting principles. The Court agrees with the challengers on this point.

A threshold requirement that the enacted plan must conflict with traditional principles might have been reconcilable with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s case law at an earlier time. In Shaw I, the Court recognized a claim of racial gerrymandering for the first time. See 509 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 652. Certain language in Shaw I can be read to support requiring a challenger who alleges racial gerrymandering to show an actual conflict with traditional principles. The opinion stated, for example, that strict scrutiny applies to \xe2\x80\x9credistricting legislation that is so bizarre on its face that it is unexplainable on grounds other than race.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 644 (internal quotation marks omitted). The opinion also stated that \xe2\x80\x9creapportionment is one area in which appearances do matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 647.

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in Miller, however, clarified the racial predominance inquiry. In particular, it rejected the argument that, \xe2\x80\x9cregardless of the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s purposes, a plaintiff must demonstrate that a district\xe2\x80\x99s shape is so bizarre that it is unexplainable other than on the basis of race.\xe2\x80\x9d 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 910\xe2\x80\x93911. The Court held to the contrary in language central to the instant case: \xe2\x80\x9cShape is relevant not because bizarreness is a necessary element of the constitutional wrong or a threshold requirement of proof, but because it may be persuasive circumstantial evidence that race for its own sake, and not other districting principles, was the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s dominant and controlling rationale.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 913. Parties therefore \xe2\x80\x9cmay rely on evidence other than bizarreness to establish race-based districting,\xe2\x80\x9d and may show predominance \xe2\x80\x9ceither through circumstantial evidence of a district\xe2\x80\x99s shape and demographics or more direct evidence going to legislative purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 913, 916.

The Court addressed racial gerrymandering and traditional redistricting factors again in Shaw v. Hunt,517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 899 (1996) (Shaw II). The Court there rejected the view of one of the dissents that \xe2\x80\x9cstrict scrutiny does not apply where a State \xe2\x80\x98respects\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98complies with traditional districting principles.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 906 (quoting id., at 931\xe2\x80\x93932 (Stevens, J., dissenting); alteration omitted). Race may predominate even when a reapportionment plan respects traditional principles, the Court explained, if \xe2\x80\x9c[r]ace was the criterion that, in the State\xe2\x80\x99s view, could not be compromised,\xe2\x80\x9d and race-neutral considerations \xe2\x80\x9ccame into play only after the race-based decision had been made.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 907.

The State\xe2\x80\x99s theory in this case is irreconcilable with Miller and Shaw II. The State insists, for example, that the harm from racial gerrymandering lies not in racial line-drawing per se but in grouping voters of the same race together when they otherwise lack shared interests. But \xe2\x80\x9cthe constitutional violation\xe2\x80\x9d in racial gerrymandering cases stems from the \xe2\x80\x9cracial purpose of state action, not its stark manifestation.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller, supra, at 913. The Equal Protection Clause does not prohibit misshapen districts. It prohibits unjustified racial classifications.

The State contends further that race does not have a prohibited effect on a district\xe2\x80\x99s lines if the legislature could have drawn the same lines in accordance with traditional criteria. That argument parallels the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning that a reapportionment plan is not an express racial classification unless a racial purpose is apparent from the face of the plan based on the irregular nature of the lines themselves. See 141 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d, at 524\xe2\x80\x93526. This is incorrect. The racial predominance inquiry concerns the actual considerations that provided the essential basis for the lines drawn, not post hoc justifications the legislature in theory could have used but in reality did not.

Traditional redistricting principles, moreover, are numerous and malleable. The District Court here identified no fewer than 11 race-neutral redistricting factors a legislature could consider, some of which are \xe2\x80\x9csurprisingly ethereal\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cadmi[t] of degrees.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 535, 537. By deploying those factors in various combinations and permutations, a State could construct a plethora of potential maps that look consistent with traditional, race-neutral principles. But if race for its own sake is the overriding reason for choosing one map over others, race still may predominate.

For these reasons, a conflict or inconsistency between the enacted plan and traditional redistricting criteria is not a threshold requirement or a mandatory precondition in order for a challenger to establish a claim of racial gerrymandering. Of course, a conflict or inconsistency may be persuasive circumstantial evidence tending to show racial predomination, but there is no rule requiring challengers to present this kind of evidence in every case.

As a practical matter, in many cases, perhaps most cases, challengers will be unable to prove an unconstitutional racial gerrymander without evidence that the enacted plan conflicts with traditional redistricting criteria. In general, legislatures that engage in impermissible race-based redistricting will find it necessary to depart from traditional principles in order to do so. And, in the absence of a conflict with traditional principles, it may be difficult for challengers to find other evidence sufficient to show that race was the overriding factor causing neutral considerations to be cast aside. In fact, this Court to date has not affirmed a predominance finding, or remanded a case for a determination of predominance, without evidence that some district lines deviated from traditional principles. See Alabama, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 17); Hunt v. Cromartie,526 U.\xc2\xa0S. 541,547 (1999); Bush v. Vera,517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 952,962,966,974 (1996) (plurality opinion); Shaw II, supra, at 905\xe2\x80\x93906; Miller, supra, at 917; Shaw I, supra, at 635\xe2\x80\x93636. Yet the law responds to proper evidence and valid inferences in ever-changing circumstances, as it learns more about ways in which its commands are circumvented. So there may be cases where challengers will be able to establish racial predominance in the absence of an actual conflict by presenting direct evidence of the legislative purpose and intent or other compelling circumstantial evidence.

B

The challengers submit that the District Court erred further when it considered the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s racial motive only to the extent that the challengers identified deviations from traditional redistricting criteria that were attributable to race and not to some other factor. In the challengers\xe2\x80\x99 view, this approach foreclosed a holistic analysis of each district and led the District Court to give insufficient weight to the 55% BVAP target and other relevant evidence that race predominated. Again, this Court agrees.

As explained, showing a deviation from, or conflict with, traditional redistricting principles is not a necessary prerequisite to establishing racial predominance. Supra, at 10. But even where a challenger alleges a conflict, or succeeds in showing one, the court should not confine its analysis to the conflicting portions of the lines. That is because the basic unit of analysis for racial gerrymandering claims in general, and for the racial predominance inquiry in particular, is the district. Racial gerrymandering claims proceed \xe2\x80\x9cdistrict-by-district.\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 6). \xe2\x80\x9cWe have consistently described a claim of racial gerrymandering as a claim that race was improperly used in the drawing of the boundaries of one or more specific electoral districts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And Miller\xe2\x80\x99s basic predominance test scrutinizes the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s motivation for placing \xe2\x80\x9ca significant number of voters within or without a particular district.\xe2\x80\x9d 515 U. S., at 916. Courts evaluating racial predominance therefore should not divorce any portion of the lines\xe2\x80\x94whatever their relationship to traditional principles\xe2\x80\x94from the rest of the district.

This is not to suggest that courts evaluating racial gerrymandering claims may not consider evidence pertaining to an area that is larger or smaller than the district at issue. The Court has recognized that \xe2\x80\x9c[v]oters, of course, can present statewide evidence in order to prove racial gerrymandering in a particular district.\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 7) (emphasis deleted). Districts share borders, after all, and a legislature may pursue a common redistricting policy toward multiple districts. Likewise, a legislature\xe2\x80\x99s race-based decisionmaking may be evident in a notable way in a particular part of a district. It follows that a court may consider evidence regarding certain portions of a district\xe2\x80\x99s lines, including portions that conflict with traditional redistricting principles.

The ultimate object of the inquiry, however, is the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s predominant motive for the design of the district as a whole. A court faced with a racial gerrymandering claim therefore must consider all of the lines of the district at issue; any explanation for a particular portion of the lines, moreover, must take account of the districtwide context. Concentrating on particular portions in isolation may obscure the significance of relevant districtwide evidence, such as stark splits in the racial composition of populations moved into and out of disparate parts of the district, or the use of an express racial target. A holistic analysis is necessary to give that kind of evidence its proper weight.

C

The challengers ask this Court not only to correct the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s racial predominance standard but also to apply that standard and conclude that race in fact did predominate in the 11 districts where the District Court held that it did not. For its part, the State asks the Court to hold that, even if race did predominate in these districts, the State\xe2\x80\x99s predominant use of race was narrowly tailored to the compelling interest in complying with \xc2\xa75.

The Court declines these requests. \xe2\x80\x9c[O]urs is a court of final review and not first view.\xe2\x80\x9d Department of Transportation v. Association of American Railroads, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 12) (internal quotation marks omitted). The District Court is best positioned to determine in the first instance the extent to which, under the proper standard, race directed the shape of these 11 districts. And if race did predominate, it is proper for the District Court to determine in the first instance whether strict scrutiny is satisfied. These matters are left for the District Court on remand.

III

The Court now turns to the arguments regarding District 75. Where a challenger succeeds in establishing racial predominance, the burden shifts to the State to \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrate that its districting legislation is narrowly tailored to achieve a compelling interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller, supra, at 920. The District Court here determined that the State\xe2\x80\x99s predominant use of race in District 75 was narrowly tailored to achieve compliance with \xc2\xa75. The challengers contest the finding of narrow tailoring, but they do not dispute that compliance with \xc2\xa75 was a compelling interest at the relevant time. As in previous cases, therefore, the Court assumes, without deciding, that the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in complying with the Voting Rights Act was compelling. E.g., Alabama, supra, at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 19\xe2\x80\x9323); Shaw II, 517 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 915.

Turning to narrow tailoring, the Court explained the contours of that requirement in Alabama. When a State justifies the predominant use of race in redistricting on the basis of the need to comply with the Voting Rights Act, \xe2\x80\x9cthe narrow tailoring requirement insists only that the legislature have a strong basis in evidence in support of the (race-based) choice that it has made.\xe2\x80\x9d 575 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 22) (internal quotation marks omitted). That standard does not require the State to show that its action was \xe2\x80\x9cactually .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to avoid a statutory violation, so that, but for its use of race, the State would have lost in court. Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). Rather, the requisite strong basis in evidence exists when the legislature has \xe2\x80\x9cgood reasons to believe\xe2\x80\x9d it must use race in order to satisfy the Voting Rights Act, \xe2\x80\x9ceven if a court does not find that the actions were necessary for statutory compliance.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).

The Court now finds no error in the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the State had sufficient grounds to determine that the race-based calculus it employed in District 75 was necessary to avoid violating \xc2\xa75. As explained, \xc2\xa75 at the time barred Virginia from adopting any districting change that would \xe2\x80\x9chave the effect of diminishing the ability of [members of a minority group] to elect their preferred candidates of choice.\xe2\x80\x9d52 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa710304(b). Determining what minority population percentage will satisfy that standard is a difficult task requiring, in the view of the Department of Justice, a \xe2\x80\x9cfunctional analysis of the electoral behavior within the particular .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. election district.\xe2\x80\x9d Guidance Concerning Redistricting Under Section 5 of the Voting Rights Act, 76 Fed. Reg. 7471 (2011).

Under the facts found by the District Court, the legislature performed that kind of functional analysis of District 75 when deciding upon the 55% BVAP target. Redrawing this district presented a difficult task, and the result reflected the good-faith efforts of Delegate Jones and his colleagues to achieve an informed bipartisan consensus. Delegate Jones met with Delegate Tyler \xe2\x80\x9cprobably half a dozen times to configure her district\xe2\x80\x9d in order to avoid retrogression. 141 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d, at 558 (internal quotation marks omitted). He discussed the district with incumbents from other majority-minority districts. He also considered turnout rates, the results of the recent contested primary and general elections in 2005, and the dis-trict\xe2\x80\x99s large population of disenfranchised black prisoners. The challengers, moreover, do not dispute that District 75 was an ability-to-elect district, or that white and black voters in the area tend to vote as blocs. See id., at 557\xe2\x80\x93559. In light of Delegate Jones\xe2\x80\x99 careful assessment of local conditions and structures, the State had a strong basis in evidence to believe a 55% BVAP floor was required to avoid retrogression.

The challengers\xe2\x80\x99 responses ask too much from state officials charged with the sensitive duty of reapportioning legislative districts. First, the challengers contest the sufficiency of the evidence showing that Delegate Jones in fact performed a functional analysis, in part because that analysis was not memorialized in writing. But the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s factual findings are reviewed only for clear error. See Easley v. Cromartie,532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 234,242 (2001). The findings regarding how the legislature arrived at the 55% BVAP target are well supported, and \xe2\x80\x9cwe do not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. require States engaged in redistricting to compile a comprehensive administrative record.\xe2\x80\x9d Vera, 517 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 966 (internal quotation marks omitted).

The challengers argue further that the drafters of the plan had insufficient evidence to justify a 55% BVAP floor. The 2005 elections were idiosyncratic, the challengers contend; moreover, demographic information about the prison in the district is absent from the record, and Delegate Tyler\xe2\x80\x99s perspective was influenced by a personal interest in reelection. That may have been so, and for those reasons, it is possible that, if the State had drawn District 75 with a BVAP below 55% and had sought judicial preclearance, a court would have found no \xc2\xa75 violation. But that is not the question here. \xe2\x80\x9cThe law cannot insist that a state legislature, when redistricting, determine precisely what percent minority population \xc2\xa75 demands.\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 22). The question is whether the State had \xe2\x80\x9cgood reasons\xe2\x80\x9d to believe a 55% BVAP floor was necessary to avoid liability under \xc2\xa75. Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). The State did have good reasons under these circumstances. Holding otherwise would afford state legislatures too little breathing room, leaving them \xe2\x80\x9ctrapped between the competing hazards of liability\xe2\x80\x9d under the Voting Rights Act and the Equal Protection Clause. Vera, supra, at 977 (internal quotation marks omitted).

As a final point, the challengers liken the 55% BVAP floor here to the \xe2\x80\x9cmechanically numerical view\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa75 this Court rejected in Alabama. 575 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 21). But Alabama did not condemn the use of BVAP targets to comply with \xc2\xa75 in every instance. Rather, this Court corrected the \xe2\x80\x9cmisperception\xe2\x80\x9d that \xc2\xa75 required a State to \xe2\x80\x9cmaintai[n] the same population percentages in majority-minority districts as in the prior plan.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320). \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t would seem highly unlikely,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court explained, that reducing a district\xe2\x80\x99s BVAP \xe2\x80\x9cfrom, say, 70% to 65% would have a significant impact on the black voters\xe2\x80\x99 ability to elect their preferred candidate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 21). Yet reducing the BVAP below 55% well might have that effect in some cases. The record here supports the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that this was one instance where a 55% BVAP was necessary for black voters to have a functional working majority.

IV

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in this case is controlled by precedent. The Court reaffirms the basic racial predominance analysis explained in Miller and Shaw II, and the basic narrow tailoring analysis explained in Alabama. The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment as to District 75 is consistent with these principles. Applying these principles to the remaining 11 districts is entrusted to the District Court in the first instance.

The judgment of the District Court is affirmed in part and vacated in part. The case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.

GOLDEN BETHUNE-HILL, et\xc2\xa0al., APPELLANTS v. VIRGINIA STATE BOARD OF ELECTIONS, et\xc2\xa0al.

on appeal from the united states district court for the eastern district of virginia

[March 1, 2017]

Justice Thomas, concurring in the judgment in part and dissenting in part.

Appellants contend that 12 of Virginia\xe2\x80\x99s state legislative districts are unconstitutional racial gerrymanders. The three-judge District Court rejected their challenge, holding that race was not the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s predominant motive in drawing 11 of the districts and that the remaining district survives strict scrutiny. I would reverse the District Court as to all 12 districts. I therefore concur in the judgment in part and dissent in part.

I

I concur in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment reversing the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to uphold 11 of the 12 districts at issue in this case\xe2\x80\x94House Districts 63, 69, 70, 71, 74, 77, 80, 89, 90, 92, and 95. I do not agree, however, with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to leave open the question whether race predominated in those districts and, thus, whether they are subject to strict scrutiny. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. Appellees (hereinafter State) concede that the legislature intentionally drew all 12 districts as majority-black districts. See, e.g., Brief for Appellees 1 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he legislature sought to achieve a [black voting-age population] of at least 55% in adjusting the lines of the 12 majority-minority districts\xe2\x80\x9d). That concession, in my view, mandates strict scrutiny as to each district. See Bush v. Vera,517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 952,1000 (1996) (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment) (A State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cconcession that it intentionally created majority-minority districts [i]s sufficient to show that race was a predominant, motivating factor in its redistricting\xe2\x80\x9d); League of United Latin American Citizens v. Perry,548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399,517 (2006) (LULAC) (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen a legislature intentionally creates a majority-minority district, race is necessarily its predominant motivation and strict scrutiny is therefore triggered\xe2\x80\x9d). I would therefore hold that the District Court must apply strict scrutiny to Districts 63, 69, 70, 71, 74, 77, 80, 89, 90, 92, and 95 on remand.

II

I disagree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment with respect to the remaining district, District 75. The majority affirms the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that District 75 is subject to strict scrutiny. With this I agree, because, as with the other 11 districts, the State conceded that it intentionally drew District 75 as a majority-black district.

I disagree, however, with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s determination that District 75 satisfies strict scrutiny. This Court has held that a State may draw distinctions among its citizens based on race only when it \xe2\x80\x9cis pursuing a compelling state interest\xe2\x80\x9d and has chosen \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowly tailored\xe2\x80\x9d means to accomplish that interest. Shaw v. Hunt,517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 899,908 (1996) (internal quotation marks omitted). The State asserts that it used race in drawing District 75 to further a \xe2\x80\x9ccompelling interest in complying with Section 5 of the [Voting Rights Act of 1965].\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellees 50.[1] And it argues that, based on its \xe2\x80\x9cgood-faith functional analysis\xe2\x80\x9d of the district, it narrowly tailored its use of race to achieve that interest. Id., at 56. In my view, the State has neither asserted a compelling state interest nor narrowly tailored its use of race.

A

As an initial matter, the majority errs by \xe2\x80\x9cassum[ing], without deciding, that the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in complying with the Voting Rights Act was compelling.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. To be sure, this Court has previously assumed that a State has a compelling interest in complying with the Voting Rights Act. But it has done so only in cases in which it has not upheld the redistricting plan at issue. See, e.g., Miller v. Johnson,515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 900,921 (1995) (leaving open the question \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether or not in some cases compliance with the [Voting Rights] Act, standing alone, can provide a compelling interest independent of any interest in remedying past discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d).[2] This Court has never, before today, assumed a compelling state interest while upholding a state redistricting plan. Indeed, I know of no other case, in any context, in which the Court has assumed away part of the State\xe2\x80\x99s burden to justify its intentional use of race. This should not be the first. I would hold that complying with \xc2\xa75 of the Voting Rights Act is not a compelling interest.

\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ompliance with federal antidiscrimination laws cannot justify race-based districting where the challenged district was not reasonably necessary under a constitutional reading and application of those laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 921 (emphasis added). More than a decade ago, I joined Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in LULAC, which noted that this Court had \xe2\x80\x9cupheld the constitutionality of \xc2\xa75 as a proper exercise of Congress\xe2\x80\x99s authority under \xc2\xa72 of the Fifteenth Amendment to enforce that Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on the denial or abridgment of the right to vote.\xe2\x80\x9d 548 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 518. I therefore agreed that, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n the proper case, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a covered jurisdiction may have a compelling interest in complying with \xc2\xa75.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 519.

I have since concluded that \xc2\xa75 is \xe2\x80\x9cunconstitutional.\xe2\x80\x9d Northwest Austin Municipal Util. Dist. No. One v. Holder,557 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193,216 (2009) (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part). \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he violence, intimidation, and subterfuge that led Congress to pass \xc2\xa75 and this Court to uphold it no longer remains,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 229, so \xc2\xa75 \xe2\x80\x9ccan no longer be justified as an appropriate mechanism for enforcement of the Fifteenth Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 216. Because, in my view, \xc2\xa75 is unconstitutional, I would hold that a State does not have a compelling interest in complying with it.

B

Even if compliance with \xc2\xa75 were a compelling interest, the State failed to narrowly tailor its use of race to further that interest.

1

This Court has explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny preference based on racial or ethnic criteria must necessarily receive a most searching examination.\xe2\x80\x9d Wygant v. Jackson Bd. of Ed.,476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267,273 (1986) (plurality opinion) (internal quotation marks omitted); accord, Adarand Constructors, Inc. v. Pe\xc3\xb1a,515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 200,223 (1995); Grutter v. Bollinger,539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 306,378 (2003) (Rehnquist, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting). This exacting scrutiny makes sense because \xe2\x80\x9c[d]iscrimination on the basis of race\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9codious in all aspects.\xe2\x80\x9d Rose v. Mitchell,443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 545,555 (1979). Accordingly, a State\xe2\x80\x99s use of race must bear \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the most exact connection\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to the compelling state interest. Wygant, supra, at 280 (opinion of Powell, J.). In the context of redistricting, the redistricting map must, \xe2\x80\x9cat a minimum,\xe2\x80\x9d actually \xe2\x80\x9cremedy the anticipated violation\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cachieve compliance\xe2\x80\x9d with the Voting Rights Act. Shaw, 517 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 916.

I have serious doubts about the Court\xe2\x80\x99s standard for narrow tailoring, as characterized today and in Alabama Legislative Black Caucus v. Alabama, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015). Relying on Alabama, the majority explains that narrow tailoring in the redistricting context requires \xe2\x80\x9conly that the legislature have a strong basis in evidence in support of the (race-based) choice that it has made.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14 (internal quotation marks omitted). That standard \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not demand that a State\xe2\x80\x99s actions actually be necessary to achieve a compelling state interest in order to be constitutionally valid.\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 22) (internal quotation marks omitted); see also ante, at 14. Instead, under that standard, a state legislature needs only \xe2\x80\x9cgood reasons to believe\xe2\x80\x9d that the use of race is required, even if the use of race is not \xe2\x80\x9cactually .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. necessary.\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 22) (internal quotation marks omitted).

That approach to narrow tailoring\xe2\x80\x94deferring to a State\xe2\x80\x99s belief that it has good reasons to use race\xe2\x80\x94is \xe2\x80\x9cstrict\xe2\x80\x9d in name only. To the extent the Court applies Alabama to dilute the well-settled standard established by our precedents, I demur.

2

Applying the proper narrow-tailoring standard for state classifications based on race, I conclude that the State did not narrowly tailor its use of race to comply with \xc2\xa75. As the majority recognizes, \xc2\xa75 requires a state redistricting plan to maintain the black population\xe2\x80\x99s ability to elect the candidate of its choice in the district at issue\xe2\x80\x94in other words, the State must \xe2\x80\x9cavoid retrogression\xe2\x80\x9d in the new district. Ante, at 14.

The majority observes that the redistricting plan\xe2\x80\x99s architect, Delegate Chris Jones, performed a \xe2\x80\x9cfunctional analysis\xe2\x80\x9d in deciding that District 75 required a 55% black voting-age population\xe2\x80\x94as opposed to some other percentage\xe2\x80\x94to avoid retrogression. Ibid. The Court notes that, in arriving at the 55% threshold, Delegate Jones considered turnout rates, the results of the primary and general elections in 2005, and the district\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9clarge population of disenfranchised black prisoners.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. He also met with the incumbent delegate for District 75 \xe2\x80\x9cprobably half a dozen times\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdiscussed the district with incumbents from other majority-black districts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315 (internal quotation marks omitted). Those efforts add up, in the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view, to a \xe2\x80\x9ccareful assessment of local conditions and structures.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15.

I do not agree that those efforts satisfy narrow tailoring. Delegate Jones admitted that he was \xe2\x80\x9cnot aware\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cany retrogress[ion] analysis\xe2\x80\x9d performed by \xe2\x80\x9ch[im] or any persons that worked with him in the development of the [redistricting] plan.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 288\xe2\x80\x93289. Instead, he merely \xe2\x80\x9clook[ed] at\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cpercentage of black population and the percentage of black voting age population,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9clooked at what happened over the last 10 year period given the existing population and demographic shifts,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctried to restore back\xe2\x80\x9d the levels of black voting-age population from the previous maps. Id., at 290. That approach was misguided, because \xc2\xa75 \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not require maintaining the same population percentages in majority-minority districts as in the prior plan.\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 20). And in any event, that back-of-the-envelope calculation does not qualify as rigorous analysis. I do not think we would permit so imprecise an approach with regard to any other instance of racial discrimination.

The other evidence cited by the majority is similarly weak. The majority points to the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98half a dozen\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d meetings between Delegate Jones and the incumbent delegate for District 75, ante, at 14, but it is not apparent from the record whether District 75\xe2\x80\x99s incumbent is the current black population\xe2\x80\x99s candidate of choice. Moreover, the incumbent delegate may well have wanted her district to be electorally safer than the Voting Rights Act requires. It also is not obvious to me that Delegate Jones was seeking to avoid retrogression in District 75 when he met with incumbent delegates from other majority-black districts. Ibid. In my view, those efforts fall far short of establishing that a 55% black voting-age population bears a more \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98exact connection\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest than any alternative percentage. Wygant, supra, at 279 (opinion of Powell, J.). Accordingly, I would hold that the State failed to narrowly tailor its use of race to avoid retrogression in District 75.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

In reaching these conclusions, I recognize that this Court is at least as responsible as the state legislature for these racially gerrymandered districts. As explained above, this Court has repeatedly failed to decide whether compliance with the Voting Rights Act is a compelling governmental interest. See supra, at 3, and n. 2. Indeed, this Court has refused even to decide whether \xc2\xa75 is constitutional, despite having twice taken cases to decide that question. Compare Juris. Statement in Northwest Austin, O.\xc2\xa0T. 2008, No. 08\xe2\x80\x93322, p. i (presenting the question \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the \xc2\xa75 preclearance requirement can be applied as a valid exercise of Congress\xe2\x80\x99s remedial powers under the Reconstruction Amendments\xe2\x80\x9d), and Shelby County v. Holder,568 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1006 (2012) (granting certio-rari on the question \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision in 2006 to reauthorize Section 5 of the Voting Rights Act under the pre-existing coverage formula of Section 4(b) .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. violated the Tenth Amendment and Article IV of the United States Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d), with Northwest Austin, 557 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 197 (holding that the district at issue was eligible to seek bailout under the Voting Rights Act and therefore \xe2\x80\x9cnot reach[ing] the constitutionality of \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x9d), and Shelby County v. Holder, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 24) (holding only that the coverage formula under \xc2\xa74(b) was unconstitutional and \xe2\x80\x9cissu[ing] no holding on \xc2\xa75 itself\xe2\x80\x9d). As a result, the Court has left the State without clear guidance about its redistricting obligations under \xc2\xa75.

This Court has put the State in a similar bind with respect to narrow tailoring. To comply with \xc2\xa75, a State necessarily must make a deliberate and precise effort to sort its citizens on the basis of their race. But that result is fundamentally at odds with our \xe2\x80\x9ccolor-blind\xe2\x80\x9d Constitution, which \xe2\x80\x9cneither knows nor tolerates classes among citizens.\xe2\x80\x9d Plessy v. Ferguson,163 U.\xc2\xa0S. 537,559 (1896) (Harlan, J., dissenting). That contradiction illustrates the perversity of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisprudence in this area as well as the uncomfortable position in which the State might find itself.

Despite my sympathy for the State, I cannot ignore the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s clear prohibition on state-sponsored race discrimination. \xe2\x80\x9cThe Constitution abhors classifications based on race, not only because those classifications can harm favored races or are based on illegitimate motives, but also because every time the government places citizens on racial registers .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , it demeans us all.\xe2\x80\x9d Grutter, 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 353 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). This prohibition was \xe2\x80\x9c[p]urchased at the price of immeasurable human suffering,\xe2\x80\x9d and it \xe2\x80\x9creflects our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s understanding that such classifications ultimately have a destructive impact on the individual and our society.\xe2\x80\x9d Adarand Constructors, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 240 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). I respectfully dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment as to District 75.
