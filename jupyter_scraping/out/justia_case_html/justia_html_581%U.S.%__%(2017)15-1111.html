BANK OF AMERICA CORP. et\xc2\xa0al. v. CITY OF MIAMI, FLORIDA

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x931111.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 8, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 1, 2017[1]

\n

The City of Miami filed suit against Bank of America and Wells Fargo (Banks), alleging violations of the Fair Housing Act (FHA or Act). The FHA prohibits, among other things, racial discrimination in connection with real-estate transactions, 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73604(b), 3605(a), and permits any \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d to file a civil damages action for a violation of the Act, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73613(a)(1)(A), (c)(1). The City\xe2\x80\x99s complaints charge that the Banks intentionally targeted predatory practices at African-American and Latino neighborhoods and residents, lending to minority borrowers on worse terms than equally creditworthy nonminority borrowers and inducing defaults by failing to extend refinancing and loan modifications to minority borrowers on fair terms. The City alleges that the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 discriminatory conduct led to a disproportionate number of foreclosures and vacancies in majority-minority neighborhoods, which impaired the City\xe2\x80\x99s effort to assure racial integration, diminished the City\xe2\x80\x99s property-tax revenue, and increased demand for police, fire, and other municipal services. The District Court dismissed the complaints on the grounds that (1) the harms alleged fell outside the zone of interests the FHA protects and (2) the complaints failed to show a sufficient causal connection between the City\xe2\x80\x99s injuries and the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 discriminatory conduct. The Eleventh Circuit reversed.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0The City is an \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d authorized to bring suit under the FHA. In addition to satisfying constitutional standing requirements, see Spokeo, Inc. v. Robins, 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, a plaintiff must show that the statute grants the plaintiff the cause of action he or she asserts. It is presumed that a statute ordinarily provides a cause of action \xe2\x80\x9conly to plaintiffs whose interests \xe2\x80\x98fall within the zone of interests protected by the law invoked.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lexmark Int\xe2\x80\x99l, Inc. v. Static Control Components, Inc., 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___.

\n

The City\xe2\x80\x99s claims of financial injury are, at the least, \xe2\x80\x9carguably within the zone of interests\xe2\x80\x9d the FHA protects. Association of Data Processing Service Organizations, Inc. v. Camp,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 150\n. The FHA defines an \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cany person who\xe2\x80\x9d either \xe2\x80\x9cclaims to have been injured by a discriminatory housing practice\xe2\x80\x9d or believes that such an injury \xe2\x80\x9cis about to occur,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73602(i). This Court has said that the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cperson aggrieved\xe2\x80\x9d in the original version of the FHA \xe2\x80\x9cshowed \xe2\x80\x98a congressional intention to define standing as broadly as is permitted by Article III of the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Trafficante v. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co.,\n\n409 U.\xc2\xa0S. 205\n; and has held that the Act permits suit by parties similarly situated to the City, see, e.g., Gladstone, Realtors v. Village of Bellwood,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 91\n (village alleging that it lost tax revenue and had the racial balance of its community undermined by racial-steering practices). Against the backdrop of those decisions, Congress did not materially alter the definition of person \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved\xe2\x80\x9d when it reenacted the current version of the Act.

\n

The Banks nonetheless contend that the definition sets boundaries that fall short of those the Constitution sets. Even assuming that some form of their argument is valid, this Court concludes that the City\xe2\x80\x99s financial injuries fall within the zone of interests that the FHA protects. The City\xe2\x80\x99s claims are similar in kind to those of the Village of Bellwood, which the Court held in Gladstone, supra, could bring suit under the FHA. The Court explained that the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 discriminatory conduct adversely affected the village by, among other things, producing a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant reduction in property values [that] directly injures a municipality by diminishing its tax base, thus threatening its ability to bear the costs of local government and to provide services.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 110\xe2\x80\x93111. The City\xe2\x80\x99s alleged economic injuries thus arguably fall within the FHA\xe2\x80\x99s zone of interests, as this Court has previously interpreted that statute. Stare decisis principles compel the Court\xe2\x80\x99s adherence to those precedents, and principles of statutory interpretation demand that the Court respect Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to ratify those precedents when it reenacted the relevant statutory text. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0The Eleventh Circuit erred in concluding that the complaints met the FHA\xe2\x80\x99s proximate-cause requirement based solely on the finding that the City\xe2\x80\x99s alleged financial injuries were foreseeable results of the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 misconduct. A claim for damages under the FHA is akin to a \xe2\x80\x9ctort action,\xe2\x80\x9d Meyer v. Holley,\n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 280\n, and is thus subject to the common-law requirement that loss is attributable \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to the proximate cause, and not to any remote cause,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lexmark, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___. The proximate-cause analysis asks \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the harm alleged has a sufficiently close connection to the conduct the statute prohibits.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___. With respect to the FHA, foreseeability alone does not ensure the required close connection. Nothing in the statute suggests that Congress intended to provide a remedy for any foreseeable result of an FHA violation, which may \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98cause ripples of harm to flow\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d far beyond the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misconduct, Associated Gen. Contractors of Cal., Inc. v. Carpenters,\n\n459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519\n; and doing so would risk \xe2\x80\x9cmassive and complex damages litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 545. Rather, proximate cause under the FHA requires \xe2\x80\x9csome direct relation between the injury asserted and the injurious conduct alleged.\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes v. Securities Investors Protection Corporation,\n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 258\n. The Court has repeatedly applied directness principles to statutes with \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-law foundations.\xe2\x80\x9d Anza v. Ideal Steel Supply Corp.,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 451\n. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The general tendency\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in these cases, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in regard to damages at least, is not to go beyond the first step.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hemi Group, LLC v. City of New York,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n. What falls within that step depends in part on the \xe2\x80\x9cnature of the statutory cause of action,\xe2\x80\x9d Lexmark, supra, at ___, and an assessment \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98of what is administratively possible and convenient,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes, supra, at 268.

\n

The Court declines to draw the precise boundaries of proximate cause under the FHA, particularly where neither the Eleventh Circuit nor other courts of appeals have weighed in on the issue. Instead, the lower courts should define, in the first instance, the contours of proximate cause under the FHA and decide how that standard applies to the City\xe2\x80\x99s claims for lost property-tax revenue and increased municipal expenses. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.

Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Kennedy and Justice Alito join, concurring in part and dissenting in part.

\n

These cases arise from lawsuits filed by the city of Miami alleging that residential mortgage lenders engaged in discriminatory lending practices in violation of the Fair Housing Act (FHA). The FHA prohibits \xe2\x80\x9cdiscrimination\xe2\x80\x9d against \xe2\x80\x9cany person\xe2\x80\x9d because of \xe2\x80\x9crace, color, religion, sex, handicap, familial status, or national origin\xe2\x80\x9d with respect to the \xe2\x80\x9csale or rental\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9ca dwelling.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73604; accord, \xc2\xa73605(a); \xc2\xa73606. Miami\xe2\x80\x99s complaints do not allege that any defendant discriminated against it within the meaning of the FHA. Neither is Miami attempting to bring a lawsuit on behalf of its residents against whom petitioners allegedly discriminated. Rather, Miami\xe2\x80\x99s theory is that, between 2004 and 2012, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 allegedly discriminatory mortgage-lending practices led to defaulted loans, which led to foreclosures, which led to vacant houses, which led to decreased property values, which led to reduced property taxes and urban blight. See 800 F.\xc2\xa03d 1262, 1268 (CA11 2015); 801 F. 3d 1258, 1266 (CA11 2015). Miami seeks damages from the lenders for reduced property tax revenues and for the cost of increased municipal services\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cpolice, firefighters, building inspectors, debris collectors, and others\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94deployed to attend to the blighted areas. 800 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1269; 801 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1263.

\n

The Court today holds that Congress intended to remedy those kinds of injuries when it enacted the FHA, but leaves open the question whether Miami sufficiently alleged that the discriminatory lending practices caused its injuries. For the reasons explained below, I would hold that Miami\xe2\x80\x99s injuries fall outside the FHA\xe2\x80\x99s zone of interests. I would also hold that, in any event, Miami\xe2\x80\x99s alleged injuries are too remote to satisfy the FHA\xe2\x80\x99s proximate-cause requirement.

\n

I

\n

A plaintiff seeking to bring suit under a federal statute must show not only that he has standing under Article III, ante, at 5, but also that his \xe2\x80\x9ccomplaint fall[s] within the zone of interests protected by the law\xe2\x80\x9d he invokes, Lexmark Int\xe2\x80\x99l, Inc. v. Static Control Components, Inc., 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 7) (internal quotation marks omitted). The zone-of-interests requirement is \xe2\x80\x9croot[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d in the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-law rule\xe2\x80\x9d providing that a plaintiff may \xe2\x80\x9crecover under the law of negligence for injuries caused by violation of a statute\xe2\x80\x9d only if \xe2\x80\x9cthe statute \xe2\x80\x98is interpreted as designed to protect the class of persons in which the plaintiff is included, against the risk of the type of harm which has in fact occurred as a result of its violation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___, n.\xc2\xa05 (slip op., at 11, n.\xc2\xa05) (quoting W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, R. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on Law of Torts \xc2\xa736, pp. 229\xe2\x80\x93230 (5th ed. 1984)). We have \xe2\x80\x9cmade clear\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress is presumed to legislate against the background\xe2\x80\x9d of that common-law rule. Lexmark, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10) (internal quotation marks omitted). We thus apply it \xe2\x80\x9cto all statutorily created causes of action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. unless it is expressly negated.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added; internal quotation marks omitted). \xe2\x80\x9cWhether a plaintiff comes within the zone of interests is an issue that requires us to determine, using traditional tools of statutory interpretation, whether a legislatively conferred cause of action encompasses a particular plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 8) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

A

\n

Nothing in the text of the FHA suggests that Congress intended to deviate from the zone-of-interests limitation. The statute\xe2\x80\x99s private-enforcement mechanism provides that only an \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d may sue, \xc2\xa73613(a)(1)(A), and the statute defines \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d to mean someone who \xe2\x80\x9cclaims to have been injured by a discriminatory housing practice\xe2\x80\x9d or who believes he \xe2\x80\x9cwill be injured by a discriminatory housing practice that is about to occur,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73602(i)(1), (2). That language does not hint\xe2\x80\x94much less expressly provide\xe2\x80\x94that Congress sought to depart from the common-law rule.

\n

We have considered similar language in other statutes and reached the same conclusion. In Thompson v. North American Stainless, LP,\n\n562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 170 (2011)\n, for example, we considered Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s private-enforcement provision, which provides that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a person claiming to be aggrieved\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d may file an employment discrimination charge with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission. Id., at 173 (quoting \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(b)). We unanimously concluded that Congress did not depart from the zone-of-interests limitation in Title VII by using that language. Id., at 175\xe2\x80\x93178. And in Lexmark, we interpreted a provision of the Lanham Act that permitted \xe2\x80\x9cany person who believes that he or she is likely to be damaged by a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s false advertising\xe2\x80\x9d to sue. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10) (internal quotation marks omitted). Even when faced with the broader \xe2\x80\x9cany person\xe2\x80\x9d language, we expressly rejected the argument that the statute conferred a cause of action upon anyone claiming an Article III injury in fact. We observed that it was unlikely that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress meant to allow all factually injured plaintiffs to recover,\xe2\x80\x9d and we concluded that the zone-of-interests test was the \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate tool for determining who may invoke the cause of action\xe2\x80\x9d under the statute. Id., at ___, ___ (slip op., at 10, 11) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

To be sure, some language in our older precedents suggests that the FHA\xe2\x80\x99s zone of interests extends to the limits of Article III. See Trafficante v. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co.,\n\n409 U.\xc2\xa0S. 205,\n209 (1972)\n; Gladstone, Realtors v. Village of Bellwood,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 91,\n109 (1979)\n; Havens Realty Corp. v. Coleman,\n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 363,\n372 (1982)\n. But we have since described that language as \xe2\x80\x9cill-considered\xe2\x80\x9d dictum leading to \xe2\x80\x9cabsurd consequences.\xe2\x80\x9d Thompson, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 176. And we have observed that the \xe2\x80\x9choldings of those cases are compatible with the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98zone of interests\xe2\x80\x99 limitation\xe2\x80\x9d described in Thompson. Ibid. That limitation provides that a plaintiff may not sue when his \xe2\x80\x9cinterests are so marginally related to or inconsistent with the purposes implicit in the statute that it cannot be assumed that Congress intended to permit the suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 178 (internal quotation marks omitted). It thus \xe2\x80\x9cexclud[es] plaintiffs who might technically be injured in an Article III sense but whose interests are unrelated to the statutory prohibitions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

B

\n

In my view, Miami\xe2\x80\x99s asserted injuries are \xe2\x80\x9cso marginally related to or inconsistent with the purposes\xe2\x80\x9d of the FHA that they fall outside the zone of interests. Here, as in any other case, the text of the FHA defines the zone of interests that the statute protects. See Lexmark, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 9). The FHA permits \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n aggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d to sue, \xc2\xa73613(a)(1)(A), if he \xe2\x80\x9cclaims to have been injured by a discriminatory housing practice\xe2\x80\x9d or believes that he \xe2\x80\x9cwill be injured by a discriminatory housing practice that is about to occur,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73602(i)(1), (2) (emphasis added). Specifically, the FHA makes it unlawful to do any of the following on the basis of \xe2\x80\x9crace, color, religion, sex, handicap, familial status, or national origin\xe2\x80\x9d: \xe2\x80\x9crefuse to sell or rent .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a dwelling,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73604(a); discriminate in the \xe2\x80\x9cterms, conditions, or privileges of sale or rental of a dwelling, or in the provision of services or facilities in connection therewith,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73604(b); \xe2\x80\x9cmake, print, or publish .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any notice, statement, or advertisement, with respect to the sale or rental of a dwelling that indicates any preference, limitation, or discrimination,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73604(c); \xe2\x80\x9crepresent to any person .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that any dwelling is not available for inspection, sale, or rental when such dwelling is in fact so available,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73604(d); \xe2\x80\x9cinduce any person to sell or rent any dwelling by representations regarding the entry or prospective entry into the neighborhood of a person or persons of\xe2\x80\x9d certain characteristics, \xc2\xa73604(e); or discriminate in the provision of real estate or brokerage services, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73605, 3606. The quintessential \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d in cases involving violations of the FHA is a prospective home buyer or lessee discriminated against during the home-buying or leasing process. Our cases have also suggested that the interests of a person who lives in a neighborhood or apartment complex that remains segregated (or that risks becoming segregated) as a result of a discriminatory housing practice may be arguably within the outer limit of the interests the FHA protects. See Trafficante, supra, at 211 (concluding that one purpose of the FHA was to promote \xe2\x80\x9ctruly integrated and balanced living patterns\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

Miami\xe2\x80\x99s asserted injuries are not arguably related to the interests the statute protects. Miami asserts that it received \xe2\x80\x9creduced property tax revenues,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 233, 417, and that it was forced to spend more money on \xe2\x80\x9cmunicipal services that it provided and still must provide to remedy blight and unsafe and dangerous conditions,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 417; see also ante, at 2. The city blames these expenditures on the falling property values and vacant homes that resulted from foreclosures. But nothing in the text of the FHA suggests that Congress was concerned about decreased property values, foreclosures, and urban blight, much less about strains on municipal budgets that might follow.

\n

Miami\xe2\x80\x99s interests are markedly distinct from the interests this Court confronted in Trafficante, Gladstone, and Havens. In Trafficante, one white and one black tenant of an apartment complex sued on the ground that the complex discriminated against nonwhite rental applicants. 409 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 206\xe2\x80\x93208. They argued that this discrimination deprived them of the social and economic benefits of living in an integrated community. Id., at 208. In Gladstone, residents in a village sued based on alleged discrimination in the home-buying process. 441 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 93\xe2\x80\x9395. They contended that white home buyers were steered away from a racially integrated neighborhood and toward an all-white neighborhood, whereas black home buyers were steered away from the all-white neighborhood and toward the integrated neighborhood. Id., at 95. The plaintiffs thus alleged that they were \xe2\x80\x9cdenied their right to select housing without regard to race.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). The village also sued, alleging that the FHA violations were affecting its \xe2\x80\x9cracial composition, replacing what is presently an integrated neighborhood with a segregated one\xe2\x80\x9d and that its budget was strained from resulting lost tax revenues. Id., at 110. Finally, in Havens, one white and one black plaintiff sued after having posed as \xe2\x80\x9ctesters,\xe2\x80\x9d for the purpose of \xe2\x80\x9ccollecting evidence of unlawful steering practices.\xe2\x80\x9d 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 373. According to their complaint, the owner of an apartment complex had told the white plaintiff that apartments were available, but had told the black plaintiff that apartments were not. Id., at 368. The Court held that the white plaintiff could not sue, because he had been provided truthful information, but that the black plaintiff could sue, because the FHA requires truthful information about housing without regard to race. Id., at 374\xe2\x80\x93375. In all three of these cases, the plaintiffs claimed injuries based on racial steering and segregation\xe2\x80\x94interests that, under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, at least arguably fall within the zone of interests that the FHA protects.

\n

Miami\xe2\x80\x99s asserted injuries implicate none of those interests. Miami does not assert that it was injured based on efforts by the lenders to steer certain residents into one neighborhood rather than another. Miami does not even assert that it was injured because its neighborhoods were segregated. Miami therefore is not, as the majority describes, \xe2\x80\x9csimilarly situated\xe2\x80\x9d to the plaintiffs in Trafficante, Gladstone, and Havens. Ante, at 7. Rather, Miami asserts injuries allegedly resulting from foreclosed-upon and then vacant homes. The FHA\xe2\x80\x99s zone of interests is not so expansive as to include those kinds of injuries.

\n

C

\n

The Court today reaches the opposite conclusion, resting entirely on the brief mention in Gladstone of the village\xe2\x80\x99s asserted injury of reduced tax revenues, and on principles of stare decisis. See ante, at 9. I do not think Gladstone compels the conclusion the majority reaches. Unlike these cases, Gladstone involved injuries to interests in \xe2\x80\x9cracial balance and stability,\xe2\x80\x9d 441 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 111, which, our cases have suggested, arguably fall within the zone of interests protected by the FHA, see supra, at 6. The fact that the village plaintiff asserted a budget-related injury in addition to its racial-steering injury does not mean that a city alleging only a budget-related injury is authorized to sue. A budget-related injury might be necessary to establish a sufficiently concrete and particularized injury for purposes of Article III, but it is not sufficient to satisfy the FHA\xe2\x80\x99s zone-of-interests limitation.

\n

Although the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on Gladstone is misplaced, its opinion today is notable primarily for what it does not say. First, the Court conspicuously does not reaffirm the broad language from Trafficante, Gladstone, and Havens suggesting that Congress intended to permit any person with Article III standing to sue under the FHA. The Court of Appeals felt bound by that language, see 800 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1277; 801 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1266, and we granted review, despite the absence of a circuit conflict, to decide whether the language survived Thompson and Lexmark, see Brief for Petitioner in No. 15\xe2\x80\x931111, p.\xc2\xa0i (\xe2\x80\x9cBy limiting suit to \xe2\x80\x98aggrieved person[s],\xe2\x80\x99 did Congress require that an FHA plaintiff plead more than just Article III injury-in-fact?\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Petitioner in No. 15\xe2\x80\x931112, p.\xc2\xa0i (\xe2\x80\x9cWhether the term \xe2\x80\x98aggrieved\xe2\x80\x99 in the Fair Housing Act imposes a zone-of-interests requirement more stringent than the injury-in-fact requirement of Article III\xe2\x80\x9d). Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion avoids those questions presented and thus cannot be read as retreating from our more recent precedents on the zone-of-interests limitation.

\n

Second, the Court does not reject the lenders\xe2\x80\x99 arguments about many other kinds of injuries that fall outside of the FHA\xe2\x80\x99s zone of interests. We explained in Thompson that an expansive reading of Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s zone of interests would allow a shareholder \xe2\x80\x9cto sue a company for firing a valuable employee for racially discriminatory reasons, so long as he could show that the value of his stock decreased as a consequence.\xe2\x80\x9d 562 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 177. Petitioners similarly argue that, if Miami can sue for lost tax revenues under the FHA, then \xe2\x80\x9cplumbers, utility companies, or any other participant in the local economy could sue the Banks to recover business they lost when people had to give up their homes and leave the neighborhood as a result of the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 discriminatory lending practices.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8 (citing petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 briefs). The Court today decides that it \xe2\x80\x9cneed not discuss\xe2\x80\x9d this argument because Gladstone and stare decisis compel the conclusion that Miami can sue. Ante, at 8. That conclusion is wrong, but at least it is narrow. Accordingly, it should not be read to authorize suits by local businesses alleging the same injuries that Miami alleges here.

\n

II

\n

Although I disagree with its zone-of-interests holding, I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusions about proximate cause, as far as they go. The Court correctly holds that \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeability alone is not sufficient to establish proximate cause under the FHA.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. Instead, the statute requires \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98some direct relation between the injury asserted and the injurious conduct alleged.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11 (quoting Holmes v. Securities Investor Protection Corporation,\n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 258,\n268 (1992)\n).

\n

After articulating this test for proximate cause, the Court remands to the Court of Appeals because it \xe2\x80\x9cdecline[s]\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cdraw the precise boundaries of proximate cause under the FHA\xe2\x80\x9d or to \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine on which side of the line the City\xe2\x80\x99s financial injuries fall.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. But these cases come to the Court on a motion to dismiss, and the Court of Appeals has no advantage over us in evaluating the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s proximate-cause theory. Moreover, the majority opinion leaves little doubt that neither Miami nor any similarly situated plaintiff can satisfy the rigorous standard for proximate cause that the Court adopts and leaves to the Court of Appeals to apply. See ante, at 11 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe general tendency in these cases, in regard to damages at least, is not to go beyond the first step\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

Miami\xe2\x80\x99s own account of causation shows that the link between the alleged FHA violation and its asserted injuries is exceedingly attenuated. According to Miami, the lenders\xe2\x80\x99 injurious conduct was \xe2\x80\x9ctarget[ing] black and Latino customers in Miami for predatory loans.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent in No. 15\xe2\x80\x931111, p. 4 (internal quotation marks omitted). And according to Miami, the injuries asserted are its \xe2\x80\x9closs of tax revenues\xe2\x80\x9d and its expenditure of \xe2\x80\x9cadditional monies on municipal services to address\xe2\x80\x9d the consequences of urban blight. Id., at 6.

\n

As Miami describes it, the chain of causation between the injurious conduct and its asserted injuries proceeds as follows: As a result of the lenders\xe2\x80\x99 discriminatory loan practices, borrowers from predominantly minority neighborhoods were likely to default on their home loans, leading to foreclosures. Id., at 5\xe2\x80\x936. The foreclosures led to vacant houses. Id., at 6. The vacant houses, in turn, led to decreased property values for the surrounding homes. Ibid. Finally, those decreased property values resulted in homeowners paying lower property taxes to the city government. Ibid. Also, Miami explains, the foreclosed-upon, vacant homes eventually led to \xe2\x80\x9cvagrancy, criminal activity, and threats to public health and safety,\xe2\x80\x9d which the city had to address through the expenditures of municipal resources. Ibid. And all this occurred, according to Miami, between 2004 and 2012. See ibid. The Court of Appeals will not need to look far to discern other, independent events that might well have caused the injuries Miami alleges in these cases.

\n

In light of this attenuated chain of causation, Miami\xe2\x80\x99s asserted injuries are too remote from the injurious conduct it has alleged. See Associated Gen. Contractors of Cal., Inc. v. Carpenters,\n\n459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519\n, n.\xc2\xa025 (1983). Indeed, any other conclusion would lead to disquieting consequences. Under Miami\xe2\x80\x99s own theory of causation, its injuries are one step further removed from the allegedly discriminatory lending practices than the injuries suffered by the neighboring homeowners whose houses declined in value. No one suggests that those homeowners could sue under the FHA, and I think it is clear that they cannot. Accordingly, I would hold that Miami has failed to sufficiently plead proximate cause under the FHA.

\n

III

\n

For the foregoing reasons, I would reverse the Court of Appeals.

\n

The Fair Housing Act (FHA or Act) forbids

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[ing] against any person in the terms, conditions, or privileges of sale or rental of a dwelling, or in the provision of services or facilities in connection therewith, because of race .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73604(b).

\n

It further makes it unlawful for

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cany person or other entity whose business includes engaging in residential real estate-related transactions to discriminate against any person in making available such a transaction, or in the terms or conditions of such a transaction, because of race .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73605(a).

\n

The statute allows any \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d to file a civil action seeking damages for a violation of the statute. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73613(a)(1)(A), 3613(c)(1). And it defines an \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d to include \xe2\x80\x9cany person who .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. claims to have been injured by a discriminatory housing practice.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73602(i).

\n

The City of Miami claims that two banks, Bank of America and Wells Fargo, intentionally issued riskier mortgages on less favorable terms to African-American and Latino customers than they issued to similarly situated white, non-Latino customers, in violation of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73604(b) and 3605(a). App. 185\xe2\x80\x93197, 244\xe2\x80\x93245, 350\xe2\x80\x93362, 428. The City, in amended complaints, alleges that these discriminatory practices have (1) \xe2\x80\x9cadversely impacted the racial composition of the City,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 232, 416; (2) \xe2\x80\x9cimpaired the City\xe2\x80\x99s goals to assure racial integration and desegregation,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; (3) \xe2\x80\x9cfrustrate[d] the City\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding and active interest in promoting fair housing and securing the benefits of an integrated community,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 232\xe2\x80\x93233, 416\xe2\x80\x93417; and (4) disproportionately \xe2\x80\x9ccause[d] foreclosures and vacancies in minority communities in Miami,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 229, 413. Those foreclosures and vacancies have harmed the City by decreasing \xe2\x80\x9cthe property value of the foreclosed home as well as the values of other homes in the neighborhood,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby (a) \xe2\x80\x9creduc[ing] property tax revenues to the City,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 234, 418, and (b) forcing the City to spend more on \xe2\x80\x9cmunicipal services that it provided and still must provide to remedy blight and unsafe and dangerous conditions which exist at properties that were foreclosed as a result of [the Banks\xe2\x80\x99] illegal lending practices,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 233\xe2\x80\x93234, 417. The City claims that those practices violate the FHA and that it is entitled to damages for the listed injuries.

\n

The Banks respond that the complaints do not set forth a cause of action for two basic reasons. First, they contend that the City\xe2\x80\x99s claimed harms do not \xe2\x80\x9carguably\xe2\x80\x9d fall within the \xe2\x80\x9czone of interests\xe2\x80\x9d that the statute seeks to protect, Association of Data Processing Service Organizations, Inc. v. Camp,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 150,\n153 (1970)\n; hence, the City is not an \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d entitled to sue under the Act, \xc2\xa73602(i). Second, they say that the complaint fails to draw a \xe2\x80\x9cproximate-cause\xe2\x80\x9d connection between the violation claimed and the harm allegedly suffered. In their view, even if the City proves the violations it charges, the distance between those violations and the harms the City claims to have suffered is simply too great to entitle the City to collect damages.

\n

We hold that the City\xe2\x80\x99s claimed injuries fall within the zone of interests that the FHA arguably protects. Hence, the City is an \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d able to bring suit under the statute. We also hold that, to establish proximate cause under the FHA, a plaintiff must do more than show that its injuries foreseeably flowed from the alleged statutory violation. The lower court decided these cases on the theory that foreseeability is all that the statute requires, so we vacate and remand for further proceedings.

\n

I

\n

In 2013, the City of Miami brought lawsuits in federal court against two banks, Bank of America and Wells Fargo. The City\xe2\x80\x99s complaints charge that the Banks discriminatorily imposed more onerous, and indeed \xe2\x80\x9cpreda-tory,\xe2\x80\x9d conditions on loans made to minority borrowers than to similarly situated nonminority borrowers. App. 185\xe2\x80\x93197, 350\xe2\x80\x93362. Those \xe2\x80\x9cpredatory\xe2\x80\x9d practices included, among others, excessively high interest rates, unjustified fees, teaser low-rate loans that overstated refinancing opportunities, large prepayment penalties, and\xe2\x80\x94when default loomed\xe2\x80\x94unjustified refusals to refinance or modify the loans. Id., at 225, 402. Due to the discriminatory nature of the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 practices, default and foreclosure rates among minority borrowers were higher than among otherwise similar white borrowers and were concentrated in minority neighborhoods. Id., at 225\xe2\x80\x93232, 408\xe2\x80\x93415. Higher foreclosure rates lowered property values and diminished property-tax revenue. Id., at 234, 418. Higher foreclosure rates\xe2\x80\x94especially when accompanied by vacancies\xe2\x80\x94also increased demand for municipal services, such as police, fire, and building and code enforcement services, all needed \xe2\x80\x9cto remedy blight and unsafe and dangerous conditions\xe2\x80\x9d that the foreclosures and vacancies generate. Id., at 238\xe2\x80\x93240, 421\xe2\x80\x93423. The complaints describe statistical analyses that trace the City\xe2\x80\x99s financial losses to the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 discriminatory practices. Id., at 235\xe2\x80\x93237; 419\xe2\x80\x93420.

\n

The District Court dismissed the complaints on the grounds that (1) the harms alleged, being economic and not discriminatory, fell outside the zone of interests the FHA protects; (2) the complaints fail to show a sufficient causal connection between the City\xe2\x80\x99s injuries and the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 discriminatory conduct; and (3) the complaints fail to allege unlawful activity occurring within the Act\xe2\x80\x99s 2-year statute of limitations. The City then filed amended complaints (the complaints now before us) and sought reconsideration. The District Court held that the amended complaints could solve only the statute of limitations problem. It consequently declined to reconsider the dismissals.

\n

The Court of Appeals reversed the District Court. 800 F.\xc2\xa03d 1262 (CA11 2015); 801 F.\xc2\xa03d 1258 (CA11 2015). It held that the City\xe2\x80\x99s injuries fall within the \xe2\x80\x9czone of interests,\xe2\x80\x9d Lexmark Int\xe2\x80\x99l, Inc. v. Static Control Components, Inc., 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 10), that the FHA protects. 800 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1274\xe2\x80\x931275, 1277 (relying on Trafficante v. Metropolitan Life Ins. Co.,\n\n409 U.\xc2\xa0S. 205 (1972)\n; Gladstone, Realtors v. Village of Bellwood,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 91 (1979)\n; and Havens Realty Corp. v. Coleman,\n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 363 (1982)\n); 801 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1266\xe2\x80\x931267 (similar). It added that the complaints adequately allege proximate cause. 800 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1278; 801 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1267. And it remanded the cases while ordering the District Court to accept the City\xe2\x80\x99s complaints as amended. 800 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1286; 801 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1267.

\n

The Banks filed petitions for certiorari, asking us to decide whether, as the Court of Appeals had in effect held, the amended complaints satisfied the FHA\xe2\x80\x99s zone-of-interests and proximate-cause requirements. We agreed to do so.

\n

II

\n

To satisfy the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s restriction of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction to \xe2\x80\x9cCases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cControversies,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa72, a plaintiff must demonstrate constitutional standing. To do so, the plaintiff must show an \xe2\x80\x9cinjury in fact\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cfairly traceable\xe2\x80\x9d to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct and \xe2\x80\x9cthat is likely to be redressed by a favorable judicial decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Spokeo, Inc. v. Robins, 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 6) (citing Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555\n\xe2\x80\x93561 (1992)). This Court has also referred to a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s need to satisfy \xe2\x80\x9cprudential\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory\xe2\x80\x9d standing requirements. See Lexmark, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___, and n.\xc2\xa04 (slip op., at 6\xe2\x80\x939, and n.\xc2\xa04). In Lexmark, we said that the label \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98prudential standing\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d was misleading, for the requirement at issue is in reality tied to a particular statute. Ibid. The question is whether the statute grants the plaintiff the cause of action that he asserts. In answering that question, we presume that a statute ordinarily provides a cause of action \xe2\x80\x9conly to plaintiffs whose interests fall within the zone of interests protected by the law invoked.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 10) (internal quotation marks omitted). We have added that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether a plaintiff comes within \xe2\x80\x98the zone of interests\xe2\x80\x99 is an issue that requires us to determine, using traditional tools of statutory interpretation, whether a legislatively conferred cause of action encompasses a particular plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 8) (some internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Here, we conclude that the City\xe2\x80\x99s claims of financial injury in their amended complaints\xe2\x80\x94specifically, lost tax revenue and extra municipal expenses\xe2\x80\x94satisfy the \xe2\x80\x9ccause-of-action\xe2\x80\x9d (or \xe2\x80\x9cprudential standing\xe2\x80\x9d) requirement. To use the language of Data Processing, the City\xe2\x80\x99s claims of in-jury it suffered as a result of the statutory violations are, at the least, \xe2\x80\x9carguably within the zone of interests\xe2\x80\x9d that the FHA protects. 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 153 (emphasis added).

\n

The FHA permits any \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d to bring a housing-discrimination lawsuit.\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73613(a). The statute defines \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cany person who\xe2\x80\x9d either \xe2\x80\x9cclaims to have been injured by a discriminatory housing practice\xe2\x80\x9d or believes that such an injury \xe2\x80\x9cis about to occur.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73602(i).

\n

This Court has repeatedly written that the FHA\xe2\x80\x99s definition of person \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved\xe2\x80\x9d reflects a congressional intent to confer standing broadly. We have said that the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cperson aggrieved\xe2\x80\x9d in the original version of the FHA, \xc2\xa7810(a),\n\n82Stat.\n85, \xe2\x80\x9cshowed \xe2\x80\x98a congressional intention to define standing as broadly as is permitted by Article III of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Trafficante, supra, at 209 (quoting Hackett v. McGuire Brothers, Inc., 445 F.\xc2\xa02d 442, 446 (CA3 1971)); see Gladstone, supra, at 109 (similar); Havens Realty, supra, at 372, 375\xe2\x80\x93376 (similar); see also Thompson v. North American Stainless, LP,\n\n562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 170,\n176 (2011)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cLater opinions, we must acknowledge, reiterate that the term \xe2\x80\x98aggrieved\xe2\x80\x99 [in the FHA] reaches as far as Article III permits\xe2\x80\x9d); Bennett v. Spear,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 154\n\xe2\x80\x93166 (1997) (\xe2\x80\x9c[Trafficante] held that standing was expanded to the full extent permitted under Article III by \xc2\xa7810(a) of the Civil Rights Act of 1968\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Thus, we have held that the Act allows suits by white tenants claiming that they were deprived benefits from interracial associations when discriminatory rental practices kept minorities out of their apartment complex, Trafficante, 409 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 209\xe2\x80\x93212; a village alleging that it lost tax revenue and had the racial balance of its community undermined by racial-steering practices, Gladstone, 441 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 110\xe2\x80\x93111; and a nonprofit organization that spent money to combat housing discrimination, Havens Realty, 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 379. Contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view, those cases did more than \xe2\x80\x9csugges[t]\xe2\x80\x9d that plaintiffs similarly situated to the City have a cause of action under the FHA. Post, at 5. They held as much. And the dissent is wrong to say that we characterized those cases as resting on \xe2\x80\x9cill-considered dictum.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 4 (quoting Thompson, supra, at 176). The \xe2\x80\x9cdictum\xe2\x80\x9d we cast doubt on in Thompson addressed who may sue under Title VII, the employment discrimination statute, not under the FHA.

\n

Finally, in 1988, when Congress amended the FHA, it retained without significant change the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cperson aggrieved\xe2\x80\x9d that this Court had broadly construed. Compare \xc2\xa7810(a),\n\n82Stat.\n85, with \xc2\xa75(b),\n\n102Stat.\n1619\xe2\x80\x931620 (codified at\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73602(i)) (changing \xe2\x80\x9cperson aggrieved\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d and making other minor changes to the definition). Indeed, Congress \xe2\x80\x9cwas aware of\xe2\x80\x9d our precedent and \xe2\x80\x9cmade a considered judgment to retain the relevant statutory text,\xe2\x80\x9d Texas Dept. of Housing and Community Affairs v. Inclusive Communities Project, Inc., 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 13). See H. R. Rep. No.\xc2\xa0100\xe2\x80\x93711, p. 23 (1988) (stating that the \xe2\x80\x9cbill adopts as its definition language similar to that contained in Section 810 of existing law, as modified to reaffirm the broad holdings of these cases\xe2\x80\x9d and discussing Gladstone and Havens Realty); cf. Lorillard v. Pons,\n\n434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 575,\n580 (1978)\n (Congress normally adopts our interpretations of statutes when it reenacts those statute without change).

\n

The Banks do not deny the broad reach of the words \xe2\x80\x9caggrieved person\xe2\x80\x9d as defined in the FHA. But they do contend that those words nonetheless set boundaries that fall short of those the Constitution sets. Brief for Petitioners in No.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x931112, p. 12 (Brief for Wells Fargo); Brief for Petitioners in No.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x931111, pp. 19\xe2\x80\x9320 (Brief for Bank of America). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s language in Trafficante, Gladstone, and Havens Realty, they argue, was exaggerated and unnecessary to decide the cases then before the Court. See Brief for Wells Fargo 19\xe2\x80\x9323; Brief for Bank of America 27\xe2\x80\x9333. Moreover, they warn that taking the Court\xe2\x80\x99s words literally\xe2\x80\x94providing everyone with constitutional standing a cause of action under the FHA\xe2\x80\x94would produce a legal anomaly. After all, in Thompson, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 175\xe2\x80\x93177, we held that the words \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98person claiming to be aggrieved\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the employment discrimination statute, did not stretch that statute\xe2\x80\x99s zone of interest to the limits of Article III. We reasoned that such an interpretation would produce farfetched results, for example, a shareholder in a company could bring a Title VII suit against the company for discriminatorily firing an employee. Ibid. The Banks say it would be similarly farfetched if restaurants, plumbers, utility companies, or any other participant in the local economy could sue the Banks to recover business they lost when people had to give up their homes and leave the neighborhood as a result of the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 discriminatory lending practices. Brief for Wells Fargo 18\xe2\x80\x9319; Brief for Bank of America 22, 24\xe2\x80\x9325. That, they believe, cannot have been the intent of the Congress that enacted or amended the FHA.

\n

We need not discuss the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 argument at length, for even if we assume for argument\xe2\x80\x99s sake that some form of it is valid, we nonetheless conclude that the City\xe2\x80\x99s financial injuries fall within the zone of interests that the FHA protects. Our case law with respect to the FHA drives that conclusion. The City\xe2\x80\x99s complaints allege that the Banks \xe2\x80\x9cintentionally targeted predatory practices at African-American and Latino neighborhoods and residents,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 225; id., at 409 (similar). That unlawful conduct led to a \xe2\x80\x9cconcentration\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cforeclosures and vacancies\xe2\x80\x9d in those neighborhoods. Id., at 226, 229, 410, 413. Those concentrated \xe2\x80\x9cforeclosures and vacancies\xe2\x80\x9d caused \xe2\x80\x9cstagnation and decline in African-American and Latino neighborhoods.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 225, 409. They hindered the City\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to create integrated, stable neighborhoods. Id., at 186, 351. And, highly relevant here, they reduced prop-erty values, diminishing the City\xe2\x80\x99s property-tax revenue and increasing demand for municipal services. Id., at 233\xe2\x80\x93234, 417.

\n

Those claims are similar in kind to the claims the Village of Bellwood raised in Gladstone. There, the plaintiff village had alleged that it was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98injured by having [its] housing market .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. wrongfully and illegally manipulated to the economic and social detriment of the citizens of [the] village.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 441 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 95 (quoting the complaint; alterations in original). We held that the village could bring suit. We wrote that the complaint in effect alleged that the defendant-realtors\xe2\x80\x99 racial steering \xe2\x80\x9caffect[ed] the village\xe2\x80\x99s racial composition,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9creduce[d] the total number of buyers in the Bellwood housing market,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cprecipitate[d] an exodus of white residents,\xe2\x80\x9d and caused \xe2\x80\x9cprices [to] be deflected downward.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 110. Those circumstances adversely affected the village by, among other things, producing a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant reduction in property values [that] directly injures a municipality by diminishing its tax base, thus threatening its ability to bear the costs of local government and to provide services.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 110\xe2\x80\x93111 (emphasis added).

\n

The upshot is that the City alleges economic injuries that arguably fall within the FHA\xe2\x80\x99s zone of interests, as we have previously interpreted that statute. Principles of stare decisis compel our adherence to those precedents in this context. And principles of statutory interpretation require us to respect Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to ratify those precedents when it reenacted the relevant statutory text. See supra, at 7.

\n

III

\n

The remaining question is one of causation: Did the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 allegedly discriminatory lending practices proximately cause the City to lose property-tax revenue and spend more on municipal services? The Eleventh Circuit concluded that the answer is \xe2\x80\x9cyes\xe2\x80\x9d because the City plausibly alleged that its financial injuries were foreseeable results of the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 misconduct. We conclude that foreseeability alone is not sufficient to establish proximate cause under the FHA, and therefore vacate the judgment below.

\n

It is a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98well established principle of [the common] law that in all cases of loss, we are to attribute it to the proximate cause, and not to any remote cause.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lexmark, 572 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 13). We assume Congress \xe2\x80\x9cis familiar with the common-law rule and does not mean to displace it sub silentio\xe2\x80\x9d in federal causes of action. Ibid. A claim for damages under the FHA\xe2\x80\x94which is akin to a \xe2\x80\x9ctort action,\xe2\x80\x9d Meyer v. Holley,\n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 280,\n285 (2003)\n\xe2\x80\x94is no exception to this traditional requirement. \xe2\x80\x9cProximate-cause analysis is controlled by the nature of the statutory cause of action. The question it presents is whether the harm alleged has a sufficiently close connection to the conduct the statute prohibits.\xe2\x80\x9d Lexmark, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 14).

\n

In these cases, the \xe2\x80\x9cconduct the statute prohibits\xe2\x80\x9d consists of intentionally lending to minority borrowers on worse terms than equally creditworthy nonminority borrowers and inducing defaults by failing to extend refinancing and loan modifications to minority borrowers on fair terms. The City alleges that the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 misconduct led to a disproportionate number of foreclosures and vacancies in specific Miami neighborhoods. These foreclosures and vacancies purportedly harmed the City, which lost property-tax revenue when the value of the properties in those neighborhoods fell and was forced to spend more on municipal services in the affected areas.

\n

The Eleventh Circuit concluded that the City adequately pleaded that the Banks\xe2\x80\x99 misconduct proximately caused these financial injuries. 800 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1282. The court held that in the context of the FHA \xe2\x80\x9cthe proper standard\xe2\x80\x9d for proximate cause \xe2\x80\x9cis based on foreseeability.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1279, 1282. The City, it continued, satisfied that element: Although there are \xe2\x80\x9cseveral links in the causal chain\xe2\x80\x9d between the charged discriminatory lending practices and the claimed losses, the City plausibly alleged that \xe2\x80\x9cnone are unforeseeable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1282.

\n

We conclude that the Eleventh Circuit erred in holding that foreseeability is sufficient to establish proximate cause under the FHA. As we have explained, proximate cause \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally bars suits for alleged harm that is \xe2\x80\x98too remote\xe2\x80\x99 from the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s unlawful conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Lexmark, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 14). In the context of the FHA, foreseeability alone does not ensure the close connection that proximate cause requires. The housing market is interconnected with economic and social life. A violation of the FHA may, therefore, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98be expected to cause ripples of harm to flow\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d far beyond the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misconduct. Associated Gen. Contractors of Cal., Inc. v. Carpenters,\n\n459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519,\n534 (1983)\n. Nothing in the statute suggests that Congress intended to provide a remedy wherever those ripples travel. And entertaining suits to recover damages for any foreseeable result of an FHA violation would risk \xe2\x80\x9cmassive and complex damages litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 545.

\n

Rather, proximate cause under the FHA requires \xe2\x80\x9csome direct relation between the injury asserted and the injurious conduct alleged.\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes v. Securities Investors Protection Corporation,\n\n503 U. S. 258,\n268 (1992)\n. A damages claim under the statute \xe2\x80\x9cis analogous to a number of tort actions recognized at common law,\xe2\x80\x9d Curtis v. Loether,\n\n415 U.\xc2\xa0S. 189,\n195 (1974)\n, and we have repeatedly applied directness principles to statutes with \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-law foundations,\xe2\x80\x9d Anza v. Ideal Steel Supply Corp.,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 451,\n457 (2006)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The general tendency\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in these cases, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in regard to damages at least, is not to go beyond the first step.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hemi Group, LLC v. City of New York,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n10 (2010)\n. What falls within that \xe2\x80\x9cfirst step\xe2\x80\x9d depends in part on the \xe2\x80\x9cnature of the statutory cause of action,\xe2\x80\x9d Lexmark, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 14), and an assessment \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98of what is administratively possible and convenient,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes, supra, at 268.

\n

The parties have asked us to draw the precise boundaries of proximate cause under the FHA and to determine on which side of the line the City\xe2\x80\x99s financial injuries fall. We decline to do so. The Eleventh Circuit grounded its decision on the theory that proximate cause under the FHA is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on foreseeability\xe2\x80\x9d alone. 800 F. 3d, at 1282. We therefore lack the benefit of its judgment on how the contrary principles we have just stated apply to the FHA. Nor has any other court of appeals weighed in on the issue. The lower courts should define, in the first instance, the contours of proximate cause under the FHA and decide how that standard applies to the City\xe2\x80\x99s claims for lost property-tax revenue and increased municipal expenses.

\n

IV

\n

The judgments of the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit are vacated, and the cases are remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.
