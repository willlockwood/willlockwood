\n\t\t\t\tFISHER v. UNIVERSITY OF TEXAS AT AUSTIN et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x93345.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 10, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 24, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe University of Texas at Austin considers race as one of various factors in its undergraduate admissions process. The University, which is committed to increasing racial minority enrollment, adopted its current program after this Court decided Grutter v. Bollinger, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 306\n, upholding the use of race as one of many \xe2\x80\x9cplus factors\xe2\x80\x9d in an admissions program that considered the overall individual contribution of each candidate, and decided Gratz v. Bollinger, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244\n, holding unconstitutional an admissions program that automatically awarded points to applicants from certain racial minorities.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner, who is Caucasian, was rejected for admission to the University\xe2\x80\x99s 2008 entering class. She sued the University and school officials, alleging that the University\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of race in admissions violated the Equal Protection Clause. The District Court granted summary judgment to the University. Affirming, the Fifth Circuit held that Grutter required courts to give substantial deference to the University, both in the definition of the compelling interest in diversity\xe2\x80\x99s benefits and in deciding whether its specific plan was narrowly tailored to achieve its stated goal. Applying that standard, the court upheld the University\xe2\x80\x99s admissions plan.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Because the Fifth Circuit did not hold the University to the demanding burden of strict scrutiny articulated in Grutter and Regents of Univ. of Cal. v. Bakke, \n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 265\n, its decision affirming the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of summary judgment to the University was incorrect. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Bakke, Gratz, and Grutter, which directly address the question considered here, are taken as given for purposes of deciding this case. In Bakke\xe2\x80\x99s principal opinion, Justice Powell recognized that state university \xe2\x80\x9cdecisions based on race or ethnic origin .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are reviewable under the \n \nFourteenth Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d 438 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 287, using a strict scrutiny standard, id., at 299. He identified as a compelling interest that could justify the consideration of race the interest in the educational benefits that flow from a diverse student body, but noted that this interest is complex, encompassing a broad array \xe2\x80\x9cof qualifications and characteristics of which racial or ethnic origin is but a single though important element.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 315\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Gratz and Grutter, the Court endorsed these precepts, observing that an admissions process with such an interest is subject to judicial review and must withstand strict scrutiny, Gratz, supra, at 275, i.e., a university must clearly demonstrate that its \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98purpose or interest is both constitutionally permissible and substantial, and that its use of the classification is \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to the accomplishment\xe2\x80\x9d of its purpose,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bakke, supra, at 305. Additional guidance may be found in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s broader equal protection jurisprudence. See, e.g., Rice v. Cayetano, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 495\n; Richmond v. J. A. Croson Co., \n\n488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 469\n. Strict scrutiny is a searching examination, and the government bears the burden to prove \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98that the reasons for any [racial] classification [are] clearly identified and unquestionably legitimate.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Under Grutter, strict scrutiny must be applied to any admissions program using racial categories or classifications. A court may give some deference to a university\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment that such diversity is essential to its educational mission,\xe2\x80\x9d 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 328, provided that diversity is not defined as mere racial balancing and there is a reasoned, principled explanation for the academic decision. On this point, the courts below were correct in finding that Grutter calls for deference to the University\xe2\x80\x99s experience and expertise about its educational mission. However, once the University has established that its goal of diversity is consistent with strict scrutiny, the University must prove that the means it chose to attain that diversity are narrowly tailored to its goal. On this point, the University receives no deference. Id., at 333. It is at all times the University\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to demonstrate, and the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to determine, that admissions processes \xe2\x80\x9censure that each applicant is evaluated as an individual and not in a way that makes an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s race or ethnicity the defining feature of his or her application.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 337. Narrow tailoring also requires a reviewing court to verify that it is \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d for the university to use race to achieve the educational benefits of diversity. Bakke, supra, at 305. The reviewing court must ultimately be satisfied that no workable race-neutral alternatives would produce the educational benefits of diversity.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRather than perform this searching examination, the Fifth Circuit held petitioner could challenge only whether the University\xe2\x80\x99s decision to use race as an admissions factor \xe2\x80\x9cwas made in good faith.\xe2\x80\x9d It presumed that the school had acted in good faith and gave petitioner the burden of rebutting that presumption. It thus undertook the narrow-tailoring requirement with a \xe2\x80\x9cdegree of deference\xe2\x80\x9d to the school. These expressions of the controlling standard are at odds with Grutter\xe2\x80\x99s command that \xe2\x80\x9call racial classifications imposed by government \xe2\x80\x98must be analyzed by a reviewing court under strict scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 326. Strict scrutiny does not permit a court to accept a school\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that its admissions process uses race in a permissible way without closely examining how the process works in practice, yet that is what the District Court and Fifth Circuit did here. The Court vacates the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. But fairness to the litigants and the courts that heard the case requires that it be remanded so that the admissions process can be considered and judged under a correct analysis. In determining whether summary judgment in the University\xe2\x80\x99s favor was appropriate, the Fifth Circuit must assess whether the University has offered sufficient evidence to prove that its admissions program is narrowly tailored to obtain the educational benefits of diversity. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t631 F.\xc2\xa03d 213, vacated and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tABIGAIL NOEL FISHER, PETITIONER v. UNIVERSITY OF TEXAS AT AUSTIN et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[June 24, 2013]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe University of Texas at Austin considers race as one of various factors in its undergraduate admissions process. Race is not itself assigned a numerical value for each ap-plicant, but the University has committed itself to increasing racial minority enrollment on campus. It refers to this goal as a \xe2\x80\x9ccritical mass.\xe2\x80\x9d Petitioner, who is Caucasian, sued the University after her application was re-jected. She contends that the University\xe2\x80\x99s use of race in the admissions process violated the Equal Protection Clause of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe parties asked the Court to review whether the judgment below was consistent with \xe2\x80\x9cthis Court\xe2\x80\x99s deci-sions interpreting the Equal Protection Clause of the Four- teenth Amendment, including Grutter v. Bollinger, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 306 (2003)\n.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i. The Court concludes that the Court of Appeals did not hold the University to the demanding burden of strict scrutiny articulated in Grutter and Regents of Univ. of Cal. v. Bakke, \n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 265, \n305 (1978)\n (opinion of Powell, J.). Because the Court of Appeals did not apply the correct standard of strict scrutiny, its decision affirming the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of summary judgment to the University was incorrect. That decision is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLocated in Austin, Texas, on the most renowned campus of the Texas state university system, the University is one of the leading institutions of higher education in the Nation. Admission is prized and competitive. In 2008, when petitioner sought admission to the University\xe2\x80\x99s entering class, she was 1 of 29,501 applicants. From this group 12,843 were admitted, and 6,715 accepted and enrolled. Petitioner was denied admission.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn recent years the University has used three different programs to evaluate candidates for admission. The first is the program it used for some years before 1997, when the University considered two factors: a numerical score reflecting an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s test scores and academic perform-ance in high school (Academic Index or AI), and the applicant\xe2\x80\x99s race. In 1996, this system was held unconstitutional by the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit. It ruled the University\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of race violated the Equal Protection Clause because it did not further any compelling government interest. Hopwood v. Texas, 78 F.\xc2\xa03d 932, 955 (1996).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe second program was adopted to comply with the Hopwood decision. The University stopped considering race in admissions and substituted instead a new holistic metric of a candidate\xe2\x80\x99s potential contribution to the University, to be used in conjunction with the Academic Index. This \xe2\x80\x9cPersonal Achievement Index\xe2\x80\x9d (PAI) measures a student\xe2\x80\x99s leadership and work experience, awards, extracurricular activities, community service, and other special circumstances that give insight into a student\xe2\x80\x99s background. These included growing up in a single-parent home, speaking a language other than English at home, significant family responsibilities assumed by the applicant, and the general socioeconomic condition of the student\xe2\x80\x99s family. Seeking to address the decline in minority enrollment after Hopwood, the University also expanded its outreach programs.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Texas State Legislature also responded to the Hop-wood decision. It enacted a measure known as the Top Ten Percent Law, codified at Tex. Educ. Code Ann. \xc2\xa751.803 (West 2009). Also referred to as H.\xc2\xa0B. 588, the Top Ten Percent Law grants automatic admission to any public state college, including the University, to all students in the top 10% of their class at high schools in Texas that comply with certain standards.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe University\xe2\x80\x99s revised admissions process, coupled with the operation of the Top Ten Percent Law, resulted in a more racially diverse environment at the University. Before the admissions program at issue in this case, in the last year under the post-Hopwood AI/PAI system that did not consider race, the entering class was 4.5% African-American and 16.9% Hispanic. This is in contrast with the 1996 pre-Hopwood and Top Ten Percent regime, when race was explicitly considered, and the University\xe2\x80\x99s entering freshman class was 4.1% African-American and 14.5% Hispanic.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFollowing this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions in Grutter v. Bollinger, supra, and Gratz v. Bollinger, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244 (2003)\n, the University adopted a third admissions program, the 2004 program in which the University reverted to explicit consideration of race. This is the program here at issue. In Grutter, the Court upheld the use of race as one of many \xe2\x80\x9cplus factors\xe2\x80\x9d in an admissions program that considered the overall individual contribution of each candidate. In Gratz, by contrast, the Court held unconstitutional Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s undergraduate admissions program, which automatically awarded points to applicants from certain racial minorities.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe University\xe2\x80\x99s plan to resume race-conscious admissions was given formal expression in June 2004 in an in-ternal document entitled Proposal to Consider Race and Ethnicity in Admissions (Proposal). Supp. App. 1a. The Proposal relied in substantial part on a study of a subset of undergraduate classes containing between 5 and 24 students. It showed that few of these classes had significant enrollment by members of racial minorities. In addition the Proposal relied on what it called \xe2\x80\x9canecdotal\xe2\x80\x9d reports from students regarding their \xe2\x80\x9cinteraction in the classroom.\xe2\x80\x9d The Proposal concluded that the University lacked a \xe2\x80\x9ccritical mass\xe2\x80\x9d of minority students and that to remedy the deficiency it was necessary to give explicit consideration to race in the undergraduate admissions program.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo implement the Proposal the University included a student\xe2\x80\x99s race as a component of the PAI score, begin- ning with applicants in the fall of 2004. The University asks students to classify themselves from among five predefined racial categories on the application. Race is not assigned an explicit numerical value, but it is undisputed that race is a meaningful factor.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOnce applications have been scored, they are plotted on a grid with the Academic Index on the x-axis and the Personal Achievement Index on the y-axis. On that grid students are assigned to so-called cells based on their individual scores. All students in the cells falling above a certain line are admitted. All students below the line are not. Each college\xe2\x80\x94such as Liberal Arts or Engineering\xe2\x80\x94admits students separately. So a student is considered initially for her first-choice college, then for her second choice, and finally for general admission as an undeclared major.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner applied for admission to the University\xe2\x80\x99s 2008 entering class and was rejected. She sued the University and various University officials in the United States District Court for the Western District of Texas. She alleged that the University\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of race in admissions violated the Equal Protection Clause. The parties cross-moved for summary judgment. The District Court granted summary judgment to the University. The United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit affirmed. It held that Grutter required courts to give substantial deference to the University, both in the definition of the compelling interest in diversity\xe2\x80\x99s benefits and in deciding whether its specific plan was narrowly tailored to achieve its stated goal. Applying that standard, the court upheld the University\xe2\x80\x99s admissions plan. 631 F.\xc2\xa03d 213, 217\xe2\x80\x93218 (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOver the dissent of seven judges, the Court of Appeals denied petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s request for rehearing en banc. See 644 F.\xc2\xa03d 301, 303 (CA5 2011) (per\xc2\xa0curiam). Petitioner sought a writ of certiorari. The writ was granted. 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAmong the Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases involving racial classifications in education, there are three decisions that directly address the question of considering racial minority status as a positive or favorable factor in a university\xe2\x80\x99s admissions process, with the goal of achieving the educational benefits of a more diverse student body: Bakke, \n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 265\n; Gratz, supra; and Grutter, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 306\n. We take those cases as given for purposes of deciding this case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe begin with the principal opinion authored by Justice Powell in Bakke, supra. In Bakke, the Court considered a system used by the medical school of the University of California at Davis. From an entering class of 100 students the school had set aside 16 seats for minority applicants. In holding this program impermissible under the Equal Protection Clause Justice Powell\xe2\x80\x99s opinion stated certain basic premises. First, \xe2\x80\x9cdecisions based on race or ethnic origin by faculties and administrations of state universities are reviewable under the Fourteenth Amend-ment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 287 (separate opinion). The principle of equal protection admits no \xe2\x80\x9cartificial line of a \xe2\x80\x98two- class theory\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cpermits the recognition of special wards entitled to a degree of protection greater than that accorded others.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 295. It is therefore irrelevant that a system of racial preferences in admissions may seem benign. Any racial classification must meet strict scrutiny, for when government decisions \xe2\x80\x9ctouch upon an individual\xe2\x80\x99s race or ethnic background, he is entitled to a judicial determination that the burden he is asked to bear on that basis is precisely tailored to serve a compelling governmental interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 299.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNext, Justice Powell identified one compelling interest that could justify the consideration of race: the interest in the educational benefits that flow from a diverse student body. Redressing past discrimination could not serve as a compelling interest, because a university\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cbroad mission [of] education\xe2\x80\x9d is incompatible with making the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial, legislative, or administrative findings of constitutional or statutory violations\xe2\x80\x9d necessary to justify remedial racial classification. Id., at 307\xe2\x80\x93309.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe attainment of a diverse student body, by contrast, serves values beyond race alone, including enhanced class-room dialogue and the lessening of racial isolation and stereotypes. The academic mission of a university is \xe2\x80\x9ca special concern of the \n \nFirst Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 312. Part of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the business of a university [is] to provide that atmosphere which is most conducive to speculation, experiment, and creation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and this in turn leads to the question of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98who may be admitted to study.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Sweezy v. New Hampshire, \n\n354 U.\xc2\xa0S. 234, \n263 (1957)\n (Frankfurter, J., concurring in judgment).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Powell\xe2\x80\x99s central point, however, was that this interest in securing diversity\xe2\x80\x99s benefits, although a permissible objective, is complex. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is not an interest in simple ethnic diversity, in which a specified percentage of the student body is in effect guaranteed to be members of selected ethnic groups, with the remaining percentage an undifferentiated aggregation of students. The diversity that furthers a compelling state interest encompasses a far broader array of qualifications and characteristics of which racial or ethnic origin is but a single though important element.\xe2\x80\x9d Bakke, 438 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 315 (separate opinion).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Gratz, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244\n, and Grutter, supra, the Court endorsed the precepts stated by Justice Powell. In Grutter, the Court reaffirmed his conclusion that obtaining the educational benefits of \xe2\x80\x9cstudent body diversity is a compelling state interest that can justify the use of race in university admissions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 325.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs Gratz and Grutter observed, however, this follows only if a clear precondition is met: The particular admissions process used for this objective is subject to judicial review. Race may not be considered unless the admissions process can withstand strict scrutiny. \xe2\x80\x9cNothing in Justice Powell\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in Bakke signaled that a university may employ whatever means it desires to achieve the stated goal of diversity without regard to the limits imposed by our strict scrutiny analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d Gratz, supra, at 275. \xe2\x80\x9cTo be narrowly tailored, a race-conscious admissions program cannot use a quota system,\xe2\x80\x9d Grutter, 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 334, but instead must \xe2\x80\x9cremain flexible enough to ensure that each applicant is evaluated as an individual and not in a way that makes an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s race or ethnicity the defining feature of his or her application,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 337. Strict scru-tiny requires the university to demonstrate with clarity that its \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose or interest is both constitutionally permissible and substantial, and that its use of the classification is necessary .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to the accomplishment of its purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Bakke, 438 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 305 (opinion of Powell, J.) (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile these are the cases that most specifically address the central issue in this case, additional guidance may be found in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s broader equal protection jurisprudence which applies in this context. \xe2\x80\x9cDistinctions between citizens solely because of their ancestry are by their very nature odious to a free people,\xe2\x80\x9d Rice v. Cayetano, \n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 495, \n517 (2000)\n (internal quotation marks omitted), and therefore \xe2\x80\x9care contrary to our traditions and hence constitutionally suspect,\xe2\x80\x9d Bolling v. Sharpe, \n\n347 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497, \n499 (1954)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[B]ecause racial characteristics so seldom provide a relevant basis for disparate treatment,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Richmond v. J. A. Croson Co., \n\n488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 469, \n505 (1989)\n (quoting Fullilove v. Klutznick, \n\n448 U.\xc2\xa0S. 448\n\xe2\x80\x93534 (1980) (Stevens, J., dissenting)), \xe2\x80\x9cthe Equal Protection Clause demands that racial classifications\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. be subjected to the \xe2\x80\x98most rigid scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Loving v. Virginia, \n\n388 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n11 (1967)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo implement these canons, judicial review must begin from the position that \xe2\x80\x9cany official action that treats a person differently on account of his race or ethnic origin is inherently suspect.\xe2\x80\x9d Fullilove, supra, at 523 (Stewart, J., dissenting); McLaughlin v. Florida, \n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 184, \n192 (1964)\n. Strict scrutiny is a searching examination, and it is the government that bears the burden to prove \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98that the reasons for any [racial] classification [are] clearly iden-tified and unquestionably legitimate,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Croson, supra, at 505 (quoting Fullilove, 448 supra, at 533\xe2\x80\x93535 (Stevens, J., dissenting)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGrutter made clear that racial \xe2\x80\x9cclassifications are constitutional only if they are narrowly tailored to further compelling governmental interests.\xe2\x80\x9d 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 326. And Grutter endorsed Justice Powell\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion in Bakke that \xe2\x80\x9cthe attainment of a diverse student body .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is a consti-tutionally permissible goal for an institution of higher education.\xe2\x80\x9d 438 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 311\xe2\x80\x93312 (separate opinion). Thus, under Grutter, strict scrutiny must be applied to any admissions program using racial categories or classifications.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAccording to Grutter, a university\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ceducational judgment that such diversity is essential to its educational mission is one to which we defer.\xe2\x80\x9d 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 328. Grutter concluded that the decision to pursue \xe2\x80\x9cthe educational benefits that flow from student body diversity,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 330, that the University deems integral to its mission is, in substantial measure, an academic judgment to which some, but not complete, judicial deference is proper under Grutter. A court, of course, should ensure that there is a reasoned, principled explanation for the academic decision. On this point, the District Court and Court of Appeals were correct in finding that Grutter calls for de-ference to the University\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98based on its experience and expertise,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 631 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 230 (quoting 645 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 587, 603 (WD Tex. 2009)), that a diverse student body would serve its educational goals. There is disagreement about whether Grutter was consistent with the principles of equal protection in approving this compelling interest in diversity. See post, at 1 (Scalia, J., concurring); post, at 4\xe2\x80\x935 (Thomas, J., concurring); post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting). But the parties here do not ask the Court to revisit that aspect of Grutter\xe2\x80\x99s holding.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA university is not permitted to define diversity as \xe2\x80\x9csome specified percentage of a particular group merely because of its race or ethnic origin.\xe2\x80\x9d Bakke, supra, at 307 (opinion of Powell, J.). \xe2\x80\x9cThat would amount to outright racial balancing, which is patently unconstitutional.\xe2\x80\x9d Grutter, supra, at 330. \xe2\x80\x9cRacial balancing is not transformed from \xe2\x80\x98patently unconstitutional\xe2\x80\x99 to a compelling state interest simply by relabeling it \xe2\x80\x98racial diversity.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Parents Involved in Community Schools v. Seattle School Dist. No. 1, \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 701, \n732 (2007)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOnce the University has established that its goal of di-versity is consistent with strict scrutiny, however, there must still be a further judicial determination that the admissions process meets strict scrutiny in its implementation. The University must prove that the means chosen by the University to attain diversity are narrowly tailored to that goal. On this point, the University receives no deference. Grutter made clear that it is for the courts, not for university administrators, to ensure that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he means chosen to accomplish the [government\xe2\x80\x99s] asserted purpose must be specifically and narrowly framed to accomplish that purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 333 (internal quotation marks omitted). True, a court can take account of a university\xe2\x80\x99s experience and expertise in adopting or rejecting certain admissions processes. But, as the Court said in Grutter, it remains at all times the University\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to demonstrate, and the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to determine, that admissions processes \xe2\x80\x9censure that each applicant is evaluated as an individual and not in a way that makes an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s race or ethnicity the defining feature of his or her application.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 337.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNarrow tailoring also requires that the reviewing court verify that it is \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d for a university to use race to achieve the educational benefits of diversity. Bakke, supra, at 305. This involves a careful judicial inquiry into whether a university could achieve sufficient diversity without using racial classifications. Although \xe2\x80\x9c[n]arrow tailoring does not require exhaustion of every conceivable race-neutral alternative,\xe2\x80\x9d strict scrutiny does require a court to examine with care, and not defer to, a university\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cserious, good faith consideration of workable race-neutral alternatives.\xe2\x80\x9d See Grutter, 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 339\xe2\x80\x93340 (emphasis added). Consideration by the university is of course necessary, but it is not sufficient to satisfy strict scrutiny: The reviewing court must ultimately be satisfied that no workable race-neutral alternatives would produce the edu-cational benefits of diversity. If \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a nonracial approach .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. could promote the substantial interest about as well and at tolerable administrative expense,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Wygant v. Jackson Bd. of Ed., \n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267\n, n.\xc2\xa06 (1986) (quoting Greenawalt, Judicial Scrutiny of \xe2\x80\x9cBenign\xe2\x80\x9d Racial Preference in Law School Admissions, 75 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 559, 578\xe2\x80\x93579 (1975)), then the university may not consider race. A plaintiff, of course, bears the burden of placing the validity of a university\xe2\x80\x99s adoption of an affirmative action plan in issue. But strict scrutiny imposes on the univer-sity the ultimate burden of demonstrating, before turning to racial classifications, that available, workable race-neutral alternatives do not suffice.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRather than perform this searching examination, however, the Court of Appeals held petitioner could challenge only \xe2\x80\x9cwhether [the University\xe2\x80\x99s] decision to reintroduce race as a factor in admissions was made in good faith.\xe2\x80\x9d 631 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 236. And in considering such a challenge, the court would \xe2\x80\x9cpresume the University acted in good faith\xe2\x80\x9d and place on petitioner the burden of rebutting that presumption. Id., at 231\xe2\x80\x93232. The Court of Appeals held that to \xe2\x80\x9csecond-guess the merits\xe2\x80\x9d of this aspect of the University\xe2\x80\x99s decision was a task it was \xe2\x80\x9cill-equipped to perform\xe2\x80\x9d and that it would attempt only to \xe2\x80\x9censure that [the University\xe2\x80\x99s] decision to adopt a race-conscious admissions policy followed from [a process of] good faith consideration.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 231. The Court of Appeals thus concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe narrow-tailoring inquiry\xe2\x80\x94like the compelling-interest inquiry\xe2\x80\x94is undertaken with a degree of deference to the Universit[y].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 232. Because \xe2\x80\x9cthe efforts of the University have been studied, serious, and of high purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals held that the use of race in the admissions program fell within \xe2\x80\x9ca constitutionally protected zone of discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 231.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese expressions of the controlling standard are at odds with Grutter\xe2\x80\x99s command that \xe2\x80\x9call racial classifications imposed by government \xe2\x80\x98must be analyzed by a reviewing court under strict scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 326 (quoting Adarand Constructors, Inc. v. Pe\xc3\xb1a, \n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 200, \n227 (1995)\n). In Grutter, the Court approved the plan at issue upon concluding that it was not a quota, was sufficiently flexible, was limited in time, and followed \xe2\x80\x9cserious, good faith consideration of workable race-neutral alternatives.\xe2\x80\x9d 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 339. As noted above, see supra, at 1, the parties do not challenge, and the Court therefore does not consider, the correctness of that determination.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGrutter did not hold that good faith would forgive an impermissible consideration of race. It must be remembered that \xe2\x80\x9cthe mere recitation of a \xe2\x80\x98benign\xe2\x80\x99 or legitimate purpose for a racial classification is entitled to little or no weight.\xe2\x80\x9d Croson, 488 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 500. Strict scrutiny does not permit a court to accept a school\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that its admissions process uses race in a permissible way without a court giving close analysis to the evidence of how the process works in practice.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe higher education dynamic does not change the narrow tailoring analysis of strict scrutiny applicable in other contexts. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he analysis and level of scrutiny applied to determine the validity of [a racial] classification do not vary simply because the objective appears acceptable .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. While the validity and importance of the objective may affect the outcome of the analysis, the analysis itself does not change.\xe2\x80\x9d Mississippi Univ. for Women v. Hogan, \n\n458 U.\xc2\xa0S. 718\n, n.\xc2\xa09 (1982).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court and Court of Appeals confined the strict scrutiny inquiry in too narrow a way by deferring to the University\xe2\x80\x99s good faith in its use of racial classifications and affirming the grant of summary judgment on that basis. The Court vacates that judgment, but fairness to the litigants and the courts that heard the case requires that it be remanded so that the admissions process can be considered and judged under a correct analysis. See Adarand, supra, at 237. Unlike Grutter, which was decided after trial, this case arises from cross-motions for summary judgment. In this case, as in similar cases, in determining whether summary judgment in favor of the University would be appropriate, the Court of Appeals must assess whether the University has offered sufficient evidence that would prove that its admissions program is narrowly tailored to obtain the educational benefits of diversity. Whether this record\xe2\x80\x94and not \xe2\x80\x9csimple .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. assurances of good intention,\xe2\x80\x9d Croson, supra, at 500\xe2\x80\x94is sufficient is a question for the Court of Appeals in the first instance.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStrict scrutiny must not be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98strict in theory, but fatal in fact,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Adarand, supra, at 237; see also Grutter, supra, at 326. But the opposite is also true. Strict scrutiny must not be strict in theory but feeble in fact. In order for judicial review to be meaningful, a university must make a showing that its plan is narrowly tailored to achieve the only interest that this Court has approved in this context: the benefits of a student body diversity that \xe2\x80\x9cencompasses a .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. broa[d] array of qualifications and characteristics of which racial or ethnic origin is but a single though important element.\xe2\x80\x9d Bakke, 438 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 315 (opinion of Powell, J.). The judgment of the Court of Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion because I agree that the Court of Appeals did not apply strict scrutiny to the University of Texas at Austin\xe2\x80\x99s (University) use of racial discrimi- nation in admissions decisions. Ante, at 1. I write separately to explain that I would overrule Grutter v. Bollinger, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 306 (2003)\n, and hold that a State\xe2\x80\x99s use of race in higher education admissions decisions is categorically prohibited by the Equal Protection Clause.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe \n \nFourteenth Amendment provides that no State shall \xe2\x80\x9cdeny to any person .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the equal protection of the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d The Equal Protection Clause guarantees every person the right to be treated equally by the State, without regard to race. \xe2\x80\x9cAt the heart of this [guarantee] lies the principle that the government must treat citizens as individuals, and not as members of racial, ethnic, or religious groups.\xe2\x80\x9d Missouri v. Jenkins, \n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 70\n\xe2\x80\x93121 (1995) (Thomas, J., concurring). \xe2\x80\x9cIt is for this reason that we must subject all racial classifications to the strictest of scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 121.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder strict scrutiny, all racial classifications are categorically prohibited unless they are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98necessary to further a compelling governmental interest\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowly tailored to that end.\xe2\x80\x9d Johnson v. California, \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 499, \n514 (2005)\n (quoting Grutter, supra, at 327). This most exacting standard \xe2\x80\x9chas proven automatically fatal\xe2\x80\x9d in almost every case. Jenkins, supra, at 121 (Thomas, J., concurring). And rightly so. \xe2\x80\x9cPurchased at the price of immeasurable human suffering, the equal protection principle reflects our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s understanding that [racial] classifications ultimately have a destructive impact on the individual and our society.\xe2\x80\x9d Adarand Constructors, Inc. v. Pe\xc3\xb1a, \n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 200, \n240 (1995)\n (Thomas, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). \xe2\x80\x9cThe Constitution abhors classifications based on race\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cevery time the government places citizens on racial registers and makes race relevant to the provision of burdens or benefits, it demeans us all.\xe2\x80\x9d Grutter, supra, at 353 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court first articulated the strict-scrutiny standard in Korematsu v. United States, \n\n323 U.\xc2\xa0S. 214 (1944)\n. There, we held that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]ressing public necessity may sometimes justify the existence of [racial discrimination]; racial antagonism never can.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 216.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Aside from Grutter, the Court has recognized only two instances in which a \xe2\x80\x9c[p]ressing public necessity\xe2\x80\x9d may justify racial discrimination by the government. First, in Korematsu, the Court recognized that protecting national security may satisfy this exacting standard. In that case, the Court upheld an evacuation order directed at \xe2\x80\x9call persons of Japanese ancestry\xe2\x80\x9d on the grounds that the Nation was at war with Japan and that the order had \xe2\x80\x9ca definite and close relationship to the prevention of espionage and sabotage.\xe2\x80\x9d 323 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 217\xe2\x80\x93218. Second, the Court has recognized that the government has a compelling interest in remedying past discrimination for which it is responsible, but we have stressed that a government wishing to use race must provide \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98strong basis in evidence for its conclusion that remedial action [is] necessary.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Richmond v. J. A. Croson Co., \n\n488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 469, \n500, \n504 (1989)\n (quoting Wygant v. Jackson Bd. of Ed., \n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267, \n277 (1986)\n (plurality opinion)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn contrast to these compelling interests that may, in a narrow set of circumstances, justify racial discrimination, the Court has frequently found other asserted interests insufficient. For example, in Palmore v. Sidoti, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429 (1984)\n, the Court flatly rejected a claim that the best interests of a child justified the government\xe2\x80\x99s racial discrimination. In that case, a state court awarded custody to a child\xe2\x80\x99s father because the mother was in a mixed-race marriage. The state court believed the child might be stigmatized by living in a mixed-race household and sought to avoid this perceived problem in its custody determination. We acknowledged the possibility of stigma but nevertheless concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe reality of private biases and the possible injury they might inflict\xe2\x80\x9d do not justify racial discrimination. Id., at 433. As we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cThe Constitution cannot control such prejudices but neither can it tolerate them. Private biases may be outside the reach of the law, but the law cannot, directly or indirectly, give them effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTwo years later, in Wygant, supra, the Court held that even asserted interests in remedying societal discrimination and in providing role models for minority students could not justify governmentally imposed racial discrimination. In that case, a collective-bargaining agreement between a school board and a teacher\xe2\x80\x99s union favored teachers who were \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Black, American Indian, Oriental, or of Spanish descendancy.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 270\xe2\x80\x93271, and n.\xc2\xa02 (plurality opinion). We rejected the interest in remedying societal discrimination because it had no logical stopping point. Id., at 276. We similarly rebuffed as inadequate the interest in providing role models to minority students and added that the notion that \xe2\x80\x9cblack students are better off with black teachers could lead to the very system the Court rejected in Brown v. Board of Education, \n\n347 U.\xc2\xa0S. 483 (1954)\n.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGrutter was a radical departure from our strict-scrutiny precedents. In Grutter, the University of Michigan Law School (Law School) claimed that it had a compelling reason to discriminate based on race. The reason it advanced did not concern protecting national security or remedying its own past discrimination. Instead, the Law School argued that it needed to discriminate in admissions decisions in order to obtain the \xe2\x80\x9ceducational benefits that flow from a diverse student body.\xe2\x80\x9d 539 U. S., at 317. Contrary to the very meaning of strict scrutiny, the Court deferred to the Law School\xe2\x80\x99s determination that this interest was sufficiently compelling to justify racial discrimination. Id., at 325.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI dissented from that part of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. I explained that \xe2\x80\x9conly those measures the State must take to provide a bulwark against anarchy, or to prevent violence, will constitute a \xe2\x80\x98pressing public necessity\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d sufficient to satisfy strict scrutiny. Id., at 353. Cf. Lee v. Washington, \n\n390 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333, \n334 (1968)\n (Black, J., concurring) (protecting prisoners from violence might justify narrowly tailored discrimination); J. A. Croson, supra, at 521 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cAt least where state or local action is at issue, only a social emergency rising to the level of imminent danger to life and limb .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. can justify [racial discrimination]\xe2\x80\x9d). I adhere to that view today. As should be obvious, there is nothing \xe2\x80\x9cpressing\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d about obtaining whatever educational benefits may flow from racial diversity.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe University claims that the District Court found that it has a compelling interest in attaining \xe2\x80\x9ca diverse stu- dent body and the educational benefits flowing from such diversity.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 18. The use of the conjunction, \xe2\x80\x9cand,\xe2\x80\x9d implies that the University believes its discrimination furthers two distinct interests. The first is an interest in attaining diversity for its own sake. The sec- ond is an interest in attaining educational benefits that allegedly flow from diversity.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAttaining diversity for its own sake is a nonstarter. As even Grutter recognized, the pursuit of diversity as an end is nothing more than impermissible \xe2\x80\x9cracial balancing.\xe2\x80\x9d 539 U. S., at 329\xe2\x80\x93330 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Law School\xe2\x80\x99s interest is not simply \xe2\x80\x98to assure within its student body some specified percentage of a particular group merely because of its race or ethnic origin.\xe2\x80\x99 That would amount to outright racial balancing, which is patently unconstitutional\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Regents of Univ. of Cal. v. Bakke, \n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 265, \n307 (1978)\n; citation omitted)); see also id., at 307 (\xe2\x80\x9cPreferring members of any one group for no reason other than race or ethnic origin is discrimination for its own sake. This the Constitution forbids\xe2\x80\x9d). Rather, diversity can only be the means by which the University obtains educational benefits; it cannot be an end pursued for its own sake. Therefore, the educational benefits allegedly produced by diversity must rise to the level of a compelling state interest in order for the program to survive strict scrutiny.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnfortunately for the University, the educational benefits flowing from student body diversity\xe2\x80\x94assuming they exist\xe2\x80\x94hardly qualify as a compelling state interest. Indeed, the argument that educational benefits justify racial discrimination was advanced in support of racial segregation in the 1950\xe2\x80\x99s, but emphatically rejected by this Court. And just as the alleged educational benefits of segregation were insufficient to justify racial discrimination then, see Brown v. Board of Education, \n\n347 U.\xc2\xa0S. 483 (1954)\n, the alleged educational benefits of diversity cannot justify racial discrimination today.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur desegregation cases establish that the Constitution prohibits public schools from discriminating based on race, even if discrimination is necessary to the schools\xe2\x80\x99 survival. In Davis v. School Bd. of Prince Edward Cty., decided with Brown, supra, the school board argued that if the Court found segregation unconstitutional, white students would migrate to private schools, funding for public schools would decrease, and public schools would either decline in quality or cease to exist altogether. Brief for Appellees in Davis v. School Bd. of Prince Edward Cty., O.\xc2\xa0T. 1952, No. 191, p.\xc2\xa030 (hereinafter Brief for Appellees in Davis) (\xe2\x80\x9cVirginians .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would no longer permit sizeable appropriations for schools on either the State or local level; private segregated schools would be greatly increased in number and the masses of our people, both white and Negro, would suffer terribly.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [M]any white parents would withdraw their children from the public schools and, as a result, the program of providing better schools would be abandoned\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). The true victims of desegregation, the school board asserted, would be black students, who would be unable to afford private school. See id., at 31 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]ith the demise of segregation, education in Virginia would receive a serious setback. Those who would suffer most would be the Negroes who, by and large, would be economically less able to afford the private school\xe2\x80\x9d); Tr. of Oral Arg. in Davis v. School Bd. of Prince Edward Cty., O.\xc2\xa0T. 1954, No. 3, p. 208 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhat is worst of all, in our opinion, you impair the public school system of Virginia and the victims will be the children of both races, we think the Negro race worse than the white race, because the Negro race needs it more by virtue of these disadvantages under which they have labored. We are up against the proposition: What does the Negro profit if he procures an immediate detailed decree from this Court now and then impairs or mars or destroys the public school system in Prince Edward County\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnmoved by this sky-is-falling argument, we held that segregation violates the principle of equality enshrined in the \n \nFourteenth Amendment. See Brown, supra, at 495 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n the field of public education the doctrine of \xe2\x80\x98separate but equal\xe2\x80\x99 has no place. Separate educational facilities are inherently unequal\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Allen v. School Bd. of Prince Edward Cty., 249 F.\xc2\xa02d 462, 465 (CA4 1957) (per curiam) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe fact that the schools might be closed if the order were enforced is no reason for not enforcing it. A person may not be denied enforcement of rights to which he is entitled under the Constitution of the United States because of action taken or threatened in defiance of such rights\xe2\x80\x9d). Within a matter of years, the warning became reality: After being ordered to desegregate, Prince Edward County closed its public schools from the summer of 1959 until the fall of 1964. See R. Sarratt, The Ordeal of Desegregation 237 (1966). Despite this fact, the Court never backed down from its rigid enforcement of the Equal Protection Clause\xe2\x80\x99s antidiscrimination principle.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, of course, Texas has not alleged that the University will close if it is prohibited from discriminating based on race. But even if it had, the foregoing cases make clear that even that consequence would not justify its use of racial discrimination. It follows, a\xc2\xa0fortiori, that the putative educational benefits of student body diversity cannot justify racial discrimination: If a State does not have a compelling interest in the existence of a university, it certainly cannot have a compelling interest in the supposed benefits that might accrue to that university from racial discrimination. See Grutter, 539 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 361 (opinion of Thomas, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] marginal improvement in legal education cannot justify racial discrimination where the Law School has no compelling interest either in its exis- tence or in its current educational and admissions policies\xe2\x80\x9d). If the Court were actually applying strict scrutiny, it would require Texas either to close the University or to stop discriminating against applicants based on their race. The Court has put other schools to that choice, and there is no reason to treat the University differently.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is also noteworthy that, in our desegregation cases, we rejected arguments that are virtually identical to those advanced by the University today. The University asserts, for instance, that the diversity obtained through its discriminatory admissions program prepares its students to become leaders in a diverse society. See, e.g., Brief for Respondents 6 (arguing that student body diversity \xe2\x80\x9cprepares students to become the next generation of leaders in an increasingly diverse society\xe2\x80\x9d). The segregationists likewise defended segregation on the ground that it provided more leadership opportunities for blacks. See, e.g., Brief for Respondents in Sweatt 96 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] very large group of Northern Negroes [comes] South to attend separate colleges, suggesting that the Negro does not secure as well-rounded a college life at a mixed college, and that the separate college offers him positive advantages; that there is a more normal social life for the Negro in a separate college; that there is a greater opportunity for full participation and for the development of leadership; that the Negro is inwardly more \xe2\x80\x98secure\xe2\x80\x99 at a college of his own people\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Appellees in Davis 25\xe2\x80\x9326 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Negro child gets an opportunity to participate in segregated schools that I have never seen accorded to him in non-segregated schools. He is important, he holds offices, he is accepted by his fellows, he is on athletic teams, he has a full place there\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). This argument was unavailing. It is irrelevant under the \n \nFourteenth Amendment whether segregated or mixed schools produce better leaders. Indeed, no court today would accept the suggestion that segregation is permissible because historically black colleges produced Booker T. Washington, Thurgood Marshall, Martin Luther King, Jr., and other prominent leaders. Likewise, the University\xe2\x80\x99s racial discrimination cannot be justified on the ground that it will produce better leaders.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe University also asserts that student body diversity improves interracial relations. See, e.g., Brief for Respondents 6 (arguing that student body diversity promotes \xe2\x80\x9ccross-racial understanding\xe2\x80\x9d and breaks down racial and ethnic stereotypes). In this argument, too, the University repeats arguments once marshaled in support of segregation. See, e.g., Brief for Appellees in Davis 17 (\xe2\x80\x9cVirginia has established segregation in certain fields as a part of her public policy to prevent violence and reduce resentment. The result, in the view of an overwhelming Virginia majority, has been to improve the relationship between the different races\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 25 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf segregation be stricken down, the general welfare will be definitely harmed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0there would be more friction developed\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Brief for Respondents in Sweatt 93 (\xe2\x80\x9cTexas has had no serious breaches of the peace in recent years in connection with its schools. The separation of the races has kept the conflicts at a minimum\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 97\xe2\x80\x9398 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe legislative acts are based not only on the belief that it is the best way to provide education for both races, and the knowledge that separate schools are necessary to keep public support for the public schools, but upon the necessity to maintain the public peace, harmony, and welfare\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Appellees in Briggs 32 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe southern Negro, by and large, does not want an end to segregation in itself any more than does the southern white man. The Negro in the South knows that discriminations, and worse, can and would multiply in such event\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). We flatly rejected this line of arguments in McLaurin v. Oklahoma State Regents for Higher Ed., \n\n339 U.\xc2\xa0S. 637 (1950)\n, where we held that segregation would be unconstitutional even if white students never tolerated blacks. Id., at 641 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt may be argued that appellant will be in no better position when these restrictions are removed, for he may still be set apart by his fellow students. This we think irrelevant. There is a vast difference\xe2\x80\x94a Constitutional difference\xe2\x80\x94between restrictions imposed by the state which prohibit the intellectual commingling of students, and the refusal of individuals to commingle where the state presents no such bar\xe2\x80\x9d). It is, thus, entirely irrelevant whether the University\xe2\x80\x99s racial discrimination increases or decreases tolerance.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, while the University admits that racial discrimination in admissions is not ideal, it asserts that it is a temporary necessity because of the enduring race consciousness of our society. See Brief for Respondents 53\xe2\x80\x9354 (\xe2\x80\x9cCertainly all aspire for a colorblind society in which race does not matter .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. But in Texas, as in America, \xe2\x80\x98our highest aspirations are yet unfulfilled\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Yet again, the University echoes the hollow justifications advanced by the segregationists. See, e.g., Brief for State of Kansas on Reargument in Brown v. Board of Education, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1953, No. 1, p. 56 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe grant that segregation may not be the ethical or political ideal. At the same time we recognize that practical considerations may prevent realization of the ideal\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Respondents in Sweatt 94 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe racial consciousness and feeling which exists today in the minds of many people may be regrettable and unjustified. Yet they are a reality which must be dealt with by the State if it is to preserve harmony and peace and at the same time furnish equal education to both groups\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 96 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[T]he mores of racial relationships are such as to rule out, for the present at least, any possibility of admitting white persons and Negroes to the same institutions\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Appellees in Briggs 26\xe2\x80\x9327 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t would be unwise in administrative practice .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to mix the two races in the same schools at the present time and under present conditions\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Appellees on Reargument in Briggs v. Elliott, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1953, No. 2, p. 79 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is not \xe2\x80\x98racism\xe2\x80\x99 to be cognizant of the fact that mankind has struggled with race problems and racial tensions for upwards of sixty centuries\xe2\x80\x9d). But these arguments too were unavailing. The \n \nFourteenth Amendment views racial bigotry as an evil to be stamped out, not as an excuse for perpetual racial tinkering by the State. See DeFunis v. Odegaard, \n\n416 U.\xc2\xa0S. 312, \n342 (1974)\n (Douglas, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Equal Protection Clause commands the elimination of racial barriers, not their creation in order to satisfy our theory as to how society ought to be organized\xe2\x80\x9d). The University\xe2\x80\x99s arguments to this effect are similarly insufficient to justify discrimination.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe University\xe2\x80\x99s arguments today are no more persuasive than they were 60 years ago. Nevertheless, despite rejecting identical arguments in Brown, the Court in Grutter deferred to the University\xe2\x80\x99s determination that the diversity obtained by racial discrimination would yield educational benefits. There is no principled distinction between the University\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that diversity yields educational benefits and the segregationists\xe2\x80\x99 assertion that segregation yielded those same benefits. See Grutter, 539 U. S., at 365\xe2\x80\x93366 (opinion of Thomas, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cCon- tained within today\xe2\x80\x99s majority opinion is the seed of a new constitutional justification for a concept I thought long and rightly rejected\xe2\x80\x94racial segregation\xe2\x80\x9d). Educational benefits are a far cry from the truly compelling state interests that we previously required to justify use of racial classifications.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMy view of the Constitution is the one advanced by the plaintiffs in Brown: \xe2\x80\x9c[N]o State has any authority under the equal-protection clause of the \n \nFourteenth Amendment to use race as a factor in affording educational opportunities among its citizens.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. in Brown v. Board of Education, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1952, No. 8, p.\xc2\xa07; see also Juris. Statement in Davis v. School Bd. of Prince Edward Cty., O.\xc2\xa0T. 1952, No. 191, p.\xc2\xa08 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e take the unqualified position that the \n \nFourteenth Amendment has totally stripped the state of power to make race and color the basis for governmental action\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Appellants in Brown v. Board of Education, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1952, No. 8, p.\xc2\xa05 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe \n \nFourteenth Amendment precludes a state from imposing distinctions or classifications based upon race and color alone\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Appellants in Nos. 1, 2, and 4, and for Respondents in No. 10 on Reargument in Brown v. Board of Education, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1953, p. 65 (\xe2\x80\x9cThat the Constitution is color blind is our dedicated belief\xe2\x80\x9d). The Constitution does not pander to faddish theories about whether race mixing is in the public interest. The Equal Protection Clause strips States of all authority to use race as a factor in providing education. All applicants must be treated equally under the law, and no benefit in the eye of the beholder can justify racial discrimination.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis principle is neither new nor difficult to understand. In 1868, decades before Plessy, the Iowa Supreme Court held that schools may not discriminate against applicants based on their skin color. In Clark v. Board of Directors, 24 Iowa 266 (1868), a school denied admission to a student because she was black, and \xe2\x80\x9cpublic sentiment [was] opposed to the intermingling of white and colored children in the same schools.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 269. The Iowa Supreme Court rejected that flimsy justification, holding that \xe2\x80\x9call the youths are equal before the law, and there is no discretion vested in the board .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or elsewhere, to interfere with or disturb that equality.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 277. \xe2\x80\x9cFor the courts to sustain a board of school directors .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in limiting the rights and privileges of persons by reason of their [race], would be to sanction a plain violation of the spirit of our laws not only, but would tend to perpetuate the national differences of our people and stimulate a constant strife, if not a war of races.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 276. This simple, yet fundamental, truth was lost on the Court in Plessy and Grutter.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI would overrule Grutter and hold that the University\xe2\x80\x99s admissions program violates the Equal Protection Clause because the University has not put forward a compelling interest that could possibly justify racial discrimination.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile I find the theory advanced by the University to justify racial discrimination facially inadequate, I also believe that its use of race has little to do with the alleged educational benefits of diversity. I suspect that the University\xe2\x80\x99s program is instead based on the benighted notion that it is possible to tell when discrimination helps, rather than hurts, racial minorities. See post, at 3 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[G]overnment actors, including state universities, need not be blind to the lingering effects of \xe2\x80\x98an overtly discriminatory past,\xe2\x80\x99 the legacy of \xe2\x80\x98centuries of law-sanctioned inequality\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). But \xe2\x80\x9c[h]istory should teach greater humility.\xe2\x80\x9d Metro Broadcasting, Inc. v. FCC, \n\n497 U.\xc2\xa0S. 547, \n609 (1990)\n (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., dissenting). The worst forms of racial discrimination in this Nation have always been accompanied by straight-faced representations that discrimination helped minorities.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSlaveholders argued that slavery was a \xe2\x80\x9cpositive good\xe2\x80\x9d that civilized blacks and elevated them in every dimension of life. See, e.g., Calhoun, Speech in the U.\xc2\xa0S. Senate, 1837, in P. Finkelman, Defending Slavery 54, 58\xe2\x80\x9359 (2003) (\xe2\x80\x9cNever before has the black race of Central Africa, from the dawn of history to the present day, attained a condition so civilized and so improved, not only physically, but morally and intellectually.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [T]he relation now existing in the slaveholding States between the two [races], is, instead of an evil, a good\xe2\x80\x94a positive good\xe2\x80\x9d); Harper, Memoir on Slavery, in The Ideology of Slavery 78, 115\xe2\x80\x93116 (D. Faust ed. 1981) (\xe2\x80\x9cSlavery, as it is said in an eloquent article published in a Southern periodical work .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98has done more to elevate a degraded race in the scale of humanity; to tame the savage; to civilize the barbarous; to soften the ferocious; to enlighten the ignorant, and to spread the blessings of [C]hristianity among the heathen, than all the missionaries that philanthropy and religion have ever sent forth\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Hammond, The Mudsill Speech, 1858, in Defending Slavery, supra, at 80, 87 (\xe2\x80\x9cThey are elevated from the condition in which God first created them, by being made our slaves\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA century later, segregationists similarly asserted that segregation was not only benign, but good for black students. They argued, for example, that separate schools protected black children from racist white students and teachers. See, e.g., Brief for Appellees in Briggs 33\xe2\x80\x9334 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I have repeatedly seen wise and loving colored parents take infinite pains to force their little children into schools where the white children, white teachers, and white parents despised and resented the dark child, made mock of it, neglected or bullied it, and literally rendered its life a living hell. Such parents want their child to \xe2\x80\x9cfight\xe2\x80\x9d this thing out,\xe2\x80\x94but, dear God, at what a cost! .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. We shall get a finer, better balance of spirit; an infinitely more capable and rounded personality by putting children in schools where they are wanted, and where they are happy and inspired, than in thrusting them into hells where they are ridiculed and hated\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting DuBois, Does the Negro Need Separate Schools? 4 J. of Negro Educ. 328, 330\xe2\x80\x93331 (1935))); Tr. of Oral Arg. in Bolling v. Sharpe, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1952, No. 413, p. 56 (\xe2\x80\x9cThere was behind these [a]cts a kindly feeling [and] an intention to help these people who had been in bondage. And there was and there still is an intention by the Congress to see that these children shall be educated in a healthful atmosphere, in a wholesome atmosphere, in a place where they are wanted, in a place where they will not be looked upon with hostility, in a place where there will be a receptive atmosphere for learning for both races without the hostility that undoubtedly Congress thought might creep into these situations\xe2\x80\x9d). And they even appealed to the fact that many blacks agreed that separate schools were in the \xe2\x80\x9cbest interests\xe2\x80\x9d of both races. See, e.g., Brief for Appellees in Davis 24\xe2\x80\x9325 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98It has been my experience, in working with the people of Virginia, including both white and Negro, that the customs and the habits and the traditions of Virginia citizens are such that they believe for the best interests of both the white and the Negro that the separate school is best\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFollowing in these inauspicious footsteps, the University would have us believe that its discrimination is likewise benign. I think the lesson of history is clear enough: Racial discrimination is never benign. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[B]enign\xe2\x80\x99 carries with it no independent meaning, but reflects only acceptance of the current generation\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that a politically acceptable burden, imposed on particular citizens on the basis of race, is reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d See Metro Broadcasting, 497 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 610 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., dissenting). It is for this reason that the Court has repeatedly held that strict scrutiny applies to all racial classifications, regardless of whether the government has benevolent motives. See, e.g., Johnson, 543 U. S., at 505 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe have insisted on strict scrutiny in every context, even for so-called \xe2\x80\x98benign\xe2\x80\x99 racial classifications\xe2\x80\x9d); Adarand, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 227 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]ll racial classifications, imposed by whatever federal, state, or local governmental actor, must be analyzed by a reviewing court under strict scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d); J. A. Croson, 488 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 500 (\xe2\x80\x9cRacial classifications are suspect, and that means that simple legislative assurances of good intention cannot suffice\xe2\x80\x9d). The University\xe2\x80\x99s professed good intentions cannot excuse its outright racial discrimination any more than such intentions justified the now denounced arguments of slaveholders and segregationists.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile it does not, for constitutional purposes, matter whether the University\xe2\x80\x99s racial discrimination is benign, I note that racial engineering does in fact have insidious consequences. There can be no doubt that the University\xe2\x80\x99s discrimination injures white and Asian applicants who are denied admission because of their race. But I believe the injury to those admitted under the University\xe2\x80\x99s discriminatory admissions program is even more harmful.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBlacks and Hispanics admitted to the University as a result of racial discrimination are, on average, far less prepared than their white and Asian classmates. In the University\xe2\x80\x99s entering class of 2009, for example, among the students admitted outside the Top Ten Percent plan, blacks scored at the 52d percentile of 2009 SAT takers nationwide, while Asians scored at the 93d percentile. Brief for Richard Sander et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 3\xe2\x80\x934, and n.\xc2\xa04. Blacks had a mean GPA of 2.57 and a mean SAT score of 1524; Hispanics had a mean GPA of 2.83 and a mean SAT score of 1794; whites had a mean GPA of 3.04 and a mean SAT score of 1914; and Asians had a mean GPA of 3.07 and a mean SAT score of 1991.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTellingly, neither the University nor any of the 73 amici briefs in support of racial discrimination has presented a shred of evidence that black and Hispanic students are able to close this substantial gap during their time at the University. Cf. Thernstrom & Thernstrom, Reflections on the Shape of the River, 46 UCLA L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1583, 1605\xe2\x80\x931608 (1999) (discussing the failure of defenders of racial discrimination in admissions to consider the fact that its \xe2\x80\x9cbeneficiaries\xe2\x80\x9d are underperforming in the classroom). \xe2\x80\x9cIt is a fact that in virtually all selective schools .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. where racial preferences in admission is practiced, the majority of [black] students end up in the lower quarter of their class.\xe2\x80\x9d S. Cole & E. Barber, Increasing Faculty Diversity: The Occupational Choices of High-Achieving Minority Students 124 (2003). There is no reason to believe this is not the case at the University. The University and its dozens of amici are deafeningly silent on this point.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFurthermore, the University\xe2\x80\x99s discrimination does nothing to increase the number of blacks and Hispanics who have access to a college education generally. Instead, the University\xe2\x80\x99s discrimination has a pervasive shifting effect. See T. Sowell, Affirmative Action Around the World 145\xe2\x80\x93146 (2004). The University admits minorities who otherwise would have attended less selective colleges where they would have been more evenly matched. But, as a result of the mismatching, many blacks and Hispanics who likely would have excelled at less elite schools are placed in a position where underperformance is all but inevitable because they are less academically prepared than the white and Asian students with whom they must compete. Setting aside the damage wreaked upon the self-confidence of these overmatched students, there is no evidence that they learn more at the University than they would have learned at other schools for which they were better prepared. Indeed, they may learn less.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals believed that the University needed to enroll more blacks and Hispanics because they remained \xe2\x80\x9cclustered in certain programs.\xe2\x80\x9d 631 F.\xc2\xa03d 213, 240 (CA5 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]early a quarter of the undergraduate students in [the University\xe2\x80\x99s] College of Social Work are Hispanic, and more than 10% are [black]. In the College of Education, 22.4% of students are Hispanic and 10.1% are [black]\xe2\x80\x9d). But racial discrimination may be the cause of, not the solution to, this clustering. There is some evidence that students admitted as a result of racial discrimination are more likely to abandon their initial aspirations to become scientists and engineers than are students with similar qualifications who attend less selective schools. See, e.g., Elliott, Strenta, Adair, Matier, & Scott, The Role of Ethnicity in Choosing and Leaving Science in Highly Selective Institutions, 37 Research in Higher Educ. 681, 699\xe2\x80\x93701 (1996).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t These students may well drift towards less competitive majors because the mismatch caused by racial discrimination in admissions makes it difficult for them to compete in more rigorous majors.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, the University\xe2\x80\x99s discrimination \xe2\x80\x9cstamp[s] [blacks and Hispanics] with a badge of inferiority.\xe2\x80\x9d Adarand, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 241 (opinion of Thomas, J.). It taints the accomplishments of all those who are admitted as a result of racial discrimination. Cf. J. McWhorter, Losing the Race: Self-Sabotage in Black America 248 (2000) (\xe2\x80\x9cI was never able to be as proud of getting into Stanford as my classmates could be.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. And, it taints the accomplishments of all those who are the same race as those admitted as a result of racial discrimination. In this case, for example, most blacks and Hispanics attending the University were admitted without discrimination under the Top Ten Percent plan, but no one can distinguish those students from the ones whose race played a role in their admission. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen blacks [and Hispanics] take positions in the highest places of government, industry, or academia, it is an open question \xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. whether their skin color played a part in their advancement.\xe2\x80\x9d See Grutter, 539 U. S., at 373 (opinion of Thomas, J.). \xe2\x80\x9cThe question itself is the stigma\xe2\x80\x94because either racial discrimination did play a role, in which case the person may be deemed \xe2\x80\x98otherwise unqualified,\xe2\x80\x99 or it did not, in which case asking the question itself unfairly marks those .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. who would succeed without discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Al- though cloaked in good intentions, the University\xe2\x80\x99s racial tinkering harms the very people it claims to be helping.\n\t\t\t
