TRINITY LUTHERAN CHURCH OF COLUMBIA, INC. v. COMER, DIRECTOR, MISSOURI DEPARTMENT OF NATURAL RESOURCES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93577.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 19, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 26, 2017

\n

The Trinity Lutheran Church Child Learning Center is a Missouri preschool and daycare center. Originally established as a nonprofit organization, the Center later merged with Trinity Lutheran Church and now operates under its auspices on church property. Among the facilities at the Center is a playground, which has a coarse pea gravel surface beneath much of the play equipment. In 2012, the Center sought to replace a large portion of the pea gravel with a pour-in-place rubber surface by participating in Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s Scrap Tire Program. The program, run by the State\xe2\x80\x99s Department of Natural Resources, offers reimbursement grants to qualifying nonprofit organizations that install playground surfaces made from recycled tires. The Department had a strict and express policy of denying grants to any applicant owned or controlled by a church, sect, or other religious entity. Pursuant to that policy, the Department denied the Center\xe2\x80\x99s application. In a letter rejecting that application, the Department explained that under Article I, Section 7 of the Missouri Constitution, the Department could not provide financial assistance directly to a church. The Department ultimately awarded 14 grants as part of the 2012 program. Although the Center ranked fifth out of the 44 applicants, it did not receive a grant because it is a church.

\n

Trinity Lutheran sued in Federal District Court, alleging that the Department\xe2\x80\x99s failure to approve its application violated the Free Exercise Clause of the\n \nFirst Amendment. The District Court dismissed the suit. The Free Exercise Clause, the court stated, prohibits the government from outlawing or restricting the exercise of a religious practice, but it generally does not prohibit withholding an affirmative benefit on account of religion. The District Court likened the case before it to Locke v. Davey,\n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 712\n, where this Court upheld against a free exercise challenge a State\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to fund degrees in devotional theology as part of a scholarship program. The District Court held that the Free Exercise Clause did not require the State to make funds available under the Scrap Tire Program to Trinity Lutheran. A divided panel of the Eighth Circuit affirmed. The fact that the State could award a scrap tire grant to Trinity Lutheran without running afoul of the Establishment Clause of the Federal Constitution, the court ruled, did not mean that the Free Exercise Clause compelled the State to disregard the broader antiestablishment principle reflected in its own Constitution.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Department\xe2\x80\x99s policy violated the rights of Trinity Lutheran under the Free Exercise Clause of the\n \nFirst Amendment by denying the Church an otherwise available public benefit on account of its religious status. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0This Court has repeatedly confirmed that denying a generally available benefit solely on account of religious identity imposes a penalty on the free exercise of religion. Thus, in McDaniel v. Paty,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 618\n, the Court struck down a Tennessee statute disqualifying ministers from serving as delegates to the State\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional convention. A plurality recognized that such a law discriminated against McDaniel by denying him a benefit solely because of his \xe2\x80\x9cstatus as a \xe2\x80\x98minister.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 627. In recent years, when rejecting free exercise challenges to neutral laws of general applicability, the Court has been careful to distinguish such laws from those that single out the religious for disfavored treatment. See, e.g., Lyng v. Northwest Indian Cemetery Protective Assn.,\n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 439\n; Employment Div., Dept. of Human Resources of Ore. v. Smith,\n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 872\n; and Church of Lukumi Babalu Aye, Inc. v. Hialeah,\n\n508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 520\n. It has remained a fundamental principle of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s free exercise jurisprudence that laws imposing \xe2\x80\x9cspecial disabilities on the basis of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. religious status\xe2\x80\x9d trigger the strictest scrutiny. Id., at 533. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The Department\xe2\x80\x99s policy expressly discriminates against otherwise eligible recipients by disqualifying them from a public benefit solely because of their religious character. Like the disqualification statute in McDaniel, the Department\xe2\x80\x99s policy puts Trinity Lutheran to a choice: It may participate in an otherwise available benefit program or remain a religious institution. When the State conditions a benefit in this way, McDaniel says plainly that the State has imposed a penalty on the free exercise of religion that must withstand the most exacting scrutiny. 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 626, 628.

\n

The Department contends that simply declining to allocate to Trinity Lutheran a subsidy the State had no obligation to provide does not meaningfully burden the Church\xe2\x80\x99s free exercise rights. Absent any such burden, the argument continues, the Department is free to follow the State\xe2\x80\x99s antiestablishment objection to providing funds directly to a church. But, as even the Department acknowledges, the Free Exercise Clause protects against \xe2\x80\x9cindirect coercion or penalties on the free exercise of religion, not just outright prohibitions.\xe2\x80\x9d Lyng, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 450. Trinity Lutheran is not claiming any entitlement to a subsidy. It is asserting a right to participate in a government benefit program without having to disavow its religious character. The express discrimination against religious exercise here is not the denial of a grant, but rather the refusal to allow the Church\xe2\x80\x94solely because it is a church\xe2\x80\x94to compete with secular organizations for a grant. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The Department tries to sidestep this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents by arguing that this case is instead controlled by Locke v. Davey. It is not. In Locke, the State of Washington created a scholarship program to assist high-achieving students with the costs of postsecondary education. Scholarship recipients were free to use state funds at accredited religious and non-religious schools alike, but they could not use the funds to pursue a devotional theology degree. At the outset, the Court made clear that Locke was not like the cases in which the Court struck down laws requiring individuals to \xe2\x80\x9cchoose between their religious beliefs and receiving a government benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 720\xe2\x80\x93721. Davey was not denied a scholarship because of who he was; he was denied a scholarship because of what he proposed to do. Here there is no question that Trinity Lutheran was denied a grant simply because of what it is\xe2\x80\x94a church.

\n

The Court in Locke also stated that Washington\xe2\x80\x99s restriction on the use of its funds was in keeping with the State\xe2\x80\x99s antiestablishment interest in not using taxpayer funds to pay for the training of clergy, an \xe2\x80\x9cessentially religious endeavor,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 721. Here, nothing of the sort can be said about a program to use recycled tires to resurface playgrounds. At any rate, the Court took account of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s antiestablishment interest only after determining that the scholarship program did not \xe2\x80\x9crequire students to choose between their religious beliefs and receiving a government benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 720\xe2\x80\x93721. There is no dispute that Trinity Lutheran is put to the choice between being a church and receiving a government benefit. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0The Department\xe2\x80\x99s discriminatory policy does not survive the \xe2\x80\x9cmost rigorous\xe2\x80\x9d scrutiny that this Court applies to laws imposing special disabilities on account of religious status. Lukumi, 508 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 546. That standard demands a state interest \xe2\x80\x9cof the highest order\xe2\x80\x9d to justify the policy at issue. McDaniel, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 628 (internal quotation marks omitted). Yet the Department offers nothing more than Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s preference for skating as far as possible from religious establishment concerns. In the face of the clear infringement on free exercise before the Court, that interest cannot qualify as compelling. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

788 F.\xc2\xa03d 779, reversed and remanded.

TRINITY LUTHERAN CHURCH OF COLUMBIA, INC., PETITIONER v. CAROL S. COMER, DIRECTOR, MISSOURI DEPARTMENT OF NATURALRESOURCES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

[June 26, 2017]

\n

\n

Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court, except as to footnote 3.

\n

The Missouri Department of Natural Resources offers state grants to help public and private schools, nonprofit daycare centers, and other nonprofit entities purchase rubber playground surfaces made from recycled tires. Trinity Lutheran Church applied for such a grant for its preschool and daycare center and would have received one, but for the fact that Trinity Lutheran is a church. The Department had a policy of categorically disqualifying churches and other religious organizations from receiving grants under its playground resurfacing program. The question presented is whether the Department\xe2\x80\x99s policy violated the rights of Trinity Lutheran under the Free Exercise Clause of the\n \nFirst Amendment.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The Trinity Lutheran Church Child Learning Center is a preschool and daycare center open throughout the year to serve working families in Boone County, Missouri, and the surrounding area. Established as a nonprofit organization in 1980, the Center merged with Trinity Lutheran Church in 1985 and operates under its auspices on church property. The Center admits students of any religion, and enrollment stands at about 90 children ranging from age two to five.

\n

The Center includes a playground that is equipped with the basic playground essentials: slides, swings, jungle gyms, monkey bars, and sandboxes. Almost the entire surface beneath and surrounding the play equipment is coarse pea gravel. Youngsters, of course, often fall on the playground or tumble from the equipment. And when they do, the gravel can be unforgiving.

\n

In 2012, the Center sought to replace a large portion of the pea gravel with a pour-in-place rubber surface by participating in Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s Scrap Tire Program. Run by the State\xe2\x80\x99s Department of Natural Resources to reduce the number of used tires destined for landfills and dump sites, the program offers reimbursement grants to qualifying nonprofit organizations that purchase playground surfaces made from recycled tires. It is funded through a fee imposed on the sale of new tires in the State.

\n

Due to limited resources, the Department cannot offer grants to all applicants and so awards them on a competitive basis to those scoring highest based on several criteria, such as the poverty level of the population in the surrounding area and the applicant\xe2\x80\x99s plan to promote recycling. When the Center applied, the Department had a strict and express policy of denying grants to any applicant owned or controlled by a church, sect, or other religious entity. That policy, in the Department\xe2\x80\x99s view, was compelled by Article I, Section 7 of the Missouri Constitution, which provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThat no money shall ever be taken from the public treasury, directly or indirectly, in aid of any church, sect or denomination of religion, or in aid of any priest, preacher, minister or teacher thereof, as such; and that no preference shall be given to nor any discrimination made against any church, sect or creed of religion, or any form of religious faith or worship.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

In its application, the Center disclosed its status as a ministry of Trinity Lutheran Church and specified that the Center\xe2\x80\x99s mission was \xe2\x80\x9cto provide a safe, clean, and attractive school facility in conjunction with an educational program structured to allow a child to grow spiritually, physically, socially, and cognitively.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 131a. After describing the playground and the safety hazards posed by its current surface, the Center detailed the anticipated benefits of the proposed project: increasing access to the playground for all children, including those with disabilities, by providing a surface compliant with the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990; providing a safe, long-lasting, and resilient surface under the play areas; and improving Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s environment by putting recycled tires to positive use. The Center also noted that the benefits of a new surface would extend beyond its students to the local community, whose children often use the playground during non-school hours.

\n

The Center ranked fifth among the 44 applicants in the 2012 Scrap Tire Program. But despite its high score, the Center was deemed categorically ineligible to receive a grant. In a letter rejecting the Center\xe2\x80\x99s application, the program director explained that, under Article I, Section 7 of the Missouri Constitution, the Department could not provide financial assistance directly to a church.

\n

The Department ultimately awarded 14 grants as part of the 2012 program. Because the Center was operated by Trinity Lutheran Church, it did not receive a grant.

\n

B

\n

Trinity Lutheran sued the Director of the Department in Federal District Court. The Church alleged that the Department\xe2\x80\x99s failure to approve the Center\xe2\x80\x99s application, pursuant to its policy of denying grants to religiously affiliated applicants, violates the Free Exercise Clause of the\n \nFirst Amendment. Trinity Lutheran sought declara-tory and injunctive relief prohibiting the Department from discriminating against the Church on that basis in future grant applications.

\n

The District Court granted the Department\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss. The Free Exercise Clause, the District Court stated, prohibits the government from outlawing or restricting the exercise of a religious practice; it generally does not prohibit withholding an affirmative benefit on account of religion. The District Court likened the Department\xe2\x80\x99s denial of the scrap tire grant to the situation this Court encountered in Locke v. Davey,\n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 712 (2004)\n. In that case, we upheld against a free exercise challenge the State of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to fund degrees in devotional theology as part of a state scholarship program. Finding the present case \xe2\x80\x9cnearly indistinguishable from Locke,\xe2\x80\x9d the District Court held that the Free Exercise Clause did not require the State to make funds available under the Scrap Tire Program to religious institutions like Trinity Lutheran. Trinity Lutheran Church of Columbia, Inc. v. Pauley, 976 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1137, 1151 (WD Mo. 2013).

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit affirmed. The court recognized that it was \xe2\x80\x9crather clear\xe2\x80\x9d that Missouri could award a scrap tire grant to Trinity Lutheran without running afoul of the Establishment Clause of the United States Constitution. Trinity Lutheran Church of Columbia, Inc. v. Pauley, 788 F.\xc2\xa03d 779, 784 (2015). But, the Court of Appeals explained, that did not mean the Free Exercise Clause compelled the State to disregard the antiestablishment principle reflected in its own Constitution. Viewing a monetary grant to a religious institution as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98hallmark[\xc2\xa0] of an established religion,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the court concluded that the State could rely on an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s religious status to deny its application. Id., at 785 (quoting Locke, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 722; some internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Judge Gruender dissented. He distinguished Locke on the ground that it concerned the narrow issue of funding for the religious training of clergy, and \xe2\x80\x9cdid not leave states with unfettered discretion to exclude the religious from generally available public benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d 788 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 791 (opinion concurring in part and dissenting in part).

\n

Rehearing en banc was denied by an equally divided court.

\n

We granted certiorari sub\xc2\xa0nom. Trinity Lutheran Church of Columbia, Inc. v. Pauley, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016), and now reverse.[1]

\n

II

\n

The\n \nFirst Amendment provides, in part, that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d The parties agree that the Establishment Clause of that Amendment does not prevent Missouri from including Trinity Lutheran in the Scrap Tire Program. That does not, however, answer the question under the Free Exercise Clause, because we have recognized that there is \xe2\x80\x9cplay in the joints\xe2\x80\x9d between what the Establishment Clause permits and the Free Exercise Clause compels. Locke, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 718 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

The Free Exercise Clause \xe2\x80\x9cprotect[s] religious observers against unequal treatment\xe2\x80\x9d and subjects to the strictest scrutiny laws that target the religious for \xe2\x80\x9cspecial disabilities\xe2\x80\x9d based on their \xe2\x80\x9creligious status.\xe2\x80\x9d Church of Lukumi Babalu Aye, Inc. v. Hialeah,\n\n508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 520,\n533,\n542 (1993)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). Applying that basic principle, this Court has repeatedly confirmed that denying a generally available benefit solely on account of religious identity imposes a penalty on the free exercise of religion that can be justified only by a state interest \xe2\x80\x9cof the highest order.\xe2\x80\x9d McDaniel v. Paty,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 618,\n628 (1978)\n (plurality opinion) (quoting Wisconsin v. Yoder,\n\n406 U.\xc2\xa0S. 205,\n215 (1972)\n).

\n

In Everson v. Board of Education of Ewing,\n\n330 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1947)\n, for example, we upheld against an Establishment Clause challenge a New Jersey law enabling a local school district to reimburse parents for the public transportation costs of sending their children to public and private schools, including parochial schools. In the course of ruling that the Establishment Clause allowed New Jersey to extend that public benefit to all its citizens regardless of their religious belief, we explained that a State \xe2\x80\x9ccannot hamper its citizens in the free exercise of their own religion. Consequently, it cannot exclude individual Catholics, Lutherans, Mohammedans, Baptists, Jews, Methodists, Non-believers, Presbyterians, or the members of any other faith, because of their faith, or lack of it, from receiving the benefits of public welfare legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 16.

\n

Three decades later, in McDaniel v. Paty, the Court struck down under the Free Exercise Clause a Tennessee statute disqualifying ministers from serving as delegates to the State\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional convention. Writing for the plurality, Chief Justice Burger acknowledged that Tennessee had disqualified ministers from serving as legislators since the adoption of its first Constitution in 1796, and that a number of early States had also disqualified ministers from legislative office. This historical tradition, however, did not change the fact that the statute discriminated against McDaniel by denying him a benefit solely because of his \xe2\x80\x9cstatus as a \xe2\x80\x98minister.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 627. McDaniel could not seek to participate in the convention while also maintaining his role as a minister; to pursue the one, he would have to give up the other. In this way, said Chief Justice Burger, the Tennessee law \xe2\x80\x9ceffectively penalizes the free exercise of [McDaniel\xe2\x80\x99s] constitutional liberties.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 626 (quoting Sherbert v. Verner,\n\n374 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398,\n406 (1963)\n; internal quotation marks omitted). Joined by Justice Marshall in concurrence, Justice Brennan added that \xe2\x80\x9cbecause the challenged provision requires [McDaniel] to purchase his right to engage in the ministry by sacrificing his candidacy it impairs the free exercise of his religion.\xe2\x80\x9d McDaniel, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 634.

\n

In recent years, when this Court has rejected free exercise challenges, the laws in question have been neutral and generally applicable without regard to religion. We have been careful to distinguish such laws from those that single out the religious for disfavored treatment.

\n

For example, in Lyng v. Northwest Indian Cemetery Protective Association,\n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 439 (1988)\n, we held that the Free Exercise Clause did not prohibit the Government from timber harvesting or road construction on a particular tract of federal land, even though the Government\xe2\x80\x99s action would obstruct the religious practice of several Native American Tribes that held certain sites on the tract to be sacred. Accepting that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he building of a road or the harvesting of timber .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would interfere significantly with private persons\xe2\x80\x99 ability to pursue spiritual fulfillment according to their own religious beliefs,\xe2\x80\x9d we nonetheless found no free exercise violation, because the affected individuals were not being \xe2\x80\x9ccoerced by the Government\xe2\x80\x99s action into violating their religious beliefs.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 449. The Court specifically noted, however, that the Government action did not \xe2\x80\x9cpenalize religious activity by denying any person an equal share of the rights, benefits, and privileges enjoyed by other citizens.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

In Employment Division, Department of Human Resources of Oregon v. Smith,\n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 872 (1990)\n, we rejected a free exercise claim brought by two members of a Native American church denied unemployment benefits because they had violated Oregon\xe2\x80\x99s drug laws by ingesting peyote for sacramental purposes. Along the same lines as our decision in Lyng, we held that the Free Exercise Clause did not entitle the church members to a special dispensation from the general criminal laws on account of their religion. At the same time, we again made clear that the Free Exercise Clause did guard against the government\xe2\x80\x99s imposition of \xe2\x80\x9cspecial disabilities on the basis of religious views or religious status.\xe2\x80\x9d 494 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 877 (citing McDaniel,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 618\n).[2]

\n

Finally, in Church of Lukumi Babalu Aye, Inc. v. Hia-leah, we struck down three facially neutral city ordinances that outlawed certain forms of animal slaughter. Members of the Santeria religion challenged the ordinances under the Free Exercise Clause, alleging that despite their facial neutrality, the ordinances had a discriminatory purpose easy to ferret out: prohibiting sacrificial rituals integral to Santeria but distasteful to local residents. We agreed. Before explaining why the challenged ordinances were not, in fact, neutral or generally applicable, the Court recounted the fundamentals of our free exercise jurisprudence. A law, we said, may not discriminate against \xe2\x80\x9csome or all religious beliefs.\xe2\x80\x9d 508 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 532. Nor may a law regulate or outlaw conduct because it is religiously motivated. And, citing McDaniel and Smith, we restated the now-familiar refrain: The Free Exercise Clause protects against laws that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98impose[\xc2\xa0] special dis-abilities on the basis of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. religious status.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 508 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 533 (quoting Smith, 494 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 877); see also Mitchell v. Helms,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 793,\n828 (2000)\n (plurality opinion) (noting \xe2\x80\x9cour decisions that have prohibited governments from discriminating in the distribution of public benefits based upon religious status or sincerity\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Rosenberger v. Rector and Visitors of Univ. of Va.,\n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 819 (1995)\n; Lamb\xe2\x80\x99s Chapel v. Center Moriches Union Free School Dist.,\n\n508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 384 (1993)\n; Widmar v. Vincent,\n\n454 U.\xc2\xa0S. 263 (1981)\n)).

\n

III

\n

A

\n

The Department\xe2\x80\x99s policy expressly discriminates against otherwise eligible recipients by disqualifying them from a public benefit solely because of their religious character. If the cases just described make one thing clear, it is that such a policy imposes a penalty on the free exercise of religion that triggers the most exacting scrutiny. Lukumi, 508 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 546. This conclusion is unremarkable in light of our prior decisions.

\n

Like the disqualification statute in McDaniel, the Department\xe2\x80\x99s policy puts Trinity Lutheran to a choice: It may participate in an otherwise available benefit program or remain a religious institution. Of course, Trinity Lu-theran is free to continue operating as a church, just as McDaniel was free to continue being a minister. But that freedom comes at the cost of automatic and absolute exclusion from the benefits of a public program for which the Center is otherwise fully qualified. And when the State conditions a benefit in this way, McDaniel says plainly that the State has punished the free exercise of religion: \xe2\x80\x9cTo condition the availability of benefits .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. upon [a recipient\xe2\x80\x99s] willingness to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. surrender[\xc2\xa0] his religiously impelled [status] effectively penalizes the free exercise of his constitutional liberties.\xe2\x80\x9d 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 626 (plurality opinion) (alterations omitted).

\n

The Department contends that merely declining to extend funds to Trinity Lutheran does not prohibit the Church from engaging in any religious conduct or otherwise exercising its religious rights. In this sense, says the Department, its policy is unlike the ordinances struck down in Lukumi, which outlawed rituals central to Santeria. Here the Department has simply declined to allocate to Trinity Lutheran a subsidy the State had no obligation to provide in the first place. That decision does not meaningfully burden the Church\xe2\x80\x99s free exercise rights. And absent any such burden, the argument continues, the Department is free to heed the State\xe2\x80\x99s antiestablishment objection to providing funds directly to a church. Brief for Respondent 7\xe2\x80\x9312, 14\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

It is true the Department has not criminalized the way Trinity Lutheran worships or told the Church that it cannot subscribe to a certain view of the Gospel. But, as the Department itself acknowledges, the Free Exercise Clause protects against \xe2\x80\x9cindirect coercion or penalties on the free exercise of religion, not just outright prohibitions.\xe2\x80\x9d Lyng, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 450. As the Court put it more than 50 years ago, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is too late in the day to doubt that the liberties of religion and expression may be infringed by the denial of or placing of conditions upon a benefit or privilege.\xe2\x80\x9d Sherbert, 374 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 404; see also McDaniel, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 633 (Brennan, J., concurring in judgment) (The \xe2\x80\x9cproposition\xe2\x80\x94that the law does not interfere with free exercise because it does not directly prohibit religious activity, but merely conditions eligibility for office on its abandonment\xe2\x80\x94is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. squarely rejected by precedent\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Trinity Lutheran is not claiming any entitlement to a subsidy. It instead asserts a right to participate in a government benefit program without having to disavow its religious character. The \xe2\x80\x9cimposition of such a condition upon even a gratuitous benefit inevitably deter[s] or discourage[s] the exercise of\n \nFirst Amendment rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Sherbert, 374 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 405. The express discrimination against religious exercise here is not the denial of a grant, but rather the refusal to allow the Church\xe2\x80\x94solely because it is a church\xe2\x80\x94to compete with secular organizations for a grant. Cf. Northeastern Fla. Chapter, Associated Gen. Contractors of America v. Jacksonville,\n\n508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 656,\n666 (1993)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he \xe2\x80\x98injury in fact\xe2\x80\x99 is the inability to compete on an equal footing in the bidding process, not the loss of a contract\xe2\x80\x9d). Trinity Lutheran is a member of the community too, and the State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to exclude it for purposes of this public program must withstand the strictest scrutiny.

\n

B

\n

The Department attempts to get out from under the weight of our precedents by arguing that the free exercise question in this case is instead controlled by our decision in Locke v. Davey. It is not. In Locke, the State of Washington created a scholarship program to assist high-achieving students with the costs of postsecondary education. The scholarships were paid out of the State\xe2\x80\x99s general fund, and eligibility was based on criteria such as an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s score on college admission tests and family income. While scholarship recipients were free to use the money at accredited religious and non-religious schools alike, they were not permitted to use the funds to pursue a devotional theology degree\xe2\x80\x94one \xe2\x80\x9cdevotional in nature or designed to induce religious faith.\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 716 (internal quotation marks omitted). Davey was selected for a scholarship but was denied the funds when he refused to certify that he would not use them toward a devotional degree. He sued, arguing that the State\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to allow its scholarship money to go toward such degrees violated his free exercise rights.

\n

This Court disagreed. It began by explaining what was not at issue. Washington\xe2\x80\x99s selective funding program was not comparable to the free exercise violations found in the \xe2\x80\x9cLukumi line of cases,\xe2\x80\x9d including those striking down laws requiring individuals to \xe2\x80\x9cchoose between their religious beliefs and receiving a government benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 720\xe2\x80\x93721. At the outset, then, the Court made clear that Locke was not like the case now before us.

\n

Washington\xe2\x80\x99s restriction on the use of its scholarship funds was different. According to the Court, the State had \xe2\x80\x9cmerely chosen not to fund a distinct category of instruction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 721. Davey was not denied a scholarship because of who he was; he was denied a scholarship because of what he proposed to do\xe2\x80\x94use the funds to prepare for the ministry. Here there is no question that Trinity Lutheran was denied a grant simply because of what it is\xe2\x80\x94a church.

\n

The Court in Locke also stated that Washington\xe2\x80\x99s choice was in keeping with the State\xe2\x80\x99s antiestablishment interest in not using taxpayer funds to pay for the training of clergy; in fact, the Court could \xe2\x80\x9cthink of few areas in which a State\xe2\x80\x99s antiestablishment interests come more into play.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 722. The claimant in Locke sought funding for an \xe2\x80\x9cessentially religious endeavor .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. akin to a religious calling as well as an academic pursuit,\xe2\x80\x9d and opposition to such funding \xe2\x80\x9cto support church leaders\xe2\x80\x9d lay at the historic core of the Religion Clauses. Id., at 721\xe2\x80\x93722. Here nothing of the sort can be said about a program to use recycled tires to resurface playgrounds.

\n

Relying on Locke, the Department nonetheless emphasizes Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s similar constitutional tradition of not furnishing taxpayer money directly to churches. Brief for Respondent 15\xe2\x80\x9316. But Locke took account of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s antiestablishment interest only after determining, as noted, that the scholarship program did not \xe2\x80\x9crequire students to choose between their religious beliefs and receiving a government benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 720\xe2\x80\x93721 (citing McDaniel,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 618\n). As the Court put it, Washington\xe2\x80\x99s scholarship program went \xe2\x80\x9ca long way toward including religion in its benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d Locke, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 724. Students in the program were free to use their scholarships at \xe2\x80\x9cpervasively religious schools.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Davey could use his scholarship to pursue a secular degree at one institution while studying devotional theology at another. Id., at 721, n.\xc2\xa04. He could also use his scholarship money to attend a religious college and take devotional theology courses there. Id., at 725. The only thing he could not do was use the scholarship to pursue a degree in that subject.

\n

In this case, there is no dispute that Trinity Lutheran is put to the choice between being a church and receiving a government benefit. The rule is simple: No churches need apply.[3]

\n

C

\n

The State in this case expressly requires Trinity Lutheran to renounce its religious character in order to participate in an otherwise generally available public benefit program, for which it is fully qualified. Our cases make clear that such a condition imposes a penalty on the free exercise of religion that must be subjected to the \xe2\x80\x9cmost rigorous\xe2\x80\x9d scrutiny. Lukumi, 508 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 546.[4]

\n

Under that stringent standard, only a state interest \xe2\x80\x9cof the highest order\xe2\x80\x9d can justify the Department\xe2\x80\x99s discriminatory policy. McDaniel, 435 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 628 (internal quotation marks omitted). Yet the Department offers nothing more than Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s policy preference for skating as far as possible from religious establishment concerns. Brief for Respondent 15\xe2\x80\x9316. In the face of the clear infringement on free exercise before us, that interest cannot qual-ify as compelling. As we said when considering Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s same policy preference on a prior occasion, \xe2\x80\x9cthe state interest asserted here\xe2\x80\x94in achieving greater separation of church and State than is already ensured under the Establishment Clause of the Federal Constitution\xe2\x80\x94is limited by the Free Exercise Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Widmar, 454 U. S., at 276.

\n

The State has pursued its preferred policy to the point of expressly denying a qualified religious entity a public benefit solely because of its religious character. Under our precedents, that goes too far. The Department\xe2\x80\x99s policy violates the Free Exercise Clause.[5]

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Nearly 200 years ago, a legislator urged the Maryland Assembly to adopt a bill that would end the State\xe2\x80\x99s disqualification of Jews from public office:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf, on account of my religious faith, I am subjected to disqualifications, from which others are free, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. I cannot but consider myself a persecuted man. .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. An odious exclusion from any of the benefits common to the rest of my fellow-citizens, is a persecution, differing only in degree, but of a nature equally unjustifiable with that, whose instruments are chains and torture.\xe2\x80\x9d Speech by H. M. Brackenridge, Dec. Sess. 1818, in H. Brackenridge, W. Worthington, & J. Tyson, Speeches in the House of Delegates of Maryland, 64 (1829).

\n

The Missouri Department of Natural Resources has not subjected anyone to chains or torture on account of religion. And the result of the State\xe2\x80\x99s policy is nothing so dramatic as the denial of political office. The consequence is, in all likelihood, a few extra scraped knees. But the exclusion of Trinity Lutheran from a public benefit for which it is otherwise qualified, solely because it is a church, is odious to our Constitution all the same, and cannot stand.

\n

The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

First, the Court leaves open the possibility a useful distinction might be drawn between laws that discriminate on the basis of religious status and religious use. See ante, at 12. Respectfully, I harbor doubts about the stability of such a line. Does a religious man say grace before dinner? Or does a man begin his meal in a religious manner? The distinction blurs in much the same way the line between acts and omissions can blur when stared at too long, leaving us to ask (for example) whether the man who drowns by awaiting the incoming tide does so by act (coming upon the sea) or omission (allowing the sea to come upon him). Neither do I see why the\n \nFirst Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Free Exercise Clause should care. After all, that Clause guarantees the free exercise of religion, not just the right to inward belief (or status). Employment Div., Dept. of Human Resources of Ore. v. Ante, at 13.

Newdow,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n25 (2004)\n (Rehnquist, C.\xc2\xa0J., concurring in judgment). TRINITY LUTHERAN CHURCH OF COLUMBIA, INC., PETITIONER v. CAROL S. COMER, DIRECTOR, MISSOURI DEPARTMENT OF NATURALRESOURCES

\n

Justice Sotomayor, with whom Justice Ginsburg joins, dissenting.

\n

To hear the Court tell it, this is a simple case about recycling tires to resurface a playground. The stakes are higher. This case is about nothing less than the relationship between religious institutions and the civil government\xe2\x80\x94that is, between church and state. The Court today profoundly changes that relationship by holding, for the first time, that the Constitution requires the government to provide public funds directly to a church. Its decision slights both our precedents and our history, and its reasoning weakens this country\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding commitment to a separation of church and state beneficial to both.

\n

I

\n

Founded in 1922, Trinity Lutheran Church (Church) \xe2\x80\x9coperates .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for the express purpose of carrying out the commission of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Jesus Christ as directed to His church on earth.\xe2\x80\x9d Our Story, http://www.trinity-lcms.org/story (all internet materials as last visited June 22, 2017). The Church uses \xe2\x80\x9cpreaching, teaching, worship, witness, service, and fellowship according to the Word of God\xe2\x80\x9d tocarry out its mission \xe2\x80\x9cto \xe2\x80\x98make disciples.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Mission, http://www.trinity-lcms.org/mission (quoting Matthew 28:18\xe2\x80\x9320). The Church\xe2\x80\x99s religious beliefs include its desire to \xe2\x80\x9cassociat[e] with the [Trinity Church Child] Learning Center.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 101a. Located on Church property, the Learning Center provides daycare and preschool for about \xe2\x80\x9c90 children ages two to kindergarten.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 100a.

\n

The Learning Center serves as \xe2\x80\x9ca ministry of the Church and incorporates daily religion and developmentally appropriate activities into .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [its] program.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 101a. In this way, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hrough the Learning Center, the Church teaches a Christian world view to children of members of the Church, as well as children of non-member residents\xe2\x80\x9d of the area. Ibid. These activities represent the Church\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csincere religious belief .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to use [the Learning Center] to teach the Gospel to children of its members, as well to bring the Gospel message to non-members.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The Learning Center\xe2\x80\x99s facilities include a playground, the unlikely source of this dispute. The Church provides the playground and other \xe2\x80\x9csafe, clean, and attractive\xe2\x80\x9d facilities \xe2\x80\x9cin conjunction with an education program structured to allow a child to grow spiritually, physically, socially, and cognitively.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This case began in 2012 when the Church applied for funding to upgrade the playground\xe2\x80\x99s pea gravel and grass surface through Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s Scrap Tire Program, which provides grants for the purchase and installation of recycled tire material to resurface playgrounds. The Church sought $20,000 for a $30,580 project to modernize the playground, part of its effort to gain state accreditation for the Learning Center as an early childhood education program. Missouri denied the Church funding based on Article I, \xc2\xa77, of its State Constitution, which prohibits the use of public funds \xe2\x80\x9cin aid of any church, sect, or denomination of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

II

\n

Properly understood then, this is a case about whether Missouri can decline to fund improvements to the facilities the Church uses to practice and spread its religious views. This Court has repeatedly warned that funding of exactly this kind\xe2\x80\x94payments from the government to a house of worship\xe2\x80\x94would cross the line drawn by the Establishment Clause. See, e.g., Walz v. Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of City of New York,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 664,\n675 (1970)\n; Rosenberger v. Rector and Visitors of Univ. of Va.,\n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 819,\n844 (1995)\n; Mitchell v. Helms,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 793\n\xe2\x80\x93844 (2000) (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment). So it is surprising that the Court mentions the Establishment Clause only to note the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement that it \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not prevent Missouri from including Trinity Lutheran in the Scrap Tire Program.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. Constitutional questions are decided by this Court, not the parties\xe2\x80\x99 concessions. The Establishment Clause does not allow Missouri to grant the Church\xe2\x80\x99s funding request because the Church uses the Learning Center, including its playground, in conjunction with its religious mission. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s silence on this front signals either its misunderstanding of the facts of this case or a startling departure from our precedents.

\n

A

\n

The government may not directly fund religious exercise. See Everson v. Board of Ed. of Ewing,\n\n330 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n16 (1947)\n; Mitchell, 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 840 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]ur decisions provide no precedent for the use of public funds to finance religious activities\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Put in doctrinal terms, such funding violates the Establishment Clause because it impermissibly \xe2\x80\x9cadvanc[es] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. religion.\xe2\x80\x9d[1] Agostini v. Felton,\n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 203\n\xe2\x80\x93223 (1997).

\n

Nowhere is this rule more clearly implicated than when funds flow directly from the public treasury to a house of worship.[2] A house of worship exists to foster and further religious exercise. There, a group of people, bound by common religious beliefs, comes together \xe2\x80\x9cto shape its own faith and mission.\xe2\x80\x9d Hosanna-Tabor Evangelical Lutheran Church and School v. EEOC,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 171,\n188 (2012)\n. Within its walls, worshippers gather to practice and reaffirm their faith. And from its base, the faithful reach out to those not yet convinced of the group\xe2\x80\x99s beliefs. When a government funds a house of worship, it underwrites this religious exercise.

\n

Tilton v. Richardson,\n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 672 (1971)\n, held as much. The federal program at issue provided construction grants to colleges and universities but prohibited grantees from using the funds to construct facilities \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98used for sectarian instruction or as a place for religious worship\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98used primarily in connection with any part of the program of a school or department of divinity.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 675 (plurality opinion) (quoting\n\n20 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7751(a)(2) (1964 ed., Supp. V)). It allowed the Federal Government to recover the grant\xe2\x80\x99s value if a grantee violated this prohibition within twenty years of the grant. See 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 675. The Court unanimously agreed that this time limit on recovery violated the Establishment Clause. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he original federal grant w[ould] in part have the effect of advancing religion,\xe2\x80\x9d a plurality explained, if a grantee \xe2\x80\x9cconverted [a facility] into a chapel or otherwise used [it] to promote religious interests\xe2\x80\x9d after twenty years. Id., at 683; see also id., at 692 (Douglas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part); Lemon v. Kurtzman,\n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 602\n\xe2\x80\x93661 (1971) (Brennan, J., concurring); id., at 665, n.\xc2\xa01 (opinion of White, J.). Accordingly, the Court severed the twenty-year limit, ensuring that program funds would be put to secular use and thereby bringing the program in line with the Establishment Clause. See Tilton, 403 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 683 (plurality opinion).

\n

This case is no different. The Church seeks state funds to improve the Learning Center\xe2\x80\x99s facilities, which, by the Church\xe2\x80\x99s own avowed description, are used to assist the spiritual growth of the children of its members and to spread the Church\xe2\x80\x99s faith to the children of nonmembers. The Church\xe2\x80\x99s playground surface\xe2\x80\x94like a Sunday School room\xe2\x80\x99s walls or the sanctuary\xe2\x80\x99s pews\xe2\x80\x94are integrated with and integral to its religious mission. The conclusion that the funding the Church seeks would impermissibly advance religion is inescapable.

\n

True, this Court has found some direct government funding of religious institutions to be consistent with the Establishment Clause. But the funding in those cases came with assurances that public funds would not be used for religious activity, despite the religious nature of the institution. See, e.g., Rosenberger, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 875\xe2\x80\x93876 (Souter, J., dissenting) (chronicling cases). The Church has not and cannot provide such assurances here.[3] See Committee for Public Ed. & Religious Liberty v. Nyquist,\n\n413 U.\xc2\xa0S. 756,\n774 (1973)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cNo attempt is made to restrict payments to those expenditures related to the upkeep of facilities used exclusively for secular purposes, nor do we think it possible within the context of these religion-oriented institutions to impose such restrictions\xe2\x80\x9d). The Church has a religious mission, one that it pursues through the Learning Center. The playground surface cannot be confined to secular use any more than lumber used to frame the Church\xe2\x80\x99s walls, glass stained and used to form its windows, or nails used to build its altar.

\n

B

\n

The Court may simply disagree with this account of the facts and think that the Church does not put its playground to religious use. If so, its mistake is limited to this case. But if it agrees that the State\xe2\x80\x99s funding would further religious activity and sees no Establishment Clause problem, then it must be implicitly applying a rule other than the one agreed to in our precedents.

\n

When the Court last addressed direct funding of religious institutions, in Mitchell, it adhered to the rule that the Establishment Clause prohibits the direct funding of religious activities. At issue was a federal program that helped state and local agencies lend educational materials to public and private schools, including religious schools. See 530 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 801\xe2\x80\x93803 (plurality opinion). The controlling concurrence assured itself that the program would not lead to the public funding of religious activity. It pointed out that the program allocated secular aid, that it did so \xe2\x80\x9con the basis of neutral, secular criteria,\xe2\x80\x9d that the aid would not \xe2\x80\x9csupplant non-[program] funds,\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cno .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. funds ever reach the coffers of religious schools,\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cevidence of actual diversion is de minimis,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the program had \xe2\x80\x9cadequate safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d to police violations. Id., at 867 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment). Those factors, it concluded, were \xe2\x80\x9csufficient to find that the program .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [did] not have the impermissible effect of advancing religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

A plurality would have instead upheld the program based only on the secular nature of the aid and the program\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cneutrality\xe2\x80\x9d as to the religious or secular nature of the recipient. See id., at 809\xe2\x80\x93814. The controlling concurrence rejected that approach. It viewed the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s test\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9csecular content aid .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. distributed on the basis of wholly neutral criteria\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94as constitutionally insufficient. Id., at 839. This test, explained the concurrence, ignored whether the public funds subsidize religion, the touchstone of establishment jurisprudence. See id., at 844 (noting that the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s logic would allow funding of \xe2\x80\x9creligious organizations (including churches)\xe2\x80\x9d where \xe2\x80\x9cthe participating religious organizations (including churches) .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. use that aid to support religious indoctrination\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion suggests the Court has made the leap the Mitchell plurality could not. For if it agrees that the funding here will finance religious activities, then only a rule that considers that fact irrelevant could support a conclusion of constitutionality. The problems of the \xe2\x80\x9csecular and neutral\xe2\x80\x9d approach have been aired before. See, e.g., id., at 900\xe2\x80\x93902 (Souter, J., dissenting). It has no basis in the history to which the Court has repeatedly turned to inform its understanding of the Establishment Clause. It permits direct subsidies for religious indoctrination, with all the attendant concerns that led to the Establishment Clause. And it favors certain religious groups, those with a belief system that allows them to compete for public dollars and those well-organized and well-funded enough to do so successfully.[4]

\n

Such a break with precedent would mark a radical mistake. The Establishment Clause protects both religion and government from the dangers that result when the two become entwined, \xe2\x80\x9cnot by providing every religion with an equal opportunity (say, to secure state funding or to pray in the public schools), but by drawing fairly clear lines of separation between church and state\xe2\x80\x94at least where the heartland of religious belief, such as primary religious [worship], is at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Zelman v. Simmons-Harris,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 639\n\xe2\x80\x93723 (2002) (Breyer, J., dissenting).

\n

III

\n

Even assuming the absence of an Establishment Clause violation and proceeding on the Court\xe2\x80\x99s preferred front\xe2\x80\x94the Free Exercise Clause\xe2\x80\x94the Court errs. It claims that the government may not draw lines based on an entity\xe2\x80\x99s religious \xe2\x80\x9cstatus.\xe2\x80\x9d But we have repeatedly said that it can. When confronted with government action that draws such a line, we have carefully considered whether the interests embodied in the Religion Clauses justify that line. The question here is thus whether those interests support the line drawn in Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s Article I, \xc2\xa77, separating the State\xe2\x80\x99s treasury from those of houses of worship. They unquestionably do.

\n

A

\n

The Establishment Clause prohibits laws \xe2\x80\x9crespecting an establishment of religion\xe2\x80\x9d and the Free Exercise Clause prohibits laws \xe2\x80\x9cprohibiting the free exercise thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 1. \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f expanded to a logical extreme,\xe2\x80\x9d these prohibitions \xe2\x80\x9cwould tend to clash with the other.\xe2\x80\x9d Walz, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 668\xe2\x80\x93669. Even in the absence of a violation of one of the Religion Clauses, the interaction of government and religion can raise concerns that sound in both Clauses. For that reason, the government may sometimes act to accommodate those concerns, even when not required to do so by the Free Exercise Clause, without violating the Establishment Clause. And the government may sometimes act to accommodate those concerns, even when not required to do so by the Establishment Clause, without violating the Free Exercise Clause. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is room for play in the joints productive of a benevolent neutrality which will permit religious exercise to exist without sponsorship and without interference.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 669. This space between the two Clauses gives government some room to recognize the unique status of religious entities and to single them out on that basis for exclusion from otherwise generally applicable laws.

\n

Invoking this principle, this Court has held that the government may sometimes relieve religious entities from the requirements of government programs. A State need not, for example, require nonprofit houses of worship to pay property taxes. It may instead \xe2\x80\x9cspar[e] the exercise of religion from the burden of property taxation levied on private profit institutions\xe2\x80\x9d and spare the government \xe2\x80\x9cthe direct confrontations and conflicts that follow in the train of those legal processes\xe2\x80\x9d associated with taxation. See id., at 673\xe2\x80\x93674. Nor must a State require nonprofit religious entities to abstain from making employment decisions on the basis of religion. It may instead avoid imposing on these institutions a \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ear of potential liability [that] might affect the way\xe2\x80\x9d it \xe2\x80\x9ccarried out what it understood to be its religious mission\xe2\x80\x9d and on the government the sensitive task of policing compliance. Corporation of Presiding Bishop of Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints v. Amos,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 327,\n336 (1987)\n; see also id., at 343 (Brennan, J., concurring in judgment). But the government may not invoke the space between the Religion Clauses in a manner that \xe2\x80\x9cdevolve[s] into an unlawful fostering of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Cutter v. Wilkinson,\n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 709,\n714 (2005)\n (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Invoking this same principle, this Court has held that the government may sometimes close off certain government aid programs to religious entities. The State need not, for example, fund the training of a religious group\xe2\x80\x99s leaders, those \xe2\x80\x9cwho will preach their beliefs, teach their faith, and carry out their mission,\xe2\x80\x9d Hosanna-Tabor, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 196. It may instead avoid the historic \xe2\x80\x9cantiestablishment interests\xe2\x80\x9d raised by the use of \xe2\x80\x9ctaxpayer funds to support church leaders.\xe2\x80\x9d Locke v. Davey,\n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 712,\n722 (2004)\n.

\n

When reviewing a law that, like this one, singles out religious entities for exclusion from its reach, we thus have not myopically focused on the fact that a law singles out religious entities, but on the reasons that it does so.

\n

B

\n

Missouri has decided that the unique status of houses of worship requires a special rule when it comes to public funds. Its Constitution reflects that choice and provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThat no money shall ever be taken from the public treasury, directly or indirectly, in aid of any church, sect, or denomination of religion, or in aid of any priest, preacher, minister or teacher thereof, as such; and that no preference shall be given to nor any discrimination made against any church, sect or creed of religion, or any form of religious faith or worship.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. I, \xc2\xa77.

\n

Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s decision, which has deep roots in our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s history, reflects a reasonable and constitutional judgment.

\n

1

\n

This Court has consistently looked to history for guidance when applying the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Religion Clauses. Those Clauses guard against a return to the past, and so that past properly informs their meaning. See, e.g., Everson, 330 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 14\xe2\x80\x9315; Torcaso v. Watkins,\n\n367 U.\xc2\xa0S. 488,\n492 (1961)\n. This case is no different.

\n

This Nation\xe2\x80\x99s early experience with, and eventual rejection of, established religion\xe2\x80\x94shorthand for \xe2\x80\x9csponsorship, financial support, and active involvement of the sovereign in religious activity,\xe2\x80\x9d Walz, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 668\xe2\x80\x94defies easy summary. No two States\xe2\x80\x99 experiences were the same. In some a religious establishment never took hold. See T. Curry, The First Freedoms 19, 72\xe2\x80\x9374, 76\xe2\x80\x9377, 159\xe2\x80\x93160 (1986) (Curry). In others establishment varied in terms of the sect (or sects) supported, the nature and extent of that support, and the uniformity of that support across the State. Where establishment did take hold, it lost its grip at different times and at different speeds. See T. Cobb, The Rise of Religious Liberty in America 510\xe2\x80\x93511 (1970 ed.) (Cobb).

\n

Despite this rich diversity of experience, the story relevant here is one of consistency. The use of public funds to support core religious institutions can safely be described as a hallmark of the States\xe2\x80\x99 early experiences with religious establishment. Every state establishment saw laws passed to raise public funds and direct them toward houses of worship and ministers. And as the States all dises-tablished, one by one, they all undid those laws.[5]

\n

Those who fought to end the public funding of religion based their opposition on a powerful set of arguments, all stemming from the basic premise that the practice harmed both civil government and religion. The civil government, they maintained, could claim no authority over religious belief. For them, support for religion compelled by the State marked an overstep of authority that would only lead to more. Equally troubling, it risked divisiveness by giving religions reason to compete for the State\xe2\x80\x99s beneficence. Faith, they believed, was a personal matter, entirely between an individual and his god. Religion was best served when sects reached out on the basis of their tenets alone, unsullied by outside forces, allowing adherents to come to their faith voluntarily. Over and over, these arguments gained acceptance and led to the end of state laws exacting payment for the support of religion.

\n

Take Virginia. After the Revolution, Virginia debated and rejected a general religious assessment. The proposed bill would have allowed taxpayers to direct payments to a Christian church of their choice to support a minister, exempted \xe2\x80\x9cQuakers and Menonists,\xe2\x80\x9d and sent undirected assessments to the public treasury for \xe2\x80\x9cseminaries of learning.\xe2\x80\x9d A Bill Establishing a Provision for Teachers of the Christian Religion, reprinted in Everson, 330 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 74 (supplemental appendix to dissent of Rutledge, J.).

\n

In opposing this proposal, James Madison authored his famous Memorial and Remonstrance, in which he condemned the bill as hostile to religious freedom. Memorial and Remonstrance Against Religious Assessments (1785), in 5 The Founders\xe2\x80\x99 Constitution 82\xe2\x80\x9384 (P. Kurland & R. Lerner eds. 1987). Believing it \xe2\x80\x9cproper to take alarm,\xe2\x80\x9d despite the bill\xe2\x80\x99s limits, he protested \xe2\x80\x9cthat the same authority which can force a citizen to contribute three pence only of his property for the support of any one establishment, may force him to conform to any other establishment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 82. Religion had \xe2\x80\x9cflourished, not only without the support of human laws, but in spite of every opposition from them.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 83. Compelled support for religion, he argued, would only weaken believers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cconfidence in its innate excellence,\xe2\x80\x9d strengthen others\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9csuspicion that its friends are too conscious of its fallacies to trust in its own merits,\xe2\x80\x9d and harm the \xe2\x80\x9cpurity and efficacy\xe2\x80\x9d of the supported religion. Ibid. He ended by deeming the bill incompatible with Virginia\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98free exercise of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Religion according to the dictates of conscience.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 84.

\n

Madison contributed one influential voice to a larger chorus of petitions opposed to the bill. Others included \xe2\x80\x9cthe religious bodies of Baptists, Presbyterians, and Quakers.\xe2\x80\x9d T. Buckley, Church and State in Revolutionary Virginia 1776\xe2\x80\x931787, p. 148 (1977). Their petitions raised similar points. See id., at 137\xe2\x80\x93140, 148\xe2\x80\x93149. Like Madison, many viewed the bill as a step toward a dangerous church-state relationship. See id., at 151. These voices against the bill won out, and Virginia soon prohibited religious assessments. See Virginia Act for Establishing Religious Freedom (Oct. 31, 1785), in 5 The Founders\xe2\x80\x99 Constitution 84\xe2\x80\x9385.

\n

This same debate played out in nearby Maryland, with the same result. In 1784, an assessment bill was proposed that would have allowed taxpayers to direct payments to ministers (of sufficiently large churches) or to the poor. Non-Christians were exempt. See Curry 155. Controversy over the bill \xe2\x80\x9ceclipse[d] in volume of writing and bitter-ness of invective every other political dispute since the debate over the question of independence.\xe2\x80\x9d J. Rainbolt, The Struggle To Define \xe2\x80\x9cReligious Liberty\xe2\x80\x9d in Maryland, 1776\xe2\x80\x9385, 17 J. Church & State 443, 449 (1975). Critics of the bill raised the same themes as those in Virginia: that religion \xe2\x80\x9cneeds not the power of rules to establish, but only to protect it\xe2\x80\x9d; that financial support of religion leads toward an establishment; and that laws for such support are \xe2\x80\x9coppressive.\xe2\x80\x9d Curry 156, 157 (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Copy of Petition [to General Assembly], Maryland Gazette, Mar. 25, 1785, pp.\xc2\xa01, 2, col.1 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hy should such as do not desire or make conscience of it, be forced by law\xe2\x80\x9d). When the legislature next met, most representatives \xe2\x80\x9chad been elected by anti-assessment voters,\xe2\x80\x9d and the bill failed. Curry 157. In 1810, Maryland revoked the authority to levy religious assessments. See Md. Const., Amdt. XIII (1776), in 3 Federal and State Constitutions 1705 (F. Thorpe ed. 1909) (Thorpe).

\n

In New England, which took longer to reach this conclusion, Vermont went first. Its religious assessment laws were accommodating. A person who was not a member of his town\xe2\x80\x99s church was, upon securing a certificate to that effect, exempt. See L. Levy, The Establishment Clause 50 (1994) (Levy). Even so, the laws were viewed by many as violating Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional prohibition against involuntary support of religion and guarantee of freedom of conscience. See, e.g., Address of Council of Censors to the People of Vermont 5\xe2\x80\x938 (1800) (\xe2\x80\x9c[R]eligion is a concern personally and exclusively operative between the individual and his God\xe2\x80\x9d); Address of Council of Censors [Vermont] 3\xe2\x80\x937 (Dec. 1806) (the laws\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cevils\xe2\x80\x9d included \xe2\x80\x9cviolence done to the feelings of men\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctheir property,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9canimosities,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe dangerous lengths of which it is a foundation for us to go, in both civil and religious usurpation\xe2\x80\x9d). In 1807, Vermont \xe2\x80\x9crepealed all laws concerning taxation for religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Levy 51.

\n

The rest of New England heard the same arguments and reached the same conclusion. John Leland\xe2\x80\x99s sustained criticism of religious assessments over 20 years helped end the practice in Connecticut. See, e.g., Esbeck, Dissent and Disestablishment: The Church-State Settlement in the Early American Republic, 2004 B. Y. U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1385, 1498, 1501\xe2\x80\x931511. The reasons he offered in urging opposition to the State\xe2\x80\x99s laws will by now be familiar. Religion \xe2\x80\x9cis a matter between God and individuals,\xe2\x80\x9d which does not need, and would only be harmed by, government support. J. Leland, The Rights of Conscience Inalienable (1791), in The Sacred Rights of Conscience 337\xe2\x80\x93339 (D. Dreisbach & M. Hall eds. 2009). \xe2\x80\x9c[T]ruth gains honor; and men more firmly believe it,\xe2\x80\x9d when religion is subjected to the \xe2\x80\x9ccool investigation and fair argument\xe2\x80\x9d that freedom of conscience produces. Id., at 340. Religious assessments violated that freedom, he argued. See id., at 342 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf these people bind nobody but themselves, who is injured by their religious opinions? But if they bind an individual besides themselves, the bond is fraudulent and ought to be declared illegal\xe2\x80\x9d). Connecticut ended religious assessments first by statute in 1817, then by its State Constitution of 1818. See Cobb 513.

\n

In New Hampshire, a steady campaign against religious assessments led to a bill that was subjected to \xe2\x80\x9cthe scru-tiny of the people.\xe2\x80\x9d C. Kinney, Church & State: The Strug-gle for Separation in New Hampshire, 1630\xe2\x80\x931900, p. 101 (1955) (Kinney). It was nicknamed \xe2\x80\x9cDr. Whipple\xe2\x80\x99s Act\xe2\x80\x9d after its strongest advocate in the State House. Orford Union Congregational Soc. v. West Congregational Soc. of Orford, 55 N. H. 463, 468\xe2\x80\x93469, n. (1875). He defended the bill as a means \xe2\x80\x9cto take religion out of politics, to eliminate state support, to insure opportunity to worship with true freedom of conscience, [and] to put all sects and denominations of Christians upon a level.\xe2\x80\x9d Kinney 103. The bill became law and provided \xe2\x80\x9cthat no person shall be compelled to join or support, or be classed with, or associated to any congregation, church or religious society without his express consent first had and obtained.\xe2\x80\x9d Act [of July 1, 1819] Regulating Towns and Choice of Town Officers \xc2\xa73, in 1 Laws of the State of New Hampshire Enacted Since June 1, 1815, p. 45 (1824). Massachusetts held on the longest of all the States, finally ending religious assessments in 1833. See Cobb 515.[6]

\n

The course of this history shows that those who lived under the laws and practices that formed religious establishments made a considered decision that civil government should not fund ministers and their houses of worship. To us, their debates may seem abstract and this history remote. That is only because we live in a society that has long benefited from decisions made in response to these now centuries-old arguments, a society that those not so fortunate fought hard to build.

\n

2

\n

In Locke, this Court expressed an understanding of, and respect for, this history. Locke involved a provision of the State of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution that, like Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s nearly identical Article I, \xc2\xa77, barred the use of public funds for houses of worship or ministers. Consistent with this denial of funds to ministers, the State\xe2\x80\x99s college scholarship program did not allow funds to be used for devotional theology degrees. When asked whether this violated the would-be minister\xe2\x80\x99s free exercise rights, the Court invoked the play in the joints principle and answered no. The Establishment Clause did not require the prohibition because \xe2\x80\x9cthe link between government funds and religious training [was] broken by the independent and private choice of [scholarship] recipients.\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 719; see also supra, n. 2. Nonetheless, the denial did not violate the Free Exercise Clause because a \xe2\x80\x9chistoric and substantial state interest\xe2\x80\x9d supported the constitutional provision. 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 725. The Court could \xe2\x80\x9cthink of few areas in which a State\xe2\x80\x99s antiestablishment interests come more into play\xe2\x80\x9d than the \xe2\x80\x9cprocuring [of] taxpayer funds to support church leaders.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 722.

\n

The same is true of this case, about directing taxpayer funds to houses of worship, see supra, at 2. Like the use of public dollars for ministers at issue in Locke, turning over public funds to houses of worship implicates serious antiestablishment and free exercise interests. The history just discussed fully supports this conclusion. As states disestablished, they repealed laws allowing taxation to support religion because the practice threatened other forms of government support for, involved some government control over, and weakened supporters\xe2\x80\x99 control of religion. Common sense also supports this conclusion. Recall that a state may not fund religious activities without violating the Establishment Clause. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93A, supra. A state can reasonably use status as a \xe2\x80\x9chouse of worship\xe2\x80\x9d as a stand-in for \xe2\x80\x9creligious activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Inside a house of worship, dividing the religious from the secular would require intrusive line-drawing by government, and monitoring those lines would entangle government with the house of worship\xe2\x80\x99s activities. And so while not every activity a house of worship undertakes will be inseparably linked to religious activity, \xe2\x80\x9cthe likelihood that many are makes a categorical rule a suitable means to avoid chilling the exercise of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Amos, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 345 (Brennan, J., concurring in judgment). Finally, and of course, such funding implicates the free exercise rights of taxpayers by denying them the chance to decide for themselves whether and how to fund religion. If there is any \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98room for play in the joints\xe2\x80\x99 between\xe2\x80\x9d the Religion Clauses, it is here. Locke, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 718 (quoting Walz, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 669).

\n

As was true in Locke, a prophylactic rule against the use of public funds for houses of worship is a permissible accommodation of these weighty interests. The rule has a historical pedigree identical to that of the provision in Locke. Almost all of the States that ratified the Religion Clauses operated under this rule. See 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 723. Seven had placed this rule in their State Constitutions.[7] Three enforced it by statute or in practice.[8] Only one had not yet embraced the rule.[9] Today, thirty-eight States have a counterpart to Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s Article I, \xc2\xa77.[10] The provisions, as a general matter, date back to or before these States\xe2\x80\x99 original Constitutions.[11] That so many States have for so long drawn a line that prohibits public funding for houses of worship, based on principles rooted in this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of how best to foster religious liberty, supports the conclusion that public funding of houses of worship \xe2\x80\x9cis of a different ilk.\xe2\x80\x9d Locke, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 723.

\n

And as in Locke, Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s Article I, \xc2\xa77, is closely tied to the state interests it protects. See Locke, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 724 (describing the program at issue as \xe2\x80\x9cgo[ing] a long way toward including religion in its benefits\xe2\x80\x9d). A straightforward reading of Article I, \xc2\xa77, prohibits funding only for \xe2\x80\x9cany church, sect, or denomination of religion, or in aid of any priest, preacher, minister or teacher thereof, as such.\xe2\x80\x9d The Missouri courts have not read the State\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution to reach more broadly, to prohibit funding for other religiously affiliated institutions, or more broadly still, to prohibit the funding of religious believers. See, e.g., Saint Louis Univ. v. Masonic Temple Assn. of St. Louis, 220 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 721, 726 (Mo. 2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe university is not a religious institution simply because it is affiliated with the Jesuits or the Roman Catholic Church\xe2\x80\x9d). The Scrap Tire Program at issue here proves the point. Missouri will fund a religious organization not \xe2\x80\x9cowned or controlled by a church,\xe2\x80\x9d if its \xe2\x80\x9cmission and activities are secular (separate from religion, not spiritual in) nature\xe2\x80\x9d and the funds \xe2\x80\x9cwill be used for secular (separate from religion; not spiritual) purposes rather than for sectarian (denominational, devoted to a sect) purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Brief for Petitioner 3a; see also Tr. of Oral Arg. 33\xe2\x80\x9335. Article I, \xc2\xa77, thus stops Missouri only from funding specific entities, ones that set and enforce religious doctrine for their adherents. These are the entities that most acutely raise the establishment and free exercise concerns that arise when public funds flow to religion.

\n

Missouri has recognized the simple truth that, even absent an Establishment Clause violation, the transfer of public funds to houses of worship raises concerns that sit exactly between the Religion Clauses. To avoid those concerns, and only those concerns, it has prohibited such funding. In doing so, it made the same choice made by the earliest States centuries ago and many other States in the years since. The Constitution permits this choice.

\n

3

\n

In the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, none of this matters. It focuses on one aspect of Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s Article I, \xc2\xa77, to the exclusion of all else: that it denies funding to a house of worship, here the Church, \xe2\x80\x9csimply because of what it [i]s\xe2\x80\x94a church.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. The Court describes this as a constitutionally impermissible line based on religious \xe2\x80\x9cstatus\xe2\x80\x9d that requires strict scrutiny. Its rule is out of step with our precedents in this area, and wrong on its own terms.

\n

The Constitution creates specific rules that control how the government may interact with religious entities. And so of course a government may act based on a religious entity\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstatus\xe2\x80\x9d as such. It is that very status that implicates the interests protected by the Religion Clauses. Sometimes a religious entity\xe2\x80\x99s unique status requires the government to act. See Hosanna-Tabor, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 188\xe2\x80\x93190. Other times, it merely permits the government to act. See Part III\xe2\x80\x93A, supra. In all cases, the dispositive issue is not whether religious \xe2\x80\x9cstatus\xe2\x80\x9d matters\xe2\x80\x94it does, or the Religion Clauses would not be at issue\xe2\x80\x94but whether the government must, or may, act on that basis.

\n

Start where the Court stays silent. Its opinion does not acknowledge that our precedents have expressly approved of a government\xe2\x80\x99s choice to draw lines based on an entity\xe2\x80\x99s religious status. See Amos, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 339; Walz, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 680; Locke, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 721. Those cases did not deploy strict scrutiny to create a presumption of unconstitutionality, as the Court does today. Instead, they asked whether the government had offered a strong enough reason to justify drawing a line based on that status. See Amos, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 339 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e see no justification for applying strict scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d); Walz, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 679 (rejecting criticisms of a case-by-case approach as giving \xe2\x80\x9ctoo little weight to the fact that it is an essential part of adjudication to draw distinctions, including fine ones, in the process of interpreting the Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d); Locke, 540 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 725 (balancing the State\xe2\x80\x99s interests against the aspiring minister\xe2\x80\x99s).

\n

The Court takes two steps to avoid these precedents. First, it recasts Locke as a case about a restriction that prohibited the would-be minister from \xe2\x80\x9cus[ing] the funds to prepare for the ministry.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. A faithful reading of Locke gives it a broader reach. Locke stands for the reasonable proposition that the government may, but need not, choose not to fund certain religious entities (there, ministers) where doing so raises \xe2\x80\x9chistoric and substantial\xe2\x80\x9d establishment and free exercise concerns. 540 U. S., at 725. Second, it suggests that this case is different because it involves \xe2\x80\x9cdiscrimination\xe2\x80\x9d in the form of the denial of access to a possible benefit. Ante, at 11. But in this area of law, a decision to treat entities differently based on distinctions that the Religion Clauses make relevant does not amount to discrimination.[12] To understand why, keep in mind that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Court has unambiguously concluded that the individual freedom of conscience protected by the\n \nFirst Amendment embraces the right to select any religious faith or none at all.\xe2\x80\x9d Wallace v. Jaffree,\n\n472 U.\xc2\xa0S. 38\n\xe2\x80\x9353 (1985). If the denial of a benefit others may receive is discrimination that violates the Free Exercise Clause, then the accommodations of religious entities we have approved would violate the free exercise rights of nonreligious entities. We have, with good reason, rejected that idea, see, e.g., Amos, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 338\xe2\x80\x93339, and instead focused on whether the government has provided a good enough reason, based in the values the Religion Clauses protect, for its decision.[13]

\n

The Court offers no real reason for rejecting the balancing approach in our precedents in favor of strict scrutiny, beyond its references to discrimination. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s desire to avoid what it views as discrimination is understand-able. But in this context, the description is particularly inappropriate. A State\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to fund houses of worship does not disfavor religion; rather, it represents a valid choice to remain secular in the face of serious establishment and free exercise concerns. That does not make the State \xe2\x80\x9catheistic or antireligious.\xe2\x80\x9d County of Allegheny v. American Civil Liberties Union, Greater Pittsburgh Chapter,\n\n492 U.\xc2\xa0S. 573,\n610 (1989)\n. It means only that the State has \xe2\x80\x9cestablishe[d] neither atheism nor religion as its official creed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion \xe2\x80\x9cthat the only alternative to governmental support of religion is governmental hostility to it represents a giant step backward in our Religion Clause jurisprudence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 652, n.\xc2\xa011 (Stevens, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part).

\n

At bottom, the Court creates the following rule today: The government may draw lines on the basis of religious status to grant a benefit to religious persons or entities but it may not draw lines on that basis when doing so would further the interests the Religion Clauses protect in other ways. Nothing supports this lopsided outcome. Not the Religion Clauses, as they protect establishment and free exercise interests in the same constitutional breath, neither privileged over the other. Not precedent, since we have repeatedly explained that the Clauses protect not religion but \xe2\x80\x9cthe individual\xe2\x80\x99s freedom of conscience,\xe2\x80\x9d Jaffree, 472 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 50\xe2\x80\x94that which allows him to choose religion, reject it, or remain undecided. And not reason, because as this case shows, the same interests served by lifting government-imposed burdens on certain religious entities may sometimes be equally served by denying government-provided benefits to certain religious entities. Cf. Walz, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 674 (entanglement); Amos, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 336 (influence on religious activities).

\n

Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s concurrence offers a narrower rule that would limit the effects of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, but that rule does not resolve this case. Justice Breyer, like the Court, thinks that \xe2\x80\x9cdenying a generally available benefit solely on account of religious identity imposes a penalty on the free exercise of religion that can be justified only by a state interest of the highest order,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6 (majority opinion) (internal quotation marks omitted). See ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment). Few would disagree with a literal interpretation of this statement. To fence out religious persons or entities from a truly gener-ally available public benefit\xe2\x80\x94one provided to all, no questions asked, such as police or fire protections\xe2\x80\x94would violate the Free Exercise Clause. Accord, Rosenberger, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 879, n. 5 (Souter, J., dissenting). This explains why Missouri does not apply its constitutional provision in that manner. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 35\xe2\x80\x9336. Nor has it done so here. The Scrap Tire Program offers not a generally available benefit but a selective benefit for a few recipients each year. In this context, the comparison to truly generally available benefits is inapt. Cf. Everson, 330 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 61, n. 56 (Rutledge, J., dissenting) (The Religion Clauses \xe2\x80\x9cforbi[d] support, not protection from interference or destruction\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

On top of all of this, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s application of its new rule here is mistaken. In concluding that Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s Article I, \xc2\xa77, cannot withstand strict scrutiny, the Court describes Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s interest as a mere \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy preference for skating as far as possible from religious establishment concerns.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. The constitutional provisions of thirty-nine States\xe2\x80\x94all but invalidated today\xe2\x80\x94the weighty interests they protect, and the history they draw on deserve more than this judicial brush aside.[14]

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision discounts centuries of history and jeopardizes the government\xe2\x80\x99s ability to remain secular. Just three years ago, this Court claimed to understand that, in this area of law, to \xe2\x80\x9csweep away what has so long been settled would create new controversy and begin anew the very divisions along religious lines that the Establishment Clause seeks to prevent.\xe2\x80\x9d Town of Greece v. Galloway, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 8). It makes clear today that this principle applies only when preference suits.

\n

IV

\n

The Religion Clauses of the\n \nFirst Amendment contain a promise from our government and a backstop that disables our government from breaking it. The Free Exercise Clause extends the promise. We each retain our inalien-able right to \xe2\x80\x9cthe free exercise\xe2\x80\x9d of religion, to choose for ourselves whether to believe and how to worship. And the Establishment Clause erects the backstop. Government cannot, through the enactment of a \xe2\x80\x9claw respecting an establishment of religion,\xe2\x80\x9d start us down the path to the past, when this right was routinely abridged.

\n

The Court today dismantles a core protection for religious freedom provided in these Clauses. It holds not just that a government may support houses of worship with taxpayer funds, but that\xe2\x80\x94at least in this case and perhaps in others, see ante at 14, n. 3\xe2\x80\x94it must do so when-ever it decides to create a funding program. History shows that the Religion Clauses separate the public treasury from religious coffers as one measure to secure the kind of freedom of conscience that benefits both religion and government. If this separation means anything, it means that the government cannot, or at the very least need not, tax its citizens and turn that money over to houses of worship. The Court today blinds itself to the outcome this history requires and leads us instead to a place where separation of church and state is a constitutional slogan, not a constitutional commitment. I dissent.
