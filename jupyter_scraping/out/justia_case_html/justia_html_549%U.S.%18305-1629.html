GONZALES, ATTORNEY GENERAL v. DUENAS-ALVAREZ

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 05\xe2\x80\x931629.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 5, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 17, 2007

\n

Respondent, a permanent resident alien, was convicted of violating Cal. Veh. Code Ann. \xc2\xa710851(a), under which \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person who drives or takes a vehicle not his or her own, without the consent of the owner \xe2\x80\xa6 , or any person who is a party or an accessory to or an accomplice in the driving or unauthorized taking or stealing, is guilty of a public offense.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) The Federal Government then sought to remove respondent from the United States as an alien convicted of \xe2\x80\x9ca theft offense \xe2\x80\xa6 for which the term of imprisonment [is] at least one year,\xe2\x80\x9d 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(G); \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(A). The Government claimed that the California conviction qualified as such a \xe2\x80\x9ctheft offense\xe2\x80\x9d under the framework set forth in Taylor v. United States, 495 U. S. 575. In Taylor, the Court considered whether a prior conviction for violating a state statute criminalizing certain burglary-like behavior fell within the term \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d for sentence-enhancement purposes under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(e). This Court held that Congress meant that term to refer to \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cthe generic sense in which the term is now used in the criminal codes of most States,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 598; and that a sentencing court seeking to determine whether a particular prior conviction was for generic burglary should normally look to the state statute defining the crime of conviction, not to the facts of the particular prior case, id., at 599\xe2\x80\x93600; but that where state law defines burglary broadly to include crimes falling outside generic \xe2\x80\x9cburglary,\xe2\x80\x9d the sentencer should \xe2\x80\x9cgo beyond the mere fact of conviction\xe2\x80\x9d and examine, e.g., the charging document and jury instructions to determine whether the earlier \xe2\x80\x9cjury was actually required to find all the elements of generic burglary,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 602. The Federal Immigration Judge and the Bureau of Immigration Appeals (BIA) found respondent removable, but the Ninth Circuit, summarily remanded in light of its earlier Penuliar decision holding that \xe2\x80\x9caiding and abetting\xe2\x80\x9d a theft is not itself a crime under the generic definition of theft.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The term \xe2\x80\x9ctheft offense\xe2\x80\x9d in 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(G) includes the crime of \xe2\x80\x9caiding and abetting\xe2\x80\x9d a theft offense. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0One who aids or abets a theft, like a principal who actually participates, commits a crime that falls within the scope of the generic theft definition accepted by the BIA and the Ninth and other Circuits: the \xe2\x80\x9ctaking of property or an exercise of control over property without consent with the criminal intent to deprive the owner of rights and benefits of ownership, even if such deprivation is less than total or permanent.\xe2\x80\x9d Penuliar v. Gonzales, 435 F.\xc2\xa03d 961, 969. Since, as the record shows, state and federal criminal law now uniformly treats principals and aiders and abettors alike, \xe2\x80\x9cthe generic sense in which\xe2\x80\x9d the term \xe2\x80\x9ctheft\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cis now used in the criminal codes of most States,\xe2\x80\x9d Taylor, supra, at 598, covers such \xe2\x80\x9caiders and abettors\xe2\x80\x9d as well as principals. And the criminal activities of these aiders and abettors of a generic theft thus fall within the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x9ctheft\xe2\x80\x9d in the federal statute. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Court rejects respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Cal. Veh. Code \xc2\xa710851, through the California courts\xe2\x80\x99 application of a \xe2\x80\x9cnatural and probable consequences\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine, creates a subspecies of the crime falling outside the generic \xe2\x80\x9ctheft\xe2\x80\x9d definition. The fact that, under California law, an aider and abettor is criminally responsible not only for the crime he intends, but also for any crime that naturally and probably results from his intended crime, does not in itself show that the state statute covers a nongeneric theft crime. Relatively few jurisdictions have expressly rejected the \xe2\x80\x9cnatural and probable consequences\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine, and many States and the Federal Government apply some form or variation of that doctrine or permit jury inferences of intent in circumstances similar to those in which California has applied the doctrine. To succeed, respondent must show something special about California\xe2\x80\x99s version of the doctrine. His attempt to show that, unlike most other States, California makes a defendant criminally liable for conduct he did not intend, not even as a known or almost certain byproduct of his intentional acts, fails because the California cases respondent cites do not show that California\xe2\x80\x99s law is applied in such a way that is somehow broader in scope than other States\xe2\x80\x99 laws. Moreover, to find that state law creates a crime outside the generic definition of a listed crime in a federal statute requires a realistic probability, not a theoretical possibility, that the State would apply its statute to conduct falling outside the generic definition. To make that showing, an offender must at least point to his own case or other cases in which the state courts in fact did apply the statute in the special (nongeneric) manner for which he argues. Respondent makes no such showing. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s additional claims\xe2\x80\x94that \xc2\xa710851 (1) holds liable accessories after the fact, who need not be shown to have committed a theft, and (2) applies to joyriding, which falls outside the generic \xe2\x80\x9ctheft\xe2\x80\x9d definition\xe2\x80\x94are not considered here because they do not fall within the terms of the question presented, the lower court did not consider them, and this Court declines to reach them in the first instance. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

176 Fed. Appx. 820, vacated and remanded.

Stevens, J., filed an opinion concurring in part and dissenting in part.

ALBERTO R. GONZALES, ATTORNEY GENERAL,\nPETITIONER v. LUIS ALEXANDER DUENAS-\nALVAREZ

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[January 17, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Immigration law provides for removal from the United States of an alien convicted of \xe2\x80\x9ca theft offense (including receipt of stolen property) \xe2\x80\xa6 for which the term of imprisonment [is] at least one year.\xe2\x80\x9d 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(G) (emphasis added); \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(A). The question here is whether the term \xe2\x80\x9ctheft offense\xe2\x80\x9d in this federal statute includes the crime of \xe2\x80\x9caiding and abetting\xe2\x80\x9d a theft offense. We hold that it does. And we vacate a Ninth Circuit determination to the contrary.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Immigration and Nationality Act, 66 Stat. 163, 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101 et seq., lists a set of offenses, conviction for any one of which subjects certain aliens to removal from the United States, \xc2\xa71227(a). In determining whether a conviction (say, a conviction for violating a state criminal law that forbids the taking of property without permission) falls within the scope of a listed offense (e.g., \xe2\x80\x9ctheft offense\xe2\x80\x9d), the lower courts uniformly have applied the approach this Court set forth in Taylor v. United States, 495 U. S. 575 (1990). E.g., Soliman v. Gonzales, 419 F. 3d 276, 284 (CA4 2005); Abimbola v. Ashcroft, 378 F. 3d 173, 176\xe2\x80\x93177 (CA2 2004); Huerta-Guevara v. Ashcroft, 321 F. 3d 883, 886\xe2\x80\x93888 (CA9 2003); Hernandez-Mancilla v. INS, 246 F. 3d 1002, 1008\xe2\x80\x931009 (CA7 2001).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Taylor concerned offenses listed in the federal Armed Career Criminal Act, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(e) (2000 ed. and Supp. IV). That Act mandates a lengthy prison sentence for offenders with previous convictions for, e.g., a \xe2\x80\x9cviolent felony\xe2\x80\x9d; and the Act sets forth certain specific crimes, e.g., \xe2\x80\x9cburglary,\xe2\x80\x9d included in this category. The Court, in Taylor, considered whether a conviction for violating a state statute criminalizing certain burglary-like behavior fell within the listed federal term \xe2\x80\x9cburglary.\xe2\x80\x9d 495 U. S., at 589, 598.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court held that Congress meant its listed term \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d to refer to a specific crime, i.e., \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98burglary\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cthe generic sense in which the term is now used in the criminal codes of most States.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 598 (emphasis added). The Court also held that a state conviction qualifies as a burglary conviction, \xe2\x80\x9cregardless of\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cexact [state] definition or label\xe2\x80\x9d as long as it has the \xe2\x80\x9cbasic elements\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cgeneric\xe2\x80\x9d burglary, namely \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful or unprivileged entry into, or remaining in, a building or structure, with intent to commit a crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 599. The Court added that, when a sentencing court seeks to determine whether a particular prior conviction was for a generic burglary offense, it should normally look not to the facts of the particular prior case, but rather to the state statute defining the crime of conviction. Id., at 599\xe2\x80\x93600.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court further noted that a \xe2\x80\x9cfew States\xe2\x80\x99 burglary statutes,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cdefine burglary more broadly\xe2\x80\x9d to include both a (generically defined) listed crime and also one or more nonlisted crimes. Id., at 599. For example, Massachusetts defines \xe2\x80\x9cburglary\xe2\x80\x9d as including not only breaking into \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a building\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but also breaking into a \xe2\x80\x9cvehicle\xe2\x80\x9d (which falls outside the generic definition of \xe2\x80\x9cburglary,\xe2\x80\x9d for a car is not a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98building or structure\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). See Shepard v. United States, 544 U. S. 13, 16, 17 (2005); see also Taylor, 495 U. S., at 599 (discussing Missouri burglary statutes). In such cases the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccategorical approach\xe2\x80\x9d permits the sentencing court \xe2\x80\x9cto go beyond the mere fact of conviction\xe2\x80\x9d in order to determine whether the earlier \xe2\x80\x9cjury was actually required to find all the elements of generic burglary.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 602; see also Conteh v. Gonzales, 461 F.\xc2\xa03d 45, 54 (CA1 2006) (observing that some courts refer to this step of the Taylor inquiry as a \xe2\x80\x9cmodified categorical approach\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9cFor example,\xe2\x80\x9d the sentencing court might examine \xe2\x80\x9cthe indictment or information and jury instructions\xe2\x80\x9d in the earlier case. 495 U. S., at 602. In Shepard, we added that, in a nonjury case, the sentencing court might examine not only the \xe2\x80\x9ccharging document\xe2\x80\x9d but also \xe2\x80\x9cthe terms of a plea agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9ctranscript of colloquy between judge and defendant,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9csome comparable judicial record\xe2\x80\x9d of information about the \xe2\x80\x9cfactual basis for the plea.\xe2\x80\x9d 544 U. S., at 26.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The case before us concerns the application of the framework just set forth to Luis Duenas-Alvarez, the respondent here, a permanent resident alien of the United States. In 2002, Duenas-Alvarez was convicted of violating Cal. Veh. Code Ann. \xc2\xa710851(a) (West 2000). That section states:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cAny person who drives or takes a vehicle not his or her own, without the consent of the owner thereof, and with intent either to permanently or temporarily deprive the owner thereof of his or her title to or possession of the vehicle, whether with or without intent to steal the vehicle, or any person who is a party or an accessory to or an accomplice in the driving or unauthorized taking or stealing, is guilty of a public offense.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.)

\n

\nAfter Duenas-Alvarez was convicted, the Federal Government, claiming that the conviction was for a generic theft offense, began removal proceedings. A Federal Immigration Judge, agreeing with the Government that the California offense is \xe2\x80\x9ca theft offense \xe2\x80\xa6 for which the term of imprisonment [is] at least one year,\xe2\x80\x9d found Duenas-Alvarez removable. 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(G) (footnote omitted); \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(A). The Bureau of Immigration Appeals (BIA) affirmed. Duenas-Alvarez sought review of the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s decision in the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While respondent\xe2\x80\x99s petition for court review was pending, the Ninth Circuit, in Penuliar v. Ashcroft, 395 F.\xc2\xa03d 1037 (2005), held that the relevant California Vehicle Code provision, \xc2\xa710851(a), sweeps more broadly than generic theft. See id., at 1044\xe2\x80\x931045. In particular, the court said that generic theft has as an element the taking or control of others\xe2\x80\x99 property. But, the court added, the California statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[who] is a party or an accessory \xe2\x80\xa6 or an accomplice\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d would permit conviction \xe2\x80\x9cfor aiding and abetting a theft.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1044 (emphasis deleted). And the court believed that one might \xe2\x80\x9caid\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cabet\xe2\x80\x9d a theft without taking or controlling property. Id., at 1044\xe2\x80\x931045 (citing Martinez-Perez v. Ashcroft, 393 F.\xc2\xa03d 1018 (CA9 2004), withdrawn and amended, 417 F.\xc2\xa03d 1022 (2005)). Hence, in the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 view, the provision must cover some generically defined \xe2\x80\x9ctheft\xe2\x80\x9d crimes and also some other crimes (aiding and abetting crimes) that, because they are not generically defined \xe2\x80\x9ctheft\xe2\x80\x9d crimes, fall outside the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x9ctheft\xe2\x80\x9d in the immigration statute. 395 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1044\xe2\x80\x931045.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit subsequently heard Duenas-Alvarez\xe2\x80\x99s petition for review and summarily remanded the case to the agency for further proceedings in light of Penuliar. 176 Fed. Appx. 820 (2006). We granted the Government\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari in order to consider the legal validity of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s holding set forth in Penuliar and applied here, namely the holding that \xe2\x80\x9caiding and abetting\xe2\x80\x9d a theft is not itself a crime that falls within the generic definition of theft. We conclude that the Ninth Circuit erred.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit, like other Circuits and the BIA, accepted as a generic definition of theft, the \xe2\x80\x9ctaking of property or an exercise of control over property without consent with the criminal intent to deprive the owner of rights and benefits of ownership, even if such deprivation is less than total or permanent.\xe2\x80\x9d Penuliar v. Gonzales, 435 F.\xc2\xa03d 961, 969 (2006) (internal quotation marks omitted). See Abimbola, 378 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 176 (analyzing the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s definition and citing cases from three other Circuits, including the Ninth Circuit, approving that definition). The question before us is whether one who aids or abets a theft falls, like a principal, within the scope of this generic definition. We conclude that he does.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The common law divided participants in a felony into four basic categories: (1) first-degree principals, those who actually committed the crime in question; (2) second-degree principals, aiders and abettors present at the scene of the crime; (3) accessories before the fact, aiders and abettors who helped the principal before the basic criminal event took place; and (4) accessories after the fact, persons who helped the principal after the basic criminal event took place. See Standefer v. United States, 447 U. S. 10, 15 (1980). In the course of the 20th century, however, American jurisdictions eliminated the distinction among the first three categories. Id., at 16\xe2\x80\x9319; Nye & Nissen v. United States, 336 U. S. 613, 618 (1949).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, every jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x94all States and the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x94has \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly abrogated the distinction\xe2\x80\x9d among principals and aiders and abettors who fall into the second and third categories. 2 W. LaFave, Substantive Criminal Law \xc2\xa713.1(e), p.\xc2\xa0333 (2d ed. 2003) (LaFave). The Solicitor General has presented us with a comprehensive account of the law of all States and federal jurisdictions as well. And we have verified that these jurisdictions treat similarly principals and aiders and abettors who fall into the second or third common-law category. See Appendix A, infra. Since criminal law now uniformly treats those who fall into the first three categories alike, \xe2\x80\x9cthe generic sense in which\xe2\x80\x9d the term \xe2\x80\x9ctheft\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cis now used in the criminal codes of most States,\xe2\x80\x9d Taylor, 495 U. S., at 598, covers such \xe2\x80\x9caiders and abettors\xe2\x80\x9d as well as principals. And the criminal activities of these aiders and abettors of a generic theft must themselves fall within the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x9ctheft\xe2\x80\x9d in the federal statute.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Duenas-Alvarez does not defend the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s position. He agrees with the Government that generically speaking the law treats aiders and abettors during and before the crime the same way it treats principals; and that the immigration statute must then treat them similarly as well. Instead, Duenas-Alvarez argues that the California Vehicle Code provision in other ways reaches beyond generic theft to cover certain nongeneric crimes.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Duenas-Alvarez points out that California defines \xe2\x80\x9caiding and abetting\xe2\x80\x9d such that an aider and abettor is criminally responsible not only for the crime he intends, but also for any crime that \xe2\x80\x9cnaturally and probably\xe2\x80\x9d results from his intended crime. People v. Durham, 70 Cal. 2d 171, 181, 449 P.\xc2\xa02d 198, 204 (1969) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98aider and abettor \xe2\x80\xa6 liable for the natural and reasonable or probable consequences of any act that he knowingly aided or encouraged\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting People v. Villa, 156 Cal. App. 2d 128, 134 (1957); emphasis deleted). This fact alone does not show that the statute covers a nongeneric theft crime, for relatively few jurisdictions (only 10 in Duenas-Alvarez\xe2\x80\x99s own view) have expressly rejected the \xe2\x80\x9cnatural and probable consequences\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine. See Brief for Respondent 21\xe2\x80\x9322; Appendix B, infra. Moreover, many States and the Federal Government apply some form or variation of that doctrine, or permit jury inferences of intent in circumstances similar to those in which California has applied the doctrine, as explained below. See Appendix C, infra. To succeed, Duenas-Alvarez must show something special about California\xe2\x80\x99s version of the doctrine\xe2\x80\x94for example, that California in applying it criminalizes conduct that most other States would not consider \xe2\x80\x9ctheft.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Duenas-Alvarez attempts to make just such a showing. In particular, he says that California\xe2\x80\x99s doctrine, unlike that of most other States, makes a defendant criminally liable for conduct that the defendant did not intend, not even as a known or almost certain byproduct of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s intentional acts. See 1 LaFave \xc2\xa75.2(a), at 341 (person intends that which he knows \xe2\x80\x9cis practically certain to follow from his conduct\xe2\x80\x9d). At oral argument, Duenas-Alvarez\xe2\x80\x99s counsel suggested that California\xe2\x80\x99s doctrine, for example, might hold an individual who wrongly bought liquor for an underage drinker criminally responsible for that young drinker\xe2\x80\x99s later (unforeseen) reckless driving. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 44. And Duenas-Alvarez refers to several California cases in order to prove his point. See Brief for Respondent 19.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have reviewed those cases, however, and we cannot agree that they show that California\xe2\x80\x99s law is somehow special. In the first case, People v. Nguyen, 21 Cal. App. 4th 518, 26 Cal. Rptr. 2d 323 (1993), the Third Appellate District in California upheld the jury conviction of individuals who had aided several robberies at houses of prostitution, for aiding and abetting a sexual assault used by one of the individuals to convince a proprietor, by frightening her, to give up property. Id., at 528, 533\xe2\x80\x93534, 26 Cal. Rptr. 2d, at 329, 333. The court, in upholding the verdict, wrote that \xe2\x80\x9cknowledge of another\xe2\x80\x99s criminal purpose is not sufficient for aiding and abetting; the defendant must also share that purpose or intend to commit, encourage, or facilitate the commission of the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 530, 26 Cal. Rptr. 2d, at 330 (emphasis added). The court added that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hile the defendants participated in the criminal endeavor the foreseeability of sexual assault went from possible or likely to certain, yet defendants continued to lend their aid and assistance to the endeavor.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 534, 26 Cal. Rptr. 2d, at 333 (emphasis added). The court said that the jury could find that the defendants\xe2\x80\x99

\n

\xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing participation in the criminal endeavor aided the perpetrators by providing the control and security they needed to tarry long enough to commit the sexual offense, by helping to convince the victim that resistance would be useless, and by dissuading the victim\xe2\x80\x99s employee from any notion she may have formed of going to the victim\xe2\x80\x99s assistance.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nAnd the court concluded that:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cUnder these circumstances it will not do for defendants to assert that they were concerned only with robbery and bear no responsibility for the sexual assault.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 533\xe2\x80\x93534, 26 Cal. Rptr. 2d, at 333.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0People v. Simpson, 66 Cal. App. 2d 319 (1944), affirmed a kidnaping and robbery conviction on an aiding and abetting theory. Id., at 322. Although the defendant argued to the appeals court that she and her compatriots had not planned to kidnap the robbery victim, the record showed that she had brought the gun used to intimidate the victim while he was tied up and placed in a car, in which she and her corobbers rode with the victim to another location while they robbed him. Id., at 322\xe2\x80\x93323. As in Nguyen, the Court, noting that kidnaping was the means by which the robbery was committed, found that the defendant had the requisite \xe2\x80\x9cmotive,\xe2\x80\x9d or intent to commit the kidnaping. 66 Cal. App. 2d, at 326.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0People v. Montes, 74 Cal. App. 4th 1050, 88 Cal. Rptr. 2d 482 (1999), affirmed an attempted murder conviction where a confederate of the defendant shot the victim after the defendant committed armed assault, simple assault, and breach of the peace. Id., at 1055, 88 Cal. Rptr. 2d, at 485. The court found that the conduct for which the appellant was charged with assault and breach of the peace was a \xe2\x80\x9cconfrontation \xe2\x80\xa6 punctuated by threats and weaponry\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cin the context of an ongoing rivalry between \xe2\x80\xa6 two gangs [that] acted violently toward each other.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The court reasoned that the escalating violence, resulting in someone being shot, was a foreseeable consequence of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s intended act of participating in the gang confrontation. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the court in Montes applied a more expansive concept of \xe2\x80\x9cmotive\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cintent\xe2\x80\x9d than did the courts in Nguyen and Simpson, we cannot say that those concepts as used in any of these cases extend significantly beyond the concept as set forth in the cases of other States. See Appendix C, infra.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, in our view, to find that a state statute creates a crime outside the generic definition of a listed crime in a federal statute requires more than the application of legal imagination to a state statute\xe2\x80\x99s language. It requires a realistic probability, not a theoretical possibility, that the State would apply its statute to conduct that falls outside the generic definition of a crime. To show that realistic possibility, an offender, of course, may show that the statute was so applied in his own case. But he must at least point to his own case or other cases in which the state courts in fact did apply the statute in the special (nongeneric) manner for which he argues.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because Duenas-Alvarez makes no such showing here, we cannot find that California\xe2\x80\x99s statute, through the California courts\xe2\x80\x99 application of a \xe2\x80\x9cnatural and probable consequences\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine, creates a subspecies of the Vehicle Code section crime that falls outside the generic definition of \xe2\x80\x9ctheft.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Duenas-Alvarez makes two additional claims. First, he argues that \xc2\xa710851 holds liable accessories after the fact; and to prove that an individual was an accessory after the fact does not require the government to show that the individual committed a theft. Second, Duenas-Alvarez argues that \xc2\xa710851 applies, not only to auto theft, but also to joyriding, which he argues involves so limited a deprivation of the use of a car that it falls outside the generic \xe2\x80\x9ctheft\xe2\x80\x9d definition. See Van Vechten v. American Eagle Fire Ins. Co., 239 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 303, 146 N.\xc2\xa0E. 432 (1925) (Cardozo, J.) (citing cases for proposition that a very temporary use is not theft).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We shall not consider these claims. The question that we agreed to decide is whether \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98theft offense\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in the federal statute \xe2\x80\x9cincludes aiding and abetting the commission of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d See Brief for Petitioner I. Context makes clear that \xe2\x80\x9caiding and abetting\xe2\x80\x9d in this question referred to the use of that term in Penuliar, i.e., to the second and third common-law categories (principal in the second degree, accessory before the fact), see supra, at 5, see also Brief for Petitioner 13, and not to \xe2\x80\x9caccessory after the fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus neither this claim nor the \xe2\x80\x9cjoyriding\xe2\x80\x9d claim falls within the terms of the question presented. Regardless, the lower court did not consider the claims, and we decline to reach them in the first instance. See National Collegiate Athletic Assn. v. Smith, 525 U. S. 459, 469\xe2\x80\x93470 (1999); Roberts v. Galen of Va., Inc., 525 U. S. 249, 253\xe2\x80\x93254 (1999) (per curiam); United States v. Bestfoods, 524 U. S. 51, 72\xe2\x80\x9373 (1998).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons we vacate the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
