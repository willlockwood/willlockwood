SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

MICHAEL WEARRY v. BURL CAIN, WARDEN

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the district court of louisiana, livingston parish

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x9310008.\xe2\x80\x83Decided March 7, 2016

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

Michael Wearry is on Louisiana\xe2\x80\x99s death row. Urging that the prosecution failed to disclose evidence supporting his innocence and that his counsel provided ineffective assistance at trial, Wearry unsuccessfully sought postconviction relief in state court. Contrary to the state postconviction court, we conclude that the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s failure to disclose material evidence violated Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s due process rights. We reverse the state postconviction court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment on that account, and therefore do not reach Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Sometime between 8:20 and 9:30 on the evening of April 4, 1998, Eric Walber was brutally murdered. Nearly two years after the murder, Sam Scott, at the time incarcerated, contacted authorities and implicated Michael Wearry. Scott initially reported that he had been friends withthe victim; that he was at work the night of the murder; that the victim had come looking for him but had instead run into Wearry and four others; and that Wearry and the others had later confessed to shooting and driving over the victim before leaving his body on Blahut Road. In fact, the victim had not been shot, and his body had been found on Crisp Road.

\n

Scott changed his account of the crime over the course of four later statements, each of which differed from the others in material ways. By the time Scott testified as the State\xe2\x80\x99s star witness at Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s trial, his story bore little resemblance to his original account. According to the version Scott told the jury, he had been playing dice with Wearry and others when the victim drove past. Wearry, who had been losing, decided to rob the victim. After Wearry and an acquaintance, Randy Hutchinson, stopped the victim\xe2\x80\x99s car, Hutchinson shoved the victim into the cargo area. Five men, including Scott, Hutchinson, and Wearry, proceeded to drive around, at one point encountering Eric Brown\xe2\x80\x94the State\xe2\x80\x99s other main witness\xe2\x80\x94and pausing intermittently to assault the victim. Finally, Scott related, Wearry and two others killed the victim by running him over. On cross-examination, Scott admitted that he had changed his account several times.

\n

Consistent with Scott\xe2\x80\x99s testimony, Brown testified that on the night of the murder he had seen Wearry and others with a man who looked like the victim. Incarcerated on unrelated charges at the time of Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s trial, Brown acknowledged that he had made a prior inconsistent statement to the police, but had recanted and agreed to testify against Wearry, not for any prosecutorial favor, but solely because his sister knew the victim\xe2\x80\x99s sister. The State commented during its opening argument that Brown \xe2\x80\x9cis doing 15 years on a drug charge right now, [but] hasn\xe2\x80\x99t asked for a thing.\xe2\x80\x9d 7 Record 1723 (Tr., Mar. 2, 2002). During closing argument, the State reiterated that Brown \xe2\x80\x9chas no deal on the table\xe2\x80\x9d and was testifying because the victim\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfamily deserves to know.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. 19.

\n

Although the State presented no physical evidence at trial, it did offer additional circumstantial evidence linking Wearry to the victim. One witness testified that he saw Wearry in the victim\xe2\x80\x99s car on the night of the murder and, later, holding the victim\xe2\x80\x99s class ring. Another witness said he saw Wearry throwing away the victim\xe2\x80\x99s cologne. In some respects, however, these witnesses contradicted Scott\xe2\x80\x99s account. For example, the witness who reported seeing Wearry in the victim\xe2\x80\x99s car did not place Scott in the car.

\n

Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s defense at trial rested on an alibi. He claimed that, at the time of the murder, he had been at a wedding reception in Baton Rouge, 40 miles away. Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s girlfriend, her sister, and her aunt corroborated Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s account. In closing argument, the State stressed that all three witnesses had personal relationships with Wearry. The State also presented two rebuttal witnesses: the bride at the wedding, who reported that the reception had ended by 8:30 or 9:00 (potentially leaving sufficient time for Wearry to have committed the crime); and three jail employees, who testified that they had overheard Wearry say that he was a bystander when the crime occurred.

\n

The jury convicted Wearry of capital murder and sentenced him to death. His conviction and sentence were affirmed on direct appeal.[1]

\n

B

\n

After Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s conviction became final, it emerged that the prosecution had withheld relevant information that could have advanced Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s plea. Wearry argued during state postconviction proceedings that three categories of belatedly revealed information would have undermined the prosecution and materially aided Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s defense at trial.

\n

First, previously undisclosed police records showed that two of Scott\xe2\x80\x99s fellow inmates had made statements that cast doubt on Scott\xe2\x80\x99s credibility. One inmate had reported hearing Scott say that he wanted to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98make sure [Wearry] gets the needle cause he jacked over me.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 22 (quoting inmate affidavit).[2] The other inmate had told investigators\xe2\x80\x94at a meeting Scott orchestrated\xe2\x80\x94that he had witnessed the murder, but this inmate recanted the next day. \xe2\x80\x9cScott had told him what to say,\xe2\x80\x9d he explained, and had suggested that lying about having witnessed the murder \xe2\x80\x9cwould help him get out of jail.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. Exh. 13 in No. 01\xe2\x80\x93FELN\xe2\x80\x93015992, pp. 104, 107. See also Pet. for Cert. 22 (quoting police notes).

\n

Second, the State had failed to disclose that, contrary to the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s assertions at trial, Brown had twice sought a deal to reduce his existing sentence in exchange for testifying against Wearry. The police had told Brown that they would \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98talk to the D.\xc2\xa0A. if he told the truth.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. 19 (quoting police notes).

\n

Third, the prosecution had failed to turn over medical records on Randy Hutchinson. According to Scott, on the night of the murder, Hutchinson had run into the street to flag down the victim, pulled the victim out of his car, shoved him into the cargo space, and crawled into the cargo space himself. But Hutchinson\xe2\x80\x99s medical records revealed that, nine days before the murder, Hutchinson had undergone knee surgery to repair a ruptured patellar tendon. Id., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, 15\xe2\x80\x9316, 32.[3] An expert witness, Dr. Paul Dworak, testified at the state collateral-review hearing that Hutchinson\xe2\x80\x99s surgically repaired knee could not have withstood running, bending, or lifting substantial weight. The State presented an expert witness who disagreed with Dr. Dworak\xe2\x80\x99s appraisal of Hutchinson\xe2\x80\x99s physical fitness.

\n

During state postconviction proceedings, Wearry also maintained that his trial attorney had failed to uncover exonerating evidence. Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s trial attorney admitted at the state collateral-review hearing that he had conducted no independent investigation into Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s innocence and had relied solely on evidence the State and Wearry had provided.[4] For example, despite Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s alibi, his attorney undertook no effort to locate independent witnesses from among the dozens of guests who had attended the wedding reception.

\n

Counsel representing Wearry on collateral review conducted an independent investigation. This investigation revealed many witnesses lacking any personal relationship with Wearry who would have been willing to corroborate his alibi had they been called at trial. Collateral-review counsel\xe2\x80\x99s investigation also revealed that Scott\xe2\x80\x99s brother and sister-in-law would have been willing to tes-tify at trial, as they did at the collateral-review hearing, that Scott was with them, mostly at a strawberry festival, until around 11:00 on the night of the murder.

\n

Based on this new evidence, Wearry alleged violations of his due process rights under Brady v. Maryland,\n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83 (1963)\n, and of his\n \nSixth Amendment right to effective assistance of counsel. Acknowledging that the State \xe2\x80\x9cprobably ought to have\xe2\x80\x9d disclosed the withheld evidence, App. to Pet. for Cert. B\xe2\x80\x936, and that Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s counsel provided \xe2\x80\x9cperhaps not the best defense that could have been rendered,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at B\xe2\x80\x935, the postconviction court denied relief. Even if Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights were violated, the court concluded, he had not shown prejudice. Id., at B\xe2\x80\x935, B\xe2\x80\x937. In turn, the Louisiana Supreme Court also denied relief. Id., at A\xe2\x80\x931. Chief Justice Johnson would have granted Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s petition on the ground that he received ineffective assistance of counsel. Id., at A\xe2\x80\x932.[5]

\n

II

\n

Because we conclude that the Louisiana courts\xe2\x80\x99 denial of Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s Brady claim runs up against settled constitutional principles, and because a new trial is required as a result, we need not and do not consider the merits of his ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he suppression by the prosecution of evidence favorable to an accused upon request violates due process where the evidence is material either to guilt or to punishment, irrespective of the good faith or bad faith of the prosecution.\xe2\x80\x9d Brady, supra, at 87. See also Giglio v. United States,\n\n405 U.\xc2\xa0S. 150\n\xe2\x80\x93154 (1972) (clarifying that the rule stated in Brady applies to evidence undermining witness credibility). Evidence qualifies as material when there is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98any reasonable likelihood\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d it could have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98affected the judgment of the jury.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Giglio, supra, at 154 (quoting Napue v. Illinois,\n\n360 U.\xc2\xa0S. 264,\n271 (1959)\n). To prevail on his Brady claim, Wearry need not show that he \xe2\x80\x9cmore likely than not\xe2\x80\x9d would have been acquitted had the new evidence been admitted. Smith v. Cain,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 73\n, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2012) (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x933) (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted). He must show only that the new evidence is sufficient to \xe2\x80\x9cundermine confidence\xe2\x80\x9d in the verdict. Ibid.[6]

\n

Beyond doubt, the newly revealed evidence suffices to undermine confidence in Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s conviction. The State\xe2\x80\x99s trial evidence resembles a house of cards, built on the jury crediting Scott\xe2\x80\x99s account rather than Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s alibi. See United States v. Agurs,\n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 97,\n113 (1976)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f the verdict is already of questionable validity, additional evidence of relatively minor importance might be sufficient to create a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d). The dissent asserts that, apart from the testimony of Scott and Brown, there was independent evidence pointing to Wearry as the murderer. See post, at 5 (opinion of Alito, J.). But all of the evidence the dissent cites suggests, at most, that someone in Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s group of friends may have committed the crime, and that Wearry may have been involved in events related to the murder after it occurred. Perhaps, on the basis of this evidence, Louisiana might have charged Wearry as an accessory after the fact. La. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa714:25 (West 2007) (providing a maximum prison term of five years for accessories after the fact). But Louisiana instead charged Wearry with capital murder, and the only evidence directly tying him to that crime was Scott\xe2\x80\x99s dubious testimony, corroborated by the similarly suspect testimony of Brown.[7]

\n

As the dissent recognizes, \xe2\x80\x9cScott did not have an exemplary record of veracity.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 3. Scott\xe2\x80\x99s credibility, already impugned by his many inconsistent stories, would have been further diminished had the jury learned that Hutchinson may have been physically incapable of performing the role Scott ascribed to him, that Scott had coached another inmate to lie about the murder and thereby enhance his chances to get out of jail, or that Scott may have implicated Wearry to settle a personal score.[8] Moreover, any juror who found Scott more credible in light of Brown\xe2\x80\x99s testimony might have thought differently had she learned that Brown may have been motivated to come forward not by his sister\xe2\x80\x99s relationship with the victim\xe2\x80\x99s sister\xe2\x80\x94as the prosecution had insisted in its closing argument\xe2\x80\x94but by the possibility of a reduced sentence on an existing conviction. See Napue, supra, at 270 (even though the State had made no binding promises, a witness\xe2\x80\x99 attempt to obtain a deal before testifying was material because the jury \xe2\x80\x9cmight well have concluded that [the witness] had fabricated testimony in order to curry the [prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s] favor\xe2\x80\x9d). Even if the jury\xe2\x80\x94armed with all of this new evidence\xe2\x80\x94could have voted to convict Wearry, we have \xe2\x80\x9cno confidence that it would have done so.\xe2\x80\x9d Smith, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 3).

\n

Reaching the opposite conclusion, the state postconviction court improperly evaluated the materiality of each piece of evidence in isolation rather than cumulatively, see Kyles v. Whitley,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 419,\n441 (1995)\n (requiring a \xe2\x80\x9ccumulative evaluation\xe2\x80\x9d of the materiality of wrongfully withheld evidence), emphasized reasons a juror might disregard new evidence while ignoring reasons she might not, cf. Porter v. McCollum,\n\n558 U.\xc2\xa0S. 30,\n43 (2009)\n (per curiam) (\xe2\x80\x9cit was not reasonable to discount entirely the effect that [a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s expert\xe2\x80\x99s] testimony might have had on the jury\xe2\x80\x9d just because the State\xe2\x80\x99s expert provided contrary testimony), and failed even to mention the statements of the two inmates impeaching Scott.

\n

III

\n

In addition to defending the judgment of the Louisiana courts, the dissent criticizes the Court for deciding this \xe2\x80\x9cintensely factual question .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. without full briefing and argument.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6. But the Court has not shied away from summarily deciding fact-intensive cases where, as here, lower courts have egregiously misapplied settled law. See, e.g., Mullenix v. Luna, ante, at ___ (percuriam); Stanton v. Sims, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013) (per curiam); Parker v. Matthews, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012) (per curiam); Coleman v. Johnson, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012) (per curiam); Wetzel v. Lambert, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012) (per curiam); Ryburn v. Huff, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012) (per curiam); Sears v. Upton,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 945 (2010)\n (per curiam); Porter v. McCollum, supra.

\n

Because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he petition does not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fall into a category in which the Court has previously evinced an inclination to police factbound errors,\xe2\x80\x9d the dissent continues, \xe2\x80\x9cnothing warned the State,\xe2\x80\x9d when it was drafting its brief in opposition, that the Court might summarily reverse Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s conviction. Post, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. Contrary to the dissent, however, summarily deciding a capital case, when circumstances so warrant, is hardly unprecedented. See Sears, supra, at 951\xe2\x80\x93952 (vacating a state postconviction court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of relief on a penalty-phase ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim); Porter, supra, at 38\xe2\x80\x9340 (attorney provided ineffective assistance of counsel by conducting a constitutionally inadequate investigation into mitigating evidence). Perhaps anticipating the possibility of summary reversal, the State devoted the bulk of its 30-page brief in opposition to a point-by-point rebuttal of Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s claims. Given this brief, as well as the State\xe2\x80\x99s lower court filings similarly concentrating on evidence supporting its position, the chances that further briefing or argument would change the outcome are vanishingly slim.

\n

The dissent also inveighs against the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdepart[ure] from our usual procedures .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [to] decide petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s fact-intensive Brady claim at this stage .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [rather than] allow[ing] petitioner to raise that claim in a federal habeas proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 7. This Court, of course, has jurisdiction over the final judgments of state postconviction courts, see\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71257(a), and exercises that jurisdiction in appropriate circumstances. Earlier this Term, for instance, we heard argument in Foster v. Chatman, No. 14\xe2\x80\x938349, which involves the Georgia courts\xe2\x80\x99 denial of postconviction relief to a capital defendant raising a claim under Batson v. Kentucky,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 79 (1986)\n. See also Smith, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 2) (reversing a state postconviction court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of relief on a Brady claim); Sears, supra, at 946. Reviewing the Louisiana courts\xe2\x80\x99 denial of postconviction relief is thus hardly the bold departure the dissent paints it to be. The alternative to granting review, after all, is forcing Wearry to endure yet more time on Louisiana\xe2\x80\x99s death row in service of a conviction that is constitutionally flawed.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Because Wearry\xe2\x80\x99s due process rights were violated, we grant his petition for a writ of certiorari and motion for leave to proceed in forma pauperis, reverse the judgment of the Louisiana postconviction court, and remand for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

MICHAEL WEARRY v. BURL CAIN, WARDEN

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the district court of louisiana, livingston parish

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x9310008.\xe2\x80\x83Decided March 7, 2016

\n

\n

Justice Alito, with whom Justice Thomas joins, dissenting.

\n

Without briefing or argument, the Court reverses a 14-year-old murder conviction on the ground that the prosecution violated Brady v. Maryland,\n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83 (1963)\n, by failing to turn over certain information that tended to exculpate petitioner. There is no question in my mind that the prosecution should have disclosed this information, but whether the information was sufficient to warrant reversing petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction is another matter. The failure to turn over exculpatory information violates due process only \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98if there is a reasonable probability that, had the evidence been disclosed to the defense, the result of the proceeding would have been different.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Kyles v. Whitley,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 419\n\xe2\x80\x93434 (1995) (quoting United States v. Bagley,\n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 667,\n682 (1985)\n (opinion of Blackmun, J.)).

\n

The Court argues that the information in question here could have affected the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict and that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction must therefore be reversed. The Court ably makes the case for reversal, but there is a reasonable contrary argument that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction should stand because the undisclosed information would not have affected the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict. I will briefly discuss the main points made in the per curiam, not for the purpose of showing that they are necessarily wrong, but to show that the Brady issue is not open and shut. For good reason, we generally do not decide cases without allowing the parties to file briefs and present argument. Questions that seem quite simple at first glance sometimes look very different after both sides are given a chance to make their case. Of course, this process means extra work for the Court. But it leads to better results, and it gives the losing side the satisfaction of knowing that at least its arguments have been fully heard. There is no justification for departing from our usual procedures in this case.

\n

I

\n

The first item of information discussed by the Court is a police report that recounts statements made about Sam Scott, a key witness for the prosecution, by a fellow inmate. According to this report, Scott told the inmate: \xe2\x80\x9cI\xe2\x80\x99m gonna make sure Mike [i.e., petitioner] gets the needle cause he jacked over me.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. Exh. 13 in No. 01\xe2\x80\x93FELN\xe2\x80\x93015992, p. 103. Scott, who had been serving a sentence on unrelated drug charges, reportedly told the inmate that he had been expecting to be released but that he \xe2\x80\x9cstill [had not] gone home because of this,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s prosecution. Id., at 102. As stated in the report, Scott said that he was now facing the possibility of a 10-year sentence, apparently for his admitted role in the events surrounding the murder. The report did not provide any further explanation for Scott\xe2\x80\x99s alleged statement that petitioner had \xe2\x80\x9cjacked [him] over.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The Court reads the report to suggest that Scott implicated petitioner in the murder \xe2\x80\x9cto settle a personal score.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. But if petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel had actually attempted to use this evidence at trial, the net effect might well have been harmful, not helpful, to the defense. The undisclosed police report on which the Court relies may be read to mean that Scott blamed petitioner for putting him in the position of having to admit his own role in the events surrounding the murder and thereby expose himself to the 10-year sentence and lose an opportunity to secure early release from prison on the drug charges. If defense counsel had attempted to impeach Scott with this police report, the effort could have backfired by allowing the prosecution to return the jury\xe2\x80\x99s focus to a point the State emphasized often during trial, namely, that Scott\xe2\x80\x99s accusations were credible precisely because Scott had no motive to tell a story that was contrary to his own interests. See, e.g., 10 Record 2307 (Tr., Mar. 5, 2002) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf [Scott] keeps his mouth shut, he is out in less than five more months.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [But] [i]nstead of getting out in 180 days, he is going to be doing more time\xe2\x80\x9d).[1]

\n

The Court next turns to an allegation that Scott had coached another prisoner to make up lies against peti-tioner. This prisoner never testified at trial, and there is a basis for arguing that this information would not have made a difference to the jury, which was well aware that Scott did not have an exemplary record of veracity. Scott himself admitted to fabricating information that he told the police during their investigations. In addition, a witness who did testify against petitioner at trial also accused Scott of asking him to lie, although admittedly this witness later denied making this accusation. Given that the jury convicted even with these quite serious strikes against Scott\xe2\x80\x99s credibility, there is reason to question whether the jury would have seriously considered a different verdict because of an accusation from someone who never took the stand.

\n

Third, the Court observes that the prosecution failed to turn over evidence that another witness, Eric Brown, had asked for favorable treatment from the district attorney in exchange for testifying against petitioner. It is true\xe2\x80\x94and troubling\xe2\x80\x94that the prosecutor claimed in her opening statement that Brown had not sought favorable treatment. But even so, it is far from clear that disclosing the contradictory information had real potential to affect the trial\xe2\x80\x99s outcome. For one thing, there is no evidence that Brown (unlike Scott) actually received any deal, despite defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s efforts in cross-examination to establish that Brown\xe2\x80\x99s testimony might have earned him leniency from the State. Moreover, Brown admitted during the exchange that he had manipulated his initial story to the police to avoid implicating himself in criminal activity. We know, then, that the jury harbored no illusions about the purity of Brown\xe2\x80\x99s motives, notwithstanding the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s opening misstatement.

\n

Finally, the Court says that the medical records of Randy Hutchinson would have cast doubt on Scott\xe2\x80\x99s trial testimony that Hutchinson repeatedly dragged the victim into and out of a car and bludgeoned him with a stick. The records reveal that Hutchinson had knee surgery to repair his patellar tendon just nine days before the murder. But one of the State\xe2\x80\x99s witnesses testified at trial that he had seen records showing that Hutchinson had had surgery on his knee \xe2\x80\x9cabout nine days before the homicide happened.\xe2\x80\x9d 10 Record 2261 (Tr., Mar. 5, 2002); see also id., at 2263. The jury thus knew the most salient fact revealed by these records\xe2\x80\x94that Scott had attributed significant strength and mobility to a man nine days removed from knee surgery.[2] Given that these particular details about Hutchinson\xe2\x80\x99s actions were a relatively minor part of Scott\xe2\x80\x99s account of the crime and the State\xe2\x80\x99s case against petitioner, the significance of the undisclosed medical records is subject to reasonable dispute.

\n

While the Court highlights the exculpatory quality of the withheld information, the Court downplays the considerable evidence of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. Aside from Scott\xe2\x80\x99s and Brown\xe2\x80\x99s testimony, three witnesses told the jury that they saw petitioner and others driving around shortly after the murder in the victim\xe2\x80\x99s red car, which according to one of these witnesses had blood on its exterior. Petitioner offered to sell an Albany High School class ring to one of these witnesses and a set of new speakers to an-other. The third witness said he saw petitioner throw away a bottle of Tommy Hilfiger cologne. Meanwhile, the victim\xe2\x80\x99s mother testified that her son wore an Albany High class ring that was not recovered with his body, had received speakers as a gift shortly before his murder, and had a bottle of Tommy Hilfiger cologne with him on the night when he was killed. In addition, three jailers testified that petitioner called his father after his eventual arrest and stated that \xe2\x80\x9che didn\xe2\x80\x99t know what he was doing in jail because he didn\xe2\x80\x99t do anything [and] was just an innocent bystander.\xe2\x80\x9d 9 Record 2120 (Tr., Mar. 4, 2002); see also id., at 2124, 2126.

\n

In short, this is far from a case in which the withheld information would have allowed the defense to undermine \xe2\x80\x9cthe only evidence linking [petitioner] to the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Smith v. Cain,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 73\n, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 3).

\n

II

\n

Whether disclosing the information at issue realistically could have changed the trial\xe2\x80\x99s outcome is indisputably an intensely factual question. Under Brady, we must evaluate the significance of the withheld information in light of all the proof at petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s trial. See Kyles, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 435 (Brady is violated when the withheld \xe2\x80\x9cevidence could reasonably be taken to put the whole case in such a different light as to undermine confidence in the verdict\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); United States v. Agurs,\n\n427 U.\xc2\xa0S. 97,\n112 (1976)\n (Brady materiality \xe2\x80\x9cmust be evaluated in the context of the entire record\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). It is unusual and, in my judgment, unreasonable for us to decide such a question without full briefing and argument.

\n

At this stage, all that we have from the State is its brief in opposition to the petition for certiorari. And the State had ample reason to believe when it submitted that brief that the question on the table was whether the Court should hear the case, not whether petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction should be reversed. The State undoubtedly knew that we generally deny certiorari on factbound questions that do not implicate any disputed legal issue. See, e.g., this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 10; S. Shapiro, K. Geller, T. Bishop, E. Hartnett, & D. Himmelfarb, Supreme Court Practice \xc2\xa75.12(c)(3), p. 352 (10th ed. 2013). Nothing warned the State that this petition was likely to produce an exception to that general rule. The petition does not, for instance, fall into a category in which the Court has previously evinced an inclination to police factbound errors. Cf. Cash v. Maxwell, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ____, ____ (2012) (Scalia, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (slip op., at 8) (listing cases from one such category).

\n

To the contrary, we have previously told litigants that petitions like the one here, challenging a state court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of postconviction relief, are particularly unlikely to be granted: We \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98rarely gran[t] review at this stage\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of litigation, even when a petition raises \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98arguably meritorious federal constitutional claims,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d because we prefer that the claims be reviewed first by a district court and court of appeals in a federal habeas proceeding. Lawrence v. Florida,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 327,\n335 (2007)\n (quoting Kyles v. Whitley,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 931,\n932 (1990)\n (Stevens, J., concurring in denial of stay of execution)).[3]

\n

Why, then, has the Court decided to depart from our usual procedures and decide petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s fact-intensive Brady claim at this stage? Why not allow petitioner to raise that claim in a federal habeas proceeding? If the case took that course, it would not reach us until a district court and a court of appeals had studied the record and evaluated the likely impact of the information in question.

\n

One consequence of waiting until the claim was raised in a federal habeas proceeding is that our review would then be governed by the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA). Under AEDPA, relief could be granted only if it could be said that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s rejection of the claim represented an \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable application\xe2\x80\x9d of Brady.\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). By intervening now before AEDPA comes into play, the Court avoids the application of that standard and is able to exercise plenary review. But if the Brady claim is as open-and-shut as the Court maintains, AEDPA would not present an obstacle to the granting of habeas relief. On the other hand, if reasonable jurists could disagree about the application of Brady to the facts of this case, there is no good reason to dispose of this case summarily. The State should be given the opportunity to make its full case.

\n

In my view, therefore, summary reversal is highly inappropriate. The Court is anxious to vacate petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction before the State has the opportunity to make its case. But if we are going to intervene at this stage, we should grant the petition and hear the case on the merits. There is room on our docket to give this case the careful consideration it deserves.
