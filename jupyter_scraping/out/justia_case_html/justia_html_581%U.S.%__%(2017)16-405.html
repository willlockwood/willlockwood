SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

\n

BNSF RAILWAY CO. v. TYRRELL, special administrator for the ESTATE OF TYRRELL, DECEASED, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of montana

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x93405.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 25, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 30, 2017

\n

The Federal Employers\xe2\x80\x99 Liability Act (FELA),\n\n45 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa751 et\xc2\xa0seq., makes railroads liable in money damages to their employees for on-the-job injuries. Respondent Robert Nelson, a North Dakota resident, brought a FELA suit against petitioner BNSF Railway Company (BNSF) in a Montana state court, alleging that he had sustained injuries while working for BNSF. Respondent Kelli Tyrrell, appointed in South Dakota as the administrator of her husband Brent Tyrrell\xe2\x80\x99s estate, also sued BNSF under FELA in a Montana state court, alleging that Brent had developed a fatal cancer from his exposure to carcinogenic chemicals while working for BNSF. Neither worker was injured in Montana. Neither incorporated nor headquartered there, BNSF maintains less than 5% of its work force and about 6% of its total track mileage in the State. Contending that it is not \xe2\x80\x9cat home\xe2\x80\x9d in Montana, as required for the exercise of general personal jurisdiction under Daimler AG v. Bauman, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, BNSF moved to dismiss both suits. Its motion was granted in Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s case and denied in Tyrrell\xe2\x80\x99s. After consolidating the two cases, the Montana Supreme Court held that Montana courts could exercise general personal jurisdiction over BNSF because the railroad both \xe2\x80\x9cd[id] business\xe2\x80\x9d in the State within the meaning of\n\n45 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa756 and was \xe2\x80\x9cfound within\xe2\x80\x9d the State within the compass of Mont. Rule Civ. Proc. 4(b)(1). The due process limits articulated in Daimler, the court added, did not control because Daimler did not involve a FELA claim or a railroad defendant.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0Section 56 does not address personal jurisdiction over railroads. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Section 56\xe2\x80\x99s first relevant sentence provides that \xe2\x80\x9can action may be brought in a district court of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d in, among other places, the district \xe2\x80\x9cin which the defendant shall be doing business at the time of commencing such action.\xe2\x80\x9d This Court has comprehended that sentence as a venue prescription, not as one governing personal jurisdiction. Baltimore & Ohio R. Co. v. Kepner,\n\n314 U.\xc2\xa0S. 44\n. Congress generally uses the expression, where suit \xe2\x80\x9cmay be brought,\xe2\x80\x9d to indicate the federal districts in which venue is proper, see, e.g.,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71391(b), while it typically provides for the exercise of personal jurisdiction by authorizing service of process, see, e.g.,\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa722. Nelson and Tyrrell contend that the 1888 Judiciary Act provision that prompted \xc2\xa756\xe2\x80\x99s enactment concerned both personal jurisdiction and venue, but this Court has long read that Judiciary Act provision to concern venue only, see, e.g., Green v. Chicago, B. & Q. R. Co.,\n\n205 U.\xc2\xa0S. 530\n\xe2\x80\x93533. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The second relevant sentence of \xc2\xa756\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he jurisdiction of the courts of the United States under this chapter shall be concurrent with that of the courts of the several States\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94refers to concurrent subject-matter jurisdiction of state and federal courts over FELA actions. See Second Employers\xe2\x80\x99 Liability Cases,\n\n223 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9356. Congress added this clarification after the Connecticut Supreme Court held that Congress intended to confine FELA litigation to federal courts, and that state courts had no obligation to entertain FELA claims. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0None of the cases featured by the Montana Supreme Court in reaching its contrary conclusion resolved a question of personal jurisdiction. Pope v. Atlantic Coast Line R. Co.,\n\n345 U.\xc2\xa0S. 379\n; Miles v. Illinois Central R. Co.,\n\n315 U.\xc2\xa0S. 698\n; Kepner,\n\n314 U.\xc2\xa0S. 44\n; and Denver & Rio Grande Western R. Co. v. Terte,\n\n284 U.\xc2\xa0S. 284\n, distinguished. Moreover, all these cases, save Pope, were decided before this Court\xe2\x80\x99s transformative decision on personal jurisdiction in International Shoe Co. v. Washington,\n\n326 U.\xc2\xa0S. 310\n. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0The Montana courts\xe2\x80\x99 exercise of personal jurisdiction under Montana law does not comport with the\n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause. Only the propriety of general personal jurisdiction is at issue here because neither Nelson nor Tyrrell alleges injury from work in or related to Montana.

\n

A state court may exercise general jurisdiction over out-of-state corporations when their \xe2\x80\x9caffiliations with the State are so \xe2\x80\x98continuous and systematic\xe2\x80\x99 as to render them essentially at home in the forum State.\xe2\x80\x9d Daimler, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___. The \xe2\x80\x9cparadigm\xe2\x80\x9d forums in which a corporate defendant is \xe2\x80\x9cat home\xe2\x80\x9d are the corporation\xe2\x80\x99s place of incorporation and its principal place of business, e.g., id., at ___, but in an \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional case,\xe2\x80\x9d a corporate defendant\xe2\x80\x99s operations in another forum \xe2\x80\x9cmay be so substantial and of such a nature as to render the corporation at home in that State,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___, n.\xc2\xa019. Daimler involved no FELA claim or railroad defendant, but the due process constraint described there applies to all state-court assertions of general jurisdiction over nonresident defendants; that constraint does not vary with the type of claim asserted or business enterprise sued.

\n

Here, BNSF is not incorporated or headquartered in Montana and its activity there is not \xe2\x80\x9cso substantial and of such a nature as to render the corporation at home in that State.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

383 Mont. 417, 373 P. 3d 1, reversed and remanded.

\n

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 16\xe2\x80\x93405

BNSF RAILWAY CO., PETITIONER v. KELLI TYRRELL, special administrator for the ESTATE OF BRENT T. TYRRELL, DECEASED, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of montana

\n

[May 30, 2017]

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The two cases we decide today arise under the Federal Employers\xe2\x80\x99 Liability Act (FELA),\n\n35Stat.\n65, as amended,\n\n45 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa751 et\xc2\xa0seq., which makes railroads liable in money damages to their employees for on-the-job injuries. Both suits were pursued in Montana state courts although the injured workers did not reside in Montana, nor were they injured there. The defendant railroad, BNSF Railway Company (BNSF), although \xe2\x80\x9cdoing business\xe2\x80\x9d in Montana when the litigation commenced, was not incorporated in Montana, nor did it maintain its principal place of business in that State. To justify the exercise of personal jurisdiction over BNSF, the Montana Supreme Court relied on \xc2\xa756, which provides in relevant part:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cUnder this chapter an action may be brought in a district court of the United States, in the district of the residence of the defendant, or in which the cause of action arose, or in which the defendant shall be doing business at the time of commencing such action. The jurisdiction of the courts of the United States under this chapter shall be concurrent with that of the courts of the several States.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

We hold that \xc2\xa756 does not address personal jurisdiction over railroads. Its first relevant sentence is a venue prescription governing proper locations for FELA suits filed in federal court. The provision\xe2\x80\x99s second relevant sentence, using the term \xe2\x80\x9cconcurrent\xe2\x80\x9d jurisdiction, refers to subject-matter jurisdiction, not personal jurisdiction. It simply clarifies that the federal courts do not have exclusive subject-matter jurisdiction over FELA suits; state courts can hear them, too.

\n

Montana\xe2\x80\x99s Supreme Court, in the alternative, relied on state law, under which personal jurisdiction could be asserted over \xe2\x80\x9cpersons found within .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Montana.\xe2\x80\x9d Mont. Rule Civ. Proc. 4(b)(1) (2015). BNSF fit that bill, the court stated, because it has over 2,000 miles of railroad track and employs more than 2,000 workers in Montana. Our precedent, however, explains that the\n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause does not permit a State to hale an out-of-state corporation before its courts when the corporation is not \xe2\x80\x9cat home\xe2\x80\x9d in the State and theepisode-in-suit occurred elsewhere. Daimler AG v. Bauman, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 8) (internal quotation marks omitted). We therefore reverse the judgment of the Montana Supreme Court.

\n

I

\n

In March 2011, respondent Robert Nelson, a North Dakota resident, brought a FELA suit against BNSF in a Montana state court to recover damages for knee injuries Nelson allegedly sustained while working for BNSF as a fuel-truck driver. 383 Mont. 417, 419, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d 1, 3 (2016). In May 2014, respondent Kelli Tyrrell, appointed in South Dakota as the administrator of her husband Brent Tyrrell\xe2\x80\x99s estate, similarly sued BNSF under FELA in a Montana state court. Id., at 419\xe2\x80\x93420, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 3. Brent Tyrrell, his widow alleged, had developed a fatal kidney cancer from his exposure to carcinogenic chemicals while working for BNSF. Id., at 420, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 3. Neither plaintiff alleged injuries arising from or related to work performed in Montana; indeed, neither Nelson nor Brent Tyrrell appears ever to have worked for BNSF in Montana. Id., at 419\xe2\x80\x93420, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 3.

\n

BNSF is incorporated in Delaware and has its principal place of business in Texas. Id., at 419, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 3. It operates railroad lines in 28 States. No. DV 14\xe2\x80\x93699 (13th Jud. Dist., Yellowstone Cty., Mont., Oct. 7, 2014), App. to Pet. for Cert. 63a. BNSF has 2,061 miles of railroad track in Montana (about 6% of its total track mileage of 32,500), employs some 2,100 workers there (less than 5% of its total work force of 43,000), generates less than 10% of its total revenue in the State, and maintains only one of its 24 automotive facilities in Montana (4%). Ibid. Contending that it is not \xe2\x80\x9cat home\xe2\x80\x9d in Montana, as required for the exercise of general personal jurisdiction under Daimler AG v. Bauman, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 8) (internal quotation marks omitted), BNSF moved to dismiss both suits for lack of personal jurisdiction. Its motion was granted in Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s case and denied in Tyrrell\xe2\x80\x99s. 383 Mont., at 419, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 2.

\n

After consolidating the two cases, the Montana Supreme Court held that Montana courts could exercise general personal jurisdiction over BNSF. Id., at 429, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 9. Section 56, the court determined, authorizes state courts to exercise personal jurisdiction over railroads \xe2\x80\x9cdoing business\xe2\x80\x9d in the State. Id., at 426, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 7 (internal quotation marks omitted). In addition, the court observed, Montana law provides for the exercise of general jurisdiction over \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll persons found within\xe2\x80\x9d the State. Id., at 427, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 8 (quoting Mont. Rule Civ. Proc. 4(b)(1) (2015)). In view of the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s many employees and miles of track in Montana, the court concluded, BNSF is both \xe2\x80\x9cdoing business\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cfound within\xe2\x80\x9d the State, such that both FELA and Montana law authorized the exercise of personal jurisdiction. 383 Mont., at 426, 428, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 7\xe2\x80\x938 (internal quotation marks omitted). The due process limits articulated in Daimler, the court added, did not control, because Daimler did not involve a FELA claim or a railroad defendant. 383 Mont., at 424, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 6.

\n

Justice McKinnon dissented. Section 56, she wrote, is a federal-court venue prescription, and also confers subject-matter jurisdiction on state courts in FELA cases, concurrent with federal courts. Id., at 435\xe2\x80\x93437, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 13. But \xc2\xa756, she maintained, does not touch or concern personal jurisdiction. Ibid. Furthermore, she concluded, Daimler controls, rendering the Montana courts\xe2\x80\x99 exercise of personal jurisdiction impermissible because BNSF is not \xe2\x80\x9cat home\xe2\x80\x9d in Montana. 383 Mont., at 433\xe2\x80\x93434, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

We granted certiorari, 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017), to resolve whether \xc2\xa756 authorizes state courts to exercise personal jurisdiction over railroads doing business in their States but not incorporated or headquartered there, and whether the Montana courts\xe2\x80\x99 exercise of personal jurisdiction in these cases comports with due process.

\n

II

\n

Nelson and Tyrrell contend that \xc2\xa756\xe2\x80\x99s first relevant sentence confers personal jurisdiction on federal courts, and that the section\xe2\x80\x99s second relevant sentence extends that grant of jurisdiction to state courts. Neither contention is tenable. Section 56\xe2\x80\x99s first relevant sentence concerns venue; its next sentence speaks to subject-matter jurisdiction.[1]

\n

A

\n

The first sentence of \xc2\xa756 states that \xe2\x80\x9can action may be brought in a district court of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d in, among other places, the district \xe2\x80\x9cin which the defendant shall be doing business at the time of commencing such action.\xe2\x80\x9d In Baltimore & Ohio R. Co. v. Kepner,\n\n314 U.\xc2\xa0S. 44 (1941)\n, we comprehended this clause as \xe2\x80\x9cestablish[ing] venue\xe2\x80\x9d for a federal-court action. Id., at 52. Congress, we explained, designed \xc2\xa756 to expand venue beyond the limits of the 1888 Judiciary Act\xe2\x80\x99s general venue provision, which allowed suit only \xe2\x80\x9cin districts of which the defendant was an inhabitant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 49; see Act of Aug. 13, 1888, \xc2\xa71,\n\n25Stat.\n434. Nowhere in Kepner or in any other decision did we intimate that \xc2\xa756 might affect personal jurisdiction.

\n

Congress generally uses the expression, where suit \xe2\x80\x9cmay be brought,\xe2\x80\x9d to indicate the federal districts in which venue is proper. See, e.g.,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71391(b) (general venue statute specifying where \xe2\x80\x9c[a] civil action may be brought\xe2\x80\x9d); J. Oakley, ALI, Fed. Judicial Code Rev. Project 253\xe2\x80\x93290 (2004) (listing special venue statutes, many with similar language). See also Kepner, 314 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 56 (Frankfurter, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe phrasing of [\xc2\xa756] follows the familiar pattern generally employed by Congress in framing venue provisions.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In contrast, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 typical mode of providing for the exercise of personal jurisdiction has been to authorize service of process. See, e.g.,\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa722 (Clayton Act provision stating that \xe2\x80\x9call process in [cases against a corporation arising under federal antitrust laws] may be served in the district of which [the defendant] is an inhabitant, or wherever [the defendant] may be found\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa753(a) (under Federal Trade Commission Act, \xe2\x80\x9cprocess may be served on any person, partnership, or corporation wherever it may be found\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Omni Capital Int\xe2\x80\x99l, Ltd. v. Rudolf Wolff & Co.,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 97\n\xe2\x80\x93107 (1987) (discussing statutes that authorize (or fail to authorize) nationwide service of process). But cf. Schlanger v. Seamans,\n\n401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 487\n, n.\xc2\xa04 (1971) (though \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has provided for nationwide service of process\xe2\x80\x9d in\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71391(e) (1964 ed., Supp. V), that statute was meant to expand venue, not personal jurisdiction). Congress uses this terminology because, absent consent, a basis for service of a summons on the defendant is prerequisite to the exercise of personal jurisdiction. See Omni Capital, 484 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 104.

\n

Nelson and Tyrrell, however, argue that \xc2\xa756 relates to personal jurisdiction. In their view, the 1888 Judiciary Act provision that prompted \xc2\xa756\xe2\x80\x99s enactment,\n\n25Stat.\n434, concerned both personal jurisdiction and venue. According to House and Senate Reports, they contend, two cases had brought to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 attention the problem with the prior provision\xe2\x80\x94namely, that in federal-question cases it authorized suit only in the district of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s residence. Brief for Respondents 16\xe2\x80\x9318. See H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 513, 61st Cong., 2d Sess., 6 (1910) (citing Macon Grocery Co. v. Atlantic Coast Line R. Co.,\n\n215 U.\xc2\xa0S. 501 (1910)\n; Cound v. Atchison, T. & S. F. R. Co., 173 F. 527 (WD Tex. 1909)); S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 432, 61st Cong., 2d Sess.,\xc2\xa04 (1910) (same). In both cases, the courts had dismissed FELA suits for \xe2\x80\x9cwant of jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Macon Grocery, 215 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 510; Cound, 173 F., at 534. To avert such jurisdictional dismissals, they urge, Congress enacted \xc2\xa756.

\n

Legislative history \xe2\x80\x9cthrows little light\xe2\x80\x9d here. Kepner, 314 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 50.[2] Driving today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, we have long read the 1888 Judiciary Act provision to concern venue only. See Green v. Chicago, B. & Q. R. Co.,\n\n205 U.\xc2\xa0S. 530\n\xe2\x80\x93533 (1907) (analyzing personal jurisdiction separately, after concluding that venue was proper under 1888 Judiciary Act provision). See also Lee v. Chesapeake & Ohio R. Co.,\n\n260 U.\xc2\xa0S. 653,\n655 (1923)\n (noting that materially identical successor to 1888 Judiciary Act provision, Act of Mar. 3, 1911, \xc2\xa751,\n\n36Stat.\n1101, \xe2\x80\x9crelates to the venue of suits\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, reading the 1888 Judiciary Act provision to authorize the exercise of personal jurisdiction would have yielded an anomalous result: In diversity cases, the provision allowed for suit \xe2\x80\x9cin the district of the residence of either the plaintiff or the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n25Stat.\n434. Interpreting that clause to provide for jurisdiction would have allowed a plaintiff to hale a defendant into court in the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s home district, even if the district was one with which the defendant had no affiliation, and the episode-in-suit, no connection.

\n

B

\n

The second \xc2\xa756 sentence in point provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he jurisdiction of the courts of the United States under this chapter shall be concurrent with that of the courts of the several States.\xe2\x80\x9d Nelson and Tyrrell argue that this sentence extends to state courts the first sentence\xe2\x80\x99s alleged conferral of personal jurisdiction on federal courts. But, as just discussed, the first sentence concerns federal-court venue and confers no personal jurisdiction on any court.

\n

We have understood \xc2\xa756\xe2\x80\x99s second sentence to provide for the concurrent subject-matter jurisdiction of state and federal courts over actions under FELA. See Second Employers\xe2\x80\x99 Liability Cases,\n\n223 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9356 (1912). As Nelson and Tyrrell acknowledge, Congress added the provision to confirm concurrent subject-matter jurisdiction after the Connecticut Supreme Court held that Congress intended to confine FELA litigation to federal courts, and that state courts had no obligation to entertain FELA claims. See Brief for Respondents 23 (citing Hoxie v. New York, N.\xc2\xa0H. & H.\xc2\xa0R. Co., 82 Conn. 352, 73 A. 754 (1909)). As Justice McKinnon recognized in her dissent from the Montana Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s and Tyrrell\xe2\x80\x99s cases, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he phrase \xe2\x80\x98concurrent jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99 is a well-known term of art long employed by Congress and courts to refer to subject-matter jurisdiction, not personal jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d 383 Mont., at 436, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 13. See, e.g., Mims v. Arrow Financial Services, LLC,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 368,\n372 (2012)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cfederal and state courts have concurrent jurisdiction over private suits arising under the [Telephone Consumer Protection Act of 1991,\n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7227]\xe2\x80\x9d); Claflin v. Houseman,\n\n93 U.\xc2\xa0S. 130\n\xe2\x80\x93134 (1876) (State courts retain \xe2\x80\x9cconcurrent jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d over \xe2\x80\x9csuits in which a bankrupt\xe2\x80\x9d party is involved, notwithstanding exclusive federal jurisdiction over bankruptcy matters).

\n

C

\n

Pointing to a quartet of cases, the Montana Supreme Court observed that this Court \xe2\x80\x9cconsistently has interpreted [\xc2\xa7]56 to allow state courts to hear cases brought under FELA even where the only basis for jurisdiction is the railroad doing business in the forum [S]tate.\xe2\x80\x9d 383 Mont., at 421\xe2\x80\x93423, 425\xe2\x80\x93426, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 4\xe2\x80\x937 (citing Pope v. Atlantic Coast Line R. Co.,\n\n345 U.\xc2\xa0S. 379 (1953)\n; Miles v. Illinois Central R. Co.,\n\n315 U.\xc2\xa0S. 698 (1942)\n; Kepner,\n\n314 U.\xc2\xa0S. 44\n; Denver & Rio Grande Western R. Co. v. Terte,\n\n284 U.\xc2\xa0S. 284 (1932)\n).

\n

None of the decisions featured by the Montana Supreme Court resolved a question of personal jurisdiction. Terte held that a FELA plaintiff, injured in Colorado, could bring suit in Missouri state court against a railroad incorporated elsewhere. 284 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 286\xe2\x80\x93287. The dispute, however, was over the Dormant Commerce Clause, not personal jurisdiction; the railroad defendants argued that the suit would unduly burden interstate commerce, and the decision rested on two Commerce Clause decisions, Michigan Central R. Co. v. Mix,\n\n278 U.\xc2\xa0S. 492 (1929)\n, and Hoffman v. Missouri ex rel. Foraker,\n\n274 U.\xc2\xa0S. 21 (1927)\n, not on an interpretation of \xc2\xa756. See Terte, 284 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 285, 287. In Kepner and Miles, this Court held that a state court may not, based on inconvenience to a railroad defendant, enjoin its residents from bringing a FELA suit in another State\xe2\x80\x99s federal (Kepner) or state (Miles) courts. Kepner, 314 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 54; Miles, 315 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 699\xe2\x80\x93700, 704. Pope held that\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71404(a)\xe2\x80\x99s provision for transfer from one federal court to another did not bear on the question decided in Miles: A state court still could not enjoin a FELA action brought in another State\xe2\x80\x99s courts. 345 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 383\xe2\x80\x93384.

\n

Moreover, all these cases, save Pope, were decided before this Court\xe2\x80\x99s transformative decision on personal jurisdiction in International Shoe Co. v. Washington,\n\n326 U.\xc2\xa0S. 310 (1945)\n. See Daimler, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa018 (slip op., at 20, n.\xc2\xa018) (cautioning against reliance on cases \xe2\x80\x9cdecided in the era dominated by\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cterritorial thinking\xe2\x80\x9d of Pennoyer v. Neff,\n\n95 U.\xc2\xa0S. 714 (1878)\n).

\n

III

\n

Because FELA does not authorize state courts to exercise personal jurisdiction over a railroad solely on the ground that the railroad does some business in their States, the Montana courts\xe2\x80\x99 assertion of personal jurisdiction over BNSF here must rest on Mont. Rule Civ. Proc. 4(b)(1), the State\xe2\x80\x99s provision for the exercise of personal jurisdiction over \xe2\x80\x9cpersons found\xe2\x80\x9d in Montana. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. BNSF does not contest that it is \xe2\x80\x9cfound within\xe2\x80\x9d Montana as the State\xe2\x80\x99s courts comprehend that rule. We therefore inquire whether the Montana courts\xe2\x80\x99 exercise of personal jurisdiction under Montana law comports with the Due Process Clause of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment.

\n

In International Shoe, this Court explained that a state court may exercise personal jurisdiction over an out-of-state defendant who has \xe2\x80\x9ccertain minimum contacts with [the State] such that the maintenance of the suit does not offend \xe2\x80\x98traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 326 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 316. Elaborating on this guide, we have distinguished between specific or case-linked jurisdiction and general or all-purpose jurisdiction. See, e.g., Daimler, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8); Goodyear Dunlop Tires Operations, S.\xc2\xa0A. v. Brown,\n\n564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 915,\n919 (2011)\n; Helicopteros Nacionales de Colombia, S.\xc2\xa0A. v. Hall,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 408\n, nn.\xc2\xa08, 9 (1984). Because neither Nelson nor Tyrrell alleges any injury from work in or related to Montana, only the propriety of general jurisdiction is at issue here.

\n

Goodyear and Daimler clarified that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] court may assert general jurisdiction over foreign (sister-state or foreign-country) corporations to hear any and all claims against them when their affiliations with the State are so \xe2\x80\x98continuous and systematic\xe2\x80\x99 as to render them essentially at home in the forum State.\xe2\x80\x9d Daimler, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8) (quoting Goodyear, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 919). The \xe2\x80\x9cparadigm\xe2\x80\x9d forums in which a corporate defendant is \xe2\x80\x9cat home,\xe2\x80\x9d we explained, are the corporation\xe2\x80\x99s place of incorporation and its principal place of business. Daimler, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 18\xe2\x80\x9319); Goodyear, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 924. The exercise of general jurisdiction is not limited to these forums; in an \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional case,\xe2\x80\x9d a corporate defendant\xe2\x80\x99s operations in another forum \xe2\x80\x9cmay be so substantial and of such a nature as to render the corporation at home in that State.\xe2\x80\x9d Daimler, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa019 (slip op., at 20, n.\xc2\xa019). We suggested that Perkins v. Benguet Consol. Mining Co.,\n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437 (1952)\n, exemplified such a case. Daimler, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa019 (slip op., at 20, n.\xc2\xa019). In Perkins, war had forced the defendant corporation\xe2\x80\x99s owner to temporarily relocate the enterprise from the Philippines to Ohio. 342 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 447\xe2\x80\x93448. Because Ohio then became \xe2\x80\x9cthe center of the corporation\xe2\x80\x99s wartime activities,\xe2\x80\x9d Daimler, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa08 (slip op., at 12, n.\xc2\xa08), suit was proper there, Perkins, 342 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 448.

\n

The Montana Supreme Court distinguished Daimler on the ground that we did not there confront \xe2\x80\x9ca FELA claim or a railroad defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d 383 Mont., at 424, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 6. The\n \nFourteenth Amendment due process constraint described in Daimler, however, applies to all state-court assertions of general jurisdiction over nonresident defendants; the constraint does not vary with the type of claim asserted or business enterprise sued.[3]

\n

BNSF, we repeat, is not incorporated in Montana and does not maintain its principal place of business there. Nor is BNSF so heavily engaged in activity in Montana \xe2\x80\x9cas to render [it] essentially at home\xe2\x80\x9d in that State. See Daimler, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8) (internal quotation marks omitted). As earlier noted, BNSF has over 2,000 miles of railroad track and more than 2,000 employees in Montana. But, as we observed in Daimler, \xe2\x80\x9cthe general jurisdiction inquiry does not focus solely on the magnitude of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s in-state contacts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___, n.\xc2\xa020 (slip op., at 21, n.\xc2\xa020) (internal quotation marks and alterations omitted). Rather, the inquiry \xe2\x80\x9ccalls for an appraisal of a corporation\xe2\x80\x99s activities in their entirety\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9c[a] corporation that operates in many places can scarcely be deemed at home in all of them.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In short, the business BNSF does in Montana is sufficient to subject the railroad to specific personal jurisdiction in that State on claims related to the business it does in Montana. But in-state business, we clarified in Daimler and Goodyear, does not suffice to permit the assertion of general jurisdiction over claims like Nelson\xe2\x80\x99s and Tyrrell\xe2\x80\x99s that are unrelated to any activity occurring in Montana.[4]

\n

IV

\n

Nelson and Tyrrell present a further argument\xe2\x80\x94that BNSF has consented to personal jurisdiction in Montana. See Brief for Respondents 50\xe2\x80\x9351. The Montana Supreme Court did not address this contention, see 383 Mont., at 429, n.\xc2\xa03, 373 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 9, n.\xc2\xa03, so we do not reach it. See Cutter v. Wilkinson,\n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 709\n, n.\xc2\xa07 (2005) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e are a court of review, not of first view.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

For the reasons stated, the judgment of the Montana Supreme Court is reversed, and the cases are remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.

BNSF RAILWAY CO., PETITIONER v. KELLI TYRRELL, special administrator for the ESTATE OF BRENT T. TYRRELL, DECEASED, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

\n

Justice Sotomayor, concurring in part and dissenting in part.

\n

I concur in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the Federal Employers\xe2\x80\x99 Liability Act (FELA),\n\n45 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa751 et\xc2\xa0seq., does not confer personal jurisdiction over railroads on state courts. I also agree that the Montana Supreme Court erred when it concluded that the nature of the claim here\xe2\x80\x94a FELA claim against a railroad\xe2\x80\x94answers the question whether the Due Process Clause allows the exercise of personal jurisdiction over BNSF. But my agreement with the majority ends there. I continue to disagree with the path the Court struck in Daimler AG v. Bauman, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), which limits general jurisdiction over a corporate defendant only to those States where it is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98essentially at home,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___ (slip op., at 8). And even if the Court insists on adhering to that standard, I dissent from its decision to apply it here in the first instance rather than remanding to the Montana Supreme Court for it to conduct what should be a fact-intensive analysis under the proper legal framework. Accordingly, I join Parts I and II of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, but dissent from Part III and the judgment.

\n

The Court would do well to adhere more faithfully to the direction from International Shoe Co. v. Washington, 326 U.\xc2\xa0S. 310 (1945), which instructed that general jurisdiction is proper when a corporation\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuous corporate operations within a state [are] so substantial and of such a nature as to justify suit against it on causes of action arising from dealings entirely distinct from those activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 318. Under International Shoe, in other words, courts were to ask whether the benefits a defendant attained in the forum State warranted the burdens associated with general personal jurisdiction. See id., at 317\xe2\x80\x93318. The majority itself acknowledges that International Shoe should govern, describing the question as whether a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s affiliations with a State are sufficiently \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98continuous and systematic\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to warrant the exercise of general jurisdiction there. Ante, at 10. If only its analysis today reflected that directive. Instead, the majority opinion goes on to reaffirm the restrictive \xe2\x80\x9cat home\xe2\x80\x9d test set out in Daimler\xe2\x80\x94a test that, as I have explained, has no home in our precedents and creates serious inequities. See 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (Sotomayor, J., concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 8\xe2\x80\x9319).

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach grants a jurisdictional windfall to large multistate or multinational corporations that operate across many jurisdictions. Under its reasoning, it is virtually inconceivable that such corporations will ever be subject to general jurisdiction in any location other than their principal places of business or of incorporation. Foreign businesses with principal places of business outside the United States may never be subject to general jurisdiction in this country even though they have continuous and systematic contacts within the United States. See id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318). What was once a holistic, nuanced contacts analysis backed by considerations of fairness and reasonableness has now effectively been replaced by the rote identification of a corporation\xe2\x80\x99s principal place of business or place of incorporation.[1] The result? It is individual plaintiffs, harmed by the actionsof a farflung foreign corporation, who will bear the brunt of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach and be forced to sue in dis-tant jurisdictions with which they have no contacts or connection.

\n

Moreover, the comparative contacts analysis invented in Daimler resurfaces here and proves all but dispositive. The majority makes much of the fact that BNSF\xe2\x80\x99s contacts in Montana are only a percentage of its contacts with other jurisdictions. Ante, at 3, 11. But International Shoe, which the majority agrees is the springboard for our modern personal jurisdiction jurisprudence, ante, at 9, applied no comparative contacts test. There the Court analyzed whether the Delaware corporation had \xe2\x80\x9cby its activities in the State of Washington rendered itself amenable to proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d in the State. 326 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 311. The Court evaluated whether the corporation had offices in the forum State, made contracts there, delivered goods there, or employed salesmen there. See id., at 313. Despite acknowledging that the corporation maintained places of business in several States, ibid., the Court did not engage in a comparison between International Shoe\xe2\x80\x99s contacts within the State of Washington and the other States in which it operated.[2] The Court noted that the corporation employed 11 to 13 salesmen in Washington but did not query how that number compared to the number of salesmen in other States. Ibid. As well it should not have; the relative percentage of contacts is irrelevant. The focus should be on the quality and quantity of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s contacts in the forum State.[3]

\n

The majority does even Daimler itself a disservice, paying only lipservice to the question the Court purported to reserve there\xe2\x80\x94the possibility of an \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional case\xe2\x80\x9d in which general jurisdiction would be proper in a forum State that is neither a corporate defendant\xe2\x80\x99s place of incorporation nor its principal place of business. See 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n. 19 (slip op., at 20, n.\xc2\xa019). Its opinion here could be understood to limit that exception to the exact facts of Perkins v. Benguet Consol. Mining Co.,\n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437 (1952)\n. See ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. That reading is so narrow as to read the exception out of existence entirely; certainly a defendant with significant contacts with more than one State falls outside its ambit. And so it is inevitable under its own reasoning that the majority would conclude that BNSF\xe2\x80\x99s contacts with Montana are insufficient to justify the exercise of personal jurisdiction here. This result is perverse. Despite having reserved the possibility of an \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional case\xe2\x80\x9d in Daimler, the majority here has re-jected that possibility out of hand.

\n

Worse, the majority reaches its conclusion only by departing from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s normal practice.[4] Had it remanded to the Montana Supreme Court to reevaluate the due process question under the correct legal standard, that court could have examined whether this is such an \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional case.\xe2\x80\x9d Instead, with its ruling today, the Court unnecessarily sends a signal to the lower courtsthat the exceptional-circumstances inquiry is all form, no substance.

\n

I respectfully concur in part and dissent in part.
