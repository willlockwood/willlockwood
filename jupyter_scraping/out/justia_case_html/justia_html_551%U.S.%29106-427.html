TENNESSEE SECONDARY SCHOOL ATHLETIC\nASSOCIATION v. BRENTWOOD ACADEMY

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x93427.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 18, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 21, 2007

\n

Petitioner association (TSSAA) regulates interscholastic sports among its members, Tennessee public and private high schools. TSSAA sanctioned respondent (Brentwood), one of those private schools, because its football coach sent eighth-grade boys a letter that violated TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s rule prohibiting members from using \xe2\x80\x9cundue influence\xe2\x80\x9d in recruiting middle school students for their athletic programs. Following internal TSSAA review, Brentwood sued TSSAA and its executive director under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, claiming, inter alia, that enforcement of the antirecruiting rule was state action violative of the First and Fourteenth Amendments and that TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s flawed adjudication of its appeal deprived Brentwood of due process. The District Court granted Brentwood relief, but the Sixth Circuit reversed, holding that TSSAA was a private voluntary association that did not act under color of state law. This Court reversed that determination, Brentwood Academy v. Tennessee Secondary School Athletic Assn., 531 U. S. 288, and the District Court again ruled for Brentwood on remand. The Sixth Circuit affirmed, holding that the antirecruiting rule is a content-based regulation of speech that is not narrowly tailored to serve its permissible purposes and that the TSSAA board improperly considered ex parte evidence, thereby violating Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s due process rights.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is reversed, and the case is remanded.

\n

442 F.\xc2\xa03d 410, reversed and remanded.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens delivered the opinion of the Court with respect to Parts I, II\xe2\x80\x93B, III, and IV, concluding:

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Enforcing a rule that prohibits high school coaches from recruiting middle school athletes does not violate the First Amendment. Brentwood made a voluntary decision to join TSSAA and to abide by its antirecruiting rule. See 531 U. S., at 291. An athletic league\xe2\x80\x99s interest in enforcing its rules may warrant curtailing the speech of its voluntary participants. See, e.g., Pickering v. Board of Ed. of Township High School Dist. 205, Will Cty., 391 U. S. 563, 568. TSSAA does not have unbounded authority to condition membership on the relinquishment of constitutional rights, see Garcetti v. Ceballos, 547 U. S. ___, ___, and can impose only those conditions that are necessary to managing an efficient and effective state-sponsored high school athletic league. That necessity is obviously present here. No empirical data is needed to credit TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s commonsense conclusion that hard-sell tactics directed at middle school students could lead to exploitation, distort competition between high school teams, and foster an environment in which athletics are prized more highly than academics. TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s rule discourages precisely the sort of conduct that might lead to those harms, any one of which would detract from a high school sports league\xe2\x80\x99s ability to operate \xe2\x80\x9cefficiently and effectively.\xe2\x80\x9d Garcetti, 547 U. S., at ___. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0TSSAA did not violate Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s due process rights. The sanction decision was preceded by an investigation, several meetings, correspondence, the TSSAA executive director\xe2\x80\x99s adverse written determination, a hearing before the director and an advisory panel, and a de novo review by the entire TSSAA board. During the investigation, Brentwood was notified of all the charges against it. At each of the hearings, it was represented by counsel and given the opportunity to adduce evidence, none of which was excluded. The Court rejects Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s argument that its due process rights were nevertheless violated when the full TSSAA board, acting ex parte, heard from investigators and other witnesses and considered the investigators\xe2\x80\x99 notes and other evidence concerning a separate incident in which a basketball coach named King, who was not a Brentwood employee, pushed a middle school basketball star to attend Brentwood. Even accepting the questionable holding that TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s closed-door deliberations were unconstitutional, any due process violation was harmless beyond a reasonable doubt. It is unlikely the King allegations increased the severity of the penalties leveled against Brentwood. More importantly, Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s prejudice claim rests on the unsupported premise that it would have adopted a different and more effective strategy at the board hearing had it been given an opportunity to cross-examine the investigators and review their notes. Brentwood has identified nothing the investigators shared with the Board that Brentwood did not already know. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nTENNESSEE SECONDARY SCHOOL ATHLETIC ASSN. V.BRENTWOOD ACADEMY
\n551 U. S. ____ (2007)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 06-427
\n
\n

TENNESSEE SECONDARY SCHOOL ATHLETIC\nASSOCIATION, PETITIONER v. BRENTWOOD\nACADEMY

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[June 21, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens announced the judgment of the Court and delivered the opinion of the Court with respect to Parts I, II\xe2\x80\x93B, III, and IV, and an opinion with respect to Part II\xe2\x80\x93A, in which Justice Souter, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Breyer join.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The principal issue before us is whether the enforcement of a rule prohibiting high school coaches from recruiting middle school athletes violates the First Amendment. We also must decide whether the sanction imposed on respondent for violating that rule was preceded by a fair hearing.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although this case has had a long history, the relevant facts may be stated briefly. The Tennessee Secondary School Athletic Association (TSSAA) is a not-for-profit membership corporation organized to regulate interscholastic sports among its members, which include some 290 public and 55 private high schools in Tennessee. Brentwood Academy is one of those private schools.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since the early 1950\xe2\x80\x99s, TSSAA has prohibited high schools from using \xe2\x80\x9cundue influence\xe2\x80\x9d in recruiting middle school students for their athletic programs. In April 1997, Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s football coach sent a letter to a group of eighth-grade boys inviting them to attend spring practice sessions. See App. 119. The letter explained that football equipment would be distributed and that \xe2\x80\x9cgetting involved as soon as possible would definitely be to your advantage.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. It was signed \xe2\x80\x9cYour Coach.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. While the boys who received the letter had signed a contract signaling their intent to attend Brentwood, none had enrolled within the meaning of TSSAA rules. See id., at 182 (defining \xe2\x80\x9cenrolled\xe2\x80\x9d as having \xe2\x80\x9cattended 3 days of school\xe2\x80\x9d). All of the boys attended at least some of the spring practice sessions. As the case comes to us, it is settled that the coach\xe2\x80\x99s pre-enrollment solicitation violated the TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s anti-recruiting rule and that he had ample notice that his conduct was prohibited.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0TSSAA accordingly sanctioned Brentwood. After proceeding through two layers of internal TSSAA review, Brentwood brought this action against TSSAA and its executive director in federal court under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. As relevant here, Brentwood made two claims: first, that enforcement of the rule was state action in violation of the First and Fourteenth Amendments; and second, that TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s flawed adjudication of its appeal had deprived the school of due process of law. The District Court granted relief to Brentwood, but the Court of Appeals reversed, holding that TSSAA was a private voluntary association that did not act under color of state law. We granted certiorari and reversed, holding that the District Court was correct on the threshold issue. Brentwood Academy v. Tennessee Secondary School Athletic Assn., 531 U. S. 288 (2001). On remand, the Sixth Circuit sent the case back to the District Court, which once again ruled for Brentwood. 304 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 981 (MD Tenn. 2003). TSSAA appealed, and the Court of Appeals affirmed over one judge\xe2\x80\x99s dissent. 442 F.\xc2\xa03d 410 (2006). The majority held that the anti-recruiting rule is a content-based regulation of speech that is not narrowly tailored to serve its permissible purposes. Id., at 420\xe2\x80\x93431. It also concluded that the TSSAA Board improperly considered ex parte evidence during its deliberations, thereby violating Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s due process rights. Id., at 433\xe2\x80\x93438.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We again granted certiorari, 549 U. S. ___ (2007), and we again reverse.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The First Amendment protects Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s right to publish truthful information about the school and its athletic programs. It likewise protects the school\xe2\x80\x99s right to try to persuade prospective students and their parents that its excellence in sports is a reason for enrolling. But Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s speech rights are not absolute. It chose to join TSSAA, an athletic league and a state actor invested with a three-fold obligation to prevent the exploitation of children, to ensure that high school athletics remain secondary to academics, and to promote fair competition among its members. TSSAA submits that these interests adequately support the enforcement against its member schools of a rule prohibiting coaches from trying to recruit impressionable middle school athletes. Brentwood disagrees, and maintains that TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s asserted interests are too flimsy and its rule too broad to support what the school views as a serious curtailment of its constitutional rights. Two aspects of the case taken together persuade us that TSSAA should prevail.

\n

A

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The anti-recruiting rule strikes nowhere near the heart of the First Amendment. TSSAA has not banned the dissemination of truthful information relating to sports, nor has it claimed that it could. Cf. Virginia Bd. of Pharmacy v. Virginia Citizens Consumer Council, Inc., 425 U. S. 748 (1976) (striking down a prohibition on advertising prices for prescription drugs). It has only prevented its member schools\xe2\x80\x99 coaches from recruiting individual middle school students. Our cases teach that there is a difference of constitutional dimension between rules prohibiting appeals to the public at large, see 44 Liquormart, Inc. v. Rhode Island, 517 U. S. 484, 495\xe2\x80\x93500 (1996), and rules prohibiting direct, personalized communication in a coercive setting.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ohralik v. Ohio State Bar Assn., 436 U. S. 447 (1978), nicely illustrates the point. In Ohralik, we considered whether the First Amendment disabled a state bar association from disciplining a lawyer for the in-person solicitation of clients. The lawyer argued that under our decision in Bates v. State Bar of Ariz., 433 U. S.\xc2\xa0350, 384 (1977), which invalidated on First Amendment grounds a ban on truthful advertising relating to the \xe2\x80\x9cavailability and terms of routine legal services,\xe2\x80\x9d his solicitation was protected speech. We rejected the lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s argument, holding that the \xe2\x80\x9cin-person solicitation of professional employment by a lawyer does not stand on a par with truthful advertising about the availability and terms of routine legal services, let alone with forms of speech more traditionally within the concern of the First Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d 436 U. S., at 455. We reasoned that the solicitation ban was more akin to a conduct regulation than a speech restriction:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[I]t has never been deemed an abridgment of freedom of speech or press to make a course of conduct illegal merely because the conduct was in part initiated, evidenced, or carried out by means of language, either spoken, written, or printed.\xe2\x80\x99 Numerous examples could be cited of communications that are regulated without offending the First Amendment, such as the exchange of information about securities, corporate proxy statements, the exchange of price and production information among competitors, and employers\xe2\x80\x99 threats of retaliation for the labor activities of employees\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Each of these examples illustrates that the State does not lose its power to regulate commercial activity deemed harmful to the public whenever speech is a component of that activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 456 (citations omitted).

\n

\nDrawing on these examples, we found that the \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n-person solicitation by a lawyer of remunerative employment is a business transaction in which speech is an essential but subordinate component,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 457, the prohibition of which raised few (if any) First Amendment problems.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ohralik identified several evils associated with direct solicitation distinct from the harms presented by conventional commercial speech. Direct solicitation \xe2\x80\x9cmay exert pressure and often demands an immediate response, without providing an opportunity for comparison or reflection,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; its goal \xe2\x80\x9cmay be to provide a one-sided presentation and to encourage speedy and perhaps uninformed decisionmaking,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; and it short circuits the \xe2\x80\x9copportunity for intervention or counter-education by agencies of the Bar, supervisory authorities, or persons close to the solicited individual,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. For these reasons, we concluded that in-person solicitation \xe2\x80\x9cactually may disserve the individual and societal interest, identified in Bates, in facilitating \xe2\x80\x98informed and reliable decisionmaking.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 458 (quoting Bates, 433 U. S., at 364).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have since emphasized that Ohralik\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cnarrow\xe2\x80\x9d holding is limited to conduct that is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98inherently conducive to overreaching and other forms of misconduct.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Edenfield v. Fane, 507 U. S. 761, 774 (1993) (quoting Ohralik, 436 U. S., at 464); see also Zauderer v. Office of Disciplinary Counsel of Supreme Court of Ohio, 471 U. S. 626, 641 (1985) (emphasizing that Ohralik involved a \xe2\x80\x9cpractice rife with possibilities for overreaching, invasion of privacy, the exercise of undue influence, and outright fraud\xe2\x80\x9d). And we have not been chary of invalidating state restrictions on solicitation and commercial advertising in the absence of the acute risks associated with in-person legal solicitation. See Edenfield, 507 U. S., at 775 (striking down a restriction on in-person solicitation by accountants because such solicitation \xe2\x80\x9cposes none of the same dangers\xe2\x80\x9d identified in Ohralik); Zauderer, 471 U. S., at 639\xe2\x80\x93647 (invalidating a restriction on truthful, nondeceptive legal advertising directed at people with specific legal problems); Shapero v. Kentucky Bar Assn., 486 U. S. 466, 472\xe2\x80\x93478 (1988) (overturning a blanket proscription on all forms of legal solicitation). In our view, however, the dangers of undue influence and overreaching that exist when a lawyer chases an ambulance are also present when a high school coach contacts an eighth grader.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After all, it is a heady thing for an eighth-grade student to be contacted directly by a coach\xe2\x80\x94here, \xe2\x80\x9cYour Coach\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and invited to join a high school sports team. In too many cases, the invitation will come accompanied with a suggestion, subtle or otherwise, that failure to accept will hurt the student\xe2\x80\x99s chances to play high school sports and diminish the odds that she could continue on to college or (dream of dreams) professional sports. Cf. App. 119 (\xe2\x80\x9cI do feel that getting involved as soon as possible would definitely be to your advantage\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 1] Such a potent entreaty, playing as it does on youthful hopes and fears, could well exert the kind of undue pressure that \xe2\x80\x9cdisserve[s] the individual and societal interest \xe2\x80\xa6 in facilitating \xe2\x80\x98informed and reliable decisionmaking.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ohralik, 436 U. S., at 458. Given that TSSAA member schools remain free to send brochures, post billboards, and otherwise advertise their athletic programs, TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s limited regulation of recruiting conduct poses no significant First Amendment concerns.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Brentwood made a voluntary decision to join TSSAA and to abide by its antirecruiting rule. See Brentwood, 531 U. S., at 291 (\xe2\x80\x9cNo school is forced to join\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Grove City College v. Bell, 465 U. S. 555, 575 (1984). Just as the government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in running an effective workplace can in some circumstances outweigh employee speech rights, see Connick v. Myers, 461 U. S. 138 (1983), so too can an athletic league\xe2\x80\x99s interest in enforcing its rules sometimes warrant curtailing the speech of its voluntary participants. See Pickering v. Board of Ed. of Township High School Dist. 205, Will Cty., 391 U. S. 563, 568 (1968) (holding that the scope of a government employee\xe2\x80\x99s First Amendment rights depends on the \xe2\x80\x9cbalance between the interests of the [employee], as a citizen, in commenting upon matters of public concern and the interest of the State, as an employer, in promoting the efficiency of the public services it performs through its employees\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Board of Comm\xe2\x80\x99rs, Wabaunsee Cty. v. Umbehr, 518 U. S. 668, 679 (1996) (\xe2\x80\x9ceschew[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d a formal approach to determining which contractual relationships call for the application of Pickering balancing). This is not to say that TSSAA has unbounded authority to condition membership on the relinquishment of any and all constitutional rights. As we recently emphasized in the employment context, \xe2\x80\x9c[s]o long as employees are speaking as citizens about matters of public concern, they must face only those speech restrictions that are necessary for their employers to operate efficiently and effectively.\xe2\x80\x9d Garcetti v. Ceballos, 547 U. S. ___, ___ (2006) (slip op., at 7). Assuming, without deciding, that the coach in this case was \xe2\x80\x9cspeaking as [a] citize[n] about matters of public concern,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., TSSAA can similarly impose only those conditions on such speech that are necessary to managing an efficient and effective state-sponsored high school athletic league.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That necessity is obviously present here. We need no empirical data to credit TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s common-sense conclusion that hard-sell tactics directed at middle school students could lead to exploitation, distort competition between high school teams, and foster an environment in which athletics are prized more highly than academics. See Paris Adult Theatre I v. Slaton, 413 U. S. 49, 60 (1973). TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s rule discourages precisely the sort of conduct that might lead to those harms, any one of which would detract from a high school sports league\xe2\x80\x99s ability to operate \xe2\x80\x9cefficiently and effectively.\xe2\x80\x9d Garcetti, 547 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 7). For that reason, the First Amendment does not excuse Brentwood from abiding by the same anti-recruiting rule that governs the conduct of its sister schools. To hold otherwise would undermine the principle, succinctly articulated by the dissenting judge at the Court of Appeals, that \xe2\x80\x9c[h]igh school football is a game. Games have rules.\xe2\x80\x9d 442 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 444 (opinion of Rogers, J.). It is only fair that Brentwood follow them.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The decision to sanction Brentwood for engaging in prohibited recruiting was preceded by an investigation, several meetings, exchanges of correspondence, see App. 120\xe2\x80\x93123 (fax from Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s coach to TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s executive director), id., at 124\xe2\x80\x93127 (memorandum from director to Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s headmaster), id., at 128\xe2\x80\x93133 (letter from the headmaster responding to the director\xe2\x80\x99s memorandum), id., at 204\xe2\x80\x93211 (letter from TSSAA director to headmaster with further questions); id., at 212\xe2\x80\x93229 (responsive letter from Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s headmaster), an adverse written determination from TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s executive director, id., at 238\xe2\x80\x93244, a hearing before the director and an advisory panel composed of three members of TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s Board of Control, see id., at 254\xe2\x80\x93258, and finally a de novo review by the entire TSSAA Board of Directors, see id., at 269\xe2\x80\x93271. During the investigation, Brentwood was notified of all the charges against it. At each of the two hearings, Brentwood was represented by counsel and given the opportunity to adduce evidence. No evidence offered by Brentwood was excluded.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Brentwood nevertheless maintains that its due process rights were violated when the full TSSAA board, during its deliberations, heard from witnesses and considered evidence that the school had no opportunity to respond to. Some background is necessary to understand the claim. One of the matters under investigation was whether an Amateur Athletic Union basketball coach named Bart King had pushed talented middle school students\xe2\x80\x94including a basketball star named Jacques Curry\xe2\x80\x94to attend Brentwood. See, e.g., id., at 220, 222 (letter from Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s headmaster discussing the allegation that King had told Curry that if he attended Brentwood, he \xe2\x80\x9cwould probably have a car when he is in the tenth grade\xe2\x80\x9d). Brentwood consistently maintained that King had no affiliation with the school and no authority to act on its behalf. See, e.g., id., at 221\xe2\x80\x93222. Nevertheless, the initial decision by TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s executive director, as well as the subsequent decision by the director and the advisory panel, declared Curry (as well as several other players) ineligible to play for Brentwood. See id., at 243 (blanket ineligibility), 255 (ineligibility for varsity sports).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As it had in earlier stages of the case, in Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s final appeal to the TSSAA Board, the school offered live testimony from Curry and an affidavit from King denying the alleged recruiting violations. See id., at 264\xe2\x80\x93267 (Curry\xe2\x80\x99s testimony); id., at 261 (listing \xe2\x80\x9cAffidavit of Bart King\xe2\x80\x9d as an exhibit).[Footnote 2] Once Curry had testified, Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s counsel advised the board that King was available to answer any questions, but did not call him as a witness.[Footnote 3] After reviewing the evidence, the board found that Brentwood had committed three specific violations of its rules, none of which appeared to involve either King or Curry, and it reinstated Curry\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility. Id., at 269\xe2\x80\x93271. As a penalty for the three violations, the board put Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s athletic program on probation for four years, excluded the boys\xe2\x80\x99 basketball and football teams from tournament playoffs for two years, and imposed a $3,000 fine. Id., at 270.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0During its deliberations, the board discussed the case with the executive director who had presided at the earlier proceedings and two TSSAA investigators, none of whom had been cross-examined. The investigators also provided handwritten notes to the board detailing their investigations; Brentwood never received those notes. The District Court found that the consideration of the ex parte evidence influenced the board\xe2\x80\x99s penalty decision and contravened the Due Process Clause. 304 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1003\xe2\x80\x931006. The Court of Appeals accepted that finding, as well as the conclusion that the evidence tainted the fairness of the proceeding. 442 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 433\xe2\x80\x93438. TSSAA now maintains that the lower courts erred.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We agree. Even accepting the questionable holding that TSSAA\xe2\x80\x99s closed-door deliberations were unconstitutional, we can safely conclude that any due process violation was harmless beyond a reasonable doubt. To begin with, it is hard to believe that the King allegations increased the severity of the penalties leveled against Brentwood.[Footnote 4] But more importantly, Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s claim of prejudice rests on the unsupported premise that it would have adopted a different and more effective strategy at the board hearing had it been given an opportunity to cross-examine the investigators and review their notes. Despite having had nearly a decade since the hearing to undertake that cross-examination and review, Brentwood has identified nothing the investigators shared with the board that Brentwood did not already know.[Footnote 5] Perhaps that is why Brentwood never explains what a more effective strategy might have looked like. Brentwood obliquely suggests it might have had King testify at the hearing, but it gives no inkling of what his testimony would have added to the proceedings. We are not inclined to speculate on its behalf.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We accordingly reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\nFootnote 2

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although I have little difficulty concluding that the regulation at issue does not contravene the First Amendment, I do not agree with the principal opinion\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on Ohralik v. Ohio State Bar Assn., 436 U. S. 447 (1978). Ohralik, as the principal opinion notes, involved communications between attorney and client, or, more to the point, the in-person solicitation by an attorney of an accident victim as a potential client. Ohralik was later extended to attorney solicitation of accident victims through direct mail, though the Court was closely divided as to the constitutionality of that extension. See Florida Bar v. Went For It, Inc., 515 U. S. 618 (1995). But the Court has declined to extend the Ohralik rule beyond the attorney-client relationship.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Edenfield v. Fane, 507 U. S. 761 (1993), the Court struck down a ban on solicitation from accountants to potential clients. The Court there made clear that Ohralik \xe2\x80\x9cdid not hold that all personal solicitation is without First Amendment protection.\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U. S., at 774. It further noted that \xe2\x80\x9cOhralik\xe2\x80\x99s holding was narrow and depended upon certain \xe2\x80\x98unique features of in-person solicitation by lawyers\xe2\x80\x99 that were present in the circumstances of that case.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Zauderer v. Office of Disciplinary Counsel of Supreme Court of Ohio, 471 U. S. 626, 641 (1985)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view it is both unnecessary and ill advised to rely upon Ohralik in the instant matter. By doing so, the principal opinion, at a minimum, is open to the implication that the speech at issue is subject to state regulation whether or not the school has entered a voluntary contract with a state-sponsored association in order to promote a code of conduct affecting solicitation. To allow free-standing state regulation of speech by coaches and other representatives of nonmember schools would be a dramatic expansion of Ohralik to a whole new field of endeavor. Yet by relying on Ohralik the principal opinion undermines the argument that, in the absence of Brentwood Academy\xe2\x80\x99s consensual membership in the Tennessee Secondary School Athletic Association, the speech by the head coach would be entitled to First Amendment protection.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons I must decline to join Part II\xe2\x80\x93A of the principal opinion and any other portion of Part II that suggests Ohralik is applicable here. It is evident, furthermore, that a majority of the Court agrees with this position. See post, at 2 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). I do join the remainder of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion and the judgment that ensues.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In resolving this case, the Court applies the Pickering v. Board of Ed. of Township High School Dist. 205, Will Cty., 391 U. S. 563 (1968), line of cases to hold that the Tennessee Secondary School Athletic Association (TSSAA) did not violate Brentwood\xe2\x80\x99s First Amendment rights. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. Until today, Pickering governed limitations on the speech rights of government employees and contractors. The Court uproots Pickering from its context and applies it to speech by a private school that is a member of a private athletic association. The need to stretch Pickering to fit this case was occasioned by the Court when it held that TSSAA, a private organization, was a state actor. Brentwood Academy v. Tennessee Secondary School Athletic Assn., 531 U. S. 288 (2001) (Brentwood I). Because Brentwood I departed so dramatically from our earlier state-action cases, it is unsurprising that no First Amendment framework readily applies to this case. Rather than going through the bizarre exercise of extending obviously inapplicable First Amendment doctrine to these circumstances, I would simply overrule BrentwoodI.* See id., at 305\xe2\x80\x93315 (Thomas, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s extension of Pickering to this context is therefore unnecessary, but the principal opinion\xe2\x80\x99s application of Ohralik v. Ohio State Bar Assn., 436 U. S. 447 (1978), ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x936, is outright wrong. For the reasons expressed in Justice Kennedy\xe2\x80\x99s opinion concurring in part and concurring in the judgment, ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x932, Ohralik is a narrow rule addressed to a particular context that has no application to the facts of this case. For these reasons, I concur in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.
