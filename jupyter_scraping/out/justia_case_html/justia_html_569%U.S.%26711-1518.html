\n\t\t\t\tBULLOCK v. BANKCHAMPAIGN, N.\xc2\xa0A.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x931518.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 18, 2013\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 13, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner\xe2\x80\x99s father established a trust for the benefit of petitioner and his siblings, and made petitioner the (nonprofessional) trustee. The trust\xe2\x80\x99s sole asset was the father\xe2\x80\x99s life insurance policy. Petitioner borrowed funds from the trust three times; all borrowed funds were repaid with interest. His siblings obtained a judgment against him in state court for breach of fiduciary duty, though the court found no apparent malicious motive. The court imposed constructive trusts on certain of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s interests\xe2\x80\x94including his interest in the original trust\xe2\x80\x94in order to secure petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s payment of the judgment, with respondent serving as trustee for all of the trusts. Petitioner filed for bankruptcy. Respondent opposed discharge of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s state-court-imposed debts to the trust, and the Bankruptcy Court granted respondent summary judgment, holding that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s debts were not dischargeable pursuant to \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7523(a)(4), which provides that an individual cannot obtain a bankruptcy discharge from a debt \xe2\x80\x9cfor fraud or defalcation while acting in a fiduciary capacity, embezzlement, or larceny.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federal District Court and the Eleventh Circuit affirmed. The latter court reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation requires a known breach of fiduciary duty, such that the conduct can be characterized as objectively reckless.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The term \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d in the Bankruptcy Code includes a culpable state of mind requirement involving knowledge of, or gross recklessness in respect to, the improper nature of the fiduciary behavior. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x88\x929.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0While \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d has been an exception to discharge in a bankruptcy statute since 1867, legal authorities have long disagreed about its meaning. Broad definitions of the term in modern and older dictionaries are unhelpful, and courts of appeals have disagreed about what mental state must accompany defalcation\xe2\x80\x99s definition. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x88\x925.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0In Neal v. Clark, \n\n95 U.\xc2\xa0S. 704\n, this Court interpreted the term \xe2\x80\x9cfraud\xe2\x80\x9d in the Bankruptcy Code\xe2\x80\x99s exceptions to discharge to mean \xe2\x80\x9cpositive fraud, or fraud in fact, involving moral turpitude or intentional wrong, as does embezzlement; and not implied fraud, or fraud in law, which may exist without the imputation of bad faith or immorality.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 709. The term \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d should be treated similarly. Thus, where the conduct at issue does not involve bad faith, moral turpitude, or other immoral conduct, \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d requires an intentional wrong. An intentional wrong includes not only conduct that the fiduciary knows is improper but also reckless conduct of the kind that the criminal law often treats as the equivalent. Where actual knowledge of wrongdoing is lacking, conduct is considered as equivalent if, as set forth in the Model Penal Code, the fiduciary \xe2\x80\x9cconsciously disregards,\xe2\x80\x9d or is willfully blind to, \xe2\x80\x9ca substantial and unjustifiable risk\xe2\x80\x9d that his conduct will violate a fiduciary duty. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x88\x927.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Several considerations support this interpretation. First, statutory context strongly favors it. The canon noscitur a sociis argues for interpreting \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d as similar to its linguistic neighbors \xe2\x80\x9cembezzlement,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9clarceny,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cfraud,\xe2\x80\x9d which all require a showing of wrongful or felonious intent. See, e.g., Neal, supra, at 709. Second, the interpretation does not make the word identical to its statutory neighbors. \xe2\x80\x9cEmbezzlement\xe2\x80\x9d requires conversion, \xe2\x80\x9clarceny\xe2\x80\x9d requires taking and carrying away another\xe2\x80\x99s property, and \xe2\x80\x9cfraud\xe2\x80\x9d typically requires a false statement or omission; while \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d can encompass a breach of fiduciary obligation that involves neither conversion, nor taking and carrying away another\xe2\x80\x99s property, nor falsity. Third, the interpretation is consistent with the longstanding principle that \xe2\x80\x9cexceptions to discharge \xe2\x80\x98should be confined to those plainly expressed.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Kawaauhau v. Geiger, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57\n. It is also consistent with statutory exceptions to discharge that Congress normally confines to circumstances where strong, special policy considerations, such as the presence of fault, argue for preserving the debt, thereby benefiting, for example, a typically more honest creditor. See, e.g., \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A). Fourth, some Circuits have interpreted the statute similarly for many years without administrative or other difficulties. Finally, it is important to have a uniform interpretation of federal law, the choices are limited, and neither the parties nor the Government has presented strong considerations favoring a different interpretation. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x88\x929.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tRANDY CURTIS BULLOCK, PETITIONER v. BANKCHAMPAIGN, N. A.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[May 13, 2013]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 523(a)(4) of the Federal Bankruptcy Code provides that an individual cannot obtain a bankruptcy discharge from a debt \xe2\x80\x9cfor fraud or defalcation while acting in a fiduciary capacity, embezzlement, or larceny.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7523(a)(4). We here consider the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation.\xe2\x80\x9d We hold that it includes a culpable state of mind requirement akin to that which accompanies application of the other terms in the same statutory phrase. We describe that state of mind as one involving knowledge of, or gross recklessness in respect to, the improper nature of the relevant fiduciary behavior.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1978, the father of petitioner Randy Bullock established a trust for the benefit of his five children. He made petitioner the (nonprofessional) trustee; and he transferred to the trust a single asset, an insurance policy on his life. 670 F.\xc2\xa03d 1160, 1162 (CA11 2012); App. to Pet. for Cert. 33a. The trust instrument permitted the trustee to borrow funds from the insurer against the policy\xe2\x80\x99s value (which, in practice, was available at an insurance-company-determined 6% interest rate). Id., at 17a, 34a, 50a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1981, petitioner, at his father\xe2\x80\x99s request, borrowed money from the trust, paying the funds to his mother who used them to repay a debt to the father\xe2\x80\x99s business. In 1984, petitioner again borrowed funds from the trust, this time using the funds to pay for certificates of deposit, which he and his mother used to buy a mill. In 1990, petitioner once again borrowed funds, this time using the money to buy real property for himself and his mother. 670 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1162. Petitioner saw that all of the borrowed funds were repaid to the trust along with 6% interest. App. to Pet. for Cert. 17a, 45a, 50a; Brief for Petitioner 3; Brief for Respondent 2.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1999, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s brothers sued petitioner in Illinois state court. The state court held that petitioner had committed a breach of fiduciary duty. It explained that petitioner \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not appear to have had a malicious motive in borrowing funds from the trust\xe2\x80\x9d but nonetheless \xe2\x80\x9cwas clearly involved in self-dealing.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 45a, 52a. It ordered petitioner to pay the trust \xe2\x80\x9cthe benefits he received from his breaches\xe2\x80\x9d (along with costs and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees). Id., at 47a. The court imposed constructive trusts on petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s interests in the mill and the original trust, in order to secure petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s payment of its judgment, with respondent BankChampaign serving as trustee for all of the trusts. 670 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1162; App. to Pet. for Cert. 47a\xe2\x80\x9348a. After petitioner tried unsuccessfully to liquidate his interests in the mill and other constructive trust assets to obtain funds to make the court-ordered payment, petitioner filed for bankruptcy in federal court. Id., at 27a, 30a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBankChampaign opposed petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to obtain a bankruptcy discharge of his state-court-imposed debts to the trust. And the Bankruptcy Court granted summary judgment in the bank\xe2\x80\x99s favor. It held that the debts fell within \xc2\xa7523(a)(4)\xe2\x80\x99s exception \xe2\x80\x9cas a debt for defalcation while acting in a fiduciary capacity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 40a\xe2\x80\x9341a. Hence, they were not dischargeable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Federal District Court reviewed the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination. It said that it was \xe2\x80\x9cconvinced\xe2\x80\x9d that BankChampaign was \xe2\x80\x9cabusing its position of trust by fail- ing to liquidate the assets,\xe2\x80\x9d but it nonetheless affirmed the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Id., at 27a\xe2\x80\x9328a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn turn, the Court of Appeals affirmed the District Court. It wrote that \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation requires a known breach of a fiduciary duty, such that the conduct can be characterized as objectively reckless.\xe2\x80\x9d 670 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1166. And it found that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conduct satisfied this standard. Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner sought certiorari. In effect he has asked us to decide whether the bankruptcy term \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d applies \xe2\x80\x9cin the absence of any specific finding of ill intent or evidence of an ultimate loss of trust principal.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 1. See also Pet. for Cert. i. The lower courts have long disagreed about whether \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d includes a scienter requirement and, if so, what kind of scienter it requires. Compare In\xc2\xa0re Sherman, 658 F.\xc2\xa03d 1009, 1017 (CA9 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d includes \xe2\x80\x9ceven innocent acts of failure to fully account for money received in trust\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted)), with In\xc2\xa0re Uwimana, 274 F.\xc2\xa03d 806, 811 (CA4 2001) (defalcation occurs when \xe2\x80\x9cnegligence or even an in- nocent mistake .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. results in misappropriation\xe2\x80\x9d), with 670 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1166 (\xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation requires .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. conduct [that] can be characterized as objectively reckless\xe2\x80\x9d), and with In\xc2\xa0re Baylis, 313 F.\xc2\xa03d 9, 20 (CA1 2002) (\xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation requires something close to a showing of extreme recklessness\xe2\x80\x9d). In light of that disagreement, we granted the petition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress first included the term \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d as an exception to discharge in a federal bankruptcy statute in 1867. See id., at 17. And legal authorities have disagreed about its meaning almost ever since. Dictionary definitions of \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d are not particularly helpful. On the one hand, a law dictionary in use in 1867 defines the word \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe act of a defaulter,\xe2\x80\x9d which, in turn, it defines broadly as one \xe2\x80\x9cwho is deficient in his accounts, or fails in making his accounts correct.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 J. Bouvier, Law Dictionary 387, 388 (4th ed. 1852). See also 4 Oxford English Dictionary 369 (2d ed. 1989) (quoting an 1846 definition that defines the term as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a breach of trust by one who has charge or management of money\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Modern dictionaries contain similarly broad definitional language. Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary, for example, defines \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d first as \xe2\x80\x9cEmbezzlement,\xe2\x80\x9d but, second, as \xe2\x80\x9c[l]oosely, the failure to meet an obligation; a nonfraudulent default.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 479 (9th ed. 2009) (hereinafter Black\xe2\x80\x99s). See also American Heritage Dictionary 474 (5th ed. 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cTo misuse funds; embezzle\xe2\x80\x9d); 4 Oxford English Dictionary, supra, at 369 (\xe2\x80\x9cmonetary deficiency through breach of trust by one who has the management or charge of funds; a fraudulent deficiency in money matters\xe2\x80\x9d); Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 686 (2d ed. 1954) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn abstraction or misappropriation of money by one, esp. an officer or agent, having it in trust\xe2\x80\x9d); Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 590 (1986) (\xe2\x80\x9cmisappropriation of money in one\xe2\x80\x99s keeping\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn the other hand, an 1842 bankruptcy treatise warns that fiduciaries \xe2\x80\x9care not supposed to commit defalcation in the matter of their trust, without .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. at least such criminal negligence as admits of no excuse.\xe2\x80\x9d G. Bicknell, Commentary on the Bankrupt Law of 1841, Showing Its Operation and Effect 12 (2d ed. 1842). Modern dictionaries often accompany their broad definitions with illustrative terms such as \xe2\x80\x9cembezzle,\xe2\x80\x9d American Heritage Dictionary, supra, at 474, or \xe2\x80\x9cfraudulent deficiency,\xe2\x80\x9d 4 Oxford English Dictionary, supra, at 369. And the editor of Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary has written that the term should be read as limited to deficiencies that are \xe2\x80\x9cfraudulent\xe2\x80\x9d and which are \xe2\x80\x9cthe fault of someone put in trust of the money.\xe2\x80\x9d B. Garner, Modern American Usage 232 (3d ed. 2009) (emphasis added). \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSimilarly, courts of appeals have long disagreed about the mental state that must accompany the bankruptcy-related definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation.\xe2\x80\x9d Many years ago Judge Augustus Hand wrote that \xe2\x80\x9cthe misappropriation must be due to a known breach of the duty, and not to mere negligence or mistake.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Bernard, 87 F.\xc2\xa02d 705, 707 (CA2 1937). But Judge Learned Hand suggested that the term \xe2\x80\x9cmay have included innocent defaults.\xe2\x80\x9d Central Hanover Bank & Trust Co. v. Herbst, 93 F.\xc2\xa02d 510, 511 (CA2 1937) (emphasis added). A more modern treatise on trusts ends its discussion of the subject with a question mark. 4 A. Scott, W. Fratcher, & M. Ascher, Scott and Ascher on Trusts \xc2\xa724.26 p. 1797 (5th ed. 2007).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn resolving these differences, we note that this long- standing disagreement concerns state of mind, not whether \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d can cover a trustee\xe2\x80\x99s failure (as here) to make a trust more than whole. We consequently shall assume without deciding that the statutory term is broad enough to cover the latter type of conduct and answer only the \xe2\x80\x9cstate of mind\xe2\x80\x9d question.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe base our approach and our answer upon one of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents. In 1878, this Court interpreted the related statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cfraud\xe2\x80\x9d in the portion of the Bankruptcy Code laying out exceptions to discharge. Justice Harlan wrote for the Court:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[D]ebts created by \xe2\x80\x98fraud\xe2\x80\x99 are associated directly with debts created by \xe2\x80\x98embezzlement.\xe2\x80\x99 Such association justifies, if it does not imperatively require, the conclusion that the \xe2\x80\x98fraud\xe2\x80\x99 referred to in that section means positive fraud, or fraud in fact, involving moral turpitude or intentional wrong, as does embezzlement; and not implied fraud, or fraud in law, which may exist without the imputation of bad faith or immorality.\xe2\x80\x9d Neal v. Clark, \n\n95 U.\xc2\xa0S. 704, \n709 (1878)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tWe believe that the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d should be treated similarly.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, where the conduct at issue does not involve bad faith, moral turpitude, or other immoral conduct, the term requires an intentional wrong. We include as intentional not only conduct that the fiduciary knows is improper but also reckless conduct of the kind that the criminal law often treats as the equivalent. Thus, we include reckless conduct of the kind set forth in the Model Penal Code. Where actual knowledge of wrongdoing is lacking, we consider conduct as equivalent if the fiduciary \xe2\x80\x9cconsciously disregards\xe2\x80\x9d (or is willfully blind to) \xe2\x80\x9ca substantial and unjustifiable risk\xe2\x80\x9d that his conduct will turn out to violate a fiduciary duty. ALI, Model Penal Code \xc2\xa72.02(2)(c), p. 226 (1985). See id., \xc2\xa72.02 Comment 9, at 248 (explaining that the Model Penal Code\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cknowledge\xe2\x80\x9d was designed to include \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98wilful blindness\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). That risk \xe2\x80\x9cmust be of such a nature and degree that, considering the nature and purpose of the actor\xe2\x80\x99s conduct and the cir- cumstances known to him, its disregard involves a gross deviation from the standard of conduct that a law-abiding person would observe in the actor\xe2\x80\x99s situation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa72.02(2)(c), at 226 (emphasis added). Cf. Ernst & Ernst v. Hochfelder, \n\n425 U.\xc2\xa0S. 185\n, n.\xc2\xa012 (1976) (defining scienter for securities law purposes as \xe2\x80\x9ca mental state embracing intent to deceive, manipulate, or defraud\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSeveral considerations lead us to interpret the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d in this way. First, as Justice Harlan pointed out in Neal, statutory context strongly favors this interpretation. Applying the canon of interpretation noscitur a sociis, the Court there looked to fraud\xe2\x80\x99s linguistic neighbor, \xe2\x80\x9cembezzlement.\xe2\x80\x9d It found that both terms refer to different forms of generally similar conduct. It wrote that both are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98ejusdem generis,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of the same kind, and that both are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98referable to the same subject-matter.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 95 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 709. Moreover, embezzlement requires a showing of wrongful intent. Ibid. (noting that embezzlement \xe2\x80\x9cinvolv[es] moral turpitude or intentional wrong\xe2\x80\x9d). See Moore v. United States, \n\n160 U.\xc2\xa0S. 268\n\xe2\x80\x93270 (1895) (describing embezzlement and larceny as requiring \xe2\x80\x9cfelonious intent\xe2\x80\x9d). See also, e.g., W. LaFave, Criminal Law \xc2\xa719.6(a), p. 995 (5th ed. 2010) (\xe2\x80\x9cintent to deprive\xe2\x80\x9d is part of embezzlement). Hence, the Court concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cfraud\xe2\x80\x9d must require an equivalent showing. Neal, supra, at 709. Neal has been the law for more than a century. And here, the additional neighbors (\xe2\x80\x9clarceny\xe2\x80\x9d and, as defined in Neal, \xe2\x80\x9cfraud\xe2\x80\x9d) mean that the canon noscitur a sociis argues even more strongly for similarly interpreting the similar statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, this interpretation does not make the word identical to its statutory neighbors. See Babbitt v. Sweet Home Chapter, Communities for Great Ore., \n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 687, \n698 (1995)\n (noting \xe2\x80\x9c[a] reluctance to treat statutory terms as surplusage\xe2\x80\x9d). As commonly used, \xe2\x80\x9cembezzlement\xe2\x80\x9d requires conversion, and \xe2\x80\x9clarceny\xe2\x80\x9d requires taking and carrying away another\xe2\x80\x99s property. See LaFave, Criminal Law \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa719.2, 19.5 (larceny); id., \xc2\xa719.6 (embezzlement). \xe2\x80\x9cFraud\xe2\x80\x9d typically requires a false statement or omission. See id., \xc2\xa719.7 (discussing fraud in the context of false pretenses). \xe2\x80\x9cDefalcation,\xe2\x80\x9d as commonly used (hence as Congress might have understood it), can encompass a breach of fiduciary obligation that involves neither conversion, nor taking and carrying away another\xe2\x80\x99s property, nor falsity. Black\xe2\x80\x99s 479. See, e.g., In\xc2\xa0re Frankel, 77 B.\xc2\xa0R. 401 (Bkrtcy. Ct. WDNY 1987) (finding a breach of fiduciary duty and defalcation based on an unreasonable sale of assets).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor are embezzlement, larceny, and fiduciary fraud simply special cases of defalcation as so defined. The statutory provision makes clear that the first two terms apply outside of the fiduciary context; and \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation,\xe2\x80\x9d unlike \xe2\x80\x9cfraud,\xe2\x80\x9d may be used to refer to nonfraudulent breaches of fiduciary duty. Black\xe2\x80\x99s 479.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThird, the interpretation is consistent with the long-standing principle that \xe2\x80\x9cexceptions to discharge \xe2\x80\x98should be confined to those plainly expressed.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Kawaauhau v. Geiger, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57, \n62 (1998)\n (quoting Gleason v. Thaw, \n\n236 U.\xc2\xa0S. 558, \n562 (1915)\n). See Local Loan Co. v. Hunt, \n\n292 U.\xc2\xa0S. 234, \n244 (1934)\n; Neal, supra, at 709. It is also consistent with a set of statutory exceptions that Congress normally confines to circumstances where strong, special policy considerations, such as the presence of fault, argue for preserving the debt, thereby benefiting, for example, a typically more honest creditor. See, e.g., 11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7523(a)(2)(A), (a)(2)(B), (a)(6), (a)(9) (fault). See also, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7523(a)(1), (a)(7), (a)(14), (a)(14A) (taxes); \xc2\xa7523(a)(8) (educational loans); \xc2\xa7523(a)(15) (spousal and child support). In the absence of fault, it is difficult to find strong policy reasons favoring a broader exception here, at least in respect to those whom a scienter requirement will most likely help, namely nonprofessional trustees, perhaps administering small family trusts potentially immersed in intrafamily arguments that are difficult to evaluate in terms of comparative fault.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFourth, as far as the briefs before us reveal, at least some Circuits have interpreted the statute similarly for many years without administrative, or other practical, difficulties. Baylis, 313 F.\xc2\xa03d 9. See also In re Hyman, 502 F.\xc2\xa03d 61, 69 (CA2 2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis [scienter] standard .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. also has the virtue of ease of application since the courts and litigants have reference to a robust body of securities law examining what these terms mean\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, it is important to have a uniform interpreta- tion of federal law, the choices are limited, and neither the parties nor the Government has presented us with strong considerations favoring a different interpretation. In addition to those we have already discussed, the Government has pointed to the fact that in 1970 Congress rewrote the statute, eliminating the word \xe2\x80\x9cmisappropriation\xe2\x80\x9d and placing the term \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d (previously in a different exemption provision) alongside its present three neighbors. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 16\xe2\x80\x9317. The Government believes that these changes support reading \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation\xe2\x80\x9d without a scienter requirement. But one might argue, with equal plausibility, that the changes reflect a decision to make certain that courts would read in similar ways \xe2\x80\x9cdefalcation,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfraud,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cembezzlement,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clarceny.\xe2\x80\x9d In fact, we believe the 1970 changes are inconclusive.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case the Court of Appeals applied a standard of \xe2\x80\x9cobjectiv[e] reckless[ness]\xe2\x80\x9d to facts presented at summary judgment. 670 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1166. We consequently remand the case to permit the court to determine whether further proceedings are needed and, if so, to apply the heightened standard that we have set forth. For these reasons we vacate the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t
