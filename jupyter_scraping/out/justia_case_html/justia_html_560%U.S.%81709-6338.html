DILLON v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x936338.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 30, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 17, 2010

\n

In 1993, petitioner Dillon was convicted of, inter alia, crack and powder cocaine offenses, which produced a base offense level of 38 and a Guidelines range of 262-to-327 months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment. The court sentenced him at the bottom of the range for those counts. After the Sentencing Commission amended the Guidelines to reduce the base offense level associated with each quantity of crack cocaine, USSG Supp. App. C, Amdt. 706, and made that amendment retroactive, USSG Supp. App. C, Amdt. 713, Dillon moved for a sentence reduction under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). That provision authorizes a\ndistrict court to reduce an otherwise final sentence pursuant to a Guidelines amendment if a reduction is consistent with the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s policy statements. The relevant policy statement, USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10, precludes a court from reducing a sentence \xe2\x80\x9cto a term that is less than the minimum of the amended guidelines range\xe2\x80\x9d except in limited circumstances. In addition to the two-level reduction authorized by the amendment, Dillon sought a variance below the amended Guidelines range, contending that United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220, authorized the exercise of such discretion. The District Court imposed a sentence at the bottom of the revised range but declined to grant a further reduction. Finding Booker inapplicable to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings, the court concluded that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s directives in \xc2\xa71B1.10 constrained it to impose a sentence within the amended Guidelines range. The Third Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Booker\xe2\x80\x99s holdings do not apply to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings and therefore do not require treating \xc2\xa71B1.10(b) as advisory. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and narrow scope belie Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of proceedings under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) as \xe2\x80\x9cresentencing\xe2\x80\x9d proceedings governed by the same principles as other sentencing proceedings. Instead, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) authorizes only a limited adjustment to an otherwise final sentence. This conclusion is further supported by the substantial role Congress gave the Commission with respect to sentence-modification proceedings, charging it with determining whether and to what extent a Guidelines amendment will be retroactive, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(u), and authorizing a court to grant a reduction under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) only \xe2\x80\x9cif [it] is consistent with applicable policy statements issued by the Sentencing Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 3582(c)(2) establishes a two-step inquiry: A court must (1) determine the scope of the reduction, if any, authorized by \xc2\xa71B1.10, and then (2) consider whether the authorized reduction is warranted according to the applicable \xc2\xa73553(a) factors. At step one, the court must follow the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s instructions in \xc2\xa71B1.10 to impose a term of imprisonment within the amended Guidelines range unless the sentencing court originally imposed a below-Guidelines sentence. \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2). Because reference to \xc2\xa73553(a) is appropriate only at step two, that provision does not transform \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings into plenary resentencing proceedings. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Given \xc2\xa73582(c)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s limited scope and purpose, proceedings under that section do not implicate Booker. The section represents a congressional act of lenity intended to give prisoners the benefit of later enacted adjustments to the judgments reflected in the Guidelines. Taking the original sentence as given, any facts found by a judge at a \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceeding do not serve to increase the prescribed range of punishment; instead, they affect only the judge\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of discretion within that range. That exercise does not contravene the Sixth Amendment, even if it is informed by judge-found facts. Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466, 481. Thus, Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment rights were not violated by the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s adherence to \xc2\xa71B1.10\xe2\x80\x99s instruction to consider a reduction only within the amended Guidelines range. Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Booker\xe2\x80\x99s remedial opinion nonetheless requires the Guidelines to be treated as advisory in such proceedings is unpersuasive given that proceedings under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) are readily distinguishable from other sentencing proceedings. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Also rejected is Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the District Court should have corrected other mistakes in his original sentence, namely, a Booker error resulting from the initial sentencing court\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of the Guidelines as mandatory and an alleged error in the calculation of his criminal-history category. Because those aspects of Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s sentence were not affected by the crack-cocaine Guidelines amendment, they are outside the scope of the \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceeding, and the District Court properly declined to address them. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

572 F.\xc2\xa03d 146, affirmed.

PERCY DILLON, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

[June 17, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A federal court generally \xe2\x80\x9cmay not modify a term of imprisonment once it has been imposed.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c). Congress has provided an exception to that rule \xe2\x80\x9cin the case of a defendant who has been sentenced to a term of imprisonment based on a sentencing range that has subsequently been lowered by the Sentencing Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). In those circumstances, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) authorizes a court to reduce the term of imprisonment \xe2\x80\x9cif such a reduction is consistent with\xe2\x80\x9d applicable Commission policy statements. The policy statement governing \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings instructs courts not to reduce a term of imprisonment below the minimum of an amended sentencing range except to the extent the original term of imprisonment was below the range then applicable. See United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2) (Nov. 2009) (USSG). This case presents the question whether our decision in United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220 (2005), which rendered the Guidelines advisory to remedy the Sixth Amendment problems associated with a mandatory sentencing regime, requires treating \xc2\xa71B1.10(b) as nonbinding. We conclude that Booker does not demand that result.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sentencing Reform Act of 1984 (SRA or Act), 98 Stat. 1987, established the Sentencing Commission and authorized it to promulgate Sentencing Guidelines and to issue policy statements regarding the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x99 application. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7991, 994(a). The Act also charged the Commission with periodically reviewing and revising the Guidelines. See \xc2\xa7994(o). When a revision reduces the Guidelines range for a given offense, the Commission must determine \xe2\x80\x9cin what circumstances and by what amount the sentences of prisoners serving terms of imprisonment for the offense may be reduced.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7994(u).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As enacted, the SRA made the Sentencing Guidelines binding. See Booker, 543 U. S., at 233\xe2\x80\x93234. Except in limited circumstances, district courts lacked discretion to depart from the Guidelines range. See Burns v. United States, 501 U. S. 129, 133 (1991). Under that regime, facts found by a judge by a preponderance of the evidence often increased the mandatory Guidelines range and permitted the judge to impose a sentence greater than that supported by the facts established by the jury verdict or guilty plea. See Booker, 543 U. S., at 235. We held in Booker that treating the Guidelines as mandatory in these circumstances violated the Sixth Amendment right of criminal defendants to be tried by a jury and to have every element of an offense proved by the Government beyond a reasonable doubt. Id., at 243\xe2\x80\x93244.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To remedy the constitutional problem, we rendered the Guidelines advisory by invalidating two provisions of the SRA: 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(b)(1) (2000 ed., Supp. IV), which generally required a sentencing court to impose a sentence within the applicable Guidelines range, and \xc2\xa73742(e) (2000 ed. and Supp. IV), which prescribed the standard of review on appeal, including de novo review of Guidelines departures. 543 U. S., at 259. \xe2\x80\x9cWith these two sections excised (and statutory cross-references to the two sections consequently invalidated),\xe2\x80\x9d we held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe remainder of the Act satisfies the Court\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Booker thus left intact other provisions of the SRA, including those giving the Commission authority to revise the Guidelines, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(o) (2006 ed.), and to determine when and to what extent a revision will be retroactive, \xc2\xa7994(u).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With respect to drug-trafficking offenses, the Sentencing Guidelines establish a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s base offense level according to the type and weight of the drug. See USSG \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72D1.1(a), (c). When the Commission first promulgated the Guidelines in 1987, it adopted the 100-to-1 ratio selected by Congress in setting mandatory minimum sentences in the Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986, 100 Stat. 3207. Under that framework, the Commission \xe2\x80\x9ctreated every gram of crack cocaine as the equivalent of 100 grams of powder cocaine.\xe2\x80\x9d Kimbrough v. United States, 552 U. S. 85, 96 (2007). The Commission later sought to alleviate the disparity produced by this ratio. After several failed attempts at reform, see id., at 99, the Commission in 2007 amended the Guidelines to reduce by two levels the base offense level associated with each quantity of crack cocaine. See USSG Supp. App. C, Amdt. 706 (effective Nov. 1, 2007). In 2008, the Commission made that amendment retroactive. See id., Amdt. 713 (effective Mar. 3, 2008).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When the Commission makes a Guidelines amendment retroactive, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) authorizes a district court to reduce an otherwise final sentence that is based on the amended provision. Any reduction must be consistent with applicable policy statements issued by the Sentencing Commission. The relevant policy statement, USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10, instructs courts proceeding under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) to substitute the amended Guidelines range while \xe2\x80\x9cleav[ing] all other guideline application decisions unaffected.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(1).[Footnote 1] Under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), a court may then grant a reduction within the amended Guidelines range if it determines that one is warranted \xe2\x80\x9cafter considering the factors set forth in section 3553(a) to the extent that they are applicable.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 2] Except in limited circumstances, however, \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2)(A) forecloses a court acting under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) from reducing a sentence \xe2\x80\x9cto a term that is less than the minimum of the amended guideline range.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A jury convicted petitioner Percy Dillon in 1993 of conspiracy to distribute and to possess with the intent to distribute more than 500 grams of powder cocaine and more than 50 grams of crack cocaine in violation of 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7846, possession with the intent to distribute more than 500 grams of powder cocaine in violation of \xc2\xa7841(a)(1), and use of a firearm during and in relation to a drug-trafficking offense in violation of 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1). Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s convictions exposed him to a statutory sentencing range of 10 years to life for the conspiracy, 5-to-40 years for cocaine possession, and a mandatory minimum sentence of 5 years for the firearm offense, to be served consecutively to the sentence for the drug offenses.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At sentencing, the District Court made additional findings of fact and concluded that Dillon was responsible for 1.5 kilograms of crack and 1.6 kilograms of powder cocaine. Under USSG \xc2\xa72D1.1, those drug quantities produced a base offense level of 38. After offsetting adjustments for acceptance of responsibility, \xc2\xa73E1.1, and reckless endangerment during flight, \xc2\xa73C1.2, Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s total offense level remained 38. Coupled with a criminal-history category of II,[Footnote 3] that offense level produced a then-mandatory Guidelines range of 262-to-327 months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment for the drug counts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court sentenced Dillon at the bottom of the Guidelines range for those counts, followed by a mandatory 60-month sentence for the firearm count, for a total sentence of 322 months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment. At Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing, the court described the term of imprisonment as \xe2\x80\x9centirely too high for the crime [Dillon] committed.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 13. Perceiving no basis for departing from the then-mandatory Sentencing Guidelines, the District Court felt constrained to impose a sentence within the prescribed range. The Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit affirmed Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s convictions and sentence on appeal. See 100 F.\xc2\xa03d 949 (1996).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the Sentencing Commission made the amendment to the crack-cocaine Guidelines retroactive in 2008, Dillon filed a pro se motion for a sentence reduction pursuant to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). In the motion, Dillon asked the court to grant not just the two-level reduction authorized by the amendment but also a further reduction consistent with the sentencing factors found in \xc2\xa73553(a). Based largely on his postsentencing conduct, including his determined pursuit of educational and community-outreach opportunities, Dillon contended that a variance from the amended Guidelines range was warranted in his case. He further urged that, after Booker, the court was authorized to grant such a variance because the amended Guidelines range was advisory notwithstanding any contrary statement in \xc2\xa71B1.10.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court reduced Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s sentence to 270 months\xe2\x80\x94the term at the bottom of the revised Guidelines range.[Footnote 4] But the court declined to go further. Concluding that the sentencing proceedings at issue in Booker are readily distinguishable from those under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), the court found Booker\xe2\x80\x99s holdings inapplicable to the instant proceeding and accordingly held that it lacked authority to impose a sentence inconsistent with \xc2\xa71B1.10.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Third Circuit affirmed. 572 F.\xc2\xa03d 146, 150 (2009). The court noted that \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) is codified in a different section than the provisions invalidated in Booker and contains no cross-reference to those provisions. Finding no other indication that Booker \xe2\x80\x9cobviate[d] the congressional directive in \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) that a sentence reduction pursuant to that section be consistent with Sentencing Commission policy statements,\xe2\x80\x9d 572 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 149, the Third Circuit held that \xc2\xa71B1.10 is binding. It therefore agreed that the District Court lacked authority to reduce Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s sentence below the amended Guidelines range.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to consider Booker\xe2\x80\x99s applicability to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings. 558 U. S. ___ (2009).

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[A] judgment of conviction that includes [a sentence of imprisonment] constitutes a final judgment\xe2\x80\x9d and may not be modified by a district court except in limited circumstances. \xc2\xa73582(b). Section 3582(c)(2) establishes an exception to the general rule of finality \xe2\x80\x9cin the case of a defendant who has been sentenced to a term of imprisonment based on a sentencing range that has subsequently been lowered by the Sentencing Commission pursuant to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(o)\xe2\x80\x9d and made retroactive pursuant to \xc2\xa7994(u). In such cases, Congress has authorized courts to \xe2\x80\x9creduce the term of imprisonment, after considering the factors set forth in section 3553(a) to the extent that they are applicable, if such a reduction is consistent with applicable policy statements issued by the Sentencing Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73582(c)(2).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Characterizing proceedings under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) as \xe2\x80\x9cresentencing\xe2\x80\x9d proceedings, Dillon contends that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no practical or functional difference between a resentencing pursuant to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) and any other resentencing.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 18. Accordingly, Dillon urges, the same principles that govern other sentencing proceedings likewise govern \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings, and courts have authority under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) to vary from the revised Guidelines range consistent with \xc2\xa73553(a), see Kimbrough, 552 U. S., at 101. Dillon cites as support for this view \xc2\xa73582(c)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s instruction to consider the factors in \xc2\xa73553(a) in determining whether a sentence reduction is warranted. Under Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s approach, Booker would preclude the Commission from issuing a policy statement that generally forecloses below-Guidelines sentences at \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings, as USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10 purports to do. Dillon thus asks us to excise the mandatory language of \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2)(A) and treat that provision as advisory, just as we did the offending statutory provisions in Booker.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The language of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) belies Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of proceedings under that section. By its terms, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) does not authorize a sentencing or resentencing proceeding. Instead, it provides for the \xe2\x80\x9cmodif[ication of] a term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d by giving courts the power to \xe2\x80\x9creduce\xe2\x80\x9d an otherwise final sentence in circumstances specified by the Commission. Compare 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(a)(2)(C) (referring to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) as a \xe2\x80\x9csentence modification provisio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d), with 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73742(f) (authorizing courts of appeals to remand \xe2\x80\x9cfor further sentencing\xe2\x80\x9d upon a finding of error), and \xc2\xa73742(g) (establishing the terms of \xe2\x80\x9csentencing upon remand\xe2\x80\x9d and describing the proceeding as a \xe2\x80\x9cresentenc[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d (capitalization omitted)). It is also notable that the provision applies only to a limited class of prisoners\xe2\x80\x94namely, those whose sentence was based on a sentencing range subsequently lowered by the Commission. Section 3582(c)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s text, together with its narrow scope, shows that Congress intended to authorize only a limited adjustment to an otherwise final sentence and not a plenary resentencing proceeding.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The substantial role Congress gave the Commission with respect to sentence-modification proceedings further supports this conclusion. The SRA charges the Commission both with deciding whether to amend the Guidelines, \xc2\xa7994(o), and with determining whether and to what extent an amendment will be retroactive, \xc2\xa7994(u).[Footnote 5] A court\xe2\x80\x99s power under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) thus depends in the first instance on the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s decision not just to amend the Guidelines but to make the amendment retroactive. The court is also constrained by the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s statements dictating \xe2\x80\x9cby what amount\xe2\x80\x9d the sentence of a prisoner serving a term of imprisonment affected by the amendment \xe2\x80\x9cmay be reduced.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7994(u); see also Braxton v. United States, 500 U. S. 344, 348 (1991) (noting that the Commission implemented that power through \xc2\xa71B1.10).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Read in this context, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xc2\xa73553(a) does not undermine our narrow view of proceedings under the former provision. Section 3582(c)(2) instructs a district court to \xe2\x80\x9cconside[r] the factors set forth in section 3553(a) to the extent that they are applicable,\xe2\x80\x9d but it authorizes a reduction on that basis only \xe2\x80\x9cif such a reduction is consistent with applicable policy statements issued by the Sentencing Commission\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94namely, \xc2\xa71B1.10. The statute thus establishes a two-step inquiry. A court must first determine that a reduction is consistent with \xc2\xa71B1.10 before it may consider whether the authorized reduction is warranted, either in whole or in part, according to the factors set forth in \xc2\xa73553(a).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Following this two-step approach, a district court proceeding under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) does not impose a new sentence in the usual sense. At step one, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) requires the court to follow the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s instructions in \xc2\xa71B1.10 to determine the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility for a sentence modification and the extent of the reduction authorized. Specifically, \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(1) requires the court to begin by \xe2\x80\x9cdetermin[ing] the amended guideline range that would have been applicable to the defendant\xe2\x80\x9d had the relevant amendment been in effect at the time of the initial sentencing. \xe2\x80\x9cIn making such determination, the court shall substitute only the amendments listed in subsection (c) for the corresponding guideline provisions that were applied when the defendant was sentenced and shall leave all other guideline application decisions unaffected.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consistent with the limited nature of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings, \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2) also confines the extent of the reduction authorized. Courts generally may \xe2\x80\x9cnot reduce the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s term of imprisonment under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) \xe2\x80\xa6 to a term that is less than the minimum of the amended guideline range\xe2\x80\x9d produced by the substitution. \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2)(A). Only if the sentencing court originally imposed a term of imprisonment below the Guidelines range does \xc2\xa71B1.10 authorize a court proceeding under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) to impose a term \xe2\x80\x9ccomparably\xe2\x80\x9d below the amended range. \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2)(B).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At step two of the inquiry, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) instructs a court to consider any applicable \xc2\xa73553(a) factors and determine whether, in its discretion, the reduction authorized by reference to the policies relevant at step one is warranted in whole or in part under the particular circumstances of the case. Because reference to \xc2\xa73553(a) is appropriate only at the second step of this circumscribed inquiry, it cannot serve to transform the proceedings under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) into plenary resentencing proceedings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This understanding of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) as a narrow exception to the rule of finality finds further support outside the statute. Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 43 requires that a defendant be present at \xe2\x80\x9csentencing,\xe2\x80\x9d see Rule 43(a)(3), but it excludes from that requirement proceedings that \xe2\x80\x9cinvolv[e] the correction or reduction of sentence under Rule 35 or 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c),\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 43(b)(4). Like \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), Rule 35 delineates a limited set of circumstances in which a sentence may be corrected or reduced. Specifically, it authorizes a court to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect a sentence that resulted from arithmetical, technical, or other clear error\xe2\x80\x9d within 14 days after sentencing, Rule 35(a), and it authorizes a reduction for substantial assistance on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s motion, Rule 35(b). Rule 43 therefore sets the proceedings authorized by \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) and Rule 35 apart from other sentencing proceedings.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given the limited scope and purpose of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), we conclude that proceedings under that section do not implicate the interests identified in Booker. Notably, the sentence-modification proceedings authorized by \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) are not constitutionally compelled. We are aware of no constitutional requirement of retroactivity that entitles defendants sentenced to a term of imprisonment to the benefit of subsequent Guidelines amendments. Rather, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) represents a congressional act of lenity intended to give prisoners the benefit of later enacted adjustments to the judgments reflected in the Guidelines.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Viewed that way, proceedings under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) do not implicate the Sixth Amendment right to have essential facts found by a jury beyond a reasonable doubt. Taking the original sentence as given, any facts found by a judge at a \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceeding do not serve to increase the prescribed range of punishment; instead, they affect only the judge\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of discretion within that range. \xe2\x80\x9c[J]udges in this country have long exercised discretion of this nature in imposing sentence within [established] limits in the individual case,\xe2\x80\x9d and the exercise of such discretion does not contravene the Sixth Amendment even if it is informed by judge-found facts. Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466, 481 (2000) (emphasis in original). Because \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings give judges no more than this circumscribed discretion, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no encroachment here by the judge upon facts historically found by the jury, nor any threat to the jury\xe2\x80\x99s domain as a bulwark at trial between the State and the accused.\xe2\x80\x9d Oregon v. Ice, 555 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 8). Accordingly, Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment rights were not violated by the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s adherence to the instruction in \xc2\xa71B1.10 to consider a reduction only within the amended Guidelines range.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dillon contends that, even if \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) does not implicate the constitutional rights vindicated in Booker\xe2\x80\x94something the dissent appears to concede\xe2\x80\x94the remedial aspect of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision applies to proceedings under that section and requires that the Guidelines be treated as advisory in such proceedings just as they are in other sentencing proceedings. In support of his position, Dillon invokes the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning in United States v. Hicks, 472 F.\xc2\xa03d 1167, 1170 (2007).[Footnote 6] Relying on our rejection in Booker of a remedy that would have made the Guidelines advisory only in certain cases\xe2\x80\x94namely, when treating them as binding would run afoul of the Sixth Amendment, see 543 U. S., at 265\xe2\x80\x93267\xe2\x80\x94the Ninth Circuit held that Booker precludes treating the Guidelines as mandatory for purposes of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) and advisory in other contexts, see Hicks, 472 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1171\xe2\x80\x931172.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This argument is unpersuasive. The incomplete remedy we rejected in Booker would have required courts to treat the Guidelines differently in similar proceedings, leading potentially to unfair results and considerable administrative challenges. See 543 U. S., at 266. As already explained, the sentence-modification proceedings authorized by \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) are readily distinguishable from other sentencing proceedings. Given the substantially different purpose of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) and the circumscribed nature of proceedings under that section, requiring courts to honor \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s instruction not to depart from the amended Guidelines range at such proceedings will create none of the confusion or unfairness that led us in Booker to reject the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument for a partial fix.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s contrary conclusion rests on two erroneous premises. First, the dissent ignores the fundamental differences between sentencing and sentence-modification proceedings and asserts without explanation that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing turns on\xe2\x80\x9d the distinction between them. Post, at 11. For the reasons stated above, the statutory differences between the proceedings are highly significant. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the dissent gives short shrift to the fact that, after Booker, the Commission retains at least some authority to bind the courts. Through \xc2\xa7994(u), Congress charged the Commission with determining \xe2\x80\x9cin what circumstances and by what amount\xe2\x80\x9d the sentences of prisoners affected by Guidelines amendments \xe2\x80\x9cmay be reduced.\xe2\x80\x9d No one disputes that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s retroactivity determinations made pursuant to the first part of that authorization are binding. See post, at 17, and n.\xc2\xa08. This aspect of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s power emphatically undermines the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s insistence that the Guidelines after Booker are \xe2\x80\x9ccompletely advisory.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 9. Moreover, while the dissent criticizes our approach for leaving the Commission with only the \xe2\x80\x9cthe tiniest sliver of lawmaking power,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 11, the dissent would leave the Commission with an even smaller and less explicable sliver by dissecting the authority granted by \xc2\xa7994(u).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For all of these reasons, we conclude that neither Booker\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional nor remedial holding requires the result that Dillon urges.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dillon additionally contends that the District Court erred in failing to correct two mistakes in his original sentence. Under his view of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), a district court is required to recalculate a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. Thus, any mistakes committed at the initial sentencing are imposed anew if they are not corrected. According to Dillon, the District Court in the instant proceeding should have corrected the Booker error that resulted from the initial sentencing court\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of the Guidelines as mandatory, and it should have adjusted his criminal-history category, which he now contends was erroneously inflated.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s arguments in this regard are premised on the same misunderstanding of the scope of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings dispelled above. As noted, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) does not authorize a resentencing. Instead, it permits a sentence reduction within the narrow bounds established by the Commission. The relevant policy statement instructs that a court proceeding under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) \xe2\x80\x9cshall substitute\xe2\x80\x9d the amended Guidelines range for the initial range \xe2\x80\x9cand shall leave all other guideline application decisions unaffected.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(1). Because the aspects of his sentence that Dillon seeks to correct were not affected by the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s amendment to \xc2\xa72D1.1, they are outside the scope of the proceeding authorized by \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), and the District Court properly declined to address them.

PERCY DILLON, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit

\n

[June 17, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When sentencing petitioner Percy Dillon for crack cocaine-related offenses in 1993, the District Court stated that the punishment Dillon received was \xe2\x80\x9centirely too high for the crime [he] committed.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 13. Bound by a sentencing regime that was mandatory at the time, the judge had no choice but to sentence Dillon to 322 months of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x94nearly 27 years behind bars. The judge later explained that, were it within his discretion, he would have sentenced Dillon to 5 years of imprisonment. Id., at 62. Had Dillon been sentenced after our decision in United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220 (2005), the judge would have had that discretion. Instead, the District Court was compelled to mete out a punishment that it believed to be grossly disproportionate to the offense and, therefore, \xe2\x80\x9cgreater than necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to meet the goals of our criminal justice system, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The punishment Dillon received was so high, in part, because at the time of his conviction our drug laws punished crack cocaine offenses 100 times more severely than powder cocaine offenses. In 2007, as the Court explains, see ante, at 3, the United States Sentencing Commission (Commission) proposed a partial fix to this disparity, lowering its Guidelines Manual[Footnote 1] ranges for crack cocaine offenses to a 20:1 ratio. See United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual Supp. App. C, Amdt. 706 (Nov. 2009) (USSG) (effective Nov. 1, 2007). Pursuant to its congressional mandate, see 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(u), the Commission made this change retroactive for those individuals, like Dillon, who were still serving sentences for crack cocaine offenses. See USSG Supp. App. C, Amdt. 713 (effective Mar. 3, 2008).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although Dillon does not have a constitutional right to obtain the benefit of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s change, it is undisputed that he has a statutory right to do so. Under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), a federal prisoner \xe2\x80\x9cwho has been sentenced to a term of imprisonment based on a sentencing range that has subsequently been lowered\xe2\x80\x9d by the Commission may seek a sentence reduction, but only after the court \xe2\x80\x9cconsider[s] the factors set forth in section 3553(a),\xe2\x80\x9d and only \xe2\x80\x9cif such a reduction is consistent with applicable policy statements issued by the Sentencing Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d Dillon sought such relief. His 322-month sentence was reduced to a 270-month sentence\xe2\x80\x94still 17 years more than the sentencing judge thought necessary as an initial matter.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In his \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceeding, Dillon alleged that his circumstances warranted an additional reduction in light of the fact that his sentence was \xe2\x80\x9cgreater than necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to effectuate the goals of our sentencing system, \xc2\xa73553(a). He also emphasized that he has been a model inmate during his 17 years in federal prison. Once again, however, the District Court felt that its hands were tied, this time because USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2) purports to place a mandatory limit on the extent of any sentence reduction that a court may order pursuant to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). And so, giving the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s statement the effect of law, the District Court denied Dillon further relief.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today, the Court holds that in this one limited nook of sentencing law, the Commission retains the power to bind judges that we struck down in Booker. In my view, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to treat the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s policy statement as a mandatory command rather than an advisory recommendation is unfaithful to Booker. It is also on dubious constitutional footing, as it permits the Commission to exercise a barely constrained form of lawmaking authority. And it is manifestly unjust. I would therefore hold that in the context of a \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) sentence modification proceeding, the District Court may consider, but is not bound by, any applicable policy statements promulgated by the Commission. In other words, I would apply Booker\xe2\x80\x99s remedial holding to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although I did not join Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s remedial opinion for the Court in Booker, it is nevertheless clear to me that its scope applies to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As an initial matter, it is of no moment that the Booker Court did not excise any portion of \xc2\xa73582 when crafting its remedy. At the time, there was nothing in \xc2\xa73582(c)(2)\xe2\x80\x94separate and apart from the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x99 general mandatory nature\xe2\x80\x94that would have limited the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion in a \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceeding. There was, consequently, nothing that needed excising. Relief under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) is available if it is \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent with\xe2\x80\x9d the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s related policy statement. And when we decided Booker, the particular policy statement at issue, \xc2\xa71B1.10(b), had no explicit binding effect.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Prior to our decision in Booker, the Guidelines were mandatory only by virtue of congressional mandate, and not by virtue of Commission decree. See 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(b)(1). Following Booker, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s policy statement in \xc2\xa71B1.10 took effect in March 2008. That statement, I will explain more fully in Part II, infra, is now the only source of binding authority in \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings, as it purports to have the effect of reinstating a mandatory Guidelines regime within the context of a sentence modification proceeding. It is now the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s policy statement, and not an explicit congressional mandate, that makes the Guidelines ranges binding under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a matter of textual analysis, divorced from judicial precedent, it is certainly reasonable for the Court to find that the Commission can set mandatory limits on sentence reductions under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). But it is a mistake, in my view, to take such a narrow approach to the question presented by this case. The Court has turned a blind eye to the fundamental sea-change that was our decision in Booker.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is useful to put Booker in context. During the deliberations that led to the enactment of the Sentencing Reform Act of 1984, 18 U. S. C. \xc2\xa73551 et seq., 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7991, et seq., Congress considered\xe2\x80\x94and rejected\xe2\x80\x94a proposal that would have made the Guidelines only advisory. See Mistretta v. United States, 488 U. S. 361, 387 (1989). Ultimately, the decision to authorize the Commission to issue rules that \xe2\x80\x9chave the force and effect of laws\xe2\x80\x9d generated a serious debate over the constitutionality of the Commission itself. See id., at 413 (Scalia, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While we resolved that constitutional debate in the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s favor in Mistretta, it became apparent during the next two decades that the mandatory character of the Guidelines, coupled with the practice of judicial factfinding, not only produced a host of excessively severe sentences but also created an unacceptable risk of depriving defendants of long-settled constitutional protections. See, e.g., Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466, 490 (2000) (holding that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ther than the fact of a prior conviction, any fact that increases the penalty for a crime beyond the prescribed statutory maximum must be submitted to a jury, and proved beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x9d); Ring v. Arizona, 536 U. S. 584, 602 (2002) (holding that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f a State makes an increase in a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s authorized punishment contingent on the finding of a fact, that fact\xe2\x80\x94no matter how the State labels it\xe2\x80\x94must be found by a jury beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x9d); Blakely v. Washington, 542 U. S. 296, 304 (2004) (holding that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a judge inflicts punishment that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict alone does not allow, the jury has not found all the facts \xe2\x80\x98which the law makes essential to the punishment,\xe2\x80\x99 and the judge exceeds his proper authority\xe2\x80\x9d (citation omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Over a series of cases, we arrived at our present understanding of determinate sentencing schemes: They are constitutionally infirm if they mandate enhanced punishments based on facts found only by a judge by a preponderance of the evidence. By restoring the principles outlined in landmark cases such as In re Winship, 397 U. S. 358 (1970), Apprendi and its progeny fundamentally changed the landscape of modern sentencing law,[Footnote 3] and in so doing paved the way for Booker.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Booker Court considered whether the Sentencing Reform Act\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory determinate sentencing scheme infringed the jury-trial right. In the first of two opinions, we held that the two applications of the Guidelines before us violated the Sixth Amendment because the sentencing judge in each case imposed a more severe sentence than the facts found by the jury warranted. 543 U. S., at 235. We recognized that if the Guidelines \xe2\x80\x9ccould be read as merely advisory provisions that recommended, rather than required, the selection of particular sentences in response to differing sets of facts, their use would not implicate the Sixth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 233. But we rejected such an advisory reading of the Guidelines, as they then stood. Id., at 234. To satisfy constitutional guarantees, we explained that any fact that has the effect of increasing the mandatory range must be \xe2\x80\x9cestablished by a plea of guilty or . . . must be admitted by the defendant or proved to a jury beyond a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 244. Otherwise, the sentence would violate the Sixth (and the Fifth) Amendment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of the potential for mandatory Guidelines sentences to violate the Constitution, the Court had to elect among possible remedies. As I explained in my dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s second Booker opinion (the remedial one), there was no need to find any constitutional infirmity in any provision of the Sentencing Reform Act to provide relief for the defendants in Booker, or to apply the Guidelines in a mandatory fashion in future cases\xe2\x80\x94so long as juries were allowed to decide the factual issues raised by requests for enhanced sentences. See id., at 272\xe2\x80\x93303 (Stevens, J., dissenting in part). Notwithstanding the fact that the Court could have retained the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x99 mandatory prescriptive effect in a manner consonant with the jury-trial right, the Court nevertheless adopted a broad remedy that recast the Guidelines in their entirety.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That change did not respond to a determination that the mandatory Guidelines regime itself violated the Sixth Amendment. Neither my opinion for the Court with respect to our constitutional holding, nor Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s remedial opinion, contained any such determination. Instead, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to make the Guidelines discretionary rested entirely on the majority\xe2\x80\x99s judgment that Congress would have preferred that result to either an increase in the jury\xe2\x80\x99s role in making factual findings or a decision invalidating the entire regime. Id., at 249. When Congress was wrestling with the Sentencing Reform Act of 1984, it did not foresee Apprendi, Ring, and Blakely. The Court made a policy-based prediction that, were Congress to have had such foresight, it would not have elected\xe2\x80\x94in any respect\xe2\x80\x94a mandatory sentencing regime.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court openly acknowledged this methodology:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn essence, in what follows, we explain both (1) why Congress would likely have preferred the total invalidation of the Act to an Act with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment requirement engrafted onto it, and (2) why Congress would likely have preferred the excision of some of the Act, namely the Act\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory language, to the invalidation of the entire Act. That is to say, in light of today\xe2\x80\x99s holding, we compare maintaining the Act as written with jury factfinding added (the dissenters\xe2\x80\x99 proposed remedy) to the total invalidation of the statute, and conclude that Congress would have preferred the latter. We then compare our own remedy to the total invalidation of the statute, and conclude that Congress would have preferred our remedy.\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U. S., at 249.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, rather than \xe2\x80\x9cmaintaining the Act as written with jury factfinding added,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., the Court opted to alter the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s power in a more fundamental way: It did away with a fixed, determinate sentencing regime based on mandatory Guidelines. Henceforth the Commission would guide and advise federal courts in the exercise of their sentencing authority. But the Commission would not bind.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court held as follows:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cWe answer the question of remedy by finding the provision of the federal sentencing statute that makes the Guidelines mandatory, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(b)(1) (Supp. IV), incompatible with today\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional holding. We conclude that this provision must be severed and excised, as must one other statutory section, \xc2\xa73742(e) (2000 ed. and Supp. IV), which depends upon the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x99 mandatory nature. So modified, the federal sentencing statute, see Sentencing Reform Act of 1984 (Sentencing Act), as amended, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73551 et seq., 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7991 et seq., makes the Guidelines effectively advisory. It requires a sentencing court to consider Guidelines ranges, see 18 U.S.C. \xc2\xa73553(a)(4) (Supp. IV), but it permits the court to tailor the sentence in light of other statutory concerns as well, see \xc2\xa73553(a).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 245\xe2\x80\x93246.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The only fair way to read the Booker majority\xe2\x80\x99s remedy is that it eliminated the mandatory features of the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x94all of them.[Footnote 4] It is true that the Court explicitly severed only two specific statutory sections. But there was not, at the time, even a whisper of a suggestion that any other mandatory provision existed or that any should be preserved.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Were it not clear from the foregoing discussion of Booker itself, our post-Booker decisions have repeatedly emphasized the completely advisory nature of the Guidelines. See, e.g., Cunningham v. California, 549 U. S. 270, 286\xe2\x80\x93287 (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder the system described in Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s opinion for the Court in Booker, judges would no longer be tied to the sentencing range indicated in the Guidelines. But they would be obliged to \xe2\x80\x98take account of\xe2\x80\x99 that range along with the sentencing goals Congress enumerated in the [Sentencing Reform Act of 1984] at 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a)\xe2\x80\x9d); Rita v. United States, 551 U. S. 338, 351 (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he sentencing court does not enjoy the benefit of a legal presumption that the Guidelines sentence should apply\xe2\x80\x9d); Gall v. United States, 552 U. S. 38, 46 (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cAs a result of our decision [in Booker], the Guidelines are now advisory, and appellate review of sentencing decisions is limited to determining whether they are \xe2\x80\x98reasonable\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Kimbrough v. United States, 552 U. S. 85, 101 (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn sum, while the statute still requires a court to give respectful consideration to the Guidelines, Booker permits the court to tailor the sentence in light of other statutory concerns as well\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and citation omitted)); Spears v. United States, 555 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (per curiam) (slip op., at 5) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e now clarify that district courts are entitled to reject and vary categorically from the crack-cocaine Guidelines based on a policy disagreement with those Guidelines\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 6] Our case law is quite clear: The Guidelines no longer have mandatory and binding effect, and the sentencing court may not presume them correct or reasonable when it considers an individual sentencing decision.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of this history, the limited nature of the \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceeding is beside the point. Nothing turns on whether the proceeding is best understood as a resentencing or as a sentence modification procedure. Nor is it relevant that Dillon has no right to be present at a proceeding under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, or that a sentence reduction proceeding may not be \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutionally compelled,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s general reliance on Booker in this case, see ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312, is odd because the Booker Court explained its belief \xe2\x80\x9cthat Congress would not have authorized a mandatory system in some cases and a nonmandatory system in others,\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U. S., at 266. Yet, this is precisely the system the Court approves today.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Approaching this case as the Booker Court did, one must ask whether it is likely that a fully informed Congress would have created this kind of Commission: one endowed with vast responsibilities for drafting advisory Guidelines and policy statements, but also with the tiniest sliver of lawmaking power to tie the hands of a district court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of grace under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). I think the answer is obvious.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0My understanding of the scope of the Booker remedy is reinforced by an additional consideration: The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s policy statement, to which the Court today allows binding effect, may exceed the scope of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s powers. No one disputes that Congress could have rejected the Court\xe2\x80\x99s remedial holding in Booker if it so wished. Instead, it is the Commission that has rejected Booker\xe2\x80\x99s application to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), by purporting to give mandatory force to its own policy statement. That action presses the bounds of the authority Congress validly gave the Commission in 1984, for it is not clear that Congress has authorized the Commission to create this type of policy statement or to circumvent a decision such as Booker on its own accord.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have been quite permissive of congressional delegations in our separation-of-powers jurisprudence. \xe2\x80\x9cSo long as Congress \xe2\x80\x98shall lay down by legislative act an intelligible principle to which the person or body authorized to [exercise the delegated authority] is directed to conform, such legislative action is not a forbidden delegation of legislative power.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Mistretta, 488 U. S., at 372 (quoting J. W. Hampton, Jr., & Co. v. United States, 276 U. S. 394, 409 (1928)). Few legislative actions have been found to offend this principle. 488 U. S., at 373.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More than 20 years ago, the Court upheld the constitutionality of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s work from just such an attack in Mistretta. We took sanctuary then in the fact that, in enacting the Sentencing Reform Act and creating the Commission, Congress had \xe2\x80\x9cse[t] forth more than merely an \xe2\x80\x98intelligible principle\xe2\x80\x99 or minimal standard\xe2\x80\x9d for the exercise of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s discretion, and had \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98explain[ed] what the Commission should do and how it should do it, and se[t] out specific directives to govern particular situations.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 379. To this end, Congress gave the Commission clear \xe2\x80\x9cgoals,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 374; specified the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98purposes of sentencing,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; \xe2\x80\x9cprescribed the specific tool\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthe guidelines system\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the Commission was to use in its work, ibid.; set limits on the appropriate Guidelines ranges the Commission was to promulgate, id., at 375; and set forth \xe2\x80\x9cseven factors\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c11 factors,\xe2\x80\x9d respectively, to assist the Commission with \xe2\x80\x9cits formulation of offense categories\xe2\x80\x9d and its establishment of \xe2\x80\x9ccategories of defendants\xe2\x80\x9d for sentencing purposes, id., at 375\xe2\x80\x93376.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We explained that \xe2\x80\x9calthough Congress granted the Commission substantial discretion in formulating guidelines, in actuality it legislated a full hierarchy of punishment\xe2\x80\x94from near maximum imprisonment, to substantial imprisonment, to some imprisonment, to alternatives\xe2\x80\x94and stipulated the most important offense and offender characteristics to place defendants within these categories.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 377. There was, accordingly, no \xe2\x80\x9cconcern of encroachment and aggrandizement that has animated our separation-of-powers jurisprudence and aroused our vigilance against the \xe2\x80\x98hydraulic pressure inherent within each of the separate Branches to exceed the outer limits of its power.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 382 (quoting INS v. Chadha, 462 U. S. 919, 951 (1983)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia disagreed. He argued forcefully that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 creation of the Commission was itself \xe2\x80\x9ca pure delegation of legislative power\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore an abuse of separation of powers. 488 U. S., at 420 (dissenting opinion). \xe2\x80\x9cCongress\xe2\x80\x99 commitment of such broad policy responsibility to any institution,\xe2\x80\x9d in Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s view, violated a core principle of our governing system: that \xe2\x80\x9cbasic policy decisions governing society are to be made by the Legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 415.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although we acknowledged in Mistretta that Congress had permissibly granted substantial powers to the Commission to set law and policy on sentencing generally, we had no occasion to consider whether it had spoken with sufficient clarity respecting the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s authority to prescribe sentence reductions. That question has now reared its head, and in my view it raises separation-of-powers concerns significantly more difficult than those presented in Mistretta.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, I am doubtful that Congress authorized the type of \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy statement\xe2\x80\x9d we find in USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10. Congress instructed the Commission to promulgate \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral policy statements regarding application of the guidelines or any other aspect of sentencing or sentence implementation that in the view of the Commission would further the purposes set forth in section 3553(a)(2) of title 18 . . . including the appropriate use of,\xe2\x80\x9d inter alia, various \xe2\x80\x9csentence modification provisions.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(a)(2). As envisioned by the Sentencing Reform Act, the role of policy statements was merely to inform the judge\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of discretion within an otherwise mandatory Guidelines regime. See S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 98\xe2\x80\x93225, p. 167 (1983) (explaining that the \xe2\x80\x9csentencing judge is required to take the policy statements into account in deciding what sentence to impose,\xe2\x80\x9d but that departure from a policy statement is not itself grounds for appeal); see also id., at 166 (identifying potential use of policy statement to \xe2\x80\x9coffe[r] recommendations as to how\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9ctrea[t]\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cin the future\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cexisting disparities which are not adequately cured by the guidelines\xe2\x80\x9d). Congress reserved binding effect for the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cguidelines,\xe2\x80\x9d which the Commission was to promulgate pursuant to a distinct statutory provision, \xc2\xa7994(a)(1). The Sentencing Reform Act thus drew a basic distinction: Guidelines would bind; policy statements would advise.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given that distinction, it is significant that Congress elected to use the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s policy-statement power to set limitations on the sentencing modification procedures, rather than invoking the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s Guidelines power. The Commission is now trying to use a policy statement to have the mandatory effect of a guideline\xe2\x80\x94inverting the Sentencing Reform Act\xe2\x80\x99s original design. I find no provision within \xc2\xa7994(a)(2) that would authorize the Commission, via a policy statement, to create a binding Guidelines regime. With respect to the type of action the Commission has taken, there is certainly no provision that even approximates the detailed prescriptions on the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s power we considered in Mistretta.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, not only does nothing in \xc2\xa7994(a)(2) appear to authorize this type of policy statement, but there is also nothing that appears to authorize the Commission, by its own fiat, to limit the effect of our decision in Booker.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0How to respond to Booker, and whether to retain mandatory Guidelines, was a decision for Congress\xe2\x80\x94and Congress alone. Booker expressly left \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he ball\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cin Congress\xe2\x80\x99 court,\xe2\x80\x9d explaining that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he National Legislature is equipped to devise and install, long term, the sentencing system, compatible with the Constitution, that Congress judges best for the federal system of justice.\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U. S., at 265; see also supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. That Congress has declined to disturb Booker in the five years since its issuance demonstrates not only that Justice Breyer is more clairvoyant than I am, but also that Congress has acquiesced to a discretionary Guidelines regime. Congress\xe2\x80\x99 silence has deprived the Commission of any \xe2\x80\x9cintelligible principle[s],\xe2\x80\x9d J. W. Hampton, 276 U. S., at 409, by which to steer its consideration of the appropriate response to Booker. And without such guidance, I fear that, in promulgating USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10, the Commission may have made the type of \xe2\x80\x9cbasic policy decisio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d that Justice Scalia reminded us is the province of the Legislature, Mistretta, 488 U. S., at 415 (dissenting opinion).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Prior to the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s 2008 overhaul of its policy statement in \xc2\xa71B1.10\xe2\x80\x94and even under the applicable policy statement in effect when the Court decided Booker\xe2\x80\x94nothing in the Guidelines, see supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, and n.\xc2\xa02, as understood in light of Booker, would have precluded Dillon from obtaining the type of discretionary sentence reduction he now seeks (assuming he was so eligible). Standing in Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s way presently are two provisions of \xc2\xa71B1.10, revised contemporaneously with the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s decision to make its amendments to the crack cocaine offense Guidelines retroactive.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There can be no question that the purpose of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s amendments to its policy statement in \xc2\xa71B1.10 was to circumvent the Booker remedy. See Brief for Federal Public and Community Defenders et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 3\xe2\x80\x939 (describing history of promulgation of current version of \xc2\xa71B1.10). To this end, the Commission disclaimed that proceedings under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) \xe2\x80\x9cconstitute a full resentencing of the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10(a)(3). And it advised that \xe2\x80\x9cthe court shall not reduce the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s term of imprisonment under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) and this policy statement to a term that is less than the minimum of the amended guideline range determined\xe2\x80\x9d under the new range. \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2)(A). In other words, the Commission told federal courts that its Guidelines, at least in \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings, remain mandatory and binding.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Had the Commission taken it upon itself, by issuance of a general policy statement, to make its Guidelines mandatory but subject to jury findings in all cases, we would either strike down such an act on separation-of-powers grounds or apply the same remedy we did in Booker to render the statement advisory. It makes little difference, in my view, that the Commission has only rejected the Booker remedy in this single procedure. The encroachment is the same, if only more subtle. Any legislative response to Booker was a decision for Congress to make\xe2\x80\x94 not the Commission.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Separate from the arguments noted above, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision today may reflect a concern that a contrary holding would discourage the Commission from issuing retroactive amendments to the Guidelines, owing to a fear of burdening the district courts. In what might be described as a subtle threat, the Commission has highlighted this point in its amicus brief supporting the Government. The brief explains that holding for Dillon would introduce uncertainty into the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cassessments about the effects of retroactivity decisions,\xe2\x80\x9d making these decisions \xe2\x80\x9cvery difficult\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cweigh[ing] against making Guideline amendments retroactive in the future.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States Sentencing Commission as Amicus Curiae 21.[Footnote 7]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if that explanation were accurate, it should not influence our assessment of the legal question before us. The Commission has a statutory obligation to review and amend Guidelines ranges. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(o). And Congress has commanded that the Commission \xe2\x80\x9cshall specify in what circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d an amendment is retroactive, indicating that most, if not all, substantial amendments are to receive some type of retroactive effect. \xc2\xa7994(u); see also S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 98\xe2\x80\x93225, at 180 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt should be noted that the Committee does not expect that the Commission will recommend adjusting existing sentences under [\xc2\xa73582(c)(2)] when guidelines are simply refined in a way that might cause isolated instances of existing sentences falling above the old guidelines or when there is only a minor downward adjustment in the guidelines\xe2\x80\x9d). In other words, while Congress has left the retroactivity decision to the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s discretion, it has done so with the presumption that some form of retroactive relief is appropriate when a Guidelines amendment is nontrivial.[Footnote 8] I cannot accept that the Commission would ignore its obligations, and would withhold retroactive application of a Guidelines reduction, simply because a judge would have discretion\nto enter a below-Guidelines sentence in a \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceeding.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Undoubtedly, discretionary application of the Guidelines in \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings would impose a greater burden on the district courts. Such a process would require case-specific evaluations rather than the rote, two-level reductions the Commission envisioned when it made Amendment 706 retroactive. But it is important to remember that \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) already requires the district court to consider the \xc2\xa73553(a) factors when it determines whether to grant a reduction, as well as the extent of the reduction. And any additional consideration of evidence proffered to justify a downward departure need not create a great deal of work. Indeed, it need not create any particular adversarial process at all: The Commission could simply advise the district courts to review paper submissions, including the original presentence report and objections, as well as any new submissions. By now, courts are intimately familiar with our post-Booker sentencing regime and the discretionary application of the \xc2\xa73553(a) factors.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The facts of Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s case show why any additional burden on the courts caused by applying Booker\xe2\x80\x99s remedial holding likely pales in comparison to the benefit of achieving more tailored, proportionate sentences for those individuals currently serving terms of imprisonment that exceed what is \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary\xe2\x80\x9d to meet the goals of our sentencing system, \xc2\xa73553(a). Dillon was 23 years old when he was sentenced to nearly 27 years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment for his drug crimes. His attorney urged the District Court to enter a below-Guidelines sentence because of, inter alia, the gross disparity between sentences for crack and powder cocaine offenses. App. 8\xe2\x80\x939. It would take another 14 years for this Court to agree, finally, in Kimbrough, 552 U. S. 85, that sentencing courts could consider this unjust disparity.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But the District Court, constrained by the then-mandatory Guidelines, increased Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s sentence based on judge-found facts by more than 10 years over the sentence authorized by the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict. See Brief for Petitioner 2, and n.\xc2\xa02. The court could only lament: \xe2\x80\x9cI personally don\xe2\x80\x99t believe that you should be serving 322 months. But I feel I am bound by those Guidelines and I don\xe2\x80\x99t feel there is any grounds for . . . depart[ing] from those Guidelines.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 12\xe2\x80\x9313. The court acknowledged: \xe2\x80\x9cI don\xe2\x80\x99t say to you that these penalties are fair. I don\xe2\x80\x99t think they are fair.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13. The court also implored Dillon to make something of the hand he had dealt himself: \xe2\x80\x9cI hope that while you are in prison . . . that you will take some time to consider the direction that your life will take when you do return to society. . . . It is only through people like you if you spread the word that other young men of your age will hesitate to get involved in [dealing drugs].\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dillon has done just that. He has participated in outreach efforts in the communities in which he has been imprisoned, doing extensive work with adolescents to steer them away from a life of drugs and crime. Brief for Petitioner 5\xe2\x80\x936. Working with two universities, he has facilitated the initiation of an African-American Studies program at Hunters Point Family, a Bay Area organization devoted to assisting at-risk youth. He has also played a large role in initiating a similar program at his prison facility. Berkeley\xe2\x80\x99s Prison Outreach Coordinator stated to the District Court that \xe2\x80\x9cwithout [Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s] insight and advice, our project would not have succeeded and grown the way it has.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 6 (internal quotation marks omitted). Dillon has also prepared himself for a successful life once he returns to society. He has obtained his general equivalency diploma (GED), taken vocational classes in property management, and has job prospects awaiting him upon release. Id., at 6\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government concedes that Dillon has undertaken \xe2\x80\x9csignificant institutional rehabilitation and education.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 11. The Court of Appeals acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f Booker did apply in proceedings pursuant to \xc2\xa73582, Dillon would likely be an ideal candidate for a non-Guidelines sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d 572 F.\xc2\xa03d 146, 147 (CA3 2009). And yet, now, the Government will continue to spend more than $25,000 a year to keep Dillon behind bars until his release date.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given the circumstances of his case, I can scarcely think of a greater waste of this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s precious resources. Cf. Barber v. Thomas, ante, at ___ (2010) (slip op., at 1) (Kennedy, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cAnd if the only way to call attention to the human implications of this case is to speak in terms of economics, then it should be noted that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation comes at a cost to the taxpayers of untold millions of dollars\xe2\x80\x9d). Dillon\xe2\x80\x99s continued imprisonment is a truly sad example of what I have come to view as an exceptionally, and often mindlessly, harsh federal punishment scheme.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither the interests of justice nor common sense lends any support to the decision to preserve the single sliver of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s lawmaking power that the Court resurrects today. I had thought Booker dismantled the mandatory Guidelines regime. The Court ought to finish the job.

\n
