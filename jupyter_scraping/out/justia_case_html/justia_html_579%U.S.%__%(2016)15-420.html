SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

\n

UNITED STATES v. BRYANT

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93420.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 19, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 13, 2016

\n

In response to the high incidence of domestic violence against Native American women, Congress enacted a felony offense of domestic assault in Indian country by a habitual offender.\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7117(a). Section 117(a)(1) provides that any person who \xe2\x80\x9ccommits a domestic assault within .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Indian country\xe2\x80\x9d and who has at least two prior final convictions for domestic violence rendered \xe2\x80\x9cin Federal, State, or Indian tribal court proceedings .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be fined .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , imprisoned for a term of not more than 5 years, or both .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Having two prior tribal-court convictions for domestic violence crimes is thus a predicate of the new offense.

\n

This case raises the question whether \xc2\xa7117(a)\xe2\x80\x99s inclusion of tribal-court convictions as predicate offenses is compatible with the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s right to counsel. The\n \nSixth Amendment guarantees indigent defendants appointed counsel in any state or federal criminal proceeding in which a term of imprisonment is imposed, Scott v. Illinois,\n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367\n\xe2\x80\x93374, but it does not apply in tribal-court proceedings, see Plains Commerce Bank v. Long Family Land & Cattle Co.,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 316\n. The Indian Civil Rights Act of 1968 (ICRA), which governs tribal-court proceedings, accords a range of procedural safeguards to tribal-court defendants \xe2\x80\x9csimilar, but not identical, to those contained in the Bill of Rights and the\n \nFourteenth Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d Santa Clara Pueblo v. Martinez,\n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 49\n. In particular, ICRA provides indigent defendants with a right to appointed counsel only for sentences exceeding one year.\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71302(c)(2). ICRA\xe2\x80\x99s right to counsel therefore is not coextensive with the\n \nSixth Amendment right.

\n

This Court has held that a conviction obtained in state or federal court in violation of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel cannot be used in a subsequent proceeding \xe2\x80\x9cto support guilt or enhance punishment for another offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Burgett v. Texas,\n\n389 U.\xc2\xa0S. 109\n. Use of a constitutionally infirm conviction would cause \xe2\x80\x9cthe accused in effect [to] suffe[r] anew from the [prior] deprivation of [his]\n \nSixth Amendment right.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Burgett\xe2\x80\x99s principle was limited by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in Nichols v. United States,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 738\n, that \xe2\x80\x9can uncounseled misdemeanor conviction, valid under Scott because no prison term was imposed, is also valid when used to enhance punishment at a subsequent conviction,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 748\xe2\x80\x93749.

\n

Respondent Michael Bryant, Jr., has multiple tribal-court convictions for domestic assault. When convicted, Bryant was indigent and was not appointed counsel. For most of his convictions, he was sentenced to terms of imprisonment not exceeding one year\xe2\x80\x99s duration. Because of his short prison terms, the prior tribal-court proceedings complied with ICRA, and his convictions were therefore valid when entered. Based on domestic assaults he committed in 2011, Bryant was indicted on two counts of domestic assault by a habitual offender, in violation of \xc2\xa7117(a). Represented in federal court by appointed counsel, he contended that the\n \nSixth Amendment precluded use of his prior, uncounseled, tribal-court misdemeanor convictions to satisfy \xc2\xa7117(a)\xe2\x80\x99s predicate-offense element and moved to dismiss the indictment. The District Court denied the motion; Bryant pleaded guilty, reserving the right to appeal. The Ninth Circuit reversed the conviction and directed dismissal of the indictment. It comprehended that Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s uncounseled tribal-court convictions were valid when entered because the\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel does not apply in tribal-court proceedings. It held, however, that Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s tribal-court convictions could not be used as predicate convictions within \xc2\xa7117(a)\xe2\x80\x99s compass because they would have violated the\n \nSixth Amendment had they been rendered in state or federal court.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Because Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s tribal-court convictions occurred in proceedings that complied with ICRA and were therefore valid when entered, use of those convictions as predicate offenses in a \xc2\xa7117(a) prosecution does not violate the Constitution.

\n

Nichols instructs that convictions valid when entered retain that status when invoked in a subsequent proceeding. Nichols reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]nhancement statutes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. do not change the penalty imposed for the earlier conviction\xe2\x80\x9d; rather, repeat-offender laws \xe2\x80\x9cpenaliz[e] only the last offense committed by the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 747. Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence for violating \xc2\xa7117(a) punishes his most recent acts of domestic assault, not his prior crimes prosecuted in tribal court. He was denied no right to counsel in tribal court, and his\n \nSixth Amendment right was honored in federal court. Bryant acknowledges that Nichols would have allowed reliance on uncounseled tribal-court convictions resulting in fines to satisfy \xc2\xa7117(a)\xe2\x80\x99s prior-crimes predicate. But there is no cause to distinguish for \xc2\xa7117(a) purposes between fine-only tribal-court convictions and valid but uncounseled tribal-court convictions resulting in imprisonment for a term not exceeding one year. Neither violates the\n \nSixth Amendment. Bryant is not aided by Burgett. A defendant convicted in tribal court suffered no\n \nSixth Amendment violation in the first instance, so he cannot \xe2\x80\x9csuffe[r] anew\xe2\x80\x9d from a prior deprivation in his federal prosecution.

\n

Bryant also invokes the Due Process Clause of the\n \nFifth Amendment to support his assertion that tribal-court judgments should not be used as predicate offenses under \xc2\xa7117(a). ICRA, however, guarantees \xe2\x80\x9cdue process of law,\xe2\x80\x9d accords other procedural safeguards, and permits a prisoner to challenge the fundamental fairness of tribal court proceedings in federal habeas corpus proceedings. Because proceedings in compliance with ICRA sufficiently ensure the reliability of tribal-court convictions, the use of those convictions in a federal prosecution does not violate a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s due process right. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

769 F.\xc2\xa03d 671, reversed and remanded.

\n

Ginsburg, J., delivered the opinion for a unanimous Court. Thomas, J., filed a concurring opinion.

UNITED STATES, PETITIONER v.MICHAEL BRYANT, JR.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 13, 2016]

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

In response to the high incidence of domestic violence against Native American women, Congress, in 2005, enacted\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7117(a), which targets serial offenders. Section 117(a) makes it a federal crime for any person to \xe2\x80\x9ccommi[t] a domestic assault within .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Indian country\xe2\x80\x9d if the person has at least two prior final convictions for domestic violence rendered \xe2\x80\x9cin Federal, State, or Indian tribal court proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d See Violence Against Women and Department of Justice Reauthorization Act of 2005 (VAWA Reauthorization Act), Pub. L. 109\xe2\x80\x93162, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7901, 909,\n\n119Stat.\n3077,\n3084.[1] Respondent Michael Bryant, Jr., has multiple tribal-court convictions for domestic assault. For most of those convictions, he was sentenced to terms of imprisonment, none of them exceeding one year\xe2\x80\x99s duration. His tribal-court convictions do not count for \xc2\xa7117(a) purposes, Bryant maintains, because he was uncounseled in those proceedings.

\n

The\n \nSixth Amendment guarantees indigent defendants, in state and federal criminal proceedings, appointed counsel in any case in which a term of imprisonment is imposed. Scott v. Illinois,\n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367\n\xe2\x80\x93374 (1979). But the\n \nSixth Amendment does not apply to tribal-court proceedings. See Plains Commerce Bank v. Long Family Land & Cattle Co.,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 316,\n337 (2008)\n. The Indian Civil Rights Act of 1968 (ICRA), Pub. L. 90\xe2\x80\x93284,\n\n82Stat.\n77,\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71301 et\xc2\xa0seq., which governs criminal proceedings in tribal courts, requires appointed counsel only when a sentence of more than one year\xe2\x80\x99s imprisonment is imposed. \xc2\xa71302(c)(2). Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s tribal-court convictions, it is undisputed, were valid when entered. This case presents the question whether those convictions, though uncounseled, rank as predicate offenses within the compass of \xc2\xa7117(a). Our answer is yes. Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s tribal-court convictions did not violate the\n \nSixth Amendment when obtained, and they retain their validity when invoked in a \xc2\xa7117(a) prosecution. That proceeding generates no\n \nSixth Amendment defect where none previously existed.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ompared to all other groups in the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d Native American women \xe2\x80\x9cexperience the highest rates of domestic violence.\xe2\x80\x9d 151 Cong. Rec. 9061 (2005) (remarks of Sen. McCain). According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, as many as 46% of American Indian and Alaska Native women have been victims of physical violence by an intimate partner. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, National Center for Injury Prevention and Control, M. Black et\xc2\xa0al., National Intimate Partner and Sexual Violence Survey 2010 Summary Report 40 (2011) (Table 4.3), online at http://www.cdc.gov/ViolencePrevention/pdf/NISVS_report2010-a.pdf (all Internet materials as last visited June 9, 2016). American Indian and Alaska Native women \xe2\x80\x9care 2.5 times more likely to be raped or sexually assaulted than women in the United States in general.\xe2\x80\x9d Dept. of Justice, Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s Advisory Committee on American Indian and Alaska Native Children Exposed to Violence, Ending Violence So Children Can Thrive 38 (Nov. 2014), online at https:// www.justice.gov\xc2\xa0/sites\xc2\xa0/default/files/defendingchildhood/ pages/attachments/2015/03/23/ending_violence_so_children_can_thrive.pdf. American Indian women experience battery \xe2\x80\x9cat a rate of 23.2 per 1,000, compared with 8 per 1,000 among Caucasian women,\xe2\x80\x9d and they \xe2\x80\x9cexperience 7 sexual assaults per 1,000, compared with 4 per 1,000 among Black Americans, 3 per 1,000 among Caucasians, 2 per 1,000 among Hispanic women, and 1 per 1,000 among Asian women.\xe2\x80\x9d VAWA Reauthorization Act, \xc2\xa7901,\n\n119Stat.\n3077.

\n

As this Court has noted, domestic abusers exhibit high rates of recidivism, and their violence \xe2\x80\x9coften escalates in severity over time.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Castleman, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 2). Nationwide, over 75% of female victims of intimate partner violence have been previously victimized by the same offender, Dept. of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics, S. Catalano, Intimate Partner Violence 1993\xe2\x80\x932010, p.\xc2\xa04 (rev. 2015) (Figure 4), online at http://www.bjs.gov/content/pub/pdf/ipv9310.pdf, often multiple times, Dept. of Justice, National Institute of Justice, P. Tjaden & N. Thoennes, Extent, Nature, and Consequences of Intimate Partner Violence, p. iv (2000), online at https://www.ncjrs.gov/pdffiles1/nij/181867.pdf (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]omen who were physically assaulted by an intimate partner averaged 6.9 physical assaults by the same partner.\xe2\x80\x9d). Incidents of repeating, escalating abuse more than occasionally culminate in a fatal attack. See VAWA Reauthorization Act, \xc2\xa7901,\n\n119Stat.\n3077\xe2\x80\x933078 (\xe2\x80\x9c[D]uring the period 1979 through 1992, homicide was the third leading cause of death of Indian females aged 15 to 34, and 75 percent were killed by family members or acquaintances.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The \xe2\x80\x9ccomplex patchwork of federal, state, and tribal law\xe2\x80\x9d governing Indian country, Duro v. Reina,\n\n495 U.\xc2\xa0S. 676\n, n.\xc2\xa01 (1990), has made it difficult to stem the tide of domestic violence experienced by Native American women. Although tribal courts may enforce the tribe\xe2\x80\x99s criminal laws against Indian defendants, Congress has curbed tribal courts\xe2\x80\x99 sentencing authority. At the time of \xc2\xa7117(a)\xe2\x80\x99s passage, ICRA limited sentences in tribal court to a maximum of one year\xe2\x80\x99s imprisonment.\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71302(a)(7) (2006 ed.).[2] Congress has since expanded tribal courts\xe2\x80\x99 sentencing authority, allowing them to impose up to three years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment, contingent on adoption of additional procedural safeguards.\n\n124Stat.\n2279\xe2\x80\x932280 (codified at\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71302(a)(7)(C), (c)).[3] To date, however, few tribes have employed this enhanced sentencing authority. See Tribal Law and Policy Inst., Implementation Chart: VAWA Enhanced Jurisdiction and TLOA Enhanced Sentencing, online at http://www.tribal-institute.org/download/VAWA/VAWAImplementationChart.pdf.[4]

\n

States are unable or unwilling to fill the enforcement gap. Most States lack jurisdiction over crimes committed in Indian country against Indian victims. See United States v. John,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 634,\n651 (1978)\n. In 1953, Congress increased the potential for state action by giving six States \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction over specified areas of Indian country within the States and provid[ing] for the [voluntary] assumption of jurisdiction by other States.\xe2\x80\x9d California v. Cabazon Band of Mission Indians,\n\n480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 202,\n207 (1987)\n (footnote omitted). See Act of Aug. 15, 1953, Pub. L. 280,\n\n67Stat.\n588 (codified, as amended, at\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71162 and 25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71321\xe2\x80\x931328, 1360). States so empowered may apply their own criminal laws to \xe2\x80\x9coffenses committed by or against Indians within all Indian country within the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Cabazon Band of Mission Indians, 480 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 207; see\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71162(a). Even when capable of exercising jurisdiction, however, States have not de-voted their limited criminal justice resources to crimes committed in Indian country. Jimenez & Song, Concurrent Tribal and State Jurisdiction Under Public Law 280, 47 Am. U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1627, 1636\xe2\x80\x931637 (1998); Tribal Law and Policy Inst., S. Deer, C. Goldberg, H. Valdez Singleton, & M. White Eagle, Final Report: Focus Group on Public Law 280 and the Sexual Assault of Native Women 7\xe2\x80\x938 (2007), online at http://www.tribal-institute.org/download/Final%20280%20FG%20Report.pdf.

\n

That leaves the Federal Government. Although federal law generally governs in Indian country, Congress has long excluded from federal-court jurisdiction crimes committed by an Indian against another Indian.\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71152; see Ex parte Crow Dog,\n\n109 U.\xc2\xa0S. 556,\n572 (1883)\n (requiring \xe2\x80\x9ca clear expression of the intention of Congress\xe2\x80\x9d to confer federal jurisdiction over crimes committed by an Indian against another Indian). In the Major Crimes Act, Congress authorized federal jurisdiction over enumerated grave criminal offenses when both perpetrator and victim are Indians, including murder, manslaughter, and felony assault. \xc2\xa71153. At the time of \xc2\xa7117(a)\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, felony assault subject to federal prosecution required \xe2\x80\x9cserious bodily injury,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7113(a)(6) (2006 ed.), meaning \xe2\x80\x9ca substantial risk of death,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cextreme physical pain,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cprotracted and obvious disfigurement,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cprotracted loss or impairment of the function of a bodily member, organ, or mental faculty.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71365(h)(3) (incorporated through \xc2\xa7113(b)(2)).[5] In short, when \xc2\xa7117(a) was before Congress, Indian perpetrators of domestic violence \xe2\x80\x9cescape[d] felony charges until they seriously injure[d] or kill[ed] someone.\xe2\x80\x9d 151 Cong. Rec. 9062 (2005) (remarks of Sen. McCain).

\n

As a result of the limitations on tribal, state, and federal jurisdiction in Indian country, serial domestic violence offenders, prior to the enactment of \xc2\xa7117(a), faced at most a year\xe2\x80\x99s imprisonment per offense\xe2\x80\x94a sentence insufficient to deter repeated and escalating abuse. To ratchet up the punishment of serial offenders, Congress created the federal felony offense of domestic assault in Indian country by a habitual offender. \xc2\xa7117(a) (2012 ed.); see No. 12\xe2\x80\x9330177 (CA9, July 6, 2015), App. to Pet. for Cert. 41a (Owens, J., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc) (\xe2\x80\x9cTailored to the unique problems .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that American Indian and Alaska Native Tribes face, \xc2\xa7117(a) provides felony-level punishment for serial domestic violence offenders, and it represents the first true effort to remove these recidivists from the communities that they repeatedly terrorize.\xe2\x80\x9d). The section provides in pertinent part:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cAny person who commits a domestic assault within .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Indian country and who has a final conviction on at least 2 separate prior occasions in Federal, State, or Indian tribal court proceedings for offenses that would be, if subject to Federal jurisdiction any assault, sex-ual abuse, or serious violent felony against a spouse or intimate partner .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be fined .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , imprisoned for a term of not more than 5 years, or both .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7117(a)(1).[6]

\n

Having two prior convictions for domestic violence crimes\xe2\x80\x94including tribal-court convictions\xe2\x80\x94is thus a predicate of the new offense.

\n

B

\n

This case requires us to determine whether \xc2\xa7117(a)\xe2\x80\x99s inclusion of tribal-court convictions is compatible with the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s right to counsel. The\n \nSixth Amendment to the U.\xc2\xa0S. Constitution guarantees a criminal defendant in state or federal court \xe2\x80\x9cthe Assistance of Counsel for his defence.\xe2\x80\x9d See Gideon v. Wainwright,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335,\n339 (1963)\n. This right, we have held, requires appointment of counsel for indigent defendants whenever a sentence of imprisonment is imposed. Argersinger v. Hamlin,\n\n407 U.\xc2\xa0S. 25,\n37 (1972)\n. But an indigent defendant has no constitutional right to appointed counsel if his conviction results in a fine or other noncustodial punishment. Scott, 440 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 373\xe2\x80\x93374.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cAs separate sovereigns pre-existing the Constitution, tribes have historically been regarded as unconstrained by those constitutional provisions framed specifically as limitations on federal or state authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Santa Clara Pueblo v. Martinez,\n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 49,\n56 (1978)\n. The Bill of Rights, including the\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel, therefore, does not apply in tribal-court proceedings. See Plains Commerce Bank, 554 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 337.

\n

In ICRA, however, Congress accorded a range of procedural safeguards to tribal-court defendants \xe2\x80\x9csimilar, but not identical, to those contained in the Bill of Rights and the\n \nFourteenth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Martinez, 436 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 57; see id., at 62\xe2\x80\x9363 (ICRA \xe2\x80\x9cmodified the safeguards of the Bill of Rights to fit the unique political, cultural, and economic needs of tribal governments\xe2\x80\x9d). In addition to other enumerated protections, ICRA guarantees \xe2\x80\x9cdue process of law,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71302(a)(8), and allows tribal-court defendants to seek habeas corpus review in federal court to test the legality of their imprisonment, \xc2\xa71303.

\n

The right to counsel under ICRA is not coextensive with the\n \nSixth Amendment right. If a tribal court imposes a sentence in excess of one year, ICRA requires the court to accord the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to effective assistance of counsel at least equal to that guaranteed by the United States Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d including appointment of counsel for an indigent defendant at the tribe\xe2\x80\x99s expense. \xc2\xa71302(c)(1), (2). If the sentence imposed is no greater than one year, however, the tribal court must allow a defendant only the opportunity to obtain counsel \xe2\x80\x9cat his own expense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71302(a)(6). In tribal court, therefore, unlike in federal or state court, a sentence of imprisonment up to one year may be imposed without according indigent defendants the right to appointed counsel.

\n

The question here presented: Is it permissible to use uncounseled tribal-court convictions\xe2\x80\x94obtained in full compliance with ICRA\xe2\x80\x94to establish the prior-crimes predicate of \xc2\xa7117(a)? It is undisputed that a conviction obtained in violation of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel cannot be used in a subsequent proceeding \xe2\x80\x9ceither to support guilt or enhance punishment for another offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Burgett v. Texas,\n\n389 U.\xc2\xa0S. 109,\n115 (1967)\n. In Burgett, we held that an uncounseled felony conviction obtained in state court in violation of the right to counsel could not be used in a subsequent proceeding to prove the prior-felony element of a recidivist statute. To permit such use of a constitutionally infirm conviction, we explained, would cause \xe2\x80\x9cthe accused in effect [to] suffe[r] anew from the [prior] deprivation of [his]\n \nSixth Amendment right.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see United States v. Tucker,\n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 443,\n448 (1972)\n (invalid, uncounseled prior convictions could not be relied upon at sentencing to impose a longer term of imprisonment for a subsequent conviction); cf. Loper v. Beto,\n\n405 U.\xc2\xa0S. 473\n\xe2\x80\x93484 (1972) (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9cuse of convictions constitutionally invalid under Gideon v. Wainwright to impeach a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s credibility deprives him of due process of law\xe2\x80\x9d because the prior convictions \xe2\x80\x9clac[k] reliability\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In Nichols v. United States,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 738 (1994)\n, we stated an important limitation on the principle recognized in Burgett. In the case under review, Nichols pleaded guilty to a federal felony drug offense. 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 740. Several years earlier, unrepresented by counsel, he had been convicted of driving under the influence (DUI), a state-law misdemeanor, and fined $250 but not imprisoned. Ibid. Nichols\xe2\x80\x99 DUI conviction, under the then-mandatory Sentencing Guidelines, effectively elevated by about two years the sentencing range for Nichols\xe2\x80\x99 federal drug offense. Ibid. We rejected Nichols\xe2\x80\x99 contention that, as his later sentence for the federal drug offense involved imprisonment, use of his uncounseled DUI conviction to elevate that sentence violated the\n \nSixth Amendment. Id., at 746\xe2\x80\x93747. \xe2\x80\x9c[C]onsistent with the Sixth and\n \nFourteenth Amendments of the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d we held, \xe2\x80\x9can uncounseled misdemeanor conviction, valid under Scott because no prison term was imposed, is also valid when used to enhance punishment at a subsequent conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 748\xe2\x80\x93749.

\n

C

\n

Respondent Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct is illustrative of the domestic violence problem existing in Indian country. During the period relevant to this case, Bryant, an enrolled member of the Northern Cheyenne Tribe, lived on that Tribe\xe2\x80\x99s reservation in Montana. He has a record of over 100 tribal-court convictions, including several misdemeanor convictions for domestic assault. Specifically, between 1997 and 2007, Bryant pleaded guilty on at least five occasions in Northern Cheyenne Tribal Court to committing domestic abuse in violation of the Northern Cheyenne Tribal Code. On one occasion, Bryant hit his live-in girlfriend on the head with a beer bottle and attempted to strangle her. On another, Bryant beat a different girlfriend, kneeing her in the face, breaking her nose, and leaving her bruised and bloodied.

\n

For most of Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s repeated brutal acts of domestic violence, the Tribal Court sentenced him to terms of imprisonment, never exceeding one year. When convicted of these offenses, Bryant was indigent and was not appointed counsel. Because of his short prison terms, Bryant acknowledges, the prior tribal-court proceedings complied with ICRA, and his convictions were therefore valid when entered. Bryant has never challenged his tribal-court convictions in federal court under ICRA\xe2\x80\x99s habeas corpus provision.

\n

In 2011, Bryant was arrested yet again for assaulting women. In February of that year, Bryant attacked his then girlfriend, dragging her off the bed, pulling her hair, and repeatedly punching and kicking her. During an interview with law enforcement officers, Bryant admitted that he had physically assaulted this woman five or six times. Three months later, he assaulted another woman with whom he was then living, waking her by yelling that he could not find his truck keys and then choking her until she almost lost consciousness. Bryant later stated that he had assaulted this victim on three separate occasions during the two months they dated.

\n

Based on the 2011 assaults, a federal grand jury in Montana indicted Bryant on two counts of domestic assault by a habitual offender, in violation of \xc2\xa7117(a). Bryant was represented in federal court by appointed counsel. Contending that the\n \nSixth Amendment precluded use of his prior, uncounseled, tribal-court misdemeanor convictions to satisfy \xc2\xa7117(a)\xe2\x80\x99s predicate-offense element, Bryant moved to dismiss the indictment. The District Court denied the motion, App. to Pet. for Cert. 32a, and Bryant entered a conditional guilty plea, reserving the right to appeal that decision. Bryant was sentenced to concurrent terms of 46 months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment on each count, to be followed by three years of supervised release.

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed the conviction and directed dismissal of the indictment. 769 F.\xc2\xa03d 671 (2014). Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s tribal-court convictions were not themselves constitutionally infirm, the Ninth Circuit comprehended, because \xe2\x80\x9cthe\n \nSixth Amendment right to appointed counsel does not apply in tribal court proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 675. But, the court continued, had the convictions been obtained in state or federal court, they would have violated the\n \nSixth Amendment because Bryant had received sentences of imprisonment although he lacked the aid of appointed counsel. Adhering to its prior decision in United States v. Ant, 882 F.\xc2\xa02d 1389 (CA9 1989),[7] the Court of Appeals held that, subject to narrow exceptions not relevant here, \xe2\x80\x9ctribal court convictions may be used in subsequent [federal] prosecutions only if the tribal court guarantees a right to counsel that is, at minimum, coextensive with the\n \nSixth Amendment right.\xe2\x80\x9d 769 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 677. Rejecting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument that our decision in Nichols required the opposite result, the Ninth Circuit concluded that Nichols applies only when the prior conviction did comport with the\n \nSixth Amendment, i.e., when no sentence of imprisonment was imposed for the prior conviction. 769 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 677\xe2\x80\x93678.

\n

Judge Watford concurred, agreeing that Ant controlled the outcome of this case, but urging reexamination of Ant in light of Nichols. 769 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 679. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Nichols, Judge Watford wrote, \xe2\x80\x9cundermines the notion that uncounseled convictions are, as a categorical matter, too unreliable to be used as a basis for imposing a prison sentence in a subsequent case.\xe2\x80\x9d 769 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 679. The Court of Appeals declined to rehear the case en banc over vigorous dissents by Judges Owens and O\xe2\x80\x99Scannlain.

\n

In disallowing the use of an uncounseled tribal-court conviction to establish a prior domestic violence conviction within \xc2\xa7117(a)\xe2\x80\x99s compass, the Ninth Circuit created a Circuit split. The Eighth and Tenth Circuits have both held that tribal-court \xe2\x80\x9cconvictions, valid at their inception, and not alleged to be otherwise unreliable, may be used to prove the elements of \xc2\xa7117.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Cavanaugh, 643 F.\xc2\xa03d 592, 594 (CA8 2011); see United States v. Shavanaux, 647 F.\xc2\xa03d 993, 1000 (CA10 2011). To resolve this disagreement, we granted certiorari, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s tribal-court convictions, he recognizes, infringed no constitutional right because the\n \nSixth Amendment does not apply to tribal-court proceedings. Brief for Respondent 5. Those prior convictions complied with ICRA, he concedes, and therefore were valid when entered. But, had his convictions occurred in state or federal court, Bryant observes, Argersinger and Scott would have rendered them invalid because he was sentenced to incarceration without representation by court-appointed counsel. Essentially, Bryant urges us to treat tribal-court convictions, for \xc2\xa7117(a) purposes, as though they had been entered by a federal or state court. We next explain why we decline to do so.

\n

As earlier recounted, we held in Nichols that \xe2\x80\x9can uncounseled misdemeanor conviction, valid under Scott because no prison term was imposed, is also valid when used to enhance punishment at a subsequent conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 748\xe2\x80\x93749. \xe2\x80\x9cEnhancement statutes,\xe2\x80\x9d we reasoned, \xe2\x80\x9cdo not change the penalty imposed for the earlier conviction\xe2\x80\x9d; rather, repeat-offender laws \xe2\x80\x9cpenaliz[e] only the last offense committed by the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 747; see United States v. Rodriquez,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 377,\n386 (2008)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a defendant is given a higher sentence under a recidivism statute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. 100% of the punishment is for the offense of conviction. None is for the prior convictions or the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98status as a recidivist.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Nichols thus instructs that convictions valid when entered\xe2\x80\x94that is, those that, when rendered, did not violate the Constitution\xe2\x80\x94retain that status when invoked in a subsequent proceeding.

\n

Nichols\xe2\x80\x99 reasoning steers the result here. Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s 46-month sentence for violating \xc2\xa7117(a) punishes his most recent acts of domestic assault, not his prior crimes prosecuted in tribal court. Bryant was denied no right to counsel in tribal court, and his\n \nSixth Amendment right was honored in federal court, when he was \xe2\x80\x9cadjudicated guilty of the felony offense for which he was imprisoned.\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama v. Shelton,\n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 654,\n664 (2002)\n. It would be \xe2\x80\x9codd to say that a conviction untainted by a violation of the\n \nSixth Amendment triggers a violation of that same amendment when it\xe2\x80\x99s used in a subsequent case where the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to appointed counsel is fully respected.\xe2\x80\x9d 769 F. 3d, at 679 (Watford, J., concurring).[8]

\n

Bryant acknowledges that had he been punished only by fines in his tribal-court proceedings, Nichols would have allowed reliance on his uncounseled convictions to satisfy \xc2\xa7117(a)\xe2\x80\x99s prior-crimes predicate. Brief for Respondent 50. We see no cause to distinguish for \xc2\xa7117(a) purposes between valid but uncounseled convictions resulting in a fine and valid but uncounseled convictions resulting in imprisonment not exceeding one year. \xe2\x80\x9cBoth Nichols\xe2\x80\x99s and Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s uncounseled convictions \xe2\x80\x98comport\xe2\x80\x99 with the\n \nSixth Amendment, and for the same reason: the\n \nSixth Amendment right to appointed counsel did not apply to either conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 50a (O\xe2\x80\x99Scannlain, J., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc).

\n

In keeping with Nichols, we resist creating a \xe2\x80\x9chybrid\xe2\x80\x9d category of tribal-court convictions, \xe2\x80\x9cgood for the punishment actually imposed but not available for sentence enhancement in a later prosecution.\xe2\x80\x9d 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 744. Nichols indicates that use of Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s uncounseled tribal-court convictions in his \xc2\xa7117(a) prosecution did not \xe2\x80\x9ctransform his prior, valid, tribal court convictions into new, invalid, federal ones.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 50a (opinion of O\xe2\x80\x99Scannlain, J.).

\n

Our decision in Burgett, which prohibited the subsequent use of a conviction obtained in violation of the right to counsel, does not aid Bryant. Reliance on an invalid conviction, Burgett reasoned, would cause the accused to \xe2\x80\x9csuffe[r] anew from the deprivation of [his]\n \nSixth Amendment right.\xe2\x80\x9d 389 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 115. Because a defendant convicted in tribal court suffers no\n \nSixth Amendment violation in the first instance, \xe2\x80\x9c[u]se of tribal convictions in a subsequent prosecution cannot violate [the\n \nSixth Amendment] \xe2\x80\x98anew.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Shavanaux, 647 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 998.

\n

Bryant observes that reliability concerns underlie our right-to-counsel decisions and urges that those concerns remain even if the\n \nSixth Amendment itself does not shelter him. Scott and Nichols, however, counter the argument that uncounseled misdemeanor convictions are categorically unreliable, either in their own right or for use in a subsequent proceeding. Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s recognition that a tribal-court conviction resulting in a fine would qualify as a \xc2\xa7117(a) predicate offense, we further note, diminishes the force of his reliability-based argument. There is no reason to suppose that tribal-court proceedings are less reliable when a sentence of a year\xe2\x80\x99s imprisonment is imposed than when the punishment is merely a fine. No evidentiary or procedural variation turns on the sanction\xe2\x80\x94fine only or a year in prison\xe2\x80\x94ultimately imposed.

\n

Bryant also invokes the Due Process Clause of the\n \nFifth Amendment in support of his assertion that tribal-court judgments should not be used as predicate offenses. But, as earlier observed, ICRA itself requires tribes to ensure \xe2\x80\x9cdue process of law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71302(a)(8), and it accords defendants specific procedural safeguards resembling those contained in the Bill of Rights and the\n \nFourteenth Amendment. See supra, at 8. Further, ICRA makes habeas review in federal court available to persons incarcerated pursuant to a tribal-court judgment. \xc2\xa71303. By that means, a prisoner may challenge the fundamental fairness of the proceedings in tribal court. Proceedings in compliance with ICRA, Congress determined, and we agree, sufficiently ensure the reliability of tribal-court convictions. Therefore, the use of those convictions in a federal prosecution does not violate a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to due process. See Shavanaux, 647 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1000; cf. State v. Spotted Eagle, 316 Mont. 370, 378\xe2\x80\x93379, 71 P.\xc2\xa03d 1239, 1245\xe2\x80\x931246 (2003) (principles of comity support recognizing uncounseled tribal-court convictions that complied with ICRA).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Because Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s tribal-court convictions occurred in proceedings that complied with ICRA and were therefore valid when entered, use of those convictions as predicate offenses in a \xc2\xa7117(a) prosecution does not violate the Constitution. We accordingly reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, concurring.

\n

The Court holds that neither the\n \nSixth Amendment nor the\n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause prohibits the Government from using Michael Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s uncounseled tribal-court convictions as predicates for the federal crime of committing a domestic assault within Indian country. Ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316; see\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7117(a) (making it a federal crime to \xe2\x80\x9ccommi[t] a domestic assault within .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Indian country\xe2\x80\x9d if the person \xe2\x80\x9chas a final conviction on at least 2 separate prior occasions in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Indian tribal court proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d for domestic assault and similar crimes). Because our precedents dictate that holding, I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

\n

The fact that this case arose at all, however, illustrates how far afield our\n \nSixth Amendment and Indian-law precedents have gone. Three basic assumptions underlie this case: that the\n \nSixth Amendment ordinarily bars the Government from introducing, in a later proceeding, convictions obtained in violation of the right to counsel, ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939; that tribes\xe2\x80\x99 retained sovereignty entitles them to prosecute tribal members in proceedings that are not subject to the Constitution, ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938; and that Congress can punish assaults that tribal members commit against each other on Indian land, ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x937. Although our precedents have endorsed these assumptions for decades, the Court has never identified a sound constitutional basis for any of them, and I see none.

\n

Start with the notion that the\n \nSixth Amendment generally prohibits the government from using a prior, uncounseled conviction obtained in violation of the right to counsel as a predicate for a new offense in a new proceeding. Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. All that the text of the\n \nSixth Amendment requires in a criminal prosecution is that the accused enjoy the \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ssistance of [c]ounsel\xe2\x80\x9d in that proceeding. The Court was likely wrong in Burgett v. Texas, \n\n389 U.\xc2\xa0S. 109 (1967)\n, when it created a\n \nSixth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cexclusionary rule\xe2\x80\x9d that prohibits the government from using prior convictions obtained in violation of the right to counsel in subsequent proceedings to avoid \xe2\x80\x9cerod[ing] the principle\xe2\x80\x9d of the right to counsel. Id., at 115. I would be open to reconsidering Burgett in a future case.

\n

The remaining two assumptions underpinning this case exemplify a central tension within our Indian-law jurisprudence. On the one hand, the only reason why tribal courts had the power to convict Bryant in proceedings where he had no right to counsel is that such prosecutions are a function of a tribe\xe2\x80\x99s core sovereignty. See United States v. Lara,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193,\n197 (2004)\n; United States v. Wheeler,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 313\n\xe2\x80\x93323 (1978). By virtue of tribes\xe2\x80\x99 status as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98separate sovereigns pre-existing the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d tribal prosecutions need not, under our precedents, comply with \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98those constitutional provisions framed specifically as limitations on federal or state authority.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938 (quoting Santa Clara Pueblo v. Martinez,\n\n436 U.\xc2\xa0S. 49,\n56 (1978)\n).

\n

On the other hand, the validity of Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s ensuing federal conviction rests upon a contrary view of tribal sovereignty. Congress ordinarily lacks authority to enact a general federal criminal law proscribing domestic abuse. See United States v. Morrison,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 598\n\xe2\x80\x93613 (2000). But, the Court suggests, Congress must intervene on reservations to ensure that prolific domestic abusers receive sufficient punishment. See ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. The Court does not explain where Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power to act comes from, but our precedents leave no doubt on this score. Congress could make Bryant\xe2\x80\x99s domestic assaults a federal crime subject to federal prosecution only because our precedents have endowed Congress with an \xe2\x80\x9call-encompassing\xe2\x80\x9d power over all aspects of tribal sovereignty. Wheeler, supra, at 319. Thus, even though tribal prosecutions of tribal members are purportedly the apex of tribal sovereignty, Congress can second-guess how tribes prosecute domestic abuse perpetrated by Indians against other Indians on Indian land by virtue of its \xe2\x80\x9cplenary power\xe2\x80\x9d over Indian tribes. See United States v. Kagama,\n\n118 U.\xc2\xa0S. 375\n\xe2\x80\x93384 (1886); accord, Lara, 541 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 200.

\n

I continue to doubt whether either view of tribal sovereignty is correct. See id., at 215 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). Indian tribes have varied origins, discrete treaties with the United States, and different patterns of assimilation and conquest. In light of the tribes\xe2\x80\x99 distinct histories, it strains credulity to assume that all tribes necessarily retained the sovereign prerogative of prosecuting their own members. And by treating all tribes as possessing an identical quantum of sovereignty, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents have made it all but impossible to understand the ultimate source of each tribe\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty and whether it endures. See Prakash, Against Tribal Fungibility, 89 Cornell L. Rev. 1069, 1070\xe2\x80\x931074, 1107\xe2\x80\x931110 (2004).

\n

Congress\xe2\x80\x99 purported plenary power over Indian tribes rests on even shakier foundations. No enumerated power\xe2\x80\x94not Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power to \xe2\x80\x9cregulate Commerce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with In-dian Tribes,\xe2\x80\x9d not the Senate\xe2\x80\x99s role in approving treaties, nor anything else\xe2\x80\x94gives Congress such sweeping authority. See Lara, supra, at 224\xe2\x80\x93225 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment); Adoptive Couple v. Baby Girl, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2013) (Thomas, J., concurring) (slip op., at 3\xe2\x80\x935). Indeed, the Court created this new power because it was unable to find an enumerated power justifying the federal Major Crimes Act, which for the first time punished crimes committed by Indians against Indians on Indian land. See Kagama, supra, at 377\xe2\x80\x93380; cf. ante, at 5. The Court asserted: \xe2\x80\x9cThe power of the General Government over these remnants of a race once powerful, now weak and diminished in numbers, is necessary to their protection .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. It must exist in that government, because it has never existed anywhere else.\xe2\x80\x9d Kagama, supra, at 384. Over a century later, Kagama endures as the foundation of this doctrine, and the Court has searched in vain for any valid constitutional justification for this unfettered power. See, e.g., Lone Wolf v. Hitchcock,\n\n187 U.\xc2\xa0S. 553\n\xe2\x80\x93567 (1903) (relying on Kagama\xe2\x80\x99s race-based plenary power theory); Winton v. Amos,\n\n255 U.\xc2\xa0S. 373\n\xe2\x80\x93392 (1921) (Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cplenary authority\xe2\x80\x9d is based on Indians\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9ccondition of tutelage or dependency\xe2\x80\x9d); Wheeler, supra, at 319 (Winton and Lone Wolf illustrate the \xe2\x80\x9cundisputed fact that Congress has plenary authority\xe2\x80\x9d over tribes); Lara, supra, at 224 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court utterly fails to find any provision of the Constitution that gives Congress enumerated power to alter tribal sovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

It is time that the Court reconsider these precedents. Until the Court ceases treating all Indian tribes as an undifferentiated mass, our case law will remain bedeviled by amorphous and ahistorical assumptions about the scope of tribal sovereignty. And, until the Court rejects the fiction that Congress possesses plenary power over Indian affairs, our precedents will continue to be based on the paternalistic theory that Congress must assume all-encompassing control over the \xe2\x80\x9cremnants of a race\xe2\x80\x9d for its own good. Kagama, supra, at 384.
