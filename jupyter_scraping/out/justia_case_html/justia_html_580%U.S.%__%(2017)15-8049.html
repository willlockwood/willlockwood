BUCK v. DAVIS, DIRECTOR, TEXAS DEPARTMENT OF CRIMINAL JUSTICE, CORRECTIONAL INSTITUTIONS DIVISION

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

No. 15\xe2\x80\x938049.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 5, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 22, 2017

Petitioner Duane Buck was convicted of capital murder in a Texas court. Under state law, the jury was permitted to impose a death sentence only if it found unanimously and beyond a reasonable doubt that Buck was likely to commit acts of violence in the future. Buck\xe2\x80\x99s attorney called a psychologist, Dr. Walter Quijano, to offer his opinion on that issue. Dr. Quijano had been appointed to evaluate Buck by the presiding judge and had prepared a report setting out his conclusions. To determine the likelihood that Buck would act violently in the future, Dr. Quijano had considered a number of statistical factors, including Buck\xe2\x80\x99s race. Although Dr. Quijano ultimately concluded that Buck was unlikely to be a future danger, his report also stated that Buck was statistically more likely to act violently because he is black. The report read, in relevant part: \xe2\x80\x9cRace. Black: Increased probability.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 19a. Despite knowing the contents of the report, Buck\xe2\x80\x99s counsel called Dr. Quijano to the stand, where he testified that race is a factor \xe2\x80\x9cknow[n] to predict future dangerousness.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 146a. Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s report was admitted into evidence at the close of his testimony. The prosecution questioned Dr. Quijano about his conclusions on race and violence during cross-examination, and it relied on his testimony in summation. During deliberations, the jury requested and received the expert reports admitted into evidence, including Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s. The jury returned a sentence of death.

Buck contends that his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s introduction of this evidence violated his Sixth Amendment right to the effective assistance of counsel. Buck failed to raise this claim in his first state postconviction proceeding. While that proceeding was pending, this Court received a petition for certiorari in Saldano v. Texas,530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1212, a case in which Dr. Quijano had testified that the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Hispanic heritage weighed in favor of a finding of future dangerousness. Texas confessed error on that ground, and this Court vacated the judgment below. Soon afterward, the Texas Attorney General issued a public statement identifying six similar cases in which Dr. Quijano had testified. Buck\xe2\x80\x99s was one of them. In the other five cases, the Attorney General confessed error and consented to resentencing. But when Buck filed a second state habeas petition alleging that his attorney had been ineffective in introducing Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony, the State did not confess error, and the court dismissed the petition as an abuse of the writ on the ground that Buck had failed to raise the claim in his first petition.

Buck then sought federal habeas relief under28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. The State again declined to confess error, and Buck\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective assistance claim was held procedurally defaulted and unreviewable under Coleman v. Thompson,501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 722. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s later decisions in Martinez v. Ryan,566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, and Trevino v. Thaler, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, modified the rule of Coleman. Had they been decided before Buck filed his federal habeas petition, Buck\xe2\x80\x99s claim could have been heard on the merits provided he had demonstrated that (1) state postconviction counsel had been constitutionally ineffective in failing to raise the claim, and (2) the claim had some merit. Following the decision in Trevino, Buck sought to reopen his \xc2\xa72254 case under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b)(6). To demonstrate the \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d required for relief, Gonzalez v. Crosby,545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 524, Buck cited the change in law effected by Martinez and Trevino, as well as ten other factors, including the introduction of expert testimony linking Buck\xe2\x80\x99s race to violence and the State\xe2\x80\x99s confession of error in similar cases. The District Court denied relief. Reasoning that \xe2\x80\x9cthe introduction of any mention of race\xe2\x80\x9d during Buck\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing was \xe2\x80\x9cde minimis,\xe2\x80\x9d the court concluded, first, that Buck had failed to demonstrate extraordinary circumstances; and second, that even if the circumstances were extraordinary, Buck had failed to demonstrate ineffective assistance under Strickland v. Washington,466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668. Buck sought a certificate of appealability (COA) from the Fifth Circuit to appeal the denial of his Rule 60(b)(6) motion. The Fifth Circuit denied his application, concluding that he had not shown extraordinary circumstances justifying relief from the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

Held:

1.\xc2\xa0The Fifth Circuit exceeded the limited scope of the COA analysis. The COA statute sets forth a two-step process: an initial determination whether a claim is reasonably debatable, and, if so, an appeal in the normal course.28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72253. At the first stage, the only question is whether the applicant has shown that \xe2\x80\x9cjurists of reason could disagree with the district court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of his constitutional claims or .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. could conclude the issues presented are adequate to deserve encouragement to proceed further.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller-El v. Cockrell,537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 322. Here, the Fifth Circuit phrased its determination in proper terms. But it reached its conclusion only after essentially deciding the case on the merits, repeatedly faulting Buck for having failed to demonstrate extraordinary circumstances. The question for the Court of Appeals was not whether Buck had shown that his case is extraordinary; it was whether jurists of reason could debate that issue. The State points to the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s thorough consideration of the merits to defend that court\xe2\x80\x99s approach, but this hurts rather than helps its case. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9315.

2.\xc2\xa0Buck has demonstrated ineffective assistance of counsel under Strickland. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9320.

(a)\xc2\xa0To satisfy Strickland, a defendant must first show that counsel performed deficiently. 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 687. Buck\xe2\x80\x99s trial counsel knew that Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s report reflected the view that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s race predisposed him to violent conduct and that the principal point of dispute during the penalty phase was Buck\xe2\x80\x99s future dangerousness. Counsel nevertheless called Dr. Quijano to the stand, specifically elicited testimony about the connection between race and violence, and put Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s report into evidence. No competent defense attorney would introduce evidence that his client is liable to be a future danger because of his race. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9317.

(b)\xc2\xa0Strickland further requires a defendant to demonstrate prejudice\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 694. It is reasonably probable that without Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony on race and violence, at least one juror would have harbored a reasonable doubt on the question of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s future dangerousness. This issue required the jury to make a predictive judgment inevitably entailing a degree of speculation. But Buck\xe2\x80\x99s race was not subject to speculation, and according to Dr. Quijano, that immutable characteristic carried with it an increased probability of future violence. Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony appealed to a powerful racial stereotype and might well have been valued by jurors as the opinion of a medical expert bearing the court\xe2\x80\x99s imprimatur. For these reasons, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that any mention of race during the penalty phase was de minimis is rejected. So is the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Buck was not prejudiced by Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony because it was introduced by his own counsel, rather than the prosecution. Jurors understand that prosecutors seek convictions and may reasonably be expected to evaluate the government\xe2\x80\x99s evidence in light of its motivations. When damaging evidence is introduced by a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own lawyer, it is in the nature of an admission against interest, more likely to be taken at face value. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9320.

3.\xc2\xa0The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b)(6) motion was an abuse of discretion. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9326.

(a)\xc2\xa0Relief under Rule 60(b)(6) is available only in \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzalez, 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 535. Determining whether such circumstances are present may include consideration of a wide range of factors, including \xe2\x80\x9cthe risk of injustice to the parties\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe risk of undermining the public\xe2\x80\x99s confidence in the judicial process.\xe2\x80\x9d Liljeberg v. Health Services Acquisition Corp.,486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 847\xe2\x80\x93864. The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s motion rested largely on its determination that race played only a de minimis role in his sentencing. But there is a reasonable probability that Buck was sentenced to death in part because of his race. This is a disturbing departure from the basic premise that our criminal law punishes people for what they do, not who they are. That it concerned race amplifies the problem. Relying on race to impose a criminal sanction \xe2\x80\x9cpoisons public confidence\xe2\x80\x9d in the judicial process, Davis v. Ayala, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, a concern that supports Rule 60(b)(6) relief. The extraordinary nature of this case is confirmed by the remarkable steps the State itself took in response to Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony in other cases. Although the State attempts to justify its decision to treat Buck differently from the other five defendants identified in the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s public statement, its explanations for distinguishing Buck\xe2\x80\x99s case from Saldano have nothing to do with the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s stated reasons for confessing error in that case. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9324.

(b)\xc2\xa0Unless Martinez and Trevino, rather than Coleman, would govern Buck\xe2\x80\x99s case were it reopened, his claim would remain unreviewable and Rule 60(b)(6) relief would be inappropriate. The State argues that Martinez and Trevino would not govern Buck\xe2\x80\x99s case because they announced a \xe2\x80\x9cnew rule\xe2\x80\x9d under Teague v. Lane,489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288, that does not apply retroactively to cases (like Buck\xe2\x80\x99s) on collateral review. This argument, however, has been waived: the State failed to advance it in District Court, before the Fifth Circuit, or in its brief in opposition to Buck\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari. Pp. 24\xe2\x80\x9326.

623 Fed. Appx. 668, reversed and remanded.

Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., delivered the opinion of the Court, in which Kennedy, Ginsburg, Breyer, Sotomayor, and Kagan, JJ., joined. Thomas, J., filed a dissenting opinion, in which Alito, J., joined.

DUANE EDWARD BUCK, PETITIONER v. LORIE DAVIS, DIRECTOR, TEXAS DEPARTMENT OF CRIMINAL JUSTICE, CORRECTIONAL INSTITUTIONS DIVISION

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

[February 22, 2017]

Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

A Texas jury convicted petitioner Duane Buck of capital murder. Under state law, the jury could impose a death sentence only if it found that Buck was likely to commit acts of violence in the future. Buck\xe2\x80\x99s attorney called a psychologist to offer his opinion on that issue. The psychologist testified that Buck probably would not engage in violent conduct. But he also stated that one of the factors pertinent in assessing a person\xe2\x80\x99s propensity for violence was his race, and that Buck was statistically more likely to act violently because he is black. The jury sentenced Buck to death.

Buck contends that his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s introduction of this evidence violated his Sixth Amendment right to the effective assistance of counsel. This claim has never been heard on the merits in any court, because the attorney who represented Buck in his first state postconviction proceeding failed to raise it. In 2006, a Federal District Court relied on that failure\xe2\x80\x94properly, under then-governing law\xe2\x80\x94to hold that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s claim was procedurally defaulted and unreviewable.

In 2014, Buck sought to reopen that 2006 judgment by filing a motion under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b)(6). He argued that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions in Martinez v. Ryan,566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (2012), and Trevino v. Thaler, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013), had changed the law in a way that provided an excuse for his procedural default, permitting him to litigate his claim on the merits. In addition to this change in the law, Buck\xe2\x80\x99s motion identified ten other factors that, he said, constituted the \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d required to justify reopening the 2006 judgment under the Rule. See Gonzalez v. Crosby,545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 524,535 (2005).

The District Court below denied the motion, and the Fifth Circuit declined to issue the certificate of appealability (COA) requested by Buck to appeal that decision. We granted certiorari, and now reverse.

I

A

On the morning of July 30, 1995, Duane Buck arrived at the home of his former girlfriend, Debra Gardner. He was carrying a rifle and a shotgun. Buck entered the home, shot Phyllis Taylor, his stepsister, and then shot Gardner\xe2\x80\x99s friend Kenneth Butler. Gardner fled the house, and Buck followed. So did Gardner\xe2\x80\x99s young children. While Gardner\xe2\x80\x99s son and daughter begged for their mother\xe2\x80\x99s life, Buck shot Gardner in the chest. Gardner and Butler died of their wounds. Taylor survived.

Police officers arrived soon after the shooting and placed Buck under arrest. An officer would later testify that Buck was laughing at the scene. He remained \xe2\x80\x9chappy\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cupbeat\xe2\x80\x9d as he was driven to the police station, \xe2\x80\x9c[s]miling and laughing\xe2\x80\x9d in the back of the patrol car. App. 134a\xe2\x80\x93135a, 252a.

Buck was tried for capital murder, and the jury convicted. During the penalty phase of the trial, the jury was charged with deciding two issues. The first was what the parties term the \xe2\x80\x9cfuture dangerousness\xe2\x80\x9d question. At the time of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s trial, a Texas jury could impose the death penalty only if it found\xe2\x80\x94unanimously and beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ca probability that the defendant would commit criminal acts of violence that would constitute a continuing threat to society.\xe2\x80\x9d Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Ann., Art. 37.071, \xc2\xa72(b)(1) (Vernon 1998). The second issue, to be reached only if the jury found Buck likely to be a future danger, was whether mitigating circumstances nevertheless warranted a sentence of life imprisonment instead of death. See \xc2\xa72(e).

The parties focused principally on the first question. The State called witnesses who emphasized the brutality of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s crime and his evident lack of remorse in its aftermath. The State also called another former girlfriend, Vivian Jackson. She testified that, during their relationship, Buck had routinely hit her and had twice pointed a gun at her. Finally, the State introduced evidence of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s criminal history, including convictions for delivery of cocaine and unlawfully carrying a weapon. App. 125a\xe2\x80\x93127a, 185a.

Defense counsel answered with a series of lay witnesses, including Buck\xe2\x80\x99s father and stepmother, who testified that they had never known him to be violent. Counsel also called two psychologists to testify as experts. The first, Dr. Patrick Lawrence, observed that Buck had previously served time in prison and had been held in minimum custody. From this he concluded that Buck \xe2\x80\x9cdid not present any problems in the prison setting.\xe2\x80\x9d Record in No. 4:04\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9303965 (SD Tex.), Doc. 5\xe2\x80\x93116, pp. 12\xe2\x80\x9313. Dr. Lawrence further testified that murders within the Texas penal system tend to be gang related (there was no evidence Buck had ever been a member of a gang) and that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s offense had been a \xe2\x80\x9ccrime of passion\xe2\x80\x9d occurring within the context of a romantic relationship. Id., at 4, 19, 21. Based on these considerations, Dr. Lawrence determined that Buck was unlikely to be a danger if he were sentenced to life in prison. Id., at 20\xe2\x80\x9321.

Buck\xe2\x80\x99s second expert, Dr. Walter Quijano, had been appointed by the presiding judge to conduct a psychological evaluation. Dr. Quijano had met with Buck in prison prior to trial and shared a report of his findings with defense counsel.

Like Dr. Lawrence, Dr. Quijano thought it significant that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s prior acts of violence had arisen from romantic relationships with women; Buck, of course, would not form any such relationships while incarcerated. And Dr. Quijano likewise considered Buck\xe2\x80\x99s behavioral record in prison a good indicator that future violence was unlikely. App. 36a, 39a\xe2\x80\x9340a.

But there was more to the report. In determining whether Buck was likely to pose a danger in the future, Dr. Quijano considered seven \xe2\x80\x9cstatistical factors.\xe2\x80\x9d The fourth factor was \xe2\x80\x9crace.\xe2\x80\x9d His report read, in relevant part: \xe2\x80\x9c4. Race. Black: Increased probability. There is an over-representation of Blacks among the violent offenders.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 19a.

Despite knowing Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s view that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s race was competent evidence of an increased probability of future violence, defense counsel called Dr. Quijano to the stand and asked him to discuss the \xe2\x80\x9cstatistical factors\xe2\x80\x9d he had \xe2\x80\x9clooked at in regard to this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 145a\xe2\x80\x93146a. Dr. Quijano responded that certain factors were \xe2\x80\x9cknow[n] to predict future dangerousness\xe2\x80\x9d and, consistent with his report, identified race as one of them. Id., at 146a. \xe2\x80\x9cIt\xe2\x80\x99s a sad commentary,\xe2\x80\x9d he testified, \xe2\x80\x9cthat minorities, Hispanics and black people, are over represented in the Criminal Justice System.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Through further questioning, counsel elicited testimony concerning factors Dr. Quijano thought favorable to Buck, as well as his ultimate opinion that Buck was unlikely to pose a danger in the future. At the close of Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony, his report was admitted into evidence. Id., at 150a\xe2\x80\x93152a.

After opening cross-examination with a series of general questions, the prosecutor likewise turned to the report. She asked first about the statistical factors of past crimes and age, then questioned Dr. Quijano about the roles of sex and race: \xe2\x80\x9cYou have determined that the sex factor, that a male is more violent than a female because that\xe2\x80\x99s just the way it is, and that the race factor, black, increases the future dangerousness for various complicated reasons; is that correct?\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 170a. Dr. Quijano replied, \xe2\x80\x9cYes.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

During closing arguments, defense counsel emphasized that Buck had proved to be \xe2\x80\x9ccontrollable in the prison population,\xe2\x80\x9d and that his crime was one of \xe2\x80\x9cjealousy, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. passion and emotion\xe2\x80\x9d unlikely to be repeated in jail. Id., at 189a\xe2\x80\x93191a. The State stressed the crime\xe2\x80\x99s brutal nature and Buck\xe2\x80\x99s lack of remorse, along with the inability of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s own experts to guarantee that he would not act violently in the future\xe2\x80\x94a point it supported by reference to Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony. See id., at 198a\xe2\x80\x93199a (\xe2\x80\x9cYou heard from Dr. Quijano, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. who told you that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the probability did exist that [Buck] would be a continuing threat to society.\xe2\x80\x9d).

The jury deliberated over the course of two days. During that time it sent out four notes, one of which requested the \xe2\x80\x9cpsychology reports\xe2\x80\x9d that had been admitted into evidence. Id., at 209a. These reports\xe2\x80\x94including Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x94were provided. The jury returned a sentence of death.

B

Buck\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and sentence were affirmed on direct appeal. Buck v. State, No. 72,810 (Tex. Crim. App., Apr. 28, 1999). His case then entered a labyrinth of state and federal collateral review, where it has wandered for the better part of two decades.

Buck filed his first petition for a writ of habeas corpus in Texas state court in 1999. The four claims advanced in his petition, however, were all frivolous or noncognizable. See Ex\xc2\xa0parte Buck, No. 699684\xe2\x80\x93A (Dist. Ct. Harris Cty., Tex., July 11, 2003), pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937. The petition failed to mention defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s introduction of expert testimony that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s race increased his propensity for violence.

But Dr. Quijano had testified in other cases, too, and in 1999, while Buck\xe2\x80\x99s first habeas petition was pending, one of those cases reached this Court. The petitioner, Victor Hugo Saldano, argued that his death sentence had been tainted by Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony that Saldano\xe2\x80\x99s Hispanic heritage \xe2\x80\x9cwas a factor weighing in the favor of future dangerousness.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 302a. Texas confessed error on that ground and asked this Court to grant Saldano\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari, vacate the state court judgment, and remand the case. In June 2000, the Court did so. Saldano v. Texas,530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1212.

Within days, the Texas Attorney General, John Cornyn, issued a public statement concerning the cases in which Dr. Quijano had testified. The statement affirmed that \xe2\x80\x9cit is inappropriate to allow race to be considered as a factor in our criminal justice system.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 213a. In keeping with that principle, the Attorney General explained that his office had conducted a \xe2\x80\x9cthorough audit\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cidentified eight more cases in which testimony was offered by Dr. Quijano that race should be a factor for the jury to consider in making its determination about the sentence in a capital murder trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Six of those cases were \xe2\x80\x9csimilar to that of Victor Hugo Saldano\xe2\x80\x9d; in those cases, letters had been sent to counsel apprising them of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s findings. Id., at 213a\xe2\x80\x93214a. The statement closed by identifying the defendants in those six cases. Buck was one of them. Id., at 215a\xe2\x80\x93217a. By the close of 2002, the Attorney General had confessed error, waived any avail-able procedural defenses, and consented to resentencing in the cases of five of those six defendants. See Alba v. Johnson, 232 F. 3d 208 (CA5 2000) (Table); Memorandum and Order in Blue v. Johnson, No. 4:99\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9300350 (SD Tex.), pp. 15\xe2\x80\x9317; Order in Garcia v. Johnson, No. 1:99\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9300134 (ED Tex.), p. 1; Order in Broxton v. Johnson, No. 4:00\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9301034 (SD Tex.), pp. 10\xe2\x80\x9311; Final Judgment in Gonzales v. Cockrell, No. 7:99\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9300072 (WD Tex.), p. 1.

Not, however, in Buck\xe2\x80\x99s. In 2002, Buck\xe2\x80\x99s attorney filed a new state habeas petition alleging that trial counsel had rendered ineffective assistance by introducing Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony. The State was not represented by the Attorney General in this proceeding\xe2\x80\x94the Texas Attorney General represents state respondents in federal habeas cases, but not state habeas cases\xe2\x80\x94and it did not confess error. Because Buck\xe2\x80\x99s petition was successive, the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals dismissed it as an abuse of the writ. Ex\xc2\xa0parte Buck, Nos. 57,004\xe2\x80\x9301, 57,004\xe2\x80\x9302 (Tex. Crim. App., Oct. 15, 2003) (\xc2\xa0per curiam).

Buck turned to the federal courts. He filed a petition for habeas corpus under28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254 in October 2004, by which time Attorney General Cornyn had left office. See Buck v. Dretke, 2006 WL 8411481, *2 (SD Tex., July 24, 2006). Buck sought relief on the ground that trial counsel\xe2\x80\x99s introduction of Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony was constitutionally ineffective. The State responded that the state court had dismissed Buck\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective assistance claim because Buck had failed to press it in his first petition, raising it for the first time in a procedurally improper second petition. The State argued that such reliance on an established state rule of procedure was an adequate and independent state ground precluding federal review. Texas acknowledged that it had waived similar procedural defenses in Saldano\xe2\x80\x99s case. But it argued that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s case was different because \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n Saldano\xe2\x80\x99s case Dr. Quijano testified for the State\xe2\x80\x9d; in Buck\xe2\x80\x99s, \xe2\x80\x9cit was Buck who called Dr. Quijano to testify.\xe2\x80\x9d Answer and Motion for Summary Judgment in No. 4:04\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9303965 (SD Tex.), p.\xc2\xa020.

Buck countered that, notwithstanding his procedural default, the District Court should reach the merits of his claim because a failure to do so would result in a miscarriage of justice. Buck did not argue that his default should be excused on a showing of \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprejudice\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, cause for the default, and prejudice from the denial of a federal right. And for good reason: At the time Buck filed his \xc2\xa72254 petition, our decision in Coleman v. Thompson,501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 722\xe2\x80\x93753 (1991), made clear that an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s failure to raise an ineffective assistance claim during state postconviction review could not constitute cause. The District Court rejected Buck\xe2\x80\x99s miscarriage of justice argument and held that, because of his procedural default, his ineffective assistance claim was unreviewable. Buck v. Dretke, 2006 WL 8411481, at *8. Buck unsuccessfully sought review of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling. See Buck v. Thaler, 345 Fed. Appx. 923 (CA5 2009) (\xc2\xa0per curiam) (denying application for a COA), cert. denied,559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1072 (2010).

In 2011, Buck sought to reopen his case, arguing that the prosecution had violated the Equal Protection and Due Process Clauses by asking Dr. Quijano about the relationship between race and future violence on cross-examination and referring to his testimony during summation. Buck also argued that the State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to treat him differently from the other defendants affected by Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony justified relieving him of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s adverse judgment. The Fifth Circuit disagreed, see Buck v. Thaler, 452 Fed. Appx. 423, 427\xe2\x80\x93428 (CA5 2011) (\xc2\xa0per curiam), and we denied certiorari, Buck v. Thaler,565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1022 (2011). Buck, still barred by Coleman from avoiding the consequences of his procedural default, did not pursue his ineffective assistance claim.

C

In 2012, this Court \xe2\x80\x9cmodif[ied] the unqualified statement in Coleman that an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s ignorance or inadvertence in a postconviction proceeding does not qualify as cause to excuse a procedural default.\xe2\x80\x9d Martinez, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 9. We held that when a state formally limits the adjudication of claims of ineffective assistance of trial counsel to collateral review, a prisoner may establish cause for procedural default if (1) \xe2\x80\x9cthe state courts did not appoint counsel in the initial-review collateral proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cappointed counsel in [that] proceeding .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was ineffective under the standards of Strickland v. Washington,466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668 (1984)\xe2\x80\x9d; and (2) \xe2\x80\x9cthe underlying .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. claim is a substantial one, which is to say that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the claim has some merit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14.

By its terms, Martinez did not bear on Buck\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective assistance claim. At the time of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and appeal, Texas did not formally require criminal defendants to reserve such claims for collateral review. In Tre-vino, however, the Court concluded that the exception an-nounced in Martinez extended to state systems that, as a practical matter, deny criminal defendants \xe2\x80\x9ca meaningful opportunity\xe2\x80\x9d to press ineffective assistance claims on direct appeal. 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 13). The Court further concluded that the system in Texas, where petitioner had been convicted, was such a system. Ibid. The upshot: Had Martinez and Trevino been decided before Buck filed his \xc2\xa72254 petition, a federal court could have reviewed Buck\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective assistance claim if he demonstrated that (1) state postconviction counsel had been constitutionally ineffective in failing to raise it, and (2) the claim had \xe2\x80\x9csome merit.\xe2\x80\x9d Martinez, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 14.

D

When Trevino was decided, Buck\xe2\x80\x99s third state habeas petition was pending in Texas court. That petition was denied in November 2013. Ex\xc2\xa0parte Buck, 418 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 98 (Tex. Crim. App. 2013) (\xc2\xa0per curiam). Two months later, Buck returned to federal court, where he filed a motion to reopen his \xc2\xa72254 case under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b)(6). Rule 60(b) enumerates specific circumstances in which a party may be relieved of the effect of a judgment, such as mistake, newly discovered evidence, fraud, and the like. The Rule concludes with a catchall category\xe2\x80\x94subdivision (b)(6)\xe2\x80\x94providing that a court may lift a judgment for \xe2\x80\x9cany other reason that justifies relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Relief is available under subdivision (b)(6), however, only in \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d and the Court has explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]uch circumstances will rarely occur in the habeas context.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzalez, 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 535.

In his motion, Buck identified 11 factors that, in his view, justified reopening the judgment. These included his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s introduction of expert testimony linking Buck\xe2\x80\x99s race to violence, the central issue at sentencing; the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s questions about race and violence on cross-examination and reliance on Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony in summation; the State\xe2\x80\x99s confession of error in other cases in which Dr. Quijano testified, but its refusal to concede error in Buck\xe2\x80\x99s case; and the change in law effected by Martinez and Trevino, which, if they had been decided earlier, would have permitted federal review of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s defaulted claim. App. 283a\xe2\x80\x93285a.

The District Court denied relief on two grounds. First, the court concluded that Buck had failed to demonstrate extraordinary circumstances. To that end, the court observed that a change in decisional law is rarely extraordinary by itself. Buck v. Stephens, 2014 WL 11310152, *4 (SD Tex., Aug. 29, 2014). It further determined that the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpromise\xe2\x80\x9d not to oppose resentencing did not count for much, reasoning that \xe2\x80\x9cBuck\xe2\x80\x99s case is different in critical respects from the cases in which Texas confessed error\xe2\x80\x9d in that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer, not the prosecutor, had first elicited the objectionable testimony. Id., at *4\xe2\x80\x93*5. The court also dismissed the contention that the nature of Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony argued for reopening the case. Although \xe2\x80\x9cthe introduction of any mention of race was,\xe2\x80\x9d in the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xe2\x80\x9cill[ ]advised at best and repugnant at worst,\xe2\x80\x9d it was also \xe2\x80\x9cde minimis\xe2\x80\x9d: Dr. Quijano had discussed the connection between race and violence only twice. Id., at *5. The court accordingly concluded that Buck had failed to make out the predicate for Rule 60(b)(6) relief.

Second, the court determined that\xe2\x80\x94even if the circumstances were extraordinary\xe2\x80\x94Buck\xe2\x80\x99s claim would fail on the merits. The court noted that under Strickland, Buck was obliged to show that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance was both deficient and prejudicial. The court held that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer had indeed performed deficiently in calling Dr. Quijano to give testimony that \xe2\x80\x9clen[t] credence to any potential latent racial prejudice held by the jury.\xe2\x80\x9d 2014 WL 11310152, at *6. But, the court concluded, Buck had failed to demonstrate prejudice. It observed that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s crime had been \xe2\x80\x9chorrific.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And the court had already concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe introduction of any mention of race was .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. de minimis.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at *5. For those reasons, it held, Buck had failed to show a reasonable probability that he would not have been sentenced to death but for Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony about race and violence.

Buck sought to appeal the denial of his Rule 60(b)(6) motion. He accordingly filed an application for a COA with the Fifth Circuit. To obtain a COA, Buck was required to make \xe2\x80\x9ca substantial showing of the denial of a constitutional right.\xe2\x80\x9d[1]*28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72253(c)(2).

The Fifth Circuit denied a COA, concluding that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s case was \xe2\x80\x9cnot extraordinary at all in the habeas context.\xe2\x80\x9d Buck v. Stephens, 623 Fed. Appx. 668, 673 (2015). The panel agreed with the District Court that Martinez and Trevino were not significant factors in the analysis. It characterized most of the other factors Buck had identified as \xe2\x80\x9cvariations on the merits\xe2\x80\x9d of his claim, which was \xe2\x80\x9cat least unremarkable as far as [ineffective assistance] claims go.\xe2\x80\x9d 623 Fed. Appx., at 673. The panel likewise rejected Buck\xe2\x80\x99s argument that he was entitled to relief because the State had issued a press release indicating that his case would be treated like Saldano\xe2\x80\x99s, and then had confessed error in the other cases identified as similar in the statement, but not in Buck\xe2\x80\x99s. Id., at 674. Because Buck had \xe2\x80\x9cnot shown extraordinary circumstances that would permit relief under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b)(6),\xe2\x80\x9d the panel \xe2\x80\x9cden[ied] the application for a COA.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 669.

Buck\xe2\x80\x99s motion for rehearing en banc was denied over two dissenting votes. Buck v. Stephens, 630 Fed. Appx. 251 (CA5 2015) (\xc2\xa0per curiam). We granted certiorari. Buck v. Stephens, 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016).

II

A state prisoner whose petition for a writ of habeas corpus is denied by a federal district court does not enjoy an absolute right to appeal. Federal law requires that he first obtain a COA from a circuit justice or judge.28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72253(c)(1). A COA may issue \xe2\x80\x9conly if the applicant has made a substantial showing of the denial of a constitutional right.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72253(c)(2). Until the prisoner secures a COA, the Court of Appeals may not rule on the merits of his case. Miller-El v. Cockrell,537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 322 (2003).

The COA inquiry, we have emphasized, is not coextensive with a merits analysis. At the COA stage, the only question is whether the applicant has shown that \xe2\x80\x9cjurists of reason could disagree with the district court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of his constitutional claims or that jurists could conclude the issues presented are adequate to deserve encouragement to proceed further.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 327. This threshold question should be decided without \xe2\x80\x9cfull consideration of the factual or legal bases adduced in support of the claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 336. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen a court of appeals sidesteps [the COA] process by first deciding the merits of an appeal, and then justifying its denial of a COA based on its adjudication of the actual merits, it is in essence deciding an appeal without jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 336\xe2\x80\x93337.

The court below phrased its determination in proper terms\xe2\x80\x94that jurists of reason would not debate that Buck should be denied relief, 623 Fed. Appx., at 674\xe2\x80\x94but it reached that conclusion only after essentially deciding the case on the merits. As the court put it in the second sentence of its opinion: \xe2\x80\x9cBecause [Buck] has not shown extraordinary circumstances that would permit relief under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b)(6), we deny the application for a COA.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 669. The balance of the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion reflects the same approach. The change in law effected by Martinez and Trevino, the panel wrote, was \xe2\x80\x9cnot an extraordinary circumstance.\xe2\x80\x9d 623 Fed. Appx., at 674. Even if Texas initially indicated to Buck that he would be resentenced, its \xe2\x80\x9cdecision not to follow through\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cnot extraordinary.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Buck \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] not shown why\xe2\x80\x9d the State\xe2\x80\x99s alleged broken promise \xe2\x80\x9cwould justify relief from the judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

But the question for the Fifth Circuit was not whether Buck had \xe2\x80\x9cshown extraordinary circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cshown why [Texas\xe2\x80\x99s broken promise] would justify relief from the judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 669, 674. Those are ultimate merits determinations the panel should not have reached. We reiterate what we have said before: A \xe2\x80\x9ccourt of appeals should limit its examination [at the COA stage] to a threshold inquiry into the underlying merit of [the] claims,\xe2\x80\x9d and ask \xe2\x80\x9conly if the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was debatable.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller-El, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 327, 348.

The dissent does not accept this established rule, arguing that a reviewing court that deems a claim nondebatable \xe2\x80\x9cmust necessarily conclude that the claim is meritless.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 2 (opinion of Thomas, J.). Of course when a court of appeals properly applies the COA standard and determines that a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s claim is not even debatable, that necessarily means the prisoner has failed to show that his claim is meritorious. But the converse is not true. That a prisoner has failed to make the ultimate showing that his claim is meritorious does not logically mean he failed to make a preliminary showing that his claim was debatable. Thus, when a reviewing court (like the Fifth Circuit here) inverts the statutory order of operations and \xe2\x80\x9cfirst decid[es] the merits of an appeal, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. then justif[ies] its denial of a COA based on its adjudication of the actual merits,\xe2\x80\x9d it has placed too heavy a burden on the prisoner at the COA stage. Miller-El, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 336\xe2\x80\x93337. Miller-El flatly prohibits such a departure from the procedure prescribed by \xc2\xa72253. Ibid.

The State defends the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s approach by arguing that the court\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of an application for a COA is often quite thorough. The court \xe2\x80\x9coccasionally hears oral argument when considering whether to grant a COA in a capital case.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 50. Indeed, in one recent case, it \xe2\x80\x9creceived nearly 200 pages of initial briefing, permitted a reply brief, considered the parties\xe2\x80\x99 supplemental authorities, invited supplemental letter briefs from both sides, and heard oral argument before denying the request for a COA.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 50\xe2\x80\x9351.

But this hurts rather than helps the State\xe2\x80\x99s case. \xe2\x80\x9c[A] claim can be debatable even though every jurist of reason might agree, after the COA has been granted and the case has received full consideration, that petitioner will not prevail.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller-El, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 338. The statute sets forth a two-step process: an initial determination whether a claim is reasonably debatable, and then\xe2\x80\x94if it is\xe2\x80\x94an appeal in the normal course. We do not mean to specify what procedures may be appropriate in every case. But whatever procedures are employed at the COA stage should be consonant with the limited nature of the inquiry.

Given the approach of the court below, it is perhaps understandable that the parties have essentially briefed and argued the underlying merits at length. See, e.g., Brief for Petitioner 32 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]rial counsel rendered deficient performance under Strickland.\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 39 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is a reasonable probability that Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s race-as-dangerousness opinion swayed the judgment of jurors in favor of death.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted)); id., at 59 (Buck \xe2\x80\x9chas demonstrated his entitlement to relief under Rule 60(b)(6)\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Respondent 40 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe particular facts of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s case do not establish extraordinary circumstances justifying relief from the judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d (boldface type deleted)). With respect to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s review, \xc2\xa72253 does not limit the scope of our consideration of the underlying merits, and at this juncture we think it proper to meet the decision below and the arguments of the parties on their own terms.

III

Buck\xe2\x80\x99s request for a COA raised two separate questions for the Fifth Circuit, one substantive and one procedural: first, whether reasonable jurists could debate the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Buck was not denied his right to effective assistance of counsel under Strickland; and second, whether reasonable jurists could debate the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s procedural holding that Buck had not made the necessary showing to reopen his case under Rule 60(b)(6).

A

We begin with the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination (not specifically addressed by the Fifth Circuit) that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional claim failed on the merits. The Sixth Amendment right to counsel \xe2\x80\x9cis the right to the effective assistance of counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d Strickland, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 686 (quoting McMann v. Richardson,397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 759, n.\xc2\xa014 (1970)). A defendant who claims to have been denied ef-fective assistance must show both that counsel performed deficiently and that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s deficient performance caused him prejudice. 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 687.

1

Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s first prong sets a high bar. A defense lawyer navigating a criminal proceeding faces any number of choices about how best to make a client\xe2\x80\x99s case. The lawyer has discharged his constitutional responsibility so long as his decisions fall within the \xe2\x80\x9cwide range of professionally competent assistance.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 690. It is only when the lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s errors were \xe2\x80\x9cso serious that counsel was not functioning as the \xe2\x80\x98counsel\xe2\x80\x99 guaranteed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d that Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s first prong is satisfied. Id., at 687.

The District Court determined that, in this case, counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance fell outside the bounds of competent representation. We agree. Counsel knew that Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s report reflected the view that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s race disproportionately predisposed him to violent conduct; he also knew that the principal point of dispute during the trial\xe2\x80\x99s penalty phase was whether Buck was likely to act violently in the future. Counsel nevertheless (1) called Dr. Quijano to the stand; (2) specifically elicited testimony about the connection between Buck\xe2\x80\x99s race and the likelihood of future violence; and (3) put into evidence Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s expert report that stated, in reference to factors bearing on future dangerousness, \xe2\x80\x9cRace. Black: Increased probability.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 19a, 145a\xe2\x80\x93146a.

Given that the jury had to make a finding of future dangerousness before it could impose a death sentence, Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s report said, in effect, that the color of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s skin made him more deserving of execution. It would be patently unconstitutional for a state to argue that a defendant is liable to be a future danger because of his race. See Zant v. Stephens,462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 862,885 (1983) (identifying race among factors that are \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutionally impermissible or totally irrelevant to the sentencing process\xe2\x80\x9d). No competent defense attorney would introduce such evidence about his own client. See Buck v. Thaler, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 1022 (statement of Alito, J., joined by Scalia and Breyer, JJ., respecting denial of certiorari) (Buck\xe2\x80\x99s case \xe2\x80\x9cconcerns bizarre and objectionable testimony\xe2\x80\x9d).

2

To satisfy Strickland, a litigant must also demonstrate prejudice\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 694. Accordingly, the question before the District Court was whether Buck had demonstrated a reasonable probability that, without Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony on race, at least one juror would have harbored a reasonable doubt about whether Buck was likely to be violent in the future. The District Court concluded that Buck had not made such a showing. We disagree.

In arguing that the jury would have imposed a death sentence even if Dr. Quijano had not offered race-based testimony, the State primarily emphasizes the brutality of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s crime and his lack of remorse. A jury may conclude that a crime\xe2\x80\x99s vicious nature calls for a sentence of death. See Wong v. Belmontes,558 U.\xc2\xa0S. 15 (2009) (\xc2\xa0per curiam). In this case, however, several considerations convince us that it is reasonably probable\xe2\x80\x94notwithstanding the nature of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s crime and his behavior in its aftermath\xe2\x80\x94that the proceeding would have ended differently had counsel rendered competent representation.

Dr. Quijano testified on the key point at issue in Buck\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing. True, the jury was asked to decide two issues\xe2\x80\x94whether Buck was likely to be a future danger, and, if so, whether mitigating circumstances nevertheless justified a sentence of life imprisonment. But the focus of the proceeding was on the first question. Much of the penalty phase testimony was directed to future dangerousness, as were the summations for both sides. The jury, consistent with the focus of the parties, asked during deliberations to see the expert reports on dangerousness. See App. 187a\xe2\x80\x93196a, 198a\xe2\x80\x93203a, 209a.

Deciding the key issue of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s dangerousness involved an unusual inquiry. The jurors were not asked to determine a historical fact concerning Buck\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, but to render a predictive judgment inevitably entailing a degree of speculation. Buck, all agreed, had committed acts of terrible violence. Would he do so again?

Buck\xe2\x80\x99s prior violent acts had occurred outside of prison, and within the context of romantic relationships with women. If the jury did not impose a death sentence, Buck would be sentenced to life in prison, and no such romantic relationship would be likely to arise. A jury could conclude that those changes would minimize the prospect of future dangerousness.

But one thing would never change: the color of Buck\xe2\x80\x99s skin. Buck would always be black. And according to Dr. Quijano, that immutable characteristic carried with it an \xe2\x80\x9c[i]ncreased probability\xe2\x80\x9d of future violence. Id., at 19a. Here was hard statistical evidence\xe2\x80\x94from an expert\xe2\x80\x94to guide an otherwise speculative inquiry.

And it was potent evidence. Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony appealed to a powerful racial stereotype\xe2\x80\x94that of black men as \xe2\x80\x9cviolence prone.\xe2\x80\x9d Turner v. Murray,476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 28,35 (1986) (plurality opinion). In combination with the substance of the jury\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry, this created something of a perfect storm. Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s opinion coincided precisely with a particularly noxious strain of racial prejudice, which itself coincided precisely with the central question at sentencing. The effect of this unusual confluence of factors was to provide support for making a decision on life or death on the basis of race.

This effect was heightened due to the source of the testimony. Dr. Quijano took the stand as a medical expert bearing the court\xe2\x80\x99s imprimatur. The jury learned at the outset of his testimony that he held a doctorate in clinical psychology, had conducted evaluations in some 70 capital murder cases, and had been appointed by the trial judge (at public expense) to evaluate Buck. App. 138a\xe2\x80\x93141a. Reasonable jurors might well have valued his opinion concerning the central question before them. See Satterwhite v. Texas,486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 249,259 (1988) (testimony from \xe2\x80\x9ca medical doctor specializing in psychiatry\xe2\x80\x9d on the question of future dangerousness may have influenced the sentencing jury).

For these reasons, we cannot accept the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9cthe introduction of any mention of race\xe2\x80\x9d during the penalty phase was \xe2\x80\x9cde minimis.\xe2\x80\x9d 2014 WL 11310152, at *5. There were only \xe2\x80\x9ctwo references to race in Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94one during direct examination, the other on cross. Ibid. But when a jury hears expert testimony that expressly makes a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s race directly pertinent on the question of life or death, the impact of that evidence cannot be measured simply by how much air time it received at trial or how many pages it occupies in the record. Some toxins can be deadly in small doses.

The State acknowledges, as it must, that introducing \xe2\x80\x9crace or ethnicity as evidence of criminality\xe2\x80\x9d can in some cases prejudice a defendant. Brief for Respondent 31. But it insists that this is not such a case, because Buck\xe2\x80\x99s own counsel, not the prosecution, elicited the offending testimony. We are not convinced. In fact, the distinction could well cut the other way. A prosecutor is seeking a conviction. Jurors understand this and may reasonably be expected to evaluate the government\xe2\x80\x99s evidence and arguments in light of its motivations. When a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own lawyer puts in the offending evidence, it is in the nature of an admission against interest, more likely to be taken at face value.

The effect of Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony on Buck\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing cannot be dismissed as \xe2\x80\x9cde minimis.\xe2\x80\x9d Buck has demonstrated prejudice.

B

1

We now turn to the lower courts\xe2\x80\x99 procedural holding: that Buck failed to demonstrate that he was entitled to have the judgment against him reopened under Rule 60(b)(6). We have held that a litigant seeking a COA must demonstrate that a procedural ruling barring relief is itself debatable among jurists of reason; otherwise, the appeal would not \xe2\x80\x9cdeserve encouragement to proceed further.\xe2\x80\x9d Slack v. McDaniel,529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 473,484 (2000) (quoting Barefoot v. Estelle, 463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 880, n.\xc2\xa04 (1983)).

The Rule 60(b)(6) holding Buck challenges would be reviewed for abuse of discretion during a merits appeal, see 11 C. Wright, A. Miller, & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa72857 (3d ed. 2012), and the parties agree that the COA question is therefore whether a reasonable jurist could conclude that the District Court abused its discretion in declining to reopen the judgment. See Brief for Petitioner 54\xe2\x80\x9357; Brief for Respondent 34.

Buck brought his Rule 60(b) motion under the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s catchall category, subdivision (b)(6), which permits a court to reopen a judgment for \xe2\x80\x9cany other reason that justifies relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 60(b) vests wide discretion in courts, but we have held that relief under Rule 60(b)(6) is available only in \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzalez, 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 535. In determining whether extraordinary circumstances are present, a court may consider a wide range of factors. These may include, in an appropriate case, \xe2\x80\x9cthe risk of injustice to the parties\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe risk of undermining the public\xe2\x80\x99s confidence in the judicial process.\xe2\x80\x9d Liljeberg v. Health Services Acquisition Corp.,486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 847\xe2\x80\x93864 (1988).

In the circumstances of this case, the District Court abused its discretion in denying Buck\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b)(6) motion. The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Buck \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] failed to demonstrate that this case presents extraordinary circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d rested in large measure on itsdetermination that \xe2\x80\x9cthe introduction of any mention of race\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94though \xe2\x80\x9cill[\xc2\xa0]advised at best and repugnant at worst\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94played only a \xe2\x80\x9cde minimis\xe2\x80\x9d role in the proceeding. 2014 WL 11310152, at *5. The Fifth Circuit, for its part, failed even to mention the racial evidence in concludingthat Buck\xe2\x80\x99s claim was \xe2\x80\x9cat least unremarkable as far as [ineffective assistance] claims go.\xe2\x80\x9d 623 Fed. Appx., at 673. But our holding on prejudice makes clear that Buck may have been sentenced to death in part because of his race. As an initial matter, this is a disturbing departure from a basic premise of our criminal justice system: Our law punishes people for what they do, not who they are. Dispensing punishment on the basis of an immutable characteristic flatly contravenes this guiding principle. As petitioner correctly puts it, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t stretches credulity to characterize Mr. Buck\xe2\x80\x99s [ineffective assistance of counsel] claim as run-of-the-mill.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 57.

This departure from basic principle was exacerbated because it concerned race. \xe2\x80\x9cDiscrimination on the basis of race, odious in all aspects, is especially pernicious in the administration of justice.\xe2\x80\x9d Rose v. Mitchell,443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 545,555 (1979). Relying on race to impose a criminal sanction \xe2\x80\x9cpoisons public confidence\xe2\x80\x9d in the judicial process. Davis v. Ayala, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 28). It thus injures not just the defendant, but \xe2\x80\x9cthe law as an institution, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the community at large, and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the democratic ideal reflected in the processes of our courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Rose, 443 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 556 (internal quotation marks omitted). Such concerns are precisely among those we have identified as supporting relief under Rule 60(b)(6). See Liljeberg, 486 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 864.

The extraordinary nature of this case is confirmed by what the State itself did in response to Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony. When the case of Victor Hugo Saldano came before this Court, Texas confessed error and consented to resentencing. The State\xe2\x80\x99s response to Saldano\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari succinctly expressed the injustice Saldano had suffered: \xe2\x80\x9cthe infusion of race as a factor for the jury to weigh in making its determination violated his constitutional right to be sentenced without regard to the color of his skin.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 306a.

The Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s public statement, issued shortly after we vacated the judgment in Saldano\xe2\x80\x99s case, reflected this sentiment. It explained that the State had responded to Saldano\xe2\x80\x99s troubling petition by conducting a \xe2\x80\x9cthorough audit\xe2\x80\x9d of criminal cases, finding six similar to Saldano\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cin which testimony was offered by Dr. Quijano that race should be a factor for the jury to consider.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 213a. The statement affirmed that \xe2\x80\x9cit is inappropriate to allow race to be considered as a factor in our criminal justice system.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Consistent with this position\xe2\x80\x94and to its credit\xe2\x80\x94the State confessed error in the cases of five of the six defendants identified in the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s statement, waiving all available procedural defenses and consenting to resentencing.

These were remarkable steps. It is not every day that a State seeks to vacate the sentences of five defendants found guilty of capital murder. But then again, these were\xe2\x80\x94as the State itself put it at oral argument here\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary\xe2\x80\x9d cases. Tr. of Oral Arg. 41; see Buck v. Thaler, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 1030 (Sotomayor, J., joined by Kagan, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (\xe2\x80\x9cEspecially in light of the capital nature of this case and the express recognition by a Texas attorney general that the relevant testimony was inappropriately race charged, Buck has presented issues that \xe2\x80\x98deserve encouragement to proceed further.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Miller-El, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 327)).

To be sure, the State has repeatedly attempted to justify its decision to treat Buck differently from the other five defendants identified in the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s statement, including on asserted factual grounds that the State has been required to abjure. See Brief for Respondent 46, n.\xc2\xa010 (the State\xe2\x80\x99s initial opposition to Buck\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition \xe2\x80\x9cerroneously\xe2\x80\x9d argued that Buck was treated differently because defense counsel, not the State, called Dr. Quijano as a witness; that was also true of two of the other defendants). The State continues its efforts before this Court, arguing that Buck\xe2\x80\x99s was the only one of the six cases in which defense counsel, not the prosecution, first elicited Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s opinion on race. See also post, at 8 (opinion of Thomas, J.).

But this is beside the point. The State\xe2\x80\x99s various explanations for distinguishing Buck\xe2\x80\x99s case have nothing to do with the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s stated reasons for confessing error in Saldano and the cases acknowledged as similar. Regardless of which party first broached the subject, race was in all these cases put to the jury \xe2\x80\x9cas a factor .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to weigh in making its determination.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 306a. The statement that \xe2\x80\x9cit is inappropriate to allow race to be considered as a factor in our criminal justice system\xe2\x80\x9d is equally applicable whether the prosecution or ineffective defense counsel initially injected race into the proceeding. Id., at 213a. The terms of the State\xe2\x80\x99s announcement provide every reason for originally including Buck on the list of defendants situated similarly to Saldano, and no reason for later taking him off.

In opposition, the State reminds us of the importance of preserving the finality of judgments. Brief for Respondent 34. But the \xe2\x80\x9cwhole purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of Rule 60(b) \xe2\x80\x9cis to make an exception to finality.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzalez, 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 529. And in this case, the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in finality deserves little weight. When Texas recognized that the infusion of race into proceedings similar to Saldano\xe2\x80\x99s warranted confession of error, it effectively acknowledged that the people of Texas lack an interest in enforcing a capital sentence obtained on so flawed a basis. In concluding that the value of finality does not demand that we leave the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in place, we do no more than acknowledge what Texas itself recognized 17 years ago.

2

Our Rule 60(b)(6) analysis has thus far omitted one significant element. When Buck first sought federal ha-beas relief in 2004, Coleman barred the District Court from hearing his claim. Today, however, a claim of ineffective assistance of trial counsel defaulted in a Texas postconviction proceeding may be reviewed in federal court if state habeas counsel was constitutionally ineffective in failing to raise it, and the claim has \xe2\x80\x9csome merit.\xe2\x80\x9d Martinez, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 14; see Trevino, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 13). Buck cannot obtain relief unless he is entitled to the benefit of this rule\xe2\x80\x94that is, unless Martinez and Trevino, not Coleman, would govern his case were it reopened. If they would not, his claim would remain unreviewable, and Rule 60(b)(6) relief would be inappropriate. See 11 Wright & Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa72857 (showing \xe2\x80\x9ca good claim or defense\xe2\x80\x9d is a precondition of Rule 60(b)(6) relief\xc2\xa0).

Until merits briefing in this Court, both parties litigated this matter on the assumption that Martinez and Trevino would apply if Buck reopened his case. See Pet. for Cert. 27\xe2\x80\x9328; Brief in Opposition 11\xe2\x80\x9313; Amended Application for Certificate of Appealability and Brief in Support 26, Respondent-Appellee\xe2\x80\x99s Opposition to Pet. for En Banc Rehearing 9\xe2\x80\x9311, and Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s Opposition to Application for Certificate of Appealability 15\xe2\x80\x9317 in No. 14\xe2\x80\x9370030 (CA5); Amended Response to Motion for Relief from Judgment in No. 4:04\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9303965 (SD Tex.), pp. 11\xe2\x80\x9313. But the State\xe2\x80\x99s brief adopts a new position on this issue. The State now argues that those cases announced a \xe2\x80\x9cnew rule\xe2\x80\x9d that, under Teague v. Lane,489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288 (1989) (plurality opinion), does not apply retroactively to cases (like Buck\xe2\x80\x99s) on collateral review. Brief for Respondent 38\xe2\x80\x9340. Buck responds that Teague analysis applies only to new rules of criminal procedure that govern trial proceedings\xe2\x80\x94not new rules of habeas procedure that govern collateral proceedings\xe2\x80\x94and that the State has in any event waived its Teague argument. Reply Brief 20.

We agree that the argument has been waived. See Danforth v. Minnesota,552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 264,289 (2008) (\xe2\x80\x9cStates can waive a Teague defense .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by failing to raise it in a timely manner\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). It was not advanced in District Court, before the Fifth Circuit, or in the State\xe2\x80\x99s brief in opposition to Buck\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari. Although we may reach the issue in our discretion, we have observed before that a State\xe2\x80\x99s failure to raise a Teague argument at the petition stage is particularly \xe2\x80\x9csignificant\xe2\x80\x9d in deciding whether such an exercise of discretion is appropriate. Schiro v. Farley,510 U.\xc2\xa0S. 222\xe2\x80\x93229 (1994). When \xe2\x80\x9ca legal issue appears to warrant review, we grant certiorari in the expectation of being able to decide that issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 229. If we were to entertain the State\xe2\x80\x99s eleventh-hour Teague argument and find it persuasive, Buck\xe2\x80\x99s Strickland and Rule 60(b)(6) contentions\xe2\x80\x94the issues we thought worthy of review\xe2\x80\x94would be insulated from our consideration. We therefore decline to reach the Teague question and conclude that Martinez and Trevino apply to Buck\xe2\x80\x99s claim. We reach no broader determination concerning the application of these cases.

C

For the foregoing reasons, we conclude that Buck has demonstrated both ineffective assistance of counsel under Strickland and an entitlement to relief under Rule 60(b)(6). It follows that the Fifth Circuit erred in denying Buck the COA required to pursue these claims on appeal.

The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.

Having settled on a desired outcome, the Court bull-dozes procedural obstacles and misapplies settled law to justify it. But the majority\xe2\x80\x99s focus on providing relief to petitioner in this particular case has at least one upside: Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision has few ramifications, if any, beyond the highly unusual facts presented here. The majority leaves entirely undisturbed the black-letter principles of collateral review, ineffective assistance of counsel, and Rule 60(b)(6) law that govern day-to-day operations in federal courts.

\n

I

\n

In reversing the judgment below, the majority relies on three grounds: that the Fifth Circuit misapplied the standard for granting a certificate of appealability (COA); that the District Court erroneously rejected petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim; and that the District Court abused its discretion in rejecting petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b)(6) motion to reopen the court\xe2\x80\x99s earlier judgment denying habeas relief. On each ground, the majority simply dis-agrees with the courts below over the application of the governing standard. The majority does not announce any new standards or suggest that the District Court or the Fifth Circuit applied the wrong standards altogether. See, e.g., ante, at 13 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe court below phrased its determination in proper terms .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d). I agree with the majority that the courts below identified the correct standards for all three of these inquiries. Contrary to the majority\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion, however, I would hold that they correctly applied those standards, too.

\n

A

\n

At the outset, the Court wrongly criticizes the Fifth Circuit for its application of the COA standard. A COA is warranted only if the district court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling is \xe2\x80\x9cdebatable amongst jurists of reason.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller-El v. Cockrell,\n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 322,\n336 (2003)\n. To answer this question, a court must conduct a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral assessment\xe2\x80\x9d of the merits of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s claim. Ibid. The majority faults the Fifth Circuit for concluding outright that petitioner \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98has not shown extraordinary circumstances that would permit relief under [Rule] 60(b)(6).\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13 (quoting Buck v. Stephens, 623 Fed. Appx. 668, 669 (CA5 2015)). In the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view, the existence of extraordinary circumstances represents an \xe2\x80\x9cultimate merits determinatio[n] the panel should not have reached.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. Instead, according to the majority, the panel should have limited itself to the threshold question whether the merits were debatable.

\n

The majority\xe2\x80\x99s criticism of the Fifth Circuit is misplaced. A court may grant a COA even if it might ultimately conclude that the underlying claim is meritless, so long as the claim is debatable. Miller-El, supra, at 336. But to deny a COA, a court must necessarily conclude that the claim is meritless. A reviewing court cannot determine that a claim is indisputably meritless (that is, nondebat-able) without first deciding that it is meritless. See Slack v. McDaniel,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 473,\n484 (2000)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cWhere a plain procedural bar is present and the district court is correct to invoke it to dispose of the case,\xe2\x80\x9d the claim is not debatable (emphasis added)); Weeks v. Angelone,\n\n528 U.\xc2\xa0S. 225,\n231,\n234 (2000)\n (affirming denial of COA after determining that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim was meritless).

\n

In concluding that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claims were indisputably meritless as a prelude to denying the COA, the Fifth Circuit thus adopted the approach that courts must take in denying a COA. The majority might disagree with the conclusion that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claims are indisputably meritless, but its criticism of the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s approach is most certainly misguided. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s contrary approach would prevent a court of appeals from denying a COA in any case, an outcome that Congress and our precedents have plainly foreclosed. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72253(c)(2) (COA warranted only if petitioner makes \xe2\x80\x9ca substantial showing of the denial of a constitutional right\xe2\x80\x9d); Miller-El, supra, at 337 (The \xe2\x80\x9cissuance of a COA must not be pro\xc2\xa0forma or a matter of course\xe2\x80\x9d). The majority\xe2\x80\x99s comment that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]ntil the prisoner secures a COA, the Court of Appeals may not rule on the merits of his case,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12, should not be taken at face value.

\n

In any event, after chastising the Court of Appeals for making an end run around the COA standard in order to reach the merits of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 60(b) claim, the Court does precisely that. See ante, at 26 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e conclude that Buck has demonstrated .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an entitlement to relief under Rule 60(b)(6)\xe2\x80\x9d). Astonishingly, the Court also decides the merits of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment claim\xe2\x80\x94an issue that was not even \xe2\x80\x9caddressed by the Fifth Circuit.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16; see ante, at 26 (\xe2\x80\x9cBuck has demonstrated .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. ineffective assistance of counsel under Strickland [v. Washington,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668 (1984)\n]\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

After reaching out to adjudicate the merits, the Court relies on its merits disposition to justify reversing the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s denial of a COA. Ante, at 26 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt follows that the Fifth Circuit erred in denying Buck the COA required to pursue these claims on appeal\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). This unapologetic course reversal\xe2\x80\x94made without so much as a hint of the irony\xe2\x80\x94is striking. The majority also has things just backwards. It criticizes the Fifth Circuit for undertaking a merits inquiry to deny a COA (when such an inquiry is required) and then it conducts a merits inquiry to decide that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim is debat-able (when such an inquiry is inappropriate).

\n

B

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s application of the standard in Strickland v. Washington,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668 (1984)\n, is similarly misguided. In particular, the Court erroneously finds that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim satisfies Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s second prong, which requires a defendant to show that his counsel\xe2\x80\x99s mistake materially prejudiced his defense. Prejudice exists only when correcting the alleged error would have produced a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d likelihood of a different result. Harrington v. Richter,\n\n562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 86\n\xe2\x80\x93112 (2011). Here, the sentence of death hinged on the jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding that petitioner posed a threat of future dangerousness. Texas\xe2\x80\x99 standard for making such a finding is not difficult to satisfy: \xe2\x80\x9cThe facts of the offense alone may be sufficient to sustain the jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding of future dangerousness,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[a] jury may also infer a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s future dangerousness from evidence showing a lack of remorse.\xe2\x80\x9d Buntion v. State, 482 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 58, 66\xe2\x80\x9367 (Tex. Crim. App. 2016).

\n

The majority neglects even to mention the relevant legal standard in Texas, relying instead on rhetoric and speculation to craft a finding of prejudice. But the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s evidence of both the heinousness of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s crime and his complete lack of remorse was overwhelming. Accordingly, Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cde minimis\xe2\x80\x9d racial testimony, Buck v. Stephens, 2014 WL 11310152, *5 (SD Tex., Aug. 29, 2014), did not prejudice petitioner.

\n

First, the facts leave no doubt that this crime was premeditated and cruel. The Court recites defense testimony describing the killing spree here as a \xe2\x80\x9ccrime of passion,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 4 (internal quotation marks omitted), but the record belies that characterization. The rampage occurred at the home of Debra Gardner, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s ex-girlfriend. Prior to the shooting, petitioner called her house. His stepsister, Phyllis Taylor, answered, and petitioner asked to speak with Gardner. Gardner declined, and petitioner hung up. Petitioner then retrieved a shotgun and rifle, loaded both guns, and drove 28 miles to Gardner\xe2\x80\x99s house. Upon arrival, he broke down the door and opened fire without provocation. The shooting did not occur in the heat of the moment.

\n

In addition to describing this as a crime of passion, the majority also parrots defense testimony that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s violence was limited to \xe2\x80\x9cthe context of romantic relationships.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18. But this assertion is also quite wrong. Upon entering Gardner\xe2\x80\x99s house, petitioner first shot at an acquaintance, Harold Ebnezer. He next approached his stepsister, Taylor, who was seated on the couch. He said, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I\xe2\x80\x99m going to shoot your ass too.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. 82a. She begged him, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Duane, please don\xe2\x80\x99t shoot me. I\xe2\x80\x99m your sister. I don\xe2\x80\x99t deserve to be shot. Remember I do have children.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 83a. Petitioner ignored her pleas, placed the gun on her chest, and shot her. Petitioner does not claim that he was in a romantic relationship with either Ebnezer or Taylor.

\n

After shooting Taylor, petitioner cornered one of Gardner\xe2\x80\x99s friends, Kenneth Butler, and shot him, as well. He then exited the house and chased Gardner into the middle of the street. She turned to him and pleaded, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Please don\xe2\x80\x99t shoot me. Please don\xe2\x80\x99t shoot me. Why are you doing this in front of my kids?\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 104a. Her son, Devon, watched from the sidewalk. Her daughter, Shennel, begged petitioner to spare her mother and even attempted to restrain him. Petitioner pointed the gun at Gardner and said, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I\xe2\x80\x99m going to shoot you. I\xe2\x80\x99m going to shoot your A[ss].\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 117a. He then did so. The flight path of the bullet suggests that Gardner was on her knees when petitioner shot her.

\n

Second, the evidence of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s lack of remorse, largely ignored by the majority, is startling. After shooting Gardner, petitioner walked back to his car and placed the firearms in the trunk. He then returned to taunt Gardner where she lay mortally wounded and bleeding in the street. He said, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98It ain\xe2\x80\x99t funny now. You ain\xe2\x80\x99t laughing now.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 106a. Police arrived shortly thereafter and arrested him. In the patrol car, petitioner was \xe2\x80\x9claughing and joking and taunting.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 71a. He continued to smile and laugh during the drive to the police station. When one of the officers informed petitioner that he did not find the situation humorous, petitioner replied that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]he bitch got what she deserved.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 135a. He remained happy and upbeat for the remainder of the drive, even commenting that he was going to heaven because God had already forgiven him.

\n

C

\n

Finally, the majority incorrectly concludes that the District Court erred in denying petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion under Rule 60(b)(6), which permits district courts to reopen otherwise final judgments only in \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Ackermann v. United States,\n\n340 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193,\n199 (1950)\n. Although the majority pays lip service to the fact that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision on this point is subject to \xe2\x80\x9climited and deferential appellate review,\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzalez v. Crosby,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 524,\n535 (2005)\n; see ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9321, it proceeds to conduct a de\xc2\xa0novo review. Indeed, the majority references the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis only once in the entire section of its opinion addressing Rule 60(b)(6). But the question is not whether this Court thinks the circumstances are extraordinary; the question is whether reasonable jurists would debate that the District Court abused its discretion in reaching the equitable, highly factbound conclusion that they are not. Particularly in light of our admonition that such circumstances \xe2\x80\x9cwill rarely occur in the habeas context,\xe2\x80\x9d 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 535, I think it is not debatable that the District Court acted within its discretion in denying Rule 60(b)(6) relief here.

\n

In reversing the Fifth Circuit, the centerpiece of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis is its observation that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[d]iscrimination on the basis of race, odious in all aspects, is especially pernicious in the administration of justice.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 22 (quoting Rose v. Mitchell,\n\n443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 545,\n555 (1979)\n). I agree that racial classifications are categorically impermissible under the Equal Protection Clause\xe2\x80\x94but peti-tioner is not raising an equal protection claim. The many precedents the Court cites interpreting that Clause are therefore beside the point. Instead, petitioner is claiming that his case presents extraordinary circumstances under Rule 60(b)(6). The majority identifies no precedents regarding race in the Rule 60(b)(6) context. And certainly nothing in the text or history of Rule 60(b)(6)\xe2\x80\x94unlike the text and history of the Equal Protection Clause\xe2\x80\x94suggests that race-based claims demand unique solicitude in this context. At the very least, the District Court did not abuse its discretion in determining that they do not.[1]

\n

In a similar vein, the majority suggests that the use of race in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s capital proceeding injured the public\xe2\x80\x99s confidence in the integrity of our judicial system. Ante, at 22. This argument cannot be squared with the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that the challenged racial testimony was \xe2\x80\x9cde minimis.\xe2\x80\x9d 2014 WL 11310152, at *5. It also ignores the fact that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s own counsel elicited the testi-mony. The majority obscures this point by citing cases concerning alleged racial discrimination by an agent of the state. See ante, at 22 (citing, e.g., Davis v. Ayala, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015) (addressing the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s use of peremptory strikes and holding that any constitutional error was harmless)). There, the injury to public confidence derives from the fact that the government itself is discriminating against the defendant. The same cannot be said, however, when defense counsel introduces harmful testimony or makes a bad strategic choice.[2]

\n

In conjunction with its observations about race, the Court notes that the Texas attorney general, in response to similar testimony from Dr. Quijano in another case, issued a press release decrying the use of race in the justice system and subsequently waived all procedural obstacles to resentencing in several cases in which Dr. Quijano testified. But Texas had good reason for treating this case differently from the others. Of those cases, this is the only one where \xe2\x80\x9cit can be said that the responsibility for eliciting the offensive testimony lay squarely with the defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Buck v. Thaler,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1022,\n1025 (2011)\n (Alito, J., statement respecting denial of certiorari).

\n

Lastly, the Court belittles Texas\xe2\x80\x99 claimed interest in finality. In the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view, Texas effectively forfeited its finality interest when it waived its procedural defenses in purportedly similar cases. See ante, at 24. But Texas did not waive its procedural defenses in this case, and, in any event, Texas is not alone in possessing an interest in the finality of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. Society at large has the same interest. Finality advances values \xe2\x80\x9cessential to the operation of our criminal justice system.\xe2\x80\x9d Teague v. Lane,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288,\n309 (1989)\n (plurality opinion). It promotes the law\xe2\x80\x99s deterrent effect; it provides peace of mind to a wrongdoer\xe2\x80\x99s victims; it promotes public confidence in the justice system; it conserves limited public resources; and it ensures the clarity of legal rights and statuses.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s finality analysis also ignores the lengthy passage of time (nearly eight years) between the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s original rejection of habeas relief in this case and petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s filing of the instant Rule 60(b)(6) motion. See Gonzalez, 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 542, n.\xc2\xa04 (Stevens, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn cases where significant time has elapsed between a habeas judgment and the relevant change in procedural law, it would be within a district court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to leave such a judgment in repose\xe2\x80\x9d). Permitting a defendant to file a Rule 60(b) motion years after the fact functionally eviscerates the statute of limitations contained in the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996, thereby undermining its purpose of \xe2\x80\x9clend[ing] finality to state court judgments within a reasonable time.\xe2\x80\x9d Day v. McDonough,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 198\n\xe2\x80\x93206 (2006) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

II

\n

Despite its errors, today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion should have little effect on the broader law, for two reasons. For one thing, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning is highly factbound, and the facts presented here are unlikely to arise again. For another, although the majority misapplies settled principles, it does not purport to actually alter any of those principles.

\n

A

\n

This is an unusual case, and the majority\xe2\x80\x99s single-minded focus on according relief to this petitioner on these facts naturally limits the reach of its decision. In cases presenting different facts, today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will provide little guidance. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate conclusion relies on the convergence of three critical factors that will rarely, if ever, recur. See ante, at 19 (describing this case as involving an \xe2\x80\x9cunusual confluence of factors\xe2\x80\x9d that together create a \xe2\x80\x9cperfect storm\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

First, the Court places special weight on the fact that this is a capital case. See, e.g., ante, at 23 (noting \xe2\x80\x9cthe capital nature of this case\xe2\x80\x9d as a factor favoring Rule 60(b)(6) relief (internal quotation marks omitted)). Second, the Court notes that the testimony at issue was expressly racial and suggested that petitioner was more deserving of the death penalty because he is black. See, e.g., ante, at 21 (\xe2\x80\x9cBuck may have been sentenced to death in part because of his race.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [T]his is a disturbing departure from a basic premise of our criminal justice system\xe2\x80\x9d).[3] Third, the Court explains that the state attorney general took the \xe2\x80\x9cremarkable steps,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 23, of publicly declaring that Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony in this case was inappropriate and waiving all procedural defenses to resentencing in similar cases. See, e.g., ante, at 22 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe extraordinary nature of this case is confirmed by what the State itself did in response to Dr. Quijano\xe2\x80\x99s testimony\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

The effect of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is also limited for a second reason. Although the majority misapplies many exist-ing doctrines, it refrains from announcing any new principles of law. In particular, it leaves untouched\xe2\x80\x94andcourts should accordingly continue to apply asusual\xe2\x80\x94established principles governing collateral review, ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claims, and Rule 60(b)(6) motions.

\n

At the outset, the opinion leaves intact Miller-El\xe2\x80\x99s well-worn COA standard for habeas petitions: Courts of appeals should deny applications when the district court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling is not \xe2\x80\x9cdebatabl[y]\xe2\x80\x9d wrong. 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 336; see ante, at 13. \xe2\x80\x9cA prisoner seeking a COA must prove something more than the absence of frivolity or the existence of mere good faith on his or her part.\xe2\x80\x9d 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 338 (internal quotation marks omitted). Courts have substantial discretion in deciding how to structure this inquiry. See ante, at 15 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe do not mean to specify what procedures may be appropriate in every case\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The Court also reaffirms the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98highly deferential\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d character of the Strickland standard. Harrington, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 105 (quoting Strickland, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 689); see, e.g., ante, at 16 (first prong of Strickland \xe2\x80\x9csets a high bar\xe2\x80\x9d). Courts applying Strickland must respect \xe2\x80\x9cthe constitutionally protected independence of counsel and the wide latitude counsel must have in making tactical decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Cullen v. Pinholster,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 170,\n195 (2011)\n (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted). Counsel should be \xe2\x80\x9cstrongly presumed to have rendered adequate assistance andmade all significant decisions in the exercise of reasonable professional judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 189 (internal quotation marks omitted). And a defendant can show prejudice only if the likelihood of a different result would have been \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial, not just conceivable.\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington, supra, at 112.

\n

Perhaps most significantly, the test for reopening judgments under Rule 60(b)(6) remains the same. A \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98very strict interpretation of Rule 60(b) is essential if the finality of judgments is to be preserved.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzalez, 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 535 (quoting Liljeberg v. Health Services Acquisition Corp.,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 847,\n873 (1988)\n (Rehnquist, C. J., dissenting)). A district court may grant relief only if the movant can show \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9cwill rarely occur in the habeas context.\xe2\x80\x9d 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 535. (internal quotation marks omitted); see ante, at 10. A change in law alone is not enough. See 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 536\xe2\x80\x93537. And a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to deny relief is subject to only \xe2\x80\x9climited and deferential\xe2\x80\x9d appellate review. Id.,at 535.

\n

Although petitioner argues that the change in law effected by Martinez v. Ryan,\n\n566 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (2012)\n, and Tre-vino v. Thaler, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013), is central to the Rule 60(b)(6) inquiry, see Brief for Petitioner 56; Reply Brief 19, the Court does not even count those decisions in its tally of extraordinary circumstances. Instead, it treats a potentially viable Martinez claim as a \xe2\x80\x9cprecondition\xe2\x80\x9d to relief. Ante, at 25. This makes sense: Unless petitioner has the ability to invoke Martinez and Trevino, reopening the judgment would be futile. For those in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s position, the absence of a potentially valid Martinez claim is disqualifying, but the presence of one does nothing to demonstrate the existence of extraordinary circumstances.

\n

III

\n

Finally, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion does not require the lower courts to reflexively accord relief to petitioner on remand. In order to succeed under Martinez and Trevino, petitioner must establish that his state habeas counsel was constitutionally ineffective for failing to raise a Strickland claim as to his trial counsel. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision does not address that showing, and the court on remand should not treat it as a foregone conclusion.

\n

I respectfully dissent.
