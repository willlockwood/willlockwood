\nPER CURIAM
\nWILSON V. CORCORAN
\n562 U. S. ____ (2010)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

BILL K. WILSON, SUPERINTENDANT, INDIANA\nSTATE PRISON v. JOSEPH E. CORCORAN

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united\nstates court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 10\xe2\x80\x9391.\xe2\x80\x83Decided November 8, 2010

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Federal courts may not issue writs of habeas corpus to state prisoners whose confinement does not violate federal law. Because the Court of Appeals granted the writ to respondent without finding such a violation, we vacate its judgment and remand.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1997, respondent Joseph Corcoran shot and killed four men, including his brother and his sister\xe2\x80\x99s fianc\xc3\xa9. An Indiana jury found him guilty of four counts of murder, found the statutory aggravating circumstance of multiple murders, and unanimously recommended capital punishment. The trial judge agreed and sentenced respondent to death.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But on appeal, the Supreme Court of Indiana vacated the sentence out of concern that the trial judge might have violated Indiana law by relying partly on nonstatutory aggravating factors when imposing the death penalty. Corcoran v. State, 739 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 649, 657\xe2\x80\x93658 (2000). When addressing respondent at sentencing, the trial court had remarked:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[T]he knowing and intentional murders of four innocent people is an extremely heinous and aggravated crime\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. I don\xe2\x80\x99t think in the history of this county we\xe2\x80\x99ve had a mass murderer such as yourself. It makes you, Mr. Corcoran, a very dangerous, evil mass murderer. And I am convinced in my heart of hearts, \xe2\x80\xa6 if given the opportunity, you will murder again.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 657 (quoting transcript).

\n

\nAccording to the Indiana Supreme Court, the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s reference to the innocence of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s victims, the heinousness of his offense, and his future dangerousness was not necessarily improper; it is permissible to provide \xe2\x80\x9can appropriate context for consideration of the alleged aggravating and mitigating circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). But because the trial court might have meant that it weighed these factors as aggravating circumstances, the Indiana Supreme Court remanded for resentencing. See ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On remand, the trial court issued a revised sentencing order. It wrote:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0The trial Court, in balancing the proved aggravators and mitigators, emphasizes to the Supreme Court that it only relied upon those proven statutory aggravators. The trial Court\xe2\x80\x99s remarks at the sentencing hearing, and the language in the original sentencing order explain why such high weight was given to the statutory aggravator of multiple murder, and further support the trial Court\xe2\x80\x99s personal conclusion that the sentence is appropriate punishment for this offender and these crimes.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Corcoran v. State, 774 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 495, 498 (Ind. 2002) (quoting order).

\n

\nOn appeal, over respondent\xe2\x80\x99s objection, the Supreme Court accepted this explanation and affirmed the sentence. Id., at 498\xe2\x80\x93499, 502. It explained that it was \xe2\x80\x9cnow satisfied that the trial court has relied on only aggravators listed in Indiana Code \xc2\xa735\xe2\x80\x9350\xe2\x80\x932\xe2\x80\x939(b)\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. There is no lack of clarity in [the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s] statement and no plausible reason to believe it untrue.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 499.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent later applied to the United States District Court for the Northern District of Indiana for a writ of habeas corpus. His habeas petition asserted a number of grounds for relief, including a renewed claim that, notwithstanding its assurances to the contrary, the trial court improperly relied on nonstatutory aggravating factors when it resentenced him. Respondent also asserted that this reliance violated the Eighth and Fourteenth Amendments. Record, Doc. 13, p. 11. In its response to the petition, the State specifically disputed that contention. Id., Doc. 33, p. 16 (\xe2\x80\x9c[Respondent] fails to establish any constitutional deficiency in [the] Indiana Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s review of the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of Corcoran\xe2\x80\x99s sentence on remand, let alone does it show that the state supreme court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment is in any way inconsistent with applicable United States Supreme Court precedent\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court, however, had no need to resolve this dispute because it granted habeas relief on a wholly different ground: that an offer by the prosecutor to take the death penalty off the table in exchange for a waiver of a jury trial had violated the Sixth Amendment. Corcoran v. Buss, 483 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 709, 725\xe2\x80\x93726 (2007). It did not address the sentencing challenge because that was \xe2\x80\x9crendered moot\xe2\x80\x9d by the grant of habeas relief. Id., at 734.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State appealed, and the Seventh Circuit reversed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment ruling. Corcoran v. Buss, 551 F.\xc2\xa03d 703, 712, 714 (2008). Then, evidently overlooking respondent\xe2\x80\x99s remaining sentencing claims, the Seventh Circuit remanded the case to the District Court \xe2\x80\x9cwith instructions to deny the writ.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 714. To correct this oversight, we granted certiorari and vacated the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. Corcoran v. Levenhagen, 558 U. S. 1 (2009) (per curiam). We explained that the Court of Appeals \xe2\x80\x9cshould have permitted the District Court to consider Corcoran\xe2\x80\x99s unresolved challenges to his death sentence on remand, or should have itself explained why such consideration was unnecessary.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 2).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On remand\xe2\x80\x94and without any opportunity for briefing by the parties\xe2\x80\x94the Court of Appeals changed course and granted habeas relief. Corcoran v. Levenhagen, 593 F.\xc2\xa03d 547, 555 (2010). After determining that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing challenge had been waived by his failure to include it in his original cross-appeal, the Seventh Circuit concluded that the claim satisfied plain-error review. Id., at 551. The panel explained that, \xe2\x80\x9cunlike the Indiana Supreme Court,\xe2\x80\x9d it was unsatisfied with the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s representation that it relied only on aggravating factors authorized by Indiana law. Ibid. Because the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s revised sentencing order said that it used the nonstatutory factors of heinousness, victims\xe2\x80\x99 innocence, and future dangerousness to determine the weight given to the aggravator of multiple murders, the Seventh Circuit concluded that the Indiana Supreme Court had made an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unreasonable determination of the facts\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d when it accepted the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s representation that it did not rely on those factors as aggravating circumstances. Ibid. (quoting 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(2)). The panel therefore required the Indiana trial court to reconsider its sentencing determination in order to \xe2\x80\x9cprevent non-compliance with Indiana law.\xe2\x80\x9d 593 F. 3d, at 552\xe2\x80\x93553.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But it is only noncompliance with federal law that renders a State\xe2\x80\x99s criminal judgment susceptible to collateral attack in the federal courts. The habeas statute unambiguously provides that a federal court may issue the writ to a state prisoner \xe2\x80\x9conly on the ground that he is in custody in violation of the Constitution or laws or treaties of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(a). And we have repeatedly held that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98federal habeas corpus relief does not lie for errors of state law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Estelle v. McGuire, 502 U. S. 62, 67 (1991) (quoting Lewis v. Jeffers, 497 U. S. 764, 780 (1990)). \xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is not the province of a federal habeas court to reexamine state-court determinations on state-law questions.\xe2\x80\x9d 502 U. S., at 67\xe2\x80\x9368. But here, the panel\xe2\x80\x99s opinion contained no hint that it thought the violation of Indiana law it had unearthed also entailed the infringement of any federal right. Not only did the court frame respondent\xe2\x80\x99s claim as whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe Indiana trial court considered non-statutory aggravating circumstances \xe2\x80\xa6 in contravention of state law,\xe2\x80\x9d 593 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 551 (emphasis added), it also explicitly acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in [its] opinion prevents Indiana from adopting a rule permitting the use of non-statutory aggravators in the death sentence selection process. See Zant v. Stephens, 462 U. S. 862, 878 (1983) (permitting their use under federal law),\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 551\xe2\x80\x93552 (citations omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor did it suffice for the Court of Appeals to find an unreasonable determination of the facts under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(2). That provision allows habeas petitioners to avoid the bar to habeas relief imposed with respect to federal claims adjudicated on the merits in state court by showing that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was \xe2\x80\x9cbased on an unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented in the State court proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d It does not repeal the command of \xc2\xa72254(a) that habeas relief may be afforded to a state prisoner \xe2\x80\x9conly on the ground\xe2\x80\x9d that his custody violates federal law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In response to the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, the State filed a petition for rehearing and rehearing en banc. The State\xe2\x80\x99s petition argued that the Seventh Circuit had erred by granting relief in the absence of a federal violation. It also contended, on the authority of our opinion in Wainwright v. Goode, 464 U. S. 78 (1983) (per curiam), that the Court of Appeals erred by second-guessing the Indiana Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s factual determination that its own trial court complied with Indiana law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Seventh Circuit denied rehearing, but amended its opinion to include this language:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThis [remand for resentencing] will cure the state trial court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98unreasonable determination of the facts.\xe2\x80\x99 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) [sic]. (It will also prevent noncompliance with Indiana law. [Corcoran] contended that, under the circumstances of this case, noncompliance with state law also violates the federal Constitution and thus warrants him relief under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(2). [The State] has not advanced any contrary argument based on Wainwright v. Goode, 464 U. S. 78 (1983), or any similar decision.)\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 144a\xe2\x80\x93145a.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The amendment did not cure the defect. It is not enough to note that a habeas petitioner asserts the existence of a constitutional violation; unless the federal court agrees with that assertion, it may not grant relief. The Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion reflects no such agreement, nor does it even articulate what federal right was allegedly infringed. In fact, as to one possible federal claim, the court maintains that it would not violate federal law for Indiana to adopt a rule authorizing what the trial court did. 593 F. 3d, at 551\xe2\x80\x93552.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In lieu of finding or even describing a constitutional error, the amended opinion says only that the State had not \xe2\x80\x9cadvanced any contrary argument based on Wainwright v. Goode \xe2\x80\xa6 or any similar decision.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 145a. It is not clear what this language was meant to convey. It cannot have meant that the State forfeited the position that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s allegations do not state a constitutional violation, since (as we observed) the State explicitly disputed that point before the District Court\xe2\x80\x94the last forum in which the subject had been raised, leading the Court of Appeals to conclude that respondent had waived the claim entirely. 593 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 551. And there is no suggestion that the State has ever conceded the existence of a federal right to be sentenced in accordance with Indiana law. Under those circumstances, it was improper for the Court of Appeals to issue the writ without first concluding that a violation of federal law had been established.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The petition for a writ of certiorari and respondent\xe2\x80\x99s motion for leave to proceed in forma pauperis are granted. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion. We express no view about the merits of the habeas petition.

\n

It is so ordered.
