\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

MERRILL LYNCH, PIERCE, FENNER & SMITH, INC. v. DABIT

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x931371.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 18, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 21, 2006

\n

Respondent Dabit filed a private securities fraud class action in federal court, invoking diversity jurisdiction to advance his state-law claims that petitioner, his former employer, fraudulently manipulated stock prices, causing him and other brokers and their clients to keep their overvalued securities. The District Court dismissed his amended complaint, finding his claims pre-empted by title I of the Securities Litigation Uniform Standards Act of 1998 (SLUSA), which provides that no \xe2\x80\x9ccovered class action\xe2\x80\x9d based on state law and alleging \xe2\x80\x9ca misrepresentation or omission of a material fact in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cmay be maintained in any State or Federal court by any private party.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778bb(f)(1)(A). Vacating the judgment, the Second Circuit concluded that, to the extent the complaint alleged that brokers were fraudulently induced, not to sell or purchase, but to retain or delay selling, it fell outside SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive scope.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The background, text, and purpose of SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision demonstrate that SLUSA pre-empts state-law holder class-action claims of the kind Dabit alleges. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The magnitude of the federal interest in protecting the integrity and efficiency of the national securities market cannot be overstated. The Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 (1934 Act) anchor federal regulation of vital elements of this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s economy. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, which was promulgated pursuant to \xc2\xa710(b) of the 1934 Act, is an important part of that regulatory scheme, and, like \xc2\xa710(b), prohibits deception, misrepresentation, and fraud \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of a security. When, in Blue Chip Stamps v. Manor Drug Stores, 421 U. S. 723, this Court limited the Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 private right of action to plaintiffs who were themselves purchasers or sellers, it relied on the widespread recognition that suits by nonpurchasers and nonsellers present a special risk of vexatious litigation that could \xe2\x80\x9cfrustrate or delay normal business activity,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 740. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Similar policy considerations prompted Congress to adopt legislation (Reform Act) targeted at perceived abuses of class actions\xe2\x80\x94e.g., nuisance filings and vexatious discovery requests\xe2\x80\x94but this effort prompted members of the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 bar to avoid the federal forum altogether. To stem the shift of class actions from federal to state courts, Congress enacted SLUSA. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Both the class and the securities here are \xe2\x80\x9ccovered\xe2\x80\x9d within SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s meaning, and the complaint alleges misrepresentations and omissions of material facts. The only disputed issue is whether the alleged wrongdoing was \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of securities. Dabit\xe2\x80\x99s narrow reading would pre-empt only those actions in which Blue Chip Stamps\xe2\x80\x99 purchaser-seller requirement is met. Insofar as that argument assumes that the Blue Chip Stamps rule stems from Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x99s text, it must be rejected, for the Court relied on \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy considerations\xe2\x80\x9d in adopting that limitation, and it purported to define the scope of a private right of action under Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, not to define \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale.\xe2\x80\x9d When this Court has sought to give meaning to that phrase in the \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 context, it has broadly required that the alleged fraud \xe2\x80\x9ccoincide\xe2\x80\x9d with a securities transaction, an interpretation that comports with the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding views. Congress can hardly have been unaware of this broad construction when it imported the phrase into SLUSA. Where judicial interpretations have settled a statutory provision\xe2\x80\x99s meaning, repeating the same language in a new statute indicates the intent to incorporate the judicial interpretations as well. That presumption is particularly apt here, because Congress not only used \xc2\xa710(b)\xe2\x80\x99s and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x99s words, but used them in another provision appearing in the same statute as \xc2\xa710(b). The presumption that Congress envisioned a broad construction also follows from the particular concerns that culminated in SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, viz., preventing state private securities class-action suits from frustrating the Reform Act\xe2\x80\x99s objectives. A narrow construction also would give rise to wasteful, duplicative litigation in state and federal courts. The presumption that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress does not cavalierly pre-empt state-law causes of action,\xe2\x80\x9d Medtronic, Inc. v. Lohr, 518 U. S. 470, 485, has less force here because SLUSA does not pre-empt any cause of action. It simply denies the use of the class-action device to vindicate certain claims. Moreover, tailored exceptions to SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive command\xe2\x80\x94for, e.g., state agency enforcement proceedings\xe2\x80\x94demonstrate that Congress did not act cavalierly. Finally, federal, not state, law has long been the principal vehicle for asserting class-action securities fraud claims. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Dabit\xe2\x80\x99s holder class action is distinguishable from a typical Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 class action only in that it is brought by holders rather than sellers or purchasers. That distinction is irrelevant for SLUSA pre-emption purposes. The plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 identity does not determine whether the complaint alleges the requisite fraud, and the alleged misconduct here\xe2\x80\x94fraudulent manipulation of stock prices\xe2\x80\x94unquestionably qualifies as a fraud \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of securities as the phrase is defined in SEC v. Zandford, 535 U. S. 813, 820, and United States v. O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, 521 U. S. 642, 651. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9317.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nMERRILL LYNCH, PIERCE, FENNER & SMITH, INC. V.DABIT
\n547 U. S. ____ (2006)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 04-1371
\n
\n

MERRILL LYNCH, PIERCE, FENNER & SMITH, INC.,\nPETITIONER v. SHADI DABIT

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the second circuit

\n

[March 21, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Title I of the Securities Litigation Uniform Standards Act of 1998 (SLUSA) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o covered class action\xe2\x80\x9d based on state law and alleging \xe2\x80\x9ca misrepresentation or omission of a material fact in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cmay be maintained in any State or Federal court by any private party.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7101(b), 112 Stat. 3227 (codified at 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa078bb(f)(1)(A)). In this case the Second Circuit held that SLUSA only pre-empts state-law class-action claims brought by plaintiffs who have a private remedy under federal law. 395 F.\xc2\xa03d 25 (2005). A few months later, the Seventh Circuit ruled to the contrary, holding that the statute also pre-empts state-law class-action claims for which federal law provides no private remedy. Kircher v. Putnam Funds Trust, 403 F.\xc2\xa03d 478 (2005). The background, the text, and the purpose of SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision all support the broader interpretation adopted by the Seventh Circuit.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. (Merrill Lynch), is an investment banking firm that offers research and brokerage services to investors. Suspicious that the firm\xe2\x80\x99s loyalties to its investment banking clients had produced biased investment advice, the New York attorney general in 2002 instituted a formal investigation into Merrill Lynch\xe2\x80\x99s practices. The investigation sparked a number of private securities fraud actions, this one among them.[Footnote 1]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent, Shadi Dabit, is a former Merrill Lynch broker. He filed this class action in the United States District Court for the Western District of Oklahoma on behalf of himself and all other former or current brokers who, while employed by Merrill Lynch, purchased (for themselves and for their clients) certain stocks between December 1, 1999, and December 31, 2000. See App. 27a\xe2\x80\x9346a. Rather than rely on the federal securities laws, Dabit invoked the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s diversity jurisdiction and advanced his claims under Oklahoma state law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The gist of Dabit\xe2\x80\x99s complaint was that Merrill Lynch breached the fiduciary duty and covenant of good faith and fair dealing it owed its brokers by disseminating misleading research and thereby manipulating stock prices.[Footnote 2] Dabit\xe2\x80\x99s theory was that Merrill Lynch used its misinformed brokers to enhance the prices of its investment banking clients\xe2\x80\x99 stocks: The research analysts, under management\xe2\x80\x99s direction, allegedly issued overly optimistic appraisals of the stocks\xe2\x80\x99 value; the brokers allegedly relied on the analysts\xe2\x80\x99 reports in advising their investor clients and in deciding whether or not to sell their own holdings; and the clients and brokers both continued to hold their stocks long beyond the point when, had the truth been known, they would have sold. The complaint further alleged that when the truth was actually revealed (around the time the New York attorney general instituted his investigation), the stocks\xe2\x80\x99 prices plummeted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dabit asserted that Merrill Lynch\xe2\x80\x99s actions damaged the class members in two ways: The misrepresentations and manipulative tactics caused them to hold onto overvalued securities, and the brokers lost commission fees when their clients, now aware that they had made poor investments, took their business elsewhere.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In July 2002, Merrill Lynch moved to dismiss Dabit\xe2\x80\x99s complaint. It argued, first, that SLUSA pre-empted the action and, second, that the claims alleged were not cognizable under Oklahoma law. The District Court indicated that it was \xe2\x80\x9cnot impressed by\xe2\x80\x9d the state-law argument, but agreed that the federal statute pre-empted at least some of Dabit\xe2\x80\x99s claims. Id., at 49a\xe2\x80\x9350a. The court noted that the complaint alleged both \xe2\x80\x9cclaims and damages based on wrongfully-induced purchases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cclaims and damages based on wrongfully-induced holding.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. While the \xe2\x80\x9cholding\xe2\x80\x9d claims, the court suggested, might not be pre-empted, the \xe2\x80\x9cpurchasing\xe2\x80\x9d claims certainly were. The court dismissed the complaint with leave to amend to give Dabit the opportunity to untangle his \xe2\x80\x9chopeless m\xc3\xa9lange of purchase-related and holding-related assertions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (punctuation added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dabit promptly filed an amended complaint that omitted all direct references to purchases. What began as a class of brokers who \xe2\x80\x9cpurchased\xe2\x80\x9d the subject securities during the class period became a class of brokers who \xe2\x80\x9cowned and continued to own\xe2\x80\x9d those securities. See id., at 52a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Meanwhile, dozens of other suits, based on allegations similar to Dabit\xe2\x80\x99s, had been filed against Merrill Lynch around the country on both federal- and state-law theories of liability. The Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation transferred all of those cases, along with this one, to the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York for consolidated pretrial proceedings. Merrill Lynch then filed its second motion to dismiss Dabit\xe2\x80\x99s complaint. Senior Judge Milton Pollack granted the motion on the ground that the claims alleged fell \xe2\x80\x9csquarely within SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s ambit.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Merrill Lynch & Co., Inc., 2003 WL 1872820, *1 (Apr. 10, 2003).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit, however, vacated the judgment and remanded for further proceedings. 395 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 51. It concluded that the claims asserted by holders did not allege fraud \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of securities under SLUSA. Although the court agreed with Merrill Lynch that that phrase, as used in other federal securities laws, has been defined broadly by this Court, it held that Congress nonetheless intended a narrower meaning here\xe2\x80\x94one that incorporates the \xe2\x80\x9cstanding\xe2\x80\x9d limitation on private federal securities actions adopted in Blue Chip Stamps v. Manor Drug Stores, 421 U. S. 723 (1975). Under the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, fraud is only \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of securities, as used in SLUSA, if it is alleged by a purchaser or seller of securities. Thus, to the extent that the complaint in this action alleged that brokers were fraudulently induced, not to sell or purchase, but to retain or delay selling their securities, it fell outside SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive scope.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After determining that the class defined in Dabit\xe2\x80\x99s amended complaint did not necessarily exclude purchasers, the panel remanded with instructions that the pleading be dismissed without prejudice. The court\xe2\x80\x99s order would permit Dabit to file another amended complaint that defines the class to exclude \xe2\x80\x9cclaimants who purchased in connection with the fraud and who therefore could meet the standing requirement\xe2\x80\x9d for a federal damages action, and to include only those \xe2\x80\x9cwho came to hold [a Merrill Lynch] stock before any relevant misrepresentation.\xe2\x80\x9d 395 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 45\xe2\x80\x9346. Under the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, a class action so limited could be sustained under state law. For the reasons that follow, we disagree.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The magnitude of the federal interest in protecting the integrity and efficient operation of the market for nationally traded securities cannot be overstated. In response to the sudden and disastrous collapse in prices of listed stocks in 1929, and the Great Depression that followed, Congress enacted the Securities Act of 1933 (1933 Act), 48 Stat. 74, and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 (1934 Act), 48 Stat. 881. Since their enactment, these two statutes have anchored federal regulation of vital elements of our economy.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.10b\xe2\x80\x935 (2005), promulgated in 1942 pursuant to \xc2\xa710(b) of the 1934 Act, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j(b), is an important part of that regulatory scheme. The Rule, like \xc2\xa710(b) itself,[Footnote 4] broadly prohibits deception, misrepresentation, and fraud \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale of any security.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 5] The SEC has express statutory authority to enforce the Rule. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u (2000 ed. and Supp. III). Although no such authority is expressly granted to private individuals injured by securities fraud, in 1946 Judge Kirkpatrick of the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Pennsylvania, relying on \xe2\x80\x9cthe general purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of the Rule, recognized an implied right of action thereunder. Kardon v. National Gypsum Co., 69 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 512, 514. His holding was adopted by an \xe2\x80\x9coverwhelming consensus of the District Courts and Courts of Appeals,\xe2\x80\x9d Blue Chip Stamps, 421 U. S., at 730, and endorsed by this Court in Superintendent of Ins. of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. Bankers Life & Casualty Co., 404 U. S. 6 (1971).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A few years after Kardon was decided, the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit limited the reach of the private right of action under Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935. In Birnbaum v. Newport Steel Corp., 193 F.\xc2\xa02d 461 (1952), a panel composed of Chief Judge Swan and Judges Augustus and Learned Hand upheld the dismissal of a suit brought on behalf of a corporation and a class of its stockholders alleging that fraud \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d a director\xe2\x80\x99s sale of his controlling block of stock to third parties violated Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935. The court held that the Rule could only be invoked by a purchaser or seller of securities to remedy fraud associated with his or her own sale or purchase of securities, and did not protect those who neither purchased nor sold the securities in question but were instead injured by corporate insiders\xe2\x80\x99 sales to third parties. Id., at 464. While the Birnbaum court did not question the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cstanding\xe2\x80\x9d to enforce Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, later cases treated its holding as a standing requirement. See Eason v. General Motors Acceptance Corp., 490 F.\xc2\xa02d 654, 657 (CA7 1973).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By the time this Court first confronted the question, literally hundreds of lower court decisions had accepted \xe2\x80\x9cBirnbaum\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the plaintiff class for purposes of \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 private damages actions is limited to purchasers and sellers.\xe2\x80\x9d Blue Chip Stamps, 421 U. S., at 731\xe2\x80\x93732. Meanwhile, however, cases like Bankers Life & Casualty Co. had interpreted the coverage of the Rule more broadly to prohibit, for example, \xe2\x80\x9cdeceptive practices touching [a victim\xe2\x80\x99s] sale of securities as an investor.\xe2\x80\x9d 404 U. S., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313 (emphasis added); see Eason, 490 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 657 (collecting cases). The \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial oak which ha[d] grown from little more than a legislative acorn,\xe2\x80\x9d as then-Justice Rehnquist described the rules governing private Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 actions, Blue Chip Stamps, 421 U. S., at 737, had thus developed differently from the law defining what constituted a substantive violation of Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935. Ultimately, the Court had to decide whether to permit private parties to sue for any violation of Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 that caused them harm, or instead to limit the private remedy to plaintiffs who were themselves purchasers or sellers.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Relying principally on \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy considerations\xe2\x80\x9d which the Court viewed as appropriate in explicating a judicially crafted remedy, ibid., and following judicial precedent rather than \xe2\x80\x9cthe many commentators\xe2\x80\x9d who had criticized the Birnbaum rule as \xe2\x80\x9can arbitrary restriction which unreasonably prevents some deserving plaintiffs from recovering damages,\xe2\x80\x9d 421 U. S., at 738, the Court in Blue Chip Stamps chose to limit the private remedy. The main policy consideration tipping the scales in favor of precedent was the widespread recognition that \xe2\x80\x9clitigation under Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 presents a danger of vexatiousness different in degree and in kind from that which accompanies litigation in general.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 739. Even weak cases brought under the Rule may have substantial settlement value, the Court explained, because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he very pendency of the lawsuit may frustrate or delay normal business activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 740. Cabining the private cause of action by means of the purchaser-seller limitation would, in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, minimize these ill effects. The limitation of course had no application in Government enforcement actions brought pursuant to Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935. See id., at 751, n.\xc2\xa014.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Policy considerations similar to those that supported the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Blue Chip Stamps prompted Congress, in 1995, to adopt legislation targeted at perceived abuses of the class-action vehicle in litigation involving nationally traded securities. While acknowledging that private securities litigation was \xe2\x80\x9can indispensable tool with which defrauded investors can recover their losses,\xe2\x80\x9d the House Conference Report accompanying what would later be enacted as the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (Reform Act), 109 Stat. 737 (codified at 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa777z\xe2\x80\x931 and 78u\xe2\x80\x934), identified ways in which the class action device was being used to injure \xe2\x80\x9cthe entire U. S. economy.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x93369, p.\xc2\xa031 (1995). According to the Report, nuisance filings, targeting of deep-pocket defendants, vexatious discovery requests, and \xe2\x80\x9cmanipulation by class action lawyers of the clients whom they purportedly represent\xe2\x80\x9d had become rampant in recent years. Ibid. Proponents of the Reform Act argued that these abuses resulted in extortionate settlements, chilled any discussion of issuers\xe2\x80\x99 future prospects, and deterred qualified individuals from serving on boards of directors. Id., at 31\xe2\x80\x9332.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Title I of the Reform Act, captioned \xe2\x80\x9cReduction of Abusive Litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d represents Congress\xe2\x80\x99 effort to curb these perceived abuses. Its provisions limit recoverable damages and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, provide a \xe2\x80\x9csafe harbor\xe2\x80\x9d for forward-looking statements, impose new restrictions on the selection of (and compensation awarded to) lead plaintiffs, mandate imposition of sanctions for frivolous litigation, and authorize a stay of discovery pending resolution of any motion to dismiss. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934. Title I also imposes heightened pleading requirements in actions brought pursuant to \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935; it \xe2\x80\x9cinsists that securities fraud complaints \xe2\x80\x98specify\xe2\x80\x99 each misleading statement; that they set forth the facts \xe2\x80\x98on which [a] belief\xe2\x80\x99 that a statement is misleading was \xe2\x80\x98formed\xe2\x80\x99; and that they \xe2\x80\x98state with particularity facts giving rise to a strong inference that the defendant acted with the required state of mind.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Dura Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Broudo, 544 U. S. 336, 345 (2005) (quoting 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(1), (2)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The effort to deter or at least quickly dispose of those suits whose nuisance value outweighs their merits placed special burdens on plaintiffs seeking to bring federal securities fraud class actions. But the effort also had an unintended consequence: It prompted at least some members of the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 bar to avoid the federal forum altogether. Rather than face the obstacles set in their path by the Reform Act, plaintiffs and their representatives began bringing class actions under state law, often in state court. The evidence presented to Congress during a 1997 hearing to evaluate the effects of the Reform Act suggested that this phenomenon was a novel one; state-court litigation of class actions involving nationally traded securities had previously been rare. See H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 105\xe2\x80\x93640, p.\xc2\xa010 (1998); S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 105\xe2\x80\x93182, pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x934 (1998). To stem this \xe2\x80\x9cshif[t] from Federal to State courts\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cprevent certain State private securities class action lawsuits alleging fraud from being used to frustrate the objectives of\xe2\x80\x9d the Reform Act, SLUSA \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72(2), (5), 112 Stat. 3227, Congress enacted SLUSA.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The core provision of SLUSA reads as follows:[Footnote 6]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cClass Action Limitations.\xe2\x80\x94No covered class action based upon the statutory or common law of any State or subdivision thereof may be maintained in any State or Federal court by any private party alleging\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(A) a misrepresentation or omission of a material fact in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security; or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(B) that the defendant used or employed any manipulative or deceptive device or contrivance in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3230 (codified as amended at 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778bb(f)(1)).[Footnote 7]

\n

\nA \xe2\x80\x9ccovered class action\xe2\x80\x9d is a lawsuit in which damages are sought on behalf of more than 50 people.[Footnote 8] A \xe2\x80\x9ccovered security\xe2\x80\x9d is one traded nationally and listed on a regulated national exchange.[Footnote 9] Respondent does not dispute that both the class and the securities at issue in this case are \xe2\x80\x9ccovered\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the statute, or that the complaint alleges misrepresentations and omissions of material facts. The only disputed issue is whether the alleged wrongdoing was \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of securities.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent urges that the operative language must be read narrowly to encompass (and therefore pre-empt) only those actions in which the purchaser-seller requirement of Blue Chip Stamps is met. Such, too, was the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view. But insofar as the argument assumes that the rule adopted in Blue Chip Stamps stems from the text of Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x94specifically, the \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d language, it must be rejected. Unlike the Birnbaum court, which relied on Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x99s text in crafting its purchaser-seller limitation, this Court in Blue Chip Stamps relied chiefly, and candidly, on \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy considerations\xe2\x80\x9d in adopting that limitation. 421 U. S., at 737. The Blue Chip Stamps Court purported to define the scope of a private right of action under Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x94not to define the words \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 749 (\xe2\x80\x9cNo language in either [\xc2\xa710(b) or Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935] speaks at all to the contours of a private cause of action for their violation\xe2\x80\x9d). Any ambiguity on that score had long been resolved by the time Congress enacted SLUSA. See United States v. O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, 521 U. S. 642, 656, 664 (1997); Holmes v. Securities Investor Protection Corporation, 503 U. S. 258, 285 (1992) (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment); id., at 289\xe2\x80\x93290 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment); United States v. Naftalin, 441 U. S. 768, 774, n.\xc2\xa06 (1979); see also 395 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 39 (acknowledging that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he limitation on standing to bring [a] private suit for damages for fraud in connection with the purchase or sale of securities is unquestionably a distinct concept from the general statutory and regulatory prohibition on fraud in connection with the purchase or sale of securities\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, when this Court has sought to give meaning to the phrase in the context of \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, it has espoused a broad interpretation. A narrow construction would not, as a matter of first impression, have been unreasonable; one might have concluded that an alleged fraud is \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d a purchase or sale of securities only when the plaintiff himself was defrauded into purchasing or selling particular securities. After all, that was the interpretation adopted by the panel in the Birnbaum case. See 193 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 464. But this Court, in early cases like Superintendent of Ins. of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. Bankers Life & Casualty Co., 404 U. S. 6 (1971), and most recently in SEC v. Zandford, 535 U. S. 813, 820, 822 (2002), has rejected that view. Under our precedents, it is enough that the fraud alleged \xe2\x80\x9ccoincide\xe2\x80\x9d with a securities transaction\xe2\x80\x94whether by the plaintiff or by someone else. See O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, 521 U. S., at 651. The requisite showing, in other words, is \xe2\x80\x9cdeception \xe2\x80\x98in connection with the purchase or sale of any security,\xe2\x80\x99 not deception of an identifiable purchaser or seller.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 658. Notably, this broader interpretation of the statutory language comports with the longstanding views of the SEC. See Zandford, 535 U. S., at 819\xe2\x80\x93820.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress can hardly have been unaware of the broad construction adopted by both this Court and the SEC when it imported the key phrase\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94into SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s core provision. And when \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial interpretations have settled the meaning of an existing statutory provision, repetition of the same language in a new statute indicates, as a general matter, the intent to incorporate its . . . judicial interpretations as well.\xe2\x80\x9d Bragdon v. Abbott, 524 U. S. 624, 645 (1998); see Cannon v. University of Chicago, 441 U. S. 677, 696\xe2\x80\x93699 (1979). Application of that presumption is particularly apt here; not only did Congress use the same words as are used in \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, but it used them in a provision that appears in the same statute as \xc2\xa710(b). Generally, \xe2\x80\x9cidentical words used in different parts of the same statute are . . . presumed to have the same meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d IBP, Inc. v. Alvarez, 546 U. S. __, __ (2005) (slip op., at 11).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The presumption that Congress envisioned a broad construction follows not only from ordinary principles of statutory construction but also from the particular concerns that culminated in SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment. A narrow reading of the statute would undercut the effectiveness of the 1995 Reform Act and thus run contrary to SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s stated purpose, viz., \xe2\x80\x9cto prevent certain State private securities class action lawsuits alleging fraud from being used to frustrate the objectives\xe2\x80\x9d of the 1995 Act. SLUSA \xc2\xa72(5), 112 Stat. 3227. As the Blue Chip Stamps Court observed, class actions brought by holders pose a special risk of vexatious litigation. 421 U. S., at 739. It would be odd, to say the least, if SLUSA exempted that particularly troublesome subset of class actions from its pre-emptive sweep. See Kircher, 403 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 484.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s preferred construction also would give rise to wasteful, duplicative litigation. Facts supporting an action by purchasers under Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 (which must proceed in federal court if at all) typically support an action by holders as well, at least in those States that recognize holder claims. The prospect is raised, then, of parallel class actions proceeding in state and federal court, with different standards governing claims asserted on identical facts. That prospect, which exists to some extent in this very case,[Footnote 11] squarely conflicts with the congressional preference for \xe2\x80\x9cnational standards for securities class action lawsuits involving nationally traded securities.\xe2\x80\x9d SLUSA \xc2\xa72(5), 112 Stat. 3227.[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In concluding that SLUSA pre-empts state-law holder class-action claims of the kind alleged in Dabit\xe2\x80\x99s complaint, we do not lose sight of the general \xe2\x80\x9cpresum[ption] that Congress does not cavalierly pre-empt state-law causes of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Medtronic, Inc. v. Lohr, 518 U. S. 470, 485 (1996). But that presumption carries less force here than in other contexts because SLUSA does not actually pre-empt any state cause of action. It simply denies plaintiffs the right to use the class action device to vindicate certain claims. The Act does not deny any individual plaintiff, or indeed any group of fewer than 50 plaintiffs, the right to enforce any state-law cause of action that may exist.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, the tailored exceptions to SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive command demonstrate that Congress did not by any means act \xe2\x80\x9ccavalierly\xe2\x80\x9d here. The statute carefully exempts from its operation certain class actions based on the law of the State in which the issuer of the covered security is incorporated, actions brought by a state agency or state pension plan, actions under contracts between issuers and indenture trustees, and derivative actions brought by shareholders on behalf of a corporation. 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778bb(f)(3)(A)\xe2\x80\x93(C), (f)(5)(C). The statute also expressly preserves state jurisdiction over state agency enforcement proceedings. \xc2\xa778bb(f)(4). The existence of these carve-outs both evinces congressional sensitivity to state prerogatives in this field and makes it inappropriate for courts to create additional, implied exceptions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, federal law, not state law, has long been the principal vehicle for asserting class-action securities fraud claims. See, e.g., H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep. No. 105\xe2\x80\x93803, p.\xc2\xa014 (1998) (\xe2\x80\x9cPrior to the passage of the Reform Act, there was essentially no significant securities class action litigation brought in State court\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 13] More importantly, while state-law holder claims were theoretically available both before and after the decision in Blue Chip Stamps, the actual assertion of such claims by way of class action was virtually unheard of before SLUSA was enacted; respondent and his amici have identified only one pre-SLUSA case involving a state-law class action asserting holder claims.[Footnote 14] This is hardly a situation, then, in which a federal statute has eliminated a historically entrenched state-law remedy. Cf. Bates v. Dow Agrosciences LLC, 544 U. S. 431, 449 (2005) (observing that a \xe2\x80\x9clong history\xe2\x80\x9d of state-law tort remedy \xe2\x80\x9cadd[ed] force\xe2\x80\x9d to the presumption against pre-emption).

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The holder class action that respondent tried to plead, and that the Second Circuit envisioned, is distinguishable from a typical Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 class action in only one respect: It is brought by holders instead of purchasers or sellers. For purposes of SLUSA pre-emption, that distinction is irrelevant; the identity of the plaintiffs does not determine whether the complaint alleges fraud \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of securities. The misconduct of which respondent complains here\xe2\x80\x94fraudulent manipulation of stock prices\xe2\x80\x94unquestionably qualifies as fraud \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of securities as the phrase is defined in Zandford, 535 U. S., at 820, 822, and O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, 521 U. S., at 651.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
