MOORE v. TEXAS

\n

certiorari to the court of criminal appeals of texas

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93797.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 29, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 28, 2017

\n

Petitioner Moore was convicted of capital murder and sentenced to death for fatally shooting a store clerk during a botched robbery that occurred when Moore was 20 years old. A state habeas court subsequently determined that, under Atkins v. Virginia,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304\n, and Hall v. Florida, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, Moore qualified as intellectually disabled and that his death sentence therefore violated the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s proscription of \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments.\xe2\x80\x9d The court consulted current medical diagnostic standards\xe2\x80\x94the 11th edition of the American Association on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities clinical manual (AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311) and the 5th edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders published by the American Psychiatric Association. The habeas court followed the generally accepted intellectual-disability definition, which identifies three core elements: (1) intellectual-functioning deficits, (2) adaptive deficits, and (3) the onset of these deficits while still a minor. Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ scores, the court determined, established subaverage intellectual functioning. The court credited six scores, the average of which (70.66) indicated mild intellectual disability. And relying on testimony from mental-health professionals, the court found significant adaptive deficits in all three skill sets (conceptual, social, and practical). Based on its findings, the habeas court recommended to the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals (CCA) that Moore be granted relief. The CCA declined to adopt the judgment recommended by the habeas court. The CCA held instead that the habeas court erred by not following the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s 2004 decision in Ex\xc2\xa0parte Briseno, 135 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 1, which adopted the definition of, and standards for assessing, intellectual disability contained in the 1992 (ninth) edition of the American Association on Mental Retardation manual (AAMR\xe2\x80\x939), predecessor to the current AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311 manual. Briseno also incorporated the AAMR\xe2\x80\x939\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that adaptive deficits must be \xe2\x80\x9crelated\xe2\x80\x9d to intellectual-functioning deficits, and it recited, without citation to any medical or judicial authority, seven evidentiary factors relevant to the intellectual-disability inquiry. Based on only two of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ scores (of 74 and 78), the CCA concluded that Moore had not shown significantly subaverage intellectual functioning. And even if he had, the CCA continued, his adaptive strengths undercut any adaptive weaknesses. The habeas court also failed, the CCA determined, to inquire into relatedness. Among alternative causes for Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive deficits, the CCA suggested, were an abuse-filled childhood, undiagnosed learning disorders, multiple elementary-school transfers, racially motivated harassment and violence at school, and a history of academic failure, drug abuse, and absenteeism. Briseno\xe2\x80\x99s seven evidentiary factors, the CCA further determined, weighed against finding that Moore had satisfied the relatedness requirement.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0By rejecting the habeas court\xe2\x80\x99s application of medical guidance and by following the Briseno standard, including the nonclinical Briseno factors, the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s decision does not comport with the\n \nEighth Amendment and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The\n \nEighth Amendment, which \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reaffirms the duty of the government to respect the dignity of all persons,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, prohibits the execution of any intellectually disabled individual, Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 321. While Atkins and Hall left to the States \xe2\x80\x9cthe task of developing appropriate ways to enforce\xe2\x80\x9d the restriction on executing the intellectually disabled, Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (internal quotation marks omitted), States\xe2\x80\x99 discretion is not \xe2\x80\x9cunfettered,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___, and must be \xe2\x80\x9cinformed by the medical community\xe2\x80\x99s diagnostic framework,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___. Relying on the most recent (and still current) versions of the leading diagnostic manuals, the Court concluded in Hall that Florida had \xe2\x80\x9cdisregard[ed] established medical practice,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___, and had parted ways with practices and trends in other States, id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___. Hall indicated that being informed by the medical community does not demand adherence to everything stated in the latest medical guide. But neither does precedent license disregard of current medical standards. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The CCA\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ scores established that he is not intellectually disabled is irreconcilable with Hall, which instructs that, where an IQ score is close to, but above, 70, courts must account for the test\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstandard error of measurement.\xe2\x80\x9d See 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___. Because the lower range of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adjusted IQ score of 74 falls at or below 70, the CCA had to move on to consider Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive functioning. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The CCA\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive functioning also deviated from prevailing clinical standards and from the older clinical standards the CCA deemed applicable. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0The CCA overemphasized Moore\xe2\x80\x99s perceived adaptive strengths\xe2\x80\x94living on the streets, mowing lawns, and playing pool for money\xe2\x80\x94when the medical community focuses the adaptive-functioning inquiry on adaptive deficits. The CCA also stressed Moore\xe2\x80\x99s improved behavior in prison, but clinicians caution against reliance on adaptive strengths developed in controlled settings. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0The CCA further concluded that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s record of academic failure, along with a history of childhood abuse and suffering, detracted from a determination that his intellectual and adaptive deficits were related. The medical community, however, counts traumatic experiences as risk factors for intellectual disability. The CCA also departed from clinical practice by requiring Moore to show that his adaptive deficits were not related to \xe2\x80\x9ca personality disorder.\xe2\x80\x9d Mental-health professionals recognize that intellectually disabled people may have other co-existing mental or physical impairments, including, e.g., attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder, depressive and bipolar disorders, and autism. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(3)\xc2\xa0The CCA\xe2\x80\x99s attachment to the seven Briseno evidentiary factors further impeded its assessment of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive functioning. By design and in operation, the lay perceptions advanced by Briseno \xe2\x80\x9ccreat[e] an unacceptable risk that persons with intellectual disability will be executed.\xe2\x80\x9d Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___. The medical profession has endeavored to counter lay stereotypes, and the Briseno factors are an outlier, in comparison both to other States\xe2\x80\x99 handling of intellectual-disability pleas and to Texas\xe2\x80\x99 own practices in contexts other than the death penalty. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0States have some flexibility, but not \xe2\x80\x9cunfettered discretion,\xe2\x80\x9d in enforcing Atkins\xe2\x80\x99 holding, Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, and the medical community\xe2\x80\x99s current standards, reflecting improved understanding over time, constrain States\xe2\x80\x99 leeway in this area. Here, the habeas court applied current medical standards in reaching its conclusion, but the CCA adhered to the standard it laid out in Briseno, including the nonclinical Briseno factors. The CCA therefore failed adequately to inform itself of the \xe2\x80\x9cmedical community\xe2\x80\x99s diagnostic framework,\xe2\x80\x9d Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___. Because Briseno pervasively infected the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, the decision of that court cannot stand. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

470 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 481, vacated and remanded.

BOBBY JAMES MOORE, PETITIONER v. TEXAS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the court of criminal appeals of texas

\n

[March 28, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Bobby James Moore fatally shot a store clerk during a botched robbery. He was convicted of capital murder and sentenced to death. Moore challenged his death sentence on the ground that he was intellectually disabled and therefore exempt from execution. A state habeas court made detailed factfindings and determined that, under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions in Atkins v. Virginia,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304 (2002)\n, and Hall v. Florida, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), Moore qualified as intellectually disabled. For that reason, the court concluded, Moore\xe2\x80\x99s death sentence violated the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s proscription of \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments.\xe2\x80\x9d The habeas court therefore recommended that Moore be granted relief.

\n

The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals (CCA)[1] declined to adopt the judgment recommended by the state habeas court.[2] In the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s view, the habeas court erroneously employed intellectual-disability guides currently used in the medical community rather than the 1992 guides adopted by the CCA in Ex\xc2\xa0parte Briseno, 135 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 1 (2004). See Ex\xc2\xa0parte Moore, 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 481, 486\xe2\x80\x93487 (2015). The appeals court further determined that the evidentiary factors announced in Briseno \xe2\x80\x9cweigh[ed] heavily\xe2\x80\x9d against upsetting Moore\xe2\x80\x99s death sentence. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 526.

\n

We vacate the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. As we instructed in Hall, adjudications of intellectual disability should be \xe2\x80\x9cinformed by the views of medical experts.\xe2\x80\x9d 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 19); see id., at ___ (slip op., at 7). That instruction cannot sensibly be read to give courts leave to diminish the force of the medical community\xe2\x80\x99s consensus. Moreover, the several factors Briseno set out as indicators of intellectual disability are an invention of the CCA untied to any acknowledged source. Not aligned with the medical community\xe2\x80\x99s information, and drawing no strength from our precedent, the Briseno factors \xe2\x80\x9ccreat[e] an unacceptable risk that persons with intellectual dis-ability will be executed,\xe2\x80\x9d 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 1). Accordingly, they may not be used, as the CCA used them, to restrict qualification of an individual as intellectually disabled.

\n

I

\n

In April 1980, then-20-year-old Bobby James Moore and two others were engaged in robbing a grocery store. Ex\xc2\xa0parte Moore, 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 481, 490\xe2\x80\x93491 (Tex. Crim. App. 2015); App. 58. During the episode, Moore fatally shot a store clerk. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 490. Some two months later, Moore was convicted and sentenced to death. See id., at 492. A federal habeas court later vacated that sentence based on ineffective assistance of trial counsel, see Moore v. Collins, 1995 U.\xc2\xa0S. Dist. LEXIS 22859, *35 (SD Tex., Sept. 29, 1995), and the Fifth Circuit affirmed, see Moore v. Johnson, 194 F.\xc2\xa03d 586, 622 (1999). Moore was resentenced to death in 2001, and the CCA affirmed on direct appeal. See Moore v. State, 2004 WL 231323, *1 (Jan. 14, 2004), cert. denied,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 931 (2004)\n.

\n

Moore subsequently sought state habeas relief. In 2014, the state habeas court conducted a two-day hearing on whether Moore was intellectually disabled. See Ex\xc2\xa0parte Moore, No. 314483\xe2\x80\x93C (185th Jud. Dist., Harris Cty., Tex., Feb. 6, 2015), App. to Pet. for Cert. 129a. The court received affidavits and heard testimony from Moore\xe2\x80\x99s family members, former counsel, and a number of court-appointed mental-health experts. The evidence revealed that Moore had significant mental and social difficulties beginning at an early age. At 13, Moore lacked basic understanding of the days of the week, the months of the year, and the seasons; he could scarcely tell time or comprehend the standards of measure or the basic principle that subtraction is the reverse of addition. Id., at 187a. At school, because of his limited ability to read and write, Moore could not keep up with lessons. Id., at 146a, 182a\xe2\x80\x93183a. Often, he was separated from the rest of the class and told to draw pictures. Ibid. Moore\xe2\x80\x99s father, teachers, and peers called him \xe2\x80\x9cstupid\xe2\x80\x9d for his slow reading and speech. Id., at 146a, 183a. After failing every subject in the ninth grade, Moore dropped out of high school. Id., at 188a. Cast out of his home, he survived on the streets, eating from trash cans, even after two bouts of food poisoning. Id., at 192a\xe2\x80\x93193a.

\n

In evaluating Moore\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of intellectual disability, the state habeas court consulted current medical diagnostic standards, relying on the 11th edition of the American Association on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities (AAIDD) clinical manual, see AAIDD, Intellectual Disability: Definition, Classification, and Systems of Supports (2010) (hereinafter AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311), and on the 5th edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders published by the American Psychiatric Association (APA), see APA, Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (2013) (hereinafter DSM\xe2\x80\x935). App. to Pet. for Cert. 150a\xe2\x80\x93151a, 202a. The court followed the generally accepted, uncontroversial intellectual-disability diagnos-tic definition, which identifies three core elements: (1) intellectual-functioning deficits (indicated by an IQ score \xe2\x80\x9capproximately two standard deviations below the mean\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., a score of roughly 70\xe2\x80\x94adjusted for \xe2\x80\x9cthe standard error of measurement,\xe2\x80\x9d AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311, at 27); (2) adaptive deficits (\xe2\x80\x9cthe inability to learn basic skills and adjust behavior to changing circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d Hall v. Flor-ida, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 8)); and (3) the onset of these deficits while still a minor. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 150a (citing AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311, at 1). See also Hall, 572 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 8).[3]

\n

Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ scores, the habeas court determined, established subaverage intellectual functioning. The court credited six of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ scores, the average of which (70.66) indicated mild intellectual disability. App. to Pet. for Cert. 167a\xe2\x80\x93170a.[4] And relying on testimony from several mental-health experts, the habeas court found significant adaptive deficits. In determining the significance of adaptive deficits, clinicians look to whether an individual\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive performance falls two or more standard deviations below the mean in any of the three adaptive skill sets (conceptual, social, and practical). See AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311, at 43. Moore\xe2\x80\x99s performance fell roughly two standard deviations below the mean in all three skill categories. App. to Pet. for Cert. 200a\xe2\x80\x93201a. Based on this evidence, the state habeas court recommended that the CCA reduce Moore\xe2\x80\x99s sentence to life in prison or grant him a new trial on intellectual disability. See id., at 203a.

\n

The CCA rejected the habeas court\xe2\x80\x99s recommendations and denied Moore habeas relief. See 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 481. At the outset of its opinion, the CCA reaffirmed Ex\xc2\xa0parte Briseno, 135 S. W. 3d 1 (Tex. Crim. App. 2004), as paramount precedent on intellectual disability in Texas capital cases. See 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 486\xe2\x80\x93487. Briseno adopted the definition of, and standards for assessing, intellectual disability contained in the 1992 (ninth) edition of the American Association on Mental Retardation (AAMR) manual, predecessor to the current AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311 manual. See 135 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 7 (citing AAMR, Mental Retardation: Definition, Classification, and Systems of Supports (9th ed. 1992) (hereinafter AAMR\xe2\x80\x939)).

\n

Briseno incorporated the AAMR\xe2\x80\x939\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that adaptive deficits be \xe2\x80\x9crelated\xe2\x80\x9d to intellectual-functioning deficits. 135 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 7 (quoting AAMR\xe2\x80\x939, at 25).[5] To determine whether a defendant has satisfied the relatedness requirement, the CCA instructed in this case, Texas courts should attend to the \xe2\x80\x9cseven evidentiary factors\xe2\x80\x9d first set out in Briseno. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 489.[6] No citation to any authority, medical or judicial, accompanied the Briseno court\xe2\x80\x99s recitation of the seven factors. See 135 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 8\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

The habeas judge erred, the CCA held, by \xe2\x80\x9cus[ing] the most current position, as espoused by AAIDD, regarding the diagnosis of intellectual disability rather than the test .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in Briseno.\xe2\x80\x9d 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 486. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Atkins v. Virginia,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304 (2002)\n, the CCA emphasized, \xe2\x80\x9cleft it to the States to develop appropriate ways to enforce the constitutional restriction\xe2\x80\x9d on the execution of the intellectually disabled. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 486. Thus, even though \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t may be true that the AAIDD\xe2\x80\x99s and APA\xe2\x80\x99s positions regarding the diagnosis of intellectual disability have changed since Atkins and Briseno,\xe2\x80\x9d the CCA retained Briseno\xe2\x80\x99s instructions, both because of \xe2\x80\x9cthe subjectivity surrounding the medical diagnosis of intellectual disability\xe2\x80\x9d and because the Texas Legislature had not displaced Briseno with any other guideposts. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 486\xe2\x80\x93487. The Briseno inquiries, the court said, \xe2\x80\x9cremai[n] adequately \xe2\x80\x98informed by the medical community\xe2\x80\x99s diagnostic framework.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 487 (quoting Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320)).

\n

Employing Briseno, the CCA first determined that Moore had failed to prove significantly subaverage intellectual functioning. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 514\xe2\x80\x93519. Rejecting as unreliable five of the seven IQ tests the habeas court had considered, the CCA limited its appraisal to Moore\xe2\x80\x99s scores of 78 in 1973 and 74 in 1989. Id., at 518\xe2\x80\x93519. The court then discounted the lower end of the standard-error range associated with those scores. Id., at 519; see infra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311 (describing standard error of measurement). Regarding the score of 74, the court observed that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s history of academic failure, and the fact that he took the test while \xe2\x80\x9cexhibit[ing] withdrawn and depressive behavior\xe2\x80\x9d on death row, might have hindered his performance. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 519. Based on the two scores, but not on the lower portion of their ranges, the court concluded that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s scores ranked \xe2\x80\x9cabove the intellectually disabled range\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., above 70). Ibid.; see id., at 513.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cEven if [Moore] had proven that he suffers from significantly sub-average general intellectual functioning,\xe2\x80\x9d the court continued, he failed to prove \xe2\x80\x9csignificant and related limitations in adaptive functioning.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 520. True, the court acknowledged, Moore\xe2\x80\x99s and the State\xe2\x80\x99s experts agreed that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive-functioning test scores fell more than two standard deviations below the mean. Id., at 521; see supra, at 4. But the State\xe2\x80\x99s expert ultimately discounted those test results because Moore had \xe2\x80\x9cno exposure\xe2\x80\x9d to certain tasks the testing included, \xe2\x80\x9csuch as writing a check and using a microwave oven.\xe2\x80\x9d 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 521\xe2\x80\x93522. Instead, the expert emphasized Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive strengths in school, at trial, and in prison. Id., at 522\xe2\x80\x93524.

\n

The CCA credited the state expert\xe2\x80\x99s appraisal. Id., at 524. The habeas court, the CCA concluded, had erred by concentrating on Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive weaknesses. Id., at 489. Moore had demonstrated adaptive strengths, the CCA spelled out, by living on the streets, playing pool and mowing lawns for money, committing the crime in a sophisticated way and then fleeing, testifying and representing himself at trial, and developing skills in prison. Id., at 522\xe2\x80\x93523. Those strengths, the court reasoned, undercut the significance of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive limitations. Id., at 524\xe2\x80\x93525.

\n

The habeas court had further erred, the CCA determined, by failing to consider whether any of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive deficits were related to causes other than his intellectual-functioning deficits. Id., at 488, 526. Among alterna-tive causes for Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive deficits, the CCA suggested, were an abuse-filled childhood, undiagnosed learning disorders, multiple elementary-school transfers, racially motivated harassment and violence at school, and a his-tory of academic failure, drug abuse, and absenteeism. Ibid. Moore\xe2\x80\x99s significant improvement in prison, in the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s view, confirmed that his academic and social difficulties were not related to intellectual-functioning deficits. Ibid. The court then examined each of the seven Briseno evidentiary factors, see supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, and n.\xc2\xa06, concluding that those factors \xe2\x80\x9cweigh[ed] heavily\xe2\x80\x9d against finding that Moore had satisfied the relatedness requirement. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 526\xe2\x80\x93527.

\n

Judge Alcala dissented. Atkins and Hall, she would have held, require courts to consult current medical standards to determine intellectual disability. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 530. She criticized the majority for relying on manuals superseded in the medical community, id., at 530\xe2\x80\x93534, 536\xe2\x80\x93539, and for disregarding the habeas court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility determinations, id., at 535\xe2\x80\x93536, 538\xe2\x80\x93539. Judge Alcala questioned the legitimacy of the seven Briseno factors, recounting wide criticism of the factors and explaining how they deviate from the current medical consensus. See 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 529\xe2\x80\x93530, and n.\xc2\xa05. Most emphatically, she urged, the CCA \xe2\x80\x9cmust consult the medical community\xe2\x80\x99s current views and standards in determining whether a defendant is intellectually disabled\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9creliance on .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. standard[s] no longer employed by the medical community,\xe2\x80\x9d she objected, \xe2\x80\x9cis constitutionally unaccept-able.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 533.

\n

We granted certiorari to determine whether the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s adherence to superseded medical standards and its reliance on Briseno comply with the\n \nEighth Amendment and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents. 578 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016).

\n

II

\n

The\n \nEighth Amendment prohibits \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9creaffirms the duty of the government to respect the dignity of all persons,\xe2\x80\x9d Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 5) (quoting Roper v. Simmons,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551,\n560 (2005)\n). \xe2\x80\x9cTo enforce the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s protection of human dignity,\xe2\x80\x9d we \xe2\x80\x9cloo[k] to the evolving standards of decency that mark the progress of a maturing society,\xe2\x80\x9d recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he\n \nEighth Amendment is not fastened to the obsolete.\xe2\x80\x9d Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 5) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

In Atkins v. Virginia, we held that the Constitution \xe2\x80\x9crestrict[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the State\xe2\x80\x99s power to take the life of\xe2\x80\x9d any intellectually disabled individual. 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 321. See also Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 6); Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 563\xe2\x80\x93564. Executing intellectually disabled individuals, we concluded in Atkins, serves no penological purpose, see 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 318\xe2\x80\x93320; runs up against a national consensus against the practice, see id., at 313\xe2\x80\x93317; and creates a \xe2\x80\x9crisk that the death penalty will be imposed in spite of factors which may call for a less severe penalty,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 320 (internal quotation marks omitted); see id., at 320\xe2\x80\x93321.

\n

In Hall v. Florida, we held that a State cannot refuse to entertain other evidence of intellectual disability when a defendant has an IQ score above 70. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 21\xe2\x80\x9322). Although Atkins and Hall left to the States \xe2\x80\x9cthe task of developing appropriate ways to enforce\xe2\x80\x9d the restriction on executing the intellectually disabled, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 17) (quoting Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 317), States\xe2\x80\x99 discretion, we cautioned, is not \xe2\x80\x9cunfettered,\xe2\x80\x9d 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 17). Even if \xe2\x80\x9cthe views of medical experts\xe2\x80\x9d do not \xe2\x80\x9cdictate\xe2\x80\x9d a court\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual-disability determination, id., at ___ (slip op., at 19), we clarified, the determination must be \xe2\x80\x9cinformed by the medical community\xe2\x80\x99s diagnostic framework,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320). We relied on the most recent (and still current) versions of the leading diagnostic manuals\xe2\x80\x94the DSM\xe2\x80\x935 and AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311. Id., at ___, ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 3, 8, 10\xe2\x80\x9311, 20\xe2\x80\x9321). Florida, we concluded, had violated the\n \nEighth Amendment by \xe2\x80\x9cdisregard[ing] established medical practice.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 10). We further noted that Florida had parted ways with practices and trends in other States. Id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 12\xe2\x80\x9316). Hall indicated that being informed by the medical community does not demand adherence to everything stated in the latest medical guide. But neither does our precedent license disregard of current medical standards.

\n

III

\n

The CCA\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ scores established that he is not intellectually disabled is irreconcilable with Hall. Hall instructs that, where an IQ score is close to, but above, 70, courts must account for the test\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstandard error of measurement.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, 21\xe2\x80\x9322). See also Brumfield v. Cain, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 10) (relying on Hall to find unreasonable a state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that a score of 75 precluded an intellectual-disability finding). As we explained in Hall, the standard error of measurement is \xe2\x80\x9ca statistical fact, a reflection of the inherent imprecision of the test itself.\xe2\x80\x9d 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10). \xe2\x80\x9cFor purposes of most IQ tests,\xe2\x80\x9d this imprecision in the testing instrument \xe2\x80\x9cmeans that an individual\xe2\x80\x99s score is best understood as a range of scores on either side of the recorded score .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. within which one may say an individual\xe2\x80\x99s true IQ score lies.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 11). A test\xe2\x80\x99s standard error of measurement \xe2\x80\x9creflects the reality that an individual\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual functioning cannot be reduced to a single numerical score.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. See also id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9312); DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 37; AAIDD, User\xe2\x80\x99s Guide: Intellectual Disability: Definition, Classification, and Systems of Supports 22\xe2\x80\x9323 (11th ed. 2012) (hereinafter AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311 User\xe2\x80\x99s Guide).

\n

Moore\xe2\x80\x99s score of 74, adjusted for the standard error of measurement, yields a range of 69 to 79, see 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 519, as the State\xe2\x80\x99s retained expert acknowledged, see Brief for Petitioner 39, n.\xc2\xa018; App. 185, 189\xe2\x80\x93190. Because the lower end of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s score range falls at or below 70, the CCA had to move on to consider Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive functioning. See Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 21\xe2\x80\x9322); 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 536 (Alcala, J., dissenting) (even if the majority correctly limited the scores it would consider, \xe2\x80\x9ccurrent medical standards .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would still require [the CCA] to examine whether [Moore] has adaptive deficits\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Both Texas and the dissent maintain that the CCA properly considered factors unique to Moore in disregarding the lower end of the standard-error range. Post, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315; Brief for Respondent 41\xe2\x80\x9342; see supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937; 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 519. But the presence of other sources of imprecision in administering the test to a particular individual, see post, at 14\xe2\x80\x9316, and n.\xc2\xa03, cannot narrow the test-specific standard-error range.[7]

\n

In requiring the CCA to move on to consider Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive functioning in light of his IQ evidence, we do not suggest that \xe2\x80\x9cthe\n \nEighth Amendment turns on the slightest numerical difference in IQ score,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. Hall invalidated Florida\xe2\x80\x99s strict IQ cutoff because the cutoff took \xe2\x80\x9can IQ score as final and conclusive evidence of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual capacity, when experts in the field would consider other evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10). Here, by contrast, we do not end the intellectual-disability inquiry, one way or the other, based on Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ score. Rather, in line with Hall, we require that courts continue the inquiry and consider other evidence of intellectual disability where an individual\xe2\x80\x99s IQ score, adjusted for the test\xe2\x80\x99s standard error, falls within the clinically established range for intellectual-functioning deficits.

\n

IV

\n

The CCA\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive functioning also deviated from prevailing clinical standards and from the older clinical standards the court claimed to apply.

\n

A

\n

In concluding that Moore did not suffer significant adaptive deficits, the CCA overemphasized Moore\xe2\x80\x99s perceived adaptive strengths. The CCA recited the strengths it perceived, among them, Moore lived on the streets, mowed lawns, and played pool for money. See 470 S. W. 3d, at 522\xe2\x80\x93523, 526\xe2\x80\x93527. Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive strengths, in the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s view, constituted evidence adequate to overcome the considerable objective evidence of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive deficits, see supra, at 4; App. to Pet. for Cert. 180a\xe2\x80\x93202a. See 470 S. W. 3d, at 522\xe2\x80\x93524, 526\xe2\x80\x93527. But the medical community focuses the adaptive-functioning inquiry on adaptive deficits. E.g., AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311, at 47 (\xe2\x80\x9csignificant limitations in conceptual, social, or practical adaptive skills [are] not outweighed by the potential strengths in some adaptive skills\xe2\x80\x9d); DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 33, 38 (inquiry should focus on \xe2\x80\x9c[d]eficits in adaptive functioning\xe2\x80\x9d; deficits in only one of the three adaptive-skills domains suffice to show adaptive deficits); see Brumfield, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 15) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]ntellectually disabled persons may have \xe2\x80\x98strengths in social or physical capabilities, strengths in some adaptive skill areas, or strengths in one aspect of an adaptive skill in which they otherwise show an overall limitation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting AAMR, Mental Retardation: Definition, Classification, and Systems of Supports 8 (10th ed. 2002)).[8]

\n

In addition, the CCA stressed Moore\xe2\x80\x99s improved behavior in prison. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 522\xe2\x80\x93524, 526\xe2\x80\x93527. Clinicians, however, caution against reliance on adaptive strengths developed \xe2\x80\x9cin a controlled setting,\xe2\x80\x9d as a prison surely is. DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 38 (\xe2\x80\x9cAdaptive functioning may be difficult to assess in a controlled setting (e.g., prisons, detention centers); if possible, corroborative information reflecting functioning outside those settings should be obtained.\xe2\x80\x9d); see AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311 User\xe2\x80\x99s Guide 20 (counseling against reliance on \xe2\x80\x9cbehavior in jail or prison\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

The CCA furthermore concluded that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s record of academic failure, along with the childhood abuse and suffering he endured, detracted from a determination that his intellectual and adaptive deficits were related. See 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 488, 526; supra, at 5, 7\xe2\x80\x938. Those traumatic experiences, however, count in the medical community as \xe2\x80\x9crisk factors\xe2\x80\x9d for intellectual disability. AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311, at 59\xe2\x80\x9360 (emphasis added). Clinicians rely on such factors as cause to explore the prospect of intellectual disability further, not to counter the case for a disability determination. See id., at 60 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]t least one or more of the risk factors [described in the manual] will be found in every case of\xe2\x80\x9d intellectual disability.).

\n

The CCA also departed from clinical practice by requiring Moore to show that his adaptive deficits were not related to \xe2\x80\x9ca personality disorder.\xe2\x80\x9d 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 488; see id., at 526 (Moore\xe2\x80\x99s problems in kindergarten were \xe2\x80\x9cmore likely cause[d]\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cemotional problems\xe2\x80\x9d than by intellectual disability). As mental-health professionals recognize, how-ever, many intellectually disabled people also have other mental or physical impairments, for example, attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder, depressive and bipolar dis-orders, and autism. DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 40 (\xe2\x80\x9c[c]o-occurring men-tal, neurodevelopmental, medical, and physical conditions are frequent in intellectual disability, with rates of some conditions (e.g., mental disorders, cerebral palsy, and epilepsy) three to four times higher than in the general population\xe2\x80\x9d); see AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311, at 58\xe2\x80\x9363. Coexisting conditions frequently encountered in intellectually disabled individ-uals have been described in clinical literature as \xe2\x80\x9c[c]omorbidit[ies].\xe2\x80\x9d DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 40. See also Brief for AAIDD et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 20, and n.\xc2\xa025. The existence of a personality disorder or mental-health issue, in short, is \xe2\x80\x9cnot evidence that a person does not also have intellectual disability.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for American Psychological Association, APA, et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 19.

\n

C

\n

The CCA\xe2\x80\x99s attachment to the seven Briseno evidentiary factors further impeded its assessment of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive functioning.

\n

1

\n

By design and in operation, the Briseno factors \xe2\x80\x9ccreat[e] an unacceptable risk that persons with intellectual dis-ability will be executed,\xe2\x80\x9d Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 1). After observing that persons with \xe2\x80\x9cmild\xe2\x80\x9d intellectual disability might be treated differently under clinical standards than under Texas\xe2\x80\x99 capital system, the CCA defined its objective as identifying the \xe2\x80\x9cconsensus of Texas citizens\xe2\x80\x9d on who \xe2\x80\x9cshould be exempted from the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Briseno, 135 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 6 (emphasis added). Mild levels of intellectual disability, although they may fall outside Texas citizens\xe2\x80\x99 consensus, nevertheless remain intellectual disabilities, see Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318); Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 308, and n.\xc2\xa03; AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311, at 153, and States may not execute anyone in \xe2\x80\x9cthe entire category of [intellectually disabled] offenders,\xe2\x80\x9d Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 563\xe2\x80\x93564 (emphasis added); see supra, at 9.

\n

Skeptical of what it viewed as \xe2\x80\x9cexceedingly subjective\xe2\x80\x9d medical and clinical standards, the CCA in Briseno advanced lay perceptions of intellectual disability. 135 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 8; see supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, and n.\xc2\xa06. Briseno asks, for example, \xe2\x80\x9cDid those who knew the person best during the developmental stage\xe2\x80\x94his family, friends, teachers, employers, authorities\xe2\x80\x94think he was mentally retarded at that time, and, if so, act in accordance with that determination?\xe2\x80\x9d 135 S. W. 3d, at 8. Addressing that question here, the CCA referred to Moore\xe2\x80\x99s education in \xe2\x80\x9cnormal classrooms during his school career,\xe2\x80\x9d his father\xe2\x80\x99s reactions to his academic challenges, and his sister\xe2\x80\x99s perceptions of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual abilities. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 526\xe2\x80\x93527. But the medical profession has endeavored to counter lay stereotypes of the intellectually disabled. See AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311 User\xe2\x80\x99s Guide 25\xe2\x80\x9327; Brief for AAIDD et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Cu-riae 9\xe2\x80\x9314, and nn.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9315. Those stereotypes, much more than medical and clinical appraisals, should spark skepticism.[9]

\n

2

\n

The Briseno factors are an outlier, in comparison both to other States\xe2\x80\x99 handling of intellectual-disability pleas and to Texas\xe2\x80\x99 own practices in other contexts. See Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 12) (consensus in the States provides \xe2\x80\x9cobjective indicia of society\xe2\x80\x99s standards in the context of the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). No state legislature has approved the use of the Briseno factors or anything similar. In the 12 years since Texas adopted the factors, only one other state high court and one state intermediate appellate court have authorized their use. See, e.g., Commonwealth v. Bracey, 632 Pa. 75, ___\xe2\x80\x93___, 117 A.\xc2\xa03d 270, 286\xe2\x80\x93287 (2015); Howell v. State, 2011 WL 2420378, *18 (Tenn. Crim. App., June 14, 2011).

\n

Indeed, Texas itself does not follow Briseno in contexts other than the death penalty. See Brief for Constitution Project as Amicus Curiae 14\xe2\x80\x9317. For example, the related-ness requirement Texas defends here, see supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, is conspicuously absent from the standards the State uses to assess students for intellectual disabilities. See 19 Tex. Admin. Code \xc2\xa789.1040(c)(5) (2015). And even within Texas\xe2\x80\x99 criminal-justice system, the State requires the intellectual-disability diagnoses of juveniles to be based on \xe2\x80\x9cthe latest edition of the DSM.\xe2\x80\x9d 37 Tex. Admin. Code \xc2\xa7380.8751(e)(3) (2016). Texas cannot satisfactorily explain why it applies current medical standards for diagnosing intellectual disability in other contexts, yet clings to superseded standards when an individual\xe2\x80\x99s life is at stake.[10]

\n

V

\n

As noted supra, at 9, States have some flexibility, but not \xe2\x80\x9cunfettered discretion,\xe2\x80\x9d in enforcing Atkins\xe2\x80\x99 holding. Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 17). \xe2\x80\x9cIf the States were to have complete autonomy to define intellectual disability as they wished,\xe2\x80\x9d we have observed, \xe2\x80\x9cAtkins could become a nullity, and the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection of human dignity would not become a reality.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 18\xe2\x80\x9319).

\n

The medical community\xe2\x80\x99s current standards supply one constraint on States\xe2\x80\x99 leeway in this area. Reflecting improved understanding over time, see DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 7; AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311, at xiv\xe2\x80\x93xv, current manuals offer \xe2\x80\x9cthe best available description of how mental disorders are expressed and can be recognized by trained clinicians,\xe2\x80\x9d DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at xli. See also Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, ___, ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 2, 3, 8, 10\xe2\x80\x9311, 20\xe2\x80\x9321) (employing current clinical standards); Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 308, n.\xc2\xa03, 317, n.\xc2\xa022 (relying on then-current standards).

\n

In Moore\xe2\x80\x99s case, the habeas court applied current medical standards in concluding that Moore is intellectually disabled and therefore ineligible for the death penalty. See, e.g., App. to Pet. for Cert. 150a\xe2\x80\x93151a, 200a\xe2\x80\x93203a. The CCA, however, faulted the habeas court for \xe2\x80\x9cdisregarding [the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s] case law and employing the definition of intellectual disability presently used by the AAIDD.\xe2\x80\x9d 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 486. The CCA instead fastened its intellectual-disability determination to \xe2\x80\x9cthe AAMR\xe2\x80\x99s 1992 definition of intellectual disability that [it] adopted in Briseno for Atkins claims presented in Texas death-penalty cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. By rejecting the habeas court\xe2\x80\x99s application of medical guidance and clinging to the standard it laid out in Briseno, including the wholly nonclinical Briseno factors, the CCA failed adequately to inform itself of the \xe2\x80\x9cmedical community\xe2\x80\x99s diagnostic framework,\xe2\x80\x9d Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320). Because Briseno pervasively infected the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, the decision of that court cannot stand.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

For the reasons stated, the judgment of the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals (CCA) concluded that Bobby James Moore was not intellectually disabled so as to be exempt from the death penalty under Atkins v. Virginia,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304 (2002)\n. It reached that conclusion based on its findings that he had failed to establish either significantly subaverage intellectual functioning or related significant deficits in adaptive behavior. The latter conclusion was based, in part, on the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of a set of seven \xe2\x80\x9cevidentiary factors\xe2\x80\x9d from Ex parte Briseno, 135 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 1, 8 (Tex. Crim. App. 2004). I agree with the Court today that those factors are an unacceptable method of enforcing the guarantee of Atkins, and that the CCA therefore erred in using them to analyze adaptive deficits. But I do not agree that the CCA erred as to Moore\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual functioning. Because the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s determination on that ground is an independent basis for its judgment, I would affirm the decision below.

\n

My broader concern with today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, however, is that it abandons the usual mode of analysis this Court has employed in\n \nEighth Amendment cases. The Court overturns the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Moore failed to present sufficient evidence of both inadequate intellectual functioning and significant deficits in adaptive behavior without even considering \xe2\x80\x9cobjective indicia of society\xe2\x80\x99s standards\xe2\x80\x9d reflected in the practices among the States. Hall v. Florida, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 12) (quoting Roper v. Simmons,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551,\n563 (2005)\n). The Court instead crafts a constitutional holding based solely on what it deems to be medical consensus about intellectual disability. But clinicians, not judges, should determine clinical standards; and judges, not clinicians, should determine the content of the\n \nEighth Amendment. Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion confuses those roles, and I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

On April 25, 1980, Moore and two others were throwing dice when they decided to commit a robbery to obtain money for car payments. Moore provided the group with two firearms, and the three men began to drive around Houston looking for a target. Eventually they settled on the Birdsall Super Market. After negotiating their respective shares of the money they intended to steal and donning disguises, the three went inside, heading straight to a courtesy booth staffed by James McCarble and Edna Scott. When Scott realized a robbery was occurring and screamed, Moore shot McCarble in the head, killing the 70-year-old instantly.

\n

Moore fled Houston and remained on the run until his arrest in Louisiana ten days after the murder. After giving a written statement admitting his participation in the robbery and killing, Moore was charged with capital murder. A jury convicted him and sentenced him to death.

\n

Over the next three decades, Moore\xe2\x80\x99s case traversed the state and federal court systems, finally reaching the Atkins hearing at issue today in 2014. The state habeas court conducted a two-day evidentiary hearing, during which it heard testimony from family members, a fellow inmate, a prison official, and four mental health professionals. The court concluded that Moore had shown intellectual disability and recommended that he be granted relief.

\n

But it was just that: a recommendation. Under Texas law, the CCA, not the habeas court, is the ultimate factfinder in habeas corpus proceedings. Ex\xc2\xa0parte Reed, 271 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 698, 727 (Tex. Crim. App. 2008); see also Ex\xc2\xa0parte Moore, 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 481, 489 (Tex. Crim. App. 2015). Assuming that role, the CCA declined to adopt the habeas court\xe2\x80\x99s findings and conclusions, instead conducting its own review of the record to determine whether Moore had shown he was intellectually disabled.

\n

The CCA began by considering the appropriate legal standard for assessing intellectual disability. Following our instruction to the States to \xe2\x80\x9cdevelop[\xc2\xa0] appropriate ways to enforce\xe2\x80\x9d Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 317 (internal quotation marks omitted), the CCA had set out a legal definition for intellectual disability in its prior decision in Ex parte Briseno. Rather than follow that test, the habeas court below crafted its own standards for intellectual disability. But \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he decision to modify the legal standard for intellectual disability in the capital-sentencing context,\xe2\x80\x9d the CCA explained, \xe2\x80\x9crests with this Court unless and until the Legislature acts.\xe2\x80\x9d 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 487. Just as we have corrected lower courts for taking it upon themselves to dismiss our precedent as outdated, see, e.g., Bosse v. Okla-homa, 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (per curiam) (slip op., at2), so too the CCA rebuked the habeas court for ignoring binding CCA precedent.

\n

The CCA went on to explain why there was no reason to modify the legal standard it had previously set out. Briseno had stated a rule that in order for an Atkins claimant to demonstrate intellectual disability he must show (1) significantly subaverage general intellectual functioning and (2) related limitations in adaptive functioning, (3) which had appeared prior to age 18. See 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 486. It also laid out a set of seven evidentiary factors\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9cBriseno factors\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94designed to assist \xe2\x80\x9cfactfinders .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in weighing evidence\xe2\x80\x9d of intellectual disability. Briseno, 135 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 8.

\n

The three-prong definition of intellectual disability came directly from the ninth edition of the manual published by what is now the American Association on Intellectual and Developmental Disabilities (AAIDD). Id., at 7; see American Association on Mental Retardation, Mental Retardation: Definition, Classification, and Systems of Supports 5 (9th ed. 1992). By the time Moore\xe2\x80\x99s case reached the CCA, the AAIDD no longer included the requirement that adaptive deficits be \xe2\x80\x9crelated\xe2\x80\x9d to intellectual functioning. But, as the CCA noted, the most recent version of the other leading diagnostic manual, the DSM\xe2\x80\x935, did include that requirement. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 487, n.\xc2\xa05; American Psychiatric Association, Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders 38 (5th ed. 2013) (hereinafter DSM\xe2\x80\x935). So the CCA was faced with a choice in Moore: Keeping the relatedness requirement would be inconsistent with the AAIDD\xe2\x80\x99s current guidance; dropping it would be out of step with the newest version of the DSM. The CCA concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe legal test we established in Briseno remains adequately \xe2\x80\x98informed by the medical community\xe2\x80\x99s diagnostic framework,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and went on to evaluate the case under that approach. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 487 (quoting Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320)).

\n

Starting with intellectual functioning, the CCA conducted a painstaking analysis of the battery of tests Moore had taken over the past 40 years. The CCA concluded that five of the tests the habeas court had considered were unreli-able: two of them were neuropsychological tests rather than formal IQ measures; two were group-administered tests, which Moore\xe2\x80\x99s own experts had criticized, App. 12 (Otis-Lennon Mental Abilities Test \xe2\x80\x9cnot accepted as an instrument appropriate for the assessment of mental retardation or intellectual deficiency\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 115\xe2\x80\x93116 (Slosson is \xe2\x80\x9cnot the greatest test\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnot the most reliable approach\xe2\x80\x9d); and the administrator of the fifth test concluded it was \xe2\x80\x9cnot .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a valid score\xe2\x80\x9d because of evidence of suboptimal effort, id., at 203.

\n

That left two scores for the CCA to analyze: a 78 and a 74. Significantly subaverage intellectual functioning is \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally shown by an [IQ] of 70 or less.\xe2\x80\x9d 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 486. \xe2\x80\x9cTaking into account the standard error of measurement\xe2\x80\x9d for the 78 score yielded a range of 73 to 83\xe2\x80\x94i.e., a range that did not include an IQ of 70 or less. Id., at 519. As for the 74, the CCA again considered the standard error of measurement, which yielded a score range of 69 to 79. The lower end of that range placed Moore within the parameters for significantly subaverage intellectual functioning. The CCA found, however, that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s score was unlikely to be in the lower end of the error-generated range because he was likely exerting poor effort and experiencing depression at the time the test was administered\xe2\x80\x94both factors that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s experts agreed could artificially deflate IQ scores. Id., at 516\xe2\x80\x93517, 519; App. 46, 92. The CCA accordingly concluded that Moore had failed to present sufficient evidence of significantly subaverage intellectual functioning.

\n

Having failed one part of the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s three-part test, Moore could not be found intellectually disabled. The CCA nonetheless went on to consider the second prong of the test, Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive deficits. Moore had taken a standardized test of adaptive functioning in which he scored more than two standard deviations below the mean. But Dr. Kristi Compton, the state expert who had administered that test, explained that it was not an accurate measure of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s abilities. She reached this conclusion not because of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive strengths but instead because \xe2\x80\x9cshe had to assign zeroes to questions asking about areas to which [Moore] had no exposure, such as writing a check and using a microwave oven.\xe2\x80\x9d 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 522. Dr. Compton further opined that her evaluation of Moore and review of documentary evidence\xe2\x80\x94including school, trial, and prison records\xe2\x80\x94did not show adaptive deficits sufficient for an intellectual disability diagnosis. App. 185; see 470 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 521\xe2\x80\x93524.

\n

The CCA also considered and recounted the testimony of the other experts who, unlike Dr. Compton, concluded that Moore had shown significant adaptive deficits. As factfinders often do in confronting conflicting evidence, the CCA made a credibility determination. The opinion of Dr. Compton, the CCA concluded, was \xe2\x80\x9cfar more credible and reliable\xe2\x80\x9d than those of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s experts, given Dr. Compton\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderable experience,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthorough[\xc2\xa0] and rigorous[\xc2\xa0] review[\xc2\xa0] [of] a great deal of material,\xe2\x80\x9d and personal evaluation of Moore. Id., at 524. Based on Dr. Compton\xe2\x80\x99s expert opinion, the CCA concluded Moore had failed to demonstrate significant adaptive deficits.

\n

Finally, the CCA considered whether, even assuming that Moore had made sufficient showings as to intellectual functioning and adaptive deficits, those two were related. Again finding Dr. Compton\xe2\x80\x99s testimony the most credible, the CCA concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe record overwhelmingly supports the conclusion\xe2\x80\x9d that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s observed academic and social difficulties stemmed, not from low intellectual abilities, but instead from outside factors like the traumaand abuse he suffered as a child and his drug use at a young age. Id., at 526. The CCA explained that, in addition to Dr. Compton\xe2\x80\x99s expert testimony, considerationof the seven Briseno factors reinforced that relatedness conclusion.

\n

Given that Moore had failed to present sufficient evidence on intellectual functioning or related adaptive deficits, the CCA \xe2\x80\x9cconclude[d] that for\n \nEighth Amendment purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d Moore had not shown he was intellectually disabled. 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 527. Accordingly, he was not exempt from execution under Atkins.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents have emphasized the importance of state legislative judgments in giving content to the\n \nEighth Amendment ban on cruel and unusual punishment. \xe2\x80\x9c\n \nEighth Amendment judgments should not be .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. merely the subjective views of individual Justices.\xe2\x80\x9d Coker v. Georgia,\n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 584,\n592 (1977)\n (plurality opinion). For that reason, we have emphasized that \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment should be informed by objective factors to the maximum possible extent.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The \xe2\x80\x9cclearest and most reliable objective evidence of contemporary values\xe2\x80\x9d comes from state legislative judgments. Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 312 (internal quotation marks omitted). Such legislative judgments are critical because in \xe2\x80\x9ca democratic society legislatures, not courts, are constituted to respond to the will and consequently the moral values of the people.\xe2\x80\x9d Gregg v. Georgia,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153,\n175 (1976)\n (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.) (internal quotation marks omitted). And we have focused on state enactments in this realm because of the \xe2\x80\x9cdeference we owe to the decisions of the state legislatures under our federal system\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. where the specification of punishments is concerned.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 176. For these reasons, we have described state legislative judgments as providing \xe2\x80\x9cessential instruction\xe2\x80\x9d in conducting the\n \nEighth Amendment inquiry. Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 564.

\n

Our decisions addressing capital punishment for the intellectually disabled recognize the central significance of state consensus. In holding that the\n \nEighth Amendment prohibits the execution of intellectually disabled individuals in Atkins, the Court first identified a national consensus against the practice and then, applying our own \xe2\x80\x9cindependent evaluation of the issue,\xe2\x80\x9d concluded that there was \xe2\x80\x9cno reason to disagree\xe2\x80\x9d with that consensus. 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 321. The scope of our holding\xe2\x80\x94guided as it was by the national consensus\xe2\x80\x94swept only as far as that consensus. We recognized that there remained the potential for \xe2\x80\x9cserious disagreement .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in determining which offenders are in fact retarded.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 317. And we did not seek to provide \xe2\x80\x9cdefinitive procedural or substantive guides for determining when a person who claims mental retardation will be so impaired as to fall within Atkins\xe2\x80\x99 compass.\xe2\x80\x9d Bobby v. Bies,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825,\n831 (2009)\n (alterations and internal quotation marks omitted). Instead, we left \xe2\x80\x9cto the States the task of developing appropriate ways to enforce the constitutional restriction upon their execution ofsentences.\xe2\x80\x9d Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 317 (quoting Ford v. Wainwright,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399\n\xe2\x80\x93417 (1986); alterations omitted).

\n

Twelve years after Atkins, the Court confronted one State\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to enforce the holding of that case. Hall v. Florida considered Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule requiring a prisoner to present an IQ score of 70 or below to make out an Atkins claim. Although the Court thought it \xe2\x80\x9cproper to consider the psychiatric and professional studies that elaborate on the purpose and meaning of IQ scores,\xe2\x80\x9d it emphasized that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he legal determination of intellectual disability is distinct from a medical diagnosis.\xe2\x80\x9d 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, ___ (slip op., at 7, 19). It was \xe2\x80\x9cthe Court\xe2\x80\x99s duty\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94not that of medical experts\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cto interpret the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 19). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule was \xe2\x80\x9cin direct opposition to the views of those who design, administer, and interpret the IQ test\xe2\x80\x9d was not enough to decide the case. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 22). Instead, consistent with our settled approach, the Court canvassed \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislative policies of various States,\xe2\x80\x9d as well as \xe2\x80\x9cthe holdings of state courts,\xe2\x80\x9d because it was state policies that provided \xe2\x80\x9cessential instruction\xe2\x80\x9d for determining the scope of the constitutional guarantee. Id., at ___, ___ (slip op., at 7, 19) (quoting Roper, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 564). State policy, the Court concluded, indicated a \xe2\x80\x9cconsensus that our society does not regard [Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule] as proper or humane,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cconsensus .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. instruct[ed us] how to decide the specific issue presented.\xe2\x80\x9d 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, ___ (slip op., at 7, 16). The Court was sharply divided on that conclusion, see id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 4\xe2\x80\x937) (Alito, J., dissenting), but not on the fact that our precedent mandated such an inquiry.

\n

B

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision departs from this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, followed in Atkins and Hall, establishing that the determination of what is cruel and unusual rests on a judicial judgment about societal standards of decency, not a medical assessment of clinical practice. The Court rejects the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Moore failed to make the requisite showings with respect to intellectual functioning and adaptive deficits, without any consideration of the state practices that were, three Terms ago, \xe2\x80\x9cessential\xe2\x80\x9d to the\n \nEighth Amendment question. Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 19). The Court instead finds error in the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s analysis based solely on what the Court views to be departure from typical clinical practice.

\n

The clinical guides on which the Court relies today are \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to assist clinicians in conducting clinical assessment, case formulation, and treatment planning.\xe2\x80\x9d DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 25. They do not seek to dictate or describe who is morally culpable\xe2\x80\x94indeed, the DSM\xe2\x80\x935 cautions its readers about \xe2\x80\x9cthe imperfect fit between the questions of ultimate concern to the law and the information contained\xe2\x80\x9d within its pages. Ibid.

\n

The\n \nEighth Amendment, under our precedent, is supposed to impose a moral backstop on punishment, prohibiting sentences that our society deems repugnant. The Court, however, interprets that constitutional guarantee as turning on clinical guidelines that do not purport to reflect standards of decency. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal even to address what we previously \xe2\x80\x9cpinpointed\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe clearest and most reliable objective evidence\xe2\x80\x9d of such standards\xe2\x80\x94the practices among the States\xe2\x80\x94goes unexplained by the majority. Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 312 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

A second problem with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach is the lack of guidance it offers to States seeking to enforce the holding of Atkins. Recognizing that we have, in the very recent past, held that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the views of medical experts\xe2\x80\x99 do not \xe2\x80\x98dictate\xe2\x80\x99 a court\xe2\x80\x99s intellectual-disability determination,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court assures us that it is not requiring adherence \xe2\x80\x9cto everything stated in the latest medical guide,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (quoting Hall, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 19)); States have \xe2\x80\x9csome flexibility\xe2\x80\x9d but cannot \xe2\x80\x9cdisregard\xe2\x80\x9d medical standards. Ante, at 10, 17. Neither the Court\xe2\x80\x99s articulation of this standard nor its application sheds any light on what it means.

\n

Start with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s stated principle. \xe2\x80\x9cDisregard\xe2\x80\x9d normally means to dismiss as unworthy of attention, and that is plainly not what the CCA did here. For example, the Court faults the CCA for placing too much weight on Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive strengths and functioning in prison, implying that this marked a dismissal of clinical standards. Yet the CCA was aware of and, in a prior decision, had addressed the fact that some clinicians would counsel against considering such information. See 470 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d, at 489 (citing Ex\xc2\xa0parte Cathey, 451 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 1, 26\xe2\x80\x9327 (2014)). Both because \xe2\x80\x9c[m]ost courts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. consider all of the person\xe2\x80\x99s functional abilities\xe2\x80\x9d and because it seemed \xe2\x80\x9cfoolhardy\xe2\x80\x9d to ignore strengths, the CCA thought it proper to take note of them. Id., at 27. As to prison conduct, the CCA decided that the fundamental questions the Atkins inquiry sought to answer were best considered\xe2\x80\x94and \xe2\x80\x9csound scientific principles\xe2\x80\x9d best served\xe2\x80\x94by taking account of \xe2\x80\x9call possible data that sheds light on a person\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive functioning, including his conduct in a prison society.\xe2\x80\x9d 451 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 26\xe2\x80\x9327. The CCA considered clinical standards and explained why it decided that departure from those standards was warranted. The court did not \xe2\x80\x9cdisregard\xe2\x80\x9d medical standards.

\n

Nor do the Court\xe2\x80\x99s identified errors clarify the scope of the \xe2\x80\x9cflexibility\xe2\x80\x9d we are told States retain in this area. The Court faults the CCA for \xe2\x80\x9coveremphasiz[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d strengths and \xe2\x80\x9cstress[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d Moore\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in prison, ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313, suggesting that some\xe2\x80\x94but not too much\xe2\x80\x94consideration of strengths and prison functioning is acceptable. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s only guidance on when \xe2\x80\x9csome\xe2\x80\x9d becomes \xe2\x80\x9ctoo much\xe2\x80\x9d? Citations to clinical guides. See ibid. But if courts do have \xe2\x80\x9cflexibility\xe2\x80\x9d in enforcing the guarantee of Atkins and need not \xe2\x80\x9cadhere[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d to these guides in every instance or particular, ante, at 10, 17, then clinical texts, standing alone, cannot answer the question of why the CCA placed too much weight on adaptive strengths and prison conduct. The line between the permissible\xe2\x80\x94consideration, maybe even emphasis\xe2\x80\x94and the forbidden\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9coveremphasis\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94is not only thin, but totally undefined by today\xe2\x80\x99s decision. It is not at all clear when a State\xe2\x80\x99s deviation from medical consensus becomes so great as to \xe2\x80\x9cdiminish the force\xe2\x80\x9d of that consensus, ante, at 2, and thereby violate the Constitution.

\n

Finally, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision constitutionalizes rules for which there is not even clinical consensus\xe2\x80\x94a consequence that will often arise from the approach charted by the Court today. Consider the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that, contrary to \xe2\x80\x9cthe medical community[\xe2\x80\x99s] focus[\xc2\xa0] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. on adaptive deficits,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe CCA overemphasized Moore\xe2\x80\x99s perceived adaptive strengths.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. In support of this proposition, the Court cites the AAIDD\xe2\x80\x99s direction that \xe2\x80\x9csignificant limitations in conceptual, social, or practical adaptive skills [are] not outweighed by the potential strengths in some adaptive skills.\xe2\x80\x9d AAIDD, Intellectual Disability: Definition, Classification, and Systems of Supports 47 (11th ed. 2010) (hereinafter AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311). Even assuming that all clinicians would agree with this statement, there are a number of ways it might be interpreted: as meaning that strengths in one of the three adaptive skill areas\xe2\x80\x94conceptual, social, and practical\xe2\x80\x94should not cancel out deficits in another; as meaning that strengths should not outweigh deficits within the same skill area; or as meaning that evidence of some ability to perform a skill should not offset evidence of the inability to perform that same skill. And it appears that clinicians do, in fact, disagree about what this direction means. Compare, e.g., Brief for AAIDD et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 17 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe clinician\xe2\x80\x99s diagnostic focus does not\xe2\x80\x94and cannot\xe2\x80\x94involve any form of \xe2\x80\x98balancing\xe2\x80\x99 deficits against the abilities or strengths which the particular individual may also possess\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)) with Hagan, Drogin, & Guilmette, Assessing Adaptive Functioning in Death Penalty Cases after Hall and DSM\xe2\x80\x935, 44 J. Am. Acad. Psychiatry & L. 96, 98 (2016) (\xe2\x80\x9cAny assessment of adaptive functioning must give sufficient consideration to assets and deficits alike.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [I]n-ventorying only assets or deficits .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. departs from DSM\xe2\x80\x935, [the AAIDD\xe2\x80\x9311], and all other established frameworks\xe2\x80\x9d (footnotes omitted)).

\n

The same is true about consideration of prison conduct. The two primary clinical guides do offer caution about considering functioning in prison. But the stringency of their caution differs, with the AAIDD seeming to enact a flat ban on ever looking to functioning in prison and the DSM urging \xe2\x80\x9cif possible\xe2\x80\x9d to consider \xe2\x80\x9ccorroborative information reflecting functioning outside\xe2\x80\x9d of prison. AAIDD, User\xe2\x80\x99s Guide: Intellectual Disability: Definition, Classification, and Systems of Supports 20 (11th ed. 2012); DSM\xe2\x80\x935, at 38. The CCA followed the DSM\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x99s instruction, relying on Dr. Compton\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9ceven before [Moore] went to prison\xe2\x80\x9d he demonstrated a \xe2\x80\x9clevel of adaptive functioning .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. too great .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to support an intellectual-disability diagnosis.\xe2\x80\x9d 470 S. W. 3d, at 526. In deter-mining that the CCA erred in this regard, the Court implicitly rejects the DSM\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x99s approach to the proper consideration of prison conduct and accepts what it takes to be that of the AAIDD. The Court does not attempt to explain its justification for why the\n \nEighth Amendment should favor one side over the other in this clinical debate.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cPsychiatry is not .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an exact science.\xe2\x80\x9d Ake v. Oklahoma,\n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 68,\n81 (1985)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c[B]ecause there often is no single, accurate psychiatric conclusion,\xe2\x80\x9d we have emphasized the importance of allowing the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary factfinder[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cresolve differences in opinion .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. on the basis of the evidence offered by each party.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. You would not know it from reading the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion today, but that is precisely what the CCA\xe2\x80\x94the factfinder under Texas law\xe2\x80\x94did in the decision below: Confronted with dueling expert opinions about how to evaluate adaptive functioning and what conclusion to reach, the CCA resolved the dispute before it by accepting the testimony of the expert it deemed most credible. Of course, reliance on an expert opinion does not insulate a decision from further judicial review. But, unlike the Court, I am unwilling to upset the considered judgment of the forensic psychologist that the factfinding court deemed the most credible based on my own interpretation of a few sentences excised from medical texts.

\n

III

\n

As for how I would resolve this case, there is one aspect of the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s approach to intellectual disability that is incompatible with the\n \nEighth Amendment: the Briseno factors. As the Court explains, no state legislature has approved the use of these or any similar factors. Although the CCA reviewed these factors to determine whether Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive deficits were \xe2\x80\x9crelated\xe2\x80\x9d to his intellectual functioning, it may be that consideration of those factors tainted the whole of the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive functioning analysis. I need not decide this question, however, because the CCA reached the issue of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive functioning only after concluding that he had failed to demonstrate intellectual functioning sufficiently low to warrant a finding of intellectual disability, regardless of his adaptive deficits or their relation to his IQ. Moore has not presented sufficient reason to upset that independent holding.

\n

The Court concludes that the CCA\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ scores is \xe2\x80\x9cirreconcilable with Hall.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. Not so. Hall rejected a Florida rule that required a prisoner to present an IQ score of 70 or below to demonstrate intellectual disability, thereby barring consideration of the standard error of measurement (SEM) of an over-70 score. But the CCA did not apply Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule\xe2\x80\x94or anything like it. The court in fact began by taking account of the SEM, explaining that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s tested score of 74 led to an IQ range between 69 and 79. The court went on to consider additional expert testimony about potential factors affecting that score. Based on that evidence, the CCA discounted portions of the SEM-generated range and concluded that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ did not lie in the relevant range for intellectual disability.

\n

Hall provided no definitive guidance on this sort of approach: recognizing the inherent imprecision of IQ tests, but considering additional evidence to determine whether an SEM-generated range of scores accurately reflected a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s actual IQ.[1] Indeed, in its catalog of States that \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] taken a position contrary to that of Florida,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court in Hall included a State that granted trial courts discretion to draw \xe2\x80\x9creasonable inferences\xe2\x80\x9d about IQ scores and, where appropriate, decline to consider the full range of the SEM. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, ___ (slip op., at 15, 16) (quoting Pizzuto v. State, 146 Idaho 720, 729, 202 P.\xc2\xa03d 642, 651 (2008)).[2] That is the approach the CCA took here. If that approach was \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary\xe2\x80\x9d to Florida\xe2\x80\x99s rule in Hall, I do not understand how Hall can be read to reject that approach today.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling on intellectual functioning turns solely on the fact that Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ range was 69 to 79 rather than 70 to 80. See ante, at 11 (\xe2\x80\x9cBecause the lower end of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s score range falls at or below 70, the CCA had to move on to consider Moore\xe2\x80\x99s adaptive functioning\xe2\x80\x9d). The CCA certainly did not \xe2\x80\x9cdisregard\xe2\x80\x9d SEM in assessing Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ, and it explained why other factors led it to conclude that his actual score did not fall near the lower end of the SEM range. Only by insisting on the absolute conformity to medical standards the Court disclaims can it find a violation of the\n \nEighth Amendment based on that one-point difference.[3] Ibid. In concluding that the Eighth Amendment turns on the slightest numerical difference in IQ score, the Court today is just as wrong as the Florida Supreme Court was in Hall.

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is not compelled by Hall; it is an expansion of it. Perhaps there are reasons to expand Hall\xe2\x80\x99s holding\xe2\x80\x94to say that States must read IQ tests as rigidly encompassing the entire SEM range, regardless of any other evidentiary considerations, or to say that the reasons that the CCA gave for discounting the lower end of Moore\xe2\x80\x99s IQ range were improper. But before holding that the Constitution demands either result, our precedent requires consulting state judgments on the matter to determine whether a national consensus has developed. Moore has presented no argument as to such a consensus, and the majority does not claim that there is one. Without looking to any such \xe2\x80\x9cobjective evidence of contemporary values,\xe2\x80\x9d Atkins, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 312 (internal quotation marks omitted), there is a real danger that\n \nEighth Amendment judgments will embody \xe2\x80\x9cmerely the subjective views of individual Justices,\xe2\x80\x9d Coker, 433 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 592 (plurality opinion). As Justice Frankfurter cautioned, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne must be on guard against finding in personal disapproval a reflection of more or less prevailing condemnation.\xe2\x80\x9d Louisiana ex rel. Francis v. Resweber,\n\n329 U.\xc2\xa0S. 459,\n471 (1947)\n (concurring opinion).

\n

I respectfully dissent.
