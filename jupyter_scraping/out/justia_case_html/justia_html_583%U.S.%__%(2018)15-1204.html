\n

Jennings et\xc2\xa0al. v. Rodriguez et\xc2\xa0al., individually and on behalf of all others similarly situated

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x931204.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 30, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Reargued October 3, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 27, 2018

\n

Immigration officials are authorized to detain certain aliens in the course of immigration proceedings while they determine whether those aliens may be lawfully present in the country. For example, \xc2\xa71225(b) of Title 8 of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Code authorizes the detention of certain aliens seeking to enter the country. Section 1225(b)(1) applies to aliens initially determined to be inadmissible due to fraud, misrepresentation, or lack of valid documentation, and to certain other aliens designated by the Attorney General in his discretion. Section 1225(b)(2) is a catchall provision that applies to most other applicants for admission not covered by \xc2\xa71225(b)(1). Under \xc2\xa71225(b)(1), aliens are normally ordered removed \xe2\x80\x9cwithout further hearing or review,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(A)(i), but an alien indicating either an intention to apply for asylum or a credible fear of persecution, \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(A)(ii), \xe2\x80\x9cshall be detained\xe2\x80\x9d while that alien\xe2\x80\x99s asylum application is pending, \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(ii). Aliens covered by \xc2\xa71225(b)(2) in turn \xe2\x80\x9cshall be detained for a [removal] proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d if an immigration officer \xe2\x80\x9cdetermines that [they are] not clearly and beyond a doubt entitled\xe2\x80\x9d to admission. \xc2\xa71225(b)(2)(A).

\n

The Government is also authorized to detain certain aliens already in the country. Section 1226(a)\xe2\x80\x99s default rule permits the Attorney General to issue warrants for the arrest and detention of these aliens pending the outcome of their removal proceedings. The Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cmay release\xe2\x80\x9d these aliens on bond, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcept as provided in subsection (c) of this section.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 1226(c) in turn states that the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cshall take into custody any alien\xe2\x80\x9d who falls into one of the enumerated categories involving criminal offenses and terrorist activities, \xc2\xa71226(c)(1), and specifies that the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cmay release\xe2\x80\x9d one of those aliens \xe2\x80\x9conly if the Attorney General decides\xe2\x80\x9d both that doing so is necessary for witness-protection purposes and that the alien will not pose a danger or flight risk, \xc2\xa71226(c)(2).

\n

After a 2004 conviction, respondent Alejandro Rodriguez, a Mexican citizen and a lawful permanent resident of the United States, was detained pursuant to \xc2\xa71226 while the Government sought to remove him. In May 2007, while still litigating his removal, Rodriguez filed a habeas petition, claiming that he was entitled to a bond hearing to determine whether his continued detention was justified. As relevant here, he and the class of aliens he represents allege that \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b), 1226(a), and 1226(c) do not authorize \xe2\x80\x9cprolonged\xe2\x80\x9d detention in the absence of an individualized bond hearing at which the Government proves by clear and convincing evidence that detention remains justified. The District Court entered a permanent injunction, and the Ninth Circuit affirmed. Relying on the canon of constitutional avoidance, the Ninth Circuit construed \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b) and 1226(c) as imposing an implicit 6-month time limit on an alien\xe2\x80\x99s detention under those sections. After that point, the court held, the Government may continue to detain the alien only under the authority of \xc2\xa71226(a). The court then construed \xc2\xa71226(a) to mean that an alien must be given a bond hearing every six months and that detention beyond the initial 6-month period is permitted only if the Government proves by clear and convincing evidence that further detention is justified.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is reversed, and the case is remanded.

\n

804 F.\xc2\xa03d 1060, reversed and remanded.

\n

Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court, except as to Part II, concluding that \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b), 1226(a), and 1226(c) do not give detained aliens the right to periodic bond hearings during the course of their detention. The Ninth Circuit misapplied the canon of constitutional avoidance in holding otherwise. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The canon of constitutional avoidance \xe2\x80\x9ccomes into play only when, after the application of ordinary textual analysis, the statute is found to be susceptible of more than one [plausible] construction.\xe2\x80\x9d Clark v. Martinez,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 371\n. The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretations of the provisions at issue, however, are implausible. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Read most naturally, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2) mandate detention of applicants for admission until certain proceedings have concluded. Until that point, nothing in the statutory text imposes a limit on the length of detention, and neither provision says anything about bond hearings. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0Nothing in the text of \xc2\xa71225(b)(1) or \xc2\xa71225(b)(2) hints that those provisions have an implicit 6-month time limit on the length of detention. Respondents must show that this is a plausible reading in order to prevail under the canon of constitutional avoidance, but they simply invoke the canon without making any attempt to defend their reading.

\n

The Ninth Circuit also all but ignored the statutory text, relying instead on Zadvydas v. Davis,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678\n, as authority for grafting a time limit onto \xc2\xa71225(b)\xe2\x80\x99s text. There, this Court invoked the constitutional-avoidance canon, construing \xc2\xa71231(a)(6)\xe2\x80\x94which provides than an alien subject to a removal order \xe2\x80\x9cmay be detained\xe2\x80\x9d beyond the section\xe2\x80\x99s 90-day removal period\xe2\x80\x94to mean that the alien may not be detained beyond \xe2\x80\x9ca period reasonably necessary to secure removal,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 699, presumptively six months, id., at 701. The Court detected ambiguity in the statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmay be detained\xe2\x80\x9d and noted the absence of any explicit statutory limit on the length of permissible detention following the entry of an order of removal.

\n

Several material differences distinguish the provisions at issue in this case from Zadvydas\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa71231(a)(6). To start, the provisions here, unlike \xc2\xa71231(a)(6), mandate detention for a specified period of time: until immigration officers have finished \xe2\x80\x9cconsider[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d the asylum application, \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(ii), or until removal proceedings have concluded, \xc2\xa71225(b)(2)(A). Section 1231(a)(6) also uses the ambiguous \xe2\x80\x9cmay,\xe2\x80\x9d while \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2) use the unequivocal mandate \xe2\x80\x9cshall be detained.\xe2\x80\x9d There is also a specific provision authorizing temporary parole from \xc2\xa71225(b) detention \xe2\x80\x9cfor urgent humanitarian reasons or significant public benefit,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71182(d)(5)(A), but no similar release provision applies to \xc2\xa71231(a)(6). That express exception implies that there are no other circumstances under which aliens detained under \xc2\xa71225(b) may be released. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0Respondents also claim that the term \xe2\x80\x9cfor\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2) mandates detention only until the start of applicable proceedings. That is inconsistent with the meanings of \xe2\x80\x9cfor\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c[d]uring [or] throughout,\xe2\x80\x9d 6 Oxford English Dictionary 26, and \xe2\x80\x9cwith the object or purpose of,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 23\xe2\x80\x94that make sense in the context of the statutory scheme as a whole. Nor does respondents\xe2\x80\x99 reading align with the historical use of \xe2\x80\x9cfor\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71225. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Section 1226(c)\xe2\x80\x99s language is even clearer. By allowing aliens to be released \xe2\x80\x9conly if\xe2\x80\x9d the Attorney General decides that certain conditions are met, that provision reinforces the conclusion that aliens detained under its authority are not entitled to be released under any circumstances other than those expressly recognized by the statute. Together with \xc2\xa71226(a), \xc2\xa71226(c) makes clear that detention of aliens within its scope must continue \xe2\x80\x9cpending a decision\xe2\x80\x9d on removal. Section 1226(c) is thus not silent as to the length of detention. See Demore v. Kim,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510\n. The provision, by expressly stating that covered aliens may be released \xe2\x80\x9conly if\xe2\x80\x9d certain conditions are met, also unequivocally imposes an affirmative prohibition on releasing them under any other conditions. Finally, because \xc2\xa71226(c) and the PATRIOT Act apply to different categories of aliens in different ways, adopting \xc2\xa71226(c)\xe2\x80\x99s plain meaning will not make any part of the PATRIOT Act, see \xc2\xa71226a(a)(3), superfluous. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0Nothing in \xc2\xa71226(a), which authorizes the Attorney General to arrest and detain an alien \xe2\x80\x9cpending a decision\xe2\x80\x9d on removal and which permits the Attorney General to release the alien on bond, supports the imposition of periodic bond hearings every six months in which the Attorney General must prove by clear and convincing evidence that continued detention is necessary. Nor does it hint that the length of detention prior to the bond hearing must be considered in determining whether an alien should be released. Pp.\xc2\xa022\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

(e)\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit should consider the merits of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional arguments in the first instance. But before doing so, it should also reexamine whether respondents can continue litigating their claims as a class. Pp.\xc2\xa029\xe2\x80\x9331.

DAVID JENNINGS, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. ALEJANDRO RODRIGUEZ, et\xc2\xa0al., individ- ually and on behalf of all others similarly situated

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

[February 27, 2018]

Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court, except as to Part II.[1]*

Every day, immigration officials must determine whether to admit or remove the many aliens who have arrived at an official \xe2\x80\x9cport of entry\xe2\x80\x9d (e.g., an international airport or border crossing) or who have been apprehended trying to enter the country at an unauthorized location. Immigration officials must also determine on a daily basis whether there are grounds for removing any of the aliens who are already present inside the country. The vast majority of these determinations are quickly made, but in some cases deciding whether an alien should be admitted or removed is not as easy. As a result, Congress has authorized immigration officials to detain some classes of aliens during the course of certain immigration proceedings. Detention during those proceedings gives immigration officials time to determine an alien\xe2\x80\x99s status without running the risk of the alien\xe2\x80\x99s either absconding or engaging in criminal activity before a final decision can be made.

In this case we are asked to interpret three provisions of U.\xc2\xa0S. immigration law that authorize the Government to detain aliens in the course of immigration proceedings. All parties appear to agree that the text of these provisions, when read most naturally, does not give detained aliens the right to periodic bond hearings during the course of their detention. But by relying on the constitutional-avoidance canon of statutory interpretation, the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit held that detained aliens have a statutory right to periodic bond hearings under the provisions at issue.

Under the constitutional-avoidance canon, when statutory language is susceptible of multiple interpretations, a court may shun an interpretation that raises serious constitutional doubts and instead may adopt an alternative that avoids those problems. But a court relying on that canon still must interpret the statute, not rewrite it. Because the Court of Appeals in this case adopted implausible constructions of the three immigration provisions at issue, we reverse its judgment and remand for further proceedings.

I

A

To implement its immigration policy, the Government must be able to decide (1) who may enter the country and (2) who may stay here after entering.

1

That process of decision generally begins at the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s borders and ports of entry, where the Government must determine whether an alien seeking to enter the country is admissible. Under122Stat.867,8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71225, an alien who \xe2\x80\x9carrives in the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cis present\xe2\x80\x9d in this country but \xe2\x80\x9chas not been admitted,\xe2\x80\x9d is treated as \xe2\x80\x9can applicant for admission.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71225(a)(1). Applicants for admission must \xe2\x80\x9cbe inspected by immigration officers\xe2\x80\x9d to ensure that they may be admitted into the country consistent with U.\xc2\xa0S. immigration law. \xc2\xa71225(a)(3).

As relevant here, applicants for admission fall into one of two categories, those covered by \xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and those covered by \xc2\xa71225(b)(2). Section 1225(b)(1) applies to aliens initially determined to be inadmissible due to fraud, misrepresentation, or lack of valid documentation. See \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(A)(i) (citing \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71182(a)(6)(C), (a)(7)). Section 1225(b)(1) also applies to certain other aliens designated by the Attorney General in his discretion. See \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(A)(iii). Section 1225(b)(2) is broader. It serves as a catchall provision that applies to all applicants for admission not covered by \xc2\xa71225(b)(1) (with specific exceptions not relevant here). See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(2)(A), (B).

Both \xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and \xc2\xa71225(b)(2) authorize the detention of certain aliens. Aliens covered by \xc2\xa71225(b)(1) are normally ordered removed \xe2\x80\x9cwithout further hearing or review\xe2\x80\x9d pursuant to an expedited removal process. \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(A)(i). But if a \xc2\xa71225(b)(1) alien \xe2\x80\x9cindicates either an intention to apply for asylum .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or a fear of persecution,\xe2\x80\x9d then that alien is referred for an asylum interview. \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(A)(ii). If an immigration officer determines after that interview that the alien has a credible fear of persecution, \xe2\x80\x9cthe alien shall be detained for further consideration of the application for asylum.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(ii). Aliens who are instead covered by \xc2\xa71225(b)(2) are detained pursuant to a different process. Those aliens \xe2\x80\x9cshall be detained for a [removal] proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d if an immigration officer \xe2\x80\x9cdetermines that [they are] not clearly and beyond a doubt entitled to be admitted\xe2\x80\x9d into the country. \xc2\xa71225(b)(2)(A).

Regardless of which of those two sections authorizes their detention, applicants for admission may be temporarily released on parole \xe2\x80\x9cfor urgent humanitarian reasons or significant public benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71182(d)(5)(A); see also 8 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7212.5(b), 235.3 (2017). Such parole, however, \xe2\x80\x9cshall not be regarded as an admission of the alien.\xe2\x80\x9d8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71182(d)(5)(A). Instead, when the purpose of the parole has been served, \xe2\x80\x9cthe alien shall forthwith return or be returned to the custody from which he was paroled and thereafter his case shall continue to be dealt with in the same manner as that of any other applicant for admission to the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

2

Even once inside the United States, aliens do not have an absolute right to remain here. For example, an alien present in the country may still be removed if he or she falls \xe2\x80\x9cwithin one or more .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. classes of deportable aliens.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71227(a). That includes aliens who were inadmissible at the time of entry or who have been convicted of certain criminal offenses since admission. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71227(a)(1), (2).

Section 1226 generally governs the process of arresting and detaining that group of aliens pending their removal. As relevant here, \xc2\xa71226 distinguishes between two different categories of aliens. Section 1226(a) sets out the default rule: The Attorney General may issue a warrant for the arrest and detention of an alien \xe2\x80\x9cpending a decision on whether the alien is to be removed from the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71226(a). \xe2\x80\x9cExcept as provided in subsection (c) of this section,\xe2\x80\x9d the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cmay release\xe2\x80\x9d an alien detained under \xc2\xa71226(a) \xe2\x80\x9con bond .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or conditional parole.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

Section 1226(c), however, carves out a statutory category of aliens who may not be released under \xc2\xa71226(a). Under \xc2\xa71226(c), the \xe2\x80\x9cAttorney General shall take into custody any alien\xe2\x80\x9d who falls into one of several enumerated categories involving criminal offenses and terrorist activities. \xc2\xa71226(c)(1). The Attorney General may release aliens in those categories \xe2\x80\x9conly if the Attorney General decides .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that release of the alien from custody is necessary\xe2\x80\x9d for witness-protection purposes and \xe2\x80\x9cthe alien satisfies the Attorney General that the alien will not pose a danger to the safety of other persons or of property and is likely to appear for any scheduled proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71226(c)(2). Any release under those narrow conditions \xe2\x80\x9cshall take place in accordance with a procedure that considers the severity of the offense committed by the alien.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[2]

In sum, U.\xc2\xa0S. immigration law authorizes the Government to detain certain aliens seeking admission into the country under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2). It also authorizes the Government to detain certain aliens already in the country pending the outcome of removal proceedings under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71226(a) and (c). The primary issue is the proper interpretation of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b), 1226(a), and 1226(c).

B

Respondent Alejandro Rodriguez is a Mexican citizen. Since 1987, he has also been a lawful permanent resident of the United States. In April 2004, after Rodriguez was convicted of a drug offense and theft of a vehicle, the Government detained him under \xc2\xa71226 and sought to remove him from the country. At his removal hearing, Rodriguez argued both that he was not removable and, in the alternative, that he was eligible for relief from removal. In July 2004, an Immigration Judge ordered Rodriguez deported to Mexico. Rodriguez chose to appeal that decision to the Board of Immigration Appeals, but five months later the Board agreed that Rodriguez was subject to mandatory removal. Once again, Rodriguez chose to seek further review, this time petitioning the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit for review of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s decision.

In May 2007, while Rodriguez was still litigating his removal in the Court of Appeals, he filed a habeas petition in the District Court for the Central District of California, alleging that he was entitled to a bond hearing to determine whether his continued detention was justified. Rodriguez\xe2\x80\x99s case was consolidated with another, similar case brought by Alejandro Garcia, and together they moved for class certification. The District Court denied their motion, but the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed. See Rodriguez v. Hayes, 591 F.\xc2\xa03d 1105, 1111 (2010). It concluded that the proposed class met the certification requirements of Rule 23 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, and it remanded the case to the District Court. Id., at 1111, 1126.

On remand, the District Court certified the following class:

\xe2\x80\x9c[A]ll non-citizens within the Central District of California who: (1) are or were detained for longer than six months pursuant to one of the general immigration detention statutes pending completion of removal proceedings, including judicial review, (2) are not and have not been detained pursuant to a national security detention statute, and (3) have not been afforded a hearing to determine whether their detention is justified.\xe2\x80\x9d Class Certification Order in Rodriguez v. Hayes, CV 07\xe2\x80\x9303239 (CD Cal., Apr. 5, 2010).

The District Court named Rodriguez as class representative of the newly certified class, ibid., and then organized the class into four subclasses based on the four \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral immigration detention statutes\xe2\x80\x9d under which it understood the class members to be detained: Sections 1225(b), 1226(a), 1226(c), and 1231(a). See Order Granting Plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s Motion for Class Certification in Rodriguez v. Holder, CV 07\xe2\x80\x9303239 (CD Cal., Mar. 8, 2011) (2011 Order); Rodriguez v. Robbins, 715 F.\xc2\xa03d 1127, 1130\xe2\x80\x931131 (CA9 2013). Each of the four subclasses was certified to pursue declaratory and injunctive relief. 2011 Order. On appeal, the Court of Appeals held that the \xc2\xa71231(a) subclass had been improperly certified, but it affirmed the certification of the other three subclasses. See Rodriguez v. Robbins, 804 F.\xc2\xa03d 1060, 1074, 1085\xe2\x80\x931086 (CA9 2015).

In their complaint, Rodriguez and the other respondents argued that the relevant statutory provisions\xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b), 1226(a), and 1226(c)\xe2\x80\x94do not authorize \xe2\x80\x9cprolonged\xe2\x80\x9d detention in the absence of an individualized bond hearing at which the Government proves by clear and convincing evidence that the class member\xe2\x80\x99s detention remains justified. Absent such a bond-hearing requirement, respondents continued, those three provisions would violate the Due Process Clause of the Fifth Amendment. In their prayer for relief, respondents thus asked the District Court to require the Government \xe2\x80\x9cto provide, after giving notice, individual hearings before an immigration judge for .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. each member of the class, at which [the Government] will bear the burden to prove by clear and convincing evidence that no reasonable conditions will ensure the detainee\xe2\x80\x99s presence in the event of removal and protect the community from serious danger, despite the prolonged length of detention at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Third Amended Complaint in Rodriguez v. Holder, CV 07\xe2\x80\x9303239, p. 31 (CD Cal., Oct. 20, 2010). Respondents also sought declaratory relief. Ibid.

As relevant here, the District Court entered a permanent injunction in line with the relief sought by respondents, and the Court of Appeals affirmed. See 804 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1065. Relying heavily on the canon of constitutional avoidance, the Court of Appeals construed \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b) and 1226(c) as imposing an implicit 6-month time limit on an alien\xe2\x80\x99s detention under these sections. Id., at 1079, 1082. After that point, the Court of Appeals held, the Government may continue to detain the alien only under the authority of \xc2\xa71226(a). Ibid. The Court of Appeals then construed \xc2\xa71226(a) to mean that an alien must be given a bond hearing every six months and that detention beyond the initial 6-month period is permitted only if the Government proves by clear and convincing evidence that further detention is justified. Id., at 1085, 1087.

The Government petitioned this Court for review of that decision, and we granted certiorari. 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016).

II

Before reaching the merits of the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, we briefly address whether we have jurisdiction to entertain respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims. We discuss two potential obstacles, 8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71252(b)(9) and 1226(e).

A

Under \xc2\xa71252(b)(9):

\xe2\x80\x9cJudicial review of all questions of law and fact, including interpretation and application of constitutional and statutory provisions, arising from any action taken or proceeding brought to remove an alien from the United States under this subchapter [including \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225 and 1226] shall be available only in judicial review of a final order under this section.\xe2\x80\x9d

This provision does not deprive us of jurisdiction. We are required in this case to decide \xe2\x80\x9cquestions of law,\xe2\x80\x9d specifically, whether, contrary to the decision of the Court of Appeals, certain statutory provisions require detention without a bond hearing. We assume for the sake of argument that the actions taken with respect to all the aliens in the certified class constitute \xe2\x80\x9caction[s] taken .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to remove [them] from the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d[3] On that assumption, the applicability of \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) turns on whether the legal questions that we must decide \xe2\x80\x9caris[e] from\xe2\x80\x9d the actions taken to remove these aliens.

It may be argued that this is so in the sense that if those actions had never been taken, the aliens would not be in custody at all. But this expansive interpretation of \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) would lead to staggering results. Suppose, for example, that a detained alien wishes to assert a claim under Bivens v. Six Unknown Fed. Narcotics Agents,403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 388 (1971), based on allegedly inhumane conditions of confinement. See, e.g., Ziglar v. Abbasi, 582 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2017) (slip op., at 23\xe2\x80\x9329). Or suppose that a detained alien brings a state-law claim for assault against a guard or fellow detainee. Or suppose that an alien is injured when a truck hits the bus transporting aliens to a detention facility, and the alien sues the driver or owner of the truck. The \xe2\x80\x9cquestions of law and fact\xe2\x80\x9d in all those cases could be said to \xe2\x80\x9caris[e] from\xe2\x80\x9d actions taken to remove the aliens in the sense that the aliens\xe2\x80\x99 injuries would never have occurred if they had not been placed in detention. But cramming judicial review of those questions into the review of final removal orders would be absurd.

Interpreting \xe2\x80\x9carising from\xe2\x80\x9d in this extreme way would also make claims of prolonged detention effectively unreviewable. By the time a final order of removal was eventually entered, the allegedly excessive detention would have already taken place. And of course, it is possible that no such order would ever be entered in a particular case, depriving that detainee of any meaningful chance for judicial review.

In past cases, when confronted with capacious phrases like \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98arising from,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d we have eschewed \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98uncritical literalism\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d leading to results that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98no sensible person could have intended.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Gobeille v. Liberty Mut. Ins. Co., 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 6) (interpreting phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelate to\xe2\x80\x9d in the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision). See also, e.g., FERC v. Electric Power Supply Assn., 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2016) (slip op., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316) (interpreting term \xe2\x80\x9caffecting\xe2\x80\x9d in Federal Power Act); Maracich v. Spears,570 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48\xe2\x80\x9361 (2013) (interpreting phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d in Driver\xe2\x80\x99s Privacy Protection Act); Dan\xe2\x80\x99s City Used Cars, Inc. v. Pelkey,569 U.\xc2\xa0S. 251\xe2\x80\x93261 (2013) (interpreting phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelated to\xe2\x80\x9d in Federal Aviation Administration Authorization Act); Celotex Corp. v. Edwards,514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 300,308 (1995) (interpreting phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelated to\xe2\x80\x9d in Bankruptcy Act). In Reno v. American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Comm.,525 U.\xc2\xa0S. 471,482 (1999), we took this approach in construing the very phrase that appears in \xc2\xa71252(b)(9). A neighboring provision of the Immigration and Nationality Act refers to \xe2\x80\x9cany cause or claim by or on behalf of any alien arising from the decision or action by the Attorney General to commence proceedings, adjudicate cases, or execute removal orders against any alien under this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(g) (emphasis added). We did not interpret this language to sweep in any claim that can technically be said to \xe2\x80\x9carise from\xe2\x80\x9d the three listed actions of the Attorney General. Instead, we read the language to refer to just those three specific actions themselves. American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Comm., supra, at 482\xe2\x80\x93483.

The parties in this case have not addressed the scope of \xc2\xa71252(b)(9), and it is not necessary for us to attempt to provide a comprehensive interpretation. For present purposes, it is enough to note that respondents are not asking for review of an order of removal; they are not challenging the decision to detain them in the first place or to seek removal; and they are not even challenging any part of the process by which their removability will be determined. Under these circumstances, \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) does not present a jurisdictional bar.[4]

B

We likewise hold that \xc2\xa71226(e) does not bar us from considering respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims.

That provision states:

\xe2\x80\x9cThe Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s discretionary judgment regarding the application of [\xc2\xa71226] shall not be subject to review. No court may set aside any action or decision by the Attorney General under this section regarding the detention or release of any alien or the grant, revocation, or denial of bond or parole.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71226(e).

As we have previously explained, \xc2\xa71226(e) precludes an alien from \xe2\x80\x9cchalleng[ing] a \xe2\x80\x98discretionary judgment\xe2\x80\x99 by the Attorney General or a \xe2\x80\x98decision\xe2\x80\x99 that the Attorney General has made regarding his detention or release.\xe2\x80\x9d Demore v. Kim,538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510,516 (2003). But \xc2\xa71226(e) does not preclude \xe2\x80\x9cchallenges [to] the statutory framework that permits [the alien\xe2\x80\x99s] detention without bail.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 517.

Respondents mount that second type of challenge here. First and foremost, they are challenging the extent of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s detention authority under the \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory framework\xe2\x80\x9d as a whole. If that challenge fails, they are then contesting the constitutionality of the entire statutory scheme under the Fifth Amendment. Because the extent of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s detention authority is not a matter of \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9caction,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cdecision,\xe2\x80\x9d respondents\xe2\x80\x99 challenge to \xe2\x80\x9cthe statutory framework that permits [their] detention without bail,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., falls outside of the scope of \xc2\xa71226(e). We may therefore consider the merits of their claims.

III

When \xe2\x80\x9ca serious doubt\xe2\x80\x9d is raised about the constitutionality of an act of Congress, \xe2\x80\x9cit is a cardinal principle that this Court will first ascertain whether a construction of the statute is fairly possible by which the question may be avoided.\xe2\x80\x9d Crowell v. Benson,285 U.\xc2\xa0S. 22,62 (1932). Relying on this canon of constitutional avoidance, the Court of Appeals construed \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b), 1226(a), and 1226(c) to limit the permissible length of an alien\xe2\x80\x99s detention without a bond hearing. Without such a construction, the Court of Appeals believed, the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98prolonged detention without adequate procedural protections\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d authorized by the provisions \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98would raise serious constitutional concerns.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 804 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1077 (quoting Casas-Castrillon v. DHS, 535 F.\xc2\xa03d 942, 950 (CA9 2008)).

The canon of constitutional avoidance \xe2\x80\x9ccomes into play only when, after the application of ordinary textual analysis, the statute is found to be susceptible of more than one construction.\xe2\x80\x9d Clark v. Martinez,543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 371,385 (2005). In the absence of more than one plausible construction, the canon simply \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98has no application.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Warger v. Shauers, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 10) (quoting United States v. Oakland Cannabis Buyers\xe2\x80\x99 Cooperative,532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 483,494 (2001)).

The Court of Appeals misapplied the canon in this case because its interpretations of the three provisions at issue here are implausible. In Parts III\xe2\x80\x93A and III\xe2\x80\x93B, we hold that, subject only to express exceptions, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b) and 1226(c) authorize detention until the end of applicable proceedings. And in Part III\xe2\x80\x93C, we hold that there is no justification for any of the procedural requirements that the Court of Appeals layered onto \xc2\xa71226(a) without any arguable statutory foundation.

A

As noted, \xc2\xa71225(b) applies primarily to aliens seeking entry into the United States (\xe2\x80\x9capplicants for admission\xe2\x80\x9d in the language of the statute). Section 1225(b) divides these applicants into two categories. First, certain aliens claiming a credible fear of persecution under \xc2\xa71225(b)(1) \xe2\x80\x9cshall be detained for further consideration of the application for asylum.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(ii). Second, aliens falling within the scope of \xc2\xa71225(b)(2) \xe2\x80\x9cshall be detained for a [removal] proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71225(b)(2)(A).

Read most naturally, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2) thus mandate detention of applicants for admission until certain proceedings have concluded. Section 1225(b)(1) aliens are detained for \xe2\x80\x9cfurther consideration of the application for asylum,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xc2\xa71225(b)(2) aliens are in turn detained for \xe2\x80\x9c[removal] proceeding[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Once those proceedings end, detention under \xc2\xa71225(b) must end as well. Until that point, however, nothing in the statutory text imposes any limit on the length of detention. And neither \xc2\xa71225(b)(1) nor \xc2\xa71225(b)(2) says anything whatsoever about bond hearings.

Despite the clear language of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2), respondents argue\xe2\x80\x94and the Court of Appeals held\xe2\x80\x94that those provisions nevertheless can be construed to contain implicit limitations on the length of detention. But neither of the two limiting interpretations offered by respondents is plausible.

1

First, respondents argue that \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2) contain an implicit 6-month limit on the length of detention. Once that 6-month period elapses, respondents contend, aliens previously detained under those provisions must instead be detained under the authority of \xc2\xa71226(a), which allows for bond hearings in certain circumstances.

There are many problems with this interpretation. Nothing in the text of \xc2\xa71225(b)(1) or \xc2\xa71225(b)(2) even hints that those provisions restrict detention after six months, but respondents do not engage in any analysis of the text. Instead, they simply cite the canon of constitutional avoidance and urge this Court to use that canon to read a \xe2\x80\x9csix-month reasonableness limitation\xe2\x80\x9d into \xc2\xa71225(b). Brief for Respondents 48.

That is not how the canon of constitutional avoidance works. Spotting a constitutional issue does not give a court the authority to rewrite a statute as it pleases. Instead, the canon permits a court to \xe2\x80\x9cchoos[e] between competing plausible interpretations of a statutory text.\xe2\x80\x9d Clark, supra, at 381 (emphasis added). To prevail, respondents must thus show that \xc2\xa71225(b)\xe2\x80\x99s detention provisions may plausibly be read to contain an implicit 6-month limit. And they do not even attempt to defend that reading of the text.

In much the same manner, the Court of Appeals all but ignored the statutory text. Instead, it read Zadvydas v. Davis,533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678 (2001), as essentially granting a license to graft a time limit onto the text of \xc2\xa71225(b). Zadvydas, however, provides no such authority.

Zadvydas concerned \xc2\xa71231(a)(6), which authorizes the detention of aliens who have already been ordered removed from the country. Under this section, when an alien is ordered removed, the Attorney General is directed to complete removal within a period of 90 days,8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71231(a)(1)(A), and the alien must be detained during that period, \xc2\xa71231(a)(2). After that time elapses, however, \xc2\xa71231(a)(6) provides only that certain aliens \xe2\x80\x9cmay be detained\xe2\x80\x9d while efforts to complete removal continue. (Emphasis added.)

In Zadvydas, the Court construed \xc2\xa71231(a)(6) to mean that an alien who has been ordered removed may not be detained beyond \xe2\x80\x9ca period reasonably necessary to secure removal,\xe2\x80\x9d 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 699, and it further held that six months is a presumptively reasonable period, id., at 701. After that, the Court concluded, if the alien \xe2\x80\x9cprovides good reason to believe that there is no significant likelihood of removal in the reasonably foreseeable future,\xe2\x80\x9d the Government must either rebut that showing or release the alien. Ibid.

The Zadvydas Court justified this interpretation by invoking the constitutional-avoidance canon, and the Court defended its resort to that canon on the ground that \xc2\xa71231(a)(6) is ambiguous. Specifically, the Court detected ambiguity in the statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmay be detained.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[M]ay,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the Court said, \xe2\x80\x9csuggests discretion\xe2\x80\x9d but not necessarily \xe2\x80\x9cunlimited discretion. In that respect the word \xe2\x80\x98may\xe2\x80\x99 is ambiguous.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 697. The Court also pointed to the absence of any explicit statutory limit on the length of permissible detention following the entry of an order of removal. Ibid.

Zadvydas represents a notably generous application of the constitutional-avoidance canon, but the Court of Appeals in this case went much further. It failed to address whether Zadvydas\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning may fairly be applied in this case despite the many ways in which the provision in question in Zadvydas, \xc2\xa71231(a)(6), differs materially from those at issue here, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2). Those dif- ferences preclude the reading adopted by the Court of Appeals.

To start, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2), unlike \xc2\xa71231(a)(6), provide for detention for a specified period of time. Section 1225(b)(1) mandates detention \xe2\x80\x9cfor further consideration of the application for asylum,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(ii), and \xc2\xa71225(b)(2) requires detention \xe2\x80\x9cfor a [removal] proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71225(b)(2)(A). The plain meaning of those phrases is that detention must continue until immigration officers have finished \xe2\x80\x9cconsider[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d the application for asylum, \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(ii), or until removal proceedings have concluded, \xc2\xa71225(b)(2)(A). By contrast, Congress left the permissible length of detention under \xc2\xa71231(a)(6) unclear.

Moreover, in Zadvydas, the Court saw ambiguity in \xc2\xa71231(a)(6)\xe2\x80\x99s use of the word \xe2\x80\x9cmay.\xe2\x80\x9d Here, by contrast, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2) do not use the word \xe2\x80\x9cmay.\xe2\x80\x9d Instead, they unequivocally mandate that aliens falling within their scope \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d be detained. \xe2\x80\x9cUnlike the word \xe2\x80\x98may,\xe2\x80\x99 which implies discretion, the word \xe2\x80\x98shall\xe2\x80\x99 usually connotes a requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d Kingdomware Technologies, Inc. v. United States, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 9). That requirement of detention precludes a court from finding ambiguity here in the way that Zadvydas found ambiguity in \xc2\xa71231(a)(6).

Zadvydas\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning is particularly inapt here because there is a specific provision authorizing release from \xc2\xa71225(b) detention whereas no similar release provision applies to \xc2\xa71231(a)(6). With a few exceptions not relevant here, the Attorney General may \xe2\x80\x9cfor urgent humanitarian reasons or significant public benefit\xe2\x80\x9d temporarily parole aliens detained under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2).8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71182(d)(5)(A). That express exception to detention implies that there are no other circumstances under which aliens detained under \xc2\xa71225(b) may be released. See A. Scalia & B. Garner, Reading Law 107 (2012) (\xe2\x80\x9cNegative-Implication Canon[:] The expression of one thing implies the exclusion of others (expressio unius est exclusio al- terius)\xe2\x80\x9d). That negative implication precludes the sort of implicit time limit on detention that we found in Zadvydas.[5]

In short, a series of textual signals distinguishes the provisions at issue in this case from Zadvydas\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa71231(a)(6). While Zadvydas found \xc2\xa71231(a)(6) to be ambiguous, the same cannot be said of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2): Both provisions mandate detention until a certain point and authorize release prior to that point only under limited circumstances. As a result, neither provision can reasonably be read to limit detention to six months.

2

In this Court, respondents advance an interpretation of the language of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2) that was never made below, namely, that the term \xe2\x80\x9cfor,\xe2\x80\x9d which appears in both provisions, mandates detention only until the start of applicable proceedings rather than all the way through to their conclusion. Respondents contrast the language of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2) authorizing detention \xe2\x80\x9cfor\xe2\x80\x9d further proceedings with another provision\xe2\x80\x99s authorization of detention \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d further proceedings. See8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(iii)(IV) (\xe2\x80\x9cAny alien .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be detained pending a final determination of credible fear of persecution and, if found not to have such a fear, until removed\xe2\x80\x9d). According to respondents, that distinction between \xe2\x80\x9cfor\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d makes an enormous difference. As they see things, the word \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d authorizes detention throughout subsequent proceedings, but the term \xe2\x80\x9cfor\xe2\x80\x9d means that detention authority ends once subsequent proceedings begin. As a result, respondents argue, once the applicable proceedings commence, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2) no longer authorize detention, and the Government must instead look to \xc2\xa71226(a) for continued detention authority.

That interpretation is inconsistent with ordinary English usage and is incompatible with the rest of the statute. To be sure, \xe2\x80\x9cfor\xe2\x80\x9d can sometimes mean \xe2\x80\x9cin preparation for or anticipation of.\xe2\x80\x9d 6 Oxford English Dictionary 24 (2d ed. 1989). But \xe2\x80\x9cfor\xe2\x80\x9d can also mean \xe2\x80\x9c[d]uring [or] throughout,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 26, as well as \xe2\x80\x9cwith the object or purpose of,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 23; see also American Heritage Dictionary 709 (3d ed. 1992) (\xe2\x80\x9cUsed to indicate the object, aim, or purpose of an action or activity\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9cUsed to indicate amount, extent, or duration\xe2\x80\x9d); Random House Dictionary of the English Language 747 (2d ed. 1987) (\xe2\x80\x9cwith the object or purpose of\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9cduring the continuance of\xe2\x80\x9d); Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 886 (1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cwith the purpose or object of\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9cto the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. duration of\xe2\x80\x9d). And here, only that second set of definitions makes sense in the context of the statutory scheme as a whole.

For example, respondents argue that, once detention authority ends under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2), aliens can be detained only under \xc2\xa71226(a). But that section authorizes detention only \xe2\x80\x9c[o]n a warrant issued\xe2\x80\x9d by the Attorney General leading to the alien\xe2\x80\x99s arrest. \xc2\xa71226(a). If respondents\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of \xc2\xa71225(b) were correct, then the Government could detain an alien without a warrant at the border, but once removal proceedings began, the Attorney General would have to issue an arrest warrant in order to continue detaining the alien. To put it lightly, that makes little sense.

Nor does respondents\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of the word \xe2\x80\x9cfor\xe2\x80\x9d align with the way Congress has historically used that word in \xc2\xa71225. Consider that section\xe2\x80\x99s text prior to the enactment of the Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996,110Stat.3009\xe2\x80\x93546. Under the older version of \xc2\xa71225(b), \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very alien\xe2\x80\x9d within its scope \xe2\x80\x9cwho may not appear .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to be clearly and beyond a doubt entitled to [entry] shall be detained for further inquiry to be conducted by a special inquiry officer.\xe2\x80\x9d8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71225(b) (1994 ed.). It would make no sense to read \xe2\x80\x9cfor further inquiry\xe2\x80\x9d as authorizing detention of the alien only until the start of the inquiry; Congress obviously did not mean to allow aliens to feel free to leave once immigration officers asked their first question.

In sum, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2) mandate detention of aliens throughout the completion of applicable proceedings and not just until the moment those proceedings begin. Of course, other provisions of the immigration statutes do authorize detention \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d other proceedings or \xe2\x80\x9cuntil\xe2\x80\x9d a certain point. See post, at 22\xe2\x80\x9323 (Breyer, J., dissenting) (quoting \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(iii)(IV)). But there is no \xe2\x80\x9ccanon of interpretation that forbids interpreting different words used in different parts of the same statute to mean roughly the same thing.\xe2\x80\x9d Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc.,568 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519,540 (2013). We decline to invent and apply such a canon here.

B

While the language of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1) and (b)(2) is quite clear, \xc2\xa71226(c) is even clearer. As noted, \xc2\xa71226 applies to aliens already present in the United States. Section 1226(a) creates a default rule for those aliens by permitting\xe2\x80\x94but not requiring\xe2\x80\x94the Attorney General to issue warrants for their arrest and detention pending removal proceedings. Section 1226(a) also permits the Attorney General to release those aliens on bond, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcept as provided in subsection (c) of this section.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 1226(c) in turn states that the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cshall take into custody any alien\xe2\x80\x9d who falls into one of the enumerated categories involving criminal offenses and terrorist activities.8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71226(c)(1). Section 1226(c) then goes on to specify that the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cmay release\xe2\x80\x9d one of those aliens \xe2\x80\x9conly if the Attorney General decides\xe2\x80\x9d both that doing so is necessary for witness-protection purposes and that the alien will not pose a danger or flight risk. \xc2\xa71226(c)(2) (emphasis added).

Like \xc2\xa71225(b), \xc2\xa71226(c) does not on its face limit the length of the detention it authorizes. In fact, by allowing aliens to be released \xe2\x80\x9conly if\xe2\x80\x9d the Attorney General decides that certain conditions are met, \xc2\xa71226(c) reinforces the conclusion that aliens detained under its authority are not entitled to be released under any circumstances other than those expressly recognized by the statute. And together with \xc2\xa71226(a), \xc2\xa71226(c) makes clear that detention of aliens within its scope must continue \xe2\x80\x9cpending a decision on whether the alien is to be removed from the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71226(a).

In a reprise of their interpretation of \xc2\xa71225(b), respondents argue, and the Court of Appeals held, that \xc2\xa71226(c) should be interpreted to include an implicit 6-month time limit on the length of mandatory detention. Once again, that interpretation falls far short of a \xe2\x80\x9cplausible statutory construction.\xe2\x80\x9d

In defense of their statutory reading, respondents first argue that \xc2\xa71226(c)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csilence\xe2\x80\x9d as to the length of detention \xe2\x80\x9ccannot be construed to authorize prolonged mandatory detention, because Congress must use \xe2\x80\x98clearer terms\xe2\x80\x99 to authorize \xe2\x80\x98long-term detention.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 34 (quoting Zadvydas, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 697). But \xc2\xa71226(c) is not \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d as to the length of detention. It mandates detention \xe2\x80\x9cpending a decision on whether the alien is to be removed from the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71226(a), and it expressly prohibits release from that detention except for narrow, witness-protection purposes. Even if courts were permitted to fashion 6-month time limits out of statutory silence, they certainly may not transmute existing statutory language into its polar opposite. The constitutional-avoidance canon does not countenance such textual alchemy.

Indeed, we have held as much in connection with \xc2\xa71226(c) itself. In Demore v. Kim, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 529, we distinguished \xc2\xa71226(c) from the statutory provision in Zadvydas by pointing out that detention under \xc2\xa71226(c) has \xe2\x80\x9ca definite termination point\xe2\x80\x9d: the conclusion of removal proceedings. As we made clear there, that \xe2\x80\x9cdefinite termination point\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and not some arbitrary time limit devised by courts\xe2\x80\x94marks the end of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s detention authority under \xc2\xa71226(c).

Respondents next contend that \xc2\xa71226(c)\xe2\x80\x99s limited authorization for release for witness-protection purposes does not imply that other forms of release are forbidden, but this argument defies the statutory text. By expressly stating that the covered aliens may be released \xe2\x80\x9conly if\xe2\x80\x9d certain conditions are met,8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71226(c)(2), the statute expressly and unequivocally imposes an affirmative prohibition on releasing detained aliens under any other conditions.

Finally, respondents point to a provision enacted as part of the PATRIOT Act[6] and contend that their reading of \xc2\xa71226(c) is needed to prevent that provision from being superfluous. That argument, however, misreads both statutory provisions. Although the two provisions overlap in part, they are by no means congruent.

Two differences stand out. First, \xc2\xa71226(c) and the PATRIOT Act cover different categories of aliens. Both apply to certain terrorist suspects, but only \xc2\xa71226(c) reaches aliens convicted of other more common criminal offenses. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71226(c)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x93(C) (aliens inadmissible or deportable under \xc2\xa71182(a)(2); \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(A)(ii), (A)(iii), (B), (C), and (D); and \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(A)(i) under certain conditions). For its part, the PATRIOT Act casts a wider net than \xc2\xa71226(c) insofar as it encompasses certain threats to national security not covered by \xc2\xa71226(c). See \xc2\xa71226a(a)(3) (aliens described in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71182(a)(3)(A)(i), (iii), and 1227(a)(4)(A)(i), (iii), as well as aliens \xe2\x80\x9cengaged in any other activity that endangers the national security of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d). In addition, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s detention authority under \xc2\xa71226(c) and the PATRIOT Act is not the same. Under \xc2\xa71226(c), the Government must detain an alien until \xe2\x80\x9ca decision on whether the alien is to be removed\xe2\x80\x9d is made. \xc2\xa71226(a) (emphasis added). But, subject to exceptions not relevant here, the PATRIOT Act authorizes the Government to detain an alien \xe2\x80\x9cuntil the alien is removed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71226a(a)(2) (emphasis added).

Far from being redundant, then, \xc2\xa71226(c) and the PATRIOT Act apply to different categories of aliens in different ways. There is thus no reason to depart from the plain meaning of \xc2\xa71226(c) in order to avoid making the provision superfluous.

We hold that \xc2\xa71226(c) mandates detention of any alien falling within its scope and that detention may end prior to the conclusion of removal proceedings \xe2\x80\x9conly if\xe2\x80\x9d the alien is released for witness-protection purposes.

C

Finally, as noted, \xc2\xa71226(a) authorizes the Attorney General to arrest and detain an alien \xe2\x80\x9cpending a decision on whether the alien is to be removed from the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71226(a). As long as the detained alien is not covered by \xc2\xa71226(c), the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cmay release\xe2\x80\x9d the alien on \xe2\x80\x9cbond .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or conditional parole.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71226(a). Federal regulations provide that aliens detained under \xc2\xa71226(a) receive bond hearings at the outset of detention. See 8 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7236.1(d)(1), 1236.1(d)(1).

The Court of Appeals ordered the Government to provide procedural protections that go well beyond the initial bond hearing established by existing regulations\xe2\x80\x94namely, periodic bond hearings every six months in which the Attorney General must prove by clear and convincing evidence that the alien\xe2\x80\x99s continued detention is necessary. Nothing in \xc2\xa71226(a)\xe2\x80\x99s text\xe2\x80\x94which says only that the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cmay release\xe2\x80\x9d the alien \xe2\x80\x9con .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. bond\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94even remotely supports the imposition of either of those requirements. Nor does \xc2\xa71226(a)\xe2\x80\x99s text even hint that the length of detention prior to a bond hearing must specifically be considered in determining whether the alien should be released.

IV

For these reasons, the meaning of the relevant statutory provisions is clear\xe2\x80\x94and clearly contrary to the decision of the Court of Appeals. But the dissent is undeterred. It begins by ignoring the statutory language for as long as possible, devoting the first two-thirds of its opinion to a disquisition on the Constitution. Only after a 19-page prologue does the dissent acknowledge the relevant statutory provisions.

The dissent frames the question of interpretation as follows: Can \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b), 1226(c), and 1226(a) be read to require bond hearings every six months \xe2\x80\x9cwithout doing violence to the statutory language,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 20 (opinion of Breyer, J.)? According to the dissent, the answer is \xe2\x80\x9cyes,\xe2\x80\x9d but the dissent evidently has a strong stomach when it comes to inflicting linguistic trauma. Thus, when Congress mandated that an \xe2\x80\x9calien shall be detained,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(ii), what Congress really meant, the dissent insists, is that the alien may be released from custody provided only that his freedom of movement is restricted in some way, such as by \xe2\x80\x9cthe imposition of a curfew,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 21. And when Congress stressed that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Attorney General may release an alien .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. only if .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. release .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. from custody is necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to protect the safety of a witness, \xc2\xa71226(c)(2) (emphasis added), what Congress meant, the dissent tells us, is that the Attorney General must release an alien even when no witness is in need of protection\xe2\x80\x94so long as the alien is neither a flight risk nor a danger to the community, see post, at 25\xe2\x80\x9327. The contortions needed to reach these remarkable conclusions are a sight to behold.

Let us start with the simple term \xe2\x80\x9cdetain.\xe2\x80\x9d According to the dissent, \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d means the absence of \xe2\x80\x9cunrestrained freedom.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 21. An alien who is subject to any one of \xe2\x80\x9cnumerous restraints\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94including \xe2\x80\x9ca requirement to obtain medical treatment,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cto report at regular intervals,\xe2\x80\x9d or even simply to comply with \xe2\x80\x9ca curfew\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94is \xe2\x80\x9cdetained\xe2\x80\x9d in the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s eyes, even if that alien is otherwise free to roam the streets. Ibid.

This interpretation defies ordinary English usage. The dictionary cited by the dissent, the Oxford English Dictionary (OED), defines \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d as follows: \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o keep in confinement or under restraint; to keep prisoner.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 OED 543 (2d ed. 1989) (emphasis added); see also OED (3d ed. 2012), http://www.oed.com/view/Entry/51176 (same). Other general-purpose dictionaries provide similar definitions. See, e.g., Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 616 (1961) (\xe2\x80\x9cto hold or keep in or as if in custody \xe2\x80\x9d); Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 710 (2d ed. 1934) (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]o hold or keep as in custody\xe2\x80\x9d); American Heritage Dictionary 508 (def. 2) (3d ed. 1992) (\xe2\x80\x9cTo keep in custody or temporary confinement\xe2\x80\x9d); Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New World College Dictionary 375 (3d ed. 1997) (\xe2\x80\x9cto keep in custody; confine\xe2\x80\x9d). And legal dictionaries define \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d the same way. See, e.g., Ballentine\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 343 (3d ed. 1969) (\xe2\x80\x9cTo hold; to keep in custody; to keep\xe2\x80\x9d); Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 459 (7th ed. 1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe act or fact of holding a person in custody; confinement or compulsory delay\xe2\x80\x9d).

How does the dissent attempt to evade the clear meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d? It resorts to the legal equivalent of a sleight-of-hand trick. First, the dissent cites a passage in Blackstone stating that arrestees could always seek release on bail. Post, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. Then, having established the obvious point that a person who is initially detained may later be released from detention, the dissent reasons that this means that a person may still be regarded as detained even after he is released from custody. Post, at 21. That, of course, is a nonsequitur. Just because a person who is initially detained may later be released, it does not follow that the person is still \xe2\x80\x9cdetained\xe2\x80\x9d after his period of detention comes to an end.

If there were any doubt about the meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d in the relevant statutory provisions, the context in which they appear would put that doubt to rest. Title 8 of the United States Code, the title dealing with immigration, is replete with references that distinguish between \xe2\x80\x9cdetained\xe2\x80\x9d aliens and aliens who are free to walk the streets in the way the dissent imagines. Section 1226(a), for instance, distinguishes between the power to \xe2\x80\x9ccontinue to detain the arrested alien\xe2\x80\x9d and the power to \xe2\x80\x9crelease the alien on .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. bond.\xe2\x80\x9d But if the dissent were right, that distinction would make no sense: An \xe2\x80\x9calien released on bond\xe2\x80\x9d would also be a \xe2\x80\x9cdetained alien.\xe2\x80\x9d Here is another example: In \xc2\xa71226(b), Congress gave the Attorney General the power to \xe2\x80\x9crevoke\xe2\x80\x9d at any time \xe2\x80\x9ca bond or parole authorized under subsection (a) of this section, rearrest the alien under the original warrant, and detain the alien.\xe2\x80\x9d It beggars belief that Congress would have given the Attorney General the power to detain a class of aliens who, under the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reading, are already \xe2\x80\x9cdetained\xe2\x80\x9d because they are free on bond. But that is what the dissent would have us believe. Consider, finally, the example of \xc2\xa71226(c). As noted, that provision obligates the Attorney General to \xe2\x80\x9ctake into custody\xe2\x80\x9d certain aliens whenever they are \xe2\x80\x9creleased, without regard to whether the alien is released on parole, supervised release, or probation.\xe2\x80\x9d On the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view, however, even aliens \xe2\x80\x9creleased on parole, supervised release, or probation\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9cin custody\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and so there would be no need for the Attorney General to take them into custody again.[7]

Struggling to prop up its implausible interpretation, the dissent looks to our prior decisions for aid, but that too fails. The best case it can find is Tod v. Waldman,266 U.\xc2\xa0S. 547 (1925), a grant of a petition for rehearing in which the Court clarified that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in [its original] order .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall prejudice an application for release on bail of the respondents pending compliance with the mandate of this Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 548. According to the dissent, that two-page decision from almost a century ago supports its reading because the underlying immigration statute in that case\xe2\x80\x94like some of the provisions at issue here\xe2\x80\x94mandated that the relevant class of aliens \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98shall be detained\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d pending the outcome of an inspection process. See post, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322 (quoting Act of Feb. 5, 1917, \xc2\xa716,39Stat.886).

That reads far too much into Waldman. To start, the Court did not state that the aliens at issue were entitled to bail or even that bail was available to them. Instead, the Court merely noted that its decision should not \xe2\x80\x9cprejudice\xe2\x80\x9d any application the aliens might choose to file. That is notable, for in their petition for rehearing the aliens had asked the Court to affirmatively \xe2\x80\x9cauthorize [them] to give bail.\xe2\x80\x9d Petition for Rehearing in Tod v. Waldman, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1924, No. 95, p. 17 (emphasis added). By refusing to do so, the Court may have been signaling its skepticism about their request. But it is impossible to tell. That is precisely why we, unlike the dissent, choose not to go beyond what the sentence actually says. And Waldman says nothing about how the word \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d should be read in the context of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b), 1226(c), and 1226(a).[8]

Neither does Zadvydas. It is true, as the dissent points out, that Zadvydas found \xe2\x80\x9cthat the words \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cmay be detained\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 [are] consistent with requiring release from long-term detention,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 23 (quoting 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 682), but that is not because there is any ambiguity in the term \xe2\x80\x9cdetain.\xe2\x80\x9d As we have explained, the key statutory provision in Zadvydas said that the aliens in question \xe2\x80\x9cmay,\xe2\x80\x9d not \xe2\x80\x9cshall,\xe2\x80\x9d be detained, and that provision also failed to specify how long detention was to last. Here, the statutory provisions at issue state either that the covered aliens \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d be detained until specified events take place, see8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(ii) (\xe2\x80\x9cfurther consideration of the application for asylum\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa71225(b)(2)(A) (\xe2\x80\x9ca [removal] proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d), or provide that the covered aliens may be released \xe2\x80\x9conly if\xe2\x80\x9d specified conditions are met, \xc2\xa71226(c)(2). The term that the Zadvydas Court found to be ambiguous was \xe2\x80\x9cmay,\xe2\x80\x9d not \xe2\x80\x9cdetain.\xe2\x80\x9d See 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 697. And the opinion in that case consistently used the words \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccustody\xe2\x80\x9d to refer exclusively to physical confinement and restraint. See id., at 690 (referring to \xe2\x80\x9c[f]reedom from imprisonment\xe2\x80\x94from government custody, detention, or other forms of physical restraint\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); id., at 683 (contrasting aliens \xe2\x80\x9creleased on bond\xe2\x80\x9d with those \xe2\x80\x9cheld in custody\xe2\x80\x9d).[9]

The dissent offers no plausible interpretation of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b), 1226(c), and 1226(a). But even if we were to accept the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation and hold that \xe2\x80\x9cdetained\xe2\x80\x9d aliens in the \xe2\x80\x9ccustody\xe2\x80\x9d of the Government include aliens released on bond, that would still not justify the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s proposed resolution of this case. The Court of Appeals held that aliens detained under the provisions at issue must be given periodic bond hearings, and the dissent agrees. See post, at 2 (\xe2\x80\x9cI would interpret the statute as requiring bail hearings, presumptively after six months of confinement\xe2\x80\x9d). But the dissent draws that 6-month limitation out of thin air. However broad its interpretation of the words \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccustody,\xe2\x80\x9d nothing in any of the relevant provisions imposes a 6-month time limit on detention without the possibility of bail. So if the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is right, then aliens detained under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b), 1226(c), and 1226(a) are entitled to bail hearings as soon as their detention begins rather than six months later. \xe2\x80\x9cDetained\xe2\x80\x9d does not mean \xe2\x80\x9creleased on bond,\xe2\x80\x9d and it certainly does not mean \xe2\x80\x9creleased on bond but only after six months of mandatory physical confinement.\xe2\x80\x9d

The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s utterly implausible interpretation of the statutory language cannot support the decision of the court below.

V

Because the Court of Appeals erroneously concluded that periodic bond hearings are required under the immigration provisions at issue here, it had no occasion to consider respondents\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional arguments on their merits. Consistent with our role as \xe2\x80\x9ca court of review, not of first view,\xe2\x80\x9d Cutter v. Wilkinson,544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 709, n.\xc2\xa07 (2005), we do not reach those arguments. Instead, we remand the case to the Court of Appeals to consider them in the first instance.

Before the Court of Appeals addresses those claims, however, it should reexamine whether respondents can continue litigating their claims as a class. When the District Court certified the class under Rule 23(b)(2) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, it had their statutory challenge primarily in mind. Now that we have resolved that challenge, however, new questions emerge.

Specifically, the Court of Appeals should first decide whether it continues to have jurisdiction despite8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(f\xc2\xa0)(1). Under that provision, \xe2\x80\x9cno court (other than the Supreme Court) shall have jurisdiction or authority to enjoin or restrain the operation of [\xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71221\xe2\x80\x931232] other than with respect to the application of such provisions to an individual alien against whom proceedings under such part have been initiated.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 1252(f\xc2\xa0)(1) thus \xe2\x80\x9cprohibits federal courts from granting classwide injunctive relief against the operation of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71221\xe2\x80\x93123[2].\xe2\x80\x9d American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Comm., 525 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 481. The Court of Appeals held that this provision did not affect its jurisdiction over respondents\xe2\x80\x99 statutory claims because those claims did not \xe2\x80\x9cseek to enjoin the operation of the immigration detention statutes, but to enjoin conduct .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not authorized by the statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d 591 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1120. This reasoning does not seem to apply to an order granting relief on constitutional grounds, and therefore the Court of Appeals should consider on remand whether it may issue classwide injunctive relief based on respondents\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims. If not, and if the Court of Appeals concludes that it may issue only declaratory relief, then the Court of Appeals should decide whether that remedy can sustain the class on its own. See, e.\xc2\xa0g., Rule 23(b)(2) (requiring \xe2\x80\x9cthat final injunctive relief or corresponding declaratory relief [be] appropriate respecting the class as a whole\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

The Court of Appeals should also consider whether a Rule 23(b)(2) class action continues to be the appropriate vehicle for respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims in light of Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes,564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 338 (2011). We held in Dukes that \xe2\x80\x9cRule 23(b)(2) applies only when a single injunction or declaratory judgment would provide relief to each member of the class.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 360. That holding may be relevant on remand because the Court of Appeals has already acknowledged that some members of the certified class may not be entitled to bond hearings as a constitutional matter. See, e.\xc2\xa0g., 804 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1082; 715 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1139\xe2\x80\x931141 (citing, e.\xc2\xa0g., Shaughnessy v. United States ex rel. Mezei,345 U.\xc2\xa0S. 206 (1953)). Assuming that is correct, then it may no longer be true that the complained-of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98conduct is such that it can be enjoined or declared unlawful only as to all of the class members or as to none of them.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Dukes, supra, at 360 (quoting Nagareda, Class Certification in the Age of Aggregate Proof, 84 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0U.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 97, 132 (2009)).

Similarly, the Court of Appeals should also consider on remand whether a Rule 23(b)(2) class action litigated on common facts is an appropriate way to resolve respondents\xe2\x80\x99 Due Process Clause claims. \xe2\x80\x9c[D]ue process is flexible,\xe2\x80\x9d we have stressed repeatedly, and it \xe2\x80\x9ccalls for such procedural protections as the particular situation demands.\xe2\x80\x9d Morrissey v. Brewer,408 U.\xc2\xa0S. 471,481 (1972); see also Landon v. Plasencia,459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 21,34 (1982).

VI

We reverse the judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit and remand the case for further proceedings.

It is so ordered.

8 \xc2\xa0It should not be surprising by this point that even the aliens in Waldman understood \xe2\x80\x9cdetention\xe2\x80\x9d in contradistinction to \xe2\x80\x9cbail.\xe2\x80\x9d See Petition for Rehearing in Tod v. Waldman, O.\xc2\xa0T. 1924, No. 95, pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9318 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Court\xe2\x80\x99s mandate should authorize relators to give bail, instead of having [them] go to Ellis Island and remain there in custody pending an appeal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. which may involve very long detention pending hearing of the appeal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d (capitalization omitted and emphasis added)).
9 \xc2\xa0The dissent argues that because \xe2\x80\x9cthe question at issue [in Zadvydas] was release from detention,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe key word was consequently \xe2\x80\x98may.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 23. If, as the dissent admits, Zadvydas was about \xe2\x80\x9crelease from detention\xe2\x80\x9d and not about what qualifies as \xe2\x80\x9cdetention,\xe2\x80\x9d then it is unclear why the dissent thinks that decision supports its unorthodox interpretation of the word \xe2\x80\x9cdetention.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

In my view, no court has jurisdiction over this case. Congress has prohibited courts from reviewing aliens\xe2\x80\x99 claims related to their removal, except in a petition for review from a final removal order or in other circumstances not present here. See\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(b)(9). Respond- ents have not brought their claims in that posture, so \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) removes jurisdiction over their challenge to their detention. I would therefore vacate the judgment below with instructions to dismiss for lack of jurisdiction. But because a majority of the Court believes we have jurisdiction, and I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of the merits, I join Part I and Parts III\xe2\x80\x93VI of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

\n

I

\n

Respondents are a class of aliens whose removal proceedings are ongoing. Respondents allege that the statutes that authorize their detention during removal proceedings do not authorize \xe2\x80\x9cprolonged\xe2\x80\x9d detention unless they are given an individualized bond hearing at which the Government \xe2\x80\x9cprove[s] by clear and convincing evidence\xe2\x80\x9d that their detention remains justified. Third Amended Complaint in Rodriguez v. Holder, No. CV 07\xe2\x80\x9303239 (CD Cal., Oct. 22, 2010), pp. 30\xe2\x80\x9331 (Third Amended Complaint). If the statutes do authorize \xe2\x80\x9cprolonged\xe2\x80\x9d detention, respondents claim that the statutes violate the Due Process Clause of the\n \nFifth Amendment. Ibid. In their complaint, respondents sought declaratory and injunctive relief from detention during their removal proceedings. Id., at 31\xe2\x80\x9332. The District Court certified a class of aliens under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(2) who, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9care or were detained for longer than six months pursuant to one of the general immigration detention statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Class Certification Order in Rodriguez v. Holder, No. CV 07\xe2\x80\x9303239 (CD Cal., Apr. 5, 2010), p. 2; Rodriguez v. Hayes, 591 F.\xc2\xa03d 1105, 1122\xe2\x80\x931126 (CA9 2010). After the parties moved for summary judgment, the District Court entered a permanent injunction in favor of the class, which requires the named Government officials[1] to take steps to \xe2\x80\x9ctimely identify all current and future class members,\xe2\x80\x9d to update class member lists with the District Court every 90 days, and to provide class members with bond hearings that comply with particular substantive and procedural requirements. Order, Judgment, and Permanent Injunction in Rodriguez v. Holder, No. CV 07\xe2\x80\x9303239 (CD Cal., Aug. 6, 2013), pp. 5\xe2\x80\x936 (Order, Judgment, and Permanent Injunction).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

Although neither party raises \xc2\xa71252(b)(9), this Court has an \xe2\x80\x9cindependent obligation\xe2\x80\x9d to assess whether it deprives us and the lower courts of jurisdiction. Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp.,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 500,\n514 (2006)\n. This Court has described \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98zipper\xe2\x80\x99 clause.\xe2\x80\x9d See Reno v. American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Comm.,\n\n525 U.\xc2\xa0S. 471,\n483 (1999)\n (AADC); INS v. St. Cyr,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 289,\n313 (2001)\n. That description is apt because, when an alien raises a claim related to his removal, \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) closes all but two avenues for judicial review:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cConsolidation of questions for judicial review

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cJudicial review of all questions of law and fact, including interpretation and application of constitu- tional and statutory provisions, arising from any action taken or proceeding brought to remove an alien from the United States under [8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71151\xe2\x80\x931382] shall be available only in judicial review of a final order under this section. Except as otherwise provided in this section, no court shall have jurisdiction, by habeas corpus under section 2241 of title 28 or any other habeas corpus provision, by section 1361 or 1651 of such title, or by any other provision of law (statutory or nonstatutory), to review such an order or such questions of law or fact.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.)

\n

The text of this provision is clear. Courts generally lack jurisdiction over \xe2\x80\x9call questions of law and fact,\xe2\x80\x9d both \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory,\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9caris[e] from\xe2\x80\x9d an \xe2\x80\x9caction taken or proceeding brought to remove an alien.\xe2\x80\x9d If an alien raises a claim arising from such an action or proceeding, courts cannot review it unless they are reviewing \xe2\x80\x9ca final order\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa71252(a)(1) or exercising jurisdiction \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise provided\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71252.[2] Neither \xe2\x80\x9chabeas corpus\xe2\x80\x9d nor \xe2\x80\x9cany other provision of law\xe2\x80\x9d can be used to avoid \xc2\xa71252(b)(9)\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional bar. In short, if a claim arises from an action taken to remove an alien, \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) permits judicial review in only two circumstances: in connection with review of a final removal order and via a specific grant of jurisdiction in \xc2\xa71252.

\n

Respondents do not argue that any specific grant of jurisdiction applies here, and they do not seek review of a final removal order under \xc2\xa71252(a)(1). Thus, a court may review respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims only if they can show that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9)\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional bar does not apply in the first place because their claims do not \xe2\x80\x9caris[e] from any action taken or proceeding brought to remove an alien.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Respondents cannot make that showing. Section 1252(b)(9) is a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral jurisdictional limitation\xe2\x80\x9d that applies to \xe2\x80\x9call claims arising from deportation proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cmany .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. decisions or actions that may be part of the deportation process.\xe2\x80\x9d AADC, supra, at 482\xe2\x80\x93483. Detaining an alien falls within this definition\xe2\x80\x94indeed, this Court has described detention during removal proceedings as an \xe2\x80\x9caspect of the deportation process.\xe2\x80\x9d Demore v. Kim,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510,\n523 (2003)\n; see also Carlson v. Landon,\n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 524,\n538 (1952)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cDetention is necessarily a part of [the] deportation procedure\xe2\x80\x9d). As the Court explains today, Congress either mandates or permits the detention of aliens for the entire duration of their removal proceedings. See ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9323. This detention, the Court further explains, is meant to ensure that the Government can ultimately remove them. See ante, at 1; accord, Demore, supra, at 528 (explaining that detention during removal proceedings \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily serves the purpose of preventing deportable criminal aliens from fleeing prior to or during their removal proceedings, thus increasing the chance that, if ordered removed, the aliens will be successfully removed\xe2\x80\x9d). The phrase \xe2\x80\x9cany action taken . . . to remove an alien from the United States\xe2\x80\x9d must at least cover congressionally authorized portions of the deportation process that necessarily serve the purpose of ensuring an alien\xe2\x80\x99s removal. Claims challenging detention during removal proceedings thus fall within the heartland of \xc2\xa71252(b)(9).

\n

B

\n

The plurality, the dissent, and respondents each offer reasons why \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) does not apply to this case. The plurality reasons that applying \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) to detention claims requires an overly expansive reading of \xe2\x80\x9carising from.\xe2\x80\x9d See ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. The dissent contends that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) applies only to challenges to the removal order itself. Post, at 31. And respondents argue that, if \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) applies to their claims, they will have no meaningful way to challenge their detention during their removal proceedings.[3] Tr. of Oral Arg. 36. None of these arguments persuades me.

\n

1

\n

The plurality asserts that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) covers respond- ents\xe2\x80\x99 claims only if the words \xe2\x80\x9carising from\xe2\x80\x9d are given an \xe2\x80\x9cexpansive interpretation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. I am of a different view. Even if \xe2\x80\x9carising from\xe2\x80\x9d is read narrowly, \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) still covers the claims at issue in this case. That is because detention is an \xe2\x80\x9caction taken .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to remove\xe2\x80\x9d an alien. And even the narrowest reading of \xe2\x80\x9carising from\xe2\x80\x9d must cover claims that directly challenge such actions. See AADC, 525 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 482\xe2\x80\x93483.

\n

The main precedent that the plurality cites to support its narrow reading of \xe2\x80\x9carising from\xe2\x80\x9d demonstrates that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) applies here. See ante, at 10 (citing AADC, 525 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 482\xe2\x80\x93483). In AADC, the Court explained that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) covers \xe2\x80\x9call claims arising from deportation proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cmany .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. decisions or actions that may be part of the deportation process.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court even listed examples of the type of claims that would be covered, including challenges to the decision \xe2\x80\x9cto open an investigation\xe2\x80\x9d and the decision \xe2\x80\x9cto surveil the suspected [immigration-law] violator.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 482. If surveilling a suspected violator falls under the statute, then the detention of a known violator certainly does as well.

\n

The plurality dismisses my \xe2\x80\x9cexpansive interpretation\xe2\x80\x9d because it would lead to \xe2\x80\x9cstaggering results,\xe2\x80\x9d supposedly barring claims that are far afield from removal. See ante, at 9 (describing lawsuits challenging inhumane conditions of confinement, assaults, and negligent driving). But that is not the case. Unlike detention during removal proceedings, those actions are neither congressionally authorized nor meant to ensure that an alien can be removed. Thus, my conclusion that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) covers an alien\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to the fact of his detention (an action taken in pursuit of the lawful objective of removal) says nothing about whether it also covers claims about inhumane treatment, as- saults, or negligently inflicted injuries suffered during detention (actions that go beyond the Government\xe2\x80\x99s lawful pursuit of its removal objective). Cf. Bell v. Wolfish,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 520\n\xe2\x80\x93539 (1979) (drawing a similar distinction).

\n

2

\n

The dissent takes a different approach. Relying on the prefatory clause to \xc2\xa71252(b), it asserts that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) \xe2\x80\x9cby its terms applies only \xe2\x80\x98[w]ith respect to review of an order of removal under [\xc2\xa71252(a)(1)].\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 31 (quoting\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(b)). The dissent reads the prefatory clause to mean that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) applies only to a \xe2\x80\x9cchallenge [to] an order of removal.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 31. That reading is incorrect.

\n

Section 1252(b)(9) is not restricted to challenges to removal orders. The text refers to review of \xe2\x80\x9call questions of law and fact\xe2\x80\x9d arising from removal, not just removal orders. (Emphasis added.) And it specifies that \xc2\xa71252(a)(1) provides the only means for reviewing \xe2\x80\x9csuch an order or such questions of law or fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). The term \xe2\x80\x9cor\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98almost always disjunctive, that is, the words it connects are to be given separate meanings.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Loughrin v. United States, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 6) (quoting United States v. Woods,\n\n571 U.\xc2\xa0S. 31\n\xe2\x80\x9346 (2013)). By interpreting \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) as governing only removal orders, the dissent reads \xe2\x80\x9cor such questions of law or fact\xe2\x80\x9d out of the statute. It also renders superfluous \xc2\xa71252(a)(5), which already specifies that the review made available under \xc2\xa71252(a)(1) \xe2\x80\x9cshall be the sole and exclusive means for judicial review of an order of removal.\xe2\x80\x9d This Court typically disfavors such interpretations. See AADC, supra, at 483.

\n

The prefatory clause of \xc2\xa71252(b) does not change the meaning of \xc2\xa71252(b)(9). The prefatory clause states that the subparagraphs of \xc2\xa71252(b), including \xc2\xa71252(b)(9), impose requirements \xe2\x80\x9c[w]ith respect to review of an order of removal under subsection (a)(1).\xe2\x80\x9d The phrase \xe2\x80\x9cwith respect to\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9creferring to,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cconcerning,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9crelating to.\xe2\x80\x9d Oxford American Dictionary and Language Guide 853 (1999 ed.); accord, Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New Universal Unabridged Dictionary 1640 (2003 ed.); American Heritage Dictionary 1485 (4th ed. 2000). Read together, the prefatory clause and \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) mean that review of all questions arising from removal must occur in connection with review of a final removal order under \xc2\xa71252(a)(1), which makes sense given that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) is meant to \xe2\x80\x9c[c]onsolidat[e] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. questions for judicial review.\xe2\x80\x9d Tellingly, on the two previous occasions when this Court interpreted \xc2\xa71252(b)(9), it did not understand \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) as limited to challenges to removal orders. See AADC, supra, at 482\xe2\x80\x93483 (stating that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) is a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral jurisdictional limitation\xe2\x80\x9d that applies to \xe2\x80\x9call claims arising from deportation proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe many .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. decisions or actions that may be part of the deportation process\xe2\x80\x9d); St. Cyr, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 313, n.\xc2\xa037 (clarifying that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) requires \xe2\x80\x9cclaims that were viewed as being outside of a \xe2\x80\x98final order\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to be \xe2\x80\x9cconsolidated in a petition for review and considered by the courts of appeals\xe2\x80\x9d in their review of the final removal order under \xc2\xa71252(a)(1)). Thus, despite the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s assertion to the contrary, the prefatory clause plainly does not change the scope of \xc2\xa71252(b)(9), which covers \xe2\x80\x9call questions of law or fact\xe2\x80\x9d arising from the removal process.

\n

3

\n

At oral argument, respondents asserted that, if \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) bars their lawsuit, then the only review available would be \xe2\x80\x9ca petition for review of [a] final removal order\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa71252(a)(1), which takes place \xe2\x80\x9cafter all the detention has already happened.\xe2\x80\x9d[4] Tr. of Oral Arg. 36. I interpret respondents\xe2\x80\x99 argument as a claim that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) would be unconstitutional if it precluded meaningful review of their detention. This argument is unpersuasive and foreclosed by precedent.

\n

The Constitution does not guarantee litigants the most effective means of judicial review for every type of claim they want to raise. See AADC, 525 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 487\xe2\x80\x93492 (rejecting a similar argument); Heikkila v. Barber,\n\n345 U.\xc2\xa0S. 229,\n237 (1953)\n (explaining that limitations on judicial review of deportation must be followed \xe2\x80\x9cdespite [their] apparent inconvenience to the alien\xe2\x80\x9d). This is especially true in the context of deportation, where limits on the courts\xe2\x80\x99 jurisdiction have existed for almost as long as federal immigration laws, and where this Court has repeatedly affirmed the constitutionality of those limits.[5]

\n

Indeed, this Court has already rejected essentially the same argument that respondents raise here. In AADC, the Court held that \xc2\xa71252(g), a provision similar to \xc2\xa71252(b)(9), barred the aliens\xe2\x80\x99 claim that the Government was violating the\n \nFirst Amendment by selectively enforc- ing the immigration laws against them. 525 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 487\xe2\x80\x93492. The aliens argued that constitutional avoidance required the Court to interpret \xc2\xa71252(g) as not applying to their claims because the only remaining avenue for review\xe2\x80\x94a petition for review of a final removal order under \xc2\xa71252(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x94would be \xe2\x80\x9cunavailing\xe2\x80\x9d and would \xe2\x80\x9ccome too late to prevent the \xe2\x80\x98chilling effect\xe2\x80\x99 upon their\n \nFirst Amendment rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 487\xe2\x80\x93488. The Court rejected this argument because \xe2\x80\x9can alien unlawfully in this country has no constitutional right to assert selective enforcement as a defense against his deportation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 488. The Court further explained that it had a duty to enforce Congress\xe2\x80\x99 limitations on judicial review, except perhaps in \xe2\x80\x9ca rare case in which the alleged basis of discrimination is so outrageous that the foregoing considerations [\xc2\xa0justifying limited review could] be overcome.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 491.

\n

Like in AADC, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 lack-of-meaningful-review argument does not allow us to ignore the jurisdictional limitations that Congress has imposed. This Court has never held that detention during removal proceedings is unconstitutional. To the contrary, this Court has repeatedly recognized the constitutionality of that practice. See Demore, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 523 (explaining that detention is \xe2\x80\x9ca constitutionally valid aspect of the deportation process\xe2\x80\x9d); accord, Reno v. Flores,\n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 292\n\xe2\x80\x93306 (1993); Shaughnessy v. United States ex rel. Mezei,\n\n345 U.\xc2\xa0S. 206,\n215 (1953)\n; Carlson, 342 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 538, 542. Nor does this lawsuit qualify as the \xe2\x80\x9crare case in which the alleged [executive action] is so outrageous\xe2\x80\x9d that it could thwart the jurisdictional limitations in \xc2\xa71252(b)(9). AADC, supra, at 491. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s detention of respondents is entirely routine and indistinguishable from the detention that we have repeatedly upheld in the past. Thus, regardless of the inconvenience that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) might pose for respondents, this Court must enforce it as written. Respondents must raise their claims in petitions for review of their final removal orders.[6]

\n

III

\n

Because I conclude that \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) bars jurisdiction to hear respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims, I will also address whether its application to this case violates the Suspension Clause, see Art. I, \xc2\xa79, cl.\xc2\xa02 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Privilege of the Writ of Habeas Corpus shall not be suspended, unless when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it\xe2\x80\x9d). It does not. Even assuming the Suspension Clause bars Congress from stripping habeas jurisdiction over respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims, but see St. Cyr, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 337\xe2\x80\x93346 (Sca- lia, J., dissenting), this case does not involve a habeas petition.

\n

Respondents do not seek habeas relief, as understood by our precedents. Although their complaint references the general habeas statute, see Third Amended Complaint, at 1, it is not a habeas petition. The complaint does not request that the District Court issue any writ. See id., at 31\xe2\x80\x9332. Rather, it seeks a declaration and an injunction that would provide relief for both present and future class members, including future class members not yet detained. Ibid. Indeed, respondents obtained class certification under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(2), which applies only when the class seeks \xe2\x80\x9cfinal injunctive relief or corresponding declaratory relief.\xe2\x80\x9d[7]

\n

Nor did respondents obtain habeas relief. When their case concluded, respondents obtained a classwide permanent injunction. See Order, Judgment, and Permanent Injunction, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. That classwide injunction looks nothing like a typical writ. It is not styled in the form of a conditional or unconditional release order. Cf. United States v. Jung Ah Lung,\n\n124 U.\xc2\xa0S. 621,\n622 (1888)\n (describing habeas relief as \xe2\x80\x9corder[ing] the discharge from custody of the person in whose behalf the writ was sued out\xe2\x80\x9d); Chin Yow v. United States,\n\n208 U.\xc2\xa0S. 8,\n13 (1908)\n (awarding habeas relief by ordering the release of the alien if certain conditions were not satisfied). It applies to future class members, including individuals who were not in custody when the injunction was issued. Cf.\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72241(c) (generally precluding issuance of the writ unless the petitioner is \xe2\x80\x9cin custody\xe2\x80\x9d). And it is directed to at least one individual, the Director for the Executive Office for Immigration Review, who is not a custodian. Cf. Rumsfeld v. Padilla,\n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 426,\n434 (2004)\n (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cthe proper respondent to a habeas petition is \xe2\x80\x98the person who has custody over [the petitioner]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72242)).

\n

Immigration law has long drawn a distinction between the declaratory and injunctive relief that respondents sought here and habeas relief. In Heikkila, for instance, this Court distinguished habeas relief from \xe2\x80\x9cinjunctions, declaratory judgments and other types of relief\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ccourts ha[d] consistently rejected\xe2\x80\x9d in immigration cases. 345 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 230. The Court rejected the alien\xe2\x80\x99s request for \xe2\x80\x9cinjunctive and declaratory relief\xe2\x80\x9d because Congress had authorized courts to grant relief only in habeas proceedings. Id., at 230, 237. We reaffirmed this distinction in St. Cyr, where we noted that the 1961 Immigration and Nationality Act,\n\n75Stat.\n650, withdrew the district courts\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cauthority to grant declaratory and injunctive relief,\xe2\x80\x9d but not habeas relief. 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 309\xe2\x80\x93310; see also Shaughnessy v. Pedreiro,\n\n349 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48\n\xe2\x80\x9353 (1955) (holding that the Administrative Procedure Act, which authorizes courts to grant declaratory and injunctive relief, authorized \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial review of deportation orders other than by habeas corpus\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). And Congress has confirmed this distinction in its immigration statutes by allowing one form of relief, but not the other, in particular circumstances. Compare, e.g., \xc2\xa71252(e)(1) (prohibiting courts from granting \xe2\x80\x9cdeclaratory, injunctive, or other equitable relief in any action pertaining to an order to exclude an alien in accordance with section 1225(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x9d) with \xc2\xa71252(e)(2) (allowing \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial review .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in habeas corpus proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d of particular \xe2\x80\x9cdetermination[s] made under section 1225(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suit for declaratory and injunctive relief, in sum, is not a habeas petition. The Suspension Clause protects \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Privilege of the Writ of Habeas Corpus,\xe2\x80\x9d not requests for injunctive relief. Because respondents have not sought a writ of habeas corpus, applying \xc2\xa71252(b)(9) to bar their suit does not implicate the Suspension Clause.

But a majority of the Court has decided to exercise jurisdiction. Because I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s disposition of the merits, I concur in Part I and Parts III\xe2\x80\x93VI of its opinion.

\n

This case focuses upon three groups of noncitizens held in confinement. Each of these individuals believes he or she has the right to enter or to remain within the United States. The question is whether several statutory provisions of the Immigration and Nationality Act,\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101 et\xc2\xa0seq., forbid granting them bail.

\n

The noncitizens at issue are asylum seekers, persons who have finished serving a sentence of confinement (for a crime), or individuals who, while lacking a clear entitlement to enter the United States, claim to meet the criteria for admission, see infra, at 20, 25\xe2\x80\x9326, 29\xe2\x80\x9330. The Government has held all the members of the groups before us in confinement for many months, sometimes for years, while it looks into or contests their claims. But ultimately many members of these groups win their claims and the Government allows them to enter or to remain in the United States. Does the statute require members of these groups to receive a bail hearing, after, say, six months of confinement, with the possibility of release on bail into the community provided that they do not pose a risk of flight or a threat to the community\xe2\x80\x99s safety?

\n

The Court reads the statute as forbidding bail, hence forbidding a bail hearing, for these individuals. In my view, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the statute would likely render the statute unconstitutional. Thus, I would follow this Court\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding practice of construing a statute \xe2\x80\x9cso as to avoid not only the conclusion that it is unconstitutional but also grave doubts upon that score.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Jin Fuey Moy,\n\n241 U.\xc2\xa0S. 394,\n401 (1916)\n. And I would interpret the statute as requiring bail hearings, presumptively after six months of confinement. Cf. Zadvydas v. Davis,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678,\n701 (2001)\n.

\n

I

\n

The Respondents

\n

Because of their importance to my conclusion, I shall repeat, with references to record support, the key characteristics of the groups of noncitizens who appear before us.

\n

First, as I have said, the respondents in this case are members of three special classes of noncitizens, the most important of whom (1) arrive at our borders seeking asylum or (2) have committed crimes but have finished serving their sentences of imprisonment. We also consider those who (3) arrive at our borders believing they are entitled to enter the United States for reasons other than asylum seeking, but lack a clear entitlement to enter.

\n

Second, all members of the first group, the asylum seekers, have been found (by an immigration official) to have a \xe2\x80\x9ccredible fear of persecution\xe2\x80\x9d in their home coun-try should the United States deny them admittance.\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(ii). All members of the second group have, as I have said, finished serving their criminal sentences of confinement. \xc2\xa71226(c)(1). All members of the third group may have (or may simply believe they have) a strong claim for admittance, but they are neither \xe2\x80\x9cclearly and beyond a doubt entitled to be admitted\xe2\x80\x9d nor conclusively determined to be inadmissible by an immigration officer on grounds of fraud or lack of required documentation. \xc2\xa71225(b)(2)(A); see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(A)(i), 1182(a)(6)(C), (a)(7).

\n

Third, members of the first two classes number in the thousands. See Brief for 46 Social Science Researchers and Professors as Amici Curiae 6, 8 (identifying, in 2015, 7,500 asylum seekers and 12,220 noncitizens who have finished serving sentences of criminal confinement, a portion of whom are class members detained for more than six months).

\n

Fourth, detention is often lengthy. The classes before us consist of people who were detained for at least six months and on average one year. App. 92, 97. The record shows that the Government detained some asylum seekers for 831 days (nearly 2\xc2\xbd years), 512 days, 456 days, 421 days, 354 days, 319 days, 318 days, and 274 days\xe2\x80\x94before they won their cases and received asylum. Id., at 97, 228\xe2\x80\x93236. It also shows that the Government detained one noncitizen for nearly four years after he had finished serving a criminal sentence, and the Government detained other members of this class for 608 days, 561 days, 446 days, 438 days, 387 days, and 305 days\xe2\x80\x94all before they won their cases and received relief from removal. Id., at 92, 213\xe2\x80\x93220.

\n

Fifth, many of those whom the Government detains eventually obtain the relief they seek. Two-thirds of the asylum seekers eventually receive asylum. Id., at 98 (Table 28); id., at 135 (Table 38); App. to Pet. for Cert. 40a. Nearly 40% of those who have served criminal sentences receive relief from removal, because, for example, their earlier conviction involved only a short sentence. See App. 95 (Table 23); id., at 135 (Table 38). See also App. to Pet. for Cert. 34a; App. 210, 216\xe2\x80\x93217, 312\xe2\x80\x93313 (between one-half and two-thirds of the class served sentences less than six months, e.g., a 2-month sentence for being under the influence of a controlled substance, or an 8-day jail term for a minor firearms offense).

\n

Sixth, these very asylum seekers would have received bail hearings had they first been taken into custody within the United States rather than at the border. See In\xc2\xa0re X-K-, 23 I.\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0N. Dec. 731, 734\xe2\x80\x93735 (BIA 2005);\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71226(a).

\n

Seventh, as for those who have finished serving their sentences (for crimes), some of those who are less dangerous would (on the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view) be held without bail the longest, because their claims will take longer to adjudicate. Moreover, those noncitizens would have no opportunity to obtain bail while they pursue their claims, but if they lose their claims, the Government must release them, typically within six months, if the Government can find no other country willing to take them. See Zadvydas, supra, at 701.

\n

Eighth, all the respondents are held in detention within the geographical boundaries of the United States, either in facilities controlled by United States Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) or in state or local jails that hold them on ICE\xe2\x80\x99s behalf. App. 302\xe2\x80\x93304; see ICE, Detention Facility Locator, online at http://www.ice.gov/ detention-facilities (all Internet materials as last visited Feb. 21, 2018).

\n

Ninth, the circumstances of their detention are similar, so far as we can tell, to those in many prisons and jails. And in some cases the conditions of their confinement are inappropriately poor. See Dept. of Homeland Security (DHS), Office of Inspector General (OIG), DHS OIG Inspection Cites Concerns With Detainee Treatment and Care at ICE Detention Facilities (2017) (reporting instances of invasive procedures, substandard care, and mistreatment, e.g., indiscriminate strip searches, long waits for medical care and hygiene products, and, in the case of one detainee, a multiday lock down for sharing a cup of coffee with another detainee).

\n

These record-based facts make evident what I said at the outset: The case concerns persons whom immigration authorities believe are not citizens and may not have a right to enter into, or remain within, the United States. Nonetheless they likely have a reasonable claim that they do have such a right. The Government detains them, often for many months while it determines the merits of, or contests, their claims. To repeat the question before us: Does the statute entitle an individual member of one of these classes to obtain, say, after six months of detention, a bail hearing to decide whether he or she poses a risk of flight or danger to the community and, if not, to receive bail?

\n

II

\n

The Constitutional Question

\n

The majority reads the relevant statute as prohibiting bail and hence prohibiting a bail hearing. In my view, the relevant constitutional language, purposes, history, tradition, and case law all make clear that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation at the very least would raise \xe2\x80\x9cgrave doubts\xe2\x80\x9d about the statute\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality. See Jin Fuey Moy, 241 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 401.

\n

A

\n

Consider the relevant constitutional language and the values that language protects. The\n \nFifth Amendment says that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o person shall be .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law.\xe2\x80\x9d An alien is a \xe2\x80\x9cperson.\xe2\x80\x9d See Wong Wing v. United States,\n\n163 U.\xc2\xa0S. 228\n\xe2\x80\x93238 (1896). To hold him without bail is to deprive him of bodily \xe2\x80\x9cliberty.\xe2\x80\x9d See United States v. Salerno,\n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 739\n\xe2\x80\x93751 (1987). And, where there is no bail proceeding, there has been no bail-related \xe2\x80\x9cprocess\xe2\x80\x9d at all. The Due Process Clause\xe2\x80\x94itself reflecting the language of the Magna Carta\xe2\x80\x94prevents arbitrary detention. Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9c[f]reedom from bodily restraint has always been at the core of the liberty protected by the Due Process Clause from arbitrary governmental action.\xe2\x80\x9d Foucha v. Louisiana,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 71,\n80 (1992)\n; see also Demore v. Kim,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510,\n532 (2003)\n (Kennedy, J., concurring); Zadvydas, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 718 (Kennedy, J., dissenting).

\n

The Due Process Clause foresees eligibility for bail as part of \xe2\x80\x9cdue process.\xe2\x80\x9d See Salerno, supra, at 748\xe2\x80\x93751; Schilb v. Kuebel,\n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 357,\n365 (1971)\n; Stack v. Boyle,\n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n4 (1951)\n. Bail is \xe2\x80\x9cbasic to our system of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Schilb, supra, at 365. It not only \xe2\x80\x9cpermits the unhampered preparation of a defense,\xe2\x80\x9d but also \xe2\x80\x9cprevent[s] the infliction of punishment prior to conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d Stack, supra, at 4. It consequently limits the Government\xe2\x80\x99s ability to deprive a person of his physical liberty where doing so is not needed to protect the public, see Salerno, supra, at 750\xe2\x80\x93751, or to assure his appearance at, say, a trial or the equivalent, see Stack, supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. Why would this constitutional language and its bail-related purposes not apply to members of the classes of detained persons at issue here?

\n

The\n \nEighth Amendment reinforces the view that the\n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause does apply. The\n \nEighth Amendment forbids \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcessive bail.\xe2\x80\x9d It does so in order to prevent bail being set so high that the level itself (rather than the reasons that might properly forbid release on bail) prevents provisional release. See Carlson v. Landon,\n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 524,\n545 (1952)\n (explaining that the English clause from which the\n \nEighth Amendment was copied was understood \xe2\x80\x9cto provide that bail shall not be excessive in those cases where it is proper to grant bail\xe2\x80\x9d). That rationale applies a fortiori to a refusal to hold any bail hearing at all. Thus, it is not surprising that this Court has held that both the\n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause and the\n \nEighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Excessive Bail Clause apply in cases challenging bail procedures. See, e.g., Salerno, supra, at 746\xe2\x80\x93755; Carlson, supra, at 537\xe2\x80\x93546.

\n

It is clear that the\n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protections extend to \xe2\x80\x9call persons within the territory of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Wong Wing, supra, at 238. But the Government suggests that those protections do not apply to asylum seekers or other arriving aliens because the law treats arriving aliens as if they had never entered the United States; hence they are not held within its territory.

\n

This last-mentioned statement is, of course, false. All of these noncitizens are held within the territory of the United States at an immigration detention facility. Those who enter at JFK airport are held in immigration detention facilities in, e.g., New York; those who arrive in El Paso are held in, e.g., Texas. At most one might say that they are \xe2\x80\x9cconstructively\xe2\x80\x9d held outside the United States: the word \xe2\x80\x9cconstructive\xe2\x80\x9d signaling that we indulge in a \xe2\x80\x9clegal fiction,\xe2\x80\x9d shutting our eyes to the truth. But once we admit to uttering a legal fiction, we highlight, we do not answer, the relevant question: Why should we engage in this legal fiction here?

\n

The legal answer to this question is clear. We cannot here engage in this legal fiction. No one can claim, nor since the time of slavery has anyone to my knowledge successfully claimed, that persons held within the United States are totally without constitutional protection. Whatever the fiction, would the Constitution leave the Government free to starve, beat, or lash those held within our boundaries? If not, then, whatever the fiction, how can the Constitution authorize the Government to imprison arbitrarily those who, whatever we might pretend, are in reality right here in the United States? The answer is that the Constitution does not authorize arbitrary detention. And the reason that is so is simple: Freedom from arbitrary detention is as ancient and important a right as any found within the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s boundaries. See Zadvydas, supra, at 720\xe2\x80\x93721 (Kennedy, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cinadmissible aliens\xe2\x80\x9d who are \xe2\x80\x9cstopped at the border\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9centitled to be free from detention that is arbitrary or capricious\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

The Due Process Clause, among other things, protects \xe2\x80\x9cthose settled usages and modes of proceeding existing in the common and statute law of England, before the emigration of our ancestors,\xe2\x80\x9d and which were brought by them to this country. Murray\xe2\x80\x99s Lessee v. Hoboken Land & Improvement Co., 18 How. 272, 277 (1856). A brief look at Blackstone makes clear that at the time of the American Revolution the right to bail was \xe2\x80\x9csettled\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94in both civil and criminal cases.

\n

Blackstone tells us that every prisoner (except for a convict serving his sentence) was entitled to seek release on bail. 4 Commentaries on the Laws of England 296\xe2\x80\x93297 (1769). This right applied in every criminal case. Ibid. A noncapital defendant could seek bail from a local magistrate; a capital defendant could seek bail at a hearing before the Court of King\xe2\x80\x99s Bench. See ibid. Although a capital defendant had no right to obtain bail, he could always seek it, because \xe2\x80\x9cthe court of king\xe2\x80\x99s bench .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may bail for any crime whatsoever, be it treason, murder, or any other offense, according to the circumstances of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 296. And although King Charles I initially claimed the right to hold a prisoner without bail on secret national security grounds, see Darnel\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 3 How. St. Tr. 1 (K. B. 1627), Parliament responded by extracting from the King (via the 1628 Petition of Right) a promise to cease such detention. See 2 W. Hawkins, A Treatise of the Pleas of the Crown 107\xe2\x80\x93110 (4th ed. 1771). From then on, bail was available even when a prisoner was held on the personal command of the King. Ibid. That is why Blackstone says that the King\xe2\x80\x99s Bench or its judges \xe2\x80\x9cmay bail in any Case whatsoever,\xe2\x80\x9d 4 Analysis of the Laws of England 148 (6th ed. 1771), indeed, in civil cases too, for in Blackstone\xe2\x80\x99s time some private civil cases might have begun with an arrest. See 3 Blackstone, Commentaries 290 (1768). And bail was likewise an alternative to detention where a judgment debtor was unable to pay a civil judgment in the era of debtor\xe2\x80\x99s prison. See, e.g., Beers v. Haughton, 9 Pet. 329, 356 (1835) (explaining that under Ohio law, \xe2\x80\x9cif a defendant, upon a [writ of] capias, does not give sufficient appearance bail, he shall be committed to prison\xe2\x80\x9d); Hamilton v. Dunklee, 1 N.\xc2\xa0H. 172 (1818).

\n

American history makes clear that the settlers brought this practice with them to America. The Judiciary Act of 1789 conferred rights to bail proceedings in all federal criminal cases. \xc2\xa733,\n\n1Stat.\n91. It said that for a noncapital defendant \xe2\x80\x9cbail shall be admitted\xe2\x80\x9d and for a capital defendant bail may be admitted in the discretion of a district judge, a circuit judge, or a Justice of the Supreme Court, taking account of \xe2\x80\x9cthe offence, and of the evidence, and the usages of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Congress enacted this law during its debate over the Bill of Rights, which it subsequently sent to the States for ratification. See 1 Annals of Cong. 90 (1789); see also Martin v. Hunter\xe2\x80\x99s Lessee, 1 Wheat. 304, 351 (1816) (Members of the First Congress were \xe2\x80\x9cmen of great learning and ability, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. who had acted a principal part in framing, supporting, or opposing\xe2\x80\x9d the Constitution itself). Colonial law had been similarly, or in some instances even more, protective. See Foote, The Coming Constitutional Crisis in Bail: I, 113 U. Pa. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 959, 974\xe2\x80\x93977 (1965).

\n

Similar laws have consistently remained part of our legal tradition. In all federal criminal cases federal Acts have provided for bail proceedings. Bail Reform Act of 1984,\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73141 et\xc2\xa0seq.; Bail Reform Act of 1966,\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73146 et\xc2\xa0seq. (1964 ed., Supp. II). Every State has similar or more generous laws. See Appendix B, infra.

\n

Standards for granting bail have changed somewhat over time. Initially the sole factor determining the outcome of a bail proceeding was risk of flight. See Stack, 342 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 4\xe2\x80\x935 (interpreting the 1789 bail law, applied to a noncapital defendant and in light of the\n \nEighth Amendment, to require bail no higher than required to provide \xe2\x80\x9cadequate assurance\xe2\x80\x9d that the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cwill stand trial and submit to sentence if found guilty,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon standards relevant to the purpose of assuring the presence of that defendant\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Congress gradually added community safety as a bail factor. In 1966, Congress provided that for capital defendants and convicted defendants pursuing appeals, bail would be granted unless the appeal was frivolous or a court had \xe2\x80\x9creason to believe that no one or more conditions of release will reasonably assure that the person will not flee or pose a danger to any other person or to the community.\xe2\x80\x9d Bail Reform Act of 1966 \xc2\xa73148. In 1984, Congress modified the bail standard for noncapital defendants by adding concern for community safety. \xc2\xa73142(e)(1). This Court, applying the Due Process Clause and the Excessive Bail Clause to these changes, found that the 1984 Act passed constitutional muster. See Salerno, 481 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 746\xe2\x80\x93755. Again, the States typically apply roughly similar or more generous standards. See Appendix B, infra.

\n

The cases before us, however, are not criminal cases. Does that fact make a difference? The problem is that there are not many instances of civil confinement (aside from immigration detention, which I address below). Mental illness does sometimes provide an example. Individuals dangerous to themselves or to others may be confined involuntarily to a mental hospital. See, e.g., United States v. Comstock,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 126 (2010)\n; Kansas v. Hendricks,\n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 346 (1997)\n. Those persons normally do not have what we would call \xe2\x80\x9ca right to a bail hearing.\xe2\x80\x9d But they do possess equivalent rights: They have the right to a hearing prior to confinement and the right to review of the circumstances at least annually. See Comstock, supra, at 130\xe2\x80\x93131 (initial hearing followed by review every six months); Hendricks, supra, at 353 (initial hearing followed by yearly review). And the mentally ill persons detained under these schemes are being detained because they are dangerous. That being so, there would be no point in providing a bail hearing as well. See Salerno, supra, at 748\xe2\x80\x93749 (analogizing denial of bail to dangerous individuals to the civil commitment of the mentally ill). But there is every reason for providing a bail proceeding to the noncitizens at issue here, because they have received no individualized determination that they pose a risk of flight or present a danger to others, nor is there any evidence that most or all of them do.

\n

This Court has also protected the right to a bail hearing during extradition proceedings. Wright v. Henkel,\n\n190 U.\xc2\xa0S. 40 (1903)\n, concerned the arrest and confinement of Whitaker Wright, an American citizen, pending extradition for a crime that Wright was accused of having committed in Great Britain. Wright sought bail. Id., at 43. Since the federal bail laws applied only to those charged with committing crimes against the United States, they did not cover Wright\xe2\x80\x99s confinement. Id., at 61\xe2\x80\x9362. The relevant extradition statute said nothing about bail. Id., at 62. Its language (stronger than the language at issue here) said that the individual was \xe2\x80\x9cto remain\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cthe proper jail\xe2\x80\x9d until the \xe2\x80\x9csurrender shall be made\xe2\x80\x9d to the nation seeking extradition; and it added that he was \xe2\x80\x9cto remain\xe2\x80\x9d in custody \xe2\x80\x9cuntil delivered up\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94though after two months he could seek release. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa75270, 5273.

\n

In an opinion by Chief Justice Fuller, this Court unanimously wrote that, despite the lack of express statutory authorization and the risk of \xe2\x80\x9cembarrassment\xe2\x80\x9d to the United States if Wright fled, Wright could seek release on bail prior to the expiration of the 2-month period. Wright, 190 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 62\xe2\x80\x9363. Given the universal entitlement to bail under English law, the Court was \xe2\x80\x9cunwilling to hold that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. courts may not in any case, and whatever the special circumstances, extend that relief\xe2\x80\x9d to prisoners awaiting extradition. Id., at 63. It consequently read a silent statute as authorizing bail proceedings (though the Court went on to hold that, under applicable standards, Wright\xe2\x80\x99s request for bail should be denied). Ibid.

\n

The strongest basis for reading the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s bail requirements as extending to these civil, as well as criminal, cases, however, lies in the simple fact that the law treats like cases alike. And reason tells us that the civil confinement at issue here and the pretrial criminal confinement that calls for bail are in every relevant sense identical. There is no difference in respect to the fact of confinement itself. And I can find no relevant difference in respect to bail-related purposes.

\n

Which class of persons\xe2\x80\x94criminal defendants or asylum seekers\xe2\x80\x94seems more likely to have acted in a manner that typically warrants confinement? A person charged with a crime cannot be confined at all without a finding of probable cause that he or she committed the crime. And the majority of criminal defendants lose their cases. See Dept. of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics, B. Reaves, Felony Defendants in Large Urban Counties, 2009\xe2\x80\x93Statistical Tables, p. 24 (Dec. 2013) (reporting that 66% of felony defendants were convicted). A high percentage of the noncitizens before us, however, ultimately win the right they seek, the right to be in the United States.

\n

Nor am I aware of any evidence indicating that the noncitizens seeking to enter, or to remain within, the United States are more likely than criminal defendants to threaten the safety of the community if released. In any event, this is a matter to be determined, case by case, at bail hearings.

\n

Which group is more likely to present a risk of flight? Again, I can find no evidence suggesting that asylum seekers or other noncitizens generally present a greater risk of flight than persons imprisoned for trial where there is probable cause to believe that the confined person has committed a crime. In any event, this matter too is to be determined, case by case, at bail hearings.

\n

If there is no reasonable basis for treating these confined noncitizens worse than ordinary defendants charged with crimes,\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73142; worse than convicted criminals appealing their convictions, \xc2\xa73143(b); worse than civilly committed citizens, supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311; worse than identical noncitizens found elsewhere within the United States, supra, at 4; and worse than noncitizens who have committed crimes, served their sentences, and been definitively ordered removed (but lack a country willing to take them), supra, at 4, their detention without bail is arbitrary. Thus, the constitutional language, purposes, and tradition that require bail in instances of criminal confinement also very likely require bail in these instances of civil confinement. That perhaps is why Blackstone wrote that the law provides for the possibility of \xe2\x80\x9cbail in any case whatsoever.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 Analysis of the Laws of England, at 148.

\n

C

\n

My examination of the cases from this Court that considered detention of noncitizens and bail suggests that this Court, while sometimes denying bail to individuals, generally has not held that bail proceedings are unnecessary. Indeed, it almost always has suggested the contrary.

\n

1. In 1882 Congress enacted two laws that restricted immigration: The first prohibited the entry of \xe2\x80\x9cChinese laborers.\xe2\x80\x9d The Chinese Exclusion Act, ch. 126,\n\n22Stat.\n58. The second prohibited the entry of \xe2\x80\x9cany convict, lunatic, idiot, or any person unable to take care of himself or herself without becoming a public charge.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Aug. 3, 1882,\n\n22Stat.\n214. Neither said a word about bail. But in one instance, an excluded Chinese woman was detained in jail in San Francisco pending her return to China. She sought bail. In re Ah Moy, 21 F. 808 (CC Cal. 1884). Justice Field, sitting as a Circuit Judge, wrote that the court lacked the authority to order bail because doing so would allow her to enter the United States\xe2\x80\x94just what the statute forbade. Id., at 809. The other sitting Circuit Judge (Judge Sawyer) disagreed. Id., at 810 (dissenting opinion). He pointed out that the alien would remain \xe2\x80\x9cin the custody and control of the law while lawfully on bail.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. He added that it \xe2\x80\x9cwould be a great hardship, not to say a gross violation of her personal rights,\xe2\x80\x9d to refuse bail for 15 days before her ship arrived as long as she could provide \xe2\x80\x9csecurity satisfactory to the court\xe2\x80\x9d that she would indeed depart when it did. Id., at 809\xe2\x80\x93810. Two other Circuit Judges noted their agreement with Judge Sawyer. Id., at 809, n.\xc2\xa01. But they did not participate in the case, ibid., the two participating judges split 1 to 1, and so the views of presiding Justice Field prevailed. The alien appealed to this Court, Cheong Ah Moy v. United States,\n\n113 U.\xc2\xa0S. 216 (1885)\n, but before this Court could decide, the ship departed with Cheong Ah Moy aboard.

\n

2. In Wong Wing v. United States,\n\n163 U.\xc2\xa0S. 228 (1896)\n, the Court struck down as unconstitutional a statute that said alien Chinese laborers should be \xe2\x80\x9cimprisoned at hard labor\xe2\x80\x9d for up to a year before being deported. Id., at 235. In doing so, the Court wrote that although a sentence to hard labor was unlawful, \xe2\x80\x9cdetention, or temporary confinement,\xe2\x80\x9d was constitutional, because \xe2\x80\x9c[d]etention is a usual feature of every case of arrest on a criminal charge, even when an innocent person is wrongfully accused.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But an analogy to criminal detention is an analogy to instances in which bail hearings are required.

\n

3. In Tod v. Waldman,\n\n266 U.\xc2\xa0S. 113 (1924)\n, the Waldman family, like many of the respondents here, challenged their exclusion. They had arrived at Ellis Island fleeing religious persecution in Ukraine. They were detained because the immigration inspector believed the mother illiterate, one of the daughters disabled, and the whole family likely to become public charges. They appealed to the Labor Department, which ordered Mrs. Waldman retested for literacy, requiring her to read both Yiddish and Hebrew. She could not. She then petitioned for a writ of habeas corpus on the grounds that (1) as a religious refugee she was exempt from the literacy requirement; (2) in any event, she need read only one language, not two; (3) her daughter was not disabled; and (4) the Department of Labor should have allowed her to appeal administratively. Id., at 114\xe2\x80\x93115.

\n

The relevant statutory provisions, just like the present statute, see infra, at 20, 29, said that an arriving person, unless \xe2\x80\x9cclearly and beyond a doubt entitled\xe2\x80\x9d to land, \xe2\x80\x9cshall be detained for examination .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by a board of special inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Feb. 5, 1917, \xc2\xa716,\n\n39Stat.\n886 (emphasis added). By the time the case reached this Court, however, the family had been allowed bail. See Waldman, 266 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 117. This Court ordered the Department of Labor to provide the family with an administrative appeal. Then, after initially \xe2\x80\x9cremand[ing] the petitioners to the custody of immigration authorities\xe2\x80\x9d pending the outcome of the appeal, id., at 120, the Court clarified in a rehearing order that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in the order of this Court shall prejudice an application for release on bail of the respondents pending compliance with the mandate of this Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Tod v. Waldman,\n\n266 U.\xc2\xa0S. 547\n (1925). This statement is inconsistent with the earlier opinion of Justice Field, sitting as a Circuit Judge, because it shows that even an alien challenging her exclusion could be released on bail. Supra, at 14.

\n

4. In Carlson v. Landon,\n\n342 U.\xc2\xa0S. 524 (1952)\n, this Court upheld the denial of bail to noncitizen Communists being held pending deportation, despite a statute that permitted bail proceedings. Id., at 541\xe2\x80\x93546. It did so because it considered the individuals to be a risk to security. It said nothing to suggest that bail proceedings were unnecessary.

\n

5. In Shaughnessy v. United States ex rel. Mezei,\n\n345 U.\xc2\xa0S. 206 (1953)\n, the Attorney General had ordered a noncitizen permanently excluded from the United States on the ground that his \xe2\x80\x9centry would be prejudicial to the public interest for security reasons.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 208; see Subversive Activities Control Act of 1950, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa722\xe2\x80\x9323,\n\n64Stat.\n1006\xe2\x80\x931012. He \xe2\x80\x9csat on Ellis Island because this country shut him out and others were unwilling to take him in.\xe2\x80\x9d 345 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 209. After 21 months in confinement he filed a petition for a writ of habeas corpus seeking judicial review of the exclusion decision or release on bail until he could be removed to another country. Id., at 207, 209. This Court refused to review the exclusion decision on the ground that the security matter fell totally within the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority, pursuant to an express congressional delegation of power. Id., at 210. The Court also denied Mezei a bail proceeding because in an \xe2\x80\x9cexclusion proceeding grounded on danger to the national security .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. neither the rationale nor the statutory authority for\xe2\x80\x9d release on bail exists. Id., at 216. It denied bail, however, after the Attorney General had already found, on an individualized basis, not only that Mezei was a security risk and consequently not entitled to either admission or bail, but also that he could be denied a hearing on the matter because the basis for that decision could not be disclosed without harm to national security. Id., at 208\xe2\x80\x93209. The respondents in this case have been the subject of no such individualized findings. And unlike Mezei, who was requesting bail after his exclusion proceedings had ended (while the Attorney General searched for a country that would take him\xe2\x80\x94a matter that we again confronted in Zadvydas), the respondents here continue to litigate the lawfulness of their exclusion itself. Thus, Mezei, but not the respondents here, was in a sense in the position of a convicted criminal who had lost his appeal, not a criminal awaiting trial (or the results of an appeal).

\n

6. Zadvydas v. Davis,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678 (2001)\n, concerned a noncitizen who had lawfully resided in this country, committed a serious crime, completed his prison sentence, and was then ordered deported. Id., at 684. Zadvydas sought release on bail during the time the Government searched for a country that would take him. Id., at 684\xe2\x80\x93685. The governing statute said an alien such as Zadvydas \xe2\x80\x9cmay be detained\xe2\x80\x9d pending his removal to another country.\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71231(a)(6). We interpreted those words as requiring release from detention once it became clear that there was \xe2\x80\x9cno significant likelihood of removal in the reasonably foreseeable future\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94presumptively after a period of confinement of six months. 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 701. We read the statute as requiring this release because a \xe2\x80\x9cstatute permitting indefinite detention of an alien would raise a serious constitutional problem.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 690.

\n

From a constitutional perspective, this case follows a fortiori from Zadvydas. Here only a bail hearing is at issue, not release on bail, much less permanent release. And here there has been no final determination that any of the respondents lacks a legal right to stay in the United States\xe2\x80\x94the bail hearing at issue concerns conditional release pending that final determination. It is immaterial that detention here is not literally indefinite, because while the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 removal proceedings must end eventually, they last an indeterminate period of at least six months and a year on average, thereby implicating the same constitutional right against prolonged arbitrary detention that we recognized in Zadvydas.

\n

7. In Demore v. Kim,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510 (2003)\n, we held that the Government could constitutionally hold without bail noncitizens who had committed certain crimes, had completed their sentences, and were in removal proceedings. See \xc2\xa71226(c). But we based our holding on the short-term nature of the confinement necessary to complete proceedings. See id., at 529\xe2\x80\x93530. The Court wrote that the \xe2\x80\x9cdetention at stake .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. lasts roughly a month and a half in the vast majority of cases in which it is invoked, and about five months in the minority of cases in which the alien chooses to appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 530. We added:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n 85% of the cases in which aliens are detained [\xc2\xa0pursuant to the relevant statute], removal proceedings are completed in an average time of 47 days and a median of 30 days. In the remaining 15% of cases, in which the alien appeals the decision of the immigration judge to the Board of Immigration Appeals, appeal takes an average of four months, with a median time that is slightly shorter.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 529 (citation omitted).

\n

Demore himself, an outlier, was detained for six months. Id., at 530\xe2\x80\x93531.

\n

The Court then found detention constitutional \xe2\x80\x9cduring the limited period\xe2\x80\x9d necessary to arrange for removal, and we contrasted that period of detention with the detention at issue in Zadvydas, referring to the detention in Demore as being \xe2\x80\x9cof a much shorter duration.\xe2\x80\x9d 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 526, 528. Justice Kennedy stated in a concurrence that the Due Process Clause might require bail hearings \xe2\x80\x9cif the continued detention became unreasonable or unjustified.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 532. Dissenting, I wrote that, had I believed that Demore \xe2\x80\x9chad conceded that he [was] deportable,\xe2\x80\x9d then, despite Zadvydas, \xe2\x80\x9cI would conclude that the Government could detain him without bail for the few weeks ordinarily necessary for formal entry of a removal order.\xe2\x80\x9d 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 576 (opinion concurring in part and dissenting in part).

\n

The Government now tells us that the statistics it gave to the Court in Demore were wrong. Detention normally lasts twice as long as the Government then said it did. And, as I have pointed out, thousands of people here are held for considerably longer than six months without an opportunity to seek bail. See supra, at 3. We deal here with prolonged detention, not the short-term detention at issue in Demore. Hence Demore, itself a deviation from the history and tradition of bail and alien detention, cannot help the Government.

\n

The upshot is the following: The Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s language, its basic purposes, the relevant history, our tradition, and many of the relevant cases point in the same interpretive direction. They tell us that an interpretation of the statute before us that would deny bail proceedings where detention is prolonged would likely mean that the statute violates the Constitution. The interpretive principle that flows from this conclusion is clear and longstanding: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[A]s between two possible interpretations of a statute, by one of which it would be unconstitutional and by the other valid, our plain duty is to adopt that which will save the Act.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Rust v. Sullivan,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 173,\n190 (1991)\n (quoting Blodgett v. Holden,\n\n275 U.\xc2\xa0S. 142,\n148 (1927)\n (opinion of Holmes, J.)). Moreover, a \xe2\x80\x9cstatute must be construed, if fairly possible, so as to avoid not only the conclusion that it is unconstitutional, but also grave doubts upon that score.\xe2\x80\x9d Jin Fuey Moy, 241 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 401. These legal principles reflect a realistic assumption, namely, that Congress\xe2\x80\x94particularly a Congress that did not consider a constitutional matter\xe2\x80\x94would normally have preferred a constitutional interpretation to an interpretation that may render a statute an unconstitutional nullity. And that is so even where the constitutional interpretation departs from the most natural reading of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language. See Edward J. DeBartolo Corp. v. Florida Gulf Coast Building & Constr. Trades Council,\n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 568,\n575 (1988)\n; see also National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius,\n\n567 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519\n\xe2\x80\x93576 (2012) (majority opinion and opinion of Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J.).

\n

III

\n

The Statutory Provisions

\n

The question remains whether it is possible to read the statute as authorizing bail. As desirable as a constitutional interpretation of a statute may be, we cannot read it to say the opposite of what its language states. The word \xe2\x80\x9canimal\xe2\x80\x9d does not include minerals, no matter how strongly one might wish that it did. Indeed, where \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Congress has made its intent in the statute clear, we must give effect to that intent,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d even if doing so requires us to consider the constitutional question, and even if doing so means that we hold the statute unconstitutional. Zadvydas, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 696 (quoting Miller v. French,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 327,\n336 (2000)\n). In my view, however, we can, and should, read the relevant statutory provisions to require bail proceedings in instances of prolonged detention without doing violence to the statutory language or to the provisions\xe2\x80\x99 basic purposes.

\n

A

\n

Asylum Seekers

\n

The relevant provision governing the first class of noncitizens, the asylum seekers, is \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(ii). It says that, if an immigration \xe2\x80\x9cofficer determines at the time\xe2\x80\x9d of an initial interview with an alien seeking to enter the United States \xe2\x80\x9cthat [the] alien has a credible fear of persecution .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, the alien shall be detained for further consideration of the application for asylum.\xe2\x80\x9d See Appendix A\xe2\x80\x931, infra. I have emphasized the three key words, namely, \xe2\x80\x9cshall be detained.\xe2\x80\x9d Do those words mean that the asylum seeker must be detained without bail?

\n

They do not. First, in ordinary English and in light of the history of bail, the word \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d is ambiguous in respect to the relevant point. The Oxford English Dictionary (OED), surveying the history of the word, notes that Edward Hall, a famous 16th-century legal scholar and author of Hall\xe2\x80\x99s Chronicle, wrote: \xe2\x80\x9cA traytor .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is apprehended and deteigned in prisone for his offence,\xe2\x80\x9d a use of the word, as we know from Blackstone, that is consistent with bail. See supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x939; OED (3d ed., Dec. 2012), http://www.oed.com/view/Entry/51176 (annot. to def. 1). David Hume, the famous 18th-century historian and philosopher, writes of being \xe2\x80\x9cdetained in strict confinement,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby implying the existence of detention without strict confinement. Ibid. A 19th-century novelist writes, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Beg your pardon, sir,\xe2\x80\x99 said the constable, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98I shall be obliged to detain you till this business is settled\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94again a use of \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d that we know (from Blackstone) is consistent with bail. Ibid. And the OED concludes that the primary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o keep in confinement or under restraint; to keep prisoner.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). To grant bail, we know, is not to grant unrestrained freedom. Rather, where the Act elsewhere expressly permits bail, it requires \xe2\x80\x9cbond of at least $1,500 with security approved by, and containing conditions prescribed by, the Attorney General.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71226(a)(2)(A). Similarly in the criminal context, bail imposes numerous restraints, ranging from the provision of a bond, to restrictions on residences and travel, to the imposition of a curfew, to a requirement to obtain medical treatment, to report at regular intervals, or even to return to custody at specified hours. See\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73142(c)(1)(B) (listing possible conditions for the pretrial release of federal criminal defendants).

\n

At the very least, because the word \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d in this context refers to a comparatively long period of time, it can readily coexist with a word such as \xe2\x80\x9cbail\xe2\x80\x9d that refers to a shorter period of conditional release. For instance, there is nothing inconsistent in saying: During his exile, he was permitted to pay short visits to his home country; during the period of active hostilities, the soldiers would lay down their arms and fraternize on Christmas Day; during his overseas detention, he was allowed home to see his sick mother; or during his detention pending proceedings, he was permitted bail.

\n

Second, our precedent treats the statutory word \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d as consistent with bail. In Waldman,\n\n266 U.\xc2\xa0S. 547\n, we considered an immigration statute that stated (in respect to arriving aliens) that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very alien who may not appear to the examining inspector at the port of arrival to be clearly and beyond a doubt entitled to land shall be detained for examination in relation thereto by a board of special inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Feb. 5, 1917, \xc2\xa716,\n\n39Stat.\n886 (emphasis added). The Court indicated that bail was available, stating that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in the order of this court shall prejudice an application for release on bail.\xe2\x80\x9d 266 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 548. In so stating, the Court was simply following precedent, such as Wright v. Henkel, where the Court wrote that bail is available even where not \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically vested by statute.\xe2\x80\x9d 190 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 63; see supra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. When Congress passed the relevant provisions of the Act in 1996, it legislated against this historical backdrop, at a time when the precise language that it adopted had been interpreted by this Court to permit bail. See Monessen Southwestern R. Co. v. Morgan,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 330,\n338 (1988)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress\xe2\x80\x99 failure to disturb a consistent judicial interpretation of a statute may provide some indication that \xe2\x80\x98Congress at least acquiesces in, and apparently affirms, that [interpretation]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Cannon v. University of Chicago,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677,\n703 (1979)\n)).

\n

Third, the Board of Immigration Appeals reads the word \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d as consistent with bail, for it has held that its regulations, implementing the same statutory provision as is before us, allow bail for asylum seekers who are apprehended inside the United States within 100 miles of the border, rather than at a border crossing. See In re X-K-, 23 I.\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0N. Dec., at 732, 734\xe2\x80\x93735 (discussing 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.19(h)(2)(i) (2004)). The same statute, same language applies to the detention of those asylum seekers and the ones before us, so the statute must be consistent with bail in the Board of Immigration Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 view.

\n

Fourth, in Zadvydas we found (to avoid similar constitutional questions) that the words \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98may be detained\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d were consistent with requiring release from long-term detention. 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 682 (quoting\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71231(a)(6)). The majority correctly notes that here the language substitutes the word \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d for the word \xe2\x80\x9cmay.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9316. But the majority is wrong to distinguish Zadvydas on this basis. There the Court did not emphasize the word \xe2\x80\x9cdetain,\xe2\x80\x9d for the question at issue was release from detention. And the key word was consequently \xe2\x80\x9cmay,\xe2\x80\x9d suggesting discretion. Here the question concerns the right to a bail hearing during detention. And the key linguistic ambiguity concerns the word \xe2\x80\x9cdetention.\xe2\x80\x9d Is that word consistent with bail proceedings? The answer, for the reasons I have stated, is \xe2\x80\x9cyes.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Fifth, the statute does not even mention long-term detention without bail. Whether the statute speaks in terms of discretion (\xe2\x80\x9cmay,\xe2\x80\x9d as in Zadvydas) or mandatory action (\xe2\x80\x9cshall,\xe2\x80\x9d as in this case), the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument is wrong for the same reason: Congress does not unambiguously authorize long-term detention without bail by failing to say when detention must end. As we recognized in Zadvydas, Congress anticipated long-term detention elsewhere in the Act, providing for review every six months of terrorist aliens detained under\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71537(b)(2)(C), but it did not do so here. See 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 697.

\n

Sixth, the Act provides that an asylum applicant whose proceedings last longer than six months may be given work authorization. \xc2\xa71158(d)(2). The majority would apply this provision to some asylum applicants but not the ones before us. Ante, at 26, n.\xc2\xa06. Of course, the statute does not contain that limitation. Read most naturally, the provision offers some indication that Congress, in the same statute, did not require asylum seekers to remain confined without bail at the 6-month mark.

\n

Seventh, there is a separate statutory provision that purports to do precisely what the majority says this one does, providing that certain aliens \xe2\x80\x9cshall be detained .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. until removed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(B)(iii)(IV) (emphasis added); ante, at 16 (detention must continue until proceedings \xe2\x80\x9chave finished\xe2\x80\x9d). The problem for the majority is that this other provision applies only to those who, unlike the respondents, have no credible fear of persecution. The provision that applies here lacks similar language.

\n

Linguistic ambiguity, while necessary, is not sufficient. I would also ask whether the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purposes suggest a congressional refusal to permit bail where confinement is prolonged. The answer is \xe2\x80\x9cno.\xe2\x80\x9d There is nothing in the statute or in the legislative history that reveals any such congressional intent. The most likely reason for its absence is that Congress, like the Government when it appeared before us in Demore, believed there were no such instances, or at least that there were very few. Indeed, the Act suggests that asylum proceedings ordinarily finish quickly. See \xc2\xa71158(d)(5)(A) (providing that absent \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d final administrative adjudication (not including appeal) must be completed \xe2\x80\x9cwithin 180 days,\xe2\x80\x9d and any appeal must be filed \xe2\x80\x9cwithin 30 days\xe2\x80\x9d of the decision). And for those proceedings that last longer than six months, we know that two-thirds of asylum seekers win their cases. Thus, legislative silence suggests not disapproval of bail, but a lack of consideration of the matter. For present purposes that is sufficient. It means that Congress did not intend to forbid bail. An interpretation that permits bail\xe2\x80\x94based upon history, tradition, statutory context, and precedent\xe2\x80\x94is consistent, not inconsistent, with what Congress intended the statutory provision to do.

\n

The majority apparently finds a contrary purpose in the fact that other provisions of the statute permit the Attorney General to release an alien on parole \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98for urgent humanitarian reasons or significant public benefit\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and impose bail-like conditions. Ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317 (discussing\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71182(d)(5)(A)). Yet under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cdetain,\xe2\x80\x9d the same argument could have been made in Zadvydas. We held that noncitizens presumptively are entitled to release after six months of detention, notwithstanding an available alternative avenue for relief, namely, bail. 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 683. There is no reason to reach a different result here. While the Government historically used this provision to take account of traditional bail factors (flight risk, safety risk), the President since issued an Executive Order directing parole to be granted \xe2\x80\x9cin all circumstances only when an individual demonstrates urgent humanitarian reasons or a significant public benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d Exec. Order. No. 13767, 82 Fed. Reg. 8793 (2017). And besides, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 provision of parole to permit, for example, release for the purpose of medical care or to testify in a court proceeding\xe2\x80\x94which adds to the circumstances under which a noncitizen can be released from confinement\xe2\x80\x94says nothing about whether Congress intended to cut back on those circumstances in respect to the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d and the historical understanding that detention permits bail.

\n

B

\n

Criminals Who Have Served Their Sentences

\n

The relevant statutory provision, \xc2\xa71226(c), says in paragraph (1) that the \xe2\x80\x9cAttorney General shall take into cus- tody any alien who .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is deportable [or inadmissible] by reason of having committed [certain crimes] when the alien is released,\xe2\x80\x9d presumably (or ordinarily) after having served his sentence. It then goes on to say, in paragraph (2), that the \xe2\x80\x9cAttorney General may release [that] alien .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. only if the Attorney General decides pursuant to section 3521 of title 18 that release of the alien from custody is necessary to provide protection to a witness [or to certain related others].\xe2\x80\x9d See Appendix A\xe2\x80\x932, infra.

\n

I have emphasized the relevant phrases: \xe2\x80\x9ctake into custody\xe2\x80\x9d in the first paragraph, and \xe2\x80\x9cmay release [that] alien .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. only if\xe2\x80\x9d in the second paragraph. We have long interpreted \xe2\x80\x9cin custody\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cnot requir[ing] that a prisoner be physically confined.\xe2\x80\x9d Maleng v. Cook,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 488,\n491 (1989)\n (per curiam). In the habeas context, we have held that \xe2\x80\x9ca person released on bail or on his own recognizance\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in custody\xe2\x80\x99 within the meaning of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Hensley v. Municipal Court, San Jose-Milpitas Judicial Dist., Santa Clara Cty.,\n\n411 U.\xc2\xa0S. 345,\n349 (1973)\n; Justices of Boston Municipal Court v. Lydon,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 294\n\xe2\x80\x93301 (1984) (same). The reason is simple, as I already have explained, supra, at 21: A person who is released on bail \xe2\x80\x9cis subject to restraints \xe2\x80\x98not shared by the public gener- ally.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hensley, supra, at 351 (quoting Jones v. Cunningham,\n\n371 U.\xc2\xa0S. 236,\n240 (1963)\n); see also Maleng, supra, at 491 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] prisoner who had been placed on parole was still \xe2\x80\x98in custody\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d because his \xe2\x80\x9crelease from physical confinement .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was not unconditional; instead, it was explicitly conditioned on his reporting regularly to his parole officer, remaining in a particular community, residence, and job, and refraining from certain activities\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Jones, supra, at 242)).

\n

Moreover, there is no reason to interpret \xe2\x80\x9ccustody\xe2\x80\x9d differently than \xe2\x80\x9cdetain.\xe2\x80\x9d The OED defines \xe2\x80\x9ccustody\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he state of being detained,\xe2\x80\x9d http://www.oed.com/view/ Entry/46305 (def. 5). \xe2\x80\x9cDetained,\xe2\x80\x9d as I have previously pointed out, can be read consistently with bail. See supra, at 20\xe2\x80\x9323. The OED also defines the statutory phrase, \xe2\x80\x9ctake (a person) into custody,\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cto arrest and imprison (a person),\xe2\x80\x9d http://www.oed.com/view/Entry/46305 (def. 5). And we know from the history, tradition, case law, and other sources earlier discussed, including Blackstone, that arresting and imprisoning a person is consistent with a bail hearing and a subsequent grant of bail, even where a statute contains words such as \xe2\x80\x9ccommitment\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cdetain.\xe2\x80\x9d See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x9319 (citing, e.g., Wright, 190 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 62 (reading as consistent with a bail proceeding the statutory language \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98shall issue [a] warrant for the commitment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to the proper jail, there to remain\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d until \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98surrender\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for extradition)).

\n

But what about the second phrase, stating that the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cmay release [that] alien .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. only if the Attorney General decides pursuant to section 3521 of title 18 that release of the alien from custody is necessary to provide protection to a witness\xe2\x80\x9d? Does the presence of the words \xe2\x80\x9conly if\xe2\x80\x9d show that the statute automatically denies bail for any other reason?

\n

It does not. That is because the phrase has nothing to do with bail. It has to do with a special program, the Witness Protection Program, set forth in\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73521. That program allows the Attorney General to relocate the witness, to give him an entirely new identity, to help his family similarly, and to pay him a stipend, among other things. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73521(a)(1), (b)(1). The Attorney General may \xe2\x80\x9ctake such action as [he] determines to be necessary to protect the person,\xe2\x80\x9d presumably even free the witness from whatever obligations might require him to report to an immigration or judicial authority. \xc2\xa73521(b)(1). Accordingly, when the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9crelease[s]\xe2\x80\x9d an alien under\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71226(c)(2), he does not grant bail; he may well do far more, freeing the witness from a host of obligations and restraints, including those many obligations and restraints that accompany bail. See supra, at 21.

\n

This understanding of \xe2\x80\x9crelease\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71226(c) is consistent with the OED\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9crelease\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cto free from restraint\xe2\x80\x9d or even \xe2\x80\x9cto liberate from .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an obligation\xe2\x80\x9d (not simply \xe2\x80\x9cto free from .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. captivity\xe2\x80\x9d), http://www.oed.com/ view/Entry/161859 (def. 6(a)). And it is consistent with our earlier reading of the word \xe2\x80\x9cdetain.\xe2\x80\x9d Supra, at 20\xe2\x80\x9324. Following the OED\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdetain\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cunder restraint,\xe2\x80\x9d we have understood the word \xe2\x80\x9cdetention\xe2\x80\x9d to include the state of being \xe2\x80\x9cunder\xe2\x80\x9d those \xe2\x80\x9crestraints\xe2\x80\x9d that typically accompany bail. Supra, at 20\xe2\x80\x9324. That is to say, both the individual on bail and the individual not on bail are \xe2\x80\x9cdetained\xe2\x80\x9d; and the Attorney General, through his Witness Protection Program powers can free the individual from both. To repeat: The provision at issue means that the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cmay release\xe2\x80\x9d the detained person from the restraints that accompany detainment\xe2\x80\x94whether that individual has been detained with, or without, bail.

\n

So understood the phrase has nothing to do with the issue before us: whether a confined individual is, or is not, entitled to bail or a bail hearing. It simply means that the Attorney General cannot free that person from all, or most, restraining conditions (including those that accompany bail) unless the alien is placed in the Witness Protection Program. So read, the words \xe2\x80\x9conly if\xe2\x80\x9d neither favor nor disfavor a reading of the statute consistent with the right to a bail proceeding.

\n

The purpose-related reasons that argue for a bail-favorable reading are also applicable here. Congress did not consider the problem of long-term detention. It wrote the statute with brief detention in mind. See H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x93469, pt. 1, p. 123, and n. 25 (1996) (stating that the \xe2\x80\x9caverage stay [was] 28 days\xe2\x80\x9d). Congress did not know (for apparently the Government did not know in Demore) that the average length of detention for this class would turn out to be about a year. Nor did Congress necessarily know that about 40% of class members eventually obtain the right to remain in the United States.

\n

I should add that reading the statute as denying bail to those whose detention is prolonged is anomalous. Those whose removal is legally or factually questionable could be imprisoned indefinitely while the matter is being decided. Those whose removal is not questionable (for they are under a final removal order) could be further imprisoned for no more than six months. See supra, at 4, 17. In fact, even before our decision in Zadvydas, the Government gave bail hearings to noncitizens under a final order of removal after six months of detention. See 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 683.

\n

C

\n

Other Applicants for Admission

\n

The statutory provision that governs the third category of noncitizens seeking admission at the border is \xc2\xa71225(b)(2)(A). It says that \xe2\x80\x9cif the examining immigration officer determines that an alien seeking admission is not clearly and beyond a doubt entitled to be admitted, the alien shall be detained for a proceeding under section 1229a of this title.\xe2\x80\x9d See Appendix A\xe2\x80\x933, infra.

\n

The Government tells us that this miscellaneous cate- gory consists of persons who are neither (1) clearly eligible for admission, nor (2) clearly ineligible. Pet. for Cert. 3\xe2\x80\x934. A clearly eligible person is, of course, immediately admitted. A clearly ineligible person\xe2\x80\x94someone who lacks the required documents, or provides fraudulent ones\xe2\x80\x94is \xe2\x80\x9cremoved .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. without further hearing or review.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71225(b)(1)(A)(i); see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71182(a)(6)(C), (a)(7). But where the matter is not clear, i.e., where the immigration officer determines that an alien \xe2\x80\x9cis not clearly and beyond a doubt entitled to be admitted,\xe2\x80\x9d he is detained for a re- moval proceeding. \xc2\xa71225(b)(2)(A). Like all respondents, this class has been detained for at least six months. It may include persons returning to the United States who have work permits or other documents seemingly entitling them to entry, but whom an immigration officer suspects are inadmissible for some other reason, such as because they may have incomplete vaccinations or have committed student visa abuse or a crime of \xe2\x80\x9cmoral turpitude.\xe2\x80\x9d See \xc2\xa71182(a) (delineating classes of aliens ineligible for admission). For instance, the Federal Register is replete with examples of offenses that immigration authorities have thought are crimes of moral turpitude but that the courts of appeals later determine are not. See, e.g., Goldeshtein v. INS, 8 F.\xc2\xa03d 645, 648 (CA9 1993) (structuring financial transactions to avoid currency reports); Nunez v. Holder, 594 F.\xc2\xa03d 1124, 1138 (CA9 2010) (indecent exposure). It also may include individuals who claim citizenship by virtue of birth or parentage but who lack documents clearly proving their claim.

\n

The critical statutory words are the same as those I have just discussed in the context of the asylum seekers\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cshall be detained.\xe2\x80\x9d There is no more plausible reason here than there was there to believe those words foreclose bail. See supra, at 20\xe2\x80\x9324. The constitutional considerations, the statutory language, and the purposes underlying the statute are virtually the same. Thus, the result should be the same: Given the constitutional considerations, we should interpret the statute as permitting bail.

\n

IV

\n

The majority concludes in Part V, ante, at 29\xe2\x80\x9331, by saying that, before considering bail-related constitutional arguments, the lower courts \xe2\x80\x9cshould reexamine whether respondents can continue litigating their claims as a class.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 29. Relying on dicta in Reno v. American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Comm.,\n\n525 U.\xc2\xa0S. 471 (1999)\n (AADC), it then suggests that the respondents may not be able to continue litigating because the Act says that

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cno court (other than the Supreme Court) shall have jurisdiction or authority to enjoin or restrain the operation [of the statutory provisions here at issue] other than with respect to the application of such provi- sions to an individual alien against whom proceedings under such part have been initiated.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(f\xc2\xa0)(1).

\n

Were the majority\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion correct as to this jurisdictional question, it would have shown, at most, that we should decide the constitutional question here and now. We have already asked for and received briefs on that question. But I do not believe the majority is correct. Every member of the classes before us falls within the provision\xe2\x80\x99s exception. Every one of them is an \xe2\x80\x9cindividual alien against whom proceedings under such part have been initiated.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court in AADC did not con- sider, and had no reason to consider, the application of \xc2\xa71252(f)(1) to such a class. Regardless, a court could order declaratory relief. Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(2) permits a class action where \xe2\x80\x9cfinal injunctive relief or corresponding declaratory relief is appropriate respecting the class as a whole.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) And the Advisory Committee says that declaratory relief can fall within the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s term \xe2\x80\x9ccorresponding\xe2\x80\x9d if it \xe2\x80\x9cserves as a basis for later injunctive relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Notes on Rule 23(b)(2)\xe2\x80\x931966 Amendment, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 812.

\n

Jurisdiction also is unaffected by\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(b)(9), which by its terms applies only \xe2\x80\x9c[w]ith respect to review of an order of removal under [\xc2\xa71252(a)(1)].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71252(b). Respondents challenge their detention without bail, not an order of removal.

\n

Neither does Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes,\n\n564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 338 (2011)\n, bar these class actions. Every member of each class seeks the same relief (a bail hearing), every member has been denied that relief, and the differences in situation among members of the class are not relevant to their entitlement to a bail hearing.

\n

At a minimum I can find nothing in the statute or in the cases to which the majority refers that would prevent the respondents from pursuing their action, obtaining a declaratory judgment, and then using that judgment to obtain relief, namely, a bail hearing, in an individual case. Thus, I believe the lower courts are free to consider the constitutionality of the relevant statutory provisions as the majority now interprets them.

\n

V

\n

Conclusion

\n

The relevant constitutional language, purposes, history, traditions, context, and case law, taken together, make it likely that, where confinement of the noncitizens before us is prolonged (presumptively longer than six months), bail proceedings are constitutionally required. Given this serious constitutional problem, I would interpret the statutory provisions before us as authorizing bail. Their language permits that reading, it furthers their basic purposes, and it is consistent with the history, tradition, and constitutional values associated with bail proceedings. I believe that those bail proceedings should take place in accordance with customary rules of procedure and burdens of proof rather than the special rules that the Ninth Circuit imposed.

\n

The bail questions before us are technical but at heart they are simple. We need only recall the words of the Declaration of Independence, in particular its insistence that all men and women have \xe2\x80\x9ccertain unalienable Rights,\xe2\x80\x9d and that among them is the right to \xe2\x80\x9cLiberty.\xe2\x80\x9d We need merely remember that the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause protects each person\xe2\x80\x99s liberty from arbitrary deprivation. And we need just keep in mind the fact that, since Blackstone\xe2\x80\x99s time and long before, liberty has included the right of a confined person to seek release on bail. It is neither technical nor unusually difficult to read the words of these statutes as consistent with this basic right. I would find it far more difficult, indeed, I would find it alarming, to believe that Congress wrote these statutory words in order to put thousands of individuals at risk of lengthy confinement all within the United States but all without hope of bail. I would read the statutory words as consistent with, indeed as requiring protection of, the basic right to seek bail.

\n

Because the majority does not do so, with respect, I dissent.
