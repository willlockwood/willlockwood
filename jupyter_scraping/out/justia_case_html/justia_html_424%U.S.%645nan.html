APPEAL TO THE COMMONWEALTH COURT\n

\n

OF PENNSYLVANIA, EASTERN DISTRICT\n

\n

Syllabus\n

\n

Philadelphia municipal regulation requiring city employees to be residents of the city held to be constitutional as a bona fide continuing residence requirement and not to violate the right of interstate travel of appellant, whose employment as a city fireman was terminated under the regulation because he moved his residence from Philadelphia to New Jersey.

\n

19 Pa.Commw. 383, 339 A.2d 634, affirmed.

\n

PER CURIAM.

\n

After 16 years of service, appellant\'s employment in the Philadelphia Fire Department was terminated because he moved his permanent residence from Philadelphia to New Jersey in contravention of a municipal regulation requiring employees of the city of Philadelphia to be residents of the city. He challenges the constitutionality of the regulation and the authorizing ordinances [Footnote 1] as violative of his federally protected right of interstate travel. The regulation was sustained by the Commonwealth Court of Pennsylvania, [Footnote 2] and review was denied by the Pennsylvania Supreme Court. [Footnote 3] His timely appeal is here pursuant to 28 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 1257(2).

\n

The Michigan Supreme Court held that Detroit\'s similar

Page 424 U. S. 646

requirement for police officers was not irrational, and did not violate the Due Process Clause or the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. [Footnote 4] We dismissed the appeal from that judgment because no substantial federal question was presented. Detroit Police Officers Assn. v. City of Detroit, 405 U.S. 950 (1972). We have therefore held that this kind of ordinance is not irrational. Hicks v. Miranda, 422 U. S. 332, 422 U. S. 343-345 (1975); see Wardwell v. Board of Education of Cincinnati, 529 F.2d 625, 628 (CA6 1976).

\n

We have not, however, specifically addressed the contention made by appellant in this case that his constitutionally recognized right to travel interstate as defined in Shapiro v. Thompson, 394 U. S. 618 (1969); Dunn v. Blumstein, 405 U. S. 330 (1972); and Memorial Hospital v. Maricopa County, 415 U. S. 250 (1974), is impaired. Each of those cases involved a statutory requirement of residence in the State for at least one year before becoming eligible either to vote, as in Dunn, or to receive welfare benefits, as in Shapiro and Memorial Hospital. [Footnote 5] Neither in those cases nor in any others have we questioned the validity of a condition placed upon municipal employment that a person be a resident at the time of his application. [Footnote 6] In this case, appellant claims a constitutional right to be employed by the city of Philadelphia

Page 424 U. S. 647

while he is living elsewhere. [Footnote 7] There is no support in our cases for such a claim.

\n

We have previously differentiated between a requirement of continuing residency and a requirement of prior residency of a given duration. Thus, in Shapiro, supra at 394 U. S. 636, we stated: "The residence requirement and the one-year waiting period requirement are distinct and independent prerequisites." And in Memorial Hospital, supra at 415 U. S. 255, quoting Dunn, supra at 405 U. S. 342 n. 13, the Court explained that Shapiro and Dunn did not question "the validity of appropriately defined and uniformly applied bona fide residence requirements.\'"\n

\n

This case involves that kind of bona fide continuing residence requirement. The judgment of the Commonwealth Court of Pennsylvania is therefore affirmed.

\n

THE CHIEF JUSTICE, MR. JUSTICE BRENNAN, and MR. JUSTICE BLACKMUN would note probable jurisdiction and set the case for argument.
