ALTRIA GROUP, INC., et\xc2\xa0al. v. GOOD et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93562.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 6, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided December 15, 2008

\n

Respondents, smokers of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d cigarettes, filed suit, alleging that petitioners violated the Maine Unfair Trade Practices Act (MUTPA) by fraudulently advertising that their \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d cigarettes delivered less tar and nicotine than regular brands. The District Court granted summary judgment for petitioners, finding the state-law claim pre-empted by the Federal Cigarette Labeling and Advertising Act (Labeling Act). The First Circuit reversed, holding that the Labeling Act neither expressly nor impliedly pre-empts respondents\xe2\x80\x99 fraud claim.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Neither the Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision nor the Federal Trade Commission\xe2\x80\x99s actions in this field pre-empt respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law fraud claim. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Congress may indicate pre-emptive intent through a statute\xe2\x80\x99s express language or through its structure and purpose. See Jones v. Rath Packing Co., 430 U. S. 519, 525. When the text of an express pre-emption clause is susceptible of more than one plausible reading, courts ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9caccept the reading that disfavors pre-emption.\xe2\x80\x9d Bates v. Dow Agrosciences LLC, 544 U. S. 431, 449. The Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s stated purposes are to inform the public of the health risks of smoking while protecting commerce and the economy from the ill effects of nonuniform requirements to the extent consistent with the first goal. Although fidelity to these purposes does not demand the pre-emption of state fraud rules, the principal question here is whether that result is nevertheless required by 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71334(b), which provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o requirement or prohibition based on smoking and health shall be imposed under State law with respect to the advertising or promotion of any cigarettes the packages of which are labeled in conformity with the provisions of this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim is not expressly pre-empted by \xc2\xa71334(b). As determined in Cipollone v. Liggett Group, Inc., 505 U. S. 504, and Lorillard Tobacco Co. v. Reilly, 533 U. S. 525, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cbased on smoking and health\xe2\x80\x9d modifies the state-law rule at issue rather than a particular application of that rule. The Cipollone plurality concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe phrase \xe2\x80\x98based on smoking and health\xe2\x80\x99 fairly but narrowly construed\xe2\x80\x9d did not pre-empt the Cipollone plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s common-law claim that cigarette manufacturers had fraudulently misrepresented and concealed a material fact, because the claim alleged a violation of a duty not to deceive\xe2\x80\x94a duty that is not \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d smoking and health. 505 U. S., at 528\xe2\x80\x93529. Respondents here also allege a violation of the duty not to deceive as codified in the MUTPA, which, like the common-law duty in Cipollone, has nothing to do with smoking and health. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim is not analogous to the \xe2\x80\x9cwarning neutralization\xe2\x80\x9d claim found to be pre-empted in Cipollone. Reilly is consistent with Cipollone\xe2\x80\x99s analysis. This Court disagrees with petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 alternative argument that the express pre-emption framework of Cipollone and Reilly should be rejected. American Airlines, Inc. v. Wolens, 513 U. S. 219, and Riegel v. Medtronic, Inc., 552 U. S. ___, are distinguished. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Various Federal Trade Commission decisions with respect to statements of tar and nicotine content do not impliedly pre-empt state deceptive practices rules like the MUTPA. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9320.

ALTRIA GROUP, INC., et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v.\nSTEPHANIE GOOD et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

[December 15, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents, who have for over 15 years smoked \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d cigarettes manufactured by petitioners, Philip Morris USA, Inc., and its parent company, Altria Group, Inc., claim that petitioners violated the Maine Unfair Trade Practices Act (MUTPA). Specifically, they allege that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 advertising fraudulently conveyed the message that their \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d cigarettes deliver less tar and nicotine to consumers than regular brands despite petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 knowledge that the message was untrue. Petitioners deny the charge, asserting that their advertisements were factually accurate. The merits of the dispute are not before us because the District Court entered summary judgment in favor of petitioners on the ground that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law claim is pre-empted by the Federal Cigarette Labeling and Advertising Act, as amended (Labeling Act). The Court of Appeals reversed that judgment, and we granted certiorari to review its holding that the Labeling Act neither expressly nor impliedly pre-empts respondents\xe2\x80\x99 fraud claim. We affirm.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents are Maine residents and longtime smokers of Marlboro Lights and Cambridge Lights cigarettes, which are manufactured by petitioners. Invoking the diversity jurisdiction of the Federal District Court, respondents filed a complaint alleging that petitioners deliberately deceived them about the true and harmful nature of \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d cigarettes in violation of the MUTPA, Me. Rev. Stat. Ann., Tit. 5, \xc2\xa7207 (Supp. 2008).[Footnote 1] Respondents claim that petitioners fraudulently marketed their cigarettes as being \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d and containing \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[l]owered [t]ar and [n]icotine\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to convey to consumers that they deliver less tar and nicotine and are therefore less harmful than regular cigarettes. App. 28a\xe2\x80\x9329a.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents acknowledge that testing pursuant to the Cambridge Filter Method[Footnote 2] indicates that tar and nicotine yields of Marlboro Lights and Cambridge Lights are lower than those of regular cigarettes. Id., at 30a. Respondents allege, however, that petitioners have known at all relevant times that human smokers unconsciously engage in compensatory behaviors not registered by Cambridge Filter Method testing that negate the effect of the tar- and nicotine-reducing features of \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d cigarettes. Id., at 30a\xe2\x80\x9331a. By covering filter ventilation holes with their lips or fingers, taking larger or more frequent puffs, and holding the smoke in their lungs for a longer period of time, smokers of \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d cigarettes unknowingly inhale as much tar and nicotine as do smokers of regular cigarettes. Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cLight\xe2\x80\x9d cigarettes are in fact more harmful because the increased ventilation that results from their unique design features produces smoke that is more mutagenic per milligram of tar than the smoke of regular cigarettes. Id., at 31a\xe2\x80\x9332a. Respondents claim that petitioners violated the MUTPA by fraudulently concealing that information and by affirmatively representing, through the use of \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clowered tar and nicotine\xe2\x80\x9d descriptors, that their cigarettes would pose fewer health risks. Id., at 32a, 33a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners moved for summary judgment on the ground that the Labeling Act, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71334(b), expressly pre-empts respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law cause of action. Relying on our decisions in Cipollone v. Liggett Group, Inc., 505 U. S. 504 (1992), and Lorillard Tobacco Co. v. Reilly, 533 U. S. 525 (2001), the District Court concluded that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 MUTPA claim is pre-empted. The court recast respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim as a failure-to-warn or warning neutralization claim of the kind pre-empted in Cipollone: The claim charges petitioners with \xe2\x80\x9cproduc[ing] a product it knew contained hidden risks \xe2\x80\xa6 not apparent or known to the consumer\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a claim that \xe2\x80\x9cruns to what [petitioners] actually said about Lights and what [respondents] claim they should have said.\xe2\x80\x9d 436 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 132, 151 (Me. 2006). And the difference between what petitioners said and what respondents would have them say is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98intertwined with the concern about cigarette smoking and health.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 153 (quoting Reilly, 533 U. S., at 548). The District Court thus concluded that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim rests on a state-law requirement based on smoking and health of precisely the kind that \xc2\xa71334(b) pre-empts, and it granted summary judgment for petitioners.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents appealed, and the Court of Appeals reversed. The Court of Appeals first rejected the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim as a warning neutralization claim akin to the pre-empted claim in Cipollone. 501 F.\xc2\xa03d 29, 37, 40 (CA1 2007). Instead, the court concluded that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim is in substance a fraud claim that alleges that petitioners falsely represented their cigarettes as \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d or having \xe2\x80\x9clowered tar and nicotine\xe2\x80\x9d even though they deliver to smokers the same quantities of those components as do regular cigarettes. Id., at 36. \xe2\x80\x9cThe fact that these alleged misrepresentations were unaccompanied by additional statements in the nature of a warning does not transform the claimed fraud into failure to warn\xe2\x80\x9d or warning neutralization. Id., at 42\xe2\x80\x9343. Finding respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim indistinguishable from the non-pre-empted fraud claim at issue in Cipollone, the Court of Appeals held that it is not expressly pre-empted. The court also rejected petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim is impliedly pre-empted because their success on that claim would stand as an obstacle to the purported policy of the FTC allowing the use of descriptive terms that convey Cambridge Filter Method test results. Accordingly, it reversed the judgment of the District Court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In concluding that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim is not expressly pre-empted, the Court of Appeals considered and rejected the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning in a similar case. 501 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 45. Unlike the court below, the Fifth Circuit likened the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 challenge to the use of \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d descriptors to Cipollone\xe2\x80\x99s warning neutralization claim and thus found it expressly pre-empted. Brown v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., 479 F.\xc2\xa03d 383, 392\xe2\x80\x93393 (2007). We granted the petition for certiorari to resolve this apparent conflict. 552 U. S. ___ (2008).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Article VI, cl.\xc2\xa02, of the Constitution provides that the laws of the United States \xe2\x80\x9cshall be the supreme Law of the Land; . . . any Thing in the Constitution or Laws of any state to the Contrary notwithstanding.\xe2\x80\x9d Consistent with that command, we have long recognized that state laws that conflict with federal law are \xe2\x80\x9cwithout effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Maryland v. Louisiana, 451 U. S. 725, 746 (1981).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our inquiry into the scope of a statute\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive effect is guided by the rule that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]he purpose of Congress is the ultimate touchstone\xe2\x80\x99 in every pre-emption case.\xe2\x80\x9d Medtronic, Inc. v. Lohr, 518 U. S. 470, 485 (1996) (quoting Retail Clerks v. Schermerhorn, 375 U. S. 96, 103 (1963)). Congress may indicate pre-emptive intent through a statute\xe2\x80\x99s express language or through its structure and purpose. See Jones v. Rath Packing Co., 430 U. S. 519, 525 (1977). If a federal law contains an express pre-emption clause, it does not immediately end the inquiry because the question of the substance and scope of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 displacement of state law still remains. Pre-emptive intent may also be inferred if the scope of the statute indicates that Congress intended federal law to occupy the legislative field, or if there is an actual conflict between state and federal law. Freightliner Corp. v. Myrick, 514 U. S. 280, 287 (1995).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When addressing questions of express or implied pre-emption, we begin our analysis \xe2\x80\x9cwith the assumption that the historic police powers of the States [are] not to be superseded by the Federal Act unless that was the clear and manifest purpose of Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Rice v. Santa Fe Elevator Corp., 331 U. S. 218, 230 (1947). That assumption applies with particular force when Congress has legislated in a field traditionally occupied by the States. Lohr, 518 U. S., at 485; see also Reilly, 533 U. S., at 541\xe2\x80\x93542 (\xe2\x80\x9cBecause \xe2\x80\x98federal law is said to bar state action in [a] fiel[d] of traditional state regulation,\xe2\x80\x99 namely, advertising, we \xe2\x80\x98wor[k] on the assumption that the historic police powers of the States [a]re not to be superseded by the Federal Act unless that [is] the clear and manifest purpose of Congress\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (citation omitted)). Thus, when the text of a pre-emption clause is susceptible of more than one plausible reading, courts ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9caccept the reading that disfavors pre-emption.\xe2\x80\x9d Bates v. Dow Agrosciences LLC, 544 U. S. 431, 449 (2005).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress enacted the Labeling Act in 1965[Footnote 3] in response to the Surgeon General\xe2\x80\x99s determination that cigarette smoking is harmful to health. The Act required that every package of cigarettes sold in the United States contain a conspicuous warning, and it pre-empted state-law positive enactments that added to the federally prescribed warning. 79 Stat. 283. Congress amended the Labeling Act a few years later by enacting the Public Health Cigarette Smoking Act of 1969.[Footnote 4] The amendments strengthened the language of the prescribed warning, 84 Stat. 88, and prohibited cigarette advertising in \xe2\x80\x9cany medium of electronic communication subject to [FCC] jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 89. They also broadened the Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision. See Cipollone, 505 U. S., at 520 (plurality opinion) (discussing the difference in scope of the pre-emption clauses of the 1965 and 1969 Acts). The Labeling Act has since been amended further to require cigarette manufacturers to include four more explicit warnings in their packaging and advertisements on a rotating basis.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The stated purpose of the Labeling Act is

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cto establish a comprehensive Federal program to deal with cigarette labeling and advertising with respect to any relationship between smoking and health, whereby\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(1) the public may be adequately informed that cigarette smoking may be hazardous to health by inclusion of a warning to that effect on each package of cigarettes; and

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2) commerce and the national economy may be (A) protected to the maximum extent consistent with this declared policy and (B) not impeded by diverse, nonuniform, and confusing cigarette labeling and advertising regulations with respect to any relationship between smoking and health.\xe2\x80\x9d 79 Stat. 282, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331.

\n

\nThe requirement that cigarette manufacturers include in their packaging and advertising the precise warnings mandated by Congress furthers the Act\xe2\x80\x99s first purpose. And the Act\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provisions promote its second purpose.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As amended, the Labeling Act contains two express pre-emption provisions. Section 5(a) protects cigarette manufacturers from inconsistent state labeling laws by prohibiting the requirement of additional statements relating to smoking and health on cigarette packages. 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71334(a). Section 5(b), which is at issue in this case, provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o requirement or prohibition based on smoking and health shall be imposed under State law with respect to the advertising or promotion of any cigarettes the packages of which are labeled in conformity with the provisions of this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71334(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Together, the labeling requirement and pre-emption provisions express Congress\xe2\x80\x99 determination that the prescribed federal warnings are both necessary and sufficient to achieve its purpose of informing the public of the health consequences of smoking. Because Congress has decided that no additional warning statement is needed to attain that goal, States may not impede commerce in cigarettes by enforcing rules that are based on an assumption that the federal warnings are inadequate. Although both of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s purposes are furthered by prohibiting States from supplementing the federally prescribed warning, neither would be served by limiting the States\xe2\x80\x99 authority to prohibit deceptive statements in cigarette advertising. Petitioners acknowledge that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress had no intention of insulating tobacco companies from liability for inaccurate statements about the relationship between smoking and health.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 28. But they maintain that Congress could not have intended to permit the enforcement of state fraud rules because doing so would defeat the Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s purpose of preventing nonuniform state warning requirements. 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331.[Footnote 6] As we observed in Cipollone, however, fraud claims \xe2\x80\x9crely only on a single, uniform standard: falsity.\xe2\x80\x9d 505 U. S., at 529 (plurality opinion).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although it is clear that fidelity to the Act\xe2\x80\x99s purposes does not demand the pre-emption of state fraud rules, the principal question that we must decide is whether the text of \xc2\xa71334(b) nevertheless requires that result.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have construed the operative phrases of \xc2\xa71334(b) in two prior cases: Cipollone, 505 U. S. 504, and Reilly, 533 U. S. 525. On both occasions we recognized that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cbased on smoking and health\xe2\x80\x9d modifies the state-law rule at issue rather than a particular application of that rule.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Cipollone, the plurality, which consisted of Chief Justice Rehnquist and Justices White, O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, and Stevens, read the pre-emption provision in the 1969 amendments to the Labeling Act to pre-empt common-law rules as well as positive enactments. Unlike Justices Blackmun, Kennedy, and Souter, the plurality concluded that the provision does not preclude all common-law claims that have some relationship to smoking and health. 505 U. S., at 521\xe2\x80\x93523. To determine whether a particular common-law claim is pre-empted, the plurality inquired \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the legal duty that is the predicate of the common-law damages action constitutes a \xe2\x80\x98requirement or prohibition based on smoking and health \xe2\x80\xa6 with respect to \xe2\x80\xa6 advertising or promotion,\xe2\x80\x99 giving that clause a fair but narrow reading.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 524.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying this standard, the plurality held that the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim that cigarette manufacturers had fraudulently misrepresented and concealed a material fact was not pre-empted. That claim alleged a violation of the manufacturers\xe2\x80\x99 duty not to deceive\xe2\x80\x94a duty that is not \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d smoking and health. Id., at 528\xe2\x80\x93529. Respondents in this case also allege a violation of the duty not to deceive as that duty is codified in the MUTPA. The duty codified in that state statute, like the duty imposed by the state common-law rule at issue in Cipollone, has nothing to do with smoking and health.[Footnote 7]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners endeavor to distance themselves from that holding by arguing that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim is more analogous to the \xe2\x80\x9cwarning neutralization\xe2\x80\x9d claim found to be pre-empted in Cipollone. Although the plurality understood the plaintiff to have presented that claim as a \xe2\x80\x9ctheory of fraudulent misrepresentation,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 528, the gravamen of the claim was the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 failure to warn, as it was \xe2\x80\x9cpredicated on a state-law prohibition against statements in advertising and promotional materials that tend to minimize the health hazards associated with smoking,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 527. Thus understood, the Cipollone plurality\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of the warning neutralization claim has no application in this case.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners nonetheless contend that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim is like the pre-empted warning neutralization claim because it is based on statements that \xe2\x80\x9cmight create a false impression\xe2\x80\x9d rather than statements that are \xe2\x80\x9cinherently false.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 39. But the extent of the falsehood alleged does not alter the nature of the claim. Nothing in the Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s text or purpose or in the plurality opinion in Cipollone suggests that whether a claim is pre-empted turns in any way on the distinction between misleading and inherently false statements. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 misunderstanding is the same one that led the Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit, when confronted with a \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d descriptors claim, to reach a result at odds with the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision in this case. See Brown, 479 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 391\xe2\x80\x93393. Certainly, the extent of the falsehood alleged may bear on whether a plaintiff can prove her fraud claim, but the merits of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim are not before us.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once that erroneous distinction is set aside, it is clear that our holding in Cipollone that the common-law fraud claim was not pre-empted is directly applicable to the statutory claim at issue in this case. As was true of the claim in Cipollone, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim that the deceptive statements \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clowered tar and nicotine\xe2\x80\x9d induced them to purchase petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 product alleges a breach of the duty not to deceive.[Footnote 9] To be sure, the presence of the federally mandated warnings may bear on the materiality of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 allegedly fraudulent statements, \xe2\x80\x9cbut that possibility does not change [respondents\xe2\x80\x99] case from one about the statements into one about the warnings.\xe2\x80\x9d 501 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 44.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decision in Reilly is consistent with Cipollone\xe2\x80\x99s analysis. Reilly involved regulations promulgated by the Massachusetts attorney general \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in order to address the incidence of cigarette smoking and smokeless tobacco use by children under legal age \xe2\x80\xa6 [and] in order to prevent access to such products by underage customers.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 533 U. S., at 533 (quoting 940 Code Mass. Regs. \xc2\xa721.01 (2000)). The regulations did not pertain to the content of any advertising; rather, they placed a variety of restrictions on certain cigarette sales and the location of outdoor and point-of-sale cigarette advertising. The attorney general promulgated those restrictions pursuant to his statutory authority to prevent unfair or deceptive trade practices. Mass. Gen. Laws, ch. 93A, \xc2\xa72 (West 1996). But although the attorney general\xe2\x80\x99s authority derived from a general deceptive practices statute like the one at issue in this case, the challenged regulations targeted advertising that tended to promote tobacco use by children instead of prohibiting false or misleading statements. Thus, whereas the \xe2\x80\x9cprohibition\xe2\x80\x9d in Cipollone was the common-law fraud rule, the \xe2\x80\x9cprohibitions\xe2\x80\x9d in Reilly were the targeted regulations. Accordingly, our holding in Reilly that the regulations were pre-empted provides no support for an argument that a general prohibition of deceptive practices is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d the harm caused by the specific kind of deception to which the prohibition is applied in a given case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true, as petitioners argue, that the appeal of their advertising is based on the relationship between smoking and health. And although respondents have expressly repudiated any claim for damages for personal injuries, see App. 26a, their actual injuries likely encompass harms to health as well as the monetary injuries they allege. These arguments are unavailing, however, because the text of \xc2\xa71334(b) does not refer to harms related to smoking and health. Rather, it pre-empts only requirements and prohibitions\xe2\x80\x94i.e., rules\xe2\x80\x94that are based on smoking and health. The MUTPA says nothing about either \xe2\x80\x9csmoking\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9chealth.\xe2\x80\x9d It is a general rule that creates a duty not to deceive and is therefore unlike the regulations at issue in Reilly.[Footnote 11]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners argue in the alternative that we should reject the express pre-emption framework established by the Cipollone plurality and relied on by the Court in Reilly. In so doing, they invoke the reasons set forth in the separate opinions of Justice Blackmun (who especially criticized the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s holding that the failure-to-warn claim was pre-empted) and Justice Scalia (who argued that the fraud claim also should be pre-empted). While we again acknowledge that our analysis of these claims may lack \xe2\x80\x9ctheoretical elegance,\xe2\x80\x9d we remain persuaded that it represents \xe2\x80\x9ca fair understanding of congressional purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Cipollone, 505 U. S., at 529\xe2\x80\x93530, n. 27 (plurality opinion).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners also contend that the plurality opinion is inconsistent with our decisions in American Airlines, Inc. v. Wolens, 513 U. S. 219 (1995), and Riegel v. Medtronic, Inc., 552 U. S. ___ (2008). Both cases, however, are inapposite\xe2\x80\x94the first because it involved a pre-emption provision much broader than the Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s, and the second because it involved precisely the type of state rule that Congress had intended to pre-empt.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At issue in Wolens was the pre-emptive effect of the Airline Deregulation Act of 1978 (ADA), 49\xc2\xa0U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa71305(a)(1) (1988 ed.), which prohibits States from enacting or enforcing any law \xe2\x80\x9crelating to rates, routes, or services of any air carrier.\xe2\x80\x9d The plaintiffs in that case sought to bring a claim under the Illinois Consumer Fraud and Deceptive Business Practices Act, Ill. Comp. Stat., ch. 815, \xc2\xa7505 (West 1992). Our conclusion that the state-law claim was pre-empted turned on the unusual breadth of the ADA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision. We had previously held that the meaning of the key phrase in the ADA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98relating to rates, routes, or services,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is a broad one. Morales v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 504 U. S. 374, 383\xe2\x80\x93384 (1992) (emphasis added). Relying on precedents construing the pre-emptive effect of the same phrase in the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71144(a), we concluded that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d indicates Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to pre-empt a large area of state law to further its purpose of deregulating the airline industry. 504 U. S., at 383\xe2\x80\x93384.[Footnote 12] Unquestionably, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d has a broader scope than the Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s reference to rules \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d smoking and health; whereas \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d is synonymous with \xe2\x80\x9chaving a connection with,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 384, \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d describes a more direct relationship, see Safeco Ins. Co. of America v. Burr, 551 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 13) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn common talk, the phrase \xe2\x80\x98based on\xe2\x80\x99 indicates a but-for causal relationship and thus a necessary logical condition\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on Riegel is similarly misplaced. The plaintiffs in Riegel sought to bring common-law design, manufacturing, and labeling defect claims against the manufacturer of a faulty catheter. The case presented the question whether those claims were expressly pre-empted by the Medical Device Amendments of 1976 (MDA), 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7360c et seq. The MDA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption clause provides that no State \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98may establish or continue in effect with respect to a device \xe2\x80\xa6 any requirement\xe2\x80\x99 relating to safety or effectiveness that is different from, or in addition to, federal requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d Riegel, 552 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 14) (quoting 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7360k(a); emphasis deleted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The catheter at issue in Riegel had received premarket approval from the Food and Drug Administration (FDA). We concluded that premarket approval imposes \xe2\x80\x9crequirement[s] relating to safety [and] effectiveness\xe2\x80\x9d because the FDA requires a device that has received premarket approval to be made with almost no design, manufacturing, or labeling deviations from the specifications in its approved application. The plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 products liability claims fell within the core of the MDA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision because they sought to impose different requirements on precisely those aspects of the device that the FDA had approved. Unlike the Cipollone plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s fraud claim, which fell outside of the Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive reach because it did not seek to impose a prohibition \xe2\x80\x9cbased on smoking and health,\xe2\x80\x9d the Riegel plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 common-law products liability claims unquestionably sought to enforce \xe2\x80\x9crequirement[s] relating to safety or effectiveness\xe2\x80\x9d under the MDA. That the \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d language of the MDA\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision is, like the ADA\xe2\x80\x99s, much broader than the operative language of the Labeling Act provides an additional basis for distinguishing Riegel. Thus, contrary to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 suggestion, Riegel is entirely consistent with our holding in Cipollone.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, we conclude now, as the plurality did in Cipollone, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe phrase \xe2\x80\x98based on smoking and health\xe2\x80\x99 fairly but narrowly construed does not encompass the more general duty not to make fraudulent statements.\xe2\x80\x9d 505 U. S., at 529.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As an alternative to their express pre-emption argument, petitioners contend that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim is impliedly pre-empted because, if allowed to proceed, it would present an obstacle to a longstanding policy of the FTC. According to petitioners, the FTC has for decades promoted the development and consumption of low tar cigarettes and has encouraged consumers to rely on representations of tar and nicotine content based on Cambridge Filter Method testing in choosing among cigarette brands. Even if such a regulatory policy could provide a basis for obstacle pre-emption, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 description of the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s actions in this regard are inaccurate. The Government itself disavows any policy authorizing the use of \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clow tar\xe2\x80\x9d descriptors. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 16\xe2\x80\x9333.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1966, following the publication of the Surgeon General\xe2\x80\x99s report on smoking and health, the FTC issued an industry guidance stating its view that \xe2\x80\x9ca factual statement of the tar and nicotine content (expressed in milligrams) of the mainstream smoke from a cigarette,\xe2\x80\x9d as measured by Cambridge Filter Method testing, would not violate the FTC Act. App. 478a. The Commission made clear, however, that the guidance applied only to factual assertions of tar and nicotine yields and did not invite \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral representations \xe2\x80\xa6 made, expressly or by implication, as to reduction or elimination of health hazards.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 479a. A year later, the FTC reiterated its position in a letter to the National Association of Broadcasters. The letter explained that, as a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral rule,\xe2\x80\x9d the Commission would not challenge statements of tar and nicotine content when \xe2\x80\x9cthey are shown to be accurate and fully substantiated by tests conducted in accordance with the [Cambridge Filter Method].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 368a. In 1970, the FTC considered providing further guidance, proposing a rule that would have required manufacturers to disclose tar and nicotine yields as measured by Cambridge Filter Method testing. 35 Fed. Reg. 12671. The leading cigarette manufacturers responded by submitting a voluntary agreement under which they would disclose tar and nicotine content in their advertising, App. 899a\xe2\x80\x93900a, and the FTC suspended its rulemaking, 36 Fed. Reg. 784 (1971).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Based on these events, petitioners assert that \xe2\x80\x9cthe FTC has required tobacco companies to disclose tar and nicotine yields in cigarette advertising using a government-mandated testing methodology and has authorized them to use descriptors as shorthand references to those numerical test results.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 2 (emphasis in original). As the foregoing history shows, however, the FTC has in fact never required that cigarette manufacturers disclose tar and nicotine yields, nor has it condoned representations of those yields through the use of \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9clow tar\xe2\x80\x9d descriptors.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Subsequent Commission actions further undermine petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claim. After the tobacco companies agreed to report tar and nicotine yields as measured by the Cambridge Filter Method, the FTC continued to police cigarette companies\xe2\x80\x99 misleading use of test results. In 1983, the FTC responded to findings that tar and nicotine yields for Barclay cigarettes obtained through Cambridge Filter Method testing were deceptive because the cigarettes in fact delivered disproportionately more tar to smokers than other cigarettes with similar Cambridge Filter Method ratings. 48 Fed. Reg. 15954. And in 1995, the FTC found that a manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s representation \xe2\x80\x9cthat consumers will get less tar by smoking ten packs of Carlton brand cigarettes than by smoking a single pack of the other brands\xe2\x80\x9d was deceptive even though it was based on the results of Cambridge Filter Method testing. In\xc2\xa0re American Tobacco Co., 119 F.\xc2\xa0T. C. 3, 4. The FTC\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion was based on its recognition that, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n truth and in fact, consumers will not necessarily get less tar\xe2\x80\x9d due to \xe2\x80\x9csuch behavior as compensatory smoking.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[Footnote 13]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This history shows that, contrary to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 suggestion, the FTC has no longstanding policy authorizing collateral representations based on Cambridge Filter Method test results. Rather, the FTC has endeavored to inform consumers of the comparative tar and nicotine content of different cigarette brands and has in some instances prevented misleading representations of Cambridge Filter Method test results. The FTC\xe2\x80\x99s failure to require petitioners to correct their allegedly misleading use of \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d descriptors is not evidence to the contrary; agency nonenforcement of a federal statute is not the same as a policy of approval. Cf. Sprietsma v. Mercury Marine, 537 U. S. 51 (2002) (holding that the Coast Guard\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to regulate propeller guards did not impliedly pre-empt petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s tort claims).[Footnote 14]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More telling are the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s recent statements regarding the use of \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clow tar\xe2\x80\x9d descriptors. In 1997, the Commission observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here are no official definitions for\xe2\x80\x9d the terms \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clow tar,\xe2\x80\x9d and it sought comments on whether \xe2\x80\x9cthere [is] a need for official guidance with respect to the terms\xe2\x80\x9d and whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe descriptors convey implied health claims.\xe2\x80\x9d 62 Fed. Reg. 48163. In November 2008, following public notice and comment, the Commission rescinded its 1966 guidance concerning the Cambridge Filter Method. 73 Fed. Reg. 74500. The rescission is a response to \xe2\x80\x9ca consensus among the public health and scientific communities that the Cambridge Filter method is sufficiently flawed that statements of tar and nicotine yields as measured by that method are not likely to help consumers make informed decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 74503. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s notice of its proposal to rescind the guidance also reiterated the original limits of that guidance, noting that it \xe2\x80\x9conly addresse[d] simple factual statements of tar and nicotine yields. It d[id] not apply to other conduct or express or implied representations, even if they concern[ed] tar and nicotine yields.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 40351.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, neither the handful of industry guidances and consent orders on which petitioners rely nor the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s inaction with regard to \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d descriptors even arguably justifies the pre-emption of state deceptive practices rules like the MUTPA.

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude, as we did in Cipollone, that the Labeling Act does not pre-empt state-law claims like respondents\xe2\x80\x99 that are predicated on the duty not to deceive. We also hold that the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s various decisions with respect to statements of tar and nicotine content do not impliedly pre-empt respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim. Respondents still must prove that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 use of \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clowered tar\xe2\x80\x9d descriptors in fact violated the state deceptive practices statute, but neither the Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision nor the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s actions in this field prevent a jury from considering that claim. Accordingly, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This appeal requires the Court to revisit its decision in Cipollone v. Liggett Group, Inc., 505 U. S. 504 (1992). As in that case, the question before us is whether state-law claims alleging that cigarette manufacturers misled the public about the health effects of cigarettes are pre-empted by the Federal Cigarette Labeling and Advertising Act, as amended in 1969 (Labeling Act or Act). The Labeling Act requires that specific health warnings be placed on all cigarette packaging and advertising, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71333, in order to eliminate \xe2\x80\x9cdiverse, nonuniform, and confusing cigarette labeling and advertising regulations with respect to any relationship between smoking and health,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71331. To that end, \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act pre-empts any \xe2\x80\x9crequirement or prohibition based on smoking and health \xe2\x80\xa6 imposed under State law with respect to the advertising or promotion of any cigarettes.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71334(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whether \xc2\xa75(b) pre-empts state common-law claims divided the Court in Cipollone. The plurality opinion found some claims expressly pre-empted and others not, depending on whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe legal duty that is the predicate of the common-law damages action constitutes a requirement or prohibition based on smoking and health \xe2\x80\xa6 imposed under State law with respect to \xe2\x80\xa6 advertising or promotion.\xe2\x80\x9d 505 U. S., at 524 (internal quotation marks omitted; emphasis added). A majority of the Court disagreed with the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s predicate-duty approach. Id., at 543 (Blackmun, J., concurring in part, concurring in judgment in part, and dissenting in part); id., at 552\xe2\x80\x93554 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part). In particular, Justice Scalia recognized that the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa75(b) created an unworkable test for pre-emption with little or no relationship to the text of the statute. Id., at 544, 555\xe2\x80\x93556. The intervening years have vindicated Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s critical assessment; the lower courts have consistently expressed frustration at the difficulty in applying the Cipollone plurality\xe2\x80\x99s test. Moreover, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s recent pre-emption decisions have undermined, and in some cases overruled, central aspects of the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s atextual approach to express pre-emption generally, Riegel v. Medtronic, Inc., 552 U. S. ___ (2008), and to \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act specifically, Lorillard Tobacco Co. v. Reilly, 533 U. S. 525 (2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority today ignores these problems and adopts the methodology of the Cipollone plurality as governing law. As a consequence, the majority concludes that state-law liability for deceiving purchasers about the health effects of smoking light cigarettes is not a \xe2\x80\x9crequirement or prohibition based on smoking and health\xe2\x80\x9d under the Labeling Act. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s fidelity to Cipollone is unwise and unnecessary. The Court should instead provide the lower courts with a clear test that advances Congress\xe2\x80\x99 stated goals by interpreting \xc2\xa75(b) to expressly pre-empt any claim that \xe2\x80\x9cimposes an obligation \xe2\x80\xa6 because of the effect of smoking upon health.\xe2\x80\x9d Cipollone, supra, at 554 (opinion of Scalia, J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 lawsuit under the Maine Unfair Trade Practices Act (MUTPA), Me. Rev. Stat. Ann., Tit. 5, \xc2\xa7207 (Supp. 2008), is expressly pre-empted under \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act. The civil action is premised on the allegation that the cigarette manufacturers misled respondents into believing that smoking light cigarettes would be healthier for them than smoking regular cigarettes. A judgment in respondents\xe2\x80\x99 favor will thus result in a \xe2\x80\x9crequirement\xe2\x80\x9d that petitioners represent the effects of smoking on health in a particular way in their advertising and promotion of light cigarettes. Because liability in this case is thereby premised on the effect of smoking on health, I would hold that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law claims are expressly pre-empted by \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Cipollone, a smoker and her spouse brought state common-law claims for fraud, breach of warranty, and failure to warn against cigarette manufacturers for their alleged failure to adequately disclose the health risks of smoking. 505 U. S., at 509. As here, the cigarette manufacturer asserted that the claims were pre-empted by \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In deciding the case, the Court could not agree on the meaning of the Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s express pre-emption provision. It produced three separate opinions, none of which reflected the views of a majority of Justices. Relying heavily on a \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption against the pre-emption of state police power regulations,\xe2\x80\x9d a plurality opinion by Justice Stevens settled on a \xe2\x80\x9cnarrow reading\xe2\x80\x9d of the Labeling Act that tested \xc2\xa75(b)\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive effect under a claim-by-claim approach. Id., at 524. This approach considered each state-law claim and asked whether it is predicated \xe2\x80\x9con a duty \xe2\x80\x98based on smoking and health.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 528; see also id., at 524. If so, the claim is pre-empted. Id., at 524, 528. If, however, the claim is predicated on a \xe2\x80\x9cmore general obligation\xe2\x80\x9d under state law, it may proceed. Id., at 528\xe2\x80\x93529.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying a test that it conceded lacked \xe2\x80\x9ctheoretical elegance,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 530, n.\xc2\xa027, the plurality held that the failure-to-warn claims were pre-empted \xe2\x80\x9cto the extent that those claims rel[ied] on omissions or inclusions in \xe2\x80\xa6 advertising or promotions\xe2\x80\x9d of cigarettes. Id., at 531. The same was true for one of the fraud claims, which alleged that the cigarette manufacturers had used their advertising to neutralize the federally required warning labels. Id., at 527\xe2\x80\x93528. The plurality determined that these claims were \xe2\x80\x9cpredicated on a state-law prohibition against statements \xe2\x80\xa6 that tend to minimize the health hazards associated with smoking.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 527. Thus, according to the plurality, these state-law claims sought recovery under the theory that the cigarette manufacturer breached a duty based on smoking or health. But the plurality found that the other fraud claim, which alleged misrepresentation or concealment of a material fact, was not pre-empted because it was based on a more general state-law obligation: \xe2\x80\x9cthe duty not to deceive.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 528\xe2\x80\x93529.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Blackmun, writing for three Justices, departed from the plurality on the antecedent question whether the Labeling Act pre-empted state common-law damages claims at all. Id., at 535\xe2\x80\x93542 (opinion, joined by Kennedy and Souter, JJ., concurring in part, concurring in judgment in part, and dissenting in part). He concluded that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98State law\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa75(b) referred only to \xe2\x80\x9cpositive enactments such as statutes and regulations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 535. But Justice Blackmun specifically noted that even if state common-law claims were within the scope of the Labeling Act, he could not join the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s claim-by-claim approach because he \xe2\x80\x9cperceive[d] no principled basis for many of the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s asserted distinctions among the common-law claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 543. Justice Blackmun wrote that Congress could not have \xe2\x80\x9cintended to create such a hodgepodge of allowed and disallowed claims when it amended the pre-emption provision in 1970,\xe2\x80\x9d and lamented the \xe2\x80\x9cdifficulty lower courts w[ould] encounter in attempting to implement\xe2\x80\x9d the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s test. Id., at 543\xe2\x80\x93544.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, writing for two Justices, also faulted the plurality for its claim-by-claim approach. Id., at 544\xe2\x80\x93556 (opinion, joined by Thomas, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part). Although he agreed with the plurality that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98State law\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa75(b) encompassed state common-law claims as well as state statutes and regulations, id., at 548\xe2\x80\x93549, Justice Scalia objected to the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s invocation of a presumption against pre-emption to narrowly interpret \xc2\xa75(b), id., at 544, 545\xe2\x80\x93547. Because Congress had expressed its intent to pre-empt state law by enacting \xc2\xa75(b), the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cresponsibility [was] to apply to the text ordinary principles of statutory construction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 545.[Footnote 1] By employing its \xe2\x80\x9cnewly crafted doctrine of narrow construction,\xe2\x80\x9d Justice Scalia wrote, the plurality arrived at a cramped and unnatural construction of \xc2\xa75(b) that failed to give effect to the statutory text. Id., at 544\xe2\x80\x93548.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying \xe2\x80\x9cordinary principles\xe2\x80\x9d of statutory construction, id., at 548, Justice Scalia determined that the proper test for pre-emption of state-law claims under \xc2\xa75(b) was far less complicated than the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s claim-by-claim approach. As he explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nce one is forced to select a consistent methodology for evaluating whether a given legal duty is \xe2\x80\x98based on smoking and health,\xe2\x80\x99 it becomes obvious that the methodology must focus not upon the ultimate source of the duty \xe2\x80\xa6 but upon its proximate application.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 553. This \xe2\x80\x9cproximate application\xe2\x80\x9d test, therefore, focuses not on the state-law duty invoked by the plaintiff, but on the effect of the suit on the cigarette manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the \xe2\x80\x9crequirement\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cprohibition\xe2\x80\x9d that would be imposed under state law. Put simply, if, \xe2\x80\x9cwhatever the source of the duty, [the claim] imposes an obligation \xe2\x80\xa6 because of the effect of smoking upon health,\xe2\x80\x9d it is pre-empted. Id., at 554; see also id., at 555 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe test for pre-emption in this setting should be one of practical compulsion, i.e., whether the law practically compels the manufacturers to engage in behavior that Congress has barred the States from prescribing directly\xe2\x80\x9d). Justice Scalia also seconded Justice Blackmun\xe2\x80\x99s concern that the lower courts would find the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s distinctions between materially identical state-law claims to be incapable of application: \xe2\x80\x9cA disposition that raises more questions than it answers does not serve the country well.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 556.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sixteen years later, we must confront Cipollone to resolve the question presented in this case: whether respondents\xe2\x80\x99 class-action claims for fraudulent marketing under the MUTPA are pre-empted by \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act. The majority adheres to Cipollone because it \xe2\x80\x9cremain[s] persuaded\xe2\x80\x9d that the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the \xc2\xa75(b) was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fair.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. I disagree. The Court should discard the Cipollone plurality\xe2\x80\x99s ill-conceived predicate-duty approach and replace it with Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s far more workable and textually sound \xe2\x80\x9cproximate application\xe2\x80\x9d test.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority does not assert that the Cipollone plurality opinion is binding precedent, and rightly so. Because the \xe2\x80\x9cplurality opinion \xe2\x80\xa6 did not represent the views of a majority of the Court, we are not bound by its reasoning.\xe2\x80\x9d CTS Corp. v. Dynamics Corp. of America, 481 U. S. 69, 81 (1987) (footnote omitted). At most, Cipollone is a \xe2\x80\x9cpoint of reference for further discussion.\xe2\x80\x9d Texas v. Brown, 460 U. S. 730, 737 (1983) (plurality opinion). But even if the plurality opinion had some force beyond its mere persuasive value, it nevertheless should be abandoned. It is unworkable; it has been overtaken by more recent decisions of this Court; and it cannot be reconciled with a commonsense reading of the text of \xc2\xa75(b).

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As predicted by a majority of the Justices in Cipollone, the plurality opinion\xe2\x80\x99s claim-by-claim approach has proved unworkable in the lower federal courts and state courts. The District Court in this case properly observed that \xe2\x80\x9ccourts remain divided about what the decision means and how to apply it\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cCipollone\xe2\x80\x99s distinctions, though clear in theory, defy clear application.\xe2\x80\x9d 436 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa02d 132, 142 (Me. 2006). Other courts have expressed similar frustration with the Cipollone framework. See, e.g., Glassner v. R.\xc2\xa0J. Reynolds Tobacco Co., 223 F.\xc2\xa03d 343, 348 (CA6 2000) (\xe2\x80\x9cApplying the plurality opinion in Cipollone to the Complaint in the present case is no easy task\xe2\x80\x9d); Huddleston v. R.\xc2\xa0J. Reynolds Tobacco Co., 66 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa02d 1370, 1380 (ND Ga. 1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt would be an understatement to say that it is difficult to apply the plurality opinion in Cipollone to the Amended Complaint in this case. It is an impossibility\xe2\x80\x9d); In\xc2\xa0re Welding Fume Prods. Liability Litigation, 364 F. Supp. 2d 669, 681, n.\xc2\xa013 (ND Ohio 2005) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n Cipollone, the Supreme Court \xe2\x80\xa6 delivered a fractured plurality opinion that is not easy to comprehend\xe2\x80\x9d); Whiteley v. Philip Morris, Inc., 117 Cal. App. 4th 635, 670, 11 Cal. Rptr. 3d 807, 835\xe2\x80\x93836 (2004) (\xe2\x80\x9c[Cipollone is] \xe2\x80\x98difficult\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 due to the inherent contradiction at the core of the case\xe2\x80\x9d); Mangini v. R.\xc2\xa0J. Reynolds Tobacco Co., 21 Cal. Rptr. 2d 232, 244 (Cal. App. 1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cCipollone draws no bright lines amenable to easy application\xe2\x80\x9d), aff\xe2\x80\x99d, 7 Cal. 4th 1057, 875 P.\xc2\xa02d 73 (1994).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court should not retain an interpretative test that has proved incapable of implementation. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he mischievous consequences to litigants and courts alike from the perpetuation of an unworkable rule are too great.\xe2\x80\x9d Swift & Co. v. Wickham, 382 U. S. 111, 116 (1965); Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Wisconsin Right to Life, Inc., 551 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 20) (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cStare decisis considerations carry little weight when an erroneous \xe2\x80\x98governing decisio[n]\xe2\x80\x99 has created an \xe2\x80\x98unworkable\xe2\x80\x99 legal regime\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Payne v. Tennessee, 501 U. S. 808, 827 (1991)). We owe far more to the lower courts, which depend on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s guidance, and to litigants, who must conform their actions to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of federal law. The Cipollone plurality\xe2\x80\x99s test for pre-emption under \xc2\xa75(b) should be abandoned for this reason alone.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, in the years since Cipollone was decided, this Court has altered its doctrinal approach to express pre-emption. The Cipollone plurality justified what it described as the \xe2\x80\x9ctheoretical [in]elegance\xe2\x80\x9d of its construction of \xc2\xa75(b) by relying on the presumption against pre-emption, which, it argued, required a narrow, but \xe2\x80\x9cfair,\xe2\x80\x9d construction of the statute. 505 U. S., at 530, n.\xc2\xa027. See, e.g., id., at 518 (majority opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis presumption reinforces the appropriateness of a narrow reading of \xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 523 (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e must \xe2\x80\xa6 narrowly construe the precise language of \xc2\xa75(b)\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 524 (\xc2\xa75(b) must be given \xe2\x80\x9ca fair but narrow reading\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 529 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e conclude that the phrase \xe2\x80\x98based on smoking and health\xe2\x80\x99 fairly but narrowly construed does not encompass the more general duty not to make fraudulent statements\xe2\x80\x9d). Of course, as Justice Scalia explained, there was nothing \xe2\x80\x9cfair\xe2\x80\x9d about imposing an artificially narrow construction on the Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emption provision. See id., at 545 (explaining that the presumption against pre-emption \xe2\x80\x9cdissolves once there is conclusive evidence of intent to pre-empt in the express words of the statute itself\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since Cipollone, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the presumption against pre-emption has waned in the express pre-emption context. In 2002, for example, the Court unanimously explained that the \xe2\x80\x9ctask of statutory construction must in the first instance focus on the plain wording of the [express pre-emption] clause, which necessarily contains the best evidence of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 pre-emptive intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Sprietsma v. Mercury Marine, 537 U. S. 51, 62\xe2\x80\x9363 (internal quotation marks omitted). Without referring to any presumption against pre-emption, the Court decided that the Federal Boat Safety Act of 1971\xe2\x80\x99s express pre-emption provision did not pre-empt state-law claims. Id., at 62\xe2\x80\x9364. Most other decisions since Cipollone also have refrained from invoking the presumption in the context of express pre-emption. See, e.g., Rowe v. New Hampshire Motor Transp. Assn., 552 U. S. ___ (2008); Engine Mfrs. Assn. v. South Coast Air Quality Management Dist., 541 U. S. 246 (2004); Buckman Co. v. Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 Legal Comm., 531 U. S. 341 (2001); United States v. Locke, 529 U. S. 89 (2000); Geier v. American Honda Motor Co., 529 U. S. 861 (2000).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court has invoked the presumption sporadically during this time frame. As the majority notes, ante, at 5, Medtronic, Inc. v. Lohr, 518 U. S. 470 (1996), applied the presumption against pre-emption in deciding that the federal manufacturing and labeling requirements of the Medical Device Amendments of 1976 (MDA) did not pre-empt state common-law claims. Id., at 500\xe2\x80\x93501. Like Cipollone before it, Lohr produced a fractured decision featuring three opinions. 518 U. S., at 474 (opinion of Stevens, J.), id., at 503 (Breyer, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment), id., at 509 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). And, like Cipollone, Lohr\xe2\x80\x99s approach to express pre-emption predates the Court\xe2\x80\x99s recent jurisprudence on the topic. In fact, this Court last year revisited the pre-emption provision of the MDA, 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7360k(a)(1), and did not employ any presumption against pre-emption. Riegel v. Medtronic, Inc., 552 U. S. ___ (2008). See infra, at 11-13.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More recently, in Reilly, 533 U. S. 525, a case revisiting the meaning of \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act, the Court briefly alluded to the presumption, but did not rely on it to reach its decision. See id., at 541\xe2\x80\x93542, 546\xe2\x80\x93551. Indeed, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s cursory treatment of the presumption in Reilly stands in stark contrast to the First Circuit decision it reversed; the First Circuit relied heavily on the \xe2\x80\x9cfull force\xe2\x80\x9d of the presumption to determine that the regulations at issue were not pre-empted. See Consolidated Cigar Corp. v. Reilly, 218 F.\xc2\xa03d 30, 38\xe2\x80\x9341 (2000). This Court, in overturning that judgment, declined to employ the presumption in its construction of \xc2\xa75(b). See Reilly, 533 U. S., at 546\xe2\x80\x93551. Justice Stevens highlighted this very point in dissent, arguing that if the presumption had been faithfully applied, the result would have been different. Id., at 591\xe2\x80\x93593.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority also relies on Bates v. Dow Agrosciences LLC, 544 U. S. 431 (2005), where the presumption was again mentioned, but only in dicta. As in Reilly, the presumption did not drive the Court\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the statute at issue. 544 U. S., at 449 (explaining that the presumption meant just that the holding of no pre-emption would have been the same \xe2\x80\x9ceven if [respondent\xe2\x80\x99s] alternative [construction of the statute] were just as plausible as our reading of the text\xe2\x80\x9d); see also id., at 457 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part) (agreeing that the case should be vacated and remanded and reiterating that the \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption does not apply \xe2\x80\xa6 when Congress has included within a statute an express pre-emption provision\xe2\x80\x9d). At bottom, although the Court\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of the presumption against pre-emption has not been uniform, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s express pre-emption cases since Cipollone have marked a retreat from reliance on it to distort the statutory text.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If any doubt remained, it was eliminated last Term in Riegel. The question in Riegel, as noted above, was whether the MDA expressly pre-empts state common-law claims \xe2\x80\x9cchallenging the safety and effectiveness of a medical device given premarket approval by the Food and Drug Administration.\xe2\x80\x9d 552 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 1). Over the dissent of one Justice, the Court held that the state-law claims were pre-empted because the requirements the plaintiffs sought to impose were \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98different from, or in addition to, any requirement applicable \xe2\x80\xa6 to the device\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d under federal law. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 2) (quoting 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7360k(a)(1)). The Court interpreted the statute without reference to the presumption or any perceived need to impose a narrow construction on the provision in order to protect the police power of the States. Rather, the Court simply construed the MDA in accordance with ordinary principles of statutory construction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This was not accidental. The dissent focused on the Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to invoke the presumption in order to save the state-law claims from pre-emption. 552 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at ___) (opinion of Ginsburg, J.). The dissent was adamant that \xe2\x80\x9c[f]ederal laws containing a preemption clause do not automatically escape the presumption against pre-emption.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x933); id., at ___ (slip op., at 3) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhere the text of a pre-emption clause is open to more than one plausible reading, courts ordinarily \xe2\x80\x98accept the reading that disfavors pre-emption\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Bates, supra, at 449)). In accordance with the presumption, the dissent would have found the state-law claims under review to fall beyond the reach of the MDA\xe2\x80\x99s express pre-emption provision. 552 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at ___); see also id., at ___, n.\xc2\xa08 (slip op., at 6, n.\xc2\xa08); id., at ___, n.\xc2\xa09 (slip op., at 7, n.\xc2\xa09) (rejecting the majority\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa7360(d) because \xe2\x80\x9cthe presumption against pre-emption [is] operative even in construing a preemption clause\xe2\x80\x9d). Given the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s clear call for the use of the presumption against pre-emption, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to invoke it was necessarily a rejection of any role for the presumption in construing the statute.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens also declined to invoke the presumption in his opinion. Id., at ___ (opinion concurring in part and concurring in judgment). In his view, the \xe2\x80\x9csignificance of the pre-emption provision in the [MDA] was not fully appreciated until many years after it was enacted\xe2\x80\x9d and, therefore, it is \xe2\x80\x9ca statute whose text and general objective cover territory not actually envisioned by its authors.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 1). But Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 opinion in Riegel\xe2\x80\x94unlike the majority opinion here, the plurality opinion in Cipollone, and the dissenting opinion in Riegel\xe2\x80\x94did not invoke the presumption to bend the text of the statute to meet the perceived purpose of Congress. Instead, Justice Stevens correctly found that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98it is ultimately the provisions of our laws rather than the principal concerns of our legislators by which we are governed.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 552 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 1) (quoting Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc., 523 U. S. 75, 79\xe2\x80\x9380 (1998)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of Riegel, there is no authority for invoking the presumption against pre-emption in express pre-emption cases. The majority here thus turns to Lohr to revive the presumption and, in turn, to justify its restrictive reading of the Labeling Act\xe2\x80\x99s express pre-emption provision. But, as Riegel plainly shows, the Court is no longer willing to unreasonably interpret expressly pre-emptive federal laws in the name of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98congressional purpose,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 14, or because \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has legislated in a field traditionally occupied by the States,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 5. The text of the statute must control.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Riegel also undermined Cipollone in an even more fundamental way: It conclusively decided that a common-law cause of action imposes a state-law \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98requiremen[t]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that may be pre-empted by federal law. 552 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 11) (\xe2\x80\x9cAbsent other indication, reference to a State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98requirements\xe2\x80\x99 includes its common-law duties . . . . Indeed, one would think that tort law, applied by juries under a negligence or strict-liability standard, is less deserving of preservation [than regulatory legislation]\xe2\x80\x9d). Justice Blackmun\xe2\x80\x99s contrary interpretation of \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act in Cipollone, 505 U. S., at 538\xe2\x80\x93539 (opinion concurring in part, concurring in judgment in part, and dissenting in part), which provided the votes necessary for the judgment, thus is no longer tenable. In light of Riegel\xe2\x80\x99s rejection of the presumption against pre-emption relied on by the plurality, as well as the definition of \xe2\x80\x9crequirements\xe2\x80\x9d relied on in Justice Blackmun\xe2\x80\x99s concurring opinion, Cipollone\xe2\x80\x99s approach to express pre-emption is nothing more than \xe2\x80\x9ca remnant of abandoned doctrine.\xe2\x80\x9d Planned Parenthood of Southeastern Pa. v. Casey, 505 U. S. 833, 855 (1992).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Cipollone plurality\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act was further undermined by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Reilly, 533 U. S. 525. There, the Court confronted regulations imposed by the Massachusetts attorney general on the location of tobacco advertising pursuant to the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s unfair trade practices statute. Id., at 533\xe2\x80\x93536. The Court found the regulations\xe2\x80\x94to the extent they applied to cigarettes\xe2\x80\x94expressly pre-empted because, although Massachusetts remained free to enact \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally applicable zoning restrictions,\xe2\x80\x9d its imposition of \xe2\x80\x9cspecial requirements or prohibitions \xe2\x80\x98based on smoking and health\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98with respect to the advertising or promotion of cigarettes\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d fell within the ambit of \xc2\xa75(b)\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive sweep. Id., at 551.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reilly did not ignore Cipollone. It cited the plurality opinion extensively in its discussion of the basic history and text of the Labeling Act. 533 U. S., at 540\xe2\x80\x93546. But in analyzing whether the regulations enacted by the Massachusetts attorney general were expressly pre-empted, the Court was silent about Cipollone. 533 U. S., at 546\xe2\x80\x93551. Unlike the District Court, which saw \xe2\x80\x9cthe central question for purposes of pre-emption [as] whether the regulations create[d] a predicate legal duty based on smoking and health,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 537, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s substantive examination of the regulations under \xc2\xa75(b) included no mention of the Cipollone plurality\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpredicate duty\xe2\x80\x9d test. See 533 U. S., at 546\xe2\x80\x93551. Instead, the Court disagreed with \xe2\x80\x9cthe Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s narrow construction\xe2\x80\x9d of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98based on smoking and health\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d language, and concluded that the regulations were pre-empted because they were \xe2\x80\x9cmotivated by\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cintertwined with\xe2\x80\x9d the concerns about smoking and health. Id., at 547\xe2\x80\x93548.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reilly, therefore, cannot be reconciled with the Cipollone plurality\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act. The regulations at issue in Reilly were enacted to implement a Massachusetts state law imposing a duty against unfair and deceptive trade practices\xe2\x80\x94the same predicate duty asserted under the MUTPA in this case. 533 U. S., at 533. The state-law duty at issue in Reilly was no less general than the state-law duty at issue in this case or the state-law fraud claims confronted in Cipollone. Compare Mass. Gen. Laws, ch.\xc2\xa093A, \xc2\xa72(a) (West 1996) (\xe2\x80\x9cUnfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce are hereby declared unlawful\xe2\x80\x9d), with Me. Rev. Stat. Ann., Tit. 5, \xc2\xa7207 (Supp. 2008) (\xe2\x80\x9cUnfair methods of competition and unfair or deceptive acts or practices in the conduct of any trade or commerce are declared unlawful\xe2\x80\x9d), and Cipollone, supra, at 528 (explaining that the \xe2\x80\x9cpredicate\xe2\x80\x9d of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s fraudulent misrepresentation claim was \xe2\x80\x9ca state-law duty not to make false statements of material fact or to conceal such facts\xe2\x80\x9d). Faithful application of the Cipollone plurality opinion, therefore, would have required the Court in Reilly to uphold the regulations. Indeed, Justice Stevens argued as much in his dissent. 533 U. S., at 597 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]ary a word in any of the three Cipollone opinions supports the thesis that \xc2\xa75 should be interpreted to pre-empt state regulation of the location of signs advertising cigarettes\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And yet, the majority today finds that Reilly and Cipollone are perfectly compatible. It contends that, although the regulations in question in Reilly \xe2\x80\x9cderived from a general deceptive practices statute like the one at issue in this case,\xe2\x80\x9d they were pre-empted because they \xe2\x80\x9ctargeted advertising that tended to promote tobacco use by children instead of prohibiting false or misleading statements.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. According to the majority, that legal duty contrasts with the regulations here, as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he MUTPA says nothing about either \xe2\x80\x98smoking\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98health.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13; see also ante, at 10. But the Cipollone plurality expressly rejected any distinction between targeted regulations like those in Reilly and general duties imposed by the common law. 505 U. S., at 522. In fact, the general duties underlying the failure-to-warn and warning-neutralization claims in Cipollone\xe2\x80\x94which the plurality found to be pre-empted\xe2\x80\x94say nothing about smoking and health. Id., at 524; see also id., at 553 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part) (noting that the duty to warn about a product\xe2\x80\x99s dangers was not \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically crafted with an eye toward \xe2\x80\x98smoking and health\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, Reilly is better understood as establishing that even a general duty can impose requirements or prohibitions based on smoking and health. Reilly weakened the force of the Cipollone plurality\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpredicate duty\xe2\x80\x9d approach to the pre-emptive effect of \xc2\xa75(b) and cast doubt on its continuing utility.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the Cipollone plurality\xe2\x80\x99s approach should be discarded because its \xe2\x80\x9cpredicate duty\xe2\x80\x9d approach is unpersuasive as an initial matter. In considering the warning-neutralization claim, for example, the Cipollone plurality asserted that the claim is predicated on a state-law prohibition against minimizing the health risks associated with smoking. 505 U. S., at 527. The Court today reaffirms this view. Ante, at 10; see also ante, at 13 (describing \xc2\xa75(b) as expressly pre-empting \xe2\x80\x9crules \xe2\x80\xa6 that are based on smoking and health\xe2\x80\x9d). But every products liability action, including a failure-to-warn action, applies generally to all products. See Cipollone, supra, at 553 (opinion of Scalia, J.). Thus, the \xe2\x80\x9cduty\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9crule\xe2\x80\x9d involved in a failure-to-warn claim is no more specific to smoking and health than is a common-law fraud claim based on the \xe2\x80\x9cduty\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9crule\xe2\x80\x9d not to use deceptive or misleading trade practices. Yet only for the latter was the Cipollone plurality content to ignore the context in which the claim is asserted. This shifting level of generality was identified as a logical weakness in the original Cipollone plurality decision by a majority of the Court, 505 U. S., at 543 (Blackmun, J., concurring in part, concurring in judgment in part, and dissenting in part); id., at 553\xe2\x80\x93554 (opinion of Scalia, J.), and it remains equally unconvincing today.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is therefore unsurprising that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s defense of the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s confusing test is confined to one sentence and a footnote. See ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhile we again acknowledge that our analysis of these claims may lack \xe2\x80\x98theoretical elegance,\xe2\x80\x99 we remain persuaded that it represents \xe2\x80\x98a fair understanding of congressional purpose\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Cipollone, supra, at 529\xe2\x80\x93530, n.\xc2\xa027)); ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa07. The majority instead argues that this approach \xe2\x80\x9cfails to explain why Congress would . . . permi[t] cigarette manufacturers to engage in fraudulent advertising.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa07. But no explanation is necessary; the text speaks for itself. Congress has pre-empted only those claims that would impose \xe2\x80\x9crequirement[s] or prohibition[s] based on smoking and health.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71334(b). Thus, if cigarette manufacturers were to falsely advertise their products as \xe2\x80\x9cAmerican-made,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cthe official cigarette of Major League Baseball,\xe2\x80\x9d state-law claims arising from that wrongful behavior would not be pre-empted.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, contrary to the majority\xe2\x80\x99s policy arguments, faithful application of the statutory language does not authorize fraudulent advertising with respect to smoking and health.[Footnote 3] Any misleading promotional statements for cigarettes remain subject to federal regulatory oversight under the Labeling Act. See \xc2\xa71336. The relevant question thus is not whether \xe2\x80\x9cpetitioners will be prohibited from selling as \xe2\x80\x98light\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98low tar\xe2\x80\x99 only those cigarettes that are not actually light and do not actually deliver less tar and nicotine.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12, n.\xc2\xa010. Rather, the issue is whether the Labeling Act allows regulators and juries to decide, on a state-by-state basis, whether petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 light and low-tar descriptors were in fact fraudulent, or instead whether \xc2\xa75(b) charged the Federal Government with reaching a comprehensive judgment with respect to this question.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress chose a uniform federal standard. Under the Labeling Act, Congress \xe2\x80\x9cestablish[ed] a comprehensive Federal Program to deal with cigarette labeling and advertising,\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331, so that \xe2\x80\x9ccommerce and the national economy may . . . not [be] impeded by diverse, nonuniform, and confusing cigarette labeling and advertising regulations with respect to any relationship between smoking and health,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71331(2)(B).[Footnote 4] The majority\xe2\x80\x99s distorted interpretation of \xc2\xa75(b) defeats this express congressional purpose, opening the door to an untold number of deceptive-practices lawsuits across the country. The question whether marketing a light cigarette is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98misrepresentative\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in light of compensatory behavior \xe2\x80\x9cwould almost certainly be answered differently from State to State.\xe2\x80\x9d Cipollone, supra, 505 U. S., at 553 (opinion of Scalia, J.). This will inevitably result in the nonuniform imposition of liability for the marketing of light and/or low-tar cigarettes\xe2\x80\x94the precise problem that Congress intended \xc2\xa75(b) to remedy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of these serious flaws in the majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach, even if the Cipollone plurality opinion were binding precedent, the Court \xe2\x80\x9cshould not hesitate to allow our precedent to yield to the true meaning of an Act of Congress when our statutory precedent is \xe2\x80\x98unworkable\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98badly reasoned.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Clark v. Martinez, 543 U. S. 371, 402 (2005) (Thomas, J., dissenting) (quoting Holder v. Hall, 512 U. S. 874, 936 (1994) (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment), in turn quoting Payne, 501 U. S., at 827 (some internal quotation marks omitted)). Where, as here, there is \xe2\x80\x9cconfusion following a splintered decision,\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cis itself a reason for reexamining that decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Nichols v. United States, 511 U. S. 738, 746 (1994). When a decision of this Court has failed to properly interpret a statute, we should not \xe2\x80\x9cplace on the shoulders of Congress the burden of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own error.\xe2\x80\x9d Girouard v. United States, 328 U. S. 61, 69\xe2\x80\x9370 (1946).[Footnote 5]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying the proper test\xe2\x80\x94i.e., whether a jury verdict on respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims would \xe2\x80\x9cimpos[e] an obligation\xe2\x80\x9d on the cigarette manufacturer \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of the effect of smoking upon health,\xe2\x80\x9d Cipollone, supra, at 554 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part), respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law claims are expressly pre-empted by \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act. Respondents, longtime smokers of Marlboro Lights, claim that they have suffered an injury as a result of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 decision to advertise these cigarettes as \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d and/or \xe2\x80\x9clow-tar and low nicotine products.\xe2\x80\x9d 436 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 144\xe2\x80\x93145. They claim that petitioners marketed their cigarettes as \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d and/or \xe2\x80\x9clow-tar and low-nicotine products\xe2\x80\x9d despite knowledge that light-cigarette smokers would engage in compensatory behavior causing them to inhale at least as much tar and nicotine as smokers of regular cigarettes. Ibid. Respondents thus allege that they were misled into thinking that they were gaining a health advantage by smoking the light cigarettes, ibid., and, as a result, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 conduct was an \xe2\x80\x9cunfair or deceptive act or practice\xe2\x80\x9d under the MUTPA. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann., Tit. 5, \xc2\xa7207; 436 F. Supp. 2d, at 133.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims seek to impose liability on petitioners because of the effect that smoking light cigarettes had on their health. The alleged misrepresentation here\xe2\x80\x94that \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clow-tar\xe2\x80\x9d cigarettes are not as healthy as advertised\xe2\x80\x94is actionable only because of the effect that smoking light and low-tar cigarettes had on respondents\xe2\x80\x99 health. Otherwise, any alleged misrepresentation about the effect of the cigarettes on health would be immaterial for purposes of the MUTPA and would not be the source of the injuries that provided the impetus for the class-action lawsuit. See State v. Weinschenk, 2005 ME 28, \xc2\xb617, 868 A.\xc2\xa02d 200, 206 (\xe2\x80\x9cAn act or practice is deceptive [under the MUTPA] if it is a material representation, omission, act or practice that is likely to mislead consumers acting reasonably under the circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Therefore, with this suit, respondents seek to require the cigarette manufacturers to provide additional warnings about compensatory behavior, or to prohibit them from selling these products with the \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9clow-tar\xe2\x80\x9d descriptors. This is exactly the type of lawsuit that is pre-empted by the Labeling Act. Cf. Rowe, 552 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 6) (finding pre-emption of a Maine regulation of shipping of tobacco products where \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Maine law \xe2\x80\xa6 produces the very effect that the federal law sought to avoid\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the proper test for pre-emption is to look at the factual basis of a complaint to determine if a claim imposes a requirement based on smoking and health, there is no meaningful distinction to be drawn in this case between common-law failure-to-warn claims and claims under the MUTPA.[Footnote 6] As the majority readily admits, both types of claims impose duties with respect to the same conduct\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the marketing of \xe2\x80\x9clight,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9clow-tar,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clow-nicotine\xe2\x80\x9d cigarettes. See ante, at 11, n.\xc2\xa09. If the claims arise from identical conduct, the claims impose the same requirement or prohibition with respect to that conduct. And when that allegedly wrongful conduct involves misleading statements about the health effects of smoking a particular brand of cigarette, the liability and resulting requirement or prohibition are, by definition, based on smoking and health.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, at oral argument, respondents argued that their claims do not impose requirements based on smoking and health because the damages they seek to recover are not based on the effect of smoking on their health; rather, respondents are \xe2\x80\x9casking \xe2\x80\xa6 for the difference in value between a product [they] thought [they] were buying and a product [they] actually bought.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 29. But the requirement or prohibition covered by \xc2\xa75(b) is created by the imposition of liability for particular conduct\xe2\x80\x94here, the way in which petitioners marketed \xe2\x80\x9clight\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clow-tar,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clow-nicotine\xe2\x80\x9d cigarettes\xe2\x80\x94not by the manner in which respondents have chosen to measure their damages. No matter how respondents characterize their damages claim, they have not been injured for purposes of the MUTPA, and thus cannot recover, unless their decision to purchase the cigarettes had a negative effect on their health.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, respondents sought \xe2\x80\x9csuch injunctive relief as may be appropriate\xe2\x80\x9d in this case. App. 42a. The MUTPA specifically authorizes \xe2\x80\x9cother equitable relief, including an injunction,\xe2\x80\x9d to remedy unfair or deceptive trade practices. Me. Rev. Stat. Ann., Tit. 5, \xc2\xa7213(1) (West 2002). And a court-crafted injunction prohibiting petitioners from marketing light cigarettes would be no less a requirement or prohibition than the regulations found to be pre-empted in Reilly. In the end, no matter what form the remedy takes, the liability with respect to the specific claim still creates the requirement or prohibition. When that liability is necessarily premised on the effects of smoking on health, as respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims are here, the civil action is pre-empted by \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 The Court today elects to convert the Cipollone plurality opinion into binding law, notwithstanding its weakened doctrinal foundation, its atextual construction of the statute, and the lower courts\xe2\x80\x99 inability to apply its methodology. The resulting confusion about the nature of a claim\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpredicate duty\xe2\x80\x9d and inevitable disagreement in the lower courts as to what type of representations are \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmisleading\xe2\x80\x9d will have the perverse effect of increasing the nonuniformity of state regulation of cigarette advertising, the exact problem that Congress intended \xc2\xa75(b) to remedy. It may even force us to yet again revisit the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the Labeling Act. Because I believe that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims are pre-empted under \xc2\xa75(b) of the Labeling Act, I respectfully dissent.
