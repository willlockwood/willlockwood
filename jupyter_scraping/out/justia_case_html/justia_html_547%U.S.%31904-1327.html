HOLMES v. SOUTH CAROLINA

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of south carolina

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x931327.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 22, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 1, 2006

\n

At petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s South Carolina trial for murder and related crimes, the prosecution relied heavily on forensic evidence that strongly supported petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. Petitioner sought to undermine the State\xe2\x80\x99s forensic evidence by introducing expert testimony suggesting that the evidence had been contaminated and that the police had engaged in a plot to frame him. Petitioner also sought to introduce evidence that another man, Jimmy McCaw White, had been in the victim\xe2\x80\x99s neighborhood on the morning of the assault and that White had either acknowledged petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s innocence or admitted to committing the crimes himself. In White\xe2\x80\x99s pretrial testimony, he denied making the incriminating statements and provided an alibi for the time of the assault.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial court excluded petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s third-party guilt evidence citing the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s Gregory decision, which held such evidence admissible if it raises a reasonable inference as to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own innocence, but inadmissible if it merely casts a bare suspicion or raises a conjectural inference as to another\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. Affirming the trial court, the State Supreme Court cited both Gregory and its later decision in Gay, and held that where there is strong forensic evidence of an appellant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt, proffered evidence about a third party\xe2\x80\x99s alleged guilt does not raise a reasonable inference as to the appellant\xe2\x80\x99s own innocence. Applying this standard, the court held that petitioner could not overcome the forensic evidence against him.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s federal constitutional rights are violated by an evidence rule under which the defendant may not introduce evidence of third-party guilt if the prosecution has introduced forensic evidence that, if believed, strongly supports a guilty verdict. \xe2\x80\x9c[S]tate and federal rulemakers have broad latitude under the Constitution to establish rules excluding evidence from criminal trials.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Scheffer, 523 U. S. 303, 308. This latitude, however, has limits. \xe2\x80\x9cWhether rooted directly in the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment or in the Compulsory Process or Confrontation clauses of the Sixth Amendment, the Constitution guarantees criminal defendants \xe2\x80\x98a meaningful opportunity to present a complete defense.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Crane v. Kentucky, 476 U. S. 683, 690. This right is abridged by evidence rules that \xe2\x80\x9cinfring[e] upon a weighty interest of the accused\xe2\x80\x9d and are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98arbitrary\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98disproportionate to the purposes they are designed to serve.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Scheffer, supra, at 308.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While the Constitution thus prohibits the exclusion of defense evidence under rules that serve no legitimate purpose or that are disproportionate to the ends that they are asserted to promote, well-established rules of evidence permit trial judges to exclude evidence if its probative value is outweighed by certain other factors such as unfair prejudice, confusion of the issues, or potential to mislead the jury. An application of this principle is found in rules regulating the admission of evidence proffered by criminal defendants to show that someone else committed the crime with which they are charged. Such rules are widely accepted and are not challenged here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Gregory, the South Carolina Supreme Court adopted and applied a rule intended to be of this type. In Gay and this case, however, that court radically changed and extended the Gregory rule by holding that, where there is strong evidence of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt, especially strong forensic evidence, proffered evidence about a third party\xe2\x80\x99s alleged guilt may (or perhaps must) be excluded. Under this rule, the trial judge does not focus on the probative value or the potential adverse effects of admitting the defense evidence of third-party guilt. Instead, the critical inquiry concerns the strength of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case: If the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case is strong enough, the evidence of third-party guilt is excluded even if that evidence, if viewed independently, would have great probative value and even if it would not pose an undue risk of harassment, prejudice, or confusion of the issues. Furthermore, as applied below, the rule seems to call for little, if any, examination of the credibility of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s witnesses or the reliability of its evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By evaluating the strength of only one party\xe2\x80\x99s evidence, no logical conclusion can be reached regarding the strength of contrary evidence offered by the other side to rebut or cast doubt. Because the rule applied below did not heed this point, the rule is \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary\xe2\x80\x9d in the sense that it does not rationally serve the end that the Gregory rule and other similar third-party guilt rules were designed to further. Nor has the State identified any other legitimate end served by the rule. Thus, the rule violates a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a meaningful opportunity to present a complete defense.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Crane, supra, at 690. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9311.

BOBBY LEE HOLMES, PETITIONER v. SOUTH\nCAROLINA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of south\ncarolina

\n

[May 1, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case presents the question whether a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s federal constitutional rights are violated by an evidence rule under which the defendant may not introduce proof of third-party guilt if the prosecution has introduced forensic evidence that, if believed, strongly supports a guilty verdict.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the morning of December 31, 1989, 86-year-old Mary Stewart was beaten, raped, and robbed in her home. She later died of complications stemming from her injuries. Petitioner was convicted by a South Carolina jury of murder, first-degree criminal sexual conduct, first-degree burglary, and robbery, and he was sentenced to death. State v. Holmes, 320 S.\xc2\xa0C. 259, 262, 464 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 334, 336 (1995). The South Carolina Supreme Court affirmed his convictions and sentence, and this Court denied certiorari. Ibid., cert. denied, 517 U. S. 1248 (1996). Upon state postconviction review, however, petitioner was granted a new trial. 361 S.\xc2\xa0C. 333, 335, n. 1, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 19, 20, n.\xc2\xa01 (2004).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the second trial, the prosecution relied heavily on the following forensic evidence:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(1) [Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] palm print was found just above the door knob on the interior side of the front door of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s house; (2) fibers consistent with a black sweatshirt owned by [petitioner] were found on the victim\xe2\x80\x99s bed sheets; (3) matching blue fibers were found on the victim\xe2\x80\x99s pink nightgown and on [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] blue jeans; (4) microscopically consistent fibers were found on the pink nightgown and on [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] underwear; (5) [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] underwear contained a mixture of DNA from two individuals, and 99.99% of the population other than [petitioner] and the victim were excluded as contributors to that mixture; and (6) [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] tank top was found to contain a mixture of [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] blood and the victim\xe2\x80\x99s blood.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 343, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 24.

\n

\nIn addition, the prosecution introduced evidence that petitioner had been seen near Stewart\xe2\x80\x99s home within an hour of the time when, according to the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s evidence, the attack took place. Id., at 337\xe2\x80\x93338, 343, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 21, 24.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a major part of his defense, petitioner attempted to undermine the State\xe2\x80\x99s forensic evidence by suggesting that it had been contaminated and that certain law enforcement officers had engaged in a plot to frame him. Id., at 339, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 22. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s expert witnesses criticized the procedures used by the police in handling the fiber and DNA evidence and in collecting the fingerprint evidence. App. 299\xe2\x80\x93311, 313\xe2\x80\x93323. Another defense expert provided testimony that petitioner cited as supporting his claim that the palm print had been planted by the police. Id., at 326\xe2\x80\x93327.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner also sought to introduce proof that another man, Jimmy McCaw White, had attacked Stewart. 361 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 340, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 22. At a pretrial hearing, petitioner proffered several witnesses who placed White in the victim\xe2\x80\x99s neighborhood on the morning of the assault, as well as four other witnesses who testified that White had either acknowledged that petitioner was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98innocent\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or had actually admitted to committing the crimes. Id., at 340\xe2\x80\x93342, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 22\xe2\x80\x9323. One witness recounted that when he asked White about the \xe2\x80\x9cword \xe2\x80\xa6 on the street\xe2\x80\x9d that White was responsible for Stewart\xe2\x80\x99s murder, White \xe2\x80\x9cput his head down and he raised his head back up and he said, well, you know I like older women.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 119. According to this witness, White added that \xe2\x80\x9che did what they say he did\xe2\x80\x9d and that he had \xe2\x80\x9cno regrets about it at all.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 120. Another witness, who had been incarcerated with White, testified that White had admitted to assaulting Stewart, that a police officer had asked the witness to testify falsely against petitioner, and that employees of the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s office, while soliciting the witness\xe2\x80\x99 cooperation, had spoken of manufacturing evidence against petitioner. Id., at 38\xe2\x80\x9350. White testified at the pretrial hearing and denied making the incriminating statements. 361 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 341\xe2\x80\x93342, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 23. He also provided an alibi for the time of the crime, but another witness refuted his alibi. Id., at 342, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 23.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial court excluded petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s third-party guilt evidence citing State v. Gregory, 198 S.\xc2\xa0C. 98, 16 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 532 (1941), which held that such evidence is admissible if it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98raise[s] a reasonable inference or presumption as to [the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] own innocence\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but is not admissible if it merely \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98cast[s] a bare suspicion upon another\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98raise[s] a conjectural inference as to the commission of the crime by another.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. 133\xe2\x80\x93134 (quoting Gregory, supra, at 104, 16 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 534). On appeal, the South Carolina Supreme Court found no error in the exclusion of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s third-party guilt evidence. Citing both Gregory and its later decision in State v. Gay, 343 S.\xc2\xa0C. 543, 541 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 541 (2001), the State Supreme Court held that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere there is strong evidence of an appellant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt, especially where there is strong forensic evidence, the proffered evidence about a third party\xe2\x80\x99s alleged guilt does not raise a reasonable inference as to the appellant\xe2\x80\x99s own innocence.\xe2\x80\x9d 361 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 342\xe2\x80\x93343, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 24. Applying this standard, the court held that petitioner could not \xe2\x80\x9covercome the forensic evidence against him to raise a reasonable inference of his own innocence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 343, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 24. We granted certiorari. 545 U. S. ___ (2005).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[S]tate and federal rulemakers have broad latitude under the Constitution to establish rules excluding evidence from criminal trials.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Scheffer, 523 U. S. 303, 308 (1998); see also Crane v. Kentucky, 476 U. S. 683, 689\xe2\x80\x93690 (1986); Marshall v. Lonberger, 459 U. S. 422, 438, n. 6 (1983); Chambers v. Mississippi, 410 U. S. 284, 302\xe2\x80\x93303 (1973); Spencer v. Texas, 385 U. S. 554, 564 (1967). This latitude, however, has limits. \xe2\x80\x9cWhether rooted directly in the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment or in the Compulsory Process or Confrontation clauses of the Sixth Amendment, the Constitution guarantees criminal defendants \xe2\x80\x98a meaningful opportunity to present a complete defense.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Crane, supra, at 690 (quoting California v. Trombetta, 467 U. S. 479, 485 (1984); citations omitted). This right is abridged by evidence rules that \xe2\x80\x9cinfring[e] upon a weighty interest of the accused\xe2\x80\x9d and are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98arbitrary\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98disproportionate to the purposes they are designed to serve.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Scheffer, supra, at 308 (quoting Rock v. Arkansas, 483 U. S. 44, 58, 56 (1987)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases contain several illustrations of \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary\xe2\x80\x9d rules, i.e., rules that excluded important defense evidence but that did not serve any legitimate interests. In Washington v. Texas, 388 U. S. 14 (1967), state statutes barred a person who had been charged as a participant in a crime from testifying in defense of another alleged participant unless the witness had been acquitted. As a result, when the defendant in Washington was tried for murder, he was precluded from calling as a witness a person who had been charged and previously convicted of committing the same murder. Holding that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to put on a defense had been violated, we noted that the rule embodied in the statutes could not \xe2\x80\x9ceven be defended on the ground that it rationally sets apart a group of persons who are particularly likely to commit perjury\xe2\x80\x9d since the rule allowed an alleged participant to testify if he or she had been acquitted or was called by the prosecution. Id., at 22\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A similar constitutional violation occurred in Chambers v. Mississippi, supra. A murder defendant called as a witness a man named McDonald, who had previously confessed to the murder. When McDonald repudiated the confession on the stand, the defendant was denied permission to examine McDonald as an adverse witness based on the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98voucher\xe2\x80\x99 rule,\xe2\x80\x9d which barred parties from impeaching their own witnesses. Id., at 294. In addition, because the state hearsay rule did not include an exception for statements against penal interest, the defendant was not permitted to introduce evidence that McDonald had made self-incriminating statements to three other persons. Noting that the State had not even attempted to \xe2\x80\x9cdefend\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cexplain [the] underlying rationale\xe2\x80\x9d of the \xe2\x80\x9cvoucher rule,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 297, this Court held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe exclusion of [the evidence of McDonald\xe2\x80\x99s out-of-court statements], coupled with the State\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to permit [the defendant] to cross-examine McDonald, denied him a trial in accord with traditional and fundamental standards of due process,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 302.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Another arbitrary rule was held unconstitutional in Crane v. Kentucky, supra. There, the defendant was prevented from attempting to show at trial that his confession was unreliable because of the circumstances under which it was obtained, and neither the State Supreme Court nor the prosecution \xe2\x80\x9cadvanced any rational justification for the wholesale exclusion of this body of potentially exculpatory evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 691.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Rock v. Arkansas, supra, this Court held that a rule prohibiting hypnotically refreshed testimony was unconstitutional because \xe2\x80\x9c[w]holesale inadmissibility of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s testimony is an arbitrary restriction on the right to testify in the absence of clear evidence by the State repudiating the validity of all post-hypnotic recollections.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 61. By contrast, in United States v. Scheffer, supra, we held that a rule excluding all polygraph evidence did not abridge the right to present a defense because the rule \xe2\x80\x9cserve[d] several legitimate interests in the criminal trial process,\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cneither arbitrary nor disproportionate in promoting these ends,\xe2\x80\x9d and did not \xe2\x80\x9cimplicate a sufficiently weighty interest of the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 309.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While the Constitution thus prohibits the exclusion of defense evidence under rules that serve no legitimate purpose or that are disproportionate to the ends that they are asserted to promote, well-established rules of evidence permit trial judges to exclude evidence if its probative value is outweighed by certain other factors such as unfair prejudice, confusion of the issues, or potential to mislead the jury. See, e.g., Fed. Rule Evid. 403; Uniform Rule of Evid. 45 (1953); ALI, Model Code of Evidence Rule 303 (1942); 3 J. Wigmore, Evidence \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71863, 1904 (1904). Plainly referring to rules of this type, we have stated that the Constitution permits judges \xe2\x80\x9cto exclude evidence that is \xe2\x80\x98repetitive \xe2\x80\xa6 , only marginally relevant\xe2\x80\x99 or poses an undue risk of \xe2\x80\x98harassment, prejudice, [or] confusion of the issues.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Crane, supra, at 689\xe2\x80\x93690 (quoting Delaware v. Van Arsdall, 475 U. S. 673, 679 (1986); ellipsis and brackets in original). See also Montana v. Egelhoff, 518 U. S. 37, 42 (1996) (plurality opinion) (terming such rules \xe2\x80\x9cfamiliar and unquestionably constitutional\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A specific application of this principle is found in rules regulating the admission of evidence proffered by criminal defendants to show that someone else committed the crime with which they are charged. See, e.g., 41 C.\xc2\xa0J.\xc2\xa0S., Homicide \xc2\xa7216, pp. 56\xe2\x80\x9358 (1991) (\xe2\x80\x9cEvidence tending to show the commission by another person of the crime charged may be introduced by accused when it is inconsistent with, and raises a reasonable doubt of, his own guilt; but frequently matters offered in evidence for this purpose are so remote and lack such connection with the crime that they are excluded\xe2\x80\x9d); 40A Am. Jur. 2d, Homicide \xc2\xa7286, pp. 136\xe2\x80\x93138 (1999) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he accused may introduce any legal evidence tending to prove that another person may have committed the crime with which the defendant is charged\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [Such evidence] may be excluded where it does not sufficiently connect the other person to the crime, as, for example, where the evidence is speculative or remote, or does not tend to prove or disprove a material fact in issue at the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s trial\xe2\x80\x9d (footnotes omitted)). Such rules are widely accepted,* and neither petitioner nor his amici challenge them here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Gregory, the South Carolina Supreme Court adopted and applied a rule apparently intended to be of this type, given the court\xe2\x80\x99s references to the \xe2\x80\x9capplicable rule\xe2\x80\x9d from Corpus Juris and American Jurisprudence:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[E]vidence offered by accused as to the commission of the crime by another person must be limited to such facts as are inconsistent with his own guilt, and to such facts as raise a reasonable inference or presumption as to his own innocence; evidence which can have (no) other effect than to cast a bare suspicion upon another, or to raise a conjectural inference as to the commission of the crime by another, is not admissible\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [B]efore such testimony can be received, there must be such proof of connection with it, such a train of facts or circumstances, as tends clearly to point out such other person as the guilty party.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 198 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 104\xe2\x80\x93105, 16 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 534\xe2\x80\x93535 (quoting 16 C.\xc2\xa0J., Criminal Law \xc2\xa71085, p. 560 (1918) and 20 Am. Jur., Evidence \xc2\xa7265, p. 254 (1939); footnotes omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Gay and this case, however, the South Carolina Supreme Court radically changed and extended the rule. In Gay, after recognizing the standard applied in Gregory, the court stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n view of the strong evidence of appellant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt\xe2\x80\x94especially the forensic evidence\xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6 the proffered evidence \xe2\x80\xa6 did not raise \xe2\x80\x98a reasonable inference\xe2\x80\x99 as to appellant\xe2\x80\x99s own innocence.\xe2\x80\x9d Gay, 343 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 550, 541 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 545 (quoting Gregory, supra, at 104, 16 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 534, in turn quoting 16 C.\xc2\xa0J., \xc2\xa71085, at 560). Similarly, in the present case, as noted, the State Supreme Court applied the rule that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere there is strong evidence of [a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] guilt, especially where there is strong forensic evidence, the proffered evidence about a third party\xe2\x80\x99s alleged guilt\xe2\x80\x9d may (or perhaps must) be excluded. 361 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 342, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 24.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under this rule, the trial judge does not focus on the probative value or the potential adverse effects of admitting the defense evidence of third-party guilt. Instead, the critical inquiry concerns the strength of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case: If the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case is strong enough, the evidence of third-party guilt is excluded even if that evidence, if viewed independently, would have great probative value and even if it would not pose an undue risk of harassment, prejudice, or confusion of the issues.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, as applied in this case, the South Carolina Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s rule seems to call for little, if any, examination of the credibility of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s witnesses or the reliability of its evidence. Here, for example, the defense strenuously claimed that the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s forensic evidence was so unreliable (due to mishandling and a deliberate plot to frame petitioner) that the evidence should not have even been admitted. The South Carolina Supreme Court responded that these challenges did not entirely \xe2\x80\x9ceviscerate\xe2\x80\x9d the forensic evidence and that the defense challenges went to the weight and not to the admissibility of that evidence. Id., at 343, n.\xc2\xa08, 605 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 24, n.\xc2\xa08. Yet, in evaluating the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s forensic evidence and deeming it to be \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and thereby justifying exclusion of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s third-party guilt evidence\xe2\x80\x94the South Carolina Supreme Court made no mention of the defense challenges to the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Interpreted in this way, the rule applied by the State Supreme Court does not rationally serve the end that the Gregory rule and its analogues in other jurisdictions were designed to promote, i.e., to focus the trial on the central issues by excluding evidence that has only a very weak logical connection to the central issues. The rule applied in this case appears to be based on the following logic: Where (1) it is clear that only one person was involved in the commission of a particular crime and (2) there is strong evidence that the defendant was the perpetrator, it follows that evidence of third-party guilt must be weak. But this logic depends on an accurate evaluation of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s proof, and the true strength of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s proof cannot be assessed without considering challenges to the reliability of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s evidence. Just because the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s evidence, if credited, would provide strong support for a guilty verdict, it does not follow that evidence of third-party guilt has only a weak logical connection to the central issues in the case. And where the credibility of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s witnesses or the reliability of its evidence is not conceded, the strength of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case cannot be assessed without making the sort of factual findings that have traditionally been reserved for the trier of fact and that the South Carolina courts did not purport to make in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The rule applied in this case is no more logical than its converse would be, i.e., a rule barring the prosecution from introducing evidence of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt if the defendant is able to proffer, at a pretrial hearing, evidence that, if believed, strongly supports a verdict of not guilty. In the present case, for example, the petitioner proffered evidence that, if believed, squarely proved that White, not petitioner, was the perpetrator. It would make no sense, however, to hold that this proffer precluded the prosecution from introducing its evidence, including the forensic evidence that, if credited, provided strong proof of the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s guilt.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The point is that, by evaluating the strength of only one party\xe2\x80\x99s evidence, no logical conclusion can be reached regarding the strength of contrary evidence offered by the other side to rebut or cast doubt. Because the rule applied by the State Supreme Court in this case did not heed this point, the rule is \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary\xe2\x80\x9d in the sense that it does not rationally serve the end that the Gregory rule and other similar third-party guilt rules were designed to further. Nor has the State identified any other legitimate end that the rule serves. It follows that the rule applied in this case by the State Supreme Court violates a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a meaningful opportunity to present a complete defense.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Crane, 476 U. S., at 690 (quoting Trombetta, 467 U. S., at 485).

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, we vacate the judgment of the South Carolina Supreme Court and remand the case for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
