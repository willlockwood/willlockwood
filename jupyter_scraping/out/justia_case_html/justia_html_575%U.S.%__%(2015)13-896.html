\n

COMMIL USA, LLC v. CISCO SYSTEMS, INC.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93896.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 31, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 26, 2015

\n

Petitioner Commil USA, LLC, holder of a patent for a method of implementing short-range wireless networks, filed suit, claiming that respondent Cisco Systems, Inc., a maker and seller of wireless networking equipment, had directly infringed Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent in its networking equipment and had induced others to infringe the patent by selling the infringing equipment for them to use. After two trials, Cisco was found liable for both direct and induced infringement. With regard to inducement, Cisco had raised the defense that it had a good-faith belief that Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent was invalid, but the District Court found Cisco\xe2\x80\x99s supporting evidence inadmissible. The Federal Circuit affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in part, vacated in part, and remanded, holding, as relevant here, that the trial court erred in excluding Cisco\xe2\x80\x99s evidence of its good-faith belief that Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent was invalid.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A defendant\xe2\x80\x99s belief regarding patent validity is not a defense to an induced infringement claim. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0While this case centers on inducement liability,\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(b), which attaches only if the defendant knew of the patent and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe induced acts constitute patent infringement,\xe2\x80\x9d Global-Tech Appliances, Inc. v. SEB S.\xc2\xa0A., 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, the discussion here also refers to direct infringement, \xc2\xa7271(a), a strict-liability offense in which a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mental state is irrelevant, and contributory infringement, \xc2\xa7271(c), which, like inducement liability, requires knowledge of the patent in suit and knowledge of patent infringement, Aro Mfg. Co. v. Convertible Top Replacement Co.,\n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 476\n (Aro II). Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0In Global-Tech, this Court held that \xe2\x80\x9cinduced infringement .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. requires knowledge that the induced acts constitute patent infringement,\xe2\x80\x9d 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, relying on the reasoning of Aro II, a contributory infringement case, because the mental state imposed in each instance is similar. Contrary to the claim of Commil and the Government as amicus, it was not only knowledge of the existence of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s patent that led the Court to affirm the liability finding in Global-Tech, but also the fact that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s actions demonstrated that it knew it would be causing customers to infringe respondent\xe2\x80\x99s patent. 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___. Qualifying or limiting that holding could make a person, or entity, liable for induced or contributory infringement even though he did not know the acts were infringing. Global-Tech requires more, namely proof the defendant knew the acts were infringing. And that opinion was clear in rejecting any lesser mental state as the standard. Id., at ___. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Because induced infringement and validity are separate issues and have separate defenses under the Act, belief regarding validity cannot negate \xc2\xa7271(b)\xe2\x80\x99s scienter requirement of \xe2\x80\x9cactively induce[d] infringement,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., the intent to \xe2\x80\x9cbring about the desired result\xe2\x80\x9d of infringement, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___. When infringement is the issue, the patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity is not the question to be confronted. See Cardinal Chemical Co. v. Morton Int\xe2\x80\x99l, Inc.,\n\n508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83\n. Otherwise, the long held presumption that a patent is valid, \xc2\xa7282(a), would be undermined, permitting circumvention of the high bar\xe2\x80\x94the clear and convincing standard\xe2\x80\x94that defendants must surmount to rebut the presumption. See Microsoft Corp. v. i4i Ltd. Partnership, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___. To be sure, if a patent is shown to be invalid, there is no patent to be infringed. But the orderly administration of the patent system requires courts to interpret and implement the statutory framework to determine the procedures and sequences that the parties must follow to prove the act of wrongful inducement and any related issues of patent validity.

\n

There are practical reasons not to create a defense of belief in invalidity for induced infringement. Accused inducers who believe a patent is invalid have other, proper ways to obtain a ruling to that effect, including, e.g., seeking ex parte reexamination of the patent by the Patent and Trademark Office, something Cisco did here. Creating such a defense could also have negative consequences, including, e.g., rendering litigation more burdensome for all involved. Pp. 9\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0District courts have the authority and responsibility to ensure that frivolous cases\xe2\x80\x94brought by companies using patents as a sword to go after defendants for money\xe2\x80\x94are dissuaded, though no issue of frivolity has been raised here. Safeguards\xe2\x80\x94including, e.g., sanctioning attorneys for bringing such suits, see Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x94combined with the avenues that accused inducers have to obtain rulings on the validity of patents, militate in favor of maintaining the separation between infringement and validity expressed in the Patent Act. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

720 F.\xc2\xa03d 1361, vacated and remanded.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93896

_________________

\n

COMMIL USA, LLC, PETITIONER v.CISCO SYSTEMS, INC.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

[May 26, 2015]

\n

\n

Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.[1]*

\n

A patent holder, and the holder\xe2\x80\x99s lawful licensees, can recover for monetary injury when their exclusive rights are violated by others\xe2\x80\x99 wrongful conduct. One form of patent injury occurs if unauthorized persons or entities copy, use, or otherwise infringe upon the patented invention. Another form of injury to the patent holder or his licensees can occur when the actor induces others to infringe the patent. In the instant case, both forms of in-jury\xe2\x80\x94direct infringement and wrongful inducement of others to commit infringement\xe2\x80\x94were alleged. After two trials, the defendant was found liable for both types of injury. The dispute now before the Court concerns the inducement aspect of the case.

\n

I

\n

The patent holder who commenced this action is the petitioner here, Commil USA, LLC. The technical details of Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent are not at issue. So it suffices to say, with much oversimplification, that the patent is for a method of implementing short-range wireless networks. Suppose an extensive business headquarters or a resort or a college campus wants a single, central wireless system (sometimes called a Wi-Fi network). In order to cover the large space, the system needs multiple base stations so a user can move around the area and still stay connected. Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent relates to a method of providing faster and more reliable communications between devices and base stations. The particular claims of Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent are discussed in the opinion of the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit. 720 F.\xc2\xa03d 1361, 1364\xe2\x80\x931365, 1372 (2013).

\n

Commil brought this action against Cisco Systems, Inc., which makes and sells wireless networking equipment. In 2007, Commil sued Cisco in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Texas. Cisco is the respondent here. Commil alleged that Cisco had infringed Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent by making and using networking equipment. In addition Commil alleged that Cisco had induced others to infringe the patent by selling the infringing equipment for them to use, in contravention of Commil\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive patent rights.

\n

At the first trial, the jury concluded that Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent was valid and that Cisco had directly infringed. The jury awarded Commil $3.7 million in damages. As to induced infringement, the jury found Cisco not liable. Commil filed a motion for a new trial on induced infringement and damages, which the District Court grantedbecause of certain inappropriate comments Cisco\xe2\x80\x99s counsel had made during the first trial.

\n

A month before the second trial Cisco went to the United States Patent and Trademark Office and asked it to re-examine the validity of Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent. The Office granted the request; but, undoubtedly to Cisco\xe2\x80\x99s disappointment, it confirmed the validity of Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent. App. 159, 162.

\n

Back in the District Court, the second trial proceeded, limited to the issues of inducement and damages on that issue and direct infringement. As a defense to the claim of inducement, Cisco argued it had a good-faith belief that Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent was invalid. It sought to introduce evidence to support that assertion. The District Court, however, ruled that Cisco\xe2\x80\x99s proffered evidence of its good-faith belief in the patent\xe2\x80\x99s invalidity was inadmissible. While the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order does not provide the reason for the ruling, it seems the court excluded this evidence on the assumption that belief in invalidity is not a defense to a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim that the defendant induced others to infringe.

\n

At the close of trial, and over Cisco\xe2\x80\x99s objection, the District Court instructed the jury that it could find inducement if \xe2\x80\x9cCisco actually intended to cause the acts that constitute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. direct infringement and that Cisco knew or should have known that its actions would induce actual infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 21. The jury returned a verdict for Commil on induced infringement and awarded $63.7 million in damages.

\n

After the verdict, but before judgment, this Court issued its decision in Global-Tech Appliances, Inc. v. SEB S.\xc2\xa0A., 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011). That case, as will be discussed in more detail, held that, in an action for induced infringement, it is necessary for the plaintiff to show that the alleged inducer knew of the patent in question and knew the induced acts were infringing. Id., at ___ (slip op.,at 10). Relying on that case, Cisco again urged that the jury instruction was incorrect because it did not state knowledge as the governing standard for inducement liability. The District Court denied Cisco\xe2\x80\x99s motion and entered judgment in Commil\xe2\x80\x99s favor.

\n

Cisco appealed to the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit. The Court of Appeals affirmed in part, vacated in part, and remanded for further proceedings. The court concluded it was error for the District Court to have instructed the jury that Cisco could be liable for induced infringement if it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98knew or should have known\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that its customers infringed. 720 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1366. The panel held that \xe2\x80\x9cinduced infringement \xe2\x80\x98requires knowledge that the induced acts constitute patent infringement.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Global-Tech, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 10)). By stating that Cisco could be found liable if it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98knew or should have known that its actions would induce actual infringement,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals explained, the District Court had allowed \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury to find [Cisco] liable based on mere negligence where knowledge is required.\xe2\x80\x9d 720 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1366. That ruling, which requires a new trial on the inducement claim with a corrected instruction on knowledge, is not in question here.

\n

What is at issue is the second holding of the Court of Appeals, addressing Cisco\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the trial court committed further error in excluding Cisco\xe2\x80\x99s evidence that it had a good-faith belief that Commil\xe2\x80\x99s patent was in-valid. Beginning with the observation that it is \xe2\x80\x9caxiomatic that one cannot infringe an invalid patent,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9cevidence of an accused inducer\xe2\x80\x99s good-faith belief of invalidity may negate the requisite intent for induced infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1368. The court saw \xe2\x80\x9cno principled distinction between a good-faith belief of invalidity and a good-faith belief of non-infringement for the purpose of whether a defendant possessed the specific intent to induce infringement of a patent.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Judge Newman dissented on that point. In Judge Newman\xe2\x80\x99s view a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s good-faith belief in a patent\xe2\x80\x99s invalidity is not a defense to induced infringement. She reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9cwhether there is infringement in fact does not depend on the belief of the accused infringer that it might succeed in invalidating the patent.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1374 (opinion concurring in part and dissenting in part). Both parties filed petitions for rehearing en banc, which were denied. 737 F.\xc2\xa03d 699, 700 (2013). Five judges, however, would have granted rehearing en banc to consider the question whether a good-faith belief in invalidity is a defense to induced infringement. Id., at 700 (Reyna, J., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc).

\n

This Court granted certiorari to decide that question. 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014).

\n

II

\n

Although the precise issue to be addressed concerns a claim of improper inducement to infringe, the discussion to follow refers as well to direct infringement and contributory infringement, so it is instructive at the outset to set forth the statutory provisions pertaining to these three forms of liability. These three relevant provisions are found in \xc2\xa7271 of the Patent Act.\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271.

\n

Subsection (a) governs direct infringement and provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cExcept as otherwise provided in this title, whoever without authority makes, uses, offers to sell, or sells any patented invention, within the United States or imports into the United States any patented invention during the term of the patent therefor, infringes the patent.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Under this form of liability, a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mental state is irrelevant. Direct infringement is a strict-liability offense. Global-Tech, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 5, n.\xc2\xa02).

\n

Subsection (b) governs induced infringement:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhoever actively induces infringement of a patent shall be liable as an infringer.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

In contrast to direct infringement, liability for inducing infringement attaches only if the defendant knew of the patent and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe induced acts constitute patent infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 10). In Commil and the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view, not only is knowledge or belief in the patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity irrelevant, they further argue the party charged with inducing infringement need not know that the acts it induced would infringe. On this latter point, they are incorrect, as will be explained below.

\n

Subsection (c) deals with contributory infringement:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhoever offers to sell or sells within the United States or imports into the United States a component of a patented machine, manufacture, combination or composition, or a material or apparatus for use in practicing a patented process, constituting a material part of the invention, knowing the same to be especially made or especially adapted for use in an infringement of such patent, and not a staple article or commodity of commerce suitable for substantial noninfringing use, shall be liable as a contributoryinfringer.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Like induced infringement, contributory infringement requires knowledge of the patent in suit and knowledge of patent infringement. Aro Mfg. Co. v. Convertible Top Replacement Co.,\n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 476,\n488 (1964)\n (Aro II).

\n

This case asks a question of first impression: whether knowledge of, or belief in, a patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity is required for induced infringement under \xc2\xa7271(b).

\n

A

\n

Before turning to the question presented, it is necessary to reaffirm what the Court held in Global-Tech. Commil and the Government (which supports Commil in this case) argue that Global-Tech should be read as holding that only knowledge of the patent is required for induced infringement. That, as will be explained, would contravene Global-Tech\xe2\x80\x99s explicit holding that liability for induced infringement can only attach if the defendant knew of the patent and knew as well that \xe2\x80\x9cthe induced acts constitute patent infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10).

\n

In Global-Tech, the plaintiff, SEB, had invented and patented a deep fryer. A few years later, Sunbeam asked Pentalpha to supply deep fryers for Sunbeam to sell. To make the deep fryer, Pentalpha bought an SEB fryer and copied all but the cosmetic features. Pentalpha then sold the fryers to Sunbeam, which in turn sold them to customers. SEB sued Pentalpha for induced infringement, arguing Pentalpha had induced Sunbeam and others to sell the infringing fryers in violation of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent rights. In defense, Pentalpha argued it did not know the deep fryer it copied was patented and therefore could not be liable for inducing anyone to infringe SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent. The question presented to this Court was \xe2\x80\x9cwhether a party who \xe2\x80\x98actively induces infringement of a patent\xe2\x80\x99 under\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(b) must know that the induced acts constitute patent infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 1).

\n

After noting the language of \xc2\xa7271(b) and the case law prior to passage of the Patent Act did not resolve the question, the Global-Tech Court turned to Aro II, a case about contributory infringement. The Global-Tech Court deemed that rules concerning contributory infringement were relevant to induced infringement, because the mental state imposed in each instance is similar. Before the Patent Act, inducing infringement was not a separate theory of indirect liability but was evidence of contributory infringement. 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 5). Thus, in many respects, it is proper to find common ground in the two theories of liability.

\n

Aro II concluded that to be liable for contributory infringement, a defendant must know the acts were infringing. 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 488. In Global-Tech, the Court said this reasoning was applicable, explaining as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cBased on this premise, it follows that the same knowledge is needed for induced infringement under \xc2\xa7271(b). As noted, the two provisions have a common origin in the pre-1952 understanding of contributory infringement, and the language of the two provisions creates the same difficult interpretive choice. It would thus be strange to hold that knowledge of the relevant patent is needed under \xc2\xa7271(c) but not under \xc2\xa7271(b).

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cAccordingly, we now hold that induced infringement under \xc2\xa7271(b) requires knowledge that the induced acts constitute patent infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10).

\n

In support of Commil, the Government argues against the clear language of Global-Tech. According to the Government, all Global-Tech requires is knowledge of the patent: \xe2\x80\x9cThe Court did not definitively resolve whether Section 271(b) additionally requires knowledge of the infringing nature of the induced acts.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 9. See also Brief for Petitioner 17. Together, Commil and the Government claim the \xe2\x80\x9cfactual circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d of Global-Tech \xe2\x80\x9cdid not require\xe2\x80\x9d the Court to decide whether knowledge of infringement is required for inducement liability. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 12. See also Brief for Petitioner 23\xe2\x80\x9324. But in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s Global-Tech decision, its description of the factual circumstances suggests otherwise. The Court concluded there was enough evidence to support a finding that Pentalpha knew \xe2\x80\x9cthe infringing nature of the sales it encouraged Sunbeam to make.\xe2\x80\x9d 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 14). It was not only knowledge of the existence of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent that led the Court to affirm the liability finding but also it was the fact that Pentalpha copied \xe2\x80\x9call but the cosmetic features of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s fryer,\xe2\x80\x9d demonstrating Pentalpha knew it would be causing customers to infringe SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 15).

\n

Accepting the Government and Commil\xe2\x80\x99s argument would require this Court to depart from its prior holding. See id., at ___ (slip op., at 10). See also id., at ___ (Kennedy, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 1) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court is correct, in my view, to conclude that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to induce infringement a defendant must know the acts constitute patent infringement\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). And the Global-Tech rationale is sound. Qualifying or limiting its holding, as the Government and Commil seek to do, would lead to the conclusion, both in inducement and contributory infringement cases, that a person, or entity, could be liable even though he did not know the acts were infringing. In other words, even if the defendant reads the patent\xe2\x80\x99s claims differently from the plaintiff, and that reading is reasonable, he would still be liable because he knew the acts might infringe. Global-Tech requires more. It requires proof the defendant knew the acts were infringing. And the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion was clear in rejecting any lesser mental state as the standard. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314).

\n

B

\n

The question the Court confronts today concerns whether a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s belief regarding patent validity is a defense to a claim of induced infringement. It is not. The scienter element for induced infringement concerns infringement; that is a different issue than validity. Section 271(b) requires that the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cactively induce[d] infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d That language requires intent to \xe2\x80\x9cbring about the desired result,\xe2\x80\x9d which is infringement. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 4). And because infringement and validity are separate issues under the Act, belief regarding validity cannot negate the scienter required under \xc2\xa7271(b).

\n

When infringement is the issue, the validity of the patent is not the question to be confronted. In Cardinal Chemical Co. v. Morton Int\xe2\x80\x99l, Inc.,\n\n508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83 (1993)\n, the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cA party seeking a declaratory judgment of invalidity presents a claim independent of the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s charge of infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 96. It further held noninfringement and invalidity were \xe2\x80\x9calternative grounds\xe2\x80\x9d for dismissing the suit. Id., at 98. And in Deposit Guaranty Nat. Bank v. Roper,\n\n445 U.\xc2\xa0S. 326 (1980)\n, the Courtexplained that an accused infringer \xe2\x80\x9cmay prevail either by successfully attacking the validity of the patent or by successfully defending the charge of infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 334. These explanations are in accord with the long-accepted truth\xe2\x80\x94perhaps the axiom\xe2\x80\x94that infringement and invalidity are separate matters under patent law. See Pandrol USA, LP v. Airboss R. Prods., Inc., 320 F.\xc2\xa03d 1354, 1365 (CA Fed. 2003).

\n

Indeed, the issues of infringement and validity appear in separate parts of the Patent Act. Part III of the Act deals with \xe2\x80\x9cPatents and Protection of Patent Rights,\xe2\x80\x9d including the right to be free from infringement. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7251\xe2\x80\x93329. Part II, entitled \xe2\x80\x9cPatentability of Inventions and Grants of Patents,\xe2\x80\x9d defines what constitutes a valid patent. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7100\xe2\x80\x93212. Further, noninfringement and invalidity are listed as two separate defenses, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7282(b)(1), (2), and defendants are free to raise either or both of them. See Cardinal, supra, at 98. Were this Court to interpret \xc2\xa7271(b) as permitting a defense of belief in invalidity, it would conflate the issues of infringement and validity.

\n

Allowing this new defense would also undermine a presumption that is a \xe2\x80\x9ccommon core of thought and truth\xe2\x80\x9d reflected in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents for a century. Radio Corp. of America v. Radio Engineering Laboratories, Inc.,\n\n293 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n8 (1934)\n. Under the Patent Act, and the case law before its passage, a patent is \xe2\x80\x9cpresumed valid.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7282(a); id., at 8. That presumption takes away any need for a plaintiff to prove his patent is valid to bring a claim. But if belief in invalidity were a defense to induced infringement, the force of that presumption would be lessened to a drastic degree, for a defendant could prevail if he proved he reasonably believed the patent was invalid. That would circumvent the high bar Congress is presumed to have chosen: the clear and convincing standard. See Microsoft Corp. v. i4i Ltd. Partnership, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2011) (slip op., at 8\xe2\x80\x9310). Defendants must meet that standard to rebut the presumption of validity. Ibid.

\n

To say that an invalid patent cannot be infringed, or that someone cannot be induced to infringe an invalid patent, is in one sense a simple truth, both as a matter of logic and semantics. See M. Swift & Sons, Inc. v. W. H. Coe Mfg. Co., 102 F.\xc2\xa02d 391, 396 (CA1 1939). But the questions courts must address when interpreting and implementing the statutory framework require a determination of the procedures and sequences that the parties must follow to prove the act of wrongful inducement and any related issues of patent validity. \xe2\x80\x9cValidity and infringement are distinct issues, bearing different burdens, different presumptions, and different evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d 720 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1374 (opinion of Newman, J.). To be sure, if at the end of the day, an act that would have been an infringement or an inducement to infringe pertains to a patent that is shown to be invalid, there is no patent to be infringed. But the allocation of the burden to persuade on these questions, and the timing for the presentations of the relevant arguments, are concerns of central relevance to the orderly administration of the patent system.

\n

Invalidity is an affirmative defense that \xe2\x80\x9ccan preclude enforcement of a patent against otherwise infringing conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d 6A Chisum on Patents \xc2\xa719.01, p. 19\xe2\x80\x935 (2015). An accused infringer can, of course, attempt to prove that the patent in suit is invalid; if the patent is indeed invalid, and shown to be so under proper procedures, there is no liability. See i4i, supra, at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312). That is because invalidity is not a defense to infringement, it is a defense to liability. And because of that fact, a belief as to invalidity cannot negate the scienter required for induced infringement.

\n

There are also practical reasons not to create a defense based on a good-faith belief in invalidity. First and foremost, accused inducers who believe a patent is invalid have various proper ways to obtain a ruling to that effect. They can file a declaratory judgment action asking a federal court to declare the patent invalid. See MedImmune, Inc. v. Genentech, Inc.,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 118,\n137 (2007)\n. They can seek inter partes review at the Patent Trial and Appeal Board and receive a decision as to validity within 12 to 18 months. See \xc2\xa7316. Or they can, as Cisco didhere, seek ex\xc2\xa0parte reexamination of the patent by the Patent and Trademark Office. \xc2\xa7302. And, of course, any accused infringer who believes the patent in suit is invalid may raise the affirmative defense of invalidity. \xc2\xa7282(b)(2). If the defendant is successful, he will be immune from liability.

\n

Creating a defense of belief in invalidity, furthermore, would have negative consequences. It can render litigation more burdensome for everyone involved. Every accused inducer would have an incentive to put forth a theory of invalidity and could likely come up with myriad arguments. See Sloan, Think it is Invalid? A New Defense to Negate Intent for Induced Infringement, 23 Fed. Cir. B.\xc2\xa0J. 613, 618 (2013). And since \xe2\x80\x9cit is often more difficult to determine whether a patent is valid than whether it has been infringed,\xe2\x80\x9d Cardinal, 508 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 99, accused inducers would likely find it easier to prevail on a defense regarding the belief of invalidity than noninfringement. In addition the need to respond to the defense will increase discovery costs and multiply the issues the jury must resolve. Indeed, the jury would be put to the difficult task of separating the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s belief regarding validity from the actual issue of validity.

\n

As a final note, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ur law is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. no stranger to the possibility that an act may be \xe2\x80\x98intentional\xe2\x80\x99 for purposes of civil liability, even if the actor lacked actual knowledge that her conduct violated the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Jerman v. Carlisle, McNellie, Rini, Kramer & Ulrich, L.\xc2\xa0P.\xc2\xa0A.,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 573\n\xe2\x80\x93583 (2010). Tortious interference with a contract provides an apt example. While the invalidity of a contract is a defense to tortious interference, belief in validity is irrelevant. Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7766, Comment i (1979). See also W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, R. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on Law of Torts 110 (5th ed. 1984). In a similar way, a trespass \xe2\x80\x9ccan be committed despite the actor\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken belief that she has a legal right to enter the property.\xe2\x80\x9d Jerman, supra, at 583 (citing Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7164, and Comment e (1963\xe2\x80\x931964)). And of course, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he general rule that ignorance of the law or a mistake of law is no defense to criminal prosecution is deeply rooted in the American legal system.\xe2\x80\x9d Cheek v. United States,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 192,\n199 (1991)\n. In the usual case, \xe2\x80\x9cI thought it was legal\xe2\x80\x9d is no defense. That concept mirrors this Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that belief in invalidity will not negate the scienter required under \xc2\xa7271(b).

\n

III

\n

The Court is well aware that an \xe2\x80\x9cindustry has developed in which firms use patents not as a basis for producing and selling goods but, instead, primarily for obtaining licensing fees.\xe2\x80\x9d eBay Inc. v. MercExchange, L.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0C.,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 388,\n396 (2006)\n (Kennedy, J., concurring). Some companies may use patents as a sword to go after defendants for money, even when their claims are frivolous. This tactic is often pursued through demand letters, which \xe2\x80\x9cmay be sent very broadly and without prior investigation, may assert vague claims of infringement, and may be designed to obtain payments that are based more on the costs of defending litigation than on the merit of the patent claims.\xe2\x80\x9d L. Greisman, Prepared Statement of the Federal Trade Commission on Discussion Draft of Patent Demand Letter Legislation before the Subcommittee on Commerce, Manufacturing, and Trade of the House Committee on Energy and Commerce 2 (2014). This behavior can impose a \xe2\x80\x9charmful tax on innovation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

No issue of frivolity has been raised by the parties in this case, nor does it arise on the facts presented to this Court. Nonetheless, it is still necessary and proper to stress that district courts have the authority and responsibility to ensure frivolous cases are dissuaded. If frivolous cases are filed in federal court, it is within the power of the court to sanction attorneys for bringing such suits. Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa011. It is also within the district court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to award attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees to prevailing parties in \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional cases.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7285; see also Octane Fitness, LLC v. ICON Health & Fitness, Inc., 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2014) (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x938). These safeguards, combined with the avenues that accused inducers have to obtain rulings on the validity of patents, militate in favor of maintaining the separation expressed throughout the Patent Act between infringement and validity. This dichotomy means that belief in invalidity is no defense to a claim of induced infringement.

\n

The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

Justice Scalia, with whom The Chief Justice joins, dissenting.

\n

I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s rejection of the main argument advanced by Commil and the United States, that induced infringement under\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(b) does not \xe2\x80\x9crequir[e] knowledge of the infringing nature of the induced acts.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 9; see also Brief for Petitioner 15\xe2\x80\x9344.I disagree, however, with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that good-faith belief in a patent\xe2\x80\x99s invalidity is not a defense to induced infringement.

\n

Infringing a patent means invading a patentee\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive right to practice his claimed invention. Crown Die & Tool Co. v. Nye Tool & Machine Works,\n\n261 U.\xc2\xa0S. 24,\n40 (1923)\n (quoting 3 W. Robinson, Law of Patents \xc2\xa7937, pp. 122\xe2\x80\x93123 (1890)). Only valid patents confer this right to exclusivity\xe2\x80\x94invalid patents do not. FTC v. Actavis, Inc., 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 8). It follows, as night the day, that only valid patents can be infringed. To talk of infringing an invalid patent is to talk nonsense.

\n

Induced infringement, we have said, \xe2\x80\x9crequires knowledge that the induced acts constitute patent infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Global-Tech Appliances, Inc. v. SEB S.\xc2\xa0A., 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 10). Because only valid patents can be infringed, anyone with a good-faith belief in a patent\xe2\x80\x99s invalidity necessarily believes the patent cannot be infringed. And it is impossible for anyone who believes that a patent cannot be infringed to induce actions that he knows will infringe it. A good-faith belief that a patent is invalid is therefore a defense to induced infringement of that patent.

\n

The Court makes four arguments in support of the contrary position. None seems to me persuasive. First, it notes that the Patent Act treats infringement and validity as distinct issues. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. That is true. It is also irrelevant. Saying that infringement cannot exist without a valid patent does not \xe2\x80\x9cconflate the issues of infringement and validity,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10, any more than saying that water cannot exist without oxygen \xe2\x80\x9cconflates\xe2\x80\x9d water and oxygen. Recognizing that infringement requires validity is entirely consistent with the \xe2\x80\x9clong-accepted truth .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that infringement and invalidity are separate matters under patent law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The Court next insists that permitting the defense at issue would undermine the statutory presumption of validity. Ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. It would do no such thing. By reason of the statutory presumption of validity, \xc2\xa7282(a), patents can be held invalid only by \xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Microsoft Corp. v. i4i Ltd. Partnership, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 1). This presumption is not weakened by treating a good-faith belief in invalidity as a defense to induced infringement. An alleged inducer who succeeds in this defense does not thereby call a patent\xe2\x80\x99s validity into question. He merely avoids liability for a third party\xe2\x80\x99s infringement of a valid patent, in no way undermining that patent\xe2\x80\x99s presumed validity.

\n

Next, the Court says that \xe2\x80\x9cinvalidity is not a defense to infringement, it is a defense to liability.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. That is an assertion, not an argument. Again, to infringe a patent is to invade the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s right of exclusivity. An invalid patent confers no such right. How is it possible to interfere with rights that do not exist? The Court has no answer.

\n

That brings me to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s weakest argument: that there are \xe2\x80\x9cpractical reasons not to create a defense based on a good-faith belief in invalidity.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12 (emphasis added); see also ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cCreating a defense of belief in invalidity, furthermore, would have negative consequences\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Ours is not a common-law court.Erie R. Co. v. Tompkins,\n\n304 U.\xc2\xa0S. 64,\n78 (1938)\n. We do not, or at least should not, create defenses to statutory liability\xe2\x80\x94and that is not what this dissent purports to do. Our task is to interpret the Patent Act, and to decide whether it makes a good-faith belief in a patent\xe2\x80\x99s invalid-ity a defense to induced infringement. Since, as we said in Global-Tech, supra, the Act makes knowledge of infringement a requirement for induced-infringement liability; and since there can be no infringement (and hence no knowledge of infringement) of an invalid patent; good-faith belief in invalidity is a defense. I may add, however, that if the desirability of the rule we adopt were a proper consideration, it is by no means clear that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding, which increases the in terrorem power of patent trolls, is preferable. The Court seemingly acknowledges that consequence in Part III of its opinion.

\n

For the foregoing reasons, I respectfully dissent.
