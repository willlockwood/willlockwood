14 PENN PLAZA LLC et\xc2\xa0al. v. PYETT et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93581.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 1, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 1, 2009

\n

Respondents are members of the Service Employees International Union, Local 32BJ (Union). Under the National Labor Relations Act, the Union is the exclusive bargaining representative of employees within the building-services industry in New York City, which includes building cleaners, porters, and doorpersons. The Union has exclusive authority to bargain on behalf of its members over their \xe2\x80\x9crates of pay, wages, hours of employment, or other conditions of employment,\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7159(a), and engages in industry-wide collective bargaining with the Realty Advisory Board on Labor Relations, Inc. (RAB), a multiemployer bargaining association for the New York City real-estate industry. The agreement between the Union and the RAB is embodied in their Collective Bargaining Agreement for Contractors and Building Owners (CBA). The CBA requires union members to submit all claims of employment discrimination to binding arbitration under the CBA\xe2\x80\x99s grievance and dispute resolution procedures.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner 14 Penn Plaza LLC is a member of the RAB. It owns and operates the New York City office building where respondents worked as night lobby watchmen and in other similar capacities. Respondents were directly employed by petitioner Temco Service Industries, Inc. (Temco), a maintenance service and cleaning contractor. After 14 Penn Plaza, with the Union\xe2\x80\x99s consent, engaged a unionized security contractor affiliated with Temco to provide licensed security guards for the building, Temco reassigned respondents to jobs as porters and cleaners. Contending that these reassignments led to a loss in income, other damages, and were otherwise less desirable than their former positions, respondents asked the Union to file grievances alleging, among other things, that petitioners violated the CBA\xe2\x80\x99s ban on workplace discrimination by reassigning respondents on the basis of their age in violation of Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA), 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7621 et seq. The Union requested arbitration under the CBA, but after the initial hearing, withdrew the age-discrimination claims on the ground that its consent to the new security contract precluded it from objecting to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 reassignments as discriminatory. Respondents then filed a complaint with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) alleging that petitioners had violated their ADEA rights, and the EEOC issued each of them a right-to-sue notice. In the ensuing lawsuit, the District Court denied petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 motion to compel arbitration of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 age discrimination claims. The Second Circuit affirmed, holding that Alexander v. Gardner-Denver Co., 415 U. S. 36, forbids enforcement of collective-bargaining provisions requiring arbitration of ADEA claims.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A provision in a collective-bargaining agreement that clearly and unmistakably requires union members to arbitrate ADEA claims is enforceable as a matter of federal law. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Examination of the two federal statutes at issue here, the ADEA and the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), yields a straightforward answer to the question presented. The Union and the RAB, negotiating on behalf of 14 Penn Plaza, collectively bargained in good faith and agreed that employment-related discrimination claims, including ADEA claims, would be resolved in arbitration. This freely negotiated contractual term easily qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9cconditio[n] of employment\xe2\x80\x9d subject to mandatory bargaining under the NLRA, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7159(a). See, e.g., Litton Financial Printing Div., Litton Business Systems, Inc. v. NLRB, 501 U. S. 190, 199. As in any contractual negotiation, a union may agree to the inclusion of an arbitration provision in a collective-bargaining agreement in return for other concessions from the employer, and courts generally may not interfere in this bargained-for exchange. See NLRB v. Magnavox Co., 415 U. S. 322, 328. Thus, the CBA\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration provision must be honored unless the ADEA itself removes this particular class of grievances from the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s broad sweep. See Mitsubishi Motors Corp. v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth, Inc., 473 U. S. 614, 628. It does not. This Court has squarely held that the ADEA does not preclude arbitration of claims brought under the statute. See Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp., 500 U. S. 20, 26\xe2\x80\x9333. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310. Accordingly, there is no legal basis for the Court to strike down the arbitration clause in this CBA, which was freely negotiated by the Union and the RAB, and which clearly and unmistakably requires respondents to arbitrate the age-discrimination claims at issue in this appeal. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The CBA\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration provision is also fully enforceable under the Gardner-Denver line of cases. Respondents incorrectly interpret Gardner-Denver and its progeny as holding that an agreement to arbitrate ADEA claims provided for in a collective-bargaining agreement cannot waive an individual employee\xe2\x80\x99s right to a judicial forum under federal antidiscrimination statutes.. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(i)\xc2\xa0The facts underlying Gardner-Denver and its progeny reveal the narrow scope of the legal rule they engendered. Those cases \xe2\x80\x9cdid not involve the issue of the enforceability of an agreement to arbitrate statutory claims,\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9cthe quite different issue whether arbitration of contract-based claims precluded subsequent judicial resolution of statutory claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Gilmer, supra, at 35. Gardner-Denver does not control the outcome where, as here, the collective-bargaining agreement\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration provision expressly covers both statutory and contractual discrimination claims. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(ii)\xc2\xa0Apart from their narrow holdings, the Gardner-Denver line of cases included broad dicta highly critical of using arbitration to vindicate statutory antidiscrimination rights. That skepticism, however, rested on a misconceived view of arbitration that this Court has since abandoned. First, contrary to Gardner-Denver\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous assumption, 415 U. S., at 51, the decision to resolve ADEA claims by way of arbitration instead of litigation does not waive the statutory right to be free from workplace age discrimination; it waives only the right to seek relief from a court in the first instance, see, e.g., Gilmer, supra, at 26. Second, Gardner-Denver\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken suggestion that certain informal features of arbitration made it a forum \xe2\x80\x9cwell suited to the resolution of contractual disputes,\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9ca comparatively inappropriate forum for the final resolution of [employment] rights.\xe2\x80\x9d 415 U. S., at 56, has been corrected. See, e.g., Shearson/American Express Inc. v. McMahon, 482 U. S. 220, 232. Third, Gardner-Denver\xe2\x80\x99s concern that, in arbitration, a union may subordinate an individual employee\xe2\x80\x99s interests to the collective interests of all employees in the bargaining unit, 415 U. S., at 58, n.\xc2\xa019, cannot be relied on to introduce a qualification into the ADEA that is not found in its text. Until Congress amends the ADEA to meet the conflict-of-interest concern identified in the Gardner-Denver dicta, there is \xe2\x80\x9cno reason to color the lens through which the arbitration clause is read.\xe2\x80\x9d Mitsubishi, supra, at 628. In any event, the conflict-of-interest argument amounts to an unsustainable collateral attack on the NLRA, see Emporium Capwell Co. v. Western Addition Community Organization, 420 U. S. 50, 62, and Congress has accounted for the conflict in several ways: union members may bring a duty of fair representation claim against the union; a union can be subjected to direct liability under the ADEA if it discriminates on the basis of age; and union members may also file age-discrimination claims with the EEOC and the National Labor Relations Board. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Because respondents\xe2\x80\x99 arguments that the CBA does not clearly and unmistakably require them to arbitrate their ADEA claims were not raised in the lower courts, they have been forfeited. Moreover, although a substantive waiver of federally protected civil rights will not be upheld, see, e.g., Mitsubishi, supra, at 637, and n.\xc2\xa019, this Court is not positioned to resolve in the first instance respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim that the CBA allows the Union to prevent them from effectively vindicating their federal statutory rights in the arbitral forum, given that this question would require resolution of contested factual allegations, was not fully briefed here or below, and is not fairly encompassed within the question presented. Resolution now would be particularly inappropriate in light of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s hesitation to invalidate arbitration agreements based on speculation. See, e.g., Green Tree Financial Corp.-Ala. v. Randolph, 531 U. S. 79. Pp.\xc2\xa023\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question presented by this case is whether a provision in a collective-bargaining agreement that clearly and unmistakably requires union members to arbitrate claims arising under the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA), 81 Stat. 602, as amended, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7621 et seq., is enforceable. The United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit held that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Alexander v. Gardner-Denver Co., 415 U. S. 36 (1974), forbids enforcement of such arbitration provisions. We disagree and reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents are members of the Service Employees International Union, Local 32BJ (Union). Under the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA), 49 Stat. 449, as amended, the Union is the exclusive bargaining representative of employees within the building-services industry in New York City, which includes building cleaners, porters, and doorpersons. See 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7159(a). In this role, the Union has exclusive authority to bargain on behalf of its members over their \xe2\x80\x9crates of pay, wages, hours of employment, or other conditions of employment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Since the 1930\xe2\x80\x99s, the Union has engaged in industry-wide collective bargaining with the Realty Advisory Board on Labor Relations, Inc. (RAB), a multiemployer bargaining association for the New York City real-estate industry. The agreement between the Union and the RAB is embodied in their Collective Bargaining Agreement for Contractors and Building Owners (CBA). The CBA requires union members to submit all claims of employment discrimination to binding arbitration under the CBA\xe2\x80\x99s grievance and dispute resolution procedures:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa730 NO DISCRIMINATION. There shall be no discrimination against any present or future employee by reason of race, creed, color, age, disability, national origin, sex, union membership, or any other characteristic protected by law, including, but not limited to, claims made pursuant to Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, the Americans with Disabilities Act, the Age Discrimination in Employment Act, the New York State Human Rights Law, the New York City Human Rights Code, \xe2\x80\xa6 or any other similar laws, rules, or regulations. All such claims shall be subject to the grievance and arbitration procedures (Articles V and VI) as the sole and exclusive remedy for violations. Arbitrators shall apply appropriate law in rendering decisions based upon claims of discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 48a.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner 14 Penn Plaza LLC is a member of the RAB. It owns and operates the New York City office building where, prior to August 2003, respondents worked as night lobby watchmen and in other similar capacities. Respondents were directly employed by petitioner Temco Service Industries, Inc. (Temco), a maintenance service and cleaning contractor. In August 2003, with the Union\xe2\x80\x99s consent, 14 Penn Plaza engaged Spartan Security, a unionized security services contractor and affiliate of Temco, to provide licensed security guards to staff the lobby and entrances of its building. Because this rendered respondents\xe2\x80\x99 lobby services unnecessary, Temco reassigned them to jobs as night porters and light duty cleaners in other locations in the building. Respondents contend that these reassignments led to a loss in income, caused them emotional distress, and were otherwise less desirable than their former positions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At respondents\xe2\x80\x99 request, the Union filed grievances challenging the reassignments. The grievances alleged that petitioners: (1) violated the CBA\xe2\x80\x99s ban on workplace discrimination by reassigning respondents on account of their age; (2) violated seniority rules by failing to promote one of the respondents to a handyman position; and (3) failed to equitably rotate overtime. After failing to obtain relief on any of these claims through the grievance process, the Union requested arbitration under the CBA.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the initial arbitration hearing, the Union withdrew the first set of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 grievances\xe2\x80\x94the age-discrimination claims\xe2\x80\x94from arbitration. Because it had consented to the contract for new security personnel at 14 Penn Plaza, the Union believed that it could not legitimately object to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 reassignments as discriminatory. But the Union continued to arbitrate the seniority and overtime claims, and, after several hearings, the claims were denied.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In May 2004, while the arbitration was ongoing but after the Union withdrew the age-discrimination claims, respondents filed a complaint with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) alleging that petitioners had violated their rights under the ADEA. Approximately one month later, the EEOC issued a Dismissal and Notice of Rights, which explained that the agency\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98review of the evidence \xe2\x80\xa6 fail[ed] to indicate that a violation ha[d] occurred,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and notified each respondent of his right to sue. Pyett v. Pennsylvania Building Co., 498 F.\xc2\xa03d 88, 91 (CA2 2007).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents thereafter filed suit against petitioners in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York, alleging that their reassignment violated the ADEA and state and local laws prohibiting age discrimination.[Footnote 2] Petitioners filed a motion to compel arbitration of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims pursuant to \xc2\xa73 and \xc2\xa74 of the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA), 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73, 4.[Footnote 3] The District Court denied the motion because under Second Circuit precedent, \xe2\x80\x9ceven a clear and unmistakable union-negotiated waiver of a right to litigate certain federal and state statutory claims in a judicial forum is unenforceable.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 21a. Respondents immediately appealed the ruling under \xc2\xa716 of the FAA, which authorizes an interlocutory appeal of \xe2\x80\x9can order \xe2\x80\xa6 refusing a stay of any action under section 3 of this title\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cdenying a petition under section 4 of this title to order arbitration to proceed.\xe2\x80\x9d 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa716(a)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x93(B).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals affirmed. 498 F.\xc2\xa03d 88. According to the Court of Appeals, it could not compel arbitration of the dispute because Gardner-Denver, which \xe2\x80\x9cremains good law,\xe2\x80\x9d held \xe2\x80\x9cthat a collective bargaining agreement could not waive covered workers\xe2\x80\x99 rights to a judicial forum for causes of action created by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d 498 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 92, 91, n.\xc2\xa03 (citing Gardner-Denver, 415 U. S., at 49\xe2\x80\x9351). The Court of Appeals observed that the Gardner-Denver decision was in tension with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s more recent decision in Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp., 500 U. S. 20 (1991), which \xe2\x80\x9cheld that an individual employee who had agreed individually to waive his right to a federal forum could be compelled to arbitrate a federal age discrimination claim.\xe2\x80\x9d 498 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 91, n.\xc2\xa03 (citing Gilmer, supra, at 33\xe2\x80\x9335; emphasis in original). The Court of Appeals also noted that this Court previously declined to resolve this tension in Wright v. Universal Maritime Service Corp., 525 U. S. 70, 82 (1998), where the waiver at issue was not \xe2\x80\x9cclear and unmistakable.\xe2\x80\x9d 498 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 91, n.\xc2\xa03.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals attempted to reconcile Gardner-Denver and Gilmer by holding that arbitration provisions in a collective-bargaining agreement, \xe2\x80\x9cwhich purport to waive employees\xe2\x80\x99 rights to a federal forum with respect to statutory claims, are unenforceable.\xe2\x80\x9d 498 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 93\xe2\x80\x9394. As a result, an individual employee would be free to choose compulsory arbitration under Gilmer, but a labor union could not collectively bargain for arbitration on behalf of its members. We granted certiorari, 552 U. S. ___ (2008), to address the issue left unresolved in Wright, which continues to divide the Courts of Appeals,[Footnote 4] and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The NLRA governs federal labor-relations law. As permitted by that statute, respondents designated the Union as their \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive representativ[e] \xe2\x80\xa6 for the purposes of collective bargaining in respect to rates of pay, wages, hours of employment, or other conditions of employment.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7159(a). As the employees\xe2\x80\x99 exclusive bargaining representative, the Union \xe2\x80\x9cenjoys broad authority \xe2\x80\xa6 in the negotiation and administration of [the] collective bargaining contract.\xe2\x80\x9d Communications Workers v. Beck, 487 U. S. 735, 739 (1988) (internal quotation marks omitted). But this broad authority \xe2\x80\x9cis accompanied by a responsibility of equal scope, the responsibility and duty of fair representation.\xe2\x80\x9d Humphrey v. Moore, 375 U. S. 335, 342 (1964). The employer has a corresponding duty under the NLRA to bargain in good faith \xe2\x80\x9cwith the representatives of his employees\xe2\x80\x9d on wages, hours, and conditions of employment. 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7158(a)(5); see also \xc2\xa7158(d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this instance, the Union and the RAB, negotiating on behalf of 14 Penn Plaza, collectively bargained in good faith and agreed that employment-related discrimination claims, including claims brought under the ADEA, would be resolved in arbitration. This freely negotiated term between the Union and the RAB easily qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9cconditio[n] of employment\xe2\x80\x9d that is subject to mandatory bargaining under \xc2\xa7159(a). See Litton Financial Printing Div., Litton Business Systems, Inc. v. NLRB, 501 U. S. 190, 199 (1991) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]rrangements for arbitration of disputes are a term or condition of employment and a mandatory subject of bargaining\xe2\x80\x9d); Steelworkers v. Warrior & Gulf Nav. Co., 363 U. S. 574, 578 (1960) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]rbitration of labor disputes under collective bargaining agreements is part and parcel of the collective bargaining process itself\xe2\x80\x9d); Textile Workers v. Lincoln Mills of Ala., 353 U. S. 448, 455 (1957) (\xe2\x80\x9cPlainly the agreement to arbitrate grievance disputes is the quid pro quo for an agreement not to strike\xe2\x80\x9d). The decision to fashion a CBA to require arbitration of employment-discrimination claims is no different from the many other decisions made by parties in designing grievance machinery.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents, however, contend that the arbitration clause here is outside the permissible scope of the collective-bargaining process because it affects the \xe2\x80\x9cemployees\xe2\x80\x99 individual, non-economic statutory rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 22; see also post, at 5\xe2\x80\x936 (Souter, J., dissenting). We disagree. Parties generally favor arbitration precisely because of the economics of dispute resolution. See Circuit City Stores, Inc. v. Adams, 532 U. S. 105, 123 (2001) (\xe2\x80\x9cArbitration agreements allow parties to avoid the costs of litigation, a benefit that may be of particular importance in employment litigation, which often involves smaller sums of money than disputes concerning commercial contracts\xe2\x80\x9d). As in any contractual negotiation, a union may agree to the inclusion of an arbitration provision in a collective-bargaining agreement in return for other concessions from the employer. Courts generally may not interfere in this bargained-for exchange. \xe2\x80\x9cJudicial nullification of contractual concessions \xe2\x80\xa6 is contrary to what the Court has recognized as one of the fundamental policies of the National Labor Relations Act\xe2\x80\x94freedom of contract.\xe2\x80\x9d NLRB v. Magnavox Co., 415 U. S. 322, 328 (1974) (Stewart, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a result, the CBA\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration provision must be honored unless the ADEA itself removes this particular class of grievances from the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s broad sweep. See Mitsubishi Motors Corp. v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth, Inc., 473 U. S. 614, 628 (1985). It does not. This Court has squarely held that the ADEA does not preclude arbitration of claims brought under the statute. See Gilmer, 500 U. S., at 26\xe2\x80\x9333.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Gilmer, the Court explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]lthough all statutory claims may not be appropriate for arbitration, \xe2\x80\x98having made the bargain to arbitrate, the party should be held to it unless Congress itself has evinced an intention to preclude a waiver of judicial remedies for the statutory rights at issue.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 26 (quoting Mitsubishi Motors Corp., supra, at 628). And \xe2\x80\x9cif Congress intended the substantive protection afforded by the ADEA to include protection against waiver of the right to a judicial forum, that intention will be deducible from text or legislative history.\xe2\x80\x9d 500 U. S., at 29 (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted). The Court determined that \xe2\x80\x9cnothing in the text of the ADEA or its legislative history explicitly precludes arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 26\xe2\x80\x9327. The Court also concluded that arbitrating ADEA disputes would not undermine the statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cremedial and deterrent function.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 28 (internal quotation marks omitted). In the end, the employee\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cgeneralized attacks\xe2\x80\x9d on \xe2\x80\x9cthe adequacy of arbitration procedures\xe2\x80\x9d were \xe2\x80\x9cinsufficient to preclude arbitration of statutory claims,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 30, because there was no evidence that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress, in enacting the ADEA, intended to preclude arbitration of claims under that Act,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 35.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Gilmer Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the ADEA fully applies in the collective-bargaining context. Nothing in the law suggests a distinction between the status of arbitration agreements signed by an individual employee and those agreed to by a union representative. This Court has required only that an agreement to arbitrate statutory antidiscrimination claims be \xe2\x80\x9cexplicitly stated\xe2\x80\x9d in the collective-bargaining agreement. Wright, 525 U. S., at 80 (internal quotation marks omitted). The CBA under review here meets that obligation. Respondents incorrectly counter that an individual employee must personally \xe2\x80\x9cwaive\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9c[substantive] right\xe2\x80\x9d to proceed in court for a waiver to be \xe2\x80\x9cknowing and voluntary\xe2\x80\x9d under the ADEA. 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(f)(1). As explained below, however, the agreement to arbitrate ADEA claims is not the waiver of a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive right\xe2\x80\x9d as that term is employed in the ADEA. Wright, supra, at 80; see infra, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. Indeed, if the \xe2\x80\x9cright\xe2\x80\x9d referred to in \xc2\xa7626(f)(1) included the prospective waiver of the right to bring an ADEA claim in court, even a waiver signed by an individual employee would be invalid as the statute also prevents individuals from \xe2\x80\x9cwaiv[ing] rights or claims that may arise after the date the waiver is executed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7626(f)(1)(C).[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Examination of the two federal statutes at issue in this case, therefore, yields a straightforward answer to the question presented: The NLRA provided the Union and the RAB with statutory authority to collectively bargain for arbitration of workplace discrimination claims, and Congress did not terminate that authority with respect to federal age-discrimination claims in the ADEA. Accordingly, there is no legal basis for the Court to strike down the arbitration clause in this CBA, which was freely negotiated by the Union and the RAB, and which clearly and unmistakably requires respondents to arbitrate the age-discrimination claims at issue in this appeal. Congress has chosen to allow arbitration of ADEA claims. The Judiciary must respect that choice.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The CBA\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration provision is also fully enforceable under the Gardner-Denver line of cases. Respondents interpret Gardner-Denver and its progeny to hold that \xe2\x80\x9ca union cannot waive an employee\xe2\x80\x99s right to a judicial forum under the federal antidiscrimination statutes\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9callowing the union to waive this right would substitute the union\xe2\x80\x99s interests for the employee\xe2\x80\x99s antidiscrimination rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 12. The \xe2\x80\x9ccombination of union control over the process and inherent conflict of interest with respect to discrimination claims,\xe2\x80\x9d they argue, \xe2\x80\x9cprovided the foundation for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding [in Gardner-Denver] that arbitration under a collective-bargaining agreement could not preclude an individual employee\xe2\x80\x99s right to bring a lawsuit in court to vindicate a statutory discrimination claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 15. We disagree.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The holding of Gardner-Denver is not as broad as respondents suggest. The employee in that case was covered by a collective-bargaining agreement that prohibited \xe2\x80\x9cdiscrimination against any employee on account of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, or ancestry\xe2\x80\x9d and that guaranteed that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o employee will be discharged \xe2\x80\xa6 except for just cause.\xe2\x80\x9d 415 U. S., at 39 (internal quotation marks omitted). The agreement also included a \xe2\x80\x9cmultistep grievance procedure\xe2\x80\x9d that culminated in compulsory arbitration for any \xe2\x80\x9cdifferences aris[ing] between the Company and the Union as to the meaning and application of the provisions of this Agreement\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cany trouble aris[ing] in the plant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 40\xe2\x80\x9341 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The employee was discharged for allegedly producing too many defective parts while working for the respondent as a drill operator. He filed a grievance with his union claiming that he was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unjustly discharged\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in violation of the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98just cause\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d provision within the CBA. Then at the final prearbitration step of the grievance process, the employee added a claim that he was discharged because of his race. Id., at 38\xe2\x80\x9342.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The arbitrator ultimately ruled that the employee had been \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98discharged for just cause,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9cmade no reference to [the] claim of racial discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 42. After obtaining a right-to-sue letter from the EEOC, the employee filed a claim in Federal District Court, alleging racial discrimination in violation of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. The District Court issued a decision, affirmed by the Court of Appeals, which granted summary judgment to the employer because it concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe claim of racial discrimination had been submitted to the arbitrator and resolved adversely to [the employee].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 43. In the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xe2\x80\x9chaving voluntarily elected to pursue his grievance to final arbitration under the nondiscrimination clause of the collective-bargaining agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d the employee was \xe2\x80\x9cbound by the arbitral decision\xe2\x80\x9d and precluded from suing his employer on any other grounds, such as a statutory claim under Title VII. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court reversed the judgment on the narrow ground that the arbitration was not preclusive because the collective-bargaining agreement did not cover statutory claims. As a result, the lower courts erred in relying on the \xe2\x80\x9cdoctrine of election of remedies\xe2\x80\x9d to bar the employee\xe2\x80\x99s Title VII claim. Id., at 49. \xe2\x80\x9cThat doctrine, which refers to situations where an individual pursues remedies that are legally or factually inconsistent\xe2\x80\x9d with each other, did not apply to the employee\xe2\x80\x99s dual pursuit of arbitration and a Title VII discrimination claim in district court. The employee\xe2\x80\x99s collective-bargaining agreement did not mandate arbitration of statutory antidiscrimination claims. Id., at 49\xe2\x80\x9350. \xe2\x80\x9cAs the proctor of the bargain, the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s task is to effectuate the intent of the parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53. Because the collective-bargaining agreement gave the arbitrator \xe2\x80\x9cauthority to resolve only questions of contractual rights,\xe2\x80\x9d his decision could not prevent the employee from bringing the Title VII claim in federal court \xe2\x80\x9cregardless of whether certain contractual rights are similar to, or duplicative of, the substantive rights secured by Title VII.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53\xe2\x80\x9354; see also id., at 50.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court also explained that the employee had not waived his right to pursue his Title VII claim in federal court by participating in an arbitration that was premised on the same underlying facts as the Title VII claim. See id., at 52. Thus, whether the legal theory of preclusion advanced by the employer rested on \xe2\x80\x9cthe doctrines of election of remedies\xe2\x80\x9d or was recast \xe2\x80\x9cas resting instead on the doctrine of equitable estoppel and on themes of res judicata and collateral estoppel,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 49, n.\xc2\xa010 (internal quotation marks omitted), it could not prevail in light of the collective-bargaining agreement\xe2\x80\x99s failure to address arbitration of Title VII claims. See id., at 46, n.\xc2\xa06 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e hold that the federal policy favoring arbitration does not establish that an arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of a contractual claim is dispositive of a statutory claim under Title VII\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions following Gardner-Denver have not broadened its holding to make it applicable to the facts of this case. In Barrentine v. Arkansas-Best Freight System, Inc., 450 U. S. 728 (1981), the Court considered \xe2\x80\x9cwhether an employee may bring an action in federal district court, alleging a violation of the minimum wage provisions of the Fair Labor Standards Act, \xe2\x80\xa6 after having unsuccessfully submitted a wage claim based on the same underlying facts to a joint grievance committee pursuant to the provisions of his union\xe2\x80\x99s collective-bargaining agreement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 729\xe2\x80\x93730. The Court held that the unsuccessful arbitration did not preclude the federal lawsuit. Like the collective-bargaining agreement in Gardner-Denver, the arbitration provision under review in Barrentine did not expressly reference the statutory claim at issue. See 450 U. S., at 731, n.\xc2\xa05. The Court thus reiterated that an \xe2\x80\x9carbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s power is both derived from, and limited by, the collective-bargaining agreement\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[h]is task is limited to construing the meaning of the collective-bargaining agreement so as to effectuate the collective intent of the parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 744.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0McDonald v. West Branch, 466 U. S. 284 (1984), was decided along similar lines. The question presented in that case was \xe2\x80\x9cwhether a federal court may accord preclusive effect to an unappealed arbitration award in a case brought under [42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 285. The Court declined to fashion such a rule, again explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cbecause an arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s authority derives solely from the contract, Barrentine, supra, at 744, an arbitrator may not have authority to enforce \xc2\xa71983\xe2\x80\x9d when that provision is left unaddressed by the arbitration agreement. Id., at 290. Accordingly, as in both Gardner-Denver and Barrentine, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in McDonald hinged on the scope of the collective-bargaining agreement and the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s parallel mandate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The facts underlying Gardner-Denver, Barrentine, and McDonald reveal the narrow scope of the legal rule arising from that trilogy of decisions. Summarizing those opinions in Gilmer, this Court made clear that the Gardner-Denver line of cases \xe2\x80\x9cdid not involve the issue of the enforceability of an agreement to arbitrate statutory claims.\xe2\x80\x9d 500 U. S., at 35. Those decisions instead \xe2\x80\x9cinvolved the quite different issue whether arbitration of contract-based claims precluded subsequent judicial resolution of statutory claims. Since the employees there had not agreed to arbitrate their statutory claims, and the labor arbitrators were not authorized to resolve such claims, the arbitration in those cases understandably was held not to preclude subsequent statutory actions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also Wright, 525 U. S., at 76; Livadas v. Bradshaw, 512 U. S. 107, 127, n.\xc2\xa021 (1994).[Footnote 7] Gardner-Denver and its progeny thus do not control the outcome where, as is the case here, the collective-bargaining agreement\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration provision expressly covers both statutory and contractual discrimination claims.[Footnote 8]

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We recognize that apart from their narrow holdings, the Gardner-Denver line of cases included broad dicta that was highly critical of the use of arbitration for the vindication of statutory antidiscrimination rights. That skepticism, however, rested on a misconceived view of arbitration that this Court has since abandoned.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the Court in Gardner-Denver erroneously assumed that an agreement to submit statutory discrimination claims to arbitration was tantamount to a waiver of those rights. See 415 U. S., at 51. (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here can be no prospective waiver of an employee\xe2\x80\x99s rights under Title VII\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). For this reason, the Court stated, \xe2\x80\x9cthe rights conferred [by Title VII] can form no part of the collective-bargaining process since waiver of these rights would defeat the paramount congressional purpose behind Title VII.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also id., at 56 (\xe2\x80\x9cwe have long recognized that \xe2\x80\x98the choice of forums inevitably affects the scope of the substantive right to be vindicated\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting U. S. Bulk Carriers, Inc. v. Arguelles, 400 U. S. 351, 359\xe2\x80\x93360 (1971) (Harlan, J., concurring))).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court was correct in concluding that federal antidiscrimination rights may not be prospectively waived, see 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(f)(1)(C); see supra, at 9, but it confused an agreement to arbitrate those statutory claims with a prospective waiver of the substantive right. The decision to resolve ADEA claims by way of arbitration instead of litigation does not waive the statutory right to be free from workplace age discrimination; it waives only the right to seek relief from a court in the first instance. See Gilmer, supra, at 26 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[B]y agreeing to arbitrate a statutory claim, a party does not forgo the substantive rights afforded by the statute; it only submits to their resolution in an arbitral, rather than a judicial, forum\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Mitsubishi Motors Corp., 473 U. S., at 628)). This \xe2\x80\x9cCourt has been quite specific in holding that arbitration agreements can be enforced under the FAA without contravening the policies of congressional enactments giving employees specific protection against discrimination prohibited by federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Circuit City Stores, Inc., 532 U. S., at 123. The suggestion in Gardner-Denver that the decision to arbitrate statutory discrimination claims was tantamount to a substantive waiver of those rights, therefore, reveals a distorted understanding of the compromise made when an employee agrees to compulsory arbitration.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this respect, Gardner-Denver is a direct descendant of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Wilko v. Swan, 346 U. S. 427 (1953), which held that an agreement to arbitrate claims under the Securities Act of 1933 was unenforceable. See id., at 438. The Court subsequently overruled Wilko and, in so doing, characterized the decision as \xe2\x80\x9cpervaded by \xe2\x80\xa6 \xe2\x80\x98the old judicial hostility to arbitration.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Rodriguez de Quijas v. Shearson/American Express, Inc., 490 U. S. 477, 480 (1989). The Court added: \xe2\x80\x9cTo the extent that Wilko rested on suspicion of arbitration as a method of weakening the protections afforded in the substantive law to would-be complainants, it has fallen far out of step with our current strong endorsement of the federal statutes favoring this method of resolving disputes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 481; see also Mitsubishi Motors Corp., supra, at 626\xe2\x80\x93627 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e are well past the time when judicial suspicion of the desirability of arbitration and of the competence of arbitral tribunals inhibited the development of arbitration as an alternative means of dispute resolution\xe2\x80\x9d). The timeworn \xe2\x80\x9cmistrust of the arbitral process\xe2\x80\x9d harbored by the Court in Gardner-Denver thus weighs against reliance on anything more than its core holding. Shearson/American Express Inc. v. McMahon, 482 U. S. 220, 231\xe2\x80\x93232 (1987); see also Gilmer, 500 U. S., at 34, n.\xc2\xa05 (reiterating that Gardner-Denver\xe2\x80\x99s view of arbitration \xe2\x80\x9chas been undermined by [the Court\xe2\x80\x99s] recent arbitration decisions\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, in light of the \xe2\x80\x9cradical change, over two decades, in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s receptivity to arbitration,\xe2\x80\x9d Wright, 525 U. S., at 77, reliance on any judicial decision similarly littered with Wilko\xe2\x80\x99s overt hostility to the enforcement of arbitration agreements would be ill advised. [Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, Gardner-Denver mistakenly suggested that certain features of arbitration made it a forum \xe2\x80\x9cwell suited to the resolution of contractual disputes,\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9ca comparatively inappropriate forum for the final resolution of rights created by Title VII.\xe2\x80\x9d 415 U. S., at 56. According to the Court, the \xe2\x80\x9cfactfinding process in arbitration\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cnot equivalent to judicial factfinding\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cinformality of arbitral procedure \xe2\x80\xa6 makes arbitration a less appropriate forum for final resolution of Title VII issues than the federal courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 57, 58. The Court also questioned the competence of arbitrators to decide federal statutory claims. See id., at 57 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he specialized competence of arbitrators pertains primarily to the law of the shop, not the law of the land\xe2\x80\x9d); Barrentine, 450 U. S., at 743 (\xe2\x80\x9cAlthough an arbitrator may be competent to resolve many preliminary factual questions, such as whether the employee \xe2\x80\x98punched in\xe2\x80\x99 when he said he did, he may lack competence to decide the ultimate legal issue whether an employee\xe2\x80\x99s right to a minimum wage or to overtime pay under the statute has been violated\xe2\x80\x9d). In the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xe2\x80\x9cthe resolution of statutory or constitutional issues is a primary responsibility of courts, and judicial construction has proved especially necessary with respect to Title VII, whose broad language frequently can be given meaning only by reference to public law concepts.\xe2\x80\x9d Gardner-Denver, supra, at 57; see also McDonald, 466 U. S., at 290 (\xe2\x80\x9cAn arbitrator may not \xe2\x80\xa6 have the expertise required to resolve the complex legal questions that arise in \xc2\xa71983 actions\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These misconceptions have been corrected. For example, the Court has \xe2\x80\x9crecognized that arbitral tribunals are readily capable of handling the factual and legal complexities of antitrust claims, notwithstanding the absence of judicial instruction and supervision\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no reason to assume at the outset that arbitrators will not follow the law.\xe2\x80\x9d McMahon, supra, at 232; Mitsubishi Motors Corp., 473 U. S., at 634 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe decline to indulge the presumption that the parties and arbitral body conducting a proceeding will be unable or unwilling to retain competent, conscientious, and impartial arbitrators\xe2\x80\x9d). An arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s capacity to resolve complex questions of fact and law extends with equal force to discrimination claims brought under the ADEA. Moreover, the recognition that arbitration procedures are more streamlined than federal litigation is not a basis for finding the forum somehow inadequate; the relative informality of arbitration is one of the chief reasons that parties select arbitration. Parties \xe2\x80\x9ctrad[e] the procedures and opportunity for review of the courtroom for the simplicity, informality, and expedition of arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 628. In any event, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is unlikely \xe2\x80\xa6 that age discrimination claims require more extensive discovery than other claims that we have found to be arbitrable, such as RICO and antitrust claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Gilmer, 500 U. S., at 31. At bottom, objections centered on the nature of arbitration do not offer a credible basis for discrediting the choice of that forum to resolve statutory antidiscrimination claims.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, the Court in Gardner-Denver raised in a footnote a \xe2\x80\x9cfurther concern\xe2\x80\x9d regarding \xe2\x80\x9cthe union\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive control over the manner and extent to which an individual grievance is presented.\xe2\x80\x9d 415 U. S., at 58, n.\xc2\xa019. The Court suggested that in arbitration, as in the collective-bargaining process, a union may subordinate the interests of an individual employee to the collective interests of all employees in the bargaining unit. Ibid.; see also McDonald, supra, at 291 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe union\xe2\x80\x99s interests and those of the individual employee are not always identical or even compatible. As a result, the union may present the employee\xe2\x80\x99s grievance less vigorously, or make different strategic choices, than would the employee\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Barrentine, supra, at 742; post, at 8, n.\xc2\xa04 (Souter,\xc2\xa0J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We cannot rely on this judicial policy concern as a source of authority for introducing a qualification into the ADEA that is not found in its text. Absent a constitutional barrier, \xe2\x80\x9cit is not for us to substitute our view of \xe2\x80\xa6 policy for the legislation which has been passed by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Florida Dept. of Revenue v. Piccadilly Cafeterias, Inc., 554 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 18) (internal quotation marks omitted). Congress is fully equipped \xe2\x80\x9cto identify any category of claims as to which agreements to arbitrate will be held unenforceable.\xe2\x80\x9d Mitsubishi Motors Corp., supra, at 627. Until Congress amends the ADEA to meet the conflict-of-interest concern identified in the Gardner-Denver dicta, and seized on by respondents here, there is \xe2\x80\x9cno reason to color the lens through which the arbitration clause is read\xe2\x80\x9d simply because of an alleged conflict of interest between a union and its members. Mitsubishi Motors Corp., supra, at 628. This is a \xe2\x80\x9cbattl[e] that should be fought among the political branches and the industry. Those parties should not seek to amend the statute by appeal to the Judicial Branch.\xe2\x80\x9d Barnhart v. Sigmon Coal Co., 534 U. S. 438, 462 (2002).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The conflict-of-interest argument also proves too much. Labor unions certainly balance the economic interests of some employees against the needs of the larger work force as they negotiate collective-bargain agreements and implement them on a daily basis. But this attribute of organized labor does not justify singling out an arbitration provision for disfavored treatment. This \xe2\x80\x9cprinciple of majority rule\xe2\x80\x9d to which respondents object is in fact the central premise of the NLRA. Emporium Capwell Co. v. Western Addition Community Organization, 420 U. S. 50, 62 (1975). \xe2\x80\x9cIn establishing a regime of majority rule, Congress sought to secure to all members of the unit the benefits of their collective strength and bargaining power, in full awareness that the superior strength of some individuals or groups might be subordinated to the interest of the majority.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (footnote omitted); see also Ford Motor Co. v. Huffman, 345 U. S. 330, 338 (1953) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe complete satisfaction of all who are represented is hardly to be expected\xe2\x80\x9d); Pennsylvania R. Co. v. Rychlik, 352 U. S. 480, 498 (1957) (Frankfurter, J., concurring). It was Congress\xe2\x80\x99 verdict that the benefits of organized labor outweigh the sacrifice of individual liberty that this system necessarily demands. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 argument that they were deprived of the right to pursue their ADEA claims in federal court by a labor union with a conflict of interest is therefore unsustainable; it amounts to a collateral attack on the NLRA.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, Congress has accounted for this conflict of interest in several ways. As indicated above, the NLRA has been interpreted to impose a \xe2\x80\x9cduty of fair representation\xe2\x80\x9d on labor unions, which a union breaches \xe2\x80\x9cwhen its conduct toward a member of the bargaining unit is arbitrary, discriminatory, or in bad faith.\xe2\x80\x9d Marquez v. Screen Actors, 525 U. S. 33, 44 (1998). This duty extends to \xe2\x80\x9cchallenges leveled not only at a union\xe2\x80\x99s contract administration and enforcement efforts but at its negotiation activities as well.\xe2\x80\x9d Beck, 487 U. S., at 743 (citation omitted). Thus, a union is subject to liability under the NLRA if it illegally discriminates against older workers in either the formation or governance of the collective-bargaining agreement, such as by deciding not to pursue a grievance on behalf of one of its members for discriminatory reasons. See Vaca v. Sipes, 386 U. S. 171, 177 (1967) (describing the duty of fair representation as the \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory obligation to serve the interests of all members without hostility or discrimination toward any, to exercise its discretion with complete good faith and honesty, and to avoid arbitrary conduct\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Respondents in fact brought a fair representation suit against the Union based on its withdrawal of support for their age-discrimination claims. See n.\xc2\xa02, supra. Given this avenue that Congress has made available to redress a union\xe2\x80\x99s violation of its duty to its members, it is particularly inappropriate to ask this Court to impose an artificial limitation on the collective-bargaining process.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, a union is subject to liability under the ADEA if the union itself discriminates against its members on the basis of age. See 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7623(d); see also 1 B.\xc2\xa0Lindemann & P.\xc2\xa0Grossman, Employment Discrimination Law 1575\xe2\x80\x931581 (4th ed. 2007) (explaining that a labor union may be held jointly liable with an employer under federal antidiscrimination laws for discriminating in the formation of a collective-bargaining agreement, knowingly acquiescing in the employer\xe2\x80\x99s discrimination, or inducing the employer to discriminate); cf. Goodman v. Lukens Steel Co., 482 U. S. 656, 669 (1987). Union members may also file age-discrimination claims with the EEOC and the National Labor Relations Board, which may then seek judicial intervention under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent. See EEOC v. Waffle House, Inc., 534 U. S. 279, 295\xe2\x80\x93296 (2002). In sum, Congress has provided remedies for the situation where a labor union is less than vigorous in defense of its members\xe2\x80\x99 claims of discrimination under the ADEA.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, respondents offer a series of arguments contending that the particular CBA at issue here does not clearly and unmistakably require them to arbitrate their ADEA claims. See Brief for Respondents 44\xe2\x80\x9347. But respondents did not raise these contract-based arguments in the District Court or the Court of Appeals. To the contrary, respondents acknowledged on appeal that the CBA provision requiring arbitration of their federal antidiscrimination statutory claims \xe2\x80\x9cis sufficiently explicit\xe2\x80\x9d in precluding their federal lawsuit. Brief for Plaintiffs-Appellees in No. 06\xe2\x80\x933047\xe2\x80\x93cv(L) etc. (CA2), p.\xc2\xa09. In light of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 litigating position, both lower courts assumed that the CBA\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration clause clearly applied to respondents and proceeded to decide the question left unresolved in Wright. We granted review of the question presented on that understanding.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cWithout cross-petitioning for certiorari, a prevailing party may, of course, \xe2\x80\x98defend its judgment on any ground properly raised below whether or not that ground was relied upon, rejected, or even considered by the District Court or the Court of Appeals.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Granfinanciera, S. A. v. Nordberg, 492 U. S. 33, 38\xe2\x80\x9339 (1989) (quoting Washington v. Confederated Bands and Tribes of Yakima Nation, 439 U. S. 463, 476, n.\xc2\xa020 (1979)). But this Court will affirm on grounds that have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98not been raised below \xe2\x80\xa6 \xe2\x80\x9conly in exceptional cases.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Nordberg, supra, at 39 (quoting Heckler v. Campbell, 461 U. S. 458, 468\xe2\x80\x93469, n.\xc2\xa012 (1983)). This is not an \xe2\x80\x9cexceptional case.\xe2\x80\x9d As a result, we find that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 alternative arguments for affirmance have been forfeited. See, e.g., Rita v. United States, 551 U. S. 338, 360 (2007); Sprietsma v. Mercury Marine, 537 U. S. 51, 56, n.\xc2\xa04 (2002). We will not resurrect them on respondents\xe2\x80\x99 behalf.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents also argue that the CBA operates as a substantive waiver of their ADEA rights because it not only precludes a federal lawsuit, but also allows the Union to block arbitration of these claims. Brief for Respondents 28\xe2\x80\x9330. Petitioners contest this characterization of the CBA, see Reply Brief for Petitioners 23\xe2\x80\x9327, and offer record evidence suggesting that the Union has allowed respondents to continue with the arbitration even though the Union has declined to participate, see App. to Pet. for Cert. 42a. But not only does this question require resolution of contested factual allegations, it was not fully briefed to this or any court and is not fairly encompassed within the question presented, see this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 14.1(a). Thus, although a substantive waiver of federally protected civil rights will not be upheld, see Mitsubishi Motors Corp., 473 U. S., at 637, and n.\xc2\xa019; Gilmer, 500 U. S., at 29, we are not positioned to resolve in the first instance whether the CBA allows the Union to prevent respondents from \xe2\x80\x9ceffectively vindicating\xe2\x80\x9d their \xe2\x80\x9cfederal statutory rights in the arbitral forum,\xe2\x80\x9d Green Tree Financial Corp.-Ala. v. Randolph, 531 U. S. 79, 90 (2000). Resolution of this question at this juncture would be particularly inappropriate in light of our hesitation to invalidate arbitration agreements on the basis of speculation. See id., at 91.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that a collective-bargaining agreement that clearly and unmistakably requires union members to arbitrate ADEA claims is enforceable as a matter of federal law. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Souter\xe2\x80\x99s dissenting opinion, which I join in full, explains why our decision in Alexander v. Gardner-Denver Co., 415 U. S. 36 (1974), answers the question presented in this case. My concern regarding the Court\xe2\x80\x99s subversion of precedent to the policy favoring arbitration prompts these additional remarks.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notwithstanding the absence of change in any relevant statutory provision, the Court has recently retreated from, and in some cases reversed, prior decisions based on its changed view of the merits of arbitration. Previously, the Court approached with caution questions involving a union\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of an employee\xe2\x80\x99s right to raise statutory claims in a federal judicial forum. After searching the text and purposes of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Court in Gardner-Denver held that a clause of a collective-bargaining agreement (CBA) requiring arbitration of discrimination claims could not waive an employee\xe2\x80\x99s right to a judicial forum for statutory claims. See 415 U. S., at 51. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision rested on several features of the statute, including the individual nature of the rights it confers, the broad remedial powers it grants federal courts, and its expressed preference for overlapping remedies. See id., at 44\xe2\x80\x9348. The Court also noted the problem of entrusting a union with certain arbitration decisions given the potential conflict between the collective interest and the interests of an individual employee seeking to assert his rights. See id., at 58, n.\xc2\xa019. That concern later provided a basis for our decisions in Barrentine v. Arkansas-Best Freight System, Inc., 450 U. S. 728, 742 (1981), and McDonald v. West Branch, 466 U. S. 284, 291 (1984), which similarly held that a CBA may not commit enforcement of certain rights-creating statutes exclusively to a union-controlled arbitration process. Congress has taken no action signaling disagreement with those decisions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The statutes construed by the Court in the foregoing cases and in Wilko v. Swan, 346 U. S. 427 (1953), have not since been amended in any relevant respect. But the Court has in a number of cases replaced our predecessors\xe2\x80\x99 statutory analysis with judicial reasoning espousing a policy favoring arbitration and thereby reached divergent results. I dissented in those cases to express concern that my colleagues were making policy choices not made by Congress. See Mitsubishi Motors Corp. v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth, Inc., 473 U. S. 614, 640 (1985); Rodriguez de Quijas v. Shearson/American Express, Inc., 490 U. S. 477, 486 (1989); Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp., 500 U. S. 20, 36 (1991); and Circuit City Stores, Inc. v. Adams, 532 U. S. 105, 124 (2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today the majority\xe2\x80\x99s preference for arbitration again leads it to disregard our precedent. Although it purports to ascertain the relationship between the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA), the National Labor Relations Act, and the Federal Arbitration Act, the Court ignores our earlier determination of the relevant provisions\xe2\x80\x99 meaning. The Court concludes that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t was Congress\xe2\x80\x99 verdict that the benefits of organized labor outweigh the sacrifice of individual liberty\xe2\x80\x9d that the system of organized labor \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily demands,\xe2\x80\x9d even when the sacrifice demanded is a judicial forum for asserting an individual statutory right. Ante, at 22. But in Gard-\nner-Denver we determined that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress\xe2\x80\x99 verdict\xe2\x80\x9d was otherwise when we held that Title VII does not permit a CBA to waive an employee\xe2\x80\x99s right to a federal judicial forum. Because the purposes and relevant provisions of Title VII and the ADEA are not meaningfully distinguishable, it is only by reexamining the statutory questions resolved in Gardner-Denver through the lens of the policy favoring arbitration that the majority now reaches a different result.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the circumstances, I believe a passage from one of my earlier dissents merits repetition. The Court in Rodriguez de Quijas overruled our decision in Wilko and held that predispute agreements to arbitrate claims under the Securities Act of 1933 are enforceable. 490 U. S., at 484; see also id., at 481 (noting Wilko\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on \xe2\x80\x9cthe outmoded presumption of disfavoring arbitration proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d). I observed in dissent:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIn the final analysis, a Justice\xe2\x80\x99s vote in a case like this depends more on his or her views about the respective lawmaking responsibilities of Congress and this Court than on conflicting policy interests. Judges who have confidence in their own ability to fashion public policy are less hesitant to change the law than those of us who are inclined to give wide latitude to the views of the voters\xe2\x80\x99 representatives on nonconstitutional matters. Cf. Boyle v. United Technologies Corp., 487 U. S. 500 (1988). As I pointed out years ago, Alberto-Culver Co. v. Scherk, 484 F.\xc2\xa02d 611 (CA7 1973) (dissenting opinion), rev\xe2\x80\x99d, 417 U. S. 506 (1974), there are valid policy and textual arguments on both sides regarding the interrelation of federal securities and arbitration Acts. None of these arguments, however, carries sufficient weight to tip the balance between judicial and legislative authority and overturn an interpretation of an Act of Congress that has been settled for many years.\xe2\x80\x9d Rodriguez de Quijas, 490 U. S., at 487 (footnote and citation omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As was true in Rodriguez de Quijas, there are competing arguments in this case regarding the interaction of the relevant statutory provisions. But the Court in Gardner-Denver considered these arguments, including \xe2\x80\x9cthe federal policy favoring arbitration of labor disputes,\xe2\x80\x9d 415 U. S., at 59, and held that Congress did not intend to permit the result petitioners seek. In the absence of an intervening amendment to the relevant statutory language, we are bound by that decision. It is for Congress, rather than this Court, to reassess the policy arguments favoring arbitration and revise the relevant provisions to reflect its views.

\n

\xc2\xa0Referring to the potential conflict between individual and collective interests, the Court asserts that it \xe2\x80\x9ccannot rely on this judicial policy concern as a source of authority for introducing a qualification into the ADEA that is not found in its text.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21. That potential conflict of interests, however, was a basis for our decision in several pertinent cases, including Alexander v. Gardner-Denver Co., 415 U. S. 36 (1974), and Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp., 500 U. S. 20, 35 (1991), and in the intervening years Congress has not seen fit to correct that interpretation. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s derision of that \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy concern\xe2\x80\x9d is particularly disingenuous given its subversion of Gardner-Denver\xe2\x80\x99s holding in the service of an extratextual policy favoring arbitration.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The issue here is whether employees subject to a collective-bargaining agreement (CBA) providing for conclusive arbitration of all grievances, including claimed breaches of the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967 (ADEA), 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7621 et seq., lose their statutory right to bring an ADEA claim in court, \xc2\xa7626(c). Under the 35- year-old holding in Alexander v. Gardner-Denver Co., 415 U. S. 36 (1974), they do not, and I would adhere to stare decisis and so hold today.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Like Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e et seq., the ADEA is aimed at \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the elimination of discrimination in the workplace,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d McKennon v. Nashville Banner Publishing Co., 513 U. S. 352, 358 (1995) (quoting Oscar Mayer & Co. v. Evans, 441 U. S. 750, 756 (1979)), and, again like Title VII, the ADEA \xe2\x80\x9ccontains a vital element \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0: It grants an injured employee a right of action to obtain the authorized relief,\xe2\x80\x9d 513 U. S., at 358. \xe2\x80\x9cAny person aggrieved\xe2\x80\x9d under the Act \xe2\x80\x9cmay bring a civil action in any court of competent jurisdiction for legal or equitable relief,\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7626(c), thereby \xe2\x80\x9cnot only redress[ing] his own injury but also vindicat[ing] the important congressional policy against discriminatory employment practices,\xe2\x80\x9d Gardner-Denver, supra, at 45.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Gardner-Denver considered the effect of a CBA\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration clause on an employee\xe2\x80\x99s right to sue under Title VII. One of the employer\xe2\x80\x99s arguments was that the CBA entered into by the union had waived individual employees\xe2\x80\x99 statutory cause of action subject to a judicial remedy for discrimination in violation of Title VII. Although Title VII, like the ADEA, \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not speak expressly to the relationship between federal courts and the grievance-arbitration machinery of collective-bargaining agreements,\xe2\x80\x9d 415 U. S., at 47, we unanimously held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe rights conferred\xe2\x80\x9d by Title VII (with no exception for the right to a judicial forum) cannot be waived as \xe2\x80\x9cpart of the collective bargaining process,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 51. We stressed the contrast between two categories of rights in labor and employment law. There were \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory rights related to collective activity,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9care conferred on employees collectively to foster the processes of bargaining[, which] properly may be exercised or relinquished by the union as collective-bargaining agent to obtain economic benefits for union members.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But \xe2\x80\x9cTitle VII \xe2\x80\xa6 stands on plainly different [categorical] ground; it concerns not majoritarian processes, but an individual\xe2\x80\x99s right to equal employment opportunities.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Thus, as the Court previously realized, Gardner-Denver imposed a \xe2\x80\x9cseemingly absolute prohibition of union waiver of employees\xe2\x80\x99 federal forum rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Wright v. Universal Maritime Service Corp., 525 U. S. 70, 80 (1998).[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We supported the judgment with several other lines of complementary reasoning. First, we explained that antidiscrimination statutes \xe2\x80\x9chave long evinced a general intent to accord parallel or overlapping remedies against discrimination,\xe2\x80\x9d and Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s statutory scheme carried \xe2\x80\x9cno suggestion \xe2\x80\xa6 that a prior arbitral decision either forecloses an individual\xe2\x80\x99s right to sue or divests federal courts of jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Gardner-Denver, 415 U. S., at 47. We accordingly concluded that \xe2\x80\x9can individual does not forfeit his private cause of action if he first pursues his grievance to final arbitration under the nondiscrimination clause of a collective-bargaining agreement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 49.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, we rejected the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s view that simply participating in the arbitration amounted to electing the arbitration remedy and waiving the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s right to sue. We said that the arbitration agreement at issue covered only a contractual right under the CBA to be free from discrimination, not the \xe2\x80\x9cindependent statutory rights accorded by Congress\xe2\x80\x9d in Title VII. Id., at 49\xe2\x80\x9350. Third, we rebuffed the employer\xe2\x80\x99s argument that federal courts should defer to arbitral rulings. We declined to make the \xe2\x80\x9cassumption that arbitral processes are commensurate with judicial processes,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 56, and described arbitration as \xe2\x80\x9ca less appropriate forum for final resolution of Title VII issues than the federal courts,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 58.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, we took note that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n arbitration, as in the collective bargaining process, the interests of the individual employee may be subordinated to the collective interests of all employees in the bargaining unit,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., n.\xc2\xa019, a result we deemed unacceptable when it came to Title VII claims. In sum, Gardner-Denver held that an individual\xe2\x80\x99s statutory right of freedom from discrimination and access to court for enforcement were beyond a union\xe2\x80\x99s power to waive.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our analysis of Title VII in Gardner-Denver is just as pertinent to the ADEA in this case. The \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretation of Title VII \xe2\x80\xa6 applies with equal force in the context of age discrimination, for the substantive provisions of the ADEA \xe2\x80\x98were derived in haec verba from Title VII,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and indeed neither petitioners nor the Court points to any relevant distinction between the two statutes. Trans World Airlines, Inc. v. Thurston, 469 U. S. 111, 121 (1985) (quoting Lorillard v. Pons, 434 U. S. 575, 584 (1978)); see also McKennon, 513 U. S., at 358 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe ADEA and Title VII share common substantive features and also a common purpose\xe2\x80\x9d). Given the unquestionable applicability of the Gardner-Denver rule to this ADEA issue, the argument that its precedent be followed in this case of statutory interpretation is equally unquestionable. \xe2\x80\x9cPrinciples of stare decisis \xe2\x80\xa6 demand respect for precedent whether judicial methods of interpretation change or stay the same. Were that not so, those principles would fail to achieve the legal stability that they seek and upon which the rule of law depends.\xe2\x80\x9d CBOCS West, Inc. v. Humphries, 553 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 14). And \xe2\x80\x9c[c]onsiderations of stare decisis have special force\xe2\x80\x9d over an issue of statutory interpretation, which is unlike constitutional interpretation owing to the capacity of Congress to alter any reading we adopt simply by amending the statute. Patterson v. McLean Credit Union, 491 U. S. 164, 172\xe2\x80\x93173 (1989). Once we have construed a statute, stability is the rule, and \xe2\x80\x9cwe will not depart from [it] without some compelling justification.\xe2\x80\x9d Hilton v. South Carolina Public Railways Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 502 U. S. 197, 202 (1991). There is no argument for abandoning precedent here, and Gardner-Denver controls.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority evades the precedent of Gardner-Denver as long as it can simply by ignoring it. The Court never mentions the case before concluding that the ADEA and the National Labor Relations Act, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7151 et seq., \xe2\x80\x9cyiel[d] a straightforward answer to the question presented,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10, that is, that unions can bargain away individual rights to a federal forum for antidiscrimination claims. If this were a case of first impression, it would at least be possible to consider that conclusion, but the issue is settled and the time is too late by 35 years to make the bald assertion that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in the law suggests a distinction between the status of arbitration agreements signed by an individual employee and those agreed to by a union representative.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. In fact, we recently and unanimously said that the principle that \xe2\x80\x9cfederal forum rights cannot be waived in union-negotiated CBAs even if they can be waived in individually executed contracts \xe2\x80\xa6 assuredly finds support in\xe2\x80\x9d our case law, Wright, 525 U. S., at 77, and every Court of Appeals save one has read our decisions as holding to this position, Air Line Pilots Assn., Int\xe2\x80\x99l v. Northwest Airlines, Inc., 199 F.\xc2\xa03d 477, 484 (CADC 1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe see a clear rule of law emerging from Gardner-Denver and Gilmer [v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp., 500 U. S. 20 (1991)]: \xe2\x80\xa6 an individual may prospectively waive his own statutory right to a judicial forum, but his union may not prospectively waive that right for him. All of the circuits to have considered the meaning of Gardner-Denver after Gilmer, other than the Fourth, are in accord with this view\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Equally at odds with existing law is the majority\xe2\x80\x99s statement that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he decision to fashion a CBA to require arbitration of employment-discrimination claims is no different from the many other decisions made by parties in designing grievance machinery.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. That is simply impossible to square with our conclusion in Gardner-Denver that \xe2\x80\x9cTitle VII \xe2\x80\xa6 stands on plainly different ground\xe2\x80\x9d from \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory rights related to collective activity\xe2\x80\x9d: \xe2\x80\x9cit concerns not majoritarian processes, but an individual\xe2\x80\x99s right to equal employment opportunities.\xe2\x80\x9d 415 U. S., at 51; see also Atchison, T. & S. F. R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Buell, 480 U. S. 557, 565 (1987) (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]otwithstanding the strong policies encouraging arbitration, \xe2\x80\x98different considerations apply where the employee\xe2\x80\x99s claim is based on rights arising out of a statute designed to provide minimum substantive guarantees to individual workers\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Barrentine v. Arkansas-Best Freight System, Inc., 450 U. S. 728, 737 (1981))).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When the majority does speak to Gardner-Denver, it misreads the case in claiming that it turned solely \xe2\x80\x9con the narrow ground that the arbitration was not preclusive because the collective-bargaining agreement did not cover statutory claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. That, however, was merely one of several reasons given in support of the decision, see Gardner-Denver, 415 U. S., at 47\xe2\x80\x9359, and we raised it to explain why the District Court made a mistake in thinking that the employee lost his Title VII rights by electing to pursue the contractual arbitration remedy, see id., at 49\xe2\x80\x9350. One need only read Gardner-Denver itself to know that it was not at all so narrowly reasoned, and we have noted already how later cases have made this abundantly clear. Barrentine v. Arkansas-Best Freight System, Inc., 450 U. S., at 737, provides further testimony:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cNot all disputes between an employee and his employer are suited for binding resolution in accordance with the procedures established by collective bargaining. While courts should defer to an arbitral decision where the employee\xe2\x80\x99s claim is based on rights arising out of a collective-bargaining agreement, different considerations apply where the employee\xe2\x80\x99s claim is based on rights arising out of a statute designed to provide minimum substantive guarantees to individual workers.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThese considerations were the basis for our decision in [Gardner-Denver].\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nSee also Gilmer, supra, at 35 (\xe2\x80\x9cAn important concern\xe2\x80\x9d in Gardner-Denver \xe2\x80\x9cwas the tension between collective representation and individual statutory rights \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, if the Court can read Gardner-Denver as resting on nothing more than a contractual failure to reach as far as statutory claims, it must think the Court has been wreaking havoc on the truth for years, since (as noted) we have unanimously described the case as raising a \xe2\x80\x9cseemingly absolute prohibition of union waiver of employees\xe2\x80\x99 federal forum rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Wright, supra, at 80.[Footnote 2] Human ingenuity is not equal to the task of reconciling statements like this with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s representation that Gardner-Denver held only that \xe2\x80\x9cthe arbitration was not preclusive because the collective-bargaining agreement did not cover statutory claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor, finally, does the majority have any better chance of being rid of another of Gardner-Denver\xe2\x80\x99s statements supporting its rule of decision, set out and repeated in previous quotations: \xe2\x80\x9cin arbitration, as in the collective-bargaining process, a union may subordinate the interests of an individual employee to the collective interests of all employees in the bargaining unit,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 20 (citing 415 U. S., at 58, n.\xc2\xa019), an unacceptable result when it comes to \xe2\x80\x9can individual\xe2\x80\x99s right to equal employment opportunities,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 51. The majority tries to diminish this reasoning, and the previously stated holding it supported, by making the remarkable rejoinder that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e cannot rely on this judicial policy concern as a source of authority for introducing a qualification into the ADEA that is not found in its text.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 20.[Footnote 4] It is enough to recall that respondents are not seeking to \xe2\x80\x9cintroduc[e] a qualification into\xe2\x80\x9d the law; they are justifiably relying on statutory-interpretation precedent decades old, never overruled, and serially reaffirmed over the years. See, e.g., McDonald v. West Branch, 466 U. S. 284, 291 (1984); Barrentine, supra, at 742. With that precedent on the books, it makes no sense for the majority to claim that \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial policy concern[s]\xe2\x80\x9d about unions sacrificing individual antidiscrimination rights should be left to Congress.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For that matter, Congress has unsurprisingly understood Gardner-Denver the way we have repeatedly explained it and has operated on the assumption that a CBA cannot waive employees\xe2\x80\x99 rights to a judicial forum to enforce antidiscrimination statutes. See, e.g., H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 102\xe2\x80\x9340, pt. 1, p.\xc2\xa097 (1991) (stating that, \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent with the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of Title VII in [Gardner-Denver],\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cany agreement to submit disputed issues to arbitration \xe2\x80\xa6 in the context of a collective bargaining agreement \xe2\x80\xa6 does not preclude the affected person from seeking relief under the enforcement provisions of Title VII\xe2\x80\x9d). And Congress apparently does not share the Court\xe2\x80\x99s demotion of Gardner-Denver\xe2\x80\x99s holding to a suspect judicial policy concern: \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has had [over] 30 years in which it could have corrected our decision \xe2\x80\xa6 if it disagreed with it, and has chosen not to do so. We should accord weight to this continued acceptance of our earlier holding.\xe2\x80\x9d Hilton, 502 U. S., at 202; see also Patterson, 491 U. S., at 172\xe2\x80\x93173.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On one level, the majority opinion may have little effect, for it explicitly reserves the question whether a CBA\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of a judicial forum is enforceable when the union controls access to and presentation of employees\xe2\x80\x99 claims in arbitration, ante, at 24\xe2\x80\x9325, which \xe2\x80\x9cis usually the case,\xe2\x80\x9d McDonald, supra, at 291. But as a treatment of precedent in statutory interpretation, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion cannot be reconciled with the Gardner-Denver Court\xe2\x80\x99s own view of its holding, repeated over the years and generally understood, and I respectfully dissent.
