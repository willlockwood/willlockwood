SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

CHARLOTTE JENKINS, WARDEN v. PERCY HUTTON

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x931116.\xe2\x80\x83Decided June 19, 2017

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

Respondent Percy Hutton accused two friends, Derek Mitchell and Samuel Simmons Jr., of stealing a sewing machine, in which he had hidden $750. Mitchell and Simmons denied the accusation, but Hutton remained suspicious. On the night of September 16, 1985, he lured the pair into his car and, after pointing a gun at each, drove them around town in search of the machine. By night\xe2\x80\x99s end, Hutton had recovered his sewing machine, Simmons was in the hospital with two gunshot wounds to the head, and Mitchell was nowhere to be found. Simmons survived, but Mitchell was found dead a few weeks later, also having been shot twice.

\n

More than 30 years ago, an Ohio jury convicted Hutton of aggravated murder, attempted murder, and kidnaping. In connection with the aggravated murder conviction, the jury made two additional findings: that Hutton engaged in \xe2\x80\x9ca course of conduct involving the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. attempt to kill two or more persons,\xe2\x80\x9d and that Hutton murdered Mitchell while \xe2\x80\x9ccommitting, attempting to commit, or fleeing immediately after .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. kidnapping,\xe2\x80\x9d Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72929.04(A)(5), (7) (Lexis 1982). Because of these \xe2\x80\x9caggravating circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d Ohio law required that Hutton be sentenced to \xe2\x80\x9cdeath, life imprisonment without parole, [or] life imprisonment with parole eligibility after\xe2\x80\x9d no fewer than 20 years in prison. \xc2\xa72929.03(C)(2).

\n

Several days after rendering its verdict, the jury reconvened for the penalty phase of the trial. The State argued for the death penalty. In opposition, Hutton gave an unsworn statement professing his innocence and presented evidence about his background and psychological pro-file. When the presentations concluded, the trial court instructed the jury that it could recommend a death sentence only if it unanimously found that the State had \xe2\x80\x9cprove[d] beyond a reasonable doubt that the aggravating circumstances, of which the Defendant was found guilty, outweigh[ed] the [mitigating factors].\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Hutton, 100 Ohio St. 3d 176, 185, 2003-Ohio-5607, 797 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 948, 958; see Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa72929.03(D)(2). The jury deliberated and recommended death. The trial court accepted the recommendation after also finding, \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond a reasonable doubt, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the aggravating circumstances .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. outweigh[ed] the mitigating factors.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72929.03(D)(3).

\n

The Ohio Court of Appeals and the Ohio Supreme Court affirmed Hutton\xe2\x80\x99s death sentence. In doing so, both concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe evidence support[ed] the finding of the aggravating circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72929.05(A); see Hutton, 100 Ohio St. 3d, at 187, 797 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 961; State v. Hutton, 72 Ohio App. 3d 348, 350, 594 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 692, 694 (1995). The courts also \xe2\x80\x9cindependently weigh[ed] all of the facts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to determine whether the aggravating circumstances [Hutton] was found guilty of committing outweigh[ed] the mitigating factors.\xe2\x80\x9d Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa72929.05(A). Both agreed with the jury and the trial court that \xe2\x80\x9caggravating circumstances outweigh[ed] the mitigating factors,\xe2\x80\x9d and that a death sentence was warranted. Hutton, 100 Ohio St. 3d, at 191, 797 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 963\xe2\x80\x93964; see Hutton, 72 Ohio App. 3d, at 352, 594 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 695.

\n

The case before this Court concerns Hutton\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent petition for federal habeas relief. In 2005, Hutton filed such a petition pursuant to\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254, arguing that the trial court violated his due process rights during the penalty phase of his trial. According to Hutton, the court gave the jurors insufficient guidance because it failed to tell them that, when weighing aggravating and mitigating factors, they could consider only the two aggravating factors they had found during the guilt phase. Hutton, however, had not objected to the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s instruction or raised this argument on direct appeal, and the District Court on federal habeas concluded that his due process claim was procedurally defaulted. Hutton v. Mitchell, 2013 WL 2476333, *64 (ND Ohio, June 7, 2013); see State v. Hutton, 53 Ohio St. 3d 36, 39, n.\xc2\xa01, 559 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 432, 437\xe2\x80\x93438, n. 1 (1990) (declining to address trial court\xe2\x80\x99s instructions because Hutton \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically declined to object .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. at trial, and ha[d] not raised or briefed the issue\xe2\x80\x9d on appeal).

\n

The United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit reversed. The court concluded that, notwithstanding the procedural default, it could \xe2\x80\x9creach the merits\xe2\x80\x9d of Hutton\xe2\x80\x99s claim to \xe2\x80\x9cavoid a fundamental miscarriage of justice.\xe2\x80\x9d Hutton v. Mitchell, 839 F. 3d 486, 498 (2016) (internal quotation marks omitted). The Sixth Circuit began its analysis with Sawyer v. Whitley,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333 (1992)\n. In that decision, this Court established that a habeas petitioner may obtain review of a defaulted claim upon \xe2\x80\x9cshow[ing] by clear and convincing evidence that, but for a constitutional error, no reasonable jury would have found [him] eligible for the death penalty under the applicable state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 336.

\n

Hutton had not argued that this exception to default applied to his case. Nonetheless, the Sixth Circuit held that the exception justified reviewing his claim. The court gave two reasons: First, Hutton was not eligible to receive a death sentence because \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury had not made the necessary finding of the existence of aggravating circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d 839 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 498\xe2\x80\x93499. And second, since the trial court \xe2\x80\x9cgave the jury no guidance as to what to con-sider as aggravating circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d when weighing aggravating and mitigating factors, the record did not show that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s death recommendation \xe2\x80\x9cwas actually based on a review of any valid aggravating circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 500. On the merits, the court concluded that the trial court violated Hutton\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights by giving an erroneous jury instruction. Judge Rogers dissented on the ground that Hutton could not overcome the procedural default.

\n

The Sixth Circuit was wrong to reach the merits of Hutton\xe2\x80\x99s claim. The court\xe2\x80\x99s first reason for excusing default was that \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury had not [found] the existence of aggravating circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 498\xe2\x80\x93499. But it had, at the guilt phase of Hutton\xe2\x80\x99s trial. As Judge Rogers pointed out, \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury found two such factors\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94engaging in a course of conduct designed to kill multiple people and committing kidnaping\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cin the process of convicting Hutton .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of aggravated murder.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 511. Each of those findings \xe2\x80\x9crendered Hutton eligible for the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Hutton has not argued that the trial court improperly instructed the jury about aggravating circumstances at the guilt phase. Nor did the Sixth Circuit identify any such error. Instead, the instruction that Hutton contends is incorrect, and that the Sixth Circuit analyzed, was given at the penalty phase of trial. That penalty phase instruction plainly had no effect on the jury\xe2\x80\x99s decision\xe2\x80\x94delivered after the guilt phase and pursuant to an unchallenged instruction\xe2\x80\x94that aggravating circumstances were present when Hutton murdered Mitchell.

\n

The Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s second reason for reaching the merits rests on a legal error. Under Sawyer, a court may review a procedurally defaulted claim if, \xe2\x80\x9cbut for a constitutional error, no reasonable jury would have found the petitioner eligible for the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d 505 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 336 (emphasis added). Here, the alleged error was the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to specify that, when weighing aggravating and mitigating factors, the jury could consider only the aggravating circumstances it found at the guilt phase. Assuming such an error can provide a basis for excusing default, the Sixth Circuit should have considered the following: Whether, given proper instructions about the two aggravating circumstances, a reasonable jury could have decided that those aggravating circumstances outweighed the mitigating circumstances.

\n

But the court did not ask that question. Instead, it considered whether, given the (alleged) improper instructions, the jury might have been relying on invalid aggravating circumstances when it recommended a death sentence. See 839 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 500 (explaining that, because the trial court gave \xe2\x80\x9cno guidance as to what to consider as aggravating circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d the court could not determine whether the death recommendation \xe2\x80\x9cwas actually based on a review of any valid aggravating circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d). The court, in other words, considered whether the alleged error might have affected the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict, not whether a properly instructed jury could have recommended death. That approach, which would justify excusing default whenever an instructional error could have been relevant to a jury\xe2\x80\x99s decision, is incompatible with Sawyer.

\n

Neither Hutton nor the Sixth Circuit has \xe2\x80\x9cshow[n] by clear and convincing evidence that\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94if properly in-structed\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cno reasonable juror would have\xe2\x80\x9d concluded that the aggravating circumstances in Hutton\xe2\x80\x99s case outweigh the mitigating circumstances. Sawyer, 505 U. S., at 336. In fact, the trial court, Ohio Court of Appeals, and Ohio Supreme Court each independently weighed those factors and concluded that the death penalty was justified. On the facts of this case, the Sixth Circuit was wrong to hold that it could review Hutton\xe2\x80\x99s claim under the miscarriage of justice exception to procedural default.

\n

The petition for certiorari and motion for leave to proceed in forma pauperis are granted, the judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
