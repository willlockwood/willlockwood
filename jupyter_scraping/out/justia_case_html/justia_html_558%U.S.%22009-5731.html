\nWELLONS V. HALL

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

MARCUS A. WELLONS v. HILTON HALL, WARDEN

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united\nstates court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x935731.\xe2\x80\x83Decided January 19, 2010

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From beginning to end, judicial proceedings conducted for the purpose of deciding whether a defendant shall be put to death must be conducted with dignity and respect. The disturbing facts of this case raise serious questions concerning the conduct of the trial, and this petition raises a serious question about whether the Court of Appeals carefully reviewed those facts before addressing petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional claims. We know that the Court of Appeals committed the same procedural error that we corrected in Cone v. Bell, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318). We do not know how the court would have ruled if it had the benefit of our decision in that case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Marcus Wellons was convicted in Georgia state court of rape and murder and sentenced to death. Although the trial looked typical, there were unusual events going on behind the scenes. Only after the trial did defense counsel learn that there had been unreported ex parte contacts between the jury and the judge, that jurors and a bailiff had planned a reunion, and that \xe2\x80\x9ceither during or immediately following the penalty phase, some jury members gave the trial judge chocolate shaped as male genitalia and the bailiff chocolate shaped as female breasts,\xe2\x80\x9d 554 F.\xc2\xa03d 923, 930 (CA11 2009). The judge had not reported any of this to the defense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither Wellons nor any court has ascertained exactly what went on at this capital trial or what prompted such \xe2\x80\x9cgifts.\xe2\x80\x9d Wellons has repeatedly tried, in both state and federal court, to find out what occurred, but he has found himself caught in a procedural morass: He raised the issue on direct appeal but was constrained by the nonexistent record, and the State Supreme Court affirmed his conviction and sentence. Wellons v. State, 266 Ga. 77, 88, 463 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 868, 880 (1995). He sought state habeas relief and moved to develop evidence. But the court held that the matter had been decided on appeal and thus was res judicata. See 554 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 932. He raised the issue again in his federal habeas petition, seeking discovery and an evidentiary hearing. But the District Court \xe2\x80\x9cconcluded that Wellons\xe2\x80\x99 claims \xe2\x80\xa6 were procedurally barred, and accordingly denied his motion for an evidentiary hearing on these claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 933.[Footnote 1] Before the Eleventh Circuit, Wellons \xe2\x80\x9cargue[d] that the district court erred in denying his motions for discovery and an evidentiary hearing to develop his judge, juror, and bailiff misconduct claims because they are not procedurally barred.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 935. The court disagreed, holding that Wellons\xe2\x80\x99 claims were procedurally barred. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As our dissenting colleagues acknowledge, post, at 1 (opinion of Scalia, J.); post, at 1 (opinion of Alito, J.), the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s holding was an error under Cone, 556 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318). \xe2\x80\x9cWhen a state court declines to review the merits of a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim on the ground that it has done so already, it creates no bar to federal habeas review.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at __ (slip op., at 17). Both dissenting opinions assume that \xe2\x80\x9cthe issue on which Cone throws light does not affect the outcome\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cthe Eleventh Circuit \xe2\x80\xa6 also decided that petitioner was not entitled to habeas relief on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (opinion of Scalia, J.). Having found a procedural bar, however, the Eleventh Circuit had no need to address whether petitioner was otherwise entitled to an evidentiary hearing and gave this question, at most, perfunctory consideration that may well have turned on the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding of a procedural bar.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although Wellons appealed the denial of \xe2\x80\x9chis motions for discovery and an evidentiary hearing,\xe2\x80\x9d 554 F. 3d, at 935, the Eleventh Circuit did not purport to address the merits of that issue at all.[Footnote 2] The court stated only that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ven if we assume that Wellons\xe2\x80\x99s misconduct claims are not procedurally barred, they do not entitle Wellons to habeas relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 936 (emphasis added). This opaque statement appears to address only whether petitioner was entitled to ultimate relief in the form of a new trial, not whether petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s allegations, combined with the facts he had learned, entitled him to the discovery and evidentiary hearing that he sought.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning does not suggest otherwise. The court observed that Wellons\xe2\x80\x99 claims of misconduct were \xe2\x80\x9cgrounded in his speculation as to the meaning underlying the jurors\xe2\x80\x99 chocolate \xe2\x80\x98gifts\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe surmise attached to their passive receipt of these gifts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This statement likewise indicates only that on the existing record, habeas relief was inappropriate, not that an evidentiary hearing should be denied. After all, had there been discovery or an evidentiary hearing, Wellons may have been able to present more than \xe2\x80\x9cspeculation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csurmise.\xe2\x80\x9d The Eleventh Circuit also pointed to the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision on direct appeal, see id., at 937, and reviewed that decision \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n light of the evidence presented before the Georgia Supreme Court,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. This, too, is typical of a court reviewing the denial of habeas relief, not the denial of discovery or an evidentiary hearing. [Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, even assuming that the Eleventh Circuit intended to address Wellons\xe2\x80\x99 motions for discovery and an evidentiary hearing, we cannot be sure that its reasoning really was independent of the Cone error. The fact that his claims rested on \xe2\x80\x9cspeculation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csurmise\xe2\x80\x9d was due to the absence of a record, which was in part based on the Cone error. And as the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning turned on \xe2\x80\x9cthe evidence presented before the Georgia Supreme Court,\xe2\x80\x9d 554 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 937, there is serious doubt about whether it necessarily relied on the very holes in the record that Wellons was trying to fill.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our dissenting colleagues allege that the Court is \xe2\x80\x9cdegrad[ing] \xe2\x80\xa6 our traditional requirements for a GVR.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 2 (opinion of Scalia, J.); see post, at 4 (opinion of Alito, J.). But the standard for an order granting certiorari, vacating the judgment below, and remanding the case (GVR) remains as it always has been: A GVR is appropriate when \xe2\x80\x9cintervening developments \xe2\x80\xa6 reveal a reasonable probability that the decision below rests upon a premise that the lower court would reject if given the opportunity for further consideration, and where it appears that such a redetermination may determine the ultimate outcome\xe2\x80\x9d of the matter. Lawrence v. Chater, 516 U. S. 163, 167 (1996) (per curiam). As already discussed, there is, at least, a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable probability,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., that the denial of discovery and an evidentiary hearing rested in part on the Cone error. And in light of the unusual facts of the case, a \xe2\x80\x9credetermination may determine the ultimate outcome,\xe2\x80\x9d 516 U. S., at 167; cf. Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 420, 442 (2000) (holding that several \xe2\x80\x9comissions as a whole disclose the need for an evidentiary hearing\xe2\x80\x9d); Smith v. Phillips, 455 U. S. 209, 215 (1982) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis Court has long held that the remedy for allegations of juror partiality is a hearing in which the defendant has an opportunity to prove actual bias\xe2\x80\x9d). The Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is ambiguous in significant respects. It would be highly inappropriate to assume away that ambiguity in respondent\xe2\x80\x99s favor. That is especially so in a case in which petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s allegations and the unusual facts raise a serious question about the fairness of a capital trial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both dissenting opinions suggest that if there is a strong case for discovery and an evidentiary hearing, then the Court \xe2\x80\x9cshould summarily reverse or set the case for argument.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 2 (opinion of Scalia, J.); see also post, at 4\xe2\x80\x935 (opinion of Alito, J.). But as we have explained, \xe2\x80\x9ca GVR order conserves the scarce resources of this Court,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cassists the court below by flagging a particular issue that it does not appear to have fully considered,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cassists this Court by procuring the benefit of the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s insight before we rule on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Lawrence, supra, at 167.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike Justice Scalia, post, at 3, we do not believe that a \xe2\x80\x9cself-respecting\xe2\x80\x9d court of appeals would or should respond to our remand order with a \xe2\x80\x9csummary reissuance\xe2\x80\x9d of essentially the same opinion, absent the procedural default discussion. To the contrary, in light of our decision in Cone, we assume the court will consider, on the merits, whether petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s allegations, together with the undisputed facts, warrant discovery and an evidentiary hearing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The petition for writ of certiorari to the United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit and the motion of petitioner for leave to proceed in forma pauperis are granted. The judgment is vacated, and the case is remanded to the Eleventh Circuit for further consideration in light of Cone v. Bell, 556 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Contrary to our dissenting colleagues, post, at 4 (opinion of Alito, J.), we do not find it dispositive that the section of the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion about judge, juror, and bailiff misconduct began with a full page statement of the standard of review, which in turn included a sentence about the circumstances under which an evidentiary hearing is warranted. See 554 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 934\xe2\x80\x93935. Immediately following the standard of review that Justice Alito quotes, the panel explained that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98if the record \xe2\x80\xa6 precludes habeas relief, a district court is not required to hold an evidentiary hearing,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe record reveals that [Wellons\xe2\x80\x99] claims \xe2\x80\xa6 are procedurally barred.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, the allegedly \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocal\xe2\x80\x9d holding that Justice Alito quotes was preceded by a discussion of the deference owed under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) to the \xe2\x80\x9cGeorgia Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment as to the substance and effect of the ex parte communication.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 937. This is the classic formulation of a decision of whether to grant habeas relief. Indeed, it would be bizarre if a federal court had to defer to state-court factual findings, made without any evidentiary record, in order to decide whether it could create an evidentiary record to decide whether the factual findings were erroneous. If that were the case, then almost no habeas petitioner could ever get an evidentiary hearing: So long as the state court found a fact that the petitioner was trying to disprove through the presentation of evidence, then there could be no hearing. AEDPA does not require such a crabbed and illogical approach to habeas procedures, and there is no reason to believe that the Eleventh Circuit thought otherwise.

MARCUS A. WELLONS v. HILTON HALL, WARDEN

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united\nstates court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x935731.\xe2\x80\x83Decided January 19, 2010

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, with whom Justice Thomas joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Marcus Wellons was convicted in Georgia state court of capital murder and sentenced to death. After exhausting direct appeal and state postconviction review, he filed a petition for habeas corpus in federal court under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. Wellons claims, among other things, that misconduct on the part of the trial judge, jurors, and court bailiff deprived him of a fair trial. The District Court denied relief, and the Eleventh Circuit affirmed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today the Court grants Wellons\xe2\x80\x99 petition for certiorari, vacates the judgment of the Eleventh Circuit, and remands (\xe2\x80\x9cGVRs\xe2\x80\x9d) in light of Cone v. Bell, 556 U. S. ___ (2009). The Eleventh Circuit concluded that Wellons\xe2\x80\x99 claims were procedurally barred because the state postconviction court, noting that the State Supreme Court had rejected them on direct appeal, held the claims were res judicata. See 554 F.\xc2\xa03d 923, 936, and n. 6 (2009). This was error under Cone, see 556 U. S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318), as respondent recognizes; indeed, the Eleventh Circuit has already recognized the abrogation of the opinion below on this point, see Owen v. Secretary for Dept. of Corrections, 568 F.\xc2\xa03d 894, 915, n. 23, (2009). But, as Justice Alito\xe2\x80\x99s dissent demonstrates, post, p. ___, the Eleventh Circuit (like the District Court) also decided that petitioner was not entitled to habeas relief on the merits. 554 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 936\xe2\x80\x93938. Thus the Court GVRs in light of Cone even though the issue on which Cone throws light does not affect the outcome.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court has previously asserted a power to GVR whenever there is \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable probability that the decision below rests upon a premise that the lower court would reject if given the opportunity for further consideration, and where it appears that such a redetermination may determine the ultimate outcome of the litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Lawrence v. Chater, 516 U. S. 163, 167 (1996) (per curiam). I have protested even that flabby standard, see id., at 190\xe2\x80\x93191 (Scalia, J., dissenting), but today the Court outdoes itself. It GVRs where the decision below does not \xe2\x80\x9crest upon\xe2\x80\x9d the objectionable faulty premise, but is independently supported by other grounds\xe2\x80\x94so that redetermination of the faulty ground will assuredly not \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine the ultimate outcome of the litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d The power to \xe2\x80\x9crevise and correct for error,\xe2\x80\x9d which the Court has already turned into \xe2\x80\x9ca power to void for suspicion,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 190 (same) (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted), has now become the power to send back for a re-do. We have no authority to decree that. If the Court thinks that the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s merits holding is wrong, then it should summarily reverse or set the case for argument; otherwise, the judgment below must stand. The same is true if (as the Court evidently believes) the Court of Appeals should have required an evidentiary hearing before resolving the merits question. If they erred in that regard their judgment should be reversed rather than remanded \xe2\x80\x9cin light of Cone v. Bell\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a disposition providing no hint that what we really want them to do (as the Court believes) is to consider an evidentiary hearing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The systematic degradation of our traditional requirements for a GVR has spawned a series of unusual dispositions, including the GVR so the government can try a less extravagant argument on remand, see Department of Interior v. South Dakota, 519 U. S. 919, 921 (1996) (Scalia, J., dissenting), the GVR in light of nothing, see Youngblood v. West Virginia, 547 U. S. 867, 872 (2006) (same), and the newly-minted Summary Remand for More Extensive Opinion than Petitioner Requested (SRMEOPR), see Webster v. Cooper, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 3). Today the Court adds another beast to our growing menagerie: the SRIE, Summary Remand for Inconsequential Error\xe2\x80\x94or, as the Court would have it, the SRTAEH, Summary Remand to Think About an Evidentiary Hearing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It disrespects the judges of the Courts of Appeals, who are appointed and confirmed as we are, to vacate and send back their authorized judgments for inconsequential imperfection of opinion\xe2\x80\x94as though we were schoolmasters grading their homework. An appropriately self-respecting response to today\xe2\x80\x99s summary vacatur would be summary reissuance of the same opinion, minus the discussion of Cone. That would also serve the purpose of minimizing the delay of justice that today\xe2\x80\x99s GVR achieves (Wellons has already outlived his victim by 20 years; he committed his murder in 1989).

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito, with whom The Chief Justice joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s disposition of this case represents a misuse of our authority to grant, vacate, and remand (GVR). The decision of the Court of Appeals plainly rests on two independent grounds: first, that petitioner procedurally defaulted his claim that the judge, bailiff, and jurors had an inappropriate relationship that impaired his right to a fair trial and, second, that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim failed on the merits. See 554 F.\xc2\xa03d 923, 936 (CA11 2009). While it is true that the first of these grounds is inconsistent with Cone v. Bell, 556 U. S. ___ , ___ (2009) (slip op., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318), there is no basis for vacating the decision below unless some recent authority or development provides a basis for reconsideration of the second ground as well. But the per curiam identifies no such authority. Instead, the per curiam uses Cone as a vehicle for suggesting that the Court of Appeals should reconsider its decision on the merits of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In order to defend this disposition, the per curiam refuses to credit the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 explanation of the basis of its decision. The Court of Appeals twice stated in unequivocal terms that its holding on the merits of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim was independent of its holding on the question of procedural default. See 554 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 937\xe2\x80\x93938 (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven if these claims were properly before us on habeas review, we would not disturb the Georgia Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion on the merits of these claims\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 936 (\xe2\x80\x9cEven if we assume that Wellons\xe2\x80\x99s misconduct claims are not procedurally barred, they do not entitle Wellons to habeas relief\xe2\x80\x9d). But the per curiam states that the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 consideration of the merits \xe2\x80\x9cmay well have turned on the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding of a procedural bar\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cwe cannot be sure that [the panel\xe2\x80\x99s] reasoning really was independent of the Cone error.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3, 5.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even worse, the per curiam unjustifiably suggests that the Court of Appeals gave at most only \xe2\x80\x9cperfunctory consideration\xe2\x80\x9d to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim that he was entitled to an evidentiary hearing and may not have \xe2\x80\x9ccarefully reviewed\xe2\x80\x9d the relevant facts. Ante, at 3, 1. The majority may not be satisfied with the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 discussion, but the majority has no good reason for suggesting that the lower court did not give the issue careful consideration.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court refused petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s discovery request on the ground that petitioner did not make a sufficient showing to warrant interrogation of the jurors. As the detailed opinion of the District Court reveals, the state habeas judge allowed petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys to contact all of the jurors and relevant court personnel; the attorneys succeeded in contacting all but 1 of the jurors; 6 of the 11 jurors who were contacted, as well as the bailiffs and court reporter, were interviewed; and the attorneys made a proffer of the information provided by these interviewees.[Footnote 1] There is no suggestion that the attorneys were restricted in the questions that they were permitted to ask the interviewees, and it appears that the jurors who were interviewed spoke freely, even discussing their understanding of the judge\xe2\x80\x99s instructions on the law and the jury\xe2\x80\x99s deliberations.[Footnote 2] Cf. Fed. Rule Evid. 606(b). Interestingly, the proffer does not reflect that the attorneys asked any of the jurors what would appear to be the most critical question, namely, why the strange gifts were given to the judge or the bailiff.[Footnote 3] See App. C to Pet. for Cert. 34\xe2\x80\x9338. If any such questions had been asked and answers favorable to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s position had been provided, one would expect that information to appear in the proffer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After examining the proffer made by petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys, the District Court concluded that this submission did not justify formal discovery. With respect to what the per curiam describes as the \xe2\x80\x9cunreported ex parte contacts between the jury and the judge,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x94which apparently consisted of a brief exchange of words that occurred when the judge entered the room in a restaurant where the jurors were dining\xe2\x80\x94the District Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cnothing that Petitioner has presented provides even the slightest indication that anything more than a simple greeting occurred,\xe2\x80\x9d App. C to Pet. for Cert. 43.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With respect to the gifts that were given to the judge and a bailiff after the trial ended, the District Court stressed that they were \xe2\x80\x9cinappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d and represented \xe2\x80\x9can unusual display of poor taste in the context of a proceeding so grave as a capital trial,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., but the Court noted that petitioner had not proffered any evidence that any of the jurors or court personnel who were interviewed had said anything that substantiated the assertion that \xe2\x80\x9can inappropriate relationship existed between the judge, the bailiff, and the jury,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 44.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A fair reading of the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 opinion is that that court likewise held that petitioner was not entitled to the discovery he sought because that discovery was unlikely to yield evidence substantiating his claim. See 554 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 935 (quoting Schriro v. Landrigan, 550 U. S. 465, 474 (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen deciding whether to grant a federal habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s request for an evidentiary hearing, \xe2\x80\x98a federal court must consider whether such a hearing could enable an applicant to prove the petition\xe2\x80\x99s factual allegations, which, if true, would entitle the applicant to federal habeas relief\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that the strange and tasteless gifts that were given to the trial judge and bailiff are facially troubling, and I am certainly not prepared at this point to say that the decision below on the discovery issue was correct. But unlike the Court, I do not think it is proper for us to use a GVR to address this matter. The lower courts have decided the discovery issue, and now this Court has two options. First, if we wish to review the question whether petitioner made a sufficient showing to justify interrogation of the jurors, we should grant the petition for a writ of certiorari and decide that question. Second, if we do not wish to tackle that fact-bound question, we should deny review or GVR in light of a recent authority or development that casts doubt on the judgment of the court below. What the Court has done\xe2\x80\x94using a GVR as a vehicle for urging the Court of Appeals to reconsider its holding on a question that is entirely independent of the ground for the GVR\xe2\x80\x94is extraordinary and, in my view, improper.
