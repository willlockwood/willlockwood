\nPER CURIAM
\nROPER V. WEAVER
\n550 U. S. ____ (2007)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

DON ROPER, SUPERINTENDENT, POTOSI CORRECTIONAL CENTER, PETITIONER\nv. WILLIAM WEAVER

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

[May 21, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari in this case, 549 U. S. ___ (2006), to decide whether the Court of Appeals had exceeded its authority under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) by setting aside a capital sentence on the ground that the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s closing statement was \xe2\x80\x9cunfairly inflammatory.\xe2\x80\x9d Weaver v. Bowersox, 438 F.\xc2\xa03d 832, 841 (CA8 2006). Our primary concern was whether the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 application of the more stringent standard of review mandated by the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996, (AEDPA), 110 Stat. 1214, was consistent with our interpretation of that statute. Cf. Carey v. Musladin, 549 U. S. ___ (2006). We are now aware of circumstances that persuade us that dismissal of the writ is the appropriate manner in which to dispose of this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The argument made by the prosecutor in this case was essentially the same as the argument that he made in two other cases\xe2\x80\x94one of which involved respondent\xe2\x80\x99s codefendant. See Shurn v. Delo, 177 F.\xc2\xa03d 662, 666 (CA8 1999); Newlon v. Armontrout, 693 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 799 (WD Mo. 1988), aff\xe2\x80\x99d, 885 F.\xc2\xa02d 1328 (CA8 1989). In each of those cases, the defendant received a death sentence. Also in each case, the defendant filed a petition seeking federal habeas relief before AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s effective date. Federal habeas relief was granted in all three cases. The State does not question the propriety of relief in the other two cases because it was clear at the time, as it is now, that AEDPA did not apply to either of them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent argues, for the following reasons, that AEDPA should not govern his case either. Like the defendants in Newlon and Shurn, respondent filed his federal habeas petition before the effective date of AEDPA. Instead of considering respondent\xe2\x80\x99s claims, however, the District Court sU. S.onte stayed the habeas proceedings, noting that respondent had indicated his intention to file a petition for writ of certiorari seeking this Court\xe2\x80\x99s review of the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 denial of postconviction relief. Though the District Court recognized that respondent was not required to seek certiorari from this Court, it concluded that, if \xe2\x80\x9ca state prisoner chooses to pursue writ of certiorari, he must first exhaust that remedy before filing a federal habeas corpus petition.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Brief for National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers as Amicus Curiae 15. Thus, the District Court put respondent to a choice: He could forgo filing a petition for certiorari, or his habeas petition would be dismissed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent moved for reconsideration and for the appointment of counsel. The District Court denied both motions, reiterating its view that if respondent sought certiorari, his federal habeas petition would be premature. When respondent notified the District Court that a petition for certiorari had been filed, the court made good on its promise: It dismissed respondent\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition \xe2\x80\x9cwithout prejudice\xe2\x80\x9d to his refiling \xe2\x80\x9cfollowing exhaustion of his state proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13. Though respondent had filed his habeas petition before AEDPA took effect, the District Court dismissed his petition after the statute was in force.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Still without an attorney, respondent requested a certificate of appealability from the District Court. The court denied the request, opining that reasonable jurists could not disagree with the dismissal of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s petition. Id., at 5\xe2\x80\x936. Respondent also filed a notice of appeal, which the Court of Appeals construed as a request for a certificate of appealability and rejected.*

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent refiled his habeas petition after this Court denied review of his state postconviction proceedings. The Eighth Circuit eventually concluded that, because respondent\xe2\x80\x99s petition was filed after AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s effective date, his claims must be evaluated under that statute\xe2\x80\x99s strict standard of review. See Weaver v. Bowersox, 241 F.\xc2\xa03d 1024, 1029 (2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our recent decision in Lawrence v. Florida, 549 U. S. ___ (2007), conclusively establishes that the District Court was wrong to conclude that, if respondent chose to seek certiorari, he had to exhaust that remedy before filing a federal habeas petition. Lawrence clarified that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tate review ends when the state courts have finally resolved an application for state postconviction relief\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94even if a prisoner files a certiorari petition. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 4); see also id., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 4\xe2\x80\x935) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e have said that state prisoners need not petition for certiorari to exhaust state remedies\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Fay v. Noia, 372 U. S. 391, 435\xe2\x80\x93438 (1963))). Thus, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s habeas petition, which was fully exhausted when filed, did not become unexhausted upon his decision to seek certiorari. Because the petition was not premature, the District Court had no cause to dismiss it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whether this unusual procedural history leads to the conclusion, as respondent colorably contends, that the AEDPA standard is simply inapplicable to this case, is a question we find unnecessary to resolve. Regardless of the answer to that question, we find it appropriate to exercise our discretion to prevent these three virtually identically situated litigants from being treated in a needlessly disparate manner, simply because the District Court erroneously dismissed respondent\xe2\x80\x99s pre-AEDPA petition.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, the writ of certiorari is dismissed as improvidently granted.

\n

\nIt is so ordered.

\n

*\xc2\xa0Respondent did not seek rehearing or rehearing en banc in the Court of Appeals, nor did he file a petition for writ of certiorari from the denial of the certificate of appealability. Pursuit of either would almost certainly have been futile.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, with whom Justice Thomas and Justice Alito join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Eighth Circuit held in this case that the Missouri Supreme Court had unreasonably applied clearly established precedent of this Court in concluding that certain statements made by the prosecutor during the penalty phase of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s capital trial did not rise to the level of a due process violation. Weaver v. Bowersox, 438 F.\xc2\xa03d 832, 839\xe2\x80\x93842 (2006). As the Court says, ante, at 1, we granted certiorari to decide whether this holding comported with the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). We received briefing, and heard an hour\xe2\x80\x99s argument, on that question. Yet now the Court declines to answer it, dismissing the writ as improvidently granted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The reason is that the Court has become \xe2\x80\x9caware,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 1, that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s post-AEDPA habeas petition was filed only because the District Court had erroneously dismissed an earlier petition filed prior to AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s effective date, ante, at 3. Believing that respondent is \xe2\x80\x9cvirtually identically situated\xe2\x80\x9d to two other litigants whose federal habeas petitions were not governed by AEDPA, and seeking to avoid \xe2\x80\x9ctreat[ing the three] in a needlessly disparate manner \xe2\x80\xa6 simply because the District Court erroneously dismissed respondent\xe2\x80\x99s pre-AEDPA petition,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court has decided to let stand the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s flagrant misapplication of AEDPA, whether or not (and without deciding whether) AEDPA governs this case. Ante, at 4.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I fully agree with the Court that the District Court erred in dismissing respondent\xe2\x80\x99s pre-AEDPA petition, but that seems to me no justification for aborting this argued case. The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s previous error does not affect the legal conclusion that AEDPA applies to this new petition. And once it is admitted that AEDPA governs, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s error should in no way alter our prior determination that the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s application of AEDPA\ndeserves our scrutiny. I discuss these two points in\nsuccession.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court provides no legal argument to support its assertion that respondent has a \xe2\x80\x9ccolorabl[e]\xe2\x80\x9d claim, ante, at 4, that the prior erroneous dismissal renders AEDPA inapplicable to this case. Nor does respondent. See Brief for Respondent 39, n.\xc2\xa044. I am aware of no authority supporting the proposition that respondent is legally or equitably entitled to evade the collateral consequences of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s error.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To begin with, any resort to equity would founder on respondent\xe2\x80\x99s failure to exhaust his appeals of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous decision. See ante, at 3, n. The Court is untroubled by respondent\xe2\x80\x99s lack of diligence because, it says, further appellate review \xe2\x80\x9cwould almost certainly have been futile.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court does not explain the basis for this pessimistic assessment, but the reason seems to be its belief that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s error was not clear until our recent decision in Lawrence v. Florida, 549 U. S. ____ (2007). See ante, at 3 (describing Lawrence as \xe2\x80\x9cclarif[ying]\xe2\x80\x9d the exhaustion rule).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This seems to me quite wrong. The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s error was as apparent in 1996 as it was in 1966. In Fay v. Noia, 372 U. S. 391, 435\xe2\x80\x93438 (1963), we announced in no uncertain terms that a federal habeas petitioner need not seek certiorari in order to exhaust state-court remedies. \xe2\x80\x9c[N]o less an authority than Hart & Wechsler\xe2\x80\x99s The Federal Courts and the Federal System,\xe2\x80\x9d Massachusetts v. EPA, 549 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 17, n.\xc2\xa017), has long understood Noia to stand for that proposition. See P. Bator, D. Meltzer, P. Mishkin, & D. Shapiro, Hart & Wechsler\xe2\x80\x99s The Federal Courts and the Federal System 1555 (3d ed. 1988); id., at 1446 (4th ed. 1996); id., at 1391 (5th ed. 2003). Indeed, Lawrence\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cclarifi[cation]\xe2\x80\x9d consisted of nothing more than citing the same old pages in Noia. See Lawrence, supra, at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 4\xe2\x80\x935). It logically follows from Noia no less inescapably than from Lawrence that final disposition of a pending certiorari petition is also unnecessary to exhaust state-court\nremedies.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That the District Court had erred was no mystery to respondent in 1996. He correctly asked the District Court to reconsider its decision to dismiss his habeas action, and instead to stay it pending disposition of his petition for certiorari (which is the proper procedural way to handle such duplicative filings). See App. to Brief for National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers as Amicus Curiae 8\xe2\x80\x9311 (hereinafter NACDL Brief). And he then filed a notice of appeal and unsuccessfully sought a certificate of appealability. See id., at 1\xe2\x80\x937. Respondent (who theretofore had shown himself to be a highly capable pro se litigant, undoubtedly aware of the availability of en banc and certiorari review) simply gave up too early. There is no more reason in this case than in any other to excuse the failure to make use of all available means of review. Far from thinking that a petition for certiorari \xe2\x80\x9cwould almost certainly have been futile,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 3, n., I think it would almost certainly have been successful. We give special attention to capital cases (as today\xe2\x80\x99s delicate disposition shows), and since the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of a certificate of appealability occurred on August 1, 1996, see App. to NACDL Brief 1, more than three months after AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s effective date, see Woodford v. Garceau, 538 U. S. 202, 204 (2003), it would have been obvious that our refusal to correct the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s clear error would subject this defendant\xe2\x80\x99s renewed request for federal habeas relief to AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s restrictions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More fundamentally, however, even were the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conjecture correct that diligence on respondent\xe2\x80\x99s part would not have been rewarded, neither AEDPA nor any principle of law would entitle him to relief from the collateral consequences of an uncorrected judicial error. We held in Daniels v. United States, 532 U. S. 374, 382 (2001) that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f \xe2\x80\xa6 a prior conviction used to enhance a federal sentence is no longer open to direct or collateral attack in its own right because the defendant failed to pursue those remedies while they were available (or because the defendant did so unsuccessfully), then that defendant is without recourse.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) If a defendant is subject to additional jail time because a prior erroneous conviction went unreversed, surely respondent cannot complain about the fact that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior uncorrected error has caused this habeas petition to be subject to AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s entirely reasonable restrictions.*

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There having been eliminated the possibility that AEDPA is inapplicable to this case (and hence that the question on which we granted certiorari and heard argument is not presented) what possible justification remains for canceling our grant of certiorari after full briefing and argument? There disappears, along with the claim of AEDPA inapplicability, any substance to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s contention that respondent is \xe2\x80\x9cvirtually identically situated\xe2\x80\x9d to the two other litigants with similar claims, and that he is being treated differently \xe2\x80\x9csimply because the District Court erroneously dismissed [his] pre-AEDPA petition.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4. No. He is being treated differently because he, unlike them, seeks federal habeas relief by means of a petition filed after AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s effective date. Is what happened here any less rational, any less fair, a basis for differential treatment than the random fact that one petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s habeas action was filed a day before AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s effective date, and another petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s could not be filed until one day after? Would the Court entertain the thought that if those two petitions involved the same sort of closing argument by the same prosecutor, the second of them would have to be exempted from AEDPA? If anything, the differential treatment is more justified here, since the later filing was not randomly determined, but was likely the consequence of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s failure to exhaust his appeals.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court seems to be affected by a vague and discomforting feeling that things are different now from what they were when we granted certiorari. They are so only in the respect that we now know, as we did not then, that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s earlier petition was wrongfully dismissed. That fact has relevance neither to the law governing this case (as discussed in Part I, supra) nor to any equities that might justify our bringing to naught the parties\xe2\x80\x99 briefing and arguments, and the Justices\xe2\x80\x99 deliberations, on the question for which this petition was granted. But what makes today\xe2\x80\x99s wasteful action particularly perverse is that it is the fault of respondent that we did not know of the wrongful dismissal earlier. Before we granted plenary review, respondent had never argued that AEDPA should not apply because of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s error. He made no such claim either time he was before the Eighth Circuit. See Brief for Appellee in Bowersox v. Weaver, No. 99\xe2\x80\x933462, pp.\xc2\xa0xvii\xe2\x80\x93xix; Brief for Appellee/Cross-Appellant in Bowersox v. Weaver, No. 03\xe2\x80\x932880 et\xc2\xa0al., p.\xc2\xa07. And, more significantly, he remained completely silent in his brief in opposition, despite his obligation to raise the issue under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 15.2. Indeed, even in respondent\xe2\x80\x99s merits brief, his argument (if it can be called that) consists of three sentences explaining the procedural history followed by a conclusory assertion, all buried in footnote 44 on page 39.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s delayed invocation of this issue has not only not been sanctioned; it has been rewarded. Had respondent raised his specious claim of AEDPA inapplicability in a timely manner, petitioner would have had the opportunity to blow it out of the water. Whether by way of calculus or through dumb luck, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s tardiness has succeeded in confounding the Court. We promulgated Rule 15.2 precisely to prohibit such sandbagging\xe2\x80\x94and to avoid the ill effects that minimal briefing has on the quality of our decisionmaking, as perfectly demonstrated by this case. Respondent and his counsel should not profit from their flouting of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rules.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I would thus answer the question on which we granted certiorari and received full briefing and argument. Because plenary review has convinced me beyond doubt that the Missouri Supreme Court did not unreasonably apply clearly established precedent of this Court, I would reverse the judgment of the Eighth Circuit.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A postscript is warranted in light of the unusual circumstances in which we dispose of this case. The greatest harm done by today\xe2\x80\x99s cancellation is not to the State of Missouri, which will have to retry this murder case almost two decades after the original trial\xe2\x80\x94though that is harm enough. The greatest harm is that done to AEDPA, since dismissing the writ of certiorari leaves the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s grossly erroneous precedent on the books. (That precedent, by the way, cannot be explained away\xe2\x80\x94as perhaps the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own opinion can\xe2\x80\x94as the product of law-distorting compassion for a defendant wronged by a District Court\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous action. As noted earlier, the Eighth Circuit was not informed of that erroneous action. It presumably really believes that this is the way AEDPA should be applied.) Other courts should be warned that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to reverse the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision is a rare manifestation of judicial clemency unrestrained by law. They would be well advised to do unto the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision just what it did unto AEDPA: ignore it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the foregoing reasons, I respectfully dissent.
