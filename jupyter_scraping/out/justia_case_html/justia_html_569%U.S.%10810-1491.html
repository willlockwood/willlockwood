\n\t\t\t\tKIOBEL, individually and on behalf of her late husband KIOBEL, et\xc2\xa0al. v. ROYAL DUTCH PETROLEUM CO. et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x931491.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 28, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Reargued October 1, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 17, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners, Nigerian nationals residing in the United States, filed suit in federal court under the Alien Tort Statute, alleging that respondents\xe2\x80\x94certain Dutch, British, and Nigerian corporations\xe2\x80\x94aided and abetted the Nigerian Government in committing violations of the law of nations in Nigeria. The ATS provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he district courts shall have original jurisdiction of any civil action by an alien for a tort only, committed in violation of the law of nations or a treaty of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71350. The District Court dismissed several of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims, but on interlocutory appeal, the Second Circuit dismissed the entire complaint, reasoning that the law of nations does not recognize corporate liability. This Court granted certiorari, and ordered supplemental briefing on whether and under what circumstances courts may recognize a cause of action under the ATS, for violations of the law of nations occurring within the territory of a sovereign other than the United States.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The presumption against extraterritoriality applies to claims under the ATS, and nothing in the statute rebuts that presumption. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Passed as part of the Judiciary Act of 1789, the ATS is a jurisdictional statute that creates no causes of action. It permits federal courts to \xe2\x80\x9crecognize private claims [for a modest number of international law violations] under federal common law.\xe2\x80\x9d Sosa v. Alvarez-Machain, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 692\n. In contending that a claim under the ATS does not reach conduct occurring in a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s territory, respondents rely on the presumption against extraterritorial application, which provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a statute gives no clear indication of an extraterritorial application, it has none,\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison v. National Australia Bank Ltd., 561 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. The presumption \xe2\x80\x9cserves to protect against unintended clashes between our laws and those of other nations which could result in international discord.\xe2\x80\x9d EEOC v. Arabian American Oil Co., \n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244\n. It is typically applied to discern whether an Act of Congress regulating conduct applies abroad, see, e.g., id., at 246, but its underlying principles similarly constrain courts when considering causes of action that may be brought under the ATS. Indeed, the danger of unwarranted judicial interference in the conduct of foreign policy is magnified in this context, where the question is not what Congress has done but what courts may do. These foreign policy concerns are not diminished by the fact that Sosa limited federal courts to recognizing causes of action only for alleged violations of international law norms that are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98specific, universal, and obligatory,\xe2\x80\x9d 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 732. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x936.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The presumption is not rebutted by the text, history, or purposes of the ATS. Nothing in the ATS\xe2\x80\x99s text evinces a clear indication of extraterritorial reach. Violations of the law of nations affecting aliens can occur either within or outside the United States. And generic terms, like \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d in the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cany civil action,\xe2\x80\x9d do not rebut the presumption against extraterritoriality. See, e.g., Morrison, supra, at ___. Petitioners also rely on the common-law \xe2\x80\x9ctransitory torts\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine, but that doctrine is inapposite here; as the Court has explained, \xe2\x80\x9cthe only justification for allowing a party to recover when the cause of action arose in another civilized jurisdiction is a well-founded belief that it was a cause of action in that place,\xe2\x80\x9d Cuba R. Co. v. Crosby, \n\n222 U.\xc2\xa0S. 473\n. The question under Sosa is not whether a federal court has jurisdiction to entertain a cause of action provided by foreign or even international law. The question is instead whether the court has authority to recognize a cause of action under U.\xc2\xa0S. law to enforce a norm of international law. That question is not answered by the mere fact that the ATS mentions torts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe historical background against which the ATS was enacted also does not overcome the presumption. When the ATS was passed, \xe2\x80\x9cthree principal offenses against the law of nations\xe2\x80\x9d had been identified by Blackstone: violation of safe conducts, infringement of the rights of ambassadors, and piracy. Sosa, supra, at 723, 724. Prominent contemporary examples of the first two offenses\xe2\x80\x94immediately before and after passage of the ATS\xe2\x80\x94provide no support for the proposition that Congress expected causes of action to be brought under the statute for violations of the law of nations occurring abroad. And although the offense of piracy normally occurs on the high seas, beyond the territorial jurisdiction of the United States or any other country, applying U.\xc2\xa0S. law to pirates does not typically impose the sovereign will of the United States onto conduct occurring within the territorial jurisdiction of another sovereign, and therefore carries less direct foreign policy consequences. A 1795 opinion of Attorney General William Bradford regarding the conduct of U.\xc2\xa0S. citizens on both the high seas and a foreign shore is at best ambiguous about the ATS\xe2\x80\x99s extraterritorial application; it does not suffice to counter the weighty concerns underlying the presumption against extraterritoriality. Finally, there is no indication that the ATS was passed to make the United States a uniquely hospitable forum for the enforcement of international norms. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tESTHER KIOBEL, individually and on behalf of her late husband, Dr. BARINEM KIOBEL, et\xc2\xa0al., PETI- TIONERS v. ROYAL DUTCH PETROLEUM CO. et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[April 17, 2013]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners, a group of Nigerian nationals residing in the United States, filed suit in federal court against certain Dutch, British, and Nigerian corporations. Petitioners sued under the Alien Tort Statute, \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71350, alleging that the corporations aided and abetted the Nigerian Government in committing violations of the law of nations in Nigeria. The question presented is whether and under what circumstances courts may recognize a cause of action under the Alien Tort Statute, for violations of the law of nations occurring within the territory of a sovereign other than the United States.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners were residents of Ogoniland, an area of 250 square miles located in the Niger delta area of Nigeria and populated by roughly half a million people. When the complaint was filed, respondents Royal Dutch Petroleum Company and Shell Transport and Trading Company, p.l.c., were holding companies incorporated in the Netherlands and England, respectively. Their joint subsidiary, respondent Shell Petroleum Development Company of Nigeria, Ltd. (SPDC), was incorporated in Nigeria, and engaged in oil exploration and production in Ogoniland. According to the complaint, after concerned residents of Ogoniland began protesting the environmental effects of SPDC\xe2\x80\x99s practices, respondents enlisted the Nigerian Government to violently suppress the burgeoning demonstrations. Throughout the early 1990\xe2\x80\x99s, the complaint alleges, Nigerian military and police forces attacked Ogoni vil- lages, beating, raping, killing, and arresting residents and destroying or looting property. Petitioners further allege that respondents aided and abetted these atrocities by, among other things, providing the Nigerian forces with food, transportation, and compensation, as well as by al- lowing the Nigerian military to use respondents\xe2\x80\x99 property as a staging ground for attacks.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFollowing the alleged atrocities, petitioners moved to the United States where they have been granted political asylum and now reside as legal residents. See Supp. Brief for Petitioners 3, and n.\xc2\xa02. They filed suit in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York, alleging jurisdiction under the Alien Tort Statute and requesting relief under customary international law. The ATS provides, in full, that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he district courts shall have original jurisdiction of any civil action by an alien for a tort only, committed in violation of the law of nations or a treaty of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71350. According to petitioners, respondents violated the law of nations by aiding and abetting the Nigerian Government in committing (1) extrajudicial killings; (2) crimes against humanity; (3) torture and cruel treatment; (4) arbitrary arrest and detention; (5) violations of the rights to life, liberty, security, and association; (6) forced exile; and (7) property destruction. The District Court dismissed the first, fifth, sixth, and seventh claims, reasoning that the facts alleged to support those claims did not give rise to a violation of the law of nations. The court denied respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion to dismiss with respect to the remaining claims, but certified its order for interlocutory appeal pursuant to \xc2\xa71292(b).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Second Circuit dismissed the entire complaint, rea- soning that the law of nations does not recognize corpo- rate liability. 621 F.\xc2\xa03d 111 (2010). We granted certiorari to consider that question. 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011). After oral argument, we directed the parties to file supplemen- tal briefs addressing an additional question: \xe2\x80\x9cWhether and under what circumstances the [ATS] allows courts to recognize a cause of action for violations of the law of nations occurring within the territory of a sovereign other than the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012). We heard oral argument again and now affirm the judgment below, based on our answer to the second question.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPassed as part of the Judiciary Act of 1789, the ATS was invoked twice in the late 18th century, but then only once more over the next 167 years. Act of Sept. 24, 1789, \xc2\xa79, 1 Stat 77; see Moxon v. The Fanny, 17 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 942 (No. 9,895) (DC Pa. 1793); Bolchos v. Darrel, 3 F. Cas. 810 (No. 1,607) (DC SC 1795); O\xe2\x80\x99Reilly de Camara v. Brooke, \n\n209 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45 (1908)\n; Khedivial Line, S.A.E. v. Seafarers\xe2\x80\x99 Int\xe2\x80\x99l Union, 278 F.\xc2\xa02d 49, 51\xe2\x80\x9352 (CA2 1960) (per curiam). The statute provides district courts with jurisdiction to hear certain claims, but does not expressly provide any causes of action. We held in Sosa v. Alvarez-Machain, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 692, \n714 (2004)\n, however, that the First Congress did not intend the provision to be \xe2\x80\x9cstillborn.\xe2\x80\x9d The grant of jurisdiction is instead \xe2\x80\x9cbest read as having been enacted on the understanding that the common law would provide a cause of action for [a] modest number of international law violations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 724. We thus held that federal courts may \xe2\x80\x9crecognize private claims [for such violations] under federal common law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 732. The Court in Sosa rejected the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim in that case for \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary arrest and detention,\xe2\x80\x9d on the ground that it failed to state a violation of the law of nations with the requisite \xe2\x80\x9cdefinite content and acceptance among civilized nations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 699, 732.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question here is not whether petitioners have stated a proper claim under the ATS, but whether a claim may reach conduct occurring in the territory of a foreign sovereign. Respondents contend that claims under the ATS do not, relying primarily on a canon of statutory interpretation known as the presumption against extraterritorial application. That canon provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a statute gives no clear indication of an extraterritorial application, it has none,\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison v. National Australia Bank Ltd., 561 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 6), and reflects the \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption that United States law governs domestically but does not rule the world,\xe2\x80\x9d Microsoft Corp. v. AT&T Corp., \n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437, \n454 (2007)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis presumption \xe2\x80\x9cserves to protect against unintended clashes between our laws and those of other nations which could result in international discord.\xe2\x80\x9d EEOC v. Arabian American Oil Co., \n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244, \n248 (1991)\n (Aramco). As this Court has explained:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cFor us to run interference in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a delicate field of international relations there must be present the affirmative intention of the Congress clearly expressed. It alone has the facilities necessary to make fairly such an important policy decision where the possibilities of international discord are so evident and retaliative action so certain.\xe2\x80\x9d Benz v. Compania Naviera Hidalgo, S. A., \n\n353 U.\xc2\xa0S. 138, \n147 (1957)\n. The presumption against extraterritorial application helps ensure that the Judiciary does not erroneously adopt an interpretation of U.\xc2\xa0S. law that carries foreign pol- icy consequences not clearly intended by the political branches.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe typically apply the presumption to discern whether an Act of Congress regulating conduct applies abroad. See, e.g., Aramco, supra, at 246 (\xe2\x80\x9cThese cases present the issue whether Title VII applies extraterritorially to regulate the employment practices of United States employers who employ United States citizens abroad\xe2\x80\x9d); Morrison, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 4) (noting that the question of extraterritorial application was a \xe2\x80\x9cmerits question,\xe2\x80\x9d not a question of jurisdiction). The ATS, on the other hand, is \xe2\x80\x9cstrictly jurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Sosa, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 713. It does not directly regulate conduct or afford relief. It instead allows federal courts to recognize certain causes of action based on sufficiently definite norms of international law. But we think the principles underlying the canon of interpretation similarly constrain courts considering causes of action that may be brought under the ATS.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, the danger of unwarranted judicial interference in the conduct of foreign policy is magnified in the context of the ATS, because the question is not what Congress has done but instead what courts may do. This Court in Sosa repeatedly stressed the need for judicial caution in considering which claims could be brought under the ATS, in light of foreign policy concerns. As the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cthe potential [foreign policy] implications\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of recog- nizing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. causes [under the ATS] should make courts particularly wary of impinging on the discretion of the Legislative and Executive Branches in managing foreign affairs.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 727; see also id., at 727\xe2\x80\x93728 (\xe2\x80\x9cSince many attempts by federal courts to craft remedies for the violation of new norms of international law would raise risks of adverse foreign policy consequences, they should be undertaken, if at all, with great caution\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 727 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he possible collateral consequences of making international rules privately actionable argue for judicial caution\xe2\x80\x9d). These concerns, which are implicated in any case arising under the ATS, are all the more pressing when the question is whether a cause of action under the ATS reaches conduct within the territory of another sovereign.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese concerns are not diminished by the fact that Sosa limited federal courts to recognizing causes of action only for alleged violations of international law norms that are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98specific, universal, and obligatory.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 732 (quoting In\xc2\xa0re Estate of Marcos, Human Rights Litigation, 25 F.\xc2\xa03d 1467, 1475 (CA9 1994)). As demonstrated by Congress\xe2\x80\x99s enactment of the Torture Victim Protection Act of 1991, \n\n106Stat.\n73, note following \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71350, identifying such a norm is only the beginning of defining a cause of action. See id., \xc2\xa73 (providing detailed definitions for extrajudicial killing and torture); id., \xc2\xa72 (specifying who may be liable, creating a rule of exhaustion, and establishing a statute of limitations). Each of these decisions carries with it significant foreign policy implications.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe principles underlying the presumption against ex- traterritoriality thus constrain courts exercising their power under the ATS.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners contend that even if the presumption applies, the text, history, and purposes of the ATS rebut it for causes of action brought under that statute. It is true that Congress, even in a jurisdictional provision, can indicate that it intends federal law to apply to conduct occurring abroad. See, e.g., \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71091(e) (2006 ed., Supp. V) (providing jurisdiction over the offense of genocide \xe2\x80\x9cregardless of where the offense is committed\xe2\x80\x9d if the alleged offender is, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9cpresent in the United States\xe2\x80\x9d). But to rebut the presumption, the ATS would need to evince a \xe2\x80\x9cclear indication of extraterritoriality.\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison, 561 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 16). It does not.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo begin, nothing in the text of the statute suggests that Congress intended causes of action recognized under it to have extraterritorial reach. The ATS covers actions by aliens for violations of the law of nations, but that does not imply extraterritorial reach\xe2\x80\x94such violations affect- ing aliens can occur either within or outside the United States. Nor does the fact that the text reaches \xe2\x80\x9cany civil action\xe2\x80\x9d suggest application to torts committed abroad; it is well established that generic terms like \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cevery\xe2\x80\x9d do not rebut the presumption against extraterritoriality. See, e.g., id., at ___ (slip op., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314); Small v. United States, \n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385, \n388 (2005)\n; Aramco, 499 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 248\xe2\x80\x93250; Foley Bros., Inc. v. Filardo, \n\n336 U.\xc2\xa0S. 281, \n287 (1949)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners make much of the fact that the ATS provides jurisdiction over civil actions for \xe2\x80\x9ctorts\xe2\x80\x9d in violation of the law of nations. They claim that in using that word, the First Congress \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily meant to provide for jurisdiction over extraterritorial transitory torts that could arise on foreign soil.\xe2\x80\x9d Supp. Brief for Petitioners 18. For support, they cite the common-law doctrine that allowed courts to assume jurisdiction over such \xe2\x80\x9ctransitory torts,\xe2\x80\x9d including actions for personal injury, arising abroad. See Mostyn v. Fabrigas, 1 Cowp.\xc2\xa0161, 177, 98 Eng. Rep. 1021, 1030 (1774) (Mansfield, L.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]ll actions of a transitory nature that arise abroad may be laid as happening in an English county\xe2\x80\x9d); Dennick v. Railroad Co., \n\n103 U.\xc2\xa0S. 11, \n18 (1881)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cWherever, by either the common law or the statute law of a State, a right of action has become fixed and a legal liability incurred, that liability may be enforced and the right of action pursued in any court which has jurisdiction of such matters and can obtain jurisdiction of the parties\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder the transitory torts doctrine, however, \xe2\x80\x9cthe only justification for allowing a party to recover when the cause of action arose in another civilized jurisdiction is a well founded belief that it was a cause of action in that place.\xe2\x80\x9d Cuba R. Co. v. Crosby, \n\n222 U.\xc2\xa0S. 473, \n479 (1912)\n (majority opinion of Holmes, J.). The question under Sosa is not whether a federal court has jurisdiction to entertain a cause of action provided by foreign or even international law. The question is instead whether the court has authority to recognize a cause of action under U.\xc2\xa0S. law to enforce a norm of international law. The reference to \xe2\x80\x9ctort\xe2\x80\x9d does not demonstrate that the First Congress \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily meant\xe2\x80\x9d for those causes of action to reach conduct in the territory of a foreign sovereign. In the end, nothing in the text of the ATS evinces the requisite clear indication of extraterritoriality.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor does the historical background against which the ATS was enacted overcome the presumption against ap- plication to conduct in the territory of another sovereign. See Morrison, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 16) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ssuredly context can be consulted\xe2\x80\x9d in determining whether a cause of action applies abroad). We explained in Sosa that when Congress passed the ATS, \xe2\x80\x9cthree principal offenses against the law of nations\xe2\x80\x9d had been identified by Blackstone: violation of safe conducts, infringement of the rights of ambassadors, and piracy. 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 723, 724; see 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 68 (1769). The first two offenses have no necessary extraterritorial application. Indeed, Blackstone\xe2\x80\x94in describing them\xe2\x80\x94did so in terms of conduct occur- ring within the forum nation. See ibid. (describing the right of safe conducts for those \xe2\x80\x9cwho are here\xe2\x80\x9d); 1 id., at 251 (1765) (explaining that safe conducts grant a member of one society \xe2\x80\x9ca right to intrude into another\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 245\xe2\x80\x93248 (recognizing the king\xe2\x80\x99s power to \xe2\x80\x9creceiv[e] ambassadors at home\xe2\x80\x9d and detailing their rights in the state \xe2\x80\x9cwherein they are appointed to reside\xe2\x80\x9d); see also E. De Vattel, Law of Nations 465 (J. Chitty et\xc2\xa0al. transl. and ed. 1883) (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]n his entering the country to which he is sent, and making himself known, [the ambassador] is under the protection of the law of nations .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTwo notorious episodes involving violations of the law of nations occurred in the United States shortly before passage of the ATS. Each concerned the rights of ambas- sadors, and each involved conduct within the Union. In 1784, a French adventurer verbally and physically assaulted Francis Barbe Marbois\xe2\x80\x94the Secretary of the French Legion\xe2\x80\x94in Philadelphia. The assault led the French Minister Plenipotentiary to lodge a formal protest with the Continental Congress and threaten to leave the country unless an adequate remedy were provided. Respublica v. De Longschamps, 1 Dall. 111 (O.\xc2\xa0T. Phila. 1784); Sosa, supra, at 716\xe2\x80\x93717, and n.\xc2\xa011. And in 1787, a New York constable entered the Dutch Ambassador\xe2\x80\x99s house and arrested one of his domestic servants. See Casto, The Federal Courts\xe2\x80\x99 Protective Jurisdiction over Torts Committed in Violation of the Law of Nations, 18 Conn. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 467, 494 (1986). At the request of Secretary of Foreign Affairs John Jay, the Mayor of New York City arrested the constable in turn, but cautioned that because \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98neither Congress nor our [State] Legislature have yet passed any act respecting a breach of the privileges of Ambassadors,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the extent of any available relief would depend on the common law. See Bradley, The Alien Tort Statute and Article III, 42 Va. J. Int\xe2\x80\x99l L. 587, 641\xe2\x80\x93642 (2002) (quoting 3 Dept. of State, The Diplomatic Correspondence of the United States of America 447 (1837)). The two cases in which the ATS was invoked shortly after its passage also concerned conduct within the territory of the United States. See Bolchos, 3 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 810 (wrongful seizure of slaves from a vessel while in port in the United States); Moxon, 17 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 942 (wrongful seizure in United States territorial waters).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese prominent contemporary examples\xe2\x80\x94immediately before and after passage of the ATS\xe2\x80\x94provide no support for the proposition that Congress expected causes of action to be brought under the statute for violations of the law of nations occurring abroad.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe third example of a violation of the law of nations familiar to the Congress that enacted the ATS was piracy. Piracy typically occurs on the high seas, beyond the territorial jurisdiction of the United States or any other country. See 4 Blackstone, supra, at 72 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe offence of piracy, by common law, consists of committing those acts of robbery and depredation upon the high seas, which, if committed upon land, would have amounted to felony there\xe2\x80\x9d). This Court has generally treated the high seas the same as foreign soil for purposes of the presumption against extraterritorial application. See, e.g., Sale v. Haitian Centers Council, Inc., \n\n509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 155\n\xe2\x80\x93174 (1993) (declining to apply a provision of the Immigration and Nationality Act to conduct occurring on the high seas); Argentine Republic v. Amerada Hess Shipping Corp., \n\n488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 428, \n440 (1989)\n (declining to apply a provision of the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 to the high seas). Petitioners contend that because Congress surely intended the ATS to provide jurisdiction for actions against pirates, it necessarily anticipated the statute would apply to conduct occurring abroad.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApplying U.\xc2\xa0S. law to pirates, however, does not typi- cally impose the sovereign will of the United States onto conduct occurring within the territorial jurisdiction of another sovereign, and therefore carries less direct foreign policy consequences. Pirates were fair game wherever found, by any nation, because they generally did not operate within any jurisdiction. See 4 Blackstone, supra, at 71. We do not think that the existence of a cause of action against them is a sufficient basis for concluding that other causes of action under the ATS reach conduct that does occur within the territory of another sovereign; pirates may well be a category unto themselves. See Morrison, 561 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 16) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen a statute provides for some extraterritorial application, the presumption against extraterritoriality operates to limit that provision to its terms\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Microsoft Corp., 550 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 455\xe2\x80\x93456.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners also point to a 1795 opinion authored by Attorney General William Bradford. See Breach of Neutrality, 1 Op. Atty. Gen. 57. In 1794, in the midst of war between France and Great Britain, and notwithstanding the American official policy of neutrality, several U.\xc2\xa0S. citizens joined a French privateer fleet and attacked and plundered the British colony of Sierra Leone. In response to a protest from the British Ambassador, Attorney General Bradford responded as follows:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSo far .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as the transactions complained of originated or took place in a foreign country, they are not within the cognizance of our courts; nor can the actors be legally prosecuted or punished for them by the United States. But crimes committed on the high seas are within the jurisdiction of the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. courts of the United States; and, so far as the offence was committed thereon, I am inclined to think that it may be legally prosecuted in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. those courts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. But some doubt rests on this point, in consequence of the terms in which the [applicable criminal law] is expressed. But there can be no doubt that the company or individuals who have been injured by these acts of hostil- ity have a remedy by a civil suit in the courts of the United States; jurisdiction being expressly given to these courts in all cases where an alien sues for a tort only, in violation of the laws of nations, or a treaty of the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 58\xe2\x80\x9359.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners read the last sentence as confirming that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Founding generation understood the ATS to apply to law of nations violations committed on the territory of a foreign sovereign.\xe2\x80\x9d Supp. Brief for Petitioners 33. Respondents counter that when Attorney General Bradford referred to \xe2\x80\x9cthese acts of hostility,\xe2\x80\x9d he meant the acts only insofar as they took place on the high seas, and even if his conclusion were broader, it was only because the applicable treaty had extraterritorial reach. See Supp. Brief for Respondents 28\xe2\x80\x9330. The Solicitor General, having once read the opinion to stand for the proposition that an \xe2\x80\x9cATS suit could be brought against American citizens for breaching neutrality with Britain only if acts did not take place in a foreign country,\xe2\x80\x9d Supp. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 8, n.\xc2\xa01 (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted), now suggests the opinion \xe2\x80\x9ccould have been meant to encompass .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. conduct [occurring within the foreign territory],\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 8.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAttorney General Bradford\xe2\x80\x99s opinion defies a definitive reading and we need not adopt one here. Whatever its pre- cise meaning, it deals with U.\xc2\xa0S. citizens who, by partic- ipating in an attack taking place both on the high seas and on a foreign shore, violated a treaty between the United States and Great Britain. The opinion hardly suffices to counter the weighty concerns underlying the presumption against extraterritoriality.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, there is no indication that the ATS was passed to make the United States a uniquely hospitable forum for the enforcement of international norms. As Justice Story put it, \xe2\x80\x9cNo nation has ever yet pretended to be the custos morum of the whole world .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. The La Jeune Eugenie, 26 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 832, 847 (No. 15,551) (CC. Mass. 1822). It is implausible to suppose that the First Congress wanted their fledgling Republic\xe2\x80\x94struggling to receive international recognition\xe2\x80\x94to be the first. Indeed, the parties offer no evidence that any nation, meek or mighty, presumed to do such a thing.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe United States was, however, embarrassed by its potential inability to provide judicial relief to foreign officials injured in the United States. Bradley, 42 Va. J. Int\xe2\x80\x99l L., at 641. Such offenses against ambassadors vio- lated the law of nations, \xe2\x80\x9cand if not adequately redressed could rise to an issue of war.\xe2\x80\x9d Sosa, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 715; cf. The Federalist No. 80, p.\xc2\xa0536 (J. Cooke ed. 1961) (A. Hamilton) (\xe2\x80\x9cAs the denial or perversion of justice .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is with reason classed among the just causes of war, it will follow that the federal judiciary ought to have cognizance of all causes in which the citizens of other countries are concerned\xe2\x80\x9d). The ATS ensured that the United States could provide a forum for adjudicating such incidents. See Sosa, supra, at 715\xe2\x80\x93718, and n. 11. Nothing about this historical context suggests that Congress also intended federal common law under the ATS to provide a cause of action for conduct occurring in the territory of another sovereign.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, far from avoiding diplomatic strife, providing such a cause of action could have generated it. Recent experience bears this out. See Doe v. Exxon Mobil Corp., 654 F.\xc2\xa03d 11, 77\xe2\x80\x9378 (CADC 2011) (Kavanaugh, J., dissenting in part) (listing recent objections to extraterritorial applications of the ATS by Canada, Germany, Indonesia, Papua New Guinea, South Africa, Switzerland, and the United Kingdom). \n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, accepting petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 view would imply that other nations, also applying the law of nations, could hale our citizens into their courts for alleged violations of the law of nations occurring in the United States, or anywhere else in the world. The presumption against extraterritoriality guards against our courts triggering such serious foreign policy consequences, and instead defers such decisions, quite appropriately, to the political branches.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe therefore conclude that the presumption against extraterritoriality applies to claims under the ATS, and that nothing in the statute rebuts that presumption. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is no clear indication of extraterritoriality here,\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison, 561 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 16), and petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 case seeking relief for violations of the law of nations occurring outside the United States is barred.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn these facts, all the relevant conduct took place outside the United States. And even where the claims touch and concern the territory of the United States, they must do so with sufficient force to displace the presumption against extraterritorial application. See Morrison, 561 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (slip op. at 17\xe2\x80\x9324). Corporations are often present in many countries, and it would reach too far to say that mere corporate presence suffices. If Congress were to determine otherwise, a statute more specific than the ATS would be required.\n\t\t\t

\n

Specifically, I agree that when Alien Tort Statute (ATS) \xe2\x80\x9cclaims touch and concern the territory of the United States, they must do so with sufficient force to displace the presumption against extraterritorial application.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Morrison v. National Australia Bank Ltd., 561 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2010), we explained that \xe2\x80\x9cthe presumption against extraterritorial application would be a craven watchdog indeed if it retreated to its kennel whenever some domestic activity is involved in the case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 17). We also reiterated that a cause of action falls out- side the scope of the presumption\xe2\x80\x94and thus is not barred by the presumption\xe2\x80\x94only if the event or relationship that was \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98focus\xe2\x80\x99 of congressional concern\xe2\x80\x9d under the relevant statute takes place within the United States. Ibid. (quoting EEOC v. Arabian American Oil Co., \n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244, \n255 (1991)\n). For example, because \xe2\x80\x9cthe focus of the [Securities] Exchange Act [of 1934] is not upon the place where the deception originated, but upon purchases and sales of securities in the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d we held in Mor-rison that \xc2\xa710(b) of the Exchange Act applies \xe2\x80\x9conly\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9ctransactions in securities listed on domestic exchanges, and domestic transactions in other securities.\xe2\x80\x9d 561 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (slip op., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Sosa v. Alvarez-Machain, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 692 (2004)\n, makes clear that when the ATS was enacted, \xe2\x80\x9ccongressional concern\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98focus[ed],\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 17), on the \xe2\x80\x9cthree principal offenses against the law of nations\xe2\x80\x9d that had been identified by Blackstone: violation of safe conducts, infringement of the rights of ambassadors, and piracy, Sosa, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 723\xe2\x80\x93724. The Court therefore held that \xe2\x80\x9cfederal courts should not recognize private claims under federal common law for violations of any international law norm with less definite content and acceptance among civilized nations than the historical paradigms familiar when [the ATS] was enacted.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 732. In other words, only conduct that satisfies Sosa\xe2\x80\x99s requirements of definiteness and ac-ceptance among civilized nations can be said to have been \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98focus\xe2\x80\x99 of congressional concern,\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 17), when Congress enacted the ATS. The Court sets forth four key propositions of law: First, the \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption against extraterritoriality applies to claims under\xe2\x80\x9d the Alien Tort Statute. Ante, at 13. Second, \xe2\x80\x9cnothing in the statute rebuts that presumption.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Third, there \xe2\x80\x9cis no clear indication of extraterritoria[l application] here,\xe2\x80\x9d where \xe2\x80\x9call the relevant conduct took place outside the United States\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the claims\xe2\x80\x9d do not \xe2\x80\x9ctouch and concern the territory of the United States\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0with sufficient force to displace the presumption.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (internal quotation marks omitted). \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnlike the Court, I would not invoke the presumption against extraterritoriality. See Sosa v. Alvarez-Machain, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 692, \n732 (2004)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[F]or purposes of civil liability, the torturer has become\xe2\x80\x94like the pirate and slave trader before him\xe2\x80\x94hostis humani generis, an enemy of all mankind.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Filartiga v. Pena-Irala, 630 F.\xc2\xa02d 876, 890 (CA2 1980) (alteration in original))). See also 1 Re-statement (Third) of Foreign Relations Law of the United States \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 402, 403, 404 (1986). In this case, however, the parties and relevant conduct lack sufficient ties to the United States for the ATS to provide jurisdiction.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur decision in Sosa frames the question. In Sosa the Court specified that the Alien Tort Statute (ATS), when enacted in 1789, \xe2\x80\x9cwas intended as jurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 714. We added that the statute gives today\xe2\x80\x99s courts the power to apply certain \xe2\x80\x9cjudge-made\xe2\x80\x9d damages law to victims of certain foreign affairs-related misconduct, including \xe2\x80\x9cthree specific offenses\xe2\x80\x9d to which \xe2\x80\x9cBlackstone referred,\xe2\x80\x9d namely \xe2\x80\x9cviolation of safe conducts, infringement of the rights of ambassadors, and piracy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 715. We held that the statute provides today\xe2\x80\x99s federal judges with the power to fashion \xe2\x80\x9ca cause of action\xe2\x80\x9d for a \xe2\x80\x9cmodest number\xe2\x80\x9d of claims, \xe2\x80\x9cbased on the present-day law of nations,\xe2\x80\x9d and which \xe2\x80\x9crest on a norm of international character accepted by the civilized world and defined with a specificity comparable to the features\xe2\x80\x9d of those three \xe2\x80\x9c18th-century paradigms.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 724\xe2\x80\x93725.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe further said that, in doing so, a requirement of \xe2\x80\x9cexhaust[ion]\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cremedies\xe2\x80\x9d might apply. Id., at 733, n.\xc2\xa021. We noted \xe2\x80\x9ca strong argument that federal courts should give serious weight to the Executive Branch\xe2\x80\x99s view of the case\xe2\x80\x99s impact on foreign policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Adjudicating any such claim must, in my view, also be consistent with those notions of comity that lead each nation to respect the sovereign rights of other nations by limiting the reach of its own laws and their enforcement. Id., at 761 (Breyer, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). See also F. Hoffmann-La Roche Ltd v. Empagran S.\xc2\xa0A., \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 155\n\xe2\x80\x93169 (2004).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRecognizing that Congress enacted the ATS to permit recovery of damages from pirates and others who violated basic international law norms as understood in 1789, Sosa essentially leads today\xe2\x80\x99s judges to ask: Who are today\xe2\x80\x99s pirates? See 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 724\xe2\x80\x93725 (majority opinion). We provided a framework for answering that question by setting down principles drawn from international norms and designed to limit ATS claims to those that are similar in character and specificity to piracy. Id., at 725.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case we must decide the extent to which this jurisdictional statute opens a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s doors to those harmed by activities belonging to the limited class that Sosa set forth when those activities take place abroad. To help answer this question here, I would refer both to Sosa and, as in Sosa, to norms of international law. See Part II, infra.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn my view the majority\xe2\x80\x99s effort to answer the question by referring to the \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption against extraterritoriality\xe2\x80\x9d does not work well. That presumption \xe2\x80\x9crests on the perception that Congress ordinarily legislates with respect to domestic, not foreign matters.\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison v. National Australia Bank Ltd., 561 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 5\xe2\x80\x936). See ante, at 4. The ATS, however, was enacted with \xe2\x80\x9cforeign matters\xe2\x80\x9d in mind. The statute\xe2\x80\x99s text refers explicitly to \xe2\x80\x9calien[s],\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ctreat[ies],\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe law of nations.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71350. The statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose was to address \xe2\x80\x9cviolations of the law of nations, admitting of a judicial remedy and at the same time threatening serious con-sequences in international affairs.\xe2\x80\x9d Sosa, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 715. And at least one of the three kinds of activities that we found to fall within the statute\xe2\x80\x99s scope, namely piracy, ibid., normally takes place abroad. See 4 W.\xc2\xa0Blackstone, Commentaries on the Law of England 72 (1769).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority cannot wish this piracy example away by emphasizing that piracy takes place on the high seas. See ante, at 10. That is because the robbery and murder that make up piracy do not normally take place in the water; they take place on a ship. And a ship is like land, in that it falls within the jurisdiction of the nation whose flag it flies. See McCulloch v. Sociedad Nacional de Marineros de Honduras, \n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 10\n\xe2\x80\x9321 (1963); 2 Restatement \xc2\xa7502, Comment d (\xe2\x80\x9c[F]lag state has jurisdiction to prescribe with respect to any activity aboard the ship\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, in the early 19th century Chief Justice Marshall described piracy as an \xe2\x80\x9coffenc[e] against the nation under whose flag the vessel sails, and within whose particular jurisdiction all on board the vessel are.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Palmer, 3 Wheat. 610, 632 (1818). See United States v. Furlong, 5 Wheat. 184, 197 (1820) (a crime committed \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d of a foreign state and a crime committed \xe2\x80\x9cin the vessel of another nation\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9cthe same thing\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority nonetheless tries to find a distinction between piracy at sea and similar cases on land. It writes, \xe2\x80\x9cApplying U.\xc2\xa0S. law to pirates .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does not typically impose the sovereign will of the United States onto conduct occurring within the territorial jurisdiction of another sovereign and therefore carries less direct foreign policy consequences.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10 (emphasis added). But, as I have just pointed out, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]pplying U.\xc2\xa0S. law to pirates\xe2\x80\x9d does typically involve applying our law to acts taking place within the jurisdiction of another sovereign. Nor can the majority\xe2\x80\x99s words \xe2\x80\x9cterritorial jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d sensibly dis-tinguish land from sea for purposes of isolating adverse foreign policy risks, as the Barbary Pirates, the War of 1812, the sinking of the Lusitania, and the Lockerbie bombing make all too clear.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority also writes, \xe2\x80\x9cPirates were fair game wherever found, by any nation, because they generally did not operate within any jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. I very much agree that pirates were fair game \xe2\x80\x9cwherever found.\xe2\x80\x9d Indeed, that is the point. That is why we asked, in Sosa, who are today\xe2\x80\x99s pirates? Certainly today\xe2\x80\x99s pirates include torturers and perpetrators of genocide. And today, like the pirates of old, they are \xe2\x80\x9cfair game\xe2\x80\x9d where they are found. Like those pirates, they are \xe2\x80\x9ccommon enemies of all mankind and all nations have an equal interest in their apprehension and punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Restatement \xc2\xa7404 Reporters\xe2\x80\x99 Note 1, p.\xc2\xa0256 (quoting In\xc2\xa0re Demjanjuk, 612 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 544, 556 (ND Ohio 1985) (internal quotation marks omitted)). See Sosa, supra, at 732. And just as a nation that harbored pirates provoked the concern of other nations in past centuries, see infra, at 8, so harboring \xe2\x80\x9ccommon enemies of all mankind\xe2\x80\x9d provokes similar concerns today.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, as applied to the narrow class of cases that Sosa described, fails to provide significant support for the use of any presumption against extraterritoriality; rather, it suggests the contrary. See also ante, at 10 (conceding and citing cases showing that this Court has \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally treated the high seas the same as foreign soil for purposes of the presumption against extraterritorial application\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn any event, as the Court uses its \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption against extraterritorial application,\xe2\x80\x9d it offers only limited help in deciding the question presented, namely \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98under what circumstances the Alien Tort Statute .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. allows courts to recognize a cause of action for violations of the law of nations occurring within the territory of a sovereign other than the United States.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012). The majority echoes in this jurisdictional context Sosa\xe2\x80\x99s warning to use \xe2\x80\x9ccaution\xe2\x80\x9d in shaping federal common-law causes of action. Ante, at 5. But it also makes clear that a statutory claim might sometimes \xe2\x80\x9ctouch and concern the territory of the United States . . . with sufficient force to displace the presumption.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. It leaves for another day the determination of just when the presumption against extraterritoriality might be \xe2\x80\x9covercome.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t In applying the ATS to acts \xe2\x80\x9coccurring within the territory of a[nother] sovereign,\xe2\x80\x9d I would assume that Congress intended the statute\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional reach to match the statute\xe2\x80\x99s underlying substantive grasp. That grasp, defined by the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purposes set forth in Sosa, includes compensation for those injured by piracy and its modern-day equivalents, at least where allowing such compensation avoids \xe2\x80\x9cserious\xe2\x80\x9d negative international \xe2\x80\x9cconsequences\xe2\x80\x9d for the United States. 542 U. S., at 715. And just as we have looked to established international substantive norms to help determine the statute\xe2\x80\x99s substantive reach, id., at 729, so we should look to international jurisdictional norms to help determine the statute\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional scope.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Restatement (Third) of Foreign Relations Law is helpful. Section 402 recognizes that, subject to \xc2\xa7403\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crea-sonableness\xe2\x80\x9d requirement, a nation may apply its law (for example, federal common law, see 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 729\xe2\x80\x93730) not only (1) to \xe2\x80\x9cconduct\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ctakes place [or to persons or things] within its territory\xe2\x80\x9d but also (2) to the \xe2\x80\x9cactivities, interests, status, or relations of its nationals outside as well as within its territory,\xe2\x80\x9d (3) to \xe2\x80\x9cconduct outside its territory that has or is intended to have substantial effect within its territory,\xe2\x80\x9d and (4) to certain foreign \xe2\x80\x9cconduct outside its territory .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that is directed against the security of the state or against a limited class of other state interests.\xe2\x80\x9d In addition, \xc2\xa7404 of the Restatement explains that a \xe2\x80\x9cstate has jurisdiction to define and prescribe punishment for certain offenses recognized by the community of nations as of universal concern, such as piracy, slave trade,\xe2\x80\x9d and analogous behavior.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsidering these jurisdictional norms in light of both the ATS\xe2\x80\x99s basic purpose (to provide compensation for those injured by today\xe2\x80\x99s pirates) and Sosa\xe2\x80\x99s basic caution (to avoid international friction), I believe that the statute provides jurisdiction where (1) the alleged tort occurs on American soil, (2) the defendant is an American national, or (3) the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct substantially and adversely affects an important American national interest, and that includes a distinct interest in preventing the United States from becoming a safe harbor (free of civil as well as criminal liability) for a torturer or other common enemy of mankind.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI would interpret the statute as providing jurisdiction only where distinct American interests are at issue. Doing so reflects the fact that Congress adopted the present statute at a time when, as Justice Story put it, \xe2\x80\x9cNo nation ha[d] ever yet pretended to be the custos morum of the whole world.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. La Jeune Eugenie, 26 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 832, 847 (No. 15,551) (CC Mass. 1822). That restriction also should help to minimize international friction. Further limiting principles such as exhaustion, forum non conveniens, and comity would do the same. So would a practice of courts giving weight to the views of the Executive Branch. See Sosa, 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 733, n.\xc2\xa021; id., at 761 (opinion of Breyer, J.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs I have indicated, we should treat this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s interest in not becoming a safe harbor for violators of the most fundamental international norms as an important jurisdiction-related interest justifying application of the ATS in light of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s basic purposes\xe2\x80\x94in particular that of compensating those who have suffered harm at the hands of, e.g., torturers or other modern pirates. Nothing in the statute or its history suggests that our courts should turn a blind eye to the plight of victims in that \xe2\x80\x9chandful of heinous actions.\xe2\x80\x9d Tel-Oren v. Libyan Arab Republic, 726 F.\xc2\xa02d 774, 781 (CADC 1984) (Edwards, J., concurring). See generally Leval, The Long Arm of International Law: Giving Victims of Human Rights Abuses Their Day in Court, 92 Foreign Affairs 16 (Mar. / Apr. 2013). To the contrary, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language, history, and purposes suggest that the statute was to be a weapon in the \xe2\x80\x9cwar\xe2\x80\x9d against those modern pirates who, by their conduct, have \xe2\x80\x9cdeclar[ed] war against all mankind.\xe2\x80\x9d 4\xc2\xa0Blackstone 71.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tInternational norms have long included a duty not to permit a nation to become a safe harbor for pirates (or their equivalent). See generally A. Bradford, Flying the Black Flag: A Brief History of Piracy 19 (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cEvery polis by the sea .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. which was suspected of sponsoring piracy or harboring pirates could be attacked and destroyed by the Athenians\xe2\x80\x9d); F. Sanborn, Origins of the Early English Maritime and Commercial Law 313 (1930) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn 1490 Henry VII made a proclamation against harboring pirates or purchasing goods from them\xe2\x80\x9d); N. Risjord, Representative Americans: The Colonists 146 (1981) (\xe2\x80\x9cWilliam Markham, Penn\xe2\x80\x99s lieutenant governor in the 1690s, was accused of harboring pirates in Philadelphia .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0Governor Benjamin Fletcher of New York became the target of a royal inquiry after he issued privateering commissions to a band of notorious pirates\xe2\x80\x9d); 3 C.\xc2\xa0Yonge, A Pictorial History of the World\xe2\x80\x99s Great Nations 954 (1882) (\xe2\x80\x9c[In the early 18th century, t]he government of Connecticut was accused of harboring pirates\xe2\x80\x9d); S. Menefee, Piracy, Terrorism, and the Insurgent Passenger: A Historical and Legal Perspective, in Maritime Terrorism and International Law 51 (N. Ronzitti ed. 1990) (quoting the judge who handled the seizure of the Chesapeake during the Civil War as stating that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98piracy jure gentium was justiciable by the court of New Brunswick, wherever committed\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); D. Field, Outlines of an International Code 33, Art. 84 (2d ed. 1876) (citing the 1794 treaty between the United States and Great Britain (\xe2\x80\x9cHarboring pirates forbidden. No nation can receive pirates into its territory, or permit any person within the same to receive, protect, conceal or assist them in any manner; but must punish all persons guilty of such acts\xe2\x80\x9d)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMore recently two lower American courts have, in effect, rested jurisdiction primarily upon that kind of concern. In Filartiga, 630 F.\xc2\xa02d 876, an alien plaintiff brought a lawsuit against an alien defendant for damages suffered through acts of torture that the defendant allegedly inflicted in a foreign nation, Paraguay. Neither plaintiff nor defendant was an American national and the actions underlying the lawsuit took place abroad. The defendant, however, \xe2\x80\x9chad\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0resided in the United States for more than ninth months\xe2\x80\x9d before being sued, having overstayed his visitor\xe2\x80\x99s visa. Id., at 878\xe2\x80\x93879. Jurisdiction was deemed proper because the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s alleged conduct violated a well-established international law norm, and the suit vindicated our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s interest in not providing a safe harbor, free of damages claims, for those defendants who commit such conduct.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Marcos, the plaintiffs were nationals of the Philippines, the defendant was a Philippine national, and the alleged wrongful act, death by torture, took place abroad. In\xc2\xa0re Estate of Marcos, Human Rights Litigation, 25 F.\xc2\xa03d 1467, 1469, 1475 (CA9 1994); In\xc2\xa0re Estate of Marcos Human Rights Litigation, 978 F.\xc2\xa02d 493, 495\xe2\x80\x93496, 500 (CA9 1992). A month before being sued, the defendant, \xe2\x80\x9chis family,\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0and others loyal to [him] fled to Hawaii,\xe2\x80\x9d where the ATS case was heard. Marcos, 25 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1469. As in Filartiga, the court found ATS jurisdiction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd in Sosa we referred to both cases with approval, suggesting that the ATS allowed a claim for relief in such circumstances. 542 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 732. See also Flomo v. Firestone Natural Rubber Co., 643 F.\xc2\xa03d 1013, 1025 (CA7 2011) (Posner, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cSosa was a case of nonmaritime extraterritorial conduct yet no Justice suggested that therefore it couldn\xe2\x80\x99t be maintained\xe2\x80\x9d). Not surprisingly, both before and after Sosa, courts have consistently rejected the notion that the ATS is categorically barred from extraterritorial application. See, e.g., 643 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1025 (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]o court to our knowledge has ever held that it doesn\xe2\x80\x99t apply extraterritorially\xe2\x80\x9d); Sarei v. Rio Tinto, PLC, 671 F.\xc2\xa03d 736, 747 (CA9 2011) (en banc) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe therefore conclude that the ATS is not limited to conduct occurring within the United States\xe2\x80\x9d); Doe v. Exxon Mobil Corp., 654 F.\xc2\xa03d 11, 20 (CADC 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e hold that there is no extraterritoriality bar\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApplication of the statute in the way I have suggested is consistent with international law and foreign practice. Nations have long been obliged not to provide safe harbors for their own nationals who commit such serious crimes abroad. See E. de Vattel, Law of Nations, Book\xc2\xa0II, p.\xc2\xa0163 (\xc2\xa776) (\xe2\x80\x9cpretty generally observed\xe2\x80\x9d practice in \xe2\x80\x9crespect to great crimes, which are equally contrary to the laws and safety of all nations,\xe2\x80\x9d that a sovereign should not \xe2\x80\x9csuffer his subjects to molest the subjects of other states, or to do them an injury,\xe2\x80\x9d but should \xe2\x80\x9ccompel the transgressor to make reparation for the damage or injury,\xe2\x80\x9d or be \xe2\x80\x9cdeliver[ed] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. up to the offended state, to be there brought to justice\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMany countries permit foreign plaintiffs to bring suits against their own nationals based on unlawful conduct that took place abroad. See, e.g., Brief for Government of the Kingdom of the Netherlands et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 19\xe2\x80\x9323 (hereinafter Netherlands Brief) (citing inter alia Guerrero v. Monterrico Metals PLc [2009] EWHC (QB) 2475 (Eng.) (attacking conduct of U.\xc2\xa0K. companies in Peru); Lubbe and Others v. Cape PLc [2000] UKHL 41 (attacking conduct of U. K. companies in South Africa); Rb. Gravenhage [Court of the Hague], 30 December 2009, JOR 2010, 41 m.nt. Mr. RGJ de Haan (Oguro/Royal Dutch Shell PLC) (Neth.) (attacking conduct of Dutch respondent in Nigeria)). See also Brief for European Commission as Amicus Curiae 11 (It is \xe2\x80\x9cuncontroversial\xe2\x80\x9d that the \xe2\x80\x9cUnited States may .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. exercise jurisdiction over ATS claims involving conduct committed by its own nationals within the terri-tory of another sovereign, consistent with international law\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOther countries permit some form of lawsuit brought by a foreign national against a foreign national, based upon conduct taking place abroad and seeking damages. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, the jurisdictional approach that I would use is analogous to, and consistent with, the approaches of a number of other nations. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApplying these jurisdictional principles to this case, however, I agree with the Court that jurisdiction does not lie. The defendants are two foreign corporations. Their shares, like those of many foreign corporations, are traded on the New York Stock Exchange. Their only presence in the United States consists of an office in New York City (actually owned by a separate but affiliated company) that helps to explain their business to potential investors. See Supp. Brief for Petitioners 4, n. 3 (citing Wiwa v. Royal Dutch Petroleum Co., 226 F. 3d 88, 94 (CA2 2000)); App. 55. The plaintiffs are not United States nationals but nationals of other nations. The conduct at issue took place abroad. And the plaintiffs allege, not that the defendants directly engaged in acts of torture, genocide, or the equivalent, but that they helped others (who are not American nationals) to do so.\n\t\t\t
