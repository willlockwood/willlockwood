KOKESH v. SECURITIES AND EXCHANGE COMMISSION

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x93529.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 18, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 5, 2017

\n

The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC or Commission) possesses authority to investigate violations of federal securities laws and to commence enforcement actions in federal district court if its investigations uncover evidence of wrongdoing. Initially, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authority in enforcement actions was limited to seeking an injunction barring future violations. Beginning in the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s, federal district courts, at the request of the Commission, began ordering disgorgement in SEC enforcement proceedings. Although Congress has since authorized the Commission to seek monetary civil penalties, the Commission has continued to seek disgorgement. This Court has held that\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72462, which establishes a 5-year limitations period for \xe2\x80\x9can action, suit or proceeding for the enforcement of any civil fine, penalty, or forfeiture,\xe2\x80\x9d applies when the Commission seeks monetary civil penalties. See Gabelli v. SEC,\n\n568 U.\xc2\xa0S. 442\n.

\n

In 2009, the Commission brought an enforcement action, alleging that petitioner Charles Kokesh violated various securities laws by concealing the misappropriation of $34.9 million from four business-development companies from 1995 to 2009. The Commission sought monetary civil penalties, disgorgement, and an injunction barring Kokesh from future violations. After a jury found that Kokesh\xe2\x80\x99s actions violated several securities laws, the District Court determined that \xc2\xa72462\xe2\x80\x99s 5-year limitations period applied to the monetary civil penalties. With respect to the $34.9 million disgorgement judgment, however, the court concluded that \xc2\xa72462 did not apply because disgorgement is not a \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the statute. The Tenth Circuit affirmed, holding that disgorgement was neither a penalty nor a forfeiture.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Because SEC disgorgement operates as a penalty under \xc2\xa72462, any claim for disgorgement in an SEC enforcement action must be commenced within five years of the date the claim accrued. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The definition of \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty\xe2\x80\x9d as a \xe2\x80\x9cpunishment, whether corporal or pecuniary, imposed and enforced by the State, for a crime or offen[s]e against its laws,\xe2\x80\x9d Huntington v. Attrill,\n\n146 U.\xc2\xa0S. 657\n, gives rise to two principles. First, whether a sanction represents a penalty turns in part on \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the wrong sought to be redressed is a wrong to the public, or a wrong to the individual.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 668. Second, a pecuniary sanction operates as a penalty if it is sought \xe2\x80\x9cfor the purpose of punishment, and to deter others from offending in like manner\xe2\x80\x9d rather than to compensate victims. Ibid. This Court has applied these principles in construing the term \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty,\xe2\x80\x9d holding, e.g., that a statute providing a compensatory remedy for a private wrong did not impose a \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty,\xe2\x80\x9d Brady v. Daly,\n\n175 U.\xc2\xa0S. 148\n. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The application of these principles here readily demonstrates that SEC disgorgement constitutes a penalty within the meaning of \xc2\xa72462. First, SEC disgorgement is imposed by the courts as a consequence for violating public laws, i.e., a violation committed against the United States rather than an aggrieved individual. Second, SEC disgorgement is imposed for punitive purposes. Sanctions imposed for the purpose of deterring infractions of public laws are inherently punitive because \xe2\x80\x9cdeterrence [is] not [a] legitimate nonpunitive governmental objectiv[e].\xe2\x80\x9d Bell v. Wolfish,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 520\n, n.\xc2\xa020. Finally, SEC disgorgement is often not compensatory. Disgorged profits are paid to the district courts, which have discretion to determine how the money will be distributed. They may distribute the funds to victims, but no statute commands them to do so. When an individual is made to pay a noncompensatory sanction to the government as a consequence of a legal violation, the payment operates as a penalty. See Porter v. Warner Holding Co.,\n\n328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 395\n. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The Government responds that SEC disgorgement is not punitive but a remedial sanction that operates to restore the status quo. It is not clear, however, that disgorgement simply returns the defendant to the place he would have occupied had he not broken the law. It sometimes exceeds the profits gained as a result of the violation. And, as demonstrated here, SEC disgorgement may be ordered without consideration of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s expenses that reduced the amount of illegal profit. In such cases, disgorgement does not simply restore the status quo; it leaves the defendant worse off and is therefore punitive. Although disgorgement may serve compensatory goals in some cases, \xe2\x80\x9csanctions frequently serve more than one purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Austin v. United States,\n\n509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 602\n. Because they \xe2\x80\x9cgo beyond compensation, are intended to punish, and label defendants wrongdoers\xe2\x80\x9d as a consequence of violating public laws, Gabelli, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 451\xe2\x80\x93452, disgorgement orders represent a penalty and fall within \xc2\xa72462\xe2\x80\x99s 5-year limitations period. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n

CHARLES R. KOKESH, PETITIONER v. SECURITIES AND EXCHANGE COMMISSION

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

[June 5, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

A 5-year statute of limitations applies to any \xe2\x80\x9caction, suit or proceeding for the enforcement of any civil fine, penalty, or forfeiture, pecuniary or otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72462. This case presents the question whether \xc2\xa72462 applies to claims for disgorgement imposed as a sanction for violating a federal securities law. The Court holds that it does. Disgorgement in the securities-enforcement context is a \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of \xc2\xa72462, and so disgorgement actions must be commenced within five years of the date the claim accrues.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

After rampant abuses in the securities industry led to the 1929 stock market crash and the Great Depression, Congress enacted a series of laws to ensure that \xe2\x80\x9cthe highest ethical standards prevail in every facet of the securities industry.\xe2\x80\x9d[1] SEC v. Capital Gains Research Bureau, Inc.,\n\n375 U.\xc2\xa0S. 180\n\xe2\x80\x93187 (1963) (internal quotation marks omitted). The second in the series\xe2\x80\x94the Securities Exchange Act of 1934\xe2\x80\x94established the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC or Commission) to enforce federal securities laws. Congress granted the Com-mission power to prescribe \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98rules and regulations .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as necessary or appropriate in the public interest or for the protection of investors.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Blue Chip Stamps v. Manor Drug Stores,\n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 723,\n728 (1975)\n. In addition to rulemaking, Congress vested the Commission with \xe2\x80\x9cbroad authority to conduct investigations into possible violations of the federal securities laws.\xe2\x80\x9d SEC v. Jerry T. O\xe2\x80\x99Brien, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 735,\n741 (1984)\n. If an investigation uncovers evidence of wrongdoing, the Commission may initiate enforcement actions in federal district court.

\n

Initially, the only statutory remedy available to the SEC in an enforcement action was an injunction barring future violations of securities laws. See 1 T. Hazen, Law of Securities Regulation \xc2\xa71:37 (7th ed., rev. 2016). In the absence of statutory authorization for monetary remedies, the Commission urged courts to order disgorgement as an exercise of their \xe2\x80\x9cinherent equity power to grant relief ancillary to an injunction.\xe2\x80\x9d SEC v. Texas Gulf Sulphur Co., 312 F. Supp. 77, 91 (SDNY 1970), aff\xe2\x80\x99d in part and rev\xe2\x80\x99d in part, 446 F.\xc2\xa02d 1301 (CA2 1971). Generally, disgorgement is a form of \xe2\x80\x9c[r]estitution measured by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s wrongful gain.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Third) of Restitution and Unjust Enrichment \xc2\xa751, Comment a, p. 204 (2010) (Restatement (Third)). Disgorgement requires that the defendant give up \xe2\x80\x9cthose gains .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. properly attribut-able to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s interference with the claimant\xe2\x80\x99s legally protected rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Beginning in the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s, courts ordered disgorgement in SEC enforcement proceedings in order to \xe2\x80\x9cdeprive .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. defendants of their profits in order to remove any monetary reward for violating\xe2\x80\x9d securities laws and to \xe2\x80\x9cprotect the investing public by providing an effective deterrent to future violations.\xe2\x80\x9d Texas Gulf, 312 F. Supp., at 92.

\n

In 1990, as part of the Securities Enforcement Remedies and Penny Stock Reform Act, Congress authorized the Commission to seek monetary civil penalties.\n\n104Stat.\n932, codified at\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa777t(d). The Act left the Commission with a full panoply of enforcement tools: It may promulgate rules, investigate violations of those rules and the securities laws generally, and seek monetary penal-ties and injunctive relief for those violations. In the years since the Act, however, the Commission has con-tinued its practice of seeking disgorgement in enforcement proceedings.

\n

This Court has already held that the 5-year statute of limitations set forth in\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72462 applies when the Commission seeks statutory monetary penalties. See Gabelli v. SEC,\n\n568 U.\xc2\xa0S. 442,\n454 (2013)\n. The question here is whether \xc2\xa72462, which applies to any \xe2\x80\x9caction, suit or proceeding for the enforcement of any civil fine, penalty, or forfeiture, pecuniary or otherwise,\xe2\x80\x9d also applies when the SEC seeks disgorgement.

\n

B

\n

Charles Kokesh owned two investment-adviser firms that provided investment advice to business-development companies. In late 2009, the Commission commenced an enforcement action in Federal District Court alleging that between 1995 and 2009, Kokesh, through his firms, misappropriated $34.9 million from four of those development companies. The Commission further alleged that, in order to conceal the misappropriation, Kokesh caused the filing of false and misleading SEC reports and proxy statements. The Commission sought civil monetary penalties, disgorgement, and an injunction barring Kokesh from violating securities laws in the future.

\n

After a 5-day trial, a jury found that Kokesh\xe2\x80\x99s actions violated the Investment Company Act of 1940,\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa780a\xe2\x80\x9336; the Investment Advisers Act of 1940, 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa780b\xe2\x80\x935, 80b\xe2\x80\x936; and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778m, 78n. The District Court then turned to the task of imposing penalties sought by the Commission. As to the civil monetary penalties, the District Court determined that \xc2\xa72462\xe2\x80\x99s 5-year limitations period pre-cluded any penalties for misappropriation occurring prior to October 27, 2004\xe2\x80\x94that is, five years prior to the date the Commission filed the complaint. App. to Pet. for Cert. 26a. The court ordered Kokesh to pay a civil penalty of $2,354,593, which represented \xe2\x80\x9cthe amount of funds that [Kokesh] himself received during the limitations period.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 31a\xe2\x80\x9332a. Regarding the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s request for a $34.9 million disgorgement judgment\xe2\x80\x94$29.9 million of which resulted from violations outside the limitations period\xe2\x80\x94the court agreed with the Commission that because disgorgement is not a \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of \xc2\xa72462, no limitations period applied. The court therefore entered a disgorgement judgment in the amount of $34.9 million and ordered Kokesh to pay an additional $18.1 million in prejudgment interest.

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit affirmed. 834 F.\xc2\xa03d 1158 (2016). It agreed with the District Court that disgorgement is not a penalty, and further found that disgorgement is not a forfeiture. Id., at 1164\xe2\x80\x931167. The court thus concluded that the statute of limitations in \xc2\xa72462 does not apply to SEC disgorgement claims.

\n

This Court granted certiorari, 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017), to resolve disagreement among the Circuits over whether disgorgement claims in SEC proceedings are subject to the 5-year limitations period of \xc2\xa72462.[2]

\n

II

\n

Statutes of limitations \xe2\x80\x9cse[t] a fixed date when exposure to the specified Government enforcement efforts en[d].\xe2\x80\x9d Gabelli, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 448. Such limits are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98vital to the welfare of society\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and rest on the principle that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98even wrongdoers are entitled to assume that their sins may be forgotten.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 449. The statute of limitations at issue here\xe2\x80\x94\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72462\xe2\x80\x94finds its roots in a law enacted nearly two centuries ago. 568 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 445. In its current form, \xc2\xa72462 establishes a 5-year limitations period for \xe2\x80\x9can action, suit or proceeding for the enforcement of any civil fine, penalty, or forfeiture.\xe2\x80\x9d This limitations period applies here if SEC disgorgement qualifies as either a fine, penalty, or forfeiture. We hold that SEC disgorgement constitutes a penalty.[3]

\n

A

\n

A \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty\xe2\x80\x9d is a \xe2\x80\x9cpunishment, whether corporal or pecuniary, imposed and enforced by the State, for a crime or offen[s]e against its laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Huntington v. Attrill,\n\n146 U.\xc2\xa0S. 657,\n667 (1892)\n. This definition gives rise to two principles. First, whether a sanction represents a penalty turns in part on \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the wrong sought to be redressed is a wrong to the public, or a wrong to the individual.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 668. Although statutes creating private causes of action against wrongdoers may appear\xe2\x80\x94or even be labeled\xe2\x80\x94penal, in many cases \xe2\x80\x9cneither the liability imposed nor the remedy given is strictly penal.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 667. This is because \xe2\x80\x9c[p]enal laws, strictly and properly, are those imposing punishment for an offense committed against the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Second, a pecuniary sanction operates as a penalty only if it is sought \xe2\x80\x9cfor the purpose of punishment, and to deter others from offending in like manner\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94as opposed to compensating a victim for his loss. Id., at 668.

\n

The Court has applied these principles in construing the term \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty.\xe2\x80\x9d In Brady v. Daly,\n\n175 U.\xc2\xa0S. 148 (1899)\n, for example, a playwright sued a defendant in Federal Circuit Court under a statute providing that copyright infringers \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98shall be liable for damages .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not less than one hundred dollars for the first [act of infringement], and fifty dollars for every subsequent performance, as to the court shall appear to be just.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 153. The defendant argued that the Circuit Court lacked jurisdiction on the ground that a separate statute vested district courts with exclusive jurisdiction over actions \xe2\x80\x9cto recover a penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 152. To determine whether the statutory damages represented a penalty, this Court noted first that the statute provided \xe2\x80\x9cfor a recovery of damages for an act which violates the rights of the plaintiff, and gives the right of action solely to him\xe2\x80\x9d rather than the public generally, and second, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe whole recovery is given to the proprietor, and the statute does not provide for a recovery by any other person.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 154, 156. By providing a compensatory remedy for a private wrong, the Court held, the statute did not impose a \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 154.

\n

Similarly, in construing the statutory ancestor of \xc2\xa72462, the Court utilized the same principles. In Meeker v. Lehigh Valley R. Co.,\n\n236 U.\xc2\xa0S. 412\n\xe2\x80\x93422 (1915), the Interstate Commerce Commission, a now-defunct federal agency charged with regulating railroads, ordered a railroad company to refund and pay damages to a shipping company for excessive shipping rates. The railroad company argued that the action was barred by Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71047, Comp. Stat. 1913, \xc2\xa71712 (now\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72462), which imposed a 5-year limitations period upon any \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98suit or prosecution for a penalty or forfeiture, pecuniary or otherwise, accruing under the laws of the United States.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 236 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 423. The Court rejected that argument, reasoning that \xe2\x80\x9cthe words \xe2\x80\x98penalty or forfeiture\xe2\x80\x99 in [the statute] refer to something imposed in a punitive way for an infraction of a public law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. A penalty, the Court held, does \xe2\x80\x9cnot include a liability imposed [solely] for the purpose of redressing a private injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Because the liability imposed was compensatory and paid entirely to a private plaintiff, it was not a \xe2\x80\x9cpenalty\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the statute of limitations. Ibid.; see also Gabelli, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 451\xe2\x80\x93452 (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]enalties\xe2\x80\x9d in the context of \xc2\xa72462 \xe2\x80\x9cgo beyond compensation, are intended to punish, and label defendants wrongdoers\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

Application of the foregoing principles readily demonstrates that SEC disgorgement constitutes a penalty within the meaning of \xc2\xa72462.

\n

First, SEC disgorgement is imposed by the courts as a consequence for violating what we described in Meeker as public laws. The violation for which the remedy is sought is committed against the United States rather than an aggrieved individual\xe2\x80\x94this is why, for example, a securities-enforcement action may proceed even if victims do not support or are not parties to the prosecution. As the Government concedes, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen the SEC seeks disgorgement, it acts in the public interest, to remedy harm to the public at large, rather than standing in the shoes of particular injured parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 22. Courts agree. See, e.g., SEC v. Rind, 991 F.\xc2\xa02d 1486, 1491 (CA9 1993) (\xe2\x80\x9c[D]isgorgement actions further the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s public policy mission of protecting investors and safeguarding the integrity of the markets\xe2\x80\x9d); SEC v. Teo, 746 F.\xc2\xa03d 90, 102 (CA3 2014) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he SEC pursues [disgorgement] \xe2\x80\x98independent of the claims of individual investors\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in order to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98promot[e] economic and social policies\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Second, SEC disgorgement is imposed for punitive purposes. In Texas Gulf\xe2\x80\x94one of the first cases requiring disgorgement in SEC proceedings\xe2\x80\x94the court emphasized the need \xe2\x80\x9cto deprive the defendants of their profits in order to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. protect the investing public by providing an effective deterrent to future violations.\xe2\x80\x9d 312 F. Supp., at 92. In the years since, it has become clear that deterrence is not simply an incidental effect of disgorgement. Rather, courts have consistently held that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he primary purpose of disgorgement orders is to deter violations of the securities laws by depriving violators of their ill-gotten gains.\xe2\x80\x9d SEC v. Fischbach Corp., 133 F.\xc2\xa03d 170, 175 (CA2 1997); see also SEC v. First Jersey Securities, Inc., 101 F.\xc2\xa03d 1450, 1474 (CA2 1996) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe primary purpose of disgorgement as a remedy for violation of the securities laws is to deprive violators of their ill-gotten gains, thereby effectuating the deterrence objectives of those laws\xe2\x80\x9d); Rind, 991 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1491 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The deterrent effect of [an SEC] enforcement action would be greatly undermined if securities law violators were not required to disgorge illicit profits\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Sanctions imposed for the purpose of deterring infractions of public laws are inherently punitive because \xe2\x80\x9cdeterrence [is] not [a] legitimate nonpunitive governmental objectiv[e].\xe2\x80\x9d Bell v. Wolfish,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 520,\n539, n.\n20 (1979)\n; see also United States v. Bajakajian,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 321,\n329 (1998)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cDeterrence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. has traditionally been viewed as a goal of punishment\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Finally, in many cases, SEC disgorgement is not compensatory. As courts and the Government have employed the remedy, disgorged profits are paid to the district court, and it is \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to determine how and to whom the money will be distributed.\xe2\x80\x9d Fischbach Corp., 133 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 175. Courts have required disgorgement \xe2\x80\x9cregardless of whether the disgorged funds will be paid to such investors as restitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 176; see id., at 175 (\xe2\x80\x9cAlthough disgorged funds may often go to compensate securities fraud victims for their losses, such compensation is a distinctly secondary goal\xe2\x80\x9d). Some disgorged funds are paid to victims; other funds are dispersed to the United States Treasury. See, e.g., id., at 171 (affirming distribution of disgorged funds to Treasury where \xe2\x80\x9cno party before the court was entitled to the funds and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the persons who might have equitable claims were too dispersed for feasible identification and payment\xe2\x80\x9d); SEC v. Lund, 570 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 1397, 1404\xe2\x80\x931405 (CD Cal. 1983) (ordering disgorgement and directing trustee to disperse funds to victims if \xe2\x80\x9cfeasible\xe2\x80\x9d and to disperse any remaining money to the Treasury). Even though district courts may distribute the funds to the victims, they have not identified any statutory command that they do so. When an individual is made to pay a noncompensatory sanction to the Government as a consequence of a legal violation, the payment operates as a penalty. See Porter v. Warner Holding Co.,\n\n328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 395,\n402 (1946)\n (distinguishing between restitution paid to an aggrieved party and penalties paid to the Government).

\n

SEC disgorgement thus bears all the hallmarks of a penalty: It is imposed as a consequence of violating a public law and it is intended to deter, not to compensate. The 5-year statute of limitations in \xc2\xa72462 therefore applies when the SEC seeks disgorgement.

\n

C

\n

The Government\xe2\x80\x99s primary response to all of this is that SEC disgorgement is not punitive but \xe2\x80\x9cremedial\xe2\x80\x9d in that it \xe2\x80\x9clessen[s] the effects of a violation\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98restor[ing] the status quo.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 17. As an initial matter, it is not clear that disgorgement, as courts have applied it in the SEC enforcement context, simply returns the defendant to the place he would have occupied had he not broken the law. SEC disgorgement sometimes exceeds the profits gained as a result of the violation. Thus, for example, \xe2\x80\x9can insider trader may be ordered to disgorge not only the unlawful gains that accrue to the wrongdoer directly, but also the benefit that accrues to third parties whose gains can be attributed to the wrongdoer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d SEC v. Contorinis, 743 F.\xc2\xa03d 296, 302 (CA2 2014). Individuals who illegally provide confidential trading information have been forced to disgorge profits gained by individuals who received and traded based on that information\xe2\x80\x94even though they never received any profits. Ibid; see also SEC v. Warde, 151 F.\xc2\xa03d 42, 49 (CA2 1998) (\xe2\x80\x9cA tippee\xe2\x80\x99s gains are attributable to the tipper, regardless whether benefit accrues to the tipper\xe2\x80\x9d); SEC v. Clark, 915 F.\xc2\xa02d 439, 454 (CA9 1990) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is well settled that a tipper can be required to disgorge his tippees\xe2\x80\x99 profits\xe2\x80\x9d). And, as demonstrated by this case, SEC disgorgement sometimes is ordered without consideration of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s expenses that reduced the amount of illegal profit. App. to Pet. for Cert. 43a; see Restatement (Third) \xc2\xa751, Comment h, at 216 (\xe2\x80\x9cAs a general rule, the defendant is entitled to a deduction for all marginal costs incurred in producing the revenues that are subject to disgorgement. Denial of an otherwise appropriate deduction, by making the defendant liable in excess of net gains, results in a punitive sanction that the law of restitution normally attempts to avoid\xe2\x80\x9d). In such cases, disgorgement does not simply restore the status quo; it leaves the defendant worse off. The justification for this practice given by the court below demonstrates that disgorgement in this context is a punitive, rather than a remedial, sanction: Disgorgement, that court explained, is intended not only to \xe2\x80\x9cprevent the wrongdoer\xe2\x80\x99s unjust enrichment\xe2\x80\x9d but also \xe2\x80\x9cto deter others\xe2\x80\x99 violations of the securities laws.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 43a.

\n

True, disgorgement serves compensatory goals in some cases; however, we have emphasized \xe2\x80\x9cthe fact that sanctions frequently serve more than one purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Austin v. United States,\n\n509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 602,\n610 (1993)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98A civil sanction that cannot fairly be said solely to serve a remedial purpose, but rather can only be explained as also serving either retributive or deterrent purposes, is punishment, as we have come to understand the term.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 621; cf. Bajakajian, 524 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 331, n. 6 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] modern statutory forfeiture is a \xe2\x80\x98fine\xe2\x80\x99 for\n \nEighth Amendment purposes if it constitutes punishment even in part\xe2\x80\x9d). Because disgorgement orders \xe2\x80\x9cgo beyond compensation, are intended to punish, and label defendants wrongdoers\xe2\x80\x9d as a consequence of violating public laws, Gabelli, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 451\xe2\x80\x93452, they represent a penalty and thus fall within the 5-year statute of limitations of \xc2\xa72462.

\n

III

\n

Disgorgement, as it is applied in SEC enforcement proceedings, operates as a penalty under \xc2\xa72462. Accordingly, any claim for disgorgement in an SEC enforcement action must be commenced within five years of the date the claim accrued.

\n

The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.
