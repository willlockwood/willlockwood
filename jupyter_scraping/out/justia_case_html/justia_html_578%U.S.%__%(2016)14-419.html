LUIS v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93419.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 10, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 30, 2016

\n

A federal statute provides that a court may freeze before trial certain assets belonging to a defendant accused of violations of federal health care or banking laws. Those assets include (1) property \xe2\x80\x9cobtained as a result of\xe2\x80\x9d the crime, (2) property \xe2\x80\x9ctraceable\xe2\x80\x9d to the crime, and (3), as relevant here, other \xe2\x80\x9cproperty of equivalent value.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71345(a)(2). The Government has charged petitioner Luis with fraudulently obtaining nearly $45 million through crimes related to health care. In order to preserve the $2 million remaining in Luis\xe2\x80\x99 possession for payment of restitution and other criminal penalties, the Government secured a pretrial order prohibiting Luis from dissipating her assets, including assets unrelated to her alleged crimes. Though the District Court recognized that the order might prevent Luis from obtaining counsel of her choice, it held that the\n \nSixth Amendment did not give her the right to use her own untainted funds for that purpose. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is vacated, and the case is remanded.

\n

564 Fed. Appx. 493, vacated and remanded.

\n

Justice Breyer, joined by The Chief Justice, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Sotomayor, concluded that the pretrial restraint of legitimate, untainted assets needed to retain counsel of choice violates the\n \nSixth Amendment. The nature and importance of the constitutional right taken together with the nature of the assets lead to this conclusion. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel grants a defendant \xe2\x80\x9ca fair opportunity to secure counsel of his own choice,\xe2\x80\x9d Powell v. Alabama,\n\n287 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45\n, that he \xe2\x80\x9ccan afford to hire,\xe2\x80\x9d Caplin & Drysdale, Chartered v. United States,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 617\n. This Court has consistently referred to the right to counsel of choice as \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0While the Government does not deny Luis\xe2\x80\x99 fundamental right to be represented by a qualified attorney whom she chooses and can afford to hire, it would nonetheless undermine the value of that right by taking from Luis the ability to use funds she needs to pay for her chosen attorney. The Government attempts to justify this consequence by pointing out that there are important interests on the other side of the legal equation. It wishes to guarantee that funds will be available later to help pay for statutory penalties and restitution, for example. The Government further argues that two previous cases from this Court, Caplin & Drysdale, supra, at 619, and United States v. Monsanto,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600\n, support the issuance of a restraining order in this case. However, the nature of the assets at issue here differs from the assets at issue in those earlier cases. And that distinction makes a difference. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0Here, the property is untainted, i.e., it belongs to Luis. As described in Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto, the Government may well be able to freeze before trial \xe2\x80\x9ctainted\xe2\x80\x9d assets\xe2\x80\x94e.g., loot, contraband, or property otherwise associated with the planning, implementing, or concealing of a crime. As a matter of property law, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s ownership interest in such property is imperfect. For example, a different federal statute provides that title to property used to commit a crime (or otherwise \xe2\x80\x9ctraceable\xe2\x80\x9d to a crime) passes to the Government at the instant the crime is planned or committed. See\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7853(c). But here, the Government seeks to impose restrictions upon Luis\xe2\x80\x99 untainted property without any showing of any equivalent governmental interest in that property. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0This distinction does not by itself answer the constitutional question because the law of property may allow a person without a present interest in a piece of property to impose restrictions upon a current owner, say, to prevent waste. However, insofar as innocent funds are needed to obtain counsel of choice, the\n \nSixth Amendment prohibits the court order sought here.

\n

Three basic considerations lead to this conclusion. First, the nature of the competing interests argues against this kind of court order. On the one side is a fundamental\n \nSixth Amendment right to assistance of counsel. On the other side is the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in securing its punishment of choice, as well as the victim\xe2\x80\x99s interest in securing restitution. These latter interests are important, but\xe2\x80\x94compared to the right to counsel\xe2\x80\x94they seem to lie somewhat further from the heart of a fair, effective criminal justice system. Second, relevant, common-law legal tradition offers virtually no significant support for the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position and in fact argues to the contrary. Indeed, there appears to be no decision of this Court authorizing unfettered, pretrial forfeiture of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own \xe2\x80\x9cinnocent\xe2\x80\x9d property. Third, as a practical matter, accepting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position could erode the right to counsel considerably. It would, in fact, unleash a principle of constitutional law with no obvious stopping place, as Congress could write more statutes authorizing restraints in other cases involving illegal behavior that come with steep financial consequences. These defendants, often rendered indigent, would fall back upon publicly paid counsel, including overworked and underpaid public defenders. The upshot is a substantial risk that accepting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s views would render less effective the basic right the\n \nSixth Amendment seeks to protect. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

(3)\xc2\xa0The constitutional line between a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s tainted funds and innocent funds needed to pay for counsel should prove workable. Money may be fungible, but courts, which use tracing rules in cases of, e.g., fraud and pension rights, have experience separating tainted assets from untainted assets, just as they have experience determining how much money is needed to cover the costs of a lawyer. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

Justice Thomas concluded that the rule that a pretrial freeze of untainted assets violates a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel of choice rests strictly on the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s text and common-law backdrop. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The\n \nSixth Amendment abolished the common-law rule that generally prohibited representation in felony cases. \xe2\x80\x9cThe right to select counsel of one\xe2\x80\x99s choice\xe2\x80\x9d is thus \xe2\x80\x9cthe root meaning\xe2\x80\x9d of the\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel. United States v. Gonzalez-Lopez,\n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 140\n\xe2\x80\x93148. Constitutional rights protect the necessary prerequisites for their exercise. As a result, the\n \nSixth Amendment denies the Government unchecked power to freeze a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets before trial simply to secure potential forfeiture upon conviction. Unless the right to counsel protects the right to use lawfully owned property to pay for an attorney, the right to counsel\xe2\x80\x94originally understood to protect only the right to hire counsel of choice\xe2\x80\x94would be meaningless. Without pretrial protection for at least some of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets, the Government could nullify the right to counsel of choice, eviscerating the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s original meaning and purpose. The modern, judicially created right to government-appointed counsel does not obviate these concerns. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0History confirms this textual understanding. The common-law forfeiture tradition provides an administrable rule for the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection: A criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s untainted assets are protected from government interference before trial and judgment, but his tainted assets may be seized before trial as contraband or through a separate in\xc2\xa0rem proceeding. Reading the\n \nSixth Amendment to track the historical line between tainted and untainted assets avoids case-by-case adjudication and ensures that the original meaning of the right to counsel does real work. Here, the incursion of the pretrial asset freeze into untainted assets, for which there is no historical tradition, violates the\n \nSixth Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0This conclusion leaves no room for an atextual balancing analysis. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

Thomas, J., filed an opinion concurring in the judgment. Kennedy, J., filed a dissenting opinion, in which Alito, J., joined. Kagan, J., filed a dissenting opinion.

SILA LUIS, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[March 30, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Breyer announced the judgment of the Court and delivered an opinion in which The Chief Justice, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Sotomayor join.

\n

A federal statute provides that a court may freeze before trial certain assets belonging to a criminal defendant accused of violations of federal health care or banking laws. See\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71345. Those assets include: (1) property \xe2\x80\x9cobtained as a result of\xe2\x80\x9d the crime, (2) property \xe2\x80\x9ctraceable\xe2\x80\x9d to the crime, and (3) other \xe2\x80\x9cproperty of equivalent value.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71345(a)(2). In this case, the Government has obtained a court order that freezes assets belonging to the third category of property, namely, property that is untainted by the crime, and that belongs fully to the defendant. That order, the defendant says, prevents her from paying her lawyer. She claims that insofar as it does so, it violates her\n \nSixth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cright .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to have the Assistance of Counsel for [her] defence.\xe2\x80\x9d We agree.

\n

I

\n

In October 2012, a federal grand jury charged the petitioner, Sila Luis, with paying kickbacks, conspiring to commit fraud, and engaging in other crimes all related to health care. See \xc2\xa71349; \xc2\xa7371;\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71320a\xe2\x80\x937b(b)(2)(A). The Government claimed that Luis had fraudulently obtained close to $45 million, almost all of which she had already spent. Believing it would convict Luis of the crimes charged, and hoping to preserve the $2 million remaining in Luis\xe2\x80\x99 possession for payment of restitution and other criminal penalties (often referred to as criminal forfeitures, which can include innocent\xe2\x80\x94not just tainted\xe2\x80\x94assets, a point of critical importance here), the Government sought a pretrial order prohibiting Luis from dissipating her assets. See\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71345(a)(2). And the District Court ultimately issued an order prohibiting her from \xe2\x80\x9cdissipating, or otherwise disposing of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. assets, real or personal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. up to the equivalent value of the proceeds of the Federal health care fraud ($45 million).\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. A\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

The Government and Luis agree that this court order will prevent Luis from using her own untainted funds, i.e., funds not connected with the crime, to hire counsel to defend her in her criminal case. See App. 161 (stipulating \xe2\x80\x9cthat an unquantified amount of revenue not connected to the indictment [had] flowed into some of the accounts\xe2\x80\x9d subject to the restraining order); ibid. (similarly stipulating that Luis used \xe2\x80\x9crevenue not connected to the indictment\xe2\x80\x9d to pay for real property that she possessed). Al-though the District Court recognized that the order mightprevent Luis from obtaining counsel of her choice, it held \xe2\x80\x9cthat there is no\n \nSixth Amendment right to use untainted, substitute assets to hire counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d 966 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1321, 1334 (SD Fla. 2013).

\n

The Eleventh Circuit upheld the District Court. See 564 Fed. Appx. 493, 494 (2014) (\xc2\xa0per curiam) (referring to, e.g., Kaley v. United States, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014); Caplin & Drysdale, Chartered v. United States,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 617,\n631 (1989)\n; United States v. Monsanto,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600,\n616 (1989)\n). We granted Luis\xe2\x80\x99 petition for certiorari.

\n

II

\n

The question presented is \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether the pretrial restraint of a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate, untainted assets (those not traceable to a criminal offense) needed to retain counsel of choice violates the Fifth and\n \nSixth Amendments.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. ii. We see no reasonable way to interpret the relevant statutes to avoid answering this constitutional question. Cf. Monsanto, supra, at 614. Hence, we answer it, and our answer is that the pretrial restraint of legitimate, untainted assets needed to retain counsel of choice violates the\n \nSixth Amendment. The nature and importance of the constitutional right taken together with the nature of the assets lead us to thisconclusion.

\n

A

\n

No one doubts the fundamental character of a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment right to the \xe2\x80\x9cAssistance of Counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d In Gideon v. Wainwright,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335 (1963)\n, the Court explained:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The right to be heard would be, in many cases, of little avail if it did not comprehend the right to be heard by counsel. Even the intelligent and educated layman has small and sometimes no skill in the science of law. If charged with crime, he is incapable, generally, of determining for himself whether the indictment is good or bad. He is unfamiliar with the rules of evidence. Left without the aid of counsel he may be put on trial without a proper charge, and convicted upon incompetent evidence, or evidence irrelevant to the issue or otherwise inadmissible. He lacks both the skill and knowledge adequately to prepare his defense, even though he have a perfect one. He requires the guiding hand of counsel at every step in the proceedings against him. Without it, though he be not guilty, he faces the danger of conviction because he does not know how to establish his innocence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 344\xe2\x80\x93345 (quoting Powell v. Alabama,\n\n287 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45\n\xe2\x80\x9369 (1932)).

\n

It is consequently not surprising: first, that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinions often refer to the right to counsel as \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 68; see Grosjean v. American Press Co.,\n\n297 U.\xc2\xa0S. 233\n\xe2\x80\x93244 (1936) (similar); Johnson v. Zerbst,\n\n304 U.\xc2\xa0S. 458\n\xe2\x80\x93463 (1938) (similar); second, that commentators describe the right as a \xe2\x80\x9cgreat engin[e] by which an innocent man can make the truth of his innocence visible,\xe2\x80\x9d Amar,\n \nSixth Amendment First Principles, 84 Geo. L.\xc2\xa0J. 641, 643 (1996); see Herring v. New York,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 853,\n862 (1975)\n; third, that we have understood the right to require that the Government provide counsel for an indigent defendant accused of all but the least serious crimes, see Gideon, supra, at 344; and fourth, that we have considered the wrongful deprivation of the right to counsel a \xe2\x80\x9cstructural\xe2\x80\x9d error that so \xe2\x80\x9caffec[ts] the framework within which the trial proceeds\xe2\x80\x9d that courts may not even ask whether the error harmed the defendant. United States v. Gonzalez-Lopez,\n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 140,\n148 (2006)\n (internal quotation marks omitted); see id., at 150.

\n

Given the necessarily close working relationship between lawyer and client, the need for confidence, and the critical importance of trust, neither is it surprising that the Court has held that the\n \nSixth Amendment grants a defendant \xe2\x80\x9ca fair opportunity to secure counsel of his own choice.\xe2\x80\x9d Powell, supra, at 53; see Gonzalez-Lopez, supra, at 150 (describing \xe2\x80\x9cthese myriad aspects of representation\xe2\x80\x9d). This \xe2\x80\x9cfair opportunity\xe2\x80\x9d for the defendant to secure counsel of choice has limits. A defendant has no right, for example, to an attorney who is not a member of the bar, or who has a conflict of interest due to a relationship with an opposing party. See Wheat v. United States,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153,\n159 (1988)\n. And an indigent defendant, while entitled to adequate representation, has no right to have the Government pay for his preferred representational choice. See Caplin & Drysdale, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 624.

\n

We nonetheless emphasize that the constitutional right at issue here is fundamental: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he\n \nSixth Amendment guarantees a defendant the right to be represented by an otherwise qualified attorney whom that defendant can afford to hire.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

B

\n

The Government cannot, and does not, deny Luis\xe2\x80\x99 right to be represented by a qualified attorney whom she chooses and can afford. But the Government would underminethe value of that right by taking from Luis the ability to use the funds she needs to pay for her chosen attorney. The Government points out that, while freezing the funds may have this consequence, there are important interests on the other side of the legal equation: It wishes to guarantee that those funds will be available later to help pay for statutory penalties (including forfeiture of untainted assets) and restitution, should it secure convictions. And it points to two cases from this Court, Caplin & Drysdale, supra, at 619, and Monsanto, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 615, which, in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view, hold that the\n \nSixth Amendment does not pose an obstacle to its doing so here. In our view, however, the nature of the assets at issue here differs from the assets at issue in those earlier cases. And that distinction makes a difference.

\n

1

\n

The relevant difference consists of the fact that the property here is untainted; i.e., it belongs to the defendant, pure and simple. In this respect it differs from a robber\xe2\x80\x99s loot, a drug seller\xe2\x80\x99s cocaine, a burglar\xe2\x80\x99s tools, or other property associated with the planning, implementing, or concealing of a crime. The Government may well be able to freeze, perhaps to seize, assets of the latter, \xe2\x80\x9ctainted\xe2\x80\x9d kind before trial. As a matter of property law the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s ownership interest is imperfect. The robber\xe2\x80\x99s loot belongs to the victim, not to the defendant. See Telegraph Co. v. Davenport,\n\n97 U.\xc2\xa0S. 369,\n372 (1878)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe great principle that no one can be deprived of his property without his assent, except by the processes of the law, requires .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the property wrongfully transferred or stolen should be restored to its rightful owner\xe2\x80\x9d). The cocaine is contraband, long considered forfeitable to the Government wherever found. See, e.g.,\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7881(a) (\xe2\x80\x9c[Controlled substances] shall be subject to forfeiture to the United States and no property right shall exist in them\xe2\x80\x9d); Carroll v. United States,\n\n267 U.\xc2\xa0S. 132,\n159 (1925)\n (describing the seizure of \xe2\x80\x9ccontraband forfeitable prop-erty\xe2\x80\x9d). And title to property used to commit a crime (orotherwise \xe2\x80\x9ctraceable\xe2\x80\x9d to a crime) often passes to the Government at the instant the crime is planned or committed. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7853(c) (providing that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s ownership interest in such property relates back to the time of the crime).

\n

The property at issue here, however, is not loot, contraband, or otherwise \xe2\x80\x9ctainted.\xe2\x80\x9d It belongs to the defendant. That fact undermines the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reliance upon precedent, for both Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto relied critically upon the fact that the property at issue was \xe2\x80\x9ctainted,\xe2\x80\x9d and that title to the property therefore had passed from the defendant to the Government before the court issued its order freezing (or otherwise disposing of\xc2\xa0) the assets.

\n

In Caplin & Drysdale, the Court considered a post-conviction forfeiture that took from a convicted defendant funds he would have used to pay his lawyer. The Court held that the forfeiture was constitutional. In doing so, however, it emphasized that the forfeiture statute at issue provided that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[a]ll right, title, and interest in property [constituting or derived from any proceeds obtained from the crime] vests in the United States upon the commission of the act giving rise to [the] forfeiture.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 625, n. 4 (quoting \xc2\xa7853(c)) (emphasis added). It added that the law had \xe2\x80\x9clong-recognized\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9clawful\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cpractice of vesting title to any forfeitable asset[s] in the United State[s] at the time of the crim[e].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 627. It pointed out that the defendant did not \xe2\x80\x9cclaim, as a general proposition, that the [vesting] provision is unconstitutional, or that Congress cannot, as a general matter, vest title to assets derived from the crime in the Government, as of the date of the criminal act in question.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 627\xe2\x80\x93628. And, given the vesting language, the Court explained that the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cdid not hold good title\xe2\x80\x9d to the property. Id., at 627. The Court therefore concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no constitutional principle that gives one person [namely, the defendant] the right to give another\xe2\x80\x99s [namely, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s] property to a third party,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, the lawyer. Id., at 628.

\n

In Monsanto, the Court considered a pretrial restraining order that prevented a not-yet-convicted defendant from using certain assets to pay for his lawyer. The defendant argued that, given this difference, Caplin & Drysdale\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion should not apply. The Court noted, however, that the property at issue was forfeitable under the same statute that was at issue in Caplin & Drysdale. See Monsanto, supra, at 614. And, as in Caplin & Drysdale, the application of that statute to Monsanto\xe2\x80\x99s case concerned only the pretrial restraint of assets that were traceable to the crime, see 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 602\xe2\x80\x93603; thus, the statute passed title to those funds at the time the crime was committed (i.e., before the trial), see \xc2\xa7853(c). The Court said that Caplin & Drysdale had already \xe2\x80\x9cweigh[ed] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. th[e] very interests\xe2\x80\x9d at issue. Monsanto, supra, at 616. And it \xe2\x80\x9crel[ied] on\xe2\x80\x9d its \xe2\x80\x9cconclusion\xe2\x80\x9d in Caplin & Drysdale to dispose of, and to reject, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csimilar constitutional claims.\xe2\x80\x9d 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 614.

\n

Justice Kennedy prefers to read Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto broadly, as holding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Government, having established probable cause to believe that Luis\xe2\x80\x99 substitute [i.e., innocent] assets will be forfeitable upon conviction, should be permitted to obtain a restraining order barring her from spending those funds prior to trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6\xe2\x80\x937 (dissenting opinion). In other words, he believes that those cases stand for the proposition that property\xe2\x80\x94whether tainted or untainted\xe2\x80\x94is subject to pretrial restraint, so long as the property might someday be subject to forfeiture. But this reading asks too much of our precedents. For one thing, as discussed, Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto involved the restraint only of tainted assets, and thus we had no occasion to opine in those cases about the constitutionality of pretrial restraints of other, untainted assets.

\n

For another thing, Justice Kennedy\xe2\x80\x99s broad rule ignores the statutory background against which Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto were decided. The Court in those cases referenced \xc2\xa7853(c) more than a dozen times. And it acknowledged that whether property is \xe2\x80\x9cforfeitable\xe2\x80\x9d or subject to pretrial restraint under Congress\xe2\x80\x99 scheme is a nuanced inquiry that very much depends on who has the superior interest in the property at issue. See Caplin & Drysdale, supra, at 626\xe2\x80\x93628; Monsanto, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 616. We see this in, for example, \xc2\xa7853(e)(1), which explicitly authorizes restraining orders or injunctions against \xe2\x80\x9cproperty described in subsection (a) of this section\xe2\x80\x9d (i.e., tainted assets). We see this too in \xc2\xa7853(e)(1)(B), which requires the Government\xe2\x80\x94in certain circumstances\xe2\x80\x94to give \xe2\x80\x9cnotice to persons appearing to have an interest in the property and opportunity for hearing\xe2\x80\x9d before obtaining a restraining order against such property. We see this in \xc2\xa7853(c), which allows \xe2\x80\x9cbona fide purchaser[s] for value\xe2\x80\x9d to keep property that would otherwise be subject to forfeiture. And we see this in \xc2\xa7853(n)(6)(A), which exempts certain property from forfeiture when a third party can show a vested interest in the property that is \xe2\x80\x9csuperior\xe2\x80\x9d to that of the Government.

\n

The distinction that we have discussed is thus an important one, not a technicality. It is the difference between what is yours and what is mine. In Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto, the Government wanted to impose restrictions upon (or seize) property that the Government had probable cause to believe was the proceeds of, or traceable to, a crime. See Monsanto, supra, at 615. The relevant statute said that the Government took title to those tainted assets as of the time of the crime. See \xc2\xa7853(c). And the defendants in those cases consequently had to concede that the disputed property was in an important sense the Government\xe2\x80\x99s at the time the court imposed the restrictions. See Caplin & Drysdale, supra, at 619\xe2\x80\x93620; Monsanto, supra, at 602\xe2\x80\x93603.

\n

This is not to say that the Government \xe2\x80\x9cowned\xe2\x80\x9d the tainted property outright (in the sense that it could take possession of the property even before obtaining a conviction). See post, at 7\xe2\x80\x9310 (Kennedy, J., dissenting). Rather, it is to say that the Government even before trial had a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d interest in the tainted property sufficient to justify the property\xe2\x80\x99s pretrial restraint. See Caplin & Drysdale, supra, at 627 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he property rights given the Government by virtue of [\xc2\xa7853(c)\xe2\x80\x99s relation-back provision] are more substantial than petitioner acknowledges\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Stowell,\n\n133 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n19 (1890)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cAs soon as [the possessor of the forfeitable asset committed the violation] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , the forfeiture .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. took effect, and (though needing judicial condemnation to perfect it) operated from that time as a statutory conveyance to the United States of all right, title and interest then remaining in the [possessor]; and was as valid and effectual, against all the world, as a recorded deed\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

If we analogize to bankruptcy law, the Government, by application of \xc2\xa7853(c)\xe2\x80\x99s relation-back provision, became something like a secured creditor with a lien on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s tainted assets superior to that of most any other party. See 4 Collier on Bankruptcy \xc2\xb6506.03[1] (16th ed. 2015). For this reason, \xc2\xa7853(c) has operated in our cases as a significant limitation on criminal defendants\xe2\x80\x99 prop-erty rights in such assets\xe2\x80\x94even before conviction. SeeMonsanto, supra, at 613 (\xe2\x80\x9cPermitting a defendant to use [tainted] assets for his private purposes that, under this [relation-back] provision, will become the property of the United States if a conviction occurs cannot be sanctioned\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Grupo Mexicano de Desarrollo, S.\xc2\xa0A. v. Alliance Bond Fund, Inc.,\n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 308,\n326 (1999)\n (noting that the Court had previously authorized injunctions against the further dissipation of property where, among other things, \xe2\x80\x9cthe creditor (the Government) asserted an equitable lien on the property\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Here, by contrast, the Government seeks to impose restrictions upon Luis\xe2\x80\x99 untainted property without any showing of any equivalent governmental interest in that property. Again, if this were a bankruptcy case, the Government would be at most an unsecured creditor. Al-though such creditors someday might collect from a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s general assets, they cannot be said to have any present claim to, or interest in, the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s property. See id., at 330 (\xe2\x80\x9c[B]efore judgment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an unsecured creditor has no rights at law or in equity in the property of his debtor\xe2\x80\x9d); see also 5 Collier on Bankruptcy \xc2\xb6541.05[1][b] (\xe2\x80\x9c[G]eneral unsecured creditor[s]\xe2\x80\x9d have \xe2\x80\x9cno specific property interest in the goods held or sold by the debtor\xe2\x80\x9d). The competing property interests in the tainted- and untainted-asset contexts therefore are not \xe2\x80\x9cexactly the same.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 2 (Kagan, J., dissenting). At least regarding her untainted assets, Luis can at this point reasonably claim that the property is still \xe2\x80\x9cmine,\xe2\x80\x9d free and clear.

\n

2

\n

This distinction between (1) what is primarily \xe2\x80\x9cmine\xe2\x80\x9d (the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s) and (2) what is primarily \xe2\x80\x9cyours\xe2\x80\x9d (the Government\xe2\x80\x99s) does not by itself answer the constitutional question posed, for the law of property sometimes allows a person without a present interest in a piece of property to impose restrictions upon a current owner, say, to prevent waste. A holder of a reversionary interest, for example, can prevent the owner of a life estate from wasting the property. See, e.g., Peterson v. Ferrell, 127 N.\xc2\xa0C. 169, 170, 37 S.\xc2\xa0E. 189, 190 (1900). Those who later may become beneficiaries of a trust are sometimes able to prevent the trustee from dissipating the trust\xe2\x80\x99s assets. See, e.g., Kollock v. Webb, 113 Ga. 762, 769, 39 S.\xc2\xa0E. 339, 343 (1901). And holders of a contingent, future executory interest in property (an interest that might become possessory at some point down the road) can, in limited circumstances, enjoin the activities of the current owner. See, e.g., Dees v. Cheuvronts, 240 Ill. 486, 491, 88 N.\xc2\xa0E. 1011, 1012 (1909) (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]quity w[ill] interfere .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. only when it is made to appear that the contingency .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is reasonably certain to happen, and the waste is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. wanton and conscienceless\xe2\x80\x9d). The Government here seeks a somewhat analogous order, i.e., an order that will preserve Luis\xe2\x80\x99 untainted assets so that they will be available to cover the costs of forfeiture and restitution if she is convicted, and if the court later determines that her tainted assets are insufficient or otherwise unavailable.

\n

The Government finds statutory authority for its request in language authorizing a court to enjoin a criminal defendant from, for example, disposing of innocent \xe2\x80\x9cproperty of equivalent value\xe2\x80\x9d to that of tainted property.\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71345(a)(2)(B)(i). But Luis needs some portion of those same funds to pay for the lawyer of her choice. Thus, the legal conflict arises. And, in our view, insofar as innocent (i.e., untainted) funds are needed to obtain counsel of choice, we believe that the\n \nSixth Amendment prohibits the court order that the Government seeks.

\n

Three basic considerations lead us to this conclusion. First, the nature of the competing interests argues against this kind of court order. On the one side we find, as we have previously explained, supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x935, a\n \nSixth Amendment right to assistance of counsel that is a fundamental constituent of due process of law, see Powell, 287 U.\xc2\xa0S.,at 68\xe2\x80\x9369. And that right includes \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to be represented by an otherwise qualified attorney whom that defendant can afford to hire.\xe2\x80\x9d Caplin & Drysdale, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 624. The order at issue in this case would seriously undermine that constitutional right.

\n

On the other side we find interests that include the Government\xe2\x80\x99s contingent interest in securing its punishment of choice (namely, criminal forfeiture) as well as the victims\xe2\x80\x99 interest in securing restitution (notably, from funds belonging to the defendant, not the victims). While these interests are important, to deny the Government the order it requests will not inevitably undermine them, for, at least sometimes, the defendant may possess other assets\xe2\x80\x94say, \xe2\x80\x9ctainted\xe2\x80\x9d property\xe2\x80\x94that might be used for forfeitures and restitution. Cf. Gonzalez-Lopez, 548 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 148 (\xe2\x80\x9cDeprivation of the right\xe2\x80\x9d to counsel of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s choice \xe2\x80\x9cis \xe2\x80\x98complete\xe2\x80\x99 when the defendant is erroneously prevented from being represented by the lawyer he wants\xe2\x80\x9d). Nor do the interests in obtaining payment of a criminal forfeiture or restitution order enjoy constitutional protection. Rather, despite their importance, compared to the right to counsel of choice, these interests would seem to lie somewhat further from the heart of a fair, effective criminal justice system.

\n

Second, relevant legal tradition offers virtually no significant support for the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position. Rather, tradition argues to the contrary. Describing the 18th-century English legal world (which recognized only a limited right to counsel), Blackstone wrote that \xe2\x80\x9conly\xe2\x80\x9d those \xe2\x80\x9cgoods and chattels\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9ca man has at the time of conviction shall be forfeited.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 388 (1765) (emphasisadded); see 1 J. Chitty, Practical Treatise on the CriminalLaw 737 (1816) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he party indicted may sell any of [his property] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to assist him in preparing for his defense on the trial\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Describing the common law as understood in 19th-century America (which recognized a broader right to counsel), Justice Story wrote:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIt is well known, that at the common law, in many cases of felonies, the party forfeited his goods and chattels to the crown. The forfeiture .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. was a part, or at least a consequence, of the judgment of conviction. It is plain from this statement, that no right to the goods and chattels of the felon could be acquired by the crown by the mere commission of the offense; but the right attached only by the conviction of the offender. .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. In the contemplation of the common law, the offender\xe2\x80\x99s right was not divested until the conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d The Palmyra, 12 Wheat. 1, 14 (1827).

\n

See generally Powell, supra, at 60\xe2\x80\x9361 (describing the scope of the right to counsel in 18th-century Britain and colonial America).

\n

As we have explained, supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x9310, cases such as Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto permit the Government to freeze a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets pretrial, but the opinions in those cases highlight the fact that the property at issue was \xe2\x80\x9ctainted,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., it did not belong entirely to the defendant. We have found no decision of this Court authorizing unfettered, pretrial forfeiture of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own \xe2\x80\x9cinnocent\xe2\x80\x9d property\xe2\x80\x94property with no connection to the charged crime. Nor do we see any grounds for distinguishing the historic preference against preconviction forfeitures from the preconviction restraint at issue here. As far as Luis\xe2\x80\x99\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel of choice is concerned, a restraining order might as well be a forfeiture; that is, the restraint itself suffices to completely deny this constitutional right. See Gonzalez-Lopez, supra, at 148.

\n

Third, as a practical matter, to accept the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position could well erode the right to counsel to a considerably greater extent than we have so far indicated. To permit the Government to freeze Luis\xe2\x80\x99 untainted assets would unleash a principle of constitutional law that would have no obvious stopping place. The statutory provision before us authorizing the present restraining order refers only to \xe2\x80\x9cbanking law violation[s]\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cFederal health care offense[s].\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71345(a)(2). But, in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view, Congress could write more statutes authorizing pretrial restraints in cases involving other illegal behavior\xe2\x80\x94after all, a broad range of such behavior can lead to postconviction forfeiture of untainted assets. See, e.g., \xc2\xa71963(m) (providing for forfeiture of innocent, substitute assets for any violation of the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act).

\n

Moreover, the financial consequences of a criminal conviction are steep. Even beyond the forfeiture itself, criminal fines can be high, and restitution orders expensive. See, e.g., \xc2\xa71344 ($1 million fine for bank fraud); \xc2\xa73571 (mail and wire fraud fines of up to $250,000 for individuals and $500,000 for organizations); United States v. Gushlak, 728 F.\xc2\xa03d 184, 187, 203 (CA2 2013) ($17.5 million restitution award against an individual defendant in a fraud-on-the-market case); FTC v. Trudeau, 662 F.\xc2\xa03d 947, 949 (CA7 2011) ($37.6 million remedial sanction for fraud). How are defendants whose innocent assets are frozen in cases like these supposed to pay for a lawyer\xe2\x80\x94particularly if they lack \xe2\x80\x9ctainted assets\xe2\x80\x9d because they are innocent, a class of defendants whom the right to counsel certainly seeks to protect? See Powell, 287 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 69; Amar, 84 Geo. L. J., at 643 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he\n \nSixth Amendment is generally designed to elicit truth and protect innocence\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

These defendants, rendered indigent, would fall back upon publicly paid counsel, including overworked and underpaid public defenders. As the Department of Justice explains, only 27 percent of county-based public defender offices have sufficient attorneys to meet nationally recommended caseload standards. Dept. of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics, D. Farole & L. Langton, Census of Public Defender Offices, 2007: County-based and Local Public Defender Offices, 2007, p. 10 (Sept. 2010). And as one amicus points out, \xe2\x80\x9c[m]any federal public defender organizations and lawyers appointed under the Criminal Justice Act serve numerous clients and have only limited resources.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for New York Council of Defense Lawyers 11. The upshot is a substantial risk that accept-ing the Government\xe2\x80\x99s views would\xe2\x80\x94by increasing the government-paid-defender workload\xe2\x80\x94render less effective the basic right the\n \nSixth Amendment seeks to protect.

\n

3

\n

We add that the constitutional line we have drawn should prove workable. That line distinguishes between a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s (1) tainted funds and (2) innocent funds needed to pay for counsel. We concede, as Justice Kennedy points out, post, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313, that money is fungible; and sometimes it will be difficult to say whether a particular bank account contains tainted or untainted funds. But the law has tracing rules that help courts implement the kind of distinction we require in this case. With the help of those rules, the victim of a robbery, for example, will likely obtain the car that the robber used stolen money to buy. See, e.g., 1 G. Palmer, Law of Restitution \xc2\xa72.14, p. 175 (1978) (\xe2\x80\x9ctracing\xe2\x80\x9d permits a claim against \xe2\x80\x9can asset which is traceable to or the product of\xe2\x80\x9d tainted funds); 4 A. Scott, Law of Trusts \xc2\xa7518, pp. 3309\xe2\x80\x933314 (1956) (describing the tracing rules governing commingled accounts). And those rules will likely also prevent Luis from benefiting from many of the money transfers and purchases Justice Kennedy describes. See post, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

Courts use tracing rules in cases involving fraud, pension rights, bankruptcy, trusts, etc. See, e.g., Montanile v. Board of Trustees of Nat. Elevator Industry Health Benefit Plan, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2016) (slip op., at 8\xe2\x80\x939). They consequently have experience separating tainted assets from untainted assets, just as they have experience determining how much money is needed to cover the costs of a lawyer. See, e.g.,\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71345(b) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe court shall proceed as soon as practicable to the hearing and determination of [actions to freeze a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s tainted or untainted assets]\xe2\x80\x9d);\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72412(d) (courts must determine reasonable attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees under the Equal Access to Justice Act); see also Kaley, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, and n. 3 (slip op., at 3, and n. 3) (\xe2\x80\x9cSince Monsanto, the lower courts have generally provided a hearing.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [to determine] whether probable cause exists to believe that the assets in dispute are traceable .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to the crime charged in the indictment\xe2\x80\x9d). We therefore see little reason to worry, as Justice Kennedy seems to, that defendants will \xe2\x80\x9cbe allowed to circumvent [the usual forfeiture rules] by using .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. funds to pay for a high, or even the highest, priced defense team [they] can find.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 7.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

For the reasons stated, we conclude that the defendant in this case has a\n \nSixth Amendment right to use her own \xe2\x80\x9cinnocent\xe2\x80\x9d property to pay a reasonable fee for the assistance of counsel. On the assumptions made here, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order prevents Luis from exercising that right. We consequently vacate the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings.

\n

It is so ordered.

SILA LUIS, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[March 30, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, concurring in the judgment.

\n

I agree with the plurality that a pretrial freeze of untainted assets violates a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel of choice. But I do not agree with the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s balancing approach. Rather, my reasoning rests strictly on the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s text and common-law backdrop.

\n

The\n \nSixth Amendment provides important limits on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s power to freeze a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s forfeitable assets before trial. And, constitutional rights necessarily protect the prerequisites for their exercise. The right \xe2\x80\x9cto have the Assistance of Counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 6, thus implies the right to use lawfully owned property to pay for an attorney. Otherwise the right to counsel\xe2\x80\x94originally understood to protect only the right to hire counsel of choice\xe2\x80\x94would be meaningless. History confirms this textual understanding. The common law limited pretrial asset restraints to tainted assets. Both this textual understanding and history establish that the\n \nSixth Amendment prevents the Government from freezing untainted assets in order to secure a potential forfeiture. The freeze here accordingly violates the Constitution.

\n

I

\n

The\n \nSixth Amendment provides, \xe2\x80\x9cIn all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to have the Assistance of Counsel for his defence.\xe2\x80\x9d As originally understood, this right guaranteed a defendant the right \xe2\x80\x9cto employ a lawyer to assist in his defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Scott v. Illinois,\n\n440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367,\n370 (1979)\n. The common law permitted counsel to represent defendants charged with misdemeanors, but not felonies other than treason. W. Beaney, The Right to Counsel in American Courts 8\xe2\x80\x939 (1955). The\n \nSixth Amendment abolished the rule prohibiting representation in felony cases, but was \xe2\x80\x9cnot aimed to compel the State to provide counsel for a defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Betts v. Brady,\n\n316 U.\xc2\xa0S. 455,\n466 (1942)\n, overruled by Gideon v. Wainwright,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335 (1963)\n; see Beaney, supra, at 27\xe2\x80\x9336. \xe2\x80\x9cThe right to select counsel of one\xe2\x80\x99s choice\xe2\x80\x9d is thus \xe2\x80\x9cthe root meaning\xe2\x80\x9d of the\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel. United States v. Gonzalez-Lopez,\n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 140\n\xe2\x80\x93148 (2006).

\n

The\n \nSixth Amendment denies the Government unchecked power to freeze a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets before trial simply to secure potential forfeiture upon conviction. If that bare expectancy of criminal punishment gave the Government such power, then a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to counsel of choice would be meaningless, because retaining an attorney requires resources. The law has long recognized that the \xe2\x80\x9c[a]uthorization of an act also authorizes a necessary predicate act.\xe2\x80\x9d A. Scalia & B. Garner, Reading Law: The Interpretation of Legal Texts 192 (2012) (discussing the \xe2\x80\x9cpredicate-act canon\xe2\x80\x9d). As Thomas Cooley put it with respect to Government powers, \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a general power is conferred or duty enjoined, every particular power necessary for the exercise of the one, or the performance of the other, is also conferred.\xe2\x80\x9d Constitutional Limitations 63 (1868); see 1 J. Kent, Commentaries on American Law 464 (13th ed. 1884) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]henever a power is given by a statute, everything necessary to the making of it effectual or requisite to attain the end is implied\xe2\x80\x9d). This logic equally applies to individual rights. After all, many rights are powers reserved to the People rather than delegated to the Government. Cf. U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 10 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe powers not delegated to the United States by the Constitution, nor prohibited by it to the States, are reserved to the States respectively, or to the people\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Constitutional rights thus implicitly protect those closely related acts necessary to their exercise. \xe2\x80\x9cThere comes apoint .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. at which the regulation of action intimately and unavoidably connected with [a right] is a regulation of [the right] itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Hill v. Colorado,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 703,\n745 (2000)\n (Scalia, J., dissenting). The right to keep and bear arms, for example, \xe2\x80\x9cimplies a corresponding right to obtain the bullets necessary to use them,\xe2\x80\x9d Jackson v. City and County of San Francisco, 746 F.\xc2\xa03d 953, 967 (CA9 2014) (inter-nal quotation marks omitted), and \xe2\x80\x9cto acquire and maintain proficiency in their use,\xe2\x80\x9d Ezell v. Chicago, 651 F.\xc2\xa03d 684, 704 (CA7 2011). See District of Columbia v. Heller,\n\n554 U.\xc2\xa0S. 570\n\xe2\x80\x93618 (2008) (citing T. Cooley, General Principles of Constitutional Law 271 (2d ed. 1891) (discussing the implicit right to train with weapons)); United States v. Miller,\n\n307 U.\xc2\xa0S. 174,\n180 (1939)\n (citing 1 H. Osgood, The American Colonies in the 17th Century 499 (1904) (discussing the implicit right to possess ammunition)); Andrews v. State, 50 Tenn. 165, 178 (1871) (discussing both rights). Without protection for these closely related rights, the\n \nSecond Amendment would be toothless. Likewise, the\n \nFirst Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cright to speak would be largely ineffective if it did not include the right to engage in financial transactions that are the incidents of its exercise.\xe2\x80\x9d McConnell v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 93,\n252 (2003)\n (Scalia, J., concurring in part, concurring in judgment in part, and dissenting in part).

\n

The same goes for the\n \nSixth Amendment and the financial resources required to obtain a lawyer. Without constitutional protection for at least some of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets, the Government could nullify the right to counsel of choice. As the plurality says, an unlimited power to freeze assets before trial \xe2\x80\x9cwould unleash a principle of constitutional law that would have no obvious stopping place.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14; cf. McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 431 (1819) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he power to tax involves the power to destroy\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cpower to destroy may defeat and render useless the power to create\xe2\x80\x9d). Unless the right to counsel also protects the prerequisite right to use one\xe2\x80\x99s financial resources for an attorney, I doubt that the Framers would have gone through the trouble of adopting such a flimsy \xe2\x80\x9cparchment barrie[r].\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 48, p. 308 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961) (J. Madison).

\n

An unlimited power to freeze a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s potentially forfeitable assets in advance of trial would eviscerate the\n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s original meaning and purpose. At English common law, forfeiture of all real and personal property was a standard punishment for felonies. See 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 95 (1769) (Blackstone). That harsh penalty never caught on in America. See Calero-Toledo v. Pearson Yacht Leasing Co.,\n\n416 U.\xc2\xa0S. 663\n\xe2\x80\x93683 (1974). The First Congress banned it. See Crimes Act of 1790, \xc2\xa724,\n\n1Stat.\n117 (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]o conviction or judgment for any of the offences aforesaid, shall work corruption of blood, or any forfeiture of estate\xe2\x80\x9d). But the Constitution did not. See Art. III, \xc2\xa73, cl. 2 (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]o Attainder of Treason shall work Corruption of Blood, or Forfeiture except during the Life of the Person attainted\xe2\x80\x9d). If the Government\xe2\x80\x99s mere expectancy of a total forfeiture upon conviction were sufficient to justify a complete pretrial asset freeze, then Congress could render the right to counsel a nullity in felony cases. That would have shocked the Framers. As discussed, before adoption of the\n \nSixth Amendment, felony cases (not misdemeanors) were precisely when the common law denied defendants the right to counsel. See supra, at ___. With an unlimited power to freeze assets before trial, the Government could well revive the common-law felony rule that the\n \nSixth Amendment was designed to abolish.

\n

The modern, judicially created right to Government-appointed counsel does not obviate these concerns. As understood in 1791, the\n \nSixth Amendment protected a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to retain an attorney he could afford. It is thus no answer, as the principal dissent replies, that defendants rendered indigent by a pretrial asset freeze can resort to public defenders. Post, at 14 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approach nullifies the original understanding of the right to counsel. To ensure that the right to counsel has meaning, the\n \nSixth Amendment limits the assets the Government may freeze before trial to secure eventual forfeiture.

\n

II

\n

The longstanding rule against restraining a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s untainted property before conviction guarantees a meaningful right to counsel. The common-law forfeiture tradition provides the limits of this\n \nSixth Amendment guarantee. That tradition draws a clear line between tainted and untainted assets. The only alternative to this common-law reading is case-by-case adjudication to determine which freezes are \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate\xe2\x80\x9d and which are an \xe2\x80\x9cabuse of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. power.\xe2\x80\x9d McCulloch, 4 Wheat., at 430. This piecemeal approach seems woefully inadequate. Such questions of degree are \xe2\x80\x9cunfit for the judicial department.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But see Caplin & Drysdale, Chartered v. United States,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 617,\n635 (1989)\n (stating in dicta that \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ases involving particular abuses can be dealt with individually .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. when (and if) any such cases arise\xe2\x80\x9d). Fortunately the common law drew a clear line between tainted and untainted assets.

\n

Pretrial freezes of untainted forfeitable assets did not emerge until the late 20th century. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[T]he lack of historical precedent\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for the asset freeze here is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[p]erhaps the most telling indication of a severe constitutional problem.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Bd.,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477\n\xe2\x80\x93506 (2010) (quoting Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Bd., 537 F.\xc2\xa03d 667, 699 (CADC 2008) (Kavanaugh, J., dissenting)). Indeed, blanket asset freezes are so tempting that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprolonged reticence would be amazing if [they] were not understood to be constitutionally proscribed.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut v. Spendthrift Farm, Inc.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 211,\n230 (1995)\n; see Printz v. United States,\n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 898\n\xe2\x80\x93908 (1997) (reasoning that the lack of early federal statutes commandeering state executive officers \xe2\x80\x9csuggests an assumed absence of such power\xe2\x80\x9d given \xe2\x80\x9cthe attractiveness of that course to Congress\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The common law prohibited pretrial freezes of criminal defendants\xe2\x80\x99 untainted assets. As the plurality notes, ante, at 13, for in personam criminal forfeitures like that at issue here, any interference with a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s property traditionally required a conviction. Forfeiture was \xe2\x80\x9ca part, or at least a consequence, of the judgment of conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d The Palmyra, 12 Wheat. 1, 14 (1827) (Story, J.). The defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cproperty cannot be touched before .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the forfeiture is completed.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 J. Chitty, A Practical Treatise on the Criminal Law 737 (5th ed. 1847). This rule applied equally \xe2\x80\x9cto money as well as specific chattels.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 736. And it was not limited to full-blown physical seizures. Although the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s goods could be appraised and inventoried before trial, he remained free to \xe2\x80\x9csell any of them for his own support in prison, or that of his family, or to assist him in preparing for his defence on the trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 737 (emphasis added). Blackstone likewise agreed that a defendant \xe2\x80\x9cmay bona fide sell any of his chattels, real or personal, for the sustenance of himself and family between the [offense] and conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 Blackstone 380; see Fleetwood\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 8 Co. Rep. 171a, 171b, 77 Eng. Rep. 731, 732 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1611) (endorsing this rule). At most, a court could unwind prejudgment fraudulent transfers after conviction. 4 Blackstone 381; see Jones v. Ashurt, Skin. 357, 357\xe2\x80\x93358, 90 Eng. Rep. 159 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1693) (unwinding a fraudulent sale after conviction because it was designed to defeat forfeiture). Numerous English authorities confirm these common-law principles. Chitty, supra, at 736\xe2\x80\x93737 (collecting sources).

\n

The common law did permit the Government, however, to seize tainted assets before trial. For example, \xe2\x80\x9cseizure of the res has long been considered a prerequisite to the initiation of in\xc2\xa0rem forfeiture proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. James Daniel Good Real Property,\n\n510 U.\xc2\xa0S. 43,\n57 (1993)\n (emphasis added); see The Brig Ann, 9 Cranch 289, 291 (1815) (Story, J.). But such forfeitures were traditionally \xe2\x80\x9cfixed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by determining what property has been \xe2\x80\x98tainted\xe2\x80\x99 by unlawful use.\xe2\x80\x9d Austin v. United States,\n\n509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 602,\n627 (1993)\n (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). So the civil in\xc2\xa0rem forfeiture tradition tracks the tainted-untainted line. It provides no support for the asset freeze here.

\n

There is a similarly well-established\n \nFourth Amendment tradition of seizing contraband and stolen goods before trial based only on probable cause. See Carroll v. United States,\n\n267 U.\xc2\xa0S. 132\n\xe2\x80\x93152 (1925) (discussing this history); Boyd v. United States,\n\n116 U.\xc2\xa0S. 616\n\xe2\x80\x93624 (1886) (same). Tainted assets fall within this tradition because they are the fruits or instrumentalities of crime. So the Government may freeze tainted assets before trial based on probable cause to believe that they are forfeitable. See United States v. Monsanto,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600\n\xe2\x80\x93603, 615\xe2\x80\x93616 (1989). Nevertheless, our precedents require \xe2\x80\x9ca nexus . . . between the item to be seized and criminal behavior.\xe2\x80\x9d Warden, Md. Penitentiary v. Hayden,\n\n387 U.\xc2\xa0S. 294,\n307 (1967)\n. Untainted assets almost never have such a nexus. The only exception is that some property that is evidence of crime might technically qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cuntainted\xe2\x80\x9d but nevertheless has a nexus to criminal behavior. See ibid. Thus, untainted assets do not fall within the\n \nFourth Amendment tradition either.

\n

It is certainly the case that some early American statutes did provide for civil forfeiture of untainted substitute property. See Registry Act, \xc2\xa712,\n\n1Stat.\n293 (providing for forfeiture of a ship or \xe2\x80\x9cthe value thereof\xe2\x80\x9d); Collection Act of July 31, 1789, \xc2\xa722,\n\n1Stat.\n42 (similar for goods); United States v. Bajakajian,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 321,\n341 (1998)\n (collecting statutes). These statutes grew out of a broader \xe2\x80\x9csix-century-long tradition of in personam customs fines equal to one, two, three, or even four times the value of the goods at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 345\xe2\x80\x93346 (Kennedy, J., dissenting).

\n

But this long tradition of in personam customs fines does not contradict the general rule against pretrial seizures of untainted property. These fines\xe2\x80\x99 in personam status strongly suggests that the Government did not collect them by seizing property at the outset of litigation. As described, that process was traditionally required for in\xc2\xa0rem forfeiture of tainted assets. See supra, at ___. There appears to be scant historical evidence, however, that forfeiture ever involved seizure of untainted assets before trial and judgment, except in limited circumstances not relevant here. Such summary procedures were reserved for collecting taxes and seizures during war. See Phillips v. Commissioner,\n\n283 U.\xc2\xa0S. 589,\n595 (1931)\n; Miller v. United States, 11 Wall. 268, 304\xe2\x80\x93306 (1871). The Government\xe2\x80\x99s right of action in tax and custom-fine cases may have been the same\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ca civil action of debt.\xe2\x80\x9d Bajakajian, supra, at 343, n. 18; Stockwell v. United States, 13 Wall. 531, 543 (1871); Adams v. Woods, 2 Cranch 336, 341 (1805). Even so, nothing suggests trial and judgment were expendable. See Miller, supra, at 304\xe2\x80\x93305 (stating in dicta that confiscating Confederate property through in\xc2\xa0rem proceedings would have raised Fifth and\n \nSixth Amendment concerns had they not been a war measure).

\n

The common law thus offers an administrable line: A criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s untainted assets are protected from Government interference before trial and judgment. His tainted assets, by contrast, may be seized before trial as contraband or through a separate in\xc2\xa0rem proceeding. Reading the\n \nSixth Amendment to track the historical line between tainted and untainted assets makes good sense. It avoids case-by-case adjudication, and ensures that the original meaning of the right to counsel does real work. The asset freeze here infringes the right to counsel because it \xe2\x80\x9cis so broad that it differs not only in degree, but in kind, from its historical antecedents.\xe2\x80\x9d James Daniel Good, supra, at 82 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part).

\n

The dissenters object that, before trial, a defendant has an identical property interest in tainted and untainted assets. See post, at 8\xe2\x80\x939 (opinion of Kennedy, J.); post, at 2 (opinion of Kagan, J.). Perhaps so. I need not take a position on the matter. Either way, that fact is irrelevant. Because the pretrial asset freeze here crosses into untainted assets, for which there is no historical tradition, it is unconstitutional. Any such incursion violates the\n \nSixth Amendment.

\n

III

\n

Since the asset freeze here violates the\n \nSixth Amendment, the plurality correctly concludes that the judgment below must be reversed. But I cannot go further and endorse the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s atextual balancing analysis. The\n \nSixth Amendment guarantees the right to counsel of choice. As discussed, a pretrial freeze of untainted assets infringes that right. This conclusion leaves no room for balancing. Moreover, I have no idea whether, \xe2\x80\x9ccompared to the right to counsel of choice,\xe2\x80\x9d the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interests in securing forfeiture and restitution lie \xe2\x80\x9cfurther from the heart of a fair, effective criminal justice system.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. Judges are not well suited to strike the right \xe2\x80\x9cbalance\xe2\x80\x9d between those incommensurable interests. Nor do I think it is our role to do so. The People, through ratification, have already weighed the policy tradeoffs that constitutional rights entail. See Heller, 554 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 634\xe2\x80\x93635. Those tradeoffs are thus not for us to reevaluate. \xe2\x80\x9cThe very enumeration of the right\xe2\x80\x9d to counsel of choice denies us \xe2\x80\x9cthe power to decide .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. whether the right is really worth insisting upon.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 634. Such judicial balancing \xe2\x80\x9cdo[es] violence\xe2\x80\x9d to the constitutional design. Crawford v. Washington,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 36\n\xe2\x80\x9368 (2004). And it is out of step with our interpretive tradition. See Aleinikoff, Constitutional Law in the Age of Balancing, 96 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 943, 949\xe2\x80\x93952 (1987) (noting that balancing did not appear in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional analysis until the mid-20th century).

\n

The plurality\xe2\x80\x99s balancing analysis also casts doubt on the constitutionality of incidental burdens on the right to counsel. For the most part, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents hold that a generally applicable law placing only an incidental burden on a constitutional right does not violate that right. See R.\xc2\xa0A.\xc2\xa0V. v. St. Paul,\n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 377\n\xe2\x80\x93390 (1992) (explaining that content-neutral laws do not violate the\n \nFirst Amendment simply because they incidentally burden expressive conduct); Employment Div., Dept. of Human Resources of Ore. v. Smith,\n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 872\n\xe2\x80\x93882 (1990) (likewise for religion-neutral laws that burden religious exercise).

\n

Criminal-procedure rights tend to follow the normal incidental-burden rule. The Constitution does not \xe2\x80\x9cforbi[d] every government-imposed choice in the criminal process that has the effect of discouraging the exercise of constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Chaffin v. Stynchcombe,\n\n412 U.\xc2\xa0S. 17,\n30 (1973)\n. The threat of more severe charges if a defendant refuses to plead guilty does not violate his right to trial. See Bordenkircher v. Hayes,\n\n434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 357\n (1978). And, in my view, prosecutorial arguments that raise the \xe2\x80\x9ccost\xe2\x80\x9d of remaining silent do not violate a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right against self-incrimination (at least as a matter of original meaning). See Mitchell v. United States,\n\n526 U.\xc2\xa0S. 314\n\xe2\x80\x93343 (1999) (Thomas, J., dissenting); id., at 331\xe2\x80\x93336 (Scalia, J., dissenting).

\n

The\n \nSixth Amendment arguably works the same way. \xe2\x80\x9c[A] defendant may not insist on representation by an attorney he cannot afford.\xe2\x80\x9d Wheat v. United States,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153,\n159 (1988)\n. The Constitution perhaps guarantees only a \xe2\x80\x9cfreedom of counsel\xe2\x80\x9d akin to the\n \nFirst Amendment freedoms of speech and religion that also \xe2\x80\x9cdepen[d] in part on one\xe2\x80\x99s financial wherewithal.\xe2\x80\x9d Caplin & Drysdale, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 628. Numerous laws make it more difficult for defendants to retain a lawyer. But that fact alone does not create a\n \nSixth Amendment problem. For instance, criminal defendants must still pay taxes even though \xe2\x80\x9cthese financial levies may deprive them of resources that could be used to hire an attorney.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 631\xe2\x80\x93632. So I lean toward the principal dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view that incidental burdens on the right to counsel of choice would not violate the\n \nSixth Amendment. See post, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, 11\xe2\x80\x9312 (opinion of Kennedy, J.).

\n

On the other hand, the Court has said that the right to counsel guarantees defendants \xe2\x80\x9ca fair opportunity to secure counsel of [their] choice.\xe2\x80\x9d Powell v. Alabama,\n\n287 U.\xc2\xa0S. 45\n\xe2\x80\x9353 (1932) (emphasis added). The state court in Powell denied the defendants such an opportunity, the Court held, by moving to trial so quickly (six days after indictment) that the defendants had no chance to communicate with family or otherwise arrange for representation. Ibid. The schedule in Powell was not designed to block counsel, which suggests the usual incidental-burden rule might be inapt in the\n \nSixth Amendment context. I leave the question open because this case does not require an answer.

\n

The asset freeze here is not merely an incidental burden on the right to counsel of choice; it targets a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets, which are necessary to exercise that right, simply to secure forfeiture upon conviction. The prospect of that criminal punishment, however, is precisely why the Constitution guarantees a right to counsel. The\n \nSixth Amendment does not permit the Government\xe2\x80\x99s bare expectancy of forfeiture to void that right. When the potential of a conviction is the only basis for interfering with a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets before trial, the Constitution requires the Government to respect the longstanding common-law protection for a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s untainted property.

\n

For these reasons, I concur only in the judgment.

SILA LUIS, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[March 30, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Kennedy, with whom Justice Alito joins, dissenting.

\n

The plurality and Justice Thomas find in the\n \nSixth Amendment a right of criminal defendants to pay for an attorney with funds that are forfeitable upon conviction so long as those funds are not derived from the crime alleged. That unprecedented holding rewards criminals who hurry to spend, conceal, or launder stolen property by assuring them that they may use their own funds to pay for an attorney after they have dissipated the proceeds of their crime. It matters not, under today\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s remaining assets must be preserved if the victim or the Government is to recover for the property wrong-fully taken. By granting a defendant a constitutional right to hire an attorney with assets needed to make a property-crime victim whole, the plurality and Justice Thomas ignore this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents and distort the\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel.

\n

The result reached today makes little sense in cases that involve fungible assets preceded by fraud, embezzlement, or other theft. An example illustrates the point. Assume a thief steals $1 million and then wins another $1 million in a lottery. After putting the sums in separate accounts, he or she spends $1 million. If the thief spends his or her lottery winnings, the Government can restrain the stolen funds in their entirety. The thief has no right to use those funds to pay for an attorney. Yet if the thief heeds today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, he or she will spend the stolen money first; for if the thief is apprehended, the $1 million won in the lottery can be used for an attorney. This result is not required by the Constitution.

\n

The plurality reaches its conclusion by weighing a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel of choice against the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in preventing the dissipation of assets forfeitable upon conviction. In so doing, it\xe2\x80\x94like Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x94sweeps aside the decisions in Caplin & Drysdale, Chartered v. United States,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 617 (1989)\n, and United States v. Monsanto,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600 (1989)\n, both of which make clear that a defendant has no\n \nSixth Amendment right to spend forfeitable assets (or assets that will be forfeitable) on an attorney. The principle the Court adopted in those cases applies with equal force here. Rather than apply that principle, however, the plurality and concurrence adopt a rule found nowhere in the Constitution or this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents\xe2\x80\x94that the\n \nSixth Amendment protects a person\xe2\x80\x99s right to spend otherwise forfeitable assets on an attorney so long as those assets are not related to or the direct proceeds of the charged crime. Ante, at 1 (plurality opinion); ante, at 1 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). The reasoning in these separate opinions is incorrect, and requires this respectful dissent.

\n

I

\n

This case arises from petitioner Sila Luis\xe2\x80\x99 indictment for conspiring to commit health care fraud against the United States. The Government alleges that, as part of her illegal scheme, Luis used her health care companies to defraud Medicare by billing for services that were not medically necessary or actually provided. The charged crimes, the Government maintains, resulted in the payment of $45 million in improper Medicare benefits to Luis\xe2\x80\x99 companies.

\n

The same day Luis was indicted, the Government initiated a civil action under\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71345 to restrain Luis\xe2\x80\x99 assets before her criminal trial, including substitute property of an amount equivalent to the value of the proceeds of her alleged crimes. To establish its entitlement to a restraining order, the Government showed that Luis and her co-conspirators were dissipating the illegally obtained assets. In particular, they were transferring money involved in the scheme to various individuals and entities, including shell corporations owned by Luis\xe2\x80\x99 family members. As part of this process, Luis opened and closed well over 40 bank accounts and withdrew large amounts of cash to hide the conspiracy\xe2\x80\x99s proceeds. Luis personally received almost $4.5 million in funds and used at least some of that money to purchase luxury items, real estate, and automobiles, and to travel. Based on this and other evidence, the District Court entered an order prohibiting Luis from spending up to $45 million of her assets.

\n

Before the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit, Luis argued that the\n \nSixth Amendment required that she be allowed to spend the restrained substitute assets on an attorney. The Court of Appeals disagreed, concluding that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he arguments made by Luis .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are foreclosed by the United States Supreme Court decisions in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Caplin & Drysdale [and] Monsanto.\xe2\x80\x9d 564 Fed. Appx. 493, 494 (2014) (\xc2\xa0per curiam). In my view the Court of Appeals was correct, and its judgment should be affirmed.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

In Caplin & Drysdale, a law firm had represented a defendant charged with running a massive drug-distribution scheme. The defendant pleaded guilty and agreed to forfeit his assets. The law firm then sought to recover a portion of the forfeited assets for its legal fees. The firm argued that, when a defendant needs forfeitable assets to pay for an attorney, the forfeiture of those assets violates the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nSixth Amendment right to be represented by his counsel of choice.

\n

The Court rejected the firm\xe2\x80\x99s argument. The\n \nSixth Amendment, the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cguarantees defendants in criminal cases the right to adequate representation, but those who do not have the means to hire their own lawyers have no cognizable complaint so long as they are adequately represented by attorneys appointed by the courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Caplin & Drysdale, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 624. As for the right to choose one\xe2\x80\x99s own attorney, the Court observed that \xe2\x80\x9cnothing in [the forfeiture statute] prevents a defendant from hiring the attorney of his choice, or disqualifies any attorney from serving as a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 625. Even defendants who possess \xe2\x80\x9cnothing but assets the Government seeks to have forfeited .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may be able to find lawyers willing to represent them, hoping that their fees will be paid in the event of acquittal, or via some other means that a defendant might come by in the future.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The burden imposed by forfeiture law, the Court concluded, is thus \xe2\x80\x9ca limited one.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Caplin & Drysdale also repudiated the firm\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the Government has only a modest interest in forfeitable assets that may be used to retain an attorney. In light of the importance of separating criminals from their ill-gotten gains and providing restitution to victims of crime, the Court found \xe2\x80\x9ca strong governmental interest in obtaining full recovery of all forfeitable assets, an interest that overrides any\n \nSixth Amendment interest in permitting criminals to use assets adjudged forfeitable to pay for their defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 631.

\n

The same day the Court decided Caplin & Drysdale it decided Monsanto, which addressed the pretrial restraint of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the defendant seeks to use those assets to pay an attorney.\xe2\x80\x9d 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 602. The Court rejected the notion that there is a meaningful distinction, for\n \nSixth Amendment purposes, between the restraint of assets before trial and the forfeiture of assets after trial: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f the Government may, post-trial, forbid the use of forfeited assets to pay an attorney, then surely no constitutional violation occurs when, after probable cause is adequately established, the Government obtains an order barring a defendant from frustrating that end by dissipating his assets prior to trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 616. The Court noted, moreover, that \xe2\x80\x9cit would be odd to conclude that the Government may not restrain property .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in [a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] possession, based on a finding of probable cause, when we have held that (under appropriate circumstances), the Government may restrain persons where there is a finding of probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 615\xe2\x80\x93616. When a defendant himself can be restrained pretrial, there is \xe2\x80\x9cno constitutional infirmity\xe2\x80\x9d in a similar pretrial restraint of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s property \xe2\x80\x9cto protect its \xe2\x80\x98appearance\xe2\x80\x99 at trial and protect the community\xe2\x80\x99s interest in full recovery of any ill-gotten gains.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 616.

\n

B

\n

The principle the Court announced in Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto controls the result here. Those cases establish that a pretrial restraint of assets forfeitable upon conviction does not contravene the\n \nSixth Amendment even when the defendant possesses no other funds with which to pay for an attorney. The restraint itself does not prevent a defendant from seeking to convince his or her counsel of choice to take on the representation without advance payment. See Caplin & Drysdale, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 625. It does not disqualify any attorney the defendant might want. Ibid. And it does not prevent a defendant from borrowing funds to pay for an attorney who is otherwise too expensive. To be sure, a pretrial restraint may make it difficult for a defendant to secure counsel who insists that high defense costs be paid in advance. That difficulty, however, does not result in a\n \nSixth Amendment violation any more than high taxes or other government exactions that impose a similar burden. See, e.g., id., at 631\xe2\x80\x93632 (\xe2\x80\x9cCriminal defendants .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are not exempted from federal, state, and local taxation simply because these financial levies may deprive them of resources that could be used to hire an attorney\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The pretrial restraint in Monsanto was no more burdensome than the pretrial restraint at issue here. Luis, like the defendant in Monsanto, was not barred from obtaining the assistance of any particular attorney. She was free to seek lawyers willing to represent her in the hopes that their fees would be paid at some future point. In short, \xc2\xa71345\xe2\x80\x99s authorization of a pretrial restraint of substitute assets places no greater burden on a defendant like Luis than the forfeiture and pretrial restraint statute placed on the defendant in Monsanto.

\n

In addition, the Government has the same \xe2\x80\x9cstrong .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. interest in obtaining full recovery of all forfeitable assets\xe2\x80\x9d here as it did in Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto. See Caplin & Drysdale, supra, at 631. If Luis is convicted,the Government has a right to recover Luis\xe2\x80\x99 substituteassets\xe2\x80\x94the money she kept for herself while spending the taxpayer dollars she is accused of stealing. Just as the Government has an interest in ensuring Luis\xe2\x80\x99 presence at trial\xe2\x80\x94an interest that can justify a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s pretrial detention\xe2\x80\x94so too does the Government have an interest in ensuring the availability of her substitute assets after trial, an interest that can justify pretrial restraint.

\n

One need look no further than the Court\xe2\x80\x99s concluding words in Monsanto to know the proper result here: \xe2\x80\x9c[N]o constitutional violation occurs when, after probable cause [to believe that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets will be forfeitable] is adequately established, the Government obtains an order barring a defendant from .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. dissipating his assets prior to trial.\xe2\x80\x9d 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 616. The Government, having established probable cause to believe that Luis\xe2\x80\x99 substitute assets will be forfeitable upon conviction, should be permitted to obtain a restraining order barring her from spending those funds prior to trial. Luis should not be allowed to circumvent that restraint by using the funds to pay for a high, or even the highest, priced defense team she can find.

\n

III

\n

The plurality maintains that Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto do not apply because \xe2\x80\x9cthe nature of the assets at issue here differs from the assets at issue in those earlier cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. According to the plurality, the property here \xe2\x80\x9cbelongs to the defendant, pure and simple.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. It states that, while \xe2\x80\x9ctitle to property used to commit a crime .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. often passes to the Government at the instant the crime is planned or committed,\xe2\x80\x9d title to Luis\xe2\x80\x99 untainted property has not passed to the Government. Ante, at 6. \xe2\x80\x9cThat fact,\xe2\x80\x9d the plurality concludes, \xe2\x80\x9cundermines the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reliance upon precedent, for both Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto relied critically upon the fact that the property at issue was \xe2\x80\x98tainted,\xe2\x80\x99 and that title to the property therefore had passed from the defendant to the Government before the court issued its order freezing (or otherwise disposing of) the assets.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

These conclusions depend upon a key premise: The Government owns tainted assets before a defendant is convicted. That premise is quite incorrect, for the common law and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents establish that the opposite is true. The Government does not own property subject to forfeiture, whether tainted or untainted, until the Government wins a judgment of forfeiture or the defendant is convicted. As Blackstone noted with emphasis, \xe2\x80\x9cgoods and chattels are forfeited by conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 380 (1769) (Blackstone). Justice Story likewise observed that \xe2\x80\x9cno right to the goods and chattels of the felon could be acquired by the crown by the mere commission of the offence; but the right attached only by the conviction of the offender.\xe2\x80\x9d The Palmyra, 12 Wheat. 1, 14 (1827); ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cIn the contemplation of the common law, the offender\xe2\x80\x99s right was not devested until the conviction\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

These authorities demonstrate that Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto cannot be distinguished based on \xe2\x80\x9cthe nature of the assets at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Title to the assets in those cases did not pass from the defendant to the Government until conviction. As a result, the assets restrained before conviction in Monsanto were on the same footing as the assets restrained here: There was probable cause to believe that the assets would belong to the Government upon conviction. But when the court issued its restraining order, they did not. The Government had no greater ownership interest in Monsanto\xe2\x80\x99s tainted assets than it has in Luis\xe2\x80\x99 substitute assets.

\n

The plurality seeks to avoid this conclusion by relying on the relation-back doctrine. In its view the doctrine gives the Government title to tainted assets upon the commission of a crime rather than upon conviction or judgment of forfeiture. Even assuming, as this reasoning does, that the relation-back doctrine applies only to tainted assets\xe2\x80\x94but see United States v. McHan, 345 F.\xc2\xa03d 262, 270\xe2\x80\x93272 (CA4 2003)\xe2\x80\x94the doctrine does not do the work the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s analysis requires.

\n

The relation-back doctrine, which is incorporated in some forfeiture statutes, see, e.g.,\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7853(c), has its origins in the common law. Under this legal construct, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s title to certain types of forfeitable property relates back to the time at which the defendant committed the crime giving rise to the forfeiture. See 4 Blackstone 375 (\xe2\x80\x9cforfeiture [of real estates] relates backwards to the time of the treason committed; so as to avoid all intermediate sales and incumbrances\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Parcel of Rumson, N. J., Land,\n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 111,\n125 (1993)\n (plurality opinion). The doctrine\xe2\x80\x99s purpose is to prevent defendants from avoiding forfeiture by transferring their property to third parties. The doctrine, however, does not alter the time at which title to forfeitable property passes to the Government. Title is transferred only when a conviction is obtained or the assets are otherwise forfeited; it is only once this precondition is met that relation back to the time of the offense is permitted. See ibid. (The relation-back doctrine\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfictional and retroactive vesting\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cnot self-executing\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 132 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe relation-back rule applies only in cases where the Government\xe2\x80\x99s title has been consummated by seizure, suit, and judgment, or decree of condemnation, whereupon the doctrine of relation carries back the title to the commission of the offense\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks, brackets, and citations omitted)); United States v. Grundy, 3 Cranch 337, 350\xe2\x80\x93351 (1806) (Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J., opinion for the Court) (a forfeitable asset does not \xe2\x80\x9cves[t] in the government until some legal step shall be taken for the assertion of its right\xe2\x80\x9d); 4 Blackstone 375 (\xe2\x80\x9cBut, though after attainder the forfeiture relates back to the time of the treason committed, yet it does not take effect unless an attainder be had\xe2\x80\x9d). In short, forfeitable property does not belong to the Government in any sense before judgment or conviction. Cf. ante, at 9 (plurality opinion). Until the Government wins a judgment or conviction, \xe2\x80\x9csomeone else owns the property.\xe2\x80\x9d Parcel of Rumson, supra, at 127.

\n

The plurality is correct to note that Caplin & Drysdale discussed the relation-back provision in the forfeiture statute at issue. The Caplin & Drysdale Court did not do so, however, to suggest that forfeitable assets can be restrained only when the assets are tainted. Rather, the Court referred to the provision to rebut the law firm\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the United States has less of an interest in forfeitable property than robbery victims have in their stolen property. 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 627\xe2\x80\x93628. More to the point, central to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was its observation that, because the Government obtained \xe2\x80\x9ctitle to [the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] assets upon conviction,\xe2\x80\x9d it would be \xe2\x80\x9cpeculiar\xe2\x80\x9d to hold that the\n \nSixth Amendment still gave the defendant the right to pay his attorney with those assets. Id., at 628. Monsanto reinforced that view, holding that the pretrial restraint of assets\xe2\x80\x94money to which the Government does not yet have title\xe2\x80\x94is permissible even when the defendant wants to use those assets to pay for counsel. 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 616. True, the assets in Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto happened to be derived from the criminal activity alleged; but the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning in those cases was based on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s entitlement to recoup money from criminals who have profited from their crimes, not on tracing or identifying the actual assets connected to the crime. For this reason, the principle the Court announced in those cases applies whenever the Government obtains (or will obtain) title to assets upon conviction. Nothing in either case depended on the assets being tainted or justifies refusing to apply the rule from those cases here.

\n

The plurality makes much of various statutory provisions that, in its view, give the United States a superior interest before trial in tainted assets but not untainted ones. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. That view, however, turns not on any reasoning specific to the\n \nSixth Amendment but rather on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 differential treatment of tainted versus untainted assets. The plurality makes no attempt to explain why Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision in \xc2\xa71345 to permit the pretrial restraint of substitute assets is not also relevant to its analysis. More to the point, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 statutory treatment of property is irrelevant to a\n \nSixth Amendment analysis. The protections afforded by the\n \nSixth Amendment should not turn on congressional whims.

\n

The plurality\xe2\x80\x99s concern over the implications of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position appears animated by a hypothetical future case where a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets are restrained not to return stolen funds but, for example, to pay a fine. That case, however, is not the case before the Court. Section 1345 authorizes pretrial restraints to preserve substitute assets, not to provide for fines greater than the amounts stolen. The holdings in Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto, and what should be the holding today, thus, do not address the result in a case involving a fine. The governmental interests at stake when a fine is at issue are quite separate and distinct from the interests implicated here. This case implicates the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in preventing the dissipation, transfer, and concealment of stolen funds, as well as its interest in preserving for victims any funds that remain. Those interests justify, in cases like this one, the pretrial restraint of substitute assets.

\n

IV

\n

The principle the plurality and Justice Thomas announce today\xe2\x80\x94that a defendant has a right to pay for an attorney with forfeitable assets so long as those assets are not related to or the direct proceeds of the crime alleged\xe2\x80\x94has far-reaching implications. There is no clear explanation why this principle does not extend to the exercise of other constitutional rights. \xe2\x80\x9cIf defendants have a right to spend forfeitable assets on attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, why not on exercises of the right to speak, practice one\xe2\x80\x99s religion, or travel?\xe2\x80\x9d Caplin & Drysdale, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 628. Nor does either opinion provide any way to distinguish between the restraint at issue here and other governmental interferences with a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets. If the restraint of Luis\xe2\x80\x99 assets violates the\n \nSixth Amendment, could the same be said of any imposition on a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets? Cf. id., at 631 (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]eizures of assets to secure potential tax liabilities .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may impair a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s ability to retain counsel .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [y]et these assessments have been upheld against constitutional attack\xe2\x80\x9d). If a defendant is fined in a prior matter, is the Government barred from collecting the fine if it will leave the defendant unable to afford a particular attorney in a current case? No explanation is pro-vided for what, if any, limits there are on the invented exemption for attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees.

\n

The result today also creates arbitrary distinctions between defendants. Money, after all, is fungible. There is no difference between a defendant who has preserved his or her own assets by spending stolen money and a defendant who has spent his or her own assets andpreserved stolen cash instead. Yet the plurality andconcurrence\xe2\x80\x94for different reasons\xe2\x80\x94find in the\n \nSixth Amendment the rule that greater protection is given to the defendant who, by spending, laundering, exporting, or concealing stolen money first, preserves his or her remaining funds for use on an attorney.

\n

The true winners today are sophisticated criminals who know how to make criminal proceeds look untainted. They do so every day. They \xe2\x80\x9cbuy cashier\xe2\x80\x99s checks, money orders, nonbank wire transfers, prepaid debit cards, and traveler\xe2\x80\x99s checks to use instead of cash for purchases or bank deposits.\xe2\x80\x9d Dept. of Treasury, National Money Laundering Risk Assessment 2015, p. 3. They structure their transactions to avoid triggering recordkeeping and reporting requirements. Ibid. And they open bank accounts in other people\xe2\x80\x99s names and through shell companies, all to disguise the origins of their funds. Ibid.

\n

The facts of this case illustrate the measures one might take to conceal or dispose of ill-gotten gains. In declarations relied on by the District Court, the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI) Special Agent investigating the case explained that \xe2\x80\x9cLuis transferred monies or caused the transfer of monies received from Medicare to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. family members and companies owned by family members,\xe2\x80\x9d including $1,471,000 to her husband, and over a million dollars to her children and former daughter-in-law. App. 72\xe2\x80\x9373. She also \xe2\x80\x9cused Medicare monies for foreign travel,\xe2\x80\x9d including approximately 31 trips to Mexico, \xe2\x80\x9cwhere she owns several properties and has numerous bank accounts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 73. She \xe2\x80\x9ctransferred Medicare monies overseas through international wire transfers to Mexico.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And the Government was \xe2\x80\x9cable to trace Medicare proceeds going into [all but one of the] bank account[s] owned by Defendant Luis and/or her companies listed in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x9d temporary restraining order. Id., at 74. No doubt Luis would have enjoyed her travel and expenditures even more had she known that, were her alleged wrongs discovered, a majority of the Justices would insist that she be allowed to pay her chosen legal team at the price they set rather than repay her victim.

\n

Notwithstanding that the Government established probable cause to believe that Luis committed numerous crimes and used the proceeds of those crimes to line her and her family\xe2\x80\x99s pockets, the plurality and Justice Thomas reward Luis\xe2\x80\x99 decision to spend the money she is accused of stealing rather than her own. They allow Luis to bankroll her private attorneys as well as \xe2\x80\x9cthe best and most industrious investigators, experts, paralegals, and law clerks\xe2\x80\x9d money can buy\xe2\x80\x94a legal defense team Luis claims she cannot otherwise afford. See Corrected Motion to Modify the Restraining Order in No. 12\xe2\x80\x93Civ\xe2\x80\x9323588, p.\xc2\xa013 (SD Fla., Nov. 16, 2012). The\n \nSixth Amendment does not provide such an unfettered right to counsel of choice.

\n

It is well settled that the right to counsel of choice is limited in important respects. A defendant cannot demand a lawyer who is not a member of the bar. Wheat v. United States,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 153,\n159 (1988)\n. Nor may a defendant insist on an attorney who has a conflict of interest. Id., at 159, 164. And, as quite relevant here, \xe2\x80\x9ca defendant may not insist on representation by an attorney he cannot afford.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 159. As noted earlier, \xe2\x80\x9cthose who do not have the means to hire their own lawyers have no cognizable complaint so long as they are adequately represented by attorneys appointed by the courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Caplin & Drysdale, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 624. As a result of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order, Luis simply cannot afford the legal team she desires unless they are willing to represent her without advance payment. For\n \nSixth Amendment purposes, the only question here is whether Luis\xe2\x80\x99 right to adequate representation is protected. That question is not before the Court. Neither Luis nor the plurality nor Justice Thomas suggests that Luis will receive inadequate representation if she is not able to use the restrained funds. And this is for good reason. Given the large volume of defendants in the criminal justice system who rely on public representation, it would be troubling to suggest that a defendant who might be represented by a public defender will receive inadequate representation. See generally T. Giovanni & R. Patel, Gideon at 50: Three Reforms to Revive the Right to Counsel 1 (2013), online at http://www.brennancenter.org/sites/default/files/publications/Gideon_Report_040913.pdf (as last visited Mar. 28, 2016). Since Luis cannot afford the legal team she desires, and because there is no indication that she will receive inadequate representation as a result, she does not have a cognizable\n \nSixth Amendment complaint.

\n

The plurality does warn that accepting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position \xe2\x80\x9cwould\xe2\x80\x94by increasing the government-paid-defender workload\xe2\x80\x94render less effective the basic right the\n \nSixth Amendment seeks to protect.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. Public-defender offices, the plurality suggests, already lack sufficient attorneys to meet nationally recommended caseload standards. Ibid. But concerns about the caseloads of public-defender offices do not justify a constitutional command to treat a defendant accused of committing a lucrative crime differently than a defendant who is indigent from the outset. The Constitution does not require victims of property crimes to fund subsidies for members of the private defense bar.

\n

Because the rule announced today is anchored in the\n \nSixth Amendment, moreover, it will frustrate not only the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s use of \xc2\xa71345 but also the States\xe2\x80\x99 administration of their forfeiture schemes. Like the Federal Government, States also face criminals who engage in money laundering through extensive enterprises that extend to other States and beyond. Where a defendant has put stolen money beyond a State\xe2\x80\x99s reach, a State should not be precluded from freezing the assets the defendant has in hand. The obstacle that now stands in the States\xe2\x80\x99 way is not found in the Constitution. It is of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s making.

\n

Finally, the plurality posits that its decision \xe2\x80\x9cshould prove workable\xe2\x80\x9d because courts \xe2\x80\x9chave experience separating tainted assets from untainted assets, just as they have experience determining how much money is needed to cover the costs of a lawyer.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. Neither of these assurances is adequate.

\n

As to the first, the plurality cites a number of sources for the proposition that courts have rules that allow them to implement the distinction it adopts. Ibid. Those rules, however, demonstrate the illogic of the conclusion that there is a meaningful difference between the actual dollars stolen and the dollars of equivalent value in a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s bank account. The plurality appears to agree that, if a defendant is indicted for stealing $1 million, the Government can obtain an order preventing the defendant from spending the $1 million he or she is believed to have stolen. The situation gets more complicated, however, when the defendant deposits the stolen $1 million into an account that already has $1 million. If the defendant then spends $1 million from the account, it cannot be determined with certainty whether the money spent was stolen money rather than money the defendant already had. The question arises, then, whether the Government can restrain the remaining million.

\n

One of the treatises on which the plurality relies answers that question. The opinion cites A. Scott\xe2\x80\x99s Law of Trusts to support the claim that \xe2\x80\x9cthe law has tracing rules that help courts implement the kind of distinction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. require[d] in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. The treatise says that, if a \xe2\x80\x9cwrongdoer has mingled misappropriated money with his own money and later makes withdrawals from the mingled fund,\xe2\x80\x9d assuming the withdrawals do not result in a zero balance, a person who has an interest in the misappropriated money can recover it from the amount remaining in the account. 4 A. Scott, Law of Trusts \xc2\xa7518, pp. 3309\xe2\x80\x933310 (1956). Based on this rule, one would expect the plurality to agree that, in the above hypothetical, the Government could restrain up to the full amount of the stolen funds\xe2\x80\x94that is, the full $1 million\xe2\x80\x94without having to establish whether the $1 million the defendant spent was stolen money or not. If that is so, it is hard to see why its opinion treats as different a situation where the defendant has two bank accounts\xe2\x80\x94one with the $1 million from before the crime and one with the stolen $1 million. If the defendant spends the money in the latter account, the Government should be allowed to freeze the money in the former account in the same way it could if the defendant spent the money out of a single, commingled account. The\n \nSixth Amendment provides no justification for the decision to mandate different treatment in these all-but-identical situations.

\n

The plurality sees \xe2\x80\x9clittle reason to worry\xe2\x80\x9d about defendants circumventing forfeiture because courts can use rules like the tracing rule discussed above. Ante, at 16. It also asserts that these rules \xe2\x80\x9cwill likely .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. prevent Luis from benefiting from many of [her] money transfers and purchases.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That proposition is doubtful where, as here, \xe2\x80\x9ca lot of money was taken out in cash from the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s bank accounts\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[y]ou can\xe2\x80\x99t trace cash.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 155. Even were that not the case, this assertion fails to appreciate that it takes time to trace tainted assets. As the FBI agent testified, at the time of the hearing both the tracing and the FBI\xe2\x80\x99s analysis were \xe2\x80\x9cstill ongoing.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The whole purpose of a pretrial restraint under \xc2\xa71345 is to maintain the status quo in cases, like this one, where a defendant is accused of committing crimes that involve fungible property, e.g., a banking law violation or a federal health care offense. The plurality\xe2\x80\x99s approach serves to benefit the most sophisticated of criminals whose web of transfers and concealment will take the longest to un-ravel. For if the Government cannot establish at the outset that every dollar subject to restraint is derived from the crime alleged, the defendant can spend that money on whatever defense team he or she desires.

\n

Of equal concern is the assertion that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to counsel of choice is limited to only those attorneys who charge a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable fee.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. If Luis has a right to use the restrained substitute assets to pay for the counsel of her choice, then why can she not hire the most expensive legal team she can afford? In the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s view, the reason Luis can use the restrained funds for an attorney is because they are still hers. But if that is so, then she should be able to use all $2 million of her remaining assets to pay for a lawyer. The plurality\xe2\x80\x99s willingness to curtail the very right it recognizes reflects the need to preserve substitute assets from further dissipation.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Today\xe2\x80\x99s ruling abandons the principle established in Caplin & Drysdale and Monsanto. In its place is an approach that creates perverse incentives and provides protection for defendants who spend stolen money rather than their own.

\n

In my respectful view this is incorrect, and the judgment of the Court of Appeals should be affirmed.

Justice Kagan, dissenting.

\n

I find United States v. Monsanto,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600 (1989)\n, a troubling decision. It is one thing to hold, as this Court did in Caplin & Drysdale, Chartered v. United States,\n\n491 U.\xc2\xa0S. 617 (1989)\n, that a convicted felon has no\n \nSixth Amendment right to pay his lawyer with funds adjudged forfeitable. Following conviction, such assets belong to the Government, and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no constitutional principle that gives one person the right to give another\xe2\x80\x99s property to a third party.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 628. But it is quite another thing to say that the Government may, prior to trial, freeze assets that a defendant needs to hire an attorney, based on nothing more than \xe2\x80\x9cprobable cause to believe that the property will ultimately be proved forfeitable.\xe2\x80\x9d Monsanto, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 615. At that time, \xe2\x80\x9cthe presumption of innocence still applies,\xe2\x80\x9d and the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the assets is wholly contingent on future judgments of conviction and forfeiture. Kaley v. United States, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 6). I am not altogether convinced that, in this decidedly different circumstance, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in recovering the proceeds of crime ought to trump the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s (often highly consequential) right to retain counsel of choice.

\n

But the correctness of Monsanto is not at issue today. Petitioner Sila Luis has not asked this Court either to overrule or to modify that decision; she argues only that it does not answer the question presented here. And because Luis takes Monsanto as a given, the Court must do so as well.

\n

On that basis, I agree with the principal dissent that Monsanto controls this case. See ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x937 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). Because the Government has established probable cause to believe that it will eventually recover Luis\xe2\x80\x99s assets, she has no right to use them to pay an attorney. See Monsanto, 491 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 616 (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]o constitutional violation occurs when, after probable cause is adequately established, the Government obtains an order barring a defendant from .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. dissipating his assets prior to trial\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The plurality reaches a contrary result only by differentiating between the direct fruits of criminal activity and substitute assets that become subject to forfeiture when the defendant has run through those proceeds. See ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. But as the principal dissent shows, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s and the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s respective legal interests in those two kinds of property, prior to a judgment of guilt, are exactly the same: The defendant maintains ownership of either type, with the Government holding only a contingent interest. See ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x9310. Indeed, the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s use of the word \xe2\x80\x9ctainted,\xe2\x80\x9d to describe assets at the pre-conviction stage, makes an unwarranted assumption about the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. See ante, at 5 (characterizing such assets as, for example, \xe2\x80\x9crobber\xe2\x80\x99s loot\xe2\x80\x9d). Because the Government has not yet shown that the defendant committed the crime charged, it also has not shown that allegedly tainted assets are actually so.

\n

And given that money is fungible, the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s approach leads to utterly arbitrary distinctions as among criminal defendants who are in fact guilty. See ante, at 12 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). The thief who immediately dissipates his ill-gotten gains and thereby preserves his other assets is no more deserving of chosen counsel than the one who spends those two pots of money in reverse order. Yet the plurality would enable only the first defendant, and not the second, to hire the lawyer he wants. I cannot believe the\n \nSixth Amendment draws that irrational line, much as I sympathize with the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s effort to cabin Monsanto. Accordingly, I would affirm the judgment below.
