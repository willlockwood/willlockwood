BANK MARKAZI, aka CENTRAL BANK OF IRAN v. PETERSON et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93770.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 13, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 20, 2016

\n

American nationals may seek money damages from state sponsors of terrorism in the courts of the United States. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71605A. Prevailing plaintiffs, however, often face practical and legal difficulties enforcing their judgments. To place beyond dispute the availability of certain assets for satisfaction of judgments rendered in terrorism cases against Iran, Congress enacted the Iran Threat Reduction and Syria Human Rights Act of 2012. As relevant here, the Act makes a designated set of assets available to satisfy the judgments underlying a consolidated enforcement proceeding which the statute identifies by the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s docket number.\n\n22 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa78772. Section 8772(a)(2) requires a court, before allowing execution against these assets, to determine, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9cwhether Iran holds equitable title to, or the beneficial interest in, the assets.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Respondents\xe2\x80\x94more than 1,000 victims of Iran-sponsored acts of terrorism, their estate representatives, and surviving family members\xe2\x80\x94rank within 16 discrete groups, each of which brought suit against Iran. To enforce judgments they obtained by default, the 16 groups moved for turnover of about $1.75 billion in bond assets held in a New York bank account\xe2\x80\x94assets that, respondents alleged, were owned by Bank Markazi, the Central Bank of Iran. The turnover proceeding began in 2008. In 2012, the judgment holders updated their motions to include execution claims under \xc2\xa78772. Bank Markazi maintained that \xc2\xa78772 could not withstand inspection under the separation-of-powers doctrine, contending that Congress had usurped the judicial role by directing a particular result in the pending enforcement proceeding. The District Court disagreed, concluding that \xc2\xa78772 permissibly changed the law applicable in a pending litigation. The Second Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section 8772 does not violate the separation of powers. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9324.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Article III of the Constitution establishes an independent Judiciary with the \xe2\x80\x9cprovince and duty .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to say what the law is\xe2\x80\x9d in particular cases and controversies. Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 177. Necessarily, that endowment of authority blocks Congress from \xe2\x80\x9crequir[ing] federal courts to exercise the judicial power in a manner that Article III forbids.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut v. Spendthrift Farm, Inc.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 211\n. Although Article III bars Congress from telling a court how to apply pre-existing law to particular circumstances, Robertson v. Seattle Audubon Soc.,\n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429\n\xe2\x80\x93439, Congress may amend a law and make the amended prescription retroactively applicable in pending cases, Landgraf v. USI Film Products,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244\n\xe2\x80\x93268; United States v. Schooner Peggy, 1 Cranch 103, 110. In United States v. Klein, 13 Wall. 128, 146, this Court enigmatically observed that Congress may not \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe rules of decision to the Judicial Department .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in [pending] cases.\xe2\x80\x9d More recent decisions have clarified that Klein does not inhibit Congress from \xe2\x80\x9camend[ing] applicable law.\xe2\x80\x9d Robertson, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 441; Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 218. Section 8772 does just that: It requires a court to apply a new legal standard in a pending postjudgment enforcement proceeding. No different result obtains because, as Bank Markazi argues, the outcome of applying \xc2\xa78772 to the facts in the proceeding below was a \xe2\x80\x9cforegone conclusio[n].\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 47. A statute does not impinge on judicial power when it directs courts to apply a new legal standard to undisputed facts. See Pope v. United States,\n\n323 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Nor is \xc2\xa78772 invalid because, as Bank Markazi further objects, it prescribes a rule for a single, pending case identified by caption and docket number. The amended law upheld in Robertson also applied to cases identified in the statute by caption and docket number. 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 440. Moreover, \xc2\xa78772 is not an instruction governing one case only: It facilitates execution of judgments in 16 suits. While consolidated for administrative purposes at the execution stage, the judgment-execution claims were not independent of the original actions for damages and each retained its separate character. In any event, the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s argument rests on the flawed assumption that legislation must be generally applicable. See Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 239, n.\xc2\xa09. This Court and lower courts have upheld as a valid exercise of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 legislative power laws governing one or a very small number of specific subjects. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Adding weight to this decision, \xc2\xa78772 is an exercise of congressional authority regarding foreign affairs, a domain in which the controlling role of the political branches is both necessary and proper. Measures taken by the political branches to control the disposition of foreign-state property, including blocking specific foreign-state assets or making them available for attachment, have never been rejected as invasions upon the Article III judicial power. Cf. Dames & Moore v. Regan,\n\n453 U.\xc2\xa0S. 654\n. Notably, before enactment of the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act, the Executive regularly made case-specific determinations whether sovereign immunity should be recognized, and courts accepted those determinations as binding. See, e.g., Republic of Austria v. Altmann,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677\n\xe2\x80\x93691. This practice, too, was never perceived as an encroachment on the federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 jurisdiction. Dames & Moore, 453 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 684\xe2\x80\x93685. Pp.\xc2\xa021\xe2\x80\x9323.

BANK MARKAZI, aka THE CENTRAL BANK OF IRAN, PETITIONER v. DEBORAH PETERSON, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.[1]*

\n

A provision of the Iran Threat Reduction and Syria Human Rights Act of 2012,\n\n22 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa78772, makes available for postjudgment execution a set of assets held at a New York bank for Bank Markazi, the Central Bank of Iran. The assets would partially satisfy judgments gained in separate actions by over 1,000 victims of terrorist acts sponsored by Iran. The judgments remain unpaid. Section 8772 is an unusual statute: It designates a particular set of assets and renders them available to satisfy the liability and damages judgments underlying a consoli-dated enforcement proceeding that the statute identifies by the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s docket number. The question raised by petitioner Bank Markazi: Does \xc2\xa78772 violate the separation of powers by purporting to change the law for, and directing a particular result in, a single pending case?

\n

Section 8772, we hold, does not transgress constraints placed on Congress and the President by the Constitution. The statute, we point out, is not fairly portrayed as a \xe2\x80\x9cone-case-only regime.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 27. Rather, it covers a category of postjudgment execution claims filed by numerous plaintiffs who, in multiple civil actions, obtained evidence-based judgments against Iran together amounting to billions of dollars. Section 8772 subjects the designated assets to execution \xe2\x80\x9cto satisfy any judgment\xe2\x80\x9d against Iran for damages caused by specified acts of terrorism. \xc2\xa78772(a)(1) (emphasis added). Congress, our decisions make clear, may amend the law and make the change applicable to pending cases, even when the amendment is outcome determinative.

\n

Adding weight to our decision, Congress passed, and the President signed, \xc2\xa78772 in furtherance of their stance on a matter of foreign policy. Action in that realm warrants respectful review by courts. The Executive has histori-cally made case-specific sovereign-immunity determinations to which courts have deferred. And exercise by Congress and the President of control over claims against foreign governments, as well as foreign-government-owned property in the United States, is hardly a novelty. In accord with the courts below, we perceive in \xc2\xa78772 no violation of separation-of-powers principles, and no threat to the independence of the Judiciary.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

We set out here statutory provisions relevant to this case. American nationals may file suit against state sponsors of terrorism in the courts of the United States. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71605A. Specifically, they may seek \xe2\x80\x9cmoney damages .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. against a foreign state for personal injury or death that was caused by\xe2\x80\x9d acts of terrorism, including \xe2\x80\x9ctorture, extrajudicial killing, aircraft sabotage, hostage taking, or the provision of material support\xe2\x80\x9d to terrorist activities. \xc2\xa71605A(a)(1). This authorization\xe2\x80\x94known as the \xe2\x80\x9cterrorism exception\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94is among enumerated excep-

\n

\n

tions prescribed in the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 (FSIA) to the general rule of sovereign immunity.[2]

\n

Victims of state-sponsored terrorism, like others proceeding under an FSIA exception, may obtain a judgment against a foreign state on \xe2\x80\x9cestablish[ing] [their] claim[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by evidence satisfactory to the court.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71608(e). After gaining a judgment, however, plaintiffs proceeding under the terrorism exception \xe2\x80\x9chave often faced practical and legal difficulties\xe2\x80\x9d at the enforcement stage. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 2. Subject to stated excep-tions, the FSIA shields foreign-state property from execution. \xc2\xa71609. When the terrorism exception was adopted, only foreign-state property located in the United States and \xe2\x80\x9cused for a commercial activity\xe2\x80\x9d was available for the satisfaction of judgments. \xc2\xa71610(a)(7), (b)(3). Further limiting judgment-enforcement prospects, the FSIA shields from execution property \xe2\x80\x9cof a foreign central bank or monetary authority held for its own account.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71611(b)(1).

\n

To lessen these enforcement difficulties, Congress enacted the Terrorism Risk Insurance Act of 2002 (TRIA), which authorizes execution of judgments obtained under the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s terrorism exception against \xe2\x80\x9cthe blocked assets of [a] terrorist party (including the blocked assets of any agency or instrumentality of that terrorist party).\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7201(a),\n\n116Stat.\n2337, note following\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71610.

\n

A \xe2\x80\x9cblocked asset\xe2\x80\x9d is any asset seized by the Executive Branch pursuant to either the Trading with the Enemy Act (TWEA),\n\n40Stat.\n411,\n50 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App. 1 et\xc2\xa0seq., or the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA),\n\n91Stat.\n1625,\n\n50 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71570 et\xc2\xa0seq. See TRIA \xc2\xa7201(d)(2). Both measures, TWEA and IEEPA, authorize the President to freeze the assets of \xe2\x80\x9cforeign enemy state[s]\xe2\x80\x9d and their agencies and instrumentalities. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 25. These blocking regimes \xe2\x80\x9cput control of foreign assets in the hands of the President so that he may dispose of them in the manner that best furthers the United States\xe2\x80\x99 foreign-relations and national-security interests.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).[3]

\n

Invoking his authority under the IEEPA, the President, in February 2012, issued an Executive Order blocking \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll property and interests in property of any Iranian financial institution, including the Central Bank of Iran, that are in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Exec. Order No. 13599, 3 CFR 215 (2012 Comp.). The availability of these assets for execution, however, was contested.[4]

\n

\n

To place beyond dispute the availability of some of the Executive Order No. 13599-blocked assets for satisfaction of judgments rendered in terrorism cases, Congress passed the statute at issue here: \xc2\xa7502 of the Iran Threat Reduction and Syria Human Rights Act of 2012,\n\n126Stat.\n1258,\n\n22 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa78772. Enacted as a freestanding measure, not as an amendment to the FSIA or the TRIA,[5] \xc2\xa78772 provides that, if a court makes specified findings, \xe2\x80\x9ca financial asset .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be subject to execution .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in order to sat-isfy any judgment to the extent of any compensatory dam-ages awarded against Iran for damages for personal injury or death caused by\xe2\x80\x9d the acts of terrorism enumerated in the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s terrorism exception. \xc2\xa78772(a)(1). Section 8772(b) defines as available for execution by holders of terrorism judgments against Iran \xe2\x80\x9cthe financial assets that are identified in and the subject of proceedings in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York in Peterson et al. v. Islamic Republic of Iran et al., Case No. 10 Civ. 4518 (BSJ) (GWG), that were restrained by restraining notices and levies secured by the plaintiffs in those proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Before allowing execution against an asset described in \xc2\xa78772(b), a court must determine that the asset is:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(A) held in the United States for a foreign securities intermediary doing business in the United States;

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(B) a blocked asset (whether or not subsequently unblocked) .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. ; and

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(C) equal in value to a financial asset of Iran, including an asset of the central bank or monetary authority of the Government of Iran .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78772(a)(1).

\n

In addition, the court in which execution is sought must

\n

determine \xe2\x80\x9cwhether Iran holds equitable title to, or the beneficial interest in, the assets .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and that no other person possesses a constitutionally protected interest in the assets .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. under the\n \nFifth Amendment to the Constitution of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78772(a)(2).

\n

B

\n

Respondents are victims of Iran-sponsored acts of terrorism, their estate representatives, and surviving family members. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 52a\xe2\x80\x9353a; Brief for Respondents 6. Numbering more than 1,000, respondents rank within 16 discrete groups, each of which brought a lawsuit against Iran pursuant to the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s terrorism exception. App. to Brief for Respondents 1a\xe2\x80\x932a. All of the suits were filed in United States District Court for the District of Columbia.[6] Upon finding a clear evidentiary basis for Iran\xe2\x80\x99s liability to each suitor, the court entered judgments by default. See, e.g., Peterson v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 264 F. Supp. 2d 46, 49 (2003). The majority of respondents sought redress for injuries suffered in connection with the 1983 bombing of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Marine barracks in Beirut, Lebanon. App. to Pet. for Cert. 21a.[7] \xe2\x80\x9cTogether, [respondents] have obtained billions of dollars in judgments against Iran, the vast majority of which remain unpaid.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53a.[8] The validity of those judgments is not in dispute. Id., at 55a.

\n

To enforce their judgments, the 16 groups of respondents first registered them in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71963 (\xe2\x80\x9cA judgment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may be registered .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in any other district .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. A judgment so registered shall have the same effect as a judgment of the district court of the district where registered and may be enforced in like manner.\xe2\x80\x9d). They then moved under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 69 for turnover of about $1.75 billion in bond

\n

assets held in a New York bank account\xe2\x80\x94assets that, respondents alleged, were owned by Bank Markazi. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 52a\xe2\x80\x9354a, 60a, and n.\xc2\xa01; Second Amended Complaint in No. 10\xe2\x80\x93CIV\xe2\x80\x934518 (SDNY), p.\xc2\xa06.[9] This turnover proceeding began in 2008 when the terrorism judgment holders in Peterson, 264 F. Supp. 2d 46, filed writs of execution and the District Court restrained the bonds. App. to Pet. for Cert. 14a\xe2\x80\x9315a, 62a. Other groups of terrorism judgment holders\xe2\x80\x94some of which had filed their own writs of execution against the bonds\xe2\x80\x94were joined in No. 10\xe2\x80\x93CIV\xe2\x80\x934518, the Peterson enforcement proceeding, through a variety of procedural mechanisms.[10] It is this consolidated judgment-enforcement proceed-ing and assets restrained in that proceeding that \xc2\xa78772 addresses.

\n

Although the enforcement proceeding was initiated prior to the issuance of Executive Order No. 13599 and the enactment of \xc2\xa78772, the judgment holders updated their motions in 2012 to include execution claims under \xc2\xa78772. Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 Supplemental Memorandum of Law in Support of Their Motion for Partial Summary Judgment in No. 10\xe2\x80\x93CIV\xe2\x80\x934518 (SDNY).[11] Making the findings necessary under \xc2\xa78772, the District Court ordered the requested turn-over. App. to Pet. for Cert. 109a.[12]

\n

In reaching its decision, the court reviewed the financial history of the assets and other record evidence showing that Bank Markazi owned the assets. See id., at 111a\xe2\x80\x93113a, and n.\xc2\xa017. Since at least early 2008, the court recounted, the bond assets have been held in a New York account at Citibank directly controlled by Clearstream Banking, S.\xc2\xa0A. (Clearstream), a Luxembourg-based company that serves \xe2\x80\x9cas an intermediary between financial institutions worldwide.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 56a\xe2\x80\x9357a (internal quotation makes omitted). Initially, Clearstream held the assets for Bank Markazi and deposited interest earned on the bonds into Bank Markazi\xe2\x80\x99s Clearstream account. At some point in 2008, Bank Markazi instructed Clearstream to position another intermediary\xe2\x80\x94Banca UBAE, S.\xc2\xa0p.\xc2\xa0A., an Italian bank\xe2\x80\x94between the bonds and Bank Markazi. Id., at 58a\xe2\x80\x9359a. Thereafter, Clearstream deposited interest payments in UBAE\xe2\x80\x99s account, which UBAE then remitted to Bank Markazi. Id., at 60a\xe2\x80\x9361a.[13]

\n

Resisting turnover of the bond assets, Bank Markazi and Clearstream, as the District Court observed, \xe2\x80\x9cfilled

\n

the proverbial kitchen sink with arguments.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 111a. They argued, inter alia, the absence of subject-matter and personal jurisdiction, id., at 73a\xe2\x80\x93104a, asserting that the blocked assets were not assets \xe2\x80\x9cof\xe2\x80\x9d the Bank, see supra, at 4, n.\xc2\xa03, and that the assets in question were located in Luxembourg, not New York, App. to Pet. for Cert. 100a. Several of their objections to execution became irrelevant following enactment of \xc2\xa78772, which, the District Court noted, \xe2\x80\x9csweeps away .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. federal or state law impediments that might otherwise exist, so long as the appropriate judicial determination is made.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 73a; \xc2\xa78772(a)(1) (Act applies \xe2\x80\x9cnotwithstanding any other provision of law\xe2\x80\x9d). After \xc2\xa78772\xe2\x80\x99s passage, Bank Markazi changed its defense. It conceded that Iran held the requisite \xe2\x80\x9cequitable title to, or beneficial interest in, the assets,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78772(a)(2)(A), but maintained that \xc2\xa78772 could not withstand inspection under the separation-of-powers doctrine. See Defendant Bank Markazi\xe2\x80\x99s Supplemental Memorandum of Law in Opposition to Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 Motion for Partial Summary Judgment in No. 10\xe2\x80\x93CIV\xe2\x80\x934518 (SDNY), pp. 1\xe2\x80\x933, 10\xe2\x80\x9316.[14]

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n passing \xc2\xa78772,\xe2\x80\x9d Bank Markazi argued, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress effectively dictated specific factual findings in connection with a specific litigation\xe2\x80\x94invading the province of the courts.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 114a. The District Court disagreed. The ownership determinations \xc2\xa78772 required, see supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x939, the court said, \xe2\x80\x9c[were] not mere fig leaves,\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cit [was] quite possible that the [c]ourt could have found that defendants raised a triable issue as to whether the [b]locked [a]ssets were owned by Iran, or that Clearstream and/or UBAE ha[d] some form of beneficial or equitable interest.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 115a. Observing from the voluminous filings that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here [was] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. plenty .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to [litigate],\xe2\x80\x9d the court described \xc2\xa78772 as a measure that \xe2\x80\x9cmerely chang[es] the law applicable to pending cases; it does not usurp the adjudicative function assigned to federal courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). Further, the court reminded, \xe2\x80\x9cIran\xe2\x80\x99s liability and its required payment of damages was .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. established years prior to the [enactment of \xc2\xa78772]\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t issue [here] is merely execution [of judgments] on assets present in this district.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 116a.[15]

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit unanimously affirmed. Peterson v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 758 F.\xc2\xa03d 185 (2014).[16] On appeal, Bank Markazi again argued that \xc2\xa78772 violates the separation of powers \xe2\x80\x9cby compelling the courts to reach a predetermined result in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 191. In accord with the District Court, the Second

\n

Circuit responded that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa78772 does not compel judicial findings [or results] under old law\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9crather, it retroac-tively changes the law applicable in this case, a permissible exercise of legislative authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Congress may so prescribe, the appeals court noted, \xe2\x80\x9ceven when the result under the revised law is clear.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

To consider the separation-of-powers question Bank Markazi presents, we granted certiorari, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015), and now affirm.[17]

\n

II

\n

Article III of the Constitution establishes an independent Judiciary, a Third Branch of Government with the \xe2\x80\x9cprovince and duty .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to say what the law is\xe2\x80\x9d in particular cases and controversies. Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 177 (1803). Necessarily, that endowment of authority blocks Congress from \xe2\x80\x9crequir[ing] federal courts to exercise the judicial power in a manner that Article III forbids.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut v. Spendthrift Farm, Inc.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 211,\n218 (1995)\n. Congress, no doubt, \xe2\x80\x9cmay not usurp a court\xe2\x80\x99s power to interpret and apply the law to the [circum-stances] before it,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Former Senior Officials of the Office of Legal Counsel as Amici Curiae 3, 6, for \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hose who apply [a] rule to particular cases, must of necessity expound and interpret that rule,\xe2\x80\x9d Marbury, 1 Cranch, at 177.[18] And our decisions place off limits to Congress \xe2\x80\x9cvest[ing] review of the decisions of Article III courts in officials of the Executive Branch.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 218 (citing Hayburn\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 2 Dall. 409 (1792), and, e.g., Chicago & Southern Air Lines, Inc. v. Waterman S. S. Corp.,\n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103,\n114 (1948)\n). Congress, we have also held, may not \xe2\x80\x9cretroactively comman[d] the federal courts to reopen final judgments.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 219.

\n

A

\n

Citing United States v. Klein, 13 Wall. 128 (1872), Bank Markazi urges a further limitation. Congress treads impermissibly on judicial turf, the Bank maintains, when it \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe[s] rules of decision to the Judicial Department .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in [pending] cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 146. According to the Bank, \xc2\xa78772 fits that description. Brief for Petitioner 19, 43. Klein has been called \xe2\x80\x9ca deeply puzzling decision,\xe2\x80\x9d Meltzer, Congress, Courts, and Constitutional Remedies, 86 Geo. L. J. 2537, 2538 (1998).[19] More recent decisions, however, have made it clear that Klein does not inhibit Congress from \xe2\x80\x9camend[ing] applicable law.\xe2\x80\x9d Robertson v. Seattle Audubon Soc.,\n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429,\n441 (1992)\n; see id., at 437\xe2\x80\x93438; Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 218 (Klein\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprohibition does not take hold when Congress \xe2\x80\x98amend[s] applicable law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Robertson, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 441)). Section 8772, we hold, did just that.

\n

Klein involved Civil War legislation providing that persons whose property had been seized and sold in wartime could recover the proceeds of the sale in the Court of Claims upon proof that they had \xe2\x80\x9cnever given any aid or comfort to the present rebellion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ch. 120, \xc2\xa73,\n\n12Stat.\n820; see Klein, 13 Wall., at 139. In 1863, President Lincoln pardoned \xe2\x80\x9cpersons who .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. participated in the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. rebellion\xe2\x80\x9d if they swore an oath of loyalty to the United States. Presidential Proclamation No. 11,\n\n13Stat.\n737. One of the persons so pardoned was a southerner named Wilson, whose cotton had been seized and sold by Government agents. Klein was the administrator of Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s estate. 13 Wall., at 132. In United States v. Padelford, 9 Wall. 531, 543 (1870), this Court held that the recipient of a Presidential pardon must be treated as loyal, i.e., the pardon operated as \xe2\x80\x9ca complete substitute for proof that [the recipient] gave no aid or comfort to the rebellion.\xe2\x80\x9d Thereafter, Klein prevailed in an action in the Court of Claims, yielding an award of $125,300 for Wilson\xe2\x80\x99s cotton. 13 Wall., at 132.

\n

During the pendency of an appeal to this Court from the Court of Claims judgment in Klein, Congress enacted a statute providing that no pardon should be admissible as proof of loyalty. Moreover, acceptance of a pardon without disclaiming participation in the rebellion would serve as conclusive evidence of disloyalty. The statute directed the Court of Claims and the Supreme Court to dismiss for want of jurisdiction any claim based on a pardon.\n\n16Stat.\n235; R. Fallon, J. Manning, D. Meltzer, & D. Shapiro, Hart and Wechsler\xe2\x80\x99s The Federal Courts and the Federal System 323, n. 29 (7th ed. 2015) (Hart and Wechsler). Affirming the judgment of the Court of Claims, this Court held that Congress had no authority to \xe2\x80\x9cimpai[r] the effect of a pardon,\xe2\x80\x9d for the Constitution entrusted the pardon power \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o the executive alone.\xe2\x80\x9d Klein, 13 Wall., at 147. The Legislature, the Court stated, \xe2\x80\x9ccannot change the effect of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a pardon any more than the executive can change a law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 148. Lacking authority to impair the pardon power of the Executive, Congress could not \xe2\x80\x9cdirec[t] [a] court to be instrumental to that end.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In other words, the statute in Klein infringed the judicial power, not because it left too little for courts to do, but because it attempted to direct the result without altering the legal standards governing the effect of a pardon\xe2\x80\x94standards Congress was powerless to prescribe. See id., at 146\xe2\x80\x93147; Robertson, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 438 (Congress may not \xe2\x80\x9ccompe[l] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. findings or results under old law\xe2\x80\x9d).[20]

\n

Bank Markazi, as earlier observed, supra, at 13, argues that \xc2\xa78772 conflicts with Klein. The Bank points to a statement in the Klein opinion questioning whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislature may prescribe rules of decision to the Judicial Department .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in cases pending before it.\xe2\x80\x9d 13 Wall., at 146. One cannot take this language from Klein \xe2\x80\x9cat face value,\xe2\x80\x9d however, \xe2\x80\x9cfor congressional power to make valid statutes retroactively applicable to pending cases has often been recognized.\xe2\x80\x9d Hart and Wechsler 324. See, e.g., United States v. Schooner Peggy, 1 Cranch 103, 110 (1801). As we explained in Landgraf v. USI Film Products,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 244,\n267 (1994)\n, the restrictions that the Constitution places on retroactive legislation \xe2\x80\x9care of limited scope\xe2\x80\x9d:

\n

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe Ex Post Facto Clause flatly prohibits retroactive application of penal legislation. Article I, \xc2\xa710, cl.\xc2\xa01, prohibits States from passing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. laws \xe2\x80\x98impairing the Obligation of Contracts.\xe2\x80\x99 The\n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Takings Clause prevents the Legislature (and other government actors) from depriving private persons of vested property rights except for a \xe2\x80\x98public use\xe2\x80\x99 and upon payment of \xe2\x80\x98just compensation.\xe2\x80\x99 The prohibitions on \xe2\x80\x98Bills of Attainder\xe2\x80\x99 in Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7 9\xe2\x80\x9310, prohibit legislatures from singling out disfavored persons and meting out summary punishment for past conduct. The Due Process Clause also protects the interests in fair notice and repose that may be compromised by retroactive legislation; a justification sufficient to validate a statute\xe2\x80\x99s prospective application under the Clause \xe2\x80\x98may not suffice\xe2\x80\x99 to warrant its retroactive application.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 266\xe2\x80\x93267 (citation and footnote omitted).

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cAbsent a violation of one of those specific provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d when a new law makes clear that it is retroactive, the arguable \xe2\x80\x9cunfairness of retroactive civil legislation is not a sufficient reason for a court to fail to give [that law] its intended scope.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 267\xe2\x80\x93268. So yes, we have affirmed, Congress may indeed direct courts to apply newly enacted, outcome-altering legislation in pending civil cases. See Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 226. Any lingering doubts on that score have been dispelled by Robertson, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 441, and Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 218.

\n

Bank Markazi argues most strenuously that \xc2\xa78772 did not simply amend pre-existing law. Because the judicial findings contemplated by \xc2\xa78772 were \xe2\x80\x9cforegone conclusions,\xe2\x80\x9d the Bank urges, the statute \xe2\x80\x9ceffectively\xe2\x80\x9d directed certain factfindings and specified the outcome under the amended law. See Brief for Petitioner 42, 47. See also post, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. Recall that the District Court, closely monitoring the case, disagreed. Supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311; App. to Pet. for Cert. 115a (\xe2\x80\x9c[The] determinations [required by \xc2\xa78772] [were] not mere fig leaves,\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cit [was] quite possible that the [c]ourt could have found that defendants raised a triable issue as to whether the [b]locked [a]ssets were owned by Iran, or that Clearstream and/or UBAE ha[d] some form of beneficial or equitable interest.\xe2\x80\x9d).[21]

\n

In any event, a statute does not impinge on judicial power when it directs courts to apply a new legal standard to undisputed facts. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen a plaintiff brings suit to enforce a legal obligation it is not any less a case or controversy upon which a court possessing the federal judicial power may rightly give judgment, because the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim is uncontested or incontestable.\xe2\x80\x9d Pope v. United States,\n\n323 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n11 (1944)\n. In Schooner Peggy, 1

\n

Cranch, at 109\xe2\x80\x93110, for example, this Court applied a newly ratified treaty that, by requiring the return of captured property, effectively permitted only one possible outcome. And in Robertson, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 434\xe2\x80\x93435, 438\xe2\x80\x93439, a statute replaced governing environmental-law restraints on timber harvesting with new legislation that permitted harvesting in all but certain designated areas. Without inquiring whether the new statute\xe2\x80\x99s application in pending cases was a \xe2\x80\x9cforegone conclusio[n],\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 47, we upheld the legislation because it left for judicial determination whether any particular actions violated the new prescription. In short, \xc2\xa78772 changed the law by establishing new substantive standards, entrusting to the District Court application of those standards to the facts (contested or uncontested) found by the court.

\n

Resisting this conclusion, The Chief Justice compares \xc2\xa78772 to a hypothetical \xe2\x80\x9claw directing judgment for Smith if the court finds that Jones was duly served with notice of the proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313.[22] Of course, the hypothesized law would be invalid\xe2\x80\x94as would a law directing judgment for Smith, for instance, if the court finds that the sun rises in the east. For one thing, a law so cast may well be irrational and, therefore, unconstitutional for reasons distinct from the separation-of-powers issues considered here. See, e.g., infra, at 21, n. 27. For another, the law imagined by the dissent does what Robertson says Congress cannot do: Like a statute that directs, in \xe2\x80\x9cSmith v. Jones,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cSmith wins,\xe2\x80\x9d supra, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313, n.\xc2\xa017, it\xe2\x80\x9ccompel[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. findings or results under old law,\xe2\x80\x9d for itfails to supply any new legal standard effectuating

\n

the lawmakers\xe2\x80\x99 reasonable policy judgment, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 438.[23] By contrast, \xc2\xa78772 provides a new standard clarifying that, if Iran owns certain assets, the victims of Iran-sponsored terrorist attacks will be permitted to execute against those assets. Applying laws implementing Congress\xe2\x80\x99 policy judgments, with fidelity to those judgments, is commonplace for the Judiciary.

\n

B

\n

Section 8772 remains \xe2\x80\x9cunprecedented,\xe2\x80\x9d Bank Markazi charges, because it \xe2\x80\x9cprescribes a rule for a single pending case\xe2\x80\x94identified by caption and docket number.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 17.[24] The amended law in Robertson, however, also applied to cases identified by caption and docket number, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 440, and was nonetheless upheld. Moreover, \xc2\xa78772, as already described, see supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x938, facilitates execution of judgments in 16 suits, together encompassing more than 1,000 victims of Iran-sponsored terrorist attacks.[25] Although consolidated for administrative purposes at the execution stage,[26] the judgment-execution claims brought pursuant to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 69 were not independent of the original actions for damages and each claim retained its separate character. See Mackey v. Lanier Collection Agency & Service, Inc.,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825\n\xe2\x80\x93835, n.\xc2\xa010 (1988) (postjudgment garnishment action brought under Rule 69 is part of the \xe2\x80\x9cprocess to enforce a judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d not a new suit (alteration omitted and emphasis deleted)); 10 Cyclopedia of Federal Procedure \xc2\xa736:8, p. 385 (3 ed. 2010) (\xe2\x80\x9cProceedings in execution are proceedings in the action itself .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d); 9A C. Wright & A. Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa72382, p. 10 (3d ed. 2008) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]ctions do not lose their separate identity because of consolidation.\xe2\x80\x9d).[27]

\n

The Bank\xe2\x80\x99s argument is further flawed, for it rests on the assumption that legislation must be generally applic-able, that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is something wrong with particularized legislative action.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 239, n.\xc2\xa09. We have

\n

found that assumption suspect:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhile legislatures usually act through laws of general applicability, that is by no means their only legitimate mode of action. Private bills in Congress are still common, and were even more so in the days before establishment of the Claims Court. Even laws that impose a duty or liability upon a single individ-ual or firm are not on that account invalid\xe2\x80\x94or else we would not have the extensive jurisprudence that we do concerning the Bill of Attainder Clause, including cases which say that [the Clause] requires not merely \xe2\x80\x98singling out\xe2\x80\x99 but also punishment, see, e.g., United States v. Lovett,\n\n328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 303\n\xe2\x80\x93318 (1946), [or] a case [holding] that Congress may legislate \xe2\x80\x98a legitimate class of one,\xe2\x80\x99 Nixon v. Administrator of General Services,\n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 425,\n472 (1977)\n.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[28]

\n

This Court and lower courts have upheld as a valid exercise of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 legislative power diverse laws that governed one or a very small number of specific subjects. E.g., Regional Rail Reorganization Act Cases,\n\n419 U.\xc2\xa0S. 102\n\xe2\x80\x93161 (1974) (upholding Act that applied to specific railroads in a single region); Pope, 323 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 9\xe2\x80\x9314 (upholding special Act giving a contractor the right to recover additional compensation from the Government); The Clinton Bridge, 10 Wall. 454, 462\xe2\x80\x93463 (1870) (upholding Act governing a single bridge); Pennsylvania v. Wheeling & Belmont Bridge Co., 18 How. 421, 430\xe2\x80\x93432 (1856) (similar); Biodiversity Assoc. v. Cables, 357 F.\xc2\xa03d 1152, 1156, 1164\xe2\x80\x931171 (CA10 2004) (upholding law that abro-

\n

gated specific settlement agreement between U.\xc2\xa0S. Forest Service and environmental groups); SeaRiver Maritime Financial Holdings, Inc. v. Mineta, 309 F.\xc2\xa03d 662, 667, 674\xe2\x80\x93675 (CA9 2002) (upholding law that effectively applied to a single oil tanker); National Coalition To Save Our Mall v. Norton, 269 F.\xc2\xa03d 1092, 1097 (CADC 2001) (upholding law that applied to a single memorial).

\n

C

\n

We stress, finally, that \xc2\xa78772 is an exercise of congressional authority regarding foreign affairs, a domain in which the controlling role of the political branches is both necessary and proper. See, e.g., Zivotofsky v. Kerry, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 19). In furtherance of their authority over the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s foreign relations, Congress and the President have, time and again, as exigencies arose, exercised control over claims against foreign states and the disposition of foreign-state property in the United States. See Dames & Moore v. Regan,\n\n453 U.\xc2\xa0S. 654\n\xe2\x80\x93674, 679\xe2\x80\x93681 (1981) (describing this history). In pursuit of foreign policy objectives, the political branches have regulated specific foreign-state assets by, inter alia, blocking them or governing their availability for attachment. See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934 (describing the TWEA and the IEEPA); e.g., Dames & Moore, 453 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 669\xe2\x80\x93674. Such measures have never been rejected as invasions upon the Article III judicial power. Cf. id., at 674 (Court resists the notion \xe2\x80\x9cthat the Federal Government as a whole lacked the power\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cnullif[y] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. attachments and orde[r] the transfer of [foreign-state] assets.\xe2\x80\x9d).[29]

\n

Particularly pertinent, the Executive, prior to the enactment of the FSIA, regularly made case-specific determinations whether sovereign immunity should be recognized, and courts accepted those determinations as binding. See Republic of Austria v. Altmann,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677\n\xe2\x80\x93691 (2004); Ex parte Peru,\n\n318 U.\xc2\xa0S. 578\n\xe2\x80\x93590 (1943). As this Court explained in Republic of Mexico v. Hoffman,\n\n324 U.\xc2\xa0S. 30,\n35 (1945)\n, it is \xe2\x80\x9cnot for the courts to deny an immunity which our government has seen fit to allow, or to allow an immu-nity on new grounds which the government has not seen fit to recognize.\xe2\x80\x9d This practice, too, was never perceived as an encroachment on the federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 jurisdiction. See Dames & Moore, 453 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 684\xe2\x80\x93685 (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]rior to the enactment of the FSIA [courts would not have] reject[ed] as an encroachment on their jurisdiction the President\xe2\x80\x99s determination of a foreign state\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Enacting the FSIA in 1976, Congress transferred from the Executive to the courts the principal responsibility for determining a foreign state\xe2\x80\x99s amenability to suit. See Verlinden B. V. v. Central Bank of Nigeria,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 480\n\xe2\x80\x93489 (1983). But it remains Congress\xe2\x80\x99 prerogative to alter a foreign state\xe2\x80\x99s immunity and to render the alteration dispositive of judicial proceedings in progress. See Republic of Iraq v. Beaty,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 848\n\xe2\x80\x93857, 865 (2009). By altering the law governing the attachment of particular property belonging to Iran, Congress acted comfortably within the political branches\xe2\x80\x99 authority over foreign sovereign immunity and foreign-state assets.

\n

\n

For the reasons stated, we are satisfied that \xc2\xa78772\xe2\x80\x94a

\n

statute designed to aid in the enforcement of federal-court judgments\xe2\x80\x94does not offend \xe2\x80\x9cseparation of powers principles .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. protecting the role of the independent Judiciary within the constitutional design.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller v. French,\n\n530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 327,\n350 (2000)\n. The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit is therefore

\n

Affirmed.

BANK MARKAZI, aka THE CENTRAL BANK OF IRAN, PETITIONER v. DEBORAH PETERSON, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

[April 20, 2016]

\n

Chief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Sotomayor joins, dissenting.

\n

Imagine your neighbor sues you, claiming that your fence is on his property. His evidence is a letter from the previous owner of your home, accepting your neighbor\xe2\x80\x99s version of the facts. Your defense is an official county map, which under state law establishes the boundaries of your land. The map shows the fence on your side of the property line. You also argue that your neighbor\xe2\x80\x99s claim is six months outside the statute of limitations.

\n

Now imagine that while the lawsuit is pending, your neighbor persuades the legislature to enact a new statute. The new statute provides that for your case, and your case alone, a letter from one neighbor to another is conclusive of property boundaries, and the statute of limitations is one year longer. Your neighbor wins. Who would you say decided your case: the legislature, which targeted your specific case and eliminated your specific defenses so as to ensure your neighbor\xe2\x80\x99s victory, or the court, which pre-sided over the fait accompli?

\n

That question lies at the root of the case the Court confronts today. Article III of the Constitution commits the power to decide cases to the Judiciary alone. See Stern v. Marshall,\n\n564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 462,\n484 (2011)\n. Yet, in this case, Congress arrogated that power to itself. Since 2008, respondents have sought $1.75 billion in assets owned by Bank Markazi, Iran\xe2\x80\x99s central bank, in order to satisfy judgments against Iran for acts of terrorism. The Bank has vigorously opposed those efforts, asserting numerous legal defenses. So, in 2012, four years into the litigation, respondents persuaded Congress to enact a statute,\n\n22 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa78772, that for this case alone eliminates each of the defenses standing in respondents\xe2\x80\x99 way. Then, having gotten Congress to resolve all outstanding issues in their favor, respondents returned to court .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and won.

\n

Contrary to the majority, I would hold that \xc2\xa78772 violates the separation of powers. No less than if it had passed a law saying \xe2\x80\x9crespondents win,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress has decided this case by enacting a bespoke statute tailored to this case that resolves the parties\xe2\x80\x99 specific legal disputes to guarantee respondents victory.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Article III, \xc2\xa71 of the Constitution vests the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial Power of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d in the Federal Judiciary. That provision, this Court has observed, \xe2\x80\x9csafeguards the role of the Judicial Branch in our tripartite system.\xe2\x80\x9d Commodity Futures Trading Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Schor,\n\n478 U.\xc2\xa0S. 833,\n850 (1986)\n. It establishes the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s independence by giving the Judiciary distinct and inviolable authority. \xe2\x80\x9cUnder the basic concept of separation of powers,\xe2\x80\x9d the judicial power \xe2\x80\x9ccan no more be shared with another branch than the Chief Executive, for example, can share with the Judiciary the veto power, or the Congress share with the Judiciary the power to override a Presidential veto.\xe2\x80\x9d Stern, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 483 (internal quotation marks omitted). The separation of powers, in turn, safeguards individual freedom. See Bond v. United States,\n\n564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 211,\n223 (2011)\n. As Hamilton wrote, quoting Montesquieu, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98there is no liberty if the power of judging be not separated from the legislative and executive powers.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 78, p. 466 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961); see Montesquieu, The Spirit of the Laws 157 (A. Cohler, B. Miller, & H. Stone eds. 1989) (Montesquieu).

\n

The question we confront today is whether \xc2\xa78772 violates Article III by invading the judicial power.

\n

B

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe Framers of our Constitution lived among the ruins of a system of intermingled legislative and judicial powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut v. Spendthrift Farm, Inc.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 211,\n219 (1995)\n. We surveyed those ruins in Plaut to determine the scope of the judicial power under Article III, and we ought to return to them today for that same purpose.

\n

Throughout the 17th and 18th centuries, colonial legislatures performed what are now recognized as core judicial roles. They \xe2\x80\x9cfunctioned as courts of equity of last resort, hearing original actions or providing appellate review of judicial judgments.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. They \xe2\x80\x9cconstantly heard private petitions, which often were only the complaints of one individual or group against another, and made final judgments on these complaints.\xe2\x80\x9d G. Wood, The Creation of the American Republic 1776\xe2\x80\x931787, pp. 154\xe2\x80\x93155 (1969). And they routinely intervened in cases still pending before courts, granting continuances, stays of judgments, \xe2\x80\x9cnew trials, and other kinds of relief in an effort to do what \xe2\x80\x98is agreeable to Right and Justice.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 155; see Judicial Action by the Provincial Legislature of Massachusetts, 15 Harv. L. Rev. 208, 216\xe2\x80\x93218 (1902) (collecting examples of such laws).

\n

The judicial power exercised by colonial legislatures was often expressly vested in them by the colonial charter or statute. In the Colonies of Massachusetts, Connecticut, and Rhode Island, for example, the assemblies officially served as the highest court of appeals. See 1 The Public Records of the Colony of Connecticut 25 (Trumbull ed. 1850); M. Clarke, Parliamentary Privilege in the American Colonies 31\xe2\x80\x9333 (1943). Likewise, for more than a half century, the colonial assembly of Virginia could review and set aside court judgments. Id., at 37\xe2\x80\x9338. And in New Hampshire, where British authorities directed judicial appeals to the governor and his council, those officials often referred such matters to the assembly for decision. Id., at 33. Colonial assemblies thus sat atop the judicial pyramid, with the final word over when and how private disputes would be resolved.

\n

Legislative involvement in judicial matters intensified during the American Revolution, fueled by the \xe2\x80\x9cvigorous, indeed often radical, populism of the revolutionary legislatures and assemblies.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 219; see Wood, supra, at 155\xe2\x80\x93156. The Pennsylvania Constitution of 1776 epitomized the ethos of legislative supremacy. It established a unicameral assembly unconstrained by judicial review and vested with authority to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98redress grievances.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Report of the Committee of the Pennsylvania Council of Censors 42 (F. Bailey ed. 1784) (Council Report); see Williams, The State Constitutions of the Founding Decade: Pennsylvania\xe2\x80\x99s Radical 1776 Constitution and Its Influences on American Constitutionalism, 62 Temp. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 541, 547\xe2\x80\x93548, 556 (1989). The assembly, in turn, invoked that authority to depart from legal rules in resolving private disputes in order to ease the \xe2\x80\x9chardships which will always arise from the operation of general laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Council Report 42\xe2\x80\x9343.

\n

The Revolution-era \xe2\x80\x9ccrescendo of legislative interference with private judgments of the courts,\xe2\x80\x9d however, soon prompted a \xe2\x80\x9csense of a sharp necessity to separate the legislative from the judicial power.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 221. In 1778, an influential critique of a proposed (and ultimately rejected) Massachusetts constitution warned that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the legislative and judicial powers are united, the maker of the law will also interpret it; and the law may then speak a language, dictated by the whims, the caprice, or the prejudice of the judge.\xe2\x80\x9d The Essex Result, in The Popular Sources of Political Authority: Documents on the Massachusetts Constitution of 1780, p. 337 (O. Handlin & M. Handlin eds. 1966). In Virginia, Thomas Jefferson complained that the assembly had, \xe2\x80\x9cin many instances, decided rights which should have been left to judiciary controversy.\xe2\x80\x9d Jefferson, Notes on the State of Virginia 120 (Peden ed. 1982). And in Pennsylvania, the Council of Censors\xe2\x80\x94a body appointed to assess compliance with the state constitution\xe2\x80\x94decried the state assembly\xe2\x80\x99s practice of \xe2\x80\x9cextending their deliberations to the cases of individuals\xe2\x80\x9d instead of deferring to \xe2\x80\x9cthe usual process of law,\xe2\x80\x9d citing instances when the assembly overturned fines, settled estates, and suspended prosecutions. Council Report 38, 42. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is reason to think,\xe2\x80\x9d the Censors observed, \xe2\x80\x9cthat favour and partiality have, from the nature of public bodies of men, predominated in the distribution of this relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 38.

\n

Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s Council of Censors sounded similar warnings. Its 1786 report denounced the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cassumption of the judicial power,\xe2\x80\x9d which the legislature had exercised by staying and vacating judgments, suspending lawsuits, resolving property disputes, and \xe2\x80\x9clegislating for individ-uals, and for particular cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Vermont State Papers 1779\xe2\x80\x931786, pp. 537\xe2\x80\x93542 (W. Slade ed. 1823). The Censors concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he legislative body is, in truth, by no means competent to the determination of causes between party and party,\xe2\x80\x9d having exercised judicial power \xe2\x80\x9cwithout being shackled with rules,\xe2\x80\x9d guided only by \xe2\x80\x9ccrude notions of equity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 537, 540.

\n

The States\xe2\x80\x99 experiences ultimately shaped the Federal Constitution, figuring prominently in the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 decision to devise a system for securing liberty through the division of power:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cBefore and during the debates on ratification, Madison, Jefferson, and Hamilton each wrote of the factional disorders and disarray that the system of legislative equity had produced in the years before the framing; and each thought that the separation of the legislative from the judicial power in the new Constitution would cure them.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 221.

\n

As Professor Manning has concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cArticle III, in large measure, reflects a reaction against the practice\xe2\x80\x9d of legislative interference with state courts. Manning, Response, Deriving Rules of Statutory Interpretation from the Constitution, 101 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1648, 1663 (2001).

\n

Experience had confirmed Montesquieu\xe2\x80\x99s theory. The Framers saw that if the \xe2\x80\x9cpower of judging .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. were joined to legislative power, the power over the life and liberty of the citizens would be arbitrary.\xe2\x80\x9d Montesquieu 157. They accordingly resolved to take the unprecedented step of establishing a \xe2\x80\x9ctruly distinct\xe2\x80\x9d judiciary. The Federalist No. 78, at 466 (A. Hamilton). To help ensure the \xe2\x80\x9ccomplete independence of the courts of justice,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., they provided life tenure for judges and protection against diminution of their compensation. But such safeguards against indirect interference would have been meaningless if Congress could simply exercise the judicial power di-rectly. The central pillar of judicial independence was Article III itself, which vested \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he judicial Power of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cone supreme Court\xe2\x80\x9d and such \xe2\x80\x9cinferior Courts\xe2\x80\x9d as might be established. The judicial power was to be the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s alone.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

Mindful of this history, our decisions have recognized three kinds of \xe2\x80\x9cunconstitutional restriction[s] upon the exercise of judicial power.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 218. Two concern the effect of judgments once they have been rendered: \xe2\x80\x9cCongress cannot vest review of the decisions of Article III courts in officials of the Executive Branch,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., for to do so would make a court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment merely \xe2\x80\x9can advisory opinion in its most obnoxious form,\xe2\x80\x9d Chicago & Southern Air Lines, Inc. v. Waterman S. S. Corp.,\n\n333 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103,\n113 (1948)\n. And Congress cannot \xe2\x80\x9cretroactively command[\xc2\xa0] the federal courts to reopen final judgments,\xe2\x80\x9d because Article III \xe2\x80\x9cgives the Federal Judiciary the power, not merely to rule on cases, but to decide them, subject to review only by superior courts in the Article III hierarchy.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 218\xe2\x80\x93219. Neither of these rules is directly implicated here.

\n

This case is about the third type of unconstitutional interference with the judicial function, whereby Congress assumes the role of judge and decides a particular pending case in the first instance. Section 8772 does precisely that, changing the law\xe2\x80\x94for these proceedings alone\xe2\x80\x94simply to guarantee that respondents win. The law serves no other purpose\xe2\x80\x94a point, indeed, that is hardly in dispute. As the majority acknowledges, the statute \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98sweeps away .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. federal or state law impediments that might otherwise exist\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to bar respondents from obtaining Bank Markazi\xe2\x80\x99s assets. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (quoting App. to Pet. for Cert. 73a). In the District Court, Bank Markazi had invoked sovereign immunity under the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71611(b)(1). Brief for Petitioner 28. Section 8772(a)(1) eliminates that immunity. Bank Markazi had argued that its status as a separate juridical entity under federal common law and international law freed it from liability for Iran\xe2\x80\x99s debts. See First Nat. City Bank v. Banco Para el Comercio Exterior de Cuba,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 611\n\xe2\x80\x93627 (1983); Brief for Petitioner 27\xe2\x80\x9328. Section 8772(d)(3) ensures that the Bank is liable. Bank Markazi had argued that New York law did not allow respondents to execute their judgments against the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s assets. See N.\xc2\xa0Y. U. C. C. Law Ann. \xc2\xa78\xe2\x80\x93112(c) (West 2002); see also App. to Pet. for Cert. 126a (agreeing with this argument). Section 8772(a)(1) makes those assets subject to execution. See id., at 97a.

\n

Section 8772 authorized attachment, moreover, only for the

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cfinancial assets that are identified in and the subject of proceedings in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York in Peterson et\xc2\xa0al. v. Islamic Republic of Iran et\xc2\xa0al., Case No. 10 Civ. 4518 (BSJ) (GWG), that were restrained by restraining notices and levies secured by the plaintiffs in those proceedings .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78772(b).

\n

And lest there be any doubt that Congress\xe2\x80\x99s sole concern was deciding this particular case, rather than establishing any generally applicable rules, \xc2\xa78772 provided that nothing in the statute \xe2\x80\x9cshall be construed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to affect the availability, or lack thereof, of a right to satisfy a judgment in any other action against a terrorist party in any proceedings other than\xe2\x80\x9d these. \xc2\xa78772(c).[1]

\n

B

\n

There has never been anything like \xc2\xa78772 before. Neither the majority nor respondents have identified another statute that changed the law for a pending case in an outcome-determinative way and explicitly limited its effect to particular judicial proceedings. That fact alone is \xe2\x80\x9c[p]erhaps the most telling indication of the severe constitutional problem\xe2\x80\x9d with the law. Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Bd.,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477,\n505 (2010)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). Congress\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprolonged reticence would be amazing if such interference were not understood to be constitutionally proscribed.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 230.

\n

Section 8772 violates the bedrock rule of Article III that the judicial power is vested in the Judicial Branch alone. We first enforced that rule against an Act of Congress during the Reconstruction era in United States v. Klein, 13 Wall. 128 (1872). Klein arose from congressional opposition to conciliation with the South, and in particular to the pardons Presidents Lincoln and Johnson had offered to former Confederate rebels. See id., at 140\xe2\x80\x93141; see, e.g., Presidential Proclamation No. 11,\n\n13Stat.\n737. Although this Court had held that a pardon was proof of loyalty and entitled its holder to compensation in the Court of Claims for property seized by Union forces during the war, see United States v. Padelford, 9 Wall. 531, 543 (1870), the Radical Republican Congress wished to prevent pardoned rebels from obtaining such compensation. It therefore enacted a law prohibiting claimants from using a pardon as evidence of loyalty, instead requiring the Court of Claims and Supreme Court to dismiss for want of jurisdiction any suit based on a pardon. See Act of July 12, 1870, ch. 251,\n\n16Stat.\n235; see also United States v. Sioux Nation,\n\n448 U.\xc2\xa0S. 371,\n403 (1980)\n.

\n

Klein\xe2\x80\x99s suit was among those Congress wished to block. Klein represented the estate of one V. F. Wilson, a Confederate supporter whom Lincoln had pardoned. On behalf of the estate, Klein had obtained a sizable judg-ment in the Court of Claims for property seized by the Union. Klein, 13 Wall., at 132\xe2\x80\x93134. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s appeal from that judgment was pending in the Supreme Court when the law targeting such suits took effect. The Government accordingly moved to dismiss the entire proceeding.

\n

This Court, however, denied that motion and instead declared the law unconstitutional. It held that the law \xe2\x80\x9cpassed the limit which separates the legislative from the judicial power.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 147. The Court acknowledged that Congress may \xe2\x80\x9cmake exceptions and prescribe regulations to the appellate power,\xe2\x80\x9d but it refused to sustain the law as an exercise of that authority. Id., at 146. Instead, the Court held that the law violated the separation of powers by attempting to \xe2\x80\x9cdecide\xe2\x80\x9d the case by \xe2\x80\x9cprescrib[ing] rules of decision to the Judicial Department of the government in cases pending before it.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 145\xe2\x80\x93146. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is of vital importance,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court stressed, that the legislative and judicial powers \xe2\x80\x9cbe kept distinct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 147.

\n

The majority characterizes Klein as a delphic, puzzling decision whose central holding\xe2\x80\x94that Congress may not prescribe the result in pending cases\xe2\x80\x94cannot be taken at face value.[2] It is true that Klein can be read too broadly, in a way that would swallow the rule that courts generally must apply a retroactively applicable statute to pending cases. See United States v. Schooner Peggy, 1 Cranch 103, 110 (1801). But Schooner Peggy can be read too broadly, too. Applying a retroactive law that says \xe2\x80\x9cSmith wins\xe2\x80\x9d to the pending case of Smith v. Jones implicates profound issues of separation of powers, issues not adequately answered by a citation to Schooner Peggy. And just because Klein did not set forth clear rules defining the limits on Congress\xe2\x80\x99s authority to legislate with respect to a pending case does not mean\xe2\x80\x94as the majority seems to think\xe2\x80\x94that Article III itself imposes no such limits.

\n

The same \xe2\x80\x9crecord of history\xe2\x80\x9d that drove the Framers to adopt Article III to implement the separation of powers ought to compel us to give meaning to their design. Plaut, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 218. The nearly two centuries of experience with legislative assumption of judicial power meant that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Framers were well acquainted with the danger of subjecting the determination of the rights of one person to the tyranny of shifting majorities.\xe2\x80\x9d INS v. Chadha,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 919,\n961 (1983)\n (Powell, J., concurring in judgment) (internal quotation marks omitted). Article III vested the judicial power in the Judiciary alone to protect against that threat to liberty. It defined not only what the Judiciary can do, but also what Congress cannot.

\n

The Court says it would reject a law that says \xe2\x80\x9cSmith wins\xe2\x80\x9d because such a statute \xe2\x80\x9cwould create no new substantive law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12, n.\xc2\xa017. Of course it would: Prior to the passage of the hypothetical statute, the law did not provide that Smith wins. After the passage of the law, it does. Changing the law is simply how Congress acts. The question is whether its action constitutes an exercise of judicial power. Saying Congress \xe2\x80\x9ccreates new law\xe2\x80\x9d in one case but not another simply expresses a conclusion on that issue; it does not supply a reason.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cSmith wins\xe2\x80\x9d is a new law, tailored to one case in the same way as \xc2\xa78772 and having the same effect. All that both statutes \xe2\x80\x9ceffectuat[e],\xe2\x80\x9d in substance, is lawmakers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy judgment\xe2\x80\x9d that one side in one case ought to prevail. Ante, at 18. The cause for concern is that though the statutes are indistinguishable, it is plain that the majority recognizes no limit under the separation of powers beyond the prohibition on statutes as brazen as \xe2\x80\x9cSmith wins.\xe2\x80\x9d Hamilton warned that the Judiciary must take \xe2\x80\x9call possible care .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to defend itself against [the] attacks\xe2\x80\x9d of the other branches. The Federalist No. 78, at 466. In the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, however, Article III is but a constitutional Maginot Line, easily circumvented by the simplest maneuver of taking away every defense against Smith\xe2\x80\x99s victory, without saying \xe2\x80\x9cSmith wins.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Take the majority\xe2\x80\x99s acceptance of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that \xc2\xa78772 left \xe2\x80\x9cplenty\xe2\x80\x9d of factual determinations for the court \xe2\x80\x9cto adjudicate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317, and n.\xc2\xa020 (internal quotation marks omitted). All \xc2\xa78772 actually required of the court was two factual determinations\xe2\x80\x94that Bank Markazi has an equitable or beneficial interest in the assets, and that no other party does, \xc2\xa78772(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x94both of which were well established by the time Congress enacted \xc2\xa78772. Not only had the assets at issue been frozen pursuant to an Executive Order blocking \xe2\x80\x9cproperty of the Government of Iran,\xe2\x80\x9d Exec. Order No. 13599, 77 Fed. Reg. 6659 (2012), but the Bank had \xe2\x80\x9crepeatedly insisted that it is the sole beneficial owner of the Blocked Assets,\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 113a. By that measure of \xe2\x80\x9cplenty,\xe2\x80\x9d the majority would have to uphold a law directing judgment for Smith if the court finds that Jones was duly served with notice of the proceedings, and that Smith\xe2\x80\x99s claim was within the statute of limitations. In reality, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cplenty\xe2\x80\x9d is plenty of nothing, and, apparently, nothing is plenty for the Court. See D. Heyward & I. Gershwin, Porgy and Bess: Libretto 28 (1958).

\n

It is true that some of the precedents cited by the majority, ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9319, have allowed Congress to approach the boundary between legislative and judicial power. None, however, involved statutes comparable to \xc2\xa78772. In Robertson v. Seattle Audubon Soc.,\n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 429 (1992)\n, for example, the statute at issue referenced particular cases only as a shorthand for describing certain environmental law requirements, id., at 433\xe2\x80\x93435, not to limit the statute\xe2\x80\x99s effect to those cases alone. And in Plaut, the Court explicitly distinguished the statute before it\xe2\x80\x94which directed courts to reopen final judgments in an entire class of cases\xe2\x80\x94from one that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98single[s] out\xe2\x80\x99 any defendant for adverse treatment (or any plaintiff for favorable treatment).\xe2\x80\x9d 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 238. Plaut, in any event, held the statute before it invalid, concluding that it violated Article III based on the same historical understanding of the judicial power outlined above. Id., at 219\xe2\x80\x93225, 240.[3]

\n

I readily concede, without embarrassment, that it can sometimes be difficult to draw the line between legislative and judicial power. That should come as no surprise; Chief Justice Marshall\xe2\x80\x99s admonition \xe2\x80\x9cthat \xe2\x80\x98it is a constitution we are expounding\xe2\x80\x99 is especially relevant when the Court is required to give legal sanctions to an underlying principle of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x94that of separation of powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer,\n\n343 U.\xc2\xa0S. 579\n\xe2\x80\x93597 (1952) (Frankfurter, J., concurring) (quoting McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 407 (1819)). But however difficult it may be to discern the line between the Legislative and Judicial Branches, the entire constitu-tional enterprise depends on there being such a line. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to enforce that boundary in a case as clear as this reduces Article III to a mere \xe2\x80\x9cparchment barrier[\xc2\xa0] against the encroaching spirit\xe2\x80\x9d of legislative power. The Federalist No. 48, at 308 (J. Madison).

\n

C

\n

Finally, the majority suggests that \xc2\xa78772 is analogous to the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s historical power to recognize foreign state sovereign immunity on a case-by-case basis. As discussed above, however, \xc2\xa78772 does considerably more than withdraw the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity. Supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. It strips the Bank of any protection that federal common law, international law, or New York State law might have offered against respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims. That is without analogue or precedent. In any event, the practice of applying case-specific Executive submissions on sovereign immunity was not judicial acquiescence in an intrusion on the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s role. It was instead the result of substantive sovereign immunity law, developed and applied by the courts, which treated such a submission as a dispositive fact. See Verlinden B. V. v. Central Bank of Nigeria,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 480\n\xe2\x80\x93487 (1983); Ex parte Peru,\n\n318 U.\xc2\xa0S. 578\n\xe2\x80\x93588 (1943).

\n

The majority also compares \xc2\xa78772 to the political branches\xe2\x80\x99 authority to \xe2\x80\x9cexercise[\xc2\xa0] control over claims against foreign states and the disposition of foreign-state property in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21 (citing Dames & Moore v. Regan,\n\n453 U.\xc2\xa0S. 654 (1981)\n). In Dames & Moore, we considered whether the President had authority to suspend claims against Iran, and to nullify existing court orders attaching Iran\xe2\x80\x99s property, in order to fulfill U.\xc2\xa0S. obligations under a claims settlement agreement with that country. Id., at 664\xe2\x80\x93667. We held that the President had that power, based on a combination of statutory authorization, congressional acquiescence, and inherent Executive power. See id., at 674\xe2\x80\x93675, 686.

\n

The majority suggests that Dames & Moore supports the validity of \xc2\xa78772. But Dames & Moore was self-consciously \xe2\x80\x9ca restricted railroad ticket, good for this day and train only.\xe2\x80\x9d Smith v. Allwright,\n\n321 U.\xc2\xa0S. 649,\n669 (1944)\n (Roberts, J., dissenting). The Court stressed in Dames & Moore that it \xe2\x80\x9cattempt[ed] to lay down no general \xe2\x80\x98guidelines\xe2\x80\x99 covering other situations not involved here, and attempt[ed] to confine the opinion only to the very questions necessary to [the] decision of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d 453 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 661; see also American Ins. Assn. v. Garamendi,\n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 396,\n438 (2003)\n (Ginsburg, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cNotably, the Court in Dames & Moore was emphatic about the \xe2\x80\x98narrowness\xe2\x80\x99 of its decision.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

There are, moreover, several important differences between Dames & Moore and this case. For starters, the executive action Dames & Moore upheld did not dictate how particular claims were to be resolved, but simply required such claims to be submitted to a different tribunal. 453 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 660. Furthermore, Dames & Moore sanctioned that action based on the political branches\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9clongstanding\xe2\x80\x9d practice of \xe2\x80\x9csettl[ing] the claims of [U.\xc2\xa0S.] nationals against foreign countries\xe2\x80\x9d by treaty or executive agreement. Id., at 679. The Court emphasized that throughout our history, the political branches have at times \xe2\x80\x9cdisposed of the claims of [U.\xc2\xa0S.] citizens without their consent, or even without consultation with them,\xe2\x80\x9d by renouncing claims, settling them, or establishing arbitration proceedings. Id., at 679\xe2\x80\x93681 (internal quotation marks omitted). Those dispositions, crucially, were not exercises of judicial power, as is evident from the fact that the Judiciary lacks authority to order settlement or establish new tribunals. That is why Klein was not at issue in Dames & Moore. By contrast, no comparable history sustains Congress\xe2\x80\x99s action here, which seeks to provide relief to respondents not by transferring their claims in a manner only the political branches could do, but by commandeering the courts to make a political judgment look like a judicial one. See Medell\xc3\xadn v. Texas,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 491,\n531 (2008)\n (refusing to extend the President\xe2\x80\x99s claims-settlement authority beyond the \xe2\x80\x9cnarrow set of circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d defined by the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98systematic, unbroken, executive practice, long pursued to the knowledge of the Congress and never before questioned\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Dames & Moore, 453 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 686)).

\n

If anything, what Dames & Moore reveals is that the political branches have extensive powers of their own in this area and could have chosen to exercise them to give relief to the claimants in this case. Cf.\n\n50 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71702(a)(1)(C) (authorizing the President, in certain emergency circumstances, to confiscate and dispose of foreign sovereign property). The authority of the political branches is sufficient; they have no need to seize ours.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

At issue here is a basic principle, not a technical rule. Section 8772 decides this case no less certainly than if Congress had directed entry of judgment for respondents. As a result, the potential of the decision today \xe2\x80\x9cto effect important change in the equilibrium of power\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cimmediately evident.\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison v. Olson,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 654,\n699 (1988)\n (Scalia, J., dissenting). Hereafter, with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s seal of approval, Congress can unabashedly pick the winners and losers in particular pending cases. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will indeed become a \xe2\x80\x9cblueprint for extensive expansion of the legislative power\xe2\x80\x9d at the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s expense, Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority v. Citizens for Abatement of Aircraft Noise, Inc.,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 252,\n277 (1991)\n, feeding Congress\xe2\x80\x99s tendency to \xe2\x80\x9cextend[\xc2\xa0] the sphere of its activity and draw[\xc2\xa0] all power into its impetuous vortex,\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 48, at 309 (J. Madison).

\n

I respectfully dissent.
