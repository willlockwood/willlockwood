Syllabus

\n

U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank N.\xc2\xa0A., Trustee, by and through CWCapital Asset Management LLC v. Village at Lakeridge, LLC

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x931509.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 31, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 5, 2018

\n

Respondent Lakeridge is a corporate entity with a single owner, MBP Equity Partners. When Lakeridge filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy, it had a pair of substantial debts: It owed petitioner U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank over $10 million and MBP another $2.76 million. Lakeridge submitted a reorganization plan, proposing to impair the interests of both U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank and MBP. U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank refused the offer, thus blocking Lake- ridge\xe2\x80\x99s option for reorganization through a fully consensual plan. See\n\n11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71129(a)(8). Lakeridge then turned to the so-called \xe2\x80\x9ccramdown\xe2\x80\x9d plan option for imposing a plan impairing the interests of a non-consenting class of creditors. See \xc2\xa71129(b). Among the prerequisites for judicial approval of such a plan is that another impaired class of creditors has consented to it. See \xc2\xa71129(a)(10). But crucially here, the consent of a creditor who is also an \xe2\x80\x9cinsider\xe2\x80\x9d of the debtor does not count for that purpose. Ibid. The Bankruptcy Code\xe2\x80\x99s definition of an insider \xe2\x80\x9cincludes\xe2\x80\x9d any director, officer, or \xe2\x80\x9cperson in control\xe2\x80\x9d of the entity. \xc2\xa7101(31)(B)(i)\xe2\x80\x93(iii). Courts have devised tests for identifying other, so-called \xe2\x80\x9cnon-statutory\xe2\x80\x9d insiders, focusing, in whole or in part, on whether a person\xe2\x80\x99s transactions with the debtor were at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length.

\n

Here, MBP (an insider of Lakeridge) could not provide the partial agreement needed for a cramdown plan, and Lakeridge\xe2\x80\x99s reorganization was thus impeded. MBP sought to transfer its claim against Lakeridge to a non-insider who could agree to the cramdown plan. Kathleen Bartlett, an MBP board member and Lakeridge officer, offered MBP\xe2\x80\x99s claim to Robert Rabkin, a retired surgeon, for $5,000. Rabkin purchased the claim and consented to Lakeridge\xe2\x80\x99s proposed reorganization. U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank objected, arguing that Rabkin was a non-statutory insider because he had a \xe2\x80\x9cromantic\xe2\x80\x9d relationship with Bartlett and the purchase was not an arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length transaction. The Bankruptcy Court rejected U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank\xe2\x80\x99s argument. The Ninth Circuit affirmed. Viewing the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision as one based on a finding that the relevant transaction was conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length, the Ninth Circuit held that that finding was entitled to clear-error review, and could not be reversed under that deferential standard.

\n

Held: The Ninth Circuit was right to review the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination for clear error (rather than de\xc2\xa0novo). At the heart of this case is a so-called \xe2\x80\x9cmixed question\xe2\x80\x9d of law and fact\xe2\x80\x94whether the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s findings of fact satisfy the legal test chosen for conferring non-statutory insider status. U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank contends that the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of this mixed question must be reviewed de novo, while Lakeridge (joined by the Federal Government) argues for a clear-error standard.

\n

For all their differences, both parties rightly point to the same query: What is the nature of the mixed question here and which kind of court (bankruptcy or appellate) is better suited to resolve it? Mixed questions are not all alike. Some require courts to expound on the law, and should typically be reviewed de\xc2\xa0novo. Others immerse courts in case-specific factual issues, and should usually be reviewed with deference. In short, the standard of review for a mixed question depends on whether answering it entails primarily legal or factual work.

\n

Here, the Bankruptcy Court confronted the question whether the basic facts it had discovered (concerning Rabkin\xe2\x80\x99s relationships, motivations, etc.) were sufficient to make Rabkin a non-statutory insider. Using the transactional prong of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s legal test for identifying such insiders (whether the transaction was conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length, i.e., as though the two parties were strangers) the mixed question became: Given all the basic facts found, was Rabkin\xe2\x80\x99s purchase of MBP\xe2\x80\x99s claim conducted as if the two were strangers to each other? That is about as factual sounding as any mixed question gets. Such an inquiry primarily belongs in the court that has presided over the presentation of evidence, that has heard all the witnesses, and that has both the closest and deepest understanding of the record\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the bankruptcy court. One can arrive at the same point by asking how much legal work applying the arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length test requires. It is precious little\xe2\x80\x94as shown by judicial opinions applying the familiar legal term without further elaboration. Appellate review of the arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length issue\xe2\x80\x94even if conducted de novo\xe2\x80\x94will not much clarify legal principles or provide guidance to other courts resolving other disputes. The issue is therefore one that primarily rests with a bankruptcy court, subject only to review for clear error. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

814 F.\xc2\xa03d 993, affirmed.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 15\xe2\x80\x931509

_________________

U.S. BANK NATIONAL ASSOCIATION, TRUSTEE, by and through CWCAPITAL ASSET MANAGEMENT LLC, PETITIONER v. THE VILLAGE AT LAKERIDGE, LLC

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

[March 5, 2018]

Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

The Bankruptcy Code places various restrictions on anyone who qualifies as an \xe2\x80\x9cinsider\xe2\x80\x9d of a debtor. The statutory definition of that term lists a set of persons related to the debtor in particular ways. See11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7101(31). Courts have additionally recognized as insiders some persons not on that list\xe2\x80\x94commonly known as \xe2\x80\x9cnon-statutory insiders.\xe2\x80\x9d The conferral of that status often turns on whether the person\xe2\x80\x99s transactions with the debtor (or another of its insiders) were at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length. In this case, we address how an appellate court should review that kind of determination: de\xc2\xa0novo or for clear error? We hold that a clear-error standard should apply.

I

Chapter 11 of the Bankruptcy Code enables a debtor company to reorganize its business under a court-approved plan governing the distribution of assets to creditors. See11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101 et\xc2\xa0seq. The plan divides claims against the debtor into discrete \xe2\x80\x9cclasses\xe2\x80\x9d and specifies the \xe2\x80\x9ctreatment\xe2\x80\x9d each class will receive. \xc2\xa71123; see \xc2\xa71122. Usually, a bankruptcy court may approve such a plan only if every affected class of creditors agrees to its terms. See \xc2\xa71129(a)(8). But in certain circumstances, the court may confirm what is known as a \xe2\x80\x9ccramdown\xe2\x80\x9d plan\xe2\x80\x94that is, a plan impairing the interests of some non-consenting class. See \xc2\xa71129(b). Among the prerequisites for judicial approval of a cramdown plan is that another impaired class of creditors has consented to it. See \xc2\xa71129(a)(10). But crucially for this case, the consent of a creditor who is also an \xe2\x80\x9cinsider\xe2\x80\x9d of the debtor does not count for that purpose. See ibid. (requiring \xe2\x80\x9cat least one\xe2\x80\x9d impaired class to have \xe2\x80\x9caccepted the plan, determined with-out including any acceptance of the plan by any insider\xe2\x80\x9d).

The Code enumerates certain insiders, but courts have added to that number. According to the Code\xe2\x80\x99s defini- tional section, an insider of a corporate debtor \xe2\x80\x9cincludes\xe2\x80\x9d any director, officer, or \xe2\x80\x9cperson in control\xe2\x80\x9d of the entity. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7101(31)(B)(i)\xe2\x80\x93(iii). Because of the word \xe2\x80\x9cincludes\xe2\x80\x9d in that section, courts have long viewed its list of insiders as non-exhaustive. See \xc2\xa7102(3) (stating as one of the Code\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c[r]ules of construction\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98includes\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98including\xe2\x80\x99 are not limiting\xe2\x80\x9d); 2 A. Resnick & H. Sommer, Collier on Bankruptcy \xc2\xb6101.31, p. 101\xe2\x80\x93142 (16th ed. 2016) (discussing cases). Accordingly, courts have devised tests for identifying other, so-called \xe2\x80\x9cnon-statutory\xe2\x80\x9d insiders. The decisions are not entirely uniform, but many focus, in whole or in part, on whether a person\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ctransaction of business with the debtor is not at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting In\xc2\xa0re U.\xc2\xa0S. Medical, Inc., 531 F.\xc2\xa03d 1272, 1280 (CA10 2008)).

This case came about because the Code\xe2\x80\x99s list of insiders placed an obstacle in the way of respondent Lakeridge\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to reorganize under Chapter 11. Lakeridge is a corporate entity which, at all relevant times, had a single owner, MBP Equity Partners, and a pair of substantial debts. The company owed petitioner U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank over $10 million for the balance due on a loan. And it owed MBP another $2.76 million. In 2011, Lakeridge filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. The reorganization plan it submitted placed its two creditors in separate classes and proposed to impair both of their interests. U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank refused that offer, thus taking a fully consensual plan off the table. But likewise, a cramdown plan based only on MBP\xe2\x80\x99s consent could not go forward. Recall that an insider cannot provide the partial agreement needed for a cramdown plan. See supra, at 2; \xc2\xa71129(a)(10). And MBP was the consummate insider: It owned Lakeridge and so was\xe2\x80\x94according to the Code\xe2\x80\x99s definition\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cin control\xe2\x80\x9d of the debtor. \xc2\xa7101(31)(B)(iii). The path to a successful reorganization was thus impeded, and Lakeridge was faced with liquidation. Unless .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0

Unless MBP could transfer its claim against Lakeridge to a non-insider who would then agree to the reorganization plan. So that was what MBP attempted. Kathleen Bartlett, a member of MBP\xe2\x80\x99s board and an officer of Lake- ridge, approached Robert Rabkin, a retired surgeon, and offered to sell him MBP\xe2\x80\x99s $2.76 million claim for $5,000. Rabkin took the deal. And as the new holder of MBP\xe2\x80\x99s old loan, he consented to Lakeridge\xe2\x80\x99s proposed reorganization. As long as he was not himself an insider, Rabkin\xe2\x80\x99s agreement would satisfy one of the prerequisites for a cramdown plan. See \xc2\xa71129(a)(10); supra, at 2. That would bring Lakeridge a large step closer to reorganizing its business over U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank\xe2\x80\x99s objection.

Hence commenced this litigation about whether Rabkin, too, was an insider. U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank argued that he qualified as a non-statutory insider because he had a \xe2\x80\x9cromantic\xe2\x80\x9d relationship with Bartlett and his purchase of MBP\xe2\x80\x99s loan \xe2\x80\x9cwas not an arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length transaction.\xe2\x80\x9d Motion to Designate Claim of Robert Rabkin as an Insider Claim in No. 11\xe2\x80\x9351994 (Bkrtcy. Ct. Nev.), Doc. 194, p.\xc2\xa011 (Motion).[1] At an evidentiary hearing, both Rabkin and Bartlett testified that their relationship was indeed \xe2\x80\x9cromantic.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 128, 142\xe2\x80\x93143.[2] But the Bankruptcy Court still rejected U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank\xe2\x80\x99s view that Rabkin was a non-statutory insider. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 66a. The court found that Rabkin purchased the MBP claim as a \xe2\x80\x9cspeculative investment\xe2\x80\x9d for which he did adequate due diligence. Id., at 67a. And it noted that Rabkin and Bartlett, for all their dating, lived in separate homes and managed their finances independently. See id., at 66a.

The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed by a divided vote. According to the court, a creditor qualifies as a non-statutory insider if two conditions are met: \xe2\x80\x9c(1) the closeness of its relationship with the debtor is comparable to that of the enumerated insider classifications in [the Code], and (2) the relevant transaction is negotiated at less than arm\xe2\x80\x99s length.\xe2\x80\x9d In re Village at Lakeridge, LLC, 814 F.\xc2\xa03d 993, 1001 (2016). The majority viewed the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision as based on a finding that the relevant transaction here (Rabkin\xe2\x80\x99s purchase of MBP\xe2\x80\x99s claim) \xe2\x80\x9cwas conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1003, n.\xc2\xa015. That finding, the majority held, was entitled to clear-error review, and could not be reversed under that deferential standard. See id., at 1001\xe2\x80\x931003. Rabkin\xe2\x80\x99s consent could therefore support the cramdown plan. See id., at 1003. Judge Clifton dissented. He would have applied de\xc2\xa0novo review, but in any event thought the Bankruptcy Court committed clear error in declining to classify Rabkin as an insider. See id., at 1006.

This Court granted certiorari to decide a single question: Whether the Ninth Circuit was right to review for clear error (rather than de\xc2\xa0novo) the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination that Rabkin does not qualify as a non-statutory insider because he purchased MBP\xe2\x80\x99s claim in an arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length transaction. 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017).

II

To decide whether a particular creditor is a non-statutory insider, a bankruptcy judge must tackle three kinds of issues\xe2\x80\x94the first purely legal, the next purely factual, the last a combination of the other two. And to assess the judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision, an appellate court must consider all its component parts, each under the appropriate standard of review. In this case, only the standard for the final, mixed question is contested. But to resolve that dispute, we begin by describing the unalloyed legal and factual questions that both kinds of courts have to address along the way, as well as the answers that the courts below provided.

Initially, a bankruptcy court must settle on a legal test to determine whether someone is a non-statutory insider (again, a person who should be treated as an insider even though he is not listed in the Bankruptcy Code). But that choice of standard really resides with the next court: As all parties agree, an appellate panel reviews such a legal conclusion without the slightest deference. See Highmark Inc. v. Allcare Health Management. System, Inc., 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 4) (\xe2\x80\x9cTraditionally, decisions on questions of law are reviewable de\xc2\xa0novo\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Tr. of Oral Arg. 29\xe2\x80\x9330, 33. The Ninth Circuit here, as noted earlier, endorsed a two-part test for non-statutory insider status, asking whether the person\xe2\x80\x99s relationship with the debtor was similar to those of listed insiders and whether the relevant prior transaction was at \xe2\x80\x9cless than arm\xe2\x80\x99s length.\xe2\x80\x9d 814 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1001; see supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. And the Ninth Circuit held that the Bankruptcy Court had used just that standard\xe2\x80\x94more specifically, that it had denied insider status under the test\xe2\x80\x99s second, transactional prong. See 814 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1002\xe2\x80\x931003, and n.\xc2\xa015; supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. We do not address the correctness of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s legal test; indeed, we specifically rejected U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank\xe2\x80\x99s request to include that question in our grant of certiorari. See 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___; Pet. for Cert. i. We simply take that test as a given in deciding the standard-of-review issue we chose to resolve.

Along with adopting a legal standard, a bankruptcy court evaluating insider status must make findings of what we have called \xe2\x80\x9cbasic\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9chistorical\xe2\x80\x9d fact\xe2\x80\x94addressing questions of who did what, when or where, how or why. Thompson v. Keohane,516 U.\xc2\xa0S. 99,111 (1995). The set of relevant historical facts will of course depend on the legal test used: So under the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s test, the facts found may relate to the attributes of a particular relationship or the circumstances and terms of a prior transaction. By well-settled rule, such factual findings are reviewable only for clear error\xe2\x80\x94in other words, with a serious thumb on the scale for the bankruptcy court. See Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 52(a)(6) (clear-error standard); Fed. Rules Bkrtcy. Proc. 7052 and 9014(c) (applying Rule 52 to various bankruptcy proceedings). Accordingly, as all parties again agree, the Ninth Circuit was right to review deferentially the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s findings about Rabkin\xe2\x80\x99s relationship with Bartlett (e.g., that they did not \xe2\x80\x9ccohabitate\xe2\x80\x9d or pay each other\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cbills or living expenses\xe2\x80\x9d) and his motives for purchasing MBP\xe2\x80\x99s claim (e.g., to make a \xe2\x80\x9cspeculative investment\xe2\x80\x9d). App. to Pet. for Cert. 66a\xe2\x80\x9367a; see Tr. of Oral Arg. 8, 39.

What remains for a bankruptcy court, after all that, is to determine whether the historical facts found satisfy the legal test chosen for conferring non-statutory insider status. We here arrive at the so-called \xe2\x80\x9cmixed question\xe2\x80\x9d of law and fact at the heart of this case. Pullman-Standard v. Swint,456 U. S. 273,289, n.19 (1982) (A mixed question asks whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe historical facts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. satisfy the statutory standard, or to put it another way, whether the rule of law as applied to the established facts is or is not violated\xe2\x80\x9d). As already described, the Bankruptcy Court below had found a set of basic facts about Rabkin; and it had adopted a legal test for non-statutory insider status that requires (as one of its two prongs) a less-than-arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length transaction. See supra, at 4, 6. As its last move, the court compared the one to the other\xe2\x80\x94and determined that the facts found did not show the kind of preferential transaction necessary to turn a creditor into a non-statutory insider. For that decisive determination, what standard of review should apply?

The parties, after traveling so far together, part ways at this crucial point. U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank contends that the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of the mixed question must be reviewed de\xc2\xa0novo. That is because, U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank claims, application of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cvery general\xe2\x80\x9d standard to a set of basic facts requires the further elaboration of legal principles\xe2\x80\x94a task primarily for appellate courts. Brief for Petitioner 35; see id., at 53 (The \xe2\x80\x9copen-ended nature of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s standard\xe2\x80\x9d compels courts to \xe2\x80\x9cdevelop the norms and criteria they deem most appropriate\xe2\x80\x9d and so should be viewed as \xe2\x80\x9cquasi-legal\xe2\x80\x9d). By contrast, Lakeridge (joined by the Federal Government as amicus curiae) thinks a clear-error standard should apply. In Lakeridge\xe2\x80\x99s view, the ultimate law-application question is all \xe2\x80\x9cbound up with the case-specific details of the highly factual circumstances below\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and thus falls naturally within the domain of bankruptcy courts. Brief for Respondent 17; see Brief for United States 21 (similarly describing the mixed question as \xe2\x80\x9cfact-intensive\xe2\x80\x9d).

For all their differences, both parties rightly point us to the same query: What is the nature of the mixed question here and which kind of court (bankruptcy or appellate) is better suited to resolve it? See Miller v. Fenton,474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104,114 (1985) (When an \xe2\x80\x9cissue falls somewhere between a pristine legal standard and a simple historical fact,\xe2\x80\x9d the standard of review often reflects which \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial actor is better positioned\xe2\x80\x9d to make the decision).[3] Mixed questions are not all alike. As U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank suggests, some require courts to expound on the law, particularly by amplifying or elaborating on a broad legal standard. When that is so\xe2\x80\x94when applying the law involves developing auxiliary legal principles of use in other cases\xe2\x80\x94appellate courts should typically review a decision de\xc2\xa0novo. See Salve Regina College v. Russell,499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 225\xe2\x80\x93233 (1991) (discussing appellate courts\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cinstitutional advantages\xe2\x80\x9d in giving legal guidance). But as Lakeridge replies, other mixed questions immerse courts in case-specific factual issues\xe2\x80\x94compelling them to marshal and weigh evidence, make credibility judgments, and otherwise address what we have (emphatically if a tad redundantly) called \xe2\x80\x9cmultifarious, fleeting, special, narrow facts that utterly resist generalization.\xe2\x80\x9d Pierce v. Underwood,487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 552\xe2\x80\x93562 (1988) (internal quotation marks omitted). And when that is so, appellate courts should usually review a decision with deference. See Anderson v. Bessemer City,470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564\xe2\x80\x93576 (1985) (discussing trial courts\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9csuperiority\xe2\x80\x9d in resolving such issues).[4] In short, the standard of review for a mixed question all depends\xe2\x80\x94on whether answering it entails primarily legal or factual work.

Now again, recall the mixed question the Bankruptcy Court confronted in this case. See supra, at 7. At a high level of generality, the court needed to determine whether the basic facts it had discovered (concerning Rabkin\xe2\x80\x99s relationships, motivations, and so on) were sufficient to make Rabkin a non-statutory insider. But the court\xe2\x80\x99s use of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s legal test for identifying such in- siders reduced that question to a more particular one: whether the facts found showed an arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length transaction between Rabkin and MBP. See ibid.[5] And still, we can further delineate that issue just by plugging in the widely (universally?) understood definition of an arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length transaction: a transaction conducted as though the two parties were strangers. See, e.g., Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1726 (10th ed. 2014). Thus the mixed question becomes: Given all the basic facts found, was Rabkin\xe2\x80\x99s purchase of MBP\xe2\x80\x99s claim conducted as if the two were strangers to each other?

That is about as factual sounding as any mixed question gets. Indeed, application of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length legal standard really requires what we have previously described as a \xe2\x80\x9cfactual inference[\xc2\xa0] from undisputed basic facts.\xe2\x80\x9d Commissioner v. Duberstein,363 U.\xc2\xa0S. 278,291 (1960) (holding that clear-error review applied to a decision that a particular transfer was a statutory \xe2\x80\x9cgift\xe2\x80\x9d). The court takes a raft of case-specific historical facts,[6] considers them as a whole, balances them one against another\xe2\x80\x94all to make a determination that when two particular persons entered into a particular transaction, they were (or were not) acting like strangers. Just to describe that inquiry is to indicate where it (primarily) belongs: in the court that has presided over the presentation of evidence, that has heard all the witnesses, and that has both the closest and the deepest understanding of the record\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the bankruptcy court.

And we can arrive at the same point from the opposite direction\xe2\x80\x94by asking how much legal work applying the arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length test requires. Precious little, in our view\xe2\x80\x94as shown by judicial opinions addressing that concept. Our own decisions, arising in a range of contexts, have never tried to elaborate on the established idea of a transaction conducted as between strangers; nor, to our knowledge, have lower courts. See, e.g., Jones v. Harris Associates L.\xc2\xa0P.,559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335,346 (2010); Commissioner v. Wemyss,324 U.\xc2\xa0S. 303,307 (1945); Pepper v. Litton,308 U.\xc2\xa0S. 295\xe2\x80\x93307 (1939). The stock judicial method is merely to state the requirement of such a transaction and then to do the fact-intensive job of exploring whether, in a particular case, it occurred. See, e.g., Wemyss, 324 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 307. Contrary to U.\xc2\xa0S. Bank\xe2\x80\x99s view, there is no apparent need to further develop \xe2\x80\x9cnorms and criteria,\xe2\x80\x9d or to devise a supplemental multi-part test, in order to apply the familiar term. Brief for Petitioner 53; see Tr. of Oral Arg. 18; supra, at 7. So appellate review of the arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length issue\xe2\x80\x94even if conducted de\xc2\xa0novo\xe2\x80\x94will not much clarify legal principles or provide guidance to other courts resolving other disputes. And that means the issue is not of the kind that appellate courts should take over.[7]

The Court of Appeals therefore applied the appropriate standard in reviewing the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination that Rabkin did not qualify as an insider because his transaction with MBP was conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length. A conclusion of that kind primarily rests with a bankruptcy court, subject only to review for clear error. We accordingly affirm the judgment below.

It is so ordered.

U.S. BANK NATIONAL ASSOCIATION, TRUSTEE, by and through CWCAPITAL ASSET MANAGEMENT LLC, PETITIONER v. THE VILLAGE AT LAKERIDGE, LLC

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

[March 5, 2018]

Justice Sotomayor, with whom Justice Kennedy, Justice Thomas, and Justice Gorsuch join, concurring.

The Court granted certiorari to decide \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether the appropriate standard of review for determining non-statutory insider status\xe2\x80\x9d under the Bankruptcy Code is de\xc2\xa0novo or clear error. Pet. for Cert. i. To answer that question, the Court \xe2\x80\x9ctake[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as a given\xe2\x80\x9d the two-prong test that the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit has adopted for determining whether a person or entity is an insider. Ante, at 6. I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in full because, within that context, I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis that a determination whether a particular transaction was conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length is a mixed question of law and fact that should be reviewed for clear error. See ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311.

I write separately, however, because I am concerned that our holding eludes the more fundamental question whether the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s underlying test is correct. If that test is not the right one, our holding regarding the standard of review may be for naught. That is because the appropriate standard of review is deeply intertwined with the test being applied. As the Court puts it, \xe2\x80\x9cthe standard of review for a mixed question all depends\xe2\x80\x94on whether answering it entails primarily legal or factual work.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9.

Here, the Court identifies the Ninth Circuit as having affirmed on the basis of the second prong of its test, pursuant to which the Ninth Circuit concluded that the relevant transaction between Robert Rabkin and MBP Equity Partners was conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length. Ante, at 6. Because that analysis is primarily factual in nature, the Court rightly concludes that appellate review of the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is for clear error. Ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. However, if the proper inquiry did not turn solely on an arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length analysis but rather involved a different balance of legal and factual work, the Court may have come to a different conclusion on the standard of review.

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s discussion of the standard of review thus begs the question of what the appropriate test for determining non-statutory insider status is. I do not seek to answer that question, as the Court expressly declined to grant certiorari on it. I have some concerns with the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s test, however, that would benefit from additional consideration by the lower courts.

As the Ninth Circuit interpreted the Code, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] creditor is not a non-statutory insider unless: (1) the closeness of its relationship with the debtor is comparable to that of the enumerated insider classifications in [11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C.] \xc2\xa7101(31), and (2) the relevant transaction is negotiated at less than arm\xe2\x80\x99s length.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Village at Lakeridge, LLC, 814 F.\xc2\xa03d 993, 1001 (2016) (emphasis added). Under this test, because prongs one and two are conjunctive, a court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the relevant transaction was conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length necessarily defeats a finding of non-statutory insider status, regardless of how close a person\xe2\x80\x99s relationship with the debtor is or whether he is otherwise comparable to a statutorily enumerated insider.[1]

It is not clear to me, however, that the Ninth Circuit has explained how this two-prong test is consistent with the plain meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cinsider\xe2\x80\x9d as it appears in the Code. The concept of \xe2\x80\x9cinsider\xe2\x80\x9d generally rests on the presumption that a person or entity alleged to be an in- sider is so connected with the debtor that any business conducted between them necessarily cannot be conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length. See Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 915 (10th ed. 2014) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cinsider\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n entity or person who is so closely related to a debtor that any deal between them will not be considered an arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length transaction and will be subject to close scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d). Title11 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7101(31) defines \xe2\x80\x9cinsider\xe2\x80\x9d by identifying certain individuals or entities who are considered insiders merely on the basis of their status, without regard to whether any relevant transaction is conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length. Such an individual is not under any circumstance able to vote for a reorganization plan. See \xc2\xa71129(a)(10).

In contrast, under prong two of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s test, an individual who is similar to, but does not fall precisely within, one of the categories of insiders listed in \xc2\xa7101(31) will not be considered an insider and will be able to vote under \xc2\xa71129(a)(10) so long as the transaction relevant to the bankruptcy proceeding is determined to have been conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length. This would include, for example, a romantic partner of an insider, even one who in all or most respects acts like a spouse.

Given that courts have interpreted \xe2\x80\x9cnon-statutory insiders\xe2\x80\x9d as deriving from the same statutory definition as the enumerated insiders in \xc2\xa7101(31), the basis for the disparate treatment of two similar individuals is not immediately apparent. Lower courts have concluded that the Code\xe2\x80\x99s use of the term \xe2\x80\x9cincludes\xe2\x80\x9d in the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cinsider\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7101(31) signals that Congress contemplated that certain other persons or entities in addition to those listed would qualify as insiders. See ante, at 2. Notably, this Court has never addressed that issue directly, although the Court has held in other contexts that \xe2\x80\x9cthe term \xe2\x80\x98including\xe2\x80\x99 is not one of all-embracing definition, but connotes simply an illustrative application of the general principle.\xe2\x80\x9d Federal Land Bank of St. Paul v. Bismarck Lumber Co.,314 U.\xc2\xa0S. 95,100 (1941).

Assuming \xc2\xa7101(31) encompasses such \xe2\x80\x9cnon-statutory insiders,\xe2\x80\x9d the only clue we have as to which persons or entities fall within that category is the list of enumerated insiders and the presumption of lack of arm\xe2\x80\x99s length that follows from that label. Because each of those persons or entities are considered insiders regardless of whether a particular transaction appears to have been conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length, it is not clear why the same should not be true of non-statutory insiders. That is, an enumerated \xe2\x80\x9cinsider\xe2\x80\x9d does not cease being an insider just because a court finds that a relevant transaction was conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length. Then why should a finding that a transaction was conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length, without more, conclusively foreclose a finding that a person or entity is a \xe2\x80\x9cnon-statutory insider\xe2\x80\x9d?

Of course, courts must develop some principled method of determining what other individuals or entities fall within the term \xe2\x80\x9cinsider\xe2\x80\x9d other than those expressly provided. I can conceive of at least two possible legal standards that are consistent with the understanding that insider status inherently presumes that transactions are not conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length. First, it could be that the inquiry should focus solely on a comparison between the characteristics of the alleged non-statutory insider and the enumerated insiders, and if they share sufficient commonalities, the alleged person or entity should be deemed an insider regardless of the apparent arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length nature of any transaction. Cf. In re Longview Aluminum, LLC, 657 F. 3d 507, 510\xe2\x80\x93511 (CA7 2011) (considering only whether a manager of a debtor corporation was comparable to the enumerated insiders, regardless of whether any transaction was conducted at less-than-arm\xe2\x80\x99s length).

Second, it could be that the test should focus on a broader comparison that includes consideration of the circumstances surrounding any relevant transaction. If a transaction is determined to have been conducted at less-than-arm\xe2\x80\x99s length, it may provide strong evidence in the context of the relationship as a whole that the alleged non-statutory insider should indeed be considered an insider. Relatedly, if the transaction does appear to have been undertaken at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length, that may be evidence, considered together with other aspects of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 relationship, that the alleged non-statutory insider should not, in fact, be deemed an insider.

Neither of these conceptions reflects the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s test. Rather, the Ninth Circuit considered separately whether Rabkin was comparable to an enumerated insider and whether the transaction between Rabkin and MBP was conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length. See 814 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1002\xe2\x80\x931003. Because the Ninth Circuit concluded that the transaction was undertaken at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length, that finding was dispositive of non-statutory insider status under their test, leading this Court, in turn, to consider the standard of review only with respect to that prong.

It is conceivable, however, that if the appropriate test were different from the one articulated by the Ninth Circuit, such as the two examples I outlined above, the applicable standard of review would be different as well. See ante, at 6, 9, n. 5. To make more concrete how this may play out in practice, I briefly walk through how I might apply my two proposed tests to the facts of this case.

If a comparative analysis were the right test, and assuming, arguendo, that it involves more legal than factual work thus resulting in de\xc2\xa0novo review, certain aspects of Rabkin\xe2\x80\x99s relationship with Kathleen Bartlett, an undisputed insider of the debtor, strike me as suggesting that Rabkin should have been designated as a non-statutory insider. Rabkin purchased the claim from MBP, but Bartlett, a member of MBP\xe2\x80\x99s board, facilitated the transaction. Even though Rabkin and Bartlett kept separate finances and lived separately, they shared a \xe2\x80\x9cromantic\xe2\x80\x9d relationship, see ante, at 4; Rabkin knew that the debtor was in bankruptcy, 814 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1003; and Bartlett approached only Rabkin with the offer to sell MBP\xe2\x80\x99s claim, id., at 1002. In a strict comparative analysis, Rabkin\xe2\x80\x99s interactions with Bartlett and MBP suggest that he may have been acting comparable to an enumerated insider, for example, like a relative of an officer of an insider. See \xc2\xa7101(31)(B)(vi).

Even if the comparative analysis included a broader consideration of features of the transaction that suggest it was conducted at arm\xe2\x80\x99s length, and assuming, arguendo, that de\xc2\xa0novo review would apply, it is not obvious that those features would outweigh the aspects of the relationship that are concerning. Even though Rabkin purport- edly lacked knowledge of the cramdown plan prior to his purchase and considered the purchase a \xe2\x80\x9csmall investment\xe2\x80\x9d not warranting due diligence, 814 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1003, there was no evidence of negotiation over the price, id., at 1004 (Clifton, J., dissenting), or any concrete evidence that MBP obtained real value in the deal aside from the prospect of Rabkin\xe2\x80\x99s vote in the cramdown.[2]

Even if the proper test for insider status called for clear error review, it is possible that the facts of this case when considered through the lens of that test, as opposed to one focused solely on arm\xe2\x80\x99s length, may have warranted a finding that Rabkin was a non-statutory insider.

This is all to say that I hope that courts will continue to grapple with the role that an arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length inquiry should play in a determination of insider status. In the event that the appropriate test for determining non-statutory insider status is different from the one that the Ninth Circuit applied, and involves a different balance of legal and factual work than the Court addresses here, it is possible I would view the applicable standard of review differently. Because I do not read the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion as foreclosing that result, I join it in full.

\n

As the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion makes clear, courts of appeals may continue to elaborate in more detail the legal standards that will govern whether a person or entity is a non-statutory insider under the Bankruptcy Code. Ante, at 6, 11, n.\xc2\xa07. As courts of appeals address these issues and make more specific rulings based on the facts and circumstances of individual cases, it may be that instructive, more specifically defined rules will develop.

\n

This leads to an additional point. Under the test that the Court of Appeals applied here, there is some room for doubt that the Bankruptcy Judge was correct in concluding that Rabkin was not an insider, especially without further inquiry into whether the offer Bartlett made to Rabkin could and should have been made to other parties who might have paid a higher price. See In re Village at Lakeridge, LLC, 814 F.\xc2\xa03d 993, 1006 (CA9 2016) (Clifton, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven if the clear error standard applies, the finding that Rabkin was not a non-statutory insider cannot survive scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d). As a result, whether the test for non-statutory insider status as formulated and used by courts in the Ninth Circuit is sufficient is not before us; and whether on these facts it was clear error to find that Rabkin was not an insider is also not before us.
