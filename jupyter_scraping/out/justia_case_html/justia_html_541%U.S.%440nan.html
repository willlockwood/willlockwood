TENNESSEE STUDENT ASSISTANCE CORPORATION v. HOOD

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x931606. Argued March 1, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 17, 2004

\n

Respondent Hood had an outstanding balance on student loans guaranteed by petitioner Tennessee Student Assistance Corporation (TSAC), a state entity, at the time she filed a Chapter 7 bankruptcy petition. Hood\xe2\x80\x99s general discharge did not cover her student loans, as she did not list them and they are only dischargeable if a bankruptcy court determines that excepting the debt from the order would be an \xe2\x80\x9cundue hardship\xe2\x80\x9d on the debtor, 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7523(a)(8). Hood subsequently reopened the petition, seeking an \xe2\x80\x9cundue hardship\xe2\x80\x9d determination. As prescribed by Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure 7001(6), 7003, and 7004, she filed a complaint and, later, an amended complaint, and served them with a summons on TSAC and others. The Bankruptcy Court denied TSAC\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss the complaint for lack of jurisdiction, holding that 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7106(a) abrogated the State\xe2\x80\x99s Eleventh Amendment sovereign immunity. The Sixth Circuit Bankruptcy Appellate Panel affirmed, as did the Sixth Circuit, which held that the Bankruptcy Clause gave Congress the authority to abrogate state sovereign immunity in \xc2\xa7106(a). This Court granted certiorari to determine whether the Bankruptcy Clause grants Congress such authority.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Because the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s discharge of a student loan debt does not implicate a State\xe2\x80\x99s Eleventh Amendment immunity, this Court does not reach the question on which certiorari was granted. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0States may be bound by some judicial actions without their consent. For example, the Eleventh Amendment does not bar federal jurisdiction over in rem admiralty actions when the State does not possess the res. California v. Deep Sea Research, Inc., 523 U. S. 491, 507\xe2\x80\x93\n508. A debt\xe2\x80\x99s discharge by a bankruptcy court is similarly an in rem proceeding. The court has exclusive jurisdiction over a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s property, wherever located, and over the estate. Once debts are discharged, a creditor who did not submit a proof of claim will be unable to collect on his unsecured loans. A bankruptcy court is able to provide the debtor a fresh start, even if all of his creditors do not participate, because the court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction is premised on the debtor and his estate, not on the creditors. Because the court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction is premised on the res, however, a nonparticipating creditor cannot be personally liable. States, whether or not they choose to participate in the proceeding, are bound by a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s discharge order no less than other creditors, see, e.g., New York v. Irving Trust Co., 288 U. S. 329, 333. And when the bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over the res is unquestioned, the exercise of its in rem jurisdiction to discharge the debt does not infringe a State\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty. TSAC argues, however, that the individualized process by which student loan debts are discharged unconstitutionally infringes its sovereignty. If a debtor does not affirmatively secure \xc2\xa7523(a)(8)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cundue hardship\xe2\x80\x9d determination, States choosing not to submit themselves to the court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction might receive some benefit: The debtor\xe2\x80\x99s personal liability on the loan may survive the discharge. TSAC misunderstands the proceeding\xe2\x80\x99s fundamental nature when it claims that Congress, by making a student loan debt presumptively nondischargeable and singling it out for an individualized determination, has authorized a suit against a State. The bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction is premised on the res, not the persona; that States were granted the presumptive benefit of nondischargeability does not alter the court\xe2\x80\x99s underlying authority. A debtor does not seek damages or affirmative relief from a State or subject an unwilling State to a coercive judicial process by seeking to discharge his debts. Indeed, this Court has endorsed individual determinations of States\xe2\x80\x99 interests within the federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 in rem jurisdiction, e.g., Deep Sea Research, supra. Although bankruptcy and admiralty are specialized areas of the law, there is no reason why the exercise of federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 in rem bankruptcy jurisdiction is more threatening to state sovereignty than the exercise of their in rem admiralty jurisdiction. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0With regard to the procedure used in this case, the Bankruptcy Rules require a debtor to file an adversary proceeding against the State to discharge student loan debts. While this is part of the original bankruptcy case and within the bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s in rem jurisdiction, it requires the service of a summons and a complaint, see Rules 7001(6), 7003, and 7004. The issuance of process is normally an indignity to a State\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty, because its purpose is to establish personal jurisdiction; but the court\xe2\x80\x99s in rem jurisdiction allows it to adjudicate the debtors\xe2\x80\x99 discharge claim without in personam jurisdiction over the State. Section 523(a)(8) does not require a summons, and absent Rule 7001(6) a debtor could proceed by motion, which would raise no constitutional concern. There is no reason why service of a summons, which in this case is indistinguishable in practical effect from a motion, should be given dispositive weight. Dismissal of the complaint is not appropriate here where the court has in rem jurisdiction and has not attempted to adjudicate any claims outside of that jurisdiction. This case is unlike an adversary proceeding by a bankruptcy trustee seeking to recover property in the State\xe2\x80\x99s hands on the grounds that the transfer was a voidable preference. Even if this Court were to hold that Congress lacked the ability to abrogate state sovereign immunity under the Bankruptcy Clause, the Bankruptcy Court would still have authority to make the undue hardship determination Hood seeks. Thus, this Court declines to decide whether a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of personal jurisdiction over a State would be valid under the Eleventh Amendment. If the Bankruptcy Court on remand exceeds its in rem jurisdiction, TSAC would be free to challenge the court\xe2\x80\x99s authority. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

319 F.\xc2\xa03d 755, affirmed and remanded.

TENNESSEE STUDENT ASSISTANCE CORPORATION,\nPETITIONER v. PAMELA L. HOOD

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[May 17, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Rehnquist delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Article I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa04, of the Constitution provides that Congress shall have the power \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o establish .\xc2\xa0 .\xc2\xa0. uniform Laws on the subject of Bankruptcies throughout the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d We granted certiorari to determine whether this Clause grants Congress the authority to abrogate state sovereign immunity from private suits. Because we conclude that a proceeding initiated by a debtor to determine the dischargeability of a student loan debt is not a suit against the State for purposes of the Eleventh Amendment, we affirm the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment, and we do not reach the question on which certiorari was granted.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner, Tennessee Student Assistance Corporation (TSAC), is a governmental corporation created by the Tennessee Legislature to administer student assistance programs. Tenn. Code Ann. \xc2\xa749\xe2\x80\x934\xe2\x80\x93201 (2002). TSAC guarantees student loans made to residents of Tennessee and to nonresidents who are either enrolled in an eligible school in Tennessee or make loans through an approved Tennessee lender. \xc2\xa749\xe2\x80\x934\xe2\x80\x93203.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Between July 1988 and February 1990, respondent, Pamela Hood, a resident of Tennessee, signed promissory notes for educational loans guaranteed by TSAC. In February 1999, Hood filed a \xe2\x80\x9cno asset\xe2\x80\x9d Chapter 7 bankruptcy petition in the United States Bankruptcy Court for the Western District of Tennessee; at the time of the filing, her student loans had an outstanding balance of $4,169.31. TSAC did not participate in the proceeding, but Sallie Mae Service, Inc. (Sallie Mae), submitted a proof of claim to the Bankruptcy Court, which it subsequently assigned to TSAC.[Footnote 1] The Bankruptcy Court granted Hood a general discharge in June 1999. See 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7727(a).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hood did not list her student loans in the bankruptcy proceeding, and the general discharge did not cover them. See \xc2\xa7727(b) (providing that a discharge under \xc2\xa7727(a) discharges the debtor from all prepetition debts except as listed in \xc2\xa7523(a)); \xc2\xa7523(a)(8) (providing that student loans guaranteed by governmental units are not included in a general discharge order unless the bankruptcy court determines that excepting the debt from the order would impose an \xe2\x80\x9cundue hardship\xe2\x80\x9d on the debtor). In September 1999, Hood reopened her bankruptcy petition for the limited purpose of seeking a determination by the Bankruptcy Court that her student loans were dischargeable as an \xe2\x80\x9cundue hardship\xe2\x80\x9d pursuant to \xc2\xa7523(a)(8). As prescribed by the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure, Hood filed a complaint against the United States of America, the Department of Education, and Sallie Mae, see Fed. Rules Bkrtcy. Proc. 7001(6) and 7003, and later filed an amended complaint in which she included TSAC and University Account Services as additional defendants and deleted Sallie Mae. The complaint and the amended complaint were served along with a summons on each of the named parties. See Rule 7004.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In response, TSAC filed a motion to dismiss the complaint for lack of jurisdiction, asserting Eleventh Amendment sovereign immunity.[Footnote 2] The Bankruptcy Court denied the motion, holding that 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7106(a) was a valid abrogation of TSAC\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity. App. to Pet. for Cert. A\xe2\x80\x9362. TSAC took an interlocutory appeal, see Puerto Rico Aqueduct and Sewer Authority v. Metcalf & Eddy, Inc., 506 U. S. 139, 147 (1993), and a unanimous Bankruptcy Appellate Panel of the Sixth Circuit affirmed, 262 B.\xc2\xa0R. 412 (2001). TSAC appealed the Panel\xe2\x80\x99s decision to the United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit. That court affirmed, holding that the States ceded their immunity from private suits in bankruptcy in the Constitutional Convention, and therefore, the Bankruptcy Clause, U. S. Const., Art. 1, \xc2\xa78, cl. 4, provided Congress with the necessary authority to abrogate state sovereign immunity in 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7106(a). 319 F.\xc2\xa03d 755, 767 (2003). One judge concurred in the judgment, concluding that TSAC waived its sovereign immunity when it accepted Sallie Mae\xe2\x80\x99s proof of claim.[Footnote 3] Id., at 768. We granted certiorari, 539 U. S. 986 (2003), and now affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals. Because we hold that a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s discharge of a student loan debt does not implicate a State\xe2\x80\x99s Eleventh Amendment immunity, we do not reach the broader question addressed by the Court of Appeals.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By its terms, the Eleventh Amendment precludes suits \xe2\x80\x9cin law or equity, commenced or prosecuted against one of the United States by Citizens of another State, or by Citizens or Subjects of any Foreign State.\xe2\x80\x9d For over a century, however, we have recognized that the States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign immunity is not limited to the literal terms of the Eleventh Amendment. See Hans v. Louisiana, 134 U. S. 1 (1890). Although the text of the Amendment refers only to suits against a State by citizens of another State, we have repeatedly held that an unconsenting State also is immune from suits by its own citizens. See, e.g., id., at 15; Duhne v. New Jersey, 251 U. S. 311, 313 (1920); Great Northern Life Ins. Co. v. Read, 322 U. S. 47, 51 (1944); Employees of Dept. of Public Health and Welfare of Mo. v. Department of Public Health and Welfare of Mo., 411 U. S. 279, 280 (1973); Edelman v. Jordan, 415 U. S. 651, 662\xe2\x80\x93663 (1974); Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Florida, 517 U. S. 44, 55 (1996).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0States, nonetheless, may still be bound by some judicial actions without their consent. In California v. Deep Sea Research, Inc., 523 U. S. 491 (1998), we held that the Eleventh Amendment does not bar federal jurisdiction over in rem admiralty actions when the State is not in possession of the property. In that case, a private corporation located a historic shipwreck, the S.\xc2\xa0S. Brother Jonathan, in California\xe2\x80\x99s territorial waters. The corporation filed an in rem action in federal court seeking rights to the wreck and its cargo. The State of California intervened, arguing that it possessed title to the wreck and that its sovereign immunity precluded the court from adjudicating its rights. While acknowledging that the Eleventh Amendment might constrain federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 admiralty jurisdiction in some instances, id., at 503 (citing Ex parte New York, 256 U. S. 490 (1921) (New York I); Ex parte New York, 256 U. S. 503 (1921) (New York II); Florida Dept. of State v. Treasure Salvors, Inc., 458 U. S. 670 (1982)), we held that the States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign immunity did not prohibit in rem admiralty actions in which the State did not possess the res, 523 U. S., at 507\xe2\x80\x93508 (citing e.g., The Davis, 10 Wall. 15 (1870); The Pesaro, 255 U. S. 216 (1921)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The discharge of a debt by a bankruptcy court is similarly an in rem proceeding. See Gardner v. New Jersey, 329 U. S. 565, 574 (1947); Straton v. New, 283 U. S. 318, 320\xe2\x80\x93321 (1931); Hanover Nat. Bank v. Moyses, 186 U. S. 181, 192 (1902); New Lamp Chimney Co. v. Ansonia Brass & Copper Co., 91 U. S. 656, 662 (1876). Bankruptcy courts have exclusive jurisdiction over a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s property, wherever located, and over the estate. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71334(e). In a typical voluntary bankruptcy proceeding under Chapter 7, the debtor files a petition for bankruptcy in which he lists his debts or his creditors, Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 1007(a)(1); the petition constitutes an order for relief, 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7301. The court clerk notifies the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s creditors of the order for relief, see Rule 2002(l), and if a creditor wishes to participate in the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets, he files a proof of claim, Rule 3002(a); see 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7726. If a creditor chooses not to submit a proof of claim, once the debts are discharged, the creditor will be unable to collect on his unsecured loans. Rule 3002(a); see 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7726. The discharge order releases a debtor from personal liability with respect to any discharged debt by voiding any past or future judgments on the debt and by operating as an injunction to prohibit creditors from attempting to collect or to recover the debt. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7524(a)(1), (2); 3 W. Norton, Bankruptcy Law and Practice 2d \xc2\xa748:1, p. 48\xe2\x80\x933 (1998) (hereinafter Norton).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A bankruptcy court is able to provide the debtor a fresh start in this manner, despite the lack of participation of all of his creditors, because the court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction is premised on the debtor and his estate, and not on the creditors. In\xc2\xa0re Collins, 173 F.\xc2\xa03d 924, 929 (CA4 1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cA federal court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over the dischargeability of debt \xe2\x80\xa6 derives not from jurisdiction over the state or other creditors, but rather from jurisdiction over debtors and their estates\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and citation omitted)); see also Gardner, supra, at 572; In\xc2\xa0re Ellett, 254 F.\xc2\xa03d 1135, 1141 (CA9 2001); Texas v. Walker, 142 F.\xc2\xa03d 813, 822 (CA5 1998). A bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s in rem jurisdiction permits it to \xe2\x80\x9cdetermin[e] all claims that anyone, whether named in the action or not, has to the property or thing in question. The proceeding is \xe2\x80\x98one against the world.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 16 J. Moore, et\xc2\xa0al., Moore\xe2\x80\x99s Federal Practice \xc2\xa7108.70[1], p. 108\xe2\x80\x93106 (3d ed. 2004). Because the court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction is premised on the res, however, a nonparticipating creditor cannot be subjected to personal liability. See Freeman v. Alderson, 119 U. S. 185, 188\xe2\x80\x93189 (1886) (citing Cooper v. Reynolds, 10 Wall. 308 (1870)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under our longstanding precedent, States, whether or not they choose to participate in the proceeding, are bound by a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s discharge order no less than other creditors. In New York v. Irving Trust Co., 288 U. S. 329 (1933), we sustained an order of the Bankruptcy Court which barred the State of New York\xe2\x80\x99s tax claim because it was not filed within the time fixed for the filing of claims. We held that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f a state desires to participate in the assets of a bankrupt, she must submit to the appropriate requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 333; see also Gardner, supra, at 574 (holding that a State waives its sovereign immunity by filing a proof of claim). And in Van Huffel v. Harkelrode, 284 U. S. 225, 228\xe2\x80\x93229 (1931), we held that the Bankruptcy Court had the authority to sell a debtor\xe2\x80\x99s property \xe2\x80\x9cfree and clear\xe2\x80\x9d of a State\xe2\x80\x99s tax lien. At least when the bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over the res is unquestioned, cf. United States v. Nordic Village, Inc., 503 U. S. 30 (1992), our cases indicate that the exercise of its in rem jurisdiction to discharge a debt does not infringe state sovereignty.[Footnote 4] Cf. Hoffman v. Connecticut Dept. of Income Maintenance, 492 U. S. 96, 102 (1989) (plurality opinion) (applying Eleventh Amendment analysis where a Bankruptcy Court sought to issue a money judgment against a nonconsenting State).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0TSAC concedes that States are generally bound by a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s discharge order, see Tr. of Oral Arg. 17, but argues that the particular process by which student loan debts are discharged unconstitutionally infringes its sovereignty. Student loans used to be presumptively discharged in a general discharge. But in 1976, Congress provided a significant benefit to the States by making it more difficult for debtors to discharge student loan debts guaranteed by States. Education Amendments of 1976, \xc2\xa7439A(a), 90 Stat. 2141 (codified at 20 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71087\xe2\x80\x933 (1976 ed.), repealed by Pub. L. 95\xe2\x80\x93598, \xc2\xa7317, 92 Stat. 2678). That benefit is currently governed by 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7523(a)(8), which provides that student loan debts guaranteed by governmental units are not included in a general discharge order unless excepting the debt from the order would impose an \xe2\x80\x9cundue hardship\xe2\x80\x9d on the debtor. See also \xc2\xa7727(b) (providing that a discharge under \xc2\xa7727(a) discharges the debtor from all prepetition debts except as listed in \xc2\xa7523(a)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 523(a)(8) is \xe2\x80\x9cself-executing.\xe2\x80\x9d Norton \xc2\xa747:52, at 47\xe2\x80\x93137 to 47\xe2\x80\x93138; see also S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 95\xe2\x80\x93989, p. 79 (1978). Unless the debtor affirmatively secures a hardship determination, the discharge order will not include a student loan debt. Norton \xc2\xa747:52, at 47\xe2\x80\x93137 to 47\xe2\x80\x93138. Thus, the major difference between the discharge of a student loan debt and the discharge of most other debts is that governmental creditors, including States, that choose not to submit themselves to the court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction might still receive some benefit: The debtor\xe2\x80\x99s personal liability on the loan may survive the discharge.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is this change that TSAC contends infringes state sovereignty. Tr. of Oral Arg. 15\xe2\x80\x9316. By making a student loan debt presumptively nondischargeable and singling it out for an \xe2\x80\x9cindividualized adjudication,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., TSAC argues that Congress has authorized a suit against a State. But TSAC misunderstands the fundamental nature of the proceeding.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No matter how difficult Congress has decided to make the discharge of student loan debt, the bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction is premised on the res, not on the persona; that States were granted the presumptive benefit of nondischargeability does not alter the court\xe2\x80\x99s underlying authority. A debtor does not seek monetary damages or any affirmative relief from a State by seeking to discharge a debt; nor does he subject an unwilling State to a coercive judicial process. He seeks only a discharge of his debts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, we have previously endorsed individualized determinations of States\xe2\x80\x99 interests within the federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 in rem jurisdiction. In Van Huffel, we affirmed the bankruptcy courts\xe2\x80\x99 power to sell property free from incumbrances, including States\xe2\x80\x99 liens, and approvingly noted that some courts had chosen specifically to discharge States\xe2\x80\x99 liens for taxes. 284 U. S., at 228; cf. Gardner, 329 U. S., at 572\xe2\x80\x93574 (noting \xe2\x80\x9cthat the reorganization court had jurisdiction over the proof and allowance of the tax claims and that the exercise of that power was not a suit against the State\xe2\x80\x9d). Our decision in California v. Deep Sea Research, Inc., 523 U. S. 491 (1998), also involved an individualized in rem adjudication in which a State claimed an interest, as have other in rem admiralty cases involving sovereigns, e.g., The Davis, 10 Wall., at 19; The Siren, 7 Wall. 152, 159 (1869); The Pesaro, 255 U. S., at 219. Although both bankruptcy and admiralty are specialized areas of the law, we see no reason why the exercise of the federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 in\xc2\xa0rem bankruptcy jurisdiction is more threatening to state sovereignty than the exercise of their in rem admiralty jurisdiction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We find no authority, in fine, that suggests a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of its in rem jurisdiction to discharge a student loan debt would infringe state sovereignty in the manner suggested by TSAC. We thus hold that the undue hardship determination sought by Hood in this case is not a suit against a State for purposes of the Eleventh Amendment.[Footnote 5]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lastly, we deal with the procedure that was used in this case. Creditors generally are not entitled to personal service before a bankruptcy court may discharge a debt. Hanover Nat. Bank, 186 U. S., at 192. Because student loan debts are not automatically dischargeable, however, the Federal Rules of Bankruptcy Procedure provide creditors greater procedural protection. See Fed. Rules Bkrtcy. Proc. 7001(6), 7003, and 7004. The current Bankruptcy Rules require the debtor to file an \xe2\x80\x9cadversary proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d against the State in order to discharge his student loan debt. The proceeding is considered part of the original bankruptcy case, see 10 Collier on Bankruptcy \xc2\xb6 ;7003.02 (15th ed. rev. 2003), and still within the bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s in rem jurisdiction as discussed above. But, as prescribed by the Rules, an \xe2\x80\x9cadversary proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d requires the service of a summons and a complaint. Rules 7001(6), 7003, and 7004.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because this \xe2\x80\x9cadversary proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d has some similarities to a traditional civil trial, Justice Thomas contends that the Bankruptcy Court cannot make an undue hardship determination without infringing TSAC\xe2\x80\x99s sovereignty under Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. South Carolina Ports Authority, 535 U. S. 743 (2002). See post, at 2\xe2\x80\x936. In Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, we held that the Eleventh Amendment precluded a private party from haling an unconsenting State into a proceeding before the Federal Maritime Commission (FMC). We noted that we have applied a presumption since Hans v. Louisiana, 134 U. S. 1 (1890), \xe2\x80\x9cthat the Constitution was not intended to \xe2\x80\x98rais[e] up\xe2\x80\x99 any proceedings against the States that were \xe2\x80\x98anomalous and unheard of when the Constitution was adopted.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 535 U. S., at 755. Because agency adjudications were unheard of at the time of the founding, we had to determine whether the FMC proceeding was \xe2\x80\x9cthe type of proceedin[g] from which the Framers would have thought the States possessed immunity when they agreed to enter the Union.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 756. Noting the substantial similarities between a proceeding before the FMC and one before an Article III court, we concluded that the Hans presumption applied, see 535 U. S., at 756\xe2\x80\x93763, and that the Eleventh Amendment therefore precluded private suits in such a forum, id., at 769.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, however, there is no need to engage in a comparative analysis to determine whether the adjudication would be an affront to States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereignty. As noted above, we have long held that the bankruptcy courts\xe2\x80\x99 exercise of in rem jurisdiction is not such an offense. Supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x939. Nor is there any dispute that, if the Bankruptcy Court had to exercise personal jurisdiction over TSAC, such an adjudication would implicate the Eleventh Amendment. Our precedent has drawn a distinction between in rem and in personam jurisdiction, even when the underlying proceedings are, for the most part, identical. Thus, whether an in rem adjudication in a bankruptcy court is similar to civil litigation in a district court is irrelevant. If Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n were adopted, Deep Sea Research, Van Huffle, and Irving Trust, all of which involved proceedings resembling traditional civil adjudications, would likely have to be overruled. We are not willing to take such a step.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The issuance of process, nonetheless, is normally an indignity to the sovereignty of a State because its purpose is to establish personal jurisdiction over the State. We noted in Seminole Tribe, \xe2\x80\x9cThe Eleventh Amendment does not exist solely in order to prevent federal-court judgments that must be paid out of a State\xe2\x80\x99s treasury; it also serves to avoid the indignity of subjecting a State to the coercive process of judicial tribunals at the instance of private parties.\xe2\x80\x9d 517 U. S., at 58 (citations and internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, however, the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s in rem jurisdiction allows it to adjudicate the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s discharge claim without in personam jurisdiction over the State. See 4A C. Wright & A. Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa71070, pp. 280\xe2\x80\x93281 (3d ed. 2002) (noting jurisdiction over the person is irrelevant if the court has jurisdiction over the property). Hood does not argue that the court should exercise personal jurisdiction; all she wants is a determination of the dischargeability of her debt. The text of \xc2\xa7523(a)(8) does not require a summons, and absent Rule 7001(6) a debtor could proceed by motion, see Rule 9014 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n a contested matter \xe2\x80\xa6 not otherwise governed by these rules, relief shall be requested by motion\xe2\x80\x9d), which would raise no constitutional concern. Hood concedes that even if TSAC ignores the summons and chooses not to participate in the proceeding the Bankruptcy Court cannot discharge her debt without making an undue hardship determination. Tr. of Oral Arg. 33\xe2\x80\x9334.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We see no reason why the service of a summons, which in this case is indistinguishable in practical effect from a motion, should be given dispositive weight. As we said in Idaho v. Coeur d\xe2\x80\x99Alene Tribe of Idaho, 521 U. S. 261, 270 (1997), \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he real interests served by the Eleventh Amendment are not to be sacrificed to elementary mechanics of captions and pleading.\xe2\x80\x9d See New York I, 256 U. S., at 500 (a suit against a State \xe2\x80\x9cis to be determined not by the mere names of the titular parties but by the essential nature and effect of the proceeding, as it appears from the entire record\xe2\x80\x9d). To conclude that the issuance of a summons, which is required only by the Rules, precludes Hood from exercising her statutory right to an undue hardship determination would give the Rules an impermissible effect. 28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa72075 (\xe2\x80\x9c[The Bankruptcy Rules] shall not abridge, enlarge, or modify any substantive right\xe2\x80\x9d). And there is no reason to take such a step. TSAC sought only to dismiss the complaint for lack of jurisdiction in the Bankruptcy Court. Motion to Dismiss Complaint for Lack of Jurisdiction in No. 99\xe2\x80\x930847 (Bkrtcy. Ct. WD Tenn.), pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x932. Clearly dismissal of the complaint is not appropriate as the court has in rem jurisdiction over the matter, and the court here has not attempted to adjudicate any claims outside of that jurisdiction. The case before us is thus unlike an adversary proceeding by the bankruptcy trustee seeking to recover property in the hands of the State on the grounds that the transfer was a voidable preference. Even if we were to hold that Congress lacked the ability to abrogate state sovereign immunity under the Bankruptcy Clause, as TSAC urges us to do, the Bankruptcy Court would still have the authority to make the undue hardship determination sought by Hood.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We therefore decline to decide whether a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of personal jurisdiction over a State would be valid under the Eleventh Amendment. See Liverpool, New York & Philadelphia S.\xc2\xa0S. Co. v. Commissioners of Emigration, 113 U. S. 33, 39 (1885) (\xe2\x80\x9c[We are bound] never to anticipate a question of constitutional law in advance of the necessity of deciding it\xe2\x80\x9d). If the Bankruptcy Court on remand exceeds its in rem jurisdiction, TSAC, of course, would be free to challenge the court\xe2\x80\x99s authority. At this point, however, any such constitutional concern is merely hypothetical. The judgment of the United States Court\nof Appeals for the Sixth Circuit is affirmed, and the case\nis remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari in this case to decide whether Congress has the authority to abrogate state sovereign immunity under the Bankruptcy Clause. 539 U. S. 986 (2003). Instead of answering this question, the Court addresses a more difficult one regarding the extent to which a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of its in rem jurisdiction could offend the sovereignty of a creditor-State. I recognize that, as the Court concludes today, the in rem nature of bankruptcy proceedings might affect the ability of a debtor to obtain, by motion, a bankruptcy court determination that affects a creditor-State\xe2\x80\x99s rights, but I would not reach this difficult question here. Even if the Bankruptcy Court could have exercised its in rem jurisdiction to make an undue hardship determination by motion, I cannot ignore the fact that the determination in this case was sought pursuant to an adversary proceeding. Under Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. South Carolina Ports Authority, 535 U. S. 743 (2002), the adversary proceeding here clearly constitutes a suit against the State for sovereign immunity purposes. I would thus reach the easier question presented and conclude that Congress lacks authority to abrogate state sovereign immunity under the Bankruptcy Clause.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court avoids addressing respondent\xe2\x80\x99s principal argument\xe2\x80\x94which was the basis for the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision and which this Court granted certiorari in order to address\xe2\x80\x94namely, that Congress possesses the power under the Bankruptcy Clause to abrogate a State\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity from suit. Instead, the Court affirms the judgment of the Court of Appeals based on respondent\xe2\x80\x99s alternative argument, ante, at 3, that the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was \xe2\x80\x9can appropriate exercise of [its] in rem jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 35. Although respondent advanced this argument in the proceedings before the Bankruptcy Appellate Panel of the Sixth Circuit, Brief for Appellee in No. 00\xe2\x80\x938062, p. 8, she declined to do so in the Court of Appeals. Indeed, before that court, respondent relied entirely on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 ability to abrogate state sovereign immunity under the Bankruptcy Clause rather than on any in rem theory because, under her reading of Missouri v. Fiske, 290 U. S. 18 (1933), \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no in rem exception to a state\xe2\x80\x99s Eleventh Amendment immunity\xe2\x80\x9d in bankruptcy. Brief for Appellee in No. 01\xe2\x80\x935769 (CA6), p. 24. Furthermore, respondent did not raise the in rem argument in her brief in opposition before this Court. Under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 15.2, we may deem this argument waived. Caterpillar Inc. v. Lewis, 519 U. S. 61, 75, n. 13 (1996). And, we should do so here both because the argument is irrelevant to this case, and because the in rem question is both complex and uncertain, see Baldwin v. Reese, 541 U. S. ___ (2004).

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, the South Carolina Maritime Services, Inc. (SCMS), filed a complaint with the Federal Maritime Commission (FMC), an independent agency, alleging that a state-run port had violated the Shipping Act of 1984, 46 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App. \xc2\xa71701 et seq. We assumed without deciding that the FMC does not exercise \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial power,\xe2\x80\x9d Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 535 U. S., at 754, and nonetheless held that state sovereign immunity barred the adjudication of the SCMS\xe2\x80\x99 complaint. Id., at 769.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n turned on the \xe2\x80\x9coverwhelming\xe2\x80\x9d similarities between FMC proceedings and civil litigation in federal courts. Id., at 759. For example, FMC\xe2\x80\x99s rules governing pleadings and discovery are very similar to the analogous Federal Rules of Civil Procedure. Id., at 757\xe2\x80\x93758. Moreover, we noted that \xe2\x80\x9cthe role of the [administrative law judge], the impartial officer designated to hear a case, is similar to that of an Article III judge.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 758 (footnote and citation omitted). Based on these similarities, we held that, for purposes of state sovereign immunity, the adjudication before the FMC was indistinguishable from an adjudication in an Article III tribunal. See id., at 760\xe2\x80\x93761. Thus, Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n recognized that if the Framers would have found it an \xe2\x80\x9cimpermissible affront to a State\xe2\x80\x99s dignity to be required to answer the complaints of private parties in federal courts,\xe2\x80\x9d the Framers would have found it equally impermissible to compel States to do so simply because the adjudication takes place in an Article I rather than an Article III court. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the Court ignores Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n altogether, its reasoning applies to this case. The similarities between adversary proceedings in bankruptcy and federal civil litigation are striking. Indeed, the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure govern adversary proceedings in substantial part. The proceedings are commenced by the filing of a complaint, Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 7003; process is served, Rule 7005; the opposing party is required to file an answer, Rule 7007; and the opposing party can file counterclaims against the movant, Rule 7013. Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 8 applies to the parties\xe2\x80\x99 pleadings. Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 7008. Even the form of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 pleadings must comply with the federal rules for civil litigation. Rule 7010. \xe2\x80\x9cLikewise, discovery in [adversary proceedings] largely mirrors discovery in federal civil litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, supra, at 758. See Fed. Rules Bkrtcy. Proc. 7026\xe2\x80\x937037 (applying Fed. Rules Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa026\xe2\x80\x9337 to adversary proceedings). And, when a party fails to answer or appear in an adversary proceeding, the Federal Rule governing default judgments applies. Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc.7055 (adopting Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 55).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In spite of these similarities, the Court concludes that, because the bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction is premised on the res, the issuance of process in this case, as opposed to all others, does not subject an unwilling State to a coercive judicial process. Ante, at 10. The Court also views the adversary proceeding in this case differently than a typical adversary proceeding because, absent Fed. Rule Bkrtcy. Proc. 7001(6), the Court concludes that a debtor could obtain an undue hardship determination by motion consistent with a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s in rem jurisdiction and consistent with the Constitution. See ante, at 11.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Critically, however, the Court fails to explain why, simply because it asserts that this determination could have been made by motion, the adversary proceeding utilized in this case is somehow less offensive to state sovereignty. After all, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he very object and purpose of the 11th Amendment [is] to prevent the indignity of subjecting a State to the coercive process of judicial tribunals at the instance of private parties.\xe2\x80\x9d In re Ayers, 123 U. S. 443, 505 (1887); Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, supra, at 760; Alden v. Maine, 527 U. S. 706, 748 (1999); Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Florida, 517 U. S. 44, 58 (1996). The fact that an alternative proceeding exists, the use of which might not be offensive to state sovereignty, is irrelevant to whether the particular proceeding actually used subjects a particular State to the indignities of coercive process. Indeed, the dissent in Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, much like the Court does today, focused on the fact that the FMC was not required by statute to evaluate complaints through agency adjudication, 535 U. S., at 774\xe2\x80\x93776 (opinion of Breyer, J.,), and could have opted to evaluate complaints in some other manner. But this fact had no bearing on our decision in that case, nor should it control here. I simply cannot ignore the fact that respondent filed a complaint in the Bankruptcy Court \xe2\x80\x9cpray[ing] that proper process issue and that upon a hearing upon the merits that [the court] issue a judgment for [respondent] and against [petitioner] allowing [respondent\xe2\x80\x99s] debt to be discharged.\xe2\x80\x9d Complaint for Hardship Discharge, in No. 99\xe2\x80\x9322606\xe2\x80\x93K, Adversary No. 99\xe2\x80\x930847 (Bkrtcy. Ct. WD Tenn.), p. 1.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More importantly, although the adversary proceeding in this case does not require the State to \xe2\x80\x9cdefend itself\xe2\x80\x9d against petitioner in the ordinary sense, the effect is the same, whether done by adversary proceeding or by motion, and whether the proceeding is in personam or in rem. In order to preserve its rights, the State is compelled either to subject itself to the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction or to forfeit its rights. And, whatever the nature of the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction, it maintains at least as much control over nonconsenting States as the FMC, which lacks the power to enforce its own orders. Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n rejected the view that the FMC\xe2\x80\x99s lack of enforcement power means that parties are not coerced to participate in its proceedings because the effect is the same\xe2\x80\x94a State must submit to the adjudication or compromise its ability to defend itself in later proceedings. 535 U. S., at 761\xe2\x80\x93764. Here, if the State does not oppose the debtor\xe2\x80\x99s claim of undue hardship, the Bankruptcy Court is authorized to enter a default judgment without making an undue hardship determination. See Fed. Rules Bkrtcy. Proc. 7055, 9014 (adopting Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 55 in both adversary proceedings and in contested matters governed by motion). The Court apparently concludes otherwise, but, tellingly, its only support for that questionable proposition is a statement made at oral argument. See ante, at 11.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As I explain in Part I-B, infra, I do not contest the assertion that in bankruptcy, like admiralty, there might be a limited in rem exception to state sovereign immunity from suit. Nor do I necessarily reject the argument that this proceeding could have been resolved by motion without offending the dignity of the State. However, because this case did not proceed by motion, I cannot resolve the merits based solely upon what might have, but did not, occur. I would therefore hold that the adversary proceeding in this case constituted a suit against the State for sovereign immunity purposes.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The difficulty and complexity of the question of the scope of the Bankruptcy Court\xe2\x80\x99s in rem jurisdiction as it relates to a State\xe2\x80\x99s interests is a further reason that the Court should not address the question here without complete briefing and full consideration by the Court of\nAppeals.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Relying on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s recent recognition of a limited in rem exception to state sovereign immunity in certain admiralty actions, see California v. Deep Sea Research, Inc., 523 U. S. 491 (1998), the Court recognizes that \xe2\x80\x9cStates \xe2\x80\xa6 may still be bound by some judicial actions without their consent,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 4. The Court then acknowledges the undisputed fact that bankruptcy discharge proceedings are in rem proceedings. Ante, at 5. These facts, however, standing alone, do not compel the conclusion that the in rem exception should extend to this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Deep Sea Research, supra, does not make clear the extent of the in rem exception in admiralty, much less its potential application in bankruptcy. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s recognition of an in rem exception to state sovereign immunity in admiralty actions was informed, in part, by Justice Story\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the difference between admiralty actions and regular civil litigation. Justice Story doubted whether the Eleventh Amendment extended to admiralty and maritime suits at all because, in admiralty, \xe2\x80\x9cthe jurisdiction of the [federal] court is founded upon the possession of the thing; and if the State should interpose a claim for the property, it does not act merely in the character of a defendant, but as an actor.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Commentaries on the Constitution of the United States \xc2\xa71689, p. 491 (5th ed. 1891). Justice Story supported this view by contrasting suits in law or equity with suits in admiralty, which received a separate grant of jurisdiction under Article III. Id., at 491\xe2\x80\x93492. The Court, however, has since adopted a more narrow understanding of the in rem maritime exception. See Ex parte New York, 256 U. S. 490, 497 (1921) (\xe2\x80\x9cNor is the admiralty and maritime jurisdiction exempt from the operation of the rule [that a State may not be sued without its consent]\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, our holding in Deep Sea Research, was limited to actions where the res is not within the State\xe2\x80\x99s possession. 523 U. S., at 507\xe2\x80\x93508.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whatever the scope of the in rem exception in admiralty, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases reveal no clear principle to govern which, if any, bankruptcy suits are exempt from the Eleventh Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s bar. In Fiske, 290 U. S., at 28, the Court stated in no uncertain terms that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he fact that a suit in a federal court is in rem, or quasi in rem, furnishes no ground for the issue of process against a non-consenting State.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court contends that Fiske supports its argument because there the Court \xe2\x80\x9cnoted the State might still be bound by the federal court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication even if an injunction could not issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7, n.\xc2\xa05. But the Court in Fiske also suggested that the State might not be bound by the federal court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication\xe2\x80\x94a more weighty proposition given the circumstances of the case. Fiske, in part, involved the validity of a federal court decree entered in 1927, which determined that Sophie Franz had only a life interest in certain shares of stock previously held by her deceased husband. When Franz died in 1930, Franz\xe2\x80\x99s executor did not inventory the shares because the federal court decree declared Franz to have only a life interest in them. The dispute arose because the State sought to inventory those shares as assets of Franz\xe2\x80\x99s estate so that it could collect inheritance taxes on those shares. Although Fiske did not decide whether the 1927 federal decree was binding on the State, 290 U. S., at 29, the mere suggestion that the State might not be bound by the decree because it was not a party to an in rem proceeding in which it had no interest, see ibid., at least leaves in doubt the extent of any in rem exception in bankruptcy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our more recent decision in United States v. Nordic Village, Inc., 503 U. S. 30 (1992), casts some doubt upon the Court\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of any in rem exception in bankruptcy. Nordic Village explicitly recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cwe have never applied an in rem exception to the sovereign-immunity bar against monetary recovery, and have suggested that no such exception exists.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 38. Although Nordic Village involved the sovereign immunity of the Federal Government, it also supports the argument that no in rem exception exists for other types of relief against a State. Nordic Village interpreted 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7106(c) to waive claims for declaratory and injunctive, though not monetary, relief against the Government. 503 U. S., at 34\xe2\x80\x9337. We noted that this interpretation did not render \xc2\xa7106(c) irrelevant because a waiver of immunity with respect to claims for declaratory and injunctive relief would \xe2\x80\x9cperform a significant function\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cpermit[ing] a bankruptcy court to determine the amount and dischargeability of an estate\xe2\x80\x99s liability to the Government \xe2\x80\xa6 whether or not the Government filed a proof of claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 36. Our interpretation of \xc2\xa7106(c) to waive liability only for declaratory and injunctive relief strongly suggests that such a waiver is necessary\xe2\x80\x94i.e., that without the waiver, despite the bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s in rem jurisdiction, the bankruptcy court could not order declaratory or injunctive relief against a State without the State\xe2\x80\x99s consent. Cf. Raygor v. Regents of Univ. of Minn., 534 U. S. 533, 554, n.\xc2\xa011 (2002).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, the Court has previously held that a State can be bound by a bankruptcy court adjudication that affects a State\xe2\x80\x99s interest. See New York v. Irving Trust Co., 288 U. S. 329 (1933); Van Huffel v. Harkelrode, 284 U. S. 225 (1931). But, in neither of those cases did the Court attempt to undertake a sovereign immunity analysis. Irving Trust, for instance, rested on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cpower to establish uniform laws on the subject of bankruptcies,\xe2\x80\x9d 288 U. S., at 331, and the need for \xe2\x80\x9corderly and expeditious proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 333. And in Van Huffel, the Court appeared to rest its decision more on \xe2\x80\x9cthe requirements of bankruptcy administration,\xe2\x80\x9d 284 U. S., at 228, than the effect of the in rem nature of the proceedings on state sovereign immunity.* Perhaps recognizing that these precedents cannot support the weight of its reasoning, the Court attempts to limit its holding by explicitly declining to find an in rem exception to every exercise of a bankruptcy court\xe2\x80\x99s in rem jurisdiction that might offend state sovereignty, ante, at 9, n.\xc2\xa06. But, I can find no principle in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion to distinguish this case from any other. For this reason, I would not undertake this complicated inquiry.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress has made its intent to abrogate state sovereign immunity under the Bankruptcy Clause clear. See 11 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7106(a). The only question, then, is whether the Bankruptcy Clause grants Congress the power to do so. This Court has repeatedly stated that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress may not \xe2\x80\xa6 base its abrogation of the States\xe2\x80\x99 Eleventh Amendment immunity upon the powers enumerated in Article I.\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Trustees of Univ. of Ala. v. Garrett, 531 U. S. 356, 364 (2001). See also, e.g., Kimel v. Florida Bd. of Regents, 528 U. S. 62, 80 (2000) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress\xe2\x80\x99 powers under Article I of the Constitution do not include the power to subject States to suit at the hands of private individuals\xe2\x80\x9d); Florida Prepaid Postsecondary Ed. Expense Bd. v. College Savings Bank, 527 U. S. 627, 636 (1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cSeminole Tribe makes clear that Congress may not abrogate state sovereign immunity pursuant to its Article I powers\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite the clarity of these statements, the Court of Appeals held that the Bankruptcy Clause operates differently than Congress\xe2\x80\x99 other Article I powers because of its \xe2\x80\x9cuniformity requirement\xe2\x80\x9d, 319 F.\xc2\xa03d 755, 764 (CA6 2003). Our discussions of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 inability to abrogate state sovereign immunity through the use of its Article I powers reveal no such limitation. I would therefore reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the foregoing reasons, I respectfully dissent.
