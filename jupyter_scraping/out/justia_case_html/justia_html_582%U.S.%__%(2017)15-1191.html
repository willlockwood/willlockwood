SESSIONS, ATTORNEY GENERAL v. MORALES-SANTANA

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x931191.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 9, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 12, 2017

\n

The Immigration and Nationality Act provides the framework for acquisition of U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship from birth by a child born abroad, when one parent is a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen and the other a citizen of another nation. Applicable to married couples, the main rule in effect at the time here relevant,\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71401(a)(7) (1958 ed.), required the U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen parent to have ten years\xe2\x80\x99 physical presence in the United States prior to the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth, \xe2\x80\x9cat least five of which were after attaining\xe2\x80\x9d age 14. The rule is made applicable to unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen fathers by \xc2\xa71409(a), but \xc2\xa71409(c) creates an exception for an unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mother, whose citizenship can be transmitted to a child born abroad if she has lived continuously in the United States for just one year prior to the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth.

\n

Respondent Luis Ram\xc3\xb3n Morales-Santana, who has lived in the United States since he was 13, asserts U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship at birth based on the U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship of his biological father, Jos\xc3\xa9 Morales. Jos\xc3\xa9 moved to the Dominican Republic 20 days short of his 19th birthday, therefore failing to satisfy \xc2\xa71401(a)(7)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement of five years\xe2\x80\x99 physical presence after age 14. There, he lived with the Dominican woman who gave birth to Morales-Santana. Jos\xc3\xa9 accepted parental responsibility and included Morales-Santana in his household; he married Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s mother and his name was then added to hers on Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificate. In 2000, the Government sought to remove Morales-Santana based on several criminal convictions, ranking him as alien because, at his time of birth, his father did not satisfy the requirement of five years\xe2\x80\x99 physical presence after age 14. An immigration judge rejected Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s citizenship claim and ordered his removal. Morales-Santana later moved to reopen the proceedings, asserting that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to recognize that he derived citizenship from his U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen father violated the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s equal protection guarantee. The Board of Immigration Appeals denied the motion, but the Second Circuit reversed. Relying on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s post-1970 construction of the equal protection principle as it bears on gender-based classifications, the court held unconstitutional the differential treatment of unwed mothers and fathers. To cure this infirmity, the Court of Appeals held that Morales-Santana derived citizenship through his father, just as he would were his mother the U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0The gender line Congress drew is incompatible with the\n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that the Government accord to all persons \xe2\x80\x9cthe equal protection of the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Morales-Santana satisfies the requirements for third-party standing in seeking to vindicate his father\xe2\x80\x99s right to equal protection. Jos\xc3\xa9 Morales\xe2\x80\x99 ability to pass citizenship to his son easily satisfies the requirement that the third party have a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98close\xe2\x80\x99 relationship with the person who possesses the right.\xe2\x80\x9d Kowalski v. Tesmer,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 125\n. And Jos\xc3\xa9\xe2\x80\x99s death many years before the current controversy arose is \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98hindrance\xe2\x80\x99 to [Jos\xc3\xa9\xe2\x80\x99s] ability to protect his own interests.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Sections 1401 and 1409 date from an era when the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s lawbooks were rife with overbroad generalizations about the way men and women are. Today, such laws receive the heightened scrutiny that now attends \xe2\x80\x9call gender-based classifications,\xe2\x80\x9d J.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa0B. v. Alabama ex rel. T. B.,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 127\n, including laws granting or denying benefits \xe2\x80\x9con the basis of the sex of the qualifying parent,\xe2\x80\x9d Califano v. Westcott,\n\n443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 76\n. Prescribing one rule for mothers, another for fathers, \xc2\xa71409 is of the same genre as the classifications declared unconstitutional in Westcott; Reed v. Reed,\n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 71\n\xe2\x80\x9377; Frontiero v. Richardson,\n\n411 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677\n\xe2\x80\x93691; Weinberger v. Wiesenfeld,\n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 636\n\xe2\x80\x93653; and Califano v. Goldfarb,\n\n430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 199\n\xe2\x80\x93207. A successful defense therefore requires an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98exceedingly persuasive justification.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Virginia,\n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 515\n. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The Government must show, at least, that its gender-based \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98classification serves \xe2\x80\x9cimportant governmental objectives and that the discriminatory means employed\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9csubstantially related to [achieving] those objectives.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 533. The classification must serve an important governmental interest today, for \xe2\x80\x9cnew insights and societal understandings can reveal unjustified inequality .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that once passed unnoticed and unchallenged.\xe2\x80\x9d Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0At the time \xc2\xa71409 was enacted as part of the Nationality Act of 1940 (1940 Act), two once habitual, but now untenable, assumptions pervaded the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s citizenship laws and underpinned judicial and administrative rulings: In marriage, husband is dominant, wife subordinate; unwed mother is the sole guardian of a nonmarital child. In the 1940 Act, Congress codified the mother-as-sole-guardian perception for unmarried parents. According to the stereotype, a residency requirement was justified for unwed citizen fathers, who would care little about, and have scant contact with, their nonmarital children. Unwed citizen mothers needed no such prophylactic, because the alien father, along with his foreign ways, was presumptively out of the picture. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0For close to a half century, this Court has viewed with suspicion laws that rely on \xe2\x80\x9coverbroad generalizations about the different talents, capacities, or preferences of males and females.\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 533. No \xe2\x80\x9cimportant [governmental] interest\xe2\x80\x9d is served by laws grounded, as \xc2\xa71409(a) and (c) are, in the obsolescing view that \xe2\x80\x9cunwed fathers [are] invariably less qualified and entitled than mothers\xe2\x80\x9d to take responsibility for nonmarital children. Caban v. Mohammed,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 380\n. In light of this equal protection jurisprudence, \xc2\xa71409(a) and (c)\xe2\x80\x99s discrete duration-of-residence requirements for mothers and fathers are anachronistic. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0The Government points to Fiallo v. Bell,\n\n430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 787\n; Miller v. Albright,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 420\n; and Nguyen v. INS,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 53\n, for support. But Fiallo involved entry preferences for alien children; the case did not present a claim of U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship. And Miller and Nguyen addressed a paternal-acknowledgment requirement well met here, not the length of a parent\xe2\x80\x99s prebirth residency in the United States. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(e)\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s suggested rationales for \xc2\xa71409(a) and (c)\xe2\x80\x99s gender-based differential do not survive heightened scrutiny. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0The Government asserts that Congress sought to ensure that a child born abroad has a strong connection to the United States. The statute, the Government suggests, bracketed an unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mother with a married couple in which both parents are U.\xc2\xa0S. citizens because she is the only legally recognized parent at birth; and aligned an unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen father with a married couple, one spouse a citizen, the other, an alien, because of the competing national influence of the alien mother. This rationale conforms to the long-held view that unwed fathers care little about their children. And the gender-based means scarcely serve the suggested congressional interest. Citizenship may be transmitted to children who have no tie to the United States so long as their U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mother was continuously present in the United States for one year at any point in her life prior to the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth; but it may not be transmitted by a U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen father who falls a few days short of meeting \xc2\xa71401(a)(7)\xe2\x80\x99s longer physical-presence requirements, even if he acknowledges paternity on the day the child is born and raises the child in the United States. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0The Government also maintains that Congress wished to reduce the risk of statelessness for the foreign-born child of a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen. But congressional hearings and reports offer no support for the assertion that a statelessness concern prompted the diverse physical-presence requirements. Nor has the Government shown that the risk of statelessness disproportionately endangered the children of unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mothers. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0Because this Court is not equipped to convert \xc2\xa71409(c)\xe2\x80\x99s exception for unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mothers into the main rule displacing \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71401(a)(7) and 1409(a), it falls to Congress to select a uniform prescription that neither favors nor disadvantages any person on the basis of gender. In the interim, \xc2\xa71401(a)(7)\xe2\x80\x99s current requirement should apply, prospectively, to children born to unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mothers. The legislature\xe2\x80\x99s intent, as revealed by the statute at hand, governs the choice between the two remedial alternatives: extending favorable treatment to the excluded class or withdrawing favorable treatment from the favored class. Ordinarily, the preferred rule is to extend favorable treatment. Westcott, 443 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 89\xe2\x80\x9390. Here, however, extension to fathers of \xc2\xa71409(c)\xe2\x80\x99s favorable treatment for mothers would displace Congress\xe2\x80\x99 general rule, the longer physical-presence requirements of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71401(a)(7) and 1409 applicable to unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen fathers and U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen parents, male as well as female, married to the child\xe2\x80\x99s alien parent. Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98commitment to th[is] residual policy\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the degree of potential disruption of the statutory scheme that would occur by extension as opposed to abrogation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Heckler v. Mathews,\n\n465 U.\xc2\xa0S. 728\n, n.\xc2\xa05, indicate that Congress would likely have abrogated \xc2\xa71409(c)\xe2\x80\x99s special exception, preferring to preserve \xe2\x80\x9cthe importance of residence in this country as the talisman of dedicated attachment,\xe2\x80\x9d Rogers v. Bellei,\n\n401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 815\n. Pp. 23\xe2\x80\x9328.

\n

804 F.\xc2\xa03d 520, affirmed in part, reversed in part, and remanded.

JEFFERSON B. SESSIONS, III, ATTORNEY GENERAL, PETITIONER v. LUIS RAMON MORALES-SANTANA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

[June 12, 2017]

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case concerns a gender-based differential in the law governing acquisition of U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship by a child born abroad, when one parent is a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen, the other, a citizen of another nation. The main rule appears in 8\xc2\xa0U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71401(a)(7) (1958 ed.), now \xc2\xa71401(g) (2012 ed.). Applicable to married couples, \xc2\xa71401(a)(7) requires a period of physical presence in the United States for the U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen parent. The requirement, as initially prescribed, was ten years\xe2\x80\x99 physical presence prior to the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth, \xc2\xa7601(g) (1940 ed.); currently, the requirement is five years prebirth, \xc2\xa71401(g) (2012 ed.). That main rule is rendered applicable to unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen fathers by \xc2\xa71409(a). Congress ordered an exception, however, for unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mothers. Contained in \xc2\xa71409(c), the exception allows an unwed mother to transmit her citizenship to a child born abroad if she has lived in the United States for just one year prior to the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth.

\n

The respondent in this case, Luis Ram\xc3\xb3n Morales-Santana, was born in the Dominican Republic when his father was just 20 days short of meeting \xc2\xa71401(a)(7)\xe2\x80\x99s physical-presence requirement. Opposing removal to the Dominican Republic, Morales-Santana asserts that the equal protection principle implicit in the\n \nFifth Amendment[1] entitles him to citizenship stature. We hold that the gender line Congress drew is incompatible with the requirement that the Government accord to all persons \xe2\x80\x9cthe equal protection of the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Nevertheless, we cannot convert \xc2\xa71409(c)\xe2\x80\x99s exception for unwed mothers into the main rule displacing \xc2\xa71401(a)(7) (covering married couples) and \xc2\xa71409(a) (covering unwed fathers). We must therefore leave it to Congress to select, going forward, a physical-presence requirement (ten years, one year, or some other period) uniformly applicable to all children born abroad with one U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen and one alien parent, wed or unwed. In the interim, the Government must ensure that the laws in question are administered in a manner free from gender-based discrimination.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

We first describe in greater detail the regime Congress constructed. The general rules for acquiring U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship are found in\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71401, the first section in Chapter 1 of Title III of the Immigration and Nationality Act (1952 Act or INA), \xc2\xa7301,\n\n66Stat.\n235\xe2\x80\x93236. Section 1401 sets forth the INA\xe2\x80\x99s rules for determining who \xe2\x80\x9cshall be nationals and citizens of the United States at birth\xe2\x80\x9d by establishing a range of residency and physical-presence requirements calibrated primarily to the parents\xe2\x80\x99 nationality and the child\xe2\x80\x99s place of birth. \xc2\xa71401(a) (1958 ed.); \xc2\xa71401 (2012 ed.). The primacy of \xc2\xa71401 in the statutory scheme is evident. Comprehensive in coverage, \xc2\xa71401 provides the general framework for the acquisition of citizenship at birth. In particular, at the time relevant here,[2] \xc2\xa71401(a)(7) provided for the U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship of

\n

\xe2\x80\x9ca person born outside the geographical limits of the United States and its outlying possessions of parents one of whom is an alien, and the other a citizen of the United States who, prior to the birth of such person, was physically present in the United States or its outlying possessions for a period or periods totaling not less than ten years, at least five of which were after attaining the age of fourteen years: Provided, That any periods of honorable service in the Armed Forces of the United States by such citizen parent may be included in computing the physical presence requirements of this paragraph.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Congress has since reduced the duration requirement to five years, two after age 14. \xc2\xa71401(g) (2012 ed.).[3]

\n

Section 1409 pertains specifically to children with unmarried parents. Its first subsection, \xc2\xa71409(a), incorporates by reference the physical-presence requirements of \xc2\xa71401, thereby allowing an acknowledged unwed citizen parent to transmit U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship to a foreign-born child under the same terms as a married citizen parent. Section 1409(c)\xe2\x80\x94a provision applicable only to unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mothers\xe2\x80\x94states an exception to the physical-presence requirements of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71401 and 1409(a). Under \xc2\xa71409(c)\xe2\x80\x99s exception, only one year of continuous physical presence is required before unwed mothers may pass citizenship to their children born abroad.

\n

B

\n

Respondent Luis Ram\xc3\xb3n Morales-Santana moved to the United States at age 13, and has resided in this country most of his life. Now facing deportation, he asserts U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship at birth based on the citizenship of his biological father, Jos\xc3\xa9 Morales, who accepted parental responsibility and included Morales-Santana in his household.

\n

Jos\xc3\xa9 Morales was born in Gu\xc3\xa1nica, Puerto Rico, on March 19, 1900. Record 55\xe2\x80\x9356. Puerto Rico was then, as it is now, part of the United States, see Puerto Rico v. Sanchez Valle, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2016) (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x934);\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(38) (1958 ed.) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe term United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. means the continental United States, Alaska, Hawaii, Puerto Rico, Guam, and the [U.\xc2\xa0S.] Virgin Islands.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); \xc2\xa71101(a)(38) (2012 ed.) (similar), and Jos\xc3\xa9 became a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen under the Organic Act of Puerto Rico, ch. 145, \xc2\xa75,\n\n39Stat.\n953 (a predecessor to\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71402). After living in Puerto Rico for nearly two decades, Jos\xc3\xa9 left his childhood home on February 27, 1919, 20 days short of his 19th birthday, therefore failing to satisfy \xc2\xa71401(a)(7)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement of five years\xe2\x80\x99 physical presence after age 14. Record 57, 66. He did so to take up employment as a builder-mechanic for a U.\xc2\xa0S. company in the then-U.\xc2\xa0S.-occupied Dominican Republic. Ibid.[4]

\n

By 1959, Jos\xc3\xa9 attested in a June 21, 1971 affidavit presented to the U.\xc2\xa0S. Embassy in the Dominican Republic, he was living with Yrma Santana Montilla, a Dominican woman he would eventually marry. Id., at 57. In 1962, Yrma gave birth to their child, respondent Luis Morales-Santana. Id., at 166\xe2\x80\x93167. While the record before us reveals little about Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s childhood, the Dominican archives disclose that Yrma and Jos\xc3\xa9 married in 1970, and that Jos\xc3\xa9 was then added to Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s birth certificate as his father. Id., at 163\xe2\x80\x93164, 167. Jos\xc3\xa9 also related in the same affidavit that he was then saving money \xe2\x80\x9cfor the susten[ance] of [his] family\xe2\x80\x9d in anticipation of undergoing surgery in Puerto Rico, where members of his family still resided. Id., at 57. In 1975, when Morales-Santana was 13, he moved to Puerto Rico, id., at 368, and by 1976, the year his father died, he was attending public school in the Bronx, a New York City borough, id., at 140, 369.[5]

\n

C

\n

In 2000, the Government placed Morales-Santana in removal proceedings based on several convictions for offenses under New York State Penal Law, all of them rendered on May 17, 1995. Id., at 426. Morales-Santana ranked as an alien despite the many years he lived in the United States, because, at the time of his birth, his father did not satisfy the requirement of five years\xe2\x80\x99 physical presence after age 14. See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, and n.\xc2\xa03. An immigration judge rejected Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s claim to citizenship derived from the U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship of his father, and ordered Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s removal to the Dominican Republic. Record 253, 366; App. to Pet. for Cert. 45a\xe2\x80\x9349a. In 2010, Morales-Santana moved to reopen the proceedings, asserting that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to recognize that he derived citizenship from his U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen father violated the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s equal protection guarantee. See Record 27, 45. The Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA) denied the motion. App. to Pet. for Cert. 8a, 42a\xe2\x80\x9344a.

\n

The United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit reversed the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s decision. 804 F.\xc2\xa03d 520, 524 (2015). Relying on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s post-1970 construction of the equal protection principle as it bears on gender-based classifications, the court held unconstitutional the differential treatment of unwed mothers and fathers. Id., at 527\xe2\x80\x93535. To cure the constitutional flaw, the court further held that Morales-Santana derived citizenship through his father, just as he would were his mother the U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen. Id., at 535\xe2\x80\x93538. In so ruling, the Second Circuit declined to follow the conflicting decision of the Ninth Circuit in United States v. Flores-Villar, 536 F.\xc2\xa03d 990 (2008), see 804 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 530, 535, n.\xc2\xa017. We granted certiorariin Flores-Villar, but ultimately affirmed by an equally divided Court. Flores-Villar v. United States,\n\n564 U.\xc2\xa0S. 210 (2011)\n (\xc2\xa0per curiam). Taking up Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s request for review, 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016), we consider the matter anew.

\n

II

\n

Because \xc2\xa71409 treats sons and daughters alike, Morales-Santana does not suffer discrimination on the basis of his gender. He complains, instead, of gender-based discrimination against his father, who was unwed at the time of Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s birth and was not accorded the right an unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mother would have to transmit citizenship to her child. Although the Government does not contend otherwise, we briefly explain why Morales-Santana may seek to vindicate his father\xe2\x80\x99s right to the equal protection of the laws.[6]

\n

Ordinarily, a party \xe2\x80\x9cmust assert his own legal rights\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccannot rest his claim to relief on the legal rights .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of third parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Warth v. Seldin,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 490,\n499 (1975)\n. But we recognize an exception where, as here, \xe2\x80\x9cthe party asserting the right has a close relationship with the person who possesses the right [and] there is a hindrance to the possessor\xe2\x80\x99s ability to protect his own interests.\xe2\x80\x9d Kowalski v. Tesmer,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 125,\n130 (2004)\n (quoting Powers v. Ohio,\n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 400,\n411 (1991)\n). Jos\xc3\xa9 Morales\xe2\x80\x99 ability to pass citizenship to his son, respondent Morales-Santana, easily satisfies the \xe2\x80\x9cclose relationship\xe2\x80\x9d requirement. So, too, is the \xe2\x80\x9chindrance\xe2\x80\x9d requirement well met. Jos\xc3\xa9 Morales\xe2\x80\x99 failure to assert a claim in his own right \xe2\x80\x9cstems from disability,\xe2\x80\x9d not \xe2\x80\x9cdisinterest,\xe2\x80\x9d Miller v. Albright,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 420,\n450 (1998)\n (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment), for Jos\xc3\xa9 died in 1976, Record 140, many years before the current controversy arose. See Hodel v. Irving,\n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 704\n\xe2\x80\x93712, 723, n.\xc2\xa07 (1987) (children and their guardians may assert\n \nFifth Amendment rights of deceased relatives). Morales-Santana is thus the \xe2\x80\x9cobvious claimant,\xe2\x80\x9d see Craig v. Boren,\n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 190,\n197 (1976)\n, the \xe2\x80\x9cbest available proponent,\xe2\x80\x9d Singleton v. Wulff,\n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 106,\n116 (1976)\n, of his father\xe2\x80\x99s right to equal protection.

\n

III

\n

Sections 1401 and 1409, we note, date from an era when the lawbooks of our Nation were rife with overbroad generalizations about the way men and women are. See, e.g., Hoyt v. Florida,\n\n368 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57,\n62 (1961)\n (women are the \xe2\x80\x9ccenter of home and family life,\xe2\x80\x9d therefore they can be \xe2\x80\x9crelieved from the civic duty of jury service\xe2\x80\x9d); Goesaert v. Cleary,\n\n335 U.\xc2\xa0S. 464,\n466 (1948)\n (States may draw \xe2\x80\x9ca sharp line between the sexes\xe2\x80\x9d). Today, laws of this kind are subject to review under the heightened scrutiny that now attends \xe2\x80\x9call gender-based classifications.\xe2\x80\x9d J.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa0B. v. Alabama ex rel. T. B.,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 127,\n136 (1994)\n; see, e.g., United States v. Virginia,\n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 515\n\xe2\x80\x93556 (1996) (state-maintained military academy may not deny admission to qualified women).

\n

Laws granting or denying benefits \xe2\x80\x9con the basis of the sex of the qualifying parent,\xe2\x80\x9d our post-1970 decisions affirm, differentiate on the basis of gender, and therefore attract heightened review under the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s equal protection guarantee. Califano v. Westcott,\n\n443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 76,\n84 (1979)\n; see id., at 88\xe2\x80\x9389 (holding unconstitutional provision of unemployed-parent benefits exclusively to fathers). Accord Califano v. Goldfarb,\n\n430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 199\n\xe2\x80\x93207 (1977) (plurality opinion) (holding unconstitutional a Social Security classification that denied widowers survivors\xe2\x80\x99 benefits available to widows); Weinberger v. Wiesenfeld,\n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 636\n\xe2\x80\x93653 (1975) (holding unconstitutional a Social Security classification that excluded fathers from receipt of child-in-care benefits available to mothers); Frontiero v. Richardson,\n\n411 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677\n\xe2\x80\x93691 (1973) (plurality opinion) (holding unconstitutional exclusion of married female officers in the military from benefits automatically accorded married male officers); cf. Reed v. Reed,\n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 71\n\xe2\x80\x9377 (1971) (holding unconstitutional a probate-code preference for a father over a mother as administrator of a deceased child\xe2\x80\x99s estate).[7]

\n

Prescribing one rule for mothers, another for fathers, \xc2\xa71409 is of the same genre as the classifications we declared unconstitutional in Reed, Frontiero, Wiesenfeld, Goldfarb, and Westcott. As in those cases, heightened scrutiny is in order. Successful defense of legislation that differentiates on the basis of gender, we have reiterated, requires an \xe2\x80\x9cexceedingly persuasive justification.\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 531 (internal quotation marks omitted); Kirchberg v. Feenstra,\n\n450 U.\xc2\xa0S. 455,\n461 (1981)\n (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

A

\n

The defender of legislation that differentiates on the basis of gender must show \xe2\x80\x9cat least that the [challenged] classification serves important governmental objectives and that the discriminatory means employed are substantially related to the achievement of those objectives.\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 533 (quoting Mississippi Univ. for Women v. Hogan,\n\n458 U.\xc2\xa0S. 718,\n724 (1982)\n; alteration in original); see Tuan Anh Nguyen v. INS,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 53,\n60,\n70 (2001)\n. Moreover, the classification must substantially serve an important governmental interest today, for \xe2\x80\x9cin interpreting the [e]qual [p]rotection [guarantee], [we have] recognized that new insights and societal understandings can reveal unjustified inequality .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that once passed unnoticed and unchallenged.\xe2\x80\x9d Obergefell v. Hodges, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 20). Here, the Government has supplied no \xe2\x80\x9cexceedingly persuasive justification,\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 531 (internal quotation marks omitted), for \xc2\xa71409(a) and (c)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cgender-based\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cgender-biased\xe2\x80\x9d disparity, Westcott, 443 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 84 (internalquotation marks omitted).

\n

1

\n

History reveals what lurks behind \xc2\xa71409. Enacted in the Nationality Act of 1940 (1940 Act), see\n\n54Stat.\n1139\xe2\x80\x931140, \xc2\xa71409 ended a century and a half of congressional silence on the citizenship of children born abroad to unwed parents.[8] During this era, two once habitual, but now untenable, assumptions pervaded our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s citizenship laws and underpinned judicial and administrative rulings: In marriage, husband is dominant, wife subordinate; unwed mother is the natural and sole guardian of a nonmarital child.

\n

Under the once entrenched principle of male dominance in marriage, the husband controlled both wife and child. \xe2\x80\x9c[D]ominance [of] the husband,\xe2\x80\x9d this Court observed in 1915, \xe2\x80\x9cis an ancient principle of our jurisprudence.\xe2\x80\x9d Mackenzie v. Hare,\n\n239 U.\xc2\xa0S. 299,\n311 (1915)\n.[9] See generally Brief for Professors of History et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 4\xe2\x80\x9315. Through the early 20th century, a male citizen automatically conferred U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship on his alien wife. Act of Feb. 10, 1855, ch. 71, \xc2\xa72,\n\n10Stat.\n604; see Kelly v. Owen, 7 Wall. 496, 498 (1869) (the 1855 Act \xe2\x80\x9cconfers the privileges of citizenship upon women married to citizens of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d); C. Bredbenner, A Nationality of Her Own:Women, Marriage, and the Law of Citizenship 15\xe2\x80\x9316, 20\xe2\x80\x9321 (1998). A female citizen, however, was incapable of conferring citizenship on her husband; indeed, she was subject to expatriation if she married an alien.[10] The family of a citizen or a lawfully admitted permanent resident enjoyed statutory exemptions from entry requirements, but only if the citizen or resident was male. See, e.g., Act of Mar. 3, 1903, ch. 1012, \xc2\xa737,\n\n32Stat.\n1221 (wives and children entering the country to join permanent-resident aliens and found to have contracted contagious diseases during transit shall not be deported if the diseases were easily curable or did not present a danger to others); S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 1515, 81st Cong., 2d Sess., 415\xe2\x80\x93417 (1950) (wives exempt from literacy and quota requirements). And from 1790 until 1934, the foreign-born child of a married couple gained U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship only through the father.[11]

\n

For unwed parents, the father-controls tradition never held sway. Instead, the mother was regarded as the child\xe2\x80\x99s natural and sole guardian. At common law, the mother, and only the mother, was \xe2\x80\x9cbound to maintain [a nonmarital child] as its natural guardian.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 J. Kent, Commentaries on American Law *215\xe2\x80\x93*216 (8th ed. 1854); see Nguyen, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 91\xe2\x80\x9392 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., dissenting). In line with that understanding, in the early 20th century, the State Department sometimes permitted unwed mothers to pass citizenship to their children, despite the absence of any statutory authority for the practice. See Hearings on H.\xc2\xa0R. 6127 before the House Committee on Immigration and Naturalization, 76th Cong., 1st Sess., 43, 431 (1940) (hereinafter 1940 Hearings); 39 Op. Atty. Gen. 397, 397\xe2\x80\x93398 (1939); 39 Op. Atty. Gen. 290, 291 (1939). See also Collins, Illegitimate Borders: Jus Sanguinis Citizenship and the Legal Construction of Family, Race, and Nation, 123 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 2134, 2199\xe2\x80\x932205 (2014) (hereinafter Collins).

\n

In the 1940 Act, Congress discarded the father-controls assumption concerning married parents, but codified the mother-as-sole-guardian perception regarding unmarried parents. The Roosevelt administration, which proposed \xc2\xa71409, explained: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he mother [of a nonmarital child] stands in the place of the father .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [,] has a right tothe custody and control of such a child as against the putative father, and is bound to maintain it as its natu-ral guardian.\xe2\x80\x9d 1940 Hearings 431 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

This unwed-mother-as-natural-guardian notion renders \xc2\xa71409\xe2\x80\x99s gender-based residency rules understandable. Fearing that a foreign-born child could turn out \xe2\x80\x9cmore alien than American in character,\xe2\x80\x9d the administration believed that a citizen parent with lengthy ties to the United States would counteract the influence of the alien parent. Id., at 426\xe2\x80\x93427. Concern about the attachment of foreign-born children to the United States explains the treatment of unwed citizen fathers, who, according to the familiar stereotype, would care little about, and have scant contact with, their nonmarital children. For unwed citizen mothers, however, there was no need for a prolonged residency prophylactic: The alien father, who might transmit foreign ways, was presumptively out of the picture. See id., at 431; Collins 2203 (in \xe2\x80\x9cnearly uniform view\xe2\x80\x9d of U.\xc2\xa0S. officials, \xe2\x80\x9calmost invariably,\xe2\x80\x9d the mother alone \xe2\x80\x9cconcern[ed] herself with [a nonmarital] child\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

2

\n

For close to a half century, as earlier observed, see supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, this Court has viewed with suspicion laws that rely on \xe2\x80\x9coverbroad generalizations about the different talents, capacities, or preferences of males and females.\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 533; see Wiesenfeld, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 643, 648. In particular, we have recognized that if a \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory objective is to exclude or \xe2\x80\x98protect\xe2\x80\x99 members of one gender\xe2\x80\x9d in reliance on \xe2\x80\x9cfixed notions concerning [that gender\xe2\x80\x99s] roles and abilities,\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cobjective itself is illegitimate.\xe2\x80\x9d Mississippi Univ. for Women, 458 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 725.

\n

In accord with this eventual understanding, the Court has held that no \xe2\x80\x9cimportant [governmental] interest\xe2\x80\x9d is served by laws grounded, as \xc2\xa71409(a) and (c) are, in the obsolescing view that \xe2\x80\x9cunwed fathers [are] invariably less qualified and entitled than mothers\xe2\x80\x9d to take responsibility for nonmarital children. Caban v. Mohammed,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 380,\n382,\n394 (1979)\n.[12] Overbroad generalizations of that order, the Court has come to comprehend, have a constraining impact, descriptive though they may be of the way many people still order their lives.[13] Laws according or denying benefits in reliance on \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tereotypes about women\xe2\x80\x99s domestic roles,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court has observed, may \xe2\x80\x9ccreat[e] a self-fulfilling cycle of discrimination that force[s] women to continue to assume the role of primary family caregiver.\xe2\x80\x9d Nevada Dept. of Human Resources v. Hibbs,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 721,\n736 (2003)\n. Correspondingly, such laws may disserve men who exercise responsibility for raising their children. See ibid. In light of the equal protection jurisprudence this Court has developed since 1971, see Virginia, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 531\xe2\x80\x93534, \xc2\xa71409(a) and (c)\xe2\x80\x99s discrete duration-of-residence requirements for unwed mothers and fathers who have accepted parental responsibility is stunningly anachronistic.

\n

B

\n

In urging this Court nevertheless to reject Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s equal protection plea, the Government cites three decisions of this Court: Fiallo v. Bell,\n\n430 U.\xc2\xa0S. 787 (1977)\n; Miller v. Albright,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 420\n; and Nguyen v. INS,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 53\n. None controls this case.

\n

The 1952 Act provision at issue in Fiallo gave special immigration preferences to alien children of citizen (or lawful-permanent-resident) mothers, and to alien unwed mothers of citizen (or lawful-permanent-resident) children. 430 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 788\xe2\x80\x93789, and n.\xc2\xa01. Unwed fathers and their children, asserting their right to equal protection, sought the same preferences. Id., at 791. Applying minimal scrutiny (rational-basis review), the Court upheld the provision, relying on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cexceptionally broad power\xe2\x80\x9d to admit or exclude aliens. Id., at 792, 794.[14] This case, however, involves no entry preference for aliens. Morales-Santana claims he is, and since birth has been, a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen. Examining a claim of that order, the Court has not disclaimed, as it did in Fiallo, the application of an exacting standard of review. See Nguyen, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 60\xe2\x80\x9361, 70; Miller, 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 434\xe2\x80\x93435, n.\xc2\xa011 (opinion of Stevens, J.).

\n

The provision challenged in Miller and Nguyen as violative of equal protection requires unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen fathers, but not mothers, to formally acknowledge parenthood of their foreign-born children in order to transmit their U.\xc2\xa0S. citizenship to those children. See \xc2\xa71409(a)(4) (2012 ed.).[15] After Miller produced no opinion for the Court, see 523 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 423, we took up the issue anew in Nguyen. There, the Court held that imposing a paternal-acknowledgment requirement on fathers was a justifiable, easily met means of ensuring the existence of a biological parent-child relationship, which the mother establishes by giving birth. See 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 62\xe2\x80\x9363. Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s challenge does not renew the contest over \xc2\xa71409\xe2\x80\x99s paternal-acknowledgment requirement (whether the current version or that in effect in 1970), and the Government does not dispute that Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s father, by marrying Morales-Santana\xe2\x80\x99s mother, satisfied that requirement.

\n

Unlike the paternal-acknowledgment requirement at issue in Nguyen and Miller, the physical-presence requirements now before us relate solely to the duration of the parent\xe2\x80\x99s prebirth residency in the United States, notto the parent\xe2\x80\x99s filial tie to the child. As the Court of Appeals observed in this case, a man needs no more time in the United States than a woman \xe2\x80\x9cin order to have assimilated citizenship-related values to transmit to [his]child.\xe2\x80\x9d 804 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 531. And unlike Nguyen\xe2\x80\x99s parental-acknowledgment requirement, \xc2\xa71409(a)\xe2\x80\x99s age-calibrated physical-presence requirements cannot fairly be described as \xe2\x80\x9cminimal.\xe2\x80\x9d 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 70.

\n

C

\n

Notwithstanding \xc2\xa71409(a) and (c)\xe2\x80\x99s provenance in traditional notions of the way women and men are, the Government maintains that the statute serves two important objectives: (1) ensuring a connection between the child to become a citizen and the United States and (2) preventing \xe2\x80\x9cstatelessness,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., a child\xe2\x80\x99s possession of no citizenship at all. Even indulging the assumption that Congress intended \xc2\xa71409 to serve these interests, but see supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9313, neither rationale survives heightened scrutiny.

\n

1

\n

We take up first the Government\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that \xc2\xa71409(a) and (c)\xe2\x80\x99s gender-based differential ensures that a child born abroad has a connection to the United States of sufficient strength to warrant conferral of citizenship at birth. The Government does not contend, nor could it, that unmarried men take more time to absorb U.\xc2\xa0S. values than unmarried women do. See supra, at 16. Instead, it presents a novel argument, one it did not advance in Flores-Villar.[16]

\n

An unwed mother, the Government urges, is the child\xe2\x80\x99s only \xe2\x80\x9clegally recognized\xe2\x80\x9d parent at the time of childbirth. Brief for Petitioner 9\xe2\x80\x9310, 28\xe2\x80\x9332.[17] An unwed citizen father enters the scene later, as a second parent. A longer physical connection to the United States is warranted for the unwed father, the Government maintains, because of the \xe2\x80\x9ccompeting national influence\xe2\x80\x9d of the alien mother. Id., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. Congress, the Government suggests, designed the statute to bracket an unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mother with a married couple in which both parents are U.\xc2\xa0S. citizens,[18] and to align an unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen father with a married couple, one spouse a citizen, the other, an alien.

\n

Underlying this apparent design is the assumption that the alien father of a nonmarital child born abroad to a U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mother will not accept parental responsibility. For an actual affiliation between alien father and nonmarital child would create the \xe2\x80\x9ccompeting national influence\xe2\x80\x9d that, according to the Government, justifies imposing on unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen fathers, but not unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mothers, lengthy physical-presence requirements. Hardly gender neutral, see id., at 9, that assumption conforms to the long-held view that unwed fathers care little about, indeed are strangers to, their children. See supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9313. Lump characterization of that kind, however, no longer passes equal protection inspection. See supra, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314, and n.\xc2\xa013.

\n

Accepting, arguendo, that Congress intended the diverse physical-presence prescriptions to serve an interest in ensuring a connection between the foreign-born nonmarital child and the United States, the gender-based means scarcely serve the posited end. The scheme permits the transmission of citizenship to children who have no tie to the United States so long as their mother was a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen continuously present in the United States for one year at any point in her life prior to the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth. The transmission holds even if the mother marries the child\xe2\x80\x99s alien father immediately after the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth and never returns with the child to the United States. At the same time, the legislation precludes citizenship transmission by a U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen father who falls a few days short of meeting \xc2\xa71401(a)(7)\xe2\x80\x99s longer physical-presence requirements, even if the father acknowledges paternity on the day of the child\xe2\x80\x99s birth and raises the child in the United States.[19] One cannot see in this driven-by-gender scheme the close means-end fit required to survive heightened scrutiny. See, e.g., Wengler v. Druggists Mut. Ins. Co.,\n\n446 U.\xc2\xa0S. 142\n\xe2\x80\x93152 (1980) (holding unconstitutional state workers\xe2\x80\x99 compensation death-benefits statute presuming widows\xe2\x80\x99 but not widowers\xe2\x80\x99 dependence on their spouse\xe2\x80\x99s earnings); Westcott, 443 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 88\xe2\x80\x9389.

\n

2

\n

The Government maintains that Congress established the gender-based residency differential in \xc2\xa71409(a) and (c) to reduce the risk that a foreign-born child of a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen would be born stateless. Brief for Petitioner 33. This risk, according to the Government, was substantially greater for the foreign-born child of an unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mother than it was for the foreign-born child of an unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen father. Ibid. But there is little reason to believe that a statelessness concern prompted the diverse physical-presence requirements. Nor has the Government shown that the risk of statelessness disproportionately endangered the children of unwed mothers.

\n

As the Court of Appeals pointed out, with one exception,[20] nothing in the congressional hearings and reports on the 1940 and 1952 Acts \xe2\x80\x9crefer[s] to the problem of statelessness for children born abroad.\xe2\x80\x9d 804 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 532\xe2\x80\x93533. See Collins 2205, n.\xc2\xa0283 (author examined \xe2\x80\x9cmany hundreds of pre-1940 administrative memos .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. defend[ing] or explain[ing] recognition of the nonmarital foreign-born children of American mothers as citizens\xe2\x80\x9d; of the hundreds, \xe2\x80\x9cexactly one memo by a U.\xc2\xa0S. official .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. mentions the risk of statelessness for the foreign-born nonmarital children of American mothers as a concern\xe2\x80\x9d). Reducing the incidence of statelessness was the express goal of other sections of the 1940 Act. See 1940 Hearings 430 (\xe2\x80\x9cstateless[ness]\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cobject\xe2\x80\x9d of section on foundlings). The justification for \xc2\xa71409\xe2\x80\x99s gender-based dichotomy, however, was not the child\xe2\x80\x99s plight, it was the mother\xe2\x80\x99s role as the \xe2\x80\x9cnatural guardian\xe2\x80\x9d of a nonmarital child. See supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9313; Collins 2205 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he pronounced gender asymmetry of the Nationality Act\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of nonmarital foreign-born children of American mothers and fathers was shaped by contemporary maternalist norms regarding the mother\xe2\x80\x99s relationship with her nonmarital child\xe2\x80\x94and the father\xe2\x80\x99s lack of such a relationship.\xe2\x80\x9d). It will not do to \xe2\x80\x9chypothesiz[e] or inven[t]\xe2\x80\x9d governmental purposes for gender classifications \xe2\x80\x9cpost hoc in response to litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 533, 535\xe2\x80\x93536.

\n

Infecting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s risk-of-statelessness argument is an assumption without foundation. \xe2\x80\x9c[F]oreign laws that would put the child of the U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mother at risk of statelessness (by not providing for the child to acquire the father\xe2\x80\x99s citizenship at birth),\xe2\x80\x9d the Government asserts, \xe2\x80\x9cwould protect the child of the U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen father against statelessness by providing that the child would take his mother\xe2\x80\x99s citizenship.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 35. The Government, however, neglected to expose this supposed \xe2\x80\x9cprotection\xe2\x80\x9d to a reality check. Had it done so, it would have recognized the formidable impediments placed by foreign laws on an unwed mother\xe2\x80\x99s transmission of citizenship to her child. See Brief for Scholars on Statelessness as Amici Curiae 13\xe2\x80\x9322, A1\xe2\x80\x93A15.

\n

Experts who have studied the issue report that, at the time relevant here, in \xe2\x80\x9cat least thirty countries,\xe2\x80\x9d citizen mothers generally could not transmit their citizenship to nonmarital children born within the mother\xe2\x80\x99s country. Id., at 14; see id., at 14\xe2\x80\x9317. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]s many as forty-five countries,\xe2\x80\x9d they further report, \xe2\x80\x9cdid not permit their female citizens to assign nationality to a nonmarital child born outside the subject country with a foreign father.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 18; see id., at 18\xe2\x80\x9321. In still other countries, they also observed, there was no legislation in point, leaving the nationality of nonmarital children uncertain. Id., at 21\xe2\x80\x9322; see Sandifer, A Comparative Study of Laws Relating to Nationality at Birth and to Loss of Nationality, 29 Am. J. Int\xe2\x80\x99l L. 248, 256, 258 (1935) (of 79 nations studied, about half made no specific provision for the nationality of nonmarital children). Taking account of the foreign laws actually in force, these experts concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cthe risk of parenting stateless children abroad was, as of [1940 and 1952], and remains today, substantial for unmarried U.\xc2\xa0S. fathers, a risk perhaps greater than that for unmarried U.\xc2\xa0S. mothers.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Scholars on Statelessness as Amici Curiae 9\xe2\x80\x9310; see id., at 38\xe2\x80\x9339. One can hardly characterize as gender neutral a scheme allegedly attending to the risk of statelessness for children of unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mothers while ignoring the same risk for children of unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen fathers.

\n

In 2014, the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) undertook a ten-year project to eliminate statelessness by 2024. See generally UNHCR, Ending Statelessness Within 10 Years, online at http://www.unhcr.org/en-us/protection/statelessness/546217229/special-report-ending-statelessness-10-years.html (all Internet materials as last visited June 9, 2017). Cognizant that discrimination against either mothers or fathers in citizenship and nationality laws is a major cause of statelessness, the Commissioner has made a key component of its project the elimination of gender discrimination in such laws. UNHCR, The Campaign To End Statelessness: April 2016 Update 1 (referring to speech of UNHCR \xe2\x80\x9chighlight[ing] the issue of gender discrimination in the nationality laws of 27 countries\xe2\x80\x94a major cause of statelessness globally\xe2\x80\x9d), online at http://www.unhcr.org/ibelong/wp-content\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0uploads\xc2\xa0/\xc2\xa0Campaign-Update-April-2016.pdf;UNHCR, Background Note on Gender Equality, Nationality Laws and Statelessness 2016, p.\xc2\xa01 (\xe2\x80\x9cEnsuring gender equality in nationality laws can mitigate the risks of statelessness.\xe2\x80\x9d), online at http://www.refworld.org/docid/56de83ca4.html. In this light, we cannot countenance risk of statelessness as a reason to uphold, rather than strike out, differential treatment of unmarried women andmen with regard to transmission of citizenship to their children.

\n

In sum, the Government has advanced no \xe2\x80\x9cexceedingly persuasive\xe2\x80\x9d justification for \xc2\xa71409(a) and (c)\xe2\x80\x99s gender-specific residency and age criteria. Those disparate criteria, we hold, cannot withstand inspection under a Constitution that requires the Government to respect the equal dignity and stature of its male and female citizens.[21]

\n

IV

\n

While the equal protection infirmity in retaining a longer physical-presence requirement for unwed fathers than for unwed mothers is clear, this Court is not equipped to grant the relief Morales-Santana seeks, i.e., extending to his father (and, derivatively, to him) the benefit of the one-year physical-presence term \xc2\xa71409(c) reserves for unwed mothers.

\n

There are \xe2\x80\x9ctwo remedial alternatives,\xe2\x80\x9d our decisions instruct, Westcott, 443 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 89 (quoting Welsh v. United States,\n\n398 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333,\n361 (1970)\n (Harlan, J., concurringin result)), when a statute benefits one class (in this case, unwed mothers and their children), as \xc2\xa71409(c) does, and excludes another from the benefit (here, unwed fathers and their children). \xe2\x80\x9c[A] court may either declare [the statute] a nullity and order that its benefits not extend to the class that the legislature intended to benefit, or it may extend the coverage of the statute to include those who are aggrieved by exclusion.\xe2\x80\x9d Westcott, 443 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 89 (quoting Welsh, 398 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 361 (opinion of Harlan, J.)).[22] \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen the \xe2\x80\x98right invoked is that to equal treatment,\xe2\x80\x99 the appropriate remedy is a mandate of equal treatment, a result that can be accomplished by withdrawal of benefits from the favored class as well as by extension of benefits to the excluded class.\xe2\x80\x9d Heckler v. Mathews,\n\n465 U.\xc2\xa0S. 728,\n740 (1984)\n (quoting Iowa-Des Moines Nat. Bank v. Bennett,\n\n284 U.\xc2\xa0S. 239,\n247 (1931)\n; emphasis deleted). \xe2\x80\x9cHow equality is accomplished .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is a matter on which the Constitution is silent.\xe2\x80\x9d Levin v. Commerce Energy, Inc.,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 413\n\xe2\x80\x93427 (2010).[23]

\n

The choice between these outcomes is governed by the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s intent, as revealed by the statute at hand. See id., at 427 (\xe2\x80\x9cOn finding unlawful discrimination, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. courts may attempt, within the bounds of their institutional competence, to implement what the legislature would have willed had it been apprised of the constitutional infirmity.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Ayotte v. Planned Parenthood of Northern New Eng.,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 320,\n330 (2006)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cthe touchstone for any decision about remedy is legislative intent\xe2\x80\x9d).[24]

\n

Ordinarily, we have reiterated, \xe2\x80\x9cextension, rather than nullification, is the proper course.\xe2\x80\x9d Westcott, 443 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 89. Illustratively, in a series of cases involving federal financial assistance benefits, the Court struck discriminatory exceptions denying benefits to discrete groups, which meant benefits previously denied were extended. See, e.g., Goldfarb, 430 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 202\xe2\x80\x93204, 213\xe2\x80\x93217 (plurality opinion) (survivors\xe2\x80\x99 benefits), aff\xe2\x80\x99g 396 F. Supp. 308, 309 (EDNY 1975) (per curiam); Jimenez v. Weinberger,\n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 628\n\xe2\x80\x93631, and n.\xc2\xa02, 637\xe2\x80\x93638 (1974) (disability benefits); Department of Agriculture v. Moreno,\n\n413 U.\xc2\xa0S. 528\n\xe2\x80\x93530, 538 (1973) (food stamps); Frontiero, 411 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 678\xe2\x80\x93679, and n.\xc2\xa02, 691, and n.\xc2\xa025 (plurality opinion) (military spousal benefits). Here, however, the discriminatory exception consists of favorable treatment for a discrete group (a shorter physical-presence requirement for unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mothers giving birth abroad). Following the same approach as in those benefits cases\xe2\x80\x94striking the discriminatory exception\xe2\x80\x94leads here to extending the general rule of longer physical-presence requirements to cover the previously favored group.

\n

The Court has looked to Justice Harlan\xe2\x80\x99s concurring opinion in Welsh v. United States, 398 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 361\xe2\x80\x93367, in considering whether the legislature would have struck an exception and applied the general rule equally to all, or instead, would have broadened the exception to cure the equal protection violation. In making this assessment, a court should \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98measure the intensity of commitment to the residual policy\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the main rule, not the exception\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98and consider the degree of potential disruption of the statutory scheme that would occur by extension as opposed to abrogation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Heckler, 465 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 739, n.\xc2\xa05 (quoting Welsh, 398 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 365 (opinion of Harlan, J.)).

\n

The residual policy here, the longer physical-presence requirement stated in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71401(a)(7) and 1409, evidences Congress\xe2\x80\x99 recognition of \xe2\x80\x9cthe importance of residence in this country as the talisman of dedicated attachment.\xe2\x80\x9d Rogers v. Bellei,\n\n401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 815,\n834 (1971)\n; see Weedin v. Chin Bow,\n\n274 U.\xc2\xa0S. 657\n\xe2\x80\x93666 (1927) (Congress \xe2\x80\x9cattached more importance to actual residence in the United States as indicating a basis for citizenship than it did to descent.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [T]he heritable blood of citizenship was thus associated unmistakeably with residence within the country which was thus recognized as essential to full citizenship.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). And the potential for \xe2\x80\x9cdisruption of the statutory scheme\xe2\x80\x9d is large. For if \xc2\xa71409(c)\xe2\x80\x99s one-year dispensation were extended to unwed citizen fathers, would it not be irrational to retain the longer term when the U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen parent is married? Disadvantageous treatment of marital children in comparison to nonmarital children is scarcely a purpose one can sensibly attribute to Congress.[25]

\n

Although extension of benefits is customary in federal benefit cases, see supra, at 23\xe2\x80\x9324, n.\xc2\xa022, 25, all indicators in this case point in the opposite direction.[26] Put to the choice, Congress, we believe, would have abrogated \xc2\xa71409(c)\xe2\x80\x99s exception, preferring preservation of the general rule.[27]

\n

V

\n

The gender-based distinction infecting \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71401(a)(7) and 1409(a) and (c), we hold, violates the equal protection principle, as the Court of Appeals correctly ruled. For the reasons stated, however, we must adopt the remedial course Congress likely would have chosen \xe2\x80\x9chad it been apprised of the constitutional infirmity.\xe2\x80\x9d Levin, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 427. Although the preferred rule in the typical case is to extend favorable treatment, see Westcott, 443 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 89\xe2\x80\x9390, this is hardly the typical case.[28] Extension here would render the special treatment Congress prescribed in \xc2\xa71409(c), the one-year physical-presence requirement for U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mothers, the general rule, no longer an exception. Section 1401(a)(7)\xe2\x80\x99s longer physical-presence requirement, applicable to a substantial majority of children born abroad to one U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen parent and one foreign-citizen parent, therefore, must hold sway.[29] Going forward, Congress may address the issue and settle on a uniform prescription that neither favors nor disadvantages any person on the basis of gender. In the interim,as the Government suggests, \xc2\xa71401(a)(7)\xe2\x80\x99s now-five-year requirement should apply, prospectively, to children born to unwed U.\xc2\xa0S.-citizen mothers. See Brief for Petitioner 12, 51; Reply Brief 19, n.\xc2\xa03.

\n

The Court today holds that we are \xe2\x80\x9cnot equipped to\xe2\x80\x9d remedy the equal protection injury that respondent claims his father suffered under the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA) of 1952. Ante, at 23. I agree with that holding. As the majority concludes, extending\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71409(c)\xe2\x80\x99s 1-year physical presence requirement to unwed citizen fathers (as respondent requests) is not, under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent, an appropriate remedy for any equal protection violation. See ante, at 23. Indeed, I am skeptical that we even have the \xe2\x80\x9cpower to provide relief of the sort requested in this suit\xe2\x80\x94namely, conferral of citizenship on a basis other than that prescribed by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Tuan Anh Nguyen v. Albright,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 420,\n452 (1998)\n (Scalia, J., joined by Thomas, J., concurring in judgment)).

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s remedial holding resolves this case. Because respondent cannot obtain relief in any event, it is unnecessary for us to decide whether the 1952 version of the INA was constitutional, whether respondent has third-party standing to raise an equal protection claim on behalf of his father, or whether other immigration laws (such as the current versions of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71401(g) and 1409) are constitu-tional. I therefore concur only in the judgment reversing the Second Circuit.
