\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

TELLABS, INC., et\xc2\xa0al. v. MAKOR ISSUES & RIGHTS, LTD., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x93484.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 28, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 21, 2007

\n

As a check against abusive litigation in private securities fraud actions, the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PSLRA) includes exacting pleading requirements. The Act requires plaintiffs to state with particularity both the facts constituting the alleged violation, and the facts evidencing scienter, i.e., the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s intention \xe2\x80\x9cto deceive, manipulate, or defraud.\xe2\x80\x9d Ernst & Ernst v. Hochfelder, 425 U. S. 185, 194, and n. 12. As set out in \xc2\xa721D(b)(2), plaintiffs must \xe2\x80\x9cstate with particularity facts giving rise to a strong inference that the defendant acted with the required state of mind.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2). Congress left the key term \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d undefined.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Tellabs, Inc., manufactures specialized equipment for fiber optic networks. Respondents (Shareholders) purchased Tellabs stock between December 11, 2000, and June 19, 2001. They filed a class action, alleging that Tellabs and petitioner Notebaert, then Tellabs\xe2\x80\x99 chief executive officer and president, had engaged in securities fraud in violation of \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and Securities and Exchange Commission Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, and that Notebaert was a \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolling person\xe2\x80\x9d under the 1934 Act, and therefore derivatively liable for the company\xe2\x80\x99s fraudulent acts. Tellabs moved to dismiss the complaint on the ground that the Shareholders had failed to plead their case with the particularity the PSLRA requires. The District Court agreed, dismissing the complaint without prejudice. The Shareholders then amended their complaint, adding references to 27 confidential sources and making further, more specific, allegations concerning Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s mental state. The District Court again dismissed, this time with prejudice. The Shareholders had sufficiently pleaded that Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s statements were misleading, the court determined, but they had insufficiently alleged that he acted with scienter. The Seventh Circuit reversed in relevant part. Like the District Court, it found that the Shareholders had pleaded the misleading character of Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s statements with sufficient particularity. Unlike the District Court, however, it concluded that the Shareholders had sufficiently alleged that Notebaert acted with the requisite state of mind. In evaluating whether the PSLRA\xe2\x80\x99s pleading standard is met, the Circuit said, courts should examine all of the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s allegations to decide whether collectively they establish an inference of scienter; the complaint would survive, the court stated, if a reasonable person could infer from the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s allegations that the defendant acted with the requisite state of mind.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0To qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d within the intendment of \xc2\xa721D(b)(2), an inference of scienter must be more than merely plausible or reasonable\xe2\x80\x94it must be cogent and at least as compelling as any opposing inference of nonfraudulent intent. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Setting a uniform pleading standard for \xc2\xa710(b) actions was among Congress\xe2\x80\x99 objectives in enacting the PSLRA. Designed to curb perceived abuses of the \xc2\xa710(b) private action, the PSLRA installed both substantive and procedural controls. As relevant here, \xc2\xa721D(b) of the PSLRA \xe2\x80\x9cimpose[d] heightened pleading requirements in [\xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935] actions.\xe2\x80\x9d Dabit, 547 U. S., at 81. In the instant case, the District Court and the Seventh Circuit agreed that the complaint sufficiently specified Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misleading statements and the reasons why the statements were misleading. But those courts disagreed on whether the Shareholders, as required by \xc2\xa721D(b)(2), \xe2\x80\x9cstate[d] with particularity facts giving rise to a strong inference that [Notebaert] acted with [scienter],\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2). Congress did not shed much light on what facts would create a strong inference or how courts could determine the existence of the requisite inference. With no clear guide from Congress other than its \xe2\x80\x9cinten[tion] to strengthen existing pleading requirements,\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep., at 41, Courts of Appeals have diverged in construing the term \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference.\xe2\x80\x9d Among the uncertainties, should courts consider competing inferences in determining whether an inference of scienter is \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d? This Court\xe2\x80\x99s task is to prescribe a workable construction of the \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d standard, a reading geared to the PSLRA\xe2\x80\x99s twin goals: to curb frivolous, lawyer-driven litigation, while preserving investors\xe2\x80\x99 ability to recover on meritorious claims. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Court establishes the following prescriptions: First, faced with a Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6) motion to dismiss a \xc2\xa710(b) action, courts must, as with any motion to dismiss for failure to plead a claim on which relief can be granted, accept all factual allegations in the complaint as true. See Leatherman v. Tarrant County Narcotics Intelligence and Coordination Unit, 507 U. S. 163, 164. Second, courts must consider the complaint in its entirety, as well as other sources courts ordinarily examine when ruling on Rule 12(b)(6) motions. The inquiry is whether all of the facts alleged, taken collectively, give rise to a strong inference of scienter, not whether any individual allegation, scrutinized in isolation, meets that standard. Third, in determining whether the pleaded facts give rise to a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d inference of scienter, the court must take into account plausible opposing inferences. The Seventh Circuit expressly declined to engage in such a comparative inquiry. But in \xc2\xa721D(b)(2), Congress did not merely require plaintiffs to allege facts from which an inference of scienter rationally could be drawn. Instead, Congress required plaintiffs to plead with particularity facts that give rise to a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., a powerful or cogent\xe2\x80\x94inference. To determine whether the plaintiff has alleged facts giving rise to the requisite \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference,\xe2\x80\x9d a court must consider plausible nonculpable explanations for the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, as well as inferences favoring the plaintiff. The inference that the defendant acted with scienter need not be irrefutable, but it must be more than merely \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpermissible\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94it must be cogent and compelling, thus strong in light of other explanations. A complaint will survive only if a reasonable person would deem the inference of scienter cogent and at least as compelling as any plausible opposing inference one could draw from the facts alleged. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Tellabs contends that when competing inferences are considered, Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s evident lack of pecuniary motive will be dispositive. The Court agrees that motive can be a relevant consideration, and personal financial gain may weigh heavily in favor of a scienter inference. The absence of a motive allegation, however, is not fatal for allegations must be considered collectively; the significance that can be ascribed to an allegation of motive, or lack thereof, depends on the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s entirety. Tellabs also maintains that several of the Shareholders\xe2\x80\x99 allegations are too vague or ambiguous to contribute to a strong inference of scienter. While omissions and ambiguities count against inferring scienter, the court\xe2\x80\x99s job is not to scrutinize each allegation in isolation but to access all the allegations holistically. Pp. 13\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0The Seventh Circuit was unduly concerned that a court\xe2\x80\x99s comparative assessment of plausible inferences would impinge upon the Seventh Amendment right to jury trial. Congress, as creator of federal statutory claims, has power to prescribe what must be pleaded to state the claim, just as it has power to determine what must be proved to prevail on the merits. It is the federal lawmaker\xe2\x80\x99s prerogative, therefore, to allow, disallow, or shape the contours of\xe2\x80\x94including the pleading and proof requirements for\xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa710(b) private actions. This Court has never questioned that authority in general, or suggested, in particular, that the Seventh Amendment inhibits Congress from establishing whatever pleading requirements it finds appropriate for federal statutory claims. Provided that the Shareholders have satisfied the congressionally \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe[d] \xe2\x80\xa6 means of making an issue,\xe2\x80\x9d Fidelity & Deposit Co. of Md. v. United States, 187 U. S. 315, 320, the case will fall within the jury\xe2\x80\x99s authority to assess the credibility of witnesses, resolve genuine issues of fact, and make the ultimate determination whether Notebaert and, by imputation, Tellabs acted with scienter. Under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d standard, a plaintiff is not forced to plead more than she would be required to prove at trial. A plaintiff alleging fraud under \xc2\xa710(b) must plead facts rendering an inference of scienter at least as likely as any plausible opposing inference. At trial, she must then prove her case by a \xe2\x80\x9cpreponderance of the evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(e)\xc2\xa0Neither the District Court nor the Court of Appeals had the opportunity to consider whether the Shareholders\xe2\x80\x99 allegations warrant \xe2\x80\x9ca strong inference that [Notebaert and Tellabs] acted with the required state of mind,\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2), in light of the prescriptions announced today. Thus, the case is remanded for a determination under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa721D(b)(2). P.\xc2\xa018.

\n

437 F.\xc2\xa03d 588, vacated and remanded.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has long recognized that meritorious private actions to enforce federal antifraud securities laws are an essential supplement to criminal prosecutions and civil enforcement actions brought, respectively, by the Department of Justice and the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). See, e.g., Dura Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Broudo, 544 U. S. 336, 345 (2005); J. I. Case Co. v. Borak, 377 U. S. 426, 432 (1964). Private securities fraud actions, however, if not adequately contained, can be employed abusively to impose substantial costs on companies and individuals whose conduct conforms to the law. See Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith Inc. v. Dabit, 547 U. S. 71, 81 (2006). As a check against abusive litigation by private parties, Congress enacted the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PSLRA), 109 Stat. 737.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Exacting pleading requirements are among the control measures Congress included in the PSLRA. The Act requires plaintiffs to state with particularity both the facts constituting the alleged violation, and the facts evidencing scienter, i.e., the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s intention \xe2\x80\x9cto deceive, manipulate, or defraud.\xe2\x80\x9d Ernst & Ernst v. Hochfelder, 425 U. S. 185, 194, and n. 12 (1976); see 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(1),(2). This case concerns the latter requirement. As set out in \xc2\xa721D(b)(2) of the PSLRA, plaintiffs must \xe2\x80\x9cstate with particularity facts giving rise to a strong inference that the defendant acted with the required state of mind.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S. C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress left the key term \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d undefined, and Courts of Appeals have divided on its meaning. In the case before us, the Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit held that the \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d standard would be met if the complaint \xe2\x80\x9callege[d] facts from which, if true, a reasonable person could infer that the defendant acted with the required intent.\xe2\x80\x9d 437 F.\xc2\xa03d 588, 602 (2006). That formulation, we conclude, does not capture the stricter demand Congress sought to convey in \xc2\xa721D(b)(2). It does not suffice that a reasonable factfinder plausibly could infer from the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s allegations the requisite state of mind. Rather, to determine whether a complaint\xe2\x80\x99s scienter allegations can survive threshold inspection for sufficiency, a court governed by \xc2\xa721D(b)(2) must engage in a comparative evaluation; it must consider, not only inferences urged by the plaintiff, as the Seventh Circuit did, but also competing inferences rationally drawn from the facts alleged. An inference of fraudulent intent may be plausible, yet less cogent than other, nonculpable explanations for the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. To qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d within the intendment of \xc2\xa721D(b)(2), we hold, an inference of scienter must be more than merely plausible or reasonable\xe2\x80\x94it must be cogent and at least as compelling as any opposing inference of nonfraudulent intent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Tellabs, Inc., manufactures specialized equipment used in fiber optic networks. During the time period relevant to this case, petitioner Richard Notebaert was Tellabs\xe2\x80\x99 chief executive officer and president. Respondents (Shareholders) are persons who purchased Tellabs stock between December 11, 2000, and June 19, 2001. They accuse Tellabs and Notebaert (as well as several other Tellabs executives) of engaging in a scheme to deceive the investing public about the true value of Tellabs\xe2\x80\x99 stock. See 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 591; App. 94\xe2\x80\x9398.[Footnote 1]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Beginning on December 11, 2000, the Shareholders allege, Notebaert (and by imputation Tellabs) \xe2\x80\x9cfalsely reassured public investors, in a series of statements \xe2\x80\xa6 that Tellabs was continuing to enjoy strong demand for its products and earning record revenues,\xe2\x80\x9d when, in fact, Notebaert knew the opposite was true. Id., at 94\xe2\x80\x9395, 98. From December 2000 until the spring of 2001, the Shareholders claim, Notebaert knowingly misled the public in four ways. 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 596. First, he made statements indicating that demand for Tellabs\xe2\x80\x99 flagship networking device, the TITAN 5500, was continuing to grow, when in fact demand for that product was waning. Id., at 596, 597. Second, Notebaert made statements indicating that the TITAN 6500, Tellabs\xe2\x80\x99 next-generation networking device, was available for delivery, and that demand for that product was strong and growing, when in truth the product was not ready for delivery and demand was weak. Id., at 596, 597\xe2\x80\x93598. Third, he falsely represented Tellabs\xe2\x80\x99 financial results for the fourth quarter of 2000 (and, in connection with those results, condoned the practice of \xe2\x80\x9cchannel stuffing,\xe2\x80\x9d under which Tellabs flooded its customers with unwanted products). Id., at 596, 598. Fourth, Notebaert made a series of overstated revenue projections, when demand for the TITAN 5500 was drying up and production of the TITAN 6500 was behind schedule. Id., at 596, 598\xe2\x80\x93599. Based on Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s sunny assessments, the Shareholders contend, market analysts recommended that investors buy Tellabs\xe2\x80\x99 stock. See id., at 592.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first public glimmer that business was not so healthy came in March 2001 when Tellabs modestly reduced its first quarter sales projections. Ibid. In the next months, Tellabs made progressively more cautious statements about its projected sales. On June 19, 2001, the last day of the class period, Tellabs disclosed that demand for the TITAN 5500 had significantly dropped. Id., at 593. Simultaneously, the company substantially lowered its revenue projections for the second quarter of 2001. The next day, the price of Tellabs stock, which had reached a high of $67 during the period, plunged to a low of $15.87. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On December 3, 2002, the Shareholders filed a class action in the District Court for the Northern District of Illinois. Ibid. Their complaint stated, inter alia, that Tellabs and Notebaert had engaged in securities fraud in violation of \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, 48 Stat. 891, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j(b), and SEC Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.10b\xe2\x80\x935 (2006), also that Notebaert was a \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolling person\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa720(a) of the 1934 Act, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778t(a), and therefore derivatively liable for the company\xe2\x80\x99s fraudulent acts. See App. 98\xe2\x80\x93101, 167\xe2\x80\x93171. Tellabs moved to dismiss the complaint on the ground that the Shareholders had failed to plead their case with the particularity the PSLRA requires. The District Court agreed, and therefore dismissed the complaint without prejudice. App. to Pet. for Cert. 80a\xe2\x80\x93117a; see Johnson v. Tellabs, Inc., 303 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 941, 945 (ND Ill. 2004).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Shareholders then amended their complaint, adding references to 27 confidential sources and making further, more specific, allegations concerning Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s mental state. See 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 594; App. 91\xe2\x80\x9393, 152\xe2\x80\x93160. The District Court again dismissed, this time with prejudice. 303 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 971. The Shareholders had sufficiently pleaded that Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s statements were misleading, the court determined, id., at 955\xe2\x80\x93961, but they had insufficiently alleged that he acted with scienter, id., at 954\xe2\x80\x93955, 961\xe2\x80\x93969.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit reversed in relevant part. 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 591. Like the District Court, the Court of Appeals found that the Shareholders had pleaded the misleading character of Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s statements with sufficient particularity. Id., at 595\xe2\x80\x93600. Unlike the District Court, however, the Seventh Circuit concluded that the Shareholders had sufficiently alleged that Notebaert acted with the requisite state of mind. Id., at 603\xe2\x80\x93605.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals recognized that the PSLRA \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocally raise[d] the bar for pleading scienter\xe2\x80\x9d by requiring plaintiffs to \xe2\x80\x9cplea[d] sufficient facts to create a strong inference of scienter.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 601 (internal quotation marks omitted). In evaluating whether that pleading standard is met, the Seventh Circuit said, \xe2\x80\x9ccourts [should] examine all of the allegations in the complaint and then \xe2\x80\xa6 decide whether collectively they establish such an inference.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]e will allow the complaint to survive,\xe2\x80\x9d the court next and critically stated, \xe2\x80\x9cif it alleges facts from which, if true, a reasonable person could infer that the defendant acted with the required intent \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. If a reasonable person could not draw such an inference from the alleged facts, the defendants are entitled to dismissal.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 602.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In adopting its standard for the survival of a complaint, the Seventh Circuit explicitly rejected a stiffer standard adopted by the Sixth Circuit, i.e., that \xe2\x80\x9cplaintiffs are entitled only to the most plausible of competing inferences.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 601, 602 (quoting Fidel v. Farley, 392 F.\xc2\xa03d 220, 227 (CA6 2004)). The Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s standard, the court observed, because it involved an assessment of competing inferences, \xe2\x80\x9ccould potentially infringe upon plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 Seventh Amendment rights.\xe2\x80\x9d 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 602. We granted certiorari to resolve the disagreement among the Circuits on whether, and to what extent, a court must consider competing inferences in determining whether a securities fraud complaint gives rise to a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d of scienter.[Footnote 2] 549 U. S. ___ (2007).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 forbids the \xe2\x80\x9cuse or employ, in connection with the purchase or sale of any security \xe2\x80\xa6 , [of] any manipulative or deceptive device or contrivance in contravention of such rules and regulations as the [SEC] may prescribe as necessary or appropriate in the public interest or for the protection of investors.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j(b). SEC Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 implements \xc2\xa710(b) by declaring it unlawful:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(a)\xe2\x80\x82To employ any device, scheme, or artifice to defraud,

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c(b)\xe2\x80\x82To make any untrue statement of a material fact or to omit to state a material fact necessary in order to make the statements made \xe2\x80\xa6 not misleading, or

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c(c)\xe2\x80\x82To engage in any act, practice, or course of business which operates or would operate as a fraud or deceit upon any person, in connection with the purchase or sale of any security.\xe2\x80\x9d 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.10b\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\nSection 10(b), this Court has implied from the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and purpose, affords a right of action to purchasers or sellers of securities injured by its violation. See, e.g., Dura Pharmaceuticals, 544 U. S., at 341. See also id., at 345 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe securities statutes seek to maintain public confidence in the marketplace \xe2\x80\xa6 . by deterring fraud, in part, through the availability of private securities fraud actions.\xe2\x80\x9d); Borak, 377 U. S., at 432 (private securities fraud actions provide \xe2\x80\x9ca most effective weapon in the enforcement\xe2\x80\x9d of securities laws and are \xe2\x80\x9ca necessary supplement to Commission action\xe2\x80\x9d). To establish liability under \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, a private plaintiff must prove that the defendant acted with scienter, \xe2\x80\x9ca mental state embracing intent to deceive, manipulate, or defraud.\xe2\x80\x9d Ernst & Ernst, 425 U. S., at 193\xe2\x80\x93194, and n. 12.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In an ordinary civil action, the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure require only \xe2\x80\x9ca short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 8(a)(2). Although the rule encourages brevity, the complaint must say enough to give the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cfair notice of what the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim is and the grounds upon which it rests.\xe2\x80\x9d Dura Pharmaceuticals, 544 U. S., at 346 (internal quotation marks omitted). Prior to the enactment of the PSLRA, the sufficiency of a complaint for securities fraud was governed not by Rule 8, but by the heightened pleading standard set forth in Rule 9(b). See Greenstone v. Cambex Corp., 975 F.\xc2\xa02d 22, 25 (CA1 1992) (Breyer, J.) (collecting cases). Rule 9(b) applies to \xe2\x80\x9call averments of fraud or mistake\xe2\x80\x9d; it requires that \xe2\x80\x9cthe circumstances constituting fraud \xe2\x80\xa6 be stated with particularity\xe2\x80\x9d but provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[m]alice, intent, knowledge, and other condition of mind of a person, may be averred generally.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Courts of Appeals diverged on the character of the Rule 9(b) inquiry in \xc2\xa710(b) cases: Could securities fraud plaintiffs allege the requisite mental state \xe2\x80\x9csimply by stating that scienter existed,\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re GlenFed, Inc. Securities Litigation, 42 F.\xc2\xa03d 1541, 1546\xe2\x80\x931547 (CA9 1994) (en banc), or were they required to allege with particularity facts giving rise to an inference of scienter? Compare id., at 1546 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe are not permitted to add new requirements to Rule 9(b) simply because we like the effects of doing so.\xe2\x80\x9d), with, e.g., Greenstone, 975 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 25 (were the law to permit a securities fraud complaint simply to allege scienter without supporting facts, \xe2\x80\x9ca complaint could evade too easily the \xe2\x80\x98particularity\xe2\x80\x99 requirement in Rule 9(b)\xe2\x80\x99s first sentence\xe2\x80\x9d). Circuits requiring plaintiffs to allege specific facts indicating scienter expressed that requirement variously. See 5A C. Wright & A. Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa71301.1, pp.\xc2\xa0300\xe2\x80\x93302 (3d ed. 2004) (hereinafter Wright & Miller). The Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s formulation was the most stringent. Securities fraud plaintiffs in that Circuit were required to \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically plead those [facts] which they assert give rise to a strong inference that the defendants had\xe2\x80\x9d the requisite state of mind. Ross v. A. H. Robins Co., 607 F.\xc2\xa02d 545, 558 (1979) (emphasis added). The \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d formulation was appropriate, the Second Circuit said, to ward off allegations of \xe2\x80\x9cfraud by hindsight.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Shields v. Citytrust Bancorp, Inc., 25 F.\xc2\xa03d 1124, 1129 (1994) (quoting Denny v. Barber, 576 F.\xc2\xa02d 465, 470 (CA2 1978) (Friendly, J.)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Setting a uniform pleading standard for \xc2\xa710(b) actions was among Congress\xe2\x80\x99 objectives when it enacted the PSLRA. Designed to curb perceived abuses of the \xc2\xa710(b) private action\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cnuisance filings, targeting of deep-pocket defendants, vexatious discovery requests and manipulation by class action lawyers,\xe2\x80\x9d Dabit, 547 U. S., at 81 (quoting H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x93369, p.\xc2\xa031 (1995) (hereinafter H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep.))\xe2\x80\x94the PSLRA installed both substantive and procedural controls.[Footnote 4] Notably, Congress prescribed new procedures for the appointment of lead plaintiffs and lead counsel. This innovation aimed to increase the likelihood that institutional investors\xe2\x80\x94parties more likely to balance the interests of the class with the long-term interests of the company\xe2\x80\x94would serve as lead plaintiffs. See id., at 33\xe2\x80\x9334; S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x9398, p.\xc2\xa011 (1995). Congress also \xe2\x80\x9climit[ed] recoverable damages and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, provide[d] a \xe2\x80\x98safe harbor\xe2\x80\x99 for forward-looking statements, \xe2\x80\xa6 mandate[d] imposition of sanctions for frivolous litigation, and authorize[d] a stay of discovery pending resolution of any motion to dismiss.\xe2\x80\x9d Dabit, 547 U. S., at 81. And in \xc2\xa721D(b) of the PSLRA, Congress \xe2\x80\x9cimpose[d] heightened pleading requirements in actions brought pursuant to \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the PSLRA\xe2\x80\x99s heightened pleading instructions, any private securities complaint alleging that the defendant made a false or misleading statement must: (1) \xe2\x80\x9cspecify each statement alleged to have been misleading [and] the reason or reasons why the statement is misleading,\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(1); and (2) \xe2\x80\x9cstate with particularity facts giving rise to a strong inference that the defendant acted with the required state of mind,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2). In the instant case, as earlier stated, see supra, at 5, the District Court and the Seventh Circuit agreed that the Shareholders met the first of the two requirements: The complaint sufficiently specified Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misleading statements and the reasons why the statements were misleading. 303 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 955\xe2\x80\x93961; 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 596\xe2\x80\x93600. But those courts disagreed on whether the Shareholders, as required by \xc2\xa721D(b)(2), \xe2\x80\x9cstate[d] with particularity facts giving rise to a strong inference that [Notebaert] acted with [scienter],\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2). See supra, at 5.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d standard \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocally raise[d] the bar for pleading scienter,\xe2\x80\x9d 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 601, and signaled Congress\xe2\x80\x99 purpose to promote greater uniformity among the Circuits, see H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep., p.\xe2\x80\x8241. But \xe2\x80\x9cCongress did not \xe2\x80\xa6 throw much light on what facts \xe2\x80\xa6 suffice to create [a strong] inference,\xe2\x80\x9d or on what \xe2\x80\x9cdegree of imagination courts can use in divining whether\xe2\x80\x9d the requisite inference exists. 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 601. While adopting the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d standard, Congress did not codify that Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s case law interpreting the standard. See \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2). See also Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 18. With no clear guide from Congress other than its \xe2\x80\x9cinten[tion] to strengthen existing pleading requirements,\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep., p.\xe2\x80\x8241, Courts of Appeals have diverged again, this time in construing the term \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference.\xe2\x80\x9d Among the uncertainties, should courts consider competing inferences in determining whether an inference of scienter is \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d? See 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 601\xe2\x80\x93602 (collecting cases). Our task is to prescribe a workable construction of the \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d standard, a reading geared to the PSLRA\xe2\x80\x99s twin goals: to curb frivolous, lawyer-driven litigation, while preserving investors\xe2\x80\x99 ability to recover on meritorious claims.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We establish the following prescriptions: First, faced with a Rule 12(b)(6) motion to dismiss a \xc2\xa710(b) action, courts must, as with any motion to dismiss for failure to plead a claim on which relief can be granted, accept all factual allegations in the complaint as true. See Leatherman v. Tarrant County Narcotics Intelligence and Coordination Unit, 507 U. S. 163, 164 (1993). On this point, the parties agree. See Reply Brief 8; Brief for Respondents 26; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 8, 20, 21.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, courts must consider the complaint in its entirety, as well as other sources courts ordinarily examine when ruling on Rule 12(b)(6) motions to dismiss, in particular, documents incorporated into the complaint by reference, and matters of which a court may take judicial notice. See 5B Wright & Miller \xc2\xa71357 (3d ed. 2004 and Supp. 2007). The inquiry, as several Courts of Appeals have recognized, is whether all of the facts alleged, taken collectively, give rise to a strong inference of scienter, not whether any individual allegation, scrutinized in isolation, meets that standard. See, e.g., Abrams v. Baker Hughes Inc., 292 F.\xc2\xa03d 424, 431 (CA5 2002); Gompper v. VISX, Inc., 298 F.\xc2\xa03d 893, 897 (CA9 2002). See also Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 25.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, in determining whether the pleaded facts give rise to a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d inference of scienter, the court must take into account plausible opposing inferences. The Seventh Circuit expressly declined to engage in such a comparative inquiry. A complaint could survive, that court said, as long as it \xe2\x80\x9calleges facts from which, if true, a reasonable person could infer that the defendant acted with the required intent\xe2\x80\x9d; in other words, only \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f a reasonable person could not draw such an inference from the alleged facts\xe2\x80\x9d would the defendant prevail on a motion to dismiss. 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 602. But in \xc2\xa721D(b)(2), Congress did not merely require plaintiffs to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide a factual basis for [their] scienter allegations,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (quoting In\xc2\xa0re Cerner Corp. Securities Litigation, 425 F.\xc2\xa03d 1079, 1084, 1085 (CA8 2005)), i.e., to allege facts from which an inference of scienter rationally could be drawn. Instead, Congress required plaintiffs to plead with particularity facts that give rise to a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., a powerful or cogent\xe2\x80\x94inference. See American Heritage Dictionary 1717 (4th ed. 2000) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[p]ersuasive, effective, and cogent\xe2\x80\x9d); 16 Oxford English Dictionary 949 (2d ed. 1989) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[p]owerful to demonstrate or convince\xe2\x80\x9d (definition 16b)); cf. 7 id., at 924 (defining \xe2\x80\x9cinference\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9ca conclusion [drawn] from known or assumed facts or statements\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9creasoning from something known or assumed to something else which follows from it\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The strength of an inference cannot be decided in a vacuum. The inquiry is inherently comparative: How likely is it that one conclusion, as compared to others, follows from the underlying facts? To determine whether the plaintiff has alleged facts that give rise to the requisite \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d of scienter, a court must consider plausible nonculpable explanations for the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, as well as inferences favoring the plaintiff. The inference that the defendant acted with scienter need not be irrefutable, i.e., of the \xe2\x80\x9csmoking-gun\xe2\x80\x9d genre, or even the \xe2\x80\x9cmost plausible of competing inferences,\xe2\x80\x9d Fidel, 392 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 227 (quoting Helwig v. Vencor, Inc., 251 F.\xc2\xa03d 540, 553 (CA6 2001) (en banc)). Recall in this regard that \xc2\xa721D(b)\xe2\x80\x99s pleading requirements are but one constraint among many the PSLRA installed to screen out frivolous suits, while allowing meritorious actions to move forward. See supra, at 9, and n.\xc2\xa04. Yet the inference of scienter must be more than merely \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpermissible\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94it must be cogent and compelling, thus strong in light of other explanations. A complaint will survive, we hold, only if a reasonable person would deem the inference of scienter cogent and at least as compelling as any opposing inference one could draw from the facts alleged.[Footnote 5]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Tellabs contends that when competing inferences are considered, Notebaert\xe2\x80\x99s evident lack of pecuniary motive will be dispositive. The Shareholders, Tellabs stresses, did not allege that Notebaert sold any shares during the class period. See Brief for Petitioners 50 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe absence of any allegations of motive color all the other allegations putatively giving rise to an inference of scienter.\xe2\x80\x9d). While it is true that motive can be a relevant consideration, and personal financial gain may weigh heavily in favor of a scienter inference, we agree with the Seventh Circuit that the absence of a motive allegation is not fatal. See 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 601. As earlier stated, supra, at 11, allegations must be considered collectively; the significance that can be ascribed to an allegation of motive, or lack thereof, depends on the entirety of the complaint.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Tellabs also maintains that several of the Shareholders\xe2\x80\x99 allegations are too vague or ambiguous to contribute to a strong inference of scienter. For example, the Shareholders alleged that Tellabs flooded its customers with unwanted products, a practice known as \xe2\x80\x9cchannel stuffing.\xe2\x80\x9d See supra, at 3. But they failed, Tellabs argues, to specify whether the channel stuffing allegedly known to Notebaert was the illegitimate kind (e.g., writing orders for products customers had not requested) or the legitimate kind (e.g., offering customers discounts as an incentive to buy). Brief for Petitioners 44\xe2\x80\x9346; Reply Brief 8. See also id., at 8\xe2\x80\x939 (complaint lacks precise dates of reports critical to distinguish legitimate conduct from culpable conduct). But see 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 598, 603\xe2\x80\x93604 (pointing to multiple particulars alleged by the Shareholders, including specifications as to timing). We agree that omissions and ambiguities count against inferring scienter, for plaintiffs must \xe2\x80\x9cstate with particularity facts giving rise to a strong inference that the defendant acted with the required state of mind.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2). We reiterate, however, that the court\xe2\x80\x99s job is not to scrutinize each allegation in isolation but to assess all the allegations holistically. See supra, at 11; 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 601. In sum, the reviewing court must ask: When the allegations are accepted as true and taken collectively, would a reasonable person deem the inference of scienter at least as strong as any opposing inference?\xc2\xa0[Footnote 6]

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accounting for its construction of \xc2\xa721D(b)(2), the Seventh Circuit explained that the court \xe2\x80\x9cth[ought] it wis[e] to adopt an approach that [could not] be misunderstood as a usurpation of the jury\xe2\x80\x99s role.\xe2\x80\x9d 437 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 602. In our view, the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s concern was undue.[Footnote 7] A court\xe2\x80\x99s comparative assessment of plausible inferences, while constantly assuming the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s allegations to be true, we think it plain, does not impinge upon the Seventh Amendment right to jury trial.[Footnote 8]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress, as creator of federal statutory claims, has power to prescribe what must be pleaded to state the claim, just as it has power to determine what must be proved to prevail on the merits. It is the federal lawmaker\xe2\x80\x99s prerogative, therefore, to allow, disallow, or shape the contours of\xe2\x80\x94including the pleading and proof requirements for\xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa710(b) private actions. No decision of this Court questions that authority in general, or suggests, in particular, that the Seventh Amendment inhibits Congress from establishing whatever pleading requirements it finds appropriate for federal statutory claims. Cf. Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.\xc2\xa0A., 534 U. S. 506, 512\xe2\x80\x93513 (2002); Leatherman, 507 U. S., at 168 (both recognizing that heightened pleading requirements can be established by Federal Rule, citing Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 9(b),\nwhich requires that fraud or mistake be pleaded with particularity).[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decision in Fidelity & Deposit Co. of Md. v. United States, 187 U. S. 315 (1902), is instructive. That case concerned a rule adopted by the Supreme Court of the District of Columbia in 1879 pursuant to rulemaking power delegated by Congress. The rule required defendants, in certain contract actions, to file an affidavit \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically stating \xe2\x80\xa6 , in precise and distinct terms, the grounds of his defen[s]e.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 318 (internal quotation marks omitted). The defendant\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit was found insufficient, and judgment was entered for the plaintiff, whose declaration and supporting affidavit had been found satisfactory. Ibid. This Court upheld the District\xe2\x80\x99s rule against the contention that it violated the Seventh Amendment. Id., at 320. Just as the purpose of \xc2\xa721D(b) is to screen out frivolous complaints, the purpose of the prescription at issue in Fidelity & Deposit Co. was to \xe2\x80\x9cpreserve the courts from frivolous defen[s]es,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Explaining why the Seventh Amendment was not implicated, this Court said that the heightened pleading rule simply \xe2\x80\x9cprescribes the means of making an issue,\xe2\x80\x9d and that, when \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he issue [was] made as prescribed, the right of trial by jury accrues.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; accord Ex parte Peterson, 253 U. S. 300, 310 (1920) (Brandeis, J.) (citing Fidelity & Deposit Co., and reiterating: \xe2\x80\x9cIt does not infringe the constitutional right to a trial by jury [in a civil case], to require, with a view to formulating the issues, an oath by each party to the facts relied upon.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Walker v. New Mexico & Southern Pacific R. Co., 165 U. S. 593, 596 (1897) (Seventh Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not attempt to regulate matters of pleading\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the instant case, provided that the Shareholders have satisfied the congressionally \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe[d] \xe2\x80\xa6 means of making an issue,\xe2\x80\x9d Fidelity & Deposit Co., 187 U. S., at 320, the case will fall within the jury\xe2\x80\x99s authority to assess the credibility of witnesses, resolve any genuine issues of fact, and make the ultimate determination whether Notebaert and, by imputation, Tellabs acted with scienter. We emphasize, as well, that under our construction of the \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d standard, a plaintiff is not forced to plead more than she would be required to prove at trial. A plaintiff alleging fraud in a \xc2\xa710(b) action, we hold today, must plead facts rendering an inference of scienter at least as likely as any plausible opposing inference. At trial, she must then prove her case by a \xe2\x80\x9cpreponderance of the evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Stated otherwise, she must demonstrate that it is more likely than not that the defendant acted with scienter. See Herman & MacLean v. Huddleston, 459 U. S. 375, 390 (1983).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While we reject the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s approach to \xc2\xa721D(b)(2), we do not decide whether, under the standard we have described, see supra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9314, the Shareholders\xe2\x80\x99 allegations warrant \xe2\x80\x9ca strong inference that [Notebaert and Tellabs] acted with the required state of mind,\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2). Neither the District Court nor the Court of Appeals had the opportunity to consider the matter in light of the prescriptions we announce today. We therefore vacate the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment so that the case may be reexamined in accord with our construction of \xc2\xa721D(b)(2).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I fail to see how an inference that is merely \xe2\x80\x9cat least as compelling as any opposing inference,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 2, can conceivably be called what the statute here at issue requires: a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference,\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2). If a jade falcon were stolen from a room to which only A and B had access, could it possibly be said there was a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d that B was the thief? I think not, and I therefore think that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s test must fail. In my view, the test should be whether the inference of scienter (if any) is more plausible than the inference of innocence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s explicit rejection of this reading, ante, at 12, rests on two assertions. The first (doubtless true) is that the statute does not require that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he inference that the defendant acted with scienter \xe2\x80\xa6 be irrefutable, i.e., of the \xe2\x80\x98smoking-gun\xe2\x80\x99 genre,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. It is up to Congress, however, and not to us, to determine what pleading standard would avoid those extremities while yet effectively deterring baseless actions. Congress has expressed its determination in the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d; it is our job to give that phrase its normal meaning. And if we are to abandon text in favor of unexpressed purpose, as the Court does, it is inconceivable that Congress\xe2\x80\x99s enactment of stringent pleading requirements in the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 somehow manifests the purpose of giving plaintiffs the edge in close cases.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s second assertion (also true) is that \xe2\x80\x9can inference at least as likely as competing inferences can, in some cases, warrant recovery.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13, n. 5 (citing Summers v. Tice, 33 Cal. 2d 80, 84\xe2\x80\x9387, 199 P.\xc2\xa02d 1, 3\xe2\x80\x935 (1948) (in bank)). Summers is a famous case, however, because it sticks out of the ordinary body of tort law like a sore thumb. It represented \xe2\x80\x9ca relaxation\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9csuch proof as is ordinarily required\xe2\x80\x9d to succeed in a negligence action. Id., at 86, 199 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 4 (internal quotation marks omitted). There is no indication that the statute at issue here was meant to relax the ordinary rule under which a tie goes to the defendant. To the contrary, it explicitly strengthens that rule by extending it to the pleading stage of a case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 amici suggests that my reading of the statute would transform the text from requiring a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong\xe2\x80\x9d inference to requiring the \xe2\x80\x9cstrongest\xe2\x80\x9d inference. See Brief for American Association for Justice as Amicus Curiae 27. The point might have some force if Congress could have more clearly adopted my standard by using the word \xe2\x80\x9cstrongest\xe2\x80\x9d instead of the word \xe2\x80\x9cstrong.\xe2\x80\x9d But the use of the superlative would not have made any sense given the provision\xe2\x80\x99s structure: What does it mean to require a plaintiff to plead \xe2\x80\x9cfacts giving rise to the strongest inference that the defendant acted with the required state of mind\xe2\x80\x9d? It is certainly true that, if Congress had wanted to adopt my standard with even greater clarity, it could have restructured the entire provision\xe2\x80\x94to require, for example, that the plaintiff plead \xe2\x80\x9cfacts giving rise to an inference of scienter that is more compelling than the inference that the defendant acted with a nonculpable state of mind.\xe2\x80\x9d But if one is to consider the possibility of total restructuring, it is equally true that, to express the Court\xe2\x80\x99s standard, Congress could have demanded \xe2\x80\x9can inference of scienter that is at least as compelling as the inference that the defendant acted with a nonculpable state of mind.\xe2\x80\x9d Argument from the possibility of saying it differently is clearly a draw. We must be content to give \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d its normal meaning. I hasten to add that, while precision of interpretation should always be pursued for its own sake, I doubt that in this instance what I deem to be the correct test will produce results much different from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s. How often is it that inferences are precisely in equipoise? All the more reason, I think, to read the language for what it says.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court and the dissent criticize me for suggesting that there is only one reading of the text. Ante, at 13, n.\xc2\xa05; post, at 2, n.\xc2\xa01 (Stevens, J., dissenting). They are both mistaken. I assert only that mine is the natural reading of the statute (i.e., the normal reading), not that it is the only conceivable one. The Court has no standing to object to this approach, since it concludes that, in another respect, the statute admits of only one natural reading, namely, that competing inferences must be weighed because the strong-inference requirement \xe2\x80\x9cis inherently comparative\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12. As for the dissent, it asserts that the statute cannot possibly have a natural and discernible meaning, since \xe2\x80\x9ccourts of appeals\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cMembers of this Court\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9chave divided\xe2\x80\x9d over the question. It was just weeks ago, however, that the author of the dissent, joined by the author of today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion for the Court, concluded that a statute\xe2\x80\x99s meaning was \xe2\x80\x9cplain,\xe2\x80\x9d Rockwell Int\xe2\x80\x99l Corp. v. United States, 549 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 1) (Stevens, J., dissenting), even though the Courts of Appeals and Members of this Court divided over the question, id., at __, n.\xc2\xa05 (slip op., at 12, n.\xc2\xa05). Was plain meaning then, as the dissent claims it is today, post, at 2, n.\xc2\xa01, \xe2\x80\x9cin the eye of the beholder\xe2\x80\x9d?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is unremarkable that various Justices in this case reach different conclusions about the correct interpretation of the statutory text. It is remarkable, however, that the dissent believes that Congress \xe2\x80\x9cimplicitly delegated significant lawmaking authority to the Judiciary in determining how th[e] [strong-inference] standard should operate in practice.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 1. This is language usually employed to describe the discretion conferred upon administrative agencies, which need not adopt what courts would consider the interpretation most faithful to the text of the statute, but may choose some other interpretation, so long as it is within the bounds of the reasonable, and may later change to some other interpretation that is within the bounds of the reasonable. See Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837 (1984). Courts, by contrast, must give the statute its single, most plausible, reading. To describe this as an exercise of \xe2\x80\x9cdelegated lawmaking authority\xe2\x80\x9d seems to me peculiar\xe2\x80\x94unless one believes in lawmakers who have no discretion. Courts must apply judgment, to be sure. But judgment is not discretion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if I agreed with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference,\xe2\x80\x9d I would not join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion because of its frequent indulgence in the last remaining legal fiction of the West: that the report of a single committee of a single House expresses the will of Congress. The Court says, for example, that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress\xe2\x80\x99[s] purpose\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cto promote greater uniformity among the Circuits,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10, relying for that certitude upon the statement of managers accompanying a House Conference Committee Report whose text was never adopted by the House, much less by the Senate, and as far as we know was read by almost no one. The Court is sure that Congress \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98inten[ded] to strengthen existing pleading requirements,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., because\xe2\x80\x94again\xe2\x80\x94the statement of managers said so. I come to the same conclusion for the much safer reason that the law which Congress adopted (and which the Members of both Houses actually voted on) so indicates. And had the legislation not done so, the statement\nof managers assuredly could not have remedied the\ndeficiency.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With the above exceptions, I am generally in agreement with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, and so concur in its judgment.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito, concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that the Seventh Circuit used an erroneously low standard for determining whether the plaintiffs in this case satisfied their burden of pleading \xe2\x80\x9cwith particularity facts giving rise to a strong inference that the defendant acted with the required state of mind.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S. C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2). I further agree that the case should be remanded to allow the lower courts to decide in the first instance whether the allegations survive under the correct standard. In two respects, however, I disagree with the opinion of the Court. First, the best interpretation of the statute is that only those facts that are alleged \xe2\x80\x9cwith particularity\xe2\x80\x9d may properly be considered in determining whether the allegations of scienter are sufficient. Second, I agree with Justice Scalia that a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d of scienter, in the present context, means an inference that is more likely than not correct.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the first point, the statutory language is quite clear. Section 78u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2) states that \xe2\x80\x9cthe complaint shall, with respect to each act or omission alleged to violate this chapter, state with particularity facts giving rise to a strong inference that the defendant acted with the required state of mind.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, \xe2\x80\x9ca strong inference\xe2\x80\x9d of scienter must arise from those facts that are stated \xe2\x80\x9cwith particularity.\xe2\x80\x9d It follows that facts not stated with the requisite particularity cannot be considered in determining whether the strong-inference test is met.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In dicta, however, the Court states that \xe2\x80\x9comissions and ambiguities\xe2\x80\x9d merely \xe2\x80\x9ccount against\xe2\x80\x9d inferring scienter, and that a court should consider all allegations of scienter, even nonparticularized ones, when considering whether a complaint meets the \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d requirement. Ante, at 14. Not only does this interpretation contradict the clear statutory language on this point, but it undermines the particularity requirement\xe2\x80\x99s purpose of preventing a plaintiff from using vague or general allegations in order to get by a motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim. Allowing a plaintiff to derive benefit from such allegations would permit him to circumvent this important provision.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the particularity requirement in no way distinguishes it from normal pleading review, under which a court naturally gives less weight to allegations containing \xe2\x80\x9comissions and ambiguities\xe2\x80\x9d and more weight to allegations stating particularized facts. The particularity requirement is thus stripped of all meaning.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Questions certainly may arise as to whether certain allegations meet the statutory particularity requirement, but where that requirement is violated, the offending allegations cannot be taken into account.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I would also hold that a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference that the defendant acted with the required state of mind\xe2\x80\x9d is an inference that is stronger than the inference that the defendant lacked the required state of mind. Congress has provided very little guidance regarding the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference,\xe2\x80\x9d and the difference between the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation (the inference of scienter must be at least as strong as the inference of no scienter) and Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s (the inference of scienter must be at least marginally stronger than the inference of no scienter) is unlikely to make any practical difference. The two approaches are similar in that they both regard the critical question as posing a binary choice (either the facts give rise to a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference\xe2\x80\x9d of scienter or they do not). But Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation would align the pleading test under \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b)(2) with the test that is used at the summary-judgment and judgment-as-a-matter-of-law stages, whereas the Court\xe2\x80\x99s test would introduce a test previously unknown in civil litigation. It seems more likely that Congress meant to adopt a known quantity and thus to adopt Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s approach.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court explains, when Congress enacted a heightened pleading requirement for private actions to enforce the federal securities laws, it \xe2\x80\x9cleft the key term \xe2\x80\x98strong inference\xe2\x80\x99 undefined.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2. It thus implicitly delegated significant lawmaking authority to the Judiciary in determining how that standard should operate in practice. Today the majority crafts a perfectly workable definition of the term, but I am persuaded that a different interpretation would be both easier to apply and more consistent with the statute.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The basic purpose of the heightened pleading requirement in the context of securities fraud litigation is to protect defendants from the costs of discovery and trial in unmeritorious cases. Because of its intrusive nature, discovery may also invade the privacy interests of the defendants and their executives. Like citizens suspected of having engaged in criminal activity, those defendants should not be required to produce their private effects unless there is probable cause to believe them guilty of misconduct. Admittedly, the probable-cause standard is not capable of precise measurement, but it is a concept that is familiar to judges. As a matter of normal English usage, its meaning is roughly the same as \xe2\x80\x9cstrong inference.\xe2\x80\x9d Moreover, it is most unlikely that Congress intended us to adopt a standard that makes it more difficult to commence a civil case than a criminal case.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to the benefit of its grounding in an already familiar legal concept, using a probable-cause standard would avoid the unnecessary conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9cin determining whether the pleaded facts give rise to a \xe2\x80\x98strong\xe2\x80\x99 inference of scienter, the court must take into account plausible opposing inferences.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11 (emphasis added). There are times when an inference can easily be deemed strong without any need to weigh competing inferences. For example, if a known drug dealer exits a building immediately after a confirmed drug transaction, carrying a suspicious looking package, a judge could draw a strong inference that the individual was involved in the aforementioned drug transaction without debating whether the suspect might have been leaving the building at that exact time for another unrelated reason.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If, using that same methodology, we assume (as we must, see ante, at 11, 14) the truth of the detailed factual allegations attributed to 27 different confidential informants described in the complaint, App. 91\xe2\x80\x9393, and view those allegations collectively, I think it clear that they establish probable cause to believe that Tellabs\xe2\x80\x99 chief executive officer \xe2\x80\x9cacted with the required intent,\xe2\x80\x9d as the Seventh Circuit held.[Footnote 2] 437 F.\xc2\xa03d 588, 602 (2006).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, I would affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.
