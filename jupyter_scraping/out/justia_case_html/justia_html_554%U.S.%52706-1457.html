MORGAN STANLEY CAPITAL GROUP INC. v. PUBLIC UTILITY DISTRICT NO. 1 OF SNOHOMISH COUNTY et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x931457.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 19, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 26, 2008*

\n

Under the Mobile-Sierra doctrine, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) must presume that the electricity rate set in a freely negotiated wholesale-energy contract meets the \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable\xe2\x80\x9d requirement of the Federal Power Act (FPA), see 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7824d(a), and the presumption may be overcome only if FERC concludes that the contract seriously harms the public interest. See United Gas Pipe Line Co. v. Mobile Gas Service Corp., 350 U. S. 332; FPC v. Sierra Pacific Power Co., 350 U. S. 348. Under FERC\xe2\x80\x99s current regulatory regime, a wholesale electricity seller may file a \xe2\x80\x9cmarket-based\xe2\x80\x9d tariff, which simply states that the utility will enter into freely negotiated contracts with purchasers. Those contracts are not filed with FERC before they go into effect. In 2000 and 2001, there was a dramatic increase in the price of electricity in the western United States. As a result, respondents entered into long-term contracts with petitioners that locked in rates that were very high by historical standards. Respondents subsequently asked FERC to modify the contracts, contending that the rates should not be presumed just and reasonable under Mobile-Sierra. The Administrative Law Judge concluded that the presumption applied and that the contracts did not seriously harm the public interest. FERC affirmed, but the Ninth Circuit remanded. The court held that contract rates are presumptively reasonable only where FERC has had an initial opportunity to review the contracts without applying the Mobile-Sierra presumption and therefore that the presumption should not apply to contracts entered into under \xe2\x80\x9cmarket-based\xe2\x80\x9d tariffs. The court alternatively held that there is a different standard for overcoming the Mobile-Sierra presumption when a purchaser challenges a contract: whether the contract exceeds a \xe2\x80\x9czone of reasonableness.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The Commission was required to apply the Mobile-Sierra presumption in evaluating the contracts here. Sierra held that a rate set out in a contract must be presumed to be just and reasonable absent serious harm to the public interest, regardless of when the contract is challenged. FPC v. Texaco Inc., 417 U. S. 380, distinguished. Also, the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s rule requiring FERC to ask whether a contract was formed in an environment of market \xe2\x80\x9cdysfunction\xe2\x80\x9d is not supported by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases and plainly undermines the role of contracts in the FPA\xe2\x80\x99s statutory scheme. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9czone of reasonableness\xe2\x80\x9d test fails to accord an adequate level of protection to contracts. The standard for a buyer\xe2\x80\x99s rate-increase challenge must be the same, generally, as the standard for a seller\xe2\x80\x99s challenge: The contract rate must seriously harm the public interest. The Ninth Circuit misread Sierra in holding that the standard for evaluating a high-rate challenge and setting aside a contract rate is whether consumers\xe2\x80\x99 electricity bills were higher than they would have been had the contract rates equaled \xe2\x80\x9cmarginal cost.\xe2\x80\x9d Under the Mobile-Sierra presumption, setting aside a contract rate requires a finding of \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocal public necessity,\xe2\x80\x9d Permian Basin Area Rate Cases, 390 U. S. 747, 822, or \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d Arkansas Louisiana Gas Co. v. Hall, 453 U. S. 571, 582. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0The judgment below is nonetheless affirmed on alternative grounds, based on two defects in FERC\xe2\x80\x99s analysis. First, the analysis was flawed or incomplete to the extent FERC looked simply to whether consumers\xe2\x80\x99 rates increased immediately upon conclusion of the relevant contracts, rather than determining whether the contracts imposed an excessive burden \xe2\x80\x9cdown the line,\xe2\x80\x9d relative to the rates consumers could have obtained (but for the contracts) after elimination of the dysfunctional market. Sierra\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cexcessive burden\xe2\x80\x9d on customers was the current burden, not just the burden imposed at the contract\xe2\x80\x99s outset. See 350 U. S., at 355. Second, it is unclear from FERC\xe2\x80\x99s orders whether it found respondents\xe2\x80\x99 evidence inadequate to support their claim that petitioners engaged in unlawful market manipulation that altered the playing field for contract negotiations. In such a case, the Commission should not presume that a contract is just and reasonable. Like fraud and duress, unlawful market activity directly affecting contract negotiations eliminates the premise on which the Mobile-Sierra presumption rests: that the contract rates are the product of fair, arms-length negotiations. On remand, FERC should amplify or clarify its findings on these two points. Pp. 23\xe2\x80\x9326.

\n

471 F.\xc2\xa03d 1053, affirmed and remanded.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the Mobile-Sierra doctrine, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC or Commission) must presume that the rate set out in a freely negotiated wholesale-energy contract meets the \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable\xe2\x80\x9d requirement imposed by law. The presumption may be overcome only if FERC concludes that the contract seriously harms the public interest. These cases present two questions about the scope of the Mobile-Sierra doctrine: First, does the presumption apply only when FERC has had an initial opportunity to review a contract rate without the presumption? Second, does the presumption impose as high a bar to challenges by purchasers of wholesale electricity as it does to challenges by sellers?

\n

I

\n

A\nStatutory Background

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Federal Power Act (FPA), 41 Stat. 1063, as amended, gives the Commission[Footnote 1] the authority to regulate the sale of electricity in interstate commerce\xe2\x80\x94a market historically characterized by natural monopoly and therefore subject to abuses of market power. See 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7824 et seq. Modeled on the Interstate Commerce Act, the FPA requires regulated utilities to file compilations of their rate schedules, or \xe2\x80\x9ctariffs,\xe2\x80\x9d with the Commission, and to provide service to electricity purchasers on the terms and prices there set forth. \xc2\xa7824d(c). Utilities wishing to change their tariffs must notify the Commission 60 days before the change is to go into effect. \xc2\xa7824d(d). Unlike the Interstate Commerce Act, however, the FPA also permits utilities to set rates with individual electricity purchasers through bilateral contracts. \xc2\xa7824d(c), (d). As we have explained elsewhere, the FPA \xe2\x80\x9cdeparted from the scheme of purely tariff-based regulation and acknowledged that contracts between commercial buyers and sellers could be used in ratesetting.\xe2\x80\x9d Verizon Communications Inc. v. FCC, 535 U. S. 467, 479 (2002). Like tariffs, contracts must be filed with the Commission before they go into effect. 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7824d(c), (d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The FPA requires all wholesale-electricity rates to be \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7824d(a). When a utility files a new rate with the Commission, through a change to its tariff or a new contract, the Commission may suspend the rate for up to five months while it investigates whether the rate is just and reasonable. \xc2\xa7824d(e). The Commission may, however, decline to investigate and permit the rate to go into effect\xe2\x80\x94which does not amount to a determination that the rate is \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d See 18 CFR \xc2\xa735.4 (2007). After a rate goes into effect, whether or not the Commission deemed it just and reasonable when filed, the Commission may conclude, in response to a complaint or on its own motion, that the rate is not just and reasonable and replace it with a lawful rate. 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7824e(a) (2000 ed., Supp. V).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The statutory requirement that rates be \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable\xe2\x80\x9d is obviously incapable of precise judicial definition, and we afford great deference to the Commission in its rate decisions. See FPC v. Texaco Inc., 417 U. S. 380, 389 (1974); Permian Basin Area Rate Cases, 390 U. S. 747, 767 (1968). We have repeatedly emphasized that the Commission is not bound to any one ratemaking formula. See Mobil Oil Exploration & Producing Southeast, Inc. v. United Distribution Cos., 498 U. S. 211, 224 (1991); Permian Basin, supra, at 776\xe2\x80\x93777. But FERC must choose a method that entails an appropriate \xe2\x80\x9cbalancing of the investor and the consumer interests.\xe2\x80\x9d FPC v. Hope Natural Gas Co., 320 U. S. 591, 603 (1944). In exercising its broad discretion, the Commission traditionally reviewed and set tariff rates under the \xe2\x80\x9ccost-of-service\xe2\x80\x9d method, which ensures that a seller of electricity recovers its costs plus a rate of return sufficient to attract necessary capital. See J. McGrew, Federal Energy Regulatory Commission 152, 160\xe2\x80\x93161 (2003) (hereinafter McGrew).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In two cases decided on the same day in 1956, we addressed the authority of the Commission to modify rates set bilaterally by contract rather than unilaterally by tariff. In United Gas Pipe Line Co. v. Mobile Gas Service Corp., 350 U. S. 332, we rejected a natural-gas utility\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the Natural Gas Act\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that it file all new rates with the Commission authorized it to abrogate a lawful contract with a purchaser simply by filing a new tariff, see id., at 336\xe2\x80\x93337. The filing requirement, we explained, is merely a precondition to changing a rate, not an authorization to change rates in violation of a lawful contract (i.e., a contract that sets a just and reasonable rate). See id., at 339\xe2\x80\x93344.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In FPC v. Sierra Pacific Power Co., 350 U. S. 348, 352\xe2\x80\x93353 (1956), we applied the holding of Mobile to the analogous provisions of the FPA, concluding that the complaining utility could not supersede a contract rate simply by filing a new tariff. In Sierra, however, the Commission had concluded not only (contrary to our holding) that the newly filed tariff superseded the contract, but also that the contract rate itself was not just and reasonable, \xe2\x80\x9csolely because it yield[ed] less than a fair return on the net invested capital\xe2\x80\x9d of the utility. Id., at 355. Thus, we were confronted with the question of how the Commission may evaluate whether a contract rate is just and reasonable.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We answered that question in the following way:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Commission\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion appears on its face to be based on an erroneous standard\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [W]hile it may be that the Commission may not normally impose upon a public utility a rate which would produce less than a fair return, it does not follow that the public utility may not itself agree by contract to a rate affording less than a fair return or that, if it does so, it is entitled to be relieved of its improvident bargain\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. In such circumstances the sole concern of the Commission would seem to be whether the rate is so low as to adversely affect the public interest\xe2\x80\x94as where it might impair the financial ability of the public utility to continue its service, cast upon other consumers an excessive burden, or be unduly discriminatory.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 354\xe2\x80\x93355 (emphasis deleted).

\n

\nAs we said in a later case, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he regulatory system created by the [FPA] is premised on contractual agreements voluntarily devised by the regulated companies; it contemplates abrogation of these agreements only in circumstances of unequivocal public necessity.\xe2\x80\x9d Permian Basin, supra, at 822.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Over the past 50 years, decisions of this Court and the Courts of Appeals have refined the Mobile-Sierra presumption to allow greater freedom of contract. In United Gas Pipe Line Co. v. Memphis Light, Gas and Water Div., 358 U. S. 103, 110\xe2\x80\x93113 (1958), we held that parties could contract out of the Mobile-Sierra presumption by specifying in their contracts that a new rate filed with the Commission would supersede the contract rate. Courts of Appeals have held that contracting parties may also agree to a middle option between Mobile-Sierra and Memphis Light: A contract that does not allow the seller to supersede the contract rate by filing a new rate may nonetheless permit the Commission to set aside the contract rate if it results in an unfair rate of return, not just if it violates the public interest. See, e.g., Papago Tribal Util. Auth. v. FERC, 723 F. 2d 950, 953 (CADC 1983); Louisiana Power & Light Co. v. FERC, 587 F. 2d 671, 675\xe2\x80\x93676 (CA5 1979). Thus, as the Mobile-Sierra doctrine has developed, regulated parties have retained broad authority to specify whether FERC can review a contract rate solely for whether it violates the public interest or also for whether it results in an unfair rate of return. But the Mobile-Sierra presumption remains the default rule.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, even though the challenges in Mobile and Sierra were brought by sellers, lower courts have concluded that the Mobile-Sierra presumption also applies where a purchaser, rather than a seller, asks FERC to modify a contract. See Potomac Elec. Power Co. v. FERC, 210 F. 3d 403, 404\xe2\x80\x93405, 409\xe2\x80\x93410 (CADC 2000); Boston Edison Co. v. FERC, 856 F. 2d 361, 372 (CA1 1988). This Court has seemingly blessed that conclusion, explaining that under the FPA, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen commercial parties \xe2\x80\xa6 avail themselves of rate agreements, the principal regulatory responsibility [is] not to relieve a contracting party of an unreasonable rate.\xe2\x80\x9d Verizon, 535 U. S., at 479 (citing Sierra, supra, at 355).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Over the years, the Commission began to refer to the two modes of review\xe2\x80\x94one with the Mobile-Sierra presumption and the other without\xe2\x80\x94as the \xe2\x80\x9cpublic interest standard\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable standard.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Southern Co. Servs., Inc. Gulf States Utils. Co. v. Southern Co. Servs., Inc., 39 FERC \xc2\xb663,026, pp.\xc2\xa065,134, 65,141 (1987). Decisions from the Courts of Appeals did likewise. See, e.g., Kansas Cities v. FERC, 723 F. 2d 82, 87\xe2\x80\x9388 (CADC 1983); Northeast Utils. Serv. Co. v. FERC, 993 F. 2d 937, 961 (CA1 1993). We do not take this nomenclature to stand for the obviously indefensible proposition that a standard different from the statutory just-and-reasonable standard applies to contract rates. Rather, the term \xe2\x80\x9cpublic interest standard\xe2\x80\x9d refers to the differing application of that just-and-reasonable standard to contract rates. See Philadelphia Elec. Co., 58 F.\xc2\xa0P.\xc2\xa0C. 88, 90 (1977). (It would be less confusing to adopt the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x99s terminology, referring to the two differing applications of the just-and-reasonable standard as the \xe2\x80\x9cordinary\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable standard\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cpublic interest standard.\xe2\x80\x9d See Reply Brief for Respondent FERC 6.)

\n

B

\n

Recent FERC Innovations; Market-Based Tariffs

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In recent decades, the Commission has undertaken an ambitious program of market-based reforms. Part of the impetus for those changes was technological evolution. Historically, electric utilities had been vertically integrated monopolies. For a particular geographic area, a single utility would control the generation of electricity, its transmission, and its distribution to consumers. See Midwest ISO Transmission Owners v. FERC, 373 F. 3d 1361, 1363 (CADC 2004). Since the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s, however, engineering innovations have lowered the cost of generating electricity and transmitting it over long distances, enabling new entrants to challenge the regional generating monopolies of traditional utilities. See generally New York v. FERC, 535 U. S. 1, 7\xe2\x80\x938 (2002); Public Util. Dist. No. 1 of Snohomish Cty. v. FERC, 272 F.\xc2\xa03d 607, 610 (CADC 2001).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To take advantage of these changes, the Commission has attempted to break down regulatory and economic barriers that hinder a free market in wholesale electricity. It has sought to promote competition in those areas of the industry amenable to competition, such as the segment that generates electric power, while ensuring that the segment of the industry characterized by natural monopoly\xe2\x80\x94namely, the transmission grid that conveys the generated electricity\xe2\x80\x94cannot exert monopolistic influence over other areas. See New York, supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310; Snohomish, supra. To that end, FERC required in Order No. 888 that each transmission provider offer transmission service to all customers on an equal basis by filing an \xe2\x80\x9copen access transmission tariff.\xe2\x80\x9d Promoting Wholesale Competition Through Open Access Non-Discriminatory Transmission Services by Public Utilities, 61 Fed. Reg. 21540 (1996); see New York, supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9312. That requirement prevents the utilities that own the grid from offering more favorable transmission terms to their own affiliates and thereby extending their monopoly power to other areas of the industry.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To further pry open the wholesale-electricity market and to reduce technical inefficiencies caused when different utilities operate different portions of the grid independently, the Commission has encouraged transmission providers to establish \xe2\x80\x9cRegional Transmission Organizations\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94entities to which transmission providers would transfer operational control of their facilities for the purpose of efficient coordination. Order No. 2000, 65 Fed. Reg. 810, 811\xe2\x80\x93812 (2000); see Midwest ISO, supra, at 1364. It has encouraged the management of those entities by \xe2\x80\x9cIndependent System Operators,\xe2\x80\x9d not-for-profit entities that operate transmission facilities in a nondiscriminatory manner. See Midwest ISO, supra. In addition to coordinating transmission service, Regional Transmission Organizations perform other functions, such as running auction markets for electricity sales and offering contracts for hedging against potential grid congestion. See Blumsack, Measuring the Benefits and Costs of Regional Electric Grid Integration, 28 Energy L.\xc2\xa0J. 147, 147 (2007).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Against this backdrop of technological change and market-based reforms, the Commission over the past two decades has begun to permit sellers of wholesale electricity to file \xe2\x80\x9cmarket-based\xe2\x80\x9d tariffs. These tariffs, instead of setting forth rate schedules or rate-fixing contracts, simply state that the seller will enter into freely negotiated contracts with purchasers. See generally Market-Based Rates For Wholesale Sales Of Electric Energy, Capacity And Ancillary Services By Public Utilities, Order No. 697, 72 Fed. Reg. 39904 (2007) (hereinafter Market-Based Rates); McGrew 160\xe2\x80\x93167. FERC does not subject the contracts entered into under these tariffs (as it subjected traditional wholesale-power contracts) to \xc2\xa7824d\xe2\x80\x99s requirement of immediate filing, apparently on the theory that the requirement has been satisfied by the initial filing of the market-based tariffs themselves. See Brief for Respondent FERC 28\xe2\x80\x9329 (hereinafter Brief for FERC).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0FERC will grant approval of a market-based tariff only if a utility demonstrates that it lacks or has adequately mitigated market power, lacks the capacity to erect other barriers to entry, and has avoided giving preferences to its affiliates. See Market-Based Rates, \xc2\xb67, 72 Fed. Reg. 39907. In addition to the initial authorization of a market-based tariff, FERC imposes ongoing reporting requirements. A seller must file quarterly reports summarizing the contracts that it has entered into, even extremely short-term contracts. See California ex rel. Lockyer v. FERC, 383 F.\xc2\xa03d 1006, 1013 (CA9 2004). It must also demonstrate every four months that it still lacks or has adequately mitigated market power. See ibid. If FERC determines from these filings that a seller has reattained market power, it may revoke the authority prospectively. See Market-Based Rates, \xc2\xb65, 72 Fed. Reg. 39906. And if the Commission finds that a seller has violated its Regional Transmission Organization\xe2\x80\x99s market rules, its tariff, or Commission orders, the Commission may take appropriate remedial action, such as ordering refunds, requiring disgorgement of profits, and imposing civil penalties. See ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both the Ninth Circuit and the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit have generally approved FERC\xe2\x80\x99s scheme of market-based tariffs. See Lockyer, supra, at 1011\xe2\x80\x931013; Louisiana Energy & Power Auth. v. FERC, 141 F. 3d 364, 365 (CADC 1998). We have not hitherto approved, and express no opinion today, on the lawfulness of the market-based-tariff system, which is not one of the issues before us. It suffices for the present cases to recognize that when a seller files a market-based tariff, purchasers no longer have the option of buying electricity at a rate set by tariff and contracts no longer need to be filed with FERC (and subjected to its investigatory power) before going into effect.

\n

C

\n

California\xe2\x80\x99s Electricity Regulation and\nIts Consequences

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1996, California enacted Assembly Bill 1890 (AB 1890), which massively restructured the California electricity market. See 1996 Cal. Stat. ch. 854 (codified at Cal. Pub. Util. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7330\xe2\x80\x93398.5 (West 2004 and Supp. 2008)); see generally Cudahy, Whither Deregulation: A Look at the Portents, 58 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0U. Annual Survey of Am. Law 155, 172\xe2\x80\x93185 (2001) (hereinafter Cudahy). The bill transferred operational control of the transmission facilities of California\xe2\x80\x99s three largest investor-owned utilities to an Independent Service Operator (Cal-ISO). See Pacific Gas & Elec. Co. v. FERC, 464 F.\xc2\xa03d 861, 864 (CA9 2006). It also established the California Power Exchange (CalPX), a nonprofit entity that operated a short-term market\xe2\x80\x94or \xe2\x80\x9cspot market\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94for electricity. The bill required California\xe2\x80\x99s three largest investor-owned utilities to divest most of their electricity-generation facilities. It then required those utilities to purchase and sell the bulk of their electricity from and to the CalPX\xe2\x80\x99s spot market, permitting only limited leeway for them to enter into long-term contracts. See Public Util. Dist. No. 1 of Snohomish Cty. v. FERC, 471 F.\xc2\xa03d 1053, 1068 (CA9 2006) (case below).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1997, FERC approved the Cal-ISO as consistent with the requirements for an Independent Service Operator established in Order No. 888. FERC also approved the CalPX and the investor-owned utilities\xe2\x80\x99 authority to make sales at market-based rates in the CalPX, finding that, in light of the divesture of their generation units and other conditions imposed under the restructuring plan, those utilities had adequately mitigated their market power. See Pacific Gas & Elec. Co., 81 FERC \xc2\xb661,122, pp.\xc2\xa061,435, 61,435\xe2\x80\x9361,436, 61,537\xe2\x80\x9361,548 (1997).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The CalPX opened for business in March 1998. In the summer of 1999, it expanded to include an auction for sales of electricity under \xe2\x80\x9cforward contracts\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94contracts in which sellers promise to deliver electricity more than one day in the future (sometimes many years). But the participation of California\xe2\x80\x99s large investor-owned utilities in that forward market was limited because, as we have said, AB 1890 strictly capped the amount of power that they could purchase outside of the spot market. See 471 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1068.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That diminishment of the role of long-term contracts in the California electricity market turned out to be one of the seeds of an energy crisis. In the summer of 2000, the price of electricity in the CalPX\xe2\x80\x99s spot market jumped dramatically\xe2\x80\x94more than fifteenfold. See ibid. The increase was the result of a combination of natural, economic, and regulatory factors: \xe2\x80\x9cflawed market rules; inadequate addition of generating facilities in the preceding years; a drop in available hydropower due to drought conditions; a rupture of a major pipeline supplying natural gas into California; strong growth in the economy and in electricity demand; unusually high temperatures; an increase in unplanned outages of extremely old generating facilities; and market manipulation.\xe2\x80\x9d CAlifornians for Renewable Energy, Inc. v. Sellers of Energy and Ancillary Servs., 119 FERC \xc2\xb661,058, pp.\xc2\xa061,243, 61,246 (2007). Because California\xe2\x80\x99s investor-owned utilities had for the most part been forbidden to obtain their power through long-term contracts, the turmoil in the spot market hit them hard. See Cudahy 174. The high prices led to rolling blackouts and saddled utilities with mounting debt.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In late 2000, the Commission took action. A central plank of its emergency effort was to eliminate the utilities\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on the CalPX\xe2\x80\x99s spot market and to shift their purchases to the forward market. To that end, FERC abolished the requirement that investor-owned utilities purchase and sell all power through the CalPX and encouraged them to enter into long-term contracts. See San Diego Gas & Electric Co. v. Sellers of Energy and Ancillary Servs., 93 FERC \xc2\xb661,294, pp.\xc2\xa061,980, 61,982 (2000); see also 471 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1069. The Commission also put price caps on wholesale electricity. See San Diego Gas & Elec. Co. v. Sellers of Energy and Ancillary Servs., 95 FERC \xc2\xb661,418, p.\xc2\xa062,545 (2001). By June 2001, electricity prices began to decline to normal levels. Id., at 62,456.

\n

D

\n

Genesis of These Cases

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The principal respondents in these cases are western utilities that purchased power under long-term contracts during that tumultuous period in 2000 and 2001. Although they are not located in California, the high prices in California spilled over into other Western States. See 471 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1069. Petitioners are the sellers that entered into the contracts with respondents.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The contracts between the parties included rates that were very high by historical standards. For example, respondent Snohomish signed a 9-year contract to purchase electricity from petitioner Morgan Stanley at a rate of $105/megawatt hour (MWh), whereas prices in the Pacific Northwest have historically averaged $24/MWh. The contract prices were substantially lower, however, than the prices that Snohomish would have paid in the spot market during the energy crisis, when prices peaked at $3,300/MWh. See id., at 1069\xe2\x80\x931070.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the crisis had passed, buyer\xe2\x80\x99s remorse set in and respondents asked FERC to modify the contracts. They contended that the rates in the contracts should not be presumed to be just and reasonable under Mobile-Sierra because, given the sellers\xe2\x80\x99 market-based tariffs, the contracts had never been initially approved by the Commission without the presumption. See Nevada Power Co. v. Enron Power Marketing, Inc., 103 FERC \xc2\xb661,353, pp.\xc2\xa062,382, 62,387 (2003). Respondents also argued that contract modification was warranted even under the Mobile-Sierra presumption because the contract rates were so high that they violated the public interest. See 103 FERC, at 62,383, 62,387\xe2\x80\x9362,395.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a preliminary order, the Commission instructed the Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) to consider 12 different factors in deciding whether the presumption could be overcome for the contracts, such as the terms of the contracts, the available alternatives at the time of sale, the relationship of the rates to Commission benchmarks, the effect of the contracts on the financial health of the purchasers, and the impact of contract modification on national energy markets. After a hearing, the ALJ concluded that the Mobile-Sierra presumption should apply to the contracts and that the contracts did not seriously harm the public interest. In fact, according to the ALJ, even if the Mobile-Sierra presumption did not apply, respondents would not be entitled to have the contracts modified. 103 FERC, at 62,390\xe2\x80\x9362,394.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Between the ALJ\xe2\x80\x99s decision and the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s staff issued a report (Staff Report) concluding that unlawful activities of various sellers in the spot market had affected prices in the forward market. See id., at 62,396. Respondents raised the report at oral argument before the Commission, and some of them argued that petitioners \xe2\x80\x9cwere unlawfully manipulating market prices, thereby engaging in fraud and deception in violation of their market-based rate tariffs.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Petitioners contended, however, that the Staff Report demonstrated only a correlation between rates in the spot and forward markets, not a causal connection. See ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0FERC affirmed the ALJ. The Commission first held that the Mobile-Sierra presumption did apply to the contracts at issue. Although agreeing with respondents that the presumption applies only where FERC has had an initial opportunity to review a contract rate, the Commission relied on the somewhat metaphysical ground that the grant of market-based authority to petitioners qualified as that initial opportunity. See 103 FERC, at 62,388\xe2\x80\x9362,389. The Commission then held that respondents could not overcome the Mobile-Sierra presumption. It recognized that the Staff Report had \xe2\x80\x9cfound that spot market distortions flowed through to forward power prices,\xe2\x80\x9d 103 FERC, at 62,396\xe2\x80\x9362,397, but concluded that this finding, even if true, was not \xe2\x80\x9cdeterminative\xe2\x80\x9d because:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9ca finding that the unjust and unreasonable spot market caused forward bilateral prices to be unjust and unreasonable would be relevant to contract modification only where there is a \xe2\x80\x98just and reasonable\xe2\x80\x99 standard of review\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Under the \xe2\x80\x98public interest\xe2\x80\x99 standard, to justify contract modification it is not enough to show that forward prices became unjust and unreasonable due to the impact of spot market dysfunctions; it must be shown that the rates, terms and conditions are contrary to the public interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 62,397.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission determined that under the factors identified in Sierra, as well as under a totality-of-the-circumstances test, respondents had not demonstrated that the contracts threatened the public interest. See 103 FERC, at 62,397\xe2\x80\x9362,399. On rehearing, respondents reiterated their complaints, including their charge that \xe2\x80\x9ctheir contracts were the product of market manipulation by Enron, Morgan Stanley and other [sellers].\xe2\x80\x9d 105 FERC \xc2\xb661,185, pp.\xc2\xa061,979, 61,989 (2003). The Commission answered that there was \xe2\x80\x9cno evidence to support a finding of market manipulation that specifically affected the contracts at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents filed petitions for review in the Ninth Circuit, which granted the petitions and remanded to the Commission, finding two flaws in the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s analysis.[Footnote 2] First, the court agreed with respondents that rates set by contract (whether pursuant to a market-based tariff or not) are presumptively reasonable only where FERC has had an initial opportunity to review the contracts without applying the Mobile-Sierra presumption. To satisfy that prerequisite under the market-based tariff regime, the court said, the Commission must promptly review the terms of contracts after their formation and must modify those that do not appear to be just and reasonable when evaluated without the Mobile-Sierra presumption (rather than merely revoking market-based authority prospectively but leaving pre-existing contracts intact). See 471 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1075\xe2\x80\x931077, 1079\xe2\x80\x931085. This initial review must include an inquiry into \xe2\x80\x9cthe market conditions in which the contracts at issue were formed,\xe2\x80\x9d and market \xe2\x80\x9cdysfunction\xe2\x80\x9d is a ground for finding a contract not to be just and reasonable. Id., at 1085\xe2\x80\x931087. Second, the Ninth Circuit held that even assuming that the Mobile-Sierra presumption applied, the standard for overcoming that presumption is different for a purchaser\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to a contract, namely, whether the contract rate exceeds a \xe2\x80\x9czone of reasonableness.\xe2\x80\x9d 471 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1088\xe2\x80\x931090.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari. See 551 U. S. ___ (2007).

\n

II

\n

A\nApplication of Mobile-Sierra Presumption to\nContracts Concluded under Market-Based\nRate Authority

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As noted earlier, the FERC order under review here agreed with the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s premise that the Commission must have an initial opportunity to review a contract without the Mobile-Sierra presumption, but maintained that the authorization for market-based rate authority qualified as that initial review. Before this Court, however, FERC changes its tune, arguing that there is no such prerequisite\xe2\x80\x94or at least that FERC could reasonably conclude so and therefore that Chevron deference is in order. See Brief for FERC 20\xe2\x80\x9321, 33\xe2\x80\x9334; Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837 (1984). We will not uphold a discretionary agency decision where the agency has offered a justification in court different from what it provided in its opinion. See SEC v. Chenery Corp., 318 U. S. 80, 94\xe2\x80\x9395 (1943). But FERC has lucked out: The Chenery doctrine has no application to these cases, because we conclude that the Commission was required, under our decision in Sierra, to apply the Mobile-Sierra presumption in its evaluation of the contracts here. That it provided a different rationale for the necessary result is no cause for upsetting its ruling. \xe2\x80\x9cTo remand would be an idle and useless formality. Chenery does not require that we convert judicial review of agency action into a ping-pong game.\xe2\x80\x9d NLRB v. Wyman-Gordon Co., 394 U. S. 759, 766\xe2\x80\x93767, n.\xc2\xa06 (1969) (plurality opinion).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are in broad agreement with the Ninth Circuit on a central premise: There is only one statutory standard for assessing wholesale electricity rates, whether set by contract or tariff\xe2\x80\x94the just-and-reasonable standard. The plain text of the FPA states that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll rates \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be just and reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7824d(a); see also \xc2\xa7824e(a) (2000 ed., Supp. V). But we disagree with the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of Sierra as requiring (contrary to the statute) that the Commission apply the standard differently, depending on when a contract rate is challenged. In the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view, Sierra was premised on the idea that \xe2\x80\x9cas long as the rate was just and reasonable when the contract was formed, there would be a presumption\xe2\x80\xa6that the reasonableness continued throughout the term of the contract.\xe2\x80\x9d 471 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1077. In other words, so long as the Commission concludes (either after a hearing or by allowing a rate to go into effect) that a contract rate is just and reasonable when initially filed, the rate will be presumed just and reasonable in future proceedings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That is a misreading of Sierra. Sierra was grounded in the commonsense notion that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n wholesale markets, the party charging the rate and the party charged [are] often sophisticated businesses enjoying presumptively equal bargaining power, who could be expected to negotiate a \xe2\x80\x98just and reasonable\xe2\x80\x99 rate as between the two of them.\xe2\x80\x9d Verizon, 535 U. S., at 479. Therefore, only when the mutually agreed-upon contract rate seriously harms the consuming public may the Commission declare it not to be just and reasonable.[Footnote 3] Sierra thus provided a definition of what it means for a rate to satisfy the just-and-reasonable standard in the contract context\xe2\x80\x94a definition that applies regardless of when the contract is reviewed. The Ninth Circuit, by contrast, essentially read Sierra \xe2\x80\x9cas the equivalent of an estoppel doctrine,\xe2\x80\x9d whereby an initial Commission opportunity for review prevents the Commission from modifying the rates absent serious future harm to the public interest. Tewksbury & Lim, Applying the Mobile-Sierra Doctrine to Market-Based Rate Contracts, 26 Energy L. J. 437, 457\xe2\x80\x93458 (2005). But Sierra said nothing of the sort. And given that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s passive permission for a rate to go into effect does not constitute a finding that the rate is just and reasonable, it would be odd to treat that initial \xe2\x80\x9copportunity for review\xe2\x80\x9d as curtailing later challenges.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit found support for its prerequisite in our decision in FPC v. Texaco Inc., 417 U. S. 380 (1974). In that case, we warned that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to rely solely on market forces to evaluate rates charged by small natural-gas producers was inconsistent with the Natural Gas Act\xe2\x80\x99s insistence that rates be just and reasonable. See id., at 397. The Ninth Circuit apparently took this to mean that all initially filed contracts must be subject to review without the Mobile-Sierra presumption. But Texaco had nothing to do with that doctrine. It held that the Commission had improperly implemented a scheme of total deregulation by applying no standard of review at all to small-producer rates. See 417 U. S., at 395\xe2\x80\x93397. It did not cast doubt on the proposition that in a proper regulatory scheme, the ordinary mode for evaluating contractually set rates is to look to whether the rates seriously harm the public interest, not to whether they are unfair to one of the parties that voluntarily assented to the contract. Cf. id., at 391, n.\xc2\xa04.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor do we agree with the Ninth Circuit that FERC must inquire into whether a contract was formed in an environment of market \xe2\x80\x9cdysfunction\xe2\x80\x9d before applying the Mobile-Sierra presumption. Markets are not perfect, and one of the reasons that parties enter into wholesale-power contracts is precisely to hedge against the volatility that market imperfections produce. That is why one of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s responses to the energy crisis was to remove regulatory barriers to long-term contracts. It would be a perverse rule that rendered contracts less likely to be enforced when there is volatility in the market. (Such a rule would come into play, after all, only when a contract formed in a period of \xe2\x80\x9cdysfunction\xe2\x80\x9d did not significantly harm the consuming public, since contracts that seriously harm the public should be set aside even under the Mobile-Sierra presumption.) By enabling sophisticated parties who weathered market turmoil by entering long-term contracts to renounce those contracts once the storm has passed, the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s holding would reduce the incentive to conclude such contracts in the future. Such a rule has no support in our case law and plainly undermines the role of contracts in the FPA\xe2\x80\x99s statutory scheme.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, FERC has ample authority to set aside a contract where there is unfair dealing at the contract formation stage\xe2\x80\x94for instance, if it finds traditional grounds for the abrogation of the contract such as fraud or duress. See 103 FERC, at 62,399\xe2\x80\x9362,400 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is no evidence of unfairness, bad faith, or duress in the original negotiations\xe2\x80\x9d). In addition, if the \xe2\x80\x9cdysfunctional\xe2\x80\x9d market conditions under which the contract was formed were caused by illegal action of one of the parties, FERC should not apply the Mobile-Sierra presumption. See Part III, infra. But the mere fact that the market is imperfect, or even chaotic, is no reason to undermine the stabilizing force of contracts that the FPA embraced as an alternative to \xe2\x80\x9cpurely tariff-based regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Verizon, 535 U. S., at 479. We may add that evaluating market \xe2\x80\x9cdysfunction\xe2\x80\x9d is a very difficult and highly speculative task\xe2\x80\x94not one that the FPA would likely require the agency to engage in before holding sophisticated parties to their bargains.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We reiterate that we do not address the lawfulness of FERC\xe2\x80\x99s market-based-rates scheme, which assuredly has its critics. But any needed revision in that scheme is properly addressed in a challenge to the scheme itself, not through a disfigurement of the venerable Mobile-Sierra doctrine. We hold only that FERC may abrogate a valid contract only if it harms the public interest.

\n

B

\n

Application of \xe2\x80\x9cExcessive Burden\xe2\x80\x9d\nException to High-Rate Challenges

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn now to the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s second holding: that a \xe2\x80\x9czone of reasonableness\xe2\x80\x9d test should be used to evaluate a buyer\xe2\x80\x99s challenge that a rate is too high. In our view that fails to accord an adequate level of protection to contracts. The standard for a buyer\xe2\x80\x99s challenge must be the same, generally speaking, as the standard for a seller\xe2\x80\x99s challenge: The contract rate must seriously harm the public interest. That is the standard that the Commission applied in the proceedings below.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are again in agreement with the Ninth Circuit on a starting premise: It is clear that the three factors we identified in Sierra\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cwhere [a rate] might impair the financial ability of the public utility to continue its service, cast upon other consumers an excessive burden, or be unduly discriminatory,\xe2\x80\x9d 350 U. S., at 355\xe2\x80\x94are not all precisely applicable to the high-rate challenge of a purchaser (where, for example, the relevant question is not whether \xe2\x80\x9cother customers\xe2\x80\x9d [of the utility] would be excessively burdened, but whether any customers of the purchaser would be); and that those three factors are in any event not the exclusive components of the public interest. In its decision below, the Commission recognized both these realities. See 103 FERC, at 62,397 (\xe2\x80\x9cNevada Companies failed to show that the contract terms at issue impose an excessive burden on their customers\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); id., at 62,398 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe record also demonstrates that Snohomish presented no evidence that its contract with Morgan Stanley adversely affected Snohomish or its ratepayers\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); id., at 62,398\xe2\x80\x9362,399 (evaluating the \xe2\x80\x9ctotality of circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Brief for FERC 41\xe2\x80\x9342.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Where we disagree with the Ninth Circuit is on the overarching \xe2\x80\x9czone of reasonableness\xe2\x80\x9d standard it established for evaluating a high-rate challenge and setting aside a contract rate: whether consumers\xe2\x80\x99 electricity bills \xe2\x80\x9care higher than they would otherwise have been had the challenged contracts called for rates within the just and reasonable range,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., rates that equal \xe2\x80\x9cmarginal cost.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 5] 471 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1089. The Ninth Circuit derived this test from our statement in Sierra that a contract rate would have to be modified if it were so low that it imposed an \xe2\x80\x9cexcessive burden\xe2\x80\x9d on other wholesale purchasers. The Ninth Circuit took \xe2\x80\x9cexcessive burden\xe2\x80\x9d to mean merely the burden caused when one set of consumers is forced to pay above marginal cost to compensate for below-marginal-cost rates charged other consumers. See 471 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1088. And it proceeded to apply a similar notion of \xe2\x80\x9cexcessive burden\xe2\x80\x9d to high-rate challenges (where all the burden of the above-marginal-cost contract rate falls on the purchaser\xe2\x80\x99s own customers, and does not affect the customers of third parties). Id., at 1089. That is a misreading of Sierra and our later cases. A presumption of validity that disappears when the rate is above marginal cost is no presumption of validity at all, but a reinstitution of cost-based rather than contract-based regulation. We have said that, under the Mobile-Sierra presumption, setting aside a contract rate requires a finding of \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocal public necessity,\xe2\x80\x9d Permian Basin, 390 U. S., at 822, or \xe2\x80\x9cextraordinary circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d Arkansas Louisiana Gas Co. v. Hall, 453 U. S. 571, 582 (1981). In no way can these descriptions be thought to refer to the mere exceeding of marginal cost.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s standard would give short shrift to the important role of contracts in the FPA, as reflected in our decision in Sierra, and would threaten to inject more volatility into the electricity market by undermining a key source of stability. The FPA recognizes that contract stability ultimately benefits consumers, even if short-term rates for a subset of the public might be high by historical standards\xe2\x80\x94which is why it permits rates to be set by contract and not just by tariff. As the Commission has recently put it, its \xe2\x80\x9cfirst and foremost duty is to protect consumers from unjust and unreasonable rates; however, \xe2\x80\xa6 uncertainties regarding rate stability and contract sanctity can have a chilling effect on investments and a seller\xe2\x80\x99s willingness to enter into long-term contracts and this, in turn, can harm customers in the long run.\xe2\x80\x9d Market-Based Rates, \xc2\xb66, 72 Fed. Reg. 33906\xe2\x80\x9333907.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Besides being wrong in principle, in its practical effect the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s rule would impose an onerous new burden on the Commission, requiring it to calculate the marginal cost of the power sold under a market-based contract. Assuming that FERC even ventured to undertake such an analysis, rather than reverting to the ancien r\xc3\xa9gime of cost-of-service ratesetting, the regulatory costs would be enormous. We think that the FPA intended to reserve the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s contract-abrogation power for those extraordinary circumstances where the public will be severely harmed.[Footnote 6]

\n

III

\n

Defects in FERC\xe2\x80\x99s Analysis Supporting Remand

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite our significant disagreement with the Ninth Circuit, we find two errors in the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, and we therefore affirm the judgment below on alternative grounds.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, it appears, as the Ninth Circuit concluded, see 471 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1090, that the Commission may have looked simply to whether consumers\xe2\x80\x99 rates increased immediately upon the relevant contracts\xe2\x80\x99 going into effect, rather than determining whether the contracts imposed an excessive burden on consumers \xe2\x80\x9cdown the line,\xe2\x80\x9d relative to the rates they could have obtained (but for the contracts) after elimination of the dysfunctional market. For example, the Commission concluded that two of the respondents would experience \xe2\x80\x9crate decreases of approximately 20 percent for retail service\xe2\x80\x9d during the period covered by the contracts. 103 FERC, at 62,397. But the baseline for that computation was the rate they were paying before the contracts went into effect. That disparity is certainly a relevant consideration; but so is the disparity between the contract rate and the rates consumers would have paid (but for the contracts) further down the line, when the open market was no longer dysfunctional. That disparity, past a certain point, could amount to an \xe2\x80\x9cexcessive burden.\xe2\x80\x9d That is what was contemplated by Sierra, which involved a challenge 5 years into a 15-year contract. The \xe2\x80\x9cexcessive burden\xe2\x80\x9d on other customers to which the opinion referred was assuredly the current burden, and not only the burden imposed at the very outset of the contract. See 350 U. S., at 355. The \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocal public necessity\xe2\x80\x9d that justifies overriding the Mobile-Sierra presumption does not disappear as a factor once the contract enters into force. Thus, FERC\xe2\x80\x99s analysis on this point was flawed\xe2\x80\x94or at least incomplete. As the Ninth Circuit put it, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is entirely possible that rates had increased so high during the energy crises because of dysfunction in the spot market that, even with the acknowledged decrease in rates, consumers still paid more under the forward contracts than they otherwise would have.\xe2\x80\x9d 471 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1090. If that is so, and if that increase is so great that, even taking into account the desirability of fostering market-stabilizing long-term contracts, the rates impose an excessive burden on consumers or otherwise seriously harm the public interest, the rates must be disallowed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, respondents alleged before FERC that some of the petitioners in these cases had engaged in market manipulation in the spot market. See, e.g., 105 FERC, at 61,989 (\xe2\x80\x9cSnohomish and Nevada Companies argue that their contracts were the product of market manipulation by Enron, Morgan Stanley and other Respondents, which, as established by the Commission Staff, engaged in market manipulation\xe2\x80\x9d). The Staff Report concluded, as we have said, that the abnormally high prices in the spot market during the energy crisis influenced the terms of contracts in the forward market. But the Commission dismissed the relevance of the Staff Report on the ground that it had not demonstrated that forward market prices were so high as to overcome the Mobile-Sierra presumption. We conclude, however, that if it is clear that one party to a contract engaged in such extensive unlawful market manipulation as to alter the playing field for contract negotiations, the Commission should not presume that the contract is just and reasonable. Like fraud and duress, unlawful market activity that directly affects contract negotiations eliminates the premise on which the Mobile-Sierra presumption rests: that the contract rates are the product of fair, arms-length negotiations. The mere fact that the unlawful activity occurred in a different (but related) market does not automatically establish that it had no effect upon the contract\xe2\x80\x94especially given the Staff Report\xe2\x80\x99s (unsurprising) finding that high prices in the one market produced high prices in the other. We are unable to determine from the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s orders whether it found the evidence inadequate to support the claim that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 alleged unlawful activities affected the contracts at issue here. It said in its order on rehearing, 105 FERC, at 61,989, that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e \xe2\x80\xa6 found no evidence to support a finding of market manipulation [by respondents] that specifically affected the contracts at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d But perhaps that must be read in light of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s above described rejection of the Staff Report on the ground that high spot market prices caused by manipulation are irrelevant unless the forward market prices fail the Mobile-Sierra standard; and in light of the statement in its initial order, in apparent response to the claim of spot-market manipulation by respondents, 103 FERC, at 62,397, that \xe2\x80\x9ca finding that the unjust and unreasonable spot market prices caused forward bilateral prices to be unjust and unreasonable would be relevant to contract modification only where there is a \xe2\x80\x98just and reasonable\xe2\x80\x99 standard of review.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We emphasize that the mere fact of a party\xe2\x80\x99s engaging in unlawful activity in the spot market does not deprive its forward contracts of the benefit of the Mobile-Sierra presumption. There is no reason why FERC should be able to abrogate a contract on these grounds without finding a causal connection between unlawful activity and the contract rate. Where, however, causality has been established, the Mobile-Sierra presumption should not apply.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On remand, the Commission should amplify or clarify its findings on these two points. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed, and the cases are remanded for proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The basic question presented by these complicated cases is whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC or Commission) must presume that the rate set out in a freely negotiated wholesale-energy contract meets the \xe2\x80\x98just and reasonable\xe2\x80\x99 requirement imposed by law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1. The opening sentence of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion tells us that the \xe2\x80\x9cMobile-Sierra doctrine\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a term that makes its first appearance in the United States Reports today\xe2\x80\x94mandates an affirmative answer. This holding finds no support in either case that lends its name to the doctrine. Nevertheless, in the interest of guarding against \xe2\x80\x9cdisfigurement of the venerable Mobile-Sierra doctrine,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 19, the Court mangles both the governing statute and precedent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the Federal Power Act (FPA), 41 Stat. 1063, 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7791a et seq., wholesale electricity prices are established in the first instance by public utilities, either via tariffs or in contracts with purchasers. \xc2\xa7824d(c). Whether set by tariff or contract, all rates must be filed with the Commission. See ibid.\xc2\xa0 Section 205(a) of the FPA provides, \xe2\x80\x9cAll rates and charges . . . shall be just and reasonable, and any such rate or charge that is not just and reasonable is hereby declared to be unlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7824d(a). Pursuant to \xc2\xa7206(a), if FERC determines \xe2\x80\x9cthat any rate . . . or that any rule, regulation, practice, or contract affect[ing] such rate . . . is unjust [or] unreasonable . . . , the Commission shall determine the just and reasonable rate, . . . rule, regulation, practice, or contract to be thereafter observed and in force, and shall fix the same by order.\xe2\x80\x9d 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7824e(a). These provisions distinguish between the rate-setting roles of utilities (which initially set rates) and the Commission (which may override utility-set rates that are not just and reasonable), but they do not distinguish between rates set unilaterally by tariff and rates set bilaterally by contract. However the utility sets its prices, the standard of review is the same\xe2\x80\x94rates must be just and reasonable.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court purports to acknowledge that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is only one statutory standard for assessing wholesale electricity rates, whether set by contract or tariff\xe2\x80\x94the just-and-reasonable standard.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. Unlike rates set by tariff, however, the Court holds that any \xe2\x80\x9cfreely negotiated\xe2\x80\x9d contract rate is presumptively just and reasonable unless it \xe2\x80\x9cseriously harms\xe2\x80\x9d the public interest. Ante, at 1. According to the Court, this presumption represents a \xe2\x80\x9cdiffering application of [the] just-and-reasonable standard,\xe2\x80\x9d but not a different standard altogether. Ante, at 6. I disagree. There is no significant difference between requiring a heightened showing to overcome an otherwise conclusive presumption and imposing a heightened standard of review. I agree that applying a separate standard of review to contract rates is \xe2\x80\x9cobviously indefensible,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., but that is also true with respect to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s presumption.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if the \xe2\x80\x9cMobile-Sierra presumption\xe2\x80\x9d were not tantamount to a separate standard, nothing in the statute mandates \xe2\x80\x9cdiffering application\xe2\x80\x9d of the statutory standard to rates set by contract. Ibid. Section 206(a) of the FPA provides, \xe2\x80\x9cwithout qualification or exception,\xe2\x80\x9d that FERC may replace any unjust or unreasonable contract with a lawful contract. Permian Basin Area Rate Cases, 390 U. S. 747, 783\xe2\x80\x93784 (1968) (construing identical language in the Natural Gas Act, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7717d(a)). The statute does not say anything about a mandatory presumption for contracts, much less define the burden of proof for overcoming it or delineate the circumstances for its nonapplication. Cf. ante, at 1, 19. Nor does the statute prohibit FERC from considering marginal cost when reviewing rates set by contract. Cf. ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9322, and n.\xc2\xa05.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If Congress had intended to impose such detailed constraints on the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s authority to review contract rates, it would have done so itself in the FPA. Congress instead used the general words \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable\xe2\x80\x9d because it wanted to give FERC, not the courts, wide latitude in setting policy. As we explained in Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837, 843\xe2\x80\x93844 (1984):

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The power of an administrative agency to administer a congressionally created . . . program necessarily requires the formulation of policy and the making of rules to fill any gap left, implicitly or explicitly, by Congress.\xe2\x80\x99 Morton v. Ruiz, 415 U. S. 199, 231 (1974). If Congress has explicitly left a gap for the agency to fill, there is an express delegation of authority to the agency to elucidate a specific provision of the statute by regulation. Such legislative regulations are given controlling weight unless they are arbitrary, capricious, or manifestly contrary to the statute. Sometimes the legislative delegation to an agency on a particular question is implicit rather than explicit. In such a case, a court may not substitute its own construction of a statutory provision for a reasonable interpretation made by the administrator of an agency.\xe2\x80\x9d (Footnote omitted.)

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consistent with this understanding of administrative law, our cases interpreting the FPA have invariably \xe2\x80\x9cemphasized that courts are without authority to set aside any rate adopted by the Commission which is within a \xe2\x80\x98zone of reasonableness.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Permian Basin, 390 U. S., at 797. But see ante, at 19 (asserting that \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98zone of reasonableness\xe2\x80\x99 test . . . fails to accord an adequate level of protection to contracts\xe2\x80\x9d). This deference makes eminent sense because \xe2\x80\x9crate-making agencies are not bound to the service of any single regulatory formula; they are permitted, unless their statutory authority otherwise plainly indicates, \xe2\x80\x98to make the pragmatic adjustments which may be called for by particular circumstances.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Permian Basin, 390 U. S., at 776\xe2\x80\x93777. Despite paying lipservice to this principle, see ante, at 3, the Court binds the Commission to a rigid formula of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own making.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Having found no statutory text that supports its vision of the Mobile-Sierra doctrine, the Court invokes the \xe2\x80\x9cimportant role of contracts in the FPA.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 22. But contracts play an \xe2\x80\x9cimportant role\xe2\x80\x9d in the FPA only insofar as the statute \xe2\x80\x9cdeparted from the scheme of purely tariff-based regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Verizon Communications Inc. v. FCC, 535 U. S. 467, 479 (2002). In allowing parties to establish rates by contract, Congress did not intend to immunize such rates from just-and-reasonable review. Both United Gas Pipe Line Co. v. Mobile Gas Service Corp., 350 U. S. 332 (1956), and FPC v. Sierra Pacific Power Co., 350 U. S. 348 (1956), the supposed progenitors of the \xe2\x80\x9cMobile-Sierra presumption,\xe2\x80\x9d make this point in no uncertain terms. See Sierra, 350 U. S., at 353 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Commission has undoubted power under \xc2\xa7206(a) to prescribe a change in contract rates whenever it determines such rates to be unlawful\xe2\x80\x9d); Mobile, 350 U. S., at 344 (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ontracts remain fully subject to the paramount power of the Commission to modify them when necessary in the public interest\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 1] Accordingly, the fact that the FPA tolerates contracts does not make it subservient to contracts.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither of the eponymous cases in the \xe2\x80\x9cMobile-Sierra presumption,\xe2\x80\x9d nor any of our subsequent decisions, substantiates the Court\xe2\x80\x99s atextual reading of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7205 and 206.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court acknowledges, Mobile itself says nothing about what standard of review applies to rates established by contract. See ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. Rather, Mobile merely held that utilities cannot unilaterally abrogate contracts with purchasers by filing new rate schedules with the Commission. See 350 U. S., at 339\xe2\x80\x93341. The Court neglects to mention, however, that although Mobile had no occasion to comment on the standard of review, it did imply that Congress would not have permitted parties to establish rates by contract but for \xe2\x80\x9cthe protection of the public interest being afforded by supervision of the individual contracts, which to that end must be filed with the Commission and made public.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 339.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Sierra, a public utility entered into a long-term contract to sell electricity \xe2\x80\x9cat a special low rate\xe2\x80\x9d in order to forestall potential competition. See 350 U. S., at 351\xe2\x80\x93352. Several years later the utility complained that the rate provided too little profit and was therefore not \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d The Commission agreed and set aside the rate \xe2\x80\x9csolely because it yield[ed] less than a fair return on the net invested capital.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 354\xe2\x80\x93355. The Court vacated and remanded on the ground that the Commission had applied an erroneous standard. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hile it may be that the Commission may not normally impose upon a public utility a rate which would produce less than a fair return,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court reasoned, \xe2\x80\x9cit does not follow that the public utility may not itself agree by contract to a rate affording less than a fair return or that, if it does so, it is entitled to be relieved of its improvident bargain.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 355. When the seller has agreed to a rate that it later challenges as too low, \xe2\x80\x9cthe sole concern of the Commission would seem to be whether the rate is so low as to adversely affect the public interest\xe2\x80\x94as where it might impair the financial ability of the public utility to continue its service, cast upon other consumers an excessive burden, or be unduly discriminatory.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court further elaborated on what it meant by the \xe2\x80\x9cpublic interest\xe2\x80\x9d:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThat the purpose of the power given the Commission by \xc2\xa7206(a) is the protection of the public interest, as distinguished from the private interests of the utilities, is evidenced by the recital in \xc2\xa7201 of the Act that the scheme of regulation imposed \xe2\x80\x98is necessary in the public interest.\xe2\x80\x99 When \xc2\xa7206(a) is read in the light of this purpose, it is clear that a contract may not be said to be either \xe2\x80\x98unjust\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98unreasonable\xe2\x80\x99 simply because it is unprofitable to the public utility.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sierra therefore held that, in accordance with the statement of policy in the FPA, 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7824(a), whether a rate is \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable\xe2\x80\x9d is measured against the public interest, not the private interests of regulated sellers. Contrary to the opinion of the Court, see ante, at 23, n.\xc2\xa06, Sierra instructs that the public interest is the touchstone for just-and-reasonable review of all rates, not just contract rates. Sierra drew a distinction between the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s authority to impose low rates on utilities and its authority to abrogate low rates agreed to by utilities because these actions impact the public interest differently, not because the public interest governs rates set bilaterally but not rates set unilaterally. When the Commission imposes rates that afford less than a fair return, it compromises the public\xe2\x80\x99s interest in attracting necessary capital. The impact is different, however, if a utility has agreed to a low rate because investors recognize that the utility, not the regulator, is responsible for the unattractive rate of return.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sierra used \xe2\x80\x9cpublic interest\xe2\x80\x9d as shorthand for the interest of consumers in paying \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the lowest possible reasonable rate consistent with the maintenance of adequate service in the public interest.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Permian Basin, 390 U. S., at 793 (quoting Atlantic Refining Co. v. Public Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of N. Y., 360 U. S. 378, 388 (1959)). Whereas high rates directly implicate this interest, low rates do so only indirectly, such as when the rate is so low that it \xe2\x80\x9cmight impair the financial ability of the public utility to continue its service, cast upon other consumers an excessive burden, or be unduly discriminatory.\xe2\x80\x9d Sierra, 350 U. S., at 355. Nothing in Sierra purports to mandate a \xe2\x80\x9cserious harm\xe2\x80\x9d standard of review, or to require any assumption that high rates and low rates impose symmetric burdens on the public interest. Cf. ante, at 19\xe2\x80\x9320. As we later explained in FPC v. Texaco Inc., 417 U. S. 380, 399 (1974), the Commission cannot ignore even \xe2\x80\x9ca small dent in the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s pocket\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cthe Act makes unlawful all rates which are not just and reasonable, and does not say a little unlawfulness is permitted.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Brushing aside the text of the FPA, as well as the holdings in Mobile and Sierra themselves, the Court cherry picks language from Verizon, Arkla, and Permian Basin. Both Verizon and Arkla mentioned the Mobile-Sierra line of cases only in passing, and neither case had anything to do with just-and-reasonable review of rates. See Verizon, 535 U. S., at 479; Arkla, 453 U. S. 571, 582 (1981). Furthermore, the statement in Permian Basin about \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocal public necessity,\xe2\x80\x9d 390 U. S., at 822, speaks to the difficulty of establishing injury to the public interest in the context of a low-rate challenge, not a high-rate challenge.[Footnote 2] The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on these few stray sentences calls to mind our admonishment in Permian Basin: \xe2\x80\x9cThe Commission\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of its regulatory authority must be assessed in light of its purposes and consequences, and not by references to isolated phrases from previous cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 791, n. 60.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lacking any grounding in the FPA or precedent, the Court concludes, as a matter of policy, that the Mobile-Sierra presumption is necessary to ensure stability in volatile energy markets and to reduce regulatory costs. See ante, at 22\xe2\x80\x9323. Of course, \xe2\x80\x9cthe desirability of fostering market-stabilizing long-term contracts,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 25, plays into the public interest insofar as the \xe2\x80\x9cCommission\xe2\x80\x99s responsibilities include the protection of future, as well as present, consumer interests,\xe2\x80\x9d Permian Basin, 390 U. S., at 798; see also United Gas Pipe Line Co. v. Memphis Light, Gas and Water Div., 358 U. S. 103, 113 (1958) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt seems plain that Congress . . . was not only expressing its conviction that the public interest requires the protection of consumers from excessive prices for natural gas, but was also manifesting its concern for the legitimate interests of natural gas companies in whose financial stability the gas-consuming public has a vital stake\xe2\x80\x9d). But under the FPA, Congress has charged FERC, not the courts, with balancing the short-term and long-term interests of consumers. See Permian Basin, 390 U. S., at 792 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe court\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility is not to supplant the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s balance of these interests with one more nearly to its liking, but instead to assure itself that the Commission has given reasoned consideration to each of the pertinent factors\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, not even FERC has the authority to endorse the rule announced by the Court today. The FPA does not indulge, much less require, a \xe2\x80\x9cpractically insurmountable\xe2\x80\x9d presumption, see Papago Tribal Util. Auth. v. FERC, 723 F.\xc2\xa02d 950, 954 (CADC 1983) (opinion for the court by Scalia, J.), that all rates set by contract comport with the public interest and are therefore just and reasonable. Congress enacted the FPA precisely because it concluded that regulation was necessary to protect consumers from deficient markets. It follows, then, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Commission lacks the authority to place exclusive reliance on market prices.\xe2\x80\x9d Texaco, 417 U. S., at 400; see also id., at 399 (\xe2\x80\x9cIn subjecting producers to regulation because of anticompetitive conditions in the industry, Congress could not have assumed that \xe2\x80\x98just and reasonable\xe2\x80\x99 rates could conclusively be determined by reference to market price\xe2\x80\x9d). For this reason, we have already rejected the policy rationale proffered by the Court today:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIt may be, as some economists have persuasively argued, that the assumptions of the 1930\xe2\x80\x99s about the competitive structure of the natural gas industry, if true then, are no longer true today. It may also be that control of prices in this industry, in a time of shortage, if such there be, is counterproductive to the interests of the consumer in increasing the production of natural gas. It is not the Court\xe2\x80\x99s role, however, to overturn congressional assumptions embedded into the framework of regulation established by the Act. This is a proper task for the Legislature where the public interest may be considered from the multifaceted points of view of the representational process.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 400 (footnote omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Balancing the short-term and long-term interests of consumers entails difficult judgment calls, and to the extent FERC actually engages in this balancing, its reasoned determination is entitled to deference. But FERC cannot abdicate its statutory responsibility to ensure just and reasonable rates through the expedient of a heavy-handed presumption. This is not to say that the Commission should abrogate any contract that increases rates, but to underscore that the agency is \xe2\x80\x9cobliged at each step of its regulatory process to assess the requirements of the broad public interests entrusted to its protection by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Permian Basin, 390 U. S., at 791.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if, as the Court holds today, the \xe2\x80\x9cMobile-Sierra presumption\xe2\x80\x9d is merely a \xe2\x80\x9cdiffering application\xe2\x80\x9d of the statutory just-and-reasonable standard, FERC\xe2\x80\x99s orders must be set aside because they were not decided on this basis.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The FERC orders repeatedly aver that the agency is applying a \xe2\x80\x9cpublic interest\xe2\x80\x9d standard different from and distinctly more demanding than the statutory standard. See, e.g., App. 1198a (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he burden of showing that a contract is contrary to the public interest is a higher burden than showing that a contract is not just and reasonable. . . . The fact that a contract may be found to be unjust and unreasonable under [\xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7205 and 206] does not in and of itself demonstrate that the contract is contrary to the public interest under the Supreme Court cases\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s misunderstanding of our cases is so egregious that the sellers, concerned that the orders would be overturned, asked the Commission for \xe2\x80\x9cclarification that the public interest standard of review does not authorize unjust and unreasonable rates.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1506a, 1567a. FERC clarified as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f rates . . . become unjust and unreasonable and the contract at issue is subject to the Mobile-Sierra standard of review, the Commission under court precedent may not change the contract simply because it is no longer just and reasonable. If parties\xe2\x80\x99 market-based rate contracts provide for the public interest standard of review, the Commission is bound to a higher burden to support modification of such contracts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1506a, 1567a.

\n

\nWhereas in Texaco we faulted the Commission for failing to \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly mention the just-and-reasonable standard,\xe2\x80\x9d 417 U. S., at 396, in these cases FERC refused outright to apply that standard.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to misrepresenting FERC\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the Mobile-Sierra doctrine as a presumption rather than a separate standard, the Court overstates the extent to which FERC considered the lawfulness of the rates. The Court recognizes, as it must, that the three factors identified in Sierra are neither exclusive nor \xe2\x80\x9cprecisely applicable to the high-rate challenge of a purchaser.\xe2\x80\x9d See ante, at 20; Brief for Respondent FERC 41\xe2\x80\x9342. Although FERC applied what it termed the \xe2\x80\x9cSierra Three-Prong Test,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 1276a, the Court contends the agency did not err because it also evaluated the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98totality of the circumstances,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d see ante, at 20. But FERC\xe2\x80\x99s totality-of-the-circumstances review was infected by its misapprehension of the standard \xe2\x80\x9cdictated by the U. S. Supreme Court under the Mobile-Sierra doctrine.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 1229a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whereas the focus of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7205(a) and 206(a) is on the reasonableness of the rates charged, not the conduct of the contracting parties, FERC restricted its review to the contracting parties\xe2\x80\x99 behavior around the time of formation. See id., at 1280a\xe2\x80\x931284a. FERC seems to have thought it was powerless to conduct just-and-reasonable review unless the contract was already subject to abrogation based on contract defenses such as fraud or duress. By including contracts within the scope of \xc2\xa7206(a), however, Congress must have concluded that contract defenses are insufficient to protect the public interest. But see ante, at 19 (holding that the \xe2\x80\x9cMobile-Sierra presumption\xe2\x80\x9d applies in all circumstances absent \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional grounds for \xe2\x80\xa6 abrogation\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cillegal action\xe2\x80\x9d by a contracting party).[Footnote 4] Indeed, nothing in the FPA or this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases precludes FERC from considering circumstances exogenous to contract negotiations, including natural disasters and market manipulation by entities not parties to the challenged contract.[Footnote 5] FERC\xe2\x80\x99s error is obvious from the face of the orders, which repeatedly state the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s belief that it could not consider evidence relevant to the reasonableness of the contract rates.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the Court and the Commission attempt to recast FERC\xe2\x80\x99s orders as applying the statutory standard, see ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314; Brief for Respondent FERC 21, under the doctrine set forth in SEC v. Chenery Corp., 318 U. S. 80 (1943), \xe2\x80\x9cwe cannot accept appellate counsel\xe2\x80\x99s post hoc rationalizations for agency action; for an agency\xe2\x80\x99s order must be upheld, if at all, on the same basis articulated in the order by the agency itself,\xe2\x80\x9d Texaco, 417 U. S., at 397 (internal quotation marks omitted). Furthermore, even assuming FERC subjectively believed that it was applying the just-and-reasonable standard despite its repeated declarations to the contrary, each order must be deemed \xe2\x80\x9cso ambiguous that it falls short of that standard of clarity that administrative orders must exhibit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 395\xe2\x80\x93396.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In order to get around the Chenery doctrine, the Court not only mischaracterizes FERC\xe2\x80\x99s orders, but also takes a more radical tack: It concludes that whatever the rationale set forth in FERC\xe2\x80\x99s orders, Chenery does not apply because \xe2\x80\x9cthe Commission was required, under our decision in Sierra, to apply the Mobile-Sierra presumption in its evaluation of the contracts here.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. This point prompts the Court to comment that \xe2\x80\x9cFERC has lucked out.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. If the Commission has \xe2\x80\x9clucked out,\xe2\x80\x9d it is not only a purely fortuitous victory, but also a Pyrrhic one. Although FERC prevails in these cases despite having \xe2\x80\x9coffered a justification in court different from what it provided in its opinion,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., it has paid a tremendous price. The Court has curtailed the agency\xe2\x80\x99s authority to interpret the terms \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable\xe2\x80\x9d and thereby substantially narrowed FERC\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to protect the public interest by the means it thinks best. Contrary to congressional intent, FERC no longer has the flexibility to adjust its review of contractual rates to account for changing conditions in the energy markets or among consumers. Cf. Permian Basin, 390 U. S., at 784 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]dministrative authorities must be permitted, consistently with the obligations of due process, to adapt their rules and policies to the demands of changing circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The decision of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit deserves praise for its efforts to bring the freewheeling Mobile-Sierra doctrine back in line with the FPA and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases. I cannot endorse the opinion in its entirety, however, because it verges into the same sort of improper policymaking that I have criticized in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. Both decisions would hobble the Commission, albeit from different sides. Congress has not authorized courts to prescribe energy policy by imposing presumptions or prerequisites, or by making marginal cost the sole concern or no concern at all. I would therefore vacate and remand the cases in order to give the Commission an opportunity to evaluate the contract rates in light of a proper understanding of its discretion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I respectfully dissent.
