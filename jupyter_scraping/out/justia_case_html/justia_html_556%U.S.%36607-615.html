MINISTRY OF DEFENSE AND SUPPORT FOR THE ARMED FORCES OF THE ISLAMIC REPUBLIC OF IRAN v. ELAHI

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93615.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 12, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 21, 2009

\n

In 1997, the International Court of Arbitration awarded petitioner Iranian Ministry of Defense (hereinafter Iran) $2.8 million to settle a dispute with Cubic Defense Systems, Inc., a California company, over a 1977 contract that would have provided Iran with an air combat training system. When Cubic refused to pay, Iran sued in the Federal District Court in San Diego, which ordered Cubic to pay the award plus interest (Cubic Judgment). In 2000, respondent Elahi sued Iran in the D.C. Federal District Court, claiming that Iranian agents had murdered his brother. He obtained a default judgment of about $312 million and sought to collect some of the money by attaching the Cubic Judgment. Iran opposed the lien under the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 (FSIA). The California District Court denied Iran\xe2\x80\x99s immunity claim, and the Ninth Circuit affirmed, finding an exception to sovereign immunity. This Court vacated and remanded. Ministry of Defense and Support for Armed Forces of Islamic Republic of Iran v. Elahi, 546 U. S. 450.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On remand, the Ninth Circuit found that a different immunity exception applied, citing the Terrorism Risk Insurance Act of 2002 (TRIA), which permitted holders of terrorism-related judgments against Iran to attach \xe2\x80\x9cblocked\xe2\x80\x9d Iranian assets. The United States had blocked Iranian assets following the Iranian hostage crisis in 1979, and the court held that the asset Elahi sought to attach had remained blocked notwithstanding the unblocking orders issued after the crisis was resolved by the Algiers Accords in 1981. The court reasoned that those unblocking orders had omitted military goods such as the training system underlying the Cubic Judgment. The court further rejected Iran\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Elahi had waived his right of attachment, and concluded that he could attach the Cubic Judgment.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The asset in question was not \xe2\x80\x9cblocked\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Contrary to that court\xe2\x80\x99s holding, the relevant asset is not Iran\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the air combat training system, but, rather, a judgment enforcing an arbitration award based upon Cubic\xe2\x80\x99s failure to account to Iran for its share of the proceeds of the system\xe2\x80\x99s eventual sale to Canada. And neither the Cubic Judgment nor the sale proceeds it represents were blocked assets at the time of the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 2007 decision. In a 1981 order, the Treasury Department unblocked transactions involving property in which Iran\xe2\x80\x99s interest arose after January 19, 1981. Iran\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the Cubic Judgment itself arose on December 7, 1998, when the District Court confirmed the arbitration award. And Iran\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the property underlying the judgment arose, as the arbitrators ruled, when Cubic completed its sale of the air combat system in October 1982. Thus, whether Iran\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in property\xe2\x80\x9d is considered to be its interest in the Cubic Judgment itself or its underlying interest in the sale proceeds, the interest falls within the terms of the Treasury Department\xe2\x80\x99s general unblocking order. Even assuming (as the Ninth Circuit held) that the relevant asset was Iran\xe2\x80\x99s pre-1981 interest in the training system itself, that asset still was not \xe2\x80\x9cblocked\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the decision below. Such an interest would fall directly within the scope of Executive Order No. 12281, which required that property owned by Iran be transferred \xe2\x80\x9cas directed \xe2\x80\xa6 by the Government of Iran.\xe2\x80\x9d No authority supports the contrary conclusion. Pp. 8\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Elahi cannot attach the Cubic Judgment because he has waived his right to do so. Section 2002 of the Victims of Trafficking and Violence Protection Act of 2000 (VPA) offers compensation to individuals holding terrorism-related judgments against Iran. It requires those receiving payment to relinquish \xe2\x80\x9call rights to \xe2\x80\xa6 attach property that is at issue in claims against the United States before an international tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72002(a)(2)(D). In 2003, the U. S. Government paid Elahi $2.3 million under the VPA as partial compensation for his judgment against Iran, and he signed a waiver form that mirrors the statutory language. A review of the record in Iran-U. S. Claims Tribunal Case No. B61 demonstrates that the Cubic Judgment falls within the terms of Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s waiver. Iran filed that case in 1982, claiming that between 1979 and 1981 the United States had wrongly barred the transfer of the Cubic training system and other military equipment to Iran. Iran asked the Tribunal to order the United States, among other things, to pay Iran damages. The United States answered that the Tribunal should set off the $2.8 million represented by the Cubic Judgment against any award. Iran argued that the Tribunal should not set off the $2.8 million insofar as third parties have attached the judgment. In the terms of Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s waiver, therefore, the Cubic Judgment is \xe2\x80\x9cproperty,\xe2\x80\x9d and Case No. B61 itself is a \xe2\x80\x9cclai[m] against the United States before an international tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d And there remains a significant dispute about whether the Cubic Judgment can be used by the Tribunal as a setoff, placing the Judgment \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d in Case No. B61. Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s arguments to the contrary are unavailing. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0Given Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s waiver, this Court need not decide whether the Cubic Judgment was blocked by new Executive Branch actions following the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision. P.\xc2\xa020.

\n

495 F.\xc2\xa03d 1024, reversed.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[April 21, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dariush Elahi, the respondent, sued Iran, claiming that Iran unlawfully participated in the assassination of his brother, and he obtained a default judgment of about $312 million. Seeking to collect some of the money, he has tried to attach an asset belonging to Iran, namely a $2.8 million judgment that Iran obtained against a California company called Cubic Defense Systems, Inc. (Cubic Judgment). Iran has asserted a defense of sovereign immunity in order to prevent the attachment. See Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71610.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since Iran is a sovereign nation, Elahi cannot attach the Cubic Judgment unless he finds an exception to the principle of sovereign immunity that would allow him to do so. See Ministry of Defense and Support for Armed Forces of Islamic Republic of Iran v. Elahi, 546 U. S. 450 (2006) (per curiam). As the case reaches us, the Terrorism Risk Insurance Act of 2002 (TRIA), \xc2\xa7201(a), 116 Stat. 2337, note following 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71610, provides the sole possible exception. That Act authorizes holders of terrorism-related judgments against Iran, such as Elahi, to attach Iranian assets that the United States has \xe2\x80\x9cblocked.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). And we initially decide whether Iran\xe2\x80\x99s Cubic Judgment is a \xe2\x80\x9cblocked asset\xe2\x80\x9d within the terms of that Act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if the Cubic Judgment is a blocked asset, however, Elahi still cannot attach it if he waived his right to do so. And we next decide whether Elahi waived that right when, in return for partial compensation from the Government, he agreed not to attach \xe2\x80\x9cproperty that is at issue in claims against the United States before an international tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d Victims of Trafficking and Violence Protection Act of 2000 (VPA), \xc2\xa72002(d)(5)(B), as added by TRIA \xc2\xa7201(c)(4), 116 Stat. 2339, note following 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71610 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We ultimately hold that the Cubic Judgment was not a \xe2\x80\x9cblocked asset\xe2\x80\x9d at the time the Court of Appeals handed down its decision in this case. We recognize that since that time new Executive Branch action may have \xe2\x80\x9cblocked\xe2\x80\x9d that asset; but, in light of the posture of the case, we do not decide whether it has done so. Rather, we determine that Elahi cannot attach the Cubic Judgment regardless, for the Judgment is \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d in a claim against the United States before the Iran-U. S. Claims Tribunal. The Judgment consequently falls within the terms of Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s waiver.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We initially set forth key background elements, including in this section the events necessary to understand the \xe2\x80\x9cblocked asset\xe2\x80\x9d question, while leaving for Part III, infra, additional background matters related to Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s waiver.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Cubic Judgment arose out of a 1977 contract between Cubic Defense Systems, a California company and Iran\xe2\x80\x99s Ministry of Defense. (We shall refer to the Ministry, for present purposes an inseparable part of the Iranian State, as \xe2\x80\x9cIran.\xe2\x80\x9d See Ministry of Defense and Support for Armed Forces of Islamic Republic of Iran v. Cubic Defense Systems, Inc., 495 F.\xc2\xa03d 1024, 1035\xe2\x80\x931036 (CA9 2007)). Cubic there promised to supply Iran with certain military goods, namely an air combat training system, for which Iran promised to pay approximately $18 million dollars. In 1979, after Iran had paid some of the money but before Cubic had sent the training system, the Iranian Revolution broke out, militants in Iran seized American hostages, and President Carter \xe2\x80\x9cblocked all property and interests in property of the Government of Iran \xe2\x80\xa6 subject to the jurisdiction of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Exec. Order No. 12170, 3 CFR 457 (1979 Comp.) (emphasis added), promulgated pursuant to the authority of International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA), 50 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71701\xe2\x80\x931702 (2000 ed. and Supp. V); 31 CFR \xc2\xa7535.201 (1980).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0About a year later, on January 19, 1981, Iran and the United States settled the crisis, in part with an agreement called the \xe2\x80\x9cAlgiers Accords.\xe2\x80\x9d 20 I. L. M. 224. Under the Accords, the United States agreed to \xe2\x80\x9crestore the financial position of Iran, in so far as possible, to that which existed prior to November 14, 1979,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and (with some exceptions) to \xe2\x80\x9carrange, subject to the provisions of U. S. law applicable prior to November 14, 1979, for the transfer to Iran of all Iranian properties,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 227. The President then lifted the legal prohibitions against transactions involving Iranian property. See Exec. Orders Nos. 12277\xe2\x80\x9312282, 3 CFR 105\xe2\x80\x93113 (1981 Comp.); 31 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7535.211\xe2\x80\x93535.215 (1981). In doing so, he ordered the transfer to Iran of Iranian financial assets and most other Iranian property \xe2\x80\x9cas directed \xe2\x80\xa6 by the Government of Iran,\xe2\x80\x9d Exec. Order No. 12281, 3 CFR 112 (1981 Comp.). Shortly thereafter, the Treasury Department issued a general license authorizing \xe2\x80\x9c[t]ransactions involving property in which Iran . . . has an interest\xe2\x80\x9d where \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he property comes within the jurisdiction of the United States \xe2\x80\xa6 after January 19, 1981, or \xe2\x80\xa6 [t]he interest in the property . . . arises after January 19, 1981.\xe2\x80\x9d 31 CFR \xc2\xa7535.579(a).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Algiers Accords also set up an international arbitration tribunal, the Iran-U. S. Claims Tribunal (or Tribunal), to resolve disputes between the two nations concerning each other\xe2\x80\x99s performance under the Algiers Accords. The Tribunal would also resolve disputes concerning contracts and agreements between the two nations that were outstanding on January 19, 1981. 20 I. L. M., at 230\xe2\x80\x93231. The Tribunal\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction included claims by nationals of one state against the other state, but it did not include claims by one state against nationals of the other state. Id., at 231\xe2\x80\x93232.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In January 1982, Iran filed two Cubic-based claims in the Tribunal. In Case No. B61, Iran claimed that between 1979 and 1981 the United States had wrongly barred the transfer of certain military equipment, including the Cubic air combat training system, to Iran. Iran asked the Tribunal to order the United States either to issue an export license for the equipment or to pay Iran damages. App. to Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 22a, 24a, 31a.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Case No. B66, Iran claimed that Cubic had breached its contract to deliver the training system partly because the United States had taken actions contrary to the Algiers Accords. Again Iran asked the Tribunal to order either the issuance of an export license for the equipment or the payment of damages. Id., at 1a, 2a, 9a\xe2\x80\x9310a. In April 1987 the Tribunal dismissed this second case (No. B66) on the grounds that the Iran-Cubic contract imposed no obligations on the United States and that the Tribunal lacked jurisdiction to consider a suit by a state (Iran) against a private party (Cubic). Ministry of Nat. Defense of Islamic Republic of Iran v. United States, 14 Iran-U. S.\xc2\xa0Cl. Trib. Rep. 276, 277\xe2\x80\x93278.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Iran, believing that Cubic had breached its contract, then went to arbitration before the arbitration tribunal specified in the Cubic contract, namely the International Court of Arbitration of the International Chamber of Commerce. Iran asked that arbitration tribunal to award it restitution and damages.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In May 1997 the arbitrators issued their decision. The arbitrators found that prior to the Iranian Revolution, prior to the hostage crisis, and prior to the blocking of any Iranian assets, (1) Iran and Cubic had themselves agreed that they would temporarily discontinue (but not terminate) the contract; and (2) Cubic had agreed to try to sell the training system to another buyer and to settle accounts with Iran later. The arbitrators further found that after the crisis (in September 1981) (3) Cubic successfully sold a modified version of the system to Canada. Ministry of Defense and Support for Armed Forces of Islamic Republic of Iran v. Cubic Int\xe2\x80\x99l Sales Corp., No. 7365/FMC (Int\xe2\x80\x99l Ct. of Arbitration of Int\xe2\x80\x99l Chamber of Commerce), pp. 32\xe2\x80\x9333, 36\xe2\x80\x9340, 50\xe2\x80\x9351, reprinted in 13 Mealey\xe2\x80\x99s Int\xe2\x80\x99l Arbitration Report pp. G\xe2\x80\x934, G\xe2\x80\x9315 to G\xe2\x80\x9318, G\xe2\x80\x9321 (Oct. 1998) (Arbitration Award). The arbitrators concluded that Cubic had not lived up to this modified agreement. And, after taking account of the advance payments that Iran had made to Cubic, the funds that Cubic had spent, the amount that Canada had paid Cubic, and various other items, they awarded Iran $2.8 million plus interest. Id., \xc2\xb6C.18.3(a), at G\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Cubic refused to pay Iran this money. Iran then sued in the Federal District Court for the Southern District of California to enforce the arbitration award. The District Court confirmed the award and entered a final judgment ordering Cubic to pay $2.8 million plus interest to Iran. That judgment is the Cubic Judgment. Ministry of Defense and Support for Armed Forces of Islamic Republic of Iran v. Cubic Defense Systems, Inc., 29 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1168, 1174 (1998) (final judgment entered Aug. 10, 1999).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In February 2000 Elahi brought a tort action against Iran in the Federal District Court for the District of Columbia. Elahi claimed that Iranian agents had murdered his brother. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71605(a)(7) (2000 ed.) (lifting sovereign immunity of state sponsors of certain kinds of terrorism) (subsequently replaced by National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2008, \xc2\xa71083(a)(1), 122 Stat. 338, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa71605A); Foreign Operations, Export Financing, and Related Programs Appropriations Act, 1997, \xc2\xa7589, 110 Stat. 3009\xe2\x80\x93172, note following 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71605 (providing tort cause of action). Iran did not answer the complaint. The District Court found Iran in default, and it awarded Elahi nearly $12 million in compensatory damages and $300 million in punitive damages. Elahi v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 124 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 97 (DC 2000).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 2001 Elahi filed a notice of lien against Iran\xe2\x80\x99s Cubic Judgment. He thereby sought to satisfy from the Cubic Judgment a portion of what Iran owed him under his own default judgment against Iran. Iran opposed the lien. It argued that the Cubic Judgment, as property of the sovereign state of Iran, was immune from attachment or execution. The District Court denied immunity. Ministry of Defense and Support for Armed Forces of Islamic Republic of Iran v. Cubic Defense Systems, Inc., 236 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1140, 1152 (SD Cal. 2002).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals affirmed the denial. Ministry of Defense and Support for Armed Forces of Islamic Republic of Iran v. Cubic Defense Systems, Inc., 385 F.\xc2\xa03d 1206 (CA9 2004). The Court of Appeals thought that the Ministry of Defense of Iran had lost its immunity from attachment because of a special statutory exception that permits a creditor to attach the property of an \xe2\x80\x9cagency or instrumentality of a foreign state engaged in commercial activity in the United States\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94where the creditor seeks the property to satisfy a terrorism-related judgment. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71610(b). See 385 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1219\xe2\x80\x931222. But, on review here, we pointed out (in a per curiam opinion) that the sovereign immunity exception upon which the Ninth Circuit had relied\xe2\x80\x94the exception for the property of an entity that has \xe2\x80\x9cengaged in commercial activity,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71610(b)(2)\xe2\x80\x94applies only to property of an \xe2\x80\x9cagency or instrumentality\xe2\x80\x9d of a foreign state. It does not apply to property of an entity that itself is an inseparable part of the foreign state. \xc2\xa71610(a). Elahi, 546 U. S., at 452\xe2\x80\x93453.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We remanded the case, and on remand, the Ninth Circuit held that the Ministry of Defense fell into the latter category (an inseparable part of the state of Iran), not the former (an \xe2\x80\x9cagency or instrumentality\xe2\x80\x9d of Iran). 495 F.\xc2\xa03d 1024, 1035\xe2\x80\x931036 (2007). Hence Elahi could not take advantage of the \xe2\x80\x9cengaged in commercial activities\xe2\x80\x9d exception. The Court of Appeals also found inapplicable a slightly different exception applicable to \xe2\x80\x9cproperty \xe2\x80\xa6 of a foreign state \xe2\x80\xa6 used for a commercial activity in the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71610(a). 495 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1036\xe2\x80\x931037.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nonetheless the Court of Appeals found yet another exception that it believed denied Iran its sovereign immunity defense. The court pointed out that in 2002 Congress had enacted the TRIA. That Act permitted a person with a terrorism-related judgment to attach an asset of the responsible \xe2\x80\x9cterrorist\xe2\x80\x9d state to satisfy the judgment, \xe2\x80\x9c[n]otwithstanding any other provision of law,\xe2\x80\x9d provided that the asset was a \xe2\x80\x9cblocked asset.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7201(a), 116 Stat. 2337. The Court of Appeals noted that the Cubic Judgment arose out of a pre-1981 contract with Iran involving an air combat training system for Iran, and that President Carter had blocked virtually all Iranian assets following the Iranian hostage crisis. See Exec. Order No. 12170, 44 Fed. Reg. 65729 (\xe2\x80\x9cblock[ing] all property and interests in property of the Government of Iran \xe2\x80\xa6 subject to the jurisdiction of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d), promulgated pursuant to the authority of International Emergency Economic Powers Act, 50 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71701\xe2\x80\x931702 (2000 ed. and Supp. V); 31 CFR \xc2\xa7535.201. The Court of Appeals then held that the President had never unblocked the asset in question. 495 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1033. In its view, the many unblocking orders that were issued after the 1981 Algiers Accords, see, e.g., Exec. Orders Nos. 12277\xe2\x80\x9312282, 3 CFR 105\xe2\x80\x93113; 31 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7535.211\xe2\x80\x93535.215, 535.579(a), did not apply because those unblocking orders omitted \xe2\x80\x9cmilitary goods such as the [training system that underlay the Cubic Judgment].\xe2\x80\x9d 495 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1033.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals also rejected Iran\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Elahi had waived his right to attach the Cubic Judgment regardless (a matter to which we shall turn in Part III). And the court concluded that Elahi was free to attach the Judgment. Id., at 1037.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Iran, with the support of the Department of State, asked us to grant certiorari. We did so, and we shall consider both aspects of the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 determination.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn first to the question whether the Cubic Judgment was a \xe2\x80\x9cblocked asset.\xe2\x80\x9d The Ninth Circuit held that the asset in question consisted of Iran\xe2\x80\x99s interest in military goods, namely an air combat training system, which it believed the Executive Branch had failed to unblock after the Iranian hostage crisis ended. None of the parties here, however, supports the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s determination. And neither do we.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The basic reason we cannot accept the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s rationale is that we do not believe Cubic\xe2\x80\x99s air combat training system is the asset here in question. Elahi does not seek to attach that system. Cubic sent the system itself to Canada, where, as far as we know, it remains. Rather, Elahi seeks to attach a judgment enforcing an arbitration award based upon Cubic\xe2\x80\x99s failure to account to Iran for Iran\xe2\x80\x99s share of the proceeds of that system\xe2\x80\x99s sale. And neither the Cubic Judgment nor the sale proceeds that it represents were blocked assets at the time the Court of Appeals issued its decision.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1981, the Treasury Department issued an order that authorized \xe2\x80\x9c[t]ransactions involving property in which Iran . . . has an interest\xe2\x80\x9d where \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he interest in the property . . . arises after January 19, 1981.\xe2\x80\x9d 31 CFR \xc2\xa7535.579(a)(1) (emphasis added). As the Court of Appeals itself pointed out, Iran\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the Cubic Judgment arose \xe2\x80\x9con December 7, 1998, when the district court confirmed the [arbitration] award.\xe2\x80\x9d 385 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1224. Since it arose more than 17 years \xe2\x80\x9cafter January 19, 1981,\xe2\x80\x9d the Cubic Judgment falls within the terms of Treasury\xe2\x80\x99s order. And that fact, in our view, is sufficient to treat the Judgment as unblocked.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Iran\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the property that underlies the Cubic Judgment also arose after January 19, 1981. As the International Court of Arbitration held, Cubic and Iran entered into their initial contract before 1981. But they later agreed to discontinue (but not to terminate) the contract. Arbitration Award G\xe2\x80\x9315, G\xe2\x80\x9321. They agreed that Cubic would try to sell the system elsewhere. Id., \xc2\xb6C.9.15, at G\xe2\x80\x9314. And they further agreed that they would take \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decisions\xe2\x80\x9d about who owed what to whom \xe2\x80\x9conly . . . once the result of Cubic\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to resell the System\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cknown.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xb6B.10.7, at G\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Cubic completed its sale of the system (to Canada) in October 1982. Id., \xc2\xb6B.12.14, at G\xe2\x80\x9322. And the arbitrators referred to October 1982 as \xe2\x80\x9cthe date the Parties had in mind when they agreed to await the outcome of Cubic\xe2\x80\x99s resale attempts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Only then was Cubic \xe2\x80\x9cin a position to reasonably, comprehensively and precisely account for the reuse of components originally manufactured for Iran and for any modification costs.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. For those reasons, and in light of the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 findings, we must conclude that October 1982 is the time when Iran\xe2\x80\x99s claim to proceeds arose.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The upshot is that, whether we consider Iran\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in property\xe2\x80\x9d as its interest in the Cubic Judgment itself or its underlying interest in the proceeds of the Canadian sale, the interest falls within the terms of the Treasury Department\xe2\x80\x99s general license authorizing \xe2\x80\x9c[t]ransactions involving property in which Iran . . . has an interest\xe2\x80\x9d where \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he interest in the property . . . arises after January 19, 1981.\xe2\x80\x9d 31 CFR \xc2\xa7535.579(a). And, as we said, that fact is sufficient for present purposes to treat the asset as having been unblocked at the time the Ninth Circuit issued the decision below.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, even if we were to assume (as the Ninth Circuit held) that the relevant asset were Iran\xe2\x80\x99s pre-1981 interest in the air combat training system itself, we should still conclude that that asset was not \xe2\x80\x9cblocked\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the decision below. As the Government points out, such an interest falls directly within the scope of Executive Order No. 12281, an unblocking order that required property owned by Iran to be transferred \xe2\x80\x9cas directed \xe2\x80\xa6 by the Government of Iran.\xe2\x80\x9d See also 31 CFR \xc2\xa7535.215(a). None of the four authorities upon which the Ninth Circuit relied indicates the contrary conclusion. First, the Circuit cited the Arms Export Control Act, 82 Stat. 1321, 22 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72751 et seq., and its implementing regulations, a statute and regulations which regulate arms shipments. It is true that, notwithstanding Executive Order No. 12281, the export of certain military equipment remained subject to regulation under other statutes, including the Arms Export Control Act. See 31 CFR \xc2\xa7535.215(c). But that fact does not show that military equipment remained blocked under IEEPA. The Court of Appeals next cited the 1979 Executive Order freezing Iranian assets, Exec. Order No. 12170, 3 CFR 457 (Comp. 1980)\xe2\x80\x94but it failed to consider the effect of the subsequent unblocking order just discussed. The Court of Appeals also relied on a 2005 Presidential notice extending the national emergency with respect to Iran, 70 Fed. Reg. 69039, but that notice did not impose any additional restrictions on Iranian assets. Finally, the Court of Appeals pointed to a Treasury Department guidance document, which states that \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ertain assets\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94consisting \xe2\x80\x9cmainly of military and dual-use property\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94 \xe2\x80\x9crelated to \xe2\x80\xa6 claims\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cU. S. nationals \xe2\x80\xa6 against Iran or Iranian entities\xe2\x80\x9d still being litigated in the Tribunal \xe2\x80\x9cremain blocked in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Office of Foreign Assets Control, Dept. of Treasury, Foreign Assets Control Regulations for Exporters and Importers 23 (2007). But the training system does not fall into the category of assets identified by the guidance document. The system neither \xe2\x80\x9cremain[s] \xe2\x80\xa6 in the United States\xe2\x80\x9d (having been sent to Canada), nor was it related to claims by \xe2\x80\x9cU. S. nationals \xe2\x80\xa6 against Iran or Iranian entities\xe2\x80\x9d before the Tribunal. In sum, no authority supports the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that an Iranian interest in the training system itself would be a \xe2\x80\x9cblocked asset.\xe2\x80\x9d And none of the parties defend the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion here.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the Cubic Judgment was not a blocked asset at the time the Court of Appeals reached its decision, the Government believes that it is a blocked asset now. In 2005 the President issued a new Executive Order that blocks assets held by proliferators of weapons of mass destruction. Exec. Order No. 13382, 3 CFR 170 (2005 Comp.). And in 2007, after the Court of Appeals issued its decision, the State Department designated certain component parts of Iran\xe2\x80\x99s Ministry of Defense as entities whose property and interests in property are blocked under Executive Order No. 13382. See 72 Fed. Reg. 71991\xe2\x80\x9371992. If the Iranian entity to which the Cubic Judgment belongs falls within the terms of the State Department\xe2\x80\x99s designation, then presumably that asset is blocked at this time.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The problem for the Government, however, is that Iran does not agree that the relevant parts of its Ministry of Defense fall within the scope of the State Department\xe2\x80\x99s designation. Thus the matter is in dispute. The lower courts have not considered that dispute. The relevant arguments have not been set forth in detail here. And in such circumstances we normally would remand the case, permitting the lower courts to decide the issue in the first instance. See, e.g., F. Hoffmann\xe2\x80\x93La Roche Ltd v. Empagran S.\xc2\xa0A., 542 U. S. 155, 175 (2004). Consequently, we shall not decide whether the new Executive Branch actions have blocked the Cubic Judgment. Instead, we turn to the \xe2\x80\x9cwaiver\xe2\x80\x9d question. And our answer (that Elahi has waived his right to attach the Cubic Judgment) makes it unnecessary to remand the blocking question for further consideration.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As we have just said, the second question concerns Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of his right to attach the Cubic Judgment. In 2000, Congress enacted a statute that offers some compensation to certain individuals, including Elahi, who hold terrorism-related judgments against Iran. VPA \xc2\xa72002, as amended by TRIA \xc2\xa7201(c). The Act requires those who receive that compensation to relinquish \xe2\x80\x9call rights to execute against or attach property that is at issue in claims against the United States before an international tribunal, [or] that is the subject of awards rendered by such tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72002(a)(2)(D), 114 Stat. 1542; see also \xc2\xa72002(d)(5)(B), as added by TRIA \xc2\xa7201(c)(4) (cross-referencing \xc2\xa72002(a)(2)(D)). In 2003 the Government paid Elahi $2.3 million under the Act as partial compensation for his judgment against Iran. Brief for Respondent 9. And at that time, Elahi signed a waiver form that mirrors the statutory language. App. to Pet. for Cert. 30 (citing 68 Fed. Reg. 8077, 8081 (2003)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question is whether the Cubic Judgment \xe2\x80\x9cis at issue in claims\xe2\x80\x9d against the United States before an \xe2\x80\x9cinternational tribunal,\xe2\x80\x9d namely the Iran-U. S. Claims Tribunal. If so, the Cubic Judgment falls within the terms of Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s waiver. The Court of Appeals believed the Judgment was not \xe2\x80\x9cat issue.\xe2\x80\x9d 495 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1030\xe2\x80\x931031. But we find to the contrary.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A review of the record in Iran-U. S. Claims Tribunal Case No. B61 leads us to conclude that the Cubic Judgment is \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d before that Tribunal. In Case No. B61 Iran argued that, between 1979 and 1981, the United States had wrongly prevented the transfer of Cubic\xe2\x80\x99s air combat training system to Iran. Iran asked the Tribunal, among other things, to order the United States to pay damages. Statement of Claim, Islamic Republic of Iran v. United States (filed Jan. 19, 1982), App. to Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 22a, 24a, 31a. In its briefing before the Tribunal, Iran acknowledged that any amount it recovered from Cubic would \xe2\x80\x9cbe recuperated from the remedy sought\xe2\x80\x9d against the United States. App. 76, n.\xc2\xa02. And Iran sent a letter to the United States in which it said that any amounts it actually received from Cubic would be \xe2\x80\x9crecouped from the remedy sought against the United States in Case B61.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 84a. But Iran added that the Cubic Judgment could not be used as a setoff insofar as it had been attached by creditors. Id., at 85a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Meanwhile, in a rebuttal brief before the Tribunal, the United States, while arguing that in fact it owed Iran nothing, added that at the very least Iran must set off the amount \xe2\x80\x9calready \xe2\x80\xa6 awarded\xe2\x80\x9d by the International Court of Arbitration (namely, the $2.8 million awarded to Iran from Cubic) against any money awarded by the Tribunal. Id., at 52a, 80a\xe2\x80\x9381a, and n. 32. And the United States\xe2\x80\x99 demand for a setoff applies even if third parties have attached the Cubic Judgment. See Tr. of Tribunal Hearing, in No. B61 (Iran-U. S. Cl. Trib., Dec. 7 and 12, 2006), App. to Brief for Respondent 37, 38\xe2\x80\x9339, 41, 42.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The upshot is a dispute about the Cubic Judgment. The United States argues (and argued before the Tribunal) that the Tribunal should set off the $2.8 million that the Cubic Judgment represents against any award that the Tribunal may make against the United States in Case No. B61. Iran argues (and argued before the Tribunal) that the Tribunal should not set off the $2.8 million insofar as third parties have attached the Judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To put the matter in terms of the language of Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s waiver, one can say for certain that the Cubic Judgment is \xe2\x80\x9cproperty.\xe2\x80\x9d And Case No. B61 itself is a \xe2\x80\x9cclai[m] against the United States before an international tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d We can also be reasonably certain that how the Tribunal should use that property is also under dispute or in question in that claim. Moreover, since several parties other than Elahi have already attached the Cubic Judgment, see Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 20, the question whether an attached claim can be used as a setoff is potentially significant, irrespective of Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s own efforts to attach the judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Are these circumstances sufficient to place the Cubic Judgment \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d in Case No. B61? Elahi argues not. He points out that the Cubic Judgment does not appear on a list of property contained in Iran\xe2\x80\x99s statement of claim in Case No. B61; nor is it the subject of any other claim before the Tribunal. Indeed, Iran and the United States do not dispute the Cubic Judgment\xe2\x80\x99s validity; they do not dispute the Cubic Judgment\xe2\x80\x99s ownership; and they do not dispute the fact that the United States\xe2\x80\x99 asset freeze had no adverse effect on the Cubic Judgment or on Iran\xe2\x80\x99s entitlement to the Cubic Judgment. As the dissent correctly points out, the Judgment is not \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d in any of these senses. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Judgment will neither be suspended nor modified by the Tribunal in Case No. B61, nor is the Judgment property claimed by Iran from the United States in that case, see post, at 2\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But that does not end the matter. The question is whether, for purposes of the VPA, a judgment can nevertheless be \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d before the Tribunal even when it will not be suspended or modified by the Tribunal and when it is not claimed by Iran from the United States. Here, a significant dispute about the Cubic Judgment still remains, namely a dispute about whether it can be used by the Tribunal as a setoff. And in our view, that dispute is sufficient to put the Judgment \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d in the case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For one thing, we do not doubt that the setoff matter is \xe2\x80\x9cunder dispute\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cin question\xe2\x80\x9d in Case No. B61, and those words typically define the term \xe2\x80\x9cat issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 136 (8th ed. 2004). In the event that the Tribunal finds the United States liable in Case No. B61, the total sum awarded to Iran by the Tribunal will depend on whether the Judgment is used as a setoff. And whether the Judgment can be so used depends, in turn, on whether the United States is right that an attached judgment should be set off or whether Iran is right that it should not be\xe2\x80\x94a matter in question before the Tribunal. In that sense, the Judgment is \xe2\x80\x9cunder dispute.\xe2\x80\x9d We recognize that the dispute is over the use of the Judgment, not the validity of the Judgment. But we do not see how that fact matters.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For another thing, ordinary legal disputes can easily encompass questions of setoff. Suppose Smith sues a carrier for wrongfully harming a shipment of goods. The question of liability, the question of damages, and the question of reducing damages through setoff may all be at issue in the case. Which is the more important issue in a particular case depends not upon the category (liability, damages, or setoff) but upon the circumstances of that particular case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Further, the language of the statute suggests that Congress meant the words \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d to carry the ordinary meaning just described. Elahi essentially distinguishes between property that is the subject of a claim (a claim, for example, that the United States took or harmed particular property belonging to Iran) and property that might otherwise affect a Tribunal judgment (say, through its use as a setoff). And he argues that the statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d covers only the first kind of dispute, not the second. But the statute does not limit the property that is \xe2\x80\x9cat issue in a claim\xe2\x80\x9d to property that is the subject of a claim. To the contrary, the statute says that judgment creditors such as Elahi must

\n

\xe2\x80\x9crelinquis[h] all rights to execute against or attach property [1] that is at issue in claims against the United States before an international tribunal [or] [2] that is the subject of awards rendered by such tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d VPA \xc2\xa72002(a)(2)(D), 114 Stat. 1542 (emphasis added); see also \xc2\xa72002(d)(5)(B), as added by TRIA \xc2\xa7201(c)(4) (cross-referencing \xc2\xa72002(a)(2)(D)).

\n

\nHad Congress wanted to limit the property to which it first refers (namely, property that is \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d in a claim) to property that is the subject of a claim, it seems likely that Congress straightforwardly would have used the words \xe2\x80\x9csubject of\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94words that appear later (in respect to awards rendered) in the very same sentence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose leans in the direction of a broader interpretation of the words \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d than that proposed by Elahi. Pointing to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history, Elahi says that the statute seeks to enable victims of terrorism to collect on judgments they have won against terrorist parties. See Brief of Respondent 6\xe2\x80\x937, 31 (citing H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep. No. 107\xe2\x80\x93779 (2002); 148 Cong. Rec. 23119, 23121\xe2\x80\x9323123 (2002) (statement of Sen. Harkin)). He is such a victim, and, he says, Congress would have intended an interpretation that favors his cause. But Congress had a more complicated set of purposes in mind. The statute authorizes the attachment of blocked assets, and it provides partial compensation to victims to be paid (in part) from general Treasury funds. But it does so in exchange for a right of subrogation, VPA \xc2\xa72002(c), and for the victim\xe2\x80\x99s promise not to pursue the balance of the judgment by attaching property \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d in a claim against the United States before the Tribunal. VPA \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72002(a)(2)(D), (d)(5)(B), as added by TRIA \xc2\xa7201(c)(4). The statute thereby protects property that the United States might use to satisfy its potential liability to Iran.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Cubic Judgment falls into this category. It is property that the United States could use to satisfy its potential liability to Iran, but which may be unavailable for that purpose if successfully attached. With respect to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s revenue-saving purpose, it is difficult to distinguish between property that is the subject of a claim before a tribunal and property that is in dispute before the tribunal in respect to its use as an offset.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent adds that the \xe2\x80\x9cbetter reading\xe2\x80\x9d of the words \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d is one that limits them to the \xe2\x80\x9cfoster[ing] [of] compliance with the Government\xe2\x80\x99s international obligations.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6. We agree with this statement, but we do not see how it adds anything but new phraseology to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s basic claim, namely that arguments before the Tribunal about \xe2\x80\x9csetoffs\xe2\x80\x9d do not count as \xe2\x80\x9cissues.\xe2\x80\x9d To repeat our own view of the matter, a dispute about whether one country must pay the other country more money because it cannot use particular property (because of an attachment) to satisfy an obligation raises an issue that the Tribunal must resolve, no less and no more than other issues that might be before the Tribunal in that case or other cases.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, post, at 8, there is no unfairness in our holding. Elahi could have chosen to forgo the Government\xe2\x80\x99s compensation scheme, and he then could have attached the Cubic Judgment, as have other terrorist victims with judgments against Iran. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 20. But that course carried risks: Iran had challenged Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s notice of lien and it was uncertain whether Elahi would prevail. In 2003, while litigation over his notice of lien was pending, Elahi chose to participate in the Government\xe2\x80\x99s scheme. He thereby received the benefit of immediate, guaranteed partial compensation from the Government\xe2\x80\x94in exchange for a promise not to interfere with property that the United States might need to satisfy potential liability to Iran. Having received $2.3 million in Government funds, there is nothing unfair about holding Elahi to the terms of his bargain.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Elahi makes several other arguments. He points to language in the TRIA (the statute authorizing attachment of blocked assets) which says: \xe2\x80\x9c[n]otwithstanding any other provision of law\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cblocked assets\xe2\x80\x9d of a state \xe2\x80\x9cshall be subject to . . . attachment in aid of execution\xe2\x80\x9d of a terrorism-related judgment. \xc2\xa7201(a), 116 Stat. 2337 (emphasis added). He also points to VPA \xc2\xa72002(d)(4), as added by TRIA \xc2\xa7201(c)(4), 116 Stat. 2339, which reads: \xe2\x80\x9c[N]othing in this subsection [which contains the relinquishment provision] shall bar \xe2\x80\xa6 enforcement of any\xe2\x80\x9d terrorism-related \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment . . . against assets otherwise available under this section or under any other provision of law\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added). The first provision, Elahi argues, permits him to attach blocked assets notwithstanding the VPA\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that he relinquish his right to attach property \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d before an international tribunal; and that conclusion, he says, is reinforced by VPA \xc2\xa72002(d)(4). Our interpretation, he adds, would \xe2\x80\x9cbar \xe2\x80\xa6 enforcement\xe2\x80\x9d of a terrorism-related judgment \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise available\xe2\x80\x9d under TRIA \xc2\xa7201(a)\xe2\x80\x94contrary to the statutory language just quoted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But VPA \xc2\xa72002(d)(5) requires Elahi, in exchange for having received partial compensation, to relinquish \xe2\x80\x9call rights\xe2\x80\x9d to attach property \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d in an international tribunal. VPA \xc2\xa72002(a)(2)(D), 114 Stat. 1542 (cross-referenced by \xc2\xa72002(d)(5)(B); emphasis added). And, as several courts of appeals have apparently assumed, the relinquishment of \xe2\x80\x9call rights\xe2\x80\x9d includes the right given by TRIA \xc2\xa7201(a) to attach blocked assets. See Hegna v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 376 F.\xc2\xa03d 226, 232 (CA4 2004); Hegna v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 380 F.\xc2\xa03d 1000, 1009 (CA7 2004); Hegna v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 402 F.\xc2\xa03d 97, 99 (CA2 2005) (per curiam).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, the relinquishment provision that applies to Elahi was added to the VPA by the very same statute, the TRIA, that permitted the attachment of blocked assets, and which contains the \xe2\x80\x9cnotwithstanding\xe2\x80\x9d clause upon which Elahi relies. \xc2\xa7201(a) (blocked assets); \xc2\xa7201(c) (amending VPA). Congress could not have intended the words to which Elahi refers to narrow so dramatically an important provision that it inserted in the same statute. And for those who, like Elahi, argue that the legislative history supports his reading of the statute, we point out that the history suggests that Congress placed the \xe2\x80\x9cnotwithstanding\xe2\x80\x9d clause in \xc2\xa7201(a) for totally different reasons, namely to eliminate the effect of any Presidential waiver issued under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71610(f) prior to the date of the TRIA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment. H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep. No. 107\xe2\x80\x93779, at 27.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Elahi makes three final arguments, first that setoff is not \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d because the United States has argued in Case No. B61 that it has no liability at all, second that set-off is not \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d because the United States has not formally asserted a setoff before the Tribunal, and third that the Government violated his due process rights by inadequately informing that his waiver would deprive him of his right to attach the Cubic Judgment. We find none of these arguments convincing and shall briefly indicate our reasons in summary form.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As to the first, the United States argued setoff in the alternative, thereby placing it, in the alternative, \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d before the Tribunal. As to the second, Elahi at most points to a ground for disputing the propriety, under Tribunal rules, for granting a setoff; he does not deny that the Tribunal sometimes can do so, see, e.g., Futura Trading Inc. v. National Iranian Oil Co., 13 Iran-U. S.\xc2\xa0Cl. Trib. Rep. 99, 115\xe2\x80\x93116, \xc2\xb662 (1986) (preventing collection on a claim because the claimant had already collected the sum at issue from a different party). Hence whether the Tribunal can provide for a setoff here is a matter for the Tribunal to decide, and until it does decide, one way or the other, the matter is \xe2\x80\x9cat issue.\xe2\x80\x9d As to the third, we can find nothing that shows Elahi was unfairly surprised by the scope of his waiver\xe2\x80\x94certainly not to the point of violating any Due Process rights. See, e.g., 14 Iran-U. S. Cl. Trib. Rep., at 278, \xc2\xb610 (dismissal of Iran\xe2\x80\x99s claim against Cubic was \xe2\x80\x9cwithout prejudice to any findings it may make concerning [the Cubic contract] in Case No. B61\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude: The Cubic Judgment was not blocked at the time the Court of Appeals reached its decision. We do not decide whether more recent Executive Branch actions would block the Judgment at present. Regardless, Elahi has waived his right to attach the Judgment. We reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join Parts I and II of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion but, with all respect, dissent from Parts III and IV. As to Parts I and II, the Court is correct, in my view, to hold that the Cubic Judgment was not a \xe2\x80\x9cblocked asset\xe2\x80\x9d when the Court of Appeals reached its decision. As to Parts III and IV, however, respondent Dariush Elahi has not relinquished his right to attach the Cubic Judgment. By holding otherwise, the Court departs from the plain meaning and the purpose of the statutes Congress enacted to compensate Elahi and other victims of terrorism.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The statutory phrase to be interpreted is \xe2\x80\x9cproperty that is at issue in claims against the United States before an international tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d Victims of Trafficking and Violence Protection Act of 2000 (VTVPA), \xc2\xa72002(d)(s), as added by Terrorism Risk Insurance Act of 2002 (TRIA), \xc2\xa7201(c)(4), 116 Stat. 2339, note following 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71610. The context, of course, is Case No. B61\xe2\x80\x94a suit by Iran against the United States that is pending before the Iran-U. S. Claims Tribunal. The word \xe2\x80\x9cproperty,\xe2\x80\x9d as used in the statutory phrase, surely can refer both to tangible property, such as real estate or valuables in a safe-deposit box, and to intangible property interests, such as a claim, a cause of action or, as in this case, a judgment rendered by a United States district court. Still, it must be acknowledged that the term \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d is neither precise nor much illuminated by its operation in cases or other statutes. The absence of any clear authority on this point makes it imperative to adopt an interpretation that accords with familiar and well-settled principles of law. In this case those principles are the rules designed to give full and proper respect to final judgments rendered by courts of competent jurisdiction.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To determine whether the Cubic Judgment is \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d in Case No. B61, the primary consideration must be whether the Claims Tribunal, in the exercise of its own authority and jurisdiction, can affect the ownership, disposition, or control of the property the judgment comprises. Here the property in question is a judgment rendered by the United States District Court for the Southern District of California. As all acknowledge, that court had jurisdiction over the subject and the persons then before it. And, as is further conceded, that court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment is valid and has binding force on Cubic Defense Systems, Inc., the nongovernmental party before that court. See Ministry of Defense and Support for Armed Forces of Islamic Republic of Iran v. Cubic Defense Systems, Inc., 29 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1168, 1170 (1998). Neither party to Case No. B61 questions the judgment or requests the Claims Tribunal to interpret it\xe2\x80\x94much less to alter, enforce or invalidate it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if one of the parties were to ask the Claims Tribunal to modify the Cubic Judgment, the Tribunal would simply lack power to do so. The judgment arises out of Iran\xe2\x80\x99s contractual dispute with Cubic, an American company, and the Tribunal has no \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction over claims by Iran against United States nationals.\xe2\x80\x9d Ministry of Nat. Defence of Islamic Republic of Iran v. United States, 14 Iran-U. S. Cl. Trib. Rep. 276, 278 (1987) (Case No. B66). Iran tried to sue Cubic in the Claims Tribunal 20 years ago, but the Tribunal dismissed that suit for lack of jurisdiction. Ibid. In these circumstances the Cubic Judgment is simply an extrinsic fact beyond the Claims Tribunal\xe2\x80\x99s power to affect. True, the Tribunal, when it enters its own orders, might or might not give credit to the United States for a payment, or a right to payment, arising out of the Cubic Judgment; but that does not put the judgment itself at issue.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if the Court\xe2\x80\x99s broad reading of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d were correct, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion would still be wrong because the relinquishment provision is limited to property that is at issue \xe2\x80\x9cin claims against the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d And the Cubic Judgment is not part of the claims Iran makes in Case No. B61, as both Iran and the United States have made clear in their submissions to the Claims Tribunal. To put the countries\xe2\x80\x99 filings in context, a brief review of both the Cubic Judgment and Case No. B61 is necessary.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Cubic Judgment is the result of a contract dispute between Iran and Cubic. In the late 1970\xe2\x80\x99s, Iran hired Cubic to build an air combat training system, and advanced some $12 million for the project. But Iran failed to make all the payments due. App. 43\xe2\x80\x9344. Thus rebuffed, Cubic sold the system to Canada and refused to refund any of Iran\xe2\x80\x99s advance payments. Iran brought an arbitration against Cubic. The panel of arbitrators, after ascertaining Cubic\xe2\x80\x99s costs of building the system, and after allowing the company a reasonable profit of $3.5 million, ordered Cubic to return to Iran $2.8 million of the $12 million advance. Iran brought this arbitration award to the U. S. District Court for the Southern District of California, which issued the judgment at issue here. The judgment orders Cubic to pay Iran $2.8 million. Cubic Defense Systems, supra, at 1171, 1174.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Case No. B61 is in essence a contract dispute between Iran and the United States. Iran accuses the United States of breaking its promise, made in the Algiers Accords, to \xe2\x80\x9carrange \xe2\x80\xa6 for the transfer to Iran of all Iranian properties\xe2\x80\x9d located in the United States on January 19, 1981. 20 I.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0M. 224, 227, \xc2\xb69 (1981). One of the properties Iran claims is Cubic\xe2\x80\x99s air combat training system. See Statement of Claim in No. B61, (Iran-U. S. Cl. Trib.), App. to Brief for United States 22a, 24a, 31a. Both parties have confirmed, in their joint report describing all the \xe2\x80\x9cproperty claimed by Iran,\xe2\x80\x9d that Cubic\xe2\x80\x99s system is \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d in Iran\xe2\x80\x99s claims. Cover Letter to Final Joint Report (July 14, 1989), App. to Brief for Respondent 14.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But the Cubic Judgment, in contrast to Cubic\xe2\x80\x99s training system, is not part of Iran\xe2\x80\x99s claims in Case No. B61. Both countries made this clear in their submissions to the Tribunal. Their joint report does not list the Cubic Judgment among the properties \xe2\x80\x9cat issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Final Joint Report (July 14, 1989), App. to Brief for Respondent 15\xe2\x80\x9323. And, in a statement altogether consistent with that omission, Iran told the Tribunal that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0[t]he subject matter of [Case No. B61], at variance with the [arbitration] action [against Cubic], is the losses suffered by Iran as a result of the United States\xe2\x80\x99 non-export of Iranian properties.\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Iran\xe2\x80\x99s Statement 16, App. 73, 76. The United States agreed, stating that the \xe2\x80\x9conly \xe2\x80\x98property that\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 is properly at issue\xe2\x80\x9d in Case No. B61 is property that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98has already been made the subject of a claim\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by Iran against the United States. U. S. Rebuttal (Sept. 1, 2003), 1 Lodging p.\xc2\xa0L419 (emphasis deleted) (Sealed). The United States reaffirmed this position in oral argument before the Tribunal: \xe2\x80\x9cAny losses in relation to [the Iran-Cubic] contract are not recoverable against the United States and issues regarding losses under that contract do not belong before this Tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d Tribunal Hearing 124 (Dec. 12, 2006), App. to Brief for Respondent 42.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the Claims Tribunal lacks jurisdiction over the Cubic Judgment, and because that judgment is not part of Iran\xe2\x80\x99s claims against the United States in Case No. B61, the judgment is not \xe2\x80\x9cproperty that is at issue in claims against the United States\xe2\x80\x9d under the plain meaning of the TRIA\xe2\x80\x99s relinquishment provision. TRIA \xc2\xa7201(c)(4), 116 Stat. 2339 (amending VTVPA \xc2\xa72002(d)).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if the text of the relinquishment provision were somehow ambiguous\xe2\x80\x94and it is not\xe2\x80\x94then the purpose of the VTVPA and TRIA would tip the scales in Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s favor. The text and the evident purpose of those statutes demonstrate that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 primary purpose was to compensate the victims of terrorism, not to secure from those victims a relinquishment of their claims to property owned by entities found to have sponsored terrorism.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The text of the VTVPA, and of the amendments made to it by the TRIA, shows that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 primary purpose was to enable the victims of terrorism to execute on the assets of a state found to have sponsored or assisted in a terrorist act. In the first subsection of the TRIA concerning the attachment of state assets by victims of terrorism, Congress provided that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]otwithstanding any other provision of law \xe2\x80\xa6 in every case in which a person has obtained a judgment against a terrorist party on a claim based upon an act of terrorism \xe2\x80\xa6 the blocked assets of that terrorist party \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be subject to execution or attachment in aid of execution in order to satisfy such judgment\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d TRIA \xc2\xa7201(a), id., at 2337. The effect of this subsection is to ensure that other laws do not bar victims\xe2\x80\x99 efforts to enforce judgments against terrorist states. To like effect is another paragraph of the VTVPA concerning victims of Iranian terrorism. Entitled \xe2\x80\x9cStatutory Construction,\xe2\x80\x9d this paragraph reads: \xe2\x80\x9cNothing in this subsection shall bar, or require delay in, enforcement of any judgment to which this subsection applies under any procedure\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72002(d)(4), as added by TRIA \xc2\xa7201(c)(4), id., at 2339. Though neither provision refers in direct terms to the relinquishment provision, both provisions show Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to broaden, rather than limit, the rights of victims like Elahi to execute on property owned by state sponsors of terrorism. Yet the opinion issued by the Court today does just the opposite.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To contravene the statute\xe2\x80\x99s clear design, the Court surmises that Congress also had a \xe2\x80\x9cmore complicated\xe2\x80\x9d purpose, namely, to \xe2\x80\x9cprotec[t] property that the United States might use to satisfy its potential liability to Iran.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17. This imagined purpose, the Court says, requires us to read the relinquishment provision as broadly as possible so as to prevent victims of terrorism from attaching property. But the Court does not point to evidence of this putative purpose, aside from the text of the relinquishment provision itself\xe2\x80\x94a text which, as submitted above, the Court reads the wrong way.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The better reading of the relinquishment provision\xe2\x80\x94and one much more consistent with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 protective purpose\xe2\x80\x94is not as a \xe2\x80\x9crevenue-saving\xe2\x80\x9d device, ibid., but as a way to foster compliance with the Government\xe2\x80\x99s international obligations. If Iran has asked the Claims Tribunal to resolve the status of certain property, then Iran and the Tribunal may well take the position that the United States has a responsibility under the Algiers Accords to prevent U. S. nationals from executing against that property. That concern is not present in this case. The ownership of the Cubic Judgment is not disputed, and allowing Elahi to attach it will not affect Iran\xe2\x80\x99s right to obtain full recovery from the United States in Case No. B61. At most, the attachment might affect the right of the United States to use the judgment to offset its liability.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court purports to agree with this reading of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose. Ante, at 17. But that agreement is hard to square with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s insistence upon fulfilling what it sees as the statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crevenue-saving purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. If the Court did in fact believe that the \xe2\x80\x9c\xe2\x80\x98better reading\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x9d of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose, ibid., is to foster compliance with the United States\xe2\x80\x99 international obligations, then the Court would affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals. Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s attachment of the Cubic Judgment does not hinder the U. S. Government\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to comply with its obligations under the Algiers Accords. At Algiers, the United States agreed to \xe2\x80\x9carrange \xe2\x80\xa6 for the transfer to Iran of all Iranian properties\xe2\x80\x9d located in the United States. 20 I.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0M., at 227, \xc2\xb69. That is not an obligation to pay Iran money, as the Court seems to believe. See ante, at 17. It is instead an obligation to take specific action in regard to specific properties. These specific properties do not include the Cubic Judgment\xe2\x80\x94as the Court concedes. See ante, at 9 (holding that the Cubic Judgment was not blocked). Therefore, Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s attachment of the Cubic Judgment does not impede the United States\xe2\x80\x99 efforts to make good on its obligations under the Algiers Accords.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 To be sure, a judicial lien on one of the specific properties referenced by the Algiers Accords might make it difficult for the U. S. Government to comply with its obligations, under those Accords, to arrange for that property\xe2\x80\x99s transfer to Iran. By encouraging creditors such as Elahi to give up their liens on these specific properties that are subject to the Algiers Accords, the TRIA makes it easier for the Government to comply with its obligation to \xe2\x80\x9carrange \xe2\x80\xa6 for the transfer\xe2\x80\x9d of these properties to Iran. This purpose (fostering compliance with the United States\xe2\x80\x99 obligation under the Algiers Accords) is more in keeping with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text than is the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crevenue-saving\xe2\x80\x9d purpose. And this purpose\xe2\x80\x94that is, the purpose of enabling the United States to meet its obligations under the Algiers Accords\xe2\x80\x94is not in the least frustrated by permitting Elahi to attach the Cubic Judgment, a property that, as the Court concedes, is not subject to the Algiers Accords.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The facts of this case show the injustice of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation. The Court today puts an end to Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s decade-long quest to hold Iran to account for murdering his brother Cyrus. In 2000, Elahi won a wrongful-death lawsuit against Iran and was awarded some $6 million in compensatory damages. See Elahi v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 124 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 97 (DC). In April 2003, Elahi took what he must have considered a further step toward his goal when he accepted $2.3 million from the U. S. Government under the VTVPA.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After today\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, what once appeared Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s gain of $2.3 million now seems to be a loss of $500,000. By taking the VTVPA\xe2\x80\x99s $2.3 million, the Court holds, Elahi relinquished his right to the $2.8 million Cubic Judgment he had already attached. The practical effect of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling is to turn the purpose of the VTVPA on its head. Rather than further Elahi\xe2\x80\x99s effort to obtain compensation for the murder of his brother, the Act has instead set him back half a million dollars. For the reasons given above, this result was not what Congress intended when it passed the VTVPA.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress passed the Victims of Trafficking and Violence Protection Act and the Terrorism Risk Insurance Act to compensate victims of terrorism. Congress expressed this purpose both in the text of the principal provision interpreted here and in accompanying sections of the statute. By stripping Elahi of his right to attach the valid judgment against Cubic rendered by the District Court\xe2\x80\x94a judgment not before the Claims Tribunal in any sense\xe2\x80\x94the Court fails to give the statute its intended effect. These reasons explain my respectful dissent.
