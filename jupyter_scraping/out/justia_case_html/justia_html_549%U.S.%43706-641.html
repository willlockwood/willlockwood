\nPER CURIAM
\nLANCE V. COFFMAN
\n549 U. S. ____ (2007)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

KEITH LANCE et\xc2\xa0al. v. MIKE COFFMAN, COLORADO\nSECRETARY OF STATE

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for\nthe district of colorado

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x93641.\xe2\x80\x83Decided March 5, 2007

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Elections Clause of the United States Constitution provides that the \xe2\x80\x9cManner of holding Elections for Senators and Representatives, shall be prescribed in each State by the Legislature thereof; but the Congress may at any time by Law make or alter such Regulations, except as to the Places of chusing Senators.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. I, \xc2\xa74, cl.\xc2\xa01 (emphasis added). When Colorado legislators were unable to redraw congressional districts after the 2000 census to accommodate an additional Representative, a state court did it for them. See Beauprez v. Avalos, 42 P.\xc2\xa03d 642 (Colo. 2002) (en banc). The legislature was able to pass a redistricting plan in 2003, which Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s Governor signed into law. See Colo. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x931\xe2\x80\x93101.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s attorney general, however, filed an original action in the Colorado Supreme Court to enjoin Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s secretary of state from implementing this new plan, noting that Article V, \xc2\xa744, of the Colorado Constitution limits redistricting to once per census. The Colorado General Assembly intervened in the action to defend its plan. The Colorado Supreme Court granted the injunction, holding that \xe2\x80\x9cjudicially-created districts are just as binding and permanent as districts created by the General Assembly,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the court-drawn plan must remain in effect until the next decennial census. People ex rel. Salazar v. Davidson, 79 P.\xc2\xa03d 1221, 1231 (2003) (en banc), cert. denied, 541 U. S. 1093 (2004). The court further held that this result did not offend the Elections Clause of the United States Constitution. 79 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 1232.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Immediately after Salazar was decided, four Colorado citizens\xe2\x80\x94none of whom had participated in Salazar\xe2\x80\x94filed the instant action in Federal District Court. They argued that Article V, \xc2\xa744, of the Colorado Constitution, as interpreted by the Colorado Supreme Court, violates their rights under the Elections Clause.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court initially determined that it lacked jurisdiction to hear the suit in light of the Rooker-Feldman doctrine, but we vacated and remanded for further proceedings. Lance v. Dennis, 546 U. S. 459 (2006) (per curiam). On remand, the District Court held that the citizen-plaintiffs had standing to bring their Elections Clause challenge. Lance v. Dennis, 444 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1149, 1154\xe2\x80\x931155 (2006). The court went on, however, to hold that the suit was barred by issue preclusion because the plaintiffs \xe2\x80\x9cstand in privity with the Secretary of State and the General Assembly,\xe2\x80\x9d who were on the losing side in the Salazar litigation. 444 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1161. The concurring judge concluded that appellants lacked standing to sue in the first place. Id., at 1162 (Porfilio, J., concurring in result). Plaintiffs appeal once again.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Federal courts must determine that they have jurisdiction before proceeding to the merits. Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U. S. 83, 94\xe2\x80\x9395 (1998).* Article III of the Constitution limits the jurisdiction of federal courts to \xe2\x80\x9cCases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cControversies.\xe2\x80\x9d One component of the case-or-controversy requirement is standing, which requires a plaintiff to demonstrate the now-familiar elements of injury in fact, causation, and redressability. See Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U. S. 555, 560\xe2\x80\x93561 (1992). \xe2\x80\x9cWe have consistently held that a plaintiff raising only a generally available grievance about government\xe2\x80\x94claiming only harm to his and every citizen\xe2\x80\x99s interest in proper application of the Constitution and laws, and seeking relief that no more directly and tangibly benefits him than it does the public at large\xe2\x80\x94does not state an Article III case or controversy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 573\xe2\x80\x93574. See also DaimlerChrysler Corp. v. Cuno, 547 U. S. ___, ___ (2006) (slip op., at 8) (refusing to create an exception to the general prohibition on taxpayer standing for challenges to state tax or spending decisions, and observing that taxpayer standing has been rejected \xe2\x80\x9cbecause the alleged injury is not \xe2\x80\x98concrete and particularized,\xe2\x80\x99 but instead a grievance the taxpayer \xe2\x80\x98suffers in some indefinite way in common with people generally\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (citation omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our refusal to serve as a forum for generalized grievances has a lengthy pedigree. In Fairchild v. Hughes, 258 U. S. 126 (1922), for example, a citizen sued the Secretary of State and the Attorney General to challenge the procedures by which the Nineteenth Amendment was ratified. We dismissed the suit because it was \xe2\x80\x9cnot a case within the meaning of \xe2\x80\xa6 Article III.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 129. The plaintiff sought to assert \xe2\x80\x9conly the right, possessed by every citizen, to require that the Government be administered according to law and that the public moneys be not wasted.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cObviously,\xe2\x80\x9d we held, \xe2\x80\x9cthis general right does not entitle a private citizen to institute [a suit] in the federal courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 129\xe2\x80\x93130.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, in Ex parte L\xc3\xa9vitt, 302 U. S. 633 (1937) (per curiam), we dismissed a citizen suit claiming that Justice Black\xe2\x80\x99s appointment to this Court contravened the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Ineligibility Clause, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa76, cl.\xc2\xa02. We found that the petitioner had no interest in the suit \xe2\x80\x9cother than that of a citizen and a member of the bar of this Court.\xe2\x80\x9d 302 U. S., at 634. That was not enough. To have standing, we observed, a plaintiff must have more than \xe2\x80\x9ca general interest common to all members of the public.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. See also Frothingham v. Mellon, decided with Massachusetts v. Mellon, 262 U. S. 447, 488 (1923) (taxpayer standing cannot be predicated upon an injury the plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9csuffers in some indefinite way in common with people generally\xe2\x80\x9d). Cf. Tyler v. Judges of Court of Registration, 179 U. S. 405, 406 (1900) (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven in a proceeding which he prosecutes for the benefit of the public \xe2\x80\xa6 [the plaintiff] must generally aver an injury peculiar to himself, as distinguished from the great body of his fellow citizens\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 A pair of more recent cases further illustrates the point. In United States v. Richardson, 418 U. S. 166 (1974), a federal taxpayer challenged the Government\xe2\x80\x99s failure to disclose certain CIA expenditures as a violation of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Accounts Clause, which requires that \xe2\x80\x9ca regular Statement and Account of the Receipts and Expenditures of all public Money shall be published from time to time.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa79, cl.\xc2\xa07. Relying on L\xc3\xa9vitt, this Court dismissed the claim as a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneralized grievance\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cplainly undifferentiated and \xe2\x80\x98common to all members of the public.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Richardson, 418 U. S., at 176\xe2\x80\x93177. See also id., at 191 (Powell, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe power recognized in Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137 (1803), is a potent one. Its prudent use seems to me incompatible with unlimited notions of taxpayer and citizen standing\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The same day, in Schlesinger v. Reservists Comm. to Stop the War, 418 U. S. 208 (1974), we addressed standing to bring a challenge under the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Incompatibility Clause, which provides that \xe2\x80\x9cno Person holding any Office under the United States, shall be a Member of either House during his Continuance in Office.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa76, cl.\xc2\xa02. Citizen-taxpayers brought a lawsuit contending that Members of Congress who were also members of the military Reserves violated the Incompatibility Clause. This Court dismissed for lack of standing. It \xe2\x80\x9creaffirm[ed] L\xc3\xa9vitt in holding that standing to sue may not be predicated upon an interest of the kind alleged here which is held in common by all members of the public, because of the necessarily abstract nature of the injury all citizens share.\xe2\x80\x9d 418 U. S., at 220. Refusing to entertain generalized grievances ensures that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a real need to exercise the power of judicial review\xe2\x80\x9d in a particular case, and it helps guarantee that courts fashion remedies \xe2\x80\x9cno broader than required by the precise facts to which the court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling would be applied.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 221\xe2\x80\x93222. In short, it ensures that courts exercise power that is judicial in nature.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The instant case parallels Fairchild, L\xc3\xa9vitt, and their progeny. The plaintiffs here are four Colorado voters. Three days after the Colorado Supreme Court issued its decision in Salazar, they filed a complaint alleging that \xe2\x80\x9cArticle V, \xc2\xa744 of the Colorado Constitution, as interpreted in Salazar, violated [the Elections Clause] of the U. S. Constitution by depriving the state legislature of its responsibility to draw congressional districts.\xe2\x80\x9d Lance v. Davidson, 379 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1117, 1122 (2005). In light of the discussion above, the problem with this allegation should be obvious: The only injury plaintiffs allege is that the law\xe2\x80\x94specifically the Elections Clause\xe2\x80\x94has not been followed. This injury is precisely the kind of undifferentiated, generalized grievance about the conduct of government that we have refused to countenance in the past. It is quite different from the sorts of injuries alleged by plaintiffs in voting rights cases where we have found standing. See, e.g., Baker v. Carr, 369 U. S. 186, 207\xe2\x80\x93208 (1962). Because plaintiffs assert no particularized stake in the litigation, we hold that they lack standing to bring their Elections Clause claim.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our two decisions construing the term \xe2\x80\x9cLegislature\xe2\x80\x9d in the Elections Clause do not contradict this holding. Each of these cases was filed by a relator on behalf of the State rather than private citizens acting on their own behalf, as is the case here. See State ex rel. Smiley v. Holm, 184 Min. 647, 238 N.\xc2\xa0W. 792 (1931) (per curiam), rev\xe2\x80\x99d sub\xc2\xa0nom. Smiley v. Holm, 285 U. S. 355 (1932); Ohio ex rel. Davis v. Hildebrant, 241 U. S. 565 (1916). In neither case did we address whether a private citizen had alleged a \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete and particularized\xe2\x80\x9d injury sufficient to satisfy the requirements of Article III.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the United States District Court for the District of Colorado is therefore vacated in part, and the case is remanded with instructions to dismiss the Elections Clause claim for lack of standing. We affirm the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s dismissal of the Petition Clause claim.

\n

It is so ordered.
