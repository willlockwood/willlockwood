CSX TRANSPORTATION, INC. v. ALABAMA DEPARTMENT OF REVENUE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x93520.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 10, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 22, 2011

\n

Petitioner (CSX) is an interstate rail carrier that operates, and pays taxes, in Alabama. The State imposes sales and use taxes on railroads when they purchase or consume diesel fuel, but exempts their main competitors\xe2\x80\x94interstate motor and water carriers. CSX sued respondents, the Alabama Department of Revenue and its Commissioner (Alabama), claiming that this tax scheme discriminates against railroads in violation of the Railroad Revitalization and Regulatory Reform Act of 1976 (4\xe2\x80\x93R Act or Act), which bars four forms of discriminatory taxation, 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711501(b). Three of the delineated prohibitions deal with property taxes, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa711501(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3), and the fourth is a catch-all provision that forbids a State to \xe2\x80\x9c[i]mpose another tax that discriminates against a rail carrier,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711501(b)(4). The District Court dismissed CSX\xe2\x80\x99s suit as not cognizable under the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act on the basis of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Department of Revenue of Ore. v. ACF Industries, Inc., 510 U. S. 332, and the Eleventh Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0CSX may challenge Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use taxes under \xc2\xa711501(b)(4). Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0CSX is challenging \xe2\x80\x9canother tax\xe2\x80\x9d within subsection (b)(4)\xe2\x80\x99s plain meaning. The Act does not define \xe2\x80\x9ctax.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, this Court looks to the word\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary definition, which is expansive. A State seeking to raise revenue may choose among multiple forms of taxation on property, income, transactions, or activities. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]nother tax\xe2\x80\x9d is thus best understood to encompass any tax a State might impose, on any asset or transaction, except the property taxes already addressed in subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3). There is no reason to interpret subsection (b)(4) as applying only to the gross-receipts taxes that some States imposed in lieu of property taxes at the time of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s passage. Moreover, CSX\xe2\x80\x99s complaint, contrary to the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s apparent view, does protest Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s imposition of taxes on its fuel. The exemptions the State has given may play a central role in CSX\xe2\x80\x99s argument, but the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s essential subject remains the taxes imposed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The key question thus becomes whether a tax might be said to \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate\xe2\x80\x9d against a railroad under subsection (b)(4) where the State has granted exemptions from the tax to other entities (here, the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s competitors). Because the statute does not define \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court again looks to the term\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary meaning, which is to fail to treat all persons equally when no reasonable distinction can be found between those favored and those not favored. To charge one group of taxpayers a 2% rate and another group a 4% rate, if the groups are the same in all relevant respects, is to discriminate against the latter. That discrimination continues if the favored group\xe2\x80\x99s rate goes down to 0%, which is all an exemption is. To say that such a tax does not \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate\xe2\x80\x9d is to adopt a definition at odds with the word\xe2\x80\x99s natural meaning. This Court has repeatedly recognized that tax schemes with exemptions may be discriminatory. See, e.g., Davis v. Michigan Department of Treasury, 489 U. S. 803. And even Department of Revenue of Ore. v. ACF Industries, Inc., 510 U. S. 332, on which the Eleventh Circuit heavily relied in dismissing CSX\xe2\x80\x99s suit, made clear that tax exemptions \xe2\x80\x9ccould be a variant of tax discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 343. In addition, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition of discrimination applies regardless whether the favored entities are interstate or local. The distinctions drawn in the statute are not between interstate and local actors, as Alabama suggests, but between railroads and all other actors, whether interstate or local. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0ACF Industries does not require a different result. There, the Court held that railroads could not contest property tax exemptions under subsection (b)(4), reasoning that it would be illogical to permit such a challenge when subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3)\xe2\x80\x94the \xc2\xa711501 provisions specifically addressing property taxes\xe2\x80\x94permitted States to grant property tax exemptions. Such a reading would \xe2\x80\x9csubvert the statutory plan\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccontravene the \xe2\x80\x98elementary canon of construction that a statute should be interpreted so as not to render one part inoperative.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 510 U. S., at 340. Contrary to Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s argument, this structural analysis does not apply here. Subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3) specifically allow property tax exemptions, but neither they nor any other provision of the Act speaks to non-property exemptions like those at issue here. Because Congress has expressed no intent to \xe2\x80\x9callo[w] the States to grant\xe2\x80\x9d non-property exemptions, id., at 343, reading subsection (b)(4) to encompass them poses no danger of \xe2\x80\x9cnullify[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d a congressional policy choice or otherwise \xe2\x80\x9csubverting the statutory plan,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 340, 343. Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s other efforts to borrow from ACF Industries\xe2\x80\x99 analysis similarly fail. Also unavailing is Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s argument that, even if ACF Industries\xe2\x80\x99 reasoning is limited to property tax exemptions, its holding must extend to non-property tax exemptions in order to prevent inconsistent or anomalous results in the treatment of property and non-property taxes. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9318.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Railroad Revitalization and Regulatory Reform Act of 1976 restricts the ability of state and local governments to levy discriminatory taxes on rail carriers. We consider here whether a railroad may invoke this statute to challenge sales and use taxes that apply to rail carriers (among others), but exempt their competitors in the transportation industry. We conclude that the railroad may do so.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress enacted the Railroad Revitalization and Regulatory Reform Act of 1976 (Act or 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act) to \xe2\x80\x9crestore the financial stability of the railway system of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d among other purposes. \xc2\xa7101(a), 90 Stat. 33. To help achieve this goal, Congress targeted state and local taxation schemes that discriminate against rail carriers. Burlington Northern R. Co. v. Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 481 U. S. 454, 457 (1987). The provision of the Act at issue here, now codified at 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711501,[Footnote 1] bars States and localities from engaging in four forms of discriminatory taxation. 90 Stat. 54.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 11501(b) describes the prohibited practices. It begins with three provisions addressed specifically to prop-\nerty taxes; it concludes with a catch-all provision con-\ncerning other taxes. According to \xc2\xa711501(b), States (or their subdivisions) \xe2\x80\x9cmay not\xe2\x80\x9d:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(1) Assess rail transportation property at a value that has a higher ratio to the true market value of the rail transportation property than the ratio that the assessed value of other commercial and industrial property in the same assessment jurisdiction has to the true market value of the other commercial and industrial property.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c(2) Levy or collect a tax on an assessment that may not be made under paragraph (1) of this subsection.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c(3) Levy or collect an ad\xc2\xa0valorem property tax on rail transportation property at a tax rate that exceeds the tax rate applicable to commercial and industrial property in the same assessment jurisdiction.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c(4) Impose another tax that discriminates against a rail carrier.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nThe following subsection confers jurisdiction on federal courts to \xe2\x80\x9cprevent a violation\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa711501(b) notwithstanding the Tax Injunction Act, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341, which ordinarily prohibits federal courts from enjoining the collection of state taxes when a remedy is available in state court. \xc2\xa711501(c).[Footnote 2]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner CSX Transportation, Inc. (CSX) is an interstate rail carrier that operates in Alabama and pays taxes there. Alabama imposes a sales tax of 4% on the gross receipts of retail businesses, Ala. Code \xc2\xa740\xe2\x80\x9323\xe2\x80\x932(1) (2010 Cum. Supp.), and a use tax of 4% on the storage, use, or consumption of tangible personal property, \xc2\xa740\xe2\x80\x9323\xe2\x80\x9361(a) (2003). Railroads pay these taxes when they purchase or consume diesel fuel. But railroads\xe2\x80\x99 main competitors\xe2\x80\x94interstate motor and water carriers\xe2\x80\x94are generally exempt from paying sales and use taxes on their fuel (although fuel for motor carriers is subject to a separate excise tax).[Footnote 3]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Alleging that Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme discriminates against railroads in violation of \xc2\xa711501(b)(4) of the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act, CSX sued respondents, the Alabama Department of Revenue and its Commissioner (Alabama or State), in Federal District Court. In particular, CSX complained that the State could not impose sales and use taxes on railroads\xe2\x80\x99 purchase and consumption of diesel fuel while exempting motor and water carriers from those taxes. App. 22 (Complaint \xc2\xb626).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court dismissed CSX\xe2\x80\x99s suit as not cognizable under the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act, and the United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed in a brief per curiam decision. 350 Fed. Appx. 318 (2009). The Eleventh Circuit rested on its earlier decision in Norfolk Southern R. Co. v. Alabama Dept. of Revenue, 550 F.\xc2\xa03d 1306 (2008), which involved a nearly identical challenge to the application of Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use taxes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 In Norfolk Southern, the Eleventh Circuit rejected the plaintiff railroad\xe2\x80\x99s challenge, principally in reliance on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Department of Revenue of Ore. v. ACF Industries, Inc., 510 U. S. 332 (1994). In that case, we held that a railroad could not invoke \xc2\xa711501(b)(4) to challenge a generally applicable property tax on the basis that certain non-railroad property was exempt from the tax. Id., at 335. The Eleventh Circuit recognized that the case before it involved sales and use taxes\xe2\x80\x94not property taxes, which the statutory scheme separately addresses. Norfolk Southern, 550 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1314. The court concluded, however, that this difference was immaterial, and accordingly held that a railroad could not object to Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use taxes simply because the State provides exemptions from them. Id., at 1316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CSX petitioned for a writ of certiorari, arguing that the Eleventh Circuit had misunderstood ACF Industries and noting a split of authority concerning whether railroads may bring a challenge under \xc2\xa711501(b)(4) to non-property taxes from which their competitors are exempt.[Footnote 4] We granted certiorari, 560 U. S. ____ (2010), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin, as in any case of statutory interpretation, with the language of the statute. Hardt v. Reliance Standard Life Ins. Co., 560 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 8). Section 11501(b)(4) provides that a State may not \xe2\x80\x9c[i]mpose another tax that discriminates against a rail carrier.\xe2\x80\x9d CSX wishes to bring an action under this provision because rail carriers, but not motor or water carriers, must pay Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use taxes on diesel fuel. To determine whether this suit may go forward, we must therefore answer two questions. Is CSX challenging \xe2\x80\x9canother tax\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the statute? And, if so, might that tax \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate\xe2\x80\x9d against rail carriers by exempting their competitors?[Footnote 5]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An excise tax, like Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use tax, is \xe2\x80\x9canother tax\xe2\x80\x9d under subsection (b)(4).[Footnote 6] The 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act does not define \xe2\x80\x9ctax\xe2\x80\x9d; nor does the statute otherwise place any matters within, or exclude any matters from, the term\xe2\x80\x99s ambit. In these circumstances, we look to the word\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary definition, Asgrow Seed Co. v. Winterboer, 513 U. S. 179, 187 (1995), and we note what taxpayers have long since discovered\xe2\x80\x94that the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9ctax\xe2\x80\x9d is expansive. A State (or other governmental entity) seeking to raise revenue may choose among multiple forms of taxation on property, income, transactions, or activities. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]nother tax,\xe2\x80\x9d as used in subsection (b)(4), is best understood to refer to all of these\xe2\x80\x94more precisely, to encompass any form of tax a State might impose, on any asset or transaction, except the taxes on property previously addressed in subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3). See Burlington Northern R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Superior, 932 F.\xc2\xa02d 1185, 1186 (CA7 1991) (Subsection (b)(4) includes \xe2\x80\x9can income tax, a gross-receipts tax, a use tax, an occupation tax . . . \xe2\x80\x94whatever\xe2\x80\x9d). The phrase \xe2\x80\x9canother tax\xe2\x80\x9d is a catch-all.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In particular, we see no reason to interpret subsection (b)(4) as applying only to the gross-receipts taxes\xe2\x80\x94known as \xe2\x80\x9cin lieu\xe2\x80\x9d taxes\xe2\x80\x94that some States imposed instead of property taxes at the time of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s passage. See Brief for Respondents 53\xe2\x80\x9355; Brief for State of Washington et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 20\xe2\x80\x9322. The argument in favor of this construction relies on the House Report concerning the bill, which described subsection (b)(4) as prohibiting \xe2\x80\x9cthe imposition of . . . the so-called \xe2\x80\x98in lieu tax.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d H. R. Rep. No. 94\xe2\x80\x93725, p. 77 (1975). But the Conference Report on the final bill abandoned the House Report\xe2\x80\x99s narrowing language and described the subsection as it was written\xe2\x80\x94as prohibiting, without limitation, \xe2\x80\x9cthe imposition of any other tax which results in the discriminatory treatment of any\xe2\x80\x9d railroad. S.\xc2\xa0Conf. Rep. No. 94\xe2\x80\x93595, pp. 165\xe2\x80\x93166 (1976); accord, S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 94\xe2\x80\x93499, p. 65 (1975). And the statutory language is the real crux of the matter: Subsection (b)(4) speaks both clearly and broadly, and a legislative report misdescribing the provision cannot succeed in altering it.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor do we agree with the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s apparent view that CSX does not challenge \xe2\x80\x9canother tax\xe2\x80\x9d because its complaint relies on the exemptions the State has given. See Norfolk Southern, 550 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1315 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe language of section (b)(4) prohibits a discriminatory \xe2\x80\x98tax\xe2\x80\x99 not a discriminatory tax exemption\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for American Trucking Assns., Inc. as Amicus Curiae 9. What the complaint protests is Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s imposition of taxes on the fuel CSX uses; what the complaint requests is that Alabama cease to collect those taxes from CSX. App. 23. The exemptions, no doubt, play a central role in CSX\xe2\x80\x99s argument: They demonstrate, in CSX\xe2\x80\x99s view, that the State\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use taxes discriminate against railroads. See id., at 22, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb624\xe2\x80\x9326. But the essential subject of the complaint remains the taxes Alabama levies on CSX.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The key question thus becomes whether a tax might be said to \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate\xe2\x80\x9d against a railroad under subsection (b)(4) because the State has granted exemptions from the tax to other entities (here, the railroad\xe2\x80\x99s competitors). The statute does not define \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates,\xe2\x80\x9d and so we again look to the ordinary meaning of the word. See supra, at 5. \xe2\x80\x9cDiscrimination\xe2\x80\x9d is the \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to treat all persons equally when no reasonable distinction can be found between those favored and those not favored.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 534 (9th ed. 2009); accord, id., at 420 (5th ed. 1979); see also Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 648 (1976) (\xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cto make a difference in treatment or favor on a class or categorical basis in disregard of individual merit\xe2\x80\x9d). To charge one group of taxpayers a 2% rate and another group a 4%\nrate, if the groups are the same in all relevant respects,\nis to discriminate against the latter. That discrimination continues (indeed, it increases) if the State takes the favored group\xe2\x80\x99s rate down to 0%. And that is all an exemption is. See West Lynn Creamery, Inc. v. Healy, 512 U. S. 186, 210\xe2\x80\x93211 (1994) (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment) (noting that an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98exemption\xe2\x80\x99 from \xe2\x80\xa6 a \xe2\x80\x98neutral\xe2\x80\x99 tax\xe2\x80\x9d for favored persons \xe2\x80\x9cis no different in principle\xe2\x80\x9d than \xe2\x80\x9ca discriminatory tax . . . imposing a higher liability\xe2\x80\x9d on disfavored persons). To say that such a tax (with such an exemption) does not \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94assuming the groups are similarly situated and there is no justification for the difference in treatment\xe2\x80\x94is to adopt a definition of the term at odds with its natural meaning.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In line with this understanding, our decisions have repeatedly recognized that tax schemes with exemptions may be discriminatory. In Davis v. Michigan Dept. of Treasury, 489 U. S. 803 (1989), for example, we reviewed a state income tax provision that exempted retirement benefits given by the State, but not those paid by the Federal Government. We held that the tax \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate[d]\xe2\x80\x9d against federal employees under 4 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7111, which serves to protect those employees from discriminatory state taxation. Similarly, our dormant Commerce Clause cases have often held that tax exemptions given to local businesses discriminate against interstate actors. See, e.g., Bacchus Imports, Ltd. v. Dias, 468 U. S. 263, 268\xe2\x80\x93269 (1984) (holding that a state excise tax on alcohol \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate[d]\xe2\x80\x9d against interstate businesses because of exemptions granted to local producers); Camps Newfound/Owatonna, Inc. v. Town of Harrison, 520 U. S. 564, 588\xe2\x80\x93589 (1997) (invalidating as \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminatory\xe2\x80\x9d a state property tax that exempted organizations operating for the benefit of residents, but not organizations aimed at nonresidents). And even our decision in ACF Industries, on which the Eleventh Circuit relied in dismissing CSX\xe2\x80\x99s suit, made clear that tax exemptions \xe2\x80\x9ccould be a variant of tax discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d 510 U. S., at 343.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act limit the prohibited discrimination to state tax schemes that unjustifiably exempt local actors, as opposed to interstate entities. Alabama argues for this result, claiming that \xc2\xa711501(b) is designed \xe2\x80\x9cto protect interstate carriers against discrimination vis-\xc3\x80-vis local businesses.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 29. But the text of \xc2\xa711501(b) tells a different story. Consistent with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s purpose of restoring the financial stability of railroads (not of interstate carriers generally), supra, at 1, each of subsection (b)\xe2\x80\x99s provisions proscribes taxes that specially burden a rail carrier\xe2\x80\x99s property or otherwise discriminate against a rail carrier. And not a single provision of the Act (including the references in subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3) to \xe2\x80\x9ccommercial and industrial property\xe2\x80\x9d) distinguishes between local and non-local taxpayers who receive favorable tax treatment. The distinctions drawn in \xc2\xa711501(b) are not between interstate and local actors, as the State contends, but rather between railroads and other actors, whether interstate or local. Accordingly, a state excise tax that applies to railroads but exempts their interstate competitors is subject to challenge under subsection (b)(4) as a \xe2\x80\x9ctax that discriminates against a rail carrier.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 8]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As against the plain language of subsection (b)(4), Alabama offers two arguments based on our decision in ACF Industries. The first claim, which the Eleventh Circuit accepted, rests on the reasoning we adopted in ACF Industries: We concluded there that railroads could not challenge property tax exemptions under subsection (b)(4),\nand Alabama asserts that the same analysis applies to ex-\ncise (and other non-property) tax exemptions. The second contention focuses on alleged problems that would emerge in the application of \xc2\xa711501(b) if the rule of ACF Industries did not govern all tax exemptions. On this view, even if ACF Industries\xe2\x80\x99 reasoning is irrelevant to cases involving excise taxes, its holding must extend to those cases to prevent inconsistent or anomalous results. We reject each of these arguments. We stand foursquare behind our decision in ACF Industries, but we will not extend it in the way the State wishes.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In ACF Industries, we considered whether a railroad could sue a State under subsection (b)(4) for taxing railroad property while exempting certain other commercial property. We held that the railroad could not do so. We noted that the language of subsection (b)(4), when viewed in isolation, could be read to allow such a challenge. But we reasoned that the structure of \xc2\xa711501 required the opposite result. 510 U. S., at 343. The Eleventh Circuit considered ACF Industries \xe2\x80\x9cdeterminative\xe2\x80\x9d of the question here, Norfolk Southern, 550 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1313, and Alabama agrees, Brief for Respondents 18. We think they misread that decision.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We began our analysis in ACF Industries by explaining that railroads could not challenge property tax exemptions under subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3)\xe2\x80\x94the provisions of \xc2\xa711501 specifically addressing property taxes. As noted earlier, subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3) prohibit a State from imposing higher property tax rates or assessment ratios on \xe2\x80\x9crail transportation property\xe2\x80\x9d than on \xe2\x80\x9cother commercial and industrial property.\xe2\x80\x9d The statute defines \xe2\x80\x9ccommercial and industrial property\xe2\x80\x9d as including only \xe2\x80\x9cproperty \xe2\x80\xa6 subject to a property tax levy.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711501(a)(4). We interpreted that phrase to mean \xe2\x80\x9cproperty that is taxed,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than property that is potentially taxable. 510 U. S., at 341\xe2\x80\x93342. As a result, we determined that exempt (i.e., non-taxed) property fell outside the category of \xe2\x80\x9cother commercial and industrial property\xe2\x80\x9d against which the taxation of railroad property is measured. Ibid. The conclusion followed: Subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3) permitted States to impose property taxes on railroads while exempting other entities. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And because that was so, we stated, still another conclusion followed: Subsection (b)(4)\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on discrimination likewise could not encompass property tax exemptions. Id., at 343. We viewed this holding as a matter of simple deduction: \xe2\x80\x9cIt would be illogical to conclude that Congress, having allowed the States to grant property tax exemptions in subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3), would turn around and nullify its own choice in subsection (b)(4).\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Or stated otherwise: \xe2\x80\x9c[R]eading subsection (b)(4) to prohibit what\xe2\x80\x9d other parts of the statute were \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to allow,\xe2\x80\x9d would \xe2\x80\x9csubvert the statutory plan\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccontravene the \xe2\x80\x98elementary canon of construction that a statute should be interpreted so as not to render one part inoperative.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 340. The structure of \xc2\xa711501 thus compelled our conclusion that property tax exemptions\xe2\x80\x94even if \xe2\x80\x9ca variant of tax discrimination,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 343\xe2\x80\x94fell outside subsection (b)(4)\xe2\x80\x99s reach.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But this structural analysis\xe2\x80\x94the core of ACF Industries\xe2\x80\x94has no bearing on the question here. Subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3) specifically address\xe2\x80\x94and allow\xe2\x80\x94property tax exemptions. But neither those subsections nor any other provision of the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act speaks to non-property tax exemptions like those at issue in this case. Congress has expressed no intent to \xe2\x80\x9callo[w] the States to grant\xe2\x80\x9d these exemptions. Ibid. Reading subsection (b)(4) as written\xe2\x80\x94to encompass non-property tax exemptions\xe2\x80\x94therefore poses no danger of \xe2\x80\x9cnullify[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d a congressional policy choice or otherwise \xe2\x80\x9csubvert[ing] the statutory plan.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 340, 343. To the contrary: Giving subsection (b)(4) something other than its ordinary meaning, absent any structural reason to do so, would itself contravene the expressed will of Congress.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Implicitly acknowledging that ACF Industries\xe2\x80\x99 central theory is irrelevant here, Alabama focuses on what that decision called \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ther considerations reinforc[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d its structural analysis. Id., at 343. Most notably, Alabama underscores the following sentence from ACF Industries: \xe2\x80\x9cGiven the prevalence of property tax exemptions when Congress enacted the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act, [\xc2\xa711501\xe2\x80\x99s] silence on the subject\xe2\x80\x94in light of the explicit prohibition of tax rate and assessment ratio discrimination\xe2\x80\x94reflects a determination to permit the States to leave their exemptions in place.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 344. Alabama asserts that this statement \xe2\x80\x9cholds just as true\xe2\x80\x9d for sales and use taxes. Brief for Respondents 41.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That claim rings hollow. To be sure, ACF Industries noted that Congress had declined to speak \xe2\x80\x9cwith any degree of particularity to\xe2\x80\x9d the permissibility of property tax exemptions, even though States often granted them. 510 U. S., at 343. But we thought that fact relevant only because Congress had spoken with particularity in proscribing other forms of discriminatory property taxes. The very sentence Alabama highlights makes our reasoning clear: Congress\xe2\x80\x99s silence as to the practice of granting property tax exemptions reflected its acquiescence in that practice \xe2\x80\x9cin light of the explicit prohibition [in subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3)] of [property] tax rate and assessment ratio discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 344 (emphasis added). If that explicit prohibition had not existed\xe2\x80\x94if \xc2\xa711501(b) had consisted only of subsection (b)(4)\xe2\x80\x99s broad ban on tax\ndiscrimination\xe2\x80\x94we could not have gleaned what we did from congressional silence. After all, the very purpose of a catch-all provision like subsection (b)(4) is to avoid the necessity of listing each matter (here, each kind of tax discrimination) falling within it. And with respect to non-property taxes (like Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use taxes), subsection (b)(4) is all there is. So here again, our analysis in ACF Industries does not apply because it rested on subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3)\xe2\x80\x94that is, on the highly reticulated scheme in the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act relating solely to property taxes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Alabama also emphasizes our statement in ACF Industries that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[p]rinciples of federalism\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d supported our holding, Brief for Respondents 41\xe2\x80\x9343 (quoting 510 U. S., at 345), but this final effort to borrow from that decision\xe2\x80\x99s analysis similarly fails. We indeed recognized in ACF Industries that the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act limits the traditional taxing power of the States. Because that is so, we expressed \xe2\x80\x9chesitan[ce] to extend the statute beyond its evident scope.\xe2\x80\x9d 510 U. S., at 345. But here, for all the reasons already noted, we are not \xe2\x80\x9cextend[ing] the statute\xe2\x80\x9d; we are merely giving effect to its clear meaning. To reiterate: The 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act distinguishes between property taxes and other taxes. Congress expressed its intent to insulate property tax exemptions from challenge; against that background, ACF Industries stated that permitting such suits would intrude on the States\xe2\x80\x99 rightful authority. By contrast, Congress drafted \xc2\xa711501 to enable railroads to contest all other tax exemptions; and when Congress speaks in such preemptive terms, its decision must govern. Principles of federalism cannot narrow \xc2\xa711501\xe2\x80\x99s clear scope. See, e.g., CSX Transp., Inc. v. Georgia State Bd. of Equalization, 552 U. S. 9, 20 (2007) (rejecting the idea that federalism principles preclude challenges to state valuation methodologies when \xc2\xa711501 \xe2\x80\x9cclearly authorized\xe2\x80\x9d such actions). Nothing in ACF Industries suggested otherwise.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Alabama additionally makes a subtler argument involving ACF Industries. Given that decision, Alabama contends, a ruling in CSX\xe2\x80\x99s favor here would create troubling inconsistencies. Alabama claims that subsection (b)(4)\xe2\x80\x99s singular prohibition on \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[ion]\xe2\x80\x9d would then mean one thing for property taxes (according to ACF Industries) and another for non-property taxes, even though nothing in the statute supports \xe2\x80\x9cmorphing definitions.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 32. And still worse than the difference in meaning would be the difference in result:\nA ruling for CSX, Alabama argues, would give railroads more protection against non-property taxes than against property taxes, even though no good reason exists for this distinction.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s one-word-two-meanings argument collapses because it again rests on a misunderstanding of ACF Industries. That decision did not define \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[e]\xe2\x80\x9d or say that a tax exemption could not fall within that term. Quite to the contrary: As noted earlier, ACF Industries frankly acknowledged that tax exemptions, including property tax exemptions, \xe2\x80\x9ccould be a variant of tax discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d 510 U. S., at 343; supra, at 9. We held that property tax exemptions were immune from challenge under subsection (b)(4) for structural, rather than linguistic, reasons. Even assuming these exemptions \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate[d],\xe2\x80\x9d they did so in a way that the specific provisions of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa711501(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3) allow, and accordingly \xc2\xa711501(b)(4)\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition could not include them. That reasoning, once more, does not apply here, because subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3) do not permit\xe2\x80\x94indeed, in no way address\xe2\x80\x94non-property tax exemptions. We therefore do not adopt a new definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate\xe2\x80\x9d in this case; in the context of the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act, that word has, and has always had, just one meaning.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What remains is Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s complaint that a ruling in CSX\xe2\x80\x99s favor, when combined with our decision in ACF Industries, will result in divergent treatment of property and non-property taxes. At times, Alabama dresses up this objection in Latin: It contends that the canon of ejusdem generis, which \xe2\x80\x9climits general terms [that] follow specific ones to matters similar to those specified,\xe2\x80\x9d Gooch v. United States, 297 U. S. 124, 128 (1936), has a role to play in interpreting \xc2\xa711501(b). More particularly, Alabama contends that this canon supports reading into \xc2\xa711501(b)(4) every limitation contained in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa711501(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3), including the exclusion of tax exemptions from the class of state actions subject to challenge. See Brief for Respondents 26\xe2\x80\x9327. That interpretive move, Alabama rightly notes, would ensure equal treatment of property tax and non-property tax exemptions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But we think ejusdem generis is not relevant here. As an initial matter, subsection (b)(4), \xe2\x80\x9c[a]lthough something of a catchall, . . . is not a general or collective term following a list of specific items to which a particular statutory command is applicable (e.g., \xe2\x80\x98fishing rods, nets, hooks, bobbers, sinkers, and other equipment\xe2\x80\x99).\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Aguilar, 515 U. S. 593, 615 (1995) (Scalia, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). Rather, that subsection is \xe2\x80\x9cone of . . . several distinct and independent prohibitions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Related to this structural point is a functional one. We typically use ejusdem generis to ensure that a general word will not render specific words meaningless. E.g., Circuit City Stores, Inc. v. Adams, 532 U. S. 105, 114\xe2\x80\x93115 (2001); see 2A N. Singer, Sutherland on Statutes and Statutory Construction \xc2\xa747:17 (7th ed. 2007). But that concern is absent here. Reading subsection (b)(4) to cover non-property tax exemptions will not deprive subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3) of effect, because those subsections are addressed only to property taxes. A canon meaning literally \xe2\x80\x9cof the same kind\xe2\x80\x9d has no application to provisions directed toward dissimilar subject matter.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The better version of Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s claim reads entirely in English; it is simply that distinguishing between property tax exemptions and other tax exemptions makes not a whit of sense. We are not much inclined to disagree. Neither CSX nor the United States as amicus curiae has offered a satisfying reason for why Congress drew this line\xe2\x80\x94why in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa711501(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3) it barred challenges based on property tax exemptions, but then turned around in \xc2\xa711501(b)(4) to allow challenges based on, say, excise tax exemptions. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 4\xe2\x80\x935, 24\xe2\x80\x9325. CSX, for example, has not presented any evidence that different tax exemptions posed different levels of threat to railroads\xe2\x80\x99 financial stability. So even if Congress had a good reason for distinguishing between property and non-property tax exemptions, we acknowledge that it eludes us.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But this admission does not take us far in Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s direction. Even if the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act were ambiguous, we doubt we would interpret subsection (b)(4) to replicate each facet of subsections (b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3). Treating property tax exemptions and other tax exemptions equivalently might make sense, as Alabama argues. But so too might allowing railroads to challenge all taxes (property or non-property) that contain exemptions. After all, as we noted earlier, tax exemptions are an obvious form of tax discrimination. See supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. It is hardly self-evident why Congress would prohibit a State from charging a railroad a 4% tax and a competitor a 2% tax, but allow the State to charge the railroad a 4% tax and the competitor nothing. The latter situation would frustrate the purposes of the Act even more than the former. In ACF Industries, we accepted that anomaly because the terms and structure of the Act demanded that we do so. But we could say no more in favor of the result than that it was \xe2\x80\x9cnot so bizarre that Congress could not have intended it.\xe2\x80\x9d 510 U. S., at 347 (internal quotation marks omitted). That was not a glowing recommendation, and we see no reason today to view the matter differently. Accordingly, even assuming that statutory ambiguity permitted us to do so, we would hesitate to extend the distinction between tax exemptions and differential tax rates in order to avoid a distinction between property and non-property taxes. That would seem a poor trade of statutory anomalies.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, and more importantly, the choice is not ours to make. Congress wrote the statute it wrote, and that statute draws a sharp line between property taxes and other taxes. Congress drafted \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa711501(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3) to exclude tax exemptions from the sphere of prohibited property tax discrimination. But it drafted \xc2\xa711501(b)(4) more broadly, without any of the prior subsections\xe2\x80\x99 limitations, to proscribe other \xe2\x80\x9ctax[es] that discriminat[e],\xe2\x80\x9d including through the use of exemptions. That congressional election settles this case. Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s preference for symmetry cannot trump an asymmetrical statute. And its preference for the greatest possible latitude to levy taxes cannot trump Congress\xe2\x80\x99s decision to restrict discriminatory taxation of rail carriers.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decision in this case is limited. We hold that CSX may challenge Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use taxes as \xe2\x80\x9ctax[es] that discriminat[e] against \xe2\x80\xa6 rail carrier[s]\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa711501(b)(4). We do not address whether CSX should prevail in that challenge\xe2\x80\x94whether, that is, Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s taxes in fact discriminate against railroads by exempting interstate motor and water carriers. Alabama argues, in support of barring CSX\xe2\x80\x99s challenge at the outset, that this inquiry into discrimination may pose difficulties. Brief for Respondents 35\xe2\x80\x9337. We cannot deny that assertion, but neither can we respond to it by precluding CSX\xe2\x80\x99s claim. Discrimination cases sometimes do raise knotty questions about whether and when dissimilar treatment is adequately justified. In the context of the 4\xe2\x80\x93R Act, those hard calls can arise when States charge different tax rates to different entities in a practice the statute specifically subjects to challenge. See \xc2\xa711501(b)(3). So too, difficult issues can emerge when, as here, States provide certain entities with tax exemptions. In either case, Congress has directed the federal courts to review a railroad\xe2\x80\x99s challenge; and in either case, we would flout the congressional command were we to declare the matter beyond us.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated, we reverse the judgment of the Eleventh Circuit and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Ginsburg joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use taxes are \xe2\x80\x9canother tax\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711501(b)(4) and that a scheme of tax exemptions is capable of making a tax discriminatory. Ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x938. As a general matter, therefore, I agree that Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use taxes could potentially violate subsection (b)(4), and would do so if their exemptions \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate[d] against a rail carrier.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa711501(b)(4). The majority\xe2\x80\x99s holding stops there, see ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa08, but I would go on.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I would hold that, to violate \xc2\xa711501(b)(4), a tax exemption scheme must target or single out railroads by comparison to general commercial and industrial taxpayers. Although parts of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s discussion appear to question this standard, see ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9311, the limited holding does not foreclose it. Because CSX cannot prove facts that would satisfy that standard in this case, I would affirm.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, \xe2\x80\x9canother tax that discriminates against a rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa711501(b)(4) means a tax\xe2\x80\x94or tax exemption scheme\xe2\x80\x94that targets or singles out railroads as compared to other commercial and industrial taxpayers. That reading settles the ambiguity in the word \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates\xe2\x80\x9d by reference to the rest of the statute and gives subsection (b)(4) a reach consistent with the problem the statute addressed.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cDiscriminates,\xe2\x80\x9d standing alone, is a flexible word. Compare, e.g., Clackamas Gastroenterology Associates, P.\xc2\xa0C. v. Wells, 538 U. S. 440, 446 (2003) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he statutory purpose of [the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 is] ridding the Nation of the evil of discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d), with Davis v. Bandemer, 478 U. S. 109, 132 (1986) (plurality opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9c[U]nconstitutional discrimination occurs only when the electoral system is arranged in a manner that will consistently degrade a voter\xe2\x80\x99s or a group of voters\xe2\x80\x99 influence\xe2\x80\x9d); and United Haulers Assn., Inc. v. Oneida-Herkimer Solid Waste Management Authority, 550 U. S. 330, 338 (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn this context, \xe2\x80\x98discrimination\xe2\x80\x99 simply means differential treatment of in-state and out-of-state economic interests that benefits the former and burdens the latter\xe2\x80\x9d (some internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even though \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate\xe2\x80\x9d has a general legal meaning relating to differential treatment, its precise contours still depend on its context. See Guardians Assn. v. Civil Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of New York City, 463 U. S. 582, 592 (1983) (opinion of White, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe language of Title VI on its face is ambiguous; the word \xe2\x80\x98discrimination\xe2\x80\x99 is inherently so\xe2\x80\x9d); Regents of Univ. of Cal. v. Bakke, 438 U. S. 265, 284 (1978) (opinion of Powell, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe concept of \xe2\x80\x98discrimination\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 is susceptible of varying interpretations\xe2\x80\x9d). Here, the word \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates\xe2\x80\x9d in subsection (b)(4) is ambiguous as to the appropriate comparison class. Burlington Northern\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Commissioner of Revenue, 509 N.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa02d 551, 553 (Minn. 1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cTo be discriminatory, a tax must be discriminatory as compared to someone else\xe2\x80\x9d). It is also ambiguous as to what type of difference is required to violate the statute\xe2\x80\x94e.g., any distinction, singling out, or something in between.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Therefore, I would use the context to resolve the meaning of the word as it is used in subsection (b)(4). See Robinson v. Shell Oil Co., 519 U. S. 337, 341 (1997) (statutory interpretation focuses on \xe2\x80\x9cthe language itself, the specific context in which that language is used, and the broader context of the statute as a whole\xe2\x80\x9d). We did precisely that in Department of Revenue of Ore. v. ACF Industries, Inc., 510 U. S. 332 (1994), where we similarly faced a question about the meaning of subsection (b)(4). In that case, our structural analysis of \xc2\xa711501(b) was \xe2\x80\x9ccentral to the interpretation of subsection (b)(4).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 340.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The structure of \xc2\xa711501(b) is straightforward. Subsections (b)(1) through (3) instruct that States may not assess railroad property at \xe2\x80\x9ca higher ratio to the true market value \xe2\x80\xa6 than \xe2\x80\xa6 other commercial and industrial property,\xe2\x80\x9d 49 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa711501(b)(1), collect taxes based on those inflated assessments, \xc2\xa711501(b)(2), or set property tax rates for railroad property higher than that \xe2\x80\x9capplicable to commercial and industrial property\xe2\x80\x9d in the same assessment jurisdiction, \xc2\xa711501(b)(3). Subsection (b)(4) then forbids \xe2\x80\x9c[i]mpos[ing] another tax that discriminates against a rail carrier.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I would look to subsections (b)(1) through (3) to determine the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates\xe2\x80\x9d in (b)(4). As many lower courts have correctly recognized, subsection (b)(4) \nis a residual clause, naturally appurtenant to subsections (b)(1) through (3).[Footnote 1] Moreover, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9canother tax that discriminates\xe2\x80\x9d in subsection (b)(4) suggests that the previous subsections all describe taxes that \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate\xe2\x80\x9d in a manner similar to that forbidden by subsection (b)(4). See Washington State Dept. of Social and Health Servs. v. Guardianship Estate of Keffeler, 537 U. S. 371, 384 (2003) (reading the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cother legal process\xe2\x80\x9d restrictively because \xe2\x80\x9cwhere general words follow specific words in a statutory enumeration, the general words are construed to embrace only objects similar in nature to those objects enumerated by the preceding specific words\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Subsections (b)(1) through (3) each prohibit particular types of state taxes that target or single out railroad property for less favorable tax treatment than other commercial and industrial property. First, the \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[ion]\xe2\x80\x9d addressed in subsections (b)(1) through (3) can only be described as taxes that target or single out railroad property. Those subsections specifically concern taxes that affect railroad property differently from the way they affect a larger class of comparative taxpayers\xe2\x80\x99 property. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa711501(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3); cf. ante, at 9 (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ach of subsection (b)\xe2\x80\x99s provisions proscribes taxes that specially burden a rail carrier\xe2\x80\x99s property or otherwise discriminate against a rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis deleted)). Second, each subsection refers to the same comparison class\xe2\x80\x94other \xe2\x80\x9ccommercial and industrial property.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa711501(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I think it follows that, under subsection (b)(4), a tax \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates against a rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d if it similarly targets railroads for tax treatment less favorable than other commercial and industrial taxpayers. As we found in ACF Industries, the structure of the statute provides a light by which to navigate the meaning of subsection (b)(4).

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The background of \xc2\xa711501(b) also supports this understanding of subsection (b)(4). In previous cases, we have identified the problem that made subsection (b) necessary. At the time the Railroad Revitalization and Regulatory Reform Act (4\xe2\x80\x93R Act) was enacted, it was clear that \xe2\x80\x9crailroads \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9care easy prey for State and local tax assessors\xe2\x80\x9d in that they are \xe2\x80\x9cnonvoting, often nonresident, targets for local taxation,\xe2\x80\x9d who cannot easily remove themselves from the locality.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ACF Industries, supra, at 336 (quoting Western Air Lines, Inc. v. Board of Equalization of S.\xc2\xa0D., 480 U. S. 123, 131 (1987) (quoting, in turn, S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 91\xe2\x80\x93630, p.\xc2\xa03 (1969))). The \xe2\x80\x9ctemptation to excessively tax nonvoting, nonresident businesses \xe2\x80\xa6 made federal legislation in this area necessary.\xe2\x80\x9d Western Air Lines, supra, at 131; see also Burlington Northern R. Co. v. Oklahoma Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 481 U. S. 454, 457 (1987) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]fter an extended period of congressional investigation, Congress concluded that \xe2\x80\x98railroads are over-taxed by at least $50 million each year\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 94\xe2\x80\x93725, p. 78 (1975))).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In other words, \xc2\xa711501(b) responded primarily to what its text describes\xe2\x80\x94property taxes that soaked the railroads. The obvious rationale supporting subsections (b)(1) through (3) is that the \xe2\x80\x9cway to prevent tax discrimination against the railroads is to tie their tax fate to the fate of a large and local group of taxpayers.\xe2\x80\x9d Kansas City Southern R. Co. v. McNamara, 817 F.\xc2\xa02d 368, 375 (CA5 1987); see also Atchison, T. & S. F. R. Co. v. Arizona, 78 F.\xc2\xa03d 438, 441 (CA9 1996). In this way, subsections (b)(1) through (3) establish a political check on the taxation of rail-\nroads. States cannot impose excessive property taxes on the nonvoting, nonresident railroads without imposing \nthe same taxes more generally on voting, resident local businesses.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Absent any indication that subsection (b)(4), as a residual clause, has any different aim, it is reasonable to conclude that it shares the same one as subsections (b)(1) through (3). See, e.g., Kansas City Southern\xc2\xa0R. Co., supra, at 373\xe2\x80\x93374 (Congress included subsection (b)(4) \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure that states did not shift to new forms of tax discrimination outside the letter of the first three subsections\xe2\x80\x9d); Burlington Northern\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Superior, 932 F.\xc2\xa02d 1185, 1186 (CA7 1991) (\xe2\x80\x9cSubsection (b)(4) is \xe2\x80\xa6 designed to prevent the state from accomplishing the forbidden end of discriminating against railroads by substituting another type of tax\xe2\x80\x9d). Subsection (b)(4) should be understood to tackle the issue of systemic railroad over-taxation the same way that the other subsections do\xe2\x80\x94by linking the taxation of railroads to the taxation of businesses with local political influence. Thus, a \xe2\x80\x9ctax that discriminates against a rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d is a tax that targets or singles out rail carriers compared to commercial and industrial taxpayers.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under this test, CSX\xe2\x80\x99s complaint was properly dismissed. CSX has not alleged that Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use taxes target railroads compared to general commercial and industrial taxpayers. See ACF Industries, 510 U. S., at 346\xe2\x80\x93347 (leaving open a case in which \xe2\x80\x9crailroads\xe2\x80\x94either alone or as part of some isolated and targeted group\xe2\x80\x94are the only commercial entities\xe2\x80\x9d subject to a tax); Norfolk Southern R. Co. v. Alabama Dept. of Revenue, 550 F.\xc2\xa03d 1306, 1316 (CA11 2008). CSX alleges that it paid a tax on its fuel that certain rail competitors did not have to pay. But it concedes, as it must, that the sales and use taxes are \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally applicable.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i; see Ala. Code \xc2\xa740\xe2\x80\x9323\xe2\x80\x932(1) (2010 Cum. Supp.) (imposing a four percent sales tax on \xe2\x80\x9cevery person, firm, or corporation \xe2\x80\xa6 selling at retail any tangible personal property whatsoever\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa740\xe2\x80\x9323\xe2\x80\x9361(a) (2003); see also Norfolk Southern R. Co., supra, at 1316; Tr. of Oral Arg. 36.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Discrete exemptions for certain railroad competitors\xe2\x80\x94namely, fuel exemptions for interstate motor carriers and interstate ships and barges\xe2\x80\x94do not make a generally applicable tax \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[ory]\xe2\x80\x9d under subsection (b)(4). Widespread exemptions could theoretically cause a facially general tax to target railroads, but the limited exemptions at issue here do not suggest that, and CSX has not argued it.[Footnote 2] Accordingly, CSX has not stated a cognizable claim for discrimination under \xc2\xa711501(b)(4).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court does not settle the ambiguity in the word \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates\xe2\x80\x9d in subsection (b)(4)\xe2\x80\x94leaving open both the appropriate comparison class and the type of differential treatment required to constitute discrimination.[Footnote 3] The majority \xe2\x80\x9chold[s] only that \xc2\xa711501(b)(4) enables a railroad to challenge an excise or other non-property tax as discriminatory on the basis of the tax scheme\xe2\x80\x99s exemptions.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa08. Thus, when the majority says that \xe2\x80\x9ca state excise tax that applies to railroads but exempts their interstate competitors is subject to challenge under subsection (b)(4),\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10, it must mean only that a tax exemption scheme could potentially violate subsection (b)(4).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As I understand it, the majority does not decide whether CSX has stated a claim even in this case but instead leaves that issue for remand. Accordingly, States remain free to argue\xe2\x80\x94and lower courts to hold\xe2\x80\x94that complaints like CSX\xe2\x80\x99s should be dismissed for failing to state a \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[ion]\xe2\x80\x9d claim under \xc2\xa711501(b)(4) when they do not allege that railroads are targeted or singled out compared to commercial and industrial taxpayers generally.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nonetheless, despite the majority\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that it is \xe2\x80\x9cinappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d to address whether Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s tax scheme actually discriminates within the meaning of \xc2\xa711501(b)(4), ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa08, parts of its opinion suggest an answer to that question that I believe is incorrect. Relying on the second definition in Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary, the majority defines \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98failure to treat all persons equally when no reasonable distinction can be found between those favored and those not favored.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. This definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate,\xe2\x80\x9d combined with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s insistence that the \xe2\x80\x9cdistinctions drawn in \xc2\xa711501(b) are \xe2\x80\xa6 between railroads and other actors, whether interstate or local,\xe2\x80\x9d suggests that the comparison class could be anyone.[Footnote 4] Ante, at 10. The majority ultimately implies that \xe2\x80\x9canother tax that discriminates against a rail carrier\xe2\x80\x9d is any tax that draws a distinction between a rail carrier and anyone else without sufficient justification. See ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa08 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhether the railroad will prevail \xe2\x80\xa6 depends on whether the State offers a sufficient justification for declining to provide the exemption at issue to rail carriers\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I do not read subsection (b)(4) so independently of (b)(1) through (3). Perhaps, as the majority asserts, subsection (b)(4) is not an ideal candidate for ejusdem generis. Ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317. But given the ambiguity of subsection (b)(4), (b)(1) through (3) are the best guides for understanding its proper scope\xe2\x80\x94something we recognized in ACF Industries. 510 U. S., at 343. It is more reasonable to discern the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates\xe2\x80\x9d in subsection (b)(4) using the preceding subsections than to pluck from the dictionary a definition for such a context-dependent term.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Detaching subsection (b)(4) from the rest of the section would expand its meaning well beyond the scope of the problem that necessitated \xc2\xa711501(b). Instead of simply eliminating the particular vulnerability of railroads by tying their tax fate to that of general commercial and industrial taxpayers, railroads would receive a surprising windfall: most-favored taxpayer status. This would convert subsection (b)(4) from a shield into a sword.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The implication of the majority opinion is that if every person and business in the State of Alabama paid a $1 annual tax, and one person was exempt, CSX could sue under subsection (b)(4) and require the State to either exempt CSX also or \xe2\x80\x9coffe[r] a sufficient justification\xe2\x80\x9d for the distinction. See ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa08. Although the majority denies that this would provide railroads most-favored-taxpayer status, see ibid., it acknowledges that States would have to justify any tax distinction that railroads argue may disfavor them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The only bulwark against requiring States to give railroads every tax exemption that anyone else gets would be open-ended judicial determinations of what is \xe2\x80\x9csufficient justification\xe2\x80\x9d for such distinctions. Ibid. Unsurprisingly, the statute provides no guidance for what \xe2\x80\x9csufficient justification\xe2\x80\x9d might mean, but neither does the majority. There are all sorts of reasons that might lead a State to distinguish between railroads and others for tax purposes. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 58. For instance, in this case, Alabama points out that motor carriers and interstate water carriers pay a separate\xe2\x80\x94and frequently higher\xe2\x80\x94tax on fuel from which railroads are effectively exempt. Brief for Respondents 12\xe2\x80\x9316, 59\xe2\x80\x9360. That might be a \xe2\x80\x9csufficient justification\xe2\x80\x9d for their exemptions from the taxes here, but the majority expressly disclaims reaching that question. Ante, at 5, n.\xc2\xa05.[Footnote 5] Of course, logically extending the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminates\xe2\x80\x9d from subsections (b)(1) through (3) would avoid this problem, as there is no need for \xe2\x80\x9cjustification\xe2\x80\x9d at all: A tax either targets railroads by comparison to commercial and industrial taxpayers or it does not.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I disagree with the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminat[e]\xe2\x80\x9d in subsection (b)(4) that the majority seems to imply. The rest of \xc2\xa711501(b) provides a logical and coherent way to determine what subsection (b)(4) means, and we have used that methodology before. See ACF Industries, 510 U. S., at 340. The best way to read subsection (b)(4) is as prohibiting taxes that target or single out railroads as compared to general commercial and industrial taxpayers. That is the test I would establish, and I do not understand the majority to foreclose the lower courts from utilizing it. Under that test, CSX\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s sales and use taxes was properly dismissed. Accordingly, I respectfully dissent.
