\n\t\t\t\tKAWASHIMA et ux. v. HOLDER, ATTORNEY GENERAL\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x93577.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 7, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 21, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tAn Immigration Judge ordered the removal of resident aliens Akio and Fusako Kawashima, determining that Mr. Kawashima\xe2\x80\x99s conviction for willfully making and subscribing a false tax return, \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77206(1), and Mrs. Kawashima\xe2\x80\x99s conviction for aiding and assisting in the preparation of a false tax return, \xc2\xa77206(2), qualified as crimes involving fraud or deceit under \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)(i) (Clause (i)) and thus were aggravated felonies for which they could be deported under \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(A)(iii). The Board of Immigration Appeals affirmed. Holding that convictions under 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77206(1) and (2) in which the Government\xe2\x80\x99s revenue loss exceeds $10,000 constitute aggravated felonies under Clause (i), the Ninth Circuit affirmed, but remanded for the Board to determine whether Mrs. Kawashima\xe2\x80\x99s conviction had caused a Government loss in excess of $10,000.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Convictions under 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77206(1) and (2) in which the Government\xe2\x80\x99s revenue loss exceeds $10,000 qualify as aggravated felonies pursuant to Clause (i). Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x88\x9211.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 argument that they cannot be deported for the commission of an \xe2\x80\x9caggravated felony\xe2\x80\x9d because crimes under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77206(1) and (2) do not involve the fraud or deceit required by Clause (i) is rejected. This Court looks to the statute defining the crime of conviction, rather than the specific facts underlying the crime, see Gonzales v. Duenas-Alvarez, \n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 183\n, to determine whether the Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 offenses involve fraud or deceit within the meaning of Clause (i). Section 7206(1) provides that any person who \xe2\x80\x9cwillfully makes and subscribes any return .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. which contains or is verified by a written declaration that it is made under the penalties of perjury, and which he does not believe to be true and correct as to every material matter,\xe2\x80\x9d shall be guilty of a felony. Although the words \xe2\x80\x9cfraud\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdeceit\xe2\x80\x9d are absent from \xc2\xa77206(1) and are not themselves formal elements of the crime, it does not follow that Mr. Kawashima\xe2\x80\x99s offense falls outside Clause (i). Clause (i) is not limited to offenses that include fraud or deceit as formal elements. Rather, it refers more broadly to offenses involving fraud or deceit\xe2\x80\x95meaning offenses with elements that necessarily entail fraudulent or deceitful conduct. Mr. Kawashima\xe2\x80\x99s conviction under \xc2\xa77206(1) involved deceitful conduct in that he knowingly and willfully submitted a tax return that was false as to a material matter. Mrs. Kawashima was convicted of violating \xc2\xa77206(2), which declares that any person who \xe2\x80\x9c[w]illfully aids or assists in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the preparation or presentation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of a return .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. which is fraudulent or is false as to any material matter\xe2\x80\x9d has committed a felony. She committed a felony involving deceit by knowingly and willfully assisting her husband\xe2\x80\x99s filing of a materially false tax return. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x88\x926.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 argument that Clause (i), when considered in light of \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)(ii) (Clause (ii)), must be interpreted as being inapplicable to tax crimes is also rejected. Clause (i) defines \xe2\x80\x9caggravated felony\xe2\x80\x9d to mean an offense that \xe2\x80\x9cinvolves fraud or deceit in which the loss to the victim or victims exceeds $10,000.\xe2\x80\x9d Clause (ii) defines \xe2\x80\x9caggravated felony\xe2\x80\x9d as an offense that is \xe2\x80\x9cdescribed in section 7201 of title 26 (relating to tax evasion) in which the revenue loss to the Government exceeds $10,000.\xe2\x80\x9d Contrary to the Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 claim, the difference in the clauses\xe2\x80\x99 language\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9crevenue loss to the Government\xe2\x80\x9d in Clause (ii) compared to \xe2\x80\x9closs to the victim\xe2\x80\x9d in Clause (i)\xe2\x80\x94does not establish Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to remove tax crimes from the scope of Clause (i). By its plain language, Clause (i) covers a broad class of offenses that involve fraud or deceit, and Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to tailor Clause (ii)\xe2\x80\x99s language to match the sole type of offense it covers does not demonstrate that Congress intended to implicitly circumscribe Clause (i)\xe2\x80\x99s broad scope. Furthermore, interpreting Clause (i) to include tax crimes does not violate the presumption against superfluities. The specific inclusion of tax evasion in Clause (ii) does not make it redundant to Clause (i) because the inclusion was intended to ensure that tax evasion pursuant to \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77201 was a deportable offense. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x88\x9210.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0The United States Sentencing Guidelines\xe2\x80\x99 separate treatment of tax crimes and crimes involving fraud and deceit does not support the Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 contention that Congress did not intend to include tax crimes within Clause (i). No evidence suggests that Congress considered the Guidelines when drafting \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M). Moreover, the differences between \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M) and the Guidelines undercut any inference that Congress was incorporating the distinction drawn by the Guidelines into \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M). Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x88\x9211.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0Construing \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M) in the Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 favor under the rule of lenity is not warranted in light of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s clear application. P.\xc2\xa011.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case concerns whether aliens who commit certain federal tax crimes are subject to deportation as aliens who have been convicted of an aggravated felony. We hold that violations of 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77206(1) and (2) are crimes \xe2\x80\x9cinvolv[ing] fraud or deceit\xe2\x80\x9d under \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)(i) and are therefore aggravated felonies as that term is defined in the Immigration and Nationality Act, \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101 et\xc2\xa0seq., when the loss to the Government exceeds $10,000.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners, Akio and Fusako Kawashima, are natives and citizens of Japan who have been lawful permanent residents of the United States since June 21, 1984. In 1997, Mr. Kawashima pleaded guilty to one count of willfully making and subscribing a false tax return in violation of \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77206(1). Mrs. Kawashima pleaded guilty to one count of aiding and assisting in the prep- aration of a false tax return in violation of \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77206(2).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFollowing their convictions, the Immigration and Naturalization Service charged the Kawashimas with being deportable from the United States as aliens who had been convicted of an aggravated felony.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t See \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(A)(iii) (\xe2\x80\x9cAny alien who is convicted of an aggravated felony at any time after admission is deportable\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In the Immigration and Nationality Act, Congress listed categories of offenses that qualify as \xe2\x80\x9caggravated felonies\xe2\x80\x9d for the purpose of deportation. See \xc2\xa71101(a)(43). Here, the Government charged the Kawashimas with be- ing deportable for committing offenses under subparagraph\xc2\xa0(M) of \xc2\xa71101(a)(43). That subparagraph classifies as an aggravated felony an offense that either: \xe2\x80\x9c(i) involves fraud or deceit in which the loss to the victim or vic- tims exceeds $10,000; or (ii) is described in section 7201 of title 26 (relating to tax evasion) in which the revenue loss to the Government exceeds $10,000.\xe2\x80\x9d Hereinafter, we refer to \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)(i) as \xe2\x80\x9cClause (i)\xe2\x80\x9d and to \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)(ii) as \xe2\x80\x9cClause (ii).\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt their deportation hearing, the Kawashimas argued that their convictions under \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77206 did not qualify as aggravated felonies under subparagraph\xc2\xa0(M). The Immigration Judge disagreed and ordered removal, concluding that the Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 convictions qualified as aggravated felonies under Clause\xc2\xa0(i). The Kawashimas appealed the removal order to the Board of Immigration Appeals (Board), which affirmed the Immigration Judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision. After unsuccessfully petitioning the Board to reopen its decision, the Kawashimas filed petitions for review of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s decision in the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Ninth Circuit held that \xe2\x80\x9cconvictions for violating \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77206(1) and (2) in which the tax loss to the Government exceeds $10,000 constitute aggravated felonies under subsection\xc2\xa0(M)(i).\xe2\x80\x9d 615 F.\xc2\xa03d 1043, 1053 (2010). The court concluded that Mr. Kawashima\xe2\x80\x99s conviction un- der \xc2\xa77206(1) qualified as an aggravated felony within Clause\xc2\xa0(i)\xe2\x80\x99s definition \xe2\x80\x9cbecause it involved \xe2\x80\x98fraud or deceit\xe2\x80\x99 and because his offense resulted in a loss to the government in excess of $10,000.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1055. The Ninth Circuit also determined that Mrs. Kawashima\xe2\x80\x99s conviction under \xc2\xa77206(2) \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily \xe2\x80\x98involve[d] fraud or deceit.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1055. But because Mrs. Kawashima\xe2\x80\x99s plea agreement was not in the administrative record, the Ninth Circuit remanded to the Board to determine whether Mrs. Kawashima\xe2\x80\x99s conviction had caused a loss to the Government in excess of $10,000. Id., at 1056\xe2\x80\x931057.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted the Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 petition for a writ of certiorari to determine whether their convictions for violations of 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77206(1) and (2) respectively qualify as aggravated felonies under \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)(i). 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011). We now affirm.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Kawashimas argue that they cannot be deported for commission of an \xe2\x80\x9caggravated felony\xe2\x80\x9d because crimes under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77206(1) and (2) do not \xe2\x80\x9cinvolv[e] fraud or deceit\xe2\x80\x9d as required by Clause\xc2\xa0(i). The Kawashimas also assert that their convictions under \xc2\xa77206 are not \xe2\x80\x9caggravated felonies\xe2\x80\x9d because tax crimes are not included within Clause\xc2\xa0(i) at all. We address each argument in turn.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Kawashimas contend that their offenses of conviction do not fall within the scope of Clause\xc2\xa0(i) because neither \xe2\x80\x9cfraud\xe2\x80\x9d nor \xe2\x80\x9cdeceit\xe2\x80\x9d is a formal element of a con- viction under \xc2\xa77206(1) or \xc2\xa77206(2). The Government responds that the Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 convictions necessarily in- volved deceit because they required a showing that the Kawashimas willfully made materially false statements. To determine whether the Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 offenses \xe2\x80\x9cinvolv[e] fraud or deceit\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of Clause\xc2\xa0(i), we employ a categorical approach by looking to the statute defining the crime of conviction, rather than to the specific facts underlying the crime. See Gonzales v. Duenas-Alvarez, \n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 183, \n186 (2007)\n (applying the approach set forth in Taylor v. United States, \n\n495 U.\xc2\xa0S. 575\n\xe2\x80\x93600 (1990)). If the elements of the offenses establish that the Kawashimas committed crimes involving fraud or deceit, then the first requirement of Clause\xc2\xa0(i) is satisfied.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMr. Kawashima was convicted of violating \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77206(1), which provides that any person who \xe2\x80\x9c[w]illfully makes and subscribes any return, statement, or other document, which contains or is verified by a written declaration that it is made under the penalties of perjury, and which he does not believe to be true and correct as to every material matter,\xe2\x80\x9d shall be guilty of a felony. Mr. Ka- washima does not dispute that the elements of a violation of \xc2\xa77206(1) include, inter alia, that the document in question was false as to a material matter, that the defendant did not believe the document to be true and correct as to every material matter, and that he acted willfully with the specific intent to violate the law. See, e.g., United States v. Aramony, 88 F.\xc2\xa03d 1369, 1382 (CA4 1996); United States v. Kaiser, 893 F.\xc2\xa02d 1300, 1305 (CA11 1990); United States v. Marabelles, 724 F.\xc2\xa02d 1374, 1380 (CA9 1984); United States v. Whyte, 699 F.\xc2\xa02d 375, 381 (CA7 1983). Although the words \xe2\x80\x9cfraud\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdeceit\xe2\x80\x9d are absent from the text of \xc2\xa77206(1) and are not themselves formal elements of the crime, it does not follow that his offense falls outside of Clause\xc2\xa0(i). The scope of that clause is not limited to offenses that include fraud or deceit as formal elements. Rather, Clause\xc2\xa0(i) refers more broadly to offenses that \xe2\x80\x9cinvolv[e]\xe2\x80\x9d fraud or deceit\xe2\x80\x94meaning offenses with elements that necessarily entail fraudulent or deceitful conduct.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen subparagraph\xc2\xa0(M) was enacted, the term \xe2\x80\x9cdeceit\xe2\x80\x9d meant a \xe2\x80\x9cthe act or process of deceiving (as by falsification, concealment, or cheating).\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 584 (1993). Mr. Kawashima\xe2\x80\x99s conviction under \xc2\xa77206(1) establishes that he knowingly and willfully submitted a tax return that was false as to a material matter. He therefore committed a felony that involved \xe2\x80\x9cdeceit.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTurning to Mrs. Kawashima, our analysis follows a similar path. Mrs. Kawashima was convicted of violating \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77206(2), which declares that any person who \xe2\x80\x9c[w]illfully aids or assists in\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the preparation or presentation under, or in connection with any matter arising under, the internal revenue laws, of a return, affidavit, claim, or other document, which is fraudulent or is false as to any material matter,\xe2\x80\x9d has committed a felony. Mrs. Kawashima does not dispute that the elements of a violation of \xc2\xa77206(2) include, inter alia, that the document in question was false as to a material matter and that the defendant acted willfully. See Aramony, supra, at 1382; United States v. Sassak, 881 F.\xc2\xa02d 276, 278 (CA6 1989); United States v. Hooks, 848 F.\xc2\xa02d 785, 788\xe2\x80\x93789 (CA7 1988); United States v. Dahlstrom, 713 F.\xc2\xa02d 1423, 1426\xe2\x80\x931427 (CA9 1983). We conclude that Mrs. Kawashima\xe2\x80\x99s conviction establishes that, by knowingly and willfully assisting her husband\xe2\x80\x99s filing of a materially false tax return, Mrs. Kawashima also committed a felony that involved \xe2\x80\x9cdeceit.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe language of Clause\xc2\xa0(i) is clear. Anyone who is convicted of an offense that \xe2\x80\x9cinvolves fraud or deceit in which the loss to the victim or victims exceeds $10,000\xe2\x80\x9d has committed an aggravated felony and is subject to depor- tation pursuant to \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(A)(iii). The elements of willfully making and subscribing a false corporate tax return, in violation of \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77206(1), and of aiding and assisting in the preparation of a false tax return, in violation of \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77206(2), establish that those crimes are deportable offenses because they necessarily entail deceit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 second argument is based on inferences drawn from the interaction of Clause\xc2\xa0(i) and Clause\xc2\xa0(ii). The full text of subparagraph\xc2\xa0(M) reads as follows:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n \xe2\x80\x9c(43) The term \xe2\x80\x98aggravated felony\xe2\x80\x99 means\xe2\x80\x94\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(M) an offense that\xe2\x80\x94\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(i) involves fraud or deceit in which the loss to the victim or victims exceeds $10,000; or\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(ii) is described in section 7201 of title 26 (relating to tax evasion) in which the revenue loss to the Government exceeds $10,000.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Kawashimas argue that when Clause\xc2\xa0(i) is read together with Clause (ii), Clause\xc2\xa0(i) must be interpreted as being inapplicable to tax crimes. In their view, subparagraph\xc2\xa0(M), when considered in its entirety, demonstrates that Congress was addressing two mutually exclusive categories of crimes in subparagraph (M)\xe2\x80\x99s two clauses: general, non-tax crimes involving fraud or deceit that cause actual losses to real victims in Clause\xc2\xa0(i), and tax crimes involving revenue losses to the Government in Clause\xc2\xa0(ii). For the reasons discussed below, this argument cannot overcome the plain language of Clause (i), which encompasses the Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 offenses of conviction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Kawashimas contend that textual differences between Clauses\xc2\xa0(i) and (ii) indicate that Congress intended to exclude tax crimes from Clause\xc2\xa0(i). Specifically, they note that Clause\xc2\xa0(i) addresses \xe2\x80\x9closs to the victim,\xe2\x80\x9d whereas Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) addresses \xe2\x80\x9crevenue loss to the Government.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis difference in language does not establish Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to remove tax crimes from the scope of Clause\xc2\xa0(i). Clause\xc2\xa0(i) covers a broad class of offenses that involve fraud or deceit. Clause\xc2\xa0(i) thus uses correspondingly broad language to refer to the wide range of potential losses and victims. Clause\xc2\xa0(ii), on the other hand, is limited to the single type of offense \xe2\x80\x9cdescribed in section 7201 of title 26 (relating to tax evasion),\xe2\x80\x9d which, by definition, can only cause one type of loss (revenue loss) to one type of victim (the Government). Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to tailor Clause\xc2\xa0(ii)\xe2\x80\x99s language to match the sole type of offense covered by Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) does not demonstrate that Congress also intended to implicitly circumscribe the broad scope of Clause\xc2\xa0(i)\xe2\x80\x99s plain language.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNext, the Kawashimas argue that interpreting Clause\xc2\xa0(i) to include tax crimes violates the presumption against superfluities by rendering Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) completely redundant to Clause\xc2\xa0(i). Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) explicitly states that convictions for tax evasion pursuant to \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77201 that cause a revenue loss of at least $10,000 to the Government are aggravated felonies. The Kawashimas assert that, if Clause\xc2\xa0(i) applies to tax crimes, then qualifying convictions for tax evasion under Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) would also qualify as aggravated felonies under Clause\xc2\xa0(i), because tax evasion is a crime involving fraud or deceit. To buttress this argument, the Kawashimas point to a body of law providing that a conviction for tax evasion under \xc2\xa77201 collaterally estops the convicted taxpayer from contesting a civil penalty under \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76663(b) for \xe2\x80\x9cunderpayment\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. attributable to fraud.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Gray v. Commissioner, 708 F.\xc2\xa02d 243, 246 (CA6 1983) (\xe2\x80\x9cNumerous federal courts have held that a conviction for federal income tax evasion, either upon a plea of guilty, or upon a jury verdict of guilt, conclusively establishes fraud in a subsequent civil tax fraud proceeding through application of the doctrine of collateral estoppel\xe2\x80\x9d). Therefore, according to the Kawashimas, if Clause\xc2\xa0(i) covers tax offenses, then Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) is mere surplusage.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t We disagree with the Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 contention that the specific mention of one type of tax crime in Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) impliedly limits the scope of Clause\xc2\xa0(i)\xe2\x80\x99s plain language, which extends to any offense that \xe2\x80\x9cinvolves fraud or deceit.\xe2\x80\x9d We think it more likely that Congress specifically included tax evasion offenses under \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77201 in Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) to remove any doubt that tax evasion qualifies as an aggravated felony.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSeveral considerations support this conclusion. Like \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77206(1) and (2), \xc2\xa77201 does not, on its face, mention fraud or deceit. Instead, \xc2\xa77201 simply provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person who willfully attempts in any manner to evade or defeat any tax imposed by [the Internal Revenue Code] or the payment thereof shall, in addition to other penalties provided by law, be guilty of a felony.\xe2\x80\x9d Accordingly, neither fraud nor deceit is among the elements of a conviction under \xc2\xa77201, which include: (1) willfulness; (2) the existence of a tax deficiency; and (3) an affirmative act constituting an evasion or an attempted evasion of the tax. Boulware v. United States, \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 421\n, n.\xc2\xa02 (2008). A conviction under \xc2\xa77201, therefore, only qualifies as an aggravated felony under Clause\xc2\xa0(i) if a willful, affirmative attempt to evade a tax necessarily entails fraud or deceit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in United States v. Scharton, \n\n285 U.\xc2\xa0S. 518 (1932)\n, gave Congress good reason to doubt that a conviction under \xc2\xa77201 satisfies that condition. In Scharton, the defendant was indicted for attempting to evade income taxes by falsely understating his taxable income. The question before the Court was whether the crime was subject to the 3-year statute of limitations generally applicable to tax crimes, or whether it was instead subject to the 6-year statute of limitations applicable to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98offenses involving the defrauding or attempting to defraud the United States or any agency thereof, whether by conspiracy or not, and in any manner.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 520, n.\xc2\xa02 (quoting \n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7585 (1962 ed., Supp. V)). The Government argued that the 6-year statute of limitations applied because \xe2\x80\x9cfraud is implicit in the concept of evading or defeating\xe2\x80\x9d and because any effort to evade a tax is tantamount to an attempt to defraud the taxing body. 285 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 520\xe2\x80\x93521. The Court rejected that argument, noting that, in an indictment for evasion, \xe2\x80\x9can averment [of intent to defraud] would be surplusage, for it would be sufficient to plead and prove a willful attempt to evade or defeat.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 521.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, \xc2\xa77201 includes two offenses: \xe2\x80\x9cthe offense of willfully attempting to evade or defeat the assessment of a tax as well as the offense of willfully attempting to evade or defeat the payment of a tax.\xe2\x80\x9d Sansone v. United States, \n\n380 U.\xc2\xa0S. 343, \n354 (1965)\n (emphasis in original). As the Government notes, it is possible to willfully evade or de- feat payment of a tax under \xc2\xa77201 without making any misrepresentation. For example, \xc2\xa77201 can be violated by a taxpayer who files a truthful tax return, but who also takes affirmative steps to evade payment by moving his assets beyond the reach of the Internal Revenue Service. Although the Government concedes that evasion-of-payment cases will almost invariably involve some affirmative acts of fraud or deceit, it is still true that the elements of tax evasion pursuant to \xc2\xa77201 do not necessarily involve fraud or deceit. Thus, we conclude that the specific inclusion of tax evasion in Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) was intended to ensure that tax evasion pursuant to \xc2\xa77201 was a deportable offense. Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) does not implicitly remove all other tax offenses from the scope of Clause\xc2\xa0(i)\xe2\x80\x99s plain language.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Kawashimas also assert that the separate treatment of tax crimes and crimes involving fraud and deceit in the United State Sentencing Guidelines supports their contention that Congress did not intend to include tax crimes within Clause\xc2\xa0(i). They point to the fact that, in 1987, the United States Sentencing Commission included within the Guidelines a category of \xe2\x80\x9coffenses involving fraud or deceit.\xe2\x80\x9d USSG \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72F1.1 to 2F1.2 (deleted effec- tive Nov. 1, 2001). The Commission simultaneously included \xe2\x80\x9coffenses involving taxation\xe2\x80\x9d as a separate category. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72T1.1 et seq. (Nov. 2011). Although the Kawashimas acknowledge that they have found no evidence that Congress actually considered the Guidelines, they contend that \xe2\x80\x9cit is likely that the language of [Clause\xc2\xa0(i)] and [Clause\xc2\xa0(ii)] was taken from the Sentencing Guidelines\xe2\x80\x9d by the sponsors of the bill that expanded the definition of aggravated felony to include subparagraph\xc2\xa0(M). Brief for Petitioners 29. Therefore, the theory goes, we can infer from the similar language in the Guidelines that Congress did not intend Clause\xc2\xa0(i) to include tax crimes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe reject the Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on the Guidelines. The Kawashimas\xe2\x80\x99 argument is at odds with the fact that, unlike the Guideline that the Kawashimas cite, Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) does not refer to all offenses \xe2\x80\x9cinvolving taxation.\xe2\x80\x9d Rather, Clause\xc2\xa0(ii) is expressly limited to tax evasion offenses under \xc2\xa77201. That textual difference undercuts any inference that Congress was considering, much less incorporating, the distinction drawn by the Guidelines.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the Kawashimas argue that subparagraph\xc2\xa0(M)\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of tax crimes other than tax evasion is ambiguous, and that we should therefore construe the statute in their favor. It is true that we have, in the past, construed ambiguities in deportation statutes in the alien\xe2\x80\x99s favor. See INS v. St. Cyr, \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 289, \n320 (2001)\n. We think the application of the present statute clear enough that resort to the rule of lenity is not warranted.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor the foregoing reasons, we conclude that convictions under 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77206(1) and (2) in which the revenue loss to the Government exceeds $10,000 qualify as aggravated felonies pursuant to \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)(i). Because the Kawashimas are subject to deportation as aliens who have been convicted of aggravated felonies pursuant to \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(A)(iii), the judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIn interpreting \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M), I would rely upon the familiar canon that statutes should be interpreted to avoid superfluity. See Corley v. United States, \n\n556 U. S. 303, \n314 (2009)\n (quoting Hibbs v. Winn, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 88, \n101 (2004)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]ne of the most basic interpretive canons\xe2\x80\x9d is that a \xe2\x80\x9cstatute should be construed so that effect is given to all its provisions, so that no part will be inoperative or superfluous, void or insignificant . . . .\xe2\x80\x9d)). If Clause (i) is construed to apply to tax crimes, then Clause (ii)\xe2\x80\x99s discrete inclusion of tax evasion would add nothing, for tax evasion is itself an offense that, in all actual instances of which the Government is aware, \xe2\x80\x9cinvolves fraud or deceit.\xe2\x80\x9d See \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)(i); Tr. of Oral Arg. 30\xe2\x80\x9331.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe elements of tax evasion are the existence of a tax deficiency, willfulness, and \xe2\x80\x9can affirmative act constituting an evasion or attempted evasion of the tax.\xe2\x80\x9d Sansone v. United States, \n\n380 U.\xc2\xa0S. 343, \n351 (1965)\n. As this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions indicate, the evasion of taxes involves deceit or fraud upon the Government, achieved by concealing a tax liability or misleading the Government as to the extent of the liability. See, e.g., Spies v. United States, \n\n317 U. S. 492, \n499 (1943)\n (an act of tax evasion may be \xe2\x80\x9cany conduct, the likely effect of which would be to mislead or to conceal\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, courts have determined that tax evasion is a crime of moral turpitude, because it necessarily involves fraud. See, e.g., Carty v. Ashcroft, 395 F.\xc2\xa03d 1081, 1085, n. 7 (CA9 2005) (fraud is \xe2\x80\x9cimplicit in the nature of the crime\xe2\x80\x9d of tax evasion); Considine v. United States, 683 F.\xc2\xa02d 1285, 1287 (CA9 1982) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe express language of section 7201 requires an intent to avoid tax (a legitimate synonym for fraud).\xe2\x80\x9d); Costello v. INS, 311 F.\xc2\xa02d 343, 348 (CA2 1962) (\xe2\x80\x9cThere can be no \xe2\x80\x98wilful\xe2\x80\x99 [tax] eva- sion without a specific intent to defraud.\xe2\x80\x9d), rev\xe2\x80\x99d on other grounds, \n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 120 (1964)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven more to the point, courts have held that a conviction for tax evasion under \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77201 \xe2\x80\x9cconclusively establishes fraud in a subsequent civil tax fraud proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Gray v. Commissioner, 708 F.\xc2\xa02d 243, 246 (CA6 1983); see Klein v. Commissioner, 880 F.\xc2\xa02d 260, 262 (CA10 1989) (conviction under 7201 \xe2\x80\x9ccollaterally estops a taxpayer from denying fraud [in a] civil tax case involving the same years\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t This preclusive effect obtains, courts have explained, because \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98willful\xe2\x80\x99 [tax evasion] includes all of the elements of fraud.\xe2\x80\x9d Tomlinson v. Lefkowitz, 334 F.\xc2\xa02d 262, 265 (CA5 1964); see Gray, 708 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 246 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe elements of criminal tax evasion and civil tax fraud are identical.\xe2\x80\x9d); Moore v. United States, 360 F.\xc2\xa02d 353, 356 (CA4 1966) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hile the criminal evasion statute does not explicitly require a finding of fraud, the case-by-case process of construction of the civil [fraud] and criminal tax provisions has demonstrated that their constituent elements are identical.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTax offenses span a wide range, from failure to file a tax return, \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77203, to the unauthorized use of tax stamps, \xc2\xa77209. But \xe2\x80\x9cthe gravest of offenses against the revenues,\xe2\x80\x9d this Court has said, the \xe2\x80\x9ccapstone\xe2\x80\x9d of tax law violations, is tax evasion. Spies, 317 U. S., at 497, 499; see Boulware v. United States, \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 421, \n424 (2008)\n. Tellingly, the Kawashimas pleaded guilty to a crime carrying a maximum prison term of three years, \xc2\xa77206; for tax evasion, the maximum term is five years, \xc2\xa77201. It is thus understandable that Congress would single out tax evasion, as it did in Clause (ii), specifically designating it, and no other tax crime, an \xe2\x80\x9caggravated felony\xe2\x80\x9d for deportation purposes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court ascribes a different purpose to Clause (ii). Tax evasion, made criminal by \xc2\xa77201, the Court states, \xe2\x80\x9calmost invariably,\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9cnot necessarily[,] involve[s] fraud or deceit.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. But see supra, at 4 and this page. Congress likely included Clause (ii), the Court suggests, simply \xe2\x80\x9cto remove any doubt that tax evasion qualifies as an aggravated felony.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. In other words, in holding that Clause (i) includes tax offenses, the Court finds Clause (ii) largely, but not totally, redundant.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn support of the notion that tax evasion can occur without fraud or deceit, the Court cites United States v. Scharton, \n\n285 U.\xc2\xa0S. 518 (1932)\n; see ante, at 9. In that long-obsolete case, the Court rejected the Government\xe2\x80\x99s plea for the application of an extended limitation period to a prosecution for tax evasion. The generally applicable statute of limitations was three years; for tax offenses that involve defrauding the United States, however, the limitation period was six years. An averment of intent to defraud, the Court said in Scharton, would be \xe2\x80\x9csurplusage,\xe2\x80\x9d for it would suffice \xe2\x80\x9cto plead and prove a wilful attempt to evade or defeat.\xe2\x80\x9d 285 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 521.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCourts had limited Scharton to its statute of limita- tions context several decades before Congress enacted \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M) in 1994. See Tseung Chu v. Cornell, 247 F.\xc2\xa02d 929, 936, n. 6 (CA9 1957) (distinguishing Scharton and holding that tax evasion is a crime of moral turpitude because it entails fraud); Lefkowitz, 334 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 265 (distinguishing Scharton and holding that tax evasion necessarily involves fraud). Moreover, Congress, since 1954, has expressly prescribed a six-year limitation period for tax evasion. See \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76531(2). In short, Scharton is a cryptic, thinly reasoned opinion, one that did not influence subsequent federal-court description of the crime of tax evasion. The suggestion that Congress may have worried about Scharton when framing legislation over 60 years later is hardly credible.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court presents another reason, drawn from the Government\xe2\x80\x99s brief, why Congress may have treated tax evasion discretely, while embracing tax crimes generally within the Clause (i) category. Section 7201 covers both evasion of assessment and evasion of payment. Imagine a taxpayer who files a truthful return, then moves her assets to a place \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond the reach of the Internal Revenue Service.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10; see Brief for Respondent 34. The Court acknowledges that evasion-of-payment cases almost always \xe2\x80\x9cinvolve some affirmative acts of fraud or deceit.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. Still, there may be a rare case in which that is not so. Rare, indeed; imaginary would be an apt char- acterization. The Government conceded that, to its knowledge, there have been no actual instances of indictments for tax evasion unaccompanied by any act of fraud or deceit. Tr. of Oral Arg. 30\xe2\x80\x9331.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe canon that statutes should be interpreted to avoid su- perfluity cannot be skirted as easily as the Government here urges. We have declined to interpret legislation in a way that \xe2\x80\x9cwould in practical effect render [a provision] entirely superfluous in all but the most unusual circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d TRW Inc. v. Andrews, \n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 19, \n29 (2001)\n. It is hardly sufficient for the Government to hypothesize a case in which the provision might have some independent role. See id., at 30. Where, as here, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Government concede[s] that the independent function one could attribute to the [provision] would [rarely] arise,\xe2\x80\x9d a construction moored to a case \xe2\x80\x9cmost unlikely\xe2\x80\x9d to exist should be rejected. Id., at 31. It is highly improbable that \xe2\x80\x9ca pro- viso accounting for more than half of [the] text\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M), i.e., Clause (ii), \xe2\x80\x9cwould lie dormant in all but the most unlikely situations.\xe2\x80\x9d See 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 31.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress\xe2\x80\x99 aim in drafting \xc2\xa71101(a)(43) was to determine which crimes are sufficiently serious to warrant the \xe2\x80\x9cdrastic measure\xe2\x80\x9d of deportation, and which are not. See Fong Haw Tan, 333 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 10. It is implausible that Congress, when drafting \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M), intended to address, or was even aware of, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s scenario: a taxpayer who files a truthful return, then, to thwart collection of the tax due, moves all her assets offshore. Far more likely, Congress did not intend to include tax offenses in \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)(i), but instead drafted that provision to address fraudulent schemes against private victims, then added \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)(ii) so that the \xe2\x80\x9ccapstone\xe2\x80\x9d tax offense against the Government also qualified as an aggravated felony. See supra, at 5.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the statute is even less plausible given the numerous offenses it would rank as \xe2\x80\x9caggravated felon[ies].\xe2\x80\x9d Many federal tax offenses, like \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77206, involve false statements or misleading conduct. See, e.g., \xc2\xa77202 (failing to truthfully account for and pay taxes owed). Conviction of any of these offenses, if the Court\xe2\x80\x99s construction were correct, would render an alien deportable. So would conviction of state and local tax offenses involving false statements. Ferreira v. Ashcroft, 390 F.\xc2\xa03d 1091, 1096\xe2\x80\x931097 (CA9 2004) (state-law offenses qualify as offenses involving fraud or deceit under \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M)); see, e.g., Del. Code Ann., Tit. 30, \xc2\xa7574 (2009) (submitting a tax return false as to any material matter is a criminal offense); D. C. Code \xc2\xa747\xe2\x80\x934106 (2001\xe2\x80\x932005) (same); Ala. Code \xc2\xa740\xe2\x80\x9329\xe2\x80\x93114 (2003) (same); Va. Code Ann. \xc2\xa758.1\xe2\x80\x931815 (2009) (willfully failing to account truthfully for and pay certain taxes is a criminal offense).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRendering all tax offenses involving false statements \xe2\x80\x9caggravated felon[ies]\xe2\x80\x9d that subject an alien to deportation is all the more problematic, for many of these offenses are misdemeanors. Among federal misdemeanors, see, e.g., \n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77204 (\xe2\x80\x9cfurnish[ing] a false\xe2\x80\x9d W-2 form to an employee); \xc2\xa77205 (\xe2\x80\x9csuppl[ying] false or fraudulent information\xe2\x80\x9d to an employer); \xc2\xa77207 (filing a return \xe2\x80\x9cknown . . . to be false as to any material matter\xe2\x80\x9d). On the state and local level, see, e.g., Cal. Rev. & Tax. Code Ann. \xc2\xa71610.4 (West 1998) (\xe2\x80\x9cEvery person who wilfully states anything which he knows to be false in any oral or written statement, not under oath, required or authorized to be made as the basis of an application to reduce any tax or assessment, is guilty of a misdemeanor.\xe2\x80\x9d); N. D. Cent. Code Ann. \xc2\xa757\xe2\x80\x9337.116 (Lexis 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cEvery person who willfully and knowingly subscribes or makes any false statement of facts [on an estate tax return] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is guilty of a class A misdemeanor.\xe2\x80\x9d); Columbus, Ohio City Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7361.31(a)(4), (b), (d) (2009) (any person who \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly make[s] and file[s] an incomplete, false or fraudulent [municipal] return\xe2\x80\x9d is guilty of a fourth-degree misdemeanor). Nor would the $10,000 threshold set in \n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(a)(43)(M) prevent deportation for tax crimes far less serious than willful tax evasion, for as many as six years may be included in the amount-of-loss calculation. See \n26 U. S. C. \xc2\xa76531 (setting a six-year statute of limitations for, inter alia, tax crimes involving fraud or falsity); Brief for Johnnie M. Walters as Amicus Curiae 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (hereinafter Walters Brief).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision has adverse consequences for the efficient handling of tax prosecutions. It is often easier for the Government to obtain a conviction under \xc2\xa77206 (false statements) than under \xc2\xa77201 (tax evasion). See United States v. Olgin, 745 F. 2d 263, 272 (CA3 1984) (unlike a conviction under \xc2\xa77201, a conviction under \xc2\xa77206 does not require proof of a tax deficiency); Considine, 683 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1287 (unlike a conviction under \xc2\xa77201, a conviction under \xc2\xa77206 does not require proof of an attempt to escape a tax). For this reason, the Government has allowed taxpayers to plead guilty to a \xc2\xa77206 charge in lieu of going to trial under \xc2\xa77201 on an evasion charge. See Walters Brief 19\xe2\x80\x9320. Deportation consequences are important to aliens facing criminal charges. See Padilla v. Kentucky, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. __, __ (2010) (slip op., at 10) (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]reserving the client\xe2\x80\x99s right to remain in the United States may be more important to the client than any potential jail sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting St. Cyr, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 322)). If a \xc2\xa77206 charge carries the same prospect of deportation as a \xc2\xa77201 charge, then an alien\xe2\x80\x99s incentive to plead guilty to any tax offense is significantly reduced.\n\t\t\t
