UNITED STATES ARMY CORPS OF ENGINEERS v. HAWKES CO., INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93290.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 30, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 31, 2016

\n

The Clean Water Act regulates \xe2\x80\x9cthe discharge of any pollutant\xe2\x80\x9d into \xe2\x80\x9cthe waters of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 33 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71311(a), 1362(7), (12). When property contains such waters, landowners who discharge pollutants without a permit from the Army Corps of Engineers risk substantial criminal and civil penalties, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71319(c), (d), while those who do apply for a permit face a process that is often arduous, expensive, and long. It can be difficult to determine in the first place, however, whether \xe2\x80\x9cwaters of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d are present. During the time period relevant to this case, for example, the Corps defined that term to include all wetlands, the \xe2\x80\x9cuse, degradation or destruction of which could affect interstate or foreign commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d 33 CFR \xc2\xa7328.3(a)(3). Because of that difficulty, the Corps allows property owners to obtain a standalone \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional determination\xe2\x80\x9d (JD) specifying whether a particular property contains \xe2\x80\x9cwaters of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7331.2. A JD may be either \xe2\x80\x9cpreliminary,\xe2\x80\x9d advising a property owner that such waters \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d be present, or \xe2\x80\x9capproved,\xe2\x80\x9d definitively \xe2\x80\x9cstating the presence or absence\xe2\x80\x9d of such waters. Ibid. An \xe2\x80\x9capproved\xe2\x80\x9d JD is considered an administratively appealable \xe2\x80\x9cfinal agency action,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7320.1(a)(6), 331.2, and is binding for five years on both the Corps and the Environmental Protection Agency, 33 CFR pt. 331, App. C; EPA, Memorandum of Agreement: Exemptions Under Section 404(F) of the Clean Water Act \xc2\xa7VI\xe2\x80\x93A.

\n

Respondents, three companies engaged in mining peat, sought a permit from the Corps to discharge material onto wetlands located on property that respondents own and hope to mine. In connection with the permitting process, respondents obtained an approved JD from the Corps stating that the property contained \xe2\x80\x9cwaters of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d because its wetlands had a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant nexus\xe2\x80\x9d to the Red River of the North, located some 120 miles away. After exhausting administrative remedies, respondents sought review of the approved JD in Federal District Court under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA), but the District Court dismissed for want of jurisdiction, holding that the revised JD was not a \xe2\x80\x9cfinal agency action for which there is no other adequate remedy in a court,\xe2\x80\x9d 5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7704. The Eighth Circuit reversed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Corps\xe2\x80\x99 approved JD is a final agency action judicially reviewable under the APA. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0In general, two conditions must be satisfied for an agency action to be \xe2\x80\x9cfinal\xe2\x80\x9d under the APA: \xe2\x80\x9cFirst, the action must mark the consummation of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s decisionmaking process,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csecond, the action must be one by which rights or obligations have been determined, or from which legal consequences will flow.\xe2\x80\x9d Bennett v. Spear,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 154\n\xe2\x80\x93178. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0An approved JD satisfies Bennett\xe2\x80\x99s first condition. It clearly \xe2\x80\x9cmark[s] the consummation\xe2\x80\x9d of the Corps\xe2\x80\x99 decisionmaking on the question whether a particular property does or does not contain \xe2\x80\x9cwaters of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d It is issued after extensive factfinding by the Corps regarding the physical and hydrological characteristics of the property, see U. S. Army Corps of Engineers, Jurisdictional Determination Form Instructional Guidebook 47\xe2\x80\x9360, and typically remains valid for a period of five years, see 33 CFR pt. 331, App. C. The Corps itself describes approved JDs as \xe2\x80\x9cfinal agency action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id. \xc2\xa7320.1(a)(6). Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0The definitive nature of approved JDs also gives rise to \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and appreciable legal consequences,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby satisfying Bennett\xe2\x80\x99s second condition as well. 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 178. A \xe2\x80\x9cnegative\xe2\x80\x9d JD\xe2\x80\x94i.e., an approved JD stating that property does not contain jurisdictional waters\xe2\x80\x94creates a five-year safe harbor from civil enforcement proceedings brought by the Government and limits the potential liability a property owner faces for violating the Clean Water Act. See 33 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71319, 1365(a). Each of those effects is a legal consequence. It follows that an \xe2\x80\x9caffirmative\xe2\x80\x9d JD, like the one issued here, also has legal consequences: It deprives property owners of the five-year safe harbor that \xe2\x80\x9cnegative\xe2\x80\x9d JDs afford. This conclusion tracks the \xe2\x80\x9cpragmatic\xe2\x80\x9d approach the Court has long taken to finality. Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner,\n\n387 U.\xc2\xa0S. 136\n. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0A \xe2\x80\x9cfinal\xe2\x80\x9d agency action is reviewable under the APA only if there are no adequate alternatives to APA review in court. The Corps contends that respondents have two such alternatives: They may proceed without a permit and argue in a Government enforcement action that a permit was not required, or they may complete the permit process and then seek judicial review, which, the Corps suggests, is what Congress envisioned. Neither alternative is adequate. Parties need not await enforcement proceedings before challenging final agency action where such proceedings carry the risk of \xe2\x80\x9cserious criminal and civil penalties.\xe2\x80\x9d Abbott, 387 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 153. And the permitting process is not only costly and lengthy, but also irrelevant to the finality of the approved JD and its suitability for judicial review. Furthermore, because the Clean Water Act makes no reference to standalone jurisdictional determinations, there is little basis for inferring anything from it concerning their reviewability. Given \xe2\x80\x9cthe APA\xe2\x80\x99s presumption of reviewability for all final agency action,\xe2\x80\x9d Sackett v. EPA, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he mere fact\xe2\x80\x9d that permitting decisions are reviewable is insufficient to imply \xe2\x80\x9cexclusion as to other[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d agency actions, such as approved JDs, Abbott, 387 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 141. Pp. 8\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

782 F.\xc2\xa03d 994, affirmed.

UNITED STATES ARMY CORPS OF ENGINEERS, PETITIONER v. HAWKES CO., INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

[May 31, 2016]

\n

\n

Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The Clean Water Act regulates the discharge of pollutants into \xe2\x80\x9cthe waters of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 33 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71311(a), 1362(7), (12). Because it can be difficult to determine whether a particular parcel of property contains such waters, the U.\xc2\xa0S. Army Corps of Engineers will issue to property owners an \xe2\x80\x9capproved jurisdictional determination\xe2\x80\x9d stating the agency\xe2\x80\x99s definitive view on that matter. See 33 CFR \xc2\xa7331.2 and pt. 331, App. C (2015). The question presented is whether that determination is final agency action judicially reviewable under the Administrative Procedure Act,\n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7704.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The Clean Water Act prohibits \xe2\x80\x9cthe discharge of any pollutant\xe2\x80\x9d without a permit into \xe2\x80\x9cnavigable waters,\xe2\x80\x9d which it defines, in turn, as \xe2\x80\x9cthe waters of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 33 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71311(a), 1362(7), (12). During the time period relevant to this case, the U.\xc2\xa0S. Army Corps of Engineers defined the waters of the United States to include land areas occasionally or regularly saturated with water\xe2\x80\x94such as \xe2\x80\x9cmudflats, sandflats, wetlands, sloughs, prairie potholes, wet meadows, [and] playa lakes\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9cuse, degradation or destruction of which could affect interstate or foreign commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d 33 CFR \xc2\xa7328.3(a)(3) (2012). The Corps has applied that definition to assert jurisdiction over \xe2\x80\x9c270-to-300 million acres of swampy lands in the United States\xe2\x80\x94including half of Alaska and an area the size of California in the lower 48 States.\xe2\x80\x9d Rapanos v. United States,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 715,\n722 (2006)\n (plurality opinion).[1]

\n

It is often difficult to determine whether a particular piece of property contains waters of the United States, but there are important consequences if it does. The Clean Water Act imposes substantial criminal and civil penalties for discharging any pollutant into waters covered by the Act without a permit from the Corps. See 33 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71311(a), 1319(c), (d), 1344(a). The costs of obtaining such a permit are significant. For a specialized \xe2\x80\x9cindivid-ual\xe2\x80\x9d permit of the sort at issue in this case, for example, one study found that the average applicant \xe2\x80\x9cspends 788 days and $271,596 in completing the process,\xe2\x80\x9d without \xe2\x80\x9ccounting costs of mitigation or design changes.\xe2\x80\x9d Rapanos, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 721. Even more readily available \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral\xe2\x80\x9d permits took applicants, on average, 313 days and $28,915 to complete. Ibid. See generally 33 CFR \xc2\xa7323.2(h) (limiting \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral\xe2\x80\x9d permits to activities that \xe2\x80\x9ccause only minimal individual and cumulative environmental impacts\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The Corps specifies whether particular property contains \xe2\x80\x9cwaters of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d by issuing \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional determinations\xe2\x80\x9d (JDs) on a case-by-case basis. \xc2\xa7331.2. JDs come in two varieties: \xe2\x80\x9cpreliminary\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9capproved.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. While preliminary JDs merely advise a property owner \xe2\x80\x9cthat there may be waters of the United States on a parcel,\xe2\x80\x9d approved JDs definitively \xe2\x80\x9cstat[e] the presence or absence\xe2\x80\x9d of such waters. Ibid. (emphasis added). Unlike preliminary JDs, approved JDs can be administratively appealed and are defined by regulation to \xe2\x80\x9cconstitute a Corps final agency action.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7320.1(a)(6), 331.2. They are binding for five years on both the Corps and the Environmental Protection Agency, which share authority to enforce the Clean Water Act. See 33 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71319, 1344(s); 33 CFR pt. 331, App. C; EPA, Memorandum of Agreement: Exemptions Under Section 404(F) of the Clean Water Act \xc2\xa7VI\xe2\x80\x93A (1989) (Memorandum of Agreement).

\n

B

\n

Respondents are three companies engaged in mining peat in Marshall County, Minnesota. Peat is an organic material that forms in waterlogged grounds, such as wetlands and bogs. See Xuehui & Jinming, Peat and Peatlands, in 2 Coal, Oil Shale, Natural Bitumen, Heavy Oil and Peat 267\xe2\x80\x93272 (G. Jinsheng ed. 2009) (Peat and Peatlands). It is widely used for soil improvement and burned as fuel. Id., at 277. It can also be used to provide structural support and moisture for smooth, stable greens that leave golfers with no one to blame but themselves for errant putts. See Monteith & Welton, Use of Peat and Other Organic Materials on Golf Courses, 13 Bulletin of the United States Golf Association Green Section 90, 95\xe2\x80\x93100 (1933). At the same time, peat mining can have significant environmental and ecological impacts, see Peat and Peatlands 280\xe2\x80\x93281, and therefore is regulated by both federal and state environmental protection agencies, see, e.g., Minn. Stat. \xc2\xa7103G.231 (2014).

\n

Respondents own a 530-acre tract near their existing mining operations. The tract includes wetlands, which respondents believe contain sufficient high quality peat, suitable for use in golf greens, to extend their mining operations for 10 to 15 years. App. 8, 14\xe2\x80\x9315, 31.

\n

In December 2010, respondents applied to the Corps for a Section 404 permit for the property. Id., at 15. A Section 404 permit authorizes \xe2\x80\x9cthe discharge of dredged or fill material into the navigable waters at specified disposal sites.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n33 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71344(a). Over the course of several communications with respondents, Corps officials signaled that the permitting process would be very expensive and take years to complete. The Corps also advised respondents that, if they wished to pursue their application, they would have to submit numerous assessments of various features of the property, which respondents estimate would cost more than $100,000. App. 16\xe2\x80\x9317, 31\xe2\x80\x9335.

\n

In February 2012, in connection with the permitting process, the Corps issued an approved JD stating that the property contained \xe2\x80\x9cwater of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d because its wetlands had a \xe2\x80\x9csignificant nexus\xe2\x80\x9d to the Red River of the North, located some 120 miles away. Id., at 13, 18, 20. Respondents appealed the JD to the Corps\xe2\x80\x99 Mississippi Valley Division Commander, who remanded for further factfinding. On remand, the Corps reaffirmed its original conclusion and issued a revised JD to that effect. Id., at 18\xe2\x80\x9320; App. to Pet. for Cert. 44a\xe2\x80\x9345a.

\n

Respondents then sought judicial review of the revised JD under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA),\n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7500 et\xc2\xa0seq. The District Court dismissed for want of subject matter jurisdiction, holding that the revised JD was not \xe2\x80\x9cfinal agency action for which there is no other adequate remedy in a court,\xe2\x80\x9d as required by the APA prior to judicial review,\n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7704. 963 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 868, 872, 878 (Minn. 2013). The Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit reversed, 782 F.\xc2\xa03d 994, 1002 (2015), and we granted certiorari, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

II

\n

The Corps contends that the revised JD is not \xe2\x80\x9cfinal agency action\xe2\x80\x9d and that, even if it were, there are adequate alternatives for challenging it in court. We disagree at both turns.

\n

A

\n

In Bennett v. Spear,\n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 154 (1997)\n, we distilled from our precedents two conditions that generally must be satisfied for agency action to be \xe2\x80\x9cfinal\xe2\x80\x9d under the APA. \xe2\x80\x9cFirst, the action must mark the consummation of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s decisionmaking process\xe2\x80\x94it must not be of a merely tentative or interlocutory nature. And second, the action must be one by which rights or obligations have been determined, or from which legal consequences will flow.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 177\xe2\x80\x93178 (internal quotation marks and citation omitted).[2]

\n

The Corps does not dispute that an approved JD satisfies the first Bennett condition. Unlike preliminary JDs\xe2\x80\x94 which are \xe2\x80\x9cadvisory in nature\xe2\x80\x9d and simply indicate that \xe2\x80\x9cthere may be waters of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d on a parcel of property, 33 CFR \xc2\xa7331.2\xe2\x80\x94an approved JD clearly \xe2\x80\x9cmark[s] the consummation\xe2\x80\x9d of the Corps\xe2\x80\x99 decisionmaking process on that question, Bennett, 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 178 (internal quotation marks omitted). It is issued after extensive factfinding by the Corps regarding the physical and hydrological characteristics of the property, see U.\xc2\xa0S. Army Corps of Engineers, Jurisdictional Determination Form Instructional Guidebook 47\xe2\x80\x9360 (2007), and is typically not revisited if the permitting process moves forward. Indeed, the Corps itself describes approved JDs as \xe2\x80\x9cfinal agency action,\xe2\x80\x9d see 33 CFR \xc2\xa7320.1(a)(6), and specifies that an approved JD \xe2\x80\x9cwill remain valid for a period of five years,\xe2\x80\x9d Corps, Regulatory Guidance Letter No. 05\xe2\x80\x9302, \xc2\xa71(a), p.\xc2\xa01 (June 14, 2005) (2005 Guidance Letter); see also 33 CFR pt. 331, App. C.

\n

The Corps may revise an approved JD within the five-year period based on \xe2\x80\x9cnew information.\xe2\x80\x9d 2005 Guidance Letter \xc2\xa71(a), at 1. That possibility, however, is a common characteristic of agency action, and does not make an otherwise definitive decision nonfinal. See Sackett v. EPA, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012); see also National Cable & Telecommunications Assn. v. Brand X Internet Services,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 967,\n981 (2005)\n. By issuing respondents an approved JD, the Corps for all practical purposes \xe2\x80\x9chas ruled definitively\xe2\x80\x9d that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 property contains jurisdictional waters. Sackett, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (Ginsburg, J., concurring) (slip op., at 1).

\n

The definitive nature of approved JDs also gives rise to \xe2\x80\x9cdirect and appreciable legal consequences,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby satisfying the second prong of Bennett. 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 178. Consider the effect of an approved JD stating that a party\xe2\x80\x99s property does not contain jurisdictional waters\xe2\x80\x94a \xe2\x80\x9cnegative\xe2\x80\x9d JD, in Corps parlance. As noted, such a JD will generally bind the Corps for five years. See 33 CFR pt. 331, App. C; 2005 Guidance Letter \xc2\xa71. Under a longstanding memorandum of agreement between the Corps and EPA, it will also be \xe2\x80\x9cbinding on the Government and represent the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position in any subsequent Federal action or litigation concerning that final determination.\xe2\x80\x9d Memorandum of Agreement \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7IV\xe2\x80\x93C\xe2\x80\x932, VI\xe2\x80\x93A. A negative JD thus binds the two agencies authorized to bring civil enforcement proceedings under the Clean Water Act, see\n\n33 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71319, creating a five-year safe harbor from such proceedings for a property owner. Additionally, although the property owner may still face a citizen suit under the Act, such a suit\xe2\x80\x94unlike actions brought by the Government\xe2\x80\x94cannot impose civil liability for wholly past violations. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71319(d), 1365(a); Gwaltney of Smithfield, Ltd. v. Chesapeake Bay Foundation, Inc.,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 49\n\xe2\x80\x9359 (1987). In other words, a negative JD both narrows the field of potential plaintiffs and limits the potential liability a landowner faces for discharging pollutants without a permit. Each of those effects is a \xe2\x80\x9clegal consequence[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d satisfying the second Bennett prong. 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 178; see also Sackett, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___.

\n

It follows that affirmative JDs have legal consequences as well: They represent the denial of the safe harbor that negative JDs afford. See\n\n5 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7551(13) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cagency action\xe2\x80\x9d to include an agency \xe2\x80\x9crule, order, license, sanction, relief, or the equivalent,\xe2\x80\x9d or the \xe2\x80\x9cdenial thereof\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Because \xe2\x80\x9clegal consequences . . . flow\xe2\x80\x9d from approved JDs, they constitute final agency action. Bennett, 520 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 178 (internal quotation marks omitted).[3]

\n

This conclusion tracks the \xe2\x80\x9cpragmatic\xe2\x80\x9d approach we have long taken to finality. Abbott Laboratories v. Gardner,\n\n387 U.\xc2\xa0S. 136,\n149 (1967)\n. For example, in Frozen Food Express v. United States,\n\n351 U.\xc2\xa0S. 40 (1956)\n, we considered the finality of an order specifying which commodities the Interstate Commerce Commission believed were exempt by statute from regulation, and which it believed were not. Although the order \xe2\x80\x9chad no authority except to give notice of how the Commission interpreted\xe2\x80\x9d the relevant statute, and \xe2\x80\x9cwould have effect only if and when a particular action was brought against a particular carrier,\xe2\x80\x9d Abbott, 387 U. S., at 150, we held that the order was nonetheless immediately reviewable, Frozen Food, 351 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 44\xe2\x80\x9345. The order, we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cwarns every carrier, who does not have authority from the Commission to transport those commodities, that it does so at the risk of incurring criminal penalties.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 44. So too here, while no administrative or criminal proceeding can be brought for failure to conform to the approved JD itself, that final agency determination not only deprives respondents of a five-year safe harbor from liability under the Act, but warns that if they discharge pollutants onto their property without obtaining a permit from the Corps, they do so at the risk of significant criminal and civil penalties.

\n

B

\n

Even if final, an agency action is reviewable under the APA only if there are no adequate alternatives to APA review in court.\n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7704. The Corps contends that respondents have two such alternatives: either discharge fill material without a permit, risking an EPA enforcement action during which they can argue that no permit was required, or apply for a permit and seek judicial review if dissatisfied with the results. Brief for Petitioner 45\xe2\x80\x9351.

\n

Neither alternative is adequate. As we have long held, parties need not await enforcement proceedings before challenging final agency action where such proceedings carry the risk of \xe2\x80\x9cserious criminal and civil penalties.\xe2\x80\x9d Abbott, 387 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 153. If respondents discharged fill material without a permit, in the mistaken belief that their property did not contain jurisdictional waters, they would expose themselves to civil penalties of up to $37,500 for each day they violated the Act, to say nothing of potential criminal liability. See 33 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71319(c), (d); Sackett, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa01 (citing 74 Fed. Reg. 626, 627 (2009)). Respondents need not assume such risks while waiting for EPA to \xe2\x80\x9cdrop the hammer\xe2\x80\x9d in order to have their day in court. Sackett, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 6).

\n

Nor is it an adequate alternative to APA review for a landowner to apply for a permit and then seek judicial review in the event of an unfavorable decision. As Corps officials indicated in their discussions with respondents, the permitting process can be arduous, expensive, and long. See Rapanos, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 721 (plurality opinion). On top of the standard permit application that respondents were required to submit, see 33 CFR \xc2\xa7325.1(d) (detailing contents of permit application), the Corps demanded that they undertake, among other things, a \xe2\x80\x9chydrogeologic assessment of the rich fen system including the mineral/nutrient composition and pH of the groundwater; groundwater flow spatially and vertically; discharge and recharge areas\xe2\x80\x9d; a \xe2\x80\x9cfunctional/resource assessment of the site including a vegetation survey and identification of native fen plan communities across the site\xe2\x80\x9d; an \xe2\x80\x9cinven-tory of similar wetlands in the general area (watershed), including some analysis of their quality\xe2\x80\x9d; and an \xe2\x80\x9cinven-tory of rich fen plant communities that are within sites of High and Outstanding Biodiversity Significance in the area.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 33\xe2\x80\x9334. Respondents estimate that undertaking these analyses alone would cost more than $100,000. Id., at 17. And whatever pertinence all this might have to the issuance of a permit, none of it will alter the finality of the approved JD, or affect its suitability for judicial review. The permitting process adds nothing to the JD.

\n

The Corps nevertheless argues that Congress made the \xe2\x80\x9cevident[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d decision in the Clean Water Act that a coverage determination would be made \xe2\x80\x9cas part of the permitting process, and that the property owner would obtain any necessary judicial review of that determination at the conclusion of that process.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 46. But as the Corps acknowledges, the Clean Water Act makes no reference to standalone jurisdictional determinations, ibid., so there is little basis for inferring anything from it concerning the reviewability of such distinct final agency action. And given \xe2\x80\x9cthe APA\xe2\x80\x99s presumption of reviewability for all final agency action,\xe2\x80\x9d Sackett, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8), \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he mere fact\xe2\x80\x9d that permitting decisions are \xe2\x80\x9creviewable should not suffice to support an implication of exclusion as to other[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d agency actions, such as approved JDs, Abbott, 387 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 141 (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Sackett, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 8) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f the express provision of judicial review in one section of a long and complicated statute were alone enough to overcome the APA\xe2\x80\x99s presumption of reviewability .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , it would not be much of a presumption at all\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Finally, the Corps emphasizes that seeking review in an enforcement action or at the end of the permitting process would be the only available avenues for obtaining review \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the Corps had never adopted its practice of issuing standalone jurisdictional determinations upon request.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 3; see also id., at 4, 23. True enough. But such a \xe2\x80\x9ccount your blessings\xe2\x80\x9d argument is not an adequate rejoinder to the assertion of a right to judicial review under the APA.

\n

The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit is affirmed.

My join extends to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in full. The following observation seems appropriate not to qualify what the Court says but to point out that, based on the Government\xe2\x80\x99s representations in this case, the reach and systemic consequences of the Clean Water Act remain a cause for concern. As Justice Alito has noted in an earlier case, the Act\xe2\x80\x99s reach is \xe2\x80\x9cnotoriously unclear\xe2\x80\x9d and the consequences to landowners even for inadvertent violations can be crushing. An approved Jurisdictional Determination (JD) gives a landowner at least some measure of predictability, so long as the agency\xe2\x80\x99s declaration can be relied upon. Yet, the Government has represented in this litigation that a JD has no legally binding effect on the Environmental Protection Agency\xe2\x80\x99s (EPA) enforcement decisions. It has stated that the memorandum of agreement between the EPA and the Army Corps of Engineers, which today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion relies on, does not have binding effect and can be revoked or amended at the Agency\xe2\x80\x99s unfettered discretion. Reply Brief 12; Tr. of Oral Arg. 16. If that were correct, the Act\xe2\x80\x99s ominous reach would again be unchecked by the limited relief the Court allows today. Even if, in an ordinary case, an agency\xe2\x80\x99s internal agreement with another agency cannot establish that its action is final, the Court is right to construe a JD as binding in light of the fact that in many instances it will have a significant bearing on whether the Clean Water Act comports with due process.

\n

The Act, especially without the JD procedure were the Government permitted to foreclose it, continues to raise troubling questions regarding the Government\xe2\x80\x99s power to cast doubt on the full use and enjoyment of private prop-erty throughout the Nation.
