SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

PATRICK GLEBE, SUPERINTENDENT, STAFFORD CREEK CORRECTIONS CENTER v.JOSHUA JAMES FROST

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

No. 14\xe2\x80\x9395\xe2\x80\x83Decided November 17, 2014

Per Curiam.

Over 11 days in April 2003, respondent Joshua Frost helped two associates commit a series of armed robberies in the State of Washington. In the main, Frost drove his confederates to and from their crimes. On one occasion, he also entered the house being robbed. On another, he performed surveillance in anticipation of the robbery.

Washington charged Frost with robbery and related offenses. Taking the witness stand, Frost admitted to his involvement, but claimed he acted under duress. As closing arguments drew near, however, Frost\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer expressed the desire to contend both (1) that the State failed to meet its burden of proving that Frost was an accomplice to the crimes and (2) that Frost acted under duress. The trial judge insisted that the defense choose between these alternative arguments, explaining that state law prohib-ited a defendant from simultaneously contesting the elements of the crime and presenting the affirmative defense of duress. So Frost\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer limited his summation to duress. The jury convicted Frost of six counts of robbery, one count of attempted robbery, one count of burglary, and two counts of assault.

The Washington Supreme Court sustained Frost\xe2\x80\x99s conviction. It rejected the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s view that state law prohibited Frost from simultaneously contesting criminal liability and arguing duress. State v. Frost, 160 Wash. 2d 765, 773\xe2\x80\x93776, 161 P.\xc2\xa03d 361, 366\xe2\x80\x93368 (2007) (en banc). By preventing the defense from presenting both theories during summation, it said, the trial court violated the National Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process and Assistance of Counsel Clauses. Id., at 777\xe2\x80\x93779, 161 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 368\xe2\x80\x93369. But the State Supreme Court continued, this improper restriction of closing argument qualified as a trial error (a mistake reviewable for harmlessness) rather than a structural error (a mistake that requires automatic reversal). Id., at 779\xe2\x80\x93782, 161 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 369\xe2\x80\x93370. Because the jury heard three taped confessions and Frost\xe2\x80\x99s admission of guilt on the witness stand, and because it received proper instructions on the State\xe2\x80\x99s burden of proof, the State Supreme Court held that any error was harmless beyond a reasonable doubt. Id., at 782\xe2\x80\x93783, 161 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 370\xe2\x80\x93371.

Frost filed a petition for writ of habeas corpus under28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. The District Court dismissed the petition, App. to Pet. for Cert. 76a, and a panel of the Court of Appeals affirmed, Frost v. Van Boening, 692 F.\xc2\xa03d 924 (CA9 2012). But the Court of Appeals en banc reversed and instructed the District Court to grant relief. 757 F. 3d 910 (2014).

Under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), the Court of Appeals had power to grant Frost habeas corpus only if the Washington Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision \xe2\x80\x9cwas contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cwas based on an unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented in the State court proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d). Here, the Ninth Circuit held that the Washington Supreme Court unreasonably applied clearly established federal law by failing to classify the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s restriction of closing argument as structural error.

That decision cannot stand. Assuming for argument\xe2\x80\x99s sake that the trial court violated the Constitution, it was not clearly established that its mistake ranked as structural error. Most constitutional mistakes call for reversal only if the government cannot demonstrate harmlessness. Neder v. United States,527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,8 (1999). Only the rare type of error\xe2\x80\x94in general, one that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98infect[s] the entire trial process\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98necessarily render[s] [it] fundamentally unfair\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94requires automatic reversal. Ibid. None of our cases clearly requires placing improper restriction of closing argument in this narrow category.

The Ninth Circuit claimed that the Washington Supreme Court contradicted Herring v. New York,422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 853 (1975). Herring held that complete denial of summation violates the Assistance of Counsel Clause. According to the Ninth Circuit, Herring further held that this denial amounts to structural error. We need not opine on the accuracy of that interpretation. For even assuming that Herring established that complete denial of summation amounts to structural error, it did not clearly establish that the restriction of summation also amounts to structural error. A court could reasonably conclude, after all, that prohibiting all argument differs from prohibiting argument in the alternative. That is all the more true because our structural-error cases \xe2\x80\x9cha[ve] not been characterized by [an] \xe2\x80\x98in for a penny, in for a pound\xe2\x80\x99 approach.\xe2\x80\x9d Neder, supra, at 17, n.\xc2\xa02.

Attempting to bridge the gap between Herring and this case, the Ninth Circuit cited two Circuit precedents\xe2\x80\x94United States v. Miguel, 338 F.\xc2\xa03d 995 (CA9 2003), and Conde v. Henry, 198 F.\xc2\xa03d 734 (CA9 2000)\xe2\x80\x94for the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9cpreventing a defendant from arguing a legitimate defense theory constitutes structural error.\xe2\x80\x9d 757 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 916. As we have repeatedly emphasized, how-ever, circuit precedent does not constitute \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72254(d)(1); see, e.g., Lopez v. Smith, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (per curiam) (slip op., at 6). The Ninth Circuit acknowledged this rule, but tried to get past it by claiming that circuit precedent could \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98help .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. determine what law is \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 757 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 916, n.\xc2\xa01. But neither Miguel nor Conde arose under AEDPA, so neither purports to reflect the law clearly established by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s holdings. The Ninth Circuit thus had no justification for relying on those decisions. See Parker v. Matthews, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (per curiam) (slip op., at 13).

The second rationale for the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision is no more sound than the first. The Ninth Circuit reasoned that, by allowing the prosecution to argue that it had proved the elements of the crimes, but \xe2\x80\x9cprohibit[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d the defense from responding that it had not, the trial court in effect \xe2\x80\x9cforc[ed] defense counsel to concede his client\xe2\x80\x99s guilt.\xe2\x80\x9d 757 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 917. By extracting this \xe2\x80\x9cconce[ssion],\xe2\x80\x9d the Ninth Circuit continued, the trial court \xe2\x80\x9crelieved the State of its burden of proving guilt beyond a reasonable doubt,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cshifted the burden of proof to Frost,\xe2\x80\x9d and even \xe2\x80\x9cdirected [a] verdict on guilt\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94all \xe2\x80\x9cunquestionably structural [errors].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 917\xe2\x80\x93918.

No. The trial court, to begin, did not prohibit the defense from arguing that the prosecution failed to prove the elements of the crime. It instead precluded the defense from simultaneously contesting reasonable doubt and claiming duress. Reasonable minds could disagree whether requiring the defense to choose between alternative theories amounts to requiring the defense to concede guilt. Still more could they disagree whether it amounts to eliminating the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s burden of proof, shifting the burden to the defendant, or directing a verdict. In addition, even if the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling somehow \xe2\x80\x9cforced\xe2\x80\x9d the defense \xe2\x80\x9cat least tacitly [to] admit the elements of the crimes,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 913, the Ninth Circuit still would have no basis for ruling as it did. It goes much too far to suggest that our cases clearly establish that this supposed extraction of a \xe2\x80\x9ctaci[t] admi[ssion]\xe2\x80\x9d is structural error, when they classify the introduction of a coerced confession only as trial error, Arizona v. Fulminante,499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 279,310 (1991).

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

Frost argued below that, even if it was reasonable for the State Supreme Court to treat improper restriction of summation as trial error, it was unreasonable for it to find harmlessness on the facts of this case. The Court of Appeals did not address this argument when sitting en banc, and it is not before us today.

We grant the petition for a writ of certiorari and respondent\xe2\x80\x99s motion to proceed in forma pauperis. We reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.
