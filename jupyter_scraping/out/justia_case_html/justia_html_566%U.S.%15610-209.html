\n\t\t\t\tLAFLER v. COOPER\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x93209.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 31, 2011\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 21, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tRespondent was charged under Michigan law with assault with intent to murder and three other offenses. The prosecution offered to dismiss two of the charges and to recommend a 51-to-85-month sentence on the other two, in exchange for a guilty plea. In a communication with the court, respondent admitted his guilt and expressed a willingness to accept the offer. But he rejected the offer, allegedly after his attorney convinced him that the prosecution would be unable to establish intent to murder because the victim had been shot below the waist. At trial, respondent was convicted on all counts and received a mandatory minimum 185-to-360-month sentence. In a subsequent hearing, the state trial court rejected respondent\xe2\x80\x99s claim that his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s advice to reject the plea constituted ineffective assistance. The Michigan Court of Appeals affirmed, rejecting the ineffective-assistance claim on the ground that respondent knowingly and intelligently turned down the plea offer and chose to go to trial. Respondent renewed his claim in federal habeas. Finding that the state appellate court had unreasonably applied the constitutional effective-assistance standards laid out in Strickland v. Washington, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668\n, and Hill v. Lockhart, \n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 52\n, the District Court granted a conditional writ and ordered specific performance of the original plea offer. The Sixth Circuit affirmed. Applying Strickland, it found that counsel had provided deficient performance by advising respondent of an incorrect legal rule, and that respondent suffered prejudice because he lost the opportunity to take the more favorable sentence offered in the plea.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t1.\xc2\xa0Where counsel\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective advice led to an offer\xe2\x80\x99s rejection, and where the prejudice alleged is having to stand trial, a defendant must show that but for the ineffective advice, there is a reasonable probability that the plea offer would have been presented to the court, that the court would have accepted its terms, and that the conviction or sentence, or both, under the offer\xe2\x80\x99s terms would have been less severe than under the actual judgment and sentence imposed. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Because the parties agree that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance was deficient, the only question is how to apply Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s prejudice test where ineffective assistance results in a rejection of the plea offer and the defendant is convicted at the ensuing trial. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x934. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0In that context, the Strickland prejudice test requires a defendant to show a reasonable possibility that the outcome of the plea process would have been different with competent advice. The Sixth Circuit and other federal appellate courts have agreed with the Strickland prejudice test for rejected pleas adopted here by this Court. Petitioner and the Solicitor General propose a narrow view\xe2\x80\x94that Strickland prejudice cannot arise from plea bargaining if the defendant is later convicted at a fair trial\xe2\x80\x94but their reasoning is unpersuasive. First, they claim that the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s sole purpose is to protect the right to a fair trial, but the Amendment actually requires effective assistance at critical stages of a criminal proceeding, including pretrial stages. This is consistent with the right to effective assistance on appeal, see, e.g., Halbert v. Michigan, \n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 605\n, and the right to counsel during sentencing, see, e.g., Glover v. United States, \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 198\n\xe2\x80\x93204. This Court has not followed a rigid rule that an otherwise fair trial remedies errors not occurring at trial, but has instead inquired whether the trial cured the particular error at issue. See, e.g., Vasquez v. Hillery, \n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254\n. Second, this Court has previously rejected petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Lockhart v. Fretwell, \n\n506 U.\xc2\xa0S. 364\n, modified Strickland and does so again here. Fretwell and Nix v. Whiteside, \n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 157\n, demonstrate that \xe2\x80\x9cit would be unjust to characterize the likelihood of a different outcome as legitimate \xe2\x80\x98prejudice,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Williams v. Taylor, \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 362\n\xe2\x80\x93392, where defendants would receive a windfall as a result of the application of an incorrect legal principle or a defense strategy outside the law. Here, however, respondent seeks relief from counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to meet a valid legal standard. Third, petitioner seeks to preserve the conviction by arguing that the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s purpose is to ensure a conviction\xe2\x80\x99s reliability, but this argument fails to comprehend the full scope of the \n \nSixth Amendment and is refuted by precedent. Here, the question is the fairness or reliability not of the trial but of the processes that preceded it, which caused respondent to lose benefits he would have received but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective assistance. Furthermore, a reliable trial may not foreclose relief when counsel has failed to assert rights that may have altered the outcome. See Kimmelman v. Morrison, \n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 365\n. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s position that a fair trial wipes clean ineffective assistance during plea bargaining also ignores the reality that criminal justice today is for the most part a system of pleas, not a system of trials. See Missouri v. Frye, ante, at ___. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t2.\xc2\xa0Where a defendant shows ineffective assistance has caused the rejection of a plea leading to a more severe sentence at trial, the remedy must \xe2\x80\x9cneutralize the taint\xe2\x80\x9d of a constitutional violation, United States v. Morrison, \n\n449 U.\xc2\xa0S. 361\n, but must not grant a windfall to the defendant or needlessly squander the resources the State properly invested in the criminal prosecution, see United States v. Mechanik, \n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 66\n. If the sole advantage is that the defendant would have received a lesser sentence under the plea, the court should have an evidentiary hearing to determine whether the defendant would have accepted the plea. If so, the court may exercise discretion in determining whether the defendant should receive the term offered in the plea, the sentence received at trial, or something in between. However, resentencing based on the conviction at trial may not suffice, e.g., where the offered guilty plea was for less serious counts than the ones for which a defendant was convicted after trial, or where a mandatory sentence confines a judge\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing discretion. In these circumstances, the proper remedy may be to require the prosecution to reoffer the plea. The judge can then exercise discretion in deciding whether to vacate the conviction from trial and accept the plea, or leave the conviction undisturbed. In either situation, a court must weigh various factors. Here, it suffices to give two relevant considerations. First, a court may take account of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s earlier expressed willingness, or unwillingness, to accept responsibility for his or her actions. Second, it is not necessary here to decide as a constitutional rule that a judge is required to disregard any information concerning the crime discovered after the plea offer was made. Petitioner argues that implementing a remedy will open the floodgates to litigation by defendants seeking to unsettle their convictions, but in the 30 years that courts have recognized such claims, there has been no indication that the system is overwhelmed or that defendants are receiving windfalls as a result of strategically timed Strickland claims. In addition, the prosecution and trial courts may adopt measures to help ensure against meritless claims. See Frye, ante, at ___. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t3.\xc2\xa0This case arises under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), but because the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 analysis of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim was contrary to clearly established federal law, AEDPA presents no bar to relief. Respondent has satisfied Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s two-part test. The parties concede the fact of deficient performance. And respondent has shown that but for that performance there is a reasonable probability he and the trial court would have accepted the guilty plea. In addition, as a result of not accepting the plea and being convicted at trial, he received a minimum sentence 3\xc2\xbd times greater than he would have received under the plea. As a remedy, the District Court ordered specific performance of the plea agreement, but the correct remedy is to order the State to reoffer the plea. If respondent accepts the offer, the state trial court can exercise its discretion in determining whether to vacate respondent\xe2\x80\x99s convictions and resentence pursuant to the plea agreement, to vacate only some of the convictions and resentence accordingly, or to leave the conviction and sentence resulting from the trial undisturbed. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, as in Missouri v. Frye, ante, p. ___, also decided today, a criminal defendant seeks a remedy when inadequate assistance of counsel caused nonacceptance of a plea offer and further proceedings led to a less favorable outcome. In Frye, defense counsel did not inform the defendant of the plea offer; and after the offer lapsed the defendant still pleaded guilty, but on more severe terms. Here, the favorable plea offer was reported to the client but, on advice of counsel, was rejected. In Frye there was a later guilty plea. Here, after the plea offer had been rejected, there was a full and fair trial before a jury. After a guilty verdict, the defendant received a sentence harsher than that offered in the rejected plea bargain. The instant case comes to the Court with the concession that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s advice with respect to the plea offer fell below the standard of adequate assistance of counsel guaranteed by the \n \nSixth Amendment, applicable to the States through the \n \nFourteenth Amendment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn the evening of March 25, 2003, respondent pointed a gun toward Kali Mundy\xe2\x80\x99s head and fired. From the record, it is unclear why respondent did this, and at trial it was suggested that he might have acted either in self-defense or in defense of another person. In any event the shot missed and Mundy fled. Respondent followed in pur- suit, firing repeatedly. Mundy was shot in her buttock, hip, and abdomen but survived the assault.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent was charged under Michigan law with as- sault with intent to murder, possession of a firearm by a felon, possession of a firearm in the commission of a fel- ony, misdemeanor possession of marijuana, and for being a habitual offender. On two occasions, the prosecution offered to dismiss two of the charges and to recommend a sentence of 51 to 85 months for the other two, in exchange for a guilty plea. In a communication with the court respondent admitted guilt and expressed a willingness to accept the offer. Respondent, however, later rejected the offer on both occasions, allegedly after his attorney convinced him that the prosecution would be unable to establish his intent to murder Mundy because she had been shot below the waist. On the first day of trial the prosecution offered a significantly less favorable plea deal, which respondent again rejected. After trial, respondent was convicted on all counts and received a mandatory minimum sentence of 185 to 360 months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn a so-called Ginther hearing before the state trial court, see People v. Ginther, 390 Mich. 436, 212 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 922 (1973), respondent argued his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s advice to reject the plea constituted ineffective assistance. The trial judge rejected the claim, and the Michigan Court of Appeals affirmed. People v. Cooper, No. 250583, 2005 WL 599740 (Mar. 15, 2005) (per\xc2\xa0curiam), App. to Pet. for Cert. 44a. The Michigan Court of Appeals rejected the claim of ineffective assistance of counsel on the ground that re- spondent knowingly and intelligently rejected two plea offers and chose to go to trial. The Michigan Supreme Court denied respondent\xe2\x80\x99s application for leave to file an appeal. People v. Cooper, 474 Mich. 905, 705 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 118 (2005) (table).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent then filed a petition for federal habeas relief under \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254, renewing his ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim. After finding, as required by the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), that the Michigan Court of Appeals had un- reasonably applied the constitutional standards for effective assistance of counsel laid out in Strickland v. Washington, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668 (1984)\n, and Hill v. Lockhart, \n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 52 (1985)\n, the District Court granted a conditional writ. Cooper v. Lafler, No. 06\xe2\x80\x9311068, 2009 WL 817712, *10 (ED Mich., Mar. 26, 2009), App. to Pet. for Cert. 41a\xe2\x80\x9342a. To remedy the violation, the District Court ordered \xe2\x80\x9cspecific performance of [respondent\xe2\x80\x99s] original plea agreement, for a minimum sentence in the range of fifty-one to eighty-five months.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at *9, App. to Pet. for Cert. 41a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit affirmed, 376 Fed. Appx. 563 (2010), finding \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ven full deference under AEDPA cannot salvage the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 569. Applying Strickland, the Court of Appeals found that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s attorney had provided deficient performance by informing respondent of \xe2\x80\x9can incorrect legal rule,\xe2\x80\x9d 376 Fed. Appx., at 570\xe2\x80\x93571, and that respondent suffered prejudice because he \xe2\x80\x9clost out on an opportunity to plead guilty and receive the lower sentence that was offered to him.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 573. This Court granted certiorari. 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDefendants have a \n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel, a right that extends to the plea-bargaining process. Frye, ante, at 8; see also Padilla v. Kentucky, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 16); Hill, supra, at 57. During plea negotiations defendants are \xe2\x80\x9centitled to the effective assistance of competent counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d McMann v. Richardson, \n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 759, \n771 (1970)\n. In Hill, the Court held \xe2\x80\x9cthe two-part Strickland v. Washington test applies to challenges to guilty pleas based on ineffective assistance of counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d 474 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 58. The performance prong of Strickland requires a defendant to show \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s representation fell below an objective standard of reasonableness.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 474 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 57 (quoting Strickland, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 688). In this case all parties agree the performance of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s counsel was deficient when he advised respondent to reject the plea offer on the grounds he could not be convicted at trial. In light of this concession, it is unnecessary for this Court to explore the issue.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question for this Court is how to apply Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s prejudice test where ineffective assistance results in a re- jection of the plea offer and the defendant is convicted at the ensuing trial.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo establish Strickland prejudice a defendant must \xe2\x80\x9cshow that there is a reasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 694. In the context of pleas a defendant must show the outcome of the plea process would have been different with competent advice. See Frye, ante, at 12 (noting that Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry, as applied to advice with respect to plea bargains, turns on \xe2\x80\x9cwhether \xe2\x80\x98the result of the proceeding would have been different\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Strickland, supra, at 694)); see also Hill, 474 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 59 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98prejudice,\xe2\x80\x99 requirement .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. focuses on whether counsel\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionally ineffective performance affected the outcome of the plea process\xe2\x80\x9d). In Hill, when evaluating the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim that ineffective assistance led to the improvident acceptance of a guilty plea, the Court required the petitioner to show \xe2\x80\x9cthat there is a reasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s errors, [the defendant] would not have pleaded guilty and would have insisted on going to trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn contrast to Hill, here the ineffective advice led not to an offer\xe2\x80\x99s acceptance but to its rejection. Having to stand trial, not choosing to waive it, is the prejudice alleged. In these circumstances a defendant must show that but for the ineffective advice of counsel there is a reasonable prob- ability that the plea offer would have been presented to the court (i.e., that the defendant would have accepted the plea and the prosecution would not have withdrawn it in light of intervening circumstances), that the court would have accepted its terms, and that the conviction or sentence, or both, under the offer\xe2\x80\x99s terms would have been less severe than under the judgment and sentence that in fact were imposed. Here, the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit agreed with that test for Strickland prejudice in the context of a rejected plea bargain. This is consistent with the test adopted and applied by other appellate courts without demonstrated difficulties or systemic disruptions. See 376 Fed. Appx., at 571\xe2\x80\x93573; see also, e.g., United States v. Rodriguez Rodriguez, 929 F.\xc2\xa02d 747, 753, n.\xc2\xa01 (CA1 1991) (per\xc2\xa0curiam); United States v. Gordon, 156 F.\xc2\xa03d 376, 380\xe2\x80\x93381 (CA2 1998) (per\xc2\xa0curiam); United States v. Day, 969 F.\xc2\xa02d 39, 43\xe2\x80\x9345 (CA3 1992); Beckham v. Wainwright, 639 F.\xc2\xa02d 262, 267 (CA5 1981); Julian v. Bartley, 495 F.\xc2\xa03d 487, 498\xe2\x80\x93500 (CA7 2007); Wanatee v. Ault, 259 F.\xc2\xa03d 700, 703\xe2\x80\x93704 (CA8 2001); Nunes v. Mueller, 350 F.\xc2\xa03d 1045, 1052\xe2\x80\x931053 (CA9 2003); Williams v. Jones, 571 F.\xc2\xa03d 1086, 1094\xe2\x80\x931095 (CA10 2009) (per\xc2\xa0curiam); United States v. Gaviria, 116 F.\xc2\xa03d 1498, 1512\xe2\x80\x931514 (CADC 1997) (per\xc2\xa0curiam).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner and the Solicitor General propose a different, far more narrow, view of the \n \nSixth Amendment. They contend there can be no finding of Strickland prejudice arising from plea bargaining if the defendant is later convicted at a fair trial. The three reasons petitioner and the Solicitor General offer for their approach are unpersuasive.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, petitioner and the Solicitor General claim that the sole purpose of the \n \nSixth Amendment is to protect the right to a fair trial. Errors before trial, they argue, are not cognizable under the \n \nSixth Amendment unless they affect the fairness of the trial itself. See Brief for Petitioner 12\xe2\x80\x9321; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 10\xe2\x80\x9312. The \n \nSixth Amendment, however, is not so narrow in its reach. Cf. Frye, ante, at 11 (holding that a defendant can show prejudice under Strickland even absent a showing that the deficient performance precluded him from going to trial). The \n \nSixth Amendment requires effective assistance of counsel at critical stages of a criminal proceeding. Its protections are not designed simply to protect the trial, even though \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel\xe2\x80\x99s absence [in these stages] may derogate from the accused\xe2\x80\x99s right to a fair trial.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Wade, \n\n388 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218, \n226 (1967)\n. The constitutional guarantee applies to pretrial critical stages that are part of the whole course of a criminal proceeding, a proceeding in which defendants cannot be presumed to make critical decisions without counsel\xe2\x80\x99s advice. This is consistent, too, with the rule that defendants have a right to effective assistance of counsel on appeal, even though that cannot in any way be characterized as part of the trial. See, e.g., Halbert v. Michigan, \n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 605 (2005)\n; Evitts v. Lucey, \n\n469 U.\xc2\xa0S. 387 (1985)\n. The precedents also establish that there exists a right to counsel during sentencing in both noncapital, see Glover v. United States, \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 198\n\xe2\x80\x93204 (2001); Mempa v. Rhay, \n\n389 U.\xc2\xa0S. 128 (1967)\n, and capital cases, see Wiggins v. Smith, \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 510, \n538 (2003)\n. Even though sentencing does not concern the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt or innocence, ineffective assistance of counsel during a sentencing hearing can result in Strickland prejudice because \xe2\x80\x9cany amount of [additional] jail time has \n \nSixth Amendment significance.\xe2\x80\x9d Glover, supra, at 203.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court, moreover, has not followed a rigid rule that an otherwise fair trial remedies errors not occurring at the trial itself. It has inquired instead whether the trial cured the particular error at issue. Thus, in Vasquez v. Hillery, \n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254 (1986)\n, the deliberate exclusion of all African-Americans from a grand jury was prejudicial be- cause a defendant may have been tried on charges that would not have been brought at all by a properly constituted grand jury. Id., at 263; see Ballard v. United States, \n\n329 U.\xc2\xa0S. 187, \n195 (1946)\n (dismissing an indictment returned by a grand jury from which women were excluded); see also Stirone v. United States, \n\n361 U.\xc2\xa0S. 212\n\xe2\x80\x93219 (1960) (reversing a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction because the jury may have based its verdict on acts not charged in the indictment). By contrast, in United States v. Mechanik, \n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 66 (1986)\n, the complained-of error was a violation of a grand jury rule meant to ensure probable cause existed to believe a defendant was guilty. A subsequent trial, resulting in a verdict of guilt, cured this error. See id., at 72\xe2\x80\x9373.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the instant case respondent went to trial rather than accept a plea deal, and it is conceded this was the result of ineffective assistance during the plea negotiation process. Respondent received a more severe sentence at trial, one 3\xc2\xbd times more severe than he likely would have received by pleading guilty. Far from curing the error, the trial caused the injury from the error. Even if the trial itself is free from constitutional flaw, the defendant who goes to trial instead of taking a more favorable plea may be prejudiced from either a conviction on more serious counts or the imposition of a more severe sentence.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, petitioner claims this Court refined Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s prejudice analysis in Fretwell to add an additional requirement that the defendant show that ineffective assistance of counsel led to his being denied a substantive or procedural right. Brief for Petitioner 12\xe2\x80\x9313. The Court has rejected the argument that Fretwell modified Strickland before and does so again now. See Williams v. Taylor, \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 362, \n391 (2000)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Virginia Supreme Court erred in holding that our decision in Lockhart v. Fretwell, \n\n506 U.\xc2\xa0S. 364 (1993)\n, modified or in some way supplanted the rule set down in Strickland\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Glover, supra, at 203 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court explained last Term [in Williams] that our holding in Lockhart does not supplant the Strickland analysis\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFretwell could not show Strickland prejudice resulting from his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object to the use of a sentencing factor the Eighth Circuit had erroneously (and temporarily) found to be impermissible. Fretwell, 506 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 373. Because the objection upon which his ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim was premised was meritless, Fretwell could not demonstrate an error entitling him to relief. The case presented the \xe2\x80\x9cunusual circumstance where the defendant attempts to demonstrate prejudice based on considerations that, as a matter of law, ought not inform the inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring). See also ibid. (recognizing \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he determinative question\xe2\x80\x94whether there is a reasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different\xe2\x80\x94remains unchanged\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and citation omitted)). It is for this same reason a defendant cannot show prejudice based on counsel\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to present perjured testimony, even if such testimony might have affected the outcome of the case. See Nix v. Whiteside, \n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 157, \n175 (1986)\n (holding first that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to present perjured testimony breached no professional duty and second that it cannot establish prejudice under Strickland).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBoth Fretwell and Nix are instructive in that they demonstrate \xe2\x80\x9cthere are also situations in which it would be unjust to characterize the likelihood of a different outcome as legitimate \xe2\x80\x98prejudice,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Williams, supra, at 391\xe2\x80\x93392, because defendants would receive a windfall as a result of the application of an incorrect legal principle or a defense strategy outside the law. Here, however, the injured client seeks relief from counsel\xe2\x80\x99s failure to meet a valid legal standard, not from counsel\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to violate it. He maintains that, absent ineffective counsel, he would have accepted a plea offer for a sentence the prosecution evidently deemed consistent with the sound administration of criminal justice. The favorable sentence that eluded the defendant in the criminal proceeding appears to be the sentence he or others in his position would have received in the ordinary course, absent the failings of counsel. See Bibas, Regulating the Plea-Bargaining Market: From Caveat Emptor to Consumer Protection, 99 Cal. L. Rev. 1117, 1138 (2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe expected post-trial sentence is imposed in only a few percent of cases. It is like the sticker price for cars: only an ignorant, ill-advised consumer would view full price as the norm and anything less a bargain\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Frye, ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. If a plea bargain has been offered, a defendant has the right to effective assistance of counsel in considering whether to accept it. If that right is denied, prejudice can be shown if loss of the plea opportunity led to a trial resulting in a conviction on more serious charges or the imposition of a more severe sentence.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is, of course, true that defendants have \xe2\x80\x9cno right to be offered a plea .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. nor a federal right that the judge accept it.\xe2\x80\x9d Frye, ante, at 12. In the circumstances here, that is beside the point. If no plea offer is made, or a plea deal is accepted by the defendant but rejected by the judge, the issue raised here simply does not arise. Much the same reasoning guides cases that find criminal defendants have a right to effective assistance of counsel in direct appeals even though the Constitution does not require States to provide a system of appellate review at all. See Evitts, \n\n469 U.\xc2\xa0S. 387\n; see also Douglas v. California, \n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 353 (1963)\n. As in those cases, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a State opts to act in a field where its action has significant discretionary elements, it must nonetheless act in accord with the dictates of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Evitts, supra, at 401.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThird, petitioner seeks to preserve the conviction obtained by the State by arguing that the purpose of the \n \nSixth Amendment is to ensure \xe2\x80\x9cthe reliability of [a] conviction following trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 13. This argument, too, fails to comprehend the full scope of the \n \nSixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protections; and it is refuted by precedent. Strickland recognized \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he benchmark for judging any claim of ineffectiveness must be whether counsel\xe2\x80\x99s conduct so undermined the proper functioning of the adversarial process that the trial cannot be relied on as having produced a just result.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 686. The goal of a just result is not divorced from the reliability of a conviction, see United States v. Cronic, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 648, \n658 (1984)\n; but here the question is not the fairness or reliability of the trial but the fairness and regularity of the processes that preceded it, which caused the defendant to lose benefits he would have received in the ordinary course but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective assistance.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere are instances, furthermore, where a reliable trial does not foreclose relief when counsel has failed to assert rights that may have altered the outcome. In Kimmelman v. Morrison, \n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 365 (1986)\n, the Court held that an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s failure to timely move to suppress evidence during trial could be grounds for federal habeas relief. The Court rejected the suggestion that the \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to make a timely request for the exclusion of illegally seized evidence\xe2\x80\x9d could not be the basis for a \n \nSixth Amendment violation because the evidence \xe2\x80\x9cis \xe2\x80\x98typically reliable and often the most probative information bearing on the guilt or innocence of the defendant.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 379 (quoting Stone v. Powell, \n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 465, \n490 (1976)\n). \xe2\x80\x9cThe constitutional rights of criminal defendants,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court observed, \xe2\x80\x9care granted to the innocent and the guilty alike. Consequently, we decline to hold either that the guarantee of effective assistance of counsel belongs solely to the innocent or that it attaches only to matters affecting the determination of actual guilt.\xe2\x80\x9d 477 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 380. The same logic applies here. The fact that respondent is guilty does not mean he was not entitled by the \n \nSixth Amendment to effective assistance or that he suffered no prejudice from his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s deficient performance during plea bargaining.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the end, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s three arguments amount to one general contention: A fair trial wipes clean any deficient performance by defense counsel during plea bargaining. That position ignores the reality that criminal justice today is for the most part a system of pleas, not a system of trials. Ninety-seven percent of federal convictions and ninety-four percent of state convictions are the result of guilty pleas. See Frye, ante, at 7. As explained in Frye, the right to adequate assistance of counsel cannot be defined or enforced without taking account of the central role plea bargaining plays in securing convictions and determining sentences. Ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is insufficient simply to point to the guarantee of a fair trial as a backstop that inoculates any errors in the pretrial process\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven if a defendant shows ineffective assistance of counsel has caused the rejection of a plea leading to a trial and a more severe sentence, there is the question of what constitutes an appropriate remedy. That question must now be addressed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n \nSixth Amendment remedies should be \xe2\x80\x9ctailored to the injury suffered from the constitutional violation and should not unnecessarily infringe on competing interests.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Morrison, \n\n449 U.\xc2\xa0S. 361, \n364 (1981)\n. Thus, a remedy must \xe2\x80\x9cneutralize the taint\xe2\x80\x9d of a constitutional violation, id., at 365, while at the same time not grant a windfall to the defendant or needlessly squander the considerable resources the State properly invested in the criminal prosecution. See Mechanik, 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 72 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe reversal of a conviction entails substantial social costs: it forces jurors, witnesses, courts, the prosecution, and the defendants to expend further time, energy, and other resources to repeat a trial that has already once taken place; victims may be asked to relive their disturbing experiences\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe specific injury suffered by defendants who decline a plea offer as a result of ineffective assistance of counsel and then receive a greater sentence as a result of trial can come in at least one of two forms. In some cases, the sole advantage a defendant would have received under the plea is a lesser sentence. This is typically the case when the charges that would have been admitted as part of the plea bargain are the same as the charges the defendant was convicted of after trial. In this situation the court may conduct an evidentiary hearing to determine whether the defendant has shown a reasonable probability that but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s errors he would have accepted the plea. If the showing is made, the court may exercise discretion in determining whether the defendant should receive the term of imprisonment the government offered in the plea, the sentence he received at trial, or something in between.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn some situations it may be that resentencing alone will not be full redress for the constitutional injury. If, for example, an offer was for a guilty plea to a count or counts less serious than the ones for which a defendant was convicted after trial, or if a mandatory sentence confines a judge\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing discretion after trial, a resentencing based on the conviction at trial may not suffice. See, e.g., Williams, 571 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1088; Riggs v. Fairman, 399 F.\xc2\xa03d 1179, 1181 (CA9 2005). In these circumstances, the proper exercise of discretion to remedy the constitutional injury may be to require the prosecution to reoffer the plea proposal. Once this has occurred, the judge can then exercise discretion in deciding whether to vacate the conviction from trial and accept the plea or leave the conviction undisturbed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn implementing a remedy in both of these situations, the trial court must weigh various factors; and the boundaries of proper discretion need not be defined here. Principles elaborated over time in decisions of state and federal courts, and in statutes and rules, will serve to give more complete guidance as to the factors that should bear upon the exercise of the judge\xe2\x80\x99s discretion. At this point, however, it suffices to note two considerations that are of relevance.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, a court may take account of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s earlier expressed willingness, or unwillingness, to accept responsibility for his or her actions. Second, it is not necessary here to decide as a constitutional rule that a judge is re- quired to prescind (that is to say disregard) any information concerning the crime that was discovered after the plea offer was made. The time continuum makes it difficult to restore the defendant and the prosecution to the precise positions they occupied prior to the rejection of the plea offer, but that baseline can be consulted in finding a remedy that does not require the prosecution to incur the expense of conducting a new trial.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner argues that implementing a remedy here will open the floodgates to litigation by defendants seeking to unsettle their convictions. See Brief for Petitioner 20. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s concern is misplaced. Courts have recognized claims of this sort for over 30 years, see supra, at 5, and yet there is no indication that the system is overwhelmed by these types of suits or that defendants are receiving windfalls as a result of strategically timed Strickland claims. See also Padilla, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 14) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe confronted a similar \xe2\x80\x98floodgates\xe2\x80\x99 concern in Hill,\xe2\x80\x9d but a \xe2\x80\x9cflood did not follow in that decision\xe2\x80\x99s wake\xe2\x80\x9d). In addition, the \xe2\x80\x9cprosecution and the trial courts may adopt some measures to help ensure against late, frivolous, or fabricated claims after a later, less advantageous plea offer has been accepted or after a trial leading to conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d Frye, ante, at 10. See also ibid. (listing procedures currently used by various States). This, too, will help ensure against meritless claims.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe standards for ineffective assistance of counsel when a defendant rejects a plea offer and goes to trial must now be applied to this case. Respondent brings a federal collateral challenge to a state-court conviction. Under AEDPA, a federal court may not grant a petition for a writ of habeas corpus unless the state court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication on the merits was \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). A decision is contrary to clearly established law if the state court \xe2\x80\x9capplies a rule that con- tradicts the governing law set forth in [Supreme Court] cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams v. Taylor, \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 362, \n405 (2000)\n (opinion for the Court by O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J.). The Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit could not determine whether the Michigan Court of Appeals addressed respondent\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim or, if it did, \xe2\x80\x9cwhat the court decided, or even whether the correct legal rule was identified.\xe2\x80\x9d 376 Fed. Appx., at 568\xe2\x80\x93569.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision may not be quite so opaque as the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit thought, yet the federal court was correct to note that AEDPA does not present a bar to granting respondent relief. That is because the Michigan Court of Appeals identified respondent\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim but failed to apply Strickland to assess it. Rather than applying Strickland, the state court simply found that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s rejection of the plea was knowing and voluntary. Cooper, 2005 WL 599740, *1, App. to Pet. for Cert. 45a. An inquiry into whether the rejection of a plea is knowing and voluntary, however, is not the correct means by which to address a claim of ineffective assistance of counsel. See Hill, 474 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 370 (applying Strickland to assess a claim of ineffective assistance of counsel arising out of the plea negotiation process). After stating the incorrect standard, moreover, the state court then made an irrelevant observation about counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance at trial and mischaracterized respondent\xe2\x80\x99s claim as a complaint that his attorney did not obtain a more favorable plea bargain. By failing to apply Strickland to assess the ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim respondent raised, the state court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication was contrary to clearly established federal law. And in that circumstance the federal courts in this habeas action can determine the principles necessary to grant relief. See Panetti v. Quarterman, \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 930, \n948 (2007)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent has satisfied Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s two-part test. Regarding performance, perhaps it could be accepted that it is unclear whether respondent\xe2\x80\x99s counsel believed respondent could not be convicted for assault with intent to murder as a matter of law because the shots hit Mundy below the waist, or whether he simply thought this would be a persuasive argument to make to the jury to show lack of specific intent. And, as the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit suggested, an erroneous strategic prediction about the outcome of a trial is not necessarily deficient performance. Here, however, the fact of deficient performance has been conceded by all parties. The case comes to us on that assumption, so there is no need to address this question.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs to prejudice, respondent has shown that but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s deficient performance there is a reasonable probability he and the trial court would have accepted the guilty plea. See 376 Fed. Appx., at 571\xe2\x80\x93572. In addition, as a result of not accepting the plea and being convicted at trial, respondent received a minimum sentence 3\xc2\xbd times greater than he would have received under the plea. The standard for ineffective assistance under Strickland has thus been satisfied.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs a remedy, the District Court ordered specific performance of the original plea agreement. The correct remedy in these circumstances, however, is to order the State to reoffer the plea agreement. Presuming respondent accepts the offer, the state trial court can then exercise its discretion in determining whether to vacate the convictions and resentence respondent pursuant to the plea agreement, to vacate only some of the convictions and resentence respondent accordingly, or to leave the convictions and sentence from trial undisturbed. See Mich. Ct. Rule 6.302(C)(3) (2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf there is a plea agreement and its terms provide for the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s plea to be made in exchange for a specific sentence disposition or a prosecuto- rial sentence recommendation, the court may .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. reject the agreement\xe2\x80\x9d). Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision leaves open to the trial court how best to exercise that discretion in all the circumstances of the case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cIf a plea bargain has been offered, a defendant has the right to effective assistance of counsel in considering whether to accept it. If that right is denied, prejudice can be shown if loss of the plea opportunity led to a trial resulting in a conviction on more serious charges or the imposition of a more severe sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe inquiry then becomes how to define the duty and responsibilities of defense counsel in the plea bargain process. This is a difficult question.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Bargaining is, by its nature, defined to a substantial degree by personal style.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. This case presents neither the necessity nor the occasion to define the duties of defense counsel in those respects .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Missouri v. Frye, ante, at 8.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWith those words from this and the companion case, the Court today opens a whole new field of constitutionalized criminal procedure: plea-bargaining law. The ordinary criminal process has become too long, too expensive, and unpredictable, in no small part as a consequence of an intricate federal Code of Criminal Procedure imposed on the States by this Court in pursuit of perfect justice. See Friendly, The Bill of Rights as a Code of Criminal Procedure, 53 Cal. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 929 (1965). The Court now moves to bring perfection to the alternative in which prosecutors and defendants have sought relief. Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinions deal with only two aspects of counsel\xe2\x80\x99s plea-bargaining inadequacy, and leave other aspects (who knows what they might be?) to be worked out in further constitutional litigation that will burden the criminal process. And it would be foolish to think that \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional\xe2\x80\x9d rules governing counsel\xe2\x80\x99s behavior will not be followed by rules governing the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s behavior in the plea-bargaining process that the Court today announces \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is the criminal justice system,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Frye, ante, at 7 (quoting approvingly from Scott & Stuntz, Plea Bargaining as Contract, 101 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 1909, 1912 (1992) (hereinafter Scott)). Is it constitutional, for example, for the prosecution to withdraw a plea offer that has already been accepted? Or to withdraw an offer before the defense has had adequate time to consider and accept it? Or to make no plea offer at all, even though its case is weak\xe2\x80\x94thereby excluding the defendant from \xe2\x80\x9cthe criminal justice system\xe2\x80\x9d?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnthony Cooper received a full and fair trial, was found guilty of all charges by a unanimous jury, and was given the sentence that the law prescribed. The Court nonetheless concludes that Cooper is entitled to some sort of habeas corpus relief (perhaps) because his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s allegedly incompetent advice regarding a plea offer caused him to receive a full and fair trial. That conclusion is foreclosed by our precedents. Even if it were not foreclosed, the constitutional right to effective plea-bargainers that it establishes is at least a new rule of law, which does not undermine the Michigan Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision and therefore cannot serve as the basis for habeas relief. And the remedy the Court announces\xe2\x80\x94namely, whatever the state trial court in its discretion prescribes, down to and including no remedy at all\xe2\x80\x94is unheard-of and quite absurd for violation of a constitutional right. I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case and its companion, Missouri v. Frye, ante, p.\xc2\xa0___, raise relatively straightforward questions about the scope of the right to effective assistance of counsel. Our case law originally derived that right from the Due Process Clause, and its guarantee of a fair trial, see United States v. Gonzalez-Lopez, \n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 140, \n147 (2006)\n, but the seminal case of Strickland v. Washington, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 668 (1984)\n, located the right within the \n \nSixth Amendment. As the Court notes, ante, at 6, the right to counsel does not begin at trial. It extends to \xe2\x80\x9cany stage of the prosecution, formal or informal, in court or out, where counsel\xe2\x80\x99s absence might derogate from the accused\xe2\x80\x99s right to a fair trial.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Wade, \n\n388 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218, \n226 (1967)\n. Applying that principle, we held that the \xe2\x80\x9centry of a guilty plea, whether to a misdemeanor or a felony charge, ranks as a \xe2\x80\x98critical stage\xe2\x80\x99 at which the right to counsel adheres.\xe2\x80\x9d Iowa v. Tovar, \n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 77, \n81 (2004)\n; see also Hill v. Lockhart, \n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 52, \n58 (1985)\n. And it follows from this that acceptance of a plea offer is a critical stage. That, and nothing more, is the point of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s observation in Padilla v. Kentucky, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 16), that \xe2\x80\x9cthe negotiation of a plea bargain is a critical phase of litigation for purposes of the \n \nSixth Amendment right to effective assistance of counsel.\xe2\x80\x9d The defendant in Padilla had accepted the plea bargain and pleaded guilty, abandoning his right to a fair trial; he was entitled to advice of competent counsel before he did so. The Court has never held that the rule articulated in Padilla, Tovar, and Hill extends to all aspects of plea negotiations, requiring not just advice of competent counsel before the defendant accepts a plea bargain and pleads guilty, but also the advice of competent counsel before the defendant rejects a plea bargain and stands on his constitutional right to a fair trial. The latter is a vast departure from our past cases, protecting not just the constitutionally prescribed right to a fair adjudication of guilt and punishment, but a judicially invented right to effective plea bargaining.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is also apparent from Strickland that bad plea bargaining has nothing to do with ineffective assistance of counsel in the constitutional sense. Strickland explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n giving meaning to the requirement [of effective assistance], .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. we must take its purpose\xe2\x80\x94to ensure a fair trial\xe2\x80\x94as the guide.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 686. Since \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to the effective assistance of counsel is recognized not for its own sake, but because of the effect it has on the ability of the accused to receive a fair trial,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Cronic, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 648, \n658 (1984)\n, the \xe2\x80\x9cbenchmark\xe2\x80\x9d inquiry in evaluating any claim of ineffective assistance is whether counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance \xe2\x80\x9cso undermined the proper functioning of the adversarial process\xe2\x80\x9d that it failed to produce a reliably \xe2\x80\x9cjust result.\xe2\x80\x9d Strickland, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 686. That is what Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s requirement of \xe2\x80\x9cprejudice\xe2\x80\x9d consists of: Because the right to effective assistance has as its purpose the assurance of a fair trial, the right is not infringed unless counsel\xe2\x80\x99s mistakes call into question the basic justice of a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction or sentence. That has been, until today, entirely clear. A defendant must show \xe2\x80\x9cthat counsel\xe2\x80\x99s errors were so serious as to deprive the defendant of a fair trial, a trial whose result is reliable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 687. See also Gonzalez-Lopez, supra, at 147. Impairment of fair trial is how we distinguish between unfortunate attorney error and error of constitutional significance.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo be sure, Strickland stated a rule of thumb for measuring prejudice which, applied blindly and out of context, could support the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding today: \xe2\x80\x9cThe defendant must show that there is a reasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s unprofessional errors, the result of the proceeding would have been different.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 694. Strickland itself cautioned, however, that its test was not to be applied in a mechanical fashion, and that courts were not to divert their \xe2\x80\x9cultimate focus\xe2\x80\x9d from \xe2\x80\x9cthe fundamental fairness of the proceeding whose result is being challenged.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 696. And until today we have followed that course.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Lockhart v. Fretwell, \n\n506 U.\xc2\xa0S. 364 (1993)\n, the deficient performance at issue was the failure of counsel for a defendant who had been sentenced to death to make an objection that would have produced a sentence of life imprisonment instead. The objection was fully supported by then-extant Circuit law, so that the sentencing court would have been compelled to sustain it, producing a life sentence that principles of double jeopardy would likely make final. See id., at 383\xe2\x80\x93385 (Stevens, J., dissenting); Bullington v. Missouri, \n\n451 U.\xc2\xa0S. 430 (1981)\n. By the time Fretwell\xe2\x80\x99s claim came before us, however, the Circuit law had been overruled in light of one of our cases. We determined that a prejudice analysis \xe2\x80\x9cfocusing solely on mere outcome determination, without attention to whether the result of the proceeding was fundamentally unfair or unreliable,\xe2\x80\x9d would be defective. Fretwell, 506 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 369. Because counsel\xe2\x80\x99s error did not \xe2\x80\x9cdeprive the defendant of any substantive or procedural right to which the law entitles him,\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing proceeding was fair and its result was reliable, even though counsel\xe2\x80\x99s error may have affected its outcome. Id., at 372. In Williams v. Taylor, \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 362\n\xe2\x80\x93393 (2000), we explained that even though Fretwell did not mechanically apply an outcome-based test for prejudice, its reasoning was perfectly consistent with Strickland. \xe2\x80\x9cFretwell\xe2\x80\x99s counsel had not deprived him of any substantive or procedural right to which the law entitled him.\xe2\x80\x9d 529 U.\xc2\xa0S. at 392.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThose precedents leave no doubt about the answer to the question presented here. As the Court itself observes, a criminal defendant has no right to a plea bargain. Ante, at 9. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is no constitutional right to plea bargain; the prosecutor need not do so if he prefers to go to trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Weatherford v. Bursey, \n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 545, \n561 (1977)\n. Counsel\xe2\x80\x99s mistakes in this case thus did not \xe2\x80\x9cdeprive the defendant of a substantive or procedural right to which the law entitles him,\xe2\x80\x9d Williams, supra, at 393. Far from be- ing \xe2\x80\x9cbeside the point,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9, that is critical to correct application of our precedents. Like Fretwell, this case \xe2\x80\x9cconcerns the unusual circumstance where the defendant attempts to demonstrate prejudice based on considerations that, as a matter of law, ought not inform the inquiry,\xe2\x80\x9d 506 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 373 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring); he claims \xe2\x80\x9cthat he might have been denied \xe2\x80\x98a right the law simply does not recognize,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 375 (same). Strickland, Fretwell, and Williams all instruct that the pure outcome-based test on which the Court relies is an erroneous measure of cognizable prejudice. In ignoring Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cultimate focus .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. on the fundamental fairness of the proceeding whose result is being challenged,\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 696, the Court has lost the forest for the trees, leading it to accept what we have previously rejected, the \xe2\x80\x9cnovel argument that constitutional rights are infringed by trying the defendant rather than accepting his plea of guilty.\xe2\x80\x9d Weatherford, supra, at 561.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNovelty alone is the second, independent reason why the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is wrong. This case arises on federal habeas, and hence is governed by the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA). Since, as the Court acknowledges, the Michigan Court of Appeals adjudicated Cooper\xe2\x80\x99s ineffective-assistance claim on the merits, AEDPA bars federal courts from granting habeas relief unless that court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). Yet the Court concludes that \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) does not bar relief here, because \xe2\x80\x9c[b]y failing to apply Strickland to assess the ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim respondent raised, the state court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication was contrary to clearly established federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. That is not so.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe relevant portion of the Michigan Court of Appeals decision reads as follows:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cTo establish ineffective assistance, the defendant must demonstrate that his counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance fell below an objective standard of reasonableness and that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s representation so prejudiced the defendant that he was deprived of a fair trial. With respect to the prejudice aspect of the test, the defend- ant must demonstrate a reasonable probability that, but for counsel\xe2\x80\x99s errors, the result of the proceed- ings would have been different, and that the at- tendant proceedings were fundamentally unfair and unreliable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cDefendant challenges the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s finding after a Ginther hearing that defense counsel provided effective assistance to defendant during the plea bargaining process. He contends that defense counsel failed to convey the benefits of the plea offer to him and ignored his desire to plead guilty, and that these failures led him to reject a plea offer that he now wishes to accept. However, the record shows that defendant knowingly and intelligently rejected two plea offers and chose to go to trial. The record fails to support defendant\xe2\x80\x99s contentions that defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s representation was ineffective because he rejected a defense based on [a] claim of self-defense and because he did not obtain a more favorable plea bargain for defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d People v. Cooper, No. 250583 (Mar. 15, 2005), App. to Pet. for Cert. 45a, 2005 WL 599740, *1 (per\xc2\xa0curiam) (footnote and citations omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe first paragraph above, far from ignoring Strickland, recites its standard with a good deal more accuracy than the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. The second paragraph, which is presumably an application of the standard recited in the first, says that \xe2\x80\x9cdefendant knowingly and intelligently rejected two plea offers and chose to go to trial.\xe2\x80\x9d This can be regarded as a denial that there was anything \xe2\x80\x9cfundamentally unfair\xe2\x80\x9d about Cooper\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and sentence, so that no Strickland prejudice had been shown. On the other hand, the entire second paragraph can be regarded as a contention that Cooper\xe2\x80\x99s claims of inadequate representation were unsupported by the record. The state court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis was admittedly not a model of clarity, but federal habeas corpus is a \xe2\x80\x9cguard against extreme malfunctions in the state criminal justice systems,\xe2\x80\x9d not a license to penalize a state court for its opinion-writing technique. Harrington v. Richter, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 13) (internal quotation marks omitted). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s readiness to find error in the Michigan court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with the presumption that state courts know and follow the law,\xe2\x80\x9d Woodford v. Visciotti, \n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 19, \n24 (2002)\n (per curiam), a presumption borne out here by the state court\xe2\x80\x99s recitation of the correct legal standard.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSince it is ambiguous whether the state court\xe2\x80\x99s holding was based on a lack of prejudice or rather the court\xe2\x80\x99s factual determination that there had been no deficient performance, to provide relief under AEDPA this Court must conclude that both holdings would have been unreasonable applications of clearly established law. See Premo v. Moore, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 7). The first is impossible of doing, since this Court has never held that a defendant in Cooper\xe2\x80\x99s position can establish Strickland prejudice. The Sixth Circuit thus violated AEDPA in granting habeas relief, and the Court now does the same.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is impossible to conclude discussion of today\xe2\x80\x99s extraordinary opinion without commenting upon the remedy it provides for the unconstitutional conviction. It is a remedy unheard-of in American jurisprudence\xe2\x80\x94and, I would be willing to bet, in the jurisprudence of any other country.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court requires Michigan to \xe2\x80\x9creoffer the plea agreement\xe2\x80\x9d that was rejected because of bad advice from counsel. Ante, at 16. That would indeed be a powerful remedy\xe2\x80\x94 but for the fact that Cooper\xe2\x80\x99s acceptance of that re- offered agreement is not conclusive. Astoundingly, \xe2\x80\x9cthe state trial court can then exercise its discretion in determining whether to vacate the convictions and resentence respondent pursuant to the plea agreement, to vacate only some of the convictions and resentence respondent accordingly, or to leave the convictions and sentence from trial undisturbed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhy, one might ask, require a \xe2\x80\x9creoffer\xe2\x80\x9d of the plea agreement, and its acceptance by the defendant? If the district court finds (as a necessary element, supposedly, of Strickland prejudice) that Cooper would have accepted the original offer, and would thereby have avoided trial and conviction, why not skip the reoffer-and-reacceptance minuet and simply leave it to the discretion of the state trial court what the remedy shall be? The answer, of course, is camouflage. Trial courts, after all, regularly accept or reject plea agreements, so there seems to be nothing extraordinary about their accepting or rejecting the new one mandated by today\xe2\x80\x99s decision. But the acceptance or rejection of a plea agreement that has no status whatever under the United States Constitution is worlds apart from what this is: \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d specifi- cation of a remedy for an unconstitutional criminal conviction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo be sure, the Court asserts that there are \xe2\x80\x9cfactors\xe2\x80\x9d which bear upon (and presumably limit) exercise of this discretion\xe2\x80\x94factors that it is not prepared to specify in full, much less assign some determinative weight. \xe2\x80\x9cPrinciples elaborated over time in decisions of state and federal courts, and in statutes and rules\xe2\x80\x9d will (in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s rosy view) sort all that out. Ante, at 13. I find it extraordinary that \xe2\x80\x9cstatutes and rules\xe2\x80\x9d can specify the remedy for a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s unconstitutional conviction. Or that the remedy for an unconstitutional conviction should ever be subject at all to a trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s discretion. Or, finally, that the remedy could ever include no remedy at all.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI suspect that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s squeamishness in fashioning a remedy, and the incoherence of what it comes up with, is attributable to its realization, deep down, that there is no real constitutional violation here anyway. The defendant has been fairly tried, lawfully convicted, and properly sentenced, and any \xe2\x80\x9cremedy\xe2\x80\x9d provided for this will do nothing but undo the just results of a fair adversarial process.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn many\xe2\x80\x94perhaps most\xe2\x80\x94countries of the world, American-style plea bargaining is forbidden in cases as serious as this one, even for the purpose of obtaining testimony that enables conviction of a greater malefactor, much less for the purpose of sparing the expense of trial. See, e.g., World Plea Bargaining 344, 363\xe2\x80\x93366 (S. Thaman ed. 2010). In Europe, many countries adhere to what they aptly call the \xe2\x80\x9clegality principle\xe2\x80\x9d by requiring prosecutors to charge all prosecutable offenses, which is typically incompatible with the practice of charge-bargaining. See, e.g., id., at xxii; Langbein, Land Without Plea Bargaining: How the Germans Do It, 78 Mich. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 204, 210\xe2\x80\x93211 (1979) (describing the \xe2\x80\x9cLegalit\xc3\xa4tsprinzip,\xe2\x80\x9d or rule of compulsory prosecution, in Germany). Such a system reflects an admirable belief that the law is the law, and those who break it should pay the penalty provided.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the United States, we have plea bargaining a-plenty, but until today it has been regarded as a necessary evil. It presents grave risks of prosecutorial overcharging that effectively compels an innocent defendant to avoid massive risk by pleading guilty to a lesser offense; and for guilty defendants it often\xe2\x80\x94perhaps usually\xe2\x80\x94results in a sentence well below what the law prescribes for the actual crime. But even so, we accept plea bargaining because many believe that without it our long and expensive process of criminal trial could not sustain the burden imposed on it, and our system of criminal justice would grind to a halt. See, e.g., Alschuler, Plea Bargaining and its History, 79 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 38 (1979).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tToday, however, the Supreme Court of the United States elevates plea bargaining from a necessary evil to a constitutional entitlement. It is no longer a somewhat embarrassing adjunct to our criminal justice system; rather, as the Court announces in the companion case to this one, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98it is the criminal justice system.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Frye, ante, at 7 (quoting approvingly from Scott 1912). Thus, even though there is no doubt that the respondent here is guilty of the offense with which he was charged; even though he has received the exorbitant gold standard of American justice\xe2\x80\x94a full-dress criminal trial with its innumerable constitutional and statutory limitations upon the evidence that the prosecution can bring forward, and (in Michigan as in most States\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t) the requirement of a unanimous guilty verdict by impartial jurors; the Court says that his conviction is invalid because he was deprived of his constitutional entitlement to plea-bargain.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI am less saddened by the outcome of this case than I am by what it says about this Court\xe2\x80\x99s attitude toward criminal justice. The Court today embraces the sporting-chance theory of criminal law, in which the State functions like a conscientious casino-operator, giving each player a fair chance to beat the house, that is, to serve less time than the law says he deserves. And when a player is excluded from the tables, his constitutional rights have been violated. I do not subscribe to that theory. No one should, least of all the Justices of the Supreme Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tToday\xe2\x80\x99s decision upends decades of our cases, violates a federal statute, and opens a whole new boutique of constitutional jurisprudence (\xe2\x80\x9cplea-bargaining law\xe2\x80\x9d) without even specifying the remedies the boutique offers. The result in the present case is the undoing of an adjudicatory process that worked exactly as it is supposed to. Released felon Anthony Cooper, who shot repeatedly and gravely injured a woman named Kali Mundy, was tried and convicted for his crimes by a jury of his peers, and given a punishment that Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s elected representatives have deemed appropriate. Nothing about that result is unfair or unconstitutional. To the contrary, it is wonderfully just, and infinitely superior to the trial-by-bargain that today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion affords constitutional status. I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor the reasons set out in Parts I and II of Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s dissent, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in this case mis- applies our ineffective-assistance-of-counsel case law and violates the requirements of the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996. Respondent received a trial that was free of any identified constitutional error, and, as a result, there is no basis for concluding that respondent suffered prejudice and certainly not for granting habeas relief.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe weakness in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis is highlighted by its opaque discussion of the remedy that is appropriate when a plea offer is rejected due to defective legal representation. If a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \n \nSixth Amendment rights are violated when deficient legal advice about a favorable plea offer causes the opportunity for that bargain to be lost, the only logical remedy is to give the defendant the benefit of the favorable deal. But such a remedy would cause serious injustice in many instances, as I believe the Court tacitly recognizes. The Court therefore eschews the only logical remedy and relies on the lower courts to exercise sound discretion in determining what is to be done.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTime will tell how this works out. The Court, for its part, finds it unnecessary to define \xe2\x80\x9cthe boundaries of proper discretion\xe2\x80\x9d in today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. Ante, at 13. In my view, requiring the prosecution to renew an old plea offer would represent an abuse of discretion in at least two circumstances: first, when important new information about a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpability comes to light after the offer is rejected, and, second, when the rejection of the plea offer results in a substantial expenditure of scarce prosecutorial or judicial resources.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe lower court judges who must implement today\xe2\x80\x99s holding may\xe2\x80\x94and I hope, will\xe2\x80\x94do so in a way that mitigates its potential to produce unjust results. But I would not depend on these judges to come to the rescue. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the \n \nSixth Amendment right to counsel is unsound, and I therefore respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t
