REPUBLIC OF ARGENTINA v. NML CAPITAL, LTD.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 12\xe2\x80\x93842.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 21, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 16, 2014

\n

After petitioner, Republic of Argentina, defaulted on its external debt, respondent, NML Capital, Ltd. (NML), one of Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s bondholders, prevailed in 11 debt-collection actions that it brought against Argentina in the Southern District of New York. In aid of executing the judgments, NML sought discovery of Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s property, serving subpoenas on two nonparty banks for records relating to Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s global financial transactions. The District Court granted NML\xe2\x80\x99s motions to compel compliance. The Second Circuit affirmed, rejecting Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order transgressed the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 (FSIA or Act).

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0No provision in the FSIA immunizes a foreign-sovereign judgment debtor from postjudgment discovery of information concerning its extraterritorial assets. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0This Court assumes without deciding that, in the ordinary case, a district court would have the discretion under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 69(a)(2) to permit discovery of third-party information bearing on a judgment debtor\xe2\x80\x99s extraterritorial assets. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0The FSIA replaced an executive-driven, factor-intensive, loosely common-law-based immunity regime with \xe2\x80\x9ca comprehensive framework for resolving any claim of sovereign immunity.\xe2\x80\x9d Republic of Austria v. Altmann,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677\n. Henceforth, any sort of immunity defense made by a foreign sovereign in an American court must stand or fall on the Act\xe2\x80\x99s text. The Act confers on foreign states two kinds of immunity. The first, jurisdictional immunity (\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71604), was waived here. The second, execution immunity, generally shields \xe2\x80\x9cproperty in the United States of a foreign state\xe2\x80\x9d from attachment, arrest, and execution. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71609, 1610. See also \xc2\xa71611(a), (b)(1), (b)(2). The Act has no third provision forbidding or limiting discovery in aid of execution of a foreign-sovereign judgment debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets. Far from containing the \xe2\x80\x9cplain statement\xe2\x80\x9d necessary to preclude application of federal discovery rules, Soci\xc3\xa9t\xc3\xa9 Nationale Industrielle A\xc3\xa9rospatiale v. United States Dist. Court for Southern Dist. of Iowa,\n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522\n, the Act says not a word about postjudgment discovery in aid of execution.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s arguments are unavailing. Even if Argentina were correct that \xc2\xa71609 execution immunity implies coextensive discovery-in-aid-of-execution immunity, the latter would not shield from discovery a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s extraterritorial assets, since the text of \xc2\xa71609 immunizes only foreign-state property \xe2\x80\x9cin the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d The prospect that NML\xe2\x80\x99s general request for information about Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s worldwide assets may turn up information about property that Argentina regards as immune does not mean that NML cannot pursue discovery of it. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

695 F.\xc2\xa03d 201, affirmed.

REPUBLIC OF ARGENTINA, PETITIONER v. NML CAPITAL, LTD.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

[June 16, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We must decide whether the Foreign Sovereign Immu-nities Act of 1976 (FSIA or Act), 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71330, 1602 et\xc2\xa0seq., limits the scope of discovery available to a judgment creditor in a federal postjudgment execution proceeding against a foreign sovereign.

\n

I. Background

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In 2001, petitioner, Republic of Argentina, defaulted on its external debt. In 2005 and 2010, it restructured most of that debt by offering creditors new securities (with less favorable terms) to swap out for the defaulted ones. Most bondholders went along. Respondent, NML Capital, Ltd. (NML), among others, did not.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n NML brought 11 actions against Argentina in the Southern District of New York to collect on its debt, and prevailed in every one.[1] It is owed around $2.5 billion, which Argentina has not paid. Having been unable to collect on its judgments from Argentina, NML has attempted to execute them against Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s property. That postjudgment litigation \xe2\x80\x9chas involved lengthy attachment proceedings before the district court and multiple appeals.\xe2\x80\x9d EM Ltd. v. Republic of Argentina, 695 F.\xc2\xa03d 201, 203, and n. 2 (CA2 2012) (referring the reader to prior opinions \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or additional background on Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s default and the resulting litigation\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Since 2003, NML has pursued discovery of Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s property. In 2010, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[i]n order to locate Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s assets and accounts, learn how Argentina moves its assets through New York and around the world, and accurately identify the places and times when those assets might be subject to attachment and execution (whether under [United States law] or the law of foreign jurisdictions),\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 203 (quoting NML brief), NML served subpoenas on two nonparty banks, Bank of America (BOA) and Banco de la Naci\xc3\xb3n Argentina (BNA), an Argentinian bank with a branch in New York City. For the most part, the two subpoenas target the same kinds of information: documents relating to accounts maintained by or on behalf of Argentina, documents identifying the opening and closing dates of Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s accounts, current balances, transaction histories, records of electronic fund transfers, debts owed by the bank to Argentina, transfers in and out of Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s accounts, and information about transferors and transferees.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Argentina, joined by BOA, moved to quash the BOA subpoena. NML moved to compel compliance but, before the court ruled, agreed to narrow its subpoenas by excluding the names of some Argentine officials from the ini-tial electronic-fund-transfer message search. NML also agreed to treat as confidential any documents that the banks so designated.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The District Court denied the motion to quash and granted the motions to compel. Approving the subpoenas in principle, it concluded that extraterritorial asset discovery did not offend Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity, and it reaffirmed that it would serve as a \xe2\x80\x9cclearinghouse for information\xe2\x80\x9d in NML\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to find and attach Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s assets. App. to Pet. for Cert. 31. But the court made clear that it expected the parties to negotiate further over specific production requests, which, the court said, must include \xe2\x80\x9csome reasonable definition of the information being sought.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 32. There was no point, for instance, in \xe2\x80\x9cgetting information about something that might lead to attachment in Argentina because that would be useless information,\xe2\x80\x9d since no Argentinian court would allow attachment. Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cThus, the district court .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. sought to limit the subpoenas to discovery that was reasonably calculated to lead to attachable property.\xe2\x80\x9d 695 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 204\xe2\x80\x93205.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n NML and BOA later negotiated additional changes to the BOA subpoena. NML expressed its willingness to narrow its requests from BNA as well, but BNA neither engaged in negotiation nor complied with the subpoena.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Only Argentina appealed, arguing that the court\xe2\x80\x99s order transgressed the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act because it permitted discovery of Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s extraterritorial assets. The Second Circuit affirmed, holding that \xe2\x80\x9cbecause the Discovery Order involves discovery, not attachment of sovereign property, and because it is directed at third-party banks, not at Argentina itself, Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity is not infringed.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 205.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We granted certiorari. 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014).

\n

II. Analysis

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The rules governing discovery in postjudgment execution proceedings are quite permissive. Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 69(a)(2) states that, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n aid of the judgment or execution, the judgment creditor .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may obtain discovery from any person\xe2\x80\x94including the judgment debtor\xe2\x80\x94as provided in the rules or by the procedure of thestate where the court is located.\xe2\x80\x9d See 12 C. Wright, A. Miller, & R. Marcus, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa73014, p. 160 (2d ed. 1997) (hereinafter Wright & Miller) (court \xe2\x80\x9cmay use the discovery devices provided in [the federal rules] or may obtain discovery in the manner provided by the practice of the state in which the district court is held\xe2\x80\x9d). The general rule in the federal system is that, subject to the district court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion, \xe2\x80\x9c[p]arties may obtain discovery regarding any nonprivileged matter that is relevant to any party\xe2\x80\x99s claim or defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 26(b)(1). And New York law entitles judgment creditors to discover \xe2\x80\x9call matter relevant to the satisfaction of [a] judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d N.\xc2\xa0Y. Civ. Prac. Law Ann. \xc2\xa75223 (West 1997), permitting \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigation [of] any person shown to have any light to shed on the subject of the judgment debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets or their whereabouts,\xe2\x80\x9d D. Siegel, New York Practice \xc2\xa7509, p. 891 (5th ed. 2011).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The meaning of those rules was much discussed at oral argument. What if the assets targeted by the discovery request are beyond the jurisdictional reach of the court to which the request is made? May the court nonetheless permit discovery so long as the judgment creditor shows that the assets are recoverable under the laws of the jurisdictions in which they reside, whether that be Florida or France? We need not take up those issues today, since Argentina has not put them in contention. In the Court of Appeals, Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s only asserted ground for objection to the subpoenas was the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act. See 695 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 208 (\xe2\x80\x9cArgentina argues .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the normally broad scope of discovery in aid of execution is limited in this case by principles of sovereign immunity\xe2\x80\x9d). And Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s petition for writ of certiorari asked us to decide only whether that Act \xe2\x80\x9cimposes [a] limit on a United States court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to order blanket post-judgment execution discovery on the assets of a foreign state used for any activity anywhere in the world.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. 14. Plainly, then, this is not a case about the breadth of Rule 69(a)(2).[2] We thus assume without deciding that, as the Government conceded at argument, Tr. of Oral Arg. 24, and as the Second Circuit concluded below, \xe2\x80\x9cin a run-of-the-mill execution proceeding .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the district court would have been within its discretion to order the discovery from third-party banks about the judgment debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets located outside the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 695 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 208. The single, narrow question before us is whether the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act specifies a different rule when the judgment debtor is a foreign state.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To understand the effect of the Act, one must know something about the regime it replaced. Foreign sovereign immunity is, and always has been, \xe2\x80\x9ca matter of grace and comity on the part of the United States, and not a restriction imposed by the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Verlinden B. V. v. Central Bank of Nigeria,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 480,\n486 (1983)\n. Accordingly, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s practice has been to \xe2\x80\x9cdefe[r] to the decisions of the political branches\xe2\x80\x9d about whether and when to exercise judicial power over foreign states. Ibid. For the better part of the last two centuries, the political branch making the determination was the Executive, which typically requested immunity in all suits against friendly foreign states. Id., at 486\xe2\x80\x93487. But then, in 1952, the State Department embraced (in the so-called Tate Letter) the \xe2\x80\x9crestrictive\xe2\x80\x9d theory of sovereign immunity, which holds that immunity shields only a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s public, noncommercial acts. Id., at 487, and n. 9. The Tate Letter \xe2\x80\x9cthr[ew] immunity determinations into some disarray,\xe2\x80\x9d since \xe2\x80\x9cpolitical considerations sometimes led the Department to file suggestions of immunity in cases where immunity would not have been available under the restrictive theory.\xe2\x80\x9d Republic of Austria v. Altmann,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677,\n690 (2004)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). Further muddling matters, when in particular cases the State Department did not suggest immunity, courts made immunity determinations \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally by reference to prior State Department decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Verlinden, 461 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 487. Hence it was that \xe2\x80\x9csovereign immunity decisions were [being] made in two different branches, subject to a variety of factors, sometimes including diplomatic considerations. Not surprisingly, the governing standards were neither clear nor uniformly applied.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 488.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Congress abated the bedlam in 1976, replacing the old executive-driven, factor-intensive, loosely common-law-based immunity regime with the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensive set of legal standards governing claims of immunity in every civil action against a foreign state.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The key word there\xe2\x80\x94which goes a long way toward deciding this case\xe2\x80\x94is comprehensive. We have used that term often and advisedly to describe the Act\xe2\x80\x99s sweep: \xe2\x80\x9cCongress established [in the FSIA] a comprehensive framework for resolving any claim of sovereign immunity.\xe2\x80\x9d Altman, 541 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 699. The Act \xe2\x80\x9ccompre-hensively regulat[es] the amenability of foreign nations to suit in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Verlinden, supra, at 493. This means that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]fter the enactment of the FSIA, the Act\xe2\x80\x94and not the pre-existing common law\xe2\x80\x94indisputably governs the determination of whether a foreign state is entitled to sovereign immunity.\xe2\x80\x9d Samantar v. Yousuf,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 305,\n313 (2010)\n. As the Act itself instructs, \xe2\x80\x9c[c]laims of foreign states to immunity should henceforth be decided by courts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in conformity with the principles set forth in this [Act].\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71602 (emphasis added). Thus, any sort of immunity defense made by a foreign sovereign in an American court must stand on the Act\xe2\x80\x99s text. Or it must fall.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The text of the Act confers on foreign states two kinds of immunity. First and most significant, \xe2\x80\x9ca foreign state shall be immune from the jurisdiction of the courts of the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. except as provided in sections 1605 to 1607.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71604. That provision is of no help to Argentina here: A foreign state may waive jurisdictional immunity, \xc2\xa71605(a)(1), and in this case Argentina did so, see 695 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 203. Consequently, the Act makes Argentina \xe2\x80\x9cliable in the same manner and to the same extent as a private individual under like circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71606.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Act\xe2\x80\x99s second immunity-conferring provision states that \xe2\x80\x9cthe property in the United States of a foreign state shall be immune from attachment[,] arrest[,] and execution except as provided in sections 1610 and 1611 of this chapter.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71609. The exceptions to this immunity defense (we will call it \xe2\x80\x9cexecution immunity\xe2\x80\x9d) are narrower. \xe2\x80\x9cThe property in the United States of a foreign state\xe2\x80\x9d is subject to attachment, arrest, or execution if (1) it is \xe2\x80\x9cused for a commercial activity in the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71610(a), and (2) some other enumerated exception to immunity applies, such as the one allowing for waiver, see \xc2\xa71610(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(7). The Act goes on to confer a more robust execution immu-nity on designated international-organization property, \xc2\xa71611(a), property of a foreign central bank, \xc2\xa71611(b)(1), and \xe2\x80\x9cproperty of a foreign state .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [that] is, or is intended to be, used in connection with a military activity\xe2\x80\x9d and is either \xe2\x80\x9cof a military character\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cunder the control of a military authority or defense agency,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71611(b)(2).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n That is the last of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s immunity-granting sections. There is no third provision forbidding or limiting discovery in aid of execution of a foreign-sovereign judgment debtor\xe2\x80\x99s assets. Argentina concedes that no part of the Act \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly address[es] [postjudgment] discovery.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 22. Quite right. The Act speaks of discovery only once, in an subsection requiring courts to stay discovery requests directed to the United States that would interfere with criminal or national-security matters, \xc2\xa71605(g)(1). And that section explicitly suspends certain Federal Rules of Civil Procedure when such a stay is entered, see \xc2\xa71605(g)(4). Elsewhere, it is clear when the Act\xe2\x80\x99s provisions specifically applicable to suits against sovereigns displace their general federal-rule counterparts. See, e.g., \xc2\xa71608(d). Far from containing the \xe2\x80\x9cplain statement\xe2\x80\x9d necessary to preclude application of federal discovery rules, Soci\xc3\xa9t\xc3\xa9 Nationale Industrielle A\xc3\xa9rospatiale v. United States Dist. Court for Southern Dist. of Iowa,\n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522,\n539 (1987)\n, the Act says not a word on the subject.[3]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Argentina would have us draw meaning from this silence. Its argument has several parts. First, it asserts that, before and after the Tate Letter, the State Department and American courts routinely accorded absolute execution immunity to foreign-state property. If a thing belonged to a foreign sovereign, then, no matter where it was found, it was immune from execution. And absolute immunity from execution necessarily entailed immunity from discovery in aid of execution. Second, by codifying execution immunity with only a small set of exceptions, Congress merely \xe2\x80\x9cpartially lowered the previously unconditional barrier to post-judgment relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 29. Because the Act gives \xe2\x80\x9cno indication that it was authorizing courts to inquire into state property beyond the court\xe2\x80\x99s limited enforcement authority,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., Argen-tina contends, discovery of assets that do not fall within an exception to execution immunity (plainly true of a foreign state\xe2\x80\x99s extraterritorial assets) is forbidden.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The argument founders at each step. To begin with, Argentina cites no case holding that, before the Act, a foreign state\xe2\x80\x99s extraterritorial assets enjoyed absolute execution immunity in United States courts. No surprise there. Our courts generally lack authority in the first place to execute against property in other countries, so how could the question ever have arisen? See Wright & Miller \xc2\xa73013, at 156 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] writ of execution .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. can be served anywhere within the state in which the district court is held\xe2\x80\x9d). More importantly, even if Argentina were right about the scope of the common-law execution-immunity rule, then it would be obvious that the terms of \xc2\xa71609 execution immunity are narrower, since the text of that provision immunizes only foreign-state property \xe2\x80\x9cin the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d So even if Argentina were correct that \xc2\xa71609 execution immunity implies coextensive discovery-in-aid-of-execution immunity, the latter would not shield from discovery a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s extraterritorial assets.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But what of foreign-state property that would enjoy execution immunity under the Act, such as Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s diplomatic or military property? Argentina maintains that, if a judgment creditor could not ultimately execute a judgment against certain property, then it has no business pursuing discovery of information pertaining to that prop-erty. But the reason for these subpoenas is that NML does not yet know what property Argentina has and where it is, let alone whether it is executable under the relevant jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99s law. If, bizarrely, NML\xe2\x80\x99s subpoenas had sought only \xe2\x80\x9cinformation that could not lead to executable assets in the United States or abroad,\xe2\x80\x9d then Argentina likely would be correct to say that the subpoenas were unenforceable\xe2\x80\x94not because information about nonexecutable assets enjoys a penumbral \xe2\x80\x9cdiscovery immunity\xe2\x80\x9d under the Act, but because information that could not possibly lead to executable assets is simply not \xe2\x80\x9crelevant\xe2\x80\x9d to execution in the first place, Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 26(b)(1); N.\xc2\xa0Y. Civ. Prac. Law Ann. \xc2\xa75223.[4] But of course that is not what the subpoenas seek. They ask for information about Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s worldwide assets generally, so that NML can identify where Argentina may be holding property that is subject to execution. To be sure, that request is bound to turn up information about property that Argentina regards as immune. But NML may think the same property not immune. In which case, Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s self-serving legal assertion will not automatically prevail; the District Court will have to settle the matter.

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision leaves open what Argentina thinks is a gap in the statute. Could the 1976 Congress really have meant not to protect foreign states from postjudgment discovery \xe2\x80\x9cclearinghouses\xe2\x80\x9d? The riddle is not ours to solve (if it can be solved at all). It is of course possible that, had Congress anticipated the rather unusual circumstances of this case (foreign sovereign waives immunity; foreign sovereign owes money under valid judgments; foreign sovereign does not pay and apparently has no executable assets in the United States), it would have added to the Act a sentence conferring categorical discovery-in-aid-of-execution immunity on a foreign state\xe2\x80\x99s extraterritorial assets. Or, just as possible, it would have done no such thing. Either way, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he question .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is not what Congress \xe2\x80\x98would have wanted\xe2\x80\x99 but what Congress enacted in the FSIA.\xe2\x80\x9d Republic of Argentina v. Weltover, Inc.,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 607,\n618 (1992)\n.[5]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Nonetheless, Argentina and the United States urge us to consider the worrisome international-relations consequences of siding with the lower court. Discovery orders as sweeping as this one, the Government warns, will cause \xe2\x80\x9ca substantial invasion of [foreign states\xe2\x80\x99] sovereignty,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 18, and will \xe2\x80\x9c[u]ndermin[e] international comity,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 19. Worse, such orders might provoke \xe2\x80\x9creciprocal adverse treatment of the United States in foreign courts,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 20, and will \xe2\x80\x9cthreaten harm to the United States\xe2\x80\x99 foreign relations more generally,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 21. These apprehensions are better directed to that branch of government with author-ity to amend the Act\xe2\x80\x94which, as it happens, is the same branch that forced our retirement from the immunity-by-factor-balancing business nearly 40 years ago.[6]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71330, 1602 et seq., if one of several conditions is met, permits execution of a judgment rendered in the United States against a foreign sovereign only on \xe2\x80\x9cproperty in the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. used for a commercial activity.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71610(a). Accordingly, no inquiry into a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s property in the United States that is not \xe2\x80\x9cused for a commercial activity\xe2\x80\x9d could be ordered; such an inquiry,as the Court recognizes, would not be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98relevant\xe2\x80\x99 to execution in the first place.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10 (citing Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa026(b)(1)). Yet the Court permits unlimited inquiry into Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s property outside the United States, whether or not the property is \xe2\x80\x9cused for a commercial activity.\xe2\x80\x9d By what authorization does a court in the United States become a \xe2\x80\x9cclearinghouse for information,\xe2\x80\x9d ante,at 3 (internal quotation marks omitted), about any and all property held by Argentina abroad? NML may seek such information, the Court reasons, because \xe2\x80\x9cNML does not yet know what property Argentina has [outside the United States], let alone whether it is executable under the relevant jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99s law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. But see Soci\xc3\xa9t\xc3\xa9 Nationale Industrielle A\xc3\xa9rospatiale v. United States Dist. Court for Southern Dist. of Iowa,\n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522,\n542 (1987)\n (observing that other jurisdictions generally allow much more limited discovery than is available in the United States).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A court in the United States has no warrant to indulge the assumption that, outside our country, the sky may be the limit for attaching a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s property in order to execute a U. S. judgment against the foreign sovereign. Cf. \xc2\xa71602 (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder international law, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. th[e] commercial property [of a state] may be levied upon for the satisfaction of judgments rendered against [the state] in connection with [its] commercial activities.\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Without proof of any kind that other nations broadly expose a foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s property to arrest, attachment or execution, a more modest assumption is in order. See EM Ltd. v. Republic of Argentina, 695 F.\xc2\xa03d 201, 207 (CA2 2012) (recognizing that postjudgment discovery \xe2\x80\x9cmust be calculated to assist in collecting on a judgment\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Fed. Rules Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa026(b)(1), 69(a)(2))).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Unless and until the judgment debtor, here, NML, proves that other nations would allow unconstrained access to Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s assets, I would be guided by the one law we know for sure\xe2\x80\x94our own. That guide is all the more appropriate, as our law coincides with the international norm. See \xc2\xa71602. Accordingly, I would limit NML\xe2\x80\x99s discovery to property used here or abroad \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. commercial activities.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71602, 1610(a). I therefore dissent from the sweeping examination of Argentina\xe2\x80\x99s worldwide assets the Court exorbitantly approves today.
