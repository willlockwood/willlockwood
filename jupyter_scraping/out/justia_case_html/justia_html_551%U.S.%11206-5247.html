FRY v. PLILER, WARDEN

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x935247.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 20, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 11, 2007

\n

The trial judge presiding over petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s criminal trial excluded the testimony of defense-witness Pamela Maples. After his conviction, petitioner argued on appeal, inter alia, that the exclusion of Maples\xe2\x80\x99 testimony violated Chambers v. Mississippi, 410 U. S. 284, which held that a combination of erroneous evidentiary rulings rose to the level of a due-process violation. The California Court of Appeal did not explicitly address that argument in affirming, but stated, without specifying which harmless-error standard it was applying, that \xe2\x80\x9cno possible prejudice\xe2\x80\x9d could have resulted in light of the cumulative nature of Maples\xe2\x80\x99 testimony. The State Supreme Court denied discretionary review. Petitioner then filed a federal habeas petition raising the due-process and other claims. The Magistrate Judge found the state appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to recognize Chambers error an unreasonable application of clearly established law as set forth by this Court, and disagreed with the finding of \xe2\x80\x9cno possible prejudice,\xe2\x80\x9d but concluded there was an insufficient showing that the improper exclusion of Maples\xe2\x80\x99 testimony had a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial and injurious effect\xe2\x80\x9d on the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict under Brecht v. Abrahamson, 507 U. S. 619, 631. Agreeing, the District Court denied relief, and the Ninth Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0In 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254 proceedings, a federal court must assess the prejudicial impact of constitutional error in a state-court criminal trial under Brecht\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial and injurious effect\xe2\x80\x9d standard, whether or not the state appellate court recognized the error and reviewed it for harmlessness under the \xe2\x80\x9charmless beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x9d standard set forth in Chapman v. California, 386 U. S. 18, 24. Pp. 3\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0 That Brecht applies in \xc2\xa72254 cases even if the state appellate court has not found, as did the state appellate court in Brecht, that the error was harmless under Chapman, is indicated by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Brecht opinion, which did not turn on whether the state court itself conducted Chapman review, but instead cited concerns about finality, comity, and federalism as the primary reasons for adopting a less onerous standard on collateral review. 507 U. S., at 637. Since each of these concerns applies with equal force whether or not the state court reaches the Chapman question, it would be illogical to make the standard of review turn upon that contingency. Brecht, supra, at 636, distinguished. Petitioner presents a false analogy in arguing that, if Brecht applies whether or not the state appellate court conducted Chapman review, then Brecht would apply even if a State eliminated appellate review altogether. The Court also rejects petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s contention that, even if Brecht adopted a categorical rule, post-Brecht developments\xe2\x80\x94the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), as interpreted in Mitchell v. Esparza, 540 U. S. 12\xe2\x80\x94require a different review standard. That result is not suggested by Esparza, which had no reason to decide the point, nor by AEDPA, which sets forth a precondition, not an entitlement, to the grant of habeas relief. Pp. 3\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the judgment below must still be reversed because excluding Maples\xe2\x80\x99 testimony substantially and injuriously affected the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict is rejected as not fairly encompassed by the question presented. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

Affirmed.

\n

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nFRY V. PLILER
\n551 U. S. ____ (2007)

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 06-5247

JOHN FRANCIS FRY, PETITIONER v. CHERYL\nK. PLILER, WARDEN

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 11, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We decide whether a federal habeas court must assess the prejudicial impact of constitutional error in a state-court criminal trial under the \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial and injurious effect\xe2\x80\x9d standard set forth in Brecht v. Abrahamson, 507 U. S. 619 (1993), when the state appellate court failed to recognize the error and did not review it for harmlessness under the \xe2\x80\x9charmless beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x9d standard set forth in Chapman v. California, 386 U. S. 18 (1967).

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After two mistrials on account of hung juries, a third jury convicted petitioner of the 1992 murders of James and Cynthia Bell. At trial, petitioner sought to attribute the murders to one or more other persons. To that end, he offered testimony of several witnesses who linked one Anthony Hurtz to the killings. But the trial court excluded the testimony of one additional witness, Pamela Maples, who was prepared to testify that she had heard Hurtz discussing homicides bearing some resemblance to the murder of the Bells. In the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s view, the defense had provided insufficient evidence to link the incidents described by Hurtz to the murders for which petitioner was charged.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Following his conviction, petitioner appealed to the California Court of Appeal, arguing (among other things) that the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s exclusion of Maples\xe2\x80\x99 testimony deprived him of a fair opportunity to defend himself, in violation of Chambers v. Mississippi, 410 U. S. 284 (1973) (holding that a combination of erroneous evidentiary rulings rose to the level of a due process violation). Without explicitly addressing petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Chambers argument, the state appellate court held that the trial court had not abused its discretion in excluding Maples\xe2\x80\x99 testimony under California\xe2\x80\x99s evidentiary rules, adding that \xe2\x80\x9cno possible prejudice\xe2\x80\x9d could have resulted in light of the \xe2\x80\x9cmerely cumulative\xe2\x80\x9d nature of the testimony. People v. Fry, No. A072396 (Ct. App. Cal., 1st App. Dist., Mar. 30, 2000), App. 97, n.\xc2\xa017. The court did not specify which harmless-error standard it was applying in concluding that petitioner suffered \xe2\x80\x9cno possible prejudice.\xe2\x80\x9d The Supreme Court of California denied discretionary review, and petitioner did not then seek a writ of certiorari from this Court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner next filed a petition for writ of habeas corpus in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of California, raising the aforementioned due-process claim (among others). The case was initially assigned to a Magistrate Judge, who ultimately recommended denying relief. He found the state appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to recognize error under Chambers to be \xe2\x80\x9can unreasonable application of clearly established law as set forth by the Supreme Court,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 180, and disagreed with the state appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s finding of \xe2\x80\x9cno possible prejudice.\xe2\x80\x9d But he nevertheless concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthere ha[d] been an insufficient showing that the improper exclusion of the testimony of Ms. Maples had a substantial and injurious effect on the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict\xe2\x80\x9d under the standard set forth in Brecht. App. 181\xe2\x80\x93182. The District Court adopted the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s findings and recommendations in full, and a divided panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed. We granted certiorari. 549 U. S. ___ (2006).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Chapman, supra, a case that reached this Court on direct review of a state-court criminal judgment, we held that a federal constitutional error can be considered harmless only if a court is \xe2\x80\x9cable to declare a belief that it was harmless beyond a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 24. In Brecht, supra, we considered whether the Chapman standard of review applies on collateral review of a state-court criminal judgment under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. Citing concerns about finality, comity, and federalism, we rejected the Chapman standard in favor of the more forgiving standard of review applied to nonconstitutional errors on direct appeal from federal convictions. See Kotteakos v. United States, 328 U. S. 750 (1946). Under that standard, an error is harmless unless it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98had substantial and injurious effect or influence in determining the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brecht, supra, at 631 (quoting Kotteakos, supra, at 776). The question in this case is whether a federal court must assess the prejudicial impact of the unconstitutional exclusion of evidence during a state-court criminal trial under Brecht even if the state appellate court has not found, as the state appellate court in Brecht had found, that the error was harmless beyond a reasonable doubt under Chapman.[Footnote 1]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in Brecht. The primary reasons it gave for adopting a less onerous standard on collateral review of state-court criminal judgments did not turn on whether the state court itself conducted Chapman review. The opinion explained that application of Chapman would \xe2\x80\x9cundermin[e] the States\xe2\x80\x99 interest in finality,\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U. S., at 637; would \xe2\x80\x9cinfring[e] upon [the States\xe2\x80\x99] sovereignty over criminal matters,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; would undercut the historic limitation of habeas relief to those \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98grievously wronged,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; and would \xe2\x80\x9cimpos[e] significant \xe2\x80\x98societal costs,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (quoting United States\nv. Mechanik, 475 U. S. 66, 72 (1986)). Since each of\nthese concerns applies with equal force whether or not the state court reaches the Chapman question, it would be illogical to make the standard of review turn upon that contingency.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The opinion in Brecht clearly assumed that the Kotteakos standard would apply in virtually all \xc2\xa72254 cases. It suggested an exception only for the \xe2\x80\x9cunusual case\xe2\x80\x9d in which \xe2\x80\x9ca deliberate and especially egregious error of the trial type, or one that is combined with a pattern of prosecutorial misconduct \xe2\x80\xa6 infect[s] the integrity of the proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U. S., at 638, n.\xc2\xa09. This, of course, has nothing to do with whether the state court conducted harmless-error review. The concurring and dissenting opinions shared the assumption that Kotteakos would almost always be the standard on collateral review. The former stated in categorical terms that the \xe2\x80\x9cKotteakos standard\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cwill now apply on collateral review\xe2\x80\x9d of state convictions, 507 U. S., at 643 (Stevens, J., concurring). Justice White\xe2\x80\x99s dissent complained that under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion Kotteakos would apply even where (as in this case) the state court found that \xe2\x80\x9cno violation had occurred,\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U. S., at 644; and Justice O\xe2\x80\x99Connor\xe2\x80\x99s dissent stated that Chapman would \xe2\x80\x9cno longer appl[y] to any trial error asserted on habeas,\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U. S., at 651. Later cases also assumed that Brecht\xe2\x80\x99s applicability does not turn on whether the state appellate court recognized the constitutional error and reached the Chapman question. See Penry v. Johnson, 532 U. S. 782, 795 (2001); Calderon v. Coleman, 525 U. S. 141, 145 (1998) (per curiam).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s contrary position misreads (or at least exaggerates the significance of) a lone passage from our Brecht opinion. In that passage, the Court explained:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cState courts are fully qualified to identify constitutional error and evaluate its prejudicial effect on the trial process under Chapman, and state courts often occupy a superior vantage point from which to evaluate the effect of trial error. For these reasons, it scarcely seems logical to require federal habeas courts to engage in the identical approach to harmless-error review that Chapman requires state courts to engage in on direct review.\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U. S., at 636 (citation\nomitted).

\n

\nBut the quoted passage does little to advance petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s position. To say (a) that since state courts are required to evaluate constitutional error under Chapman it makes no sense to establish Chapman as the standard for federal habeas review is not at all to say (b) that whenever a state court fails in its responsibility to apply Chapman the federal habeas standard must change. It would be foolish to equate the two, in view of the other weighty reasons given in Brecht for applying a less onerous standard on collateral review\xe2\x80\x94reasons having nothing to do with whether the state court actually applied Chapman.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner argues that, if Brecht applies whether or not the state appellate court conducted Chapman review, then Brecht would apply even if a State eliminated appellate review altogether. That is not necessarily so. The federal habeas review rule applied to the class of case in which state appellate review is available does not have to be the same rule applied to the class of case where it is not. We have no occasion to resolve that hypothetical (and highly unrealistic) question now. In the case before us petitioner did obtain appellate review of his constitutional claim; the state court simply found the underlying claim weak and therefore did not measure its prejudicial impact under Chapman. The attempted analogy\xe2\x80\x94between (1) eliminating appellate review altogether and (2) providing appellate review but rejecting a constitutional claim without assessing its prejudicial impact under Chapman\xe2\x80\x94is a false one.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner contends that, even if Brecht adopted a categorical rule, post-Brecht developments require a different standard of review. Three years after we decided Brecht, Congress passed, and the President signed, the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), under which a habeas petition may not be granted unless the state court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication \xe2\x80\x9cresulted in a decision that was contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). In Mitchell v. Esparza, 540 U. S. 12 (2003) (per curiam), we held that, when a state court determines that a constitutional violation is harmless, a federal court may not award habeas relief under \xc2\xa72254 unless the harmlessness determination itself was unreasonable. Petitioner contends that \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), as interpreted in Esparza, eliminates the requirement that a petitioner also satisfy Brecht\xe2\x80\x99s standard. We think not. That conclusion is not suggested by Esparza, which had no reason to decide the point. Nor is it suggested by the text of AEDPA, which sets forth a precondition to the grant of habeas relief (\xe2\x80\x9ca writ of habeas corpus \xe2\x80\xa6 shall not be granted\xe2\x80\x9d unless the conditions of \xc2\xa72254(d) are met), not an entitlement to it. Given our frequent recognition that AEDPA limited rather than expanded the availability of habeas relief, see, e.g., Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362, 412 (2000), it is implausible that, without saying so, AEDPA replaced the Brecht standard of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98actual prejudice,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 507 U. S., at 637 (quoting United States v. Lane, 474 U. S. 438, 449 (1986)), with the more liberal AEDPA/Chapman standard which requires only that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s harmless-beyond-a-reasonable-doubt determination be unreasonable. That said, it certainly makes no sense to require formal application of both tests (AEDPA/Chapman and Brecht) when the latter obviously subsumes the former. Accordingly, the Ninth Circuit was correct to apply the Brecht standard of review in assessing the prejudicial impact of federal constitutional error in a state-court criminal trial.[Footnote 2]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner argues that, even if Brecht provides the standard of review, we must still reverse the judgment below because the exclusion of Maples\xe2\x80\x99 testimony substantially and injuriously affected the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict in this case. That argument, however, is not fairly encompassed within the question presented. We granted certiorari to decide a question that has divided the Courts of Appeals\xe2\x80\x94whether Brecht or Chapman provides the appropriate standard of review when constitutional error in a state-court trial is first recognized by a federal court. Compare, e.g., Bains v. Cambra, 204 F.\xc2\xa03d 964, 976\xe2\x80\x93977 (CA9 2000), with Orndorff v. Lockhart, 998 F.\xc2\xa02d 1426, 1429\xe2\x80\x931430 (CA8 1993). It is true that the second sentence of the question presented asks: \xe2\x80\x9cDoes it matter which harmless error standard is employed?\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. I. But to ask whether Brecht makes any real difference is not to ask whether the Ninth Circuit misapplied Brecht in this particular case. Petitioner seems to have understood this. Only in a brief footnote of his petition did he hint that the Ninth Circuit erred in its application of the Brecht standard. Pet. for Cert. 23, n.\xc2\xa019.[Footnote 3] Indeed, if application of the Brecht standard to the facts of this case were encompassed within the question presented, so too would be the question of whether there was constitutional error in the first place. After all, it would not \xe2\x80\x9cmatter which harmless error standard is employed\xe2\x80\x9d if there were no underlying constitutional error. Unlike the dissenting Justices, some of whom would reverse the decision below on the ground that the error was harmful under Brecht, and one of whom would vacate the decision below on the ground that it is unclear whether there was constitutional error in the first instance, we read the question presented to avoid these tangential and factbound questions, and limit our review to the question of whether Chapman or Brecht provides the governing standard.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that in \xc2\xa72254 proceedings a court must assess the prejudicial impact of constitutional error in a state-court criminal trial under the \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial and injurious effect\xe2\x80\x9d standard set forth in Brecht, 507 U. S. 619, whether or not the state appellate court recognized the error and reviewed it for harmlessness under the \xe2\x80\x9charmless beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x9d standard set forth in Chapman, 386 U. S. 18. Since the Ninth Circuit correctly applied the Brecht standard rather than the Chapman standard, we affirm the judgment below.

\n

It is so ordered.

\nFootnote 1

\xc2\xa0As this case comes to the Court, we assume (without deciding) that the state appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s decision affirming the exclusion of Maples\xe2\x80\x99 testimony was an unreasonable application of Chambers v. Mississippi, 410 U. S. 284, 302 (1973). We also assume that the state appellate court did not determine the harmlessness of the error under the Chapman standard, notwithstanding its ambiguous conclusion that the exclusion of Maples\xe2\x80\x99 testimony resulted in \xe2\x80\x9cno possible prejudice.\xe2\x80\x9d

\nFootnote 2

\xc2\xa0We do not agree with petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s amicus that Brecht\xe2\x80\x99s concerns regarding the finality of state-court criminal judgments and the difficulty of retrying a defendant years after the crime \xe2\x80\x9chave been largely alleviated by [AEDPA],\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9csets strict time limitations on habeas petitions and limits second or successive petitions as well.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Innocence Network 7. Even cases governed by AEDPA can span a decade, as the nearly 12-year gap between petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction and the issuance of this decision illustrates.

\nFootnote 3

\xc2\xa0The question presented included one additional issue: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f the Brecht standard applies, does the petitioner or the State bear the burden of persuasion on the question of prejudice?\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. I. We have previously held that, when a court is \xe2\x80\x9cin virtual equipoise as to the harmlessness of the error\xe2\x80\x9d under the Brecht standard, the court should \xe2\x80\x9ctreat the error \xe2\x80\xa6 as if it affected the verdict\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d O\xe2\x80\x99Neal v. McAninch, 513 U. S. 432, 435 (1995). The majority opinion below did not refer to O\xe2\x80\x99Neal, presumably because the majority harbored no grave doubt as to the harmlessness of the error. Neither did the dissenting judge refer to O\xe2\x80\x99Neal, presumably because she did not think the majority harbored grave doubt as to the harmlessness of the error. Moreover, the State has conceded throughout this \xc2\xa72254 proceeding that it bears the burden of persuasion. Thus, there is no basis on which to conclude that the court below ignored O\xe2\x80\x99Neal.

JOHN FRANCIS FRY, PETITIONER v. CHERYL\nK. PLILER, WARDEN

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 11, 2007]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Souter and Justice Ginsburg join, and with whom Justice Breyer joins in part, concurring in part and dissenting in part.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While I join all of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion except Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, I am persuaded that we should also answer the question whether the constitutional error was harmless under the standard announced in Brecht v. Abrahamson, 507 U. S. 619 (1993). The parties and the Solicitor General as amicus curiae fully briefed and argued the question, presumably because it appears to fit within the awkwardly drafted question that we agreed to review.[Footnote 1] Moreover, our answer to the question whether the error was harmless would emphasize the important point that the Brecht standard, as more fully explained in our opinion in Kotteakos v. United States, 328 U. S. 750 (1946), imposes a significant burden of persuasion on the State.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both the history of this litigation and the nature of the constitutional error involved provide powerful support for the conclusion that if the jurors had heard the testimony of Pamela Maples, they would at least have had a reasonable doubt concerning petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. Petitioner was not found guilty until after he had been tried three times. The first trial ended in a mistrial with the jury deadlocked 6 to 6. App. 121. The second trial also resulted in a mistrial due to a deadlocked jury, this time 7 to 5 in favor of conviction. Ibid. In the third trial, after the jurors had been deliberating for 11 days, the foreperson advised the judge that they were split 7 to 5 and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98hopelessly deadlocked.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 74\xe2\x80\x9375. When the judge instructed the jury to continue its deliberations, the foreperson requested clarification on the definition of \xe2\x80\x9creasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 75. The jury deliberated for an additional 23 days after that exchange\xe2\x80\x94a total of five weeks\xe2\x80\x94before finally returning a guilty verdict.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is not surprising that some jurors harbored a reasonable doubt as to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s guilt weeks into their deliberations. The only person to offer eyewitness testimony, a disinterested truckdriver, described the killer as a man who was 5\xe2\x80\x997\xe2\x80\x9d to 5\xe2\x80\x998\xe2\x80\x9d tall, weighed about 140 pounds, and had a full head of hair. Tr. 4574 (Apr. 26, 1995). Petitioner is 6\xe2\x80\x992\xe2\x80\x9d tall, weighed 300 pounds at the time of the murder, and is bald. Record, Doc. No. 13, Exh. L (arrest report); Ibid., Exh. M (petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s driver\xe2\x80\x99s license). Seven different witnesses linked the killings to a man named Anthony Hurtz, some testifying that Hurtz had admitted to them that he was in fact the killer. App. 60\xe2\x80\x9364, 179. Each of those witnesses, unlike the truckdriver, was impeached by evidence of bias, either against Hurtz or for petitioner. Id., at 61\xe2\x80\x9364, 73, 179\xe2\x80\x93180.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0However, Pamela Maples, a cousin of Hurtz\xe2\x80\x99s who was in all other respects a disinterested witness, did not testify at either of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s first two trials. During the third trial, she testified out of the presence of the jury that she had overheard statements by Hurtz that he had committed a double murder strikingly similar to that witnessed by the truckdriver. As the Magistrate Judge found, the exclusion of Maples\xe2\x80\x99 testimony for lack of foundation was clear constitutional error under Chambers v. Mississippi, 410 U. S. 284 (1973), and the State does not argue otherwise.[Footnote 3] Cf. Skipper v. South Carolina, 476 U. S. 1, 8 (1986) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe testimony of more disinterested witnesses \xe2\x80\xa6 would quite naturally be given much greater weight by the jury\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chambers error is by nature prejudicial. We have said that Chambers \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not stand for the proposition that the defendant is denied a fair opportunity to defend himself whenever a state or federal rule excludes favorable evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Scheffer, 523 U. S. 303, 316 (1998). Rather, due process considerations hold sway over state evidentiary rules only when the exclusion of evidence \xe2\x80\x9cundermine[s] fundamental elements of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 315. Hence, as a matter of law and logical inference, it is well-nigh impossible for a reviewing court to conclude that such error \xe2\x80\x9cdid not influence the jury, or had but very slight effect\xe2\x80\x9d on its verdict. Kotteakos, 328 U. S., at 764; see also O\xe2\x80\x99Neal v. McAninch, 513 U. S. 432, 445 (1995) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen a habeas court is in grave doubt as to the harmlessness of an error that affects substantial rights, it should grant relief\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is difficult to imagine a less appropriate case for an exception to that commonsense proposition. We found in Parker v. Gladden, 385 U. S. 363 (1966) (per curiam), that 26 hours of juror deliberations in a murder trial \xe2\x80\x9cindicat[ed] a difference among them as to the guilt of petitioner.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 365. Here, the jury was deprived of significant evidence of third-party guilt, and still we measure the length of deliberations by weeks, not hours. In light of the jurors\xe2\x80\x99 evident uncertainty, the prospect of rebutting the near-conclusive presumption that the Chambers error did substantial harm vanishes completely.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have not been shy in emphasizing that federal habeas courts do not lightly find constitutional error. See Carey v. Musladin, 549 U. S. ___ (2006). It follows that when they do find an error, they may not lightly discount its significance. Rather, a harmlessness finding requires \xe2\x80\x9cfair assurance, after pondering all that happened without stripping the erroneous action from the whole, that the judgment was not substantially swayed by the error.\xe2\x80\x9d Kotteakos, 328 U. S., at 765. Given \xe2\x80\x9call that happened\xe2\x80\x9d in this case, and given the nature of the error, I cannot agree with the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the erroneous exclusion of Maples\xe2\x80\x99 testimony was harmless under that standard.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, I would reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that Brecht v. Abrahamson, 507 U. S. 619 (1993), sets forth the proper standard of review. Cf. id., at 643 (Stevens, J., concurring). At the same time, I agree with Justice Stevens that we should consider the application of the standard, that the error was not harmless, and that \xe2\x80\x9cChambers error is by nature prejudicial.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3 (opinion concurring in part and dissenting in part) (citing Chambers v. Mississippi, 410 U. S. 284 (1973)). Cf. Kyles v. Whitley, 514 U. S. 419,\n435 (1995) (similar statement as to errors under\nBrady v. Maryland, 373 U. S. 83 (1963)). Nonetheless, I\nwould remand this case rather than reversing the Court of\nAppeals.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0My reason arises out of the fact that here, for purposes of deciding whether Chambers error exists, the question of harm is inextricably tied to other aspects of the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s determination. The underlying evidentiary judgment at issue involved a weighing of the probative value of proffered evidence against, e.g., its cumulative nature, its tendency to confuse or to prejudice the jury, or the likelihood that it will simply waste the jury\xe2\x80\x99s time. See App. 96\xe2\x80\x9397; Cal. Evid. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7352 (West 1995); cf. Fed. Rule Evid. 403. In this context, to find a Chambers error a court must take account both of the way in which (and extent to which) the trial court misweighed the relevant admissibility factors and of the extent to which doing so harmed the defendant. Moreover, to find this kind of error harmless, as the Court of Appeals found it, should preclude the possibility of a Chambers error; but to find this kind of error harmful does not guarantee the contrary. A garden-variety nonharmless misapplication of evidentiary principles normally will not rise to the level of a constitutional, Chambers, mistake. Cf., e.g., United States v. Scheffer, 523 U. S. 303, 308 (1998).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0All this, it seems to me, requires reconsideration by\nthe Court of Appeals of its Chambers determination. I would not consider the question whether that exclusion of evidence amounted to Chambers error because that question is not before us, see ante, at 3, n.\xc2\xa01 (opinion of the Court). But the logically inseparable question of harm is before us; and that, I believe, is sufficient.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I would remand the case to the Ninth Circuit so that, taking account of the points Justice Stevens raises, ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x934, it can reconsider whether there was an error of admissibility sufficiently serious to violate Chambers. I therefore join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion except as to footnote 1 and Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, and I join Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 opinion in part.
