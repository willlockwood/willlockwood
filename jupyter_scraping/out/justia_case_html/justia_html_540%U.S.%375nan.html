CASTRO v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x936683. Argued October 15, 2003\xe2\x80\x94Decided December 15, 2003

\n

In 1994, petitioner Castro attacked his federal drug conviction in a pro se motion for a new trial pursuant to Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 33. The Government responded that the claims were more cognizable as federal habeas claims under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255. The District Court denied Castro\xe2\x80\x99s motion on the merits, referring to it as both a Rule 33 and a \xc2\xa72255 motion. Castro did not challenge this recharacterization of his motion on his pro se appeal, and the Eleventh Circuit summarily affirmed. In 1997, Castro, again pro se, filed a \xc2\xa72255 motion raising, inter alia, a new claim for ineffective assistance of counsel. The District Court denied the motion, but the Eleventh Circuit remanded for the District Court to consider, among other things, whether this was Castro\xe2\x80\x99s second \xc2\xa72255 motion. The District Court appointed counsel, determined that the 1997 motion was indeed Castro\xe2\x80\x99s second \xc2\xa72255 motion (the 1994 motion being his first), and dismissed the motion for failure to comply with \xc2\xa72255\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that Castro obtain the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 permission to file a \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d motion. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s review of Castro\xe2\x80\x99s claim is not barred by the requirement that the \xe2\x80\x9cgrant or denial of an authorization by a court of appeals to file a second or successive application \xe2\x80\xa6 shall not be the subject of a [certiorari] petition,\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(b)(3)(E). Castro nowhere asked the Eleventh Circuit to grant, and it nowhere denied, such authorization. Contrary to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position, the court\xe2\x80\x99s statement that Castro\xe2\x80\x99s petition could not meet the requirements for second or successive petitions cannot be taken as a statutorily relevant \xe2\x80\x9cdenial\xe2\x80\x9d of an authorization request not made. Even accepting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s characterization, the argument would founder because the certiorari petition\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csubject\xe2\x80\x9d is not the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s authorization \xe2\x80\x9cdenial,\xe2\x80\x9d but the lower courts\xe2\x80\x99 refusal to recognize that this \xc2\xa72255 motion is Castro\xe2\x80\x99s first. Moreover, reading the statute as the Government suggests would create procedural anomalies, allowing review where the lower court decision disfavors, but denying review where it favors, the Government; would close this Court\xe2\x80\x99s doors to a class of habeas petitioners without any clear indication that such was Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent; and would be difficult to reconcile with the principle that this Court reads limitations on its jurisdiction narrowly. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0A federal court cannot recharacterize a pro se litigant\xe2\x80\x99s motion as a first \xc2\xa72255 motion unless it first informs the litigant of its intent to recharacterize, warns the litigant that this recharacterization means that any subsequent \xc2\xa72255 motion will be subject to the restrictions on \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d motions, and provides the litigant an opportunity to withdraw the motion or to amend it so that it contains all the \xc2\xa72255 claims he believes he has. If these warnings are not given, the motion cannot be considered to have become a \xc2\xa72255 motion for purposes of applying to later motions the law\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d restrictions. Nine Circuits have placed such limits on recharacterization, and no one here contests the lawfulness of this judicially created requirement. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0Because the District Court failed to give the prescribed warnings, Castro\xe2\x80\x99s 1994 motion cannot be considered a first \xc2\xa72255 motion and his 1997 motion cannot be considered a second or successive one. The Government argues that Castro\xe2\x80\x99s failure to appeal the 1994 recharacterization makes the recharacterization valid as a matter of \xe2\x80\x9claw of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d And, according to the Government, since the 1994 recharacterization is valid, the 1997 \xc2\xa72255 motion is Castro\xe2\x80\x99s second, not his first. This Court disagrees. The point of a warning is to help the pro se litigant understand not only (1) whether he should withdraw or amend his motion, but also (2) whether he should contest the recharacterization, say, on appeal. The lack of warning prevents his making an informed judgment as to both. The failure to appeal simply underscores the practical importance of providing the warning. Hence, an unwarned recharacterization cannot count as a \xc2\xa72255 motion for purposes of the \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d provision whether or not the unwarned pro se litigant takes an appeal. Even assuming that the law of the case doctrine applies here, the doctrine simply expresses common judicial practice; it does not limit the courts\xe2\x80\x99 power. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

290 F.\xc2\xa03d 1270, vacated and remanded.

HERNAN O\xe2\x80\x99RYAN CASTRO, PETITIONER v. UNITED\nSTATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[December 15, 2003]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under a longstanding practice, a court sometimes treats as a request for habeas relief under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255 a motion that a pro se federal prisoner has labeled differently. Such recharacterization can have serious consequences for the prisoner, for it subjects any subsequent motion under \xc2\xa72255 to the restrictive conditions that federal law imposes upon a \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d (but not upon a first) federal habeas motion. \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb6 ;8. In light of these consequences, we hold that the court cannot so recharacterize a pro se litigant\xe2\x80\x99s motion as the litigant\xe2\x80\x99s first \xc2\xa72255 motion unless the court informs the litigant of its intent to recharacterize, warns the litigant that the recharacterization will subject subsequent \xc2\xa72255 motions to the law\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d restrictions, and provides the litigant with an opportunity to withdraw, or to amend, the filing. Where these things are not done, a recharacterized motion will not count as a \xc2\xa72255 motion for purposes of applying \xc2\xa72255\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d provision.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case focuses upon two motions that Hernan O\xe2\x80\x99Ryan Castro, a federal prisoner acting pro se, filed in federal court. He filed the first motion in 1994, the second in 1997.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The relevant facts surrounding the 1994 motion are the following:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1) On July 5, 1994, Castro filed a pro se motion attacking his federal drug conviction, a motion that he called a Rule 33 motion for a new trial. See Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 33.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2) The Government, in its response, said that Castro\xe2\x80\x99s claims were \xe2\x80\x9cmore properly cognizable\xe2\x80\x9d as federal habeas corpus claims, i.e., claims made under the authority of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255. But, the Government added, it did not object to the court\xe2\x80\x99s considering Castro\xe2\x80\x99s motion as having invoked both Rule 33 and \xc2\xa72255.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(3) The District Court denied Castro\xe2\x80\x99s motion on the merits. In its accompanying opinion, the court generally referred to Castro\xe2\x80\x99s motion as a Rule 33 motion; but the court twice referred to it as a \xc2\xa72255 motion as well. App. 137\xe2\x80\x93144.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(4) Castro, still acting pro se, appealed, but he did not challenge the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s recharacterization of his motion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(5) The Court of Appeals summarily affirmed. It said in its one-paragraph order that it was ruling on a motion based upon both Rule 33 and \xc2\xa72255. Judgt. order reported at 82 F.\xc2\xa03d 429 (CA11 1996); App. 147.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The relevant facts surrounding the 1997 motion are the following:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1) On April 18, 1997, Castro, acting pro se, filed what he called a \xc2\xa72255 motion. The motion included claims not raised in the 1994 motion, including a claim of ineffective assistance of counsel.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2) The District Court denied the motion; Castro appealed; and the Court of Appeals remanded for further consideration of the ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim. It also asked the District Court to consider whether, in light of the 1994 motion, Castro\xe2\x80\x99s motion was his second \xc2\xa72255 motion, rather than his first.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(3) On remand, the District Court appointed counsel for Castro. It then decided that the 1997 motion was indeed Castro\xe2\x80\x99s second \xc2\xa72255 motion (the 1994 motion being his first). And it dismissed the motion for failure to comply with one of \xc2\xa72255\xe2\x80\x99s restrictive \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d conditions (namely, Castro\xe2\x80\x99s failure to obtain the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 permission to file a \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d motion). \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb6 ;8. The District Court granted Castro a certificate to appeal its \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d determination. \xc2\xa72253(c)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(4) The Eleventh Circuit affirmed by a split (2-to-1) vote. 290 F.\xc2\xa03d 1270 (2002). The majority \xe2\x80\x9csuggested\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9curged\xe2\x80\x9d district courts in the future to \xe2\x80\x9cwarn prisoners of the consequences of recharacterization and provide them with the opportunity to amend or dismiss their filings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1273, 1274. But it held that the 1994 court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to do so did not legally undermine its recharacterization. Hence, Castro\xe2\x80\x99s current \xc2\xa72255 motion was indeed his second habeas motion. Id., at 1274.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Other Circuits have taken a different approach. E.g., United States v. Palmer, 296 F. 3d 1135, 1145\xe2\x80\x931147 (CADC 2002) (announcing a rule requiring courts to notify pro se litigants prior to recharacterization and refusing to find the \xc2\xa72255 motion before it \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d since such notice was lacking). We consequently granted Castro\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin with a jurisdictional matter. We asked the parties to consider the relevance of a provision in the federal habeas corpus statutes that says that the

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cgrant or denial of an authorization by a court of appeals to file a second or successive application \xe2\x80\xa6 shall not be the subject of a petition for \xe2\x80\xa6 a writ of certiorari.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa72244(b)(3)(E).

\n

\nAfter receiving the parties\xe2\x80\x99 responses, we conclude that this provision does not bar our review here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Castro\xe2\x80\x99s appeal to the Eleventh Circuit did not concern an \xe2\x80\x9cauthorization . . . to file a second or successive application.\xe2\x80\x9d The District Court certified for appeal the question whether Castro\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa72255 motion was his first such motion or his second. Castro then argued to the Eleventh Circuit that his \xc2\xa72255 motion was his first; and he asked the court to reverse the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s dismissal of that motion. He nowhere asked the Court of Appeals to grant, and it nowhere denied, any \xe2\x80\x9cauthorization \xe2\x80\xa6 to file a second or successive application.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government argues that the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion had the effect of denying \xe2\x80\x9cauthorization . . . to file a second . . . application\xe2\x80\x9d because the court said in its opinion that Castro\xe2\x80\x99s motion could not meet the requirements for second or successive motions. 290 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1273. For that reason, the Government concludes, the court\xe2\x80\x99s decision falls within the scope of the jurisdictional provision. Brief for United States 16.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In our view, however, this argument stretches the words of the statute too far. Given the context, we cannot take these words in the opinion as a statutorily relevant \xe2\x80\x9cdenial\xe2\x80\x9d of a request that was not made. Even if, for argument\xe2\x80\x99s sake, we were to accept the Government\xe2\x80\x99s characterization, the argument nonetheless would founder on the statute\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that the \xe2\x80\x9cdenial\xe2\x80\x9d must be the \xe2\x80\x9csubject\xe2\x80\x9d of the certiorari petition. The \xe2\x80\x9csubject\xe2\x80\x9d of Castro\xe2\x80\x99s petition is not the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cdenial of an authorization.\xe2\x80\x9d It is the lower courts\xe2\x80\x99 refusal to recognize that this \xc2\xa72255 motion is his first, not his second. That is a very different question. Cf. Adamo Wrecking Co. v. United States, 434 U. S. 275, 282\xe2\x80\x93283 (1978) (statute barring court review of lawfulness of agency \xe2\x80\x9cemission standard\xe2\x80\x9d in criminal case does not bar court review of whether regulation is an \xe2\x80\x9cemission standard\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, reading the statute as the Government suggests would produce troublesome results. It would create procedural anomalies, allowing review where the lower court decision disfavors, but denying review where it favors, the Government. Cf. Stewart v. Martinez-Villareal, 523 U. S. 637, 641\xe2\x80\x93642 (1998) (allowing the Government to obtain review of a decision that a habeas corpus application is not \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d). It would close our doors to a class of habeas petitioners seeking review without any clear indication that such was Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent. Cf. Felker v. Turpin, 518 U. S. 651, 660\xe2\x80\x93661 (1996). And any such conclusion would prove difficult to reconcile with the basic principle that we \xe2\x80\x9cread limitations on our jurisdiction to review narrowly.\xe2\x80\x9d Utah v. Evans, 536 U. S. 452, 463 (2002).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude that we have the power to review Castro\xe2\x80\x99s claim, and we turn to the merits of that claim.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Federal courts sometimes will ignore the legal label that a pro se litigant attaches to a motion and recharacterize the motion in order to place it within a different legal category. See, e.g., Raineri v. United States, 233 F.\xc2\xa03d 96, 100 (CA1 2000); United States v. Detrich, 940 F.\xc2\xa02d 37, 38 (CA2 1991); United States v. Miller, 197 F.\xc2\xa03d 644, 648 (CA3 1999); Raines v. United States, 423 F.\xc2\xa02d 526, 528, n.\xc2\xa01 (CA4 1970); United States v. Santora, 711 F.\xc2\xa02d 41, 42 (CA5 1983); United States v. McDowell, 305 F.\xc2\xa02d 12, 14 (CA6 1962); Henderson v. United States, 264 F.\xc2\xa03d 709, 711 (CA7 2001); McIntyre v. United States, 508 F.\xc2\xa02d 403, n. 1 (CA8 1975) (per curiam); United States v. Eatinger, 902 F.\xc2\xa02d 1383, 1385 (CA9 1990) (per curiam); United States v. Kelly, 235 F.\xc2\xa03d 1238, 1242 (CA10 2000); United States v. Jordan, 915 F.\xc2\xa02d 622, 625 (CA11 1990); United States v. Tindle, 522 F.\xc2\xa02d 689, 693 (CADC 1975) (per curiam). They may do so in order to avoid an unnecessary dismissal, e.g., id., at 692\xe2\x80\x93693, to avoid inappropriately stringent application of formal labeling requirements, see Haines v. Kerner, 404 U. S. 519, 520 (1972) (per curiam), or to create a better correspondence between the substance of a pro se motion\xe2\x80\x99s claim and its underlying legal basis. See Hughes v. Rowe, 449 U. S. 5, 10 (1980) (per curiam); Andrews v. United States, 373 U. S. 334 (1963).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We here address one aspect of this practice, namely, certain legal limits that nine Circuits have placed on recharacterization. Those Circuits recognize that, by recharacterizing as a first \xc2\xa72255 motion a pro se litigant\xe2\x80\x99s filing that did not previously bear that label, the court may make it significantly more difficult for that litigant to file another such motion. They have consequently concluded that a district court may not recharacterize a pro se litigant\xe2\x80\x99s motion as a request for relief under \xc2\xa72255\xe2\x80\x94unless the court first warns the pro se litigant about the consequences of the recharacterization, thereby giving the litigant an opportunity to contest the recharacterization, or to withdraw or amend the motion. See Adams v. United States, 155 F.\xc2\xa03d 582, 583 (CA2 1998) (per curiam); United States v. Miller, supra, at 646\xe2\x80\x93647 (CA3); United States v. Emmanuel, 288 F.\xc2\xa03d 644, 646\xe2\x80\x93647 (CA4 2002); In\xc2\xa0re Shelton, 295 F.\xc2\xa03d 620, 622 (CA6 2002) (per curiam); Henderson v. United States, supra, at 710\xe2\x80\x93711 (CA7); Morales v. United States, 304 F.\xc2\xa03d 764, 767 (CA8 2002); United States v. Seesing, 234 F.\xc2\xa03d 456, 463 (CA9 2000); United States v. Kelly, supra, at 1240\xe2\x80\x931241 (CA10); United States v. Palmer, 296 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1146 (CADC); see also 290 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1273, 1274 (case below) (suggesting that courts provide such warnings).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No one here contests the lawfulness of this judicially created requirement. The Government suggests that Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 47 provides adequate underlying legal authority for the procedural practice. Brief for United States 42. It suggests that this Court has the authority to regulate the practice through \xe2\x80\x9cthe exercise\xe2\x80\x9d of our \xe2\x80\x9csupervisory powers\xe2\x80\x9d over the federal judiciary. E.g., McNabb v. United States, 318 U. S. 332, 340\xe2\x80\x93341 (1943). And it notes that limiting the courts\xe2\x80\x99 authority to recharacterize, approximately as the Courts of Appeals have done, \xe2\x80\x9cis likely to reduce and simplify litigation over questions of characterization, which are often quite difficult.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 42.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We agree with these suggestions. We consequently hold, as almost every Court of Appeals has already held, that the lower courts\xe2\x80\x99 recharacterization powers are limited in the following way:

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The limitation applies when a court recharacterizes a pro se litigant\xe2\x80\x99s motion as a first \xc2\xa72255 motion. In such circumstances the district court must notify the pro se litigant that it intends to recharacterize the pleading, warn the litigant that this recharacterization means that any subsequent \xc2\xa72255 motion will be subject to the restrictions on \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d motions, and provide the litigant an opportunity to withdraw the motion or to amend it so that it contains all the \xc2\xa72255 claims he believes he has. If the court fails to do so, the motion cannot be considered to have become a \xc2\xa72255 motion for purposes of applying to later motions the law\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d restrictions. \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb6 ;8.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court that considered Castro\xe2\x80\x99s 1994 motion failed to give Castro warnings of the kind we have described. Moreover, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csupervisory power\xe2\x80\x9d determinations normally apply, like other judicial decisions, retroactively, at least to the case in which the determination was made. McNabb, supra, at 347 (applying new supervisory rule to case before the Court). Hence, given our holding in Part III, supra, Castro\xe2\x80\x99s 1994 motion cannot be considered a first \xc2\xa72255 motion, and his 1997 motion cannot be considered a \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d motion\xe2\x80\x94unless there is something special about Castro\xe2\x80\x99s case.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government argues that there is something special: Castro failed to appeal the 1994 recharacterization. According to the Government, that fact makes the 1994 recharacterization valid as a matter of \xe2\x80\x9claw of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d And, since the 1994 recharacterization is valid, the 1997 \xc2\xa72255 motion is Castro\xe2\x80\x99s second, not his first.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not agree. No Circuit that has considered whether to treat a \xc2\xa72255 motion as successive (based on a prior unwarned recharacterization) has found that the litigant\xe2\x80\x99s failure to challenge that recharacterization makes a difference. See Palmer, supra, at 1147; see also Henderson, supra, at 711\xe2\x80\x93712; Raineri, 233 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 100; In\xc2\xa0re Shelton, supra, at 622. That is not surprising, for the very point of the warning is to help the pro se litigant understand not only (1) whether he should withdraw or amend his motion, but also (2) whether he should contest the recharacterization, say, on appeal. The \xe2\x80\x9clack of warning\xe2\x80\x9d prevents his making an informed judgment in respect to the latter just as it does in respect to the former. Indeed, an unwarned pro se litigant\xe2\x80\x99s failure to appeal a recharacterization simply underscores the practical importance of providing the warning. Hence, an unwarned recharacterization cannot count as a \xc2\xa72255 motion for purposes of the \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d provision, whether the unwarned pro se litigant does, or does not, take an appeal.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The law of the case doctrine cannot pose an insurmountable obstacle to our reaching this conclusion. Assuming for argument\xe2\x80\x99s sake that the doctrine applies here, it simply \xe2\x80\x9cexpresses\xe2\x80\x9d common judicial \xe2\x80\x9cpractice\xe2\x80\x9d; it does not \xe2\x80\x9climit\xe2\x80\x9d the courts\xe2\x80\x99 power. See Messenger v. Anderson, 225 U. S. 436, 444 (1912) (Holmes, J.). It cannot prohibit a court from disregarding an earlier holding in an appropriate case which, for the reasons set forth, we find this case to be.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0So ordered.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, with whom Justice Thomas joins, concurring in part and concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I concur in Parts I and II of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion and in the judgment of the Court. I also agree that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s consideration of Castro\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to the status of his recharacterized motion is neither barred by nor necessarily resolved by the doctrine of law of the case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I write separately because I disagree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s laissez-faire attitude toward recharacterization. The Court promulgates a new procedure to be followed if the district court desires the recharacterized motion to count against the pro se litigant as a first 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255 motion in later litigation. (This procedure, by the way, can be ignored with impunity by a court bent upon aiding pro se litigants at all costs; the only consequence will be that the litigants\xe2\x80\x99 later \xc2\xa72255 submissions cannot be deemed \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive.\xe2\x80\x9d) The Court does not, however, place any limits on when recharacterization may occur, but to the contrary treats it as a routine practice which may be employed \xe2\x80\x9cto avoid an unnecessary dismissal,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cto avoid inappropriately stringent application of formal labeling requirements,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cto create a better correspondence between the substance of a pro se motion\xe2\x80\x99s claim and its underlying legal basis.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. The Court does not address whether Castro\xe2\x80\x99s motion filed under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 33 should have been recharacterized, and its discussion scrupulously avoids placing any limits on the circumstances in which district courts are permitted to recharacterize. That is particularly regrettable since the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new recharacterization procedure does not include an option for the pro se litigant to insist that the district court rule on his motion as filed; and gives scant indication of what might be a meritorious ground for contesting the recharacterization on appeal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, this approach gives too little regard to the exceptional nature of recharacterization within an adversarial system, and neglects the harm that may be caused pro se litigants even when courts do comply with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s newly minted procedure. The practice of judicial recharacterization of pro se litigants\xe2\x80\x99 motions is a mutation of the principle that the allegations of a pro se litigant\xe2\x80\x99s complaint are to be held \xe2\x80\x9cto less stringent standards than formal pleadings drafted by lawyers.\xe2\x80\x9d Haines v. Kerner, 404 U. S. 519, 520 (1972) (per curiam). \xe2\x80\x9cLiberal construction\xe2\x80\x9d of pro se pleadings is merely an embellishment of the notice-pleading standard set forth in the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, and thus is consistent with the general principle of American jurisprudence that \xe2\x80\x9cthe party who brings a suit is master to decide what law he will rely upon.\xe2\x80\x9d The Fair v. Kohler Die & Specialty Co., 228 U. S. 22, 25 (1913). Our adversary system is designed around the premise that the parties know what is best for them, and are responsible for advancing the facts and arguments entitling them to relief.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Recharacterization is unlike \xe2\x80\x9cliberal construction,\xe2\x80\x9d in that it requires a court deliberately to override the pro se litigant\xe2\x80\x99s choice of procedural vehicle for his claim. It is thus a paternalistic judicial exception to the principle of party self-determination, born of the belief that the \xe2\x80\x9cparties know better\xe2\x80\x9d assumption does not hold true for pro se prisoner litigants.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I am frankly not enamored of any departure from our traditional adversarial principles. It is not the job of a federal court to create a \xe2\x80\x9cbetter correspondence\xe2\x80\x9d between the substance of a claim and its underlying procedural basis. But if departure from traditional adversarial principles is to be allowed, it should certainly not occur in any situation where there is a risk that the patronized litigant will be harmed rather than assisted by the court\xe2\x80\x99s intervention. It is not just a matter of whether the litigant is more likely, or even much more likely, to be helped rather than harmed. For the overriding rule of judicial intervention must be \xe2\x80\x9cFirst, do no harm.\xe2\x80\x9d The injustice caused by letting the litigant\xe2\x80\x99s own mistake lie is regrettable, but incomparably less than the injustice of producing prejudice through the court\xe2\x80\x99s intervention.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The risk of harming the litigant always exists when the court recharacterizes into a first \xc2\xa72255 motion a claim that is procedurally or substantively deficient in the manner filed. The court essentially substitutes the litigant\xe2\x80\x99s ability to bring his merits claim now, for the litigant\xe2\x80\x99s later ability to bring the same claim (or any other claim), perhaps with stronger evidence. For the later \xc2\xa72255 motion will then be burdened by the limitations on second or successive petitions imposed by AEDPA (the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996, 110 Stat. 1214). A pro se litigant whose non-\xc2\xa72255 motion is dismissed on procedural grounds and one whose recharacterized \xc2\xa72255 claim is denied on the merits both end up as losers in their particular actions, but the loser on procedure is better off because he is not stuck with the consequences of a \xc2\xa72255 motion that he never filed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It would be an inadequate response to this concern to state that district courts should recharacterize into first \xc2\xa72255 motions only when doing so is (1) procedurally necessary (2) to grant relief on the merits of the underlying claim. Ensuring that these conditions are met would often enmesh district courts in fact- and labor-intensive inquiries. It is an inefficient use of judicial resources to analyze the merits of every claim brought by means of a questionable procedural vehicle simply in order to determine whether to recharacterize\xe2\x80\x94particularly in the common situation in which entitlement to relief turns on resolution of disputed facts. Moreover, even after that expenditure of effort the district court cannot be certain it is not prejudicing the litigant: the court of appeals may not agree with it on the merits of the claim.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In other words, even fully informed district courts that try their best not to harm pro se litigants by recharacterizing may nonetheless end up doing so because they cannot predict and protect against every possible adverse effect that may flow from recharacterization. But if district courts are unable to provide this sort of protection, they should not recharacterize into first \xc2\xa72255 motions at all. This option is available under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, even though the opinion does not prescribe it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court today relieves Castro of the consequences of the recharacterization (to-wit, causing his current \xc2\xa72255 motion to be dismissed as \xe2\x80\x9csecond or successive\xe2\x80\x9d) because he was not given the warning that its opinion prescribes. I reach the same result for a different reason. Even if one does not agree with me that, because of the risk involved, pleadings should never be recharacterized into first \xc2\xa72255 motions, surely one must agree that running the risk is unjustified when there is nothing whatever to be gained by the recharacterization. That is the situation here. Castro\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 33 motion was valid as a procedural matter, and the claim it raised was no weaker on the merits when presented under Rule 33 than when presented under \xc2\xa72255. The recharacterization was therefore unquestionably improper, and Castro should be relieved of its consequences.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, I concur in the judgment of the Court.
