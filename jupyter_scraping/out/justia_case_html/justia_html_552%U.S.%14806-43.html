\nSYLLABUS
\nOCTOBER TERM, 2007
\nSTONERIDGE INVESTMENT PARTNERS, LLC V.SCIENTIFIC-ATLANTA, INC.
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

STONERIDGE INVESTMENT PARTNERS, LLC v.\nSCIENTIFIC-ATLANTA, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x9343.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 9, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 15, 2008

\n

Alleging losses after purchasing Charter Communications, Inc., common stock, petitioner filed suit against respondents and others under \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935. Acting as Charter\xe2\x80\x99s customers and suppliers, respondents had agreed to arrangements that allowed Charter to mislead its auditor and issue a misleading financial statement affecting its stock price, but they had no role in preparing or disseminating the financial statement. Affirming the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s dismissal of respondents, the Eighth Circuit ruled that the allegations did not show that respondents made misstatements relied upon by the public or violated a duty to disclose. The court observed that, at most, respondents had aided and abetted Charter\xe2\x80\x99s misstatement, and noted that the private cause of action this Court has found implied in \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, Superintendent of Ins. of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. Bankers Life & Casualty Co., 404 U. S. 6, 13, n.\xc2\xa09, does not extend to aiding and abetting a \xc2\xa710(b) violation, see Central Bank of Denver, N. A. v. First Interstate Bank of Denver, N. A., 511 U. S. 164, 191.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The \xc2\xa710(b) private right of action does not reach respondents because Charter investors did not rely upon respondents\xe2\x80\x99 statements or representations. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Although Central Bank prompted calls for creation of an express cause of action for aiding and abetting, Congress did not follow this course. Instead, in \xc2\xa7104 of the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PSLRA), it directed the SEC to prosecute aiders and abettors. Thus, the \xc2\xa710(b) private right of action does not extend to aiders and abettors. Because the conduct of a secondary actor must therefore satisfy each of the elements or preconditions for \xc2\xa710(b) liability, the plaintiff must prove, as here relevant, reliance upon a material misrepresentation or omission by the defendant. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Court has found a rebuttable presumption of reliance in two circumstances. First, if there is an omission of a material fact by one with a duty to disclose, the investor to whom the duty was owed need not provide specific proof of reliance. Affiliated Ute Citizens of Utah v. United States, 406 U. S. 128, 153\xe2\x80\x93154. Second, under the fraud-on-the-market doctrine, reliance is presumed when the statements at issue become public. Neither presumption applies here: Respondents had no duty to disclose; and their deceptive acts were not communicated to the investing public during the relevant times. Petitioner, as a result, cannot show reliance upon any of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 actions except in an indirect chain that is too remote for liability. P.\xc2\xa08.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s reference to so-called \xe2\x80\x9cscheme liability\xe2\x80\x9d does not, absent a public statement, answer the objection that petitioner did not in fact rely upon respondents\xe2\x80\x99 deceptive conduct. Were the Court to adopt petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s concept of reliance\xe2\x80\x94i.e., that in an efficient market investors rely not only upon the public statements relating to a security but also upon the transactions those statements reflect\xe2\x80\x94the implied cause of action would reach the whole marketplace in which the issuing company does business. There is no authority for this rule. Reliance is tied to causation, leading to the inquiry whether respondents\xe2\x80\x99 deceptive acts were immediate or remote to the injury. Those acts, which were not disclosed to the investing public, are too remote to satisfy the reliance requirement. It was Charter, not respondents, that misled its auditor and filed fraudulent financial statements; nothing respondents did made it necessary or inevitable for Charter to record the transactions as it did. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents counsel against petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to extend the \xc2\xa710(b) private cause of action beyond the securities markets into the realm of ordinary business operations, which are governed, for the most part, by state law. See, e.g., Marine Bank v. Weaver, 455 U. S. 551, 556. The argument that there could be a reliance finding if this were a common-law fraud action is answered by the fact that \xc2\xa710(b) does not incorporate common-law fraud into federal law, see, e.g., SEC v. Zandford, 535 U. S. 813, 820, and should not be interpreted to provide a private cause of action against the entire marketplace in which the issuing company operates, cf. Blue Chip Stamps v. Manor Drug Stores, 421 U. S. 723, 733, n.\xc2\xa05. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s theory, moreover, would put an unsupportable interpretation on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 specific response to Central Bank in PSLRA \xc2\xa7104 by, in substance, reviving the implied cause of action against most aiders and abettors and thereby undermining Congress\xe2\x80\x99 determination that this class of defendants should be pursued only by the SEC. The practical consequences of such an expansion provide a further reason to reject petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s approach. The extensive discovery and the potential for uncertainty and disruption in a lawsuit could allow plaintiffs with weak claims to extort settlements from innocent companies. See, e.g., Blue Chip, supra, at 740\xe2\x80\x93741. It would also expose to such risks a new class of defendants\xe2\x80\x94overseas firms with no other exposure to U. S. securities laws\xe2\x80\x94thereby deterring them from doing business here, raising the cost of being a publicly traded company under U. S. law, and shifting securities offerings away from domestic capital markets. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Upon full consideration, the history of the \xc2\xa710(b) private right of action and the careful approach the Court has taken before proceeding without congressional direction provide further reasons to find no liability here. The \xc2\xa710(b) private cause of action is a judicial construct that Congress did not direct in the text of the relevant statutes. See, e.g., Lampf, Pleva, Lipkind, Prupis & Petigrow v. Gilbertson, 501 U. S. 350, 358\xe2\x80\x93359. Separation of powers provides good reason for the now-settled view that an implied cause of action exists only if the underlying statute can be interpreted to disclose the intent to create one, see, e.g., Alexander v. Sandoval, 532 U. S. 275, 286\xe2\x80\x93287. The decision to extend the cause of action is thus for the Congress, not for this Court. This restraint is appropriate in light of the PSLRA, in which Congress ratified the implied right of action after the Court moved away from a broad willingness to imply such private rights, see, e.g., Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. v. Curran, 456 U. S. 353, 381\xe2\x80\x93382, and n. 66. It is appropriate for the Court to assume that when PSLRA \xc2\xa7104 was enacted, Congress accepted the \xc2\xa710(b) private right as then defined but chose to extend it no further. See, e.g., Alexander, supra, at 286\xe2\x80\x93287. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

443 F.\xc2\xa03d 987, affirmed and remanded.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nSTONERIDGE INVESTMENT PARTNERS, LLC V.SCIENTIFIC-ATLANTA, INC.
\n552 U. S. ____ (2008)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 06-43
\n
\n

STONERIDGE INVESTMENT PARTNERS, LLC,\nPETITIONER v. SCIENTIFIC-ATLANTA,\nINC., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

[January 15, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We consider the reach of the private right of action the Court has found implied in \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, 48 Stat. 891, as amended, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j(b), and SEC Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.10b\xe2\x80\x935 (2007). In this suit investors alleged losses after purchasing common stock. They sought to impose liability on entities who, acting both as customers and suppliers, agreed to arrangements that allowed the investors\xe2\x80\x99 company to mislead its auditor and issue a misleading financial statement affecting the stock price. We conclude the implied right of action does not reach the customer/supplier companies because the investors did not rely upon their statements or representations. We affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This class-action suit by investors was filed against Charter Communications, Inc., in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Missouri. Stoneridge Investment Partners, LLC, a limited liability company organized under the laws of Delaware, was the lead plaintiff and is petitioner here.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Charter issued the financial statements and the securities in question. It was a named defendant along with some of its executives and Arthur Andersen LLP, Charter\xe2\x80\x99s independent auditor during the period in question. We are concerned, though, with two other defendants, respondents here. Respondents are Scientific-Atlanta, Inc., and Motorola, Inc. They were suppliers, and later customers, of Charter.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For purposes of this proceeding, we take these facts, alleged by petitioner, to be true. Charter, a cable operator, engaged in a variety of fraudulent practices so its quarterly reports would meet Wall Street expectations for cable subscriber growth and operating cash flow. The fraud included misclassification of its customer base; delayed reporting of terminated customers; improper capitalization of costs that should have been shown as expenses; and manipulation of the company\xe2\x80\x99s billing cutoff dates to inflate reported revenues. In late 2000, Charter executives realized that, despite these efforts, the company would miss projected operating cash flow numbers by $15 to $20 million. To help meet the shortfall, Charter decided to alter its existing arrangements with respondents, Scientific-Atlanta and Motorola. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s theory as to whether Arthur Andersen was altogether misled or, on the other hand, knew the structure of the contract arrangements and was complicit to some degree, is not clear at this stage of the case. The point, however, is neither controlling nor significant for our present disposition, and in our decision we assume it was misled.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents supplied Charter with the digital cable converter (set top) boxes that Charter furnished to its customers. Charter arranged to overpay respondents $20 for each set top box it purchased until the end of the year, with the understanding that respondents would return the overpayment by purchasing advertising from Charter. The transactions, it is alleged, had no economic substance; but, because Charter would then record the advertising purchases as revenue and capitalize its purchase of the set top boxes, in violation of generally accepted accounting principles, the transactions would enable Charter to fool its auditor into approving a financial statement showing it met projected revenue and operating cash flow numbers. Respondents agreed to the arrangement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0So that Arthur Andersen would not discover the link between Charter\xe2\x80\x99s increased payments for the boxes and the advertising purchases, the companies drafted documents to make it appear the transactions were unrelated and conducted in the ordinary course of business. Following a request from Charter, Scientific-Atlanta sent documents to Charter stating\xe2\x80\x94falsely\xe2\x80\x94that it had increased production costs. It raised the price for set top boxes for the rest of 2000 by $20 per box. As for Motorola, in a written contract Charter agreed to purchase from Motorola a specific number of set top boxes and pay liquidated damages of $20 for each unit it did not take. The contract was made with the expectation Charter would fail to purchase all the units and pay Motorola the liquidated damages.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To return the additional money from the set top box sales, Scientific-Atlanta and Motorola signed contracts with Charter to purchase advertising time for a price higher than fair value. The new set top box agreements were backdated to make it appear that they were negotiated a month before the advertising agreements. The backdating was important to convey the impression that the negotiations were unconnected, a point Arthur Andersen considered necessary for separate treatment of the transactions. Charter recorded the advertising payments to inflate revenue and operating cash flow by approximately $17 million. The inflated number was shown on financial statements filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and reported to the public.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents had no role in preparing or disseminating Charter\xe2\x80\x99s financial statements. And their own financial statements booked the transactions as a wash, under generally accepted accounting principles. It is alleged respondents knew or were in reckless disregard of Charter\xe2\x80\x99s intention to use the transactions to inflate its revenues and knew the resulting financial statements issued by Charter would be relied upon by research analysts and investors.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner filed a securities fraud class action on behalf of purchasers of Charter stock alleging that, by participating in the transactions, respondents violated \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and SEC Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court granted respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion to dismiss for failure to state a claim on which relief can be granted. The United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit affirmed. In\xc2\xa0re Charter Communications, Inc., Securities Litigation, 443 F.\xc2\xa03d 987 (2006). In its view the allegations did not show that respondents made misstatements relied upon by the public or that they violated a duty to disclose; and on this premise it found no violation of \xc2\xa710(b) by respondents. Id., at 992. At most, the court observed, respondents had aided and abetted Charter\xe2\x80\x99s misstatement of its financial results; but, it noted, there is no private right of action for aiding and abetting a \xc2\xa710(b) violation. See Central Bank of Denver, N. A. v. First Interstate Bank of Denver, N. A., 511 U. S. 164, 191 (1994). The court also affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion to amend the complaint, as the revised pleading would not change the court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion on the merits. 443 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 993.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Decisions of the Courts of Appeals are in conflict respecting when, if ever, an injured investor may rely upon \xc2\xa710(b) to recover from a party that neither makes a public misstatement nor violates a duty to disclose but does participate in a scheme to violate \xc2\xa710(b). Compare Simpson v. AOL Time Warner Inc., 452 F.\xc2\xa03d 1040 (CA9 2006), with Regents of Univ. of Cal. v. Credit Suisse First Boston (USA), Inc., 482 F.\xc2\xa03d 372 (CA5 2007). We granted certiorari. 549 U. S. ___ (2007).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act makes it

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cunlawful for any person, directly or indirectly, by the use of any means or instrumentality of interstate commerce or of the mails, or of any facility of any national securities exchange \xe2\x80\xa6 [t]o use or employ, in connection with the purchase or sale of any security \xe2\x80\xa6 any manipulative or deceptive device or contrivance in contravention of such rules and regulations as the Commission may prescribe as necessary or appropriate in the public interest or for the protection of investors.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j.

\n

\nThe SEC, pursuant to this section, promulgated Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, which makes it unlawful

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(a)\xe2\x80\x82To employ any device, scheme, or artifice to defraud,

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(b)\xe2\x80\x82To make any untrue statement of a material fact or to omit to state a material fact necessary in order to make the statements made, in the light of the circumstances under which they were made, not misleading, or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(c)\xe2\x80\x82To engage in any act, practice, or course of business which operates or would operate as a fraud or deceit upon any person,

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale of any security.\xe2\x80\x9d 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.10b\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\nRule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 encompasses only conduct already prohibited by \xc2\xa710(b). United States v. O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, 521 U. S. 642, 651 (1997). Though the text of the Securities Exchange Act does not provide for a private cause of action for \xc2\xa710(b) violations, the Court has found a right of action implied in the words of the statute and its implementing regulation. Superintendent of Ins. of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. Bankers Life & Casualty Co., 404 U. S. 6, 13, n. 9 (1971). In a typical \xc2\xa710(b) private action a plaintiff must prove (1) a material misrepresentation or omission by the defendant; (2) scienter; (3) a connection between the misrepresentation or omission and the purchase or sale of a security; (4) reliance upon the misrepresentation or omission; (5) economic loss; and (6) loss causation. See Dura Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Broudo, 544 U. S. 336, 341\xe2\x80\x93342 (2005).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Central Bank, the Court determined that \xc2\xa710(b) liability did not extend to aiders and abettors. The Court found the scope of \xc2\xa710(b) to be delimited by the text, which makes no mention of aiding and abetting liability. 511 U. S., at 177. The Court doubted the implied \xc2\xa710(b) action should extend to aiders and abettors when none of the express causes of action in the securities Acts included that liability. Id., at 180. It added the following:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWere we to allow the aiding and abetting action proposed in this case, the defendant could be liable without any showing that the plaintiff relied upon the aider and abettor\xe2\x80\x99s statements or actions. See also Chiarella [v. United States, 445 U. S. 222, 228 (1980)]. Allowing plaintiffs to circumvent the reliance requirement would disregard the careful limits on 10b\xe2\x80\x935 recovery mandated by our earlier cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The decision in Central Bank led to calls for Congress to create an express cause of action for aiding and abetting within the Securities Exchange Act. Then-SEC Chairman Arthur Levitt, testifying before the Senate Securities Subcommittee, cited Central Bank and recommended that aiding and abetting liability in private claims be established. S.\xc2\xa0Hearing No. 103\xe2\x80\x93759, pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314 (1994). Congress did not follow this course. Instead, in \xc2\xa7104 of the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PSLRA), 109 Stat. 757, it directed prosecution of aiders and abettors by the SEC. 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778t(e).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The \xc2\xa710(b) implied private right of action does not extend to aiders and abettors. The conduct of a secondary actor must satisfy each of the elements or preconditions for liability; and we consider whether the allegations here are sufficient to do so.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals concluded petitioner had not alleged that respondents engaged in a deceptive act within the reach of the \xc2\xa710(b) private right of action, noting that only misstatements, omissions by one who has a duty to disclose, and manipulative trading practices (where \xe2\x80\x9cmanipulative\xe2\x80\x9d is a term of art, see, e.g., Santa Fe Industries, Inc. v. Green, 430 U. S. 462, 476\xe2\x80\x93477 (1977)) are deceptive within the meaning of the rule. 443 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 992. If this conclusion were read to suggest there must be a specific oral or written statement before there could be liability under \xc2\xa710(b) or Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, it would be erroneous. Conduct itself can be deceptive, as respondents concede. In this case, moreover, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 course of conduct included both oral and written statements, such as the backdated contracts agreed to by Charter and respondents.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A different interpretation of the holding from the Court of Appeals opinion is that the court was stating only that any deceptive statement or act respondents made was not actionable because it did not have the requisite proximate relation to the investors\xe2\x80\x99 harm. That conclusion is consistent with our own determination that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 acts or statements were not relied upon by the investors and that, as a result, liability cannot be imposed upon respondents.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reliance by the plaintiff upon the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s deceptive acts is an essential element of the \xc2\xa710(b) private cause of action. It ensures that, for liability to arise, the \xe2\x80\x9crequisite causal connection between a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentation and a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury\xe2\x80\x9d exists as a predicate for liability. Basic Inc. v. Levinson, 485 U. S. 224, 243 (1988); see also Affiliated Ute Citizens of Utah v. United States, 406 U. S. 128, 154 (1972) (requiring \xe2\x80\x9ccausation in fact\xe2\x80\x9d). We have found a rebuttable presumption of reliance in two different circumstances. First, if there is an omission of a material fact by one with a duty to disclose, the investor to whom the duty was owed need not provide specific proof of reliance. Id., at 153\xe2\x80\x93154. Second, under the fraud-on-the-market doctrine, reliance is presumed when the statements at issue become public. The public information is reflected in the market price of the security. Then it can be assumed that an investor who buys or sells stock at the market price relies upon the statement. Basic, supra, at 247.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither presumption applies here. Respondents had no duty to disclose; and their deceptive acts were not communicated to the public. No member of the investing public had knowledge, either actual or presumed, of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 deceptive acts during the relevant times. Petitioner, as a result, cannot show reliance upon any of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 actions except in an indirect chain that we find too remote for liability.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Invoking what some courts call \xe2\x80\x9cscheme liability,\xe2\x80\x9d see, e.g., In re Enron Corp. Securities, Derivative, & \xe2\x80\x9cERISA\xe2\x80\x9d Litigation, 439 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 692, 723 (SD Tex. 2006), petitioner nonetheless seeks to impose liability on respondents even absent a public statement. In our view this approach does not answer the objection that petitioner did not in fact rely upon respondents\xe2\x80\x99 own deceptive conduct.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Liability is appropriate, petitioner contends, because respondents engaged in conduct with the purpose and effect of creating a false appearance of material fact to further a scheme to misrepresent Charter\xe2\x80\x99s revenue. The argument is that the financial statement Charter released to the public was a natural and expected consequence of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 deceptive acts; had respondents not assisted Charter, Charter\xe2\x80\x99s auditor would not have been fooled, and the financial statement would have been a more accurate reflection of Charter\xe2\x80\x99s financial condition. That causal link is sufficient, petitioner argues, to apply Basic\xe2\x80\x99s presumption of reliance to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 acts. See, e.g., Simpson, 452 F. 3d, at 1051\xe2\x80\x931052; In re Parmalat Securities Litigation, 376 F. Supp. 2d 472, 509 (SDNY 2005).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In effect petitioner contends that in an efficient market investors rely not only upon the public statements relating to a security but also upon the transactions those statements reflect. Were this concept of reliance to be adopted, the implied cause of action would reach the whole marketplace in which the issuing company does business; and there is no authority for this rule.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As stated above, reliance is tied to causation, leading to the inquiry whether respondents\xe2\x80\x99 acts were immediate or remote to the injury. In considering petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s arguments, we note \xc2\xa710(b) provides that the deceptive act must be \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale of any security.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j(b). Though this phrase in part defines the statute\xe2\x80\x99s coverage rather than causation (and so we do not evaluate the \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d requirement of \xc2\xa710(b) in this case), the emphasis on a purchase or sale of securities does provide some insight into the deceptive acts that concerned the enacting Congress. See Black, Securities Commentary: The Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s Approach to the \xe2\x80\x98In Connection With\xe2\x80\x99 Requirement of Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, 53 Brooklyn L. Rev. 539, 541 (1987) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hile the \xe2\x80\x98in connection with\xe2\x80\x99 and causation requirements are analytically distinct, they are related to each other, and discussion of the first requirement may merge with discussion of the second\xe2\x80\x9d). In all events we conclude respondents\xe2\x80\x99 deceptive acts, which were not disclosed to the investing public, are too remote to satisfy the requirement of reliance. It was Charter, not respondents, that misled its auditor and filed fraudulent financial statements; nothing respondents did made it necessary or inevitable for Charter to record the transactions as it did.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The petitioner invokes the private cause of action under \xc2\xa710(b) and seeks to apply it beyond the securities markets\xe2\x80\x94the realm of financing business\xe2\x80\x94to purchase and supply contracts\xe2\x80\x94the realm of ordinary business operations. The latter realm is governed, for the most part, by state law. It is true that if business operations are used, as alleged here, to affect securities markets, the SEC enforcement power may reach the culpable actors. It is true as well that a dynamic, free economy presupposes a high degree of integrity in all of its parts, an integrity that must be underwritten by rules enforceable in fair, independent, accessible courts. Were the implied cause of action to be extended to the practices described here, however, there would be a risk that the federal power would be used to invite litigation beyond the immediate sphere of securities litigation and in areas already governed by functioning and effective state-law guarantees. Our precedents counsel against this extension. See Marine Bank v. Weaver, 455 U. S. 551, 556 (1982) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress, in enacting the securities laws, did not intend to provide a broad federal remedy for all fraud\xe2\x80\x9d); Santa Fe, 430 U. S., at 479\xe2\x80\x93480 (\xe2\x80\x9cThere may well be a need for uniform federal fiduciary standards \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. But those standards should not be supplied by judicial extension of \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 to \xe2\x80\x98cover the corporate universe\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Cary, Federalism and Corporate Law: Reflections Upon Delaware, 83 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 663, 700 (1974))). Though \xc2\xa710(b) is \xe2\x80\x9cnot \xe2\x80\x98limited to preserving the integrity of the securities markets,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bankers Life, 404 U. S., at 12, it does not reach all commercial transactions that are fraudulent and affect the price of a security in some attenuated way.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These considerations answer as well the argument that if this were a common-law action for fraud there could be a finding of reliance. Even if the assumption is correct, it is not controlling. Section 10(b) does not incorporate common-law fraud into federal law. See, e.g., SEC v. Zandford, 535 U. S. 813, 820 (2002) (\xe2\x80\x9c[Section 10(b)] must not be construed so broadly as to convert every common-law fraud that happens to involve securities into a violation\xe2\x80\x9d); Central Bank, 511 U. S., at 184 (\xe2\x80\x9cEven assuming \xe2\x80\xa6 a deeply rooted background of aiding and abetting tort liability, it does not follow that Congress intended to apply that kind of liability to the private causes of action in the securities Acts\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Dura, 544 U. S., at 341. Just as \xc2\xa710(b) \xe2\x80\x9cis surely badly strained when construed to provide a cause of action \xe2\x80\xa6 to the world at large,\xe2\x80\x9d Blue Chip Stamps v. Manor Drug Stores, 421 U. S. 723, 733, n. 5 (1975), it should not be interpreted to provide a private cause of action against the entire marketplace in which the issuing company operates.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s theory, moreover, would put an unsupportable interpretation on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 specific response to Central Bank in \xc2\xa7104 of the PSLRA. Congress amended the securities laws to provide for limited coverage of aiders and abettors. Aiding and abetting liability is authorized in actions brought by the SEC but not by private parties. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778t(e). Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s view of primary liability makes any aider and abettor liable under \xc2\xa710(b) if he or she committed a deceptive act in the process of providing assistance. Reply Brief for Petitioner 6, n.\xc2\xa02; Tr. of Oral Arg. 24. Were we to adopt this construction of \xc2\xa710(b), it would revive in substance the implied cause of action against all aiders and abettors except those who committed no deceptive act in the process of facilitating the fraud; and we would undermine Congress\xe2\x80\x99 determination that this class of defendants should be pursued by the SEC and not by private litigants. See Alexander v. Sandoval, 532 U. S. 275, 290 (2001) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe express provision of one method of enforcing a substantive rule suggests that Congress intended to preclude others\xe2\x80\x9d); FDA v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., 529 U. S. 120, 143 (2000) (\xe2\x80\x9cAt the time a statute is enacted, it may have a range of plausible meanings. Over time, however, subsequent acts can shape or focus those meanings\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Seatrain Shipbuilding Corp. v. Shell Oil Co., 444 U. S. 572, 596 (1980) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hile the views of subsequent Congresses cannot override the unmistakable intent of the enacting one, such views are entitled to significant weight, and particularly so when the precise intent of the enacting Congress is obscure\xe2\x80\x9d (citations omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is not a case in which Congress has enacted a regulatory statute and then has accepted, over a long period of time, broad judicial authority to define substantive standards of conduct and liability. Cf. Leegin Creative Leather Products, Inc. v. PSKS, Inc., 551 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 19\xe2\x80\x9320). And in accord with the nature of the cause of action at issue here, we give weight to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 amendment to the Act restoring aiding and abetting liability in certain cases but not others. The amendment, in our view, supports the conclusion that there is no liability.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The practical consequences of an expansion, which the Court has considered appropriate to examine in circumstances like these, see Virginia Bankshares, Inc. v. Sandberg, 501 U. S. 1083, 1104\xe2\x80\x931105 (1991); Blue Chip, 421 U. S., at 737, provide a further reason to reject petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s approach. In Blue Chip, the Court noted that extensive discovery and the potential for uncertainty and disruption in a lawsuit allow plaintiffs with weak claims to extort settlements from innocent companies. Id., at 740\xe2\x80\x93741. Adoption of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s approach would expose a new class of defendants to these risks. As noted in Central Bank, contracting parties might find it necessary to protect against these threats, raising the costs of doing business. See 511 U. S., at 189. Overseas firms with no other exposure to our securities laws could be deterred from doing business here. See Brief for Organization for International Investment et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 17\xe2\x80\x9320. This, in turn, may raise the cost of being a publicly traded company under our law and shift securities offerings away from domestic capital markets. Brief for NASDAQ Stock Market, Inc., et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 12\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The history of the \xc2\xa710(b) private right and the careful approach the Court has taken before proceeding without congressional direction provide further reasons to find no liability here. The \xc2\xa710(b) private cause of action is a judicial construct that Congress did not enact in the text of the relevant statutes. See Lampf, Pleva, Lipkind, Prupis & Petigrow v. Gilbertson, 501 U. S. 350, 358\xe2\x80\x93359 (1991); Blue Chip, supra, at 729. Though the rule once may have been otherwise, see J. I. Case Co. v. Borak, 377 U. S. 426, 432\xe2\x80\x93433 (1964), it is settled that there is an implied cause of action only if the underlying statute can be interpreted to disclose the intent to create one, see, e.g., Alexander, supra, at 286\xe2\x80\x93287; Virginia Bankshares, supra, at 1102; Touche Ross & Co. v. Redington, 442 U. S. 560, 575 (1979). This is for good reason. In the absence of congressional intent the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s recognition of an implied private right of action

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily extends its authority to embrace a dispute Congress has not assigned it to resolve. This runs contrary to the established principle that \xe2\x80\x98[t]he jurisdiction of the federal courts is carefully guarded against expansion by judicial interpretation\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0,\xe2\x80\x99 American Fire & Casualty Co. v. Finn, 341 U. S. 6, 17 (1951), and conflicts with the authority of Congress under Art.\xc2\xa0III to set the limits of federal jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Cannon v. University of Chicago, 441 U. S. 677, 746 (1979) (Powell, J., dissenting) (citations and footnote omitted).

\n

The determination of who can seek a remedy has significant consequences for the reach of federal power. See Wilder v. Virginia Hospital Assn., 496 U. S. 498, 509, n. 9 (1990) (requirement of congressional intent \xe2\x80\x9creflects a concern, grounded in separation of powers, that Congress rather than the courts controls the availability of remedies for violations of statutes\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Concerns with the judicial creation of a private cause of action caution against its expansion. The decision to extend the cause of action is for Congress, not for us. Though it remains the law, the \xc2\xa710(b) private right should not be extended beyond its present boundaries. See Virginia Bankshares, supra, at 1102 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he breadth of the [private right of action] once recognized should not, as a general matter, grow beyond the scope congressionally intended\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Central Bank, supra, at 173 (determining that the scope of conduct prohibited is limited by the text of \xc2\xa710(b)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This restraint is appropriate in light of the PSLRA, which imposed heightened pleading requirements and a loss causation requirement upon \xe2\x80\x9cany private action\xe2\x80\x9d arising from the Securities Exchange Act. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934(b). It is clear these requirements touch upon the implied right of action, which is now a prominent feature of federal securities regulation. See Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith Inc. v. Dabit, 547 U. S. 71, 81\xe2\x80\x9382 (2006); Dura, 544 U. S., at 345\xe2\x80\x93346; see also S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x9398, p.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935 (1995) (recognizing the \xc2\xa710(b) implied cause of action, and indicating the PSLRA was intended to have \xe2\x80\x9cCongress \xe2\x80\xa6 reassert its authority in this area\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 26 (indicating the pleading standards covered \xc2\xa710(b) actions). Congress thus ratified the implied right of action after the Court moved away from a broad willingness to imply private rights of action. See Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. v. Curran, 456 U. S. 353, 381\xe2\x80\x93382, and n. 66 (1982); cf. Borak, supra, at 433. It is appropriate for us to assume that when \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934 was enacted, Congress accepted the \xc2\xa710(b) private cause of action as then defined but chose to extend it no further.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Secondary actors are subject to criminal penalties, see, e.g., 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778ff, and civil enforcement by the SEC, see, e.g., \xc2\xa778t(e). The enforcement power is not toothless. Since September 30, 2002, SEC enforcement actions have collected over $10 billion in disgorgement and penalties, much of it for distribution to injured investors. See SEC, 2007 Performance and Accountability Report, p.\xc2\xa026, http://www.sec.gov/about/secpar2007.shtml (as visited Jan. 2, 2008, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). And in this case both parties agree that criminal penalties are a strong deterrent. See Brief for Respondents 48; Reply Brief for Petitioner 17. In addition some state securities laws permit state authorities to seek fines and restitution from aiders and abettors. See, e.g., Del. Code Ann., Tit. 6, \xc2\xa77325 (2005). All secondary actors, furthermore, are not necessarily immune from private suit. The securities statutes provide an express private right of action against accountants and underwriters in certain circumstances, see 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa777k, and the implied right of action in \xc2\xa710(b) continues to cover secondary actors who commit primary violations. Central Bank, supra, at 191.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here respondents were acting in concert with Charter in the ordinary course as suppliers and, as matters then evolved in the not so ordinary course, as customers. Unconventional as the arrangement was, it took place in the marketplace for goods and services, not in the investment sphere. Charter was free to do as it chose in preparing its books, conferring with its auditor, and preparing and then issuing its financial statements. In these circumstances the investors cannot be said to have relied upon any of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 deceptive acts in the decision to purchase or sell securities; and as the requisite reliance cannot be shown, respondents have no liability to petitioner under the implied right of action. This conclusion is consistent with the narrow dimensions we must give to a right of action Congress did not authorize when it first enacted the statute and did not expand when it revisited the law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

STONERIDGE INVESTMENT PARTNERS, LLC,\nPETITIONER v. SCIENTIFIC-ATLANTA,\nINC., et\xc2\xa0al.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Souter and Justice Ginsburg join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Charter Communications, Inc., inflated its revenues by $17 million in order to cover up a $15 to $20 million expected cash flow shortfall. It could not have done so absent the knowingly fraudulent actions of Scientific-Atlanta, Inc., and Motorola, Inc. Investors relied on Charter\xe2\x80\x99s revenue statements in deciding whether to invest in Charter and in doing so relied on respondents\xe2\x80\x99 fraud, which was itself a \xe2\x80\x9cdeceptive device\xe2\x80\x9d prohibited by \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934. 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j(b). This is enough to satisfy the requirements of \xc2\xa710(b) and enough to distinguish this case from Central Bank of Denver, N. A. v. First Interstate Bank of Denver, N. A., 511 U. S. 164 (1994).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court seems to assume that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 alleged conduct could subject them to liability in an enforcement proceeding initiated by the Government, ante, at 15, but nevertheless concludes that they are not subject to liability in a private action brought by injured investors because they are, at most, guilty of aiding and abetting a violation of \xc2\xa710(b), rather than an actual violation of the statute. While that conclusion results in an affirmance of the judgment of the Court of Appeals, it rests on a rejection of that court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning. Furthermore, while the Court frequently refers to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to \xe2\x80\x9cexpand\xe2\x80\x9d the implied cause of action,[Footnote 1]\xe2\x80\x94a conclusion that begs the question of the contours of that cause of action\xe2\x80\x94it is today\xe2\x80\x99s decision that results in a significant departure from Central Bank.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that no violation of \xc2\xa710(b) giving rise to a private right of action has been alleged in this case rests on two faulty premises: (1) the Court\xe2\x80\x99s overly broad reading of Central Bank, and (2) the view that reliance requires a kind of super-causation\xe2\x80\x94a view contrary to both the Securities and Exchange Commission\xe2\x80\x99s (SEC) position in a recent Ninth Circuit case[Footnote 2] and our holding in Basic Inc. v. Levinson, 485 U. S. 224 (1988). These two points merit separate discussion.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals incorrectly based its decision on the view that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] device or contrivance is not \xe2\x80\x98deceptive,\xe2\x80\x99 within the meaning of \xc2\xa710(b), absent some misstatement or a failure to disclose by one who has a duty to disclose.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Charter Communications, Inc., Securities Litigation, 443 F.\xc2\xa03d 987, 992 (CA8 2006). The Court correctly explains why the statute covers nonverbal as well as verbal deceptive conduct. Ante, at 7. The allegations in this case\xe2\x80\x94that respondents produced documents falsely claiming costs had risen and signed contracts they knew to be backdated in order to disguise the connection between the increase in costs and the purchase of advertising\xe2\x80\x94plainly describe \xe2\x80\x9cdeceptive devices\xe2\x80\x9d under any standard reading of the phrase.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What the Court fails to recognize is that this case is critically different from Central Bank because the bank in that case did not engage in any deceptive act and, therefore, did not itself violate \xc2\xa710(b). The Court sweeps aside any distinction, remarking that holding respondents liable would \xe2\x80\x9creviv[e] the implied cause of action against all aiders and abettors except those who committed no deceptive act in the process of facilitating the fraud.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. But the fact that Central Bank engaged in no deceptive conduct whatsoever\xe2\x80\x94in other words, that it was at most an aider and abettor\xe2\x80\x94sharply distinguishes Central Bank from cases that do involve allegations of such conduct. 511 U. S., at 167 (stating that the question presented was \xe2\x80\x9cwhether private civil liability under \xc2\xa710(b) extends as well to those who do not engage in the manipulative or deceptive practice, but who aid and abet the violation\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Central Bank of Denver was the indenture trustee for bonds issued by a public authority and secured by liens on property in Colorado Springs. After default, purchasers of $2.1 million of those bonds sued the underwriters, alleging violations of \xc2\xa710(b); they also named Central Bank as a defendant, contending that the bank\xe2\x80\x99s delay in reviewing a suspicious appraisal of the value of the security made it liable as an aider and abettor. Id., at 167\xe2\x80\x93168. The facts of this case would parallel those of Central Bank if respondents had, for example, merely delayed sending invoices for set-top boxes to Charter. Conversely, the facts in Central Bank would mirror those in the case before us today if the bank had knowingly purchased real estate in wash transactions at above-market prices in order to facilitate the appraiser\xe2\x80\x99s overvaluation of the security. Central Bank, thus, poses no obstacle to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument that it has alleged a cause of action under \xc2\xa710(b).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s next faulty premise is that petitioner is required to allege that Scientific-Atlanta and Motorola made it \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary or inevitable for Charter to record the transactions in the way it did,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10, in order to demonstrate reliance. Because the Court of Appeals did not base its holding on reliance grounds, see 443 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 992, the fairest course to petitioner would be for the majority to remand to the Court of Appeals to determine whether petitioner properly alleged reliance, under a correct view of what \xc2\xa710(b) covers.[Footnote 3] Because the Court chooses to rest its holding on an absence of reliance, a response is required.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Basic Inc., 485 U. S., at 243, we stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[r]eliance provides the requisite causal connection between a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentation and a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court\xe2\x80\x99s view of the causation required to demonstrate reliance is unwarranted and without precedent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Basic Inc., we held that the \xe2\x80\x9cfraud-on-the-market\xe2\x80\x9d theory provides adequate support for a presumption in private securities actions that shareholders (or former shareholders) in publicly traded companies rely on public material misstatements that affect the price of the company\xe2\x80\x99s stock. Id., at 248. The holding in Basic is surely a sufficient response to the argument that a complaint alleging that deceptive acts which had a material effect on the price of a listed stock should be dismissed because the plaintiffs were not subjectively aware of the deception at the time of the securities\xe2\x80\x99 purchase or sale. This Court has not held that investors must be aware of the specific deceptive act which violates \xc2\xa710b to demonstrate reliance.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is right that a fraud-on-the-market presumption coupled with its view on causation would not support petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s view of reliance. The fraud-on-the-market presumption helps investors who cannot demonstrate that they, themselves, relied on fraud that reached the market. But that presumption says nothing about causation from the other side: what an individual or corporation must do in order to have \xe2\x80\x9ccaused\xe2\x80\x9d the misleading information that reached the market. The Court thus has it backwards when it first addresses the fraud-on-the-market presumption, rather than the causation required. See, ante, at 8. The argument is not that the fraud-on-the-market presumption is enough standing alone, but that a correct view of causation coupled with the presumption would allow petitioner to plead reliance.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lower courts have correctly stated that the causation necessary to demonstrate reliance is not a difficult hurdle to clear in a private right of action under \xc2\xa710(b). Reliance is often equated with \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98transaction causation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Dura Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Broudo, 544 U. S. 336, 341, 342 (2005). Transaction causation, in turn, is often defined as requiring an allegation that but for the deceptive act, the plaintiff would not have entered into the securities transaction. See, e.g., Lentell v. Merrill Lynch & Co., 396 F.\xc2\xa03d 161, 172 (CA2 2005); Binder v. Gillespie, 184 F.\xc2\xa03d 1059, 1065\xe2\x80\x931066 (CA9 1999).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if but-for causation, standing alone, is too weak to establish reliance, petitioner has also alleged that respondents proximately caused Charter\xe2\x80\x99s misstatement of income; petitioner has alleged that respondents knew their deceptive acts would be the basis for statements that would influence the market price of Charter stock on which shareholders would rely. Second Amended Consolidated Class Action Complaint \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb6 8, 98, 100, 109, App. 19a, 55a\xe2\x80\x9356a, 59a. Thus, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 acts had the foreseeable effect of causing petitioner to engage in the relevant securities transactions. The Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7533, pp.\xc2\xa072\xe2\x80\x9373 (1977), provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he maker of a fraudulent misrepresentation is subject to liability \xe2\x80\xa6 if the misrepresentation, although not made directly to the other, is made to a third person and the maker intends or has reason to expect that its terms will be repeated or its substance communicated to the other.\xe2\x80\x9d The sham transactions described in the complaint in this case had the same effect on Charter\xe2\x80\x99s profit and loss statement as a false entry directly on its books that included $17 million of gross revenues that had not been received. And respondents are alleged to have known that the outcome of their fraudulent transactions would be communicated to investors.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s view of reliance is unduly stringent and unmoored from authority. The Court first says that if the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s concept of reliance is adopted the implied cause of action \xe2\x80\x9cwould reach the whole marketplace in which the issuing company does business.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. The answer to that objection is, of course, that liability only attaches when the company doing business with the issuing company has itself violated \xc2\xa710(b).[Footnote 4] The Court next relies on what it views as a strict division between the \xe2\x80\x9crealm of financing business\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cordinary business operations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. But petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s position does not merge the two: A corporation engaging in a business transaction with a partner who transmits false information to the market is only liable where the corporation itself violates \xc2\xa710(b). Such a rule does not invade the province of \xe2\x80\x9cordinary\xe2\x80\x9d business transactions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority states that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ection 10(b) does not incorporate common-law fraud into federal law,\xe2\x80\x9d citing SEC v. Zandford, 535 U. S. 813 (2002). Ante, at 11. Of course, not every common-law fraud action that happens to touch upon securities is an action under \xc2\xa710(b), but the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in Zandford did not purport to jettison all reference to common-law fraud doctrines from \xc2\xa710(b) cases. In fact, our prior cases explained that to the extent that \xe2\x80\x9cthe antifraud provisions of the securities laws are not coextensive with common-law doctrines of fraud,\xe2\x80\x9d it is because common-law fraud doctrines might be too restrictive. Herman & MacLean v. Huddleston, 459 U. S. 375, 388\xe2\x80\x93389 (1983). \xe2\x80\x9cIndeed, an important purpose of the federal securities statutes was to rectify perceived deficiencies in the available common-law protections by establishing higher standards of conduct in the securities industry.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 389. I, thus, see no reason to abandon common-law approaches to causation in \xc2\xa710(b) cases.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the Court relies on the course of action Congress adopted after our decision in Central Bank to argue that siding with petitioner on reliance would run contrary to congressional intent. Senate hearings on Central Bank were held within one month of our decision.[Footnote 5] Less than one year later, Senators Dodd and Domenici introduced S.\xc2\xa0240, which became the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PSLRA), 109 Stat. 737.[Footnote 6] Congress stopped short of undoing Central Bank entirely, instead adopting a compromise which restored the authority of the SEC to enforce aiding and abetting liability.[Footnote 7] A private right of action based on aiding and abetting violations of \xc2\xa710(b) was not, however, included in the PSLRA,[Footnote 8] despite support from Senator Dodd and members of the Senate Subcommittee on Securities.[Footnote 9] This compromise surely provides no support for extending Central Bank in order to immunize an undefined class of actual violators of \xc2\xa710(b) from liability in private litigation. Indeed, as Members of Congress\xe2\x80\x94including those who rejected restoring a private cause of action against aiders and abettors\xe2\x80\x94made clear, private litigation under \xc2\xa710(b) continues to play a vital role in protecting the integrity of our securities markets.[Footnote 10] That Congress chose not to restore the aiding and abetting liability removed by Central Bank does not mean that Congress wanted to exempt from liability the broader range of conduct that today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion excludes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is concerned that such liability would deter overseas firms from doing business in the United States or \xe2\x80\x9cshift securities offerings away from domestic capital markets.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. But liability for those who violate \xc2\xa710(b) \xe2\x80\x9cwill not harm American competitiveness; in fact, investor faith in the safety and integrity of our markets is their strength. The fact that our markets are the safest in the world has helped make them the strongest in the world.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Former SEC Commissioners as Amici Curiae 9.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, while I recognize that the Central Bank opinion provides a precedent for judicial policymaking decisions in this area of the law, I respectfully dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s continuing campaign to render the private cause of action under \xc2\xa710(b) toothless. I would reverse the decision of the Court of Appeals.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While I would reverse for the reasons stated above, I must also comment on the importance of the private cause of action that Congress implicitly authorized when it enacted the Securities Exchange Act of 1934. A theme that underlies the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis is its mistaken hostility towards the \xc2\xa710(b) private cause of action.[Footnote 11] Ante, at 13. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s current view of implied causes of action is that they are merely a \xe2\x80\x9crelic\xe2\x80\x9d of our prior \xe2\x80\x9cheady days.\xe2\x80\x9d Correctional Services Corp. v. Malesko, 534 U. S. 61, 75 (2001) (Scalia, J., concurring). Those \xe2\x80\x9cheady days\xe2\x80\x9d persisted for two hundred years.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0During the first two centuries of this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s history much of our law was developed by judges in the common-law tradition. A basic principle animating our jurisprudence was enshrined in state constitution provisions guaranteeing, in substance, that \xe2\x80\x9cevery wrong shall have a remedy.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 12] Fashioning appropriate remedies for the violation of rules of law designed to protect a class of citizens was the routine business of judges. See Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 166 (1803). While it is true that in the early days state law was the source of most of those rules, throughout our history\xe2\x80\x94until 1975\xe2\x80\x94the same practice prevailed in federal courts with regard to federal statutes that left questions of remedy open for judges to answer. In Texas & Pacific R. Co. v. Rigsby, 241 U. S. 33, 39 (1916), this Court stated the following:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA disregard of the command of the statute is a wrongful act, and where it results in damage to one of the class for whose especial benefit the statute was enacted, the right to recover the damages from the party in default is implied, according to a doctrine of the common law expressed in 1 Com. Dig., tit. Action upon Statute (F), in these words: \xe2\x80\x98So, in every case, where a statute enacts, or prohibits a thing for the benefit of a person, he shall have a remedy upon the same statute for the thing enacted for his advantage, or for the recompense of a wrong done to him contrary to the said law.\xe2\x80\x99 (Per Holt, C. J., Anon., 6 Mod. 26, 27.)\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Friendly succinctly described the post-Rigsby, pre-1975 practice in his opinion in Leist v. Simplot, 638 F.\xc2\xa02d 283, 298\xe2\x80\x93299 (CA2 1980):

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cFollowing Rigsby the Supreme Court recognized implied causes of action on numerous occasions, see, e.g., Wyandotte Transportation Co. v. United States, 389 U.S. 191 \xe2\x80\xa6 (1967) (sustaining implied cause of action by United States for damages under Rivers and Harbors Act for removing negligently sunk vessel despite express remedies of in rem action and criminal penalties); United States v. Republic Steel Corp., 362 U.S. 482 \xe2\x80\xa6 (1960) (sustaining implied cause of action by United States for an injunction under the Rivers and Harbors Act); Tunstall v. Locomotive Firemen & Enginemen, 323 U.S. 210 \xe2\x80\xa6 (1944) (sustaining implied cause of action by union member against union for discrimination among members despite existence of Board of Mediation); Sullivan v. Little Hunting Park, Inc., 396 U.S. 229 \xe2\x80\xa6 (1969) (sustaining implied private cause of action under 42 U.S.C. \xc2\xa71982); Allen v. State Board of Elections, 393 U.S. 544 \xe2\x80\xa6 (1969) (sustaining implied private cause of action under \xc2\xa75 of the Voting Rights Act despite the existence of a complex regulatory scheme and explicit rights of action in the Attorney General); and, of course, the aforementioned decisions under the securities laws. As the Supreme Court itself has recognized, the period of the 1960\xe2\x80\x99s and early 1970\xe2\x80\x99s was one in which the \xe2\x80\x98Court had consistently found implied remedies.\xe2\x80\x99 Cannon v. University of Chicago, 441 U.S. 677, 698 \xe2\x80\xa6 (1979).\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a law-changing opinion written by Justice Brennan in 1975, the Court decided to modify its approach to private causes of action. Cort v. Ash, 422 U. S. 66 (constraining courts to use a strict four-factor test to determine whether Congress intended a private cause of action). A few years later, in Cannon v. University of Chicago, 441 U. S. 677 (1979), we adhered to the strict approach mandated by Cort v. Ash in 1975, but made it clear that \xe2\x80\x9cour evaluation of congressional action in 1972 must take into account its contemporary legal context.\xe2\x80\x9d 441 U. S., at 698\xe2\x80\x93699. That context persuaded the majority that Congress had intended the courts to authorize a private remedy for members of the protected class.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Until Central Bank, the federal courts continued to enforce a broad implied cause of action for the violation of statutes enacted in 1933 and 1934 for the protection of investors. As Judge Friendly explained:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cDuring the late 1940\xe2\x80\x99s, the 1950\xe2\x80\x99s, the 1960\xe2\x80\x99s and the early 1970\xe2\x80\x99s there was widespread, indeed almost general, recognition of implied causes of action for damages under many provisions of the Securities Exchange Act, including not only the antifraud provisions, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7\xc2\xa010 and 15(c)(1), see Kardon v. National Gypsum Co., 69 F.Supp. 512, 513\xe2\x80\x9314 (E.D.Pa.1946); Fischman v. Raytheon Mfg. Co., 188 F.2d 783, 787 (2 Cir. 1951) (Frank, J.); Fratt v. Robinson, 203 F.2d 627, 631\xe2\x80\x9333 (9 Cir. 1953), but many others. These included the provision, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa06(a)(1), requiring securities exchanges to enforce compliance with the Act and any rule or regulation made thereunder, see Baird v. Franklin, 141 F.2d 238, 239, 240, 244\xe2\x80\x9345 (2 Cir.), cert. denied, 323 U.S. 737 \xe2\x80\xa6 (1944), and provisions governing the solicitation of proxies, see J. I. Case Co. v. Borak, 377 U.S. 426, 431\xe2\x80\x9335 \xe2\x80\xa6 (1964)\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Writing in 1961, Professor Loss remarked with respect to violations of the antifraud provisions that with one exception \xe2\x80\x98not a single judge has expressed himself to the contrary.\xe2\x80\x99 3 Securities Regulation 1763\xe2\x80\x9364. See also Bromberg & Lowenfels, supra, \xc2\xa72.2 (462) (describing 1946\xe2\x80\x931974 as the \xe2\x80\x98expansion era\xe2\x80\x99 in implied causes of action under the securities laws). When damage actions for violation of \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 reached the Supreme Court, the existence of an implied cause of action was not deemed worthy of extended discussion. Superintendent of Insurance v. Bankers Life & Casualty Co., 404 U.S. 6 \xe2\x80\xa6 (1971).\xe2\x80\x9d Leist, 638 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 296\xe2\x80\x93297 (footnote omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of the history of court-created remedies and specifically the history of implied causes of action under \xc2\xa710(b), the Court is simply wrong when it states that Congress did not impliedly authorize this private cause of action \xe2\x80\x9cwhen it first enacted the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. Courts near in time to the enactment of the securities laws recognized that the principle in Rigsby applied to the securities laws.[Footnote 13] Congress enacted \xc2\xa710(b) with the understanding that federal courts respected the principle that every wrong would have a remedy. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision simply cuts back further on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intended remedy. I respectfully dissent.
