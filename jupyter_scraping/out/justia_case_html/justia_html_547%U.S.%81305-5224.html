DAVIS v. WASHINGTON

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of washington

\n

No. 05\xe2\x80\x935224.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 20, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 19, 2006

\n

In No. 05\xe2\x80\x935224, a 911 operator ascertained from Michelle McCottry that she had been assaulted by her former boyfriend, petitioner Davis, who had just fled the scene. McCottry did not testify at Davis\xe2\x80\x99s trial for felony violation of a domestic no-contact order, but the court admitted the 911 recording despite Davis\xe2\x80\x99s objection, which he based on the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Confrontation Clause. He was convicted. The Washington Court of Appeals affirmed, as did the State Supreme Court, which concluded that, inter alia, the portion of the 911 conversation in which McCottry identified Davis as her assailant was not testimonial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In No. 05\xe2\x80\x935705, when police responded to a reported domestic disturbance at the home of Amy and Hershel Hammon, Amy told them that nothing was wrong, but gave them permission to enter. Once inside, one officer kept petitioner Hershel in the kitchen while the other interviewed Amy elsewhere and had her complete and sign a battery affidavit. Amy did not appear at Hershel\xe2\x80\x99s bench trial for, inter alia, domestic battery, but her affidavit and testimony from the officer who questioned her were admitted over Hershel\xe2\x80\x99s objection that he had no opportunity to cross-examine her. Hershel was convicted, and the Indiana Court of Appeals affirmed in relevant part. The State Supreme Court also affirmed, concluding that, although Amy\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit was testimonial and wrongly admitted, it was harmless beyond a reasonable doubt.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The Confrontation Clause bars \xe2\x80\x9cadmission of testimonial statements of a witness who did not appear at trial unless he was unavailable to testify, and the defendant had a prior opportunity for cross-examination.\xe2\x80\x9d Crawford v. Washington, 541 U. S. 36, 53\xe2\x80\x9354. These cases require the Court to determine which police \xe2\x80\x9cinterrogations\xe2\x80\x9d produce statements that fall within this prohibition. Without attempting to produce an exhaustive classification of all conceivable statements as either testimonial or nontestimonial, it suffices to decide the present cases to hold that statements are nontestimonial when made in the course of police interrogation under circumstances objectively indicating that the primary purpose of interrogation is to enable police assistance to meet an ongoing emergency. They are testimonial when the circumstances objectively indicate that there is no such ongoing emergency, and that the primary purpose of the interrogation is to establish or prove past events potentially relevant to later criminal prosecution. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0McCottry\xe2\x80\x99s statements identifying Davis as her assailant were not testimonial. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0This case requires the Court to decide whether the Confrontation Clause applies only to testimonial hearsay, and, if so, whether the 911 recording qualifies. Crawford suggested the answer to the first question, noting that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Confrontation Clause \xe2\x80\xa6 applies to \xe2\x80\x98witnesses\xe2\x80\x99 against the accused\xe2\x80\x94in other words, those who \xe2\x80\x98bear testimony.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Only \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial statements\xe2\x80\x9d cause a declarant to be a witness. The Court is unaware of any early American case invoking the Confrontation Clause or the common-law right to confrontation that did not involve testimony as thus defined. Well into the 20th century, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisprudence was carefully applied only in the testimonial context, and its later cases never in practice dispensed with the Confrontation Clause requirements of unavailability and prior cross-examination in cases involving testimonial hearsay. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The question in Davis, therefore, is whether, objectively considered, the interrogation during the 911 call produced testimonial statements. In contrast to Crawford, where the interrogation took place at a police station and was directed solely at establishing a past crime, a 911 call is ordinarily designed primarily to describe current circumstances requiring police assistance. The difference is apparent here. McCottry was speaking of events as they were actually happening, while Crawford\xe2\x80\x99s interrogation took place hours after the events occurred. Moreover, McCottry was facing an ongoing emergency. Further, the statements elicited were necessary to enable the police to resolve the present emergency rather than simply to learn what had happened in the past. Finally, the difference in the level of formality is striking. Crawford calmly answered questions at a station house, with an officer-interrogator taping and taking notes, while McCottry\xe2\x80\x99s frantic answers were provided over the phone, in an environment that was not tranquil, or even safe. Thus, the circumstances of her interrogation objectively indicate that its primary purpose was to enable police assistance to meet an ongoing emergency. She was not acting as a witness or testifying. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0Amy Hammon\xe2\x80\x99s statements were testimonial. They were not much different from those in Crawford. It is clear from the circumstances that Amy\xe2\x80\x99s interrogation was part of an investigation into possibly criminal past conduct. There was no emergency in progress, she told the police when they arrived that things were fine, and the officer questioning her was seeking to determine not what was happening but what had happened. Objectively viewed, the primary, if not sole, purpose of the investigation was to investigate a possible crime. While the formal features of Crawford\xe2\x80\x99s interrogation strengthened her statements\xe2\x80\x99 testimonial aspect, such features were not essential to the point. In both cases, the declarants were separated from the defendants, the statements recounted how potentially criminal past events began and progressed, and the interrogation took place some time after the events were over. For the same reasons the comparison to Crawford is compelling, the comparison to Davis is unpersuasive. The statements in Davis were taken when McCottry was alone, unprotected by police, and apparently in immediate danger from Davis. She was seeking aid, not telling a story about the past. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa04.\xc2\xa0The Indiana courts may determine on remand whether a claim of forfeiture by wrongdoing\xe2\x80\x94under which one who obtains a witness\xe2\x80\x99s absence by wrongdoing forfeits the constitutional right to confrontation\xe2\x80\x94is properly raised in Hammon, and, if so, whether it is meritorious. Absent such a finding, the Sixth Amendment operates to exclude Amy Hammon\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Confrontation Clause of the Sixth Amendment provides: \xe2\x80\x9cIn all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right \xe2\x80\xa6 to be confronted with the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d In Crawford v. Washington, 541 U. S. 36, 53\xe2\x80\x9354 (2004), we held that this provision bars \xe2\x80\x9cadmission of testimonial statements of a witness who did not appear at trial unless he was unavailable to testify, and the defendant had had a prior opportunity for cross-examination.\xe2\x80\x9d A critical portion of this holding, and the portion central to resolution of the two cases now before us, is the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial statements.\xe2\x80\x9d Only statements of this sort cause the declarant to be a \xe2\x80\x9cwitness\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the Confrontation Clause. See id., at 51. It is the testimonial character of the statement that separates it from other hearsay that, while subject to traditional limitations upon hearsay evidence, is not subject to the Confrontation Clause.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our opinion in Crawford set forth \xe2\x80\x9c[v]arious formulations\xe2\x80\x9d of the core class of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98testimonial\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d statements, ibid., but found it unnecessary to endorse any of them, because \xe2\x80\x9csome statements qualify under any definition,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 52. Among those, we said, were \xe2\x80\x9c[s]tatements taken by police officers in the course of interrogations,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.; see also id., at 53. The questioning that generated the deponent\xe2\x80\x99s statement in Crawford\xe2\x80\x94which was made and recorded while she was in police custody, after having been given Miranda warnings as a possible suspect herself\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cqualifies under any conceivable definition\xe2\x80\x9d of an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98interrogation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 541 U. S., at 53, n.\xc2\xa04. We therefore did not define that term, except to say that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e use [it] . . . in its colloquial, rather than any technical legal, sense,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cone can imagine various definitions . . . , and we need not select among them in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The character of the statements in the present cases is not as clear, and these cases require us to determine more precisely which police interrogations produce testimony.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Without attempting to produce an exhaustive classification of all conceivable statements\xe2\x80\x94or even all conceivable statements in response to police interrogation\xe2\x80\x94as either testimonial or nontestimonial, it suffices to decide the present cases to hold as follows: Statements are nontestimonial when made in the course of police interrogation under circumstances objectively indicating that the primary purpose of the interrogation is to enable police assistance to meet an ongoing emergency. They are testimonial when the circumstances objectively indicate that there is no such ongoing emergency, and that the primary purpose of the interrogation is to establish or prove past events potentially relevant to later criminal prosecution.[Footnote 1]

III

\n

\n\nA

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Crawford, it sufficed for resolution of the case before us to determine that \xe2\x80\x9ceven if the Sixth Amendment is not solely concerned with testimonial hearsay, that is its primary object, and interrogations by law enforcement officers fall squarely within that class.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53. Moreover, as we have just described, the facts of that case spared us the need to define what we meant by \xe2\x80\x9cinterrogations.\xe2\x80\x9d The Davis case today does not permit us this luxury of indecision. The inquiries of a police operator in the course of a 911 call[Footnote 2] are an interrogation in one sense, but not in a sense that \xe2\x80\x9cqualifies under any conceivable definition.\xe2\x80\x9d We must decide, therefore, whether the Confrontation Clause applies only to testimonial hearsay; and, if so, whether the recording of a 911 call qualifies.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The answer to the first question was suggested in Crawford, even if not explicitly held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe text of the Confrontation Clause reflects this focus [on testimonial hearsay]. It applies to \xe2\x80\x98witnesses\xe2\x80\x99 against the accused\xe2\x80\x94in other words, those who \xe2\x80\x98bear testimony.\xe2\x80\x99 1 N. Webster, An American Dictionary of the English Language (1828). \xe2\x80\x98Testimony,\xe2\x80\x99 in turn, is typically \xe2\x80\x98a solemn declaration or affirmation made for the purpose of establishing or proving some fact.\xe2\x80\x99 Ibid. An accuser who makes a formal statement to government officers bears testimony in a sense that a person who makes a casual remark to an acquaintance does not.\xe2\x80\x9d 541 U. S., at 51.

\n

\nA limitation so clearly reflected in the text of the constitutional provision must fairly be said to mark out not merely its \xe2\x80\x9ccore,\xe2\x80\x9d but its perimeter.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are not aware of any early American case invoking the Confrontation Clause or the common-law right to confrontation that did not clearly involve testimony as thus defined.[Footnote 3] Well into the 20th century, our own Confrontation Clause jurisprudence was carefully applied only in the testimonial context. See, e.g., Reynolds v. United States, 98 U. S. 145, 158 (1879) (testimony at prior trial was subject to the Confrontation Clause, but petitioner had forfeited that right by procuring witness\xe2\x80\x99s absence); Mattox v. United States, 156 U. S. 237, 240\xe2\x80\x93244 (1895) (prior trial testimony of deceased witnesses admitted because subject to cross-examination); Kirby v. United States, 174 U. S. 47, 55\xe2\x80\x9356 (1899) (guilty pleas and jury conviction of others could not be admitted to show that property defendant received from them was stolen); Motes v. United States, 178 U. S. 458, 467, 470\xe2\x80\x93471 (1900) (written deposition subject to cross-examination was not admissible because witness was available); Dowdell v. United States, 221 U. S. 325, 330\xe2\x80\x93331 (1911) (facts regarding conduct of prior trial certified to by the judge, the clerk of court, and the official reporter did not relate to defendants\xe2\x80\x99 guilt or innocence and hence were not statements of \xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses\xe2\x80\x9d under the Confrontation Clause).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even our later cases, conforming to the reasoning of Ohio v. Roberts, 448 U. S. 56 (1980),[Footnote 4] never in practice dispensed with the Confrontation Clause requirements of unavailability and prior cross-examination in cases that involved testimonial hearsay, see Crawford, 541 U. S., at 57\xe2\x80\x9359 (citing cases), with one arguable exception, see id., at 58, n.\xc2\xa08 (discussing White v. Illinois, 502 U. S. 346 (1992)). Where our cases did dispense with those requirements\xe2\x80\x94even under the Roberts approach\xe2\x80\x94the statements at issue were clearly nontestimonial. See, e.g., Bourjaily v. United States, 483 U. S. 171, 181\xe2\x80\x93184 (1987) (statements made unwittingly to a Government informant); Dutton v. Evans, 400 U. S. 74, 87\xe2\x80\x9389 (1970) (plurality opinion) (statements from one prisoner to another).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Most of the American cases applying the Confrontation Clause or its state constitutional or common-law counterparts involved testimonial statements of the most formal sort\xe2\x80\x94sworn testimony in prior judicial proceedings or formal depositions under oath\xe2\x80\x94which invites the argument that the scope of the Clause is limited to that very formal category. But the English cases that were the progenitors of the Confrontation Clause did not limit the exclusionary rule to prior court testimony and formal depositions, see Crawford, supra, at 52, and n.\xc2\xa03. In any event, we do not think it conceivable that the protections of the Confrontation Clause can readily be evaded by having a note-taking policeman recite the unsworn hearsay testimony of the declarant, instead of having the declarant sign a deposition. Indeed, if there is one point for which no case\xe2\x80\x94English or early American, state or federal\xe2\x80\x94can be cited, that is it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question before us in Davis, then, is whether, objectively considered, the interrogation that took place in the course of the 911 call produced testimonial statements. When we said in Crawford, supra, at 53, that \xe2\x80\x9cinterrogations by law enforcement officers fall squarely within [the] class\xe2\x80\x9d of testimonial hearsay, we had immediately in mind (for that was the case before us) interrogations solely directed at establishing the facts of a past crime, in order to identify (or provide evidence to convict) the perpetrator. The product of such interrogation, whether reduced to a writing signed by the declarant or embedded in the memory (and perhaps notes) of the interrogating officer, is testimonial. It is, in the terms of the 1828 American dictionary quoted in Crawford, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[a] solemn declaration or affirmation made for the purpose of establishing or proving some fact.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 541 U. S., at 51. (The solemnity of even an oral declaration of relevant past fact to an investigating officer is well enough established by the severe consequences that can attend a deliberate falsehood. See, e.g., United States v. Stewart, 433 F.\xc2\xa03d 273, 288 (CA2 2006) (false statements made to federal investigators violate 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001); State v. Reed, 2005 WI 53, \xc2\xb630, 695 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 315, 323 (state criminal offense to \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly giv[e] false information to [an] officer with [the] intent to mislead the officer in the performance of his or her duty\xe2\x80\x9d).) A 911 call, on the other hand, and at least the initial interrogation conducted in connection with a 911 call, is ordinarily not designed primarily to \xe2\x80\x9cestablis[h] or prov[e]\xe2\x80\x9d some past fact, but to describe current circumstances requiring police assistance.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The difference between the interrogation in Davis and the one in Crawford is apparent on the face of things. In Davis, McCottry was speaking about events as they were actually happening, rather than \xe2\x80\x9cdescrib[ing] past events,\xe2\x80\x9d Lilly v. Virginia, 527 U. S. 116, 137 (1999) (plurality opinion). Sylvia Crawford\xe2\x80\x99s interrogation, on the other hand, took place hours after the events she described had occurred. Moreover, any reasonable listener would recognize that McCottry (unlike Sylvia Crawford) was facing an ongoing emergency. Although one might call 911 to provide a narrative report of a crime absent any imminent danger, McCottry\xe2\x80\x99s call was plainly a call for help against bona fide physical threat. Third, the nature of what was asked and answered in Davis, again viewed objectively, was such that the elicited statements were necessary to be able to resolve the present emergency, rather than simply to learn (as in Crawford) what had happened in the past. That is true even of the operator\xe2\x80\x99s effort to establish the identity of the assailant, so that the dispatched officers might know whether they would be encountering a violent felon. See, e.g., Hiibel v. Sixth Judicial Dist. Court of Nev., Humboldt Cty., 542 U. S. 177, 186 (2004). And finally, the difference in the level of formality between the two interviews is striking. Crawford was responding calmly, at the station house, to a series of questions, with the officer-interrogator taping and making notes of her answers; McCottry\xe2\x80\x99s frantic answers were provided over the phone, in an environment that was not tranquil, or even (as far as any reasonable 911 operator could make out) safe.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude from all this that the circumstances of McCottry\xe2\x80\x99s interrogation objectively indicate its primary purpose was to enable police assistance to meet an ongoing emergency. She simply was not acting as a witness; she was not testifying. What she said was not \xe2\x80\x9ca weaker substitute for live testimony\xe2\x80\x9d at trial, United States v. Inadi, 475 U. S. 387, 394 (1986), like Lord Cobham\xe2\x80\x99s statements in Raleigh\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 2 How. St. Tr. 1 (1603), or Jane Dingler\xe2\x80\x99s ex parte statements against her husband in King v. Dingler, 2 Leach 561, 168 Eng. Rep. 383 (1791), or Sylvia Crawford\xe2\x80\x99s statement in Crawford. In each of those cases, the ex parte actors and the evidentiary products of the ex parte communication aligned perfectly with their courtroom analogues. McCottry\xe2\x80\x99s emergency statement does not. No \xe2\x80\x9cwitness\xe2\x80\x9d goes into court to proclaim an emergency and seek help.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Davis seeks to cast McCottry in the unlikely role of a witness by pointing to English cases. None of them involves statements made during an ongoing emergency. In King v. Brasier, 1 Leach 199, 168 Eng. Rep. 202 (1779), for example, a young rape victim, \xe2\x80\x9cimmediately on her coming home, told all the circumstances of the injury\xe2\x80\x9d to her mother. Id., at 200, 168 Eng. Rep., at 202. The case would be helpful to Davis if the relevant statement had been the girl\xe2\x80\x99s screams for aid as she was being chased by her assailant. But by the time the victim got home, her story was an account of past events.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is not to say that a conversation which begins as an interrogation to determine the need for emergency assistance cannot, as the Indiana Supreme Court put it, \xe2\x80\x9cevolve into testimonial statements,\xe2\x80\x9d 829 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 457, once that purpose has been achieved. In this case, for example, after the operator gained the information needed to address the exigency of the moment, the emergency appears to have ended (when Davis drove away from the premises). The operator then told McCottry to be quiet, and proceeded to pose a battery of questions. It could readily be maintained that, from that point on, McCottry\xe2\x80\x99s statements were testimonial, not unlike the \xe2\x80\x9cstructured police questioning\xe2\x80\x9d that occurred in Crawford, 541 U. S., at 53, n. 4. This presents no great problem. Just as, for Fifth Amendment purposes, \xe2\x80\x9cpolice officers can and will distinguish almost instinctively between questions necessary to secure their own safety or the safety of the public and questions designed solely to elicit testimonial evidence from a suspect,\xe2\x80\x9d New York v. Quarles, 467 U. S. 649, 658\xe2\x80\x93659 (1984), trial courts will recognize the point at which, for Sixth Amendment purposes, statements in response to interrogations become testimonial. Through in limine procedure, they should redact or exclude the portions of any statement that have become testimonial, as they do, for example, with unduly prejudicial portions of otherwise admissible evidence. Davis\xe2\x80\x99s jury did not hear the complete 911 call, although it may well have heard some testimonial portions. We were asked to classify only McCottry\xe2\x80\x99s early statements identifying Davis as her assailant, and we agree with the Washington Supreme Court that they were not testimonial. That court also concluded that, even if later parts of the call were testimonial, their admission was harmless beyond a reasonable doubt. Davis does not challenge that holding, and we therefore assume it to be correct.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Determining the testimonial or nontestimonial character of the statements that were the product of the interrogation in Hammon is a much easier task, since they were not much different from the statements we found to be testimonial in Crawford. It is entirely clear from the circumstances that the interrogation was part of an investigation into possibly criminal past conduct\xe2\x80\x94as, indeed, the testifying officer expressly acknowledged, App. in No. 05\xe2\x80\x935705, at 25, 32, 34. There was no emergency in progress; the interrogating officer testified that he had heard no arguments or crashing and saw no one throw or break anything, id., at 25. When the officers first arrived, Amy told them that things were fine, id., at 14, and there was no immediate threat to her person. When the officer questioned Amy for the second time, and elicited the challenged statements, he was not seeking to determine (as in Davis) \xe2\x80\x9cwhat is happening,\xe2\x80\x9d but rather \xe2\x80\x9cwhat happened.\xe2\x80\x9d Objectively viewed, the primary, if not indeed the sole, purpose of the interrogation was to investigate a possible crime\xe2\x80\x94which is, of course, precisely what the officer should have done.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true that the Crawford interrogation was more formal. It followed a Miranda warning, was tape-recorded, and took place at the station house, see 541 U. S., at 53, n.\xc2\xa04. While these features certainly strengthened the statements\xe2\x80\x99 testimonial aspect\xe2\x80\x94made it more objectively apparent, that is, that the purpose of the exercise was to nail down the truth about past criminal events\xe2\x80\x94none was essential to the point. It was formal enough that Amy\xe2\x80\x99s interrogation was conducted in a separate room, away from her husband (who tried to intervene), with the officer receiving her replies for use in his \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigat[ion].\xe2\x80\x9d App. in No. 05\xe2\x80\x935705, at 34. What we called the \xe2\x80\x9cstriking resemblance\xe2\x80\x9d of the Crawford statement to civil-law ex parte examinations, 541 U. S., at 52, is shared by Amy\xe2\x80\x99s statement here. Both declarants were actively separated from the defendant\xe2\x80\x94officers forcibly prevented Hershel from participating in the interrogation. Both statements deliberately recounted, in response to police questioning, how potentially criminal past events began and progressed. And both took place some time after the events described were over. Such statements under official interrogation are an obvious substitute for live testimony, because they do precisely what a wit-\nness does on direct examination; they are inherently \ntestimonial.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both Indiana and the United States as amicus curiae argue that this case should be resolved much like Davis. For the reasons we find the comparison to Crawford compelling, we find the comparison to Davis unpersuasive. The statements in Davis were taken when McCottry was alone, not only unprotected by police (as Amy Hammon was protected), but apparently in immediate danger from Davis. She was seeking aid, not telling a story about the past. McCottry\xe2\x80\x99s present-tense statements showed immediacy; Amy\xe2\x80\x99s narrative of past events was delivered at some remove in time from the danger she described. And after Amy answered the officer\xe2\x80\x99s questions, he had her execute an affidavit, in order, he testified, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o establish events that have occurred previously.\xe2\x80\x9d App. in No. 05\xe2\x80\x935705, at 18.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although we necessarily reject the Indiana Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s implication that virtually any \xe2\x80\x9cinitial inquiries\xe2\x80\x9d at the crime scene will not be testimonial, see 829 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 453, 457, we do not hold the opposite\xe2\x80\x94that no questions at the scene will yield nontestimonial answers. We have already observed of domestic disputes that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]fficers called to investigate \xe2\x80\xa6 need to know whom they are dealing with in order to assess the situation, the threat to their own safety, and possible danger to the potential victim.\xe2\x80\x9d Hiibel, 542 U. S., at 186. Such exigencies may often mean that \xe2\x80\x9cinitial inquiries\xe2\x80\x9d produce nontestimonial statements. But in cases like this one, where Amy\xe2\x80\x99s statements were neither a cry for help nor the provision of information enabling officers immediately to end a threatening situation, the fact that they were given at an alleged crime scene and were \xe2\x80\x9cinitial inquiries\xe2\x80\x9d is immaterial. Cf. Crawford, supra, at 52, n.\xc2\xa03.[Footnote 6]

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents in both cases, joined by a number of their amici, contend that the nature of the offenses charged in these two cases\xe2\x80\x94domestic violence\xe2\x80\x94requires greater flexibility in the use of testimonial evidence. This particular type of crime is notoriously susceptible to intimidation or coercion of the victim to ensure that she does not testify at trial. When this occurs, the Confrontation Clause gives the criminal a windfall. We may not, however, vitiate constitutional guarantees when they have the effect of allowing the guilty to go free. Cf. Kyllo v. United States, 533 U. S. 27 (2001) (suppressing evidence from an illegal search). But when defendants seek to undermine the judicial process by procuring or coercing silence from witnesses and victims, the Sixth Amendment does not require courts to acquiesce. While defendants have no duty to assist the State in proving their guilt, they do have the duty to refrain from acting in ways that destroy the integrity of the criminal-trial system. We reiterate what we said in Crawford: that \xe2\x80\x9cthe rule of forfeiture by wrongdoing \xe2\x80\xa6 extinguishes confrontation claims on essentially equitable grounds.\xe2\x80\x9d 541 U. S., at 62 (citing Reynolds, 98 U. S., at 158\xe2\x80\x93159). That is, one who obtains the absence of a witness by wrongdoing forfeits the constitutional right to confrontation.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We take no position on the standards necessary to demonstrate such forfeiture, but federal courts using Federal Rule of Evidence 804(b)(6), which codifies the forfeiture doctrine, have generally held the Government to the preponderance-of-the-evidence standard, see, e.g., United States v. Scott, 284 F.\xc2\xa03d 758, 762 (CA7 2002). State courts tend to follow the same practice, see, e.g., Commonwealth v. Edwards, 444 Mass. 526, 542, 830 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 158, 172 (2005). Moreover, if a hearing on forfeiture is required, Edwards, for instance, observed that \xe2\x80\x9chearsay evidence, including the unavailable witness\xe2\x80\x99s out-of-court statements, may be considered.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 545, 830 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 174. The Roberts approach to the Confrontation Clause undoubtedly made recourse to this doctrine less necessary, because prosecutors could show the \xe2\x80\x9creliability\xe2\x80\x9d of ex parte statements more easily than they could show the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s procurement of the witness\xe2\x80\x99s absence. Crawford, in overruling Roberts, did not destroy the ability of courts to protect the integrity of their proceedings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have determined that, absent a finding of forfeiture by wrongdoing, the Sixth Amendment operates to exclude Amy Hammon\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit. The Indiana courts may (if they are asked) determine on remand whether such a claim of forfeiture is properly raised and, if so, whether it is meritorious.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Crawford v. Washington, 541 U. S. 36 (2004), we abandoned the general reliability inquiry we had long employed to judge the admissibility of hearsay evidence under the Confrontation Clause, describing that inquiry as \xe2\x80\x9cinherently, and therefore permanently, unpredictable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 68, n.\xc2\xa010 (emphasis in original). Today, a mere two years after the Court decided Crawford, it adopts an equally unpredictable test, under which district courts are charged with divining the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of police interrogations. Ante, at 7. Besides being difficult for courts to apply, this test characterizes as \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore inadmissible, evidence that bears little resemblance to what we have recognized as the evidence targeted by the Confrontation Clause. Because neither of the cases before the Court today would implicate the Confrontation Clause under an appropriately targeted standard, I concur only in the judgment in Davis v. Washington, No. 05\xe2\x80\x935224, and dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of Hammon v. Indiana, No. 05\xe2\x80\x935705.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Confrontation Clause provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right \xe2\x80\xa6 to be confronted with the witnesses against him\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Amdt. 6. We have recognized that the operative phrase in the Clause, \xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses against him,\xe2\x80\x9d could be interpreted narrowly, to reach only those witnesses who actually testify at trial, or more broadly, to reach many or all of those whose out-of-court statements are offered at trial. Crawford, supra, at 42\xe2\x80\x9343; White v. Illinois, 502 U. S. 346, 359\xe2\x80\x93363 (1992) (Thomas, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). Because the narrowest interpretation of the Clause would conflict with both the history giving rise to the adoption of the Clause and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent, we have rejected such a reading. See Crawford, supra, at 50\xe2\x80\x9351; White, supra, at 360 (opinion of Thomas, J.).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rejection of the narrowest view of the Clause does not, however, require the broadest application of the Clause to exclude otherwise admissible hearsay evidence. The history surrounding the right to confrontation supports the conclusion that it was developed to target particular practices that occurred under the English bail and committal statutes passed during the reign of Queen Mary, namely, the \xe2\x80\x9ccivil-law mode of criminal procedure, and particularly its use of ex parte examinations as evidence against the accused.\xe2\x80\x9d Crawford, supra, at 43, 50; White, supra, at 361\xe2\x80\x93362 (opinion of Thomas, J.); Mattox v. United States, 156 U. S. 237, 242 (1895). \xe2\x80\x9cThe predominant purpose of the [Marian committal] statute was to institute systematic questioning of the accused and the witnesses.\xe2\x80\x9d J. Langbein, Prosecuting Crime in the Renaissance 23 (1974) (emphasis added). The statute required an oral examination of the suspect and the accusers, transcription within two days of the examinations, and physical transmission to the judges hearing the case. Id., at 10, 23. These examinations came to be used as evidence in some cases, in lieu of a personal appearance by the witness. Crawford, supra, at 43\xe2\x80\x9344; 9 W.\xc2\xa0Holdsworth, A History of English Law 223\xe2\x80\x93229 (1926). Many statements that would be inadmissible as a matter of hearsay law bear little resemblance to these evidentiary practices, which the Framers proposed the Confrontation Clause to prevent. See, e.g., Crawford, supra, at 51 (contrasting \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n off-hand, overheard remark\xe2\x80\x9d with the abuses targeted by the Confrontation Clause). Accordingly, it is unlikely that the Framers intended the word \xe2\x80\x9cwitness\xe2\x80\x9d to be read so broadly as to include such statements. Cf. Dutton v. Evans, 400 U. S. 74, 94 (1970) (Harlan, J., concurring in result) (rejecting the \xe2\x80\x9cassumption that the core purpose of the Confrontation Clause of the Sixth Amendment is to prevent overly broad exceptions to the hearsay rule\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Crawford, we recognized that this history could be squared with the language of the Clause, giving rise to a workable, and more accurate, interpretation of the Clause. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[W]itnesses,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d we said, are those who \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98bear testimony.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 541 U. S., at 51 (quoting 1 N. Webster, An American Dictionary of the English Language (1828)). And \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]estimony\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[a] solemn declaration or affirmation made for the purpose of establishing or proving some fact.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Webster, supra). Admittedly, we did not set forth a detailed framework for addressing whether a statement is \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial\xe2\x80\x9d and thus subject to the Confrontation Clause. But the plain terms of the \xe2\x80\x9ctestimony\xe2\x80\x9d definition we endorsed necessarily require some degree of solemnity before a statement can be deemed \xe2\x80\x9ctestimonial.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This requirement of solemnity supports my view that the statements regulated by the Confrontation Clause must include \xe2\x80\x9cextrajudicial statements \xe2\x80\xa6 contained in formalized testimonial materials, such as affidavits, depositions, prior testimony, or confessions.\xe2\x80\x9d White, supra, at 365 (opinion of Thomas, J.). Affidavits, depositions, and prior testimony are, by their very nature, taken through a formalized process. Likewise, confessions, when extracted by police in a formal manner, carry sufficient indicia of solemnity to constitute formalized statements and, accordingly, bear a \xe2\x80\x9cstriking resemblance,\xe2\x80\x9d Crawford, supra, at 52, to the examinations of the accused and accusers under the Marian statutes.[Footnote 1] See generally Langbein, supra, at 21\xe2\x80\x9334.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the Court concedes that the early American cases invoking the right to confrontation or the Confrontation Clause itself all \xe2\x80\x9cclearly involve[d] testimony\xe2\x80\x9d as defined in Crawford, ante, at 9, it fails to acknowledge that all of the cases it cites fall within the narrower category of formalized testimonial materials I have proposed. See ante, at 9, n.\xc2\xa03.[Footnote 2] Interactions between the police and an accused (or witnesses) resemble Marian proceedings\xe2\x80\x94and these early cases\xe2\x80\x94only when the interactions are somehow rendered \xe2\x80\x9cformal.\xe2\x80\x9d In Crawford, for example, the interrogation was custodial, taken after warnings given pursuant to Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436 (1966). 541 U. S., at 38. Miranda warnings, by their terms, inform a prospective defendant that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98anything he says can be used against him in a court of law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Dickerson v. United States, 530 U. S. 428, 435 (2000) (quoting Miranda, supra, at 479). This imports a solemnity to the process that is not present in a mere conversation between a witness or suspect and a police officer.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court all but concedes that no case can be cited for its conclusion that the Confrontation Clause also applies to informal police questioning under certain circumstances. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9311. Instead, the sole basis for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion is its apprehension that the Confrontation Clause will \xe2\x80\x9creadily be evaded\xe2\x80\x9d if it is only applicable to formalized testimonial materials. Ante, at 11. But the Court\xe2\x80\x99s proposed solution to the risk of evasion is needlessly overinclusive. Because the Confrontation Clause sought to regulate prosecutorial abuse occurring through use of ex parte statements as evidence against the accused, it also reaches the use of technically informal statements when used to evade the formalized process. Cf. ibid. That is, even if the interrogation itself is not formal, the production of evidence by the prosecution at trial would resemble the abuses targeted by the Confrontation Clause if the prosecution attempted to use out-of-court statements as a means of circumventing the literal right of confrontation, see Coy v. Iowa, 487 U. S. 1012 (1988). In such a case, the Confrontation Clause could fairly be applied to exclude the hearsay statements offered by the prosecution, preventing evasion without simultaneously excluding evidence offered by the prosecution in good faith.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s standard is not only disconnected from history and unnecessary to prevent abuse; it also yields no predictable results to police officers and prosecutors attempting to comply with the law. Cf. Crawford, supra, at 68, n.\xc2\xa010 (criticizing unpredictability of the pre-Crawford test); White, 502 U. S., at 364\xe2\x80\x93365 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (limiting the Confrontation Clause to the discrete category of materials historically abused would \xe2\x80\x9cgreatly simplify\xe2\x80\x9d application of the Clause). In many, if not most, cases where police respond to a report of a crime, whether pursuant to a 911 call from the victim or otherwise, the purposes of an interrogation, viewed from the perspective of the police, are both to respond to the emergency situation and to gather evidence. See New York v. Quarles, 467 U. S. 649, 656 (1984) (\xe2\x80\x9cUndoubtedly most police officers [deciding whether to give Miranda warnings in a possible emergency situation] would act out of a host of different, instinctive, and largely unverifiable motives\xe2\x80\x94their own safety, the safety of others, and perhaps as well the desire to obtain incriminating evidence from the suspect\xe2\x80\x9d). Assigning one of these two \xe2\x80\x9clargely unverifiable motives,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., primacy requires constructing a hierarchy of purpose that will rarely be present\xe2\x80\x94and is not reliably discernible. It will inevitably be, quite simply, an exercise in fiction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s repeated invocation of the word \xe2\x80\x9cobjectiv[e]\xe2\x80\x9d to describe its test, see ante, at 7, 11\xe2\x80\x9313, 15, however, suggests that the Court may not mean to reference purpose at all, but instead to inquire into the function served by the interrogation. Certainly such a test would avoid the pitfalls that have led us repeatedly to reject tests dependent on the subjective intentions of police officers.[Footnote 4] It would do so, however, at the cost of being even more disconnected from the prosecutorial abuses targeted by the Confrontation Clause. Additionally, it would shift the ability to control whether a violation occurred from the police and prosecutor to the judge, whose determination as to the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of a particular interrogation would be unpredictable and not necessarily tethered to the actual purpose for which the police performed the interrogation.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither the 911 call at issue in Davis nor the police questioning at issue in Hammon is testimonial under the appropriate framework. Neither the call nor the questioning is itself a formalized dialogue.[Footnote 5] Nor do any circumstances surrounding the taking of the statements render those statements sufficiently formal to resemble the Marian examinations; the statements were neither Mirandized nor custodial, nor accompanied by any similar indicia of formality. Finally, there is no suggestion that the prosecution attempted to offer the women\xe2\x80\x99s hearsay evidence at trial in order to evade confrontation. See 829 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 444, 447 (Ind. 2005) (prosecution subpoenaed Amy Hammon to testify, but she was not present); 154 Wash. 2d 291, 296, 111 P.\xc2\xa03d 844, 847 (2005) (en banc) (State was unable to locate Michelle McCottry at the time of trial). Accordingly, the statements at issue in both cases are nontestimonial and admissible under the Confrontation Clause.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination that the evidence against Hammon must be excluded extends the Confrontation Clause far beyond the abuses it was intended to prevent. When combined with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that the evidence against Davis is perfectly admissible, however, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s Hammon holding also reveals the difficulty of applying the Court\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that courts investigate the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose[s]\xe2\x80\x9d of the investigation. The Court draws a line between the two cases based on its explanation that Hammon involves \xe2\x80\x9cno emergency in progress,\xe2\x80\x9d but instead, mere questioning as \xe2\x80\x9cpart of an investigation into possibly criminal past conduct,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315, and its explanation that Davis involves questioning for the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cenabl[ing] police assistance to meet an ongoing emergency,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 13. But the fact that the officer in Hammon was investigating Mr. Hammon\xe2\x80\x99s past conduct does not foreclose the possibility that the primary purpose of his inquiry was to assess whether Mr. Hammon constituted a continuing danger to his wife, requiring further police presence or action. It is hardly remarkable that Hammon did not act abusively towards his wife in the presence of the officers, ante, at 15, and his good judgment to refrain from criminal behavior in the presence of police sheds little, if any, light on whether his violence would have resumed had the police left without further questioning, transforming what the Court dismisses as \xe2\x80\x9cpast conduct\xe2\x80\x9d back into an \xe2\x80\x9congoing emergency.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13, 15.[Footnote 6] Nor does the mere fact that McCottry needed emergency aid shed light on whether the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of gathering, for example, the name of her assailant was to protect the police, to protect the victim, or to gather information for prosecution. In both of the cases before the Court, like many similar cases, pronouncement of the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary\xe2\x80\x9d motive behind the interrogation calls for nothing more than a guess by courts.

II

\n

Washington, No. 05\xe2\x80\x935224, and respectfully dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of Hammon v. Indiana, No. 05\xe2\x80\x935705.
