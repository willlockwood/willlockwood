UNITED STATES v. CLARKE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93301.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 23, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 19, 2014

\n

The Internal Revenue Service (IRS) issued summonses to respondents for information and records relevant to the tax obligations of Dynamo Holdings L.\xc2\xa0P. See\n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77602(a). When respondents failed to comply, the IRS brought an enforcement action in District Court. Respondents challenged the IRS\xe2\x80\x99s motives in issuing the summonses, seeking to question the responsible agents. The District Court denied the request and ordered the summonses enforced, characterizing respondents\xe2\x80\x99 arguments as conjecture and incorrect as a matter of law. The Eleventh Circuit reversed, holding that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to allow respondents to examine the agents constituted an abuse of discretion, and that Circuit precedent entitled them to conduct such questioning regardless of whether they had presented any factual support for their claims.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A taxpayer has a right to conduct an examination of IRS officials regarding their reasons for issuing a summons when he points to specific facts or circumstances plausibly raising an inference of bad faith. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0A person receiving a summons is entitled to contest it in an adversarial enforcement proceeding. Donaldson v. United States,\n\n400 U.\xc2\xa0S. 517\n. But these proceedings are \xe2\x80\x9csummary in nature,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Stuart,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 353\n, and the only relevant question is whether the summons was issued in good faith, United States v. Powell,\n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48\n. The balance struck in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior cases supports a requirement that a summons objector offer not just naked allegations, but some credible evidence to support his claim of improper motive. Circumstantial evidence can suffice to meet that burden, and a fleshed out case is not demanded: The taxpayer need only present a plausible basis for his charge. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0Here, however, the Eleventh Circuit applied a categorical rule demanding the examination of IRS agents without assessing the plausibility of the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 submissions. On remand, the Court of Appeals must consider those submissions in light of the standard set forth here, giving appropriate deference to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling on whether respondents have shown enough to entitle them to examine the agents. However, that ruling is entitled to deference only if it was based on the correct legal standard. See Fox v. Vice, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. And the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s latitude does not extend to legal issues about what counts as an illicit motive. Cf. Koon v. United States,\n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 81\n. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

517 Fed. Appx. 689, vacated and remanded.

\n

UNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. MICHAEL CLARKE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[June 19, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Kagan delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Internal Revenue Service (IRS or Service) has broad statutory authority to summon a taxpayer to produce documents or give testimony relevant to determining tax liability. If the taxpayer fails to comply, the IRS may petition a federal district court to enforce the summons. In an enforcement proceeding, the IRS must show that it issued the summons in good faith.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This case requires us to consider when a taxpayer, as part of such a proceeding, has a right to question IRS officials about their reasons for issuing a summons. We hold, contrary to the Court of Appeals below, that a bare allegation of improper purpose does not entitle a taxpayer to examine IRS officials. Rather, the taxpayer has a right to conduct that examination when he points to specific facts or circumstances plausibly raising an inference of bad faith.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Congress has \xe2\x80\x9cauthorized and required\xe2\x80\x9d the IRS \xe2\x80\x9cto make the inquiries, determinations, and assessments of all taxes\xe2\x80\x9d the Internal Revenue Code imposes. 26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa76201(a). And in support of that authority, Congress has granted the Service broad latitude to issue summonses \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or the purpose of ascertaining the correctness of any return, making a return where none has been made, determining the liability of any person for any internal revenue tax\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, or collecting any such liability.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77602(a). Such a summons directs a taxpayer (or associated person[1]) to appear before an IRS official and to provide sworn testimony or produce \xe2\x80\x9cbooks, papers, records, or other data .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. relevant or material to [a tax] inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77602(a)(1).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n If a taxpayer does not comply with a summons, the IRS may bring an enforcement action in district court. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77402(b), 7604(a). In that proceeding, we have held, the IRS \xe2\x80\x9cneed only demonstrate good faith in issuing the summons.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Stuart,\n\n489 U. S. 353,\n359 (1989)\n. More specifically, that means establishing what have become known as the Powell factors: \xe2\x80\x9cthat the investigation will be conducted pursuant to a legitimate purpose, that the inquiry may be relevant to the purpose, that the information sought is not already within the [IRS\xe2\x80\x99s] possession, and that the administrative steps required by the [Internal Revenue] Code have been followed.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Powell,\n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48\n\xe2\x80\x9358 (1964). To make that showing, the IRS usually files an affidavit from the responsible investigating agent. See Stuart, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 360. The taxpayer, however, has an opportunity to challenge that affidavit, and to urge the court to quash the summons \xe2\x80\x9con any appropriate ground\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94including, as relevant here, improper purpose. See Reisman v. Caplin,\n\n375 U.\xc2\xa0S. 440,\n449 (1964)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The summons dispute in this case arose from an IRS examination of the tax returns of Dynamo Holdings Limited Partnership (Dynamo) for the 2005\xe2\x80\x932007 tax years. The IRS harbored suspicions about large interest expenses that those returns had reported. As its investigation proceeded, the Service persuaded Dynamo to agree to two year-long extensions of the usual 3-year limitations period for assessing tax liability; in 2010, with that period again drawing to a close, Dynamo refused to grant the IRS a third extension. Shortly thereafter, in September and October 2010, the IRS issued summonses to the respondents here, four individuals associated with Dynamo whom the Service believed had information and records relevant to Dynamo\xe2\x80\x99s tax obligations. None of the respondents complied with those summonses. In December 2010 (still within the augmented limitations period), the IRS issued a Final Partnership Administrative Adjustment proposing changes to Dynamo\xe2\x80\x99s returns that would result in greater tax liability. Dynamo responded in February 2011 by filing suit in the United States Tax Court to challenge the adjustments. That litigation remains pending. A few months later, in April 2011, the IRS instituted proceedings in District Court to compel the respondents to comply with the summonses they had gotten.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Those enforcement proceedings developed into a dispute about the IRS\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for issuing the summonses. The IRS submitted an investigating agent\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit attesting to the Powell factors; among other things, that declaration maintained that the testimony and records sought were necessary to \xe2\x80\x9cproperly investigate the correctness of [Dynamo\xe2\x80\x99s] federal tax reporting\xe2\x80\x9d and that the summonses were \xe2\x80\x9cnot issued to harass or for any other improper purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 26, 34. In reply, the respondents pointed to circumstantial evidence that, in their view, suggested \xe2\x80\x9culterior motive[s]\xe2\x80\x9d of two different kinds. App. to Pet. for Cert. 72a. First, the respondents asserted that the IRS issued the summonses to \xe2\x80\x9cpunish[\xc2\xa0] [Dynamo] for refusing to agree to a further extension of the applicable statute of limitations.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 52. More particularly, they stated in sworn declarations that immediately after Dynamo declined to grant a third extension of time, the IRS, \xe2\x80\x9cdespite having not asked for additional information for some time, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. suddenly issued\xe2\x80\x9d the summonses. Id., at 95. Second, the respondents averred that the IRS decided to enforce the summonses, subsequent to Dynamo\xe2\x80\x99s filing suit in Tax Court, to \xe2\x80\x9cevad[e] the Tax Court[\xe2\x80\x99s] limitations on discovery\xe2\x80\x9d and thus gain an unfair advantage in that litigation. Id., at 53. In support of that charge, the respondents submitted an affidavit from the attorney of another Dynamo associate, who had chosen to comply with a summons issued at the same time. The attorney reported that only the IRS attorneys handling the Tax Court case, and not the original investigating agents, were present at the interview of his client. In light of those submissions, the respondents asked for an opportunity to question the agents about their motives.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The District Court denied that request and ordered the respondents to comply with the summonses. According to the court, the respondents \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] made no meaningful allegations of improper purpose\xe2\x80\x9d warranting examination of IRS agents. App. to Pet. for Cert. 18a. The court characterized the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 statute-of-limitations theory as \xe2\x80\x9cmere conjecture.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14a. And it ruled that the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 evasion-of-discovery-limits claim was \xe2\x80\x9cincorrect as a matter of law\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he validity of a summons is tested as of the date of issuance,\xe2\x80\x9d not enforcement\xe2\x80\x94and the Tax Court proceedings had not yet begun when the IRS issued the summonses. Id., at 15a.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit reversed, holding that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to allow the respondents to examine IRS agents constituted an abuse of discretion. In support of that ruling, the Court of Appeals cited binding Circuit precedent holding that a simple \xe2\x80\x9callegation of improper purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d even if lacking any \xe2\x80\x9cfactual support,\xe2\x80\x9d entitles a taxpayer to \xe2\x80\x9cquestion IRS officials concerning the Service\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for issuing the summons.\xe2\x80\x9d 517 Fed. Appx. 689, 691 (2013) (quoting United States v. Southeast First Nat. Bank of Miami Springs, 655 F. 2d 661, 667 (CA5 1981)); see Nero Trading, LLC v. United States Dept. of Treasury, 570 F. 3d 1244, 1249 (CA11 2009) (reaffirming Southeast).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Every other Court of Appeals has rejected the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view that a bare allegation of improper motive entitles a person objecting to an IRS summons to examine the responsible officials.[2] We granted certiorari to resolve that conflict, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. __ (2014), and we now vacate the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A person receiving an IRS summons is, as we have often held, entitled to contest it in an enforcement proceeding. See United States v. Bisceglia,\n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 141,\n146 (1975)\n; Powell, 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 57\xe2\x80\x9358; Reisman, 375 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 449. The power \xe2\x80\x9cvested in tax collectors may be abused, as all power\xe2\x80\x9d may be abused. Bisceglia, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 146. In recognition of that possibility, Congress made enforcement of an IRS summons contingent on a court\xe2\x80\x99s approval. See\n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77604(b). And we have time and again stated that the requisite judicial proceeding is not ex\xc2\xa0parte but adversarial. See Donaldson v. United States,\n\n400 U.\xc2\xa0S. 517,\n527 (1971)\n; Powell, 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 58; Reisman, 375 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 446. The summoned party must receive notice, and may present argument and evidence on all matters bearing on a summons\xe2\x80\x99s validity. See Powell, 379 U.\xc2\xa0S.,at 58.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Yet we have also emphasized that summons enforcement proceedings are to be \xe2\x80\x9csummary in nature.\xe2\x80\x9d Stuart, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 369. The purpose of a summons is \xe2\x80\x9cnot to accuse,\xe2\x80\x9d much less to adjudicate, but only \xe2\x80\x9cto inquire.\xe2\x80\x9d Bisceglia, 420 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 146. And such an investigatory tool, we have recognized, is a crucial backstop in a tax system based on self-reporting. See ibid. (restricting summons authority would enable \xe2\x80\x9cdishonest persons [to] escap[e] taxation[,] thus shifting heavier burdens to honest taxpayers\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, we long ago held that courts may ask only whether the IRS issued a summons in good faith, and must eschew any broader role of \xe2\x80\x9coversee[ing] the [IRS\xe2\x80\x99s] determinations to investigate.\xe2\x80\x9d Powell, 379 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 56. So too, we stated that absent contrary evidence, the IRS can satisfy that standard by submitting a simple affidavit from the investigating agent. See Stuart, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 359\xe2\x80\x93360. Thus, we have rejected rules that would \xe2\x80\x9cthwart and defeat the [Service\xe2\x80\x99s] appropriate investigatory powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Donaldson, 400 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 533.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The balance we have struck in prior cases comports with the following rule, applicable here: As part of the adversarial process concerning a summons\xe2\x80\x99s validity, the taxpayer is entitled to examine an IRS agent when he can point to specific facts or circumstances plausibly raising an inference of bad faith. Naked allegations of improper purpose are not enough: The taxpayer must offer some credible evidence supporting his charge. But circumstantial evidence can suffice to meet that burden; after all, direct evidence of another person\xe2\x80\x99s bad faith, at this threshold stage, will rarely if ever be available. And although bare assertion or conjecture is not enough, neither is a fleshed out case demanded: The taxpayer need only make a showing of facts that give rise to a plausible inference of improper motive. That standard will ensure inquiry where the facts and circumstances make inquiry appropriate, without turning every summons dispute into a fishing expedition for official wrongdoing. And the rule is little different from the one that both the respondents and the Government have recommended to us.[3]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But that is not the standard the Eleventh Circuit applied. Although the respondents gamely try to put an-other face on the opinion below, see Brief for Respondents 24\xe2\x80\x9325, and n.\xc2\xa017, we have no doubt that the Court of Appeals viewed even bare allegations of improper purpose as entitling a summons objector to question IRS agents. The court in fact had some evidence before it pertaining to the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 charges: The respondents, for example, had submitted one declaration relating the timing of the summonses to Dynamo\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to extend the limitations period, see App. 95, and another aiming to show that the IRS was using the summonses to obtain discovery it could not get in Tax Court, see id., at 97\xe2\x80\x93100. But the Eleventh Circuit never assessed whether those (or any other) materials plausibly supported an inference of improper motive; indeed, the court never mentioned the proffered evidence at all. Instead, and in line with Circuit precedent, the court applied a categorical rule, demanding the examination of IRS agents even when a taxpayer made only conclusory allegations. See supra, at 4. That was error. On remand, the Court of Appeals must consider the respond-ents\xe2\x80\x99 submissions in light of the standard we have stated.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n That consideration must as well give appropriate deference to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling. An appellate court, as the Eleventh Circuit noted, reviews for abuse of discretion a trial court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to order\xe2\x80\x94or not\xe2\x80\x94the questioning of IRS agents. See 517 Fed. Appx., at 691, n.\xc2\xa02; Tiffany Fine Arts, Inc. v. United States,\n\n469 U.\xc2\xa0S. 310\n, n.\xc2\xa07 (1985). That standard of review reflects the district court\xe2\x80\x99s supe-rior familiarity with, and understanding of, the dispute; andit comports with the way appellate courts review related matters of case management, discovery, and trial prac-tice. See, e.g., Hoffmann-La Roche Inc. v. Sperling,\n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 165\n\xe2\x80\x93173 (1989); Crawford-El v. Britton,\n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 574\n\xe2\x80\x93601 (1998). Accordingly, the Court of Appeals must take into account on remand the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s broad discretion to determine whether a tax-payer has shown enough to require the examination of IRS investigators.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But two caveats to that instruction are in order here. First, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is entitled to deference only if based on the correct legal standard. See Fox v. Vice, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 11) (\xe2\x80\x9cA trial court has wide discretion when, but only when, it calls the game by the right rules\xe2\x80\x9d). We leave to the Court of Appeals the task of deciding whether the District Court asked and answered the relevant question\xe2\x80\x94once again, whether the respondents pointed to specific facts or circumstances plausibly raising an inference of improper motive.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n And second, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s latitude does not extend to legal issues about what counts as an illicit motive. As indicated earlier, one such issue is embedded in the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim that the Government moved to enforce these summonses to gain an unfair advantage in Tax Court litigation. See supra, at 4. The Government responds, and the District Court agreed, that any such purpose is irrelevant because \xe2\x80\x9cthe validity of a summons is judged at the time\xe2\x80\x9d the IRS originally issued the summons, and here that preceded the Tax Court suit. Tr. of Oral Arg. 7; see Reply Brief 19\xe2\x80\x9320; App. to Pet. for Cert. 15a. Similarly, with respect to the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 alternative theory, the Government briefly suggested at argument that issuing a summons because \xe2\x80\x9ca taxpayer declined to extend a statute of limitations would [not] be an improper purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d even assuming that happened here. Tr. of Oral Arg. 6. We state no view on those issues; they are not within the question presented for our review. We note only that they are pure questions of law, so if they arise again on remand, the Court of Appeals has no cause to defer to the District Court. Cf. Koon v. United States,\n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 81,\n100 (1996)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cA district court by definition abuses its discretion when it makes an error of law\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For these reasons, we vacate the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
