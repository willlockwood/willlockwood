JOHN ROBERTSON, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES\nex rel. WYKENNA WATSON

\n

on writ of certiorari to the district of columbia court of appeals

\n

[May 24, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Scalia, Justice Kennedy, and Justice Sotomayor join,\ndissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is a complicated case, but it raises a straightforward and important threshold issue. When we granted certiorari, we rephrased the question presented to focus on that issue: \xe2\x80\x9cWhether an action for criminal contempt in a congressionally created court may constitutionally be brought in the name and pursuant to the power of a private person, rather than in the name and pursuant to the power of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 558 U. S.___ (2009). The answer to that question is no. The terrifying force of the criminal justice system may only be brought to bear against an individual by society as a whole, through a prosecution brought on behalf of the government. The court below held otherwise, relying on a dissenting opinion in one of our cases, and on the litigating position of the United States, which the Solicitor General has properly abandoned in this Court. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 12\xe2\x80\x9313, n.\xc2\xa03. We should correct the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s error and return the case to that court to resolve the remaining questions.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In March 1999, Wykenna Watson was assaulted by her then-boyfriend, John Robertson. App. 40. Watson sought and secured a civil protective order against Robertson, prohibiting him from approaching within 100 feet of her and from assaulting, threatening, harassing, physically abusing, or contacting her. Id., at 20. At the same time, the United States Attorney\xe2\x80\x99s Office (USAO) was independently pursuing criminal charges against Robertson arising from the assault.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On June 26, Robertson violated the protective order by again violently assaulting Watson. On July 8, he was indicted for the previous March incident; shortly thereafter, the USAO offered, and Robertson accepted, a plea agreement resolving those charges. Id., at 26\xe2\x80\x9330. At the top of the boilerplate plea form, the Assistant U. S. Attorney added in longhand: \xe2\x80\x9cIn exchange for Mr. Robertson\xe2\x80\x99s plea of guilty to attempt[ed] aggravated assault, the gov\xe2\x80\x99t agrees to: DISMISS the [remaining] charges[,] [and] [n]ot pursue any charges concerning an incident on 6-26-99.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 28. The Superior Court accepted Robertson\xe2\x80\x99s plea and sentenced him to 1 to 3 years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment. Id., at 30, 46, 53.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A few months later, Watson filed a motion to initiate criminal contempt proceedings against Robertson for violating the civil protective order, based on the June 26 assault. See D.\xc2\xa0C. Code \xc2\xa716\xe2\x80\x931005(f) (2009 Supp.); D.\xc2\xa0C. Super. Ct. Domestic Violence Rule 12(d) (Lexis 2010); In re Robertson, 940 A.\xc2\xa02d 1050, 1053 (D.\xc2\xa0C. 2008). After a 2-day bench trial, the court found Robertson guilty on three counts of criminal contempt and sentenced him to three consecutive 180-day terms of imprisonment, suspending execution of the last in favor of five years\xe2\x80\x99 probation. The court also ordered Robertson to pay Watson roughly $10,000 in restitution. App. 2, 63\xe2\x80\x9364. Robertson filed a motion to vacate the judgment, which the court denied.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Robertson appealed. Criminal contempt prosecutions, he argued, \xe2\x80\x9care between the public and the defendant,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus could \xe2\x80\x9conly be brought in the name of the relevant sovereign, \xe2\x80\xa6 the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 8, 10 (quoting Brief for Appellant in No. 00\xe2\x80\x93FM\xe2\x80\x931269 etc. (D.\xc2\xa0C.), pp. 20\xe2\x80\x9321, and 940 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 1057; internal quotation marks omitted). So viewed, the prosecution based on the June 26 incident could not be brought, because the plea agreement barred the \xe2\x80\x9cgov[ernmen]t\xe2\x80\x9d from pursuing any charges arising from that incident.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals rejected Robertson\xe2\x80\x99s arguments, in a two-step holding. Step one: \xe2\x80\x9cthe criminal contempt prosecution in this case was conducted as a private action brought in the name and interest of Ms. Watson, not as a public action brought in the name and interest of the United States or any other governmental entity.\xe2\x80\x9d 940 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 1057\xe2\x80\x931058 (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted). Step two: because the criminal contempt prosecution was brought as an exercise of private power, that prosecution did not implicate a plea agreement that bound only the government. Id., at 1059\xe2\x80\x931060.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to review the first step of that holding.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decision in United States v. Dixon, 509 U. S. 688 (1993), provides the answer to the question presented here. The question in Dixon was one of double jeopardy\xe2\x80\x94whether a private party\xe2\x80\x99s prosecution for criminal contempt barred the Government\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent prosecution for the \xe2\x80\x9csame criminal offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 696. The private prosecution in that case was brought under the same D.\xc2\xa0C. contempt law at issue here. Id., at 692 (citing D.\xc2\xa0C. Code \xc2\xa716\xe2\x80\x931005 (1989)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We thought it \xe2\x80\x9cobvious\xe2\x80\x9d in Dixon that double jeopardy protections barred the Government\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent prosecution. 509 U. S., at 696. The Double Jeopardy Clause, of course, bars the second prosecution for the same offense only if that prosecution is brought by the same sovereign as the first. See Heath v. Alabama, 474 U. S. 82, 88\xe2\x80\x9389 (1985). Thus, the only possible way the Government\xe2\x80\x99s second prosecution could have offended the Double Jeopardy Clause is if the Court understood the criminal contempt prosecution to be the Government\xe2\x80\x99s first prosecution\xe2\x80\x94i.e., one brought on behalf of the Government. See United States v. Halper, 490 U. S. 435, 451 (1989) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe protections of the Double Jeopardy Clause are not triggered by litigation between private parties\xe2\x80\x9d), overruled on other grounds by Hudson v. United States, 522 U. S. 93 (1997).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That we treated the criminal contempt prosecution in Dixon as an exercise of government power should not be surprising. More than two centuries ago, Blackstone wrote that the king is \xe2\x80\x9cthe proper person to prosecute for all public offenses and breaches of the peace, being the person injured in the eye of the law.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 W. Blackstone, Commentaries *268. Blackstone repeated that principle throughout his fourth book. See, e.g., 4 id., at *2, *8, *177. Not long after Blackstone, then-Representative John Marshall agreed, stating on the House floor that \xe2\x80\x9cadminister[ing] criminal judgment \xe2\x80\xa6 is a duty to be performed at the demand of the nation, and with which the nation has a right to dispense. If judgment \xe2\x80\xa6 is to be pronounced, it must be at the prosecution of the nation.\xe2\x80\x9d 10 Annals of Cong. 615 (1800).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This principle has deep historical roots. See, e.g., 1 F. Wharton, Criminal Law \xc2\xa710, p. 11 (9th ed. 1885) (\xe2\x80\x9cPenal justice \xe2\x80\xa6 is a distinctive prerogative of the State, to be exercised in the service [of] the State\xe2\x80\x9d); see also J. Locke, Second Treatise of Civil Government \xc2\xa788, pp. 43\xe2\x80\x9344 (J. Gough ed. 1947) (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]very man who has entered into civil society, and is become a member of any commonwealth, has thereby quitted his power to punish offences against the law of nature in prosecution of his own private judgment[.] \xe2\x80\xa6 [H]e has given a right to the commonwealth to employ his force for the execution of the judgments of the commonwealth\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)). As this Court has said before, \xe2\x80\x9c[c]rimes and offenses against the laws of any State can only be defined, prosecuted and pardoned by the sovereign authority of that State.\xe2\x80\x9d Huntington v. Attrill, 146 U. S. 657, 669 (1892); see also Heath, supra, at 88 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe dual sovereignty doctrine [of the Double Jeopardy Clause] is founded on the common-law conception of crime as an offense against the sovereignty of the government\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These core principles are embodied in the Constitution. The protections our Bill of Rights affords those facing criminal prosecution apply to \xe2\x80\x9cany person,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cany criminal case,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9call criminal prosecutions.\xe2\x80\x9d Amdts. 5, 6 (emphasis added). But those protections apply only against the government; \xe2\x80\x9c[i]ndividual invasion of individual rights\xe2\x80\x9d is not covered. Civil Rights Cases, 109 U. S. 3, 11 (1883) (Fourteenth Amendment). If the safeguards of the Bill of Rights are to be available in \xe2\x80\x9call criminal prosecutions,\xe2\x80\x9d then any such prosecution must be considered to be one on behalf of the government\xe2\x80\x94otherwise the constitutional limits do not apply. \xe2\x80\x9cThe Constitution constrains governmental action \xe2\x80\x98by whatever instruments or in whatever modes that action may be taken,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lebron v. National Railroad Passenger Corporation, 513 U. S. 374, 392 (1995) (quoting Ex parte Virginia, 100 U. S. 339, 346\xe2\x80\x93347 (1880)), but the action still must be governmental action.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court below, however, rejected this understanding, concluding that Watson\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccriminal contempt prosecution\xe2\x80\x9d was not \xe2\x80\x9ca public action\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9ca private action,\xe2\x80\x9d such that it was not covered by an agreement binding the government. 940 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 1057\xe2\x80\x931058 (internal quotation marks omitted). But as we have explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he purpose of a criminal court is not to provide a forum for the ascertainment of private rights. Rather it is to vindicate the public interest in the enforcement of the criminal law while at the same time safeguarding the rights of the individual defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Standefer v. United States, 447 U. S. 10, 25 (1980) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The holding below gives rise to a broad array of unsettling questions. Take the Due Process Clause. It guarantees particular rights in criminal prosecutions because the prosecutor is a state actor, carrying out a \xe2\x80\x9cduty on the part of the Government.\xe2\x80\x9d Kyles v. Whitley, 514 U. S. 419, 433 (1995) (emphasis added). But if the criminal prosecution is instead viewed as \xe2\x80\x9ca private action,\xe2\x80\x9d not an exercise of sovereign power, how would those rights attach? Cf. DeShaney v. Winnebago County Dept. of Social Servs., 489 U. S. 189, 195\xe2\x80\x93196 (1989). What about Brady v. Maryland, 373 U. S. 83 (1963)? The private prosecutor is likely to have evidence pertinent to the proceeding\xe2\x80\x94particularly if, as here, the private prosecutor is also the victim of the crime. But if the prosecutor is not exercising governmental authority, what would be the constitutional basis for any Brady obligations? May the private prosecutor interview the defendant without Miranda warnings, since she is not acting on behalf of any sovereign but only in a private capacity? See Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436 (1966).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our entire criminal justice system is premised on the notion that a criminal prosecution pits the government against the governed, not one private citizen against another. The ruling below is a startling repudiation of that basic understanding.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite the foregoing, the Court of Appeals determined that Watson brought this criminal prosecution under her authority as a private citizen. 940 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 1058. To reach that conclusion, the court relied on Justice Blackmun\xe2\x80\x99s separate opinion in Dixon. See 940 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 1057 (\xe2\x80\x9cAs Justice Blackmun said in United States v. Dixon, criminal contempt is \xe2\x80\x98a special situation.\xe2\x80\x99 [Dixon, 509 U. S., at 742 (opinion concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part)]. \xe2\x80\xa6 \xe2\x80\x98[T]he purpose of contempt is not to punish an offense against the community at large but rather to punish the specific offense of disobeying a court order.\xe2\x80\x99 [Ibid.]\xe2\x80\x9d (citation omitted)). In fact, the court quoted from Justice Blackmun\xe2\x80\x99s separate opinion no fewer than four times. Id., at 1057.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Blackmun\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, however, was a partial concurrence in the judgment and partial dissent, and it garnered only one vote. Moreover, the portion of the opinion relied upon by the court below was the dissenting part. A majority of the Court squarely rejected Justice Blackmun\xe2\x80\x99s view, and did so in plain terms. See Dixon, 509 U. S., at 699\xe2\x80\x93701 (opinion of Scalia, J., joined by Kennedy, J.); see id., at 720 (White, J., joined by Stevens and Souter, JJ., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before this Court, Watson understandably retreats from Justice Blackmun\xe2\x80\x99s dissenting opinion. Instead, she argues that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n England and in America at the time of the Founding, prosecutions by victims of crime and their families were the rule, not the exception.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 38\xe2\x80\x9339. But such prosecutions, though brought by a private party, were commonly understood as an exercise of sovereign power\xe2\x80\x94the private party acting on behalf of the sovereign, seeking to vindicate a public wrong.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In England, for example, private parties could initiate criminal prosecutions, but the Crown\xe2\x80\x94entrusted with the constitutional responsibility for law enforcement\xe2\x80\x94could enter a nolle prosequi to halt the prosecution. See, e.g., King v. Guerchy, 1 Black W. 545, 96 Eng. Rep. 315 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1765); King v. Fielding, 2 Burr. 719, 720, 97 Eng. Rep. 531 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1759); see also King v. State, 43 Fla. 211, 223, 31 So. 254, 257 (1901) (Private prosecutions in England were understood to be \xe2\x80\x9cconducted on behalf of the crown by the privately retained counsel of private prosecutors\xe2\x80\x9d); P.\xc2\xa0Devlin, The Criminal Prosecution in England 21 (1958).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Watson\xe2\x80\x99s arguments based on American precedent fail largely for the same reason: To say that private parties could (and still can, in some places) exercise some control over criminal prosecutions says nothing to rebut the widely accepted principle that those private parties necessarily acted (and now act) on behalf of the sovereign. See, e.g., Cronan ex rel. State v. Cronan, 774 A.\xc2\xa02d 866, 877 (R.\xc2\xa0I. 2001) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]ttorneys conducting private prosecutions stand in the shoes of the state\xe2\x80\x9d); State v. Westbrook, 279 N.\xc2\xa0C. 18, 36, 181 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 572, 583 (1971) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe prosecuting attorney, whether the solicitor or privately employed counsel, represents the State\xe2\x80\x9d); Sidman, The Outmoded Concept of Private Prosecution, 25 Am. U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 754, 774 (1976) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he privately retained attorney becomes, in effect, a temporary public prosecutor\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, many of the state court authorities Watson herself cites expressly recognize this fundamental point. See, e.g., Katz v. Commonwealth, 379 Mass. 305, 312, 399 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 1055, 1060 (1979) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is clear with respect to the criminal aspects of the present case that the Commonwealth \xe2\x80\xa6 is the adverse party\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have no need to take issue with that proposition, but this case is different. The whole point of the ruling below was that this was not a \xe2\x80\x9cpublic action\xe2\x80\x9d that happened to be litigated by a private party, but \xe2\x80\x9ca private action brought in the name and interest of [Ms.] Watson.\xe2\x80\x9d 940 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 1057\xe2\x80\x931058. That holding was critical in explaining why Watson\xe2\x80\x99s criminal action was not barred by a plea agreement that bound the government.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moving beyond criminal prosecutions generally, Watson next contends that contempt prosecutions are unique, and thus should be exempt from the general rule. See Brief for Respondent 24. If Watson means to argue that modern criminal contempts are not \xe2\x80\x9ccrimes,\xe2\x80\x9d that view was squarely rejected by this Court in Bloom v. Illinois, 391 U. S. 194 (1968). See id., at 199\xe2\x80\x93200 (holding that a criminal contempt prosecution is a criminal prosecution for the purposes of the Sixth Amendment); see also id., at 201 (\xe2\x80\x9cCriminal contempt is a crime in the ordinary sense\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Providence Journal Co., 485 U. S. 693, 700 (1988) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe fact that the allegedly criminal conduct concerns the violation of a court order instead of common law or a statutory prohibition does not render the prosecution any less an exercise of the sovereign power of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, even if contempt prosecutions might not always count as \xe2\x80\x9ccrimes,\xe2\x80\x9d this one undoubtedly does, as Watson herself concedes. Brief for Respondent 34 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]his case was clearly a criminal contempt proceeding from beginning to end\xe2\x80\x9d). That concession is well taken, given that whether a particular punishment is criminal or civil is \xe2\x80\x9ca matter of statutory construction,\xe2\x80\x9d Hudson, 522 U. S., at 99, and that the relevant provisions here make clear that contempt proceedings like this one are criminal, see D.\xc2\xa0C. Code \xc2\xa716\xe2\x80\x931005(f) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]riminal contempt shall be punished by a fine not exceeding $1,000 or imprisonment for not more than 180 days, or both\xe2\x80\x9d); see also D.\xc2\xa0C. Super. Ct. Domestic Violence Rule 12(d) (labeled \xe2\x80\x9cMotion to adjudicate criminal contempt,\xe2\x80\x9d and describing the violation as \xe2\x80\x9ccriminal contempt\xe2\x80\x9d). As Justice Holmes put it for the Court: \xe2\x80\x9cThese contempts are infractions of the law, visited with punishment as such. If such acts are not criminal, we are in error as to the most fundamental characteristic of crimes as that word has been understood in English speech.\xe2\x80\x9d Gompers v. United States, 233 U. S. 604, 610 (1914).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States bears some responsibility for leading the court below astray. In that court, the Government argued that the criminal contempt prosecution was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a private action brought in the name and interest of [Ms.] Watson, not \xe2\x80\xa6 a public action brought in the name and interest of the United States or any other governmental entity.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 940 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 1056 (quoting Brief for United States in No. 00\xe2\x80\x93FM\xe2\x80\x93925 etc. (D.\xc2\xa0C.)). The court below quoted that precise language in stating its conclusion. See 940 A.\xc2\xa02d, at 1057\xe2\x80\x931058 (same).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before this Court, the Solicitor General has properly abandoned that position, and does not defend the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s decision on this issue. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 12\xe2\x80\x9313, n.\xc2\xa03 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he United States no longer believes the contempt prosecution at issue can be understood as a purely \xe2\x80\x98private action\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). We should do our part and correct the ruling of the court below.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The ultimate issue in this case, of course, is whether the criminal contempt prosecution Watson initiated in January 2000 violated the plea agreement Robertson signed with the USAO in July 1999. The Court of Appeals said \xe2\x80\x9cno,\xe2\x80\x9d based solely on its determination that Watson was exercising private\xe2\x80\x94not sovereign\xe2\x80\x94power. With that determination in hand, the ultimate plea agreement question was straightforward: If Watson was wielding purely private power, a plea agreement that by its terms bound only the \xe2\x80\x9cgov[ernmen]t\xe2\x80\x9d would not bind her.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With a proper view of Watson\xe2\x80\x99s role in this case, however, the plea agreement question becomes significantly more difficult. The Solicitor General argues that the agreement does not bar the contempt prosecution, even if that prosecution is correctly viewed as on behalf of the sovereign. Id., at 29\xe2\x80\x9332. The difficult aspects of that legal issue, however, should not cause us to shy away from answering the fundamental threshold question whether a criminal prosecution may be brought on behalf and in the interest of a private party. Having decided that threshold question in favor of Robertson, I would remand to the court below to consider the plea agreement from the proper starting point.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of all the foregoing, it is worth stressing that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s determination not to decide that question \xe2\x80\x9ccarries with it no implication whatever regarding the Court\xe2\x80\x99s views on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Maryland v. Baltimore Radio Show, Inc., 338 U. S. 912, 919 (1950) (Frankfurter, J., respecting denial of certiorari).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Allegorical depictions of the law frequently show a figure wielding a sword\xe2\x80\x94the sword of justice, to be used to smite those who violate the criminal laws. Indeed, outside our own courthouse you will find a statue of more than 30 tons, Authority of Law, which portrays a male figure with such a sword. According to the sculptor, James Earle Fraser (who also designed the buffalo nickel), the figure sits \xe2\x80\x9cwait[ing] with concentrated attention, holding in his left hand the tablet of laws, backed by the sheathed sword, symbolic of enforcement through law.\xe2\x80\x9d Supreme Court of the United States, Office of the Curator, Contemplation of Justice and Authority of Law Information Sheet 2 (2009) (available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). A basic step in organizing a civilized society is to take that sword out of private hands and turn it over to an organized government, acting on behalf of all the people. Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he \xe2\x80\xa6 power a man has in the state of nature is the power to punish the crimes committed against that law. [But this] he gives up when he joins [a] \xe2\x80\xa6 political society, and incorporates into [a] commonwealth.\xe2\x80\x9d Locke, Second Treatise, \xc2\xa7128, at 64.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The ruling below contravenes that fundamental proposition, and should not be allowed to stand. At the very least, we should do what we decided to do when we granted certiorari, and took the unusual step of rephrasing the question presented: answer it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I respectfully dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s belated determination not to answer that question.
