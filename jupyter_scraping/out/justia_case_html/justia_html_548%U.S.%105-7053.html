DIXON v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 05\xe2\x80\x937053.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 25, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 22, 2006

\n

Petitioner was charged with receiving a firearm while under indictment in violation of 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7922(n) and with making false statements in connection with the acquisition of a firearm in violation of \xc2\xa7922(a)(6). She admitted at trial that she knew she was under indictment when she purchased the firearms and knew that doing so was a crime, but claimed that she was acting under duress because her boyfriend had threatened to harm her and her daughters if she did not buy the guns for him. Bound by Fifth Circuit precedent, the District Court declined her request for a jury instruction placing upon the Government the burden to disprove, beyond a reasonable doubt, her duress defense. Instead, the jury was instructed that petitioner had the burden to establish her defense by a preponderance of the evidence. She was convicted, and the Fifth Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The jury instructions did not run afoul of the Due Process Clause. The crimes of conviction require that petitioner have acted \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7922(a)(6)\xe2\x80\x94which \xe2\x80\x9cmerely requires proof of knowledge of the facts that constitute the offense,\xe2\x80\x9d Bryan v. United States, 524 U. S. 184, 193\xe2\x80\x94or \xe2\x80\x9cwillfully,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7924(a)(1)(D)\xe2\x80\x94which requires acting \xe2\x80\x9cwith knowledge that [the] conduct was unlawful,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Thus, the Government bore the burden of proving beyond a reasonable doubt that petitioner knew that she was making false statements and knew that she was breaking the law when she acquired a firearm while under indictment. It clearly met its burden when petitioner testified to that effect. Petitioner contends that she cannot have formed the necessary mens rea because she did not freely choose to commit the crimes. However, while the duress defense may excuse conduct that would otherwise be punishable, see United States v. Bailey, 444 U. S. 394, 409\xe2\x80\x93410, the existence of duress normally does not controvert any of the elements of the offense itself. The fact that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s crimes are statutory offenses with no counterpart in the common law supports this conclusion. The jury instructions were consistent with the requirement that the Government prove the mental states specified in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7922(a)(6) and 924(a)(1)(D) and did not run afoul of due process by placing the burden on petitioner to establish duress by a preponderance of the evidence. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Modern common law does not require the Government to bear the burden of disproving petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s duress defense beyond a reasonable doubt. The long-established common-law rule, which places the burden of proving that defense on the defendant, was not upset by Davis v. United States, 160 U. S. 469. There, the Court interpreted a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s insanity to controvert the necessary mens rea for a murder committed \xe2\x80\x9cfeloniously, wilfully, and of his malice aforethought,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 474, and required the Government to prove the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sanity beyond a reasonable doubt because the evidence tending to prove insanity also tended to disprove an essential element of the offense. The duress evidence that petitioner adduced at trial does not contradict or tend to disprove any element of her statutory offenses. She is also not helped by the resulting \xe2\x80\x9cDavis rule,\xe2\x80\x9d which was not constitutionally mandated, and which Congress overruled by statute, requiring a defendant to prove insanity by clear and convincing evidence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on Davis also ignores the fact that federal crimes are \xe2\x80\x9csolely creatures of statute,\xe2\x80\x9d Liparota v. United States, 471 U. S. 419, 424, and thus the Court must effectuate the duress defense as Congress \xe2\x80\x9cmay have contemplated\xe2\x80\x9d it in the context of these specific offenses, United States v. Oakland Cannabis Buyers\xe2\x80\x99 Cooperative, 532 U. S. 483, 490, n.\xc2\xa03. The Court can assume that, when passing the relevant 1968 Act, Congress was familiar with the long-established common-law rule and the rule of McKelvey v. United States, 260 U. S. 353, 357\xe2\x80\x94that the one relying on an affirmative defense must set it up and establish it\xe2\x80\x94and would have expected federal courts to apply a similar approach to any affirmative defense or excuse for violating the new law. To accept petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s contrary hypothesis that Davis dramatically upset well-settled law would require an overwhelming consensus among federal courts placing the burden on the Government, but conflict among the Circuits demonstrates that such consensus has never existed. For a similar reason, no weight is due the 1962 Model Penal Code. There is no evidence that Congress endorsed the Code\xe2\x80\x99s views or incorporated them into the 1968 Act. In fact, when Congress amended the Act to add a mens rea requirement, it punished \xe2\x80\x9cwillful\xe2\x80\x9d violations, a mental state not embraced by the Code. Effectuating the affirmative defense as Congress may have contemplated it, the Court presumes that, in the context of the firearms offenses here and the long-established common-law rule, Congress intended petitioner to bear the burden of proving the duress defense by a preponderance of the evidence. Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

413 F. 3d 520, affirmed.

KESHIA CHERIE ASHFORD DIXON, PETITIONER v.\nUNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[June 22, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In January 2003, petitioner Keshia Dixon purchased multiple firearms at two gun shows, during the course of which she provided an incorrect address and falsely stated that she was not under indictment for a felony. As a result of these illegal acts, petitioner was indicted and convicted on one count of receiving a firearm while under indictment in violation of 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7922(n) and eight counts of making false statements in connection with the acquisition of a firearm in violation of \xc2\xa7922(a)(6). At trial, petitioner admitted that she knew she was under indictment when she made the purchases and that she knew doing so was a crime; her defense was that she acted under duress because her boyfriend threatened to kill her or hurt her daughters if she did not buy the guns for him.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner contends that the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s instructions to the jury erroneously required her to prove duress by a preponderance of the evidence instead of requiring the Government to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that she did not act under duress. The Court of Appeals rejected petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s contention, 413 F.\xc2\xa03d 520 (CA5 2005); given contrary treatment of the issue by other federal courts,[Footnote 1] we granted certiorari, 546 U. S. __ (2006).

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At trial, in her request for jury instructions on her defense of duress, petitioner contended that she \xe2\x80\x9cshould have the burden of production, and then that the Government should be required to disprove beyond a reasonable doubt the duress.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 300. Petitioner admitted that this request was contrary to Fifth Circuit precedent, and the trial court, correctly finding itself bound by Circuit precedent, denied petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s request. Ibid. Instead, the judge\xe2\x80\x99s instructions to the jury defined the elements of the duress defense[Footnote 2] and stated that petitioner has \xe2\x80\x9cthe burden of proof to establish the defense of duress by a preponderance of the evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 312.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner argues here, as she did in the District Court and the Court of Appeals, that federal law requires the Government to bear the burden of disproving her defense beyond a reasonable doubt and that the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous instruction on this point entitles her to a new trial. There are two aspects to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument in support of her proposed instruction that merit separate discussion. First, petitioner contends that her defense \xe2\x80\x9ccontroverted the mens rea required for conviction\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore that the Due Process Clause requires the Government to retain the burden of persuasion on that element. Brief for Petitioner 41. Second, petitioner argues that Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s rule is \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to modern common law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The crimes for which petitioner was convicted require that she have acted \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7922(a)(6), or \xe2\x80\x9cwillfully,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7924(a)(1)(D).[Footnote 3] As we have explained, \xe2\x80\x9cunless the text of the statute dictates a different result, the term \xe2\x80\x98knowingly\xe2\x80\x99 merely requires proof of knowledge of the facts that constitute the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Bryan v. United States, 524 U. S. 184, 193 (1998) (footnote omitted). And the term \xe2\x80\x9cwillfully\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7924(a)(1)(D) requires a defendant to have \xe2\x80\x9cacted with knowledge that his conduct was unlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In this case, then, the Government bore the burden of proving beyond a reasonable doubt that petitioner knew she was making false statements in connection with the acquisition of firearms and that she knew she was breaking the law when she acquired a firearm while under indictment. See In\xc2\xa0re Winship, 397 U. S. 358, 364 (1970). Although the Government may have proved these elements in other ways, it clearly met its burden when petitioner testified that she knowingly committed certain acts\xe2\x80\x94she put a false address on the forms she completed to purchase the firearms, falsely claimed that she was the actual buyer of the firearms, and falsely stated that she was not under indictment at the time of the purchase\xe2\x80\x94and when she testified that she knew she was breaking the law when, as an individual under indictment at the time, she purchased a firearm. App. 221\xe2\x80\x93222.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner contends, however, that she cannot have formed the necessary mens rea for these crimes because she did not freely choose to commit the acts in question. But even if we assume that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s will was overborne by the threats made against her and her daughters, she still knew that she was making false statements and knew that she was breaking the law by buying a firearm. The duress defense, like the defense of necessity that we considered in United States v. Bailey, 444 U. S. 394, 409\xe2\x80\x93410 (1980), may excuse conduct that would otherwise be punishable, but the existence of duress normally does not controvert any of the elements of the offense itself.[Footnote 4] As we explained in Bailey, \xe2\x80\x9c[c]riminal liability is normally based upon the concurrence of two factors, \xe2\x80\x98an evil-meaning mind [and] and evil-doing hand \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 402 (quoting Morissette v. United States, 342 U. S. 246, 251 (1952)). Like the defense of necessity, the defense of duress does not negate a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s criminal state of mind when the applicable offense requires a defendant to have acted knowingly or willfully; instead, it allows the defendant to \xe2\x80\x9cavoid liability \xe2\x80\xa6 because coercive conditions or necessity negates a conclusion of guilt even though the necessary mens rea was present.\xe2\x80\x9d Bailey, 444 U. S., at 402.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The fact that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s crimes are statutory offenses that have no counterpart in the common law also supports our conclusion that her duress defense in no way disproves an element of those crimes. We have observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he definition of the elements of a criminal offense is entrusted to the legislature, particularly in the case of federal crimes, which are solely creatures of statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Liparota v. United States, 471 U. S. 419, 424 (1985). Here, consistent with the movement away from the traditional dichotomy of general versus specific intent and toward a more specifically defined hierarchy of culpable mental states, see Bailey, 444 U. S., at 403\xe2\x80\x93404, Congress defined the crimes at issue to punish defendants who act \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7922(a)(6), or \xe2\x80\x9cwillfully,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7924(a)(1)(D). It is these specific mental states, rather than some vague \xe2\x80\x9cevil mind,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 42, or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98criminal\xe2\x80\x99 intent,\xe2\x80\x9d Martin v. Ohio, 480 U. S. 228, 235 (1987), that the Government is required to prove beyond a reasonable doubt, see Patterson v. New York, 432 U. S. 197, 211, n.\xc2\xa012 (1977) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe applicability of the reasonable-doubt standard, however, has always been dependent on how a State defines the offense that is charged in any given case\xe2\x80\x9d). The jury instructions in this case were consistent with this requirement and, as such, did not run afoul of the Due Process Clause when they placed the burden on petitioner to establish the existence of duress by a preponderance of the evidence.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Having found no constitutional basis for placing upon the Government the burden of disproving petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s duress defense beyond a reasonable doubt, we next address petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the modern common law requires the Government to bear that burden. In making this argument, petitioner recognizes that, until the end of the 19th century, common-law courts generally adhered to the rule that \xe2\x80\x9cthe proponent of an issue bears the burden of persuasion on the factual premises for applying the rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Fletcher, Two Kinds of Legal Rules: A Comparative Study of Burden-of-Persuasion Practices in Criminal Cases, 77 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 880, 898 (1967\xe2\x80\x931968). In petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s view, however, two important developments have established a contrary common-law rule that now prevails in federal courts: this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Davis v. United States, 160 U. S. 469 (1895), which placed the burden on the Government to prove a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sanity, and the publication of the Model Penal Code in 1962.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although undisputed in this case, it bears repeating that, at common law, the burden of proving \xe2\x80\x9caffirmative defenses\xe2\x80\x94indeed, \xe2\x80\x98all circumstances \xe2\x80\xa6 of justification, excuse or alleviation\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94rested on the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Patterson, 432 U. S., at 202 (quoting 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries *201); see also Martin v. Ohio, 480 U. S., at 235; Mullaney v. Wilbur, 421 U. S. 684, 693 (1975). This common-law rule accords with the general evidentiary rule that \xe2\x80\x9cthe burdens of producing evidence and of persuasion with regard to any given issue are both generally allocated to the same party.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 J. Strong, McCormick on Evidence \xc2\xa7337, p. 415 (5th ed. 1999). And, in the context of the defense of duress, it accords with the doctrine that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the facts with regard to an issue lie peculiarly in the knowledge of a party, that party has the burden of proving the issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 413. Although she claims that the common-law rule placing the burden on a defendant to prove the existence of duress \xe2\x80\x9cwas the product of flawed reasoning,\xe2\x80\x9d petitioner accepts that this was the general rule, at least until this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Davis. Brief for Petitioner 18. According to petitioner, however, Davis initiated a revolution that overthrew the old common-law rule and established her proposed rule in its place.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Davis itself, however, does not support petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s position. In that case, we reviewed a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction for having committed murder \xe2\x80\x9cfeloniously, wilfully, and of his malice aforethought.\xe2\x80\x9d 160 U. S., at 474. It was undisputed that the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s evidence \xe2\x80\x9cif alone considered, made it the duty of the jury to return a verdict of guilty of the crime charged\xe2\x80\x9d; the defendant, however, adduced evidence at trial tending to show that he did not have the mental capacity to form the requisite intent. Id., at 475. At issue before the Court was the correctness of the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s instruction to the jury that the law \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98presumes every man is sane, and the burden of showing it is not true is upon the party who asserts it.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 476. Under this instruction, \xe2\x80\x9cif the evidence was in equilibrio as to the accused being sane, that is, capable of comprehending the nature and effect of his acts, he was to be treated just as he would be if there were no defence of insanity or if there was an entire absence of proof that he was insane.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 479.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In reversing the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, we found ourselves \xe2\x80\x9cunable to assent to the doctrine that in a prosecution for murder \xe2\x80\xa6 it is the duty of the jury to convict where the evidence is equally balanced on the issue as to the sanity of the accused at the time of the killing.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 484 (emphasis added). Instead, we concluded that this defendant was \xe2\x80\x9centitled to an acquittal of the specific crime charged if upon all the evidence there is reasonable doubt whether he was capable in law of committing the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). Our opinion focused on the \xe2\x80\x9cdefinition of murder,\xe2\x80\x9d explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cit is of the very essence of that heinous crime that it be committed by a person of \xe2\x80\x98sound memory and discretion,\xe2\x80\x99 and with \xe2\x80\x98malice aforethought.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (citations omitted). Reviewing \xe2\x80\x9cthe adjudged cases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9celementary treatises upon criminal law,\xe2\x80\x9d we found that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll admit that the crime of murder necessarily involves the possession by the accused of such mental capacity as will render him criminally responsible for his acts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 485. Thus, when we ultimately found that the burden of proving the accused\xe2\x80\x99s sanity rested on the Government, our holding rested on the conclusion that:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Davis\xe2\x80\x99] guilt cannot be said to have been proved beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x94his will and his acts cannot be held to have joined in perpetrating the murder charged\xe2\x80\x94if the jury, upon all the evidence, have a reasonable doubt whether he was legally capable of committing crime, or (which is the same thing) whether he wilfully, deliberately, unlawfully, and of malice aforethought took the life of the deceased. As the crime of murder involves sufficient capacity to distinguish between right and wrong, the legal interpretation of every verdict of guilty as charged is that the jury believed from all the evidence beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused was guilty, and was therefore responsible, criminally, for his acts. How then upon principle or consistently with humanity can a verdict of guilty be properly returned, if the jury entertain a reasonable doubt as to the existence of a fact which is essential to guilt, namely, the capacity in law of the accused to commit that crime?\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 488.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our opinion in Davis, then, interpreted a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sanity to controvert the necessary mens rea for the crime of murder committed \xe2\x80\x9cfeloniously, wilfully, and of his malice aforethought,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 474, as \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne who takes human life cannot be said to be actuated by malice aforethought, or to have deliberately intended to take life, or to have \xe2\x80\x98a wicked, depraved, and malignant heart,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 unless at the \ntime he had sufficient mind to comprehend the criminality or the right and wrong of such an act,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 485. We required the Government to prove the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sanity beyond a reasonable doubt because the evidence that tended to prove insanity also tended to disprove an essential element of the offense charged. See Davis v. United States, 165 U. S. 373, 378 (1897) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he fact of sanity, as any other essential fact in the case, must be established to the satisfaction of the jury beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Whether or not this reasoning correctly treated insanity as negating the mens rea for murder as defined in the statute at issue, cf., n.\xc2\xa04, supra, it does not help petitioner: The evidence of duress she adduced at trial does not contradict or tend to disprove any element of the statutory offenses that she committed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does the proposition for which Davis has come to stand help petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s cause. Although written more narrowly in the context of a prosecution for the crime of murder, Davis was later interpreted to establish a general \xe2\x80\x9crule for federal prosecutions \xe2\x80\xa6 that an accused is \xe2\x80\x98entitled to an acquittal of the specific crime charged if upon all the evidence there is reasonable doubt whether he was capable in law of committing crime.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Leland v. Oregon, 343 U. S. 790, 797 (1952) (quoting Davis, 160 U. S., at 484); see also Lynch v. Overholser, 369 U. S. 705, 713 (1962) (explaining that the Davis rule applied in all federal courts). After Davis, if a federal defendant introduced sufficient evidence to raise a reasonable doubt as to his sanity, it was sufficient to create a question for the jury on which the Government bore the ultimate burden of persuasion beyond a reasonable doubt. See, e.g., Hall v. United States, 295 F.\xc2\xa02d 26, 28 (CA4 1961); Holloway v. United States, 148 F.\xc2\xa02d 665, 666 (CADC 1945); Post v. United States, 135 F. 1, 10 (CA5 1905).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In apparent recognition of the fact that Davis relied on the heightened mens rea applicable to the particular statute at issue, we held in Leland that this rule was not constitutionally mandated, 343 U. S., at 797, and Congress overruled it by statute in 1984, requiring a defendant to prove his insanity by clear and convincing evidence, 98 Stat. 2057, codified at 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa717(b). Moreover, Congress has treated the defense of insanity differently from that of duress not only by codifying it but by requiring defendants who intend to rely on an insanity defense to provide advance notice to the Government. See Fed. Rule Crim. Proc.\xc2\xa012.2(a). Thus, even if the rule arising from Davis may have once been relevant to an evaluation of other affirmative defenses, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 differential treatment of the insanity defense and its rejection of the Davis rule are inconsistent with petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s invitation to follow Davis\xe2\x80\x99 lead in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on Davis ignores the fact that federal crimes \xe2\x80\x9care solely creatures of statute,\xe2\x80\x9d Liparota, 471 U. S., at 424, and therefore that we are required to effectuate the duress defense as Congress \xe2\x80\x9cmay have contemplated\xe2\x80\x9d it in the context of these specific offenses, United States v. Oakland Cannabis Buyers\xe2\x80\x99 Cooperative, 532 U. S. 483, 491, n.\xc2\xa03 (2001) (internal quotation marks omitted); see also id., at 499 (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment) (explaining that Court was addressing whether the statute at issue foreclosed a necessity defense to specific charges brought under the statute); Bailey, 444 U. S., at 410 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe need not speculate now, however, on the precise contours of whatever defenses of duress or necessity are available against charges brought under [18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.] \xc2\xa7751(a)\xe2\x80\x9d). The offenses at issue in this case were created by statute in 1968, when Congress enacted the Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act (hereinafter Safe Streets Act or Act). See 82 Stat. 197. There is no evidence in the Act\xe2\x80\x99s structure or history that Congress actually considered the question of how the duress defense should work in this context, and there is no suggestion that the offenses at issue are incompatible with a defense of duress.[Footnote 6] Cf. Oakland Cannabis Buyers\xe2\x80\x99 Cooperative, 532 U. S., at 491. Assuming that a defense of duress is available to the statutory crimes at issue,[Footnote 7] then, we must determine what that defense would look like as Congress \xe2\x80\x9cmay have contemplated\xe2\x80\x9d it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As discussed above, the common law long required the defendant to bear the burden of proving the existence of duress. Similarly, even where Congress has enacted an affirmative defense in the proviso of a statute, the \xe2\x80\x9csettled rule in this jurisdiction [is] that an indictment or other pleading \xe2\x80\xa6 need not negative the matter of an exception made by a proviso or other distinct clause \xe2\x80\xa6 and that it is incumbent on one who relies on such an exception to set it up and establish it.\xe2\x80\x9d McKelvey v. United States, 260 U. S. 353, 357 (1922); see also United States v. Dickson, 15 Pet. 141, 165 (1841) (calling this \xe2\x80\x9cthe general rule of law which has always prevailed, and become consecrated almost as a maxim in the interpretation of statutes\xe2\x80\x9d). Even though the Safe Streets Act does not mention the defense of duress, we can safely assume that the 1968 Congress was familiar with both the long-established common-law rule[Footnote 8] and the rule applied in McKelvey and that it would have expected federal courts to apply a similar approach to any affirmative defense that might be asserted as a justification or excuse for violating the new law.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This conclusion is surely more reasonable than petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s hypothesis that Davis dramatically upset a well-settled rule of law. Petitioner cites only one federal case decided before 1968 for the proposition that it has been well established in federal law that the Government bears the burden of disproving duress beyond a reasonable doubt. But that case involved a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s claim that he \xe2\x80\x9clacked the specific intent to defraud required by the statute for the reason that he committed the offense under duress and coercion.\xe2\x80\x9d Johnson v. United States, 291 F.\xc2\xa02d 150, 152 (CA8 1961). Thus, when the Court of Appeals explained that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no burden upon the defendant to prove his defense of coercion,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 155, that statement is best understood in context as a corollary to the by-then-unremarkable proposition that \xe2\x80\x9cthe burden of proof rests upon the Government to prove the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Properly understood, Johnson provides petitioner little help in her uphill struggle to prove that a dramatic shift in the federal common-law rule occurred between Davis and the enactment of the Safe Streets Act in 1968.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, for us to be able to accept petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposition, we would need to find an overwhelming consensus among federal courts that it is the Government\xe2\x80\x99s burden to disprove the existence of duress beyond a reasonable doubt. The existence today of disagreement among the Federal Courts of Appeals on this issue, however\xe2\x80\x94the very disagreement that caused us to grant certiorari in this case, see n.\xc2\xa01, supra\xe2\x80\x94demonstrates that no such consensus has ever existed. See also post, at 6\xe2\x80\x938 (Breyer, J., dissenting) (discussing differences in treatment of the duress defense by the various Courts of Appeals). Also undermining petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument is the fact that, in 1970, the National Commission on Reform of Federal Criminal Laws proposed that a defendant prove the existence of duress by a preponderance of the evidence. See 1 Working Papers 278. Moreover, while there seem to be few, if any, post-Davis, pre-1968 cases placing the burden on a defendant to prove the existence of duress,[Footnote 10] or even discussing the issue in any way, this lack of evidence does not help petitioner. The long-established common-law rule is that the burden of proving duress rests on the defendant. Petitioner hypothesizes that Davis fomented a revolution upsetting this rule. If this were true, one would expect to find cases discussing the matter. But no such cases exist.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is for a similar reason that we give no weight to the publication of the Model Penal Code in 1962. As petitioner notes, the Code would place the burden on the government to disprove the existence of duress beyond a reasonable doubt. See Model Penal Code \xc2\xa71.12, 10A U. L.\xc2\xa0A. 88 (2001) (hereinafter Model Penal Code or Code) (stating that each element of an offense must be proved beyond a reasonable doubt); \xc2\xa71.13(9)(c), at 91 (defining as an element anything that negatives an excuse for the conduct at issue); \xc2\xa72.09, at 131\xe2\x80\x93132 (establishing affirmative defense of duress). Petitioner argues that the Code reflects \xe2\x80\x9cwell established\xe2\x80\x9d federal law as it existed at the time. Brief for Petitioner 25. But, as discussed above, no such consensus existed when Congress passed the Safe Streets Act in 1968. And even if we assume Congress\xe2\x80\x99 familiarity with the Code and the rule it would establish, there is no evidence that Congress endorsed the Code\xe2\x80\x99s views or incorporated them into the Safe Streets Act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In fact, the Act itself provides evidence to the contrary. Despite the Code\xe2\x80\x99s careful delineation of mental states, see Model Penal Code \xc2\xa72.02, 10A U. L.\xc2\xa0A., at 94\xe2\x80\x9395, the Safe Streets Act attached no explicit mens rea requirement to the crime of receiving a firearm while under indictment, \xc2\xa7924(a), 82 Stat. 233 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhoever violates any provision of this chapter \xe2\x80\xa6 shall be fined not more than $5,000 or imprisoned not more than five years, or both\xe2\x80\x9d). And when Congress amended the Act to impose a mens rea requirement, it punished people who \xe2\x80\x9cwillfully\xe2\x80\x9d violate the statute, see 100 Stat. 456, a mental state that has not been embraced by the Code, see Model Penal Code \xc2\xa72.02(2), 10A U. L. A., at 94\xe2\x80\x9395 (defining \xe2\x80\x9cpurposely,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9crecklessly,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cnegligently\xe2\x80\x9d); Explanatory Note, p. 97 (\xe2\x80\x9cThough the term \xe2\x80\x98wilfully\xe2\x80\x99 is not used in the definitions of crimes contained in the Code, its currency and its existence in offenses outside the criminal code suggest the desirability of clarification\xe2\x80\x9d). Had Congress intended to adopt the Code\xe2\x80\x99s structure when it enacted or amended the Safe Streets Act, one would expect the Act\xe2\x80\x99s form and language to adhere much more closely to that used by the Code. It does not, and, for that reason, we cannot rely on the Model Penal Code to provide evidence as to how Congress would have wanted us to effectuate the duress defense in this context.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress can, if it chooses, enact a duress defense that places the burden on the Government to disprove duress beyond a reasonable doubt. In light of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 silence on the issue, however, it is up to the federal courts to effectuate the affirmative defense of duress as Congress \xe2\x80\x9cmay have contemplated\xe2\x80\x9d it in an offense-specific context. Oakland Cannabis Buyers\xe2\x80\x99 Cooperative, 532 U. S., at 491, n.\xc2\xa03. In the context of the firearms offenses at issue\xe2\x80\x94as will usually be the case, given the long-established common-law rule\xe2\x80\x94we presume that Congress intended the petitioner to bear the burden of proving the defense of duress by a preponderance of the evidence. Accordingly, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

It is so ordered.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No one disputes that, subject to constitutional constraints, Congress has the authority to determine the content of a duress defense with respect to federal crimes and to direct whether the burden of proof rests with the defense or the prosecution. The question here is how to proceed when Congress has enacted a criminal statute, the Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968, 82 Stat. 197 et seq. (hereinafter Safe Streets Act), without explicit instructions regarding the duress defense or its burden of proof. See ante, at 10.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When issues of congressional intent with respect to the nature, extent, and definition of federal crimes arise, we assume Congress acted against certain background understandings set forth in judicial decisions in the Anglo-American legal tradition. See United States v. Bailey, 444 U. S. 394, 415, n.\xc2\xa011 (1980). Those decisions, in turn, consult sources such as legal treatises and the American Legal Institute\xe2\x80\x99s Model Penal Code. See, e.g., United States v. Jimenez Recio, 537 U. S. 270, 275\xe2\x80\x93276 (2003); Salinas v. United States, 522 U. S. 52, 64\xe2\x80\x9365 (1997). All of these sources rely upon the insight gained over time as the legal process continues. Absent some contrary indication in the statute, we can assume that Congress would not want to foreclose the courts from consulting these newer sources and considering innovative arguments in resolving issues not confronted in the statute and not within the likely purview of Congress when it enacted the criminal prohibition applicable in the particular case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 While the Court looks to the state of the law at the time the statute was enacted, see ante, at 12, the better reading of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is that isolated authorities or writings do not control unless they were indicative of guiding principles upon which Congress likely would have relied. Otherwise, it seems altogether a fiction to attribute to Congress any intent one way or the other in assigning the burden of proof. It seems unlikely, moreover, that Congress would have wanted the burden of proof for duress to vary from statute to statute depending upon the date of enactment. Consistent with these propositions, the Court looks not only to our precedents and common-law traditions, but also to the treatment of the insanity defense in a 1984 statute and a proposal of the National Commission on Reform of Federal Criminal Laws, even though they both postdated the passage of the Safe Streets Act. See ante, at 10, 13.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As there is no reason to suppose that Congress wanted to depart from the traditional principles for allocating the burden of proof, the proper approach is simply to apply these principles to the context of duress. See, e.g., Schaffer v. Weast, 546 U. S. ___, ___ (2005) (slip op., at 6\xe2\x80\x937) (where the plain text of the statute is \xe2\x80\x9csilent on the allocation of the burden of persuasion,\xe2\x80\x9d we proceed to consider the \xe2\x80\x9cordinary default rule\xe2\x80\x9d and its exceptions). The facts needed to prove or disprove the defense \xe2\x80\x9clie peculiarly in the knowledge of\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant. 2 K. Broun, McCormick on Evidence \xc2\xa7337, p. 475 (6th ed. 2006); see ante, at 6. The claim of duress in most instances depends upon conduct that takes place before the criminal act; and, as the person who allegedly coerced the defendant is often unwilling to come forward and testify, the prosecution may be without any practical means of disproving the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s allegations. There is good reason, then, to maintain the usual rule of placing the burden of production and persuasion together on the party raising the issue. See 2 Broun, supra, \xc2\xa7337; ante, at 6. The analysis may come to a different result, of course, for other defenses.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join the opinion of the Court with the understanding that it does not hold that the allocation of the burden of persuasion on the defense of duress may vary from one federal criminal statute to another.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Duress was an established defense at common law. See 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 30 (1769). When Congress began to enact federal criminal statutes, it presumptively intended for those offenses to be subject to this defense. Moreover, Congress presumptively intended for the burdens of production and persuasion to be placed, as they were at common law, on the defendant. Although Congress is certainly free to alter this pattern and place one or both burdens on the prosecution, either for all or selected federal crimes, Congress has not done so but instead has continued to revise the federal criminal laws and to create new federal crimes without addressing the issue of duress. Under these circumstances, I believe that the burdens remain where they were when Congress began enacting federal criminal statutes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I do not assume that Congress makes a new, implicit judgment about the allocation of these burdens whenever it creates a new federal crime or, for that matter, whenever it substantially revises an existing criminal statute. It is unrealistic to assume that on every such occasion Congress surveys the allocation of the burdens of proof on duress under the existing federal case law and under the law of the States and tacitly adopts whatever the predominant position happens to be at the time. Such a methodology would create serious problems for the district courts and the courts of appeals when they are required to decide where the burden of persuasion should be allocated for federal crimes enacted on different dates. If the allocation differed for different offenses, there might be federal criminal cases in which the trial judge would be forced to instruct the jury that the defendant bears the burden of persuasion on this defense for some of the offenses charged in the indictment and that the prosecution bears the burden on others.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I would also not assume, as Justice Breyer does, see post, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, that Congress has implicitly delegated to the federal courts the task of deciding in the manner of a common-law court where the burden of persuasion should be allocated. The allocation of this burden is a debatable policy question with an important empirical component. In the absence of specific direction from Congress, cf. Fed. Rule Evid. 501, I would not assume that Congress has conferred this authority on the Judiciary.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Courts have long recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cduress\xe2\x80\x9d constitutes a defense to a criminal charge. Historically, that defense \xe2\x80\x9cexcuse[d] criminal conduct\xe2\x80\x9d if (1) a \xe2\x80\x9cthreat of imminent death or serious bodily injury\xe2\x80\x9d led the defendant to commit the crime, (2) the defendant had no reasonable, legal alternative to breaking the law, and (3) the defendant was not responsible for creating the threat. United States v. Bailey, 444 U. S. 394, 409\xe2\x80\x93410 (1980); see also 2 W. LaFave, Substantive Criminal Law \xc2\xa79.7(b), pp. 74\xe2\x80\x9382 (2003) (hereinafter LaFave); ante, at 2, n.\xc2\xa01 (opinion of the Court). The Court decides today in respect to federal crimes that the defense must bear the burden of both producing evidence of duress and persuading the jury. I agree with the majority that the burden of production lies on the defendant, that here the burden of persuasion issue is not constitutional, and that Congress may allocate that burden as it sees fit. But I also believe that, in the absence of any indication of a different congressional intent, the burden of persuading the jury beyond a reasonable doubt should lie where such burdens normally lie in criminal cases, upon the prosecution.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0My disagreement with the majority in part reflects my different view about how we should determine the relevant congressional intent. Where Congress speaks about burdens of proof, we must, of course, follow what it says. But suppose, as is normally the case, that the relevant federal statute is silent? The majority proceeds on the assumption that Congress wished courts to fill the gap by examining judicial practice at the time that Congress enacted the particular criminal statute in question. Ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9314 (opinion of the Court). I would not follow that\napproach.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To believe Congress intended the placement of such burdens to vary from statute to statute and time to time is both unrealistic and risks unnecessary complexity, jury confusion, and unfairness. It is unrealistic because the silence could well mean only that Congress did not specifically consider the \xe2\x80\x9cburden of persuasion\xe2\x80\x9d in respect to a duress defense. It simply did not think about that secondary matter. Had it done so, would Congress have wanted courts to freeze current practice statute-by-statute? Would it have wanted to impose different burden-of-proof requirements where claims of duress are identical, where statutes are similar, where the only relevant difference is the time of enactment? Why? Indeed, individual instances of criminal conduct often violate several statutes. In a trial for those violations, is the judge to instruct the jury to apply different standards of proof to a duress defense depending upon when Congress enacted the particular statute in question? What if in this very case the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s boyfriend had given her drug money and insisted (under threat of death) not only that she use some of the money to buy him a gun, but that she launder the rest? See 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71956 (2000 ed. and Supp. II); see infra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I would assume instead that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 silence typically means that Congress expected the courts to develop burden rules governing affirmative defenses as they have done in the past, by beginning with the common law and taking full account of the subsequent need for that law to evolve through judicial practice informed by reason and experience. See Davis v. United States, 160 U. S. 469 (1895); McNabb v. United States, 318 U. S. 332, 341 (1943); ante, at 11, n.\xc2\xa08 (opinion of the Court) (proposed general revision of the federal criminal code would have instructed courts to determine the contours of affirmative defenses \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98according to the principles of the common law as they may be interpreted in the light of reason and experience\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); 9 J. Wigmore, Evidence \xc2\xa72486, p. 291 (J. Chadbourn rev. ed. 1981) (allocation of the burdens of proof present courts with questions \xe2\x80\x9cof policy and fairness based on experience in the different situations\xe2\x80\x9d). That approach would produce uniform federal practice across different affirmative defenses, as well as across statutes passed at different points in time.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0My approach leads me to conclude that in federal criminal cases, the prosecution should bear the duress defense burden of persuasion. The issue is a close one. In Blackstone\xe2\x80\x99s time the accused bore the burden of proof for all affirmative defenses. See 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries *201; Patterson v. New York, 432 U. S. 197, 201\xe2\x80\x93202 (1977). And 20th-century experts have taken different positions on the matter. The Model Penal Code, for example, recommends placing the burden of persuasion on the prosecution. ALI, Model Penal Code \xc2\xa71.12, p.\xc2\xa016, \xc2\xa71.13(9)(c), p. 18, \xc2\xa72.09, pp. 37\xe2\x80\x9338 (1985). The Brown Commission recommends placing it upon the defendant. National Commission on Reform of Federal Criminal Laws, 1 Working Papers 278 (1970). And the proposed revision of the federal criminal code, agnostically, would have turned the matter over to the courts for decision. S.\xc2\xa01722, 96th Cong., 1st Sess., \xc2\xa7501 (1979). Moreover, there is a practical argument that favors the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position here, namely that defendants should bear the burden of persuasion because defendants often have superior access to the relevant proof.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nonetheless, several factors favor placing the burden on the prosecution. For one thing, in certain respects the question of duress resembles that of mens rea, an issue that is always for the prosecution to prove beyond a reasonable doubt. See In re Winship, 397 U. S. 358, 364 (1970); Martin v. Ohio, 480 U. S. 228, 234 (1987). The questions are not the same. The defendant\xe2\x80\x99s criminal activity here was voluntary; no external principle, such as the wind, propelled her when she acted. The Nicomachean Ethics of Aristotle, p. 54 (R.\xc2\xa0W. Browne transl. 1865). Moreover, her actions were intentional. Whether she wanted to buy the guns or not, and whether she wanted to lie while doing so or not, she decided to do these things and knew that she was doing them. Indeed, her action was willful in the sense that she knew that to do them was to break the law. Ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x934 (opinion of the Court); see also Ratzlaf v. United States, 510 U. S. 135, 136\xe2\x80\x93137 (1994).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nonetheless, where a defendant acts under duress, she lacks any semblance of a meaningful choice. In that sense her choice is not free. As Blackstone wrote, the criminal law punishes \xe2\x80\x9cabuse[s] of th[e] free will\xe2\x80\x9d; hence \xe2\x80\x9cit is highly just and equitable that a man should be excused for those acts, which are done through unavoidable force and compulsion.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 Commentaries *27. And it is in this \xe2\x80\x9cforce and compulsion,\xe2\x80\x9d acting upon the will, that the resemblance to lack of mens rea lies. Cf. Austin, Ifs and Cans, in Proceedings of the British Academy 123\xe2\x80\x93124 (1956) (noting difference between choosing to do something where one has the opportunity and ability to do otherwise and choosing to do something where one lacks any such opportunity or ability). Davis v. United States, supra, allocated the federal insanity defense burden to the Government partly for these reasons. That case, read in light of Leland v. Oregon, 343 U. S. 790, 797 (1952), suggests that, even if insanity does not always show the absence of mens rea, it does show the absence of a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98vicious will.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Davis, supra, at 484 (citing Blackstone; emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For another thing, federal courts (as a matter of statutory construction or supervisory power) have imposed the federal-crime burden of persuasion upon the prosecution in respect to self-defense, insanity, and entrapment, which resemble the duress defense in certain relevant ways. In respect to both duress and self-defense, for example, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s illegal act is voluntary, indeed, intentional; but the circumstances deprive the defendant of any meaningful ability or opportunity to act otherwise, depriving the defendant of a choice that is free. Insanity, as I said, may involve circumstances that resemble, but are not identical to, a lack of mens rea. And entrapment requires the prosecution to prove that the defendant was \xe2\x80\x9cpredisposed\xe2\x80\x9d to commit the crime\xe2\x80\x94a matter sometimes best known to the defendant.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As to self-defense, see First Circuit Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa75.04 (1998); United States v. Thomas, 34 F.\xc2\xa03d 44, 47 (CA2 1994); Government of Virgin Islands v. Smith, 949 F.\xc2\xa02d 677, 680 (CA3 1991); United States v. Harris, Nos. 95\xe2\x80\x935637, 95\xe2\x80\x935638, 1996 U. S. App. LEXIS 22040, *4\xe2\x80\x93*5 (CA4, Aug. 27, 1996); United States v. Branch, 91 F.\xc2\xa03d 699, 714, n.\xc2\xa01 (CA5 1996); Sixth Circuit Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa76.06 (2005); United States v. Jackson, 569 F.\xc2\xa02d 1003, 1008, n.\xc2\xa012 (CA7 1978); United States v. Pierre, 254 F.\xc2\xa03d 872, 876 (CA9 2001); United States v. Corrigan, 548 F.\xc2\xa02d 879, 883 (CA10 1977); United States v. Alvarez, 755 F.\xc2\xa02d 830, 842 (CA11 1985); Bynum v. United States, 408 F.\xc2\xa02d 1207 (CADC 1968); see also Mullaney v. Wilbur, 421 U. S. 684, 702, n.\xc2\xa030 (1975) (noting this as the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98majority rule\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As to insanity, see Davis, 160 U. S., at 486; Leland, supra, at 797 (making clear that Davis determined burden allocations as a matter of federal, but not constitutional, law); but see 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa717(b) (overruling this default rule to place the burden on the defendant by clear and convincing evidence). As to entrapment, see Jacobson v. United States, 503 U. S. 540, 554 (1992) (reversing the judgment affirming the conviction because \xe2\x80\x9cthe prosecution failed, as a matter of law, to adduce evidence to support the jury verdict that petitioner was predisposed, independent of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s acts and beyond a reasonable doubt\xe2\x80\x9d to commit the crime). See also Patterson, 432 U. S., at 202 (noting that Davis \xe2\x80\x9chad wide impact on the practice in the federal courts with respect to the burden of proving various affirmative defenses\xe2\x80\x9d); Patterson, supra, at 231 (Powell, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]ince this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Davis \xe2\x80\xa6 federal prosecutors have borne the burden of persuasion with respect to factors like insanity, self-defense, and malice or provocation, once the defendant has carried this burden of production\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Further, most federal courts, in respect to most federal crimes, have imposed the burden of persuasion in respect to the duress defense upon the Government, following Johnson v. United States, 291 F.\xc2\xa02d 150, 155 (CA8 1961), and authorities such as E. Devitt & C. Blackmar, Federal Jury Practice and Instructions \xc2\xa713.14, p. 293 (2d ed. 1970), and the Federal Judicial Center Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa756 (1988). By the mid-1990\xe2\x80\x99s, seven circuits had squarely placed the burden of persuasion upon the prosecution; one Circuit (the Fifth) placed the burden on the defendant; and four (the Third, Fourth, Eleventh, and District of Columbia) did not, as far as I can tell, have a definitive practice. Compare United States v. Arthurs, 73 F.\xc2\xa03d 444, 448 (CA1 1996); United States v. Mitchell, 725 F.\xc2\xa02d 832, 836 (CA2 1983); United States v. Campbell, 675 F.\xc2\xa02d 815, 821 (CA6 1982); United States v. Talbott, 78 F.\xc2\xa03d 1183, 1186 (CA7 1996) (per curiam); United States v. Campbell, 609 F.\xc2\xa02d 922, 925 (CA8 1979); United States v. Hearst, 563 F.\xc2\xa02d 1331, 1336, and n.\xc2\xa02 (CA9 1977); United States v. Falcon, 766 F.\xc2\xa02d 1469, 1477 (CA10 1985), with United States v. Willis, 38 F.\xc2\xa03d 170, 179 (CA5 1994) (putting the burden on the defendant by a preponderance). Compare also First Circuit Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa75.05 (1998); Sixth Circuit Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa76.05 (1991); Seventh Circuit Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa76.08 (1998); and Eighth Circuit Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73.09, 9.02 (2000), with Fifth Circuit Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa71.36 (2001). Petitioner adds, without contradiction, that the States allocate the burden similarly by a ratio of 2 to 1. Brief for Petitioner 32\xe2\x80\x9334; Brief for United States 38, n.\xc2\xa030.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Beginning in 1991, the matter became more complicated because the Ninth Circuit began to require the defendant to bear the burden of proving duress in certain circumstances. United States v. Dominguez-Mestas, 929 F.\xc2\xa02d 1379, 1382, 1384 (1991) (per curiam). And a few years later the Third, Sixth, and Eleventh Circuits followed suit in cases concerning a closely related justification defense. See United States v. Dodd, 225 F.\xc2\xa03d 340, 347\xe2\x80\x93350 (CA3 2000); United States v. Brown, 367 F.\xc2\xa03d 549, 555\xe2\x80\x93556 (CA6 2004); United States v. Deleveaux, 205 F.\xc2\xa03d 1292, 1298-1300 (CA11 2000); Eleventh Circuit Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa716 (2003). But see Sixth Circuit Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa76.05 (2005) (stating that the burden of proof issue for duress is undecided in that Circuit).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These latter cases, however, put the burden on the defendant only where the criminal statute narrows its mens rea requirement, i.e., the burden is the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s where the statute requires that the defendant act with \xe2\x80\x9cknowledge\xe2\x80\x9d but not, suggest these courts, where the statute requires that the defendant act \xe2\x80\x9cwillfully,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cintentionally,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cvoluntarily.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Dominguez-Mestas, supra, at 1382, 1384; United States v. Meraz-Solomon, 3 F.\xc2\xa03d 298, 300 (CA9 1993); Ninth Circuit Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa76.5, 6.6 (2003); but see United States v. Fei Lin, 139 F.\xc2\xa03d 1303, 1307\xe2\x80\x931308 (CA9 1998). See also Eleventh Circuit Pattern Criminal Jury Instructions \xc2\xa716 (2003); United States v. Diaz, 285 F.\xc2\xa03d 92, 97 (CA1 2002) (indicating that this bifurcated rule might be appropriate, but noting Circuit precedent to the contrary). Similarly, the Tenth Circuit placed the burden of proving duress upon the defendant in \xe2\x80\x9cstrict liability\xe2\x80\x9d cases where mens rea is not an element of the crime at all. United States v. Unser, 165 F.\xc2\xa03d 755, 763\xe2\x80\x93765 (CA10 1999).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The apparent upshot is that four Circuits now place the burden of persuasion on the prosecution across the board; one places the burden on the prosecution if the statute requires mens rea but not otherwise; and four have held or suggested that the burden should be on the prosecution if the statute requires an intentional or willful state of mind, but not if the statute requires only knowledge. While the Circuits are divided, apparently only one (the Fifth) agrees with the position taken by the Court today.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Further, while I concede the logic of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s practical argument\xe2\x80\x94that defendants have superior access to the evidence\xe2\x80\x94I remain uncertain of the argument\xe2\x80\x99s strength. After all, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n every criminal case the defendant has at least an equal familiarity with the facts and in most a greater familiarity with them than the prosecution.\xe2\x80\x9d Tot v. United States, 319 U. S. 463, 469 (1943). And the strict contours of the duress defense, as well as the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s burden of production, already substantially narrow the circumstances under which the defense may be used. A defendant may find it difficult, for example, to show duress where the relevant conduct took place too long before the criminal act. Cf. ante, at 2 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). That is because the defendant must show that he had no alternative to breaking the law. Supra, at 1. And that will be the more difficult to show the more remote the threat. See also LaFave, \xc2\xa7 9.7, p. 77-79 (duress generally requires an \xe2\x80\x9cimmediate\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cimminent\xe2\x80\x9d threat, that the defendant \xe2\x80\x9ctake advantage of a reasonable opportunity to escape,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cterminate his conduct \xe2\x80\x98as soon as the claimed duress . . . had lost its coercive force\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x9d). More important, the need to prove mens rea can easily present precisely the same practical difficulties of proof for the prosecutor. Suppose for example the defendant claims that an old lady told him that the white powder he transported across the border was medicine for her dying son. Cf. United States v. Mares, 441 F.\xc2\xa03d 1152 (CA10 2006). See also Mullaney v. Wilbur, 421 U. S., at 702 (requiring the government to prove an absence of passion in a murder conviction imposes \xe2\x80\x9cno unique hardship on the prosecution\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is particularly difficult to see a practical distinction between this affirmative defense and, say, self-defense. The Government says that the prosecution may \xe2\x80\x9cbe unable to call the witness most likely to have information bearing on the point,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, the defendant. Brief for United States 21. But what is the difference in this respect between the defendant here, who says her boyfriend threatened to kill her, and a battered woman who says that she killed her husband in self-defense, where the husband\xe2\x80\x99s evidence is certainly unavailable? See also Jacobson, 503 U. S. 540 (entrapment; need to prove \xe2\x80\x9cpropensity\xe2\x80\x9d). Regardless, unless the defendant testifies, it could prove difficult to satisfy the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s burden of production; and, of course, once the defendant testifies, cross-examination is possible.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a word, I cannot evaluate the claim of practicality without somewhat more systematic evidence of the existence of a problem, say, in those Circuits that for many years have imposed the burden on the prosecutor. And, of course, if I am wrong about the Government\xe2\x80\x99s practical need (and were my views to prevail), the Government would remain free to ask Congress to reallocate the\nburden.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, there is a virtue in uniformity, in treating the federal statutory burden of persuasion similarly in respect to actus reus, mens rea, mistake, self-defense, entrapment, and duress. The Second Circuit, when imposing the burden of persuasion for duress on the prosecution, wrote that differences in this respect create \xe2\x80\x9ca grave possibility of juror confusion.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Mitchell, 725 F.\xc2\xa02d 832, 836 (1983) (Newman, J., joined by Feinberg, C.\xc2\xa0J., and Friendly, J.). They risk unfairness as well.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons I believe that, in the absence of an indication of congressional intent to the contrary, federal criminal law should place the burden of persuasion in respect to the duress defense upon the prosecution, which, as is now common in respect to many affirmative defenses, it must prove beyond a reasonable doubt. With respect, I dissent.
