UNITED STATES v. TOHONO O\xe2\x80\x99ODHAM NATION

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x93846.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 1, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 26, 2011

\n

Respondent Tohono O\xe2\x80\x99odham Nation (Nation) filed suit in Federal District Court against federal officials who managed tribal assets held in trust by the Federal Government, alleging violations of fiduciary duty and requesting equitable relief. The next day, the Nation filed this action against the United States in the Court of Federal Claims (CFC), alleging almost identical violations and requesting money damages. The CFC case was dismissed under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71500, which bars CFC jurisdiction over a claim if the plaintiff has another suit \xe2\x80\x9cfor or in respect to\xe2\x80\x9d that claim pending against the United States or its agents in another court. The Federal Circuit reversed, finding that the two suits were not for or in respect to the same claim because, although they shared operative facts, they did not seek overlapping relief.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Two suits are for or in respect to the same claim, precluding CFC jurisdiction, if they are based on substantially the same operative facts, regardless of the relief sought in each suit. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Since 1868, Congress has restricted the jurisdiction of the CFC and its predecessors when related actions are pending elsewhere. Keene Corp. v. United States, 508 U. S. 200, 212, held that two suits are for or in respect to the same claim when they are \xe2\x80\x9cbased on substantially the same operative facts \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, at least if there [is] some overlap in the relief requested,\xe2\x80\x9d but it reserved the question whether the jurisdictional bar operates if suits based on the same operative facts do not seek overlapping relief. The rule now codified in \xc2\xa71500 was first enacted to curb duplicate lawsuits by residents of the Confederacy who, in seeking to recover for cotton taken by the Federal Government, sued the Government in the Court of Claims and, at the same time, sued federal officials in other courts, seeking tort relief for the same actions. Section 1500\xe2\x80\x99s robust response to this problem bars CFC jurisdiction not only if the plaintiff sues on an identical claim elsewhere, but also if the other action is related but not identical. The phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin respect to\xe2\x80\x9d does not resolve all doubt as to the bar\xe2\x80\x99s scope, but it suggests a broad prohibition, regardless of whether \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d carries a special or limited meaning. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Keene permits two constructions of \xe2\x80\x9cfor or in respect to\xe2\x80\x9d the same claim, one based on facts alone and the other on factual plus remedial overlap. The former is the more reasonable interpretation in light of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s use of a similar phrase in a way consistent only with factual overlap. The CFC bar applies where the other action is against a \xe2\x80\x9cperson who, \xe2\x80\xa6 when the cause of action \xe2\x80\xa6 arose, was, in respect thereto, acting\xe2\x80\x9d under color of federal law. But at the time that a cause of action arose, the person could not act in respect to the relief requested, for no complaint was yet filed. Although the phrase at issue involves a \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d rather than a cause of action, there is reason to think that both phrases refer to facts alone and not to relief. As Keene explained, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98claim\xe2\x80\x99 is used here synonymously with \xe2\x80\x98cause of action,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 508 U. S., at 210. And if the phrase that uses \xe2\x80\x9ccause of action,\xe2\x80\x9d the more technical term, does not embrace the concept of remedy, it is reasonable to conclude that neither phrase does. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0This reading also makes sense in light of the CFC\xe2\x80\x99s unique remedial powers. Because the CFC is the only judicial forum for most nontort requests for significant monetary relief against the United States and because it has no general power to provide equitable relief against the Government or its officers, a statute aimed at precluding duplicate CFC suits would be unlikely to require remedial overlap. Remedial overlap was even more unusual when \xc2\xa71500\xe2\x80\x99s rule was first enacted in 1868. The Federal Circuit could identify no purpose the statute served in light of that court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent. But courts should not render statutes nugatory through construction. The statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose is clear from its origins\xe2\x80\x94the need to save the Government from redundant litigation\xe2\x80\x94and the conclusion that two suits are for or in respect to the same claim when they share substantially the same operative facts allows the statute to achieve that aim. Concentrating on operative facts is also consistent with the doctrine of claim preclusion, or res judicata. The Nation errs in arguing that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation unjustly forces plaintiffs to choose between partial remedies available in different courts. The Nation could have recovered any losses in the CFC alone. Even if some hardship were shown, this Court \xe2\x80\x9cenjoy[s] no \xe2\x80\x98liberty to add an exception \xe2\x80\xa6 to remove apparent hardship.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Keene, supra, at 217\xe2\x80\x93218. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The substantial overlap in operative facts between the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s District Court and CFC suits precludes jurisdiction in the CFC. Both actions allege that the United States holds the same assets in trust for the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s benefit, and they describe almost identical breaches of fiduciary duty. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

559 F.\xc2\xa03d 1284, reversed and remanded.

UNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. TOHONO O\xe2\x80\x99ODHAM NATION

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

[April 26, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Tohono O\xe2\x80\x99odham Nation is an Indian Tribe with federal recognition. The Nation\xe2\x80\x99s main reservation is in the Sonoran desert of southern Arizona. Counting this and other reservation lands, the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s landholdings are approximately 3 million acres.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Nation brought two actions based on the same alleged violations of fiduciary duty with respect to the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s lands and other assets. One action was filed against federal officials in district court and the other against the United States in the Court of Federal Claims (CFC). The Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held that the CFC suit was not barred by the rule that the CFC lacks jurisdiction over an action \xe2\x80\x9cfor or in respect to\xe2\x80\x9d a claim that is also the subject of an action pending in another court. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71500. The question presented is whether a common factual basis like the one apparent in the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s suits suffices to bar jurisdiction under \xc2\xa71500.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The case turns on the relationship between the two suits the Nation filed. The first suit was filed in the United States District Court for the District of Columbia against federal officials responsible for managing tribal assets held in trust by the Federal Government. The complaint alleged various violations of fiduciary duty with respect to those assets. The Nation claimed, for example, that the officials failed to provide an accurate accounting of trust property; to refrain from self-dealing; or to use reasonable skill in investing trust assets. The complaint requested equitable relief, including an accounting.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The next day the Nation filed the instant action against the United States in the CFC. The CFC complaint described the same trust assets and the same fiduciary duties that were the subject of the District Court complaint. And it alleged almost identical violations of fiduciary duty, for which it requested money damages. The CFC case was dismissed under \xc2\xa71500 for want of jurisdiction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A divided panel of the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit reversed. 559 F.\xc2\xa03d 1284 (2009). Two suits are for or in respect to the same claim, it reasoned, only if they share operative facts and also seek overlapping relief. Finding no overlap in the relief requested, the court held that the two suits at issue were not for or in respect to the same claim.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court granted certiorari. 559 U. S. ___ (2010).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since 1868, Congress has restricted the jurisdiction of the CFC and its predecessors when related actions are pending elsewhere. Section 1500, identical in most respects to the original statute, provides:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe United States Court of Federal Claims shall not have jurisdiction of any claim for or in respect to which the plaintiff or his assignee has pending in any other court any suit or process against the United States or any person who, at the time when the cause of action alleged in such suit or process arose, was, in respect thereto, acting or professing to act, directly or indirectly under the authority of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nThe rule is more straightforward than its complex wording suggests. The CFC has no jurisdiction over a claim if the plaintiff has another suit for or in respect to that claim pending against the United States or its agents.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question to be resolved is what it means for two suits to be \xe2\x80\x9cfor or in respect to\xe2\x80\x9d the same claim. Keene Corp. v. United States, 508 U. S. 200 (1993), provided a partial answer. It held that two suits are for or in respect to the same claim when they are \xe2\x80\x9cbased on substantially the same operative facts \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, at least if there [is] some overlap in the relief requested.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 212. The Keene case did not decide whether the jurisdictional bar also operates if the suits are based on the same operative facts but do not seek overlapping relief. Still, Keene narrows the permissible constructions of \xe2\x80\x9cfor or in respect to\xe2\x80\x9d a claim to one of two interpretations. Either it requires substantial factual and some remedial overlap, or it requires substantial factual overlap without more.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress first enacted the jurisdictional bar now codified in \xc2\xa71500 to curb duplicate lawsuits brought by residents of the Confederacy following the Civil War. The\nso-called \xe2\x80\x9ccotton claimants\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94named for their suits to recover for cotton taken by the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x94sued the United States in the Court of Claims under the Abandoned Property Collection Act, 12 Stat. 820, while at the same time suing federal officials in other courts, seeking relief under tort law for the same alleged actions. See Keene, supra, at 206\xe2\x80\x93207; Schwartz, Section 1500 of the Judicial Code and Duplicate Suits Against the Government and Its Agents, 55 Geo. L.\xc2\xa0J. 573, 574\xe2\x80\x93580 (1967). Although the rule embodied in \xc2\xa71500 originated long ago, Congress reenacted the statute at various times, most recently in 1948. See Act of June 25, 1948, 62 Stat. 942; Keene, 508 U. S., at 206\xe2\x80\x93207.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The text of \xc2\xa71500 reflects a robust response to the problem first presented by the cotton claimants. It bars jurisdiction in the CFC not only if the plaintiff sues on an identical claim elsewhere\xe2\x80\x94a suit \xe2\x80\x9cfor\xe2\x80\x9d the same claim\xe2\x80\x94but also if the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s other action is related although not identical\xe2\x80\x94a suit \xe2\x80\x9cin respect to\xe2\x80\x9d the same claim. The phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin respect to\xe2\x80\x9d does not resolve all doubt as to the scope of the jurisdictional bar, but \xe2\x80\x9cit does make it clear that Congress did not intend the statute to be rendered useless by a narrow concept of identity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 213. It suggests a broad prohibition, regardless of whether \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d carries a special or limited meaning. Cf. United States v. Jones, 131 U. S. 1 (1889) (\xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d in the Little Tucker Act refers only to requests for money).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of the two constructions of \xe2\x80\x9cfor or in respect to\xe2\x80\x9d the same claim that Keene permits\xe2\x80\x94one based on facts alone and the other on factual plus remedial overlap\xe2\x80\x94the former is the more reasonable interpretation in light of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s use of a similar phrase in a way consistent only with factual overlap. The CFC bar applies even where the other action is not against the Government but instead against a \xe2\x80\x9cperson who, at the time when the cause of action alleged in such suit or process arose, was, in respect thereto, acting or professing to act, directly or indirectly under the authority of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d The statute refers to a person who acts under color of federal law in respect to a cause of action at the time it arose. But at that time, the person could not act in respect to the relief requested, for no complaint was yet filed. This use of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin respect to a cause of action\xe2\x80\x9d must refer to operative facts and not whatever remedies an aggrieved party might later request. A person acts under color of federal law in respect to a cause of action by claiming or wielding federal authority in the relevant factual context.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the two phrases are not identical\xe2\x80\x94one is in respect to a claim, the other a cause of action\xe2\x80\x94they are almost so, and there is reason to think that both phrases refer to facts alone and not to relief. As the Keene Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cthe term \xe2\x80\x98claim\xe2\x80\x99 is used here synonymously with \xe2\x80\x98cause of action.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 508 U. S., at 210. And if either of the two phrases were to include both operative facts and a specific remedy, it would be the one that uses the term \xe2\x80\x9ccause of action\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cclaim.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cCause of action\xe2\x80\x9d is the more technical term, while \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d is often used in a commonsense way to mean a right or demand. Here, for the reasons stated in the preceding paragraph, \xe2\x80\x9cin respect to a cause of action\xe2\x80\x9d refers simply to facts without regard to judicial remedies. So, if the phrase with the more technical of the two terms does not embrace the concept of remedy, it is reasonable to conclude that neither phrase does. Even if the terms \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccause of action\xe2\x80\x9d include the request for relief, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cfor or in respect to\xe2\x80\x9d gives the statutory bar a broader scope.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reading the statute to require only factual and not also remedial overlap makes sense in light of the unique remedial powers of the CFC. The CFC is the only judicial forum for most non-tort requests for significant monetary relief against the United States. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71491 (2006 ed. and Supp. III); \xc2\xa71346(a)(2) (2006 ed.). Unlike the district courts, however, the CFC has no general power to provide equitable relief against the Government or its officers. Compare United States v. King, 395 U. S. 1, 2\xe2\x80\x933 (1969), with 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7702; see also United States v. Alire, 6 Wall. 573, 575 (1868) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he only judgments which the Court of Claims are authorized to render against the government \xe2\x80\xa6 are judgments for money found due from the government to the petitioner\xe2\x80\x9d). The distinct jurisdiction of the CFC makes overlapping relief the exception and distinct relief the norm. For that reason, a statute aimed at precluding suits in the CFC that duplicate suits elsewhere would be unlikely to require remedial overlap.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Remedial overlap between CFC actions and those in other courts was even more unusual when \xc2\xa71500\xe2\x80\x99s rule was first enacted in 1868. At that time the CFC had a more limited jurisdiction than it does now, for the Tucker Act\xe2\x80\x99s general waiver of sovereign immunity for non-tort claims for monetary relief had not yet been enacted. See 24 Stat. 505. And while the district courts can today adjudicate suits against the United States for money damages under the Little Tucker Act, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71346(a)(2), and the Federal Tort Claims Act \xc2\xa71346(b), in 1868 the United States could only be sued in the Court of Claims. United States v. Mitchell, 463 U. S. 206, 212\xe2\x80\x93214 (1983); G. Sisk, Litigation with the Federal Government \xc2\xa74.02(a)(1) (4th ed. 2006). Because the kinds of suits and forms of relief available against the United States were few and constrained, remedial overlap between CFC suits and those in other courts was even less common then than now. If the statute were to require remedial as well as factual overlap, it would have had very limited application in 1868 despite its broad language that bars not only identical but also related claims. The rule in \xc2\xa71500 effects a significant jurisdictional limitation, and Congress reenacted it even as changes in the structure of the courts made suits on the same facts more likely to arise. Doing so reaffirmed the force of the bar and thus the commitment to curtailing redundant litigation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The panel of the Court of Appeals could not identify \xe2\x80\x9cany purpose that \xc2\xa71500 serves today,\xe2\x80\x9d 559 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1292, in large part because it was bound by Circuit precedent that left the statute without meaningful force. For example, the panel cited Tecon Engineers, Inc. v. United States, 170 Ct. Cl. 389, 343 F.\xc2\xa02d 943 (1965), which held that \xc2\xa71500 does not prohibit two identical suits from proceeding so long as the action in the CFC, or at that time the Court of Claims, is filed first. The Tecon holding is not presented in this case because the CFC action here was filed after the District Court suit.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Still, the Court of Appeals was wrong to allow its precedent to suppress the statute\xe2\x80\x99s aims. Courts should not render statutes nugatory through construction. In fact the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purpose is clear from its origins with the cotton claimants\xe2\x80\x94the need to save the Government from burdens of redundant litigation\xe2\x80\x94and that purpose is no less significant today. The conclusion that two suits are for or in respect to the same claim when they are based on substantially the same operative facts allows the statute to achieve its aim. Keene, supra, at 206. Developing a factual record is responsible for much of the cost of litigation. Discovery is a conspicuous example, and the preparation and examination of witnesses at trial is another. The form of relief requested matters less, except insofar as it affects what facts parties must prove. An interpretation of \xc2\xa71500 focused on the facts rather than the relief a party seeks preserves the provision as it was meant to function, and it keeps the provision from becoming a mere pleading rule, to be circumvented by carving up a single transaction into overlapping pieces seeking different relief. Cf. Casman v. United States, 135 Ct. Cl. 647 (1956) (CFC had jurisdiction notwithstanding common facts in district court suit because the plaintiff sought different relief in each forum).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Concentrating on operative facts is also consistent with the doctrine of claim preclusion, or res judicata, which bars \xe2\x80\x9crepetitious suits involving the same cause of action\xe2\x80\x9d once \xe2\x80\x9ca court of competent jurisdiction has entered a final judgment on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Commissioner v. Sunnen, 333 U. S. 591, 597 (1948). The jurisdictional bar in \xc2\xa71500 was enacted in part to address the problem that judgments in suits against officers were not preclusive in suits against the United States. Matson Nav. Co. v. United States, 284 U. S. 352, 355\xe2\x80\x93356 (1932). So it is no surprise that the statute would operate in similar fashion. The now-accepted test in preclusion law for determining whether two suits involve the same claim or cause of action depends on factual overlap, barring \xe2\x80\x9cclaims arising from the same transaction.\xe2\x80\x9d Kremer v. Chemical Constr. Corp., 456 U. S. 461, 482, n. 22 (1982); see also Restatement (Second) of Judgments \xc2\xa724 (1980). The transactional test is of course much younger than the rule embodied in \xc2\xa71500, but even in the 19th century it was not uncommon to identify a claim for preclusion purposes based on facts rather than relief. See J. Wells, Res Adjudicata and Stare Decisis \xc2\xa7241, p. 208 (1878) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe true distinction between demands or rights of action which are single and entire, and those which are several and distinct, is, that the former immediately arise out of one and the same act or contract, and the latter out of different acts or contracts\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); 2 H. Black, Law of Judgments \xc2\xa7726, p. 866 (1891) (The test for identity is: \xe2\x80\x9cWould the same evidence support and establish both the present and the former cause of action?\xe2\x80\x9d). Reading \xc2\xa71500 to depend on the underlying facts and not also on the relief requested gives effect to the principles of preclusion law embodied in the statute.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no merit to the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that the interpretation adopted here cannot prevail because it is unjust, forcing plaintiffs to choose between partial remedies available in different courts. The hardship in this case is far from clear. The Nation could have filed in the CFC alone and if successful obtained monetary relief to compensate for any losses caused by the Government\xe2\x80\x99s breach of duty. It also seems likely that Indian tribes in the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s position could go to district court first without losing the chance to later file in the CFC, for Congress has provided in every appropriations Act for the Department of Interior since 1990 that the statute of limitations on Indian trust mismanagement claims shall not run until the affected tribe has been given an appropriate accounting. See, e.g., 123 Stat. 2922; 104 Stat. 1930.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even were some hardship to be shown, considerations of policy divorced from the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and purpose could not override its meaning. Although Congress has permitted claims against the United States for monetary relief in the CFC, that relief is available by grace and not by right. See Beers v. Arkansas, 20 How. 527, 529 (1858) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]s this permission is altogether voluntary on the part of the sovereignty, it follows that it may prescribe the terms and conditions on which it consents to be sued, and the manner in which the suit shall be conducted\xe2\x80\x9d). If indeed the statute leads to incomplete relief, and if plaintiffs like the Nation are dissatisfied, they are free to direct their complaints to Congress. This Court \xe2\x80\x9cenjoy[s] no \xe2\x80\x98liberty to add an exception \xe2\x80\xa6 to remove apparent hardship.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Keene, 508 U. S., at 217\xe2\x80\x93218 (quoting Corona Coal Co. v. United States, 263 U. S. 537, 540 (1924)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Keene reserved the question whether common facts are sufficient to bar a CFC action where a similar case is pending elsewhere. To continue to reserve the question would force the CFC to engage in an unnecessary and complicated remedial inquiry, and it would increase the expense and duration of litigation. The question thus demands an answer, and the answer is yes. Two suits are for or in respect to the same claim, precluding jurisdiction in the CFC, if they are based on substantially the same operative facts, regardless of the relief sought in each suit.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The remaining question is whether the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s two suits have sufficient factual overlap to trigger the jurisdictional bar. The CFC dismissed the action here in part because it concluded that the facts in the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s two suits were, \xe2\x80\x9cfor all practical purposes, identical.\xe2\x80\x9d 79 Fed. Cl. 645, 656 (2007). It was correct to do so.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The two actions both allege that the United States holds the same assets in trust for the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s benefit. They describe almost identical breaches of fiduciary duty\xe2\x80\x94that the United States engaged in self-dealing and imprudent investment, and failed to provide an accurate accounting of the assets held in trust, for example. Indeed, it appears that the Nation could have filed two identical complaints, save the caption and prayer for relief, without changing either suit in any significant respect.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under \xc2\xa71500, the substantial overlap in operative facts between the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s District Court and CFC suits precludes jurisdiction in the CFC. The Court of Appeals erred when it concluded otherwise.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The holding here precludes the CFC from exercising jurisdiction over the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s suit while the District Court case is pending. Should the Nation choose to dismiss the latter action, or upon that action\xe2\x80\x99s completion, the Nation is free to file suit again in the CFC if the statute of limitations is no bar. In the meantime, and in light of the substantial overlap in operative facts between them, the two suits are \xe2\x80\x9cfor or in respect to\xe2\x80\x9d the same claim under \xc2\xa71500, and the CFC case must be dismissed. The contrary judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress enacted the statute currently codified at 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71500 to put an end to parallel litigation seeking duplicative relief against the United States and its agents. Respondent Tohono O\xe2\x80\x99odham Nation seeks in the Court of Federal Claims (CFC) some of the same relief on the same facts as it does in its pending District Court action. Accordingly, applying our decision in Keene Corp. v. United States, 508 U. S. 200 (1993), I agree with the Court that \xc2\xa71500 bars the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s CFC action. Because the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s two actions seek overlapping relief, this case does not present the question that the Court decides today\xe2\x80\x94whether \xc2\xa71500 bars an action in the CFC when the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s actions share a common factual basis but seek different forms of relief. Nonetheless, the Court holds that a common factual basis alone suffices to bar jurisdiction in the CFC. Under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the statute, a plaintiff cannot pursue a claim in the CFC based on the same facts as another pending action, even when Congress has required that plaintiff to file separate actions in two courts to obtain different forms of relief necessary to make the plaintiff whole. I cannot agree that \xc2\xa71500 demands this result.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 1500 bars jurisdiction in the CFC over \xe2\x80\x9cany claim for or in respect to which the plaintiff \xe2\x80\xa6 has pending in any other court any suit or process against the United States\xe2\x80\x9d or any agent of the United States. In Keene, we construed this statute to \xe2\x80\x9cturn on whether the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s other suit was based on substantially the same operative facts as the Court of Claims action, at least if there was some overlap in the relief requested.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 212. It was irrelevant for purposes of \xc2\xa71500, we observed, that the two suits proceeded on different legal theories. Id., at 212. Because the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s actions both sought the same monetary relief, albeit on different theories, we held that the CFC lacked jurisdiction. Id., at 217\xe2\x80\x93218. We thus found \xe2\x80\x9cit unnecessary to consider\xe2\x80\x9d whether \xc2\xa71500 barred a CFC claim that was based on substantially the same operative facts as another suit but that sought different relief. See id., at 212, n.\xc2\xa06, 216.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As construed in Keene, \xc2\xa71500 bars the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s CFC action. As the majority holds, see ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s CFC and District Court actions are based on nearly identical facts. The two actions also seek overlapping relief: Both complaints request money to remedy the same injury\xe2\x80\x94the Government\xe2\x80\x99s alleged breach of its fiduciary duty to maintain accurate accounts of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s assets. The Nation does not dispute that its District Court complaint requests such relief.[Footnote 1] See Brief for Respondent 51 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf \xe2\x80\xa6 the accounting reveals that assets that belong to the Nation do not appear on the books, it may be appropriate to order equitable restitution of those assets\xe2\x80\x9d). The Nation\xe2\x80\x99s CFC complaint is fairly read to do the same. The CFC complaint alleges that the Government has failed \n\xe2\x80\x9cto keep and render clear and accurate accounts.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 66a. It claims that by reason of this and other alleged breaches of fiduciary duty, the Nation \xe2\x80\x9chas been damaged in such amounts as may be proven at trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 67a. And the complaint requests \xe2\x80\x9ca determination that the Defendant is liable to the Nation in damages for the injuries and losses caused as a result of Defendant\xe2\x80\x99s breaches of fiduciary duty\xe2\x80\x9d and a \xe2\x80\x9cdetermination of the amount of damages due the Nation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 72a\xe2\x80\x9373a. Thus, just like the District Court complaint, the CFC complaint requests money to remedy the Government\xe2\x80\x99s alleged failure to keep accurate accounts.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s two complaints are \xe2\x80\x9cbased on substantially the same operative facts\xe2\x80\x9d and there is \xe2\x80\x9cat least \xe2\x80\xa6 some overlap in the relief requested,\xe2\x80\x9d Keene, 508 U. S., at 212, \xc2\xa71500 bars jurisdiction over the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s CFC action.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The case does not present the question, left open in Keene, \xe2\x80\x9cwhether common facts [alone] are sufficient to bar a CFC action where a similar case is pending elsewhere.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. Indeed, for most of the history of this case, the Government did not even argue that common facts were sufficient to preclude CFC jurisdiction; until its petition for rehearing in the Court of Appeals, the Government argued only that Keene required dismissal of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s CFC action because the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s two actions were based on the same facts and sought overlapping relief. Deciding this case on the basis of Keene would have been the \xe2\x80\x9cfar more prudent course than recharacterizing the case in an attempt to reach premature decision on an important question.\xe2\x80\x9d Missouri v. Jenkins, 495 U. S. 33, 80 (1990) (Kennedy, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). Instead, discarding the restraint we exhibited in Keene, the Court unnecessarily chooses to hold that \xc2\xa71500 bars jurisdiction in the CFC whenever a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s CFC action is based on substantially the same facts as a suit pending elsewhere.[Footnote 3] This reading of \xc2\xa71500 is, in my opinion, incorrect.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since the enactment of \xc2\xa71500 in 1868, Congress has expanded the avenues by which persons with legitimate claims against the United States may obtain relief. See ante, at 6. In some circumstances, Congress has chosen to require plaintiffs to file actions in two different courts to obtain complete relief relating to a single set of operative facts. For example, with some exceptions, the CFC has no power to issue equitable relief. See Bowen v. Massachusetts, 487 U. S. 879, 905 (1988); see also 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71491(a). As a result, a plaintiff seeking both money damages and injunctive relief to remedy distinct harms arising from the same set of facts may be forced to file actions in both the CFC and federal district court.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For half a century, the CFC has recognized that \xc2\xa71500 does not preclude jurisdiction in that court when Congress has required a plaintiff to split a claim into two actions to obtain different forms of relief necessary to make the plaintiff whole. In Casman v. United States, 135 Ct. Cl. 647 (1956), a terminated federal employee sought backpay in the Court of Claims and reinstatement to his position in District Court. The plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s two suits arose from the same facts (his termination) but sought \xe2\x80\x9centirely different\xe2\x80\x9d forms of relief within the exclusive jurisdiction of two courts.[Footnote 4] Id., at 650. In light of our previous recognition that the purpose of \xc2\xa71500 \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98was only to require an election between a suit in the Court of Claims and one brought in another court,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 649 (quoting Matson Nav. Co. v. United States, 284 U. S. 352, 355\xe2\x80\x93356 (1932)), the Court of Claims held that \xc2\xa71500 was inapplicable when a \xe2\x80\x9cplaintiff has no right to elect between two courts,\xe2\x80\x9d 135 Ct. Cl., at 650. To hold otherwise, the court acknowledged, \xe2\x80\x9cwould be to say to plaintiff, \xe2\x80\x98If you want your job back you must forget your back pay\xe2\x80\x99; conversely, \xe2\x80\x98If you want your back pay, you cannot have your job back.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also Loveladies Harbor, Inc. v. United States, 27 F.\xc2\xa03d 1545, 1551 (CA Fed. 1994) (en banc) (reaffirming Casman\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry into the form of relief sought).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By reserving the question \xe2\x80\x9cwhether two actions based on the same operative facts, but seeking completely different relief, would implicate \xc2\xa71500,\xe2\x80\x9d our decision in Keene expressly preserved the Casman holding. 508 U. S., at 212, n.\xc2\xa06. The consequence of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is clear: The Casman rule is no longer good law. Under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa71500, because Casman\xe2\x80\x99s two suits were based on common facts, \xc2\xa71500 barred jurisdiction in the CFC over his backpay claim even though he could not have obtained backpay in his District Court action.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jurisdictional scheme governing actions against the United States often requires other plaintiffs to file two actions in different courts to obtain complete relief in connection with one set of facts. As just one example, an action seeking injunctive relief to set aside agency action must proceed in district court, but a claim that the same agency action constitutes a taking of property requiring just compensation must proceed in the CFC. See, e.g., Alaska v. United States, 32 Fed. Cl. 689 (1995). After today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, \xc2\xa71500 may well prevent a plaintiff from pursuing a takings claim in the CFC if an action to set aside the agency action is pending in district court. This type of plaintiff may face a choice between equally unattractive options: forgo injunctive relief in the district court to preserve her claim for monetary relief in the CFC, or pursue injunctive relief and hope that the statute of limitations on her takings claim, see 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72501, does not expire before the district court action is resolved.[Footnote 5]

\n

B

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The text, purpose, and history of \xc2\xa71500 provide strong reason to believe that Congress did not intend for \xc2\xa71500 \nto put plaintiffs to a choice between two nonduplicative remedies that Congress has made available exclusively in two forums. The statute bars jurisdiction in the CFC over a \xe2\x80\x9cclaim for or in respect to which\xe2\x80\x9d a plaintiff has a suit or process pending elsewhere. When Congress first enacted \xc2\xa71500\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor, the statute establishing the jurisdiction of the Court of Claims used the term \xe2\x80\x9cclaims\xe2\x80\x9d to refer to demands for money damages. See Act of Mar. 3, 1863, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x933, 12 Stat. 765; see United States v. Jones, 131 U. S. 1, 17 (1889) (noting that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s provisions \xe2\x80\x9cwere inconsistent with the enforcement of any claims under the law except claims for money\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 6] Congress thus would have understood the term \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71500 to describe the particular relief sought in the Court of Claims. Cf. Commissioner v. Keystone Consol. Industries, Inc., 508 U. S. 152, 159 (1993).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Determining the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d is only part of the inquiry, however. The question remains what constitutes a suit or process \xe2\x80\x9cfor or in respect to\xe2\x80\x9d a CFC claim. The purpose and history of the statute elucidate the meaning of this ambiguous phrase. As the majority explains, Congress enacted the statute to prevent \xe2\x80\x9cduplicative lawsuits\xe2\x80\x9d brought by the so-called \xe2\x80\x9ccotton claimants\xe2\x80\x9d in the aftermath of the Civil War. Keene, 508 U. S., at 206; see ante, at 3. The cotton claimants sought monetary compensation for seized cotton in the Court of Claims pursuant to the Abandoned Property Collection Act, 12 Stat. 820. Because they had difficulty satisfying the statutory requirement that, to obtain compensation, they must not have given aid or comfort to participants in the rebellion, see \xc2\xa73 of the Act, they also sought relief\xe2\x80\x94either in the form of money damages or actual cotton\xe2\x80\x94in separate lawsuits against federal officials on tort theories such as conversion. \xe2\x80\x9cIt was these duplicative lawsuits that induced Congress\xe2\x80\x9d to enact \xc2\xa71500\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor. Keene, 508 U. S., at 206.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This historical backdrop sheds light on what Congress would have understood to be a suit or process \xe2\x80\x9cfor or in respect to\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d in the Court of Claims. Congress undoubtedly intended to preclude a claim for money in the Court of Claims when the plaintiff was pursuing a suit \xe2\x80\x9cfor\xe2\x80\x9d the same money in district court. Because, however, some cotton claimants sought return of the cotton itself in district court, it was also necessary to preclude jurisdiction in the Court of Claims when the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s other action was \xe2\x80\x9cin respect to\xe2\x80\x9d that demand for money\xe2\x80\x94i.e., when the plaintiff was seeking duplicative relief. Had the courts awarded such plaintiffs both the cotton itself and money damages, the plaintiffs would have obtained twice what they deserved. In this way, Congress eschewed \xe2\x80\x9ca narrow concept of identity\xe2\x80\x9d that would have permitted plaintiffs to pursue and obtain duplicative relief to remedy the very same harm. Id., at 213.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The legislative history confirms Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to preclude requests for duplicative relief. The statute\xe2\x80\x99s sponsor explained that the purpose of the statute was \xe2\x80\x9cto put to their election that large class of persons having cotton claims[,] \xe2\x80\xa6 who are here at the same time endeavoring to prosecute their claims, and have filed them in the Court of Claims, so that after they put the Government to the expense of beating them once in a court of law they can turn around and try the whole question in the Court of Claims.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 7] Cong. Globe, 40th Cong., 2d Sess., 2769 (1868) (statement of Sen. Edmunds); see also Matson Nav. Co., 284 U. S., at 355\xe2\x80\x93356. Congress thus appears to have had in mind cases in which \xe2\x80\x9cthe whole question\xe2\x80\x9d could be tried in the Court of Claims. The statute\xe2\x80\x99s history does not suggest that Congress intended to require an election between two nonduplicative forms of relief available exclusively in two different courts. In such a case, \xe2\x80\x9cthe whole question\xe2\x80\x9d could not be tried in either court.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0None of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for its contrary construction of the statute is convincing. First, the majority reasons that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cclaim for or in respect to\xe2\x80\x9d must refer only to factual overlap because the statute uses the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccause of action \xe2\x80\xa6 in respect thereto\xe2\x80\x9d (which the majority paraphrases as \xe2\x80\x9cin respect to a cause of action\xe2\x80\x9d) in a way that is \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent only with factual overlap.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4. This point rests on a misreading of the statutory text. The statute asks whether a plaintiff has pending a \xe2\x80\x9csuit or process\xe2\x80\x9d for or in respect to the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s CFC claim\xe2\x80\x94not whether it has pending a \xe2\x80\x9ccause of action\xe2\x80\x9d for or in respect to that claim.[Footnote 8] Even if the term \xe2\x80\x9ccause of action\xe2\x80\x9d refers only to operative facts\xe2\x80\x94such that the inquiry whether a person was acting under color of federal law in respect to \na cause of action is purely factual in nature\xe2\x80\x94a \xe2\x80\x9csuit or process\xe2\x80\x9d will inevitably include a request for relief.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the majority states that, \xe2\x80\x9cin light of the unique remedial powers of the CFC,\xe2\x80\x9d requiring remedial overlap would make no sense because it would result in a \xe2\x80\x9cvery limited application\xe2\x80\x9d of the statute. Ante, at 5, 6. Here, the majority overlooks the nearly 150-year history of the statute. It was the cotton claimants\xe2\x80\x99 parallel requests for duplicative relief that prompted passage of \xc2\xa71500 in the first place. Since then, litigants have continued to seek duplicative relief against the Government in two courts, as Keene and this very case illustrate. See 508 U. S., at 204\xe2\x80\x93205 (seeking tort damages in the District Court and compensation on a takings theory in the CFC); supra, at 1\xe2\x80\x933 (seeking restitution and disgorgement in the District Court and money damages in the CFC); see also, e.g., Ex parte Skinner & Eddy Corp., 265 U. S. 86, 91\xe2\x80\x9392 (1924) (seeking money damages against the United States in the Court of Claims and against a federal entity in state court); Corona Coal Co. v. United States, 263 U. S. 537, 539 (1924) (seeking money damages against the United States in the Court of Claims and against a federal agent in District Court); British American Tobacco Co. v. United States, 89 Ct. Cl. 438, 439\xe2\x80\x93440 (1939) (per curiam) (seeking tort damages in the District Court and contract damages in the Court of Claims). As these cases make clear, interpreting \xc2\xa71500 to prohibit requests for duplicative relief hardly renders the statute of limited application.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, the majority suggests that its construction of \xc2\xa71500 is necessary to achieve the statute\xe2\x80\x99s aim of \xe2\x80\x9csav[ing] the Government from burdens of redundant litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. Parallel actions seeking the same or duplicative relief, or different forms of relief that are available entirely in one court, are redundant; actions seeking different forms of relief that Congress has made available exclusively in different courts are not. To the extent the majority is concerned about the burdens of parallel discovery, federal courts have ample tools at their disposal, such as stays, to prevent such burdens. See Schwartz, Section 1500 of the Judicial Code and Duplicate Suits Against the Government and Its Agents, 55 Geo. L.\xc2\xa0J. 573, 599 (1967).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the majority contends that focusing on operative facts is consistent with the principles of claim preclusion embodied in the statute. Claim preclusion ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9cbar[s] claims arising from the same transaction.\xe2\x80\x9d Kremer v. Chemical Constr. Corp., 456 U. S. 461, 482, n.\xc2\xa022 (1982). There is, however, an exception to this rule when a plaintiff was unable to obtain a certain remedy in the earlier action. See Restatement (Second) of Judgments \xc2\xa726(1)(c) (1980) (claim preclusion does not apply where \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he plaintiff was unable to rely on a certain theory of the case or to seek a certain remedy or form of relief in the first action because of the limitations on the subject matter jurisdiction of the courts\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Marrese v. American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons, 470 U. S. 373, 382 (1985); 18 C. Wright, A. Miller, & E. Cooper, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa74412, p.\xc2\xa0276 (2d ed. 2002). This principle has long informed claim preclusion law. See, e.g., Restatement of Judgments \xc2\xa762, Comment k (1942) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]here a plaintiff brings an action in a State in which the courts have jurisdiction only with reference to one portion of his cause of action, he is not barred from maintaining an action in a proper court for the other portion\xe2\x80\x9d); 2 H. Black, Law of Judgments \xc2\xa7618, p.\xc2\xa0744 (1891) (\xe2\x80\x9cA judgment is not conclusive of any matter which, from the nature of the case, the form of action, or the character of the pleadings, could not have been adjudicated in the former suit\xe2\x80\x9d). For these reasons, preclusion doctrine actually undermines the majority\xe2\x80\x99s position.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the majority offers no coherent justification for its conclusion that Congress intended to preclude jurisdiction in the CFC whenever a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim in that court is based on substantially the same facts as a suit pending elsewhere without reference to the relief sought.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even before today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, \xc2\xa71500 had been described as \xe2\x80\x9canachronistic,\xe2\x80\x9d Keene, 508 U. S., at 217, \xe2\x80\x9charsh,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 222 (Stevens, J., dissenting), and \xe2\x80\x9carbitrar[y],\xe2\x80\x9d 79 Fed. Cl. 645, 659, n.\xc2\xa016 (2007). Judges and commentators have long called for congressional attention to the statute. See, e.g., Keene, 508 U. S., at 222 (Stevens, J., dissenting); Schwartz, supra, at 601. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision\xe2\x80\x94which unnecessarily considers and repudiates the Casman rule\xe2\x80\x94renders such attention all the more pressing. Under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa71500, plaintiffs whom Congress has forced to file parallel actions in the CFC and a district court to obtain complete relief must now choose either to forgo relief in the district court or to file first in the district court and risk the expiration of the statute of limitations on their claims in the CFC. I cannot agree that Congress intended, or intends, for \xc2\xa71500 to produce this result. For these reasons, I respectfully concur only in the judgment.

S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71491(a)(2) and (b)(2)), cert. pending, No. 09\xe2\x80\x931521.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority next suggests that Congress has tolled the statute of limitations governing the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s CFC claims. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. But the cited statute only applies to claims \xe2\x80\x9cconcerning losses to or mismanagement of trust funds.\xe2\x80\x9d 123 Stat. 2922. It does not appear to toll the statute of limitations for claims concerning assets other than funds, such as tangible assets. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 67a\xe2\x80\x9369a (seeking damages for the Government\xe2\x80\x99s mismanagement of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s mineral estates). Expiration of the 6-year statute of limitations governing claims in the CFC is a very real prospect in this and other cases; the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s District Court action has been pending for more than four years.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the majority notes, see ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937, the validity of the Court of Claims\xe2\x80\x99 holding in Tecon Engineers, Inc. v. United States, 170 Ct. Cl. 389, 343 F.\xc2\xa02d 943 (1965), is not presented in this case. This Court has never considered that holding. Accordingly, I do not consider whether the Nation could have avoided application of \xc2\xa71500 altogether by filing its CFC action first.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s immoderate reading of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71500 and would affirm the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0According to the Court, the Court of Federal Claims (CFC) lacks subject-matter jurisdiction over the Tohono O\xe2\x80\x99odham Nation\xe2\x80\x99s (Nation) claim because the Tribe was simultaneously pursuing in the D.\xc2\xa0C. District Court an action with \xe2\x80\x9ca common factual basis.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1. It matters not, the Court holds, that to gain complete relief, the Nation had to launch two suits, for neither of the two courts whose jurisdiction the Tribe invoked could alone provide full redress. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court concludes that \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccause of action,\xe2\x80\x9d terms the Court considers synonymous as used in \xc2\xa71500,* see ante, at 5, refers to \xe2\x80\x9coperative facts,\xe2\x80\x9d and not to the remedies a plaintiff seeks. See ante, at 4. Section 1500 speaks of \xe2\x80\x9cthe time when the cause of action \xe2\x80\xa6 arose,\xe2\x80\x9d a time antedating the commencement of suit. The Court infers, therefore, that a \xe2\x80\x9cclaim\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccause of action\xe2\x80\x9d is discrete from a pleading\xe2\x80\x99s request for relief. See ante, at 4. In fact, however, entitlement to relief is essential to the existence of a claim or cause of action, which arises when a person suffers a harm capable of judicial redress. See 2 J. Story, Equity Jurisprudence \xc2\xa71521a, p. 741 (8th ed. 1861) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he cause of action \xe2\x80\xa6 arises when \xe2\x80\xa6 the party has a right to apply to a court \xe2\x80\xa6 for relief.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A plaintiff may not, \xc2\xa71500 instructs, petition both the CFC and a district court, invoking in each a distinct legal theory appropriate to the forum, but seeking redress for a single injury. When Congress bars a plaintiff from obtaining complete relief in one suit, however, and does not call for an election of remedies, Congress is most sensibly read to have comprehended that the operative facts give rise to two discrete claims. Casman v. United States, 135 Ct. Cl. 647 (1956), as Justice Sotomayor spells out, see ante, at 5, is the paradigm case. There, a discharged federal employee, complaining of wrongful termination, sought reinstatement in a district-court action and backpay in the Court of Claims. Section 1500 does not stand in the way, the Court of Claims held in Casman, when the plaintiff suffered two distinct injuries, for which she seeks discrete forms of relief within the exclusive competence of different courts. See 135 Ct. Cl., at 649\xe2\x80\x93650 (claim for backpay \xe2\x80\x9centirely different\xe2\x80\x9d from claim for reinstatement). The Federal Circuit, in my view, rightly adhered to Casman in Loveladies Harbor, Inc. v. United States, 27 F.\xc2\xa03d 1545 (1994) (en banc), and rightly did so in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While I agree with much of Justice Sotomayor\xe2\x80\x99s opinion concurring in the judgment, I do not agree with her conclusion that \xc2\xa71500 bars the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s CFC action. Justice Sotomayor joins the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment (although not the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning) because the \xe2\x80\x9cTohono O\xe2\x80\x99odham Nation seeks in the [CFC] \xe2\x80\xa6 some of the same relief on the same facts as it does in its pending District Court action.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1 (emphasis added). But to the extent that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Nation\xe2\x80\x99s two actions seek overlapping relief,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., a disposition less harsh would be in order. Ordinarily, when a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s allegations and demands for relief are excessive, her complaint is not instantly dismissed on that account. Instead, she may seek leave to trim her pleading, permission a court \xe2\x80\x9cshould freely give \xe2\x80\xa6 when justice so requires.\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 15(a)(2) (CFC 2010). Cf. Rule 54(c) (CFC 2010) (judgment, other than default, need not conform to demand for relief, but \xe2\x80\x9cshould grant the relief to which each party is entitled\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As Justice Sotomayor and the Nation recognize, to avoid both duplication and the running of the statute of limitations, the CFC suit could be stayed while the companion District Court action proceeds. See ante, at 11; Brief for Respondent 35. That is a common practice when a prior action is pending. See Pennsylvania R. Co. v. United States, 363 U. S. 202, 204\xe2\x80\x93206 (1960) (instructing Court of Claims to stay pending proceedings to enable litigant to obtain District Court review of relevant agency order); Creppel v. United States, 41 F.\xc2\xa03d 627, 633 (CA Fed. 1994) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Court of Federal Claims may stay a takings action pending completion of a related action in a district court.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Why is this Court not positioned to direct the CFC to disregard requests for relief simultaneously sought in a district-court action, or at least to recognize that an amended CFC complaint could save the case? I see no impediment to either course, in \xc2\xa71500 or any other law or rule.

*\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Cause of action,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the Court simultaneously states, \xe2\x80\x9cis the more technical term.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. If \xe2\x80\x9cmore technical\xe2\x80\x9d means more precise, clear or certain, the Court is incorrect. See United States v. Memphis Cotton Oil Co., 288 U. S. 62, 67\xe2\x80\x9368 (1933) (\xe2\x80\x9cA \xe2\x80\x98cause of action\xe2\x80\x99 may mean one thing for one purpose and something different for another.\xe2\x80\x9d). In its discourse on the term, the Court has fallen into an old error; the drafters of the Federal Rules endeavored to \xe2\x80\x9celiminate the unfortunate rigidity and confusion surrounding the words \xe2\x80\x98cause of action.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 5 C. Wright & A. Miller, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa71216, p. 207 (3d ed. 2004). Today\xe2\x80\x99s invocation of a supposed particular or exact meaning for the phrase risks reviving that confusion.
