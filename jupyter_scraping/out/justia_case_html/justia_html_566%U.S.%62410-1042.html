\n\t\t\t\tFREEMAN et\xc2\xa0al. v. QUICKEN LOANS, INC.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x931042.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 21, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 24, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA), provides, as relevant here, that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o person shall give and no person shall accept any portion, split, or percentage of any charge made or received for the rendering of a real estate settlement service .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. other than for services actually performed.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n12 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72607(b). Petitioners, three couples who obtained mortgage loans from respondent, filed separate state-court actions, alleging that respondent had violated \xc2\xa72607(b) by charging them fees for which no services were provided in return. After the cases were removed to federal court and consolidated, respondent sought summary judgment, arguing that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims were not cognizable under \xc2\xa72607(b) because the allegedly unearned fees were not split with another party. The District Court agreed; and because petitioners had not alleged any splitting of fees, it granted respondent summary judgment. The Fifth Circuit affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0In order to establish a violation of \xc2\xa72607(b), a plaintiff must demonstrate that a charge for settlement services was divided between two or more persons. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Section 2607(b) unambiguously covers only a settlement-service provider\xe2\x80\x99s splitting of a fee with one or more other persons; it cannot be understood to reach a single provider\xe2\x80\x99s retention of an unearned fee. Pp. 3\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0Section 2607(b) clearly describes two distinct exchanges. First, a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cmade\xe2\x80\x9d to or \xe2\x80\x9creceived\xe2\x80\x9d from a consumer by a settlement-service provider. That provider then \xe2\x80\x9cgive[s],\xe2\x80\x9d and another person \xe2\x80\x9caccept[s],\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cportion, split, or percentage\xe2\x80\x9d of the charge. Congress\xe2\x80\x99s use of different sets of verbs, with distinct tenses, to distinguish between the consumer-provider transaction and the fee-sharing one would be pointless if, as petitioners contend, the two transactions could be collapsed into one. Their reading\xe2\x80\x94that a settlement-service provider can \xe2\x80\x9cmake\xe2\x80\x9d a charge and then \xe2\x80\x9caccept\xe2\x80\x9d the portion of the charge consisting of 100 percent\xe2\x80\x94does not avoid collapsing the sequential relationship of the two stages and would destroy the tandem character of activities that the text envisions at stage two (i.e., a giving and accepting). And if the consumer were the person who \xe2\x80\x9cgive[s]\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cportion, split, or percentage\xe2\x80\x9d of the charge to the provider who \xe2\x80\x9caccepts\xe2\x80\x9d it, consumers would become lawbreakers themselves. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0The normal usage of the terms \xe2\x80\x9cportion,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9csplit,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpercentage\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94which, when referring to a portion or percentage of a whole, usually mean less than 100 percent\xe2\x80\x94reinforces the conclusion that \xc2\xa72607(b) does not apply where a settlement-service provider retains the entirety of a fee received from a consumer. The meaning is also confirmed by the \xe2\x80\x9ccommonsense canon of noscitur a sociis, which counsels that a word is given more precise content by the neighboring words with which it is associated.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Williams, \n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 285\n. This connation is not undermined by the canon against surplusage. \xe2\x80\x9cPortion,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9csplit,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpercentage\xe2\x80\x9d may all mean the same thing, but the canon merely favors that interpretation which avoids surplusage, see Microsoft Corp. v. i4i Ltd. Partnership, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, and petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation no more achieves that end than the Court\xe2\x80\x99s does. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arguments in favor of their contrary interpretation are unpersuasive. Section 2607(b), as interpreted here, is not rendered surplusage by \xc2\xa72607(a)\xe2\x80\x99s express prohibition of kickbacks, for each subsection reaches conduct that the other does not. RESPA\xe2\x80\x99s gen- eral purpose\xe2\x80\x94to protect consumers from \xe2\x80\x9ccertain abusive practices,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72601(a)\xe2\x80\x94also provides no warrant for expanding \xc2\xa72607(b)\xe2\x80\x99s pro- hibition beyond the field to which it is unambiguously limited: the splitting of fees paid for settlement services. And giving \xc2\xa72607(b) its natural meaning would not lead to absurd results. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t626 F.\xc2\xa03d 799, affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tTAMMY FORET FREEMAN, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. QUICKEN LOANS, INC.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA provision of the Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA), codified at \n\n12 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72607(b), prohibits giving and accepting \xe2\x80\x9cany portion, split, or percentage of any charge made or received for the rendering of a real estate settlement service .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. other than for services actually performed.\xe2\x80\x9d We consider whether, to establish a vio-lation of \xc2\xa72607(b),\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t a plaintiff must demonstrate that a charge was divided between two or more persons.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEnacted in 1974, RESPA regulates the market for real estate \xe2\x80\x9csettlement services,\xe2\x80\x9d a term defined by statute to include \xe2\x80\x9cany service provided in connection with a real estate settlement,\xe2\x80\x9d such as \xe2\x80\x9ctitle searches, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. title insurance, services rendered by an attorney, the preparation of documents, property surveys, the rendering of credit reports or appraisals, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. services rendered by a real estate agent or broker, the origination of a federally re-lated mortgage loan[\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , and the handling of the processing, and closing or settlement.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72602(3). Among RESPA\xe2\x80\x99s consumer-protection provisions is \xc2\xa72607, which directly furthers Congress\xe2\x80\x99s stated goal of \xe2\x80\x9celiminat[ing] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. kickbacks or referral fees that tend to increase un-necessarily the costs of certain settlement services,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72601(b)(2). Section 2607(a) provides:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cNo person shall give and no person shall accept any fee, kickback, or thing of value pursuant to any agreement or understanding, oral or otherwise, that business incident to or a part of a real estate settlement service involving a federally related mortgage loan shall be referred to any person.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe neighboring provision, subsection (b), adds the following:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cNo person shall give and no person shall accept any portion, split, or percentage of any charge made or received for the rendering of a real estate settlement service in connection with a transaction involving a federally related mortgage loan other than for services actually performed.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThese substantive provisions are enforceable through, in-ter alia, actions for damages brought by consumers of settlement services against \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny person or persons who violate the prohibitions or limitations\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa72607, with recovery set at an amount equal to three times the charge paid by the plaintiff for the settlement service at issue. \xc2\xa72607(d)(2).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners in this case are three married couples who obtained mortgage loans from respondent Quicken Loans, Inc. In 2008, they filed separate actions in Louisiana state court, alleging, as pertinent here, that respondent had violated \xc2\xa72607(b) by charging them fees for which no services were provided. In particular, the Freemans and the Bennetts allege that they were charged loan discount fees of $980 and $1,100, respectively, but that respondent did not give them lower interest rates in return. The Smiths\xe2\x80\x99 allegations focus on a $575 loan \xe2\x80\x9cprocessing fee\xe2\x80\x9d and a \xe2\x80\x9cloan origination\xe2\x80\x9d fee of more than $5,100.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent removed petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 lawsuits to federal court, where the cases were consolidated. Respondent thereafter moved for summary judgment on the ground that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims are not cognizable under \xc2\xa72607(b) because the allegedly unearned fees were not split with another party. The District Court agreed; and because petitioners did not allege any splitting of fees it granted summary judgment in favor of respondent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA divided panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit affirmed. 626 F.\xc2\xa03d 799 (2010). We granted certiorari. 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question in this case pertains to the scope of \xc2\xa72607(b), which as we have said provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o person shall give and no person shall accept any portion, split, or percentage of any charge made or received for the rendering of a real estate settlement service .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. other than for services actually performed.\xe2\x80\x9d The dispute between the parties boils down to whether this provision prohibits the collection of an unearned charge by a single settlement-service provider\xe2\x80\x94what we might call an undivided unearned fee\xe2\x80\x94or whether it covers only transactions in which a provider shares a part of a settlement-service charge with one or more other persons who did nothing to earn that part.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that the former interpretation should prevail finds support in a 2001 policy statement issued by the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD), the agency that was until recently authorized by Congress to \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe such rules and regulations\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cto make such interpretations\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cmay be necessary to achieve the purposes of [RESPA],\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72617(a).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t That policy statement says that \xc2\xa72607(b) \xe2\x80\x9cprohibit[s] any person from giving or accepting any unearned fees, i.e., charges or payments for real estate settlement services other than for goods or facilities provided or services performed.\xe2\x80\x9d 66 Fed. Reg. 53057 (2001). It \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically interprets [\xc2\xa72607(b)] as not being limited to situations where at least two persons split or share an unearned fee.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. More broadly, the policy statement construes \xc2\xa72607(b) as authority for regulation of the charges paid by consumers for the provision of settlements. It says that \xe2\x80\x9ca settlement service provider may not mark-up the cost of another provider\xe2\x80\x99s services without providing additional settlement services; such payment must be for services that are actual, necessary and distinct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53059. Moreover, in addition to facing liability when it collects a fee that is entirely unearned, a provider may also \xe2\x80\x9cbe liable under [\xc2\xa72607(b)] when it charges a fee that exceeds the reason-able value of goods, facilities, or services provided,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., on the theory that the excess over reasonable value constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9cportion\xe2\x80\x9d of the charge \xe2\x80\x9cother than for services actually performed,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72607(b).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe last mentioned point, however, is manifestly in-consistent with the statute HUD purported to construe. When Congress enacted RESPA in 1974, it included a directive that HUD make a report to Congress within five years regarding the need for further legislation in the area. See \xc2\xa72612(a) (1976 ed.). Among the topics required to be included in the report were \xe2\x80\x9crecommendations on whether Federal regulation of the charges for real estate settlement services in federally related mortgage transactions is necessary and desirable,\xe2\x80\x9d and, if so, recommendations with regard to what reforms should be adopted. \xc2\xa72612(b)(2). The directive for recommendations regarding the desirability of price regulation would make no sense if Congress had already resolved the issue\xe2\x80\x94if \xc2\xa72607(b) already carried with it authority for HUD to proscribe the collection of unreasonably high fees for settlement services, i.e., to engage in price regulation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNo doubt recognizing as much, petitioners do not fully adopt HUD\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa72607(b). Noting that even those Courts of Appeals which have found \xc2\xa72607(b) not to be limited to fee-splitting situations have held that the statute does not reach unreasonably high fees, see Kruse v. Wells Fargo Home Mortgage, Inc., 383 F. 3d 49, 56 (CA2 2004); Santiago v. GMAC Mortgage Group, Inc., 417 F.\xc2\xa03d 384, 387 (CA3 2005); Friedman v. Market Street Mortgage Corp., 520 F.\xc2\xa03d 1289, 1297 (CA11 2008), petitioners ac-knowledge that the statute does not cover overcharges. They nonetheless embrace HUD\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa72607(b) insofar as it holds that a provider violates the statute by retaining a fee after providing no services at all in return. In short, petitioners contend that, by allegedly charg- ing each of them an unearned fee, respondent \xe2\x80\x9caccept[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cportion, split, or percentage\xe2\x80\x9d of a settlement, service charge (i.e., 100 percent of the charge) \xe2\x80\x9cother than for services actually performed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72607(b) (2006 ed.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe parties vigorously dispute whether the position set forth in HUD\xe2\x80\x99s 2001 policy statement should be accorded deference under the framework announced by this Court in Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984)\n. We need not resolve that dispute\xe2\x80\x94or address whether, if Chevron deference would otherwise apply, it is eliminated by the policy statement\xe2\x80\x99s palpable overreach with regard to price controls. For we conclude that even the more limited position espoused by the policy statement and urged by petitioners \xe2\x80\x9cgoes beyond the meaning that the statute can bear,\xe2\x80\x9d MCI Telecommunications Corp. v. American Telephone & Telegraph Co., \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218, \n229 (1994)\n. In our view, \xc2\xa72607(b) unambiguously covers only a settlement-service provider\xe2\x80\x99s splitting of a fee with one or more other persons; it cannot be understood to reach a single provider\xe2\x80\x99s retention of an unearned fee.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBy providing that no person \xe2\x80\x9cshall give\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cshall accept\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cportion, split, or percentage\xe2\x80\x9d of a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d that has been \xe2\x80\x9cmade or received,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cother than for services actually performed,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72607(b) clearly describes two distinct exchanges. First, a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cmade\xe2\x80\x9d to or \xe2\x80\x9creceived\xe2\x80\x9d from a consumer by a settlement-service provider. That provider then \xe2\x80\x9cgive[s],\xe2\x80\x9d and another person \xe2\x80\x9caccept[s],\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cportion, split, or percentage\xe2\x80\x9d of the charge. Congress\xe2\x80\x99s use of different sets of verbs, with distinct tenses, to distinguish between the consumer-provider transaction (the \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cmade or received\xe2\x80\x9d) and the fee-sharing transaction (the \xe2\x80\x9cportion, split, or percentage\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cgive[n]\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9caccept[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d) would be pointless if, as petitioners contend, the two transactions could be collapsed into one.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners try to merge the two stages by arguing that a settlement-service provider can \xe2\x80\x9cmake\xe2\x80\x9d a charge (stage one) and then \xe2\x80\x9caccept\xe2\x80\x9d (stage two) the portion of the charge consisting of 100 percent. See Reply Brief for Petitioners 6. But then is not the provider also \xe2\x80\x9creceiv[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d the charge at the same time he is \xe2\x80\x9caccept[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d the portion of it? And who \xe2\x80\x9cgive[s]\xe2\x80\x9d the portion of the charge consisting of 100 percent? The same provider who \xe2\x80\x9caccept[s]\xe2\x80\x9d it? This reading does not avoid collapsing the sequential relationship of the two stages, and it would simply destroy the tandem character of activities that the text envisions at stage two (i.e., a giving and accepting).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners seek to avoid this consequence, at stage two at least, by saying that the consumer is the person who \xe2\x80\x9cgive[s]\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cportion, split, or percentage\xe2\x80\x9d of the charge to the provider who \xe2\x80\x9caccept[s]\xe2\x80\x9d it. See Brief for Petitioners 21; Reply Brief for Petitioners 5. But since under this statute it is (so to speak) as accursed to give as to receive, this would make lawbreakers of consumers\xe2\x80\x94the very class for whose benefit \xc2\xa72607(b) was enacted, see \xc2\xa72601. It is no answer to say that a consumer would not face damages liability because a violator is liable only \xe2\x80\x9cto the person or persons charged for the settlement service,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72607(d)(2), and it would not make sense to render a consumer liable to himself. It is the logical consequence that a consumer would be liable to himself, not the specter of actual damages liability, which provides strong indication that something in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation is amiss.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt any rate, \xc2\xa72607(b) is also enforceable through criminal prosecutions, \xc2\xa72607(d)(1), and actions for injunctive relief brought by federal and state regulators, \xc2\xa72607(d)(4). HUD\xe2\x80\x99s 2001 policy statement asserts that \xe2\x80\x9cHUD is, of course, unlikely to direct any enforcement actions against consumers for the payment of unearned fees,\xe2\x80\x9d 66 Fed. Reg. 53059, n.\xc2\xa06, but that assurance is cold comfort. Moreover, even assuming (as seems realistic) that the Justice Department would be similarly reluctant to prosecute consumers for criminal violations of \xc2\xa72607(b), \xe2\x80\x9cprosecutorial discretion is not a reason for courts to give improbable breadth to criminal statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Abuelhawa v. United States, \n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 816, \n823, n. \n3 (2009)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor is the problem of consumer criminal liability solved by petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 suggestion that an unstated mens rea requirement be read into the criminal enforcement provision, \xc2\xa72607(d)(1), see, e.g., Staples v. United States, \n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600, \n605 (1994)\n. If that would excuse only those consumers who are unaware that they are paying for unearned services, some consumers would remain criminally liable\xe2\x80\x94those who know that the fee is unearned but decide to pay it anyway, perhaps because the provider\xe2\x80\x99s proposal is still the best deal. And if it would immunize all consumers, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s criminalization of the entire \xe2\x80\x9cgiving\xe2\x80\x9d portion of consumer-provider transactions would make little sense. We find it virtually unthinkable that Congress would leave it to imputed mens rea to preserve from criminal liability some or all of the class RESPA was designed to protect\xe2\x80\x94and entirely unthinkable that Congress would have created that strange disposition through language as obscure as that relied upon here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe phrase \xe2\x80\x9cportion, split, or percentage\xe2\x80\x9d reinforces the conclusion that \xc2\xa72607(b) does not cover a situation in which a settlement-service provider retains the entirety of a fee received from a consumer. It is certainly true that \xe2\x80\x9cportion\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpercentage\xe2\x80\x9d can be used to include the entirety, or 100 percent. See, e.g., \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7648 (\xe2\x80\x9cportion\xe2\x80\x9d); \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa78348(g) (2006 ed., Supp. IV) (\xe2\x80\x9cpercentag[e]\xe2\x80\x9d); \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa78351(b)(2)(B) (2006 ed.) (same); \n\n12 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71467a(m)(7)(B)(ii)(II) (same). But that is not the normal meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cportion\xe2\x80\x9d when one speaks of \xe2\x80\x9cgiv[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9caccept[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d a portion of the whole, as dictionary definitions uniformly show.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Aesop\xe2\x80\x99s fable would be just as wryly humorous if the lion\xe2\x80\x99s claim to the entirety of the kill he hunted in partnership with less ferocious animals had been translated into English as the \xe2\x80\x9clion\xe2\x80\x99s portion\xe2\x80\x9d instead of the lion\xe2\x80\x99s share. As for \xe2\x80\x9cpercentage,\xe2\x80\x9d that word can include 100 percent\xe2\x80\x94or even 300 percent\xe2\x80\x94when it refers to merely a ratable measure (\xe2\x80\x9cunemployment claims were up 300 percent\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t But, like \xe2\x80\x9cportion,\xe2\x80\x9d it normally means less than all when referring to a \xe2\x80\x9cpercentage\xe2\x80\x9d of a specific whole (\xe2\x80\x9che demanded a percentage of the profits\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t And it is normal usage that, in the absence of contrary indication, governs our interpretation of texts. Crawford v. Metropolitan Government of Nashville and Davidson Cty., \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 271, \n276 (2009)\n; Asgrow Seed Co. v. Winterboer, \n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 179, \n187 (1995)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the present statute, that meaning is confirmed by the \xe2\x80\x9ccommonsense canon of noscitur a sociis\xe2\x80\x94which counsels that a word is given more precise content by the neighboring words with which it is associated.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Williams, \n\n553 U. S. 285, \n294 (2008)\n. For \xe2\x80\x9cportion\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpercentage\xe2\x80\x9d do not stand in isolation, but are part of a phrase in which they are joined together by the intervening word \xe2\x80\x9csplit\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94which, as petitioners acknowledge, Brief for Petitioners 19, cannot possibly mean the entirety. We think it clear that, in employing the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cportion, split, or percentage,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress sought to invoke the words\xe2\x80\x99 common \xe2\x80\x9ccore of meaning,\xe2\x80\x9d Graham County Soil and Water Conservation Dist. v. United States ex rel. Wilson, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, n.\xc2\xa07 (2010) (slip op., at 7, n.\xc2\xa07), which is to say, a part of a whole. That is so even though the phrase is preceded by \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a word that, we have observed, has an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98expansive meaning,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Department of Housing and Urban Development v. Rucker, \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 125, \n131 (2002)\n. Expansive, yes; transformative, no. It can broaden to the maximum, but never change in the least, the clear meaning of the phrase selected by Congress here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tContrary to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 contention, the natural connotation of \xe2\x80\x9cportion, split, or percentage\xe2\x80\x9d is not undermined in this context by our \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral \xe2\x80\x98reluctan[ce] to treat statutory terms as surplusage.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Trustees of Leland Stanford Junior Univ. v. Roche Molecular Systems, Inc., 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9) (quoting Duncan v. Walker, \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 167, \n174 (2001)\n). Petitioners rightly point out that under our interpretation \xe2\x80\x9cportion,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9csplit,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpercentage\xe2\x80\x9d all mean the same thing\xe2\x80\x94a perhaps regrettable but not uncommon sort of lawyerly iteration (\xe2\x80\x9cgive, grant, bargain, sell, and convey\xe2\x80\x9d). But the canon against surplusage merely favors that interpretation which avoids surplusage, see Microsoft Corp. v. i4i Ltd. Partnership, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2011) (slip op., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313)\xe2\x80\x94and petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation no more achieves that end than ours does. It is impossible to imagine a \xe2\x80\x9cportion\xe2\x80\x9d (even a portion consisting of the entirety) or a \xe2\x80\x9csplit\xe2\x80\x9d that is not also a \xe2\x80\x9cpercentage.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners invoke the presumption against surplusage a second time, urging that if \xc2\xa72607(b) is not construed to reach undivided unearned fees, it would be rendered \xe2\x80\x9clargely surplusage\xe2\x80\x9d in light of \xc2\xa72607(a)\xe2\x80\x99s express prohibition of kickbacks. Brief for Petitioners 24. Not so. Section 2607(a) prohibits giving or accepting \xe2\x80\x9cany fee, kickback, or thing of value pursuant to any agreement or understanding .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that business incident to or a part of a real estate settlement service .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be referred to any person.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72607(a). That prohibition is at once broader than \xc2\xa72607(b)\xe2\x80\x99s (because it applies to the transfer of any \xe2\x80\x9cthing of value,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than to the dividing of a \xe2\x80\x9ccharge\xe2\x80\x9d paid by a consumer) and narrower (because it requires an \xe2\x80\x9cagreement or understanding\xe2\x80\x9d to refer business). Thus, a settlement-service provider who agrees to exchange valuable tickets to a sporting event in return for a referral of business would violate \xc2\xa72607(a), but not \xc2\xa72607(b). So too a provider who agrees to pay a monetary referral fee that is not tied in any respect to a charge paid by a particular consumer\xe2\x80\x94for instance, a \xe2\x80\x9cretainer\xe2\x80\x9d agreement pursuant to which the provider pays a monthly lump sum in exchange for the recipient\xe2\x80\x99s agreement to refer any business that comes his way. By contrast, a settlement-service provider who gives a portion of a charge to another person who has not rendered any services in return would violate \xc2\xa72607(b), even if an express referral arrangement does not exist or cannot be shown. In short, each subsection reaches conduct that the other does not; there is no need to adopt petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 improbable reading of \xc2\xa72607(b) to avoid rendering any portion of \xc2\xa72607 superfluous.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt follows that petitioners can derive no support from \xc2\xa72607\xe2\x80\x99s caption: \xe2\x80\x9cProhibition against kickbacks and unearned fees.\xe2\x80\x9d Subsection (a) prohibits certain kickbacks (those agreed to in exchange for referrals) and subsection (b) prohibits certain unearned fees (those paid from a part of the charge to the customer).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners also appeal to statutory purpose, arguing that a prohibition against the charging of undivided unearned fees would fit comfortably with RESPA\xe2\x80\x99s stated goal of \xe2\x80\x9cinsur[ing] that consumers .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are protected from unnecessarily high settlement charges caused by certain abusive practices,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72601(a). It bears noting that RESPA\xe2\x80\x99s declaration of purpose is by its terms limited to \xe2\x80\x9ccertain abusive practices\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94making the statute an even worse candidate than most for the expansion of limited text by the positing of an unlimited purpose. RESPA\xe2\x80\x99s particular language ultimately serves to drive home a broader point: \xe2\x80\x9c[N]o legislation pursues its purposes at all costs,\xe2\x80\x9d Rodriguez v. United States, \n\n480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522\n\xe2\x80\x93526 (1987) (per curiam), and \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very statute purposes, not only to achieve certain ends, but also to achieve them by particular means,\xe2\x80\x9d Director, Office of Workers\xe2\x80\x99 Compensation Programs v. Newport News Shipbuilding & Dry Dock Co., \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 122, \n136 (1995)\n. Vague notions of statutory purpose provide no warrant for expanding \xc2\xa72607(b)\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition beyond the field to which it is unambiguously limited: the splitting of fees paid for settlement services.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor is there any merit to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 related contention that \xc2\xa72607(b) should not be given its natural meaning because doing so leads to the allegedly absurd result of permitting a provider to charge and keep the entirety of a $1,000 unearned fee, while imposing liability if the provider shares even a nickel of a $10 charge with someone else. That result does not strike us as particularly anomalous. Congress may well have concluded that existing remedies, such as state-law fraud actions, were sufficient to deal with the problem of entirely fictitious fees, whereas legislative action was required to deal with the problems posed by kickbacks and fee splitting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn any event, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 reading of the statute leads to an \xe2\x80\x9cabsurdity\xe2\x80\x9d of its own: Because \xc2\xa72607(b) manifestly cannot be understood to prohibit unreasonably high fees, see supra, at 5, a service provider could avoid liability by providing just a dollar\xe2\x80\x99s worth of services in exchange for the $1,000 fee. Acknowledging that \xc2\xa72607(b)\xe2\x80\x99s coverage is limited to fee-splitting transactions at least has the virtue of making it a coherent response to that particular problem, rather than an incoherent response to the broader problem of unreasonably high fees.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn order to establish a violation of \xc2\xa72607(b), a plaintiff must demonstrate that a charge for settlement services was divided between two or more persons. Because petitioners do not contend that respondent split the challenged charges with anyone else, summary judgment was properly granted in favor of respondent. We therefore affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.\n\t\t\t
