HOWELL v. HOWELL

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of arizona

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x931031.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 20, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 15, 2017

\n

The Uniformed Services Former Spouses\xe2\x80\x99 Protection Act authorizes States to treat veterans\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cdisposable retired pay\xe2\x80\x9d as community property divisible upon divorce,\n\n10 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71408, but expressly excludes from its definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdisposable retired pay\xe2\x80\x9d amounts deducted from that pay \xe2\x80\x9cas a result of a waiver . . . required by law in order to receive\xe2\x80\x9d disability benefits, \xc2\xa71408(a)(4)(B). The divorce decree of petitioner John Howell and respondent Sandra Howell awarded Sandra 50% of John\xe2\x80\x99s future Air Force retirement pay, which she began to receive when John retired the following year. About 13 years later, the Department of Veterans Affairs found that John was partially disabled due to an earlier service-related injury. To receive disability pay, federal law required John to give up an equivalent amount of retirement pay.\n\n38 U. S. C. \xc2\xa75305. By his election, John waived about $250 of his retirement pay, which also reduced the value of Sandra\xe2\x80\x99s 50% share. Sandra petitioned the Arizona family court to enforce the original divorce decree and restore the value of her share of John\xe2\x80\x99s total retirement pay. The court held that the original divorce decree had given Sandra a vested interest in the prewaiver amount of John\xe2\x80\x99s retirement pay and ordered John to ensure that she receive her full 50% without regard for the disability waiver. The Arizona Supreme Court affirmed, holding that federal law did not pre-empt the family court\xe2\x80\x99s order.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A state court may not order a veteran to indemnify a divorced spouse for the loss in the divorced spouse\xe2\x80\x99s portion of the veteran\xe2\x80\x99s retirement pay caused by the veteran\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of retirement pay to receive service-related disability benefits. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Mansell v. Mansell,\n\n490 U. S. 581\n, determines the outcome here. There, the Court held that federal law completely pre-empts the States from treating waived military retirement pay as divisible community property. Id., at 594\xe2\x80\x93595. The Arizona Supreme Court attempted to distinguish Mansell by emphasizing the fact that the veteran\xe2\x80\x99s waiver in that case took place before the divorce proceeding while the waiver here took place several years after the divorce. This temporal difference highlights only that John\xe2\x80\x99s military pay at the time it came to Sandra was subject to a future contingency, meaning that the value of Sandra\xe2\x80\x99s share of military retirement pay was possibly worth less at the time of the divorce. Nothing in this circumstance makes the Arizona courts\xe2\x80\x99 reimbursement award to Sandra any the less an award of the portion of military pay that John waived in order to obtain disability benefits. That the Arizona courts referred to her interest in the waivable portion as having \xe2\x80\x9cvested\xe2\x80\x9d does not help: State courts cannot \xe2\x80\x9cvest\xe2\x80\x9d that which they lack the authority to give. Neither can the State avoid Mansell by describing the family court order as an order requiring John to \xe2\x80\x9creimburse\xe2\x80\x9d or to \xe2\x80\x9cindemnify\xe2\x80\x9d Sandra, rather than an order dividing property, a semantic difference and nothing more. Regardless of their form, such orders displace the federal rule and stand as an obstacle to the accomplishment and execution of the purposes and objectives of Congress. Family courts remain free to take account of the contingency that some military retirement pay might be waived or take account of reductions in value when calculating or recalculating the need for spousal support. Here, however, the state courts made clear that the original divorce decree divided the whole of John\xe2\x80\x99s military pay, and their decisions rested entirely upon the need to restore Sandra\xe2\x80\x99s lost portion. Pp. 6\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

238 Ariz. 407, 361 P.\xc2\xa03d 936, reversed and remanded.

\n

JOHN HOWELL, PETITIONER v. SANDRA HOWELL

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of arizona

\n

[May 15, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

A federal statute provides that a State may treat as community property, and divide at divorce, a military veteran\xe2\x80\x99s retirement pay. See\n\n10 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71408(c)(1). The statute, however, exempts from this grant of permission any amount that the Government deducts \xe2\x80\x9cas a result of a waiver\xe2\x80\x9d that the veteran must make \xe2\x80\x9cin order to receive\xe2\x80\x9d disability benefits. \xc2\xa71408(a)(4)(B). We have held that a State cannot treat as community property, and divide at divorce, this portion (the waived portion) of the veteran\xe2\x80\x99s retirement pay. See Mansell v. Mansell,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 581\n\xe2\x80\x93595 (1989).

\n

In this case a State treated as community property and awarded to a veteran\xe2\x80\x99s spouse upon divorce a portion of the veteran\xe2\x80\x99s total retirement pay. Long after the divorce, the veteran waived a share of the retirement pay in order to receive nontaxable disability benefits from the Federal Government instead. Can the State subsequently increase, pro\xc2\xa0rata, the amount the divorced spouse receives each month from the veteran\xe2\x80\x99s retirement pay in order to indemnify the divorced spouse for the loss caused by the veteran\xe2\x80\x99s waiver? The question is complicated, but the answer is not. Our cases and the statute make clear that the answer to the indemnification question is \xe2\x80\x9cno.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The Federal Government has long provided retirement pay to those veterans who have retired from the Armed Forces after serving, e.g., 20 years or more. It also provides disabled members of the Armed Forces with disability benefits. In order to prevent double counting, however, federal law typically insists that, to receive disability benefits, a retired veteran must give up an equivalent amount of retirement pay. And, since retirement pay is taxable while disability benefits are not, the veteran often elects to waive retirement pay in order to receive disability benefits. See\n\n10 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73911 et seq. (Army retirement benefits); \xc2\xa76321 et seq. (Navy and Marines retirement benefits); \xc2\xa78911 et seq. (Air Force retirement benefits);\n\n38 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa75305 (requiring a waiver to receive disability benefits); \xc2\xa75301(a)(1) (exempting disability benefits from taxation). See generally McCarty v. McCarty,\n\n453 U.\xc2\xa0S. 210\n\xe2\x80\x93215 (1981) (describing the military\xe2\x80\x99s nondisability retirement system).

\n

In 1981 we considered federal military retirement pay alone, i.e., not in the context of pay waived to receive disability benefits. The question was whether a State could consider any of a veteran\xe2\x80\x99s retirement pay to be a form of community property, divisible at divorce. The Court concluded that the States could not. See McCarty, supra. We noted that the relevant legislative history referred to military retirement pay as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98personal entitlement.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 224. We added that other language in the statute as well as its history made \xe2\x80\x9cclear that Congress intended that military retired pay \xe2\x80\x98actually reach the beneficiary.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 228. We found a \xe2\x80\x9cconflict between the terms of the federal retirement statutes and the [state-conferred] community property right.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 232. And we concluded that the division of military retirement pay by the States threatened to harm clear and substantial federal interests. Hence federal law pre-empted the state law. Id., at 235.

\n

In 1982 Congress responded by passing the Uniformed Services Former Spouses\xe2\x80\x99 Protection Act,\n\n10 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71408. Congress wrote that a State may treat veterans\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cdisposable retired pay\xe2\x80\x9d as divisible property, i.e., community property divisible upon divorce. \xc2\xa71408(c)(1). But the new Act expressly excluded from its definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdispos-able retired pay\xe2\x80\x9d amounts deducted from that pay \xe2\x80\x9cas a result of a waiver .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. required by law in order to receive\xe2\x80\x9d disability benefits. \xc2\xa71408(a)(4)(B). (A recent amendment to the statute renumbered the waiver provision. It now appears at \xc2\xa71408(a)(4)(A)(ii). See Pub. L. 114\xe2\x80\x93328, \xc2\xa7641(a),\n\n130Stat.\n2164.)

\n

In 1989 we interpreted the new federal language in Mansell,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 581\n. Major Gerald E. Mansell and his wife had divorced in California. At the time of the divorce, they entered into a \xe2\x80\x9cproperty settlement which provided, in part, that Major Mansell would pay Mrs. Mansell 50 percent of his total military retirement pay, including that portion of retirement pay waived so that Major Mansell could receive disability benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 586. The divorce decree incorporated this settlement and permitted the division. Major Mansell later moved to modify the decree so that it would omit the portion of the retirement pay that he had waived. The California courts refused to do so. But this Court reversed. It held that federal law forbade California from treating the waived portion as community property divisible at divorce.

\n

Justice Thurgood Marshall, writing for the Court, pointed out that federal law, as construed in McCarty, \xe2\x80\x9ccompletely pre-empted the application of state community property law to military retirement pay.\xe2\x80\x9d 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 588. He noted that Congress could \xe2\x80\x9covercome\xe2\x80\x9d this pre-emption \xe2\x80\x9cby enacting an affirmative grant of authority giving the States the power to treat military retirement pay as community property.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. He recognized that Congress, with its new Act, had done that, but only to a limited extent. The Act provided a \xe2\x80\x9cprecise and limited\xe2\x80\x9d grant of the power to divide federal military retirement pay. Ibid. It did not \xe2\x80\x9cgran[t]\xe2\x80\x9d the States \xe2\x80\x9cthe authority to treattotal retired pay as community property.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 589. Rather, Congress excluded from its grant of authority the disability-related waived portion of military retirement pay. Hence, in respect to the waived portion of retirement pay, McCarty, with its rule of federal pre-emption, still applies. Ibid.

\n

B

\n

John Howell, the petitioner, and Sandra Howell, the respondent, were divorced in 1991, while John was serving in the Air Force. Anticipating John\xe2\x80\x99s eventual retirement, the divorce decree treated John\xe2\x80\x99s future retirement pay as community property. It awarded Sandra \xe2\x80\x9cas her sole and separate property FIFTY PERCENT (50%) of [John\xe2\x80\x99s] military retirement when it begins.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 41a. It also ordered John to pay child support of $585 per month and spousal maintenance of $150 per month until the time of John\xe2\x80\x99s retirement.

\n

In 1992 John retired from the Air Force and began to receive military retirement pay, half of which went to Sandra. About 13 years later the Department of Veterans Affairs found that John was 20% disabled due to a service-related shoulder injury. John elected to receive disability benefits and consequently had to waive about $250 per month of the roughly $1,500 of military retirement pay he shared with Sandra. Doing so reduced the amount of retirement pay that he and Sandra received by about $125 per month each. In\xc2\xa0re Marriage of Howell, 238 Ariz. 407, 408, 361 P. 3d 936, 937 (2015)

\n

Sandra then asked the Arizona family court to enforce the original decree, in effect restoring the value of her share of John\xe2\x80\x99s total retirement pay. The court held that the original divorce decree had given Sandra a \xe2\x80\x9cvested\xe2\x80\x9d interest in the prewaiver amount of that pay, and ordered John to ensure that Sandra \xe2\x80\x9creceive her full 50% of the military retirement without regard for the disability.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 28a.

\n

The Arizona Supreme Court affirmed the family court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. See 238 Ariz. 407, 361 P. 3d 936. It asked whether the family court could \xe2\x80\x9corder John to indemnify Sandra for the reduction\xe2\x80\x9d of her share of John\xe2\x80\x99s military retirement pay. Id., at 409, 361 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 938. It wrote that the family court order did not \xe2\x80\x9cdivide\xe2\x80\x9d John\xe2\x80\x99s waived military retirement pay, the order did not require John \xe2\x80\x9cto rescind\xe2\x80\x9d his waiver, nor did the order \xe2\x80\x9cdirect him to pay any amount to Sandra from his disability pay.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 410, 361 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 939. Rather the family court simply ordered John to \xe2\x80\x9creimburse\xe2\x80\x9d Sandra for \xe2\x80\x9creducing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. her share\xe2\x80\x9d of military retirement pay. Ibid. The high court concluded that because John had made his waiver after, rather than before, the family court divided his military retirement pay, our decision in Mansell did not control the case, and thus federal law did not preempt the family court\xe2\x80\x99s reimbursement order. 238 Ariz., at 410, 361 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 939.

\n

Because different state courts have come to different conclusions on the matter, we granted John Howell\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari. Compare Glover v. Ranney, 314 P. 3d 535, 539\xe2\x80\x93540 (Alaska 2013); Krapf v. Krapf, 439 Mass. 97, 106\xe2\x80\x93107, 786 N. E. 2d 318, 325\xe2\x80\x93326 (2003); and Johnson v. Johnson, 37 S. W. 3d 892, 897\xe2\x80\x93898 (Tenn. 2001), with Mallard v. Burkhart, 95 So. 3d 1264, 1269\xe2\x80\x931272 (Miss. 2012); and Youngbluth v. Youngbluth, 2010 VT 40, 188 Vt. 53, 62\xe2\x80\x9365, 6 A. 3d 677, 682\xe2\x80\x93685.

\n

II

\n

This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Mansell determines the outcome here. In Mansell, the Court held that federal law completely pre-empts the States from treating waived military retirement pay as divisible community property. 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 594\xe2\x80\x93595. Yet that which federal law pre-empts is just what the Arizona family court did here. App. to Pet. for Cert. 28a, 35a (finding that the divorce decree gave Sandra a \xe2\x80\x9cvested\xe2\x80\x9d interest in John\xe2\x80\x99s retirement pay and ordering that Sandra receive her share \xe2\x80\x9cwithout regard for the disability\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The Arizona Supreme Court, the respondent, and the Solicitor General try to distinguish Mansell. But we do not find their efforts convincing. The Arizona Supreme Court, like several other state courts, emphasized the fact that the veteran\xe2\x80\x99s waiver in Mansell took place before the divorce proceeding; the waiver here took place several years after the divorce proceedings. See 238 Ariz., at 410, 361 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 939; see also Abernethy v. Fishkin, 699 So.\xc2\xa02d 235, 240 (Fla. 1997) (noting that a veteran had not yet waived retirement pay at the time of the divorce and permitting indemnification in light of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cintent to maintain level monthly payments pursuant to their property settlement agreement\xe2\x80\x9d). Hence here, as the Solicitor General emphasizes, the nonmilitary spouse and the family court were likely to have assumed that a full share of the veteran\xe2\x80\x99s retirement pay would remain available after the assets were distributed.

\n

Nonetheless, the temporal difference highlights only that John\xe2\x80\x99s military retirement pay at the time it came to Sandra was subject to later reduction (should John exercise a waiver to receive disability benefits to which he is entitled). The state court did not extinguish (and most likely would not have had the legal power to extinguish) that future contingency. The existence of that contingency meant that the value of Sandra\xe2\x80\x99s share of military retirement pay was possibly worth less\xe2\x80\x94perhaps less than Sandra and others thought\xe2\x80\x94at the time of the divorce. So too is an ownership interest in property (say, A\xe2\x80\x99s property interest in Blackacre) worth less if it is subject to defeasance or termination upon the occurrence of a later event (say, B\xe2\x80\x99s death). See generally Restatement (Third) of Property \xc2\xa724.3 (2010) (describing property interests that are defeasible); id., \xc2\xa725.3, and Comment a (describing contingent future interests subject to divestment).

\n

We see nothing in this circumstance that makes the reimbursement award to Sandra any the less an award of the portion of military retirement pay that John waived in order to obtain disability benefits. And that is the portion that Congress omitted from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s definition of \xe2\x80\x9cdisposable retired pay,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, the portion that federal law prohibits state courts from awarding to a divorced veteran\xe2\x80\x99s former spouse. Mansell, supra, at 589. That the Arizona courts referred to Sandra\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the waiv-able portion as having \xe2\x80\x9cvested\xe2\x80\x9d does not help. Statecourts cannot \xe2\x80\x9cvest\xe2\x80\x9d that which (under governing federal law) they lack the authority to give. Cf.\n\n38 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa75301(a)(1) (providing that disability benefits are gener-ally nonassignable). Accordingly, while the divorce decree might be said to \xe2\x80\x9cvest\xe2\x80\x9d Sandra with an immediate right to half of John\xe2\x80\x99s military retirement pay, that interest is, at most, contingent, depending for its amount on a subsequent condition: John\xe2\x80\x99s possible waiver of that pay.

\n

Neither can the State avoid Mansell by describing the family court order as an order requiring John to \xe2\x80\x9creimburse\xe2\x80\x9d or to \xe2\x80\x9cindemnify\xe2\x80\x9d Sandra, rather than an order that divides property. The difference is semantic and nothing more. The principal reason the state courts have given for ordering reimbursement or indemnification is that they wish to restore the amount previously awarded as community property, i.e., to restore that portion of retirement pay lost due to the postdivorce waiver. And we note that here, the amount of indemnification mirrors the waived retirement pay, dollar for dollar. Regardless of their form, such reimbursement and indemnification orders displace the federal rule and stand as an obstacle to the accomplishment and execution of the purposes and objectives of Congress. All such orders are thus pre-empted.

\n

The basic reasons McCarty gave for believing that Congress intended to exempt military retirement pay from state community property laws apply a fortiori to disability pay. See 453 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 232\xe2\x80\x93235 (describing the federal interests in attracting and retaining military personnel). And those reasons apply with equal force to a veteran\xe2\x80\x99s postdivorce waiver to receive disability benefits to which he or she has become entitled.

\n

We recognize, as we recognized in Mansell, the hardship that congressional pre-emption can sometimes work on divorcing spouses. See 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 594. But we note that a family court, when it first determines the value of a family\xe2\x80\x99s assets, remains free to take account of the contingency that some military retirement pay might be waived, or, as the petitioner himself recognizes, take account of reductions in value when it calculates or recalculates the need for spousal support. See Rose v. Rose,\n\n481 U.\xc2\xa0S. 619\n\xe2\x80\x93634, and n.\xc2\xa06 (1987);\n\n10 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71408(e)(6).

\n

We need not and do not decide these matters, for here the state courts made clear that the original divorce decree divided the whole of John\xe2\x80\x99s military retirement pay, and their decisions rested entirely upon the need to restore Sandra\xe2\x80\x99s lost portion. Consequently, the determination of the Supreme Court of Arizona must be reversed. See Mansell, supra, at 594.

\n

III

\n

The judgment of the Supreme Court of Arizona is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
