ENGQUIST v. OREGON DEPARTMENT OF AGRICULTURE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93474.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 21, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 9, 2008

\n

Petitioner Engquist, an Oregon public employee, filed suit against respondents\xe2\x80\x94her agency, her supervisor, and a co-worker\xe2\x80\x94asserting, inter alia, claims under the Equal Protection Clause:\xc2\xa0She alleged she had been discriminated against based on her race, sex, and national origin, and she also brought a so-called \xe2\x80\x9cclass-of-one\xe2\x80\x9d claim, alleging that she was fired not because she was a member of an identified class (unlike her race, sex, and national origin claims), but simply for arbitrary, vindictive, and malicious reasons. The jury rejected the class-membership equal protection claims, but found for Engquist on her class-of-one claim. The Ninth Circuit reversed in relevant part. Although recognizing that this Court had upheld a class-of-one equal protection challenge to state legislative and regulatory action in Village of Willowbrook v. Olech, 528 U. S. 562, the court below emphasized that this Court has routinely afforded government greater leeway when it acts as employer rather than regulator. The Court concluded that extending the class-of-one theory to the public-employment context would lead to undue judicial interference in state employment practices and invalidate public at-will employment.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The class-of-one theory of equal protection does not apply in the public employment context. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0There is a crucial difference between the government exercising \xe2\x80\x9cthe power to regulate or license, as lawmaker,\xe2\x80\x9d and acting \xe2\x80\x9cas proprietor, to manage [its] internal operation.\xe2\x80\x9d Cafeteria & Restaurant Workers v. McElroy, 367 U. S. 886, 896. Thus, in the public-employment context, the Court has recognized that government has significantly greater leeway in its dealings with citizen employees than in bringing its sovereign power to bear on citizens at large. See, e.g., O\xe2\x80\x99Connor v. Ortega, 480 U. S. 709, 721\xe2\x80\x93722. The relevant precedent establishes two main principles: First, government employees do not lose their constitutional rights when they go to work, but those rights must be balanced against the realities of the employment context. See, e.g., id., at 721. Second, in striking the appropriate balance, the Court considers whether the claimed employee right implicates the relevant constitutional provision\xe2\x80\x99s basic concerns, or whether the right can more readily give way to the requirements of the government as employer. See, e.g., Connick v. Myers, 461 U. S. 138. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s equal protection jurisprudence has typically been concerned with governmental classifications that \xe2\x80\x9caffect some groups of citizens differently than others.\xe2\x80\x9d McGowan v. Maryland, 366 U. S. 420, 425. Olech did recognize that a class-of-one equal protection claim can in some circumstances be sustained. Its recognition of that theory, however, was not so much a departure from the principle that the Equal Protection Clause is concerned with arbitrary government classification, as it was an application of that principle to the facts in that case: The government singled Olech out with regard to its regulation of property, and the cases upon which the Court relied concerned property assessment and taxation schemes that were applied in a singular way to particular citizens. What seems to have been significant in Olech and the cited cases was the existence of a clear standard against which departures, even for a single plaintiff, could be readily assessed. This differential treatment raised a concern of arbitrary classification, and therefore required that the State provide a rational basis for it. There are some forms of state action, however, which by their nature involve discretionary decisionmaking based on a vast array of subjective, individualized assessments. In such cases treating like individuals differently is an accepted consequence of the discretion granted to governmental officials. This principle applies most clearly in the employment context, where decisions are often subjective and individualized, resting on a wide array of factors that are difficult to articulate and quantify. Unlike the context of arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length regulation, such as in Olech, treating seemingly similarly situated individuals differently in the employment context is par for the course. It is no proper challenge to what in its nature is a subjective and individualized decision that it was subjective and individualized. That the Court has never found the Equal Protection Clause implicated in this area is not surprising, given the historical understanding of the at-will nature of government employment. See, e.g., Cafeteria & Restaurant Workers v. McElroy, 367 U. S. 886, 896. Recognition of a claim that the State treated an employee differently from others for a bad reason, or for no reason at all, is simply contrary to the at-will concept. The Constitution does not require repudiating that familiar doctrine. Finally, the Court is guided, as in the past, by the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-sense realization that government offices could not function if every employment decision became a constitutional matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Connick, supra, at 143. If class-of-one claims were recognized in the employment context, any personnel action in which a wronged employee can conjure up a claim of differential treatment would suddenly become the basis for a federal constitutional claim. The Equal Protection Clause does not require \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his displacement of managerial discretion by judicial supervision.\xe2\x80\x9d Garcetti v. Ceballos, 547 U. S. 410, 423. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

478 F.\xc2\xa03d 985, affirmed.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nENGQUIST V. OREGON DEPT. OF AGRICULTURE
\n553 U. S. ____ (2008)

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 07-474

ANUP ENGQUIST, PETITIONER v. OREGON\nDEPARTMENT OF AGRICULTURE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 9, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question in this case is whether a public employee can state a claim under the Equal Protection Clause by alleging that she was arbitrarily treated differently from other similarly situated employees, with no assertion that the different treatment was based on the employee\xe2\x80\x99s membership in any particular class. We hold that such a \xe2\x80\x9cclass-of-one\xe2\x80\x9d theory of equal protection has no place in the public employment context.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Anup Engquist, the petitioner in this case, was hired in 1992 by Norma Corristan to be an international food standard specialist for the Export Service Center (ESC), a laboratory within the Oregon Department of Agriculture (ODA). During the course of her employment, Engquist experienced repeated problems with Joseph Hyatt, another ODA employee, complaining to Corristan that he had made false statements about her and otherwise made her life difficult. Corristan responded by directing Hyatt to attend diversity and anger management training.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 2001, John Szczepanski, an assistant director of ODA, assumed responsibility over ESC, supervising Corristan, Hyatt, and Engquist. Szczepanski told a client that he could not \xe2\x80\x9ccontrol\xe2\x80\x9d Engquist, and that Engquist and Corristan \xe2\x80\x9cwould be gotten rid of.\xe2\x80\x9d When Engquist and Hyatt both applied for a vacant managerial post within ESC, Szczepanski chose Hyatt despite Engquist\xe2\x80\x99s greater experience in the relevant field. Later that year, during a round of across-the-board budget cuts in Oregon, Szczepanski eliminated Corristan\xe2\x80\x99s position. Finally, on January 31, 2002, Engquist was informed that her position was being eliminated because of reorganization. Engquist\xe2\x80\x99s collective-bargaining agreement gave her the opportunity either to \xe2\x80\x9cbump\xe2\x80\x9d to another position at her level, or to take a demotion. She was found unqualified for the only other position at her level and declined a demotion, and was therefore effectively laid off.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Engquist subsequently brought suit in the United States District Court for the District of Oregon against ODA, Szczepanski, and Hyatt, all respondents here, alleging violations of federal antidiscrimination statutes, the Equal Protection and Due Process Clauses of the Fourteenth Amendment, and state law. As to Engquist\xe2\x80\x99s equal protection claim, she alleged that the defendants discriminated against her on the basis of her race, sex, and national origin. She also brought what is known as a \xe2\x80\x9cclass-of-one\xe2\x80\x9d equal protection claim, alleging that she was fired not because she was a member of an identified class (unlike her race, sex, and national origin claims), but simply for \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary, vindictive, and malicious reasons.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 10.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court granted the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion for summary judgment as to some of Engquist\xe2\x80\x99s claims, but allowed others to go forward, including each of the equal protection claims. As relevant to this case, the District Court found Engquist\xe2\x80\x99s class-of-one equal protection claim legally viable, deciding that the class-of-one theory was fully applicable in the employment context. Civ. No. 02\xe2\x80\x931637\xe2\x80\x93AS (D. Ore., Sept. 14, 2004), App. 58, 2004 WL 2066748, *5. The court held that Engquist could succeed on that theory if she could prove \xe2\x80\x9cthat she was singled out as a result of animosity on the part of Hyatt and Szczepanski\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., \xe2\x80\x9cthat their actions were spiteful efforts to punish her for reasons unrelated to any legitimate state objective\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and if she could demonstrate, on the basis of that animosity, that \xe2\x80\x9cshe was treated differently than others who were similarly situated.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury rejected Engquist\xe2\x80\x99s claims of discrimination for membership in a suspect class\xe2\x80\x94her race, sex, and national origin claims\xe2\x80\x94but found in her favor on the class-of-one claim. Specifically, the jury found that Hyatt and Szczepanski \xe2\x80\x9cintentionally treat[ed] [Engquist] differently than others similarly situated with respect to the denial of her promotion, termination of her employment, or denial of bumping rights without any rational basis and solely for arbitrary, vindictive or malicious reasons.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 3\xe2\x80\x934. The jury also found for Engquist on several of her other claims, and awarded her $175,000 in compensatory damages and $250,000 in punitive damages.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals reversed in relevant part. It recognized that this Court had upheld a class-of-one equal protection challenge to state legislative and regulatory action in Village of Willowbrook v. Olech, 528 U. S. 562 (2000) (per curiam). 478 F.\xc2\xa03d 985, 992\xe2\x80\x93993 (CA9 2007). The court below also acknowledged that other Circuits had applied Olech in the public employment context, id., at 993 (citing cases), but it disagreed with those courts on the ground that our cases have routinely afforded government greater leeway when it acts as employer rather than regulator, id., at 993\xe2\x80\x93996. The court concluded that extending the class-of-one theory of equal protection to the public employment context would lead to undue judicial interference in state employment practices and \xe2\x80\x9ccompletely invalidate the practice of public at-will employment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 995. The court accordingly held that the class-of-one theory is \xe2\x80\x9cinapplicable to decisions made by public employers with regard to their employees.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 996.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Reinhardt dissented, \xe2\x80\x9cagree[ing] with the other circuits that the class-of-one theory of equal protection is applicable to public employment decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1010. We granted certiorari to resolve this disagreement in the lower courts, 552 U. S. __ (2008), and now affirm.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Engquist argues that the Equal Protection Clause forbids public employers from irrationally treating one employee differently from others similarly situated, regardless of whether the different treatment is based on the employee\xe2\x80\x99s membership in a particular class. She reasons that in Olech, supra, we recognized in the regulatory context a similar class-of-one theory of equal protection, Brief for Petitioner 14\xe2\x80\x9315; that the Equal Protection Clause protects individuals, not classes, id., at 15\xe2\x80\x9317; that the Clause proscribes \xe2\x80\x9cdiscrimination arising not only from a legislative act but also from the conduct of an administrative official,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 17; and that the Constitution applies to the State not only when it acts as regulator, but also when it acts as employer, id., at 23\xe2\x80\x9329. Thus, Engquist concludes that class-of-one claims can be brought against public employers just as against any other state actors, id., at 29\xe2\x80\x9332, and that differential treatment of government employees\xe2\x80\x94even when not based on membership in a class or group\xe2\x80\x94violates the Equal Protection Clause unless supported by a rational basis, id., at 32, 39\xe2\x80\x9345.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not quarrel with the premises of Engquist\xe2\x80\x99s argument. It is well settled that the Equal Protection Clause \xe2\x80\x9cprotect[s] persons, not groups,\xe2\x80\x9d Adarand Constructors, Inc. v. Pe\xc5\x84a, 515 U. S. 200, 227 (1995) (emphasis omitted), and that the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s protections apply to administrative as well as legislative acts, see, e.g., Raymond v. Chicago Union Traction Co., 207 U. S. 20, 35\xe2\x80\x9336 (1907). It is equally well settled that States do not escape the strictures of the Equal Protection Clause in their role as employers. See, e.g., New York City Transit Authority v. Beazer, 440 U. S. 568 (1979); Harrah Independent School Dist. v. Martin, 440 U. S. 194 (1979) (per curiam); Massachusetts Bd. of Retirement v. Murgia, 427 U. S. 307 (1976) (per curiam). We do not, however, agree that Engquist\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion follows from these premises. Our traditional view of the core concern of the Equal Protection Clause as a shield against arbitrary classifications, combined with unique considerations applicable when the government acts as employer as opposed to sovereign, lead us to conclude that the class-of-one theory of equal protection does not apply in the public employment context.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have long held the view that there is a crucial difference, with respect to constitutional analysis, between the government exercising \xe2\x80\x9cthe power to regulate or license, as lawmaker,\xe2\x80\x9d and the government acting \xe2\x80\x9cas proprietor, to manage [its] internal operation.\xe2\x80\x9d Cafeteria & Restaurant Workers v. McElroy, 367 U. S. 886, 896 (1961). This distinction has been particularly clear in our review of state action in the context of public employment. Thus, \xe2\x80\x9cthe government as employer indeed has far broader powers than does the government as sovereign.\xe2\x80\x9d Waters v. Churchill, 511 U. S. 661, 671 (1994) (plurality opinion). \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he extra power the government has in this area comes from the nature of the government\xe2\x80\x99s mission as employer. Government agencies are charged by law with doing particular tasks. Agencies hire employees to help do those tasks as effectively and efficiently as possible.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 674\xe2\x80\x93675. See also Connick v. Myers, 461 U. S. 138, 150\xe2\x80\x93151 (1983) (explaining that the government has a legitimate interest \xe2\x80\x9cin \xe2\x80\x98promot[ing] efficiency and integrity in the discharge of official duties, and [in] maintain[ing] proper discipline in the public service\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Ex parte Curtis, 106 U. S. 371, 373 (1882) (alterations in original))). \xe2\x80\x9cThe government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in achieving its goals as effectively and efficiently as possible is elevated from a relatively subordinate interest when it acts as sovereign to a significant one when it acts as employer.\xe2\x80\x9d Waters, supra, at 675 (plurality opinion). Given the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-sense realization that government offices could not function if every employment decision became a constitutional matter,\xe2\x80\x9d Connick, supra, at 143, \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional review of government employment decisions must rest on different principles than review of \xe2\x80\xa6 restraints imposed by the government as sovereign,\xe2\x80\x9d Waters, supra, at 674 (plurality opinion).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of these basic principles, we have often recognized that government has significantly greater leeway in its dealings with citizen employees than it does when it brings its sovereign power to bear on citizens at large. Thus, for example, we have held that the Fourth Amendment does not require public employers to obtain warrants before conducting a search of an employee\xe2\x80\x99s office. O\xe2\x80\x99Connor v. Ortega, 480 U. S. 709, 721\xe2\x80\x93722 (1987) (plurality opinion). See also id., at 732 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment). Although we recognized that the \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate privacy interests of public employees in the private objects they bring to the workplace may be substantial,\xe2\x80\x9d we found that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]gainst these privacy interests \xe2\x80\xa6 must be balanced the realities of the workplace, which strongly suggest that a warrant requirement would be unworkable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 721 (plurality opinion). We have also found that the Due Process Clause does not protect a public employee from discharge, even when such discharge was mistaken or unreasonable. See Bishop v. Wood, 426 U. S. 341, 350 (1976) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment is not a guarantee against incorrect or ill-advised personnel decisions\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our public-employee speech cases are particularly instructive. In Pickering v. Board of Ed. of Township High School Dist. 205, Will Cty., 391 U. S. 563, 568 (1968), we explained that, in analyzing a claim that a public employee was deprived of First Amendment rights by her employer, we must seek \xe2\x80\x9ca balance between the interests of the [employee], as a citizen, in commenting upon matters of public concern and the interest of the State, as an employer, in promoting the efficiency of the public services it performs through its employees.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We analyzed the contours of this balance more fully in Connick v. Myers, supra. We explained that the First Amendment protects public-employee speech only when it falls within the core of First Amendment protection\xe2\x80\x94speech on matters of public concern. We recognized that the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98First Amendment does not protect speech and assembly only to the extent it can be characterized as political,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and that the government therefore could not generally prohibit or punish, in its capacity as sovereign, speech on the ground that it does not touch upon matters of public concern, id., at 147 (quoting Mine Workers v. Illinois Bar Assn., 389 U. S. 217, 223 (1967)). But \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen employee expression cannot be fairly considered as relating to any matter of political, social, or other concern to the community, government officials should enjoy wide latitude in managing their offices.\xe2\x80\x9d Connick, 461 U. S., at 146. As we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cabsent the most unusual circumstances, a federal court is not the appropriate forum in which to review the wisdom of a personnel decision taken by a public agency allegedly in reaction to the employee\xe2\x80\x99s behavior.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 147 (citing Bishop, supra, at 349\xe2\x80\x93350).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our precedent in the public-employee context therefore establishes two main principles: First, although government employees do not lose their constitutional rights when they accept their positions, those rights must be balanced against the realities of the employment context. Second, in striking the appropriate balance, we consider whether the asserted employee right implicates the basic concerns of the relevant constitutional provision, or whether the claimed right can more readily give way to the requirements of the government as employer. With these principles in mind, we come to the question whether a class-of-one theory of equal protection is cognizable in the public employment context.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our equal protection jurisprudence has typically been concerned with governmental classifications that \xe2\x80\x9caffect some groups of citizens differently than others.\xe2\x80\x9d McGowan v. Maryland, 366 U. S. 420, 425 (1961). See, e.g., Ross v. Moffitt, 417 U. S. 600, 609 (1974) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Equal Protection\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 emphasizes disparity in treatment by a State between classes of individuals whose situations are arguably indistinguishable\xe2\x80\x9d); San Antonio Independent School Dist. v. Rodriguez, 411 U. S. 1, 60 (1973) (Stewart, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he basic concern of the Equal Protection Clause is with state legislation whose purpose or effect is to create discrete and objectively identifiable classes\xe2\x80\x9d). Plaintiffs in such cases generally allege that they have been arbitrarily classified as members of an \xe2\x80\x9cidentifiable group.\xe2\x80\x9d Personnel Administrator of Mass. v. Feeney, 442 U. S. 256, 279 (1979).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Engquist correctly argues, however, that we recognized in Olech that an equal protection claim can in some circumstances be sustained even if the plaintiff has not alleged class-based discrimination, but instead claims that she has been irrationally singled out as a so-called \xe2\x80\x9cclass of one.\xe2\x80\x9d In Olech, a property owner had asked the village of Willowbrook to connect her property to the municipal water supply. Although the village had required only a 15-foot easement from other property owners seeking access to the water supply, the village conditioned Olech\xe2\x80\x99s connection on a grant of a 33-foot easement. Olech sued the village, claiming that the village\xe2\x80\x99s requirement of an easement 18 feet longer than the norm violated the Equal Protection Clause. Although Olech had not alleged that the village had discriminated against her based on membership in an identifiable class, we held that her complaint stated a valid claim under the Equal Protection Clause because it alleged that she had \xe2\x80\x9cbeen intentionally treated differently from others similarly situated and that there is no rational basis for the difference in treatment.\xe2\x80\x9d 528 U. S., at 564 (citing Sioux City Bridge Co. v. Dakota County, 260 U. S. 441 (1923), and Allegheny Pittsburgh Coal Co. v. Commission of Webster Cty., 488 U. S. 336 (1989)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Recognition of the class-of-one theory of equal protection on the facts in Olech was not so much a departure from the principle that the Equal Protection Clause is concerned with arbitrary government classification, as it was an application of that principle. That case involved the government\xe2\x80\x99s regulation of property. Similarly, the cases upon which the Court in Olech relied concerned property assessment and taxation schemes. See Allegheny Pittsburgh, supra; Sioux City Bridge, supra. We expect such legislative or regulatory classifications to apply \xe2\x80\x9cwithout respect to persons,\xe2\x80\x9d to borrow a phrase from the judicial oath. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7453. As we explained long ago, the Fourteenth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9crequires that all persons subjected to \xe2\x80\xa6 legislation shall be treated alike, under like circumstances and conditions, both in the privileges conferred and in the liabilities imposed.\xe2\x80\x9d Hayes v. Missouri, 120 U. S. 68, 71\xe2\x80\x9372 (1887). When those who appear similarly situated are nevertheless treated differently, the Equal Protection Clause requires at least a rational reason for the difference, to assure that all persons subject to legislation or regulation are indeed being \xe2\x80\x9ctreated alike, under like circumstances and conditions.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, when it appears that an individual is being singled out by the government, the specter of arbitrary classification is fairly raised, and the Equal Protection Clause requires a \xe2\x80\x9crational basis for the difference in treatment.\xe2\x80\x9d Olech, 528 U. S., at 564.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What seems to have been significant in Olech and the cases on which it relied was the existence of a clear standard against which departures, even for a single plaintiff, could be readily assessed. There was no indication in Olech that the zoning board was exercising discretionary authority based on subjective, individualized determinations\xe2\x80\x94at least not with regard to easement length, however typical such determinations may be as a general zoning matter. See id., at 565 (Breyer, J., concurring in result). Rather, the complaint alleged that the board consistently required only a 15-foot easement, but subjected Olech to a 33-foot easement. This differential treatment raised a concern of arbitrary classification, and we therefore required that the State provide a rational basis for it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Allegheny Pittsburgh, cited by the Olech Court, the applicable standard was market value, but the county departed from that standard in basing some assessments on quite dated purchase prices. Again, there was no suggestion that the \xe2\x80\x9cdramatic differences in valuation\xe2\x80\x9d for similar property parcels, 488 U. S., at 341, were based on subjective considerations of the sort on which appraisers often rely, see id., at 338\xe2\x80\x93342, 345. Sioux City Bridge, also cited in Olech, was the same sort of case, recognizing an equal protection claim when one taxpayer\xe2\x80\x99s property was assessed at 100 percent of its value, while all other property was assessed at 55 percent, without regard to articulated differences in the properties. See 260 U. S., at 445\xe2\x80\x93447.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There are some forms of state action, however, which by their nature involve discretionary decisionmaking based on a vast array of subjective, individualized assessments. In such cases the rule that people should be \xe2\x80\x9ctreated alike, under like circumstances and conditions\xe2\x80\x9d is not violated when one person is treated differently from others, because treating like individuals differently is an accepted consequence of the discretion granted. In such situations, allowing a challenge based on the arbitrary singling out of a particular person would undermine the very discretion that such state officials are entrusted to exercise.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Suppose, for example, that a traffic officer is stationed on a busy highway where people often drive above the speed limit, and there is no basis upon which to distinguish them. If the officer gives only one of those people a ticket, it may be good English to say that the officer has created a class of people that did not get speeding tickets, and a \xe2\x80\x9cclass of one\xe2\x80\x9d that did. But assuming that it is in the nature of the particular government activity that not all speeders can be stopped and ticketed, complaining that one has been singled out for no reason does not invoke the fear of improper government classification. Such a complaint, rather, challenges the legitimacy of the underlying action itself\xe2\x80\x94the decision to ticket speeders under such circumstances. Of course, an allegation that speeding tickets are given out on the basis of race or sex would state an equal protection claim, because such discriminatory classifications implicate basic equal protection concerns. But allowing an equal protection claim on the ground that a ticket was given to one person and not others, even if for no discernible or articulable reason, would be incompatible with the discretion inherent in the challenged action. It is no proper challenge to what in its nature is a subjective, individualized decision that it was subjective and individualized.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This principle applies most clearly in the employment context, for employment decisions are quite often subjective and individualized, resting on a wide array of factors that are difficult to articulate and quantify. As Engquist herself points out, \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nlike the zoning official, the public employer often must take into account the individual personalities and interpersonal relationships of employees in the workplace. The close relationship between the employer and employee, and the varied needs and interests involved in the employment context, mean that considerations such as concerns over personality conflicts that would be unreasonable as grounds for \xe2\x80\x98arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length\xe2\x80\x99 government decisions (e.g., zoning, licensing) may well justify different treatment of a public employee.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 48. Unlike the context of arm\xe2\x80\x99s-length regulation, such as in Olech, treating seemingly similarly situated individuals differently in the employment context is par for the course.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, the class-of-one theory of equal protection\xe2\x80\x94which presupposes that like individuals should be treated alike, and that to treat them differently is to classify them in a way that must survive at least rationality review\xe2\x80\x94is simply a poor fit in the public employment context. To treat employees differently is not to classify them in a way that raises equal protection concerns. Rather, it is simply to exercise the broad discretion that typically characterizes the employer-employee relationship. A challenge that one has been treated individually in this context, instead of like everyone else, is a challenge to the underlying nature of the government action.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, that is not to say that the Equal Protection Clause, like other constitutional provisions, does not apply to public employers. Indeed, our cases make clear that the Equal Protection Clause is implicated when the government makes class-based decisions in the employment context, treating distinct groups of individuals categorically differently. See, e.g., Beazer, 440 U. S., at 593 (upholding city\xe2\x80\x99s exclusion of methadone users from employment under rational-basis review); Martin, 440 U. S., at 199\xe2\x80\x93201 (classification between teachers who had complied with a continuing-education requirement and those who had not is rational and does not violate the Equal Protection Clause); Murgia, 427 U. S., at 314\xe2\x80\x93317 (upholding a mandatory retirement age\xe2\x80\x94a classification based on age\xe2\x80\x94under rational-basis review). The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s broad statement that we \xe2\x80\x9cexcep[t] state employees from the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection against unequal and irrational treatment at the hands of the State,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 2 (opinion of Stevens, J.), is thus plainly not correct. But we have never found the Equal Protection Clause implicated in the specific circumstance where, as here, government employers are alleged to have made an individualized, subjective personnel decision in a seemingly arbitrary or irrational manner.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is not surprising, given the historical understanding of the nature of government employment. We long ago recognized the \xe2\x80\x9csettled principle that government employment, in the absence of legislation, can be revoked at the will of the appointing officer.\xe2\x80\x9d McElroy, 367 U. S., at 896. The basic principle of at-will employment is that an employee may be terminated for a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98good reason, bad reason, or no reason at all.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioner 27. See Andrews v. Louisville & Nashville R. Co., 406 U. S. 320, 324 (1972) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he very concept of \xe2\x80\x98wrongful discharge\xe2\x80\x99 implies some sort of statutory or contractual standard that modifies the traditional common-law rule that a contract of employment is terminable by either party at will\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e have never held that it is a violation of the Constitution for a government employer to discharge an employee based on substantively incorrect information.\xe2\x80\x9d Waters, 511 U. S., at 679 (plurality opinion). See also Connick, 461 U. S., at 146\xe2\x80\x93147 (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]rdinary dismissals from government service \xe2\x80\xa6 are not subject to judicial review even if the reasons for the dismissal are alleged to be mistaken or unreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Board of Regents of State Colleges v. Roth, 408 U. S. 564 (1972); Perry v. Sindermann, 408 U. S. 593 (1972); and Bishop, 426 U. S. 341)). \xe2\x80\x9cAnd an at-will government employee \xe2\x80\xa6 generally has no claim based on the Constitution at all.\xe2\x80\x9d Waters, supra, at 679 (plurality opinion). See, e.g., Bishop, supra, at 349\xe2\x80\x93350.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0State employers cannot, of course, take personnel actions that would independently violate the Constitution. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x938. But recognition of a class-of-one theory of equal protection in the public employment context\xe2\x80\x94that is, a claim that the State treated an employee differently from others for a bad reason, or for no reason at all\xe2\x80\x94is simply contrary to the concept of at-will employment. The Constitution does not require repudiating that familiar doctrine.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, Congress and all the States have, for the most part, replaced at-will employment with various statutory schemes protecting public employees from discharge for impermissible reasons. See, e.g., 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72302(b)(10) (2006 ed.) (supervisor of covered federal employee may not \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate \xe2\x80\xa6 on the basis of conduct which does not adversely affect the performance of the employee or applicant or the performance of others\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 20\xe2\x80\x9321. But a government\xe2\x80\x99s decision to limit the ability of public employers to fire at will is an act of legislative grace, not constitutional mandate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, recognizing the sort of claim Engquist presses could jeopardize the delicate balance governments have struck between the rights of public employees and \xe2\x80\x9cthe government\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate purpose in \xe2\x80\x98promot[ing] efficiency and integrity in the discharge of official duties, and [in] maintain[ing] proper discipline in the public service.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Connick, supra, at 151 (quoting Ex parte Curtis, 106 U. S., at 373; alterations in original). Thus, for example, although most federal employees are covered by the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978, Pub. L. 95\xe2\x80\x93454, Congress has specifically excluded some groups of employees from its protection, see, e.g., 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72302(a)(2)(C) (2006 ed.) (excluding from coverage, inter alia, the Federal Bureau of Investigation, the Central Intelligence Agency, and the Defense Intelligence Agency). Were we to find that the Equal Protection Clause subjects the Government to equal protection review for every allegedly arbitrary employment action, we will have undone Congress\xe2\x80\x99s (and the States\xe2\x80\x99) careful work.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In concluding that the class-of-one theory of equal protection has no application in the public employment context\xe2\x80\x94and that is all we decide\xe2\x80\x94we are guided, as in the past, by the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-sense realization that government offices could not function if every employment decision became a constitutional matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Connick, supra, at 143. If, as Engquist suggests, plaintiffs need not claim discrimination on the basis of membership in some class or group, but rather may argue only that they were treated by their employers worse than other employees similarly situated, any personnel action in which a wronged employee can conjure up a claim of differential treatment will suddenly become the basis for a federal constitutional claim. Indeed, an allegation of arbitrary differential treatment could be made in nearly every instance of an assertedly wrongful employment action\xe2\x80\x94not only hiring and firing decisions, but any personnel action, such as promotion, salary, or work assignments\xe2\x80\x94on the theory that other employees were not treated wrongfully. See 478 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 995. On Engquist\xe2\x80\x99s view, every one of these employment decisions by a government employer would become the basis for an equal protection complaint.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Engquist assures us that accepting her view would not pose too much of a practical problem. Specifically, Engquist argues that a plaintiff in a class-of-one employment case would have to prove that the government\xe2\x80\x99s differential treatment was intentional, that the plaintiff was treated differently from other similarly situated persons, and that the unequal treatment was not rationally related to a legitimate government purpose. Brief for Petitioner 36\xe2\x80\x9339. And because a \xe2\x80\x9cgovernmental employment decision is \xe2\x80\xa6 rational whenever the discrimination relates to a legitimate government interest,\xe2\x80\x9d it is in practice \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult for plaintiffs to show that the government has failed to meet this standard.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 41. Justice Stevens makes a similar argument, stating \xe2\x80\x9cthat all but a handful [of class-of-one complaints] are dismissed well in advance of trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 7.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We agree that, even if we accepted Engquist\xe2\x80\x99s claim, it would be difficult for a plaintiff to show that an employment decision is arbitrary. But this submission is beside the point. The practical problem with allowing class-of-one claims to go forward in this context is not that it will be too easy for plaintiffs to prevail, but that governments will be forced to defend a multitude of such claims in the first place, and courts will be obliged to sort through them in a search for the proverbial needle in a haystack. The Equal Protection Clause does not require \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his displacement of managerial discretion by judicial supervision.\xe2\x80\x9d Garcetti v. Ceballos, 547 U. S. 410, 423 (2006).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, ratifying a class-of-one theory of equal protection in the context of public employment would impermissibly \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutionalize the employee grievance.\xe2\x80\x9d Connick, 461 U. S., at 154. \xe2\x80\x9cThe federal court is not the appropriate forum in which to review the multitude of personnel decisions that are made daily by public agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d Bishop, supra, at 349. Public employees typically have a variety of protections from just the sort of personnel actions about which Engquist complains, but the Equal Protection Clause is not one of them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.

\nENGQUIST V. OREGON DEPT. OF AGRICULTURE
\n553 U. S. ____ (2008)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 07-474
\n
\n

ANUP ENGQUIST, PETITIONER v. OREGON\nDEPARTMENT OF AGRICULTURE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 9, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Souter and Justice Ginsburg join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress has provided a judicial remedy for individuals whose federal constitutional rights are violated by state action, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983.[Footnote 1] In prior cases, we have refused to craft new remedies for the violation of constitutional rights of federal employees, Bush v. Lucas, 462 U. S. 367 (1983), or for the nonconstitutional claims of state employees, Bishop v. Wood, 426 U. S. 341 (1976). But refusal to give effect to the congressionally mandated remedy embodied in \xc2\xa71983 would be impermissible. To avoid this result, the Court today concludes that Engquist suffered no constitutional violation at all, and that there was thus no harm to be remedied. In so holding, the Court\xe2\x80\x94as it did in Garcetti v. Ceballos, 547 U. S. 410 (2006)\xe2\x80\x94carves a novel exception out of state employees\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional rights. In Garcetti, the Court created a new substantive rule excepting a category of speech by state employees from the protection of the First Amendment. Today, the Court creates a new substantive rule excepting state employees from the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection against unequal and irrational treatment at the hands of the State. Even if some surgery were truly necessary to prevent governments from being forced to defend a multitude of equal protection \xe2\x80\x9cclass of one\xe2\x80\x9d claims, the Court should use a scalpel rather than a meat-axe.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decision in Village of Willowbrook v. Olech, 528 U. S. 562 (2000) (per curiam), applied a rule that had been an accepted part of our equal protection jurisprudence for decades: Unless state action that intentionally singles out an individual, or a class of individuals, for adverse treatment is supported by some rational justification, it violates the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s command that no State shall \xe2\x80\x9cdeny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our opinion in Olech emphasized that the legal issue would have been the same whether the class consisted of one or five members, because \xe2\x80\x9cthe number of individuals in a class is immaterial for equal protection analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 564, n. The outcome of that case was not determined by the size of the disadvantaged class, and the majority does not\xe2\x80\x94indeed cannot\xe2\x80\x94dispute the settled principle that the Equal Protection Clause protects persons, not groups. See ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor did the outcome in Olech turn on the fact that the Village was discriminating against a property owner rather than an employee. The majority does not dispute that the strictures of the Equal Protection Clause apply to the States in their role as employers as well as regulators. See ante, at 5. And indeed, we have made clear that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Equal Protection and Due Process Clauses of the Fourteenth Amendment, and other provisions of the Federal Constitution afford protection to employees who serve the government as well as to those who are served by them, and \xc2\xa71983 provides a cause of action for all citizens injured by an abridgment of those protections.\xe2\x80\x9d Collins v. Harker Heights, 503 U. S. 115, 119\xe2\x80\x93120 (1992).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rather, the outcome of Olech was dictated solely by the absence of a rational basis for the discrimination. As we explained:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cOur cases have recognized successful equal protection claims brought by a \xe2\x80\x98class of one,\xe2\x80\x99 where the plaintiff alleges that she has been intentionally treated differently from others similarly situated and that there is no rational basis for the difference in treatment. In so doing, we have explained that \xe2\x80\x98[t]he purpose of the equal protection clause of the Fourteenth Amendment is to secure every person within the State\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction against intentional and arbitrary discrimination, whether occasioned by express terms of a statute or by its improper execution through duly constituted agents.\xe2\x80\x99

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c[Olech\xe2\x80\x99s] complaint also alleged that the Village\xe2\x80\x99s demand was \xe2\x80\x98irrational and wholly arbitrary\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. These allegations, quite apart from the Village\xe2\x80\x99s subjective motivation, are sufficient to state a claim for relief under traditional equal protection analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d 528 U. S., at 564, 565 (some internal quotation marks and citations omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, as in Olech, Engquist alleged that the State\xe2\x80\x99s actions were arbitrary and irrational. In response, the State offered no explanation whatsoever for its decisions; it did not claim that Engquist was a subpar worker, or even that her personality made her a poor fit in the workplace or that her colleagues simply did not enjoy working with her. In fact, the State explicitly disclaimed the existence of any workplace or performance-based rationale.[Footnote 2] See, e.g., Reply Brief for Petitioner 17, 19. The jury proceeded to find that the respondents intentionally treated Engquist \xe2\x80\x9cdifferently than others similarly situated with respect to the \xe2\x80\xa6 termination of her employment \xe2\x80\xa6 without any rational basis and solely for arbitrary, vindictive or malicious reasons.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 3\xe2\x80\x934. The jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict thus established that there was no rational basis for either treating Engquist differently from other employees or for the termination of her employment. The State does not dispute this finding. Under our reasoning in Olech, the absence of any justification for the discrimination sufficed to establish the constitutional violation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority nonetheless concludes, based on \xe2\x80\x9cunique considerations applicable when the government acts as employer,\xe2\x80\x9d that the \xe2\x80\x9cclass of one\xe2\x80\x9d theory of equal protection is not applicable in the public employment context. Ante, at 5. Its conclusion is based upon speculation about inapt hypothetical cases, and an incorrect evaluation of the importance of the government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in preserving a regime of \xe2\x80\x9cat will\xe2\x80\x9d employment. Its reasoning is flawed on both counts.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority asserts that public-employment decisions should be carved out of our equal protection jurisprudence because employment decisions (as opposed to, for example, zoning decisions) are inherently discretionary. I agree that employers must be free to exercise discretionary authority. But there is a clear distinction between an exercise of discretion and an arbitrary decision. A discretionary decision represents a choice of one among two or more rational alternatives. See 1 H. Hart & A. Sacks, The Legal Process: Basic Problems in the Making and Application of Law 162 (Tent. ed. 1958) (defining discretion as \xe2\x80\x9cthe power to choose between two or more courses of action each of which is thought of as permissible\xe2\x80\x9d). The choice may be mistaken or unwise without being irrational. If the arguments favoring each alternative are closely balanced, the need to make a choice may justify using a coin toss as a tie breaker. Moreover, the Equal Protection Clause proscribes arbitrary decisions\xe2\x80\x94decisions unsupported by any rational basis\xe2\x80\x94not unwise ones. Accordingly, a discretionary decision with any \xe2\x80\x9creasonably conceivable\xe2\x80\x9d rational justification will not support an equal protection claim; only a truly arbitrary one will. There is therefore no need to create an exception for the public-employment context in order to prevent these discretionary decisions from giving rise equal protection claims.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The hypothetical situations posited by the majority do not prove otherwise. The hypothetical traffic officer described in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, ante, at 11, had a rational basis for giving a ticket to every speeder passing him on the highway. His inability to arrest every driver in sight provides an adequate justification for making a random choice from a group of equally guilty and equally accessible violators. As such, the Court is quite correct in stating that \xe2\x80\x9callowing an equal protection claim on the ground that a ticket was given to one person and not others, even if for no discernible or articulable reason, would be incompatible with the discretion inherent in the challenged action.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. If there were no justification for the arrest, there would be no need to invoke the Equal Protection Clause because the officer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct would violate the Fourth Amendment. But as noted, a random choice among rational alternatives does not violate the Equal Protection Clause.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A comparable hypothetical decision in the employment context (e.g., a supervisor who is required to eliminate one position due to an involuntary reduction-in-force and who chooses to terminate one of several equally culpable employees) also differs from the instant case insofar as it assumes the existence of a rational basis for the individual decision. The fact that a supervisor might not be able to explain why he terminated one employee rather than another will not give rise to an equal protection claim so long as there was a rational basis for the termination itself and for the decision to terminate just one, rather than all, of the culpable employees.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead of using a scalpel to confine so-called \xe2\x80\x9cclass of one\xe2\x80\x9d claims to cases involving a complete absence of any conceivable rational basis for the adverse action and the differential treatment of the plaintiff, the Court adopts an unnecessarily broad rule that tolerates arbitrary and irrational decisions in the employment context.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision also rests on the premise that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Constitution does not require repudiating th[e] familiar doctrine\xe2\x80\x9d of at-will employment. Ante, at 14. In the 1890\xe2\x80\x99s that doctrine applied broadly to government employment, see McAuliffe v. Mayor of New Bedford, 155 Mass. 216, 29 N. E. 517 (1892), but for many years now \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the theory that public employment which may be denied altogether may be subjected to any conditions, regardless of how unreasonable, has been uniformly rejected.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Keyishian v. Board of Regents of Univ. of State of N. Y., 385 U. S. 589, 605\xe2\x80\x93606 (1967). Indeed, recent constitutional decisions and statutory enactments have all but nullified the significance of the doctrine. See, e.g., Elrod v. Burns, 427 U. S. 347 (1976); Rutan v. Republican Party of Ill., 497 U. S. 62 (1990); see also 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72302(b)(10) (2006 ed.) (supervisor of covered federal employee may not \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminate \xe2\x80\xa6 on the basis of conduct which does not adversely affect the performance of the employee or applicant or the performance of others\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, preserving the remnants of \xe2\x80\x9cat-will\xe2\x80\x9d employment provides a feeble justification for creating a broad exception to a well-established category of constitutional protections.[Footnote 3]

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Presumably the concern that actually motivates today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is fear that governments will be forced to defend against a multitude of \xe2\x80\x9cclass of one\xe2\x80\x9d claims unless the Court wields its meat-axe forthwith. Experience demonstrates, however, that these claims are brought infrequently,[Footnote 4] that the vast majority of such claims are asserted in complaints advancing other claims as well, and that all but a handful are dismissed well in advance of trial. Experience also demonstrates that there are in fact rare cases in which a petty tyrant has misused governmental power. Proof that such misuse was arbitrary because unsupported by any conceivable rational basis should suffice to establish a violation of the Equal Protection Clause without requiring its victim also to prove that the tyrant was motivated by a particular variety of class-based animus. When the allegations of a complaint plainly identify \xe2\x80\x9cthe proverbial needle in a haystack,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 16, a federal court should not misconstrue the Constitution in order to make it even easier to dismiss unmeritorious claims.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, there is no compelling reason to carve arbitrary public-employment decisions out of the well-established category of equal protection violations when the familiar rational review standard can sufficiently limit these claims to only wholly unjustified employment actions. Accordingly, I respectfully dissent.
