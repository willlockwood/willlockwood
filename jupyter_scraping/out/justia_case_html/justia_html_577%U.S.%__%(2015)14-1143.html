SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

CHADRIN LEE MULLENIX v. BEATRICE LUNA, individually and as representative of theESTATE OF ISRAEL LEIJA, JR., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x931143.\xe2\x80\x83Decided November 9, 2015

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

On the night of March 23, 2010, Sergeant Randy Baker of the Tulia, Texas Police Department followed Israel Leija, Jr., to a drive-in restaurant, with a warrant for his arrest. 773 F. 3d 712, 715\xe2\x80\x93716 (CA5 2014). When Baker approached Leija\xe2\x80\x99s car and informed him that he was under arrest, Leija sped off, headed for Interstate 27. 2013 WL 4017124, *1 (ND Tex., Aug. 7, 2013). Baker gave chase and was quickly joined by Trooper Gabriel Rodriguez of the Texas Department of Public Safety (DPS). 773 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 716.

\n

Leija entered the interstate and led the officers on an 18-minute chase at speeds between 85 and 110 miles per hour. Ibid. Twice during the chase, Leija called the Tulia Police dispatcher, claiming to have a gun and threatening to shoot at police officers if they did not abandon their pursuit. The dispatcher relayed Leija\xe2\x80\x99s threats, together with a report that Leija might be intoxicated, to all concerned officers.

\n

As Baker and Rodriguez maintained their pursuit, other law enforcement officers set up tire spikes at three locations. Officer Troy Ducheneaux of the Canyon Police Department manned the spike strip at the first location Leija was expected to reach, beneath the overpass at Cemetery Road. Ducheneaux and the other officers had received training on the deployment of spike strips, including on how to take a defensive position so as to minimize the risk posed by the passing driver. Ibid.

\n

DPS Trooper Chadrin Mullenix also responded. He drove to the Cemetery Road overpass, initially intending to set up a spike strip there. Upon learning of the other spike strip positions, however, Mullenix began to consider another tactic: shooting at Leija\xe2\x80\x99s car in order to disable it. 2013 WL 4017124, *1. Mullenix had not received training in this tactic and had not attempted it before, but he radioed the idea to Rodriguez. Rodriguez responded \xe2\x80\x9c10\xe2\x80\x934,\xe2\x80\x9d gave Mullenix his position, and said that Leija had slowed to 85 miles per hour. Mullenix then asked the DPS dispatcher to inform his supervisor, Sergeant Byrd, of his plan and ask if Byrd thought it was \xe2\x80\x9cworth doing.\xe2\x80\x9d 773 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 716\xe2\x80\x93717. Before receiving Byrd\xe2\x80\x99s response, Mullenix exited his vehicle and, armed with his service rifle, took a shooting position on the overpass, 20 feet above I\xe2\x80\x9327. Respondents allege that from this position, Mullenix still could hear Byrd\xe2\x80\x99s response to \xe2\x80\x9cstand by\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csee if the spikes work first.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[1]*

\n

As Mullenix waited for Leija to arrive, he and another officer, Randall County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Deputy Tom Shipman, discussed whether Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s plan would work and how and where to shoot the vehicle to best carry it out. 2013 WL 4017124, *2. Shipman also informed Mullenix that another officer was located beneath the overpass. 773 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 717.

\n

Approximately three minutes after Mullenix took up his shooting position, he spotted Leija\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle, with Rodriguez in pursuit. As Leija approached the overpass, Mullenix fired six shots. Leija\xe2\x80\x99s car continued forward beneath the overpass, where it engaged the spike strip, hit the median, and rolled two and a half times. It was later determined that Leija had been killed by Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s shots, four of which struck his upper body. There was no evidence that any of Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s shots hit the car\xe2\x80\x99s radiator, hood, or engine block. Id., at 716\xe2\x80\x93717; 2013 WL 4017124, *2\xe2\x80\x93*3.

\n

Respondents sued Mullenix under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979,\n\n42 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71983, alleging that he had violated the\n \nFourth Amendment by using excessive force against Leija. Mullenix moved for summary judgment on the ground of qualified immunity, but the District Court denied his motion, finding that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here are genuine issues of fact as to whether Trooper Mullenix acted recklessly, or acted as a reasonable, trained peace officer would have acted in the same or similar circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d 2013 WL 4017124, *6.

\n

Mullenix appealed, and the Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit affirmed. 765 F. 3d 531 (2014). The court agreed with the District Court that the \xe2\x80\x9cimmediacy of the risk posed by Leija is a disputed fact that a reasonable jury could find either in the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 favor or in the officer\xe2\x80\x99s favor, precluding us from concluding that Mullenix acted objectively reasonably as a matter of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 538.

\n

Judge King dissented. She described the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fact issue\xe2\x80\x99 referenced by the majority\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9csimply a restatement of the objective reasonableness test that applies to\n \nFourth Amendment excessive force claims,\xe2\x80\x9d which, she noted, the Supreme Court has held \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is a pure question of law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 544\xe2\x80\x93545 (quoting Scott v. Harris,\n\n550 U. S. 372,\n381, n.\n8 (2007)\n). Turning to that legal question, Judge King concluded that Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s actions were objectively reasonable. When Mullenix fired, she emphasized, he knew not only that Leija had threatened to shoot the officers involved in his pursuit, but also that Leija was seconds away from encountering such an officer beneath the overpass. Judge King also dismissed the notion that Mullenix should have given the spike strips a chance to work. She explained that because spike strips are often ineffective, and because officers operating them are vulnerable to gunfire from passing cars, Mullenix reasonably feared that the officers manning them faced a significant risk of harm. 765 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 548\xe2\x80\x93549.

\n

Mullenix sought rehearing en banc before the Fifth Circuit, but the court denied his petition. Judge Jolly dissented, joined by six other members of the court. Judge King, who joined Judge Jolly\xe2\x80\x99s dissent, also filed a separate dissent of her own. 777 F. 3d 221 (2014) (\xc2\xa0per curiam). On the same day, however, the two members forming the original panel\xe2\x80\x99s majority withdrew their previous opinion and substituted a new one. 773 F.\xc2\xa03d 712. The revised opinion recognized that objective unreasonableness is a question of law that can be resolved on summary judgment\xe2\x80\x94as Judge King had explained in her dissent\xe2\x80\x94but reaffirmed the denial of qualified immunity. Id., at 715, 718. The majority concluded that Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s actions were objectively unreasonable because several of the factors that had justified deadly force in previous cases were absent here: There were no innocent bystanders, Leija\xe2\x80\x99s driving was relatively controlled, Mullenix had not first given the spike strips a chance to work, and Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s decision was not a split-second judgment. Id., at 720\xe2\x80\x93724. The court went on to conclude that Mullenix was not entitled to qualified immunity because \xe2\x80\x9cthe law was clearly established such that a reasonable officer would have known that the use of deadly force, absent a sufficiently substantial and immediate threat, violated the\n \nFourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 725.

\n

We address only the qualified immunity question, not whether there was a\n \nFourth Amendment violation in the first place, and now reverse.

\n

The doctrine of qualified immunity shields officials from civil liability so long as their conduct \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98does not violate clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Pearson v. Callahan,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 223,\n231 (2009)\n (quoting Harlow v. Fitzgerald,\n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800,\n818 (1982)\n). A clearly established right is one that is \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently clear that every reasonable official would have understood that what he is doing violates that right.\xe2\x80\x9d Reichle v. Howards, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 5) (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted). \xe2\x80\x9cWe do not require a case directly on point, but existing precedent must have placed the statutory or constitutional question beyond debate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft v. al-Kidd,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 731,\n741 (2011)\n. Put simply, qualified immunity protects \xe2\x80\x9call but the plainly incompetent or those who knowingly violate the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Malley v. Briggs,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335,\n341 (1986)\n.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWe have repeatedly told courts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not to define clearly established law at a high level of generality.\xe2\x80\x9d al-Kidd, supra, at 742. The dispositive question is \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the violative nature of particular conduct is clearly established.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). This inquiry \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98must be undertaken in light of the specific context of the case, not as a broad general proposition.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brosseau v. Haugen,\n\n543 U. S. 194,\n198 (2004)\n (per curiam) (quoting Saucier v. Katz,\n\n533 U. S. 194,\n201 (2001)\n). Such specificity is especially important in the\n \nFourth Amendment context, where the Court has recognized that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is sometimes difficult for an officer to determine how the relevant legal doctrine, here excessive force, will apply to the factual situation the officer confronts.\xe2\x80\x9d 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 205.

\n

In this case, the Fifth Circuit held that Mullenix violated the clearly established rule that a police officer may not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98use deadly force against a fleeing felon who does not pose a sufficient threat of harm to the officer or others.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 773 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 725. Yet this Court has previously considered\xe2\x80\x94and rejected\xe2\x80\x94almost that exact formulation of the qualified immunity question in the\n \nFourth Amendment context. In Brosseau, which also involved the shooting of a suspect fleeing by car, the Ninth Circuit denied qualified immu-nity on the ground that the officer had violated the clearly established rule, set forth in Tennessee v. Garner,\n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1985)\n, that \xe2\x80\x9cdeadly force is only permissible where the officer has probable cause to believe that the suspect poses a threat of serious physical harm, either to the officer or to others.\xe2\x80\x9d Haugen v. Brosseau, 339 F. 3d 857, 873 (CA9 2003) (internal quotation marks omitted). This Court summarily reversed, holding that use of Gar-ner\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral\xe2\x80\x9d test for excessive force was \xe2\x80\x9cmistaken.\xe2\x80\x9d Brosseau, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 199. The correct inquiry, the Court explained, was whether it was clearly established that the\n \nFourth Amendment prohibited the officer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct in the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98situation [she] confronted\xe2\x80\x99: whether to shoot a disturbed felon, set on avoiding capture through vehicular flight, when persons in the immediate area are at risk from that flight.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 199\xe2\x80\x93200. The Court considered three court of appeals cases discussed by the parties, noted that \xe2\x80\x9cthis area is one in which the result depends very much on the facts of each case,\xe2\x80\x9d and concluded that the officer was entitled to qualified immunity because \xe2\x80\x9c[n]one of [the cases] squarely governs the case here.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 201 (emphasis added).

\n

Anderson v. Creighton,\n\n483 U. S. 635 (1987)\n, is also instructive on the required degree of specificity. There, the lower court had denied qualified immunity based on the clearly established \xe2\x80\x9cright to be free from warrantless searches of one\xe2\x80\x99s home unless the searching officers have probable cause and there are exigent circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 640. This Court faulted that formulation for failing to address the actual question at issue: whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe circumstances with which Anderson was confronted .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. constitute[d] probable cause and exigent circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 640\xe2\x80\x93641. Without answering that question, the Court explained, the conclusion that Anderson\xe2\x80\x99s search was objectively unreasonable did not \xe2\x80\x9cfollow immediately\xe2\x80\x9d from\xe2\x80\x94and thus was not clearly established by\xe2\x80\x94the principle that warrantless searches not supported by probable cause and exigent circumstances violate the\n \nFourth Amendment. Id., at 641.

\n

In this case, Mullenix confronted a reportedly intoxi-cated fugitive, set on avoiding capture through high-speed vehicular flight, who twice during his flight had threatened to shoot police officers, and who was moments away from encountering an officer at Cemetery Road. The relevant inquiry is whether existing precedent placed the conclusion that Mullenix acted unreasonably in these circumstances \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond debate.\xe2\x80\x9d al-Kidd, supra, at 741. The general principle that deadly force requires a sufficient threat hardly settles this matter. See Pasco v. Knoblauch, 566 F.\xc2\xa03d 572, 580 (CA5 2009) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t would be unreasonable to expect a police officer to make the numerous legal conclusions necessary to apply Garner to a high-speed car chase .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Far from clarifying the issue, excessive force cases involving car chases reveal the hazy legal backdrop against which Mullenix acted. In Brosseau itself, the Court held that an officer did not violate clearly established law when she shot a fleeing suspect out of fear that he endangered \xe2\x80\x9cother officers on foot who [she] believed were in the immediate area,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe occupied vehicles in [his] path,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cany other citizens who might be in the area.\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 197 (first alteration in original; internal quotation marks omitted; emphasis added). The threat Leija posed was at least as immediate as that presented by a suspect who had just begun to drive off and was headed only in the general direction of officers and bystanders. Id., at 196\xe2\x80\x93197. By the time Mullenix fired, Leija had led police on a 25-mile chase at extremely high speeds, was reportedly intoxicated, had twice threatened to shoot officers, and was racing towards an officer\xe2\x80\x99s location.

\n

This Court has considered excessive force claims in connection with high-speed chases on only two occasions since Brosseau. In Scott v. Harris,\n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 372\n, the Court held that an officer did not violate the\n \nFourth Amendment by ramming the car of a fugitive whose reckless driving \xe2\x80\x9cposed an actual and imminent threat to the lives of any pedestrians who might have been present, to other civilian motorists, and to the officers involved in the chase.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 384. And in Plumhoff v. Rickard, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), the Court reaffirmed Scott by holding that an officer acted reasonably when he fatally shot a fugitive who was \xe2\x80\x9cintent on resuming\xe2\x80\x9d a chase that \xe2\x80\x9cpose[d] a deadly threat for others on the road.\xe2\x80\x9d 572 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10). The Court has thus never found the use of deadly force in connection with a dangerous car chase to violate the\n \nFourth Amendment, let alone to be a basis for denying qualified immunity. Leija in his flight did not pass as many cars as the drivers in Scott or Plumhoff; traffic was light on I\xe2\x80\x9327. At the same time, the fleeing fugitives in Scott and Plumhoff had not verbally threatened to kill any officers in their path, nor were they about to come upon such officers. In any event, none of our precedents \xe2\x80\x9csquarely governs\xe2\x80\x9d the facts here. Given Leija\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, we cannot say that only someone \xe2\x80\x9cplainly incompetent\xe2\x80\x9d or who \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly violate[s] the law\xe2\x80\x9d would have perceived a sufficient threat and acted as Mullenix did. Malley, 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 341.

\n

The dissent focuses on the availability of spike strips as an alternative means of terminating the chase. It argues that even if Leija posed a threat sufficient to justify deadly force in some circumstances, Mullenix nevertheless contravened clearly established law because he did not wait to see if the spike strips would work before taking action. Spike strips, however, present dangers of their own, not only to drivers who encounter them at speeds between 85 and 110 miles per hour, but also to officers manning them. See, e.g., Thompson v. Mercer, 762 F.\xc2\xa03d 433, 440 (CA5 2014); Brief for National Association of Police Organizations et al. as Amici Curiae 15\xe2\x80\x9316. Nor are spike strips always successful in ending the chase. See, e.g., Cordova v. Aragon, 569 F.\xc2\xa03d 1183, 1186 (CA10 2009); Brief for National Association of Police Organizations et al. as Amici Curiae 16 (citing examples). The dissent can cite no case from this Court denying qualified immunity because officers entitled to terminate a high-speed chase selected one dangerous alternative over another.

\n

Even so, the dissent argues, there was no governmental interest that justified acting before Leija\xe2\x80\x99s car hit the spikes. Mullenix explained, however, that he feared Leija might attempt to shoot at or run over the officers manning the spike strips. Mullenix also feared that even if Leija hit the spike strips, he might still be able to continue driving in the direction of other officers. The dissent ignores these interests by suggesting that there was no \xe2\x80\x9cpossible marginal gain in shooting at the car over using the spike strips already in place.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 4 (opinion of Sotomayor, J.). In fact, Mullenix hoped his actions would stop the car in a manner that avoided the risks to other officers and other drivers that relying on spike strips would entail. The dissent disputes the merits of the options available to Mullenix, post, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, but others with more experience analyze the issues differently. See, e.g., Brief for National Association of Police Organizations et al. as Amici Curiae 15\xe2\x80\x9316. Ultimately, whatever can be said of the wisdom of Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s choice, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents do not place the conclusion that he acted unreasonably in these circumstances \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond debate.\xe2\x80\x9d al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 741.

\n

More fundamentally, the dissent repeats the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s error. It defines the qualified immunity inquiry at a high level of generality\xe2\x80\x94whether any governmental interest justified choosing one tactic over another\xe2\x80\x94and then fails to consider that question in \xe2\x80\x9cthe specific context of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d Brosseau v. Haugen, 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 198 (internal quotation marks omitted). As in Anderson, the conclusion that Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s reasons were insufficient to justify his actions simply does not \xe2\x80\x9cfollow immediately\xe2\x80\x9d from the general proposition that force must be justified. 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 641.

\n

Cases decided by the lower courts since Brosseau likewise have not clearly established that deadly force is inappropriate in response to conduct like Leija\xe2\x80\x99s. The Fifth Circuit here principally relied on its own decision in Lytle v. Bexar County, 560 F. 3d 404 (2009), denying qualified immunity to a police officer who had fired at a fleeing car and killed one of its passengers. That holding turned on the court\xe2\x80\x99s assumption, for purposes of summary judgment, that the car was moving away from the officer and had already traveled some distance at the moment the officer fired. See id., at 409. The court held that a reasonable jury could conclude that a receding car \xe2\x80\x9cdid not pose a sufficient threat of harm such that the use of deadly force was reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 416. But, crucially, the court also recognized that if the facts were as the officer alleged, and he fired as the car was coming towards him, \xe2\x80\x9che would likely be entitled to qualified immunity\xe2\x80\x9d based on the \xe2\x80\x9cthreat of immediate and severe physical harm.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 412. Without implying that Lytle was either correct or incorrect, it suffices to say that Lytle does not clearly dictate the conclusion that Mullenix was unjustified in perceiving grave danger and responding accordingly, given that Leija was speeding towards a confrontation with officers he had threatened to kill.

\n

Cases that the Fifth Circuit ignored also suggest that Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of the threat Leija posed was reasonable. In Long v. Slaton, 508 F. 3d 576 (2007), for example, the Eleventh Circuit held that a sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s deputy did not violate the\n \nFourth Amendment by fatally shooting a mentally unstable individual who was attempting to flee in the deputy\xe2\x80\x99s car, even though at the time of the shooting the individual had not yet operated the cruiser dangerously. The court explained that \xe2\x80\x9cthe law does not require officers in a tense and dangerous situation to wait until the moment a suspect uses a deadly weapon to act to stop the suspect\xe2\x80\x9d and concluded that the deputy had reason to believe Long was dangerous based on his unstable state of mind, theft of the cruiser, and failure to heed the deputy\xe2\x80\x99s warning to stop. Id., at 581\xe2\x80\x93582. The court also rejected the notion that the deputy should have first tried less lethal methods, such as spike strips. \xe2\x80\x9c[C]onsidering the unpredictability of Long\xe2\x80\x99s behavior and his fleeing in a marked police cruiser,\xe2\x80\x9d the court held, \xe2\x80\x9cwe think the police need not have taken that chance and hoped for the best.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 583 (alteration and internal quotation marks omitted). But see Smith v. Cupp, 430 F.\xc2\xa03d 766, 774\xe2\x80\x93777 (CA6 2005) (denying qualified immunity to an officer who shot an intoxicated suspect who had stolen the officer\xe2\x80\x99s cruiser where a reasonable jury could have concluded that the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s flight did not immediately threaten the officer or any other bystander).

Other cases cited by the Fifth Circuit and respondents are simply too factually distinct to speak clearly to the specific circumstances here. Several involve suspects who may have done little more than flee at relatively low speeds. See, e.g., Walker v. Davis, 649 F. 3d 502, 503 (CA6 2011); Kirby v. Duva, 530 F. 3d 475, 479\xe2\x80\x93480 (CA6 2008); Adams v. Speers, 473 F. 3d 989, 991 (CA9 2007); Vaughan v. Cox, 343 F. 3d 1323, 1330\xe2\x80\x931331, and n. 7 (CA11 2003). These cases shed little light on whether the far greater danger of a speeding fugitive threatening to kill police officers waiting in his path could warrant deadly force. The court below noted that \xe2\x80\x9cno weapon was ever seen,\xe2\x80\x9d 773 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 723, but surely in these circumstances the police were justified in taking Leija at his word when he twice told the dispatcher he had a gun and was prepared to use it.

\n

Finally, respondents argue that the danger Leija represented was less substantial than the threats that courts have found sufficient to justify deadly force. But the mere fact that courts have approved deadly force in more extreme circumstances says little, if anything, about whether such force was reasonable in the circumstances here. The fact is that when Mullenix fired, he reasonably understood Leija to be a fugitive fleeing arrest, at speeds over 100 miles per hour, who was armed and possibly intoxicated, who had threatened to kill any officer he saw if the police did not abandon their pursuit, and who was racing towards Officer Ducheneaux\xe2\x80\x99s position. Even accepting that these circumstances fall somewhere between the two sets of cases respondents discuss, qualified immunity protects actions in the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98hazy border between excessive and acceptable force.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brosseau, supra, at 201 (quoting Saucier, 533 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 206; some internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Because the constitutional rule applied by the Fifth Circuit was not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98beyond debate,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Stanton v. Sims, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (per curiam) (slip op., at 8), we grant Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari and reverse the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s determination that Mullenix is not entitled to qualified immunity.

\n

It is so ordered.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

No. 14\xe2\x80\x931143.\xe2\x80\x83Decided November 9, 2015

\n

\n

Justice Scalia, concurring in the judgment.

\n

I join the judgment of the Court, but would not describe what occurred here as the application of deadly force in effecting an arrest. Our prior cases have reserved that description to the directing of force sufficient to kill at the person of the desired arrestee. See, e.g., Plumhoff v. Rickard, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014); Brosseau v. Haugen,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 194 (2004)\n (per curiam); Tennessee v. Garner,\n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1985)\n. It does not assist analysis to refer to all use of force that happens to kill the arrestee as the application of deadly force. The police might, for example, attempt to stop a fleeing felon\xe2\x80\x99s car by felling a large tree across the road; if they drop the tree too late, so that it crushes the car and its occupant, I would not call that the application of deadly force. Though it was force sufficient to kill, it was not applied with the object of harming the body of the felon.

\n

Thus, in Scott v. Harris,\n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 372 (2007)\n, we declined to characterize officer Scott\xe2\x80\x99s use of his pursuing vehicle\xe2\x80\x99s bumper to push the fleeing vehicle off the road as the application of deadly force. Whether or not it was that, we said, \xe2\x80\x9call that matters is whether Scott\xe2\x80\x99s actions were reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 383. So also here. But it stacks the deck against the officer, it seems to me, to describe his action as the application of deadly force.

\n

It was at least arguable in Scott that pushing a speeding vehicle off the road is targeting its occupant for injury or death. Here, however, it is conceded that Trooper Mullenix did not shoot to wound or kill the fleeing Leija, nor even to drive Leija\xe2\x80\x99s car off the road, but only to cause the car to stop by destroying its engine. That was a risky enterprise, as the outcome demonstrated; but determining whether it violated the\n \nFourth Amendment requires us to ask, not whether it was reasonable to kill Leija, but whether it was reasonable to shoot at the engine in light of the risk to Leija. It distorts that inquiry, I think, to make the question whether it was reasonable for Mullenix to \xe2\x80\x9capply deadly force.\xe2\x80\x9d

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

CHADRIN LEE MULLENIX v. BEATRICE LUNA, individually and as representative of theESTATE OF ISRAEL LEIJA, JR., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x931143.\xe2\x80\x83Decided November 9, 2015

\n

Justice Sotomayor, dissenting.

\n

Chadrin Mullenix fired six rounds in the dark at a car traveling 85 miles per hour. He did so without any training in that tactic, against the wait order of his superior officer, and less than a second before the car hit spike strips deployed to stop it. Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s rogue conduct killed the driver, Israel Leija, Jr. Because it was clearly established under the\n \nFourth Amendment that an officer in Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s position should not have fired the shots, I respectfully dissent from the grant of summary reversal.

\n

I

\n

Resolving all factual disputes in favor of plaintiffs, as the Court must on a motion for summary judgment, Mullenix knew the following facts before he shot at Leija\xe2\x80\x99s engine block: Leija had led police officers on an 18-minute car chase, at speeds ranging from 85 to 110 miles per hour. 773 F.\xc2\xa03d 712, 716 (CA5 2014). Leija had twice called the police dispatcher threatening to shoot at officers if they did not cease the pursuit. Ibid. Police officers were deploying three sets of spike strips in order to stop Leija\xe2\x80\x99s flight. Ibid. The officers were trained to stop a car using spike strips. This training included how to take a defensive position to minimize the risk of danger from the target car. Ibid. Mullenix knew that spike strips were being set up directly beneath the overpass where he was stationed. Id., at 723. There is no evidence below that any of the officers with whom Mullenix was in communication\xe2\x80\x94including Officer Troy Ducheneaux, whom Mullenix believed to be below the overpass\xe2\x80\x94had expressed any concern for their safety. Id., at 720.

\n

Mullenix had no training in shooting to disable a moving vehicle and had never seen the tactic done before. Id., at 716. He also lacked permission to take the shots: When Mullenix relayed his plan to his superior officer, Robert Byrd, Byrd responded \xe2\x80\x9cstand by\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9csee if the spikes work first.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 716\xe2\x80\x93717. Three minutes after arriving at the overpass, Mullenix fired six rounds at Leija\xe2\x80\x99s car. None hit the car\xe2\x80\x99s engine block; at least four struck Leija in the upper body, killing Leija. Id., at 717.

\n

II

\n

When confronting a claim of qualified immunity, a court asks two questions. First, the court considers whether the officer in fact violated a constitutional right. Saucier v. Katz,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 194,\n201 (2001)\n. Second, the court asks whether the contours of the right were \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently clear that a reasonable official would [have understood] that what he is doing violates that right.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 202 (quoting Anderson v. Creighton,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635,\n640 (1987)\n). This Court has rejected the idea that \xe2\x80\x9can official action is protected by qualified immunity unless the very action in question has previously been held unlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 640. Instead, the crux of the qualified immunity test is whether officers have \xe2\x80\x9cfair notice\xe2\x80\x9d that they are acting unconstitutionally. Hope v. Pelzer,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 730,\n739 (2002)\n.

\n

Respondents here allege that Mullenix violated the\n \nFourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on unreasonable seizures by using deadly force to apprehend Leija. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents clearly establish that the\n \nFourth Amendment is violated unless the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98governmental interests\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in effectuating a particular kind of seizure outweigh the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98nature and quality of the intrusion on the individual\xe2\x80\x99s Fourth Amendment interests.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Scott v. Harris,\n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 372,\n383 (2007)\n (quoting United States v. Place,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 696,\n703 (1983)\n). There must be a \xe2\x80\x9cgovernmental interes[t]\xe2\x80\x9d not only in effectuating a seizure, but also in \xe2\x80\x9chow [the seizure] is carried out.\xe2\x80\x9d Tennessee v. Garner,\n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n8 (1985)\n.

\n

Balancing a particular governmental interest in the use of deadly force against the intrusion occasioned by the use of that force is inherently a fact-specific inquiry, not susceptible to bright lines. But it is clearly established that the government must have some interest in using deadly force over other kinds of force.

\n

Here, then, the clearly established legal question\xe2\x80\x94the question a reasonable officer would have asked\xe2\x80\x94is whether,under all the circumstances as known to Mullenix, there was a governmental interest in shooting at the car rather than waiting for it to run over spike strips.

\n

The majority does not point to any such interest here. It claims that Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s goal was not merely to stop the car, but to stop the car \xe2\x80\x9cin a manner that avoided the risks\xe2\x80\x9d of relying on spike strips. Ante, at 9. But there is no evidence in the record that shooting at Leija\xe2\x80\x99s engine block would stop the car in such a manner.

\n

The majority first suggests that Mullenix did not wait for the results of the spikes, as his superior advised, because of his concern for the officers manning the strips. But Leija was going to come upon those officers whether or not Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s shooting tactic was successful: Mullenix took his shot when Leija was between 25 and 30 yards away from the spike strip, traveling at 85 miles per hour. Even if his shots hit Leija\xe2\x80\x99s engine block, the car would not have stopped instantly. Mullenix would have bought the officers he was trying to protect\xe2\x80\x94officers who had been trained to take defensive positions\xe2\x80\x94less than three-quarters of a second over waiting for the spike strips. And whatever threat Leija posed after his car was stopped existed whether the car was stopped by a shot to the engine block or by the spike strips.

\n

Nor was there any evidence that shooting at the car was more reliable than the spike strips. The majority notes that spike strips are fallible. Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. But Mullenix had no information to suggest that shooting to disable a car had a higher success rate, much less that doing so with no training and at night was more likely to succeed. Moreover, not only did officers have training in setting up the spike strips, but they had also placed two backup strips further north along the highway in case the first set failed. A reasonable officer could not have thought that shooting would stop the car with less danger or greater certainty than waiting.

\n

The majority cites Long v. Slaton, 508 F.\xc2\xa03d 576 (CA11 2007), for the proposition that Mullenix need not have \xe2\x80\x9cfirst tried less lethal methods, such as spike strips.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. But in that case, there was a clear reason to prefer deadly force over the alternatives. In Long, an officer fired to stop a suspect from fleeing in a stolen police cruiser. 508 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 583. When the officer fired, there were no alternative means of stopping the car in place. The Eleventh Circuit held that the governmental interest against waiting for a future deployment of spike strips that may never materialize justified the use of deadly force. Ibid.

\n

In this case, by contrast, neither petitioner nor the majority can point to any possible marginal gain in shooting at the car over using the spike strips already in place. It is clearly established that there must be some governmental interest that necessitates deadly force, even if it is not always clearly established what level of governmental interest is sufficient.

\n

Under the circumstances known to him at the time, Mullenix puts forth no plausible reason to choose shooting at Leija\xe2\x80\x99s engine block over waiting for the results of the spike strips. I would thus hold that Mullenix violated Leija\xe2\x80\x99s clearly established right to be free of intrusion absent some governmental interest.

\n

III

\n

The majority largely evades this key legal question by focusing primarily on the governmental interest in whether the car should be stopped rather than the dispositive ques-tion of how the car should be stopped. But even assum-ing that Leija posed a \xe2\x80\x9csufficient,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8, or \xe2\x80\x9cimme-diate,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7, threat, Mullenix did not face a \xe2\x80\x9cchoice between two evils\xe2\x80\x9d of shooting at a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s car orletting him go. Scott, 550 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 384; see, e.g., Plumhoff v. Rickard, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 3, 10); Brosseau v. Haugen,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 194\n\xe2\x80\x93197 (2004). Instead, Mullenix chose to employ a potentially lethal tactic (shooting at Leija\xe2\x80\x99s engine block) in addition to a tactic specifically designed to accomplish the same result (spike strips).[1]* By granting Mullenix qualified immunity, this Court goes a step further than our previous cases and does so without full briefing or argument.

\n

Thus framed, it is apparent that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s exhortation that the right at stake not be defined at \xe2\x80\x9ca high level of generality,\xe2\x80\x9d see ante, at 9, is a red herring. The major-ity adduces various facts that the Fifth Circuit supposedly ignored in its qualified immunity analysis, including that Leija was \xe2\x80\x9ca reportedly intoxicated fugitive, set on avoiding capture through high-speed vehicular flight, who twice during his flight had threatened to shoot police officers, and who was moments away from encountering an officer at Cemetery Road.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. But not one of those facts goes to the governmental interest in shooting over awaiting the spike strips. The majority also claims that established law does not make clear that \xe2\x80\x9cMullenix\xe2\x80\x99s reasons were insufficient to justify\xe2\x80\x9d his choice of shooting over following his superior\xe2\x80\x99s orders to wait for the spikes. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. But Mullenix seemed to have no reasons to prefer shooting to following orders.

\n

Instead of dealing with the question whether Mullenix could constitutionally fire on Leija\xe2\x80\x99s car rather than waiting for the spike strips, the majority dwells on the imminence of the threat posed by Leija. The majority recharacterizes Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s decision to shoot at Leija\xe2\x80\x99s engine block as a split-second, heat-of-the-moment choice, made when the suspect was \xe2\x80\x9cmoments away.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. Indeed, reading the majority opinion, one would scarcely believe that Mullenix arrived at the overpass several minutes before he took his shot, or that the rural road where the car chase occurred had few cars and no bystanders or businesses. 773 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 717, 720. The majority also glosses over the facts that Mullenix had time to ask Byrd for permission to fire upon Leija and that Byrd\xe2\x80\x94Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s superior officer\xe2\x80\x94told Mullenix to \xe2\x80\x9cstand by.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 717. There was no reason to believe that Byrd did not have all the same information Mullenix did, including the knowledge that an officer was stationed beneath the overpass. Even after receiving Byrd\xe2\x80\x99s response, Mullenix spent minutes in shooting position discussing his next step with a fellow officer, minutes during which he received no information that would have made his plan more suitable or his superior\xe2\x80\x99s orders less so. Ibid.

\n

An appropriate reading of the record on summary judgment would thus render Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s choice even more unreasonable. And asking the appropriate legal question would leave the majority with no choice but to conclude that Mullenix ignored the longstanding and well-settled\n \nFourth Amendment rule that there must be a governmental interest not just in seizing a suspect, but in the level of force used to effectuate that seizure.

\n

\n

When Mullenix confronted his superior officer after the shooting, his first words were, \xe2\x80\x9cHow\xe2\x80\x99s that for proactive?\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (Mullenix was apparently referencing an earlier counseling session in which Byrd suggested that he was not enterprising enough. Ibid.) The glib comment does not impact our legal analysis; an officer\xe2\x80\x99s actual intentions are irrelevant to the\n \nFourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively reasonable\xe2\x80\x9d inquiry. See Graham v. Connor,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 386,\n397 (1989)\n. But the comment seems to me revealing of the culture this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision supports when it calls it reasonable\xe2\x80\x94or even reasonably reasonable\xe2\x80\x94to use deadly force for no discernible gain and over a supervisor\xe2\x80\x99s express order to \xe2\x80\x9cstand by.\xe2\x80\x9d By sanctioning a \xe2\x80\x9cshoot first, think later\xe2\x80\x9d approach to policing, the Court renders the protections of the\n \nFourth Amendment hollow.

\n

For the reasons discussed, I would deny Mullenix\xe2\x80\x99s petition for a writ of certiorari. I thus respectfully dissent.
