Syllabus

\n

t-mobile south, llc v. city of roswell, georgia

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93975.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 10, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 14, 2015

\n

Respondent Roswell\xe2\x80\x99s city council (Council) held a public hearing to consider an application by petitioner T-Mobile South, LLC, to build a cell phone tower on residential property. During the hearing, several Council members expressed concerns about the tower\xe2\x80\x99s impact on the area. The hearing ended with the Council unanimously passing a motion to deny the application. Two days later, the City\xe2\x80\x99s Planning and Zoning Division informed petitioner by letter that the application had been denied and that minutes from the hearing would be made available. The detailed minutes were published 26 days later.

\n

\n \n \n \n Petitioner filed suit, alleging that the Council\xe2\x80\x99s denial was not supported by substantial evidence in the record. The District Court agreed, concluding that the City, by failing to issue a written decision stating its reasons for denying the application, had violated the Telecommunications Act of 1996, which provides that a locality\xe2\x80\x99s denial \xe2\x80\x9cshall be in writing and supported by substantial evidence contained in a written record,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(iii). The Eleventh Circuit, following its precedent, found that the Act\xe2\x80\x99s requirements were satisfied here because petitioner had received a denial letter and possessed a transcript of the hearing that it arranged to have recorded.

\n

Held:

\n

\n \n 1. Section 332(c)(7)(B)(iii) requires localities to provide reasons when they deny applications to build cell phone towers. This conclusion follows from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s provisions, which both preserve and specifically limit traditional state and local government authority. It would be considerably difficult for a reviewing court to determine whether a locality\xe2\x80\x99s denial was \xe2\x80\x9csupported by substantial evidence contained in a written record,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(iii), or whether a locality had \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonably discriminate[d] among providers of functionally equivalent services,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(i)(I), or regulated siting \xe2\x80\x9con the basis of the environmental effects of radio frequency emissions,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(iv), if localities were not obligated to state their reasons for denial. And nothing in the Act suggests that Congress meant to use the phrase \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial evidence\xe2\x80\x9d as anything but an administrative law \xe2\x80\x9cterm of art\xe2\x80\x9d that describes how \xe2\x80\x9can administrative record is to be judged by a reviewing court.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Carlo Bianchi & Co.,\n\n373 U.\xc2\xa0S. 709\n. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n \n 2. Localities are not required to provide their reasons for denying siting applications in the denial notice itself, but may state those reasons with sufficient clarity in some other written record issued essentially contemporaneously with the denial. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n \n \n \n (a) Nothing in the Act\xe2\x80\x99s text imposes a requirement that the reasons be given in any particular form, and the Act\xe2\x80\x99s saving clause, \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(A), makes clear that the only limitations imposed on local governments are those enumerated in the statute. Localities comply with their obligation to give written reasons so long as those reasons are stated clearly enough to enable judicial review. Because an adversely affected entity must decide whether to seek judicial review within 30 days from the date of the denial, \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v), and because a court cannot review the denial without knowing the locality\xe2\x80\x99s reasons, the locality must provide or make available its written reasons at essentially the same time as it communicates its denial. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n \n \n \n (b) Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s contrary arguments are unavailing. The statute\xe2\x80\x99s word \xe2\x80\x9cdecision\xe2\x80\x9d does not connote a written document that itself provides all the reasons for a given judgment. The absence of the word \xe2\x80\x9cnotify\xe2\x80\x9d in the provision at issue also does not signal an intention to require communication of more than a judgment. Nor does an obligation to provide reasons in the writing conveying the denial arise from the \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial evidence\xe2\x80\x9d requirement itself or from the requirement of court review \xe2\x80\x9con an expedited basis,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v). It is sufficient that a locality\xe2\x80\x99s reasons be provided in a manner that is clear enough and prompt enough to enable judicial review. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n \n 3. The City failed to comply with its statutory obligations under the Act. Although it issued its reasons in writing and did so in an acceptable form, it did not provide its written reasons essentially contemporaneously with its written denial when it issued detailed minutes 26 days after the date of the written denial and 4 days before expiration of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s time to seek judicial review. P.\xc2\xa014.

\n

731 F.\xc2\xa03d 1213, reversed and remanded.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93975

_________________

T-MOBILE SOUTH, LLC, PETITIONER v.CITY OF ROSWELL, GEORGIA

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

[January 14, 2015]

Justice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.

The Telecommunications Act of 1996 provides, in relevant part, that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny decision by a State or local government or instrumentality thereof to deny a request to place, construct, or modify personal wireless service facilities shall be in writing and supported by substantial evidence contained in a written record.\xe2\x80\x9d110Stat.151,47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(iii). The question presented is whether, and in what form, localities must provide reasons when they deny telecommunication companies\xe2\x80\x99 applications to construct cell phone towers. We hold that localities must provide or make available their reasons, but that those reasons need not appear in the written denial letter or notice provided by the locality. Instead, the locality\xe2\x80\x99s reasons may appear in some other written record so long as the reasons are sufficiently clear and are provided or made accessible to the applicant essentially contemporaneously with the written denial letter or notice.

I

In February 2010, petitioner T-Mobile South, LLC, applied to build a new, 108-foot-tall cell phone tower on 2.8 acres of vacant residential property in the city of Ros-well, Georgia (City). Roswell\xe2\x80\x99s city ordinances requirethat any cell phone tower proposed for a residential zoning district must take the form of an \xe2\x80\x9calternative tower structure\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94an artificial tree, clock tower, steeple, or light pole\xe2\x80\x94that, in the opinion of the city council (City Council or Council), is \xe2\x80\x9ccompatible with the natural setting and surrounding structures\xe2\x80\x9d and that effectively camouflages the tower. Code of Ordinances \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa721.2.2, 21.2.5(a); see App. 68, 75. In accordance with these provisions, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s application proposed a structure in the shape of an artificial tree or \xe2\x80\x9cmonopine.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 42.

The City\xe2\x80\x99s Planning and Zoning Division reviewed petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s application, along with a substantial number of letters and petitions opposing it, and ultimately issued a memorandum to the City Council concluding that the application met all of the requirements set out in the City\xe2\x80\x99s ordinances. It recommended that the City Council approve the application on three conditions to which petitioner was prepared to agree.

The City Council then held a 2-hour-long public hearing on April 12, 2010, to consider petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s application. Petitioner arranged privately to have the hearing transcribed, and, as discussed below, the City subsequently issued detailed minutes summarizing the proceedings. At the hearing, after the Planning and Zoning Division presented its recommendation and after petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s representatives made a presentation in support of the application, a number of residents raised concerns. Among these were concerns that the tower would lack aesthetic compatibility, that the technology was outdated and unnecessary, and that the tower would be too tall. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s representatives responded by reiterating that it had met all of the ordinance\xe2\x80\x99s requirements and by providing testimony from a property appraiser that placement of cell phone towers does not reduce property values.

Members of the City Council then commented on the application. One member of the six-person Council was recused, see id., at 111 (hearing transcript); id., at 322 (meeting minutes), leaving five voting members. Member Igleheart said that other carriers had sufficient coverage in the area and that the City did not need to level the playing field for petitioner. Id., at 173\xe2\x80\x93174 (hearing transcript). He also stated that his \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ottom line\xe2\x80\x9d was that he did not think it was \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate for residentially zoned properties to have the cell towers in their location.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 174 (hearing transcript); id., at 338 (meeting minutes). Member Dippolito found it difficult to believe that the tower would not negatively impact the area and doubted that it would be compatible with the natural setting. Id., at 175\xe2\x80\x93176 (hearing transcript); id., at 339 (meeting minutes). Member Wynn expressed concerns about the lack of a backup generator for emergency services, id., at 172 (hearing transcript), and did not think the tower would be \xe2\x80\x9ccompatible with this area,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 176 (hearing transcript); id., at 339 (meeting minutes). Member Orlans said only that he was impressed with the information put together by both sides. Id., at 173 (hearing transcript); id., at 337 (meeting minutes). Finally, Member Price, the liaison to the Planning and Zoning Division, made a motion to deny the application. She said that the tower would be aesthetically incompatible with the natural setting, that it would be too tall, and that its proximity to other homes would adversely affect the neighbors and the resale value of their properties. Id., at 176\xe2\x80\x93177 (hearing transcript); id., at 339\xe2\x80\x93340 (meeting minutes). The motion was seconded, and then passed unanimously. Id., at 177 (hearing transcript); id., at 340 (meeting minutes).

Two days later, on April 14, 2010, the Planning and Zoning Division sent a letter to petitioner that said in its entirety:

\xe2\x80\x9cPlease be advised the City of Roswell Mayor and City Council denied the request from T-Mobile for a 108\xe2\x80\x99 mono-pine alternative tower structure during their April 12, 2010 hearing. The minutes from the aforementioned hearing may be obtained from the city clerk. Please contact Sue Creel or Betsy Branch at [phone number].

\xe2\x80\x9cIf you have any additional questions, please contact me at [phone number].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 278.

The detailed written minutes of the hearing, however, were not approved and published by the City until 26 days later, on May 10, 2010. See id., at 321\xe2\x80\x93341 (meeting minutes).[1]

On May 13, 2010, 3 days after the detailed minutes were published\xe2\x80\x94and now 29 days after the City denied petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s application\xe2\x80\x94petitioner filed suit in Federal District Court. It alleged that the denial of the application was not supported by substantial evidence in the record, and would effectively prohibit the provision of wireless service in violation of the Telecommunications Act of1996 (Act). The parties filed cross-motions for summary judgment.

The District Court granted petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion for summary judgment, concluding that the City had violated the Act when it failed to issue a written decision that stated the reasons for denying petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s application. The District Court interpreted the Act to require that a written denial letter or notice describe the reasons for the denial and that those reasons be sufficiently explained to allow a reviewing court to evaluate them against the written record.

The Eleventh Circuit reversed. 731 F.\xc2\xa03d 1213 (2013). It explained that, in T-Mobile South, LLC v. Milton, 728 F.\xc2\xa03d 1274 (2013), which was decided after the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in this case, it had held that \xe2\x80\x9cto the extent that the decision must contain grounds or reasons or explanations, it is sufficient if those are contained in a different written document or documents that the applicant is given or has access to.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1285. The Eleventh Circuit acknowledged that the Courts of Appeals had split on that question, and that it had departed from the majority rule. Compare Southwestern Bell Mobile Systems, Inc. v. Todd, 244 F. 3d 51, 60 (CA1 2001) (requiring that a locality issue a written denial that itself contains a \xe2\x80\x9csufficient explanation of the reasons for the permit denial to allow a reviewing court to evaluate the evidence in the record supporting those reasons\xe2\x80\x9d); New Par v. Saginaw, 301 F. 3d 390, 395\xe2\x80\x93396 (CA6 2002); MetroPCS, Inc. v. City and County of San Francisco, 400 F. 3d 715, 723 (CA9 2005), with AT&T Wireless PCS, Inc. v. City Council of Virginia Beach, 155 F. 3d 423, 429 (CA4 1998) (holding that written minutes of a meeting and the word \xe2\x80\x9cdenied\xe2\x80\x9d stamped on a letter describing the application weresufficient). Applying its rule to this case, the Eleventh Circuit found that the requirements of47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(iii) were satisfied because petitioner had its own transcript as well as a written letter stating that the application had been denied and informing petitioner that it could obtain access to the minutes of the hearing. 731 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1221. It did not consider when the City provided its written reasons to petitioner.

We granted certiorari, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014), and now reverse the judgment of the Eleventh Circuit.

II

A

The first question we answer is whether the statute requires localities to provide reasons when they deny applications to build cell phone towers. We answer that question in the affirmative.

Our conclusion follows from the provisions of the Telecommunications Act. The Act generally preserves \xe2\x80\x9cthe traditional authority of state and local governments to regulate the location, construction, and modification\xe2\x80\x9d of wireless communications facilities like cell phone towers, but imposes \xe2\x80\x9cspecific limitations\xe2\x80\x9d on that authority. Rancho Palos Verdes v. Abrams,544 U. S. 113,115 (2005); see \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B). One of those limitations is that any decision to deny a request to build a tower \xe2\x80\x9cshall be in writing and supported by substantial evidence contained in a written record.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(iii). Another is that parties adversely affected by a locality\xe2\x80\x99s decision may seek judicial review. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v). In order to determine whether a locality\xe2\x80\x99s denial was supported by substantial evidence, as Congress directed, courts must be able to identify the reason or reasons why the locality denied the application. See Rancho Palos Verdes, 544 U. S., at 128 (Breyer, J., joined by O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, Souter, and Ginsburg, JJ., concurring) (observing that the Act \xe2\x80\x9crequires local zoning boards .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [to] give reasons for [their] denials \xe2\x80\x98in writing\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

The requirement that localities must provide reasons when they deny applications is further underscored by two of the other limitations on local authority set out in the Act. The Act provides that localities \xe2\x80\x9cshall not unreason-ably discriminate among providers of functionally equivalent services,\xe2\x80\x9d and may not regulate the construction of personal wireless service facilities \xe2\x80\x9con the basis of the environmental effects of radio frequency emissions to the extent that such facilities comply with the [Federal Communications Commission\xe2\x80\x99s] regulations concerning such emissions.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(i)(I), (iv).[2] Again, it would be considerably more difficult for a reviewing court to determine whether a locality had violated these substantive provisions if the locality were not obligated to state its reasons.

This conclusion is not just commonsensical, but flows directly from Congress\xe2\x80\x99 use of the term \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d The statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial evidence\xe2\x80\x9d is a \xe2\x80\x9cterm of art\xe2\x80\x9d in administrative law that describes how \xe2\x80\x9can administrative record is to be judged by a reviewing court.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Carlo Bianchi & Co.,373 U. S. 709,715 (1963). There is no reason discernible from the text of the Act to think that Congress meant to use the phrase in a different way. See FAA v. Cooper, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 6) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen Congress employs a term of art, it presumably knows and adopts the cluster of ideas that were attached to each borrowed word in the body of learning from which it was taken\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Indeed, for those who consider legislative history relevant, the Conference Report accompanying the Act confirmed as much when it noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he phrase \xe2\x80\x98substantial evidence contained in a written record\xe2\x80\x99 is the traditional standard used for review of agency actions.\xe2\x80\x9d H. R. Conf. Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x93458, p. 208 (1996).

By employing the term \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress thus invoked, among other things, our recognition that \xe2\x80\x9cthe orderly functioning of the process of [substantial-evidence] review requires that the grounds upon which the administrative agency acted be clearly disclosed,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9ccourts cannot exercise their duty of [substantial-evidence] review unless they are advised of the considerations underlying the action under review.\xe2\x80\x9d SEC v. Chenery Corp.,318 U. S. 80,94 (1943); see also Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Assn. of United States, Inc. v. State Farm Mut. Automobile Ins. Co.,463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 29,43 (1983) (explaining that an agency must \xe2\x80\x9carticulate a satisfactory explanation for its action\xe2\x80\x9d to enable substantial-evidence review); Beaumont, S.\xc2\xa0L. & W. R. Co. v. United States,282 U. S. 74,86 (1930) (\xe2\x80\x9cComplete statements by the [agency] showing the grounds upon which its determinations rest are quite as necessary as are opinions of lower courts setting forth the reasons on which they base their decisions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d).

In response, the City primarily argues that a reason-giving obligation would deprive it of local zoning author-ity. But Congress intended to place \xe2\x80\x9cspecific limitations on the traditional authority of state and local governments\xe2\x80\x9d regarding cell phone tower siting applications. Rancho Palos Verdes, 544 U. S., at 115. One of those \xe2\x80\x9climitations,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B), necessarily implied by the Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial evidence\xe2\x80\x9d requirement, is that local zoning authorities state their reasons when they deny applications.

In short, the statutory text and structure, and the concepts that Congress imported into the statutory framework, all point clearly toward the conclusion that localities must provide reasons when they deny cell phone tower siting applications. We stress, however, that these reasons need not be elaborate or even sophisticated, but rather, as discussed below, simply clear enough to enable judicial review.

B

The second question we answer is whether these reasons must appear in the same writing that conveys the locality\xe2\x80\x99s denial of an application. We answer that question in the negative.

Like our conclusion that localities must provide reasons, our conclusion that the reasons need not appear in a de-nial letter follows from the statutory text. Other than providing that a locality\xe2\x80\x99s reasons must be given in writing, nothing in that text imposes any requirement that the reasons be given in any particular form.

The Act\xe2\x80\x99s saving clause makes clear that, other than the enumerated limitations imposed on local governments by the statute itself, \xe2\x80\x9cnothing in this chapter shall limit or affect the authority of a State or local government or instrumentality thereof over decisions regarding the placement, construction, and modification of personal wireless service facilities.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(A). Given this language, and the system of \xe2\x80\x9ccooperative federalism\xe2\x80\x9d on which the Act is premised, Rancho Palos Verdes, 544 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 128 (Breyer, J., concurring), we understand the enumerated limitations to set out an exclusive list. So while the text and structure of the Act render it inescapable that localities must provide reasons in writing when they deny applications, we can locate in the Act no command\xe2\x80\x94either explicit or implicit\xe2\x80\x94that localities must provide those reasons in a specific document.

We therefore conclude that Congress imposed no specific requirement on that front, but instead permitted localities to comply with their obligation to give written reasons so long as the locality\xe2\x80\x99s reasons are stated clearly enough to enable judicial review. Although the statute does not require a locality to provide its written reasons in any particular format, and although a locality may rely on detailed meeting minutes as it did here, we agree with the Solicitor General that \xe2\x80\x9cthe local government may be better served by including a separate statement containing its reasons.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 26; see also id., at 34. If the locality writes a short statement providing its reasons, the locality can likely avoid prolonging the litigation\xe2\x80\x94and adding expense to the taxpayers, the companies, and the legal system\xe2\x80\x94while the parties argue about exactly what the sometimes voluminous record means. Moreover, in that circumstance, the locality need not worry that, upon review of the record, a court will either find that it could not ascertain the locality\xe2\x80\x99s reasons or mistakenly ascribe to the locality a rationale that was not in fact the reason for the locality\xe2\x80\x99s denial.

We hasten to add that a locality cannot stymie or burden the judicial review contemplated by the statute by delaying the release of its reasons for a substantial time after it conveys its written denial. The statute provides that an entity adversely affected by a locality\xe2\x80\x99s decision may seek judicial review within 30 days of the decision. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v). Because an entity may not be able to make a considered decision whether to seek judicial review without knowing the reasons for the denial of its application, and because a court cannot review the denial without knowing the locality\xe2\x80\x99s reasons, the locality must provide or make available its written reasons at essentially the same time as it communicates its denial.[3]

This rule ought not to unduly burden localities given the range of ways in which localities can provide their reasons. Moreover, the denial itself needs only to be issued (or the application otherwise acted upon) \xe2\x80\x9cwithin a reasonable period of time.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii). In an interpretation we have recently upheld, see Arlington v. FCC, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013), the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) has generally interpreted this provision to allow localities 90 days to act on applications to place new antennas on existing towers and 150 days to act on other siting applications. In re Petition for Declaratory Ruling to Clarify Provisions of Section 332(c)(7)(b), 24 FCC Rcd. 13994, 13995, \xc2\xb64 (2009). If a locality is not in a position to provide its reasons promptly, the locality can delay the issuance of its denial within this 90- or 150-day window, and instead release it along with its reasons once those reasons are ready to be provided. Only once the denial is issued would the 30-day commencement-of-suit clock begin.[4]

III

Petitioner offers four reasons why, in its view, our analysis in Part II\xe2\x80\x93B is incorrect. Petitioner argues that the statute requires that a locality\xe2\x80\x99s reasons appear in the writing conveying the denial itself, but none of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s reasons are persuasive.

First, petitioner argues that the word \xe2\x80\x9cdecision\xe2\x80\x9d in the statute\xe2\x80\x94the thing that must be \xe2\x80\x9cin writing\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94connotes a written document that itself provides all the reasons for a given judgment. See Brief for Petitioner 24 (quoting Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 407 (6th ed. 1990) (a \xe2\x80\x9cdecision\xe2\x80\x9d is a written document providing \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the reasons given for [a] judgment\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d)). But even petitioner concedes, with its preferred dictionary in hand, that the word \xe2\x80\x9cdecision\xe2\x80\x9d can also mean \xe2\x80\x9csomething short of a statement of reasons explaining a determination.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 24 (citing Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary, at 407).[5]

Second, petitioner claims that other provisions in the Act use the word \xe2\x80\x9cnotify\xe2\x80\x9d when the Act means to impose only a requirement that a judgment be communicated.[6] Because the provision at issue here does not use the word \xe2\x80\x9cnotify,\xe2\x80\x9d petitioner argues, it must contemplate something more than a judgment. This does not logically follow. For one thing, the statute at issue here does not use any verb at all to describe the conveying of information from a locality to an applicant; it just says that a denial \xe2\x80\x9cshall be in writing and supported by substantial evidence contained in a written record.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(iii). But more to the point, \xe2\x80\x9cnotify\xe2\x80\x9d is a verb the use\xe2\x80\x94or nonuse\xe2\x80\x94of which does not reveal what the thing to be notified of or about is.

Third, petitioner contends that the \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial evidence\xe2\x80\x9d requirement itself demands that localities identify their reasons in their written denials. See Brief for Petitioner 23. Certainly, as discussed above, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial evidence\xe2\x80\x9d requires localities to give reasons, but it says nothing on its own about the document in which those reasons must be stated or presented to a reviewing court.

Finally, petitioner invokes the statutory requirement that any adversely affected person shall have their challenge heard by a court \xe2\x80\x9con an expedited basis.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v). See Brief for Petitioner 14\xe2\x80\x9315, 28. As long as the reasons are provided in a written record, however, and as long as they are provided in such a manner that is clear enough and prompt enough to enable judicial review, there is no reason to require that those reasons be provided in the written denial itself.

We acknowledge that petitioner, along with those Courts of Appeals that have required a locality\xe2\x80\x99s reasons to appear in its written denial itself, have offered plausible bases for a rule that would require as much. See, e.g., Todd, 244 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 60 (\xe2\x80\x9cA written record can create difficulties in determining the rationale behind a board\xe2\x80\x99s decision .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). Congress could adopt such a rule if it were so inclined, but it did not do so in this statute. It is not our place to legislate another approach.

IV

Thus, we hold that the Act requires localities to provide reasons when they deny cell phone tower siting applications, but that the Act does not require localities to provide those reasons in written denial letters or notices themselves. A locality may satisfy its statutory obligations if it states its reasons with sufficient clarity in some other written record issued essentially contemporaneously with the denial. In this case, the City provided its reasons in writing and did so in the acceptable form of detailed minutes of the City Council meeting. The City, however, did not provide its written reasons essentially contemporaneously with its written denial. Instead, the City issued those detailed minutes 26 days after the date of the written denial and just 4 days before petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s time to seek judicial review would have expired.[7] The City therefore did not comply with its statutory obligations. We do not consider questions regarding the applicability of principles of harmless error or questions of remedy, and leave those for the Eleventh Circuit to address on remand.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

For the foregoing reasons, we reverse the judgment below and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.

Justice Alito, concurring.

I concur in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion because I agree that Congress, by using the term \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d intended to invoke administrative law principles. One such principle, as the Court explains, is the requirement that agencies give reasons. I write separately, however, because three other traditional administrative law principles may also apply.

First, a court must \xe2\x80\x9cuphold a decision of less than ideal clarity if the agency\xe2\x80\x99s path may reasonably be discerned.\xe2\x80\x9d Bowman Transp., Inc. v. Arkansas-Best Freight System, Inc.,419 U. S. 281,286 (1974). In the context of47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7), which leaves in place almost the entirety of a local government\xe2\x80\x99s authority, a succinct statement that a permit has been denied because the tower would be esthetically incompatible with the surrounding area should suffice. Nothing in this statute imposes an opinion-writing requirement.

Second, even if a locality has erred, a court must not invalidate the locality\xe2\x80\x99s decision if the error was harmless. \xe2\x80\x9cIn administrative law, as in federal civil and criminal litigation, there is a harmless error rule.\xe2\x80\x9d National Assn. of Home Builders v. Defenders of Wildlife,551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 644\xe2\x80\x93660 (2007) (internal quotation marks omitted). Here, for instance, I have trouble believing that T-Mobile South, LLC\xe2\x80\x94which actively participated in the decisionmaking process, including going so far as to transcribe the public hearing\xe2\x80\x94was prejudiced by the city of Roswell\xe2\x80\x99s delay in providing a copy of the minutes.

Third, the ordinary rule in administrative law is that a court must remand errors to the agency \xe2\x80\x9cexcept in rare circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Florida Power & Light Co. v. Lorion,470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 729,744 (1985). Nothing we say today should be read to suggest that when a locality has erred, the inevitable remedy is that a tower must be built. The Court has not passed on what remedial powers a \xe2\x80\x9ccourt of competent jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d may exercise. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v). This unanswered question is important given the federalism implications of this statute.

I do not understand the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion to disagree with this analysis. On that understanding, I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

T-MOBILE SOUTH, LLC, PETITIONER v.CITY OF ROSWELL, GEORGIA

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

[January 14, 2015]

Chief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Ginsburg joins, and with whom Justice Thomas joins as to Part I, dissenting.

The statute at issue in this case provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny decision .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to deny a request .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be in writing and supported by substantial evidence contained in a written record.\xe2\x80\x9d47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(iii). The Court concludes that the City loses this case not because it failed to provide its denial in writing. It did provide its denial in writing. Nor does the City lose because the denial was not supported by substantial evidence in a written record. The City compiled a written record; whether that record contained substantial evidence supporting the denial is not at issue here and has not been decided. Nor does the City lose because its denial was not accompanied by a statement of reasons apart from the written record. A sharp conflict had developed in the lower courts over the necessity of such a separate statement, and the Court today squarely holds that one is not required. Ante, at 5, 8\xe2\x80\x9311. No, the City instead loses because of a question of timing: The written record was not made available roughly the same day as the denial\xe2\x80\x94a requirement found nowhere in the text of the statute.

The Court says this timing requirement is necessary for judicial review of whether the denial is supported by substantial evidence. A reviewing court, however, can carry out its function just as easily whether the record is submitted four weeks or four days before the lawsuit is filed\xe2\x80\x94or four days after, for that matter. The Court also supports its timing rule by saying that the company whose application is denied needs the time to carefully consider whether to seek review. But cell service providers are not Mom and Pop operations. As this case illustrates, they participate extensively in the local government proceedings, and do not have to make last-second, uninformed decisions on whether to seek review.

The City here fully complied with its obligations under the statute: It issued its decision in writing, and it sub-mitted a written record containing\xe2\x80\x94so far as we know\xe2\x80\x94substantial evidence supporting that decision. I respectfully dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s contrary conclusion.

I

Section 332(c)(7), enacted as part of the Telecommunications Act of 1996, places several limits on local governments\xe2\x80\x99 authority to regulate the siting of cell towers and other telecommunications facilities. A locality\xe2\x80\x99s regulations must not unreasonably discriminate among service providers, effectively prohibit the provision of service, or rest on concerns about the environmental effects of radio emissions. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(i), (iv). In addition, the provision central to this case specifies that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny decision by a State or local government .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to deny a request to place, construct, or modify\xe2\x80\x9d a cell tower \xe2\x80\x9cshall be in writing and supported by substantial evidence contained in a written record.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(iii). And another provision authorizes expedited judicial review of a locality\xe2\x80\x99s alleged failure to comply with these rules. See \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v).

After the city council of Roswell voted to deny T-Mobile\xe2\x80\x99s application to build a cell tower, the City sent T-Mobile a short letter that announced the denial but provided no further explanation. The question T-Mobile has presented to this Court is whether such a letter satisfies the \xe2\x80\x9cdecision .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in writing\xe2\x80\x9d requirement of Section 332(c)(7)(B)(iii). See Pet. for Cert. i.

I would hold it does. The City\xe2\x80\x99s letter was certainly in writing. And it certainly memorialized the denial of T-Mobile\xe2\x80\x99s application. So T-Mobile\xe2\x80\x99s only hope is that the lack of explanation for the denial means the letter isnot truly a \xe2\x80\x9cdecision.\xe2\x80\x9d But like the majority, I reject T-Mobile\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the term \xe2\x80\x9cdecision\xe2\x80\x9d inherently demands a statement of reasons. Dictionary definitions support that conclusion. See ante, at 12 (citing Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary); see also B. Garner, A Dictionary of Modern Legal Usage 251 (2d ed. 1995) (grouping \xe2\x80\x9cdecision\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment,\xe2\x80\x9d as distinct from \xe2\x80\x9copinion\xe2\x80\x9d).

A comparison between Section 332(c)(7)(B)(iii) and other statutory provisions that were on the books in 1996 also suggests that when Congress wants decisionmakers to supply explanations, it says so. Consider first the Administrative Procedure Act. In the context of formal adjudication and rulemaking, it demands that \xe2\x80\x9cdecisions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. include a statement of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. findings and conclusions, and the reasons or basis therefor, on all the material issues.\xe2\x80\x9d5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7557(c)(A) (1994 ed.) (emphasis added). Even in informal proceedings, an agency must give prompt notice of the denial of a written application, and, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcept in affirming a prior denial or when the denial is self-explanatory, the notice shall be accompanied by a brief statement of the grounds for denial.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7555(e) (emphasis added).

The Communications Act of 1934, which the Telecommunications Act overhauled, itself contains a similar express requirement. Section 309 allows an interested party to petition the Federal Communications Commission to deny a license application.47 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7309(d)(1) (1994 ed.). If the FCC decides to grant an application despite such a petition, it must \xe2\x80\x9cissue a concise statement of the reasons for denying the petition.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7309(d)(2). And a provision added along with Section 332(c)(7) in the Telecommunications Act provides that when the FCC grants or denies a petition for regulatory forbearance, it \xe2\x80\x9cshall explain its decision in writing.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7160(c) (2000 ed.) (emphasis added). Many other statutes in effect in 1996 could be added to the list. See, e.g., 19 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71515(a) (1994 ed.) (notice of customs protest denial \xe2\x80\x9cshall include a statement of the reasons for the denial\xe2\x80\x9d);30 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7944 (1994 ed.) (individual whose claim for black lung benefits \xe2\x80\x9cis denied shall receive .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a written statement of the reasons for denial\xe2\x80\x9d);38 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa75104(b) (1994 ed.) (notice of denial of veterans benefits must include \xe2\x80\x9ca statement of the reasons for the decision\xe2\x80\x9d).

Given the commonplace nature of express requirements that reasons be given\xe2\x80\x94and the inclusion of such provisions in the Administrative Procedure Act, the original Communications Act, and another provision of the Telecommunications Act\xe2\x80\x94the absence of one in Section 332(c)(7)(B)(iii) is telling, and supports reading \xe2\x80\x9cdecision .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in writing\xe2\x80\x9d to demand nothing more than what it says: a written document that communicates the town\xe2\x80\x99s denial.

In my view, resolving that interpretive question in the City\xe2\x80\x99s favor also resolves the case as it stands in this Court. Although Section 332(c)(7)(B)(iii) goes on to require that a denial be \xe2\x80\x9csupported by substantial evidence contained in a written record,\xe2\x80\x9d the adequacy of the City\xe2\x80\x99s written record is not properly before us. As the Eleventh Circuit noted in its opinion below, the \xe2\x80\x9csole issue\xe2\x80\x9d before it was the \xe2\x80\x9cin writing\xe2\x80\x9d requirement; it did not examine whether the City\xe2\x80\x99s denial was supported by substantial evidence. 731 F.\xc2\xa03d 1213, 1221, n.\xc2\xa07 (2013). The Court today also\xe2\x80\x94correctly\xe2\x80\x94does not decide whether substantial evidence supported the City\xe2\x80\x99s denial. The Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment therefore ought to be affirmed and the case remanded to the District Court for further proceedings on T-Mobile\xe2\x80\x99s remaining challenges.

II

The Court agrees that the City was not required to explain its reasoning in its denial letter, but it nonetheless rules for T-Mobile. The improbable linchpin of this outcome is the City\xe2\x80\x99s failure to finalize the minutes of the April 12 city council meeting until May 10. Improbable because, so far as I can tell, T-Mobile never even mentioned this timeline, let alone based an argument on it, in its filings in the lower courts or its petition for certiorari. Nor did the Eleventh Circuit address this timing question in any way. Cf. Zivotofsky v. Clinton, 566 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 12) (\xe2\x80\x9cOrdinarily, we do not decide in the first instance issues not decided below.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

The Court nonetheless rules against Roswell on this ground, proceeding in two steps: First it concludes that a town must provide written reasons in some form (the minutes being the only candidate here); then it decides a town must make those reasons available \xe2\x80\x9cessentially contemporaneously\xe2\x80\x9d with its decision (the final minutes were not). Ante, at 14. In my view, the first step is justified by the statutory text, but the second is not.

The need to provide reasons in some form follows from the portion of Section 332(c)(7)(B)(iii) requiring that denials be \xe2\x80\x9csupported by substantial evidence contained in a written record.\xe2\x80\x9d Like the majority, I read this phrase as specifying a familiar standard of review to be used if a denial is challenged in court. And like the majority, I agree that substantial evidence review requires that a decisionmaker\xe2\x80\x99s reasons be identifiable in the written record. If a reviewing court cannot identify any of a town\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for denying an application, it cannot determine whether substantial evidence supports those reasons, and the town loses.

But then the Court goes a step further and creates a timing rule: A town must provide \xe2\x80\x9cits written reasons at essentially the same time as it communicates its denial.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. This timing rule is nowhere to be found in the text of Section 332(c)(7)(B)\xe2\x80\x94text that expressly establishes other time limits, both general and specific. See \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(ii) (requiring localities to act on siting requests \xe2\x80\x9cwithin a reasonable period of time\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v) (giving injured parties 30 days to seek judicial review). Despite its assertion that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cenumerated limitations\xe2\x80\x9d constitute \xe2\x80\x9can exclusive list,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9, the Court offers two justifications for its inference of this additional, unenumerated limitation.

The first is that \xe2\x80\x9ca court cannot review the denial without knowing the locality\xe2\x80\x99s reasons,\xe2\x80\x9d so it would \xe2\x80\x9cstymie\xe2\x80\x9d judicial review to allow delay between the issuance of the decision and the statement of reasons. Ante, at 10. This makes little sense. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cessentially contemporaneous\xe2\x80\x9d requirement presumably means the town must produce its reasons within a matter of days (though the majority never says how many). But a reviewing court does not need to be able to discern the town\xe2\x80\x99s reasons within mere days of the decision. At that point no one has even asked the court to review the denial. The fact that a court cannot conduct review without knowing the reasons simply means that if the town has not already made the record available, it must do so by whatever deadline the court sets. The court should proceed \xe2\x80\x9con an expedited basis,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v), but that hardly means it will need the record within days of the town\xe2\x80\x99s decision. And in this case there is no indication that the City\xe2\x80\x99s submission of the written record was untimely or delayed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s review.

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s second justification focuses on the denied applicant, which must choose within 30 days from the denial whether to take the town to court. \xc2\xa7332(c)(7)(B)(v). \xe2\x80\x9c[W]ithout knowing the locality\xe2\x80\x99s reasons,\xe2\x80\x9d the majority says, the applicant \xe2\x80\x9cmay not be able to make a considered decision whether to seek judicial review.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. This concern might have force if towns routinely made these decisions in secret, closed-door proceedings, or if applicants were unsophisticated actors. But the local zoning board or town council is not the Star Chamber, and a telecommunications company is no babe in the legal woods. Almost invariably in cases addressing Section 332(c)(7), the relevant local authority has held an open meeting at which the applicant was present and the issues publicly aired. In this case and others, T-Mobile has brought its own court reporter, ensuring that it has a verbatim transcript of the meeting well before the town is likely to finalize its minutes. See Brief for Petitioner 12, n.\xc2\xa02; T-Mobile South, LLC v. City of Milton, Georgia, 728 F.\xc2\xa03d 1274, 1277 (CA11 2013). I strongly doubt that a sophisticated, well-lawyered company like T-Mobile\xe2\x80\x94with extensive experience with these particular types of proceedings\xe2\x80\x94would have any trouble consulting its interests and deciding whether to seek review before it had received a written explanation from the town. The Court worries about towns \xe2\x80\x9csandbagg[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d companies with unexpected reasons, ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa03, but if those reasons in fact come out of nowhere, they will not be supported by substantial evidence in the record. And if the company\xe2\x80\x99s initial complaint mistakes the town\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, the company will have no difficulty amending its allegations. See Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 15(a).

In sum, there is nothing impractical about reading the statutory text to require only that the reasons implied by the term \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial evidence\xe2\x80\x9d be discernible to the court when it conducts substantial evidence review. Demanding \xe2\x80\x9cessentially contemporaneous\xe2\x80\x9d written reasons adds a requirement that Congress has included expressly in many other statutes, but not in this one. See supra, at3\xe2\x80\x934.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

For the foregoing reasons, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion and judgment are wrong. But this is not a \xe2\x80\x9cthe sky is falling\xe2\x80\x9d dissent. At the end of the day, the impact on cities and towns across the Nation should be small, although the new unwritten requirement could be a trap for the unwary hamlet or two. All a local government need do is withhold its final decision until the minutes are typed up, and make the final decision and the record of proceedings (with discernible reasons) available together.

Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is nonetheless a bad break for Roswell. Or maybe not. The Court leaves open the question of remedy, ante, at 14, and it may be that failure to comply with the \xe2\x80\x9cin writing\xe2\x80\x9d requirement as construed by the Court can be excused as harmless error in appropriate cases. It is hard to see where the harm is here. T-Mobile somehow managed to make the tough call to seek review of the denial of an application it had spent months and many thousands of dollars to obtain, based on a hearing it had attended. And nothing about Roswell\xe2\x80\x99s failure to meet the \xe2\x80\x9ccontemporaneously\xe2\x80\x9d requirement delayed, much less \xe2\x80\x9cstymied,\xe2\x80\x9d judicial review.

The Court today resolves the conflict over whether a town must provide a statement of reasons with its final decision, apart from the written record. We now know it need not. As the Court explains, \xe2\x80\x9cnothing in [the] text [of the Act] imposes any requirement that the reasons be given in any particular form,\xe2\x80\x9d and there is \xe2\x80\x9cin the Act no command\xe2\x80\x94either explicit or implicit\xe2\x80\x94that localities must provide those reasons in a specific document.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. Good analysis\xe2\x80\x94which also should have been followed to reject the timing requirement the Court creates today.

I respectfully dissent.
