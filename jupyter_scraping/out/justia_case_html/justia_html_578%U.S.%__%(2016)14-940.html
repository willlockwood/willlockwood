EVENWEL et\xc2\xa0al. v. ABBOTT, GOVERNOR OF TEXAS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for the western district of texas

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93940.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 8, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 4, 2016

\n

Under the one-person, one-vote principle, jurisdictions must design legislative districts with equal populations. See Wesberry v. Sanders,\n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x938, Reynolds v. Sims,\n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 533\n. In the context of state and local legislative districting, States may deviate somewhat from perfect population equality to accommodate traditional districting objectives. Where the maximum population deviation between the largest and smallest district is less than 10%, a state or local legislative map presumptively complies with the one-person, one-vote rule.

\n

Texas, like all other States, uses total-population numbers from the decennial census when drawing legislative districts. After the 2010 census, Texas adopted a State Senate map that has a maximum total-population deviation of 8.04%, safely within the presumptively permissible 10% range. However, measured by a voter-population baseline\xe2\x80\x94eligible voters or registered voters\xe2\x80\x94the map\xe2\x80\x99s maximum population deviation exceeds 40%. Appellants, who live in Texas Senate districts with particularly large eligible- and registered-voter populations, filed suit against the Texas Governor and Secretary of State. Basing apportionment on total population, appellants contended, dilutes their votes in relation to voters in other Senate districts, in violation of the one-person, one-vote principle of the Equal Protection Clause. Appellants sought an injunction barring use of the existing Senate map in favor of a map that would equalize the voter population in each district. A three-judge District Court dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim on which relief could be granted.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0As constitutional history, precedent, and practice demonstrate, a State or locality may draw its legislative districts based on total population. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Constitutional history shows that, at the time of the founding, the Framers endorsed allocating House seats to States based on total population. Debating what would become the\n \nFourteenth Amendment, Congress reconsidered the proper basis for apportioning House seats. Retaining the total-population rule, Congress rejected proposals to allocate House seats to States on the basis of voter population. See U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 14, \xc2\xa72. The Framers recognized that use of a total-population baseline served the principle of representational equality. Appellants\xe2\x80\x99 voter-population rule is inconsistent with the \xe2\x80\x9ctheory of the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d Cong. Globe, 39th Cong., 1st Sess., 2766\xe2\x80\x932767, this Court recognized in Wesberry as underlying not just the method of allocating House seats to States but also the method of apportioning legislative seats within States. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s past decisions reinforce the conclusion that States and localities may comply with the one-person, one-vote principle by designing districts with equal total populations. Appellants assert that language in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent supports their view that States should equalize the voter-eligible population of districts. But for every sentence appellants quote, one could respond with a line casting the one-person, one-vote guarantee in terms of equality of representation. See, e.g., Reynolds, 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 560\xe2\x80\x93561. Moreover, from Reynolds on, the Court has consistently looked to total-population figures when evaluating whether districting maps violate the Equal Protection Clause by deviating impermissibly from perfect population equality. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Settled practice confirms what constitutional history and prior decisions strongly suggest. Adopting voter-eligible apportionment as constitutional command would upset a well-functioning approach to districting that all 50 States and countless local jurisdictions have long followed. As the Framers of the Constitution and the\n \nFourteenth Amendment comprehended, representatives serve all residents, not just those eligible to vote. Nonvoters have an important stake in many policy debates and in receiving constituent services. By ensuring that each representative is subject to requests and suggestions from the same number of constituents, total-population apportionment promotes equitable and effective representation. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0Because constitutional history, precedent, and practice reveal the infirmity of appellants\xe2\x80\x99 claim, this Court need not resolve whether, as Texas now argues, States may draw districts to equalize voter-eligible population rather than total population. P.\xc2\xa019.

\n

Affirmed.

SUE EVENWEL, et\xc2\xa0al., APPELLANTS v. GREG ABBOTT, governor of texas, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for the western district of texas

\n

[April 4, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Texas, like all other States, draws its legislative districts on the basis of total population. Plaintiffs-appellants are Texas voters; they challenge this uniform method of districting on the ground that it produces un-equal districts when measured by voter-eligible population.Voter-eligible population, not total population, they urge, must be used to ensure that their votes will not be devalued in relation to citizens\xe2\x80\x99 votes in other districts. We hold, based on constitutional history, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions, and longstanding practice, that a State may draw its legislative districts based on total population.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

This Court long resisted any role in overseeing the process by which States draw legislative districts. \xe2\x80\x9cThe remedy for unfairness in districting,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court once held, \xe2\x80\x9cis to secure State legislatures that will apportion prop-erly, or to invoke the ample powers of Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Colegrovev. Green,\n\n328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549,\n556 (1946)\n. \xe2\x80\x9cCourts ought not to enter this political thicket,\xe2\x80\x9d as Justice Frankfurter put it. Ibid.

\n

Judicial abstention left pervasive malapportionment unchecked. In the opening half of the 20th century, there was a massive population shift away from rural areas and toward suburban and urban communities. Nevertheless, many States ran elections into the early 1960\xe2\x80\x99s based on maps drawn to equalize each district\xe2\x80\x99s population as it was composed around 1900. Other States used maps allocating a certain number of legislators to each county regardless of its population. These schemes left many rural districts significantly underpopulated in comparison with urban and suburban districts. But rural legislators who benefited from malapportionment had scant incentive to adopt new maps that might put them out of office.

\n

The Court confronted this ingrained structural inequal-ity in Baker v. Carr,\n\n369 U.\xc2\xa0S. 186\n\xe2\x80\x93192 (1962). Thatcase presented an equal protection challenge to a Tennessee state-legislative map that had not been redrawn since 1901. See also id., at 192 (observing that, in the meantime, there had been \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial growth and redistribution\xe2\x80\x9d of the State\xe2\x80\x99s population). Rather than steering clear of the political thicket yet again, the Court held for the first time that malapportionment claims are justiciable. Id., at 237 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe conclude that the complaint\xe2\x80\x99s allegations of a denial of equal protection present a justiciable constitutional cause of action upon which appellants are entitled to a trial and a decision.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Although the Court in Baker did not reach the merits of the equal protection claim, Baker\xe2\x80\x99s justiciability ruling set the stage for what came to be known as the one-person, one-vote principle. Just two years after Baker, in Wes-berry v. Sanders,\n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x938 (1964), the Court invalidated Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s malapportioned congressional map, underwhich the population of one congressional district was \xe2\x80\x9ctwo to three times\xe2\x80\x9d larger than the population of the others. Relying on Article I, \xc2\xa72, of the Constitution, the Court required that congressional districts be drawn with equal populations. Id., at 7, 18. Later that same Term, in Reynolds v. Sims,\n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 533,\n568 (1964)\n, the Court upheld an equal protection challenge to Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s malapportioned state-legislative maps. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Equal Protection Clause,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court concluded, \xe2\x80\x9crequires that the seats in both houses of a bicameral state legislature must be apportioned on a population basis.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Wesberry and Reynolds together instructed that jurisdictions must design both congressional and state-legislative districts with equal populations, and must regularly reapportion districts to prevent malapportionment.[1]

\n

Over the ensuing decades, the Court has several times elaborated on the scope of the one-person, one-vote rule. States must draw congressional districts with populations as close to perfect equality as possible. See Kirkpatrick v. Preisler,\n\n394 U.\xc2\xa0S. 526\n\xe2\x80\x93531 (1969). But, when drawing state and local legislative districts, jurisdictions are permitted to deviate somewhat from perfect population equality to accommodate traditional districting objectives, among them, preserving the integrity of political subdivisions, maintaining communities of interest, and creating geographic compactness. See Brown v. Thomson,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 835\n\xe2\x80\x93843 (1983). Where the maximum population deviation between the largest and smallest district is less than 10%, the Court has held, a state or local legislative map presumptively complies with the one-person, one-vote rule. Ibid.[2] Maximum deviations above 10% are presumptively impermissible. Ibid. See also Mahan v. Howell,\n\n410 U.\xc2\xa0S. 315,\n329 (1973)\n (approving a state-legislative map with maximum population deviation of 16% to accommodate the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in \xe2\x80\x9cmaintaining the integrity of political subdivision lines,\xe2\x80\x9d but cautioning that this deviation \xe2\x80\x9cmay well approach tolerable limits\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In contrast to repeated disputes over the permissibility of deviating from perfect population equality, little controversy has centered on the population base jurisdictions must equalize. On rare occasions, jurisdictions have relied on the registered-voter or voter-eligible populations of districts. See Burns v. Richardson,\n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 73\n\xe2\x80\x9394 (1966) (holding Hawaii could use a registered-voter population base because of \xe2\x80\x9cHawaii\xe2\x80\x99s special population problems\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94in particular, its substantial temporary military population). But, in the overwhelming majority of cases, jurisdictions have equalized total population, as measured by the decennial census. Today, all States use total-population numbers from the census when designing congressional and state-legislative districts, and only seven States adjust those census numbers in any meaningful way.[3]

\n

B

\n

Appellants challenge that consensus. After the 2010 census, Texas redrew its State Senate districts using a total-population baseline. At the time, Texas was subject to the preclearance requirements of \xc2\xa75 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965.\n\n52 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa710304 (requiring jurisdictions to receive approval from the U.\xc2\xa0S. Department of Justice or the U.\xc2\xa0S. District Court for the District of Columbia before implementing certain voting changes). Once it became clear that the new Senate map, S148, would not receive preclearance in advance of the 2012 elections, the U.\xc2\xa0S. District Court for the Western District of Texas drew an interim Senate map, S164, which also equalized the total population of each district. See Davis v. Perry, No. SA\xe2\x80\x9311\xe2\x80\x93CV\xe2\x80\x93788 (Nov. 23, 2011).[4] On direct appeal, this Court observed that the District Court had failed to \xe2\x80\x9ctake guidance from the State\xe2\x80\x99s recently enacted plan in drafting an interim plan,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore vacated the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s map. Perry v. Perez, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2012) (per curiam) (slip op., at 4, 8\xe2\x80\x9310).

\n

The District Court, on remand, again used census data to draw districts so that each included roughly the same size total population. Texas used this new interim map, S172, in the 2012 elections, and, in 2013, the Texas Legislature adopted S172 as the permanent Senate map. See App. to Brief for Texas Senate Hispanic Caucus et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 5 (reproducing the current Senate map). The permanent map\xe2\x80\x99s maximum total-population deviation is 8.04%, safely within the presumptively permissible 10% range. But measured by a voter-population baseline\xe2\x80\x94eligible voters or registered voters\xe2\x80\x94the map\xe2\x80\x99s maximum population deviation exceeds 40%.

\n

Appellants Sue Evenwel and Edward Pfenninger live in Texas Senate districts (one and four, respectively) with particularly large eligible- and registered-voter populations. Contending that basing apportionment on total population dilutes their votes in relation to voters in other Senate districts, in violation of the one-person, one-vote principle of the Equal Protection Clause,[5] appellants filed suit in the U.\xc2\xa0S. District Court for the Western District of Texas. They named as defendants the Governor and Secretary of State of Texas, and sought a permanent injunction barring use of the existing Senate map in favor of a map that would equalize the voter population in each district.

\n

The case was referred to a three-judge District Court for hearing and decision. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72284(a); Shapiro v. McManus, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2015) (slip op., at 5\xe2\x80\x937). That court dismissed the complaint for failure to state a claim on which relief could be granted. Appellants, the District Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9crel[y] upon a theory never before accepted by the Supreme Court or any circuit court: that the metric of apportionment employed by Texas (total population) results in an unconstitutional apportionment because it does not achieve equality as measured by Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 chosen metric\xe2\x80\x94voter population.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Juris. Statement 9a. Decisions of this Court, the District Court concluded, permit jurisdictions to use any neutral, nondiscriminatory population baseline, including total population, when drawing state and local legislative districts. Id., at 13a\xe2\x80\x9314a.[6]

\n

We noted probable jurisdiction, 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015), and now affirm.

\n

II

\n

The parties and the United States advance different positions in this case. As they did before the District Court, appellants insist that the Equal Protection Clause requires jurisdictions to draw state and local legislative districts with equal voter-eligible populations, thus protecting \xe2\x80\x9cvoter equality,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., \xe2\x80\x9cthe right of eligible voters to an equal vote.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellants 14.[7] To comply with their proposed rule, appellants suggest, jurisdictions should design districts based on citizen-voting-age-population (CVAP) data from the Census Bureau\xe2\x80\x99s American Community Survey (ACS), an annual statistical sample of the U.\xc2\xa0S. population. Texas responds that jurisdic-tions may, consistent with the Equal Protection Clause, design districts using any population baseline\xe2\x80\x94including total population and voter-eligible population\xe2\x80\x94so long as the choice is rational and not invidiously discriminatory. Although its use of total-population data from the census was permissible, Texas therefore argues, it could have used ACS CVAP data instead. Sharing Texas\xe2\x80\x99 position that the Equal Protection Clause does not mandate use of voter-eligible population, the United States urges us not to address Texas\xe2\x80\x99 separate assertion that the Constitution allows States to use alternative population baselines, including voter-eligible population. Equalizing total population, the United States maintains, vindicates the principle of representational equality by \xe2\x80\x9censur[ing] that the voters in each district have the power to elect a representative who represents the same number of constituents as all other representatives.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 5.

\n

In agreement with Texas and the United States, we reject appellants\xe2\x80\x99 attempt to locate a voter-equality mandate in the Equal Protection Clause. As history, precedent, and practice demonstrate, it is plainly permissible for jurisdictions to measure equalization by the total population of state and local legislative districts.

\n

A

\n

We begin with constitutional history. At the time of the founding, the Framers confronted a question analogous to the one at issue here: On what basis should congressional districts be allocated to States? The Framers\xe2\x80\x99 solution, now known as the Great Compromise, was to provide each State the same number of seats in the Senate, and to allocate House seats based on States\xe2\x80\x99 total populations. \xe2\x80\x9cRepresentatives and direct Taxes,\xe2\x80\x9d they wrote, \xe2\x80\x9cshall be apportioned among the several States which may be included within this Union, according to their respective Numbers.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72, cl. 3 (emphasis added). \xe2\x80\x9cIt is a fundamental principle of the proposed constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d James Madison explained in the Federalist Papers, \xe2\x80\x9cthat as the aggregate number of representatives allotted to the several states, is to be .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. founded on the aggregate number of inhabitants; so, the right of choosing this allotted number in each state, is to be exercised by such part of the inhabitants, as the state itself may designate.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 54, p. 284 (G. Carey & J. McClellan eds. 2001). In other words, the basis of representation in the House was to include all inhabitants\xe2\x80\x94although slaves were counted as only three-fifths of a person\xe2\x80\x94even though States remained free to deny many of those inhabitants the right to participate in the selection of their representatives.[8] Endorsing apportionment based on total population, Alexander Hamilton declared: \xe2\x80\x9cThere can be no truer principle than this\xe2\x80\x94that every individual of the commu-nity at large has an equal right to the protection of govern-ment.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Records of the Federal Convention of 1787, p. 473 (M. Farrand ed. 1911).[9]

\n

When debating what is now the\n \nFourteenth Amendment, Congress reconsidered the proper basis for apportioning House seats. Concerned that Southern States would not willingly enfranchise freed slaves, and aware that \xe2\x80\x9ca slave\xe2\x80\x99s freedom could swell his state\xe2\x80\x99s population for purposes of representation in the House by one person, rather than only three-fifths,\xe2\x80\x9d the Framers of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment considered at length the possibility of allocating House seats to States on the basis of voter population. J. Sneed, Footprints on the Rocks of the Mountain: An Account of the Enactment of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment 28 (1997). See also id., at 35 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he apportionment issue consumed more time in the\n \nFourteenth Amendment debates than did any other topic.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In December 1865, Thaddeus Stevens, a leader of the Radical Republicans, introduced a constitutional amendment that would have allocated House seats to States \xe2\x80\x9caccording to their respective legal voters\xe2\x80\x9d; in addition, the proposed amendment mandated that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] true census of the legal voters shall be taken at the same time with the regular census.\xe2\x80\x9d Cong. Globe, 39th Cong., 1st Sess., 10 (1866). Supporters of apportionment based on voter population employed the same voter-equality reasoning that appellants now echo. See, e.g., id., at 380 (remarks of Rep. Orth) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he true principle of representation in Congress is that voters alone should form the basis, and that each voter should have equal political weight in our Government.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 404 (remarks of Rep. Lawrence) (use of total population \xe2\x80\x9cdisregards the fundamental idea of all just representation, that every voter should be equal in political power all over the Union\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Voter-based apportionment proponents encountered fierce resistance from proponents of total-population apportionment. Much of the opposition was grounded in the principle of representational equality. \xe2\x80\x9cAs an abstract proposition,\xe2\x80\x9d argued Representative James G. Blaine, a leading critic of allocating House seats based on voter population, \xe2\x80\x9cno one will deny that population is the true basis of representation; for women, children, and other non-voting classes may have as vital an interest in the legislation of the country as those who actually deposit the ballot.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 141. See also id., at 358 (remarks of Rep. Conkling) (arguing that use of a voter-population basis \xe2\x80\x9cwould shut out four fifths of the citizens of the country\xe2\x80\x94women and children, who are citizens, who are taxed, and who are, and always have been, represented\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 434 (remarks of Rep. Ward) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hat becomes of that large class of non-voting tax-payers that are found in every section? Are they in no matter to be represented? They certainly should be enumerated in making up the whole number of those entitled to a representative.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The product of these debates was \xc2\xa72 of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment, which retained total population as the congressional apportionment base. See U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Amdt. 14, \xc2\xa72 (\xe2\x80\x9cRepresentatives shall be apportioned among the several States according to their respective numbers, counting the whole number of persons in each State, excluding Indians not taxed.\xe2\x80\x9d). Introducing the final version of the Amendment on the Senate floor, Senator Jacob Howard explained:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[The] basis of representation is numbers .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0; that is, the whole population except untaxed Indians and persons excluded by the State laws for rebellion or other crime.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. The committee adopted numbers as the most just and satisfactory basis, and this is the principle upon which the Constitution itself was originally framed, that the basis of representation should depend upon numbers; and such, I think, after all, is the safest and most secure principle upon which the Government can rest. Numbers, not voters; numbers, not property; this is the theory of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Cong. Globe, 39th Cong., 1st Sess., 2766\xe2\x80\x932767 (1866).

\n

Appellants ask us to find in the\n \nFourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Equal Protection Clause a rule inconsistent with this \xe2\x80\x9ctheory of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d But, as the Court recognized in Wesberry, this theory underlies not just the method of allocating House seats to States; it applies as well tothe method of apportioning legislative seats within States. \xe2\x80\x9cThe debates at the [Constitutional] Convention,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cmake at least one fact abundantly clear: that when the delegates agreed that the House should represent \xe2\x80\x98people,\xe2\x80\x99 they intended that in allocating Congressmen the number assigned to each state should be determined solely by the number of inhabitants.\xe2\x80\x9d 376 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 13. \xe2\x80\x9cWhile it may not be possible to draw congressional districts with mathematical precision,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court acknowledged, \xe2\x80\x9cthat is no excuse for ignoring our Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s plain objective of making equal representation for equal numbers of people the fundamental goalfor the House of Representatives.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 18 (emphasis added). It cannot be that the\n \nFourteenth Amendment calls for the apportionment of congressional districts based on total population, but simultaneously prohibits States from apportioning their own legislative districts on the same basis.

\n

Cordoning off the constitutional history of congressional districting, appellants stress two points.[10] First, they draw a distinction between allocating seats to States, and apportioning seats within States. The Framers selected total population for the former, appellants and their amici argue, because of federalism concerns inapposite to intra-state districting. These concerns included the perceived risk that a voter-population base might encourage States to expand the franchise unwisely, and the hope that a total-population base might counter States\xe2\x80\x99 incentive to undercount their populations, thereby reducing their share of direct taxes. Wesberry, however, rejected the distinction appellants now press. See supra, at 12. Even without the weight of Wesberry, we would find appellants\xe2\x80\x99 distinction unconvincing. One can accept that federalism\xe2\x80\x94or, as Justice Alito emphasizes, partisan and regional political advantage, see post, at 6\xe2\x80\x9313\xe2\x80\x94figured in the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 selection of total population as the basis for allocating congressional seats. Even so, it remains beyond doubt that the principle of representational equality figured prominently in the decision to count people, whether or not they qualify as voters.[11]

\n

Second, appellants and Justice Alito urge, see post, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, the Court has typically refused to analogize to features of the federal electoral system\xe2\x80\x94here, the constitutional scheme governing congressional apportionment\xe2\x80\x94when considering challenges to state and local election laws. True, in Reynolds, the Court rejected Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s argument that it had permissibly modeled its State Senate apportionment scheme\xe2\x80\x94one Senator for each county\xe2\x80\x94on the United States Senate. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he federal analogy,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[is] inapposite and irrelevant to state legislative districting schemes\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he system of representation in the two Houses of the Federal Congress\xe2\x80\x9d arose \xe2\x80\x9cfrom unique historical circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 573\xe2\x80\x93574. Likewise, in Gray v. Sanders,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 368\n\xe2\x80\x93372, 378 (1963), Georgia unsuccessfully attempted to defend, by analogy to the electoral college, its scheme of assigning a certain number of \xe2\x80\x9cunits\xe2\x80\x9d to the winner of each county in statewide elections.

\n

Reynolds and Gray, however, involved features of the federal electoral system that contravene the principles of both voter and representational equality to favor interests that have no relevance outside the federal context. Senate seats were allocated to States on an equal basis to respect state sovereignty and increase the odds that the smaller States would ratify the Constitution. See Wesberry, 376 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 9\xe2\x80\x9313 (describing the history of the Great Compromise). See also Reynolds, 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 575 (\xe2\x80\x9cPolitical subdivisions of States\xe2\x80\x94counties, cities, or whatever\xe2\x80\x94never were and never have been considered as sovereign entities.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. The relationship of the States to the Federal Government could hardly be less analogous.\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9cThe [Electoral] College was created to permit the most knowledge-able members of the community to choose the executive of anation whose continental dimensions were thought to preclude an informed choice by the citizenry at large.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams v. Rhodes,\n\n393 U.\xc2\xa0S. 23\n\xe2\x80\x9344 (1968) (Harlan, J., concurring in result). See also Gray, 372 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 378 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe inclusion of the electoral college in the Constitution, as the result of specific historical concerns, validated the collegiate principle despite its inherent numerical inequality.\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)). By contrast, as earlier developed, the constitutional scheme for congressional apportion-ment rests in part on the same representational concerns that exist regarding state and local legislative districting. The Framers\xe2\x80\x99 answer to the apportionment question inthe congressional context therefore undermines appellants\xe2\x80\x99 contention that districts must be based on voter population.

\n

B

\n

Consistent with constitutional history, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s past decisions reinforce the conclusion that States and localities may comply with the one-person, one-vote principle by designing districts with equal total populations. Quoting language from those decisions that, in appellants\xe2\x80\x99 view, supports the principle of equal voting power\xe2\x80\x94and emphasizing the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cone-person, one-vote\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94appellants contend that the Court had in mind, and constantly meant, that States should equalize the voter-eligible population of districts. See Reynolds, 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 568 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n individual\xe2\x80\x99s right to vote for State legislators is unconstitutionally impaired when its weight is in a substantial fashion diluted when compared with votes of citizens living on other parts of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d); Gray, 372 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 379\xe2\x80\x93380 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe concept of \xe2\x80\x98we the people\xe2\x80\x99 under the Constitution visualizes no preferred class of voters but equality among those who meet the basic qualifications.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Hadley v. Junior College Dist. of Metropolitan Kansas City,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 50,\n56 (1970)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen members of an elected body are chosen from separate districts, each district must be established on a basis that will insure, as far as is practicable, that equal numbers of voters can vote for proportionally equal numbers of officials.\xe2\x80\x9d). Appellants, however, extract far too much from selectively chosen language and the \xe2\x80\x9cone-person, one-vote\xe2\x80\x9d slogan.

\n

For every sentence appellants quote from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinions, one could respond with a line casting the one-person, one-vote guarantee in terms of equality of representation, not voter equality. In Reynolds, for instance, the Court described \xe2\x80\x9cthe fundamental principle of representative government in this country\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cone of equal representation for equal numbers of people.\xe2\x80\x9d 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 560\xe2\x80\x93561. See also Davis v. Bandemer,\n\n478 U.\xc2\xa0S. 109,\n123 (1986)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n formulating the one person, one vote formula, the Court characterized the question posed by election districts of disparate size as an issue of fair representation.\xe2\x80\x9d); Reynolds, 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 563 (rejecting state districting schemes that \xe2\x80\x9cgive the same number of representatives to unequal numbers of constituents\xe2\x80\x9d). And the Court has suggested, repeatedly, that districting based on total population serves both the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in preventing vote dilution and its interest in ensuring equality of representation. See Board of Estimate of City of New York v. Morris,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 688\n\xe2\x80\x93694 (1989) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf districts of widely unequal population elect an equal number of representatives, the voting power of each citizen in the larger constituencies is debased and the citizens in those districts have a smaller share of representation than do those in the smaller districts.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Kirkpatrick, 394 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 531 (recognizing in a congressional-districting case that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]qual representation for equal numbers of people is a principle designed to prevent debasement of voting power and diminution of access to elected representatives\xe2\x80\x9d).[12]

\n

Moreover, from Reynolds on, the Court has consistently looked to total-population figures when evaluating whether districting maps violate the Equal Protection Clause bydeviating impermissibly from perfect population equality. See Brief for Appellees 29\xe2\x80\x9331 (collecting cases brought under the Equal Protection Clause). See also id., at 31, n.\xc2\xa09 (collecting congressional-districting cases). Appellants point to no instance in which the Court has determined the permissibility of deviation based on eligible- or registered-voter data. It would hardly make sense for the Court to have mandated voter equality sub silentio and then used a total-population baseline to evaluate compliance with that rule. More likely, we think, the Court has always assumed the permissibility of drawing districts to equalize total population.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn the 1960s,\xe2\x80\x9d appellants counter, \xe2\x80\x9cthe distribution of the voting population generally did not deviate from the distribution of total population to the degree necessary to raise this issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellants 27. To support this assertion, appellants cite only a District Court decision, which found no significant deviation in the distribution of voter and total population in \xe2\x80\x9cdensely populated areas of New York State.\xe2\x80\x9d WMCA, Inc. v. Lomenzo, 238 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 916, 925 (SDNY), aff\xe2\x80\x99d,\n\n382 U.\xc2\xa0S. 4 (1965)\n (\xc2\xa0per curiam). Had this Court assumed such equivalence on a national scale, it likely would have said as much.[13] Instead, in Gaffney v. Cummings,\n\n412 U.\xc2\xa0S. 735\n\xe2\x80\x93747 (1973), the Court acknowledged that voters may be distributed un-evenly within jurisdictions. \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f it is the weight of a person\xe2\x80\x99s vote that matters,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court observed, then \xe2\x80\x9ctotal population\xe2\x80\x94even if stable and accurately taken\xe2\x80\x94may not actually reflect that body of voters whose votes must be counted and weighed for the purposes of reapportionment, because \xe2\x80\x98census persons\xe2\x80\x99 are not voters.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 746. Nonetheless, the Court in Gaffney recognized that the one-person, one-vote rule is designed to facilitate \xe2\x80\x9c[f\xc2\xa0]air and effective representation,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 748, and evaluated compliance with the rule based on total population alone, id., at 750.

\n

C

\n

What constitutional history and our prior decisions strongly suggest, settled practice confirms. Adopting voter-eligible apportionment as constitutional command would upset a well-functioning approach to districting that all 50 States and countless local jurisdictions have followed for decades, even centuries. Appellants have shown no reason for the Court to disturb this longstanding use of total population. See Walz v. Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of City of New York,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 664,\n678 (1970)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cunbroken practice\xe2\x80\x9d followed \xe2\x80\x9copenly and by affirmative state action, not covertly or by state inaction, is not something to be lightly cast aside\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Burson v. Freeman,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 191\n\xe2\x80\x93206 (1992) (plurality opinion) (upholding a law limiting campaigning in areas around polling places in part because all 50 States maintain such laws, so there is a \xe2\x80\x9cwidespread and time-tested consensus\xe2\x80\x9d that legislation of this order serves important state interests). As the Framers of the Constitution and the\n \nFourteenth Amendment comprehended, representatives serve all residents, not just those eligible or registered to vote. See supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x9312. Nonvoters have an important stake in many policy debates\xe2\x80\x94children, their parents, even their grandparents, for example, have a stake in a strong public-education system\xe2\x80\x94and in receiving constituent services, such as help navigating public-benefits bureaucracies. By ensuring that each representative is subject to requests and suggestions from the same number of constituents, total-population apportionment promotes equitable and effective representation. See McCormick v. United States,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 257,\n272 (1991)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cServing constituents and supporting legislation that will benefit the district and individ-uals and groups therein is the everyday business of a legislator.\xe2\x80\x9d).[14]

\n

In sum, the rule appellants urge has no mooring in the Equal Protection Clause. The Texas Senate map, we therefore conclude, complies with the requirements of the one-person, one-vote principle.[15] Because history, precedent, and practice suffice to reveal the infirmity of appellants\xe2\x80\x99 claims, we need not and do not resolve whether, as Texas now argues, States may draw districts to equalize voter-eligible population rather than total population.

Justice Thomas, concurring in the judgment.

\n

This case concerns whether Texas violated the Equal Protection Clause\xe2\x80\x94as interpreted by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s one-person, one-vote cases\xe2\x80\x94by creating legislative districts that contain approximately equal total population but vary widely in the number of eligible voters in each district. I agree with the majority that our precedents do not require a State to equalize the total number of voters in each district. States may opt to equalize total population. I therefore concur in the majority\xe2\x80\x99s judgment that appellants\xe2\x80\x99 challenge fails.

\n

I write separately because this Court has never provided a sound basis for the one-person, one-vote principle. For 50 years, the Court has struggled to define what right that principle protects. Many of our precedents suggest that it protects the right of eligible voters to cast votes that receive equal weight. Despite that frequent explanation, our precedents often conclude that the Equal Protection Clause is satisfied when all individuals within a district\xe2\x80\x94voters or not\xe2\x80\x94have an equal share of representation. The majority today concedes that our cases have not produced a clear answer on this point. See ante, at 16.

\n

In my view, the majority has failed to provide a sound basis for the one-person, one-vote principle because no such basis exists. The Constitution does not prescribe any one basis for apportionment within States. It instead leaves States significant leeway in apportioning their own districts to equalize total population, to equalize eligible voters, or to promote any other principle consistent with a republican form of government. The majority should recognize the futility of choosing only one of these options. The Constitution leaves the choice to the people alone\xe2\x80\x94not to this Court.

\n

I

\n

In the 1960\xe2\x80\x99s, this Court decided that the Equal Protection Clause requires States to draw legislative districts based on a \xe2\x80\x9cone-person, one-vote\xe2\x80\x9d rule.[1]* But this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions have never coalesced around a single theory about what States must equalize.

\n

The Equal Protection Clause prohibits a State from \xe2\x80\x9cdeny[ing] to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Amdt. 14, \xc2\xa71. For nearly a century after its ratification, this Court interpreted the Clauseas having no application to the politically charged issue of how States should apportion their populations in political districts. See, e.g., Colegrove v. Green,\n\n328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 549,\n556 (1946)\n (plurality opinion). Instead, the Court left the drawing of States\xe2\x80\x99 political boundaries to the States, so long as a State did not deprive people of the right to vote for reasons prohibited by the Constitution. See id., at 552, 556; Gomillion v. Lightfoot,\n\n364 U.\xc2\xa0S. 339\n\xe2\x80\x93348 (1960) (finding justiciable a claim that a city boundary was redrawn from a square shape to \xe2\x80\x9ca strangely irregular twenty-eight-sided figure\xe2\x80\x9d to remove nearly all black voters from the city). This meant that a State\xe2\x80\x99s refusalto allocate voters within districts based on population changes was a matter for States\xe2\x80\x94not federal courts\xe2\x80\x94to decide. And these cases were part of a larger jurisprudence holding that the question whether a state governmenthad a \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d republican form rested with Congress. Pacific States Telephone & Telegraph Co. v. Oregon,\n\n223 U.\xc2\xa0S. 118\n\xe2\x80\x93150 (1912).

\n

This Court changed course in Baker v. Carr,\n\n369 U.\xc2\xa0S. 186 (1962)\n, by locating in the Equal Protection Clause a right of citizens not to have a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98debasement of their votes.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 194, and n.\xc2\xa015, 200. Expanding on that decision, this Court later held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Equal Protection Clause requires that the seats in both houses of a bicameral state legislature must be apportioned on a population basis.\xe2\x80\x9d Reynolds v. Sims,\n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 533,\n568 (1964)\n. The Court created an analogous requirement for congressional redistricting rooted in Article I, \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that \xe2\x80\x9cRepresentatives be chosen \xe2\x80\x98by the People of the several States.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Wesberry v. Sanders,\n\n376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x939 (1964). The rules established by these cases have come to be known as \xe2\x80\x9cone person, one vote.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Since Baker empowered the federal courts to resolve redistricting disputes, this Court has struggled to explain whether the one-person, one-vote principle ensures equal-ity among eligible voters or instead protects some broader right of every citizen to equal representation. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s lack of clarity on this point, in turn, has left unclear whether States must equalize the number of eligible voters across districts or only total population.

\n

In a number of cases, this Court has said that States must protect the right of eligible voters to have their votes receive equal weight. On this view, there is only one way for States to comply with the one-person, one-vote principle: they must draw districts that contain a substantially equal number of eligible voters per district.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s seminal decision in Baker exemplifies this view. Decided in 1962, Baker involved the failure of the Tennessee Legislature to reapportion its districts for 60 years. 369 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 191. Since Tennessee\xe2\x80\x99s last apportionment, the State\xe2\x80\x99s population had grown by about 1.5 million residents, from about 2 to more than 3.5 million. And the number of voters in each district had changed significantly over time, producing widely varying voting populations in each district. Id., at 192. Under these facts, the Court held that reapportionment claims were justiciable because the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x94who all claimed to be eligible voters\xe2\x80\x94had alleged a \xe2\x80\x9cdebasement of their votes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 194, and n. 15, 204 (internal quotation marksomitted).

\n

The Court similarly emphasized equal treatment of eligible voters in Gray v. Sanders,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 368 (1963)\n. That case involved a challenge to Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccounty unit\xe2\x80\x9d system of voting. Id., at 370. This system, used by the State\xe2\x80\x99s Democratic Party to nominate candidates in its primary, gave each county two votes for every representative that the county had in the lower House of its General Assembly. Voting was then done by county, with the winner in each county taking all of that county\xe2\x80\x99s votes. The Democratic Party nominee was the candidate who had won the most county-unit votes, not the person who had won the most individual votes. Id., at 370\xe2\x80\x93371. The effect of this system was to give heavier weight to rural ballots than to urban ones. The Court held that the system violated the one-person, one-vote principle. Id., at 379\xe2\x80\x93381, and n.\xc2\xa012. In so holding, the Court emphasized that the right at issue belongs to \xe2\x80\x9call qualified voters\xe2\x80\x9d and is the right to have one\xe2\x80\x99s vote \xe2\x80\x9ccounted once\xe2\x80\x9d and protected against dilution. Id., at 380.

\n

In applying the one-person, one-vote principle to state legislative districts, the Court has also emphasized vote dilution, which also supports the notion that the one-person, one-vote principle ensures equality among eligible voters. It did so most notably in Reynolds. In that case, Alabama had failed to reapportion its state legislature for decades, resulting in population-variance ratios of up to about 41 to 1 in the State Senate and up to about 16 to 1 in the House. 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 545. In explaining why Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s failure to reapportion violated the Equal Protection Clause, this Court stated that \xe2\x80\x9can individual\xe2\x80\x99s right to vote for state legislators is unconstitutionally impaired when its weight is in a substantial fashion diluted when compared with votes of citizens living in other parts of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 568.

\n

This Court\xe2\x80\x99s post-Reynolds decisions likewise define the one-person, one-vote principle in terms of eligible voters, and thus imply that States should be allocating districts with eligible voters in mind. The Court suggested as much in Hadley v. Junior College Dist. of Metropolitan Kansas City,\n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 50 (1970)\n. That case involved Missouri\xe2\x80\x99s system permitting separate school districts to establish a joint junior college district. Six trustees were to oversee the joint district, and they were apportioned on the basis of the relative numbers of school-aged children in each subsidiary district. Id., at 51. The Court held that this plan violated the Equal Protection Clause because \xe2\x80\x9cthe trustees of this junior college district [must] be apportioned in a manner that does not deprive any voter of his right to have his own vote given as much weight, as far as is practicable, as that of any other voter in the junior college district.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 52. In so holding, the Court emphasized that Reynolds had \xe2\x80\x9ccalled attention to prior cases indicating that a qualified voter has a constitutional right to vote in elections without having his vote wrongfully denied, debased, or diluted.\xe2\x80\x9d Hadley, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 52; see id., at 52\xe2\x80\x9353.

\n

In contrast to this oft-stated aspiration of giving equal treatment to eligible voters, the Court has also expressed a different understanding of the one-person, one-vote principle. In several cases, the Court has suggested that one-person, one-vote protects the interests of all individuals in a district, whether they are eligible voters or not. In Reynolds, for example, the Court said that \xe2\x80\x9cthe fundamental principle of representative government in this country is one of equal representation for equal numbers of people.\xe2\x80\x9d 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 560\xe2\x80\x93561; see also ante, at 16 (collecting cases). Under this view, States cannot comply with the Equal Protection Clause by equalizing the number of eligible voters in each district. They must instead equalize the total population per district.

\n

In line with this view, the Court has generally focused on total population, not the total number of voters, when determining a State\xe2\x80\x99s compliance with the one-person, one-vote requirement. In Gaffney v. Cummings,\n\n412 U.\xc2\xa0S. 735\n\xe2\x80\x93751 (1973), for example, the Court upheld state legislative districts that had a maximum deviation of 7.83% when measured on a total-population basis. In contrast, in Chapman v. Meier,\n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9322, 26\xe2\x80\x9327 (1975), the Court struck down a court-ordered reapportionment that had a total deviation of 20.14% based on total population. This plan, in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, failed to \xe2\x80\x9cachieve the goal of population equality with little more than de minimis variation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 27.

\n

This lack of clarity in our redistricting cases has left States with little guidance about how their political institutions must be structured. Although this Court has required that state legislative districts \xe2\x80\x9cbe apportioned on a population basis,\xe2\x80\x9d Reynolds, supra, at 568, it has yet to tell the States whether they are limited in choosing \xe2\x80\x9cthe relevant population that [they] must equally distribute.\xe2\x80\x9d Chen v. Houston,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1046,\n1047 (2001)\n (Thomas, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (internal quotation marks omitted). Because the Court has not provided a firm account of what States must do when districting, States are left to guess how much flexibility (if any) they have to use different methods of apportionment.

\n

II

\n

This inconsistency (if not opacity) is not merely a consequence of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s equivocal statements on one person, one vote. The problem is more fundamental. There is simply no way to make a principled choice between interpreting one person, one vote as protecting eligible voters or as protecting total inhabitants within a State. That is because, though those theories are noble, the Constitution does not make either of them the exclusive means of apportionment for state and local representatives. In guaranteeing to the States a \xe2\x80\x9cRepublican Form of Government,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0IV, \xc2\xa74, the Constitution did not resolve whether the ultimate basis of representation is the right of citizens to cast an equal ballot or the right of all inhabitants to have equal representation. The Constitution instead reserves these matters to the people. The major-ity\xe2\x80\x99s attempt today to divine a single \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98theory of the Consti-tution\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94apportionment based on representation, ante, at 12 (quoting Cong. Globe, 39th Cong., 1st Sess., 2766\xe2\x80\x932767 (1866))\xe2\x80\x94rests on a flawed reading of history and wrongly picks one side of a debate that the Framers did not resolve in the Constitution.

\n

A

\n

The Constitution lacks a single, comprehensive theory of representation. The Framers understood the tension between majority rule and protecting fundamental rights from majorities. This understanding led to a \xe2\x80\x9cmixed\xe2\x80\x9d constitutional structure that did not embrace any single theory of representation but instead struck a compromise between those who sought an equitable system of representation and those who were concerned that the majority would abuse plenary control over public policy. As Madison wrote, \xe2\x80\x9cA dependence on the people is no doubt the primary controul on the government; but experience has taught mankind the necessity of auxiliary precautions.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 51, p. 349 (J. Cooke ed. 1961). This was the theory of the Constitution. The Framers therefore made difficult compromises on the apportionment of federal representation, and they did not prescribe any one theory of how States had to divide their legislatures.

\n

1

\n

Because, in the view of the Framers, ultimate political power derives from citizens who were \xe2\x80\x9ccreated equal,\xe2\x80\x9d The Declaration of Independence \xc2\xb62, beliefs in equality of representation\xe2\x80\x94and by extension, majority rule\xe2\x80\x94influenced the constitutional structure. In the years between the Revolution and the framing, the Framers experimented with different ways of securing the political system against improper influence. Of all the \xe2\x80\x9celectoral safeguards for the representational system,\xe2\x80\x9d the most critical was \xe2\x80\x9cequality of representation.\xe2\x80\x9d G. Wood, The Creation of the American Republic 1776\xe2\x80\x931787, p. 170 (1998) (Wood).

\n

The Framers\xe2\x80\x99 preference for apportionment by representation (and majority rule) was driven partially by the belief that all citizens were inherently equal. In a system where citizens were equal, a legislature should have \xe2\x80\x9cequal representation\xe2\x80\x9d so that \xe2\x80\x9cequal interests among the people should have equal interests in [the assembly].\xe2\x80\x9d Thoughts on Government, in 4 Works of John Adams 195 (C. Adams ed. 1851). The British Parliament fell short of this goal. In addition to having hereditary nobility, more than half of the members of the democratic House of Commons were elected from sparsely populated districts\xe2\x80\x94so-called \xe2\x80\x9crotten boroughs.\xe2\x80\x9d Wood 171; Baker, 369 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 302\xe2\x80\x93303 (Frankfurter, J., dissenting).

\n

The Framers\xe2\x80\x99 preference for majority rule also was a reaction to the shortcomings of the Articles of Confederation. Under the Articles, each State could cast one vote regardless of population and Congress could act only with the assent of nine States. Articles of Confederation, Art. IX, cl. 6; id., Art. X; id., Art. XI. This system proved undesirable because a few small States had the ability to paralyze the National Legislature. See The Federalist No. 22, at 140\xe2\x80\x93141 (Hamilton).

\n

Consequently, when the topic of dividing representation came up at the Constitutional Convention, some Framers advocated proportional representation throughout the National Legislature. 1 Records of the Federal Convention of 1787, pp. 471\xe2\x80\x93473 (M. Farrand ed. 1911). Alexander Hamilton voiced concerns about the unfairness of allowing a minority to rule over a majority. In explaining at the Convention why he opposed giving States an equal vote in the National Legislature, Hamilton asked rhetorically, \xe2\x80\x9cIf .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. three states contain a majority of the inhabitants of America, ought they to be governed by a minority?\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 473; see also The Federalist No. 22, at 141 (Hamilton) (objecting to supermajoritarian voting requirements because they allow an entrenched minority to \xe2\x80\x9ccontroul the opinion of a majority respecting the best mode of conducting [the public business]\xe2\x80\x9d). James Madison, too, opined that the general Government needed a direct mandate from the people. If federal \xe2\x80\x9cpower [were] not immediately derived from the people, in proportion to their numbers,\xe2\x80\x9d according to Madison, the Federal Government would be as weak as Congress under the Articles of Confederation. 1 Records of the Federal Convention of 1787, at 472.

\n

In many ways, the Constitution reflects this preference for majority rule. To pass Congress, ordinary legislation requires a simple majority of present members to vote in favor. And some features of the apportionment for the House of Representatives reflected the idea that States should wield political power in approximate proportion to their number of inhabitants. Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9312. Thus, \xe2\x80\x9cequal representation for equal numbers of people,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12 (internal quotation marks and emphasis omitted), features prominently in how representatives are apportioned among the States. These features of the Constitution reflect the preference of some members of the found-ing generation for equality of representation. But, as explained below, this is not the single \xe2\x80\x9ctheory of theConstitution.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

2

\n

The Framers also understood that unchecked majorities could lead to tyranny of the majority. As a result, many viewed antidemocratic checks as indispensable to republican government. And included among the antidemocratic checks were legislatures that deviated from perfect equality of representation.

\n

The Framers believed that a proper government promoted the common good. They conceived this good as objective and not inherently coextensive with majoritarian preferences. See, e.g., The Federalist No. 1, at 4 (Hamilton) (defining the common good or \xe2\x80\x9cpublic good\xe2\x80\x9d as the \xe2\x80\x9ctrue interests\xe2\x80\x9d of the community); id., No. 10, at 57 (Madison) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe permanent and aggregate interests of the community\xe2\x80\x9d). For government to promote the common good, it had to do more than simply obey the will of the majority. See, e.g., ibid. (discussing majoritarian factions). Government must also protect fundamental rights. See The Declaration of Independence \xc2\xb62; 1 W. Blackstone, Commentaries *124 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he principal aim of society is to protect individuals in the enjoyment of those absolute rights, which are vested in them by the immutable laws ofnature\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Of particular concern for the Framers was the majority of people violating the property rights of the minority. Madison observed that \xe2\x80\x9cthe most common and durable source of factions, has been the various and unequal distribution of property.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 10, at 59. A poignant example occurred in Massachusetts. In what became known as Shays\xe2\x80\x99 Rebellion, armed debtors attempted to block legal actions by creditors to recover debts. Although that rebellion was ultimately put down, debtors sought relief from state legislatures \xe2\x80\x9cunder the auspices of Constitutional forms.\xe2\x80\x9d Letter from James Madison to Thomas Jefferson (Apr. 23, 1787), in 11 The Papers of Thomas Jefferson 307 (J. Boyd ed. 1955); see Wood 412\xe2\x80\x93413. With no structural political checks on democratic lawmaking, creditors found their rights jeopardized by state laws relieving debtors of their obligation to pay and authorizing forms of payment that devalued the contracts. McConnell, Contract Rights and Property Rights: A Case Study in the Relationship Between Individual Liberties and Constitutional Structures, 76 Cal. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 267, 280\xe2\x80\x93281 (1988); see also Fletcher v. Peck, 6 Cranch 87, 137\xe2\x80\x93138 (1810) (Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J.) (explaining that the Contract Clause came from the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 desire to \xe2\x80\x9cshield themselves and their property from the effectsof those sudden and strong passions to which men are exposed\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Because of the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 concerns about placing unchecked power in political majorities, the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s majoritarian provisions were only part of a complex republican structure. The Framers also placed several anti-democratic provisions in the Constitution. The original Constitution permitted only the direct election ofrepresentatives. Art. I, \xc2\xa72, cl. 1. Senators and the President were selected indirectly. See Art. I, \xc2\xa73, cl. 1; Art. II, \xc2\xa71, cls. 2\xe2\x80\x933. And the \xe2\x80\x9cGreat Compromise\xe2\x80\x9d guaranteed large and small States voting equality in the Senate. By malapportioning the Senate, the Framers prevented large States from outvoting small States to adopt policies that would advance the large States\xe2\x80\x99 interests at the expenseof the small States. See The Federalist No. 62, at 417 (Madison).

\n

These countermajoritarian measures reflect the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 aspirations of promoting competing goals. Rejecting a hereditary class system, they thought political power resided with the people. At the same time, they sought to check majority rule to promote the common good and mitigate threats to fundamental rights.

\n

B

\n

As the Framers understood, designing a government to fulfill the conflicting tasks of respecting the fundamental equality of persons while promoting the common good requires making incommensurable tradeoffs. For this reason, they did not attempt to restrict the States to one form of government.

\n

Instead, the Constitution broadly required that the States maintain a \xe2\x80\x9cRepublican Form of Government.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. IV, \xc2\xa74. But the Framers otherwise left it to States to make tradeoffs and reconcile the competing goals.

\n

Republican governments promote the common good by placing power in the hands of the people, while curtailing the majority\xe2\x80\x99s ability to invade the minority\xe2\x80\x99s fundamental rights. The Framers recognized that there is no universal formula for accomplishing these goals. At the framing, many state legislatures were bicameral, often reflecting multiple theories of representation. Only \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ix of the original thirteen states based representation in bothhouses of their state legislatures on population.\xe2\x80\x9d Hayden, The False Promise of One Person, One Vote, 102 Mich. L. Rev. 213, 218 (2003). In most States, it was common to base representation, at least in part, on the State\xe2\x80\x99s political subdivisions, even if those subdivisions varied heavily in their populations. Wood 171; Baker, 369 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 307\xe2\x80\x93308 (Frankfurter, J., dissenting).

\n

Reflecting this history, the Constitution continued to afford States significant leeway in structuring their \xe2\x80\x9cRepublican\xe2\x80\x9d governments. At the framing, \xe2\x80\x9crepublican\xe2\x80\x9d referred to \xe2\x80\x9c[p]lacing the government in the people,\xe2\x80\x9d and a \xe2\x80\x9crepublick\xe2\x80\x9d was a \xe2\x80\x9cstate in which the power is lodged in more than one.\xe2\x80\x9d S. Johnson, A Dictionary of the English Language (7th ed. 1785); see also The Federalist No. 39, at 251 (Madison) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e may define a republic to be, or at least may bestow that name on, a government which derives all its powers directly or indirectly from the great body of the people; and is administered by persons holding their offices during pleasure, for a limited period, or during good behaviour\xe2\x80\x9d). By requiring the States to have republican governments, the Constitution prohibited them from having monarchies and aristocracies. See id., No. 43, at 291. Some would argue that the Constitution also prohibited States from adopting direct democracies. Compare Wood 222\xe2\x80\x93226 (\xe2\x80\x9cFor most constitution-makers in 1776, republicanism was not equated with democracy\xe2\x80\x9d) with A. Amar, America\xe2\x80\x99s Constitution: A Biography 276\xe2\x80\x93281 (2005) (arguing that the provision prohibited monarchies and aristocracies but not direct democracy); see also The Federalist No. 10, at 62 (Madison) (distinguishing a \xe2\x80\x9cdemocracy\xe2\x80\x9d and a \xe2\x80\x9crepublic\xe2\x80\x9d); id., No. 14, at 83\xe2\x80\x9384 (same).

\n

Beyond that, however, the Constitution left matters open for the people of the States to decide. The Constitution says nothing about what type of republican government the States must follow. When the Framers wanted to deny powers to state governments, they did so explicitly. See, e.g., Art. I, \xc2\xa710, cl. 1 (\xe2\x80\x9cNo State shall .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. pass anyBill of Attainder, ex post facto Law, or Law impairing the Obligation of Contracts\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

None of the Reconstruction Amendments changed the original understanding of republican government. Those Amendments brought blacks within the existing American political community. The\n \nFourteenth Amendment pressured States to adopt universal male suffrage by reducing a noncomplying State\xe2\x80\x99s representation in Congress. Amdt. 14, \xc2\xa72. And the\n \nFifteenth Amendment prohibited restricting the right of suffrage based on race. Amdt. 15, \xc2\xa71. That is as far as those Amendments went. As Justice Harlan explained in Reynolds, neither Amendment provides a theory of how much \xe2\x80\x9cweight\xe2\x80\x9d a vote must receive, nor do they require a State to apportion both Houses of their legislature solely on a population basis. See 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 595\xe2\x80\x93608 (dissenting opinion). And Justice Alito quite convincingly demonstrates why the majority errs by reading a theory of equal representation into the apportionment provision in \xc2\xa72 of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment. See post, at 8\xe2\x80\x9313 (opinion concurring in judgment).

\n

C

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to impose its political theory upon the States has produced a morass of problems. These problems are antithetical to the values that the Framers embraced in the Constitution. These problems confirm that the Court has been wrong to entangle itself with the political process.

\n

First, in embracing one person, one vote, the Court has arrogated to the Judiciary important value judgments that the Constitution reserves to the people. In Reynolds, for example, the Court proclaimed that \xe2\x80\x9c[l]egislators represent people, not trees or acres\xe2\x80\x9d; that \xe2\x80\x9c[l]egislators are elected by voters, not farms or cities or economic interests\xe2\x80\x9d; and that, accordingly, electoral districts must have roughly equal population. 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 562\xe2\x80\x93563. As I have explained, the Constitution permits, but does not impose, this view. Beyond that, Reynolds\xe2\x80\x99 assertions are driven by the belief that there is a single, correct answer to the question of how much voting strength an individual citizen should have. These assertions overlook that, to control factions that would legislate against the common good, individual voting strength must sometimes yield to countermajoritarian checks. And this principle has no less force within States than it has for the federal system. See The Federalist No. 10, at 63\xe2\x80\x9365 (Madison) (recognizing that smaller republics, such as the individual States, are more prone to capture by special interests). Instead of large States versus small States, those interests may pit urban areas versus rural, manufacturing versus agriculture, or those with property versus those without. Cf. Reynolds, supra, at 622\xe2\x80\x93623 (Harlan, J., dissenting). There is no single method of reconciling these competing interests. And it is not the role of this Court to calibrate democracy in the vain search for an optimum solution.

\n

The Government argues that apportioning legislators by any metric other than total population \xe2\x80\x9crisks rendering residents of this country who are ineligible, unwilling, or unable to vote as invisible or irrelevant to our system of representative democracy.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 27. But that argument rests on the faulty premise that \xe2\x80\x9cour system of representative democracy\xe2\x80\x9d requires specific groups to have representation in a specific manner. As I have explained, the Constitution does not impose that requirement. See Parts II\xe2\x80\x93A, II\xe2\x80\x93B, supra. And as the Court recently reminded us, States are free to serve as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98laboratories\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of democracy. Arizona State Legislature v. Arizona Independent Redistricting Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ____, ____ (2015) (slip op., at 28). That \xe2\x80\x9claboratory\xe2\x80\x9d extends to experimenting about the nature of democracy itself.

\n

Second, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to monitor the political process have failed to provide any consistent guidance for the States. Even if it were justifiable for this Court to enforce some principle of majority rule, it has been unable to do so in a principled manner. Our precedents do not address the myriad other ways that minorities (or fleeting majorities) entrench themselves in the political system. States can place policy choices in their constitutions or have supermajoritarian voting rules in a legislative assembly. See, e.g., N.\xc2\xa0Y. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0V, \xc2\xa77 (constitutionalizing public employee pensions); Ill. Const., Art. VII, \xc2\xa76(g) (requiring a three-fifths vote of the General Assembly to pre-empt certain local ordinances). In theory, of course, it does not seem to make a difference if a state legislature is unresponsive to the majority of residents because the state assembly requires a 60% vote to pass a bill or because 40% of the population elects 51% of the representatives.

\n

So far as the Constitution is concerned, there is no single \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d way to design a republican government. Any republic will have to reconcile giving power to the people with diminishing the influence of special interests. The wisdom of the Framers was that they recognized this dilemma and left it to the people to resolve. In trying to impose its own theory of democracy, the Court is hope-lessly adrift amid political theory and interest-group politics with no guiding legal principles.

\n

III

\n

This case illustrates the confusion that our cases have wrought. The parties and the Government offer three positions on what this Court\xe2\x80\x99s one-person, one-vote cases require States to equalize. Under appellants\xe2\x80\x99 view, the\n \nFourteenth Amendment protects the right to an equal vote. Brief for Appellants 26. Appellees, in contrast, argue that the\n \nFourteenth Amendment protects against invidious discrimination; in their view, no such discrimination occurs when States have a rational basis for the population base that they select, even if that base leaves eligible voters malapportioned. Brief for Appellees 16\xe2\x80\x9317. And, the Solicitor General suggests that reapportionment by total population is the only permissible standard because Reynolds recognized a right of \xe2\x80\x9cequal representation for equal numbers of people.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 17.

\n

Although the majority does not choose among these theories, it necessarily denies that the Equal Protection Clause protects the right to cast an equally weighted ballot. To prevail, appellants do not have to deny the importance of equal representation. Because States can equalize both total population and total voting power within the districts, they have to show only that the right to cast an equally weighted vote is part of the one-person, one-vote right that we have recognized. But the majority declines to find such a right in the Equal Protection Clause. Ante, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319. Rather, the majority acknowl-edges that \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or every sentence appellants quote from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinions [establishing a right to an equal vote], one could respond with a line casting the one-person, one-vote guarantee in terms of equality of representation, not voter equality.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. Because our precedents are not consistent with appellants\xe2\x80\x99 position\xe2\x80\x94that the only constitutionally available choice for States is to allocate districts to equalize eligible voters\xe2\x80\x94the majority concludes that appellants\xe2\x80\x99 challenge fails. Ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

I agree with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate disposition of this case. As far as the original understanding of the Constitution is concerned, a State has wide latitude in selecting its population base for apportionment. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, supra. It can use total population, eligible voters, or any other nondiscriminatory voter base. Ibid. And States with a bicameral legislature can have some mixture of these theories, such as one population base for its lower house and another for its upper chamber. Ibid.

\n

Our precedents do not compel a contrary conclusion. Appellants are correct that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents have primarily based its one-person, one-vote jurisprudence on the theory that eligible voters have a right against vote dilution. E.g., Hadley, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 52\xe2\x80\x9353; Reynolds, 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 568. But this Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisprudence has vacillated too much for me to conclude that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents preclude States from allocating districts based on total population instead. See Burns, 384 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 92 (recognizing that States may choose other nondiscriminatory population bases). Under these circumstances, the choice is best left for the people of the States to decide for themselves how they should apportion their legislature.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

There is no single \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect\xe2\x80\x9d method of apportioning state legislatures. And the Constitution did not make this Court \xe2\x80\x9ca centralized politburo appointed for life to dictate to the provinces the \xe2\x80\x98correct\xe2\x80\x99 theories of democratic representation, [or] the \xe2\x80\x98best\xe2\x80\x99 electoral systems for securing truly \xe2\x80\x98representative\xe2\x80\x99 government.\xe2\x80\x9d Holder v. Hall,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 874,\n913 (1994)\n (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). Because the majority continues that misguided search, I concur only in the judgment.

SUE EVENWEL, et\xc2\xa0al., APPELLANTS v. GREG ABBOTT, governor of texas, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for the western district of texas

\n

[April 4, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Alito, with whom Justice Thomas joins except as to Part III\xe2\x80\x93B, concurring in the judgment.

\n

The question that the Court must decide in this case is whether Texas violated the \xe2\x80\x9cone-person, one-vote\xe2\x80\x9d principle established in Reynolds v. Sims,\n\n377 U.\xc2\xa0S. 533 (1964)\n, by adopting a legislative redistricting plan that provides for districts that are roughly equal in total population. Appellants contend that Texas was required to create districts that are equal in the number of eligible voters, but I agree with the Court that Texas\xe2\x80\x99 use of total population did not violate the one-person, one-vote rule.

\n

I

\n

Both practical considerations and precedent support the conclusion that the use of total population is consistent with the one-person, one-vote rule. The decennial census required by the Constitution tallies total population. Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa03; Amdt. 14, \xc2\xa72. These statistics are more reliable and less subject to manipulation and dispute than statistics concerning eligible voters. Since Reynolds, States have almost uniformly used total population in attempting to create legislative districts that are equal in size. And with one notable exception, Burns v. Richardson,\n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 73 (1966)\n, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s post-Reynolds cases have likewise looked to total population. Moreover, much of the time, creating districts that are equal in total population also results in the creation of districts that are at least roughly equal in eligible voters. I therefore agree that States are permitted to use total population in redistricting plans.

\n

II

\n

Although this conclusion is sufficient to decide the case before us, Texas asks us to go further and to hold that States, while generally free to use total population statistics, are not barred from using eligible voter statistics. Texas points to Burns, in which this Court held that Hawaii did not violate the one-person, one-vote principle by adopting a plan that sought to equalize the number of registered voters in each district.

\n

Disagreeing with Texas, the Solicitor General dismisses Burns as an anomaly and argues that the use of total population is constitutionally required. The Solicitor General contends that the one-person, one-vote rule means that all persons, whether or not they are eligible to vote, are entitled to equal representation in the legislature. Accordingly, he argues, legislative districts must be equal in total population even if that results in districts that are grossly unequal in the number of eligible voters, a situation that is most likely to arise where aliens are disproportionately concentrated in some parts of a State.

\n

This argument, like that advanced by appellants, implicates very difficult theoretical and empirical questions about the nature of representation. For centuries, political theorists have debated the proper role of representatives,[1] and political scientists have studied the conduct of legislators and the interests that they actually advance.[2] We have no need to wade into these waters in this case, and I would not do so. Whether a State is permitted to use some measure other than total population is an important and sensitive question that we can consider if and when we have before us a state districting plan that, unlike the current Texas plan, uses something other than total population as the basis for equalizing the size of districts.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

The Court does not purport to decide whether a State may base a districting plan on something other than total population, but the Court, picking up a key component of the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x99s argument, suggests that the use of total population is supported by the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s formula for allocating seats in the House of Representatives among the States. Because House seats are allocated based on total population, the Solicitor General argues, the one-person, one-vote principle requires districts that are equal in total population. I write separately primarily because I cannot endorse this meretricious argument.

\n

First, the allocation of congressional representation sheds little light on the question presented by the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x99s argument because that allocation plainly violates one person, one vote.[3] This is obviously true with respect to the Senate: Although all States have equal representation in the Senate, the most populous State (California) has 66 times as many people as the least populous (Wyoming). See United States Census 2010, Resident Population Data, http://www.census.gov/\xe2\x80\x8c2010census/\xe2\x80\x8cdata/\xe2\x80\x8capportionment-pop-text.php. And even the allocation of House seats does not comport with one person, one vote. Every State is entitled to at least one seat in the House, even if the State\xe2\x80\x99s population is lower than the average population of House districts nationwide. U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa03. Today, North Dakota, Vermont, and Wyoming all fall into that category. See United States Census 2010, Apportionment Data, http://www.census.gov/2010census/data/apportionment-data-text.php. If one person, one vote applied to allocation of House seats among States, I very much doubt the Court would uphold a plan where one Representative represents fewer than 570,000 people in Wyoming but nearly a million people next door in Montana.[4]

\n

Second, Reynolds v. Sims squarely rejected the argument that the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s allocation of congressional representation establishes the test for the constitution-ality of a state legislative districting plan. Under one Ala-bama districting plan before the Court in that case, seats in the State Senate were allocated by county, much as seats in the United States Senate are allocated by State. (At that time, the upper houses in most state legislatures were similar in this respect.) The Reynolds Court noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he system of representation in the two Houses of the Federal Congress\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cconceived out of compromise and concession indispensable to the establishment of our federal republic.\xe2\x80\x9d 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 574. Rejecting Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s argument that this system supported the constitutionality of the State\xe2\x80\x99s apportionment of senate seats, the Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Founding Fathers clearly had no intention of establishing a pattern or model for the apportionment of seats in state legislatures when the system of representation in the Federal Congress was adopted.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 573; see also Gray v. Sanders,\n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 368,\n378 (1963)\n.

\n

Third, as the Reynolds Court recognized, reliance on the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s allocation of congressional representation is profoundly ahistorical. When the formula for allocating House seats was first devised in 1787 and reconsidered at the time of the adoption of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment in 1868, the overwhelming concern was far removed from any abstract theory about the nature of representation. Instead, the dominant consideration was the distribution of political power among the States.

\n

The original Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s allocation of House seats involved what the Reynolds Court rather delicately termed \xe2\x80\x9ccompromise and concession.\xe2\x80\x9d 377 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 574. Seats were apportioned among the States \xe2\x80\x9caccording to their respective Numbers,\xe2\x80\x9d and these \xe2\x80\x9cNumbers\xe2\x80\x9d were \xe2\x80\x9cdetermined by adding to the whole Number of free Persons . . . three fifths of all other Persons.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. I, \xc2\xa72, cl. 3. The phrase \xe2\x80\x9call other Persons\xe2\x80\x9d was a euphemism for slaves. Delegates to the Constitutional Convention from the slave States insisted on this infamous clause as a condition of their support for the Constitution, and the clause gave the slave States more power in the House and in the electoral college than they would have enjoyed if only free persons had been counted.[5] These slave-state delegates did not demand slave representation based on some philosophical notion that \xe2\x80\x9crepresentatives serve all residents, not just those eligible or registered to vote.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18.[6]

\n

B

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s account of the original Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s allocation also plucks out of context Alexander Hamilton\xe2\x80\x99s statement on apportionment. The Court characterizes Hamilton\xe2\x80\x99s words (more precisely, Robert Yates\xe2\x80\x99s summary of his fellow New Yorker\xe2\x80\x99s words) as endorsing apportionment by total population, and positions those words as if Hamilton were talking about apportionment in the House. Ante, at 9. Neither is entirely accurate. The \xe2\x80\x9cquote\xe2\x80\x9d comes from the controversy over Senate apportionment, where the debate turned on whether to apportion by population at all. See generally 1 Records of the Federal Convention of 1787, pp. 470\xe2\x80\x93474 (M. Farrand ed. 1911). Hamilton argued in favor of allocating Senate seats by population:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe question, after all is, is it our interest in modifying this general government to sacrifice individual rights to the preservation of the rights of an artificial being, called states? There can be no truer principle than this\xe2\x80\x94that every individual of the community at large has an equal right to the protection of government. If therefore three states contain a majority of the inhabitants of America, ought they to be governed by a minority? Would the inhabitants of the great states ever submit to this? If the smaller states maintain this principle, through a love of power, will not the larger, from the same motives, be equally tenacious to preserve their power?\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 473.

\n

As is clear from the passage just quoted, Hamilton (according to Yates) thought the fight over apportionment was about naked power, not some lofty ideal about the nature of representation. That interpretation is confirmed by James Madison\xe2\x80\x99s summary of the same statement by Hamilton: \xe2\x80\x9cThe truth is it [meaning the debate over apportionment] is a contest for power, not for liberty. .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. The State of Delaware having 40,000 souls will lose power, if she has 1/10 only of the votes allowed to Pa. having 400,000.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 466. Far from \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ndorsing apportionment based on total population,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9, Hamilton was merely acknowledging the obvious: that apportionment in the new National Government would be the outcome of a contest over raw political power, not abstract political theory.

\n

C

\n

After the Civil War, when the\n \nFourteenth Amendment was being drafted, the question of the apportionment formula arose again. Thaddeus Stevens, a leader of the so-called radical Republicans, unsuccessfully proposed that apportionment be based on eligible voters, rather than total population. The opinion of the Court suggests that the rejection of Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 proposal signified the adoption of the theory that representatives are properly understood to represent all of the residents of their districts, whether or not they are eligible to vote. Ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9312. As was the case in 1787, however, it was power politics, not democratic theory, that carried the day.

\n

In making his proposal, Stevens candidly explained that the proposal\xe2\x80\x99s primary aim was to perpetuate the dominance of the Republican Party and the Northern States. Cong. Globe, 39th Cong., 1st Sess., 74 (1865); Van Alstyne, The\n \nFourteenth Amendment, The \xe2\x80\x9cRight\xe2\x80\x9d to Vote, and the Understanding of the Thirty-Ninth Congress, 1965 S. Ct. Rev. 33, 45\xe2\x80\x9347 (Van Alstyne). As Stevens spelled out, if House seats were based on total population, the power of the former slave States would be magnified. Prior to the Civil War, a slave had counted for only three-fifths of a person for purposes of the apportionment of House seats. As a result of the Emancipation Proclamation and the\n \nThirteenth Amendment, the former slaves would now be fully counted even if they were not permitted to vote. By Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 calculation, this would give the South 13 additional votes in both the House and the electoral college. Cong. Globe, 39th Cong., 1st Sess., 74 (1865); VanAlstyne 46.

\n

Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 proposal met with opposition in the Joint Committee on Reconstruction, including from, as the majority notes, James Blaine. Ante, at 11. Yet, as it does with Hamilton\xe2\x80\x99s, the majority plucks Blaine\xe2\x80\x99s words out of context:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e have had several propositions to amend the Federal Constitution with respect to the basis of representation in Congress. These propositions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. give to the States in future a representation proportioned to their voters instead of their inhabitants.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe effect contemplated and intended by this change is perfectly well understood, and on all hands frankly avowed. It is to deprive the lately rebellious States of the unfair advantage of a large representation in this House, based on their colored population, so long as that population shall be denied political rights by the legislation of those States. .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe direct object thus aimed at, as it respects the rebellious States, has been so generally approved that little thought seems to have been given to the incidental evils which the proposed constitutional amendment would inflict on a large portion of theloyal States\xe2\x80\x94evils, in my judgment, so serious and alarming as to lead me to oppose the amendment in any form in which it has yet been presented. As an abstract proposition no one will deny that population is the true basis of representation; for women, children, and other non-voting classes may have as vital an interest in the legislation of the country as those who actually deposit the ballot. .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf voters instead of population shall be made the basis of representation certain results will follow, not fully appreciated perhaps by some who are now urgent for the change.\xe2\x80\x9d Cong. Globe, 39th Cong., 1st Sess., 141 (1865).

\n

The \xe2\x80\x9cnot fully appreciated\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cincidental evi[l]\xe2\x80\x9d was, in Blaine\xe2\x80\x99s view, the disruption to loyal States\xe2\x80\x99 representation in Congress. Blaine described how the varying suffrage requirements in loyal States could lead to, for instance, California\xe2\x80\x99s being entitled to eight seats in the House and Vermont\xe2\x80\x99s being entitled only to three, despite their having similar populations. Ibid.; see also 2 B. Ackerman, We the People: Transformations 164, 455, n.\xc2\xa05 (1998); Van Alstyne 47, 70. This mattered to Blaine because both States were loyal and so neither deserved to suffer a loss of relative political power. Blaine therefore proposed to apportion representatives by the \xe2\x80\x9cwhole number of persons except those to whom civil or political rights or privileges are denied or abridged by the constitution or laws of any State on account of race or color.\xe2\x80\x9d Cong. Globe, 39th Cong., 1st Sess., 142.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThis is a very simple and very direct way, it seems to me, of reaching the result aimed at without embarrassment to any other question or interest. It leaves population as heretofore the basis of representation, does not disturb in any manner the harmonious relations of the loyal States, and it conclusively deprives the southern States of all representation in Congress on account of the colored population so long as those States may choose to abridge or deny to that population the political rights and privileges accorded to others.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

As should be obvious from these lengthy passages, Blaine recognized that the \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally approved\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cresult aimed at\xe2\x80\x9d was to deprive southern States of political power; far from quibbling with that aim, he sought to achieve it while limiting the collateral damage to the loyal northern States. See Van Alstyne 47.

\n

Roscoe Conkling, whom the majority also quotes, ante, at 11, seemed to be as concerned with voter-based apportionment\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cnarrow[ing] the basis of taxation, and in some States seriously,\xe2\x80\x9d as he was with abstract notions of representational equality. Cong. Globe, 39th Cong., 1st Sess., 358; id., at 359 (\xe2\x80\x9crepresentation should go with taxation\xe2\x80\x9d); ibid. (apportionment by citizenship \xe2\x80\x9cwould narrow the basis of taxation and cause considerable inequalities in this respect, because the number of aliens in some States is very large, and growing larger now, when emigrants reach our shores at the rate of more than a State a year\xe2\x80\x9d). And Hamilton Ward, also quoted by the majority, ante, at 11, was primarily disturbed by \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he fact that one South Carolinian, whose hands are red with the blood of fallen patriots, and whose skirts are reeking with the odors of Columbia and Andersonville, will have a voice as potential in these Halls as two and a half Vermont soldiers who have come back from the grandest battle-fields in history maimed and scarred in the contest with South Carolina traitors in their efforts to destroy this Government\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94and only secondarily worried about the prospect of \xe2\x80\x9ctaxation without representation.\xe2\x80\x9d Cong. Globe, 39th Cong., 1st Sess., 434.

\n

Even Jacob Howard, he of the \xe2\x80\x9ctheory of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d language, ante, at 12, bemoaned the fact that basing representation on total population would allow southern States \xe2\x80\x9cto obtain an advantage which they did not possess before the rebellion and emancipation.\xe2\x80\x9d Cong. Globe, 39th Cong., 1st Sess., 2766. \xe2\x80\x9cI object to this. I think they cannot very consistently call upon us to grant them an additional number of Representatives simply because in consequence of their own misconduct they have lost the property [meaning slaves, whom slaveholders considered to be property] which they once possessed, and which served as a basis in great part of their representation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The list could go on. The bottom line is that in the leadup to the\n \nFourteenth Amendment, claims about representational equality were invoked, if at all, only in service of the real goal: preventing southern States from acquiring too much power in the National Government.

\n

After much debate, Congress eventually settled on the compromise that now appears in \xc2\xa72 of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment. Under that provision, House seats are apportioned based on total population, but if a State wrongfully denies the right to vote to a certain percentage of its population, its representation is supposed to be reduced proportionally.[7] Enforcement of this remedy, however, is dependent on action by Congress, and\xe2\x80\x94regrettably\xe2\x80\x94the remedy was never used during the long period when voting rights were widely abridged. Amar 399.

\n

In light of the history of Article I, \xc2\xa72, of the original Constitution and \xc2\xa72 of the\n \nFourteenth Amendment, it is clear that the apportionment of seats in the House of Representatives was based in substantial part on the distribution of political power among the States and not merely on some theory regarding the proper nature of representation. It is impossible to draw any clear constitutional command from this complex history.

\n

\n

For these reasons, I would hold only that Texas permissibly used total population in drawing the challenged legislative districts. I therefore concur in the judgment of the Court.
