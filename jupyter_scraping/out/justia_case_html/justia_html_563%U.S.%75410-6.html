SYLLABUS
OCTOBER TERM, 2010
GLOBAL-TECH APPLIANCES, INC. V. SEB S.&NBSP;A.


SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

GLOBAL-TECH APPLIANCES, INC., et\xc2\xa0al. v. SEB S.\xc2\xa0A.

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

No. 10\xe2\x80\x936.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 23, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 31, 2011

After respondent SEB invented an innovative deep fryer, obtained a U. S. patent for its design, and began selling its fryer in this country, Sunbeam Products, Inc., asked petitioner Pentalpha Enterprises, Ltd., a Hong Kong home appliance maker and wholly owned subsidiary of petitioner Global-Tech Appliances, Inc., to supply Sunbeam with deep fryers meeting certain specifications. Pentalpha purchased an SEB fryer that was made for sale in a foreign market and thus lacked U. S. patent markings, copied all but the fryer\xe2\x80\x99s cosmetic features, and retained an attorney to conduct a right-to-use study without telling him it had copied directly from SEB\xe2\x80\x99s design. Failing to locate SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent, the attorney issued an opinion letter stating that Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s deep fryer did not infringe any of the patents that he had found. Pentalpha then started selling its fryers to Sunbeam, which resold them in this country under its own trademarks at a price that undercut SEB\xe2\x80\x99s.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0SEB then sued Sunbeam for patent infringement. Though Sunbeam notified Pentalpha of the lawsuit, Pentalpha went on to sell its fryers to other companies, which resold them in the U. S. market under their respective trademarks. After settling the Sunbeam lawsuit, SEB sued Pentalpha, asserting, as relevant here, that it had contravened 35 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(b) by actively inducing Sunbeam and the other purchasers of Pentalpha fryers to sell or offer to sell them in violation of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent rights. The jury found for SEB on the induced infringement theory, and the District Court entered judgment for SEB. Affirming, the Federal Circuit stated that induced infringement under \xc2\xa7271(b) requires a showing that the alleged infringer knew or should have known that his actions would induce actual infringements; declared that this showing includes proof that the alleged infringer knew of the patent; held that, although there was no direct evidence that Pentalpha knew of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent before it received notice of the Sunbeam suit, there was adequate proof that it deliberately disregarded a known risk that SEB had a protective patent; and said that such disregard is not different from, but a form of, actual knowledge.

Held:

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Induced infringement under \xc2\xa7271(b) requires knowledge that the induced acts constitute patent infringement. Pp. 3\xe2\x80\x9310.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Section 271(b)\xe2\x80\x99s text\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c[w]hoever actively induces infringement of a patent shall be liable as an infringer\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94is ambiguous as to the intent needed to impose liability. In referring to a party that \xe2\x80\x9cinduces infringement,\xe2\x80\x9d the provision may require merely that the inducer must lead another to engage in conduct that happens to amount to infringement. On the other hand, the reference to a party that \xe2\x80\x9cinduces infringement\xe2\x80\x9d may also be read to mean that the inducer must persuade another to engage in conduct that the inducer knows is infringement. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x935.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Like \xc2\xa7271(b)\xe2\x80\x99s language, the pre-1952 case law is susceptible to conflicting interpretations. However, Aro Mfg. Co. v. Convertible Top Replacement Co., 377 U. S. 476 (Aro II), resolves the question at issue. Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x938.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Induced infringement was not considered a separate theory of indirect liability in the pre-1952 case law, but was treated as evidence of \xe2\x80\x9ccontributory infringement,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., the aiding and abetting of direct infringement by another party. When Congress enacted \xc2\xa7271, it separated the contributory infringement concept into two categories: induced infringement, covered by \xc2\xa7271(b), and sale of a component of a patented invention, covered by \xc2\xa7271(c). In the badly fractured Aro II decision, a majority concluded that a violator of \xc2\xa7271(c) must know \xe2\x80\x9cthat the combination for which his component was especially designed was both patented and infringing.\xe2\x80\x9d 377 U. S., at 488. That conclusion, now a fixture in the law, compels this same knowledge for liability under \xc2\xa7271(b), given that the two provisions have a common origin and create the same difficult interpretive choice. Pp. 8\xe2\x80\x9310.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Deliberate indifference to a known risk that a patent exists does not satisfy the knowledge required by \xc2\xa7271(b). Nevertheless, the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment must be affirmed because the evidence in this case was plainly sufficient to support a finding of Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s knowledge under the doctrine of willful blindness. Pp. 10\xe2\x80\x9316.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The doctrine of willful blindness is well established in criminal law. Many criminal statutes require proof that a defendant acted knowingly or willfully, and courts applying the doctrine have held that defendants cannot escape the reach of these statutes by deliberately shielding themselves from clear evidence of critical facts that are strongly suggested by the circumstances. The traditional rationale for the doctrine is that defendants who behave in this manner are just as culpable as those who have actual knowledge. This Court endorsed a concept similar to willful blindness over a century ago in Spurr v. United States, 174 U. S. 728, 735, and every Federal Court of Appeals but one has fully embraced willful blindness. Given the doctrine\'s long history and wide acceptance in the Federal Judiciary, there is no reason why the doctrine should not apply in civil lawsuits for induced patent infringement under \xc2\xa7271(b). Pp. 10\xe2\x80\x9313.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Although the Courts of Appeals articulate the doctrine of willful blindness in slightly different ways, all agree on two basic requirements. First, the defendant must subjectively believe that there is a high probability that a fact exists. Second, the defendant must take deliberate actions to avoid learning of that fact. These requirements give willful blindness an appropriately limited scope that surpasses recklessness and negligence. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Although the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s test departs from the proper willful blindness standard in important respects, the evidence when viewed in the light most favorable to the verdict for SEB was sufficient under the correct standard. Pentalpha believed that SEB\xe2\x80\x99s fryer embodied advanced technology that would be valuable in the U. S. market as evidenced by its decision to copy all but the fryer\xe2\x80\x99s cosmetic features. Also revealing is Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s decision to copy an overseas model of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s fryer, aware that it would not bear U. S. patent markings. Even more telling is Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to inform its attorney that the product to be evaluated was simply a knockoff of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s fryer. Taken together, the evidence was more than sufficient for a jury to find that Pentalpha subjectively believed there was a high probability that SEB\xe2\x80\x99s fryer was patented and took deliberate steps to avoid knowing that fact, and that it therefore willfully blinded itself to the infringing nature of Sunbeam\xe2\x80\x99s sales. Pp. 14\xe2\x80\x9316.

594 F.\xc2\xa03d 1360, affirmed.

GLOBAL-TECH APPLIANCES, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.,\nPETITIONERS v. SEB S.\xc2\xa0A.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We consider whether a party who \xe2\x80\x9cactively induces in-fringement of a patent\xe2\x80\x9d under 35 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(b) must know that the induced acts constitute patent infringement.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns a patent for an innovative deep fryer designed by respondent SEB S.\xc2\xa0A., a French maker of home appliances. In the late 1980\xe2\x80\x99s, SEB invented a \xe2\x80\x9ccool-touch\xe2\x80\x9d deep fryer, that is, a deep fryer for home use with external surfaces that remain cool during the frying process. The cool-touch deep fryer consisted of a metal frying pot surrounded by a plastic outer housing. Attached to the housing was a ring that suspended the metal pot and insulated the housing from heat by separating it from the pot, creating air space between the two components. SEB obtained a U. S. patent for its design in 1991, and sometime later, SEB started manufacturing the cool-touch fryer and selling it in this country under its well-known \xe2\x80\x9cT-Fal\xe2\x80\x9d brand. Superior to other products in the American market at the time, SEB\xe2\x80\x99s fryer was a commercial success.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1997, Sunbeam Products, Inc., a U. S. competitor of SEB, asked petitioner Pentalpha Enterprises, Ltd., to supply it with deep fryers meeting certain specifications. Pentalpha is a Hong Kong maker of home appliances and a wholly owned subsidiary of petitioner Global-Tech Appliances, Inc.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In order to develop a deep fryer for Sunbeam, Pentalpha purchased an SEB fryer in Hong Kong and copied all but its cosmetic features. Because the SEB fryer bought in Hong Kong was made for sale in a foreign market, it bore no U. S. patent markings. After copying SEB\xe2\x80\x99s design, Pentalpha retained an attorney to conduct a right-to-use study, but Pentalpha refrained from telling the attorney that its design was copied directly from SEB\xe2\x80\x99s.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The attorney failed to locate SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent, and in August 1997 he issued an opinion letter stating that Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s deep fryer did not infringe any of the patents that he had found. That same month, Pentalpha started selling its deep fryers to Sunbeam, which resold them in the United States under its trademarks. By obtaining its product from a manufacturer with lower production costs, Sunbeam was able to undercut SEB in the U. S. market.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After SEB\xe2\x80\x99s customers started defecting to Sunbeam, SEB sued Sunbeam in March 1998, alleging that Sunbeam\xe2\x80\x99s sales infringed SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent. Sunbeam notified Pentalpha of the lawsuit the following month. Undeterred, Pentalpha went on to sell deep fryers to Fingerhut Corp. and Montgomery Ward & Co., both of which resold them in the United States under their respective trademarks.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0SEB settled the lawsuit with Sunbeam, and then sued Pentalpha, asserting two theories of recovery: First, SEB claimed that Pentalpha had directly infringed SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent in violation of 35 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(a), by selling or offering to sell its deep fryers; and second, SEB claimed that Pentalpha had contravened \xc2\xa7271(b) by actively inducing Sunbeam, Fingerhut, and Montgomery Ward to sell or to offer to sell Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s deep fryers in violation of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent rights.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Following a 5-day trial, the jury found for SEB on both theories and also found that Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s infringement had been willful. Pentalpha filed post-trial motions seeking \na new trial or judgment as a matter of law on several grounds. As relevant here, Pentalpha argued that there was insufficient evidence to support the jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding of induced infringement under \xc2\xa7271(b) because Pentalpha did not actually know of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent until it received the notice of the Sunbeam lawsuit in April 1998.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court rejected Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s argument, as did the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, which affirmed the judgment, SEB S.\xc2\xa0A. v. Montgomery Ward & Co., 594 F.\xc2\xa03d 1360 (2010). Summarizing a recent en banc decision, the Federal Circuit stated that induced infringement under \xc2\xa7271(b) requires a \xe2\x80\x9cplaintiff [to] show that the alleged infringer knew or should have known \nthat his actions would induce actual infringements\xe2\x80\x9d and that this showing includes proof that the alleged infringer knew of the patent. Id., at 1376. Although the record contained no direct evidence that Pentalpha knew of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s patent before April 1998, the court found adequate evidence to support a finding that \xe2\x80\x9cPentalpha deliberately disregarded a known risk that SEB had a protective patent.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1377. Such disregard, the court said, \xe2\x80\x9cis not different from actual knowledge, but is a form of actual knowledge.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari. 562 U. S. ___ (2010).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pentalpha argues that active inducement liability under \xc2\xa7271(b) requires more than deliberate indifference to a known risk that the induced acts may violate an existing patent. Instead, Pentalpha maintains, actual knowledge of the patent is needed.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In assessing Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s argument, we begin with the text of \xc2\xa7271(b)\xe2\x80\x94which is short, simple, and, with respect to the question presented in this case, inconclusive. Section 271(b) states: \xe2\x80\x9cWhoever actively induces infringement of a patent shall be liable as an infringer.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the text of \xc2\xa7271(b) makes no mention of intent, we infer that at least some intent is required. The term \xe2\x80\x9cinduce\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o lead on; to influence; to prevail on; to move by persuasion or influence.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 1269 (2d ed. 1945). The addition of the adverb \xe2\x80\x9cactively\xe2\x80\x9d suggests that the inducement must involve the taking of affirmative steps to bring about the desired result, see id., at 27.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When a person actively induces another to take some action, the inducer obviously knows the action that he or she wishes to bring about. If a used car salesman induces a customer to buy a car, the salesman knows that the desired result is the purchase of the car. But what if it \nis said that the salesman induced the customer to buy a damaged car? Does this mean merely that the salesman induced the customer to purchase a car that happened to be damaged, a fact of which the salesman may have been unaware? Or does this mean that the salesman knew that the car was damaged? The statement that the salesman induced the customer to buy a damaged car is ambiguous.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0So is \xc2\xa7271(b). In referring to a party that \xe2\x80\x9cinduces infringement,\xe2\x80\x9d this provision may require merely that the inducer lead another to engage in conduct that happens to amount to infringement, i.e., the making, using, offering to sell, selling, or importing of a patented invention. See \xc2\xa7271(a).[Footnote 2] On the other hand, the reference to a party \nthat \xe2\x80\x9cinduces infringement\xe2\x80\x9d may also be read to mean that the inducer must persuade another to engage in conduct that the inducer knows is infringement. Both readings are possible.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finding no definitive answer in the statutory text, we turn to the case law that predates the enactment of \xc2\xa7271 as part the Patent Act of 1952. As we recognized in Aro Mfg. Co. v. Convertible Top Replacement Co., 377 U. S. 476 (1964) (Aro II), \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he section was designed to \xe2\x80\x98codify in statutory form principles of contributory infringement\xe2\x80\x99 which had been \xe2\x80\x98part of our law for about 80 years.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 485\xe2\x80\x93486, n.\xc2\xa06 (quoting H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 1923, 82d Cong., 2d Sess., 9 (1952)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unfortunately, the relevant pre-1952 cases are less clear than one might hope with respect to the question presented here. Before 1952, both the conduct now covered by \xc2\xa7271(b) (induced infringement) and the conduct now addressed by \xc2\xa7271(c) (sale of a component of a patented invention) were viewed as falling within the overarching concept of \xe2\x80\x9ccontributory infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Cases in the latter category\xe2\x80\x94i.e., cases in which a party sold an item that was not itself covered by the claims of a patent but that enabled another party to make or use a patented machine, process, or combination\xe2\x80\x94were more common.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The pre-1952 case law provides conflicting signals regarding the intent needed in such cases. In an oft-cited decision, then-Judge Taft suggested that it was sufficient if the seller of the component part intended that the part be used in an invention that happened to infringe a patent. He wrote that it was \xe2\x80\x9cwell settled that where one makes and sells one element of a combination covered by a patent with the intention and for the purpose of bringing about its use in such a combination he is guilty of contributory infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Thomson-Houston Elec. Co. v. Ohio Brass Co., 80 F. 712, 721 (CA6 1897).[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the other hand, this Court, in Henry v. A.\xc2\xa0B. Dick Co., 224 U. S. 1 (1912), overruled on other grounds, Motion Picture Patents Co. v. Universal Film Mfg. Co., 243 U. S. 502 (1917), stated that \xe2\x80\x9cif the defendants [who were accused of contributory infringement] knew of the patent and that [the direct infringer] had unlawfully made the patented article \xe2\x80\xa6 with the intent and purpose that [the direct infringer] should use the infringing article \xe2\x80\xa6 they would assist in her infringing use.\xe2\x80\x9d 224 U. S., at 33 (emphasis added and deleted).[Footnote 4] Our decision in Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer Studios Inc. v. Grokster, Ltd., 545 U. S. 913 (2005), which looked to the law of contributory patent infringement for guidance in determining the standard to be applied in a case claiming contributory copyright infringement, contains dicta that may be read as interpreting the pre-1952 cases this way. In Grokster, we said that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he inducement rule \xe2\x80\xa6 premises liability on purposeful, culpable expression and conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While both the language of \xc2\xa7271(b) and the pre-1952 case law that this provision was meant to codify are susceptible to conflicting interpretations, our decision in Aro II resolves the question in this case. In Aro II, a majority held that a violator of \xc2\xa7271(c) must know \xe2\x80\x9cthat the combination for which his component was especially designed was both patented and infringing,\xe2\x80\x9d 377 U. S., at 488, and as we explain below, that conclusion compels this same knowledge for liability under \xc2\xa7271(b).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As noted above, induced infringement was not considered a separate theory of indirect liability in the pre-1952 case law. Rather, it was treated as evidence of \xe2\x80\x9ccontributory infringement,\xe2\x80\x9d that is, the aiding and abetting of direct infringement by another party. See Lemley, In-ducing Patent Infringement, 39 U.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0D.\xc2\xa0L. Rev. 225, 227 (2005). When Congress enacted \xc2\xa7271, it separated what had previously been regarded as contributory infringement into two categories, one covered by \xc2\xa7271(b) and the other covered by \xc2\xa7271(c).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Aro II concerned \xc2\xa7271(c), which states in relevant part:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cWhoever offers to sell or sells \xe2\x80\xa6 a component of a patented [invention] \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, constituting a material part of the invention, knowing the same to be especially made or especially adapted for use in an infringement of such patent, and not a staple article or commodity of commerce suitable for substantial noninfringing use, shall be liable as a contributory infringer.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.)

\n

\nThis language contains exactly the same ambiguity as \xc2\xa7271(b). The phrase \xe2\x80\x9cknowing [a component] to be especially made or especially adapted for use in an infringement\xe2\x80\x9d may be read to mean that a violator must know that the component is \xe2\x80\x9cespecially adapted for use\xe2\x80\x9d in a product that happens to infringe a patent. Or the phrase may be read to require, in addition, knowledge of the patent\xe2\x80\x99s existence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This question closely divided the Aro II Court. In a badly fractured decision, a majority concluded that knowledge of the patent was needed. 377 U. S., at 488, and n. 8; id., at 514 (White, J., concurring); id., at 524\xe2\x80\x93527 (Black, J., dissenting).[Footnote 5] Justice Black\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, which explained the basis for the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view, concluded that the language of \xc2\xa7271(c) supported this interpretation. See id., at 525. His opinion also relied on an amendment to this language that was adopted when the bill was in committee. Id., at 525\xe2\x80\x93527.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Four Justices disagreed with this interpretation and would have held that a violator of \xc2\xa7271(c) need know only that the component is specially adapted for use in a product that happens to infringe a patent. See id., at 488\xe2\x80\x93490, n.\xc2\xa08. These Justices thought that this reading was supported by the language of \xc2\xa7271(c) and the pre-1952 case law, and they disagreed with the inference drawn by the majority from the amendment of \xc2\xa7271(c)\xe2\x80\x99s language. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While there is much to be said in favor of both views expressed in Aro II, the \xe2\x80\x9cholding in Aro II has become \na fixture in the law of contributory infringement under [section] 271(c),\xe2\x80\x9d 5 R. Moy, Walker on Patents \xc2\xa715:20, p.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x93131 (4th ed. 2009)\xe2\x80\x94so much so that SEB has not asked us to overrule it, see Brief for Respondent 19, n.\xc2\xa03. Nor has Congress seen fit to alter \xc2\xa7271(c)\xe2\x80\x99s intent requirement in the nearly half a century since Aro II was decided. In light of the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98special force\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of the doctrine of stare decisis with regard to questions of statutory interpretation, see John R. Sand & Gravel Co. v. United States, 552 U. S. 130, 139 (2008), we proceed on the premise that \xc2\xa7271(c) requires knowledge of the existence of the patent that is infringed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Based on this premise, it follows that the same knowledge is needed for induced infringement under \xc2\xa7271(b). As noted, the two provisions have a common origin in the pre-1952 understanding of contributory infringement, and the language of the two provisions creates the same difficult interpretive choice. It would thus be strange to hold that knowledge of the relevant patent is needed under \xc2\xa7271(c) but not under \xc2\xa7271(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, we now hold that induced infringement under \xc2\xa7271(b) requires knowledge that the induced acts constitute patent infringement.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Returning to Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s principal challenge, we agree that deliberate indifference to a known risk that a patent exists is not the appropriate standard under \xc2\xa7271(b). We nevertheless affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals because the evidence in this case was plainly sufficient to support a finding of Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s knowledge under the doctrine of willful blindness.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The doctrine of willful blindness is well established in criminal law. Many criminal statutes require proof that a defendant acted knowingly or willfully, and courts applying the doctrine of willful blindness hold that defendants cannot escape the reach of these statutes by deliberately shielding themselves from clear evidence of critical facts that are strongly suggested by the circumstances. The traditional rationale for this doctrine is that defendants who behave in this manner are just as culpable as those who have actual knowledge. Edwards, The Criminal Degrees of Knowledge, 17 Mod. L. Rev. 294, 302 (1954) (hereinafter Edwards) (observing on the basis of English authorities that \xe2\x80\x9cup to the present day, no real doubt has been cast on the proposition that [willful blindness] is as culpable as actual knowledge\xe2\x80\x9d). It is also said that persons who know enough to blind themselves to direct proof of critical facts in effect have actual knowledge of those facts. See United States v. Jewell, 532 F.\xc2\xa02d 697, 700 (CA9 1976) (en banc).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion more than a century ago in Spurr v. United States, 174 U. S. 728 (1899),[Footnote 6] while not using the term \xe2\x80\x9cwillful blindness,\xe2\x80\x9d endorsed a similar concept. The case involved a criminal statute that prohibited a bank officer from \xe2\x80\x9cwillfully\xe2\x80\x9d certifying a check drawn against insufficient funds. We said that a willful violation would occur \xe2\x80\x9cif the [bank] officer purposely keeps himself in ignorance of whether the drawer has money in the bank.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 735. Following our decision in Spurr, several federal prosecutions in the first half of the 20th century invoked the doctrine of willful blindness.[Footnote 7] Later, a 1962 proposed draft of the Model Penal Code, which has since become official, attempted to incorporate the doctrine by defining \xe2\x80\x9cknowledge of the existence of a particular fact\xe2\x80\x9d \nto include a situation in which \xe2\x80\x9ca person is aware of a \nhigh probability of [the fact\xe2\x80\x99s] existence, unless he actually believes that it does not exist.\xe2\x80\x9d ALI, Model Penal Code \xc2\xa72.02(7) (Proposed Official Draft 1962). Our Court has used the Code\xe2\x80\x99s definition as a guide in analyzing whether certain statutory presumptions of knowledge comported with due process. See Turner v. United States, 396 U. S. 398, 416\xe2\x80\x93417 (1970); Leary v. United States, 395 U. S. 6, 46\xe2\x80\x9347, and n.\xc2\xa093 (1969). And every Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x94with the possible exception of the District of Columbia Circuit, see n.\xc2\xa09, infra\xe2\x80\x94has fully embraced willful blindness, applying the doctrine to a wide range of criminal statutes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given the long history of willful blindness and its wide acceptance in the Federal Judiciary, we can see no reason why the doctrine should not apply in civil lawsuits for induced patent infringement under 35 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(b).[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pentalpha urges us not to take this step, arguing that \xc2\xa7271(b) demands more than willful blindness with respect to the induced acts that constitute infringement. See Reply Brief for Petitioners 13\xe2\x80\x9314. This question, however, is not at issue here. There is no need to invoke the doctrine of willful blindness to establish that Pentalpha knew that the retailers who purchased its fryer were selling that product in the American market; Pentalpha was indisputably aware that its customers were selling its product in this country.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pentalpha further contends that this Court in Grokster did not accept the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that Grokster and StreamCast could be held liable for inducing the infringement of copyrights under a theory of willful blindness. Reply Brief for Petitioners 14 (citing Brief for United States, O.\xc2\xa0T. 2004, No. 04\xe2\x80\x93480, pp.\xc2\xa029\xe2\x80\x9330). But the Court had no need to consider the doctrine of willful blindness in that case because the Court found ample evidence that Grokster and StreamCast were fully aware\xe2\x80\x94in the ordinary sense of the term\xe2\x80\x94that their file-sharing software was routinely used in carrying out the acts that constituted infringement (the unauthorized sharing of copyrighted works) and that these acts violated the rights of copyright holders. See 545 U. S., at 922\xe2\x80\x93927, 937\xe2\x80\x93940.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While the Courts of Appeals articulate the doctrine of willful blindness in slightly different ways, all appear to agree on two basic requirements: (1) the defendant must subjectively believe that there is a high probability that a fact exists and (2) the defendant must take deliberate actions to avoid learning of that fact.[Footnote 9] We think these requirements give willful blindness an appropriately limited scope that surpasses recklessness and negligence. Under this formulation, a willfully blind defendant is one who takes deliberate actions to avoid confirming a high probability of wrongdoing and who can almost be said to have actually known the critical facts. See G. Williams, Criminal Law \xc2\xa757, p.\xc2\xa0159 (2d ed. 1961) (\xe2\x80\x9cA court can properly find wilful blindness only where it can almost be said that the defendant actually knew\xe2\x80\x9d). By contrast, a reckless defendant is one who merely knows of a substantial and unjustified risk of such wrongdoing, see ALI, Model Penal Code \xc2\xa72.02(2)(c) (1985), and a negligent defendant is one who should have known of a similar risk but, in fact, did not, see \xc2\xa72.02(2)(d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The test applied by the Federal Circuit in this case departs from the proper willful blindness standard in two important respects. First, it permits a finding of knowledge when there is merely a \xe2\x80\x9cknown risk\xe2\x80\x9d that the induced acts are infringing. Second, in demanding only \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x9d to that risk, the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s test does not require active efforts by an inducer to avoid knowing about the infringing nature of the activities.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In spite of these flaws, we believe that the evidence when viewed in the light most favorable to the verdict for SEB is sufficient under the correct standard. The jury could have easily found that before April 1998 Pentalpha willfully blinded itself to the infringing nature of the sales it encouraged Sunbeam to make.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0SEB\xe2\x80\x99s cool-touch fryer was an innovation in the U. S. market when Pentalpha copied it. App. to Brief for Respondent 49. As one would expect with any superior product, sales of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s fryer had been growing for some time. Ibid. Pentalpha knew all of this, for its CEO and president, John Sham, testified that, in developing a product for Sunbeam, Pentalpha performed \xe2\x80\x9cmarket research\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cgather[ed] information as much as possible.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 23a. Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s belief that SEB\xe2\x80\x99s fryer embodied advanced technology that would be valuable in the U. S. market is evidenced by its decision to copy all but the cosmetic features of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s fryer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Also revealing is Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s decision to copy an overseas model of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s fryer. Pentalpha knew that the product it was designing was for the U. S. market, and Sham\xe2\x80\x94himself a named inventor on numerous U. S. patents, see id., at 78a\xe2\x80\x9386a\xe2\x80\x94was well aware that products made for overseas markets usually do not bear U. S. patent markings, App. in No. 2009\xe2\x80\x931099 etc. (CA Fed.), pp. A\xe2\x80\x931904 to A\xe2\x80\x931906. Even more telling is Sham\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to inform the attorney from whom Pentalpha sought a right-to-use opinion that the product to be evaluated was simply a knockoff of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s deep fryer. On the facts of this case, we cannot fathom what motive Sham could have had for withholding this information other than to manufacture a claim of plausible deniability in the event that his company was later accused of patent infringement. Nor does Sham\xe2\x80\x99s testimony on this subject provide any reason to doubt that inference. Asked whether the attorney would have fared better had he known of SEB\xe2\x80\x99s design, Sham was nonresponsive. All he could say was that a patent search is not an \xe2\x80\x9ceasy job\xe2\x80\x9d and that is why he hired attorneys to perform them. App. 112a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Taken together, this evidence was more than sufficient for a jury to find that Pentalpha subjectively believed there was a high probability that SEB\xe2\x80\x99s fryer was patented, that Pentalpha took deliberate steps to avoid knowing that fact, and that it therefore willfully blinded itself to the infringing nature of Sunbeam\xe2\x80\x99s sales.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is correct, in my view, to conclude that 35 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(b) must be read in tandem with \xc2\xa7271(c), and therefore that to induce infringement a defendant must know \xe2\x80\x9cthe induced acts constitute patent infringement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Yet the Court does more. Having interpreted the statute to require a showing of knowledge, the Court holds that willful blindness will suffice. This is a mistaken step. Willful blindness is not knowledge; and judges should not broaden a legislative proscription by analogy. See United States v. Jewell, 532 F.\xc2\xa02d 697, 706 (CA9 1976) (en banc) (Kennedy, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a statute specifically requires knowledge as an element of a crime, however, the substitution of some other state of mind cannot be justified even if the court deems that both are equally blameworthy\xe2\x80\x9d) In my respectful submission, the Court is incorrect in the definition it now adopts; but even on its own terms the Court should remand to the Court of Appeals to consider in the first instance whether there is sufficient evidence of knowledge to support the jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding of\ninducement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court invokes willful blindness to bring those who lack knowledge within \xc2\xa7271(b)\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition. Husak & Callender, Wilful Ignorance, Knowledge, and the \xe2\x80\x9cEqual Culpability\xe2\x80\x9d Thesis: A Study of the Deeper Significance of the Principle of Legality, 1994 Wis. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 29, 35; see also L. Alexander & K. Ferzan, Crime and Culpability: A Theory of Criminal Law 34\xe2\x80\x9335 (2009) (cautioning against\nthe temptation to \xe2\x80\x9cdistort\xe2\x80\x9d cases of willful blindness \xe2\x80\x9cinto cases of knowledge\xe2\x80\x9d); G. Williams, Criminal Law: The General Part \xc2\xa757, p. 157 (2d ed. 1961). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s definition of willful blindness reveals this basic purpose. One can believe that there is a \xe2\x80\x9chigh probability\xe2\x80\x9d that acts might infringe a patent but nonetheless conclude they do not infringe. Ante, at 14; see also ibid. (describing a willfully blind defendant as one \xe2\x80\x9cwho can almost be said\nto have actually known the critical facts\xe2\x80\x9d). The alleged inducer who believes a device is noninfringing cannot be said to know otherwise.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court justifies its substitution of willful blindness for the statutory knowledge requirement in two ways, neither of which is convincing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the Court appeals to moral theory by citing the \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional rationale\xe2\x80\x9d that willfully blind defendants \xe2\x80\x9care just as culpable as those who have actual knowledge.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. But the moral question is a difficult one. Is it true that the lawyer who knowingly suborns perjury is no more culpable than the lawyer who avoids learning that his client, a criminal defendant, lies when he testifies that he was not the shooter? See Hellman, Willfully Blind\nfor Good Reason, 3 Crim. L. & Philosophy 301, 305\xe2\x80\x93308 (2009); Luban, Contrived Ignorance, 87 Geo. L.\xc2\xa0J. 957 (1999). The answer is not obvious. Perhaps the culpability of willful blindness depends on a person\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for remaining blind. E.g., ibid. Or perhaps only the person\xe2\x80\x99s justification for his conduct is relevant. E.g., Alexander & Ferzan, supra, at 23\xe2\x80\x9368. This is a question of morality and of policy best left to the political branches. Even if one were to accept the substitution of equally blameworthy mental states in criminal cases in light of the retributive purposes of the criminal law, those purposes have no force in the domain of patent law that controls in this case. The Constitution confirms that the purpose of the patent law is a utilitarian one, to \xe2\x80\x9cpromote the Progress of Science and useful Arts,\xe2\x80\x9d Art. I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa08.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the Court appeals to precedent, noting that\na \xe2\x80\x9csimilar concept\xe2\x80\x9d to willful blindness appears in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases as early as 1899. Ante, at 11. But this Court has never before held that willful blindness can substitute for a statutory requirement of knowledge. Spurr v. United States, 174 U. S. 728, 735 (1899), explained that \xe2\x80\x9cevil design may be presumed if the [bank] officer purposefully keeps himself in ignorance of whether the drawer has money in the bank or not, or is grossly indifferent to his duty in respect to the ascertainment of that fact.\xe2\x80\x9d The question in Spurr was whether the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s admitted violation was willful, and with this sentence the Court simply explained that wrongful intent may be inferred from the circumstances. It did not suggest that blindness can substitute for knowledge. Neither did Turner v. United States, 396 U. S. 398 (1970), or Leary v. United States, 395 U. S. 6 (1969). As the Court here explains, both cases held only that certain statutory presumptions of knowledge were consistent with due process. Ante, at 12. And although most Courts of Appeals have embraced willful blindness, counting courts in a circuit split is not this Court\xe2\x80\x99s usual method for deciding important questions of law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court appears to endorse the willful blindness doctrine here for all federal criminal cases involving knowledge. It does so in a civil case where it has received no briefing or argument from the criminal defense bar, which might have provided important counsel on this difficult issue.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no need to invoke willful blindness for the first time in this case. Facts that support willful blindness are often probative of actual knowledge. Circumstantial facts like these tend to be the only available evidence in any event, for the jury lacks direct access to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mind. The jury must often infer knowledge from conduct, and attempts to eliminate evidence of knowledge may justify such inference, as where an accused inducer avoids further confirming what he already believes with good reason to be true. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision to expand the statute\xe2\x80\x99s scope appears to depend on the unstated premise that knowledge requires certainty, but the law often permits probabilistic judgments to count as knowledge. Cf. Connecticut Mut. Life Ins. Co. v. Lathrop, 111 U. S. 612, 620 (1884) (Harlan, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[B]eing founded on actual observation, and being consistent with common experience and the ordinary manifestations of the condition of the mind, it is knowledge, so far as the human intellect can acquire knowledge, upon such subjects\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The instant dispute provides a case in point. Pentalpha copied an innovative fryer. The model it copied bore no U. S. patent markings, but that could not have been a surprise, for Pentalpha knew that a fryer purchased in Hong Kong was unlikely to bear such markings. And Pentalpha failed to tell the lawyer who ran a patent search that it copied the SEB fryer. These facts may suggest knowledge that Pentalpha\xe2\x80\x99s fryers were infringing, and perhaps a jury could so find.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But examining the sufficiency of the evidence presented in the 5-day trial requires careful review of an extensive record. The trial transcript alone spans over 1,000 pages. If willful blindness is as close to knowledge and as far from the \xe2\x80\x9cknew or should have known\xe2\x80\x9d jury instruction provided in this case as the Court suggests, then reviewing the record becomes all the more difficult. I would leave that task to the Court of Appeals in the first instance on remand.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, and with respect, I dissent.
