I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Lionel Gentry was convicted in California state court of assault with a deadly weapon for stabbing his girlfriend, Tanaysha Handy. Gentry claimed he stabbed her accidentally during a dispute with a drug dealer.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Handy testified for the prosecution. She stated that she recalled being stabbed but could not remember the details of the incident. The prosecution then confronted Handy with her testimony from a preliminary hearing that Gentry had placed his hand around her throat before stabbing her twice.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Albert Williams, a security guard in a neighboring building, testified that he saw Gentry, Handy, and another man from his third-floor window. According to Williams, Gentry swung his hand into Handy\xe2\x80\x99s left side with some object, causing her to lean forward and scream. Williams was inconsistent about the quality of light at the time, stating variously that it was \xe2\x80\x9cpretty dark\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cgetting dark,\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cit wasn\xe2\x80\x99t that dark,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the area of the stabbing was \xe2\x80\x9clighted up.\xe2\x80\x9d See Gentry v. Roe, 320 F.\xc2\xa03d 891, 896\xe2\x80\x93897 (CA9 2003).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Gentry testified in his own defense that he had stabbed Handy accidentally while pushing her out of the way. When asked about prior convictions, he falsely stated that he had been convicted only once; evidence showed he had been separately convicted of burglary, grand theft, battery on a peace officer, and being a felon in possession of a firearm. He attributed his error to confusion about whether a plea bargain counted as a conviction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In her closing argument, the prosecutor expressed sympathy for Handy\xe2\x80\x99s plight as a pregnant, drug-addicted mother of three and highlighted her damaging preliminary hearing testimony. She accused Gentry of telling the jury a \xe2\x80\x9cpack of lies.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 897\xe2\x80\x93898. Defense counsel responded with the following closing argument:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I don\xe2\x80\x99t have a lot to say today. Just once I\xe2\x80\x99d like to find a prosecutor that doesn\xe2\x80\x99t know exactly what happened. Just once I\xe2\x80\x99d like to find a D. A. that wasn\xe2\x80\x99t there and that can tell and they can stand up here and be honest and say I don\xe2\x80\x99t know who is lying and who is not \xe2\x80\x98cause she wasn\xe2\x80\x99t there, ladies and gentlemen. [I] wasn\xe2\x80\x99t there. None of the 12 of you were there. None of the other people in this courtroom were there except those two people and that one guy who saw parts of it, or saw it all. Pretty dark. Dark. It was light. Those are the three versions of his testimony with regard to what he saw and what he saw. I don\xe2\x80\x99t know what happened. I can\xe2\x80\x99t tell you. And if I sit here and try to tell you what happened, I\xe2\x80\x99m lying to you. I don\xe2\x80\x99t know. I wasn\xe2\x80\x99t there. I don\xe2\x80\x99t have to judge. I don\xe2\x80\x99t have to decide. You heard the testimony come from the truth chair. You heard people testify. You heard good things that made you feel good. You heard bad things that made you feel bad.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I don\xe2\x80\x99t care that Tanaysha is pregnant. I don\xe2\x80\x99t care that she has three children. I don\xe2\x80\x99t know why that had to be brought out in closing. What does that have to do with this case? She was stabbed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The question is, did he intend to stab her? He said he did it by accident. If he\xe2\x80\x99s lying and you think he\xe2\x80\x99s lying then you have to convict him. If you don\xe2\x80\x99t think he\xe2\x80\x99s lying, bad person, lousy drug addict, stinking thief, jail bird, all that to the contrary, he\xe2\x80\x99s not guilty. It\xe2\x80\x99s as simple as that. I don\xe2\x80\x99t care if he\xe2\x80\x99s been in prison. And for the sake of this thing you ought not care because that doesn\xe2\x80\x99t have anything to do with what happened on April 30th, 1994.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98He doesn\xe2\x80\x99t know whether or not he\xe2\x80\x99s been convicted. Didn\xe2\x80\x99t understand the term conviction. The reason that they bring 12 jurors from all different walks of life, let them sit here and listen to people testify, and the reason that the court will give you instructions with regard to not having your life experience, leaving it at the door, is because you can\xe2\x80\x99t just assume that because a guy has done a bunch of bad things that he\xe2\x80\x99s now done this thing.

\n

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Thank you.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 898\xe2\x80\x93899 (one paragraph break omitted).

\n

\nAfter deliberating for about six hours, the jury convicted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On direct appeal, Gentry argued that his trial counsel\xe2\x80\x99s closing argument deprived him of his right to effective assistance of counsel. The California Court of Appeal rejected that contention, and the California Supreme Court denied review. Gentry\xe2\x80\x99s petition for federal habeas relief was denied by the District Court, but the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed. We grant the State\xe2\x80\x99s petition for a writ of certiorari and the motion for leave to proceed in forma pauperis and reverse.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Amendment guarantees criminal defendants the effective assistance of counsel. That right is denied when a defense attorney\xe2\x80\x99s performance falls below an objective standard of reasonableness and thereby prejudices the defense. Wiggins v. Smith, 539 U. S. __, __ (2003) (slip op., at 8); Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668, 687 (1984). If a state court has already rejected an ineffective-assistance claim, a federal court may grant habeas relief if the decision was \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). Where, as here, the state court\xe2\x80\x99s application of governing federal law is challenged, it must be shown to be not only erroneous, but objectively unreasonable. Wiggins, supra, at __ (slip op., at 8); Woodford v. Visciotti, 537 U. S. 19, 24\xe2\x80\x9325 (2002) (per curiam); Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362, 409 (2000).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The right to effective assistance extends to closing arguments. See Bell v. Cone, 535 U. S. 685, 701\xe2\x80\x93702 (2002); Herring v. New York, 422 U. S. 853, 865 (1975). None-\ntheless, counsel has wide latitude in deciding how best to represent a client, and deference to counsel\xe2\x80\x99s tactical decisions in his closing presentation is particularly important because of the broad range of legitimate defense strategy at that stage. Closing arguments should \xe2\x80\x9csharpen and clarify the issues for resolution by the trier of fact,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 862, but which issues to sharpen and how best to clarify them are questions with many reasonable answers. Indeed, it might sometimes make sense to forgo closing argument altogether. See Bell, supra, at 701\xe2\x80\x93702. Judicial review of a defense attorney\xe2\x80\x99s summation is therefore highly deferential\xe2\x80\x94and doubly deferential when it is conducted through the lens of federal habeas.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of these principles, the Ninth Circuit erred in finding the California Court of Appeal\xe2\x80\x99s decision objectively unreasonable. The California court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion cited state case law setting forth the correct federal standard\nfor evaluating ineffective-assistance claims and concluded that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance was not ineffective. That conclusion was supported by the record. The summation for the defense made several key points: that Williams\xe2\x80\x99s testimony about the quality of light was inconsistent; that Handy\xe2\x80\x99s personal circumstances were irrelevant to Gentry\xe2\x80\x99s guilt; that the case turned on whether the stabbing was accidental, and the jury had to acquit if it believed Gentry\xe2\x80\x99s version of events; that Gentry\xe2\x80\x99s criminal history was irrelevant to his guilt, particularly given the seriousness of the charge compared to his prior theft offenses; and that Gentry\xe2\x80\x99s misstatement of the number of times he had been convicted could be explained by his lack of education. Woven through these issues was a unifying theme\xe2\x80\x94that the jury, like the prosecutor and defense counsel himself, were not at the scene of the crime and so could only speculate about what had happened and who was lying.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit rejected the state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion in large part because counsel did not highlight various other potentially exculpatory pieces of evidence: that Handy had used drugs on the day of the stabbing and during the early morning hours of the day of her preliminary hearing; that Williams\xe2\x80\x99s inability to see the stabbing clearly was relevant to the issue of intent; that Gentry\xe2\x80\x99s testimony was consistent with Williams\xe2\x80\x99s in some respects; that the government did not call as a witness Williams\xe2\x80\x99s co-worker, who also saw the stabbing; that the stab wound was only one inch deep, suggesting it may have been accidental; that Handy testified she had been stabbed twice, but only had one wound; and that Gentry, after being confronted by Williams, did not try to retrieve his weapon but instead moved toward Handy while repeating, \xe2\x80\x9cshe\xe2\x80\x99s my girlfriend.\xe2\x80\x9d See 320 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 900\xe2\x80\x93901.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These other potential arguments do not establish that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was unreasonable. Some of the omitted items, such as Gentry\xe2\x80\x99s reaction to Williams, are thoroughly ambiguous. Some of the others might well have backfired. For example, although Handy claimed at trial she had used drugs before the preliminary hearing, she testified at the hearing that she was not under the influence and could remember exactly what had happened the day of the stabbing. And, although Handy\xe2\x80\x99s wound was only one inch deep, it still lacerated her stomach and diaphragm, spilling the stomach\xe2\x80\x99s contents into her chest cavity and requiring almost two hours of surgery. These are facts that the prosecutor could have exploited to great advantage in her rebuttal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if some of the arguments would unquestionably have supported the defense, it does not follow that counsel was incompetent for failing to include them. Focusing on a small number of key points may be more persuasive than a shotgun approach. As one expert advises: \xe2\x80\x9cThe number of issues introduced should definitely be restricted. Research suggests that there is an upper limit to the number of issues or arguments an attorney can present and still have persuasive effect.\xe2\x80\x9d R. Matlon, Opening Statements/Closing Arguments 60 (1993) (citing Calder, Insko, & Yandei, The Relation of Cognitive and Memorial Process to Persuasion in a Simulated Jury Trial, 4 J. Applied Social Psychology 62 (1974)). Another authority says: \xe2\x80\x9cThe advocate is not required to summarize or\ncomment upon all the facts, opinions, inferences, and\nlaw involved in a case. A decision not to address an\nissue, an opponent\xe2\x80\x99s theory, or a particular fact should\nbe based on an analysis of the importance of that subject and the ability of the advocate and the opponent to\nexplain persuasively the position to the fact finder.\xe2\x80\x9d\nR. Haydock & J. Sonsteng, Advocacy: Opening and Closing \xc2\xa73.10, p. 70 (1994). In short, judicious selection of argu-\nments for summation is a core exercise of defense counsel\xe2\x80\x99s\ndiscretion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When counsel focuses on some issues to the exclusion of others, there is a strong presumption that he did so for tactical reasons rather than through sheer neglect. See Strickland, 466 U. S., at 690 (counsel is \xe2\x80\x9cstrongly presumed\xe2\x80\x9d to make decisions in the exercise of professional judgment). That presumption has particular force where a petitioner bases his ineffective-assistance claim solely on the trial record, creating a situation in which a court \xe2\x80\x9cmay have no way of knowing whether a seemingly unusual or misguided action by counsel had a sound strategic motive.\xe2\x80\x9d Massaro v. United States, 538 U. S. __, __ (2003) (slip op., at 4). Moreover, even if an omission is inadvertent, relief is not automatic. The Sixth Amendment guarantees reasonable competence, not perfect advocacy judged with the benefit of hindsight. See Bell, 535 U. S., at 702; Kimmelman v. Morrison, 477 U. S. 365, 382 (1986); Strickland, supra, at 689; United States v. Cronic, 466 U. S. 648, 656 (1984). To recall the words of Justice (and former Solicitor General) Jackson: \xe2\x80\x9cI made three arguments of every case. First came the one that I planned\xe2\x80\x94as I thought, logical, coherent, complete. Second was the one actually presented\xe2\x80\x94interrupted, incoherent, disjointed, disappointing. The third was the utterly devastating argument that I thought of after going to bed that night.\xe2\x80\x9d Advocacy Before the Supreme Court, 37 A.\xc2\xa0B.\xc2\xa0A. J. 801, 803 (1951). Based on the record in this case, a state court could reasonably conclude that Gentry had failed to rebut the presumption of adequate assistance. Counsel plainly put to the jury the centerpiece of his case: that the only testimony regarding what had happened that the jury heard \xe2\x80\x9ccome from the truth chair\xe2\x80\x9d was conflicting; that none of his client\xe2\x80\x99s testimony was demonstrably a lie; and that the testimony contradicting his client came in \xe2\x80\x9cthree versions.\xe2\x80\x9d See 320 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 898. The issues counsel omitted were not so clearly more persuasive than those he discussed that their omission can only be attributed to a professional error of constitutional magnitude.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit found other flaws in counsel\xe2\x80\x99s presentation. It criticized him for mentioning \xe2\x80\x9ca host of details that hurt his client\xe2\x80\x99s position, none of which mattered as a matter of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 900. Of course the reason counsel mentioned those details was precisely to remind the jury that they were legally irrelevant. That was not an unreasonable tactic. See F. Bailey & H. Rothblatt, Successful Techniques for Criminal Trials \xc2\xa719:23, p. 461 (2d ed. 1985) (\xe2\x80\x9cFace up to [the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] defects \xe2\x80\xa6 [and] call upon the jury to disregard everything not connected to the crime with which he is charged\xe2\x80\x9d). The Ninth Circuit singled out for censure counsel\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the jury must acquit if Gentry was telling the truth, even though he was a \xe2\x80\x9cbad person, lousy drug addict, stinking thief, jail bird.\xe2\x80\x9d See 320 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 900. It apparently viewed the remark as a gratuitous swipe at Gentry\xe2\x80\x99s character. While confessing a client\xe2\x80\x99s shortcomings might remind the jury of facts they otherwise would have forgotten, it might also convince them to put aside facts they would have remembered in any event. This is precisely the sort of calculated risk that lies at the heart of an advocate\xe2\x80\x99s discretion. By candidly acknowledging his client\xe2\x80\x99s shortcomings, counsel might have built credibility with the jury and persuaded it to focus on the relevant issues in the case. See J. Stein, Closing Argument \xc2\xa7204, p. 10 (1992\xe2\x80\x931996) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f you make certain concessions showing that you are earnestly in search of the truth, then your comments on matters that are in dispute will be received without the usual apprehension surrounding the remarks of an advocate\xe2\x80\x9d). As Judge Kleinfeld pointed out in dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc, the court\xe2\x80\x99s criticism applies just as well to Clarence Darrow\xe2\x80\x99s closing argument in the Leopold and Loeb case: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I do not know how much salvage there is in these two boys. . . . [Y]our Honor would be merciful if you tied a rope around their necks and let them die; merciful to them, but not merciful to civilization, and not merciful to those who would be left behind.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 320 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 895 (quoting Famous American Jury Speeches 1086 (Hicks ed. 1925) (reprint 1990)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit rebuked counsel for making only a passive request that the jury reach some verdict, rather than an express demand for acquittal. But given a patronizing and overconfident summation by a prosecutor, a low-key strategy that stresses the jury\xe2\x80\x99s autonomy is not unreasonable. One treatise recommends just such a technique: \xe2\x80\x9cAvoid challenging the jury to find for your client, or phrasing your argument in terms suggesting what their finding must be\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [S]cientific research indicates that jurors will react against a lawyer who they think is blatantly trying to limit their freedom of thought.\xe2\x80\x9d Stein, supra, \xc2\xa7206, at 15.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit faulted counsel for not arguing explicitly that the government had failed to prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. Counsel\xe2\x80\x99s entire presentation, however, made just that point. He repeatedly stressed that no one\xe2\x80\x94not the prosecutor, the jury, nor even himself\xe2\x80\x94could be sure who was telling the truth. This is the very essence of a reasonable-doubt argument. To be sure, he did not insist that the existence of a reasonable doubt would require the jury to acquit\xe2\x80\x94but he could count on the judge\xe2\x80\x99s charge to remind them of that requirement, and by doing so he would preserve his strategy of appearing as the friend of jury autonomy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the Ninth Circuit criticized counsel\xe2\x80\x99s approach on the ground that, by confessing that he too could not be sure of the truth, counsel \xe2\x80\x9cimplied that even he did not believe Gentry\xe2\x80\x99s testimony.\xe2\x80\x9d 320 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 900. But there is nothing wrong with a rhetorical device that personalizes the doubts anyone but an eyewitness must necessarily have. Winning over an audience by empathy is a technique that dates back to Aristotle. See P. Lagarias, Effective Closing Argument \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72.05\xe2\x80\x932.06, pp. 99\xe2\x80\x93101 (1989) (citing Aristotle\xe2\x80\x99s Rhetoric for the point that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] speech should indicate to the audience that the speaker shares the attitudes of the listener, so that, in turn, the listener will respond positively to the views of the speaker\xe2\x80\x9d); id., \xc2\xa73.03, at 112 (deriving from this principle the advice that \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel may couch his arguments in terms of \xe2\x80\x98we,\xe2\x80\x99 rather than \xe2\x80\x98you, the jury\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, Gentry\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer was no Aristotle or even Clarence Darrow. But the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion\xe2\x80\x94not only that his performance was deficient, but that any disagreement with that conclusion would be objectively unreasonable\xe2\x80\x94gives too little deference to the state courts that have primary responsibility for supervising defense counsel in state criminal trials.

\n

* * *

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Ninth Circuit is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.
