\n\t\t\t\tSyllabus\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tMOHAMAD, individually and for ESTATE OF RAHIM, DECEASED, et\xc2\xa0al. v. PALESTINIAN AUTHORITY et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x9388.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 28, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 18, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tWhile visiting the West Bank, Azzam Rahim, a naturalized United States citizen, allegedly was arrested by Palestinian Authority intelligence officers, imprisoned, tortured, and ultimately killed. Rahim\xe2\x80\x99s relatives, petitioners here, sued the Palestinian Authority and the Palestinian Liberation Organization under the Torture Victim Protection Act of 1991 (TVPA), which authorizes a cause of action against \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n individual\xe2\x80\x9d for acts of torture and extrajudicial killing committed under authority or color of law of any foreign nation. \n\n106Stat.\n73, note following \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71350. The District Court dismissed the suit, concluding, as relevant here, that the TVPA\xe2\x80\x99s authorization of suit against \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n individual\xe2\x80\x9d extended liability only to natural persons. The United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0As used in the TVPA, the term \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d encompasses only natural persons. Consequently, the Act does not impose liability against organizations. Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The ordinary, everyday meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d refers to a human being, not an organization, and Congress in the normal course does not employ the word any differently. The Dictionary Act defines \xe2\x80\x9cperson\xe2\x80\x9d to include certain artificial entities \xe2\x80\x9cas well as individuals,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71, thereby marking \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d as distinct from artificial entities. Federal statutes routinely distinguish between an \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d and an organizational entity. See, e.g., 7 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa792(k), 511. And the very Congress that passed the TVPA defined \xe2\x80\x9cperson\xe2\x80\x9d in a separate Act to include \xe2\x80\x9cany individual or entity.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72331(3). Pp.\xc2\xa02\xe2\x80\x935.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Before a word will be assumed to have a meaning broader than or different from its ordinary meaning, Congress must give some indication that it intended such a result. There are no such indications in the TVPA. To the contrary, the statutory context confirms that Congress in the Act created a cause of action against natural persons alone. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s liability provision uses the word \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d five times in the same sentence: once to refer to the perpetrator and four times to refer to the victim. See TVPA \xc2\xa72(a). Since only a natural person can be a victim of torture or extrajudicial killing, it is difficult to conclude that Congress used \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d four times in the same sentence to refer to a natural person and once to refer to a natural person and any nonsovereign organization. In addition, the TVPA holds perpetrators liable for extrajudicial killing to \xe2\x80\x9cany person who may be a claimant in an action for wrongful death.\xe2\x80\x9d See TVPA \xc2\xa72(a)(2). \xe2\x80\x9cPersons\xe2\x80\x9d often has a broader meaning in the law than \xe2\x80\x9cindividual,\xe2\x80\x9d and frequently includes non-natural persons. Construing \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d in the Act to encompass solely natural persons credits Congress\xe2\x80\x99 use of disparate terms. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 counterarguments are unpersuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0Petitioners dispute that the plain text of the TVPA requires this Court\xe2\x80\x99s result. First, they rely on definitions that frame \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d in nonhuman terms, emphasizing the idea of \xe2\x80\x9coneness,\xe2\x80\x9d but these definitions make for an awkward fit in the context of the TVPA. Next they claim that federal tort statutes uniformly provide for liability against organizations, a convention they maintain is common to the legal systems of other nations. But while \xe2\x80\x9cCongress is understood to legislate against a background of common-law adjudicatory principles,\xe2\x80\x9d Astoria Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn. v. Solimino, \n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104\n, Congress plainly evinced its intent in the TVPA not to subject organizations to liability. Petitioners next argue that the TVPA\xe2\x80\x99s scope of liability should be construed to conform with other federal statutes they claim provide civil remedies to victims of torture or extrajudicial killing. But none of the statutes petitioners cite employs the term \xe2\x80\x9cindividual,\xe2\x80\x9d as the TVPA, to describe the covered defendant. Finally, although petitioners rightly note that the TVPA contemplates liability against officers who do not personally execute the torture or extrajudicial killing, it does not follow that the Act embraces liability against nonsovereign organizations. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0Petitioners also contend that legislative history supports their broad reading of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual,\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9creliance on legislative history is unnecessary in light of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s unambiguous language.\xe2\x80\x9d Milavetz, Gallop & Milavetz, P.\xc2\xa0A. v. United States, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. In any event, the history supports this Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(3)\xc2\xa0Finally, petitioners argue that precluding organizational liability may foreclose effective remedies for victims and their relatives. This purposive argument simply cannot overcome the force of the plain text. Moreover, Congress appeared well aware of the limited nature of the cause of action it established in the TVPA. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t634 F.\xc2\xa03d 604, affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tASID MOHAMAD, individually and for the ESTATE OF AZZAM RAHIM, deceased, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. PALESTINIAN AUTHORITY et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Torture Victim Protection Act of 1991 (TVPA or Act), \n\n106Stat.\n73, note following \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71350, authorizes a cause of action against \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n individual\xe2\x80\x9d for acts of torture and extrajudicial killing committed under authority or color of law of any foreign nation. We hold that the term \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d as used in the Act encompasses only natural persons. Consequently, the Act does not impose liability against organizations.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause this case arises from a motion to dismiss, we ac-cept as true the allegations of the complaint. Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 1). Petitioners are the relatives of Azzam Rahim, who immigrated to the United States in the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s and became a naturalized citizen. In 1995, while on a visit to the West Bank, Rahim was arrested by Palestinian Authority intelligence officers. He was taken to a prison in Jericho, where he was imprisoned, tortured, and ultimately killed. The following year, the U.\xc2\xa0S. Department of State issued a report concluding that Rahim \xe2\x80\x9cdied in the custody of [Palestinian Authority] intelligence officers in Jericho.\xe2\x80\x9d Dept. of State, Occupied Territories Human Rights Practices, 1995 (Mar. 1996).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2005, petitioners filed this action against respondents, the Palestinian Authority and the Palestinian Liberation Organization, asserting, inter alia, claims of torture and extrajudicial killing under the TVPA. The District Court granted respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion to dismiss, concluding, as relevant, that the Act\xe2\x80\x99s authorization of suit against \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n individual\xe2\x80\x9d extended liability only to natural persons. Mohamad v. Rajoub, 664 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 20, 22 (DC 2009). The United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit affirmed on the same ground. See Mohamad v. Rajoub, 634 F.\xc2\xa03d 604, 608 (2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress used the word \xe2\x80\x98individual\xe2\x80\x99 to denote only natural persons\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t We granted certiorari, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011), to resolve a split among the Circuits with respect to whether the TVPA authorizes actions against defendants that are not natural persons,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and now affirm.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe TVPA imposes liability on individuals for certain acts of torture and extrajudicial killing. The Act provides:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cAn individual who, under actual or apparent author-ity, or color of law, of any foreign nation\xe2\x80\x94\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(1) subjects an individual to torture shall, in a civil action, be liable for damages to that individual; or\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(2) subjects an individual to extrajudicial killing shall, in a civil action, be liable for damages to the individual\xe2\x80\x99s legal representative, or to any person who may be a claimant in an action for wrongful death.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72(a).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Act defines \xe2\x80\x9ctorture\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cextrajudicial killing,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73, and imposes a statute of limitations and an exhaustion requirement, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72(b), (c). It does not define \xe2\x80\x9cindividual.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners concede that foreign states may not be sued under the Act\xe2\x80\x94namely, that the Act does not create an exception to the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976, \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71602 et seq., which renders foreign sovereigns largely immune from suits in U.\xc2\xa0S. courts. They argue, however, that the TVPA does not similarly restrict liability against other juridical entities. In petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 view, by permitting suit against \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n individual,\xe2\x80\x9d the TVPA contemplates liability against natural persons and nonsovereign organizations (a category that, petitioners assert, includes respondents). We decline to read \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d so unnaturally. The ordinary meaning of the word, fortified by its statutory context, persuades us that the Act authorizes suit against natural persons alone.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause the TVPA does not define the term \xe2\x80\x9cindividual,\xe2\x80\x9d we look first to the word\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary meaning. See FCC v. AT&T Inc., 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 5) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a statute does not define a term, we typically give the phrase its ordinary meaning\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). As a noun, \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d ordinarily means \xe2\x80\x9c[a] human being, a person.\xe2\x80\x9d 7 Oxford English Dictionary 880 (2d ed. 1989); see also, e.g., Random House Dictionary of the English Language 974 (2d ed. 1987) (\xe2\x80\x9ca person\xe2\x80\x9d); Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 1152 (1986) (\xe2\x80\x9ca particular person\xe2\x80\x9d) (hereinafter Webster\xe2\x80\x99s). After all, that is how we use the word in everyday parlance. We say \xe2\x80\x9cthe individual went to the store,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe individual left the room,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe individual took the car,\xe2\x80\x9d each time re-ferring unmistakably to a natural person. And no one, we hazard to guess, refers in normal parlance to an organization as an \xe2\x80\x9cindividual.\xe2\x80\x9d Evidencing that common usage, this Court routinely uses \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d to denote a natural person, and in particular to distinguish between a natural person and a corporation. See, e.g., Goodyear Dunlop Tires Operations, S.\xc2\xa0A. v. Brown, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. __, __ (2011) (slip op., at 7) (\xe2\x80\x9cFor an individual, the paradigm forum for the exercise of general jurisdiction is the individual\xe2\x80\x99s domicile; for a corporation, it is an equivalent place, one in which the corporation is fairly regarded as at home\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress does not, in the ordinary course, employ the word any differently. The Dictionary Act instructs that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n determining the meaning of any Act of Congress, unless the context indicates otherwise .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the wor[d] \xe2\x80\x98person\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. include[s] corporations, companies, associations, firms, partnerships, societies, and joint stock companies, as well as individuals.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 (emphasis added). With the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cas well as,\xe2\x80\x9d the definition marks \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d as distinct from the list of artificial entities that precedes it.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn a like manner, federal statutes routinely distinguish between an \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d and an organizational entity of some kind. See, e.g., \n\n7 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa792(k) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Person\xe2\x80\x99 includes partnerships, associations, and corporations, as well as individuals\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7511 (same); \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7717a (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Person\xe2\x80\x99 includes an individual or a corporation\xe2\x80\x9d); \n\n16 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7796 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[P]erson\xe2\x80\x99 means an individual or a corporation\xe2\x80\x9d); \n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101(b)(3) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[P]erson\xe2\x80\x99 means an individual or an organization\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, the very same Congress that enacted the TVPA also established a cause of action for U.\xc2\xa0S. nationals injured \xe2\x80\x9cby reason of an act of interna-tional terrorism\xe2\x80\x9d and defined \xe2\x80\x9cperson\xe2\x80\x9d as it appears in the statute to include \xe2\x80\x9cany individual or entity capable of holding a legal or beneficial interest in property.\xe2\x80\x9d Federal Courts Administration Act of 1992, 18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72333(a), 2331(3) (emphasis added)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis is not to say that the word \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d invariably means \xe2\x80\x9cnatural person\xe2\x80\x9d when used in a statute. Congress remains free, as always, to give the word a broader or different meaning. But before we will assume it has done so, there must be some indication Congress intended such a result. Perhaps it is the rare statute (petitioners point to only one such example, located in the Internal Revenue Code) in which Congress expressly defines \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d to include corporate entities. See \n\n26 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7542(a)(2). Or perhaps, as was the case in Clinton v. City of New York, \n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 417, \n429 (1998)\n, the statutory context makes that intention clear, because any other reading of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d would lead to an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98absurd\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d result Congress could not plausibly have intended.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere are no such indications in the TVPA. As noted, the Act does not define \xe2\x80\x9cindividual,\xe2\x80\x9d much less do so in a manner that extends the term beyond its ordinary usage. And the statutory context strengthens\xe2\x80\x94not undermines\xe2\x80\x94the conclusion that Congress intended to create a cause of action against natural persons alone. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s liability provision uses the word \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d five times in the same sentence: once to refer to the perpetrator (i.e., the defendant) and four times to refer to the victim. See \xc2\xa72(a). Only a natural person can be a victim of torture or extrajudicial killing. \xe2\x80\x9cSince there is a presumption that a given term is used to mean the same thing throughout a statute, a presumption surely at its most vigorous when a term is repeated within a given sentence,\xe2\x80\x9d Brown v. Gardner, \n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 115, \n118 (1994)\n (citation omitted), it is difficult indeed to conclude that Congress employed the term \xe2\x80\x9cin-dividual\xe2\x80\x9d four times in one sentence to refer to a natural person and once to refer to a natural person and any nonsovereign organization. See also \xc2\xa73(b)(1) (using term \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d six times in referring to victims of torture).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is also revealing that the Act holds perpetrators lia-ble for extrajudicial killing to \xe2\x80\x9cany person who may be a claimant in an action for wrongful death.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72(a)(2) (emphasis added). \xe2\x80\x9cPerson,\xe2\x80\x9d we have recognized, often has a broader meaning in the law than \xe2\x80\x9cindividual,\xe2\x80\x9d see Clinton, 524 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 428, n.\xc2\xa013, and frequently includes nonnatural persons, see, e.g., \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71. We generally seek to respect Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to use different terms to de-scribe different categories of people or things. See Sosa v. Alvarez-Machain, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 692\n, n.\xc2\xa09 (2004). Our construction of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d to encompass solely natural persons credits Congress\xe2\x80\x99 use of the disparate terms; petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 construction does not.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, the text of the statute persuades us that the Act authorizes liability solely against natural persons.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 counterarguments are unpersuasive.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners first dispute that the plain text of the TVPA requires today\xe2\x80\x99s result. Although they concede that an ordinary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9chuman being,\xe2\x80\x9d petitioners point to definitions of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cframe the term .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in distinctly non-human terms, instead placing their emphases on the oneness of something.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 18 (citing, e.g., Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 1152 (defining \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9ca single or particular being or thing or group of being or things\xe2\x80\x9d)). Those definitions, however, do not account even for petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 preferred interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cin-dividual\xe2\x80\x9d in the Act, for foreign states\xe2\x80\x94which petition- ers concede are not liable under the Act\xe2\x80\x94do not differ from nonsovereign organizations in their degree of \xe2\x80\x9coneness.\xe2\x80\x9d Moreover, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]ords that can have more than one meaning are given content .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by their surroundings,\xe2\x80\x9d Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc., \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 457, \n466 (2001)\n, and for the reasons explained supra, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 definition makes for an awkward fit in the context of the TVPA.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners next claim that federal tort statutes uniformly provide for liability against organizations, a convention they maintain is common to the legal systems of other nations. We are not convinced, however, that any such \xe2\x80\x9cdomestic and international presumption of organi-zational liability\xe2\x80\x9d in tort actions overcomes the ordi- nary meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 16. It is true that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress is understood to legislate against a background of common-law adjudicatory principles.\xe2\x80\x9d Astoria Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn. v. Solimino, \n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104, \n108 (1991)\n. But Congress plainly can override those principles, see, e.g., id., at 108\xe2\x80\x93109, and, as explained supra, the TVPA\xe2\x80\x99s text evinces a clear intent not to subject nonsovereign organizations to liability.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe also decline petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 suggestion to construe the TVPA\xe2\x80\x99s scope of liability to conform with other federal statutes that petitioners contend provide civil remedies to victims of torture or extrajudicial killing. None of the three statutes petitioners identify employs the term \xe2\x80\x9cin-dividual\xe2\x80\x9d to describe the covered defendant, and so none assists in the interpretive task we face today. See \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983; 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71603(a), 1605A(c) (2006 ed., Supp. IV); 18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72333, 2334(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b), 2337. The same is true of the Alien Tort Statute, \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71350, so it offers no comparative value here regardless of whether corporate entities can be held liable in a federal common-law action brought under that statute. Compare Doe v. Exxon Mobil Corp., 654 F.\xc2\xa03d 11 (CADC 2011), with Kiobel v. Royal Dutch Petroleum Co., 621 F.\xc2\xa03d 111 (CA2 2010), cert. granted, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011). Finally, al-though petitioners rightly note that the TVPA contemplates liability against officers who do not personally execute the torture or extrajudicial killing, see, e.g., Chavez v. Carranza, 559 F.\xc2\xa03d 486 (CA6 2009), it does not follow (as petitioners argue) that the Act embraces liability against nonsovereign organizations. An officer who gives an order to torture or kill is an \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d in that word\xe2\x80\x99s ordinary usage; an organization is not.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners also contend that legislative history supports their broad reading of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual.\xe2\x80\x9d But \xe2\x80\x9creliance on legislative history is unnecessary in light of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s unambiguous language.\xe2\x80\x9d Milavetz, Gallop & Milavetz, P.\xc2\xa0A. v. United States, 559 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, n. 3 (2010) (slip op., at 6, n.\xc2\xa03). In any event, the excerpts petitioners cite do not help their cause. Petitioners note that the Senate Report states that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he legislation uses the term \xe2\x80\x98individual\xe2\x80\x99 to make crystal clear that foreign states or their entities cannot be sued under this bill under any circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 102\xe2\x80\x93249, p.\xc2\xa07 (1991) (S. Rep.); see also H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 102\xe2\x80\x93367, pt.\xc2\xa01, p.\xc2\xa04 (1991) (H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep.) (\xe2\x80\x9cOnly \xe2\x80\x98individuals,\xe2\x80\x99 not foreign states, can be sued\xe2\x80\x9d). Yet that statement, while clarifying that the Act does not encompass liability against foreign states, says nothing about liability against nonsovereign organizations. The other excerpts petitioners cite likewise are not probative of the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual,\xe2\x80\x9d for they signal only that the Act does not impose liability on perpetrators who act without authority or color of law of a foreign state. See, e.g., H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep., at 5 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe bill does not attempt to deal with torture or killing by purely private groups\xe2\x80\x9d); S. Rep., at 8 (The bill \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not cover purely private criminal acts by individuals or nongovernmental organizations\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, although we need not rely on legislative history given the text\xe2\x80\x99s clarity, we note that the history only supports our interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual.\xe2\x80\x9d The version of the TVPA that was introduced in the 100th Congress established liability against a \xe2\x80\x9cperson.\xe2\x80\x9d Hearing and Markup on H.\xc2\xa0R. 1417 before the House Committee on Foreign Affairs and Its Subcommittee on Human Rights and International Organizations, 100th Cong., 2d Sess., 82 (1988). During the markup session of the House Foreign Affairs Committee, one of the bill\xe2\x80\x99s sponsors proposed an amendment \xe2\x80\x9cto make it clear we are applying it to individuals and not to corporations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 81, 87. Counsel explained that it was a \xe2\x80\x9cfairly simple\xe2\x80\x9d matter \xe2\x80\x9cof changing the word \xe2\x80\x98person\xe2\x80\x99 to \xe2\x80\x98individuals\xe2\x80\x99 in several places in the bill.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 87\xe2\x80\x9388. The amendment was unanimously adopted, and the version of the bill reported out of Committee reflected the change. Id., at 88; H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 693, pt.\xc2\xa01, p.\xc2\xa01 (1988). A materially identical version of the bill was enacted as the TVPA by the 102d Congress. Although we are cognizant of the limitations of this drafting history, cf. Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah Services, Inc., \n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 546, \n568 (2005)\n, we nevertheless find it telling that the sole explanation for substituting \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9cperson\xe2\x80\x9d confirms what we have concluded from the text alone.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 final argument is that the Act would be rendered toothless by a construction of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d that limits liability to natural persons. They contend that pre-cluding organizational liability may foreclose effective remedies for victims and their relatives for any number of reasons. Victims may be unable to identify the men and women who subjected them to torture, all the while knowing the organization for whom they work. Personal jurisdiction may be more easily established over corporate than human beings. And natural persons may be more likely than organizations to be judgment proof. Indeed, we are told that only two TVPA plaintiffs have been able to re-cover successfully against a natural person\xe2\x80\x94one only after the defendant won the state lottery. See Jean v. Dorelien, 431 F. 3d 776, 778 (CA11 2005).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe acknowledge petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 concerns about the limitations on recovery. But they are ones that Congress imposed and that we must respect. \xe2\x80\x9c[N]o legislation pursues its purposes at all costs,\xe2\x80\x9d Rodriguez v. United States, \n\n480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522\n\xe2\x80\x93526 (1987) (per curiam), and petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 purposive argument simply cannot overcome the force of the plain text. We add only that Congress appeared well aware of the limited nature of the cause of action it established in the Act. See, e.g., 138 Cong. Rec. 4177 (1992) (remarks of Sen. Simpson) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cas a practical matter, this legislation will result in a very small number of cases\xe2\x80\x9d); 137 Cong. Rec. 2671 (1991) (remarks of Sen. Specter) (\xe2\x80\x9cLet me emphasize that the bill is a limited measure. It is estimated that only a few of these lawsuits will ever be brought\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe text of the TVPA convinces us that Congress did not extend liability to organizations, sovereign or not. There are no doubt valid arguments for such an extension. But Congress has seen fit to proceed in more modest steps in the Act, and it is not the province of this Branch to do otherwise. The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit is affirmed.\n\t\t\t
