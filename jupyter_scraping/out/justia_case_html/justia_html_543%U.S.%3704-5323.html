\nSMITH V. TEXAS
\n543 U. S. ____ (2004)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

LaROYCE LATHAIR SMITH v. TEXAS

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the court of criminal appeals of texas

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x935323.Decided November 15, 2004

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner LaRoyce Lathair Smith was convicted of capital murder and sentenced to death by a jury in Dallas County, Texas. Before the jury reached its sentence, the trial judge issued a supplemental \xe2\x80\x9cnullification instruction.\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte Smith, 132 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 407, 409 (Tex. Crim. App. 2004). That instruction directed the jury to give effect to mitigation evidence, but allowed the jury to do so only by negating what would otherwise be affirmative responses to two special issues relating to deliberateness and future dangerousness. In Penry v. Johnson, 532 U. S. 782 (2001) (Penry II), we held a similar \xe2\x80\x9cnullification instruction\xe2\x80\x9d constitutionally inadequate because it did not allow the jury to give \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98full consideration and full effect to mitigating circumstances\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in choosing the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s appropriate sentence. Id., at 797 (quoting Johnson v. Texas, 509 U. S. 350, 381 (1993) (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., dissenting)). Despite our holding in Penry II, the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals rejected petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s request for postconviction relief. The court reasoned that the instruction either was irrelevant because petitioner did not proffer \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutionally significant\xe2\x80\x9d mitigation evidence, or was sufficiently distinguishable from the instruction in Penry II to survive constitutional scrutiny. 132 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 413, n. 21. We grant the petition for certiorari and petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion for leave to proceed in forma pauperis, and reverse.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1991, petitioner was convicted of brutally murdering one of his former co-workers at a Taco Bell in Dallas County. The victim and one of her co-workers were closing down the restaurant when petitioner and several friends asked to be let in to use the telephone. The two employees recognized petitioner and let him in. Petitioner then told his former co-workers to leave because he wanted to rob the restaurant. When they did not leave, petitioner killed one co-worker by pistol-whipping her and shooting her in the back. Petitioner also threatened, but did not harm, his other former co-worker before exiting with his friends. The jury found petitioner guilty of capital murder beyond a reasonable doubt.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the punishment phase, the jury was instructed on two special issues: first, whether the killing was deliberate; and second, whether the defendant posed a continuing danger to others.[Footnote 1] Approximately two years prior to the trial, we had held that presenting only these two special issues, without additional instructions regarding the jury\xe2\x80\x99s duty to consider mitigation evidence, violated the Eighth Amendment. Penry v. Lynaugh, 492 U. S. 302, 328 (1989) (Penry I). Shortly after petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s trial, the Texas Legislature amended its capital sentencing scheme to require juries to take \xe2\x80\x9cinto consideration all of the evidence, including the circumstances of the offense, the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s character and background, and the personal moral culpability of the defendant\xe2\x80\x9d in deciding whether there are sufficient mitigating circumstances to warrant a sentence of life imprisonment rather than a death sentence. Penry II, supra, at 803 (citing Tex. Code Crim. Proc. Ann., Art. 37.071(2)(e)(1) (Vernon Supp. 2001)). Petitioner, however, did not receive the benefit of the new statutory instruction at his trial. Instead, just as in Penry II, petitioner was sentenced pursuant to a supplemental instruction provided to the jury by the trial judge.[Footnote 2] That instruction read:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98You are instructed that you shall consider any evidence which, in your opinion, is mitigating. Mitigating evidence is evidence that reduces the Defendant\xe2\x80\x99s personal or moral culpability or blameworthiness, and may include, but is not limited to, any aspect of the Defendant\xe2\x80\x99s character, record, background, or circumstances of the offense for which you have found him guilty. Our law does not specify what may or may not be considered as mitigating evidence. Neither does our law provide a formula for determining how much weight, if any, a mitigating circumstance deserves. You may hear evidence which, in your judgment, has no relationship to any of the Special Issues, but if you find such evidence is mitigating under these instructions, you shall consider it in the following instructions of the Court. You, and each of you, are the sole judges of what evidence, if any, is mitigating and how much weight, if any, the mitigating circumstances, if any, including those which have no relationship to any of the Special Issues, deserves.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98In answering the Special Issues submitted to you herein, if you believe that the State has proved beyond a reasonable doubt that the answers to the Special Issues are \xe2\x80\x9cYes,\xe2\x80\x9d and you also believe from the mitigating evidence, if any, that the Defendant should not be sentenced to death, then you shall answer at least one of the Special Issues \xe2\x80\x9cNo\xe2\x80\x9d in order to give effect to your belief that the death penalty should not be imposed due to the mitigating evidence presented to you. In this regard, you are further instructed that the State of Texas must prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the death sentence should be imposed despite the mitigating evidence, if any, admitted before you.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98You are instructed that you may deliberate as a body about mitigating circumstances, but you are not required to reach a unanimous verdict as to their existence or weight. When you vote about the Special Issues, each of you must decide for yourself whether mitigating circumstances exist and, if so, how much weight they deserve.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 132 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 409.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Employing the framework of special issues modified by the supplemental nullification instruction, the jury considered a variety of mitigation evidence. Petitioner presented evidence that (1) he had been diagnosed with potentially organic learning disabilities and speech handicaps at an early age; (2) he had a verbal IQ score of 75 and a full IQ of 78 and, as a result, had been in special education classes throughout most of his time in school; (3) despite his low IQ and learning disabilities, his behavior at school was often exemplary; (4) his father was a drug addict who was involved with gang violence and other criminal activities, and regularly stole money from family members to support a drug addiction; and (5) he was only 19 when he committed the crime.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In response, the prosecution submitted evidence demonstrating that petitioner acted deliberately and cruelly. The prosecution emphasized that petitioner knew his victim, yet stabbed her repeatedly in numerous places on her body. With respect to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s future dangerousness, the prosecution stressed that petitioner had previously been convicted of misdemeanor assault and proffered evidence suggesting that he had violated several drug laws.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0During closing arguments at the punishment phase, the prosecution reminded the jury of its duty to answer truthfully the two special issues of deliberateness and future dangerousness.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cNow, when we talked to you on voir dire, we talked to you about\xe2\x80\x94and we spent a lot of time talking to you to determine whether or not you could follow the law. You told us two very important things when we talked to you. First of all, you told us that in the appropriate case that you could give the death penalty. Second, you said, \xe2\x80\x98Mr. Nancarrow, Ms. McDaniel, if you prove to me that the answers to those special issues should be yes, then I can answer them yes.\xe2\x80\x99 If you wavered, if you hesitated one minute on that, then I guarantee you, you weren\xe2\x80\x99t going to be on this jury. We believed you then, and we believe you now.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. 6.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jury verdict form tracked the final reminders the prosecution gave the jury. The form made no mention of nullification. Nor did it say anything about mitigation evidence. Instead, the verdict form asked whether petitioner committed the act deliberately and whether there was a probability that he would commit criminal acts of violence that would constitute a continuing threat to society. The jury was allowed to give \xe2\x80\x9cYes\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cNo\xe2\x80\x9d answers only. The jury answered both questions \xe2\x80\x9cYes\xe2\x80\x9d and sentenced petitioner to death. App. 4 to Pet. for Cert.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On direct appeal, petitioner argued that our holding in Penry I rendered his jury instructions unconstitutional because the special issues did not allow the jury to give effect to his mitigation evidence. The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals affirmed petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s sentence, reasoning that the nullification instruction provided an adequate vehicle through which the jury could consider petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s evidence. We denied certiorari on May 15, 1995. Smith v. Texas, 514 U. S. 1112.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner filed an original writ of habeas corpus in the trial court in 1998. That suit was dismissed as untimely, but the Texas Legislature amended its criminal code in such a way as to allow petitioner to file a timely writ. Petitioner did so, claiming that his jury was instructed in violation of the Eighth Amendment. Before the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals, petitioner argued that the jury instructions in his case ran afoul of our holding in Penry II. The court denied petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s application on the merits. 132 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 407 (2004).[Footnote 3]

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals issued its opinion just prior to our decision in Tennard v. Dretke, 542 U. S. ___ (2004). In Tennard, we reversed the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to grant a certificate of appealability (COA) to a defendant who was sentenced under the Texas capital sentencing scheme prior to the legislative revisions which took place in the aftermath of Penry I. Tennard, relying upon Penry I, argued that Texas\xe2\x80\x99 two special issues\xe2\x80\x94deliberateness and future dangerousness\xe2\x80\x94did not allow the jury to give effect to his mitigation evidence and that the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to issue a supplemental mitigation instruction that would allow the jury to give full effect to his evidence rendered his death sentence unconstitutional. The state court and the Fifth Circuit both held that the lack of an adequate mitigation instruction was irrelevant. The courts both determined that Tennard had failed to satisfy the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s threshold standard for \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98constitutionally relevant\xe2\x80\x99 mitigating evidence, that is, evidence of a \xe2\x80\x98uniquely severe permanent handicap with which the defendant was burdened through no fault of his own,\xe2\x80\x99 and evidence that \xe2\x80\x98the criminal act was attributable to this severe permanent condition.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 542 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 6) (some internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our rejection of that threshold test was central to our decision to reverse in Tennard. We held that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s test has no foundation in the decisions of this Court. Neither Penry I nor its progeny screened mitigating evidence for \xe2\x80\x98constitutional relevance\xe2\x80\x99 before considering whether the jury instructions comported with the Eighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 9). Rather, we held that the jury must be given an effective vehicle with which to weigh mitigating evidence so long as the defendant has met a \xe2\x80\x9clow threshold for relevance,\xe2\x80\x9d which is satisfied by \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cevidence which tends logically to prove or disprove some fact or circumstance which a fact-finder could reasonably deem to have mitigating value.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 10) (quoting McKoy v. North Carolina, 494 U. S. 433, 440 (1990)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals relied on precisely the same \xe2\x80\x9cscreening test\xe2\x80\x9d we\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0held constitutionally inadequate in Tennard. 132 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 413 (holding that mitigation evidence requires a special instruction only when that evidence passes the threshold test of \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s criminal act was \xe2\x80\x98due to the uniquely severe permanent handicaps with which the defendant was burdened through no fault of his own\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Robertson v. Cockrell, 325 F.\xc2\xa03d 243, 251 (CA5 2003) (en banc)). Employing this test, the court concluded that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s low IQ and placement in special-education classes were irrelevant because they did not demonstrate that he suffered from a \xe2\x80\x9csevere disability.\xe2\x80\x9d 132 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 414. But, as we explained in Tennard, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]vidence of significantly impaired intellectual functioning is obviously evidence that \xe2\x80\x98might serve as a basis for a sentence less than death.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 542 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 13) (some internal quotation marks omitted) (quoting Skipper v. South Carolina, 476 U. S. 1, 5 (1986)). There is no question that a jury might well have considered petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s IQ scores and history of participation in special-education classes as a reason to impose a sentence more lenient than death. Indeed, we have held that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s IQ score of 79, a score slightly higher than petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s, constitutes relevant mitigation evidence. See Wiggins v. Smith, 539 U. S. 510, 535 (2003); see also Tennard, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 14\xe2\x80\x9315).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The state court also held that petitioner had offered \xe2\x80\x9cno evidence of any link or nexus between his troubled childhood or his limited mental abilities and this capital murder.\xe2\x80\x9d 132 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 414. We rejected the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cnexus\xe2\x80\x9d requirement in Tennard, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313) (noting that none of our prior opinions \xe2\x80\x9csuggested that a mentally retarded individual must establish a nexus between her mental capacity and her crime before the Eighth Amendment prohibition on executing her is triggered\xe2\x80\x9d and holding that the jury must be allowed the opportunity to consider Penry evidence even if the defendant cannot establish \xe2\x80\x9ca nexus to the crime\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s evidence was relevant for mitigation purposes is plain under our precedents, even those predating Tennard. See, e.g., Penry I, 492 U. S., at 319\xe2\x80\x93322; Payne v. Tennessee, 501 U. S. 808, 822 (1991); Boyde v. California, 494 U. S. 370, 377\xe2\x80\x93378 (1990); Eddings v. Oklahoma, 455 U. S. 104, 114 (1982). The state court, however, erroneously relied on a test we never countenanced and now have unequivocally rejected. We therefore hold that the state court \xe2\x80\x9cassessed [petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s legal] claim under an improper legal standard.\xe2\x80\x9d Tennard, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 13). Because petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proffered evidence was relevant, the Eighth Amendment required the trial court to empower the jury with a vehicle capable of giving effect to that evidence. Whether the \xe2\x80\x9cnullification instruction\xe2\x80\x9d satisfied that charge is the question to which we now turn.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals held that even if petitioner did proffer relevant mitigation evidence, the supplemental \xe2\x80\x9cnullification instruction\xe2\x80\x9d provided to the jury adequately allowed the jury to give effect to that evidence. The court found it significant that the supplemental instruction in this case \xe2\x80\x9ctold the jury that it \xe2\x80\x98shall\xe2\x80\x99 consider all mitigating evidence, even evidence unrelated to the special issues, [and] it also told the jury how to answer the special issues to give effect to that mitigation evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d 132 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 416. The court also concluded that the nullification instruction made it clear to the jury that a \xe2\x80\x9cNo\xe2\x80\x9d answer was required if it \xe2\x80\x9cbelieved that the death penalty was not warranted because of the mitigating circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Penry II, we held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe key under Penry I is that the jury be able to \xe2\x80\x98consider and give effect to [a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mitigation] evidence in imposing sentence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 532 U. S., at 797 (quoting Penry I, supra, at 319); see 532 U. S., at 797 (citing Johnson v. Texas, 509 U. S., at 381 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] sentencer [must] be allowed to give full consideration and full effect to mitigating circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis in Johnson))). We explained at length why the supplemental instruction employed by the Texas courts did not provide the jury with an adequate vehicle for expressing a \xe2\x80\x9creasoned moral response\xe2\x80\x9d to all of the evidence relevant to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s culpability. 532 U. S., at 796. Although there are some distinctions between the Penry II supplemental instruction and the instruction petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s jury received, those distinctions are constitutionally insignificant.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Penry II identified a broad and intractable problem\xe2\x80\x94a problem that the state court ignored here\xe2\x80\x94inherent in any requirement that the jury nullify special issues contained within a verdict form.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cWe generally presume that jurors follow their instructions. Here, however, it would have been both logically and ethically impossible for a juror to follow both sets of instructions. Because Penry\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence did not fit within the scope of the special issues, answering those issues in the manner prescribed on the verdict form necessarily meant ignoring the command of the supplemental instruction. And answering the special issues in the mode prescribed by the supplemental instruction necessarily meant ignoring the verdict form instructions. Indeed, jurors who wanted to answer one of the special issues falsely to give effect to the mitigating evidence would have had to violate their oath to render a \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9ctrue verdict.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe mechanism created by the supplemental instruction thus inserted \xe2\x80\x98an element of capriciousness\xe2\x80\x99 into the sentencing decision, \xe2\x80\x98making the jurors\xe2\x80\x99 power to avoid the death penalty dependent on their willingness\xe2\x80\x99 to elevate the supplemental instruction over the verdict form instructions. There is, at the very least, \xe2\x80\x98a reasonable likelihood that the jury \xe2\x80\xa6 applied the challenged instruction in a way that prevent[ed] the consideration\xe2\x80\x99 of Penry\xe2\x80\x99s mental retardation and childhood abuse. The supplemental instruction therefore provided an inadequate vehicle for the jury to make a reasoned moral response to Penry\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 799\xe2\x80\x93800 (citations omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is certainly true that the mandatory aspect of the nullification instruction made petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s instruction distinct from Penry\xe2\x80\x99s. Indeed, the \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d command in the nullification instruction resolved the ambiguity inherent in the Penry II instruction, which we held was either a nullification instruction or an instruction that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98shackled and confined\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Penry\xe2\x80\x99s mitigating evidence within the scope of the impermissibly narrow special issues. Id., at 798. That being said, the clearer instruction given to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s jury did not resolve the ethical problem described supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311.[Footnote 4] To the contrary, the mandatory language in the charge could possibly have intensified the dilemma faced by ethical jurors. Just as in Penry II, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s jury was required by law to answer a verdict form that made no mention whatsoever of mitigation evidence. And just as in Penry II, the burden of proof on the State was tied by law to findings of deliberateness and future dangerousness that had little, if anything, to do with the mitigation evidence petitioner presented.[Footnote 5] Even if we were to assume that the jurors could easily and effectively have comprehended an orally delivered instruction directing them to disregard, in certain limited circumstances, a mandatory written instruction given at a later occasion, that would not change the fact that the \xe2\x80\x9cjury was essentially instructed to return a false answer to a special issue in order to avoid a death sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Penry II, supra, at 801.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no principled distinction, for Eighth Amendment purposes, between the instruction given to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s jury and the instruction in Penry II. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s evidence was relevant mitigation evidence for the jury under Tennard and Penry I. We therefore hold that the nullification instruction was constitutionally inadequate under Penry II. The judgment of the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
