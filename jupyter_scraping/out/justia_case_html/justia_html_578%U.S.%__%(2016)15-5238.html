\n

NICHOLS v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x935238.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 1, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 4, 2016

\n

The Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act (SORNA) makes it a federal crime for certain sex offenders to \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly fai[l] to register or update a registration,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72250(a)(3), and requires that offenders who move to a different State \xe2\x80\x9cshall, not later than 3 business days after each change of name, residence, employment, or student status,\xe2\x80\x9d inform in person \xe2\x80\x9cat least 1 jurisdiction involved pursuant to [42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa716913(a)] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of all changes\xe2\x80\x9d to required information, \xc2\xa716913(c). A \xc2\xa716913(a) jurisdiction is \xe2\x80\x9ceach jurisdiction where the offender resides, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is an employee, and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is a student.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Petitioner Nichols, a registered sex offender who moved from Kansas to the Philippines without updating his registration, was arrested, escorted to the United States, and charged with violating SORNA. After conditionally pleading guilty, Nichols argued on appeal that SORNA did not require him to update his registration in Kansas. The Tenth Circuit affirmed his conviction, holding that though Nichols left Kansas, the State remained a \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction involved\xe2\x80\x9d for SORNA purposes.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0SORNA did not require Nichols to update his registration in Kansas once he departed the State. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0SORNA\xe2\x80\x99s plain text dictates this holding. Critical here is \xc2\xa716913(a)\xe2\x80\x99s use of the present tense. Nichols once resided in Kansas, but after moving, he \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d in the Philippines. It follows that once Nichols moved, he was no longer required to appear in Kansas because it was no longer a \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction involved.\xe2\x80\x9d Nor was he required to appear in the Philippines, which is not a SORNA \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa716911(10). Section 16913(c)\xe2\x80\x99s requirements point to the same conclusion: Nichols could not have appeared in person in Kansas \xe2\x80\x9cafter\xe2\x80\x9d leaving the State. SORNA\xe2\x80\x99s drafters could have required sex offenders to deregister in their departure jurisdiction before leaving the country had that been their intent. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The Government resists this straightforward reading. It argues that a jurisdiction where an offender registers remains \xe2\x80\x9cinvolved\xe2\x80\x9d even after the offender leaves, but that would require adding the extra clause \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the offender appears on a registry\xe2\x80\x9d to \xc2\xa716913(a). Also unconvincing is the claim that \xc2\xa716914(a)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s requiring the offender to provide each address where he \xe2\x80\x9cwill reside\xe2\x80\x9d shows that SORNA contemplates the possibility of an offender\xe2\x80\x99s updating his registration before he actually moves. That provision merely lists the pieces of information to be updated; it says nothing about an obligation to update in the first place. Finally, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Nichols actually experienced two \xe2\x80\x9cchanges\xe2\x80\x9d of residence\xe2\x80\x94first, when he turned in his apartment keys in Kansas, and second, when he checked into his Manila hotel\xe2\x80\x94is inconsistent with ordinary English usage. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Although \xe2\x80\x9cthe most formidable argument concerning the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purposes [cannot] overcome the clarity [found] in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text,\xe2\x80\x9d Kloeckner v. Solis, 568 U. S. ___, ___, n.\xc2\xa04, the Court is mindful of those purposes and notes that its interpretation is not likely to create deficiencies in SORNA\xe2\x80\x99s scheme. Recent legislation by Congress, as well as existing state-law registration requirements, offers reassurance that sex offenders will not be able to escape punishment for leaving the United States without notifying their departure jurisdictions. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

775 F.\xc2\xa03d 1225, reversed.

\n

Alito, J., delivered the opinion for a unanimous Court.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 15\xe2\x80\x935238

\n

LESTER RAY NICHOLS, PETITIONER v.UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

[April 4, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Lester Ray Nichols, a registered sex offender living in the Kansas City area, moved to the Philippines without notifying Kansas authorities of his change in residence. For that omission Nichols was convicted of failing to update his sex-offender registration, in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72250(a). We must decide whether federal law required Nichols to update his registration in Kansas to reflect his departure from the State.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Following the high-profile and horrific rape and murder of 7-year-old Megan Kanka by her neighbor, States in the early 1990\xe2\x80\x99s began enacting registry and community-notification laws to monitor the whereabouts of individuals previously convicted of sex crimes. See Smith v. Doe,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 84,\n89 (2003)\n; Filler, Making the Case for Megan\xe2\x80\x99s Law, 76 Ind. L.\xc2\xa0J. 315, 315\xe2\x80\x93317 (2001). Congress followed suit in 1994 with the Jacob Wetterling Crimes Against Children and Sexually Violent Offender Registration Act,\n\n108Stat.\n2038,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa714071 et\xc2\xa0seq. (1994 ed.). Named after an 11-year-old who was kidnapped at gunpoint in 1989 (and who remains missing today), the Wetterling Act conditioned federal funds on States\xe2\x80\x99 enacting sex-offender registry laws meeting certain minimum standards. Smith, 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 89\xe2\x80\x9390. \xe2\x80\x9cBy 1996, every State, the District of Columbia, and the Federal Government had enacted some variation of\xe2\x80\x9d a sex-offender registry. Id., at 90.

\n

In 2006, Congress replaced the Wetterling Act with the Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act (SORNA),\n\n120Stat.\n590,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa716901 et\xc2\xa0seq. Two changes are pertinent here. First, Congress made it a federal crime for a sex offender who meets certain requirements to \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly fai[l] to register or update a registration as required by [SORNA].\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72250(a)(3); see Carr v. United States,\n\n560 U.\xc2\xa0S. 438\n\xe2\x80\x93442 (2010). Second, Congress amended the provisions governing the registration requirements when an offender moves to a different State. The original Wetterling Act had directed States to require a sex offender to \xe2\x80\x9cregister the new address with a designated law enforcement agency in another State to which the person moves not later than 10 days after such person establishes residence in the new State, if the new State has a registration requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa714071(b)(5) (1994 ed.) (emphasis added). Congress later amended this provision to direct States to require a sex offender to \xe2\x80\x9creport the change of address to the responsible agency in the State the person is leaving, and [to] comply with any registration requirement in the new State of residence.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa714071(b)(5) (2000 ed.) (emphasis added).

\n

SORNA repealed this provision of the Wetterling Act.\n\n120Stat.\n600. In its place, federal law now provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA sex offender shall, not later than 3 business days after each change of name, residence, employment, or student status, appear in person in at least 1 jurisdiction involved pursuant to subsection (a) and inform that jurisdiction of all changes in the information required for that offender in the sex offender registry.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa716913(c) (emphasis added).

\n

Subsection (a), in turn, provides: \xe2\x80\x9cA sex offender shall register, and keep the registration current, in each jurisdiction where the offender resides, where the offender is an employee, and where the offender is a student.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa716913(a). A sex offender is required to notify only one \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction involved\xe2\x80\x9d; that jurisdiction must then notify a list of interested parties, including the other jurisdictions. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa716921(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(7). The question presented in this case is whether the State a sex offender leaves\xe2\x80\x94that is, the State where he formerly resided\xe2\x80\x94qualifies as an \xe2\x80\x9cinvolved\xe2\x80\x9d jurisdiction under \xc2\xa716913.

\n

B

\n

In 2003, Nichols was convicted of traveling with intent to engage in illicit sexual conduct with a minor, in violation of\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72423(b). Although his offense predated SORNA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, Nichols was nevertheless required upon his eventual release in December 2011 to register as a sex offender in Kansas, where he chose to settle. 28 CFR 72.3 (2015). Nichols complied with SORNA\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirements\xe2\x80\x94until November 9, 2012, when he abruptly disconnected all of his telephone lines, deposited his apartment keys in his landlord\xe2\x80\x99s drop-box, and boarded a flight to Manila. When Nichols was a no-show at mandatory sex-offender treatment, a warrant was issued revoking his supervised release. With the assistance of American security forces, local police in Manila arrested Nichols in December 2012, and federal marshals then escorted him back to the United States, where he was charged with one count of \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly fail[ing] to register or update a registration as required by [SORNA],\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72250(a)(3). After unsuccessfully moving to dismiss the indictment on the ground that SORNA did not require him to update his registration in Kansas, Nichols conditionally pleaded guilty, reserving his right to appeal the denial of his motion.

\n

The Tenth Circuit affirmed. 775 F.\xc2\xa03d 1225 (2014). Following its own precedent in United States v. Murphy, 664 F.\xc2\xa03d 798 (2011), the panel held that when a sex offender \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98leaves a residence in a state, and then leaves the state entirely, that state remains a jurisdiction involved\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa716913. 775 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1229. Over four dissenting votes, the court denied Nichols\xe2\x80\x99s petition for rehearing en banc. 784 F.\xc2\xa03d 666 (2015). In adhering to Murphy, the Tenth Circuit reentrenched a split created by the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in United States v. Lunsford, 725 F.\xc2\xa03d 859 (2013). Remarkably, Lunsford also involved a sex offender who moved from the Kansas City area\xe2\x80\x94on the Missouri side\xe2\x80\x94to the Philippines. Contra the Tenth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision below, Lunsford held that that defendant had no obligation to update his registration in Missouri because a sex offender is required \xe2\x80\x9cto \xe2\x80\x98keep the registration current\xe2\x80\x99 in the jurisdiction where he \xe2\x80\x98resides,\xe2\x80\x99 not a jurisdiction where he \xe2\x80\x98resided.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 861 (citation omitted). We granted certiorari to resolve the split. 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

II

\n

As noted, Nichols was required to \xe2\x80\x9cappear in person in at least 1 jurisdiction involved pursuant to subsection (a) and inform that jurisdiction of\xe2\x80\x9d his change of residence.\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa716913(c). Subsection (a) mentions three possible jurisdictions: \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the offender resides, where the offender is an employee, and where the offender is a student.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa716913(a). The Philippines is not a \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d under SORNA; no foreign country is. See \xc2\xa716911(10). Putting these provisions together, SORNA therefore requires a sex offender who changes his residence to appear, within three business days of the change, in person in at least one jurisdiction (but not a foreign country) where he resides, works, or studies, and to inform that jurisdiction of the address change. Critically, \xc2\xa716913(a) uses only the present tense: \xe2\x80\x9cresides,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cis an employee,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cis a student.\xe2\x80\x9d A person who moves from Leavenworth to Manila no longer \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d (present tense) in Kansas; although he once resided in Kansas, after his move he \xe2\x80\x9cresides\xe2\x80\x9d in the Philippines. It follows that once Nichols moved to Manila, he was no longer required to appear in person in Kansas to update his registration, for Kansas was no longer a \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction involved pursuant to subsection (a)\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa716913.

\n

The requirement in \xc2\xa716913(c) to appear in person and register \xe2\x80\x9cnot later than 3 business days after each change of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. residence\xe2\x80\x9d points to the same conclusion. Nichols could not have appeared in person in Kansas \xe2\x80\x9cafter\xe2\x80\x9d leaving the State. To be sure, one may argue that the day before his departure was \xe2\x80\x9cnot later than 3 business days after\xe2\x80\x9d his departure, but no one in ordinary speech uses language in such a strained and hypertechnical way.

\n

If the drafters of SORNA had thought about the problem of sex offenders who leave the country and had sought to require them to (de)register in the departure jurisdiction, they could easily have said so; indeed, that is exactly what the amended Wetterling Act had required.\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa714071(b)(5) (2000 ed.) (\xe2\x80\x9creport the change of address to the responsible agency in the State the person is leaving\xe2\x80\x9d). It is also what Kansas state law requires: Nichols had a duty to notify, among other entities, \xe2\x80\x9cthe registering law enforcement agency or agencies where last registered.\xe2\x80\x9d Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa722\xe2\x80\x934905(g) (2014 Cum. Supp.) (emphasis added). Congress could have chosen to retain the language in the amended Wetterling Act, or to adopt locution similar to that of the Kansas statute (and echoed in the statutes of many other States, cf. Brief for Petitioner 6, n.\xc2\xa01). It did neither. SORNA\xe2\x80\x99s plain text\xe2\x80\x94in particular, \xc2\xa716913(a)\xe2\x80\x99s consistent use of the present tense\xe2\x80\x94therefore did not require Nichols to update his registration in Kansas once he no longer resided there.

\n

III

\n

The Government resists this straightforward reading of the statutory text, arguing instead that once an offender registers in a jurisdiction, \xe2\x80\x9cthat jurisdiction necessarily remains \xe2\x80\x98involved pursuant to subsection (a),\xe2\x80\x99 because the offender continues to appear on its registry as a current resident.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 24. But \xc2\xa716913(a) lists only three possibilities for an \xe2\x80\x9cinvolved\xe2\x80\x9d jurisdiction: \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the offender resides, where the offender is an employee, and where the offender is a student.\xe2\x80\x9d Notably absent is \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the offender appears on a registry.\xe2\x80\x9d We decline the Government\xe2\x80\x99s invitation to add an extra clause to the text of \xc2\xa716913(a). As we long ago remarked in another context, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hat the government asks is not a construction of a statute, but, in effect, an enlargement of it by the court, so that what was omitted, presumably by inadvertence, may be included within its scope. To supply omissions transcends the judicial function.\xe2\x80\x9d Iselin v. United States,\n\n270 U.\xc2\xa0S. 245,\n251 (1926)\n. Just so here.

\n

Relatedly, the Government points out that among the pieces of information a sex offender must provide as part of his registration is \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he address of each residence at which the sex offender resides or will reside.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa716914(a)(3) (emphasis added). The use of the future tense, says the Government, shows that SORNA contemplates the possibility of an offender\xe2\x80\x99s updating his registration before actually moving. But \xc2\xa716914(a) merely lists the pieces of information that a sex offender must provide if and when he updates his registration; it says nothing about whether the offender has an obligation to update his registration in the first place.

\n

Finally, the Government argues that Nichols actually experienced not one but two \xe2\x80\x9cchanges\xe2\x80\x9d of residence\xe2\x80\x94the first when he \xe2\x80\x9cabandoned\xe2\x80\x9d his apartment in Leavenworth by turning in his keys, and the second when he checked into his hotel in Manila. On the Government\xe2\x80\x99s view, a sex offender\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cresidence information will change when he leaves the place where he has been residing, and it will change again when he arrives at his new residence. He must report both of those changes in a timely fashion.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 21. We think this argument too clever by half; when someone moves from, say, Kansas City, Kansas, to Kansas City, Missouri, we ordinarily would not say he moved twice: once from Kansas City, Kansas, to a state of homelessness, and then again from homelessness to Kansas City, Missouri. Nor, were he to drive an RV between the cities, would we say that he changed his residence four times (from the house on the Kansas side of the Missouri River to a state of homelessness when he locks the door behind him; then to the RV when he climbs into the vehicle; then back to homelessness when he alights in the new house\xe2\x80\x99s driveway; and then, finally, to the new house in Missouri). And what if he were to move from Kansas to California and spend several nights in hotels along the way? Such ponderings cannot be the basis for imposing criminal punishment. \xe2\x80\x9cWe interpret criminal statutes, like other statutes, in a manner consistent with ordinary English usage.\xe2\x80\x9d Abramski v. United States, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (Scalia, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 4); Flores-Figueroa v. United States,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 646,\n652 (2009)\n. In ordinary English, Nichols changed his residence just once: from Kansas to the Philippines.

\n

We are mindful that SORNA\xe2\x80\x99s purpose was to \xe2\x80\x9cmake more uniform what had remained \xe2\x80\x98a patchwork of federal and 50 individual state registration systems,\xe2\x80\x99 with \xe2\x80\x98loopholes and deficiencies\xe2\x80\x99 that had resulted in an estimated 100,000 sex offenders becoming \xe2\x80\x98missing\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98lost.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Kebodeaux, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2013) (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312) (citation omitted). Yet \xe2\x80\x9ceven the most formidable argument concerning the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purposes couldnot overcome the clarity we find in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text.\xe2\x80\x9d Kloeckner v. Solis, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, n.\xc2\xa04 (2012) (slip op., at 14, n.\xc2\xa04).

\n

Our interpretation of the SORNA provisions at issue in this case in no way means that sex offenders will be able to escape punishment for leaving the United States without notifying the jurisdictions in which they lived while in this country. Congress has recently criminalized the \xe2\x80\x9cknowin[g] fail[ure] to provide information required by [SORNA] relating to intended travel in foreign commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d International Megan\xe2\x80\x99s Law to Prevent Child Exploitation and Other Sexual Crimes Through Advanced Notification of Traveling Sex Offenders, Pub. L. 114\xe2\x80\x93119, \xc2\xa76(b)(2),\n\n130Stat.\n23, to be codified at\n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72250(b). Such information includes \xe2\x80\x9canticipated dates and places of departure, arrival, or return[;] carrier and flight numbers for air travel[;] destination country and address or other contact information therein,\xe2\x80\x9d et cetera. \xc2\xa76(a)(1)(B),\n\n130Stat.\n22, to be codified at\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa716914(a)(7). Both parties agree that the new law captures Nichols\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. Supp. Brief for United States 3; Reply Brief 10; Tr. of Oral Arg. 18, 35. And, of course, Nichols\xe2\x80\x99s failure to update his registration in Kansas violated state law. Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa722\xe2\x80\x934905(g). We are thus reassured that our holding today is not likely to create \xe2\x80\x9cloopholes and deficiencies\xe2\x80\x9d in SORNA\xe2\x80\x99s nationwide sex-offender registration scheme.

The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit is reversed.
