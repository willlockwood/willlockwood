GREENLAW v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93330.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 15, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 23, 2008

\n

Petitioner Greenlaw was convicted of seven drug and firearms charges and was sentenced to imprisonment for 442 months. In calculating this sentence, the District Court made an error. Overlooking this Court\xe2\x80\x99s controlling decision in Deal v. United States, 508 U. S. 129, 132\xe2\x80\x93137, interpreting 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(C)(i), and over the Government\xe2\x80\x99s objection, the District Court imposed a 10-year sentence on a count that carried a 25-year mandatory minimum term. Greenlaw appealed urging, inter alia, that the appropriate sentence for all his convictions was 15 years. The Government neither appealed nor cross-appealed. The Eighth Circuit found no merit in any of Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s arguments, but went on to consider whether his sentence was too low. The court acknowledged that the Government, while it had objected to the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s error at sentencing, had elected not to seek alteration of Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence on appeal. Nonetheless, relying on the \xe2\x80\x9cplain-error rule\xe2\x80\x9d stated in Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 52(b), the Court of Appeals ordered the District Court to enlarge Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence by 15 years, yielding a total prison term of 662 months.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Absent a Government appeal or cross-appeal, the Eighth Circuit could not, on its own initiative, order an increase in Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0In both civil and criminal cases, in the first instance and on appeal, courts follow the principle of party presentation, i.e., the parties frame the issues for decision and the courts generally serve as neutral arbiters of matters the parties present. To the extent courts have approved departures from the party presentation principle in criminal cases, the justification has usually been to protect a pro se litigant\xe2\x80\x99s rights. See Castro v. United States, 540 U. S. 375, 381\xe2\x80\x93383. The cross-appeal rule, pivotal in this case, is both informed by, and illustrative of, the party presentation principle. Under that rule, it takes a cross-appeal to justify a remedy in favor of an appellee. See McDonough v. Dannery, 3 Dall. 188. This Court has called the rule \xe2\x80\x9cinveterate and certain,\xe2\x80\x9d Morley Constr. Co. v. Maryland Casualty Co., 300 U. S. 185, 191, and has in no case ordered an exception to it, El Paso Natural Gas Co. v. Neztsosie, 526 U. S. 473, 480. No exception is warranted here. Congress has specified that when a United States Attorney files a notice of appeal with respect to a criminal sentence, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Government may not further prosecute [the] appeal without the personal approval of the Attorney General, the Solicitor General, or a deputy solicitor general designated by the Solicitor General.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S. C. \xc2\xa73742(b). This provision gives the top representatives of the United States in litigation the prerogative to seek or forgo appellate correction of sentencing errors, however plain they may be. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Eighth Circuit held that the plain-error rule, Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 52(b), authorized it to order the sentence enhancement sU. S.onte. Nothing in the text or history of Rule\xc2\xa052(b), or in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions, suggests that the plain-error rule was meant to override the cross-appeal requirement. In every case in which correction of a plain error would result in modifying a judgment to the advantage of a party who did not seek this Court\xe2\x80\x99s review, the Court has invoked the cross-appeal rule to bar the correction. See, e.g., Chittenden v. Brewster, 2 Wall. 191; Strunk v. United States, 412 U. S. 434. Even if it would be proper for an appeals court to initiate plain-error review in some cases, sentencing errors that the Government has refrained from pursuing would not fit the bill. In \xc2\xa73742(b), Congress assigned to leading Department of Justice officers responsibility for determining when Government pursuit of a sentencing appeal is in order. Rule 52(b) does not invite appellate court interference with the assessment of those officers. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Amicus curiae, invited by the Court to brief and argue the case in support of the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment, links argument based on Rule\xc2\xa052(b) to similar argument based on 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72106. For substantially the same reasons that Rule\xc2\xa052(b) does not override the cross-appeal rule, \xc2\xa72106 does not do so either. P.\xc2\xa010.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Amicus also argues that 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73742, which governs appellate review of criminal sentences, overrides the cross-appeal rule for sentences \xe2\x80\x9cimposed in violation of law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73742(e). Amicus\xe2\x80\x99 construction of \xc2\xa73742 is novel and complex, but ultimately unpersuasive. At the time \xc2\xa73742 was enacted, the cross-appeal rule was a solidly grounded rule of appellate practice. Congress had crafted explicit exceptions to the cross-appeal rule in earlier statutes governing sentencing appeals, i.e., the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970 and the Controlled Substances Act of 1970. When Congress repealed those exceptions and enacted \xc2\xa73742, it did not similarly express in the text of \xc2\xa73742 any exception to the cross-appeal rule. This drafting history suggests that Congress was aware of the cross-appeal rule and framed \xc2\xa73742 expecting that the new provision would operate in harmony with it. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(e)\xc2\xa0In increasing Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence sU. S.onte, the Eighth Circuit did not advert to the procedural rules setting firm deadlines for launching appeals and cross-appeals. See Fed. Rules App. Proc. 3(a)(1), 4(b)(1)(B)(ii), 4(b)(4), 26(b). The strict time limits on notices of appeal and cross-appeal serve, as the cross-appeal rule does, the interests of the parties and the legal system in fair warning and finality. The time limits would be undermined if an appeals court could modify a judgment in favor of a party who filed no notice of appeal. In a criminal prosecution, moreover, the defendant would appeal at his peril, with nothing to alert him that, on his own appeal, his sentence would be increased until the appeals court so decreed. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(f)\xc2\xa0Nothing in this opinion requires courts to modify their current practice in \xe2\x80\x9csentencing package cases\xe2\x80\x9d involving multicount indictments and a successful attack on some but not all of the counts of conviction. The appeals court, in such cases, may vacate the entire sentence on all counts so that the trial court can reconfigure the sentencing plan. On remand, trial courts have imposed a sentence on the remaining counts longer than the sentence originally imposed on those particular counts, but yielding an aggregate sentence no longer than the aggregate sentence initially imposed. This practice is not at odds with the cross-appeal rule, which stops appellate judges from adding years to a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence on their own initiative. In any event, this is not a \xe2\x80\x9csentencing package\xe2\x80\x9d case. Greenlaw was unsuccessful on all his appellate issues. The Eighth Circuit, therefore, had no occasion to vacate his sentence and no warrant, in the absence of a cross-appeal, to order the addition of 15 years to his sentence. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

481 F.\xc2\xa03d 601, vacated and remanded.

\n554 U. S. ____ (2008)

\n

MICHAEL GREENLAW, aka MIKEY, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

[June 23, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns the role of courts in our adversarial system. The specific question presented: May a United States Court of Appeals, acting on its own initiative, order an increase in a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence? Petitioner Michael J. Greenlaw was convicted of various offenses relating to drugs and firearms, and was sentenced to imprisonment for 442 months. He appealed urging, inter alia, that his sentence was unreasonably long. After rejecting all of Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s arguments, the Court of Appeals determined, without Government invitation, that the applicable law plainly required a prison sentence 15 years longer than the term the trial court had imposed. Accordingly, the appeals court instructed the trial court to increase Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence to 622 months. We hold that, absent a Government appeal or cross-appeal, the sentence Greenlaw received should not have been increased. We therefore vacate the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Greenlaw was a member of a gang that, for years, controlled the sale of crack cocaine in a southside Minneapolis neighborhood. See United States v. Carter, 481 F.\xc2\xa03d 601, 604 (CA8 2007) (case below). To protect their drug stash and to prevent rival dealers from moving into their territory, gang members carried and concealed numerous weapons. See id., at\xc2\xa0605. For his part in the operation, Greenlaw was charged, in the United\xc2\xa0States District Court for the District of Minnesota, with eight offenses; after trial, he was found guilty on seven of the charges. App.\xc2\xa0to Pet. for Cert. 16a\xe2\x80\x9317a.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Among Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s convictions were two for violating 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A), which prohibits carrying a firearm during and in relation to a crime of violence or a drug trafficking crime: His first \xc2\xa7924(c) conviction was for carrying a firearm in connection with a crime committed in 1998; his second, for both carrying and discharging a firearm in connection with a crime committed in 1999. App.\xc2\xa0to Pet. for Cert. 17a. A first conviction for violating \xc2\xa7924(c) carries a mandatory minimum term of 5 years, if the firearm is simply carried. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)(i). If the firearm is also discharged, the mandatory minimum increases to 10 years. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)(iii). For \xe2\x80\x9ca second or subsequent conviction,\xe2\x80\x9d however, whether the weapon is only carried or discharged as well, the mandatory minimum jumps to 25 years. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(C)(i). Any sentence for violating \xc2\xa7924(c), moreover, must run consecutively to \xe2\x80\x9cany other term of imprisonment,\xe2\x80\x9d including any other conviction under \xc2\xa7924(c). \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(D)(ii).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At sentencing, the District Court made an error. Over the Government\xe2\x80\x99s objection, the court held that a \xc2\xa7924(c) conviction does not count as \xe2\x80\x9csecond or subsequent\xe2\x80\x9d when it is \xe2\x80\x9ccharged in the same indictment\xe2\x80\x9d as the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s first \xc2\xa7924(c) conviction. App.\xc2\xa059, 61\xe2\x80\x9362. The error was plain because this Court had held, in Deal v. United States, 508 U. S. 129 (1993), that when a defendant is charged in the same indictment with more than one offense qualifying for punishment under \xc2\xa7924(c), all convictions after the first rank as \xe2\x80\x9csecond or subsequent,\xe2\x80\x9d see id., at 132\xe2\x80\x93137.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As determined by the District Court, Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence included 262 months (without separately counting sentences that ran concurrently) for all his convictions other than the two under \xc2\xa7924(c). For the first \xc2\xa7924(c) offense, the court imposed a 5-year sentence in accord with \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)(i). As to the second \xc2\xa7924(c) conviction, the District Court rejected the Government\xe2\x80\x99s request for the 25-year minimum prescribed in \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(C) for \xe2\x80\x9csecond or subsequent\xe2\x80\x9d offenses; instead, it imposed the 10-year term prescribed in \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)(iii) for first-time offenses.[Footnote 1] The total sentence thus calculated came to 442 months.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Greenlaw appealed to the United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit, urging, inter alia, that the appropriate total sentence for all his crimes was 15 years. See 481 F.\xc2\xa03d, at\xc2\xa0607. The Court of Appeals found no merit in any of Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s arguments. Id., at\xc2\xa0606\xe2\x80\x93607. Although the Government did not appeal or cross-appeal, id., at\xc2\xa0608, it did note, on brief and at oral argument, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s error: Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence should have been 15 years longer than the 442 months imposed by the District Court, the Government observed, because his second \xc2\xa7924(c) conviction called for a 25-year (not a 10-year) mandatory minimum consecutive sentence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government made the observation that the sentence was 15 years too short only to counter Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s argument that it was unreasonably long. See App. 84\xe2\x80\x9386; Recording of Oral Arg. in United States v. Carter, No.\xc2\xa005\xe2\x80\x933391, (CA8, Sept. 26, 2006), at\xc2\xa016:53\xe2\x80\x9319:04, available at http://www.ca8.uscourts.gov/oralargs/oaFrame.html (as visited June 13, 2008). Having refrained from seeking correction of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s error by pursuing its own appeal, the Government simply urged that Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence should be affirmed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals acknowledged that the Government, while objecting at sentencing to the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous reading of \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(C), had elected to seek no appellate court alteration of Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. 481 F.\xc2\xa03d, at\xc2\xa0608. Relying on the \xe2\x80\x9cplain-error rule\xe2\x80\x9d stated in Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 52(b), however, the appeals court held that it had discretion to raise and correct the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s error on its own initiative. 481 F.\xc2\xa03d, at\xc2\xa0608\xe2\x80\x93609. The Court of Appeals therefore vacated the sentence and instructed the District Court \xe2\x80\x9cto impose the [statutorily mandated] consecutive minimum sentence of 25 years.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 611.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioning for rehearing and rehearing en banc, Greenlaw asked the Eighth Circuit to adopt the position advanced by the Seventh Circuit in United States v. Rivera, 411 F.\xc2\xa03d 864 (2005). App. 95. \xe2\x80\x9cBy deciding not to take a cross-appeal,\xe2\x80\x9d the Seventh Circuit stated, \xe2\x80\x9cthe United States has ensured that [the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] sentence cannot be increased.\xe2\x80\x9d 411 F.\xc2\xa03d, at\xc2\xa0867. The Eighth Circuit denied rehearing without an opinion. App. to Pet. for Cert. 28a. On remand, as instructed by the Court of Appeals, the District Court increased Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence by 15 years, yielding a total prison term of 622 months. App. 103\xe2\x80\x93104, 109.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Greenlaw petitioned for certiorari noting a division among the Circuits on this question: When a defendant unsuccessfully challenges his sentence as too high, may a court of appeals, on its own initiative, increase the sentence absent a cross-appeal by the Government? In response, the Government \xe2\x80\x9cagree[d] with [Greenlaw] that the court of appeals erred in sU. S.onte remanding the case with directions to enhance petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief in Opposition 12. We granted review and invited Jay T. Jorgensen to brief and argue this case, as amicus curiae, in support of the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment. 552 U. S. ___ (2008). Mr. Jorgensen accepted the appointment and has well fulfilled his assigned responsibility.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In our adversary system, in both civil and criminal cases, in the first instance and on appeal, we follow the principle of party presentation. That is, we rely on the parties to frame the issues for decision and assign to courts the role of neutral arbiter of matters the parties present. To the extent courts have approved departures from the party presentation principle in criminal cases, the justification has usually been to protect a pro se litigant\xe2\x80\x99s rights. See Castro v. United States, 540 U. S. 375, 381\xe2\x80\x93383 (2003).[Footnote 2] But as a general rule, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ur adversary system is designed around the premise that the parties know what is best for them, and are responsible for advancing the facts and arguments entitling them to relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 386 (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment).[Footnote 3] As cogently explained:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Courts] do not, or should not, sally forth each day looking for wrongs to right. We wait for cases to come to us, and when they do we normally decide only questions presented by the parties. Counsel almost always know a great deal more about their cases than we do, and this must be particularly true of counsel for the United States, the richest, most powerful, and best represented litigant to appear before us.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Samuels, 808 F.\xc2\xa02d 1298, 1301 (CA8 1987) (R. Arnold, J., concurring in denial of reh\xe2\x80\x99g en banc).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The cross-appeal rule, pivotal in this case, is both informed by, and illustrative of, the party presentation principle. Under that unwritten but longstanding rule, an appellate court may not alter a judgment to benefit a nonappealing party. This Court, from its earliest years, has recognized that it takes a cross-appeal to justify a remedy in favor of an appellee. See McDonough v. Dannery, 3 Dall. 188, 198 (1796). We have called the rule \xe2\x80\x9cinveterate and certain.\xe2\x80\x9d Morley Constr. Co. v. Maryland Casualty Co., 300 U. S. 185, 191 (1937).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Courts of Appeals have disagreed, however, on the proper characterization of the cross-appeal rule: Is it \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore exceptionless, or a \xe2\x80\x9crule of practice,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus potentially subject to judicially created exceptions? Compare, e.g., Johnson v. Teamsters Local 559, 102 F.\xc2\xa03d 21, 28\xe2\x80\x9329 (CA1 1996) (cross-appeal rule \xe2\x80\x9cis mandatory and jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d), with, e.g., American Roll-On Roll-Off Carrier, LLC v. P & O Ports Baltimore, Inc., 479 F.\xc2\xa03d 288, 295\xe2\x80\x93296 (CA4 2007) (\xe2\x80\x9ccross-appeal requirement [is] one of practice, [not] a strict jurisdictional requirement\xe2\x80\x9d). Our own opinions contain statements supporting both characterizations. Compare, e.g., Morley Constr. Co., 300 U. S., at\xc2\xa0187 (cross-appeal rule defines \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he power of an appellate court to modify a decree\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)), with, e.g., Langnes v. Green, 282 U. S. 531, 538 (1931) (cross-appeal requirement is \xe2\x80\x9ca rule of practice which generally has been followed\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In El Paso Natural Gas Co. v. Neztsosie, 526 U. S. 473, 480 (1999), we declined to decide \xe2\x80\x9cthe theoretical status\xe2\x80\x9d of the cross-appeal rule. It sufficed to point out that the rule was \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly entrenched\xe2\x80\x9d and served to advance \xe2\x80\x9cinstitutional interests in fair notice and repose.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cIndeed,\xe2\x80\x9d we noted, \xe2\x80\x9cin more than two centuries of repeatedly endorsing the cross-appeal requirement, not a single one of our holdings has ever recognized an exception to the rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Following the approach taken in Neztsosie, we again need not type the rule \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d in order to decide this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress has eased our decision by specifying the instances in which the Government may seek appellate review of a sentence, and then adding this clear instruction: Even when a United States Attorney files a notice of appeal with respect to a sentence qualifying for review, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Government may not further prosecute [the] appeal without the personal approval of the Attorney General, the Solicitor General, or a deputy solicitor general designated by the Solicitor General.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73742(b). Congress thus entrusted to named high-ranking officials within the Department of Justice responsibility for determining whether the Government, on behalf of the public, should seek a sentence higher than the one imposed. It would severely undermine Congress\xe2\x80\x99 instruction were appellate judges to \xe2\x80\x9csally forth\xe2\x80\x9d on their own motion, cf. supra, at\xc2\xa05, to take up errors adverse to the Government when the designated Department of Justice officials have not authorized an appeal from the sentence the trial court imposed.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Executive Branch has exclusive authority and absolute discretion to decide whether to prosecute a case.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Nixon, 418 U. S. 683, 693 (1974). We need not decide whether comparable authority and discretion are lodged in the Executive Branch with respect to the pursuit of issues on appeal. We need only recognize that Congress, in \xc2\xa73742(b), has accorded to the top representatives of the United States in litigation the prerogative to seek or forgo appellate correction of sentencing errors, however plain they may be. That measure should garner the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s full respect.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In ordering the District Court to add 15 years to Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence, despite the absence of a cross-appeal by the Government, the Court of Appeals identified Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 52(b) as the source of its authority. See 481 F.\xc2\xa03d, at\xc2\xa0608\xe2\x80\x93609, and n.\xc2\xa05. Rule 52(b) reads: \xe2\x80\x9cA plain error that affects substantial rights may be considered even though it was not brought to the court\xe2\x80\x99s attention.\xe2\x80\x9d Nothing in the text or history of Rule\xc2\xa052(b) suggests that the rulemakers, in codifying the plain-error doctrine, meant to override the cross-appeal requirement. See Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 52, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p.\xc2\xa01664 (describing Rule\xc2\xa052(b) as \xe2\x80\x9ca restatement of existing law\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor do our opinions support a plain-error exception to the cross-appeal rule. This Court has indeed noticed, and ordered correction of, plain errors not raised by defendants, but we have done so only to benefit a defendant who had himself petitioned the Court for review on other grounds. See, e.g., Silber v. United States, 370 U. S. 717 (1962) (per curiam). In no case have we applied plain-error doctrine to the detriment of a petitioning party. Rather, in every case in which correction of a plain error would result in modification of a judgment to the advantage of a party who did not seek this Court\xe2\x80\x99s review, we have invoked the cross-appeal rule to bar the correction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Chittenden v. Brewster, 2 Wall. 191 (1865), for example, the appellants asserted that an award entered in their favor was too small. A prior decision of this Court, however, made it plain that they were entitled to no award at all. See id., at\xc2\xa0195\xe2\x80\x93196 (citing Jones v. Green, 1 Wall. 330 (1864)). But because the appellee had not filed a cross-appeal, the Court left the award undisturbed. See 2 Wall., at\xc2\xa0196. Strunk v. United States, 412 U. S. 434 (1973), decided over a century later, is similarly illustrative. There, the Court of Appeals had determined that the defendant was denied his right to a speedy trial, but held that the proper remedy was reduction of his sentence as compensation for the delay, not dismissal of the charges against him. As petitioner in this Court, the defendant sought review of the remedial order. See id., at\xc2\xa0435. The Court suggested that there may have been no speedy trial violation, as \xe2\x80\x9cit seem[ed] clear that [the defendant] was responsible for a large part of the \xe2\x80\xa6 delay.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at\xc2\xa0436. But because the Government had not raised the issue by cross-petition, we considered the case on the premise that the defendant had been deprived of his Sixth Amendment right, id., at\xc2\xa0437, and ruled that dismissal of the indictment was the proper remedy, id., at\xc2\xa0439\xe2\x80\x93440.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if there might be circumstances in which it would be proper for an appellate court to initiate plain-error review, sentencing errors that the Government refrained from pursuing would not fit the bill. Heightening the generally applicable party presentation principle, Congress has provided a dispositive direction regarding sentencing errors that aggrieve the Government. In \xc2\xa73742(b), as earlier explained, see supra, at\xc2\xa07, Congress designated leading Department of Justice officers as the decisionmakers responsible for determining when Government pursuit of a sentencing appeal is in order. Those high officers, Congress recognized, are best equipped to determine where the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest lies. Rule\xc2\xa052(b) does not invite appellate court interference with their assessment.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Amicus supporting the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment links the argument based on Rule\xc2\xa052(b) to a similar argument based on 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72106. See Brief for Amicus Curiae by Invitation of the Court 40\xe2\x80\x9343 (hereinafter Jorgensen Brief). Section 2106 states that federal appellate courts \xe2\x80\x9cmay affirm, modify, vacate, set aside or reverse any judgment \xe2\x80\xa6 lawfully brought before it for review.\xe2\x80\x9d For substantially the same reasons that Rule\xc2\xa052(b) does not override the cross-appeal requirement, \xc2\xa72106 does not do so either. Section 2106 is not limited to plain errors, much less to sentencing errors in criminal cases\xe2\x80\x94it applies to all cases, civil and criminal, and to all errors. Were the construction amicus offers correct, \xc2\xa72106 would displace the cross-appeal rule cross-the-board. The authority described in \xc2\xa72106, we have observed, \xe2\x80\x9cmust be exercised consistent with the requirements of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure as interpreted by this Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Unitherm Food Systems, Inc. v. Swift-Eckrich, Inc., 546 U. S. 394, 402\xe2\x80\x93403, n.\xc2\xa04 (2006). No different conclusion is warranted with respect to the \xe2\x80\x9cinveterate and certain\xe2\x80\x9d cross-appeal rule. Morley Constr. Co., 300 U. S., at\xc2\xa0191.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In defending the Court of Appeals judgment, amicus places heavy weight on an argument pinned not to Rule\xc2\xa052(b) or 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72106, but to the text of 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73742, the Criminal Code provision governing appellate review of criminal sentences. As amicus reads \xc2\xa73742, once either party appeals a sentence, the Court of Appeals must remand \xe2\x80\x9cany illegal sentence regardless of whether the remand hurts or helps the appealing party.\xe2\x80\x9d Jorgensen Brief 9. Congress so directed, amicus argues, by instructing that, upon review of the record, a court of appeals \xe2\x80\x9cshall determine \xe2\x80\xa6 whether the sentence was imposed in violation of law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73742(e) (emphasis added), and \xe2\x80\x9cshall remand\xe2\x80\x9d if it so determines, \xc2\xa73742(f)(1) (2000 ed., Supp. V) (emphasis added). See Jorgensen Brief 10\xe2\x80\x9311, and n.\xc2\xa03.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Amicus makes a further text-based observation. He notes that \xc2\xa73742(f)(2)\xe2\x80\x94the provision covering sentences \xe2\x80\x9coutside the applicable [G]uideline range\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94calls for a remand only where a departure from the Federal Sentencing Guidelines harms the appellant. In contrast, amicus emphasizes, \xc2\xa73742(f)(1)\xe2\x80\x94the provision controlling sentences imposed \xe2\x80\x9cin violation of law\xe2\x80\x9d and Guideline application errors\xe2\x80\x94contains no such appellant-linked limitation. The inference amicus draws from this distinction is that Congress intended to override the cross-appeal rule for sentences controlled by \xc2\xa73742(f)(1), i.e., those imposed \xe2\x80\x9cin violation of law\xe2\x80\x9d (or incorrectly applying the Guidelines), but not for Guideline departure errors, the category covered by \xc2\xa73742(f)(2). See id., at\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This novel construction of \xc2\xa73742, presented for the first time in the brief amicus filed in this Court,[Footnote 5] is clever and complex, but ultimately unpersuasive. Congress enacted \xc2\xa73742 in 1984. See Sentencing Reform Act, \xc2\xa7213(a), 98 Stat. 2011. At that time, the cross-appeal requirement was a solidly grounded rule of appellate practice. See supra, at\xc2\xa06. The inference properly drawn, we think, is that Congress was aware of the cross-appeal rule, and framed \xc2\xa73742 expecting that the new provision would operate in harmony with the \xe2\x80\x9cinveterate and certain\xe2\x80\x9d bar to enlarging judgments in favor of an appellee who filed no cross-appeal. Cf. Astoria Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn. v. Solimino, 501 U. S. 104, 108 (1991) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress is understood to legislate against a background of common-law adjudicatory principles.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress indicated awareness of the cross-appeal rule in an earlier measure, the Organized Crime Control Act of 1970 (OCCA), Pub. L. 91\xe2\x80\x93452, 84 Stat. 922, which provided for review of sentences of \xe2\x80\x9cdangerous special offenders.\xe2\x80\x9d See \xc2\xa71001(a), id., at\xc2\xa0948\xe2\x80\x93951. For that Act, Congress crafted an explicit exception to the cross-appeal rule. It ordered that an appeal of a sentence taken by the Government \xe2\x80\x9cshall be deemed the taking of [an appeal] by the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at\xc2\xa0950. But the \xe2\x80\x9cdeeming\xe2\x80\x9d ran in only one direction: \xe2\x80\x9c[A] sentence may be made more severe\xe2\x80\x9d OCCA provided, \xe2\x80\x9conly on review \xe2\x80\xa6 taken by the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at\xc2\xa0950\xe2\x80\x93951.[Footnote 6] When Congress repealed this provision and, in \xc2\xa73742, broadly provided for appellate review of sentences, it did not similarly express in the new text any exception to the cross-appeal rule. In short, Congress formulated a precise exception to the cross-appeal rule when that was its intention. Notably, the exception Congress legislated did not expose a defendant to a higher sentence in response to his own appeal. Congress spoke plainly in the 1970 legislation, leaving nothing for a court to infer. We therefore see no reason to read the current statute in the inventive manner amicus proposes, inferring so much from so little.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Amicus\xe2\x80\x99 reading of \xc2\xa73742, moreover, would yield some strange results. We note two, in particular. Under his construction, \xc2\xa73742 would give with one hand what it takes away with the other: \xc2\xa73742(b) entrusts to certain Government officials the decision whether to appeal an illegally low sentence, see supra, at\xc2\xa07; but according to amicus, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73742(e) and (f) would instruct appellate courts to correct an error of that order on their own initiative, thereby trumping the officials\xe2\x80\x99 decision. We resist attributing to Congress an intention to render a statute so internally inconsistent. Cf. Western Air Lines, Inc. v. Board of Equalization of S.\xc2\xa0D., 480 U. S. 123, 133 (1987) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe illogical results of applying [a proffered] interpretation \xe2\x80\xa6 argue strongly against the conclusion that Congress intended th[o]se results[.]\xe2\x80\x9d). Further, the construction proposed by amicus would draw a puzzling distinction between incorrect applications of the Sentencing Guidelines, controlled by \xc2\xa73742(f)(1), and erroneous departures from the Guidelines, covered by \xc2\xa73742(f)(2). The latter would be subject to the cross-appeal rule, the former would not. We do not see why Congress would want to differentiate Guidelines decisions this way.[Footnote 7]

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In increasing Greenlaw\xe2\x80\x99s sentence by 15 years on its own initiative, the Eighth Circuit did not advert to the procedural rules setting deadlines for launching appeals and cross-appeals. Unyielding in character, these rules may be seen as auxiliary to the cross-appeal rule and the party presentation principle served by that rule. Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 3(a)(1) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n appeal permitted by law \xe2\x80\xa6 may be taken only by filing a notice of appeal \xe2\x80\xa6 within the [prescribed] time.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) Complementing Rule\xc2\xa03(a)(1), Rule 4(b)(1)(B)(ii) instructs that, when the Government has the right to cross-appeal in a criminal case, its notice \xe2\x80\x9cmust be filed \xe2\x80\xa6 within 30 days after \xe2\x80\xa6 the filing of a notice of appeal by any defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) The filing time for a notice of appeal or cross-appeal, Rule\xc2\xa04(b)(4) states, may be extended \xe2\x80\x9cfor a period not to exceed 30 days.\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 26(b) bars any extension beyond that time.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The firm deadlines set by the Appellate Rules advance the interests of the parties and the legal system in fair notice and finality. Thus a defendant who appeals but faces no cross-appeal can proceed anticipating that the appellate court will not enlarge his sentence. And if the Government files a cross-appeal, the defendant will have fair warning, well in advance of briefing and argument, that pursuit of his appeal exposes him to the risk of a higher sentence. Given early warning, he can tailor his arguments to take account of that risk. Or he can seek the Government\xe2\x80\x99s agreement to voluntary dismissal of the competing appeals, see Fed. Rule App. Proc. 42(b), before positions become hardened during the hours invested in preparing the case for appellate court consideration.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The strict time limits on notices of appeal and cross-appeal would be undermined, in both civil and criminal cases, if an appeals court could modify a judgment in favor of a party who filed no notice of appeal. In a criminal prosecution, moreover, the defendant would appeal at his peril, with nothing to alert him that, on his own appeal, his sentence would be increased until the appeals court so decreed. In this very case, Greenlaw might have made different strategic decisions had he known soon after filing his notice of appeal that he risked a 15-year increase in an already lengthy sentence.

\n

E

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We note that nothing we have said in this opinion requires courts to modify their current practice in so-called \xe2\x80\x9csentencing package cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Those cases typically involve multicount indictments and a successful attack by a defendant on some but not all of the counts of conviction. The appeals court, in such instances, may vacate the entire sentence on all counts so that, on remand, the trial court can reconfigure the sentencing plan to assure that it remains adequate to satisfy the sentencing factors in 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a) (2000 ed. and Supp. V). In remanded cases, the Government relates, trial courts have imposed a sentence on the remaining counts longer than the sentence originally imposed on those particular counts, but yielding an aggregate sentence no longer than the aggregate sentence initially imposed. See Brief for United States 23, n.\xc2\xa011 (citing, inter alia, United States v. Pimienta-Redondo, 874 F.\xc2\xa02d 9 (CA1 1989) (en banc)). Thus the defendant ultimately may gain nothing from his limited success on appeal, but he will also lose nothing, as he will serve no more time than the trial court originally ordered.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The practice the Government describes is not at odds with the cross-appeal rule, which stops appellate judges from adding years to a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence on their own initiative. It simply ensures that the sentence \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98will suit not merely the offense but the individual defendant.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Pimienta-Redondo, 874 F.\xc2\xa02d, at\xc2\xa014 (quoting Wasman v. United States, 468 U. S. 559, 564 (1984)). And the assessment will be made by the sentencing judge exercising discretion, not by an appellate panel ruling on an issue of law no party tendered to the court.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is not a \xe2\x80\x9csentencing package\xe2\x80\x9d case. Greenlaw was unsuccessful on all his appellate issues. There was no occasion for the Court of Appeals to vacate his sentence and no warrant, in the absence of a cross-appeal, to order the addition of 15 years to his sentence.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I respectfully dissent because I view the cross-appeal requirement as a rule of appellate practice. It is akin to the rule that courts invoke when they decline to consider arguments that the parties have not raised. Both rules rest on premises about the efficient use of judicial resources and the proper role of the tribunal in an adversary system. Both are sound and should generally be followed. But just as the courts have made them, the courts may make exceptions to them, and I do not understand why a reviewing court should enjoy less discretion to correct an error sua sponte than it enjoys to raise and address an argument sua sponte. Absent congressional direction to the contrary, and subject to our limited oversight as a supervisory court, we should entrust the decision to initiate error correction to the sound discretion of the courts of appeals.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before laying out my view in more detail, I must first address the question whether federal courts have subject-matter jurisdiction to enlarge an appellee\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in the absence of a cross-appeal. Because the Court would not recognize any exceptions to the cross-appeal requirement when the defendant appeals his sentence, it does not decide that question. See ante, at 7. I must confront it, though I do not regard it as a substantial question. The cross-appeal requirement seems to me a prime example of a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98rule of practice,\xe2\x80\x99 subject to exceptions, not an unqualified limit on the power of appellate courts.\xe2\x80\x9d El Paso Natural Gas Co. v. Neztsosie, 526 U. S. 473, 480 (1999). While a court should generally enforce the cross-appeal requirement, a departure from it would not divest the court of jurisdiction.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has never addressed whether an appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction to enlarge a judgment in favor of an appellee is contingent on a duly filed cross-appeal. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s contention that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ur own opinions contain statements supporting\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d characterization of the requirement, ante, at 6, relies on a misreading of that precedent. The Court may have previously characterized the cross-appeal requirement as limiting \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]he power of an appellate court to modify a decree,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (quoting Morley Constr. Co. v. Maryland Casualty Co., 300 U. S. 185, 187 (1937)), but it does not follow that jurisdiction is conditioned on a properly filed cross-appeal. A court may lack the power to do something for reasons other than want of jurisdiction, and a rule can be inflexible without being jurisdictional. See Eberhart v. United States, 546 U. S. 12, 19 (2005) (per curiam).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jurisdiction of the courts of appeals is fixed by Congress. See Bowles v. Russell, 551 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 6); Ankenbrandt v. Richards, 504 U. S. 689, 698 (1992) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[T]he judicial power of the United States \xe2\x80\xa6 is (except in enumerated instances, applicable exclusively to this Court) dependent for its distribution and organization, and for the modes of its exercise, entirely upon the action of Congress\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Cary v. Curtis, 3 How. 236, 245 (1845))). If Congress wants to withhold from the courts of appeals the power to decide questions that expand the rights of nonappealing parties, it may do so. See U. S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa71 (authorizing Congress to establish the lower courts and, by corollary, to fix their jurisdiction); Kontrick v. Ryan, 540 U. S. 443, 452 (2004) (\xe2\x80\x9cOnly Congress may determine a lower federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d). The jurisdictional question thus reduces to whether Congress intended to make a cross-appeal a condition precedent to the appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction to enlarge a judgment in favor of a nonappealing party.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As always with such questions, the text of the relevant statute provides the best evidence of congressional intent. The relevant statute in this case is 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73742 (2000 ed. and Supp.\xc2\xa0V). Section\xc2\xa03742(a) authorizes a criminal defendant to \xe2\x80\x9cfile a notice of appeal\xe2\x80\x9d to review a sentence that was, among other possibilities, \xe2\x80\x9cimposed in violation of law.\xe2\x80\x9d E.g., \xc2\xa73742(a)(1). Section 3742(b) provides parallel authority for the Government to \xe2\x80\x9cfile a notice of appeal\xe2\x80\x9d to review unlawful sentences. E.g., \xc2\xa73742(b)(1). The statute conditions the Government\xe2\x80\x99s authority to further prosecute its appeal on \xe2\x80\x9cthe personal approval of the Attorney General, the Solicitor General, or a deputy solicitor general designated by the Solicitor General.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73742(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nothing in this language remotely suggests that a court of appeals lacks subject-matter jurisdiction to increase a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence in the absence of a cross-appeal by the Government. In fact, the statute does not even mention cross-appeals. It separately authorizes either party to \xe2\x80\x9cfile a notice of appeal,\xe2\x80\x9d but it never suggests that the reviewing court\xe2\x80\x99s power is limited to correcting errors for the benefit of the appealing party. If anything, it suggests the opposite. Without qualifying the appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s power in any way, \xc2\xa73742(e) instructs the court to determine, among other things, whether the sentence was \xe2\x80\x9cimposed in violation of law.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73742(e)(1). And while \xc2\xa73742(f)(2) limits the action that a court of appeals can take depending on which party filed the appeal, compare \xc2\xa73742(f)(2)(A) (sentences set aside as \xe2\x80\x9ctoo high\xe2\x80\x9d if defendant filed) with \xc2\xa73742(f)(2)(B) (sentences set aside as \xe2\x80\x9ctoo low\xe2\x80\x9d if Government filed), no such limitation appears in \xc2\xa73742(f)(1). That paragraph requires a court of appeals simply to set aside any sentence \xe2\x80\x9cimposed in violation of law or imposed as a result of an incorrect application of the sentencing guidelines.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since a cross-appeal has no effect on the appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction, the cross-appeal requirement is best characterized as a rule of practice. It is a rule created by the courts to serve interests that are important to the Judiciary. The Court identifies two of these interests: notice to litigants and finality. Ante, at 14; see also Neztsosie, supra, at 480. One might add that the cross-appeal requirement also serves a third interest: the appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s interest in being adequately briefed on the issues that it decides. See Fed. Rule App. Proc.\xc2\xa028.1(c) and Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App., pp. 615\xe2\x80\x93616. Although these are substantial interests in the abstract, I question how well an inflexible cross-appeal requirement serves them.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notice. With respect to notice, the benefits of an unyielding cross-appeal requirement are insubstantial. When the Government files a notice of cross-appeal, the defendant is alerted to the possibility that his or her sentence may be increased as a result of the appellate decision. But if the cross-appeal rule is, as I would hold, a strong rule of practice that should be followed in all but exceptional instances, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s failure to file a notice of cross-appeal would mean in the vast majority of cases that the defendant thereafter ran little risk of an increased sentence. And the rare cases where that possibility arose would generally involve errors so plain that no conceivable response by the defendant could alter the result. It is not unreasonable to consider an appealing party to be on notice as to such serious errors of law in his favor. And while there may be rare cases in which the existence of such a legal error would come as a complete surprise to the defendant or in which argument from the parties would be of assistance to the court, the solution to such a problem is not to eliminate the courts of appeals\xe2\x80\x99 authority to correct egregious errors. Rather, the appropriate response is for the court of appeals to request supplemental briefing or\xe2\x80\x94if it deems that insufficient\xe2\x80\x94simply to refuse to exercise its authority. Cf. Irizarry v. United States, 553 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 9). In short, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding does not increase the substance of the notice that a defendant receives; it merely accelerates that notice by at most a few weeks in a very small number of cases.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court contends that \xe2\x80\x9c[g]iven early warning, [the defendant] can tailor his arguments to take account of [the risk of a higher sentence] \xe2\x80\xa6 [o]r he can seek the Government\xe2\x80\x99s agreement to voluntary dismissal of the competing appeals.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14 (citing Fed. Rule App. Proc.\xc2\xa042(b)). But the Court does not explain how a notice of cross-appeal, a boilerplate document, helps the defendant \xe2\x80\x9ctailor his arguments.\xe2\x80\x9d Whether the cross-appeal rule is ironclad, as the Court believes, or simply a strong rule of practice, a defendant who wishes to appeal his or her sentence is always free to seek the Government\xe2\x80\x99s commitment not to cross-appeal or to terminate a cross-appeal that the Government has already taken. Fed. Rule App. Proc. 42(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finality. An inflexible cross-appeal rule also does little to further the interest of the parties and the Judiciary in the finality of decisions. An appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to grant a nonappealing party additional relief does not interrupt a long, undisturbed slumber. The error\xe2\x80\x99s repose begins no earlier than the deadline for filing a cross-appeal, and it ends as soon as the reviewing court issues its opinion\xe2\x80\x94and often much sooner. Here, for example, the slumber was broken when the Government identified the error in its brief as appellee. See Brief for United States 5.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Orderly Briefing. I do not doubt that adversarial briefing improves the quality of appellate decisionmaking, but it hardly follows that appellate courts should be denied the authority to correct errors that seriously prejudice nonappealing parties. Under my interpretation of the cross-appeal rule, a court of appeals would not be obligated to address errors that are prejudicial to a nonappealing party; a court of appeals would merely have the authority to do so in appropriate cases. If a court of appeals noticed such an error and concluded that it was appropriate to address the issue, the court could, if it wished, order additional briefing. If, on the other hand, the court concluded that the issue was not adequately addressed by the briefs filed by the parties in the ordinary course and that additional briefing would interfere with the efficient administration of the court\xe2\x80\x99s work, the court would not be required to decide the issue. Therefore, I do not see how the courts of appeals\xe2\x80\x99 interest in orderly briefing is furthered by denying those courts the discretionary authority to address important issues that they find it appropriate to decide.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, the inflexible cross-appeal rule that the Court adopts may disserve the interest in judicial efficiency in some cases. For example, correcting an error that prejudiced a nonappealing defendant on direct review might obviate the need for a collateral attack. Cf. Granberry v. Greer, 481 U. S. 129, 134 (1987) (allowing the Court of Appeals to address the merits of an unexhausted habeas corpus petition if \xe2\x80\x9cthe interests of comity and federalism will be better served by addressing the merits forthwith [than] by requiring a series of additional state and district court proceedings before reviewing the merits of the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim\xe2\x80\x9d); Munaf v. Geren, 553 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 13) (recognizing \xe2\x80\x9coccasions \xe2\x80\xa6 when it is appropriate to proceed further and address the merits\xe2\x80\x9d of a habeas corpus petition rather than reverse and remand on threshold matters). Because the reviewing court is in the best position to decide whether a departure from the cross-appeal rule would be efficient, rigid enforcement of that rule is more likely to waste judicial resources than to conserve them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the Court exaggerates the interests served by the cross-appeal requirement. At the same time, it overlooks an important interest that the rule disserves: the interest of the Judiciary and the public in correcting grossly prejudicial errors of law that undermine confidence in our legal system. We have repeatedly stressed the importance of that interest, see, e.g., United States v. Olano, 507 U. S. 725, 736\xe2\x80\x93737 (1993); Press-Enterprise Co. v. Superior Court of Cal., Riverside Cty., 464 U. S. 501, 507 (1984); New York Central R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Johnson, 279 U. S. 310, 318 (1929), and it has justified departures from our traditional adversary framework in other contexts. The Court mentions one of those contexts, see ante, at 5 (pro se litigation), but there are others that deserve mention.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The most well-known is plain-error review. Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure\xc2\xa052(b) authorizes reviewing courts to correct \xe2\x80\x9c[a] plain error that affects substantial rights \xe2\x80\xa6 even though it was not brought to the court\xe2\x80\x99s attention.\xe2\x80\x9d Although I agree with the Court that this Rule does not independently justify the Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision, see ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939, I believe that the Rule\xe2\x80\x99s underlying policy sheds some light on the issue before us. We have explained that courts may rely on Rule\xc2\xa052(b) to correct only those plain errors that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98seriously affec[t] the fairness, integrity or public reputation of judicial proceedings.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Olano, supra, at 736 (quoting United States v. Atkinson, 297 U. S. 157, 160 (1936)). We have thus recognized that preservation of the \xe2\x80\x9cfairness, integrity or public reputation of judicial proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d may sometimes justify a departure from the traditional adversarial framework of issue\npresentation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Perhaps the closest analogue to the cross-appeal requirement is the rule of appellate practice that restrains reviewing courts from addressing arguments that the parties have not made. Courts typically invoke this rule to avoid resolving a case based on an unaired argument, even if the argument could change the outcome. See, e.g., Santiago v. Rumsfeld, 425 F.\xc2\xa03d 549, 552, n.\xc2\xa01 (CA9 2005); United States v. Cervini, 379 F.\xc2\xa03d 987, 994, n.\xc2\xa05 (CA10 2004). But courts also recognize that the rule is not inflexible, see, e.g., Santiago, supra, at 552, n.\xc2\xa01, and sometimes they depart from it, see, e.g., United States Nat. Bank of Ore. v. Independent Ins. Agents of America, Inc., 508 U. S. 439, 448 (1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cAfter giving the parties ample opportunity to address the issue, the Court of Appeals acted without any impropriety in refusing to accept what in effect was a stipulation on a question of law\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Swift & Co. v. Hocking Valley R.\xc2\xa0Co., 243 U. S. 281, 289 (1917))); United States v. Moyer, 282 F.\xc2\xa03d 1311, 1317\xe2\x80\x931318 (CA10 2002); Dorris v. Absher, 179 F.\xc2\xa03d 420, 425\xe2\x80\x93426 (CA6 1999).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A reviewing court will generally address an argument sua sponte only to correct the most patent and serious errors. See, e.g., id., at 426 (concluding that the error, if overlooked, would result in \xe2\x80\x9ca miscarriage of justice\xe2\x80\x9d); Consumers Union of U. S., Inc. v. Federal Power Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 510 F.\xc2\xa02d 656, 662 (CADC 1974) (balancing \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderations of judicial orderliness and efficiency against the need for the greatest possible accuracy in judicial decisionmaking\xe2\x80\x9d). Because the prejudicial effect of the error and the impact of error correction on judicial resources are matters best determined by the reviewing court, the court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to go beyond the arguments made by the parties is committed to its sound discretion. See United States Nat. Bank of Ore., supra, at 448 (reviewing an appellate court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to address an argument sU. S.onte for abuse of discretion).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This authority provides a good model for our decision in this case. The Court has not persuaded me that the interests at stake when a reviewing court awards a nonappealing party additional relief are qualitatively different from the interests at stake when a reviewing court raises an issue sU. S.onte. Authority on the latter point recognizes that the interest of the public and the Judiciary in correcting grossly prejudicial errors of law may sometimes outweigh other interests normally furthered by fidelity to our adversarial tradition. I would recognize the same possibility here. And just as reviewing courts enjoy discretion to decide for themselves when to raise and decide arguments sua sponte, I would grant them substantial latitude to decide when to enlarge an appellee\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in the absence of a cross-appeal.[Footnote 1]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The approach I advocate is not out of step with our precedent. The Court has never decided whether the cross-appeal requirement is \xe2\x80\x9csubject to exceptions [or] an unqualified limit on the power of appellate courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Neztsosie, 526 U. S., at 480. That question was reserved in Neztsosie, ibid., even as the Court recognized that lower courts had reached different conclusions, see id., at 480, n.\xc2\xa02. I would simply confirm what our precedent had assumed: that there are exceptional circumstances when it is appropriate for a reviewing court to correct an error for the benefit of a party that has not cross-appealed the decision below.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, the Court has already reached the very result that it claims to disavow today. We have long held that a sentencing court confronted with new circumstances may impose a stiffer sentence on remand than the defendant received prior to a successful appeal. See Chaffin v. Stynchcombe, 412 U. S. 17, 23 (1973); North Carolina v. Pearce, 395 U. S. 711, 719\xe2\x80\x93720 (1969), overruled on other grounds, Alabama v. Smith, 490 U. S. 794 (1989). The Court makes no effort to explain the analytical difference between those cases and this one. If a sentencing court may rely on new circumstances to justify a longer sentence on remand, why cannot one of the new circumstances be the court\xe2\x80\x99s discovery (by dint of appellate review) that its first sentence was based on an error of law?[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even today, the Court refuses to decide whether the cross-appeal requirement admits of exceptions in appropriate cases. While calling the rule \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98inveterate and certain,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6 (quoting Morley Constr. Co., 300 U. S., at 191), the Court allows that \xe2\x80\x9cthere might be circumstances in which it would be proper for an appellate court to initiate plain-error review,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9; see also ante, at 5, n.\xc2\xa02. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s mandate is limited to a single class of cases\xe2\x80\x94sentencing appeals, and then only when the appeal is brought by the Government.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court justifies the asymmetry in its decision by pointing to 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73742(b), which provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Government may not further prosecute [the] appeal without the personal approval of the Attorney General, the Solicitor General, or a deputy solicitor general designated by the Solicitor General.\xe2\x80\x9d According to the majority, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t would severely undermine Congress\xe2\x80\x99 instruction were appellate judges to \xe2\x80\x98sally forth\xe2\x80\x99 on their own motion to take up errors adverse to the Government when the designated Department of Justice officials have not authorized an appeal from the sentence the trial court imposed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7 (citation omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The problem with this argument is that \xc2\xa73742(b) does not apportion authority over sentencing appeals between the Executive and Judicial Branches. By its terms, \xc2\xa73742(b) simply apportions that authority within an executive department. It provides that the \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Government\xe2\x80\x9d may not \xe2\x80\x9cprosecute\xe2\x80\x9d the appeal without approval from one of the listed officials. It says nothing about the power of the courts to correct error in the absence of a Government appeal. Had Congress intended to restrict the power of the courts, the statute would not stop \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Government\xe2\x80\x9d from \xe2\x80\x9cprosecut[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d unauthorized appeals; instead, it would stop \xe2\x80\x9cthe Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x9d from \xe2\x80\x9cdeciding\xe2\x80\x9d them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The design that the Court imputes to the drafters of \xc2\xa73742(b) is inconsistent with the text in another important respect. Suppose that the District Court imposes a sentence below the range set forth in the Federal Sentencing Guidelines, and the Government files an authorized appeal on the ground that the sentence is unreasonable. Suppose further that the reviewing court discovers, to the surprise of both parties, that the District Court made a further error by overlooking a mandatory minimum to which the defendant was subject. The mandatory minimum would raise the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence beyond what even the Government had wanted. Under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s theory, see ante, at 7, the reviewing court should not remand for imposition of the mandatory minimum, since the decision to seek the higher sentence belonged to the Government alone. But that conclusion is plainly at odds with the text of the statute, which imposes no limits on sentencing review once the named officials have signed off on the appeal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section\xc2\xa03742(b)\xe2\x80\x99s limited effect on sentencing review implies that the statute was not designed to prevent judicial encroachment on the prerogatives of the Executive. It is more likely that Congress wanted to withhold from the Executive the power to force the courts of appeals to entertain Government appeals that are not regarded as sufficiently important by the leadership of the Department of Justice. Allowing the courts of appeals, in their discretion, to remedy errors not raised in a cross-appeal in no way trenches on the authority of the Executive. Section\xc2\xa03742(b) may have also been designed to serve the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s institutional interests. Congress may have wanted to ensure that the Government maintained a consistent legal position across different sentencing appeals. Or perhaps Congress wanted to maximize the impact of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing appeals by giving high-level officials the authority to nix meritless or marginal ones. These institutional interests of the Executive do not undermine the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s authority to correct unlawful sentences in the absence of a Government appeal, and they do not justify the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision today.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons given above, I would hold that the courts of appeals enjoy the discretion to correct error sU. S.onte for the benefit of nonappealing parties. The Court errs in vacating the judgment of the Eighth Circuit, and I respectfully dissent.[Footnote 3]

\nFootnote 1

\xc2\xa0The Court argues that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s original sentence was neither so fundamentally unfair nor so harmful to our system of justice as to warrant sU. S.onte correction by the Court of Appeals. Ante, at 16, n.\xc2\xa09. But these considerations, which may well support a conclusion that the Court of Appeals should not have exercised its authority in this case, cf. n.\xc2\xa03, infra, surely do not justify the Court\xe2\x80\x99s broad rule that sU. S.onte error correction on behalf of the Government is inappropriate in all cases.

\nFootnote 2

\xc2\xa0The Court finds it \xe2\x80\x9chard to imagine a case in which a district court, after a court of appeals vacated a criminal sentence, could properly increase the sentence based on an error the appeals court left uncorrected because of the cross-appeal rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16, n.\xc2\xa08. Happily, we need not imagine such cases, since they come before our courts every day.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For examples, we have no further to look than the sentencing cases remanded en masse following our recent decision in United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220 (2005). In Booker\xe2\x80\x99s wake, it was common for newly convicted defendants to appeal their sentences, claiming that they received enhancements that they would not have received under the advisory guidelines. Many of those cases were remanded for resentencing, and some defendants wound up with even longer sentences on remand. See, e.g., United States v. Singletary, 458 F.\xc2\xa03d 72, 77 (CA2) (affirming a sentence lengthened by 12\xc2\xa0months following a Booker remand), cert. denied, 549 U. S. 1047 (2006); United States v. Reinhart, 442 F.\xc2\xa03d 857, 860\xe2\x80\x93861 (CA5 2006) (affirming a sentence lengthened from 210\xc2\xa0months to 235\xc2\xa0months following a Booker\nremand).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These cases represent straightforward applications of the cross-appeal rule: The Government had not cross-appealed the sentence, so the reviewing court did not order the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence lengthened. And yet the sentence was ultimately lengthened when the error was corrected on remand. The Court fails to explain the conceptual distinction between those cases and this one. If the Court permits sentencing courts to correct unappealed errors on remand, why does it not permit the courts of appeals to do the same on appeal?

As framed by petitioner, the question presented asked only whether the cross-appeal requirement is subject to exceptions. Because the parties have not addressed the fact-bound subsidiary question, I would affirm without reaching it. See United States v. International Business Machines Corp., 517 U. S. 843, 855, n.\xc2\xa03 (1996).
