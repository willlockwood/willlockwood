IOWA v. TOVAR

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of iowa

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x931541. Argued January 21, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 8, 2004

\n

At respondent Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s November 1996 arraignment for operating a motor vehicle under the influence of alcohol (OWI), in response to the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s questions, Tovar affirmed that he wanted to represent himself and to plead guilty. Conducting the guilty plea colloquy required by the Iowa Rules of Criminal Procedure, the court explained that, if Tovar pleaded not guilty, he would be entitled to a speedy and public jury trial where he would have the right to counsel who could help him select a jury, question and cross-examine witnesses, present evidence, and make arguments on his behalf. By pleading guilty, the court cautioned, Tovar would give up his right to a trial and his rights at that trial to be represented by counsel, to remain silent, to the presumption of innocence, and to subpoena witnesses and compel their testimony. The court then informed Tovar of the maximum and minimum penalties for an OWI conviction, and explained that, before accepting a guilty plea, the court had to assure itself that Tovar was in fact guilty of the charged offense. To that end, the court informed Tovar of the two elements of the OWI charge:\xc2\xa0The defendant must have (1)\xc2\xa0operated a motor vehicle in Iowa (2)\xc2\xa0while intoxicated. Tovar confirmed, first, that on the date in question, he was operating a motor vehicle in Iowa and, second, that he did not dispute the result of the intoxilyzer test showing his blood alcohol level exceeded the legal limit nearly twice over. The court then accepted his guilty plea and, at a hearing the next month, imposed the minimum sentence of two days in jail and a fine. In 1998, Tovar was again charged with OWI, this time as a second offense, an aggravated misdemeanor under Iowa law. Represented by counsel in that proceeding, he pleaded guilty. In 2000, Tovar was charged with third-offense OWI, a class \xe2\x80\x9cD\xe2\x80\x9d felony under Iowa law. Again represented by counsel, Tovar pleaded not guilty to the felony charge. Counsel moved to preclude use of Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s first (1996) OWI conviction to enhance his 2000 offense from an aggravated misdemeanor to a third-offense felony. Tovar maintained that his 1996 waiver of counsel was invalid\xe2\x80\x94not fully knowing, intelligent, and voluntary\xe2\x80\x94because he was never made aware by the court of the dangers and disadvantages of self-representation. The trial court denied the motion, found Tovar guilty, and sentenced him on the OWI third-offense charge. The Iowa Court of Appeals affirmed, but the Supreme Court of Iowa reversed and remanded for entry of judgment without consideration of Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s first OWI conviction. Holding that the colloquy preceding acceptance of Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s 1996 guilty plea had been constitutionally inadequate, Iowa\xe2\x80\x99s high court ruled, as here at issue, that two warnings not given to Tovar are essential to the \xe2\x80\x9cknowing and intelligent\xe2\x80\x9d waiver of the Sixth Amendment right to counsel at the plea stage: The defendant must be advised specifically that waiving counsel\xe2\x80\x99s assistance in deciding whether to plead guilty (1) entails the risk that a viable defense will be overlooked and (2) deprives him of the opportunity to obtain an independent opinion on whether, under the facts and applicable law, it is wise to plead guilty.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Neither warning ordered by the Iowa Supreme Court is mandated by the Sixth Amendment. The constitutional requirement is satisfied when the trial court informs the accused of the nature of the charges against him, of his right to be counseled regarding his plea, and of the range of allowable punishments attendant upon the entry of a guilty plea. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Sixth Amendment secures to a defendant facing incarceration the right to counsel at all \xe2\x80\x9ccritical stages\xe2\x80\x9d of the criminal process, see, e.g., Maine v. Moulton, 474 U. S. 159, 170, including a plea hearing, White v. Maryland, 373 U. S. 59, 60 (per curiam). Because Tovar received a two-day prison term for his first OWI conviction, he had a right to counsel both at the plea stage and at trial had he elected to contest the charge. Argersinger v. Hamlin, 407 U. S. 25, 34, 37. Although an accused may choose to forgo representation, any waiver of the right to counsel must be knowing, voluntary, and intelligent, see Johnson v. Zerbst, 304 U. S. 458, 464. The information a defendant must possess in order to make an intelligent election depends on a range of case-specific factors, including his education or sophistication, the complex or easily grasped nature of the charge, and the stage of the proceeding. See Johnson, 304 U. S., at 464. Although warnings of the pitfalls of proceeding to trial uncounseled must be \xe2\x80\x9crigorous[ly]\xe2\x80\x9d conveyed, Patterson v. Illinois, 487 U. S. 285, 298; see Faretta v. California, 422 U. S. 806, 835, a less searching or formal colloquy may suffice at earlier stages of the criminal process, 487 U. S., at 299. In Patterson, this Court described a pragmatic approach to right-to-counsel waivers, one that asks \xe2\x80\x9cwhat purposes a lawyer can serve at the particular stage of the proceedings in question, and what assistance [counsel] could provide to an accused at that stage.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 298. Less rigorous warnings are required pretrial because, at that stage, \xe2\x80\x9cthe full dangers and disadvantages of self-representation \xe2\x80\xa6 are less substantial and more obvious to an accused than they are at trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 299. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Sixth Amendment does not compel the two admonitions ordered by the Iowa Supreme Court. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he law ordinarily considers a waiver knowing, intelligent, and sufficiently aware if the defendant fully understands the nature of the right and how it would likely apply in general in the circumstances \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Ruiz, 536 U. S. 622, 629. Even if the defendant lacked a full and complete appreciation of all of the consequences flowing from his waiver, the State may nevertheless prevail if it shows that the information provided to the defendant satisfied the constitutional minimum. Patterson, 487 U. S., at 294. The Iowa high court gave insufficient consideration to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s guiding decisions. In prescribing scripted admonitions and holding them necessary in every guilty plea instance, that court overlooked this Court\xe2\x80\x99s observations that the information a defendant must have to waive counsel intelligently will depend upon the particular facts and circumstances in each case, Johnson, 304 U. S., at 464. Moreover, as Tovar acknowledges, in a collateral attack on an uncounseled conviction, it is the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s burden to prove that he did not competently and intelligently waive his right to counsel. Tovar has never claimed that he did not fully understand the 1996 OWI charge or the range of punishment for that crime prior to pleading guilty. He has never \xe2\x80\x9carticulate[d] with precision\xe2\x80\x9d the additional information counsel could have provided, given the simplicity of the charge. See Patterson, 487 U. S., at 294. Nor does he assert that he was unaware of his right to be counseled prior to and at his arraignment. Before this Court, he suggests only that he may have been under the mistaken belief that he had a right to counsel at trial, but not if he was, instead, going to plead guilty. Given \xe2\x80\x9cthe particular facts and circumstances surrounding [this] case,\xe2\x80\x9d Johnson, 304 U. S., at 464, it is far from clear that warnings of the kind required by the Iowa Supreme Court would have enlightened Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s decision whether to seek counsel or to represent himself. In a case so straightforward, the two admonitions at issue might confuse or mislead a defendant more than they would inform him, i.e., the warnings might be misconstrued to convey that a meritorious defense exists or that the defendant could plead to a lesser charge, when neither prospect is a realistic one. If a defendant delays his plea in the vain hope that counsel could uncover a tenable basis for contesting or reducing the criminal charge, the prompt disposition of the case will be impeded, and the resources of either the State (if the defendant is indigent) or the defendant himself (if he is financially ineligible for appointed counsel) will be wasted. States are free to adopt by statute, rule, or decision any guides to the acceptance of an uncounseled plea they deem useful, but the Federal Constitution does not require the two admonitions here in controversy. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

656 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 112, reversed and remanded.

IOWA, PETITIONER v. FELIPE EDGARDO TOVAR

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of iowa

\n

[March 8, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Amendment safeguards to an accused who faces incarceration the right to counsel at all critical stages of the criminal process. Maine v. Moulton, 474 U. S. 159, 170 (1985); United States v. Wade, 388 U. S. 218, 224 (1967). The entry of a guilty plea, whether to a misdemeanor or a felony charge, ranks as a \xe2\x80\x9ccritical stage\xe2\x80\x9d at which the right to counsel adheres. Argersinger v. Hamlin, 407 U. S. 25, 34 (1972); White v. Maryland, 373 U. S. 59, 60 (1963) (per curiam). Waiver of the right to counsel, as of constitutional rights in the criminal process generally, must be a \xe2\x80\x9cknowing, intelligent ac[t] done with sufficient awareness of the relevant circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Brady v. United States, 397 U. S. 742, 748 (1970). This case concerns the extent to which a trial judge, before accepting a guilty plea from an uncounseled defendant, must elaborate on the right to representation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Beyond affording the defendant the opportunity to consult with counsel prior to entry of a plea and to be assisted by counsel at the plea hearing, must the court, specifically: (1) advise the defendant that \xe2\x80\x9cwaiving the assistance of counsel in deciding whether to plead guilty [entails] the risk that a viable defense will be overlooked\xe2\x80\x9d; and (2) \xe2\x80\x9cadmonis[h]\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cthat by waiving his right to an attorney he will lose the opportunity to obtain an independent opinion on whether, under the facts and applicable law, it is wise to plead guilty\xe2\x80\x9d? 656 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 112, 121 (Iowa 2003). The Iowa Supreme Court held both warnings essential to the \xe2\x80\x9cknowing and intelligent\xe2\x80\x9d waiver of the Sixth Amendment right to the assistance of counsel. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that neither warning is mandated by the Sixth Amendment. The constitutional requirement is satisfied when the trial court informs the accused of the nature of the charges against him, of his right to be counseled regarding his plea, and of the range of allowable punishments attendant upon the entry of a guilty plea.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On November 2, 1996, respondent Felipe Edgardo Tovar, then a 21-year-old college student, was arrested in Ames, Iowa, for operating a motor vehicle while under the influence of alcohol (OWI). See Iowa Code \xc2\xa7321J.2 (1995).[Footnote 1] An intoxilyzer test administered the night of Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s arrest showed he had a blood alcohol level of 0.194. App. 24. The arresting officer informed Tovar of his rights under Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S.436 (1966). Tovar signed a form stating that he waived those rights and agreed to answer questions. Iowa State Univ. Dept. of Public Safety, OWI Supplemental Report 3 (Nov. 2, 1996), Lodging of Petitioner; Iowa State Univ. Dept. of Public Safety, Rights Warnings (Nov. 2, 1996), Lodging of\nPetitioner.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Some hours after his arrest, Tovar appeared before a judge in the Iowa District Court for Story County. The judge indicated on the Initial Appearance form that Tovar appeared without counsel and waived application for court-appointed counsel. Initial Appearance in No. OWCR 23989 (Nov. 2, 1996), Lodging of Petitioner. The judge also marked on the form\xe2\x80\x99s checklist that Tovar was \xe2\x80\x9cinformed of the charge and his \xe2\x80\xa6 rights and receive[d] a copy of the Complaint.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Arraignment was set for November 18, 1996. In the interim, Tovar was released from jail.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the November 18 arraignment,[Footnote 2] the court\xe2\x80\x99s inquiries of Tovar began: \xe2\x80\x9cMr. Tovar appears without counsel and I see, Mr. Tovar, that you waived application for a court appointed attorney. Did you want to represent yourself at today\xe2\x80\x99s hearing?\xe2\x80\x9d App. 8\xe2\x80\x939. Tovar replied: \xe2\x80\x9cYes, sir.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 9. The court soon after asked: \xe2\x80\x9c[H]ow did you wish to plead?\xe2\x80\x9d Tovar answered: \xe2\x80\x9cGuilty.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Tovar affirmed that he had not been promised anything or threatened in any way to induce him to plead guilty. Id., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Conducting the guilty plea colloquy required by the Iowa Rules of Criminal Procedure, see Iowa Rule Crim. Proc. 8 (1992),[Footnote 3] the court explained that, if Tovar pleaded not guilty, he would be entitled to a speedy and public trial by jury, App. 15, and would have the right to be represented at that trial by an attorney, who \xe2\x80\x9ccould help [Tovar] select a jury, question and cross-examine the State\xe2\x80\x99s witnesses, present evidence, if any, in [his] behalf, and make arguments to the judge and jury on [his] behalf,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 16. By pleading guilty, the court cautioned, \xe2\x80\x9cnot only [would Tovar] give up [his] right to a trial [of any kind on the charge against him], [he would] give up [his] right to be represented by an attorney at that trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The court further advised Tovar that, if he entered a guilty plea, he would relinquish the right to remain silent at trial, the right to the presumption of innocence, and the right to subpoena witnesses and compel their testimony. Id., at 16\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Turning to the particular offense with which Tovar had been charged, the court informed him that an OWI conviction carried a maximum penalty of a year in jail and a $1,000 fine, and a minimum penalty of two days in jail and a $500 fine. Id., at 20. Tovar affirmed that he understood his exposure to those penalties. Ibid. The court next explained that, before accepting a guilty plea, the court had to assure itself that Tovar was in fact guilty of the charged offense. Id., at 21\xe2\x80\x9322. To that end, the court informed Tovar that the OWI charge had only two elements: first, on the date in question, Tovar was operating a motor vehicle in the State of Iowa; second, when he did so, he was intoxicated. Id., at 23. Tovar confirmed that he had been driving in Ames, Iowa, on the night he was apprehended and that he did not dispute the results of the intoxilyzer test administered by the police that night, which showed that his blood alcohol level exceeded the legal limit nearly twice over. Id., at 23\xe2\x80\x9324.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the plea colloquy, the court asked Tovar if he still wished to plead guilty, and Tovar affirmed that he did. Id., at 27\xe2\x80\x9328. The court then accepted Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s plea, observing that there was \xe2\x80\x9ca factual basis\xe2\x80\x9d for it, and that Tovar had made the plea \xe2\x80\x9cvoluntarily, with a full understanding of [his] rights, [and] \xe2\x80\xa6 of the consequences of [pleading guilty].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 28.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On December 30, 1996, Tovar appeared for sentencing on the OWI charge[Footnote 4] and, simultaneously, for arraignment on a subsequent charge of driving with a suspended license. Id., at 45\xe2\x80\x9346; see Iowa Code \xc2\xa7321J.21 (1995).[Footnote 5] Noting that Tovar was again in attendance without counsel, the court inquired: \xe2\x80\x9cMr. Tovar, did you want to represent yourself at today\xe2\x80\x99s hearing or did you want to take some time to hire an attorney to represent you?\xe2\x80\x9d App. 46.[Footnote 6] Tovar replied that he would represent himself. Ibid. The court then engaged in essentially the same plea colloquy on the suspension charge as it had on the OWI charge the previous month. Id., at 48\xe2\x80\x9351. After accepting Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s guilty plea on the suspension charge, the court sentenced him on both counts: For the OWI conviction, the court imposed the minimum sentence of two days in jail and a $500 fine, plus a surcharge and costs; for the suspension conviction, the court imposed a $250 fine, plus a surcharge and costs. Id., at 55.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On March 16, 1998, Tovar was convicted of OWI for a second time. He was represented by counsel in that proceeding, in which he pleaded guilty. Record 60; see App. to Pet. for Cert. 24, n.\xc2\xa01.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On December 14, 2000, Tovar was again charged with OWI, this time as a third offense, see Iowa Code \xc2\xa7321J.2 (1999), and additionally with driving while license barred, see \xc2\xa7321.561. Iowa law classifies first-offense OWI as a serious misdemeanor and second-offense OWI as an aggravated misdemeanor. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7321J.2(2)(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b). Third-offense OWI, and any OWI offenses thereafter, rank as class \xe2\x80\x9cD\xe2\x80\x9d felonies. \xc2\xa7321J.2(2)(c). Represented by an attorney, Tovar pleaded not guilty to both December 2000 charges. Record 55.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In March 2001, through counsel, Tovar filed a Motion for Adjudication of Law Points;[Footnote 7] the motion urged that Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s first OWI conviction, in 1996, could not be used to enhance the December 2000 OWI charge from a second-offense aggravated misdemeanor to a third-offense felony. App. 3\xe2\x80\x935.[Footnote 8] Significantly, Tovar did not allege that he was unaware at the November 1996 arraignment of his right to counsel prior to pleading guilty and at the plea hearing. Instead, he maintained that his 1996 waiver of counsel was invalid\xe2\x80\x94not \xe2\x80\x9cfull knowing, intelligent, and voluntary\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94because he \xe2\x80\x9cwas never made aware by the court \xe2\x80\xa6 of the dangers and disadvantages of self-representation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court denied Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s motion in May 2001, explaining: \xe2\x80\x9cWhere the offense is readily understood by laypersons and the penalty is not unduly severe, the duty of inquiry which is imposed upon the court is only that which is required to assure an awareness of [the] right to counsel and a willingness to proceed without counsel in the face of such awareness.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 36\xe2\x80\x9337 (brackets in original). Tovar then waived his right to a jury trial and was found guilty by the court of both the OWI third-offense charge and driving while license barred. Id., at 33. Four months after that adjudication, Tovar was sentenced. On the OWI third-offense charge, he received a 180-day jail term, with all but 30 days suspended, three years of probation, and a $2,500 fine plus surcharges and costs. App. 70\xe2\x80\x9371. For driving while license barred, Tovar received a 30-day jail term, to run concurrently with the OWI sentence, and a suspended $500 fine. Id., at 71.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Iowa Court of Appeals affirmed, App. to Pet. for Cert. 23\xe2\x80\x9330, but the Supreme Court of Iowa, by a 4 to 3 vote, reversed and remanded for entry of judgment without consideration of Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s first OWI conviction, 656 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 112 (2003). Iowa\xe2\x80\x99s highest court acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9cthe dangers of proceeding pro se at a guilty plea proceeding will be different than the dangers of proceeding pro se at a jury trial, [therefore] the inquiries made at these proceedings will also be different.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 119. The court nonetheless held that the colloquy preceding acceptance of Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s 1996 guilty plea had been constitutionally inadequate, and instructed dispositively:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[A] defendant such as Tovar who chooses to plead guilty without the assistance of an attorney must be advised of the usefulness of an attorney and the dangers of self-representation in order to make a knowing and intelligent waiver of his right to counsel\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [T]he trial judge [must] advise the defendant generally that there are defenses to criminal charges that may not be known by laypersons and that the danger in waiving the assistance of counsel in deciding whether to plead guilty is the risk that a viable defense will be overlooked. The defendant should be admonished that by waiving his right to an attorney he will lose the opportunity to obtain an independent opinion on whether, under the facts and applicable law, it is wise to plead guilty. In addition, the court must ensure the defendant understands the nature of the charges against him and the range of allowable punishments.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 121.[Footnote 9]

\n

\nWe granted certiorari, 539 U. S. \xe2\x80\x94 (2003), in view of the division of opinion on the requirements the Sixth Amendment imposes for waiver of counsel at a plea hearing, compare, e.g., United States v. Akins, 276 F. 3d 1141, 1146\xe2\x80\x931147 (CA9 2002), with State v. Cashman, 491 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 462, 465\xe2\x80\x93466 (S.\xc2\xa0D. 1992), and we now reverse the judgment of the Iowa Supreme Court.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Amendment secures to a defendant who faces incarceration the right to counsel at all \xe2\x80\x9ccritical stages\xe2\x80\x9d of the criminal process. See, e.g., Maine v. Moulton, 474 U. S., at 170; United States v. Wade, 388 U. S., at 224. A plea hearing qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9ccritical stage.\xe2\x80\x9d White v. Maryland, 373 U. S., at 60. Because Tovar received a two-day prison term for his 1996 OWI conviction, he had a right to counsel both at the plea stage and at trial had he elected to contest the charge. Argersinger v. Hamlin, 407 U. S., at 34, 37.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A person accused of crime, however, may choose to forgo representation. While the Constitution \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not force a lawyer upon a defendant,\xe2\x80\x9d Adams v. United States ex rel. McCann, 317 U. S. 269, 279 (1942), it does require that any waiver of the right to counsel be knowing, voluntary, and intelligent, see Johnson v. Zerbst, 304 U. S. 458, 464 (1938). Tovar contends that his waiver of counsel in November 1996, at his first OWI plea hearing, was insufficiently informed, and therefore constitutionally invalid. In particular, he asserts that the trial judge did not elaborate on the value, at that stage of the case, of an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s advice and the dangers of self-representation in entering a plea. Brief for Respondent 15.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have described a waiver of counsel as intelligent when the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cknows what he is doing and his choice is made with eyes open.\xe2\x80\x9d Adams, 317 U. S., at 279. We have not, however, prescribed any formula or script to be read to a defendant who states that he elects to proceed without counsel. The information a defendant must possess in order to make an intelligent election, our decisions indicate, will depend on a range of case-specific factors, including the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s education or sophistication, the complex or easily grasped nature of the charge, and the stage of the proceeding. See Johnson, 304 U. S., at 464.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As to waiver of trial counsel, we have said that before a defendant may be allowed to proceed pro se, he must be warned specifically of the hazards ahead. Faretta v. California, 422 U. S. 806 (1975), is instructive. The defendant in Faretta resisted counsel\xe2\x80\x99s aid, preferring to represent himself. The Court held that he had a constitutional right to self-representation. In recognizing that right, however, we cautioned: \xe2\x80\x9cAlthough a defendant need not himself have the skill and experience of a lawyer in order competently and intelligently to choose self-representation, he should be made aware of the dangers and disadvantages of self-representation, so that the record will establish that he knows what he is doing \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 835 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Later, in Patterson v. Illinois, 487 U. S. 285 (1988), we elaborated on \xe2\x80\x9cthe dangers and disadvantages of self-representation\xe2\x80\x9d to which Faretta referred. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]t trial,\xe2\x80\x9d we observed, \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel is required to help even the most gifted layman adhere to the rules of procedure and evidence, comprehend the subtleties of voir dire, examine and cross-examine witnesses effectively \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, object to improper prosecution questions, and much more.\xe2\x80\x9d 487 U. S., at 299, n.\xc2\xa013. Warnings of the pitfalls of proceeding to trial without counsel, we therefore said, must be \xe2\x80\x9crigorous[ly]\xe2\x80\x9d conveyed. Id., at 298. We clarified, however, that at earlier stages of the criminal process, a less searching or formal colloquy may suffice. Id., at 299.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Patterson concerned postindictment questioning by police and prosecutor. At that stage of the case, we held, the warnings required by Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436 (1966), adequately informed the defendant not only of his Fifth Amendment rights, but of his Sixth Amendment right to counsel as well. 487 U. S., at 293. Miranda warnings, we said, effectively convey to a defendant his right to have counsel present during questioning. In addition, they inform him of the \xe2\x80\x9cultimate adverse consequence\xe2\x80\x9d of making uncounseled admissions, i.e., his statements may be used against him in any ensuing criminal proceeding. 487 U. S., at 293. The Miranda warnings, we added, \xe2\x80\x9calso sufficed \xe2\x80\xa6 to let [the defendant] know what a lawyer could \xe2\x80\x98do for him,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d namely, advise him to refrain from making statements that could prove damaging to his defense. 487 U. S., at 294.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Patterson describes a \xe2\x80\x9cpragmatic approach to the waiver question,\xe2\x80\x9d one that asks \xe2\x80\x9cwhat purposes a lawyer can serve at the particular stage of the proceedings in question, and what assistance he could provide to an accused at that stage,\xe2\x80\x9d in order \xe2\x80\x9cto determine the scope of the Sixth Amendment right to counsel, and the type of warnings and procedures that should be required before a waiver of that right will be recognized.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 298. We require less rigorous warnings pretrial, Patterson explained, not because pretrial proceedings are \xe2\x80\x9cless important\xe2\x80\x9d than trial, but because, at that stage, \xe2\x80\x9cthe full dangers and disadvantages of self-representation \xe2\x80\xa6 are less substantial and more obvious to an accused than they are at trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 299 (citation and internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s case, the State maintains that, like the Miranda warnings we found adequate in Patterson, Iowa\xe2\x80\x99s plea colloquy suffices both to advise a defendant of his right to counsel, and to assure that his guilty plea is informed and voluntary. Brief for Petitioner 20; Tr. of Oral Arg. 3. The plea colloquy, according to the State, \xe2\x80\x9cmakes plain that an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s role would be to challenge the charge or sentence,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore adequately conveys to the defendant both the utility of counsel and the dangers of self-representation. Brief for Petitioner 25. Tovar, on the other hand, defends the precise instructions required by the Iowa Supreme Court, see supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, as essential to a knowing, voluntary, and intelligent plea stage waiver of counsel. Brief for Respondent 15.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To resolve this case, we need not endorse the State\xe2\x80\x99s position that nothing more than the plea colloquy was needed to safeguard Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s right to counsel. Preliminarily, we note that there were some things more in this case. Tovar first indicated that he waived counsel at his Initial Appearance, see supra, at 3, affirmed that he wanted to represent himself at the plea hearing, see supra, at 3, and declined the court\xe2\x80\x99s offer of \xe2\x80\x9ctime to hire an attorney\xe2\x80\x9d at sentencing, when it was still open to him to request withdrawal of his plea, see supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, and n.\xc2\xa04. Further, the State does not contest that a defendant must be alerted to his right to the assistance of counsel in entering a plea. See Brief for Petitioner 19 (acknowledging defendant\xe2\x80\x99s need to know \xe2\x80\x9cretained or appointed counsel can assist\xe2\x80\x9d at the plea stage by \xe2\x80\x9cwork[ing] on the issues of guilt and sentencing\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, the Iowa Supreme Court appeared to assume that Tovar was informed of his entitlement to counsel\xe2\x80\x99s aid or, at least, to have pretermitted that issue. See 656 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d, at 117. Accordingly, the State presents a narrower question: \xe2\x80\x9cDoes the Sixth Amendment require a court to give a rigid and detailed admonishment to a pro se defendant pleading guilty of the usefulness of an attorney, that an attorney may provide an independent opinion whether it is wise to plead guilty and that without an attorney the defendant risks overlooking a defense?\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Training on that question, we turn to, and reiterate, the particular language the Iowa Supreme Court employed in announcing the warnings it thought the Sixth Amendment required: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he trial judge [must] advise the defendant generally that there are defenses to criminal charges that may not be known by laypersons and that the danger in waiving the assistance of counsel in deciding whether to plead guilty is the risk that a viable defense will be overlooked,\xe2\x80\x9d 656 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d, at 121; in addition, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he defendant should be admonished that by waiving his right to an attorney he will lose the opportunity to obtain an independent opinion on whether, under the facts and applicable law, it is wise to plead guilty,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Tovar did not receive such advice, and the sole question before us is whether the Sixth Amendment compels the two admonitions here in controversy.[Footnote 11] We hold it does not.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court recently explained, in reversing a lower court determination that a guilty plea was not voluntary: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he law ordinarily considers a waiver knowing, intelligent, and sufficiently aware if the defendant fully understands the nature of the right and how it would likely apply in general in the circumstances\xe2\x80\x94even though the defendant may not know the specific detailed consequences of invoking it.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Ruiz, 536 U. S. 622, 629 (2002) (emphasis in original). We similarly observed in Patterson: \xe2\x80\x9cIf [the defendant] \xe2\x80\xa6 lacked a full and complete appreciation of all of the consequences flowing from his waiver, it does not defeat the State\xe2\x80\x99s showing that the information it provided to him satisfied the constitutional minimum.\xe2\x80\x9d 487 U. S., at 294 (internal quotation marks omitted). The Iowa Supreme Court gave insufficient consideration to these guiding decisions. In prescribing scripted admonitions and holding them necessary in every guilty plea instance, we further note, the Iowa high court overlooked our observations that the information a defendant must have to waive counsel intelligently will \xe2\x80\x9cdepend, in each case, upon the particular facts and circumstances surrounding that case,\xe2\x80\x9d Johnson, 304 U. S., at 464; supra, at 9.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, as Tovar acknowledges, in a collateral attack on an uncounseled conviction, it is the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s burden to prove that he did not competently and intelligently waive his right to the assistance of counsel. See Watts v. State, 257 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 70, 71 (Iowa 1977); Brief for Respondent 5, 26\xe2\x80\x9327. In that light, we note that Tovar has never claimed that he did not fully understand the charge or the range of punishment for the crime prior to pleading guilty. Further, he has never \xe2\x80\x9carticulate[d] with precision\xe2\x80\x9d the additional information counsel could have provided, given the simplicity of the charge. See Patterson, 487 U. S., at 294; supra, at 4. Nor does he assert that he was unaware of his right to be counseled prior to and at his arraignment. Before this Court, he suggests only that he \xe2\x80\x9cmay have been under the mistaken belief that he had a right to counsel at trial, but not if he was merely going to plead guilty.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 16 (emphasis added).[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given \xe2\x80\x9cthe particular facts and circumstances surrounding [this] case,\xe2\x80\x9d see Johnson, 304 U. S., at 464, it is far from clear that warnings of the kind required by the Iowa Supreme Court would have enlightened Tovar\xe2\x80\x99s decision whether to seek counsel or to represent himself. In a case so straightforward, the United States as amicus curiae suggests, the admonitions at issue might confuse or mislead a defendant more than they would inform him: The warnings the Iowa Supreme Court declared mandatory might be misconstrued as a veiled suggestion that a meritorious defense exists or that the defendant could plead to a lesser charge, when neither prospect is a realistic one. If a defendant delays his plea in the vain hope that counsel could uncover a tenable basis for contesting or reducing the criminal charge, the prompt disposition of the case will be impeded, and the resources of either the State (if the defendant is indigent) or the defendant himself (if he is financially ineligible for appointed counsel) will be wasted. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 9, 28\xe2\x80\x9329; Tr. of Oral Arg. 20\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We note, finally, that States are free to adopt by statute, rule, or decision any guides to the acceptance of an uncounseled plea they deem useful. See, e.g., Alaska Rule Crim. Proc. 39(a) (2003); Fla. Rule Crim. Proc. 3.111(d) (2003); Md. Ct. Rule 4\xe2\x80\x93215 (2002); Minn. Rule Crim.\nProc. 5.02 (2003); Pa. Rule Crim. Proc. 121, comment (2003). We hold only that the two admonitions the Iowa Supreme Court ordered are not required by the Federal Constitution.

\n

* * *

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated, the judgment of the Supreme Court of Iowa is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.
