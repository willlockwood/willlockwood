\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

BECK, liquidating trustee of ESTATES OF CROWN VANTAGE, INC., et\xc2\xa0al. v. PACE INTERNATIONAL \nUNION et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 05\xe2\x80\x931448.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 24, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 11, 2007

\n

Respondent PACE International Union represented employees covered by single-employer defined-benefit pension plans sponsored and administered by Crown, which had filed for bankruptcy. Crown rejected the union\xe2\x80\x99s proposal to terminate the plans by merging them with the union\xe2\x80\x99s own multiemployer plan, opting instead for a standard termination through the purchase of annuities, which would allow Crown to retain a $5 million reversion after satisfying its obligations to plan participants and beneficiaries. The union and respondent plan participants (hereinafter, collectively, PACE) filed an adversary action in the Bankruptcy Court, alleging that Crown\xe2\x80\x99s directors had breached their fiduciary duties under the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA), 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001 et seq., by neglecting to give diligent consideration to PACE\xe2\x80\x99s merger proposal. The court ruled for PACE, and petitioner bankruptcy trustee appealed to the District Court, which affirmed in relevant part, as did the Ninth Circuit. The Ninth Circuit acknowledged that the decision to terminate a pension plan is a business decision not subject to ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s fiduciary obligations, but reasoned that the implementation of a termination decision is fiduciary in nature. It then determined that merger was a permissible termination method and that Crown therefore had a fiduciary obligation to consider PACE\xe2\x80\x99s merger proposal seriously, which it had failed to do.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Crown did not breach its fiduciary obligations in failing to consider PACE\xe2\x80\x99s merger proposal because merger is not a permissible form of plan termination under ERISA. Section \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A) provides: \xe2\x80\x9cIn \xe2\x80\xa6 any final distribution of assets pursuant to \xe2\x80\xa6 standard termination \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, the plan administrator shall \xe2\x80\xa6 (i) purchase irrevocable commitments from an insurer to provide all benefit liabilities under the plan, or \xe2\x80\xa6 (ii) in accordance with the provisions of the plan and any applicable regulations, otherwise fully provide all benefit liabilities under the plan.\xe2\x80\x9d The parties agree that clause (i) refers to the purchase of annuities, and that clause (ii) allows for lump-sum distributions. These are by far the most common distribution methods. To decide that merger is also a permissible method, the Court would have to disagree with the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation (PBGC), the entity administering the federal insurance program that protects plan benefits, which takes the position that \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A) does not permit merger as a method of termination because merger is an alternative to (rather than an example of) plan termination. The Court has traditionally deferred to the PBGC when interpreting ERISA. Here, the Court believes that the PBGC\xe2\x80\x99s policy is based upon a construction of the statute that is permissible, and indeed the more plausible.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 PACE argues that \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A)(ii)\xe2\x80\x99s residual provision referring to an asset distribution that \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise fully provide[s] all benefit liabilities under the plan\xe2\x80\x9d covers merger because annuities (covered by \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A)(i)) are an example of a permissible means of \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[ing] . . . benefit liabilities,\xe2\x80\x9d and merger is the legal equivalent of annuitization. Even assuming that PACE is right about the meaning of the word \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise,\xe2\x80\x9d the clarity necessary to disregard the PBGC\xe2\x80\x99s considered views is lacking for three reasons. First, terminating a plan through purchase of annuities formally severs ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s applicability to plan assets and employer obligations, whereas merging the Crown plans into PACE\xe2\x80\x99s multiemployer plan would result in the former plans\xe2\x80\x99 assets remaining within ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s purview, where they could be used to satisfy the benefit liabilities of the multiemployer plan\xe2\x80\x99s other participants and beneficiaries. Second, although ERISA expressly allows the employer to (under certain circumstances) recoup surplus funds in a standard termination, \xc2\xa71344(d)(1), (3), as Crown sought to do here, merger would preclude the receipt of such funds by reason of \xc2\xa71103(c), which prohibits employers from misappropriating plan assets for their own benefit. Third, merger is nowhere mentioned in \xc2\xa71341, but is instead dealt with in an entirely different set of statutory sections setting forth entirely different rules and procedures, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71058, 1411, and 1412. PACE\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the procedural differences could be reconciled by requiring a plan sponsor intending to use merger as a termination method to follow the rules for both merger and termination is condemned by the confusion it would engender and by the fact that it has no apparent basis in ERISA. Even from a policy standpoint, the PBGC\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the statute is eminently reasonable because termination by merger could have detrimental consequences for the participants and beneficiaries of a single-employer plan, as well as for plan sponsors. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9314.

427 F.\xc2\xa03d 668, reversed and remanded.

JEFFREY H. BECK, liquidating trustee of the\nESTATES OF crown vantage, inc. and\ncrown paper coMpany, PETITIONER\nv. PACE INTERNATIONAL UNION et\xc2\xa0al.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We decide in this case whether an employer that sponsors and administers a single-employer defined-benefit pension plan has a fiduciary obligation under the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA), 88 Stat. 829, as amended, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001 et seq., to consider a merger with a multiemployer plan as a method of terminating the plan.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Crown Paper and its parent entity, Crown Vantage (the two hereinafter referred to in the singular as Crown), employed 2,600 persons in seven paper mills. PACE International Union, a respondent here, represented employees covered by 17 of Crown\xe2\x80\x99s defined-benefit pension plans. A defined-benefit plan, \xe2\x80\x9cas its name implies, is one where the employee, upon retirement, is entitled to a fixed periodic payment.\xe2\x80\x9d Commissioner v. Keystone Consol. Industries, Inc., 508 U. S. 152, 154 (1993). In such a plan, the employer generally shoulders the investment risk. It is the employer who must make up for any deficits, but also the employer who enjoys the fruits (whether in the form of lower plan contributions or sometimes a reversion of assets) if plan investments perform beyond expectations. See Hughes Aircraft Co. v. Jacobson, 525 U. S. 432, 439\xe2\x80\x93440 (1999). In this case, Crown served as both plan sponsor and plan administrator.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In March 2000, Crown filed for bankruptcy and proceeded to liquidate its assets. ERISA allows employers to terminate their pension plans voluntarily, see Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation v. LTV Corp., 496 U. S. 633, 638 (1990), and in the summer of 2001, Crown began to consider a \xe2\x80\x9cstandard termination,\xe2\x80\x9d a condition of which is that the terminated plans have sufficient assets to cover benefit liabilities. \xc2\xa71341(b)(1)(D); id., at 638\xe2\x80\x93639. Crown focused in particular on the possibility of a standard termination through purchase of annuities, one statutorily specified method of plan termination. See \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A)(i). PACE, however, had ideas of its own. It interjected itself into Crown\xe2\x80\x99s termination discussions and proposed that, rather than buy annuities, Crown instead merge the plans covering PACE union members with the PACE Industrial Union Management Pension Fund (PIUMPF), a multiemployer or \xe2\x80\x9cTaft-Hartley\xe2\x80\x9d plan. See \xc2\xa71002(37). Under the terms of the PACE-proposed agreement, Crown would be required to convey all plan assets to PIUMPF; PIUMPF would assume all plan liabilities.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Crown took PACE\xe2\x80\x99s merger offer under advisement. As it reviewed annuitization bids, however, it discovered that it had overfunded certain of its pension plans, so that purchasing annuities would allow it to retain a projected $5 million reversion for its creditors after satisfying its obligations to plan participants and beneficiaries. See \xc2\xa71344(d)(1) (providing for reversion upon plan termination where certain conditions are met). Under PACE\xe2\x80\x99s merger proposal, by contrast, the $5 million would go to PIUMPF. What is more, the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation (PBGC), which administers an insurance program to protect plan benefits, agreed to withdraw the proofs of claim it had filed against Crown in the bankruptcy proceedings if Crown went ahead with an annuity purchase. Crown had evidently heard enough. It consolidated 12 of its pension plans[Footnote 1] into a single plan, and terminated that plan through the purchase of an $84 million annuity. That annuity fully satisfied Crown\xe2\x80\x99s obligations to plan participants and beneficiaries and allowed Crown to reap the $5 million reversion in surplus funds.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0PACE and two plan participants, also respondents here (we will refer to all respondents collectively as PACE), thereafter filed an adversary action against Crown in the Bankruptcy Court, alleging that Crown\xe2\x80\x99s directors had breached their fiduciary duties under ERISA by neglecting to give diligent consideration to PACE\xe2\x80\x99s merger proposal. The Bankruptcy Court sided with PACE. It found that the decision whether to purchase annuities or merge with PIUMPF was a fiduciary decision, and that Crown had breached its fiduciary obligations by giving insufficient study to the PIUMPF proposal. Rather than ordering Crown to cancel its annuity (which would have resulted in a substantial penalty payable to Crown\xe2\x80\x99s annuity provider), the Bankruptcy Court instead issued a preliminary injunction preventing Crown from obtaining the $5 million reversion. It subsequently approved a distribution of that reversion for the benefit of plan participants and beneficiaries, which distribution was stayed pending appeal.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner, the trustee of the Crown bankruptcy estates, appealed the Bankruptcy-Court decision to the District Court, which affirmed in relevant part, as did the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit. The Ninth Circuit acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9cthe decision to terminate a pension plan is a business decision not subject to ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s fiduciary obligations,\xe2\x80\x9d but reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9cthe implementation of a decision to terminate\xe2\x80\x9d is fiduciary in nature. 427 F.\xc2\xa03d 668, 673 (2005). It then determined that merger was a permissible means of plan termination and that Crown therefore had a fiduciary obligation to consider PACE\xe2\x80\x99s merger proposal seriously, which it had failed to do. Petitioner thereafter sought rehearing in the Court of Appeals, this time with the support of the PBGC and the Department of Labor, who agreed with petitioner that the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment was in error. The Ninth Circuit held to its original decision, and we granted certiorari. 549 U. S. ___ (2007).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Crown\xe2\x80\x99s operation of its defined-benefit pension plans placed it in dual roles as plan sponsor and plan administrator; an employer\xe2\x80\x99s fiduciary duties under ERISA are implicated only when it acts in the latter capacity. Which hat the employer is proverbially wearing depends upon the nature of the function performed, see Hughes Aircraft Co., supra, at 444, and is an inquiry that is aided by the common law of trusts which serves as ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s backdrop, see Pegram v. Herdrich, 530 U. S. 211, 224 (2000); Lockheed Corp. v. Spink, 517 U. S. 882, 890 (1996).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is well established in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases that an employer\xe2\x80\x99s decision whether to terminate an ERISA plan is a settlor function immune from ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s fiduciary obligations. See, e.g., ibid.; Curtiss-Wright Corp. v. Schoonejongen, 514 U. S. 73, 78 (1995). And because \xe2\x80\x9cdecision[s] regarding the form or structure\xe2\x80\x9d of a plan are generally settlor functions, Hughes Aircraft Co., 525 U. S., at 444, PACE acknowledges that the decision to merge plans is \xe2\x80\x9cnormally [a] plan sponsor decisio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d as well. Brief for Respondents 13, n.\xc2\xa05, 20\xe2\x80\x9321; see also Malia v. General Electric Co., 23 F.\xc2\xa03d 828, 833 (CA3 1994) (holding that employer\xe2\x80\x99s decision to merge plans \xe2\x80\x9cd[id] not invoke the fiduciary duty provisions of ERISA\xe2\x80\x9d). But PACE says that its proposed merger was different, because the PIUMPF merger represented a method of terminating the Crown plans. And just as ERISA imposed on Crown a fiduciary obligation in its selection of an appropriate annuity provider when terminating through annuities, see 29 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72509.95\xe2\x80\x931, 4041.28(c)(3) (2006), so too, PACE argues, did it require Crown to consider merger.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The idea that the decision whether to merge could switch from a settlor to a fiduciary function depending upon the context in which the merger proposal is raised is an odd one. But once it is realized that a merger is simply a transfer of assets and liabilities, PACE\xe2\x80\x99s argument becomes somewhat more plausible: The purchase of an annuity is akin to a transfer of assets and liabilities (to an insurance company), and if Crown was subject to fiduciary duties in selecting an annuity provider, why could it automatically disregard PIUMPF simply because PIUMPF happened to be a multiemployer plan rather than an insurer? There is, however, an antecedent question. In order to affirm the judgment below, we would have to conclude (as the Ninth Circuit did) that merger is, in the first place, a permissible form of plan termination under ERISA. That requires us to delve into the statute\xe2\x80\x99s provisions for plan termination.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0ERISA sets forth the exclusive procedures for the standard termination of single-employer pension plans. \xc2\xa71341(a)(1); Hughes Aircraft Co., supra, at 446. Those procedures are exhaustive, setting detailed rules for, inter alia, notice by the plan to affected parties, \xc2\xa71341(a)(2), review by the PBGC, \xc2\xa71341(b)(2)(A), (C), and final distribution of plan funds, \xc2\xa71341(b)(2)(D), \xc2\xa71344. See generally E. Veal & E. Mackiewicz, Pension Plan Terminations 43\xe2\x80\x9361 (2d ed. 1998) (hereinafter Veal & Mackiewicz). At issue in this case is \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A), the provision of ERISA setting forth the permissible methods of terminating a single-employer plan and distributing plan assets to participants and beneficiaries. Section 1341(b)(3)(A) provides as follows:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIn connection with any final distribution of assets pursuant to the standard termination of the plan under this subsection, the plan administrator shall distribute the assets in accordance with section 1344 of this title. In distributing such assets, the plan administrator shall\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(i) purchase irrevocable commitments from an insurer to provide all benefit liabilities under the plan, or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(ii) in accordance with the provisions of the plan and any applicable regulations, otherwise fully provide all benefit liabilities under the plan. . . .\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nThe PBGC\xe2\x80\x99s regulations impose in substance the same requirements. See 29 CFR \xc2\xa74041.28(c)(1). Title 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71344, which is referred to in \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A), sets forth a specific order of priority for asset distribution, including (under certain circumstances) reversions of excess funds to the plan sponsor, see \xc2\xa71344(d)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The parties to this case all agree that \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A)(i) refers to the purchase of annuities, see 29 CFR \xc2\xa74001.2 (defining \xe2\x80\x9cirrevocable commitment\xe2\x80\x9d), and that \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A)(ii) allows for lump-sum distributions at present discounted value (including rollovers into individual retirement accounts). As PACE concedes, purchase of annuity contracts and lump-sum payments are \xe2\x80\x9cby far the most common distribution methods.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 45; see also Veal & Mackiewicz 72\xe2\x80\x9373 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe basic alternatives are the purchase of annuity contracts or some form of lump-sum cashout\xe2\x80\x9d). To affirm the Ninth Circuit, we would have to decide that merger is a permissible method as well.[Footnote 3] And we would have to do that over the objection of the PBGC, which (joined by the Department of Labor) disagrees with the Ninth Circuit, taking the position that \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A) does not permit merger as a method of termination because (in its view) merger is an alternative to (rather than an example of) plan termination. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 8, 17\xe2\x80\x9330. We have traditionally deferred to the PBGC when interpreting ERISA, for \xe2\x80\x9cto attempt to answer these questions without the views of the agencies responsible for enforcing ERISA, would be to embar[k] upon a voyage without a compass.\xe2\x80\x9d Mead Corp. v. Tilley, 490 U. S. 714, 722, 725\xe2\x80\x93726 (1989) (internal quotation marks omitted); see also LTV Corp., 496 U. S., at 648, 651. In reviewing the judgment below, we thus must examine \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the PBGC\xe2\x80\x99s policy is based upon a permissible construction of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 648.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We believe it is. PACE has \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to persuade us that the PBGC\xe2\x80\x99s views are unreasonable,\xe2\x80\x9d Mead Corp., supra, at 725. At the outset, it must be acknowledged that the statute, with its general residual clause in \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A)(ii), is potentially more embracing of alternative methods of plan termination (whatever they may be) than longstanding ERISA practice, which appears to have employed almost exclusively annuities and lump-sum payments. But we think that the statutory text need not be read to include mergers, and indeed that the PBGC offers the better reading in excluding them. Most obviously, Congress nowhere expressly provided for merger as a permissible means of termination. Merger is not mentioned in \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A), much less in any of \xc2\xa71341\xe2\x80\x99s many subsections. Indeed, merger is expressly provided for in an entirely separate set of statutory sections (of which more in a moment, see infra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9313). PACE nevertheless maintains that merger is clearly covered under \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A)(ii)\xe2\x80\x99s residual clause, which refers to a distribution of assets that \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise fully provide[s] all benefit liabilities under the plan.\xe2\x80\x9d By PACE\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning, annuities are covered under \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A)(i); annuities are\xe2\x80\x94by virtue of the word \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94an example of a means by which a plan may \xe2\x80\x9cfully provide all benefit liabilities under the plan,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A)(ii); and therefore, \xe2\x80\x9cat the least,\xe2\x80\x9d any method of termination that is the \xe2\x80\x9clegal equivalent\xe2\x80\x9d of annuitization is permitted, Brief for Respondents 23. Merger, PACE argues, is such a legal equivalent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not find the statute so clear. Even assuming that PACE is right about \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that the word indicates that annuities are one example of satisfying the residual clause in \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A)(ii)\xe2\x80\x94we still do not find mergers covered with the clarity necessary to disregard the PBGC\xe2\x80\x99s considered views. Surely the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise fully provide all benefit liabilities under the plan\xe2\x80\x9d is not without some teeth. And we think it would be reasonable for the PBGC to determine both that merger is not like the purchase of annuities in its ability to \xe2\x80\x9cfully provide all benefit liabilities under the plan,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s distinct treatment of merger and termination provides clear evidence that one is not an example of the other. Three points strike us as especially persuasive in these regards.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, terminating a plan through purchase of annuities (like terminating through distribution of lump-sum payments) formally severs the applicability of ERISA to plan assets and employer obligations. Upon purchasing annuities, the employer is no longer subject to ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s multitudinous requirements, such as (to name just one) payment of insurance premiums to the PBGC, \xc2\xa71307(a). And the PBGC is likewise no longer liable for the deficiency in the event that the plan becomes insolvent; there are no more benefits for it to guarantee. The assets of the plan are wholly removed from the ERISA system, and plan participants and beneficiaries must rely primarily (if not exclusively) on state-contract remedies if they do not receive proper payments or are otherwise denied access to their funds. Further, from the standpoint of the participants and beneficiaries, the risk associated with an annuity relates solely to the solvency of an insurance company, and not the performance of the merged plan\xe2\x80\x99s investments.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Merger is fundamentally different: it represents a continuation rather than a cessation of the ERISA regime. If Crown were to have merged its pension plans into PIUMPF, the plan assets would have been combined with the assets of the multiemployer plan, where they could then be used to satisfy the benefit liabilities of participants and beneficiaries other than those from the original Crown plans. Those assets would remain within ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s purview, the PBGC would maintain responsibility for them, and if Crown continued to employ the plan participants it too would remain subject to ERISA. Finally, plan participants and beneficiaries would have their recourse not through state-contract law, but through the ERISA system, just as they had prior to merger.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, in a standard termination ERISA allows the employer to (under certain circumstances) recoup surplus funds, \xc2\xa71344(d)(1), (3), as Crown sought to do here. But ERISA forbids employers to obtain a reversion in the absence of a termination: \xe2\x80\x9cA valid plan termination is a prerequisite to a reversion of surplus plan assets to an employer.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Brief in Opposition 15a (PBGC Opinion Letter 85\xe2\x80\x9325 (Oct. 11, 1985); see also Veal & Mackiewicz 164\xe2\x80\x93165. Crown could not simply extract the $5 million surplus from its plans, nor could it have done so once those assets had transferred to PIUMPF. This would have run up against ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s anti-inurement provision, which prohibits employers from misappropriating plan assets for their own benefit. See \xc2\xa71103(c). Consequently, we think the PBGC was entirely reasonable in declining to recognize as a form of termination a mechanism that would preclude the receipt of surplus funds, which is specifically authorized upon termination.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, the structure of ERISA amply (if not conclusively) supports the conclusion that \xc2\xa71341(b)(3)(A)(ii) does not cover merger. As noted above, merger is nowhere mentioned in \xc2\xa71341, and is instead dealt with in an entirely different set of statutory sections setting forth entirely different rules and procedures. Compare \xc2\xa71058 (general merger provision), \xc2\xa71411 (mergers between multiemployer plans), and \xc2\xa71412 (mergers between multiemployer and single-employer plans) with \xc2\xa71341 (termination of single-employer plans), \xc2\xa71341a (termination of multiemployer plans); see generally Veal & Mackiewicz 31\xe2\x80\x9340 (describing merger as an alternative to plan termination). Section 1058, the general merger provision, in fact quite clearly contemplates that merger and termination are not one and the same, forbidding merger \xe2\x80\x9cunless each participant in the plan would (if the plan then terminated) receive a benefit immediately after the merger . . . which is equal to or greater than the benefit he would have been entitled to receive immediately before the merger . . . (if the plan had then terminated).\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As for the different rules and procedures governing termination and merger: Most critically, plans seeking to terminate must provide advance notice to the PBGC, as well as extensive actuarial information. \xc2\xa71341(b)(2)(A). The PBGC has the authority to halt the termination if it determines that plan assets are insufficient to cover plan liabilities. \xc2\xa71341(b)(2)(C). Merger, by contrast, involves considerably less PBGC oversight, and the PBGC has no similar ability to cancel, see Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 24. And the rules governing notice to the PBGC are either different or nonexistent. Section 1412, the provision governing merger between a single and multiemployer plan (the form of merger contemplated by PACE\xe2\x80\x99s proposal) makes no mention of early notice to the PBGC. And while mergers between multiemployer plans do require 120-days advance notice, \xc2\xa71411(b)(1), this still differs from the general notice provision for termination of single-employer plans, which requires notice to the PBGC \xe2\x80\x9c[a]s soon as practicable\xe2\x80\x9d after notice is given to affected parties, \xc2\xa71341(b)(2)(A). Relatedly, \xc2\xa71341(a)(2) also requires that, in a standard termination, written notice to plan participants and beneficiaries include \xe2\x80\x9cany related additional information required in regulations of the [PBGC].\xe2\x80\x9d Those regulations require, among other things, that the plan inform participants and beneficiaries that upon distribution, \xe2\x80\x9cthe PBGC no longer guarantees . . . plan benefits.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 CFR \xc2\xa74041.23(b)(9). (This requirement of course has no relevance to a merger, because after a merger the PBGC continues to guarantee plan benefits.)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0PACE believes that these procedural differences can be ironed over rather easily. It insists:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cMany plan mergers take place without intent to terminate a plan; in those cases, the requirements for plan merger can be followed without consulting the requirements for plan termination. Conversely, many plan terminations take place without an associated merger; in those cases there is no need to consult the requirements for mergers. But if a plan sponsor intends to use merger as a method of implementing a plan termination, it simply must follow the rules for both merger and termination.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 36.

\n

\nPACE similarly explains that while the PBGC does not approve \xe2\x80\x9cordinary merger[s],\xe2\x80\x9d PBGC approval would be necessary when a merger is designed to terminate a plan. Id., at 37. The confusion invited by PACE\xe2\x80\x99s proposed framework is alone enough to condemn it. How could a plan be sure that it was in one box rather than the other? To avoid the risk of liability, should it simply follow both sets of rules all of the time? PACE\xe2\x80\x99s proposal is flawed for another reason as well: It has no apparent basis in the statute. The separate provisions governing termination and merger quite clearly treat the two as wholly different transactions, with no exception for the case where merger is used for termination.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For all of the foregoing reasons, we believe that the PBGC\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the statute is a permissible one, and indeed the more plausible. Crown did not breach its fiduciary obligations in failing to consider PACE\xe2\x80\x99s merger proposal because merger is not a permissible form of termination. Even from a policy standpoint, the PBGC\xe2\x80\x99s choice is an eminently reasonable one, since termination by merger could have detrimental consequences for plan beneficiaries and plan sponsors alike. When a single-employer plan is merged into a multiemployer plan, the original participants and beneficiaries become dependent upon the financial well-being of the multiemployer plan and its contributing members. Assets of the single-employer plan (which in this case were capable of fully funding plan liabilities) may be used to satisfy commitments owed to other participants and beneficiaries of the (possibly underfunded) multiemployer plan. The PBGC believes that this arrangement creates added risk for participants and beneficiaries of the original plan, particularly in view of the lesser guarantees that the PBGC provides to multiemployer plans, compare \xc2\xa71322 with \xc2\xa71322a. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 29, and n.\xc2\xa011. For employers, the ill effects are demonstrated by the facts of this very case: by diligently funding its pension plans, Crown became the bait for a union bent on obtaining a surplus that was rightfully Crown\xe2\x80\x99s. All this after Crown purchased an annuity that none dispute was sufficient to satisfy its commitments to plan participants and beneficiaries.

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that merger is not a permissible method of terminating a single-employer defined-benefit pension plan. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.
