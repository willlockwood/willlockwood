\n\t\t\t\tGOLAN et\xc2\xa0al. v. HOLDER, ATTORNEY GENERAL, et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x93545.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 5, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 18, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tThe Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works (Berne), which took effect in 1886, is the principal accord governing international copyright relations. Berne\xe2\x80\x99s 164 member states agree to provide a minimum level of copyright protection and to treat authors from other member countries as well as they treat their own. Of central importance in this case, Article 18 of Berne requires countries to protect the works of other member states unless the works\xe2\x80\x99 copyright term has expired in either the country where protection is claimed or the country of origin. A different system of transnational copyright protection long prevailed in this country. Throughout most of the 20th century, the only foreign authors eligible for Copyright Act protection were those whose countries granted reciprocal rights to American authors and whose works were printed in the United States. Despite Article 18, when the United States joined Berne in 1989, it did not protect any foreign works lodged in the U.\xc2\xa0S. public domain, many of them works never protected here. In 1994, however, the Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights mandated implementation of Berne\xe2\x80\x99s first 21 articles, on pain of enforcement by the World Trade Organization.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn response, Congress applied the term of protection available to U.\xc2\xa0S. works to preexisting works from Berne member countries. Section 514 of the Uruguay Round Agreements Act (URAA) grants copyright protection to works protected in their country of origin, but lacking protection in the United States for any of three reasons: The United States did not protect works from the country of origin at the time of publication; the United States did not protect sound recordings fixed before 1972; or the author had not complied with certain U.\xc2\xa0S. statutory formalities. Works encompassed by \xc2\xa7514 are granted the protection they would have enjoyed had the United States maintained copyright relations with the author\xe2\x80\x99s country or removed formalities incompatible with Berne. As a consequence of the barriers to U.\xc2\xa0S. copyright protection prior to \xc2\xa7514\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, foreign works \xe2\x80\x9crestored\xe2\x80\x9d to protection by the measure had entered the public domain in this country. To cushion the impact of their placement in protected status, \xc2\xa7514 provides ameliorating accommodations for parties who had exploited affected works before the URAA was enacted.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners are orchestra conductors, musicians, publishers, and others who formerly enjoyed free access to works \xc2\xa7514 removed from the public domain. They maintain that Congress, in passing \xc2\xa7514, exceeded its authority under the Copyright Clause and transgressed \n \nFirst Amendment limitations. The District Court granted the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s motion for summary judgment. Affirming in part, the Tenth Circuit agreed that Congress had not offended the Copyright Clause, but concluded that \xc2\xa7514 required further \n \nFirst Amendment inspection in light of Eldred v. Ashcroft, \n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 186\n. On remand, the District Court granted summary judgment to petitioners on the \n \nFirst Amendment claim, holding that \xc2\xa7514\xe2\x80\x99s constriction of the public domain was not justified by any of the asserted federal interests. The Tenth Circuit reversed, ruling that \xc2\xa7514 was narrowly tailored to fit the important government aim of protecting U.\xc2\xa0S. copyright holders\xe2\x80\x99 interests abroad.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t1.\xc2\xa0Section 514 does not exceed Congress\xe2\x80\x99 authority under the Copyright Clause. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9323.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The text of the Copyright Clause does not exclude application of copyright protection to works in the public domain. Eldred is largely dispositive of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claim that the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s confinement of a copyright\xe2\x80\x99s lifespan to a \xe2\x80\x9climited Tim[e]\xe2\x80\x9d prevents the removal of works from the public domain. In Eldred, the Court upheld the Copyright Term Extension Act (CTEA), which extended, by 20 years, the terms of existing copyrights. The text of the Copyright Clause, the Court observed, contains no \xe2\x80\x9ccommand that a time prescription, once set, becomes forever \xe2\x80\x98fixed\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98inalterable,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and the Court declined to infer any such command. 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 199. The construction petitioners tender here is similarly infirm. The terms afforded works restored by \xc2\xa7514 are no less \xe2\x80\x9climited\xe2\x80\x9d than those the CTEA lengthened. Nor had the \xe2\x80\x9climited Tim[e]\xe2\x80\x9d already passed for the works at issue here\xe2\x80\x94many of them works formerly denied any U.\xc2\xa0S. copyright protection\xe2\x80\x94for a period of exclusivity must begin before it may end. Petitioners also urge that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position would allow Congress to legislate perpetual copyright terms by instituting successive \xe2\x80\x9climited\xe2\x80\x9d terms as prior terms expire. But as in Eldred, such hypothetical misbehavior is far afield from this case. In aligning the United States with other nations bound by Berne, Congress can hardly be charged with a design to move stealthily toward a perpetual copyright regime. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Historical practice corroborates the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the Copyright Clause to permit the protection of previously unprotected works. In the Copyright Act of 1790, the First Congress protected works that had been freely reproducible under State copyright laws. Subsequent actions confirm that Congress has not understood the Copyright Clause to preclude protection for existing works. Several private bills restored the copyrights and patents of works and inventions previously in the public domain. Congress has also passed generally applicable legislation granting copyrights and patents to works and inventions that had lost protection. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9319.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Petitioners also argue that \xc2\xa7514 fails to \xe2\x80\x9cpromote the Progress of Science\xe2\x80\x9d as contemplated by the initial words of the Copyright Clause. Specifically, they claim that because \xc2\xa7514 affects only works already created, it cannot meet the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s objective. The creation of new works, however, is not the sole way Congress may promote \xe2\x80\x9cScience,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., knowledge and learning. In Eldred, this Court rejected a nearly identical argument, concluding that the Clause does not demand that each copyright provision, examined discretely, operate to induce new works. Rather the Clause \xe2\x80\x9cempowers Congress to determine the intellectual property regimes that, overall, in that body\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, will serve the ends of the Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 222. Nothing in the text or history of the Copyright Clause, moreover, confines the \xe2\x80\x9cProgress of Science\xe2\x80\x9d exclusively to \xe2\x80\x9cincentives for creation.\xe2\x80\x9d Historical evidence, congressional practice, and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions, in fact, suggest that inducing the dissemination of existing works is an appropriate means to promote science. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9322.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0Considered against this backdrop, \xc2\xa7514 falls comfortably within Congress\xe2\x80\x99 Copyright Clause authority. Congress had reason to believe that a well-functioning international copyright system would encourage the dissemination of existing and future works. And testimony informed Congress that full compliance with Berne would expand the foreign markets available to U.\xc2\xa0S. authors and invigorate protection against piracy of U.\xc2\xa0S. works abroad, thus benefitting copyright-intensive industries stateside and inducing greater investment in the creative process. This Court has no warrant to reject Congress\xe2\x80\x99 rational judgment that exemplary adherence to Berne would serve the objectives of the Copyright Clause. Pp.\xc2\xa022\xe2\x80\x9323.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t2.\xc2\xa0The \n \nFirst Amendment does not inhibit the restoration authorized by \xc2\xa7514. Pp.\xc2\xa023\xe2\x80\x9332.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The pathmarking Eldred decision is again instructive. There, the Court held that the CTEA\xe2\x80\x99s enlargement of a copyright\xe2\x80\x99s duration did not offend the \n \nFirst Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s freedom of expression guarantee. Recognizing that some restriction on expression is the inherent and intended effect of every grant of copyright, the Court observed that the Framers regarded copyright protection not simply as a limit on the manner in which expressive works may be used, but also as an \xe2\x80\x9cengine of free expression.\xe2\x80\x9d 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 219. The \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional contours\xe2\x80\x9d of copyright protection, i.e., the \xe2\x80\x9cidea/expression dichotomy\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cfair use\xe2\x80\x9d defense, moreover, serve as \xe2\x80\x9cbuilt-in \n \nFirst Amendment accommodations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Given the speech-protective purposes and safeguards embraced by copyright law, there was no call for the heightened review sought in Eldred. The Court reaches the same conclusion here. Section 514 leaves undisturbed the idea/expression distinction and the fair use defense. Moreover, Congress adopted measures to ease the transition from a national scheme to an international copyright regime. Pp.\xc2\xa023\xe2\x80\x9326.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Petitioners claim that \n \nFirst Amendment interests of a higher order are at stake because they\xe2\x80\x94unlike their Eldred counterparts\xe2\x80\x94enjoyed \xe2\x80\x9cvested rights\xe2\x80\x9d in works that had already entered the public domain. Their contentions depend on an argument already considered and rejected, namely, that the Constitution renders the public domain largely untouchable by Congress. Nothing in the historical record, subsequent congressional practice, or this Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisprudence warrants exceptional \n \nFirst Amendment solicitude for copyrighted works that were once in the public domain. Congress has several times adjusted copyright law to protect new categories of works as well as works previously in the public domain. Section 514, moreover, does not impose a blanket prohibition on public access. The question is whether would-be users of certain foreign works must pay for their desired use of the author\xe2\x80\x99s expression, or else limit their exploitation to \xe2\x80\x9cfair use\xe2\x80\x9d of those works. By fully implementing Berne, Congress ensured that these works, like domestic and most other foreign works, would be governed by the same legal regime. Section 514 simply placed foreign works in the position they would have occupied if the current copyright regime had been in effect when those works were created and first published. Pp.\xc2\xa026\xe2\x80\x9330.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works (Berne Convention or Berne), which took effect in 1886, is the principal accord governing international copyright relations. Latecomer to the international copyright regime launched by Berne, the United States joined the Convention in 1989. To perfect U.\xc2\xa0S. implementation of Berne, and as part of our response to the Uruguay Round of multilateral trade negotiations, Congress, in 1994, gave works enjoying copyright protection abroad the same full term of protection available to U.\xc2\xa0S. works. Congress did so in \xc2\xa7514 of the Uruguay Round Agreements Act (URAA), which grants copyright protection to preexisting works of Berne member countries, protected in their country of origin, but lacking protection in the United States for any of three reasons: The United States did not protect works from the country of origin at the time of publication; the United States did not protect sound recordings fixed before 1972; or the author had failed to comply with U.\xc2\xa0S. statutory formalities (formalities Congress no longer requires as prerequisites to copyright protection).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe URAA accords no protection to a foreign work after its full copyright term has expired, causing it to fall into the public domain, whether under the laws of the country of origin or of this country. Works encompassed by \xc2\xa7514 are granted the protection they would have enjoyed had the United States maintained copyright relations with the author\xe2\x80\x99s country or removed formalities incompatible with Berne. Foreign authors, however, gain no credit for the protection they lacked in years prior to \xc2\xa7514\xe2\x80\x99s enactment. They therefore enjoy fewer total years of exclusivity than do their U.\xc2\xa0S. counterparts. As a consequence of the barriers to U.\xc2\xa0S. copyright protection prior to the enactment of \xc2\xa7514, foreign works \xe2\x80\x9crestored\xe2\x80\x9d to protection by the measure had entered the public domain in this country. To cushion the impact of their placement in protected status, Congress included in \xc2\xa7514 ameliorating accommodations for parties who had exploited affected works before the URAA was enacted.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners include orchestra conductors, musicians, pub-lishers, and others who formerly enjoyed free access to works \xc2\xa7514 removed from the public domain. They maintain that the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Copyright and Patent Clause, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa08, and \n \nFirst Amendment both decree the invalidity of \xc2\xa7514. Under those prescriptions of our highest law, petitioners assert, a work that has entered the public domain, for whatever reason, must forever remain there.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn accord with the judgment of the Tenth Circuit, we conclude that \xc2\xa7514 does not transgress constitutional limitations on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 authority. Neither the Copyright and Patent Clause nor the \n \nFirst Amendment, we hold, makes the public domain, in any and all cases, a territory that works may never exit.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMembers of the Berne Union agree to treat authors from other member countries as well as they treat their own. Berne Convention, Sept. 9, 1886, as revised at Stockholm on July 14, 1967, Art. 1, 5(1), 828 U. N. T. S. 221, 225, 231\xe2\x80\x93233. Nationals of a member country, as well as any author who publishes in one of Berne\xe2\x80\x99s 164 member states, thus enjoy copyright protection in nations across the globe. Art. 2(6), 3. Each country, moreover, must afford at least the minimum level of protection specified by Berne. The copyright term must span the author\xe2\x80\x99s lifetime, plus at least 50 additional years, whether or not the author has complied with a member state\xe2\x80\x99s legal formalities. Art. 5(2), 7(1). And, as relevant here, a work must be protected abroad unless its copyright term has expired in either the country where protection is claimed or the country of origin. Art. 18(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA different system of transnational copyright protection long prevailed in this country. Until 1891, foreign works were categorically excluded from Copyright Act protection. Throughout most of the 20th century, the only eligible foreign authors were those whose countries granted reciprocal rights to U.\xc2\xa0S. authors and whose works were print ed in the United States. See Act of Mar. 3, 1891, \xc2\xa73, 13, \n\n26Stat.\n1107,\n1110; Patry, The United States and Inter-national Copyright Law, 40 Houston L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 749, 750 (2003).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t For domestic and foreign authors alike, protection hinged on compliance with notice, registration, and renewal formalities.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe United States became party to Berne\xe2\x80\x99s multilateral, formality-free copyright regime in 1989. Initially, Congress adopted a \xe2\x80\x9cminimalist approach\xe2\x80\x9d to compliance with the Convention. H. R. Rep. No. 100\xe2\x80\x93609, p. 7 (1988) (hereinafter BCIA House Report). The Berne Convention Implementation Act of 1988 (BCIA), \n\n102Stat.\n2853, made \xe2\x80\x9conly those changes to American copyright law that [were] clearly required under the treaty\xe2\x80\x99s provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d BCIA House Report, at 7. Despite Berne\xe2\x80\x99s instruction that member countries\xe2\x80\x94including \xe2\x80\x9cnew accessions to the Union\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94protect foreign works under copyright in the country of origin, Art. 18(1) and (4), 828 U.\xc2\xa0N.\xc2\xa0T.\xc2\xa0S., at 251, the BCIA accorded no protection for \xe2\x80\x9cany work that is in the public domain in the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa712, \n\n102Stat.\n2860. Protection of future foreign works, the BCIA indicated, satisfied Article 18. See \xc2\xa72(3), \n\n102Stat.\n2853 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe amendments made by this Act, together with the law as it exists on the date of the enactment of this Act, satisfy the obligations of the United States in adhering to the Berne Convention .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). Congress indicated, however, that it had not definitively rejected \xe2\x80\x9cretroactive\xe2\x80\x9d protection for preexisting foreign works; instead it had punted on this issue of Berne\xe2\x80\x99s implementation, deferring consideration until \xe2\x80\x9ca more thorough examination of Constitutional, commercial, and consumer considerations is possible.\xe2\x80\x9d BCIA House Report, at 51, 52.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe minimalist approach essayed by the United States did not sit well with other Berne members.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t While negotiations were ongoing over the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), Mexican authorities complained about the United States\xe2\x80\x99 refusal to grant protection, in accord with Article 18, to Mexican works that remained under copyright domestically. See Intellectual Property and International Issues, Hearings before the Subcommittee on Intellectual Property and Judicial Administration, House Committee on the Judiciary, 102d Cong., 1st Sess., 168 (1991) (statement of Ralph Oman, U.\xc2\xa0S. Register of Copyrights).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The Register of Copyrights also reported \xe2\x80\x9cquestions\xe2\x80\x9d from Turkey, Egypt, and Austria. Ibid. Thailand and Russia balked at protecting U.\xc2\xa0S. works, copyrighted here but in those countries\xe2\x80\x99 public domains, until the United States reciprocated with respect to their authors\xe2\x80\x99 works. URAA Joint Hearing 137 (statement of Ira S. Shapiro, General Counsel, Office of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Trade Representative (USTR)); id., at 208 (statement of Professor Shira Perlmutter); id., at 291 (statement of Jason S. Berman, Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA)).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBerne, however, did not provide a potent enforcement mechanism. The Convention contemplates dispute resolution before the International Court of Justice. Art. 33(1). But it specifies no sanctions for noncompliance and allows parties, at any time, to declare themselves \xe2\x80\x9cnot .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. bound\xe2\x80\x9d by the Convention\xe2\x80\x99s dispute resolution provision. Art. 33(2)\xe2\x80\x93(3) 828 U.\xc2\xa0N.\xc2\xa0T.\xc2\xa0S., at 277. Unsurprisingly, no enforcement actions were launched before 1994. D. Gervais, The TRIPS Agreement 213, and n. 134 (3d ed. 2008). \n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe first address petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that Congress lacked authority, under the Copyright Clause, to enact \xc2\xa7514. The Constitution states that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress shall have Power .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [t]o promote the Progress of Science .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by securing for limited Times to Authors .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the exclusive Right to their .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Writings.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa08. Petitioners find in this grant of authority an impenetrable barrier to the extension of copyright protection to authors whose writings, for whatever reason, are in the public domain. We see no such barrier in the text of the Copyright Clause, historical practice, or our precedents.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe text of the Copyright Clause does not exclude application of copyright protection to works in the public domain. Symposium, Congressional Power and Limitations Inherent in the Copyright Clause, 30 Colum. J. L. & Arts 259, 266 (2007). Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 contrary argument relies primarily on the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s confinement of a copyright\xe2\x80\x99s lifespan to a \xe2\x80\x9climited Tim[e].\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cRemoving works from the public domain,\xe2\x80\x9d they contend, \xe2\x80\x9cviolates the \xe2\x80\x98limited [t]imes\xe2\x80\x99 restriction by turning a fixed and predictable period into one that can be reset or resurrected at any time, even after it expires.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 22.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur decision in Eldred is largely dispositive of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 limited-time argument. There we addressed the question whether Congress violated the Copyright Clause when it extended, by 20 years, the terms of existing copyrights. 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 192\xe2\x80\x93193 (upholding Copyright Term Extension Act (CTEA)). Ruling that Congress acted within constitutional bounds, we declined to infer from the text of the Copyright Clause \xe2\x80\x9cthe command that a time prescription, once set, becomes forever \xe2\x80\x98fixed\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98inalterable.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 199. \xe2\x80\x9cThe word \xe2\x80\x98limited,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d we observed, \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not convey a meaning so constricted.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Rather, the term is best understood to mean \xe2\x80\x9cconfine[d] within certain bounds,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9crestrain[ed],\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccircumscribed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). The construction petitioners tender closely resembles the definition rejected in Eldred and is similarly infirm.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe terms afforded works restored by \xc2\xa7514 are no less \xe2\x80\x9climited\xe2\x80\x9d than those the CTEA lengthened. In light of Eldred, petitioners do not here contend that the term Congress has granted U.\xc2\xa0S. authors\xe2\x80\x94their lifetimes, plus 70 years\xe2\x80\x94is unlimited. See \n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7302(a). Nor do petitioners explain why terms of the same duration, as applied to foreign works, are not equally \xe2\x80\x9ccircumscribed\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cconfined.\xe2\x80\x9d See Eldred, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 199. Indeed, as earlier noted, see supra, at 2, 10, the copyrights of restored foreign works typically last for fewer years than those of their domestic counterparts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe difference, petitioners say, is that the limited time had already passed for works in the public domain. What was that limited term for foreign works once excluded from U.\xc2\xa0S. copyright protection? Exactly \xe2\x80\x9czero,\xe2\x80\x9d petitioners respond. Brief for Petitioners 22 (works in question \xe2\x80\x9creceived a specific term of protection .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. sometimes expressly set to zero\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9cat the end of that period,\xe2\x80\x9d they \xe2\x80\x9centered the public domain\xe2\x80\x9d); Tr. of Oral Arg. 52 (by \xe2\x80\x9crefusing to provide any protection for a work,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress \xe2\x80\x9cset[s] the term at zero,\xe2\x80\x9d and thereby \xe2\x80\x9ctell[s] us when the end has come\xe2\x80\x9d). We find scant sense in this argument, for surely a \xe2\x80\x9climited time\xe2\x80\x9d of exclusivity must begin before it may end.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t16\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCarried to its logical conclusion, petitioners persist, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position would allow Congress to institute a second \xe2\x80\x9climited\xe2\x80\x9d term after the first expires, a third after that, and so on. Thus, as long as Congress legislated in installments, perpetual copyright terms would be achievable. As in Eldred, the hypothetical legislative misbehavior petitioners posit is far afield from the case before us. See 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 198\xe2\x80\x93200, 209\xe2\x80\x93210. In aligning the United States with other nations bound by the Berne Convention, and thereby according equitable treatment to once dis-favored foreign authors, Congress can hardly be charged with a design to move stealthily toward a regime of perpetual copyrights.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHistorical practice corroborates our reading of the Copyright Clause to permit full U.\xc2\xa0S. compliance with Berne. \n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn occasion, however, Congress has seen fit to protect works once freely available. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnalogous patent statutes, however, were upheld in litigation.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t22\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In 1808, Congress passed a private bill restoring patent protection to Oliver Evans\xe2\x80\x99 flour mill. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis Court again upheld Congress\xe2\x80\x99 restoration of an invention to protected status in McClurg v. Kingsland, 1 How. 202 (1843). \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress has also passed generally applicable legislation granting patents and copyrights to inventions and works that had lost protection. \n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPointing to dictum in Graham v. John Deere Co. of Kansas City, \n\n383 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1966)\n, petitioners would have us look past this history. In Graham, we stated that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress may not authorize the issuance of patents whose effects are to remove existent knowledge from the public domain, or to restrict free access to materials already available.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 6; post, at 15. But as we explained in Eldred, this passage did not speak to the constitutional limits on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 copyright and patent authority. Rather, it \xe2\x80\x9caddressed an invention\xe2\x80\x99s very eligibility for patent protection.\xe2\x80\x9d 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 202, n.\xc2\xa07.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tInstalling a federal copyright system and ameliorating the interruptions of global war, it is true, presented Congress with extraordinary situations. Yet the TRIPS accord, leading the United States to comply in full measure with Berne, was also a signal event. See supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938; cf. Eldred, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 259, 264\xe2\x80\x93265 (Breyer, J., dissenting) (acknowledging importance of international uniformity advanced by U.\xc2\xa0S. efforts to conform to the Berne Convention). Given the authority we hold Congress has, we will not second-guess the political choice Congress made between leaving the public domain untouched and embracing Berne unstintingly. Cf. id., at 212\xe2\x80\x93213.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 ultimate argument as to the Copyright and Patent Clause concerns its initial words. Congress is empowered to \xe2\x80\x9cpromote the Progress of Science and useful Arts\xe2\x80\x9d by enacting systems of copyright and patent protection. U.\xc2\xa0S. Const., Art. I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa08. Perhaps counterintuitively for the contemporary reader, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 copyright authority is tied to the progress of science; its patent authority, to the progress of the useful arts. See Graham, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 5, and n.\xc2\xa01; Evans, 8 F.\xc2\xa0Cas., at 873 (Marshall, J.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe \xe2\x80\x9cProgress of Science,\xe2\x80\x9d petitioners acknowledge, refers broadly to \xe2\x80\x9cthe creation and spread of knowledge and learning.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 21; accord post, at 1. They nevertheless argue that federal legislation cannot serve the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s aim unless the legislation \xe2\x80\x9cspur[s] the creation of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. new works.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 24; accord post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932, 8, 17. Because \xc2\xa7514 deals solely with works already created, petitioners urge, it \xe2\x80\x9cprovides no plausible incentive to create new works\xe2\x80\x9d and is therefore invalid. Reply Brief 4.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t25\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe creation of at least one new work, however, is not the sole way Congress may promote knowledge and learning. In Eldred, we rejected an argument nearly identical to the one petitioners rehearse. The Eldred petitioners urged that the \xe2\x80\x9cCTEA\xe2\x80\x99s extension of existing copyrights categorically fails to \xe2\x80\x98promote the Progress of Science,\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. because it does not stimulate the creation of new works.\xe2\x80\x9d 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 211\xe2\x80\x93212. In response to this argument, we held that the Copyright Clause does not demand that each copyright provision, examined discretely, operate to induce new works. Rather, we explained, the Clause \xe2\x80\x9cempowers Congress to determine the intellectual property regimes that, overall, in that body\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, will serve the ends of the Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 222. And those permissible ends, we held, extended beyond the creation of new works. See id., at 205\xe2\x80\x93206 (rejecting the notion that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the only way to promote the progress of science [is] to provide incentives to create new works\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Perlmutter, Participation in the International Copyright System as a Means to Promote the Progress of Science and Useful Arts, 36 Loyola (LA) L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 323, 332 (2002))).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t26\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven were we writing on a clean slate, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument would be unavailing. Nothing in the text of the Copyright Clause confines the \xe2\x80\x9cProgress of Science\xe2\x80\x9d exclusively to \xe2\x80\x9cincentives for creation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 324, n.\xc2\xa05 (internal quotation marks omitted). Evidence from the founding, moreover, suggests that inducing dissemination\xe2\x80\x94as opposed to creation\xe2\x80\x94was viewed as an appropriate means to promote science. See Nachbar, Constructing Copyright\xe2\x80\x99s Mythology, 6 Green Bag 2d 37, 44 (2002) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe scope of copyright protection existing at the time of the framing,\xe2\x80\x9d trained as it was on \xe2\x80\x9cpublication, not creation,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cis inconsistent with claims that copyright must promote creative activity in order to be valid.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Until 1976, in fact, Congress made \xe2\x80\x9cfederal copyright contingent on publication[,] [thereby] providing incentives not primarily for creation,\xe2\x80\x9d but for dissemination. Perlmutter, supra, at 324, n.\xc2\xa05. Our decisions correspondingly recognize that \xe2\x80\x9ccopyright supplies the economic incentive to create and disseminate ideas.\xe2\x80\x9d Harper & Row, Publishers, Inc. v. Nation Enterprises, \n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 539, \n558 (1985)\n (emphasis added). See also Eldred, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 206.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t27\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsidered against this backdrop, \xc2\xa7514 falls comfortably within Congress\xe2\x80\x99 authority under the Copyright Clause. Congress rationally could have concluded that adherence to Berne \xe2\x80\x9cpromotes the diffusion of knowledge,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 4. A well-functioning international copyright system would likely encourage the dissemination of existing and future works. See URAA Joint Hearing 189 (statement of Professor Perlmutter). Full compliance with Berne, Congress had reason to believe, would expand the foreign markets available to U.\xc2\xa0S. authors and invigorate protection against piracy of U.\xc2\xa0S. works abroad, S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 103\xe2\x80\x93412, pp. 224, 225 (1994); URAA Joint Hearing 291 (statement of Berman, RIAA); id., at 244, 247 (statement of Smith, IIPA), thereby benefitting copyright-intensive industries stateside and inducing greater investment in the creative process.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe provision of incentives for the creation of new works is surely an essential means to advance the spread of knowledge and learning. We hold, however, that it is not the sole means Congress may use \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o promote the Progress of Science.\xe2\x80\x9d See Perlmutter, supra, at 332 (United States would \xe2\x80\x9close all flexibility\xe2\x80\x9d were the provision of incentives to create the exclusive way to promote the progress of science).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t28\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Congress determined that exem-plary adherence to Berne would serve the objectives of the Copyright Clause. We have no warrant to reject the rational judgment Congress made.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe next explain why the \n \nFirst Amendment does not inhibit the restoration authorized by \xc2\xa7514. To do so, we first recapitulate the relevant part of our pathmarking decision in Eldred. The petitioners in Eldred, like those here, argued that Congress had violated not only the \xe2\x80\x9climited Times\xe2\x80\x9d prescription of the Copyright Clause. In addition, and independently, the Eldred petitioners charged, Congress had offended the \n \nFirst Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s freedom of expression guarantee. The CTEA\xe2\x80\x99s 20-year enlargement of a copyright\xe2\x80\x99s duration, we held in Eldred, offended neither provision.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConcerning the \n \nFirst Amendment, we recognized that some restriction on expression is the inherent and in-tended effect of every grant of copyright. Noting that the \xe2\x80\x9cCopyright Clause and the \n \nFirst Amendment were adopted close in time,\xe2\x80\x9d 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 219, we observed that the Framers regarded copyright protection not simply as a limit on the manner in which expressive works may be used. They also saw copyright as an \xe2\x80\x9cengine of free expression[:] By establishing a marketable right to the use of one\xe2\x80\x99s expression, copyright supplies the economic incentive to create and disseminate ideas.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Harper & Row, 471 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 558 (internal quotation marks omitted)); see id., at 546 (\xe2\x80\x9crights conferred by copyright are designed to assure contributors to the store of knowledge a fair return for their labors\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe then described the \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional contours\xe2\x80\x9d of copyright protection, i.e., the \xe2\x80\x9cidea/expression dichotomy\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cfair use\xe2\x80\x9d defense.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t29\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Both are recognized in our jurisprudence as \xe2\x80\x9cbuilt-in \n \nFirst Amendment accommodations.\xe2\x80\x9d Eldred, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 219; see Harper & Row, 471 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 560 (\n \nFirst Amendment protections are \xe2\x80\x9cembodied in the Copyright Act\xe2\x80\x99s distinction between copyrightable expression and uncopyrightable facts and ideas,\xe2\x80\x9d and in the \xe2\x80\x9clatitude for scholarship and comment\xe2\x80\x9d safeguarded by the fair use defense).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe idea/expression dichotomy is codified at \n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7102(b): \xe2\x80\x9cIn no case does copyright protec[t] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any idea, procedure, process, system, method of operation, concept, principle, or discovery .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. described, explained, illustrated, or embodied in [the copyrighted] work.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cDue to this [idea/expression] distinction, every idea, theory, and fact in a copyrighted work becomes instantly available for public exploitation at the moment of publication\xe2\x80\x9d; the author\xe2\x80\x99s expression alone gains copyright protection. Eldred, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 219; see Harper & Row, 471 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 556 (\xe2\x80\x9cidea/expression dichotomy strike[s] a definitional balance between the \n \nFirst Amendment and the Copyright Act by permitting free communication of facts while still protecting an author\xe2\x80\x99s expression\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe second \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional contour,\xe2\x80\x9d the fair use defense, is codified at \n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7107: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he fair use of a copyrighted work, including such use by reproduction in copies .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , for purposes such as criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching (including multiple copies for classroom use), scholarship, or research, is not an infringement of copyright.\xe2\x80\x9d This limitation on exclusivity \xe2\x80\x9callows the public to use not only facts and ideas contained in a copyrighted work, but also [the author\xe2\x80\x99s] expression itself in certain circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Eldred, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 219; see id., at 220 (\xe2\x80\x9cfair use defense affords considerable latitude for scholarship and comment, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. even for parody\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGiven the \xe2\x80\x9cspeech-protective purposes and safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d embraced by copyright law, see id., at 219, we concluded in Eldred that there was no call for the heightened review petitioners sought in that case.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t30\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t We reach the same conclusion here.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t31\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Section 514 leaves undisturbed the \xe2\x80\x9cidea/expression\xe2\x80\x9d distinction and the \xe2\x80\x9cfair use\xe2\x80\x9d defense. Moreover, Congress adopted measures to ease the transition from a national scheme to an international copyright regime: It deferred the date from which enforcement runs, and it cushioned the impact of restoration on \xe2\x80\x9creliance parties\xe2\x80\x9d who exploited foreign works denied protection before \xc2\xa7514 took effect. See supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311 (describing \n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7104A(c), (d), and (h)). See also Eldred, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 220 (describing supplemental allowances and exemp- tions available to certain users to mitigate the CTEA\xe2\x80\x99s impact).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners attempt to distinguish their challenge from the one turned away in Eldred. \n \nFirst Amendment interests of a higher order are at stake here, petitioners say, because they\xe2\x80\x94unlike their counterparts in Eldred\xe2\x80\x94enjoyed \xe2\x80\x9cvested rights\xe2\x80\x9d in works that had already entered the public domain. The limited rights they retain under copyright law\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cbuilt-in safeguards\xe2\x80\x9d are, in their view, no substitute for the unlimited use they enjoyed before \xc2\xa7514\xe2\x80\x99s enactment. Nor, petitioners urge, does \xc2\xa7514\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cunprecedented\xe2\x80\x9d foray into the public domain possess the historical pedigree that supported the term extension at issue in Eldred. Brief for Petitioners 42\xe2\x80\x9343.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHowever spun, these contentions depend on an argument we considered and rejected above, namely, that the Constitution renders the public domain largely untouch-able by Congress. Petitioners here attempt to achieve under the banner of the \n \nFirst Amendment what they could not win under the Copyright Clause: On their view of the Copyright Clause, the public domain is inviolable; as they read the \n \nFirst Amendment, the public domain is policed through heightened judicial scrutiny of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 means and ends. As we have already shown, see supra, at 13\xe2\x80\x9319, the text of the Copyright Clause and the historical record scarcely establish that \xe2\x80\x9conce a work enters the public domain,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress cannot permit anyone\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cnot even the creator\xe2\x80\x94[to] copyright it,\xe2\x80\x9d 501 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1184. And nothing in the historical record, congressional practice, or our own jurisprudence warrants exceptional \n \nFirst Amendment solicitude for copyrighted works that were once in the public domain.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t32\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Neither this challenge nor that raised in Eldred, we stress, allege Congress transgressed a gener-ally applicable \n \nFirst Amendment prohibition; we are not faced, for example, with copyright protection that hinges on the author\xe2\x80\x99s viewpoint.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Tenth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s initial opinion determined that petitioners marshaled a stronger \n \nFirst Amendment challenge than did their predecessors in Eldred, who never \xe2\x80\x9cpossessed unfettered access to any of the works at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d 501 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1193. See also id., at 1194 (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]nce the works at issue became free for anyone to copy, [petitioners] had vested \n \nFirst Amendment interests in the expressions, [thus] \xc2\xa7514\xe2\x80\x99s interference with [petitioners\xe2\x80\x99] rights is subject to \n \nFirst Amendment scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x9d). As petitioners put it in this Court, Congress impermissibly revoked their right to exploit foreign works that \xe2\x80\x9cbelonged to them\xe2\x80\x9d once the works were in the public domain. Brief for Petitioners 44\xe2\x80\x9345.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo copyright lawyers, the \xe2\x80\x9cvested rights\xe2\x80\x9d formulation might sound exactly backwards: Rights typically vest at the outset of copyright protection, in an author or rightholder. See, e.g., \n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7201(a) (\xe2\x80\x9cCopyright in a work protected .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. vests initially in the author .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). Once the term of protection ends, the works do not revest in any rightholder. Instead, the works simply lapse into the public domain. See, e.g., Berne, Art. 18(1), 828 U.\xc2\xa0N.\xc2\xa0T.\xc2\xa0S., at 251 (\xe2\x80\x9cThis Convention shall apply to all works which .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. have not yet fallen into the public domain\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d). Anyone has free access to the public domain, but no one, after the copyright term has expired, acquires ownership rights in the once-protected works.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress recurrently adjusts copyright law to protect categories of works once outside the law\xe2\x80\x99s compass. For example, Congress broke new ground when it extended copyright protection to foreign works in 1891, Act of Mar. 3, \xc2\xa713, \n\n26Stat.\n1110; to dramatic works in 1856, Act of Aug. 18, \n\n11Stat.\n138; to photographs and photographic negatives in 1865, Act of Mar. 3, \xc2\xa71, \n\n13Stat.\n540; to motion pictures in 1912, Act of Aug. 24, \n\n37Stat.\n488; to fixed sound recordings in 1972, Act of Oct. 15, 1971, \n\n85Stat.\n391; and to architectural works in 1990, Architectural Works Copyright Protection Act, \n\n104Stat.\n5133. And on several occasions, as recounted above, Congress protected works previously in the public domain, hence freely usable by the public. See supra, at 15\xe2\x80\x9319. If Congress could grant protection to these works without hazarding heightened \n \nFirst Amendment scrutiny, then what free speech principle disarms it from protecting works prematurely cast into the public domain for reasons antithetical to the Berne Convention?\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t33\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 514, we add, does not impose a blanket prohibition on public access. Petitioners protest that fair use and the idea/expression dichotomy \xe2\x80\x9care plainly inadequate to protect the speech and expression rights that Section 514 took from petitioners, or .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the public\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, \xe2\x80\x9cthe unrestricted right to perform, copy, teach and distribute the entire work, for any reason.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 46\xe2\x80\x9347. \xe2\x80\x9cPlaying a few bars of a Shostakovich symphony,\xe2\x80\x9d petitioners observe, \xe2\x80\x9cis no substitute for performing the entire work.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 47.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t34\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut Congress has not put petitioners in this bind. The question here, as in Eldred, is whether would-be users must pay for their desired use of the author\xe2\x80\x99s expression, or else limit their exploitation to \xe2\x80\x9cfair use\xe2\x80\x9d of that work. Prokofiev\xe2\x80\x99s Peter and the Wolf could once be performed free of charge; after \xc2\xa7514 the right to perform it must be obtained in the marketplace. This is the same marketplace, of course, that exists for the music of Prokofiev\xe2\x80\x99s U.\xc2\xa0S. contemporaries: works of Copland and Bernstein, for example, that enjoy copyright protection, but nevertheless appear regularly in the programs of U.\xc2\xa0S. concertgoers.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBefore we joined Berne, domestic works and some foreign works were protected under U.\xc2\xa0S. statutes and bilateral international agreements, while other foreign works were available at an artificially low (because royalty-free) cost. By fully implementing Berne, Congress ensured that most works, whether foreign or domestic, would be governed by the same legal regime. The phenomenon to which Congress responded is not new: Distortions of the same order occurred with greater frequency\xe2\x80\x94and to the detriment of both foreign and domestic authors\xe2\x80\x94when, before 1891, foreign works were excluded entirely from U.\xc2\xa0S. copyright protection. See Kampelman, The United States and International Copyright, 41 Am. J. Int\xe2\x80\x99l L. 406, 413 (1947) (\xe2\x80\x9cAmerican readers were less inclined to read the novels of Cooper or Hawthorne for a dollar when they could buy a novel of Scott or Dickens for a quarter.\xe2\x80\x9d). Section 514 continued the trend toward a harmonized copyright regime by placing foreign works in the position they would have occupied if the current regime had been in effect when those works were created and first published. Authors once deprived of protection are spared the continuing effects of that initial deprivation; \xc2\xa7514 gives them nothing more than the benefit of their labors during whatever time remains before the normal copyright term expires.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t35\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnlike petitioners, the dissent makes much of the so-called \xe2\x80\x9corphan works\xe2\x80\x9d problem. See post, at 11\xe2\x80\x9314, 23\xe2\x80\x9324. We readily acknowledge the difficulties would-be users of copyrightable materials may face in identifying or locating copyright owners. See generally U.\xc2\xa0S. Copyright Office, Report on Orphan Works 21\xe2\x80\x9340 (2006). But as the dissent concedes, see post, at 13, this difficulty is hardly peculiar to works restored under \xc2\xa7514. It similarly afflicts, for instance, U.\xc2\xa0S. libraries that attempt to catalogue U.\xc2\xa0S. books. See post, at 12. See also Brief for American Library Association et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 22 (Section 514 \xe2\x80\x9cexacerbated,\xe2\x80\x9d but did not create, the problem of orphan works); U.\xc2\xa0S. Copyright Office, supra, at 41\xe2\x80\x9344 (tracing orphan-works problem to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 elimination of formalities, commencing with the 1976 Copyright Act).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t36\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor is this a matter appropriate for judicial, as opposed to legislative, resolution. Cf. Authors Guild v. Google, Inc., 770 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 666, 677\xe2\x80\x93678 (SDNY 2011) (rejecting proposed \xe2\x80\x9cGoogle Books\xe2\x80\x9d class settlement because, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9cthe establishment of a mechanism for exploiting unclaimed books is a matter more suited for Congress than this Court\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Eldred, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 212)). Indeed, the host of policy and logistical questions identified by the dissent speak for themselves. Post, at 12. Despite \xe2\x80\x9clongstanding efforts,\xe2\x80\x9d see Authors Guild, 770 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 678 (quoting statement of Marybeth Peters), Congress has not yet passed ameliorative orphan-works legislation of the sort enacted by other Berne members, see, e.g., Canada Copyright Act, R.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C., 1985, c. C\xe2\x80\x9342, \xc2\xa777 (authorizing Copyright Board to license use of orphan works by persons unable, after making reasonable efforts, to locate the copyright owner). Heretofore, no one has suggested that the orphan-works issue should be addressed through our implementation of Berne, rather than through overarching legislation of the sort proposed in Congress and cited by the dissent. See post, at 23\xe2\x80\x9324; U.\xc2\xa0S. Copyright Office, Legal Issues in Mass Digitization 25\xe2\x80\x9329 (2011) (discussing recent legislative efforts). Our unstinting adherence to Berne may add impetus to calls for the enactment of such legislation. But resistance to Berne\xe2\x80\x99s prescriptions surely is not a necessary or proper response to the pervasive question, what should Congress do about orphan works.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress determined that U.\xc2\xa0S. interests were best served by our full participation in the dominant system of international copyright protection. Those interests include ensuring exemplary compliance with our international obligations, securing greater protection for U.\xc2\xa0S. authors abroad, and remedying unequal treatment of foreign authors. The judgment \xc2\xa7514 expresses lies well within the ken of the political branches. It is our obligation, of course, to determine whether the action Congress took, wise or not, encounters any constitutional shoal. For the reasons stated, we are satisfied it does not. The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit is therefore\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tAffirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe possibility of eliciting new production is, and always has been, an essential precondition for American copyright protection. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tYet, as the Founders recognized, monopoly is a two-edged sword. On the one hand, it can encourage production of new works. In the absence of copyright protection, anyone might freely copy the products of an author\xe2\x80\x99s creative labor, appropriating the benefits without incurring the nonrepeatable costs of creation, thereby deterring authors from exerting themselves in the first place. On the other hand, copyright tends to restrict the dissemination (and use) of works once produced either because the absence of competition translates directly into higher consumer prices or because the need to secure copying permission sometimes imposes administrative costs that make it difficult for potential users of a copyrighted work to find its owner and strike a bargain. See W. Landes & R. Posner, The Economic Structure of Intellectual Property Law 68\xe2\x80\x9370, 213\xe2\x80\x93214 (2003). Consequently, the original British copyright statute, the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Framers, and our case law all have recognized copyright\xe2\x80\x99s resulting and necessary call for balance.\n\t\t\t

L.\xc2\xa0J. 1427, 1444\xe2\x80\x931450 (2010).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt least, that was the predominant view expressed to, or by, the Founders. Patterson 93. Thomas Jefferson, for example, initially expressed great uncertainty as to whether the Constitution should authorize the grant of copyrights and patents at all, writing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe benefit even of limited monopolies is too doubtful\xe2\x80\x9d to warrant anything other than their \xe2\x80\x9csuppression.\xe2\x80\x9d Letter from Thomas Jefferson to James Madison (July 31, 1788), in 13 Papers of Thomas Jefferson 440, 443 (J. Boyd ed. 1956). James Madison also thought that \xe2\x80\x9cMonopolies .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are justly classed among the greatest nu[i]sances in Government.\xe2\x80\x9d Letter from James Madison to Thomas Jefferson (Oct. 17, 1788), in 14 id., at 16, 21 (J. Boyd ed. 1958). But he argued that \xe2\x80\x9cin certain cases\xe2\x80\x9d such as copyright, monopolies should \xe2\x80\x9cbe granted\xe2\x80\x9d (\xe2\x80\x9cwith caution, and guarded with strictness agst abuse\xe2\x80\x9d) to serve as \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation for a benefit actually gained to the community .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. which the owner might otherwise withhold from public use.\xe2\x80\x9d Mo-nopolies. Perpetuities. Corporations. Ecclesiastical Endowments. in J. Madison, Writings 756 (J. Rakove ed. 1999) (emphasis added). Jefferson eventually came to agree with Madison, supporting a limited conferral of monopoly rights but only \xe2\x80\x9cas an encouragement to men to pursue ideas which may produce utility.\xe2\x80\x9d Letter from Thomas Jefferson to Isaac McPherson (Aug. 13, 1813), in 6 Papers of Thomas Jefferson, at 379, 383 (J. Looney ed. 2009) (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis utilitarian view of copyrights and patents, embraced by Jefferson and Madison, stands in contrast to the \xe2\x80\x9cnatural rights\xe2\x80\x9d view underlying much of continental European copyright law\xe2\x80\x94a view that the English booksellers promoted in an effort to limit their losses following the enactment of the Statute of Anne and that in part motivated the enactment of some of the colonial statutes. Patterson 158\xe2\x80\x93179, 183\xe2\x80\x93192. Premised on the idea that an author or inventor has an inherent right to the fruits of his labor, it mythically stems from a legendary 6th-century statement of King Diarmed \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to every cow her calf, and accordingly to every book its copy.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d A. Birrell, Seven Lectures on the Law and History of Copyright in Books 42 (1899). That view, though perhaps reflected in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, ante, at 30, runs contrary to the more utilitarian views that influenced the writing of our own Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Copyright Clause. See S. Ricketson, The Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works: 1886\xe2\x80\x931986, pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936 (1987) (The first French copyright laws \xe2\x80\x9cplaced authors\xe2\x80\x99 rights on a more elevated basis than the Act of Anne had done,\xe2\x80\x9d on the understanding that they were \xe2\x80\x9csimply according formal recognition to what was already inherent in the \xe2\x80\x98very nature of things\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); S. Stewart, International Copyright and Neighbouring Rights 6\xe2\x80\x937 (2d ed. 1989) (describing the European system of droit d\xe2\x80\x99auteur).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis utilitarian understanding of the Copyright Clause has long been reflected in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s case law. In Mazer, for example, the Court refers to copyright as embodying the view that \xe2\x80\x9cencouragement of individual effort by personal gain is the best way to advance public welfare through the talents of authors and inventors.\xe2\x80\x9d 347 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 219 (emphasis added). In Twentieth Century Music Corp. v. Aiken, \n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 151 (1975)\n, the Court says that underlying copyright is the understanding that \xe2\x80\x9c[c]reative work is to be encouraged and rewarded, but private motivation must ultimately serve the cause of promoting broad public availability of literature, music, and the other arts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 156 (emphasis added). And in Sony Corp. of Amer-ica v. Universal City Studios, Inc., \n\n464 U.\xc2\xa0S. 417 (1984)\n, the Court, speaking of both copyrights and patents, points out that the \xe2\x80\x9cmonopoly privileges that Congress may authorize are .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [not] primarily designed to provide a special private benefit. Rather, the limited grant is a means by which an important public purpose may be achieved. It is intended to motivate the creative activity of authors .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by the provision of a special reward.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 429 (emphasis added); see also, e.g., Graham v. John Deere Co. of Kansas City, \n\n383 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n6 (1966)\n (The \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional command .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98[to] promote the Progress [of Science]\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is the standard expressed in the Constitution and it may not be ignored\xe2\x80\x9d); Fox Film Corp. v. Doyal, \n\n286 U.\xc2\xa0S. 123, \n127 (1932)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe sole interest of the United States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. lie[s] in the general benefits derived by the public from the labors of authors\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress has expressed similar views in congressional Reports on copyright legislation. Thus, for example, an 1892 House Report states:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe object to be attained and the reason for the constitutional grant of power are imbedded in the grant itself. They are \xe2\x80\x98to promote the progress of science and the useful arts.\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [The Clause says] nothing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. about any desire or purpose to secure to the author or inventor his \xe2\x80\x98natural right to his property.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 1494, 52d Cong., 1st Sess., 2.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tSimilarly, the congressional authors of the landmark 1909 Copyright Act wrote:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe Constitution .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. provides that Congress shall have the power to grant [copyrights] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [n]ot primarily for the benefit of the author, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. but because the policy is believed to be for the benefit of the great body of people, in that it will stimulate writing and invention, to give some bonus to authors and inventors.\xe2\x80\x9d H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 2222, 60th Cong., 2d Sess., 7 (1909).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tAnd they went on to say:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cCongress must consider .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. two questions: First, how much will the legislation stimulate the producer and so benefit the public; and, second, how much will the monopoly granted be detrimental to the public? The granting of such exclusive rights, under the proper terms and conditions, confers a benefit upon the public that outweighs the evils of the temporary monop-oly.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe upshot is that text, history, and precedent demonstrate that the Copyright Clause places great value on the power of copyright to elicit new production. Congress in particular cases may determine that copyright\xe2\x80\x99s ability to do so outweighs any concomitant high prices, administrative costs, and restrictions on dissemination. And when it does so, we must respect its judgment. See Eldred, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 222. But does the Clause empower Congress to enact a statute that withdraws works from the public domain, brings about higher prices and costs, and in doing so seriously restricts dissemination, particularly to those who need it for scholarly, educational, or cultural pur- poses\xe2\x80\x94all without providing any additional incentive for the production of new material? That is the question before us. And, as I have said, I believe the answer is no. Congress in this statute has exceeded what are, under any plausible reading of the Copyright Clause, its permissible limits.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Act before us says that it \xe2\x80\x9crestores\xe2\x80\x9d American copyright to a set of works, which, for the most part, did not previously enjoy American copyright protection. These works had fallen into America\xe2\x80\x99s public domain, but as of the \xe2\x80\x9crestoration\xe2\x80\x9d date, they had not yet fallen into the pub- lic domain of the foreign country where they originated.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe statute covers works originating almost anywhere outside the United States. See \n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7104A(h)(3) (setting out eligibility criteria); U.\xc2\xa0S. Copyright Office, Circular No. 38A: International Copyright Relations of the United States (2010). The relevant set of works consists primarily of works originating abroad that did not obtain, or at some point lost, American copyright protection because (1) the author failed to comply with applicable American copyright formalities (such as notice or renewal), or (2) the nation in which they were first published then lacked copyright relations with the United States, or (3) they are sound recordings fixed before February 15, 1972. \xc2\xa7104A(h)(6)(C). A work must also satisfy other technical requirements: It must have had a rightholder who was a national or resident of an eligible country on the day it was created; and it cannot have been published in the United States within 30 days of its first publication. \xc2\xa7104A(h)(6)(D). The Act grants these works a copyright that expires at the time it would have expired had the author obtained a full American copyright term starting from the date on which the work was first published (in the foreign country). \xc2\xa7104A(a)(1)(B).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Act mainly applies to works first published abroad between 1923 and 1989. It does not apply significantly to earlier works because any work published before 1921 would have fallen into the public domain before 1977 had it received a full American copyright term, while works published between 1921 and 1923 obtained a \xe2\x80\x9crestored\xe2\x80\x9d copyright that expired before the 1998 Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act, and so could have lasted two years at most. See Tit. I, \xc2\xa7101, \n\n90Stat.\n2574 (extending the copyright term of works still under copyright in 1977 to 75 years); \n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7304(b) (extending the copyright term of works still under copyright in 1998 to 95 years). It has less impact on more recent works because in 1989 the United States became a Berne member, abolished the copyright notice requirement, and thenceforth provided prospective copyright protection throughout the Berne Union. See R. Schechter & J. Thomas, Intellectual Property: The Law of Copyrights, Patents and Trademarks 75\xe2\x80\x9377 (2003); \xc2\xa77, \n\n102Stat.\n2857\xe2\x80\x932858 (codified as amended at 17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7401\xe2\x80\x93406).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDespite these temporal limitations, the Act covers vast numbers of works. The first category includes works published in countries that had copyright relations with the United States during this time period, such as most of Western Europe and Latin America, Australia, and Japan, see Circular No. 38A, supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x9310, whose authors did not satisfy American copyright formalities, perhaps because the author, who may not have sought an American copyright, published the book abroad without proper American notice, or perhaps because the author obtained a valid American copyright but failed to renew it.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe second category (works that entered the public domain due to a lack of copyright relations) includes, among others, all works published in Russia and other countries of the former Soviet Union before May 1973 (when the U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0R. joined the Universal Copyright Convention (UCC)), all works published in the People\xe2\x80\x99s Republic of China before March 1992 (when bilateral copyright relations between the People\xe2\x80\x99s Republic and the United States were first established), all South Korean works published before October 1987 (when South Korea joined the UCC), and all Egyptian and Turkish works published before March 1989 (when the United States joined Berne). See id., at 2\xe2\x80\x9310, and 11, nn. 2, 5, 6.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe third category covers all sound recordings from eligible foreign countries published after February 15, 1972. The practical significance of federal copyright restoration to this category of works is less clear, since these works received, and continued to receive, copyright protection under state law. See \n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7301(c).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApparently there are no precise figures about the number of works the Act affects, but in 1996 the then-Register of Copyrights, Marybeth Peters, thought that they \xe2\x80\x9cprobably number in the millions.\xe2\x80\x9d The Year in Review: Accomplishments and Objectives of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Copyright Office, 7 Ford. Intellectual Property Media & Entertainment L.\xc2\xa0J. 25, 31 (1996).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe provision before us takes works from the public domain, at least as of January 1, 1996. See \xc2\xa7104A(h)(2)(A) (setting \xe2\x80\x9crestoration\xe2\x80\x9d dates). It then restricts the dissemination of those works in two ways.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, \xe2\x80\x9crestored copyright\xe2\x80\x9d holders can now charge fees for works that consumers previously used for free. The price of a score of Shostakovich\xe2\x80\x99s Preludes and Fugues Op. 87, for example, has risen by a multiple of seven. Brief for Conductors Guild et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 11. And, as the Court recognizes, an orchestra that once could perform \xe2\x80\x9cPeter and the Wolf .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. free of charge\xe2\x80\x9d will now have to buy the \xe2\x80\x9cright to perform it .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the marketplace.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 29. But for the case of certain \xe2\x80\x9cderivative\xe2\x80\x9d works, \xc2\xa7104A(d)(3), the \xe2\x80\x9crestored copyright\xe2\x80\x9d holder, like other copyright holders, can charge what the market will bear. If a school orchestra or other nonprofit organization cannot afford the new charges, so be it. They will have to do without\xe2\x80\x94aggravating the already serious problem of cultural education in the United States. See Brief for Conductors Guild et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 4\xe2\x80\x935, 7\xe2\x80\x938 (describing the inability of many orchestras to pay for the rental of sheet music covered by \xe2\x80\x9crestored copyright[s]\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, and at least as important, the statute creates administrative costs, such as the costs of determining whether a work is the subject of a \xe2\x80\x9crestored copyright,\xe2\x80\x9d searching for a \xe2\x80\x9crestored copyright\xe2\x80\x9d holder, and negotiating a fee. Congress has tried to ease the administrative burden of contacting copyright holders and negotiating prices for those whom the statute calls \xe2\x80\x9creliance part[ies],\xe2\x80\x9d namely those who previously had used such works when they were freely available in the public domain. \xc2\xa7104A(h)(4). But Congress has done nothing to ease the administrative burden of securing permission from copyright owners that is placed upon those who want to use a work that they did not previously use, and this is a particular problem when it comes to \xe2\x80\x9corphan works\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94older and more obscure works with minimal commercial value that have copyright owners who are difficult or impossible to track down. Unusually high administrative costs threaten to limit severely the distribution and use of those works\xe2\x80\x94works which, despite their characteristic lack of economic value, can prove culturally invaluable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere are millions of such works. For example, according to European Union figures, there are 13 million orphan books in the European Union (13% of the total number of books in-copyright there), 225,000 orphan films in European film archives, and 17 million orphan photographs in United Kingdom museums. A. Vuopala, Assessment of the Orphan works issue and Costs for Rights Clearance 19, 25 (2010), online at http://ec.europa.eu/ information_society/activities/digital_libraries/doc/reports_ orphan/anna_report.pdf (all Internet materials as visited Jan.\xc2\xa013, 2012, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). How is a university, a film collector, a musician, a database compiler, or a scholar now to obtain permission to use any such lesser known foreign work previously in the American public domain? Consider the questions that any such individual, group, or institution usually must answer: Is the work eligible for restoration under the statute? If so, who now holds the copyright\xe2\x80\x94the author? an heir? a publisher? an association? a long-lost cousin? Whom must we contact? What is the address? Suppose no one answers? How do we conduct a negotiation?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo find answers to these, and similar questions, costs money. The cost to the University of Michigan and the Institute of Museum and Library Services, for example, to determine the copyright status of books contained in the HathiTrust Digital Library that were published in the United States from 1923 to 1963 will exceed $1 million. Brief for American Library Assn. et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 15.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is consequently not surprising to learn that the Los Angeles Public Library has been unable to make its collection of Mexican folk music publicly available because of problems locating copyright owners, that a Jewish cultural organization has abandoned similar efforts to make available Jewish cultural music and other materials, or that film preservers, museums, universities, scholars, database compilers, and others report that the administrative costs associated with trying to locate foreign copyright owners have forced them to curtail their cultural, scholarly, or other work-preserving efforts. See, e.g., Comments of the Library Copyright Alliance in Response to the U.\xc2\xa0S. Copyright Office\xe2\x80\x99s Inquiry on Orphan Works 5 (Mar. 25, 2005), online at http://www.arl.org/bm~doc/lcacomment0305.pdf; Comments of Creative Commons and Save The Music in Response to the U.\xc2\xa0S. Copyright Office\xe2\x80\x99s Inquiry on Orphan Works (Mar. 25, 2005), online at http:// www.copyright.gov/orphan/comments/OW0643-STM- CreativeCommons.pdf; General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT): Intellectual Property Provisions, Joint Hearing before the Subcommittee on Intellectual Property and Judicial Administration of the House Committee on the Judiciary and the Subcommittee on Patents, Copyrights and Trademarks of the Senate Committee on the Judiciary, 103d Cong., 2d Sess., 131, 273 (1994) (hereinafter Joint Hearing) (statement of Larry Urbanski, Chairman of the Fairness in Copyright Coalition and President of Moviecraft, Inc.); Brief for American Library Assn. et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 6\xe2\x80\x9323; Brief for Creative Commons Corp. as Amicus Curiae 7\xe2\x80\x938; Brief for Project Petrucci, LLC, as Amicus Curiae 10\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese high administrative costs can prove counterproductive in another way. They will tempt some potential users to \xe2\x80\x9csteal\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpirate\xe2\x80\x9d works rather than do without. And piracy often begets piracy, breeding the destructive habit of taking copyrighted works without paying for them, even where payment is possible. Such habits ignore the critical role copyright plays in the creation of new works, while reflecting a false belief that new creation appears by magic without thought or hope of compensation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI recognize that ordinary copyright protection also comes accompanied with dissemination-restricting royalty charges and administrative costs. But here the restrictions work special harm. For one thing, the foreign location of restored works means higher than ordinary administrative costs. For another, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s technical requirements make it very difficult to establish whether a work has had its copyright restored by the statute. Gard, In the Trenches with \xc2\xa7104A: An Evaluation of the Parties\xe2\x80\x99 Arguments in Golan v. Holder as It Heads to the Supreme Court, 64 Vand. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. En Banc 199, 216\xe2\x80\x93220 (2011) (describing difficulties encountered in compiling the information necessary to create an online tool to determine whether the statute applies in any given case).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWorst of all, \xe2\x80\x9crestored copyright\xe2\x80\x9d protection removes material from the public domain. In doing so, it reverses the payment expectations of those who used, or intended to use, works that they thought belonged to them. Were Congress to act similarly with respect to well-established property rights, the problem would be obvious. This statute analogously restricts, and thereby diminishes, Americans\xe2\x80\x99 preexisting freedom to use formerly public domain material in their expressive activities.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, while the majority correctly observes that the dissemination-restricting harms of copyright normally present problems appropriate for legislation to resolve, ante, at 31\xe2\x80\x9332, the question is whether the Copyright Clause permits Congress seriously to exacerbate such a problem by taking works out of the public domain without a countervailing benefit. This question is appropriate for judicial resolution. Indeed, unlike Eldred where the Court had to decide a complicated line-drawing question\xe2\x80\x94when is a copyright term too long?\xe2\x80\x94here an easily administrable standard is available\xe2\x80\x94a standard that would require works that have already fallen into the public domain to stay there.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe several, just mentioned features of the present statute are important, for they distinguish it from other copyright laws. By removing material from the public domain, the statute, in literal terms, \xe2\x80\x9cabridges\xe2\x80\x9d a preexisting freedom to speak. In practical terms, members of the public might well have decided what to say, as well as when and how to say it, in part by reviewing with a view to repeating, expression that they reasonably believed was, or would be, freely available. Given these speech implications, it is not surprising that Congress has long sought to protect public domain material when revising the copyright laws. See infra, at 19 (listing instances). And this Court has assumed the particular importance of public domain material in roughly analogous circumstances. See Graham, 383 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 6 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress may not authorize the issuance of patents whose effects are to remove existent knowledge from the public domain\xe2\x80\x9d); Kewanee Oil Co. v. Bicron Corp., \n\n416 U.\xc2\xa0S. 470, \n484 (1974)\n (trade secret protection is not incompatible with \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy that matter once in the public domain must remain in the public domain\xe2\x80\x9d); Cox Broadcasting Corp. v. Cohn, \n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 469, \n496 (1975)\n (\n \nFirst Amendment prohibits sanctioning press for publishing material disclosed in public court documents); see also Dastar Corp. v. Twentieth Century Fox Film Corp., \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 23, \n33 (2003)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe right to copy .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. once a copyright has expired .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. passes to the public\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, whereas forward-looking copyright laws tend to benefit those whose identities are not yet known (the writer who has not yet written a book, the musician who has not yet composed a song), when a copyright law is primarily backward looking the risk is greater that Congress is trying to help known beneficiaries at the expense of badly organized unknown users who find it difficult to argue and present their case to Congress. In Eldred, I thought this problem was severe. See generally 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 243\xe2\x80\x93266 (dissenting opinion). And in light of the fact that Congress, with one minor exception, heard testimony only from the representatives of existing copyright holders, who hoped that passage of the statute would enable them to benefit from reciprocal treatment of American authors abroad, infra, at 21, I cannot say that even here the problem, while much diminished, was nonexistent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI agree with the majority that, in doing so, this statute does not discriminate among speakers based on their viewpoints or subject matter. Ante, at 27. But such considerations do not exhaust potential \n \nFirst Amendment problems. Cf. Sorrell v. IMS Health Inc., 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 8) (finding \n \nFirst Amendment problem in statute that prohibits drug manufacturers from using publicly available prescriber-identifying information in their marketing efforts in part because it \xe2\x80\x9cdisfavor[ed] specific speakers\xe2\x80\x9d); Turner Broadcasting System, Inc. v. FCC, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 622, \n659 (1994)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cRegulations that discriminate among media, or among different speakers within a single medium, often present serious \n \nFirst Amendment concerns\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTaken together, these speech-related harms (e.g., restricting use of previously available material; reversing payment expectations; rewarding rent-seekers at the public\xe2\x80\x99s expense) at least show the presence of a \n \nFirst Amendment interest. And that is enough. For present purposes, I need not decide whether the harms to that interest show a violation of the \n \nFirst Amendment. I need only point to the importance of interpreting the Constitution as a single document\xe2\x80\x94a document that we should not read as setting the Copyright Clause and the \n \nFirst Amendment at cross-purposes. Nor need I advocate the application here of strict or specially heightened review. I need only find that the \n \nFirst Amendment interest is important enough to require courts to scrutinize with some care the reasons claimed to justify the Act in order to determine whether they constitute reasonable copyright-related justifications for the serious harms, including speech-related harms, which the Act seems likely to impose.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis statute does not serve copyright\xe2\x80\x99s traditional public ends, namely the creation of monetary awards that \xe2\x80\x9cmotivate the creative activity of authors,\xe2\x80\x9d Sony, 464 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 429, \xe2\x80\x9cencourag[e] individual effort,\xe2\x80\x9d Mazer, 347 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 219, and thereby \xe2\x80\x9cserve the cause of promoting broad public availability of literature, music, and the other arts,\xe2\x80\x9d Twentieth Century Music, 422 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 156. The statute grants its \xe2\x80\x9crestored copyright[s]\xe2\x80\x9d only to works already produced. It provides no monetary incentive to produce anything new. Unlike other American copyright statutes from the time of the Founders onwards, including the statute at issue in Eldred, it lacks any significant copyright-related quid pro quo.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority seeks to avoid this awkward fact by referring to past congressional practice that mostly suggests that Congress may provide new or increased protection both to newly created and to previously created, works. Ante, at 16, 18; Act of May 31, 1790, \xc2\xa71, \n\n1Stat.\n124 (conferring its new federal copyright on new works as well as old); Act of July 3, 1832, \xc2\xa73, \n\n4Stat.\n559 (authorizing new patents for past and future inventors who inadvertently failed to comply with applicable statutory formalities); McClurg v. Kingsland, 1 How. 202 (1843) (applying an act deeming a past or future inventor\xe2\x80\x99s patent valid despite it being briefly used by, for example, the inventor\xe2\x80\x99s employer). I do not dispute that copyright power. Insofar as such a statute does the former, i.e., extends protection to newly created material, it embodies copyright\xe2\x80\x99s traditional justification\xe2\x80\x94eliciting new production. And I do not doubt that Congress may then also include existing works within the scope of, say, increased protection for equitable and administrative reasons. See Eldred, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S. at 204, 214\xe2\x80\x93215 (describing equitable reasons for applying newly extended copyright terms to future and existing copyrights alike). The statute before us, however, does not directly elicit any new production. Compare id., at 204\xe2\x80\x93208; (majority opinion) (noting that statute\xe2\x80\x99s extended term would apply to newly created material, and finding that the determination of the likelihood of its eliciting new production in practice was a matter for Congress to determine), with id., at 243\xe2\x80\x93267 (Breyer, J., dissenting) (expressing the view that there is little likelihood, in practice, that the statute would elicit new material). See also Walterscheid 219 (the 1790 Congress likely thought it was substituting federal protection for preexisting state common-law protections); Maher, Copyright Term, Retrospective Extension, and the Copyright Law of 1790 in Historical Context, 49 J. Copyright Soc. USA 1021, 1023\xe2\x80\x931024, and n. 8 (2002) (numerical estimate suggesting that 1790 Act removed only a small number of books from public domain).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe other statutes to which the majority refers are private bills, statutes retroactively granting protection in wartime, or the like. Ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9319; Act of Feb. 19, 1849, ch. 57, \n\n9Stat.\n763 (Levi Corson); Act of June 23, 1874, ch. 534, 18 Stat., pt. 3, p.\xc2\xa0618 (Tod Helmuth); Act of Feb. 17, 1898, ch. 29, \n\n30Stat.\n1396 (Judson Jones); Act of Dec. 18, 1919, ch. 11, \n\n41Stat.\n368; Act of Sept. 25, 1941, ch. 421, \n\n55Stat.\n732; see also Evans v. Jordan, 9 Cranch 199 (1815) (upholding a private bill restoring patent protection to a flour mill). But special circumstances, like wars, hurricanes, earthquakes, and other disasters, prevent the realization in practice of a reasonable expectation of securing or maintaining a preexisting right. Private bills are designed to provide special exceptions for comparable equitable reasons. See also Act of Mar. 3, 1893, ch. 215, \n\n27Stat.\n743 (similar, as far as I can tell). To find in these laws an important analogy to the present law, which for the most part covers works that the author did not expect to protect in America (and often did not particularly want to protect), seems somewhat farfetched.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn fact, Congressional practice shows the contrary. It consists of a virtually unbroken string of legislation preventing the withdrawal of works from the public domain. See, e.g., Berne Convention Implementation Act of 1988, \xc2\xa712, \n\n102Stat.\n2860 (the Act \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not provide copyright protection for any work that is in the public domain in the United States\xe2\x80\x9d); Copyright Act of 1976, Tit. I, \xc2\xa7101, \n\n90Stat.\n2573 (declining to extend copyright protection to any work that is in the public domain prior to the Act taking effect); Copyright Act of 1909, \xc2\xa77, \n\n35Stat.\n1077 (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]o copyright shall subsist in the original text of any work which is in the public domain, or in any work which was published in this country or any foreign country prior to the going into effect of this Act and has not been already copyrighted in the United States\xe2\x80\x9d); Act to Amend the Several Acts Respecting Copy Rights \xc2\xa716, \n\n4Stat.\n439 (the Act \xe2\x80\x9cshall not extend to any copyright heretofore secured, the term of which has already expired\xe2\x80\x9d); see also H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 1742, 87th Cong., 2d Sess., 3 (1962) (expressing concern that because \xe2\x80\x9cit is not possible to revive expired terms of copyright, it seems to the committee to be desirable to suspend further expiration of copyright for a period long enough to enable the working out of remaining obstacles to the overall revision of the copyright law\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority makes several other arguments. First, it argues that the Clause does not require the \xe2\x80\x9ccreation of at least one new work,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 20, but may instead \xe2\x80\x9cpromote the Progress of Science\xe2\x80\x9d in other ways. And it specifically mentions the \xe2\x80\x9cdissemination of existing and future works\xe2\x80\x9d as determinative here. Ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9323, and n.\xc2\xa025. The industry experts to whom the majority refers argue that copyright protection of already existing works can help, say, music publishers or film distributers raise prices, produce extra profits and consequently lead them to publish or distribute works they might otherwise have ignored. But ordinarily a copyright\xe2\x80\x94since it is a monopoly on copying\xe2\x80\x94restricts dissemination of a work once produced compared to a competitive market. And simply making the industry richer does not mean that the industry, when it makes an ordinary forward-looking economic calculus, will distribute works not previously distributed. The industry experts might mean that temporary extra profits will lead them to invest in the development of a market, say, by advertising. But this kind of argument, which can be made by distributers of all sorts of goods, ranging from kiwi fruit to Swedish furniture, has little if anything to do with the nonrepeatable costs of initial creation, which is the special concern of copyright protection. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, the argument proves too much. It is the kind of argument that the Stationers\xe2\x80\x99 Company might well have made and which the British Parliament rejected. Cf. Patterson 154\xe2\x80\x93155 (describing failed booksellers\xe2\x80\x99 bill seeking protection from foreign competition through an extension of the copyright term). It is the kind of argument that could justify a legislature\xe2\x80\x99s withdrawing from the public domain the works, say, of Hawthorne or of Swift or for that matter the King James Bible in order to encourage further publication of those works; and, it could even more easily justify similar action in the case of lesser known early works, perhaps those of the Venerable Bede. The Court has not, to my knowledge, previously accepted such a rationale\xe2\x80\x94a rationale well removed from the special economic circumstances that surround the nonrepeatable costs of the initial creation of a \xe2\x80\x9cWriting.\xe2\x80\x9d Supra, at 2. And I fear that doing so would read the Copyright Clause as if it were a blank check made out in favor of those who are not themselves creators.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is not surprising that the copyright holders\xe2\x80\x99 representatives who appeared before Congress did not emphasize this argument. (With one minor exception only those representatives appeared, see generally Joint Hearing; the Copyright Office did not testify, id., at 239.) Rather, they focused on the Berne Convention itself. By that time, Congress had already protected all new works of Berne members. But it had not provided additional protection to preexisting foreign works that were then in the American public domain. Industry witnesses testified that withdrawing such works from the American public domain would permit foreign copyright owners to charge American consumers more for their products; and that, as a result, the United States would be able to persuade foreign countries to allow American holders of preexisting copyrights to charge foreign customers more money for their products. See id., at 241 (statement of Eric Smith, Executive Director and General Counsel, International Intellectual Property Alliance) (\xe2\x80\x9c[F]ailure to [comply with Article 18] will .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. undermine the ability of the United States to press other countries to implement the same sort of protection in their implementing legislation currently pending in many legislatures around the globe\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 253 (statement of Matt Gerson, Vice President for Congressional Affairs, Motion Picture Assn. of America) (similar). See also id., at 85 (statement of Xavier Becerra, House Judiciary Committee member) (\xe2\x80\x9c[R]etroactivity .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is probably the best way to ensure that some of our older American works, anything from Motown, to \xe2\x80\x98Star Trek,\xe2\x80\x99 to \xe2\x80\x98The Hardy Boys\xe2\x80\x99 get the protection in some of these emerging foreign markets. It is important to ensure that countries no longer use our U.\xc2\xa0S. law as an excuse for not extending retroactive copyright protections to some of our own works\xe2\x80\x9d). But see id., at 272\xe2\x80\x93279 (statement of Larry Urbanski, Chairman of the Fairness in Copyright Coalition and President of Moviecraft Inc.) (testifying against restoration on grounds similar to those set out, supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9313).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis argument, whatever its intrinsic merits, is an ar-gument that directly concerns a private benefit: how to obtain more money from the sales of existing products. It is not an argument about a public benefit, such as how to promote or to protect the creative process.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t Third, the majority points out that the statute \xe2\x80\x9cgives [authors] nothing more than the benefit of their labors during whatever time remains before the normal copyright term expires.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 30. But insofar as it suggests that copyright should in general help authors obtain greater monetary rewards than needed to elicit new works, it rests upon primarily European, but not American, copyright concepts. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFourth, the majority argues that this statutory provision is necessary to fulfill our Berne Convention obligations. Ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x938. The Treaty, in Article 18, says that the \xe2\x80\x9cConvention shall apply to all works which, at the moment of its coming into force [i.e., 1989 in the case of the United States] have not yet fallen into the public domain in the country of origin through the expiry of the term of protection.\xe2\x80\x9d Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works, Art. 18(1), Sept.\xc2\xa09, 1886, as revised at Stockholm on July 14, 1967, 828 U. N. T. S. 221, 251. The majority and Government say that this means we must protect the foreign works at issue here. And since the Berne Convention, taken as a whole, provides incentives for the creation of new works, I am willing to speculate, for argument\xe2\x80\x99s sake, that the statute might indirectly encourage production of new works by making the United States\xe2\x80\x99 place in the international copyright regime more secure.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStill, I cannot find this argument sufficient to save the statute. For one thing, this is a dilemma of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s own making. The United States obtained the benefits of Berne for many years despite its failure to enact a statute implementing Article 18. But in 1994, the United States and other nations signed the Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights, which enabled signatories to use World Trade Organization dispute resolution mechanisms to complain about other members\xe2\x80\x99 Berne Convention violations. But at that time the Government, although it successfully secured reservations protecting other special features of American copyright law, made no effort to secure a reservation permitting the United States to keep some or all restored works in the American public domain. And it made no effort to do so despite the fact that Article 18 explicitly authorizes countries to negotiate exceptions to the Article\xe2\x80\x99s retroactivity principle. See Art. 18(3), ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cThe application of [the retroactivity] principle shall be subject to any provisions contained in special conventions to that effect existing or to be concluded between countries of the Union\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); Gervais, Golan v. Holder: A Look at the Constraints Imposed by the Berne Convention, 64 Vand. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. En Banc 147, 151\xe2\x80\x93152 (2011); Gard, 64 Vand. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. En Banc, at 206.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor another thing, the Convention does not require Congress to enact a statute that causes so much damage to public domain material. Article 18(3) also states that \xe2\x80\x9cthe respective countries shall determine, each in so far as it is concerned, the conditions of application of this principle.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. N. T. S., at 251 (emphasis added). Congress could have alleviated many of the costs that the statute imposes by, for example, creating forms of compulsory licensing, requiring \xe2\x80\x9crestored copyright\xe2\x80\x9d holders to provide necessary administrative information as a condition of protection, or insisting upon \xe2\x80\x9creasonable royalties.\xe2\x80\x9d Cf. S.\xc2\xa02913, 110th Cong., 2d Sess. (2008) (legislation that would have limited judicial remedies against users of orphan works); H.\xc2\xa0R. 5889, 110th Cong., 2d Sess. (2008) (House version of same); American Society of Compos- ers, Authors and Publishers, http://www.ascap.com/ licensing/termsdefined.aspx (society of music copyright owners offering blanket licenses that give users the unlimited right to perform any of its members\xe2\x80\x99 songs for a fixed fee, thus reducing negotiation and enforcement costs).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo say this is not to criticize the Convention or our joining it. Rather, it is to argue that the other branches of Government should have tried to follow the Convention and in particular its provisions offering compliance flexibility. The fact that the statute has significant \n \nFirst Amendment costs is relevant in this respect, for that Amendment ordinarily requires courts to evaluate less restrictive, alternative possibilities. Doing so here, reveals that neither Congress nor the Executive took advantage of less-restrictive methods of compliance that the Convention itself provides. And that fact means that the Convention cannot provide the statute with a constitutionally sufficient justification that is otherwise lacking.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe fact that, by withdrawing material from the public domain, the statute inhibits an important preexisting flow of information is sufficient, when combined with the other features of the statute that I have discussed, to convince me that the Copyright Clause, interpreted in the light of the \n \nFirst Amendment, does not authorize Congress to enact this statute.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI respectfully dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s contrary conclusion.\n\t\t\t
