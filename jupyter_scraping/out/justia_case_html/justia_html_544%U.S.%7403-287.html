\nWILKINSON V. DOTSON
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

WILKINSON, DIRECTOR, OHIO DEPARTMENT OF REHABILITATION AND CORRECTION, et al. v. DOTSON et al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 03\xe2\x80\x93287.Argued December 6, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 7, 2005

\n

Respondents Dotson and Johnson are Ohio state prisoners. After parole officials determined that Dotson was not eligible for parole and that Johnson was not suitable for parole, they brought separate actions for declaratory and injunctive relief under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, claiming that Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s parole procedures violate the Federal Constitution. In each case, the Federal District Court concluded that a \xc2\xa71983 action does not lie and that the prisoner would have to seek relief through a habeas corpus suit. The Sixth Circuit ultimately consolidated the cases and reversed, finding that the actions could proceed under \xc2\xa71983.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0State prisoners may bring a \xc2\xa71983 action for declaratory and injunctive relief challenging the constitutionality of state parole procedures; they need not seek relief exclusively under the federal habeas corpus statutes. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Ohio argues unsuccessfully that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims may only be brought in federal habeas (or similar state) proceedings because a state prisoner cannot use a \xc2\xa71983 action to challenge \xe2\x80\x9cthe fact or duration of his confinement,\xe2\x80\x9d e.g., Preiser v. Rodriguez, 411 U. S. 475, 489, and respondents\xe2\x80\x99 lawsuits, in effect, collaterally attack their confinements\xe2\x80\x99 duration. That argument jumps from a true premise (that in all likelihood the prisoners hope their suits will help bring about earlier release) to a faulty conclusion (that habeas is their sole avenue for relief). This Court\xe2\x80\x99s case law makes clear that the connection between the constitutionality of the prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 parole proceedings and release from confinement is too tenuous here to achieve Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s legal door-closing objective. From Preiser to Edwards v. Balisok, 520 U. S. 641, this Court has developed an exception from \xc2\xa71983\xe2\x80\x99s otherwise broad scope for actions that lie \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the core of habeas corpus,\xe2\x80\x9d Preiser, supra, at 487, i.e., where a state prisoner requests present or future release. Section 1983 remains available for procedural challenges where success would not necessarily spell immediate or speedier release for the prisoner, e.g., Wolff v. McDonnell, 418 U. S. 539, but the prisoner cannot use \xc2\xa71983 to obtain relief where success would necessarily demonstrate the invalidity of confinement or its duration, e.g., Heck v. Humphrey, 512 U. S. 477. Here, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims are cognizable under \xc2\xa71983, i.e., they do not fall within the implicit habeas exception. They seek relief that will render invalid the state procedures used to deny parole eligibility (Dotson) and parole suitability (Johnson). See Wolff, supra, at 554\xe2\x80\x93555. Neither prisoner seeks an injunction ordering his immediate or speedier release into the community. See, e.g., Preiser, supra, at 500. And as in Wolff, a favorable judgment will not \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily imply the invalidity of [their] conviction[s] or sentence[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Heck, supra, at 487. Success for Dotson does not mean immediate release or a shorter stay in prison; it means at most new eligibility review, which may speed consideration of a new parole application. Success for Johnson means at most a new parole hearing at which parole authorities may, in their discretion, decline to shorten his prison term. Because neither prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s claim would necessarily spell speedier release, neither lies at \xe2\x80\x9cthe core of habeas corpus.\xe2\x80\x9d Preiser, supra, at 489. Finally, the prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 claims for future relief (which, if successful, will not necessarily imply the invalidity of confinement or shorten its duration) are yet more distant from that core. See Balisok, supra, at 648. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s additional arguments\xe2\x80\x94(1) that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 \xc2\xa71983 actions cannot lie because a favorable judgment would \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily imply the invalidity of [their] sentence[s],\xe2\x80\x9d Heck, supra, at 487 (emphasis added), which sentences include particular state parole procedures; and (2) that a decision for them would violate principles of federal/state comity by opening the door to federal court without prior exhaustion of state-court remedies\xe2\x80\x94are not persuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nWILKINSON V. DOTSON
\n544 U. S. ____ (2005)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 03-287
\n
\n

REGINALD A. WILKINSON, DIRECTOR, OHIO\nDEPARTMENT OF REHABILITATION AND\nCORRECTION, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v.\nWILLIAM DWIGHT DOTSON et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[March 7, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Two state prisoners brought an action under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 claiming that Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s state parole procedures violate the Federal Constitution. The prisoners seek declaratory and injunctive relief. The question before us is whether they may bring such an action under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, the Civil Rights Act of 1871, or whether they must instead seek relief exclusively under the federal habeas corpus statutes. We conclude that these actions may be brought under \xc2\xa71983.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The two respondents, William Dotson and Rogerico Johnson, are currently serving lengthy terms in Ohio prisons. Dotson began to serve a life sentence in 1981. The parole board rejected his first parole request in 1995; and a parole officer, after reviewing Dotson\xe2\x80\x99s records in the year 2000, determined that he should not receive further consideration for parole for at least five more years. In reaching this conclusion about Dotson\xe2\x80\x99s parole eligibility, the officer used parole guidelines first adopted in 1998, after Dotson began to serve his term. Dotson claims that the retroactive application of these new, harsher guidelines to his preguidelines case violates the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Ex Post Facto and Due Process Clauses. He seeks a federal-court declaration to that effect as well as a permanent injunction ordering prison officials to grant him an \xe2\x80\x9cimmediate parole hearing in accordance with the statutory laws and administrative rules in place when [he] committed his crimes.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 20 (Dotson Complaint, Prospective Declaratory and Injunctive Relief, \xc2\xb6 3).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Johnson began to serve a 10- to 30-year prison term in 1992. The parole board considered and rejected his first parole request in 1999, finding him unsuitable for release. In making this determination, the board applied the new 1998 guidelines. Johnson too claims that the application of these new, harsher guidelines to his preguidelines case violated the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Ex Post Facto Clause. He also alleges that the parole board\xe2\x80\x99s proceedings (by having too few members present and by denying him an adequate opportunity to speak) violated the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause. Johnson\xe2\x80\x99s complaint seeks a new parole hearing conducted under constitutionally proper procedures and an injunction ordering the State to comply with constitutional due process and ex post facto requirements in the future.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both prisoners brought \xc2\xa71983 actions in federal court. In each case, the Federal District Court concluded that a \xc2\xa71983 action does not lie and that the prisoner would have to seek relief through a habeas corpus suit. Dotson v. Wilkinson, No. 3:00\xc2\xa0CV\xc2\xa07303 (ND Ohio, Aug. 7, 2000); Johnson v. Ghee, No. 4:00\xc2\xa0CV\xc2\xa01075 (ND Ohio, July 16, 2000). Each prisoner appealed. The Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit ultimately consolidated the two appeals and heard both cases en banc. The court found that the actions could proceed under \xc2\xa71983, and it reversed the lower courts. 329 F.\xc2\xa03d 463, 472 (2003). Ohio parole officials then petitioned for certiorari, and we granted review.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has held that a prisoner in state custody cannot use a \xc2\xa71983 action to challenge \xe2\x80\x9cthe fact or duration of his confinement.\xe2\x80\x9d Preiser v. Rodriguez, 411 U. S. 475, 489 (1973); see also Wolff v. McDonnell, 418 U. S. 539, 554 (1974); Heck v. Humphrey, 512 U. S. 477, 481 (1994); Edwards v. Balisok, 520 U. S. 641, 648 (1997). He must seek federal habeas corpus relief (or appropriate state relief) instead.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ohio points out that the inmates in these cases attack their parole-eligibility proceedings (Dotson) and parole-suitability proceedings (Johnson) only because they believe that victory on their claims will lead to speedier release from prison. Consequently, Ohio argues, the prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 lawsuits, in effect, collaterally attack the duration of their confinement; hence, such a claim may only be brought through a habeas corpus action, not through \xc2\xa71983.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The problem with Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s argument lies in its jump from a true premise (that in all likelihood the prisoners hope these actions will help bring about earlier release) to a faulty conclusion (that habeas is their sole avenue for relief). A consideration of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s case law makes clear that the connection between the constitutionality of the prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 parole proceedings and release from confinement is too tenuous here to achieve Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s legal door-closing objective.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court initially addressed the relationship between \xc2\xa71983 and the federal habeas statutes in Preiser v. Rodriguez, supra. In that case, state prisoners brought civil rights actions attacking the constitutionality of prison disciplinary proceedings that had led to the deprivation of their good-time credits. Id., at 476. The Court conceded that the language of \xc2\xa71983 literally covers their claims. See \xc2\xa71983 (authorizing claims alleging the deprivation of constitutional rights against every \xe2\x80\x9cperson\xe2\x80\x9d acting \xe2\x80\x9cunder color of\xe2\x80\x9d state law). But, the Court noted, the language of the federal habeas statutes applies as well. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(a) (permitting claims by a person being held \xe2\x80\x9cin custody in violation of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d). Moreover, the Court observed, the language of the habeas statute is more specific, and the writ\xe2\x80\x99s history makes clear that it traditionally \xe2\x80\x9chas been accepted as the specific instrument to obtain release from [unlawful] confinement.\xe2\x80\x9d Preiser, 411 U. S., at 486. Finally, habeas corpus actions require a petitioner fully to exhaust state remedies, which \xc2\xa71983 does not. Id., at 490\xe2\x80\x93491; see also Patsy v. Board of Regents of Fla., 457 U. S. 496, 507 (1982). These considerations of linguistic specificity, history, and comity led the Court to find an implicit exception from \xc2\xa71983\xe2\x80\x99s otherwise broad scope for actions that lie \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the core of habeas corpus.\xe2\x80\x9d Preiser, 411 U. S., at 487.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Defining the scope of that exception, the Court concluded that a \xc2\xa71983 action will not lie when a state prisoner challenges \xe2\x80\x9cthe fact or duration of his confinement,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 489, and seeks either \xe2\x80\x9cimmediate release from prison,\xe2\x80\x9d or the \xe2\x80\x9cshortening\xe2\x80\x9d of his term of confinement, id., at 482. Because an action for restoration of good-time credits in effect demands immediate release or a shorter period of detention, it attacks \xe2\x80\x9cthe very duration of . . . physical confinement,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 487\xe2\x80\x93488, and thus lies at \xe2\x80\x9cthe core of habeas corpus,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 487. Therefore, the Court held, the Preiser prisoners could not pursue their claims under \xc2\xa71983.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Wolff v. McDonnell, supra, the Court elaborated the contours of this habeas corpus \xe2\x80\x9ccore.\xe2\x80\x9d As in Preiser, state prisoners brought a \xc2\xa71983 action challenging prison officials\xe2\x80\x99 revocation of good-time credits by means of constitutionally deficient disciplinary proceedings. 418 U. S., at 553. The Court held that the prisoners could not use \xc2\xa71983 to obtain restoration of the credits because Preiser had held that \xe2\x80\x9can injunction restoring good time improperly taken is foreclosed.\xe2\x80\x9d 418 U. S., at 555. But the inmates could use \xc2\xa71983 to obtain a declaration (\xe2\x80\x9cas a predicate to\xe2\x80\x9d their requested damages award) that the disciplinary procedures were invalid. Ibid. They could also seek \xe2\x80\x9cby way of ancillary relief[,] an otherwise proper injunction enjoining the prospective enforcement of invalid prison regulations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). In neither case would victory for the prisoners necessarily have meant immediate release or a shorter period of incarceration; the prisoners attacked only the \xe2\x80\x9cwrong procedures, not \xe2\x80\xa6 the wrong result (i.e., [the denial of] good-time credits).\xe2\x80\x9d Heck, supra, at 483 (discussing Wolff).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Heck, the Court considered a different, but related, circumstance. A state prisoner brought a \xc2\xa71983 action for damages, challenging the conduct of state officials who, the prisoner claimed, had unconstitutionally caused his conviction by improperly investigating his crime and destroying evidence. 512 U. S., at 479. The Court pointed to \xe2\x80\x9cthe hoary principle that civil tort actions are not appropriate vehicles for challenging the validity of outstanding criminal judgments.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 486. And it held that where \xe2\x80\x9cestablishing the basis for the damages claim necessarily demonstrates the invalidity of the conviction,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 481\xe2\x80\x93482, a \xc2\xa71983 action will not lie \xe2\x80\x9cunless . . . the conviction or sentence has already been invalidated,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 487. The Court then added that, where the \xc2\xa71983 action, \xe2\x80\x9ceven if successful, will not demonstrate the invalidity of any outstanding criminal judgment \xe2\x80\xa6 , the action should be allowed to proceed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, in Edwards v. Balisok, supra, the Court returned to the prison disciplinary procedure context of the kind it had addressed previously in Preiser and Wolff. Balisok sought \xe2\x80\x9ca declaration that the procedures employed by state officials [to deprive him of good-time credits] violated due process, \xe2\x80\xa6 damages for use of the unconstitutional procedures, [and] an injunction to prevent future violations.\xe2\x80\x9d 520 U. S., at 643. Applying Heck, the Court found that habeas was the sole vehicle for the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional challenge insofar as the prisoner sought declaratory relief and money damages, because the \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal procedural defect complained of,\xe2\x80\x9d namely deceit and bias on the part of the decisionmaker, \xe2\x80\x9cwould, if established, necessarily imply the invalidity of the deprivation of [Balisok\xe2\x80\x99s] good-time credits.\xe2\x80\x9d 520 U. S., at 646. Hence, success on the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s claim for money damages (and the accompanying claim for declaratory relief) would \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily imply the invalidity of the punishment imposed.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 648. Nonetheless, the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s claim for an injunction barring future unconstitutional procedures did not fall within habeas\xe2\x80\x99 exclusive domain. That is because \xe2\x80\x9c[o]rdinarily, a prayer for such prospective relief will not \xe2\x80\x98necessarily imply\xe2\x80\x99 the invalidity of a previous loss of good-time credits.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Throughout the legal journey from Preiser to Balisok, the Court has focused on the need to ensure that state prisoners use only habeas corpus (or similar state) remedies when they seek to invalidate the duration of their confinement\xe2\x80\x94either directly through an injunction compelling speedier release or indirectly through a judicial determination that necessarily implies the unlawfulness of the State\xe2\x80\x99s custody. Thus, Preiser found an implied exception to \xc2\xa71983\xe2\x80\x99s coverage where the claim seeks\xe2\x80\x94not where it simply \xe2\x80\x9crelates to\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ccore\xe2\x80\x9d habeas corpus relief, i.e., where a state prisoner requests present or future release. Cf. post, at 5 (Kennedy, J., dissenting) (arguing that Preiser covers challenges that \xe2\x80\x9crelate \xe2\x80\xa6 to\xe2\x80\x9d the duration of confinement). Wolff makes clear that \xc2\xa71983 remains available for procedural challenges where success in the action would not necessarily spell immediate or speedier release for the prisoner. Heck specifies that a prisoner cannot use \xc2\xa71983 to obtain damages where success would necessarily imply the unlawfulness of a (not previously invalidated) conviction or sentence. And Balisok, like Wolff, demonstrates that habeas remedies do not displace \xc2\xa71983 actions where success in the civil rights suit would not necessarily vitiate the legality of (not previously invalidated) state confinement. These cases, taken together, indicate that a state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa71983 action is barred (absent prior invalidation)\xe2\x80\x94no matter the relief sought (damages or equitable relief), no matter the target of the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s suit (state conduct leading to conviction or internal prison proceedings)\xe2\x80\x94if success in that action would necessarily demonstrate the invalidity of confinement or its duration.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying these principles to the present case, we conclude that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims are cognizable under \xc2\xa71983, i.e., they do not fall within the implicit habeas exception. Dotson and Johnson seek relief that will render invalid the state procedures used to deny parole eligibility (Dotson) and parole suitability (Johnson). See Wolff, supra, at 554\xe2\x80\x93555. Neither respondent seeks an injunction ordering his immediate or speedier release into the community. See Preiser, 411 U. S., at 500; Wolff, supra, at 554. And as in Wolff, a favorable judgment will not \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily imply the invalidity of [their] conviction[s] or sentence[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Heck, supra, at 487. Success for Dotson does not mean immediate release from confinement or a shorter stay in prison; it means at most new eligibility review, which at most will speed consideration of a new parole application. Success for Johnson means at most a new parole hearing at which Ohio parole authorities may, in their discretion, decline to shorten his prison term. See Ohio Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa72967.03 (Lexis 2003) (describing the parole authority\xe2\x80\x99s broad discretionary powers); Inmates of Orient Correctional Inst. v. Ohio State Adult Parole Auth. 929 F.\xc2\xa02d 233, 236 (CA6 1991) (same); see also Tr. of Oral Arg. 18 (petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 counsel conceding that success on respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims would not inevitably lead to release). Because neither prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s claim would necessarily spell speedier release, neither lies at \xe2\x80\x9cthe core of habeas corpus.\xe2\x80\x9d Preiser, 411 U. S., at 489. Finally, the prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 claims for future relief (which, if successful, will not necessarily imply the invalidity of confinement or shorten its duration) are yet more distant from that core. See Balisok, supra, at 648.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent disagrees with our legal analysis and advocates use of a different legal standard in critical part because, in its view, (1) a habeas challenge to a sentence (a \xe2\x80\x9ccore\xe2\x80\x9d challenge) does not necessarily produce the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crelease\xe2\x80\x9d (so our standard \xe2\x80\x9cmust be \xe2\x80\xa6 wrong\xe2\x80\x9d), see post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932, 4; and (2) Heck\xe2\x80\x99s standard is irrelevant because Heck concerned only damages, see post, at 4. As to the first, we believe that a case challenging a sentence seeks a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crelease\xe2\x80\x9d in the only pertinent sense: It seeks invalidation (in whole or in part) of the judgment authorizing the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s confinement; the fact that the State may seek a new judgment (through a new trial or a new sentencing proceeding) is beside the point. As to the second, Balisok applied Heck\xe2\x80\x99s standard and addressed a claim seeking not only damages, but also a separate declaration that the State\xe2\x80\x99s procedures were unlawful. See 520 U. S., at 643, 647\xe2\x80\x93648.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ohio makes two additional arguments. First, Ohio points to language in Heck indicating that a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa71983 damages action cannot lie where a favorable judgment would \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily imply the invalidity of his conviction or sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d 512 U. S., at 487 (emphasis added). Ohio then argues that its parole proceedings are part of the prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9csentence[s]\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94indeed, an aspect of the \xe2\x80\x9csentence[s]\xe2\x80\x9d that the \xc2\xa71983 claims, if successful, will invalidate.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not find this argument persuasive. In context, Heck uses the word \xe2\x80\x9csentence\xe2\x80\x9d to refer not to prison procedures, but to substantive determinations as to the length of confinement. See Muhammad v. Close, 540 U. S. 749, 751, n.\xc2\xa01 (2004) (per curiam) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he incarceration that matters under Heck is the incarceration ordered by the original judgment of conviction\xe2\x80\x9d). Heck uses the word \xe2\x80\x9csentence\xe2\x80\x9d interchangeably with such other terms as \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing confinement\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cimprisonment.\xe2\x80\x9d 512 U. S., at 483, 486; see also Balisok, supra, at 645, 648 (referring to the invalidity of \xe2\x80\x9cthe judgment\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpunishment imposed\xe2\x80\x9d). So understood, Heck is consistent with other cases permitting prisoners to bring \xc2\xa71983 challenges to prison administrative decisions. See, e.g., Wolff, 418 U. S., at 554\xe2\x80\x93555; Muhammad, 540 U. S., at 754; see also ibid., (rejecting \xe2\x80\x9cthe mistaken view \xe2\x80\xa6 that Heck applies categorically to all suits challenging prison disciplinary proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, this Court has repeatedly permitted prisoners to bring \xc2\xa71983 actions challenging the conditions of their confinement\xe2\x80\x94conditions that, were Ohio right, might be considered part of the \xe2\x80\x9csentence.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Cooper v. Pate, 378 U. S. 546 (1964) (per curiam); Wilwording v. Swenson, 404 U. S. 249, 251 (1971) (per curiam). And this interpretation of Heck is consistent with Balisok, where the Court held the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s suit Heck-barred not because it sought nullification of the disciplinary procedures but rather because nullification of the disciplinary procedures would lead necessarily to restoration of good-time credits and hence the shortening of the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. 520 U. S., at 646.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, Ohio says that a decision in favor of respondents would break faith with principles of federal/state comity by opening the door to federal court without prior exhaustion of state-court remedies. Our earlier cases, however, have already placed the States\xe2\x80\x99 important comity considerations in the balance, weighed them against the competing need to vindicate federal rights without exhaustion, and concluded that prisoners may bring their claims without fully exhausting state-court remedies so long as their suits, if established, would not necessarily invalidate state-imposed confinement. See Part II, supra. Thus, we see no reason for moving the line these cases draw\xe2\x80\x94particularly since Congress has already strengthened the requirement that prisoners exhaust state administrative remedies as a precondition to any \xc2\xa71983 action. See 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71997e(a); Porter v. Nussle, 534 U. S. 516, 524 (2002).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment is affirmed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, with whom Justice Thomas joins, concurring.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, which in my view reads Heck v. Humphrey, 512 U. S. 477 (1994), and Edwards v. Balisok, 520 U. S. 641 (1997), correctly. And I am in full agreement with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause neither prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s claim would necessarily spell speedier release, neither lies at \xe2\x80\x98the core of habeas corpus\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and both may be brought under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. Ante, at 8. I write separately to note that a contrary holding would require us to broaden the scope of habeas relief beyond recognition.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Preiser v. Rodriguez, 411 U. S. 475 (1973), and the cases that follow it hold that Congress, in enacting \xc2\xa71983, preserved the habeas corpus statute as the sole authorization for challenges to allegedly unlawful confinement. Id., at 489\xe2\x80\x93490. At the time of \xc2\xa71983\xe2\x80\x99s adoption, the federal habeas statute mirrored the common-law writ of habeas corpus, in that it authorized a single form of relief: the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s immediate release from custody. See Act of Feb. 5, 1867, \xc2\xa71, 14 Stat. 386. Congress shortly thereafter amended the statute, authorizing federal habeas courts to \xe2\x80\x9cdispose of the party as law and justice require,\xe2\x80\x9d Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7761. The statute reads virtually the same today, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72243 (\xe2\x80\x9cdispose of the matter as law and justice require\xe2\x80\x9d). We have interpreted this broader remedial language to permit relief short of release. For example, when a habeas petitioner challenges only one of several consecutive sentences, the court may invalidate the challenged sentence even though the prisoner remains in custody to serve the others. See Peyton v. Rowe, 391 U. S. 54, 67 (1968); Walker v. Wainwright, 390 U. S. 335, 336\xe2\x80\x93337 (1968) (per curiam). Thus, in Preiser we held the prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 \xc2\xa71983 action barred because the relief it sought\xe2\x80\x94restoration of good-time credits, which would shorten the prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 incarceration and hasten the date on which they would be transferred to supervised release\xe2\x80\x94was available in habeas. See 411 U. S., at 487\xe2\x80\x93488.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is one thing to say that permissible habeas relief, as our cases interpret the statute, includes ordering a \xe2\x80\x9cquantum change in the level of custody,\xe2\x80\x9d Graham v. Broglin, 922 F. 2d 379, 381 (CA7 1991) (Posner, J.), such as release from incarceration to parole. It is quite another to say that the habeas statute authorizes federal courts to order relief that neither terminates custody, accelerates the future date of release from custody, nor reduces the level of custody. That is what is sought here: the mandating of a new parole hearing that may or may not result in release, prescription of the composition of the hearing panel, and specification of the procedures to be followed. A holding that this sort of judicial immersion in the administration of discretionary parole lies at the \xe2\x80\x9ccore of habeas\xe2\x80\x9d would utterly sever the writ from its common-law roots. Cf. Bell v. Wolfish, 441 U. S. 520, 526, n. 6 (1979) (treating as open the question whether prison-conditions claims are cognizable in habeas). The dissent suggests that because a habeas court may issue a conditional writ ordering a prisoner released unless the State conducts a new sentencing proceeding, the court may also issue a conditional writ ordering release absent a new parole proceeding. See post, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, 4 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). But the prisoner who shows that his sentencing was unconstitutional is actually entitled to release, because the judgment pursuant to which he is confined has been invalidated; the conditional writ serves only to \xe2\x80\x9cdelay the release \xe2\x80\xa6 in order to provide the State an opportunity to correct the constitutional violation.\xe2\x80\x9d Hilton v. Braunskill, 481 U. S. 770, 775 (1987); see In re Bonner, 151 U. S. 242, 259, 262 (1894) (conditional writ for proper resentencing). By contrast, the validly sentenced prisoner who shows only that the State made a procedural error in denying discretionary parole has not established a right to release, and so cannot obtain habeas relief\xe2\x80\x94conditional or otherwise. Conditional writs enable habeas courts to give States time to replace an invalid judgment with a valid one, and the consequence when they fail to do so is always release. Conditional writs are not an all-purpose weapon with which federal habeas courts can extort from the respondent custodian forms of relief short of release, whether a new parole hearing or a new mattress in the applicant\xe2\x80\x99s cell.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners counter that we need not be concerned about this expansion of habeas relief because prisoners will naturally prefer \xc2\xa71983 to habeas corpus, in light of the burdensome prerequisites attached to habeas relief by 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. But those prerequisites, such as exhaustion of state remedies, reliance on \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established Federal law,\xe2\x80\x9d and deference to previous findings of fact, apply only to \xe2\x80\x9ca person in custody pursuant to the judgment of a State court,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(b)(1), (d)(1), (e)(1). By contrast, \xc2\xa72243\xe2\x80\x99s delineation of the scope of permissible relief applies to all federal habeas proceedings, whether the petitioner is in federal or state custody, see \xc2\xa72241(c). Thus, while \xc2\xa72254 may shield petitioners and their fellow state wardens from the impact of the broadened writ they urge us to create, not every warden responding to a habeas petition can claim the same protection. And federal prisoners, whose custodians are not acting under color of state law and hence cannot be sued under \xc2\xa71983, have greater incentives to shoehorn their claims into habeas.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, I note that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion focuses correctly on whether the claims respondents pleaded were claims that may be pursued in habeas\xe2\x80\x94not on whether respondents can be successful in obtaining habeas relief on those claims. See, e.g., ante, at 6. Thus, for example, a prisoner who wishes to challenge the length of his confinement, but who cannot obtain federal habeas relief because of the statute of limitations or the restrictions on successive petitions, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72244(a), (b), (d), cannot use the unavailability of federal habeas relief in his individual case as grounds for proceeding under \xc2\xa71983. Cf. Preiser, supra, at 489\xe2\x80\x93490 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt would wholly frustrate explicit congressional intent to hold that [state prisoners] could evade [the exhaustion] requirement by the simple expedient of putting a different label on their pleadings\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With these observations, I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

REGINALD A. WILKINSON, DIRECTOR, OHIO\nDEPARTMENT OF REHABILITATION AND\nCORRECTION, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v.\nWILLIAM DWIGHT DOTSON et\xc2\xa0al.

[March 7, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the Court insists that an attack on parole proceedings brought under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, may not be dismissed on the grounds that habeas corpus is the exclusive remedy for such claims. The primary reason offered for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding is that an order entitling a prisoner to a new parole proceeding might not result in his early release. That reason, however, applies with equal logic and force to a sentencing proceeding. And since it is elementary that habeas is the appropriate remedy for challenging a sentence, something must be quite wrong with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own first premise.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Everyone knows that when a prisoner succeeds in a habeas action and obtains a new sentencing hearing, the sentence may or may not be reduced. The sentence can end up being just the same, or perhaps longer. The prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s early release is by no means assured simply because the first sentence was found unlawful. Yet no one would say that an attack on judicial sentencing proceedings following conviction may be raised through an action under \xc2\xa71983. The inconsistency in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of sentencing proceedings and parole proceedings is thus difficult to justify. It is, furthermore, in tension with our precedents. For these reasons, I write this respectful dissent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Challenges to parole proceedings are cognizable in habeas. Here respondents challenge parole determinations that not only deny release (or eligibility for consideration for release) but also guarantee continued confinement until the next scheduled parole proceeding. See ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (majority opinion). If a parole determination is made in a proceeding flawed by errors of constitutional dimensions, as these respondents now allege, their continued confinement may well be the result of constitutional violation. Respondents thus raise a cognizable habeas claim of being \xe2\x80\x9cin custody in violation of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72241(c)(3); see also 1 R. Hertz & J. Liebman, Federal Habeas Corpus Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa79.1, pp.\xc2\xa0431\xe2\x80\x93437, and n. 33 (4th ed. 2001) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he range of claims cognizable in federal habeas corpus\xe2\x80\x9d includes challenges to \xe2\x80\x9cthe duration of sentence (including on the basis of parole, good time, and other prison- or administratively, as opposed to court-administered rules)\xe2\x80\x9d and citing numerous cases to that effect). In recognition of this elementary principle, this Court and the courts of appeals have adjudicated the merits of many parole challenges in federal habeas corpus proceedings. See, e.g., California Dept. of Corrections v. Morales, 514 U. S. 499 (1995); Mickens-Thomas v. Vaughn, 321 F.\xc2\xa03d 374 (CA3 2003); Nulph v. Faatz, 27 F.\xc2\xa03d 451 (CA9 1994) (per curiam); Fender v. Thompson, 883 F.\xc2\xa02d 303 (CA4 1989).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0My concerns with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding are increased, not diminished, by the fact that the Court does not seem to deny that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims indeed could be cognizable in habeas corpus proceedings. Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s concurring opinion suggests otherwise, because respondents seek a form of relief (new parole hearings) unavailable in habeas. Ante, at 2. But the common practice of granting a conditional writ\xe2\x80\x94ordering that a State release the prisoner or else correct the constitutional error through a new hearing\xe2\x80\x94already allows a habeas court to compel the type of relief Justice Scalia supposes to be unavailable. See Hilton v. Braunskill, 481 U. S. 770, 775 (1987) (\xe2\x80\x9cFederal habeas corpus practice, as reflected by the decisions of this Court, indicates that a court has broad discretion in conditioning a judgment granting habeas relief\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because habeas is available for parole challenges like respondents\xe2\x80\x99, Preiser v. Rodriguez, 411 U. S. 475 (1973), thus requires a holding that it also provides the exclusive vehicle for them. In Preiser, the Court held that challenges to \xe2\x80\x9cthe very fact or duration of [a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s] confinement,\xe2\x80\x9d as opposed to \xe2\x80\x9cthe conditions of \xe2\x80\xa6 prison life,\xe2\x80\x9d must be brought in habeas, not under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. 411 U. S., at 499\xe2\x80\x93500. The language of \xc2\xa71983, to be sure, is capacious enough to include a challenge to the fact or duration of confinement; Preiser, nonetheless, established that because habeas is the most specific applicable remedy it should be the exclusive means for raising the challenge. Id., at 489. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 challenges to adverse parole system determinations relate not at all to conditions of confinement but rather to the fact and duration of confinement. See Butterfield v. Bail, 120 F.\xc2\xa03d 1023, 1024 (CA9 1997) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] challenge to the procedures used in the denial of parole necessarily implicates the validity of the denial of parole and, therefore, the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s continuing confinement\xe2\x80\x9d). Straightforward application of Preiser and the cases after it would yield the conclusion that these claims must be brought in habeas.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s contrary holding, permitting parole determination challenges to go forward under \xc2\xa71983, is not based on any argument that these claims should be characterized as challenges to conditions of confinement rather than to its fact or duration. That argument is unavailable to the Court. The majority must say instead that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims do not fall into the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98core of habeas.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. For this, it gives two reasons.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first is that success on the claims will not necessarily entitle respondents to immediate release. Ante, at 7. This, as noted at the very outset, proves far too much. If the Court\xe2\x80\x99s line of reasoning is sound, it would remove from the \xe2\x80\x9ccore of habeas\xe2\x80\x9d any challenge to an unconstitutional sentencing procedure.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second reason, that success on the claims does not necessarily imply the invalidity of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 convictions or sentences, ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, is both misplaced and irrelevant. It is misplaced, because it takes out of context the test employed in Heck v. Humphrey, 512 U. S. 477 (1994), and in Edwards v. Balisok, 520 U. S. 641 (1997). In both those cases there was a temptation to seek only relief unavailable in habeas, such as damages (and declaratory relief serving as a predicate to damages), and thus to do an end run around Preiser. Heck, supra, at 481; Balisok, supra, at 643\xe2\x80\x93644; see also Muhammad v. Close, 540 U. S. 749 (2004) (per curiam) (recognizing that damages are unavailable in habeas). Today\xe2\x80\x99s case does not present that problem. The fact that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims do not impugn the validity of their convictions or sentences is also irrelevant. True, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 contentions have nothing to do with their original state-court convictions or sentencing determinations. Stating this fact, however, gets the Court no closer to resolving whether parole determinations themselves are subject to direct challenge only in habeas. That is why we have held that administrative decisions denying good-time credits are subject to attack only in habeas. Preiser, supra, at 477, 500; Balisok, supra, at 643\xe2\x80\x93644.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court makes it a point to cite a sentence fragment from Close, observing that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the incarceration that matters under Heck is the incarceration ordered by the original judgment of conviction,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9 (quoting 540 U. S., at 751, n.\xc2\xa01). That statement, however, is inapplicable even on its own terms, because it addresses the Heck problem, not this one. Furthermore, even apart from Heck\xe2\x80\x99s inapplicability to this case, the full sentence from which the majority takes the quotation makes clear that the Court in Close was contrasting confinement per se with \xe2\x80\x9cspecial disciplinary confinement for infraction of prison rules,\xe2\x80\x9d 540 U. S., at 751, n.\xc2\xa01. That simply is not at issue here. In sum, neither of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s stated principles can justify its deviation from the holding Preiser demands.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s ruling blurs the Preiser formulation. It is apparent that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 challenges relate not at all to conditions of confinement but solely to its duration. Notwithstanding Preiser\xe2\x80\x99s direction that challenges to the fact or duration of confinement should be restricted to habeas, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision will allow numerous \xc2\xa71983 challenges to state parole system determinations that do relate solely to the duration of the prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 confinement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is unsurprising, then, that 18 States have filed an amicus brief joining with Ohio in urging the opposite result, see Brief for Alabama et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision allows state prisoners raising parole challenges to circumvent the state courts. Compare 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(b)(1)(A) (providing that a person in custody pursuant to a state-court judgment must in general exhaust all \xe2\x80\x9cremedies available in the courts of the State\xe2\x80\x9d before seeking federal habeas relief) with 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71997e(a) (requiring only that a prisoner exhaust administrative remedies before bringing a \xc2\xa71983 action to challenge \xe2\x80\x9cprison conditions\xe2\x80\x9d). Parole systems no doubt have variations from State to State. It is within the special province and expertise of the state courts to address challenges to their own state parole determinations in the first instance, particularly because many challenges raise state procedural questions. Today the Court, over the objection of many States, deprives the federal courts of the invaluable assistance and frontline expertise found in the state courts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons given above, I would reverse.
