UNITED STATES v. GRUBBS

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x931414.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 18, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 21, 2006

\n

A Magistrate Judge issued an \xe2\x80\x9canticipatory\xe2\x80\x9d search warrant for respondent Grubbs\xe2\x80\x99 house based on a federal officer\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit. The affidavit explained that the warrant would not be executed until a parcel containing a videotape of child pornography\xe2\x80\x94which Grubbs had ordered from an undercover postal inspector\xe2\x80\x94was received at, and physically taken into, the residence. The affidavit also referred to two attachments describing the residence and the items to be seized. After the package was delivered and the search commenced, Grubbs was given a copy of the warrant, which included the attachments but not the supporting affidavit. When he admitted ordering the videotape, he was arrested, and the videotape and other items were seized. Following his indictment for receiving child pornography, see 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72252(a)(2), Grubbs moved to suppress the seized evidence, arguing, inter alia, that the warrant was invalid because it failed to list the triggering condition. The District Court denied the motion, and Grubbs pleaded guilty. The Ninth Circuit reversed, concluding that the warrant ran afoul of the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s particularity requirement, which, under Circuit precedent, applied to the conditions precedent to an anticipatory warrant.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Anticipatory warrants are not categorically unconstitutional under the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s provision that \xe2\x80\x9cno Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Probable cause exists when \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a fair probability that contraband or evidence of a crime will be found in a particular place.\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois v. Gates, 462 U. S. 213, 238. When an anticipatory warrant is issued, the fact that the contraband is not presently at the place described is immaterial, so long as there is probable cause to believe it will be there when the warrant is executed. Anticipatory warrants are, therefore, no different in principle from ordinary warrants: They require the magistrate to determine (1) that it is now probable that (2) contraband, evidence of a crime, or a fugitive will be on the described premises (3) when the warrant is executed. Where the anticipatory warrant places a condition (other than the mere passage of time) upon its execution, the first of these determinations goes not merely to what will probably be found if the condition is met, but also to the likelihood that the condition will be met, and thus that a proper object of seizure will be on the described premises. Here, the occurrence of the triggering condition\xe2\x80\x94successful delivery of the videotape\xe2\x80\x94would plainly establish probable cause for the search, and the affidavit established probable cause to believe the triggering condition would be satisfied. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The warrant at issue did not violate the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s particularity requirement. The Amendment specifies only two matters that the warrant must \xe2\x80\x9cparticularly describ[e]\xe2\x80\x9d: \xe2\x80\x9cthe place to be searched\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe persons or things to be seized.\xe2\x80\x9d That language is decisive here; the particularity requirement does not include the conditions precedent to execution of the warrant. Cf. Dalia v. United States, 441 U. S. 238, 255, 257. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s two policy rationales\xe2\x80\x94that setting forth the triggering condition in the warrant itself is necessary (1) to delineate the limits of the executing officer\xe2\x80\x99s power and (2) to allow the individual whose property is searched or seized to police the officer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct\xe2\x80\x94find no basis in either the Fourth Amendment or Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 41. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

377 F.\xc2\xa03d 1072 and 389 F.\xc2\xa03d 1306, reversed and remanded.

\n

Souter, J., filed an opinion concurring in part and concurring in the judgment, in which Stevens and Ginsburg, JJ., joined. Alito, J., took no part in the consideration or decision of the case.

UNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. JEFFREY GRUBBS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[March 21, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Federal law enforcement officers obtained a search warrant for respondent\xe2\x80\x99s house on the basis of an affidavit explaining that the warrant would be executed only after a controlled delivery of contraband to that location. We address two challenges to the constitutionality of this anticipatory warrant.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Jeffrey Grubbs purchased a videotape containing child pornography from a Web site operated by an undercover postal inspector. Officers from the Postal Inspection Service arranged a controlled delivery of a package containing the videotape to Grubbs\xe2\x80\x99 residence. A postal inspector submitted a search warrant application to a Magistrate Judge for the Eastern District of California, accompanied by an affidavit describing the proposed operation in detail. The affidavit stated:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cExecution of this search warrant will not occur unless and until the parcel has been received by a person(s) and has been physically taken into the residence . . . . At that time, and not before, this search warrant will be executed by me and other United States Postal inspectors, with appropriate assistance from other law enforcement officers in accordance with this warrant\xe2\x80\x99s command.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 72a.

\n

\nIn addition to describing this triggering condition, the affidavit referred to two attachments, which described Grubbs\xe2\x80\x99 residence and the items officers would seize. These attachments, but not the body of the affidavit, were incorporated into the requested warrant. The affidavit concluded:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cBased upon the foregoing facts, I respectfully submit there exists probable cause to believe that the items set forth in Attachment B to this affidavit and the search warrant, will be found [at Grubbs\xe2\x80\x99 residence], which residence is further described at Attachment A.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Magistrate Judge issued the warrant as requested. Two days later, an undercover postal inspector delivered the package. Grubbs\xe2\x80\x99 wife signed for it and took the unopened package inside. The inspectors detained Grubbs as he left his home a few minutes later, then entered the house and commenced the search. Roughly 30 minutes into the search, Grubbs was provided with a copy of the warrant, which included both attachments but not the supporting affidavit that explained when the warrant would be executed. Grubbs consented to interrogation by the postal inspectors and admitted ordering the videotape. He was placed under arrest, and various items were seized, including the videotape.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A grand jury for the Eastern District of California indicted Grubbs on one count of receiving a visual depiction of a minor engaged in sexually explicit conduct. See 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72252(a)(2). He moved to suppress the evidence seized during the search of his residence, arguing as relevant here that the warrant was invalid because it failed to list the triggering condition. After an evidentiary hearing, the District Court denied the motion. Grubbs pleaded guilty, but reserved his right to appeal the denial of his motion to suppress.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed. 377 F.\xc2\xa03d 1072, amended, 389 F.\xc2\xa03d 1306 (2004). Relying on Circuit precedent, it held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe particularity requirement of the Fourth Amendment applies with full force to the conditions precedent to an anticipatory search warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d 377 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1077\xe2\x80\x931078 (citing United States v. Hotal, 143 F. 3d 1223, 1226 (CA9 1998)). An anticipatory warrant defective for that reason may be \xe2\x80\x9ccur[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d if the conditions precedent are set forth in an affidavit that is incorporated in the warrant and \xe2\x80\x9cpresented to the person whose property is being searched.\xe2\x80\x9d 377 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1079. Because the postal inspectors \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to present the affidavit\xe2\x80\x94the only document in which the triggering conditions were listed\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94to Grubbs or his wife, the \xe2\x80\x9cwarrant was \xe2\x80\xa6 inoperative, and the search was illegal.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We granted certiorari. 545 U. S. ___ (2005).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before turning to the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the warrant at issue here ran afoul of the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s particularity requirement, we address the antecedent question whether anticipatory search warrants are categorically unconstitutional.[Footnote 1] An anticipatory warrant is \xe2\x80\x9ca warrant based upon an affidavit showing probable cause that at some future time (but not presently) certain evidence of crime will be located at a specified place.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 W. LaFave, Search and Seizure \xc2\xa73.7(c), p. 398 (4th ed. 2004). Most anticipatory warrants subject their execution to some condition precedent other than the mere passage of time\xe2\x80\x94a so-called \xe2\x80\x9ctriggering condition.\xe2\x80\x9d The affidavit at issue here, for instance, explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xecution of th[e] search warrant will not occur unless and until the parcel [containing child pornography] has been received by a person(s) and has been physically taken into the residence.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 72a. If the government were to execute an anticipatory warrant before the triggering condition occurred, there would be no reason to believe the item described in the warrant could be found at the searched location; by definition, the triggering condition which establishes probable cause has not yet been satisfied when the warrant is issued. Grubbs argues that for this reason anticipatory warrants contravene the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s provision that \xe2\x80\x9cno Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We reject this view, as has every Court of Appeals to confront the issue, see, e.g., United States v. Loy, 191 F. 3d 360, 364 (CA3 1999) (collecting cases). Probable cause exists when \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a fair probability that contraband or evidence of a crime will be found in a particular place.\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois v. Gates, 462 U. S. 213, 238 (1983). Because the probable-cause requirement looks to whether evidence will be found when the search is conducted, all warrants are, in a sense, \xe2\x80\x9canticipatory.\xe2\x80\x9d In the typical case where the police seek permission to search a house for an item they believe is already located there, the magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s determination that there is probable cause for the search amounts to a prediction that the item will still be there when the warrant is executed. See People v. Glen, 30 N. Y. 2d 252, 258, 282 N. E. 2d 614, 617 (1972) (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]resent possession is only probative of the likelihood of future possession.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 2] The anticipatory nature of warrants is even clearer in the context of electronic surveillance. See, e.g., Katz v. United States, 389 U. S. 347 (1967). When police request approval to tap a telephone line, they do so based on the probability that, during the course of the surveillance, the subject will use the phone to engage in crime-related conversations. The relevant federal provision requires a judge authorizing \xe2\x80\x9cinterception of wire, oral, or electronic communications\xe2\x80\x9d to determine that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is probable cause for belief that particular communications concerning [one of various listed offenses] will be obtained through such interception.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72518(3)(b) (emphasis added); see also United States v. Ricciardelli, 998 F.\xc2\xa02d 8, 11, n. 3 (CA1 1993) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he magistrate issues the warrant on the basis of a substantial probability that crime-related conversations will ensue.\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, when an anticipatory warrant is issued, \xe2\x80\x9cthe fact that the contraband is not presently located at the place described in the warrant is immaterial, so long as there is probable cause to believe that it will be there when the search warrant is executed.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Garcia, 882 F. 2d 699, 702 (CA2 1989) (quoting United States v. Lowe, 575 F. 2d 1193, 1194 (CA6 1978); internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Anticipatory warrants are, therefore, no different in principle from ordinary warrants. They require the magistrate to determine (1) that it is now probable that (2) contraband, evidence of a crime, or a fugitive will be on the described premises (3) when the warrant is executed. It should be noted, however, that where the anticipatory warrant places a condition (other than the mere passage of time) upon its execution, the first of these determinations goes not merely to what will probably be found if the condition is met. (If that were the extent of the probability determination, an anticipatory warrant could be issued for every house in the country, authorizing search and seizure if contraband should be delivered\xe2\x80\x94though for any single location there is no likelihood that contraband will be delivered.) Rather, the probability determination for a conditioned anticipatory warrant looks also to the likelihood that the condition will occur, and thus that a proper object of seizure will be on the described premises. In other words, for a conditioned anticipatory warrant to comply with the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s requirement of probable cause, two prerequisites of probability must be satisfied. It must be true not only that if the triggering condition occurs \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a fair probability that contraband or evidence of a crime will be found in a particular place,\xe2\x80\x9d Gates, supra, at 238, but also that there is probable cause to believe the triggering condition will occur. The supporting affidavit must provide the magistrate with sufficient information to evaluate both aspects of the probable-cause determination. See Garcia, supra, at 703.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the occurrence of the triggering condition\xe2\x80\x94successful delivery of the videotape to Grubbs\xe2\x80\x99 residence\xe2\x80\x94would plainly establish probable cause for the search. In addition, the affidavit established probable cause to believe the triggering condition would be satisfied. Although it is possible that Grubbs could have refused delivery of the videotape he had ordered, that was unlikely. The Magistrate therefore \xe2\x80\x9chad a \xe2\x80\x98substantial basis for . . . conclud[ing]\xe2\x80\x99 that probable cause existed.\xe2\x80\x9d Gates, 462 U. S., at 238\xe2\x80\x93239 (quoting Jones v. United States, 362 U. S. 257, 271 (1960)).

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit invalidated the anticipatory search warrant at issue here because the warrant failed to specify the triggering condition. The Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s particularity requirement, it held, \xe2\x80\x9capplies with full force to the conditions precedent to an anticipatory search warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d 377 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1077\xe2\x80\x931078.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fourth Amendment, however, does not set forth some general \xe2\x80\x9cparticularity requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d It specifies only two matters that must be \xe2\x80\x9cparticularly describ[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d in the warrant: \xe2\x80\x9cthe place to be searched\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe persons or things to be seized.\xe2\x80\x9d We have previously rejected efforts to expand the scope of this provision to embrace unenumerated matters. In Dalia v. United States, 441 U. S. 238 (1979), we considered an order authorizing the interception of oral communications by means of a \xe2\x80\x9cbug\xe2\x80\x9d installed by the police in the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s office. The petitioner argued that, if a covert entry is necessary to install such a listening device, the authorizing order must \xe2\x80\x9cexplicitly set forth its approval of such entries before the fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 255. This argument fell before the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98precise and clear\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d words of the Fourth Amendment: \xe2\x80\x9cNothing in the language of the Constitution or in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions interpreting that language suggests that, in addition to the [requirements set forth in the text], search warrants also must include a specification of the precise manner in which they are to be executed.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 255 (quoting Stanford v. Texas, 379 U. S. 476, 481 (1965)), 257. The language of the Fourth Amendment is likewise decisive here; its particularity requirement does not include the conditions precedent to execution of the warrant.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent, drawing upon the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis below, relies primarily on two related policy rationales. First, he argues, setting forth the triggering condition in the warrant itself is necessary \xe2\x80\x9cto delineate the limits of the executing officer\xe2\x80\x99s power.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 20. This is an application, respondent asserts, of the following principle: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f there is a precondition to the valid exercise of executive power, that precondition must be particularly identified on the face of the warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 23. That principle is not to be found in the Constitution. The Fourth Amendment does not require that the warrant set forth the magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s basis for finding probable cause, even though probable cause is the quintessential \xe2\x80\x9cprecondition to the valid exercise of executive power.\xe2\x80\x9d Much less does it require description of a triggering condition.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, respondent argues that listing the triggering condition in the warrant is necessary to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98assur[e] the individual whose property is searched or seized of the lawful authority of the executing officer, his need to search, and the limits of his power to search.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 19 (quoting United States v. Chadwick, 433 U. S. 1, 9 (1977)). The Ninth Circuit went even further, asserting that if the property owner were not informed of the triggering condition, he \xe2\x80\x9cwould \xe2\x80\x98stand [no] real chance of policing the officers\xe2\x80\x99 conduct.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 377 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1079 (quoting Ramirez v. Butte-Silver Bow County, 298 F.\xc2\xa03d 1022, 1027 (CA9 2002)). This argument assumes that the executing officer must present the property owner with a copy of the warrant before conducting his search. See 377 F. 3d, at 1079, n. 9. In fact, however, neither the Fourth Amendment nor Rule 41 of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure imposes such a requirement. See Groh v. Ramirez, 540 U. S. 551, 562, n.\xc2\xa05 (2004). \xe2\x80\x9cThe absence of a constitutional requirement that the warrant be exhibited at the outset of the search, or indeed until the search has ended, is \xe2\x80\xa6 evidence that the requirement of particular description does not protect an interest in monitoring searches.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Stefonek, 179 F. 3d 1030, 1034 (CA7 1999) (citations omitted). The Constitution protects property owners not by giving them license to engage the police in a debate over the basis for the warrant, but by interposing, ex ante, the \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate, impartial judgment of a judicial officer . . . between the citizen and the police.\xe2\x80\x9d Wong Sun v. United States, 371 U. S. 471, 481\xe2\x80\x93482 (1963), and by providing, ex post, a right to suppress evidence improperly obtained and a cause of action for damages.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the Fourth Amendment does not require that the triggering condition for an anticipatory search warrant be set forth in the warrant itself, the Court of Appeals erred in invalidating the warrant at issue here. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\nOPINION OF SOUTER, J.
\nUNITED STATES V. GRUBBS
\n547 U. S. ____ (2006)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 04-1414
\n
\n

UNITED STATES, PETITIONER v. JEFFREY GRUBBS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[March 21, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Souter, with whom Justice Stevens and Justice Ginsburg join, concurring in part and concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that anticipatory warrants are constitutional for the reasons stated in Part II of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, and I join in the disposition of this case. But I would qualify some points made in Part III.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court notes that a warrant\xe2\x80\x99s failure to specify the place to be searched and the objects sought violates an express textual requirement of the Fourth Amendment, whereas the text says nothing about a condition placed by the issuing magistrate on the authorization to search (here, delivery of the package of contraband). That textual difference is, however, no authority for neglecting to specify the point or contingency intended by the magistrate to trigger authorization, and the government should beware of banking on the terms of a warrant without such specification. The notation of a starting date was an established feature even of the objectionable 18th-century writs of assistance, see, e.g., Massachusetts Writs of Assistance Bill, 1762, reprinted in M. Smith, The Writs of Assistance Case 567\xe2\x80\x93568 (1978); Writ of Assistance (English) of George III, 1761, reprinted in id., at 524\xe2\x80\x93527. And it is fair to say that the very word \xe2\x80\x9cwarrant\xe2\x80\x9d in the Fourth Amendment means a statement of authority that sets out the time at which (or, in the case of anticipatory warrants, the condition on which) the authorization begins.*

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An issuing magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s failure to mention that condition can lead to several untoward consequences with constitutional significance. To begin with, a warrant that fails to tell the truth about what a magistrate authorized cannot inform the police officer\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility to respect the limits of authorization, see Groh v. Ramirez, 540 U. S. 551, 560\xe2\x80\x93563, 561, and n.\xc2\xa04 (2004), a failing assuming real significance when the warrant is not executed by the official who applied for it and happens to know the unstated condition. The peril is that if an officer simply takes such a warrant on its face and makes the ostensibly authorized search before the unstated condition has been met, the search will be held unreasonable. It is true that we have declined to apply the exclusionary rule when a police officer reasonably relies on the product of a magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s faulty judgment or sloppy practice, see Massachusetts v. Sheppard, 468 U. S. 981, 987\xe2\x80\x93991 (1984). But when a government officer obtains what the magistrate says is an anticipatory warrant, he must know or should realize when it omits the condition on which authorization depends, and it is hard to see why the government should not be held to the condition despite the unconditional face of the warrant. Cf. Groh v. Ramirez, supra, at 554\xe2\x80\x93555, 563, and n.\xc2\xa06 (declaring unconstitutional a search conducted pursuant to a warrant failing to specify the items the government asked the magistrate permission to seize in part because \xe2\x80\x9cofficers leading a search team must \xe2\x80\x98make sure that they have a proper warrant that in fact authorizes the search and seizure they are about to conduct\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (brackets omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does an incomplete anticipatory warrant address an owner\xe2\x80\x99s interest in an accurate statement of the government\xe2\x80\x99s authority to search property. To be sure, the extent of that interest is yet to be settled; in Groh v. Ramirez, supra, the Court was careful to note that the right of an owner to demand to see a copy of the warrant before making way for the police had not been determined, id., at 562, n.\xc2\xa05, and it remains undetermined today. But regardless of any right on the owner\xe2\x80\x99s part, showing an accurate warrant reliably \xe2\x80\x9cassures the individual whose property is searched or seized of the lawful authority of the executing officer, his need to search, and the limits of his power to search.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Chadwick, 433 U. S. 1, 9 (1977), quoted in Groh v. Ramirez, supra, at 561. And if a later case holds that the homeowner has a right to inspect the warrant on request, a statement of the condition of authorization would give the owner a right to correct any misapprehension on the police\xe2\x80\x99s part that the condition had been met when in fact it had not been. If the police were then to enter anyway without a reasonable (albeit incorrect) justification, the search would certainly be open to serious challenge as unreasonable within the meaning of the Fourth Amendment.
