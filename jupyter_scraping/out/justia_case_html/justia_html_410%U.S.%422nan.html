\n

Twenty days after this Court granted a writ of certiorari, 409 U.S. 841, Congress amended the relevant statutory provisions, \xc2\xa7 202(d)(8) of the Social Security Act, 42 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 402(d)(8). See \xc2\xa7 111(a), Social Security Amendments of 1972 (Oct. 30, 1972), Pub.L. 92-603, 86 Stat. 1329. The writ of certiorari heretofore granted is dismissed as improvidently granted.

\n

MR. JUSTICE DOUGLAS, dissenting.

\n

In this case, petitioner was denied social security benefits for his dependent adopted daughter because her court-approved adoption was not supervised by a child placement agency. As noted by the Court, the section which barred his claim at the time that it was filed has now been repealed. [Footnote 1] What the Court does not deal with,

Page 410 U. S. 423

however, is the patchwork nature of the relief that Congress has provided.

\n

Section 111(b) of the new Act [Footnote 2] specifies the dates and circumstances to which \xc2\xa7 111(a) [Footnote 3] applies. As I read \xc2\xa7 111(b), should petitioner qualify for increased benefits under \xc2\xa7 111(a)\'s new standards, he could now secure retroactive application of the revised Act to cover the entire period at issue in this case. It is true that \xc2\xa7 111(a) no longer requires that court-authorized adoptions be supervised by a child-placement agency. Petitioner\'s lot is not, however, bettered, since \xc2\xa7 111(a) now imposes a new requirement which petitioner cannot meet: that benefits may be paid to an adopted child only

Page 410 U. S. 424

if the child was living with the beneficiary "for the year immediately before the month in which began the period of [compensable] disability. . . ." \xc2\xa7 111(a)(8)(D)(ii) (II). Petitioner began receiving disability insurance benefits in July, 1957. His adopted daughter was not born until 1965, however, and petitioner will accordingly not benefit under the terms of \xc2\xa7 111(a).

\n

Section 111(b) does not alter 42 U.S.C. \xc2\xa7 402(d)(8) as to the applications filed before October, 1972, for benefits accruing before October 30, 1972. Such an application is before us in this case. Petitioner is therefore entitled to benefits under the former statute, if there is merit to his claim that the old \xc2\xa7 402(d)(8) distinctions among types of adoption supervision are constitutionally infirm.

\n

Because the new Act does not provide coverage for petitioner\'s child, but the old Act remains applicable to the claim before us, I would reach the merits.
