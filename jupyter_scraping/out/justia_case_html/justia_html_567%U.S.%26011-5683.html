\n\t\t\t\tDORSEY v. UNITED STATES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x935683.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 17, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 21, 2012\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tUnder the Anti-Drug Abuse Act (1986 Drug Act), the 5- and 10-year mandatory minimum prison terms for federal drug crimes reflected a 100-to-1 disparity between the amounts of crack cocaine and powder cocaine needed to trigger the minimums. Thus, the 5-year minimum was triggered by a conviction for possessing with intent to distribute 5 grams of crack cocaine but 500 grams of powder, and the 10-year minimum was triggered by a conviction for possessing with intent to distribute 50 grams of crack but 5,000 grams of powder. The United States Sentencing Commission\xe2\x80\x94which is charged under the Sentencing Reform Act of 1984 with writing the Federal Sentencing Guidelines\xe2\x80\x94incorporated the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s 100-to-1 disparity into the Guidelines because it believed that doing so was the best way to keep similar drug-trafficking sentences proportional, thereby satisfying the Sentencing Reform Act\xe2\x80\x99s basic proportionality objective. The Fair Sentencing Act, which took effect on August 3, 2010, reduced the disparity to 18-to-1, lowering the mandatory minimums applicable to many crack offenders, by increasing the amount of crack needed to trigger the 5-year minimum from 5 to 28 grams and the amount for the 10-year minimum from 50 to 280 grams, while leaving the powder cocaine amounts intact. It also directed the Sentencing Commission to make conforming amendments to the Guidelines \xe2\x80\x9cas soon as practicable\xe2\x80\x9d (but no later than 90 days after the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s effective date). The new amendments became effective on November 1, 2010.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn No. 11\xe2\x88\x925721, petitioner Hill unlawfully sold 53 grams of crack in 2007, but was not sentenced until December 2010. Sentencing him to the 10-year minimum mandated by the 1986 Drug Act, the District Judge ruled that the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s 5-year minimum for selling that amount of crack did not apply to those whose offenses were committed before the Act\xe2\x80\x99s effective date. In No. 11\xe2\x88\x925683, petitioner Dorsey unlawfully sold 5.5 grams of crack in 2008. In September 2010, the District Judge sentenced him to the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s 10-year minimum, finding that it applied because Dorsey had a prior drug conviction and declining to apply the Fair Sentencing Act, under which there would be no mandated minimum term for an amount less than 28 grams, because Dorsey\xe2\x80\x99s offense predated that Act\xe2\x80\x99s effective date. The Seventh Circuit affirmed in both cases.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s new, lower mandatory minimums apply to the post-Act sentencing of pre-Act offenders. Pp. 10\xe2\x88\x9220.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Language in different statutes argues in opposite directions. The general federal saving statute (1871 Act) provides that a new criminal statute that \xe2\x80\x9crepeal[s]\xe2\x80\x9d an older criminal statute shall not change the penalties \xe2\x80\x9cincurred\xe2\x80\x9d under that older statute \xe2\x80\x9cunless the repealing Act shall so expressly provide.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7109. The word \xe2\x80\x9crepeal\xe2\x80\x9d applies when a new statute simply diminishes the penalties that the older statute set forth, see Warden v. Marrero, \n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 653\n\xe2\x88\x92664, and penalties are \xe2\x80\x9cincurred\xe2\x80\x9d under the older statute when an offender becomes subject to them, i.e., commits the underlying conduct that makes the offender liable, see United States v. Reisinger, \n\n128 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398\n. In contrast, the Sentencing Reform Act says that, regardless of when the offender\xe2\x80\x99s conduct occurs, the applicable sentencing guidelines are the ones \xe2\x80\x9cin effect on the date the defendant is sentenced.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a)(4)(A)(ii).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSix considerations, taken together, show that Congress intended the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s more lenient penalties to apply to offenders who committed crimes before August 3, 2010, but were sentenced after that date. First, the 1871 saving statute permits Congress to apply a new Act\xe2\x80\x99s more lenient penalties to pre-Act offenders without expressly saying so in the new Act. The 1871 Act creates what is in effect a less demanding interpretive requirement because the statute \xe2\x80\x9ccannot justify a disregard of the will of Congress as manifested, either expressly or by necessary implication, in a subsequent enactment.\xe2\x80\x9d Great Northern R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. United States, \n\n208 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452\n. Hence, this Court has treated the 1871 Act as setting forth an important background principle of interpretation that requires courts, before interpreting a new criminal statute to apply its new penalties to a set of pre-Act offenders, to assure themselves by the \xe2\x80\x9cplain import\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cfair implication\xe2\x80\x9d of the new statute that ordinary interpretive considerations point clearly in that direction. Second, the Sentencing Reform Act sets forth a special and different background principle in \xc2\xa73553(a)(4)(A)(ii), which applies unless ex post facto concerns are present. Thus, new, lower Guidelines amendments apply to offenders who committed an offense before the adoption of the amendments but are sentenced thereafter. Third, language in the Fair Sentencing Act implies that Congress intended to follow the Sentencing Reform Act\xe2\x80\x99s special background principle here. Section 8 of the Fair Sentencing Act requires the Commission to promulgate conforming amendments to the Guidelines that \xe2\x80\x9cachieve consistency with other guideline provisions and applicable law.\xe2\x80\x9d Read most naturally, \xe2\x80\x9capplicable law\xe2\x80\x9d refers to the law as changed by the Fair Sentencing Act, including the provision reducing the crack mandatory minimums. And consistency with \xe2\x80\x9cother guideline provisions\xe2\x80\x9d and with prior Commission practice would require application of the new Guidelines amendments to offenders who committed their offense before the new amendments\xe2\x80\x99 effective date but were sentenced thereafter. Fourth, applying the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s old mandatory minimums to the post-August 3 sentencing of pre-August 3 offenders would create sentencing disparities of a kind that Congress enacted the Sentencing Reform Act and the Fair Sentencing Act to prevent. Fifth, not to apply the Fair Sentencing Act would do more than preserve a disproportionate status quo; it would make matters worse by creating new anomalies\xe2\x80\x95new sets of disproportionate sentences\xe2\x80\x95not previously present. That is because sentencing courts must apply the new Guidelines (consistent with the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s new minimums) to pre-Act offenders, and the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s old minimums would trump those new Guidelines for some pre-Act offenders but not for all of them. Application of the 1986 Drug Act minimums to pre-Act offenders sentenced after the new Guidelines take effect would therefore produce a set of sentences at odds with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 basic efforts to create more uniform, more proportionate sentences. Sixth, this Court has found no strong countervailing considerations that would make a critical difference. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x88\x9219.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The new Act\xe2\x80\x99s lower minimums also apply to those who committed an offense prior to August 3 and were sentenced between that date and November 1, 2010, the effective date of the new Guidelines. The Act simply instructs the Commission to promulgate new Guidelines \xe2\x80\x9cas soon as practicable\xe2\x80\x9d (but no later than 90 days after the Act took effect), and thus as far as Congress was concerned, the Commission might have promulgated those Guidelines to be effective as early as August 3. In any event, courts, treating the Guidelines as advi-sory, possess authority to sentence in accordance with the new minimums. Finally, applying the new minimums to all who are sentenced after August 3 makes it possible to foresee a reasonably smooth transition, and this Court has no reason to believe Congress would have wanted to impose an unforeseeable, potentially complex application date. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x88\x9220.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFederal statutes impose mandatory minimum prison sentences upon those convicted of federal drug crimes. These statutes typically base the length of a minimum prison term upon the kind and amount of the drug involved. Until 2010, the relevant statute imposed upon an offender who dealt in powder cocaine the same sentence it imposed upon an offender who dealt in one one-hundredth that amount of crack cocaine. It imposed, for example, the same 5-year minimum term upon (1) an offender convicted of possessing with intent to distribute 500 grams of powder cocaine as upon (2) an offender convicted of possessing with intent to distribute 5 grams of crack.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2010, Congress enacted a new statute reducing the crack-to-powder cocaine disparity from 100-to-1 to 18-to-1. Fair Sentencing Act, \n\n124Stat.\n2372. The new statute took effect on August 3, 2010. The question here is whether the Act\xe2\x80\x99s more lenient penalty provisions apply to offenders who committed a crack cocaine crime before August 3, 2010, but were not sentenced until after August 3. We hold that the new, more lenient mandatory minimum provisions do apply to those pre-Act offenders.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe underlying question before us is one of congres-sional intent as revealed in the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s lan-guage, structure, and basic objectives. Did Congress intend the Act\xe2\x80\x99s more lenient penalties to apply to pre-Act offenders sentenced after the Act took effect?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe recognize that, because of important background principles of interpretation, we must assume that Congress did not intend those penalties to apply unless it clearly indicated to the contrary. See infra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9313. But we find that clear indication here. We rest our conclu- sion primarily upon the fact that a contrary determination would seriously undermine basic Federal Sentencing Guidelines objectives such as uniformity and proportionality in sentencing. Indeed, seen from that perspective, a contrary determination would (in respect to relevant groups of drug offenders) produce sentences less uniform and more disproportionate than if Congress had not enacted the Fair Sentencing Act at all. See infra, at 14\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause our conclusion rests upon an analysis of the Guidelines-based sentencing system Congress has established, we describe that system at the outset and include an explanation of how the Guidelines interact with federal statutes setting forth specific terms of imprisonment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Guidelines originate in the Sentencing Reform Act of 1984, \n\n98Stat.\n1987. That statute created a federal Sentencing Commission instructed to write guidelines that judges would use to determine sentences imposed upon offenders convicted of committing federal crimes. 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7991, 994. Congress thereby sought to increase transparency, uniformity, and proportionality in sentencing. United States Sentencing Commission (USSC or Commission), Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa71A1.3, p. 2 (Nov. 2011) (USSG); see 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7991(b)(1), 994(f).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Sentencing Reform Act directed the Commission to create in the Guidelines categories of offense behavior (e.g., \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98bank robbery/committed with a gun/$2500 taken\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d) and offender characteristics (e.g., \xe2\x80\x9cone prior conviction\xe2\x80\x9d). USSG \xc2\xa71A1.2, at 1; see 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7994(a)\xe2\x80\x93(e). A sentencing judge determines a Guidelines range by (1) finding the applicable offense level and offender category and then (2) consulting a table that lists proportionate sentenc- ing ranges (e.g., 18 to 24 months of imprisonment) at the intersections of rows (marking offense levels) and columns (marking offender categories). USSG ch. 5, pt. A, Sen-tencing Table, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa75E1.2, 7B1.4; see also \xc2\xa71A1.4(h), at 11. The Guidelines, after telling the judge how to determine the applicable offense level and offender category, instruct the judge to apply the intersection\xe2\x80\x99s range in an ordinary case, but they leave the judge free to depart from that range in an unusual case. See \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(b); USSG \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71A1.2, at 1\xe2\x80\x932, 1A1.4(b), at 6\xe2\x80\x937. This Court has held that the Guidelines are now advisory. United States v. Booker, \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220, \n245, \n264 (2005)\n; see Kimbrough v. United States, \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 85, \n91 (2007)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Guidelines determine most drug-crime offense lev-els in a special way. They set forth a Drug Quantity Table (or Table) that lists amounts of various drugs and associates different amounts with different \xe2\x80\x9cBase Offense Levels\xe2\x80\x9d (to which a judge may add or subtract levels depending upon the \xe2\x80\x9cspecific\xe2\x80\x9d characteristics of the offender\xe2\x80\x99s behavior). See USSG \xc2\xa72D1.1. The Table, for example, associates 400 to 499 grams of powder cocaine with a base offense level of 24, a level that would mean for a first-time offender a prison term of 51 to 63 months. \xc2\xa72D1.1(c).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1986, Congress enacted a more specific, drug-related sentencing statute, the Anti-Drug Abuse Act (1986 Drug Act), \n\n100Stat.\n3207. That statute sets forth mandatory minimum penalties of 5 and 10 years applicable to a drug offender depending primarily upon the kind and amount of drugs involved in the offense. See 21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7841(b)(1) (A)\xe2\x80\x93(C) (2006 ed. and Supp. IV). The minimum applicable to an offender convicted of possessing with intent to distribute 500 grams or more of powder cocaine is 5 years, and for 5,000 grams or more of powder the minimum is 10 years. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A)(ii), (B)(ii). The 1986 Drug Act, however, treated crack cocaine crimes as far more serious. It applied its 5-year minimum to an offender convicted of possessing with intent to distribute only 5 grams of crack (as compared to 500 grams of powder) and its 10-year minimum to one convicted of possessing with intent to distribute only 50 grams of crack (as compared to 5,000 grams of powder), thus producing a 100-to-1 crack-to-powder ratio. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A)(iii), (B)(iii) (2006 ed.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe 1986 Drug Act, like other federal sentencing statutes, interacts with the Guidelines in an important way. Like other sentencing statutes, it trumps the Guidelines. Thus, ordinarily no matter what the Guidelines provide, a judge cannot sentence an offender to a sentence beyond the maximum contained in the federal statute setting forth the crime of conviction. Similarly, ordinarily no matter what range the Guidelines set forth, a sentencing judge must sentence an offender to at least the minimum prison term set forth in a statutory mandatory minimum. See 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7994(a), (b)(1); USSG \xc2\xa75G1.1; Neal v. United States, \n\n516 U.\xc2\xa0S. 284\n\xe2\x80\x93290, 295 (1996).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNot surprisingly, the Sentencing Commission incorporated the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory minimums into the first version of the Guidelines themselves. Kimbrough, supra, at 96\xe2\x80\x9397. It did so by setting a base offense level for a first-time drug offender that corresponded to the lowest Guidelines range above the applicable mandatory minimum. USSC, Report to the Congress: Mandatory Minimum Penalties in the Federal Criminal Justice System 53\xe2\x80\x9354 (Oct. 2011) (2011 Report). Thus, the first Guidelines Drug Quantity Table associated 500 grams of powder cocaine with an offense level of 26, which for a first-time offender meant a sentencing range of 63 to 78 months (just above the 5-year minimum), and it associated 5,000 grams of powder cocaine with an offense level of 32, which for a first-time offender meant a sentencing range of 121 to 151 months (just above the 10-year minimum). USSG \xc2\xa72D1.1 (Oct. 1987). Further reflecting the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s 100-to-1 crack-to-powder ratio, the Table associated an offense level of 26 with 5 grams of crack and an offense level of 32 with 50 grams of crack. Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition, the Drug Quantity Table set offense levels for small drug amounts that did not trigger the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory minimums so that the resulting Guidelines sentences would remain proportionate to the sentences for amounts that did trigger these minimums. 2011 Report 54. Thus, the Table associated 400 grams of powder cocaine (an amount that fell just below the amount triggering the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s 5-year minimum) with an offense level of 24, which for a first-time offender meant a sentencing range of 51 to 63 months (the range just below the 5-year minimum). USSG \xc2\xa72D1.1 (Oct. 1987). Following the 100-to-1 crack-to-powder ratio, the Table associated four grams of crack (an amount that also fell just below the amount triggering the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s 5-year minimum) with an offense level of 24. Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Commission did this not because it necessarily thought that those levels were most in keeping with past sentencing practice or would independently have reflected a fair set of sentences, but rather because the Commission believed that doing so was the best way to keep similar drug-trafficking sentences proportional, thereby satisfying the Sentencing Reform Act\xe2\x80\x99s basic \xe2\x80\x9cproportionality\xe2\x80\x9d objective. See Kimbrough, 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 97; USSG \xc2\xa71A1.3 (Nov. 2011); 2011 Report 53\xe2\x80\x9354, 349, and n.\xc2\xa0845. For this reason, the Commission derived the Drug Quantity Table\xe2\x80\x99s entire set of crack and powder cocaine offense levels by using the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s two (5- and 10-year) minimum amounts as reference points and then extrapolating from those two amounts upward and downward to set proportional offense levels for other drug amounts. Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDuring the next two decades, the Commission and others in the law enforcement community strongly criticized Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to set the crack-to-powder mandatory minimum ratio at 100-to-1. The Commission issued four separate reports telling Congress that the ratio was too high and unjustified because, for example, research showed the relative harm between crack and powder cocaine less severe than 100-to-1, because sentences embodying that ratio could not achieve the Sentencing Reform Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cuniformity\xe2\x80\x9d goal of treating like offenders alike, because they could not achieve the \xe2\x80\x9cproportionality\xe2\x80\x9d goal of treating different offenders (e.g., major drug traffickers and low-level dealers) differently, and because the public had come to understand sentences embodying the 100-to-1 ratio as reflecting unjustified race-based differences. Kimbrough, supra, at 97\xe2\x80\x9398; see, e.g., USSC, Special Report to the Congress: Cocaine and Federal Sen-tencing Policy 197\xe2\x80\x93198 (Feb. 1995) (1995 Report); USSC, Special Report to Congress: Cocaine and Federal Sentencing Policy 8 (Apr. 1997) (1997 Report); USSC, Report to Congress: Cocaine and Federal Sentencing Policy 91, 103 (May 2002) (2002 Report); USSC, Report to Congress: Cocaine and Federal Sentencing Policy 8 (May 2007) (2007 Report). The Commission also asked Congress for new legislation embodying a lower crack-to-powder ratio. 1995 Report 198\xe2\x80\x93200; 1997 Report 9\xe2\x80\x9310; 2002 Report 103\xe2\x80\x93 107; 2007 Report 6\xe2\x80\x939. And the Commission recommended that the legislation \xe2\x80\x9cinclude\xe2\x80\x9d an \xe2\x80\x9cemergency amendment\xe2\x80\x9d allowing \xe2\x80\x9cthe Commission to incorporate the statutory changes\xe2\x80\x9d in the Guidelines while \xe2\x80\x9cminimiz[ing] the lag between any statutory and guideline modifications for cocaine offenders.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 9.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2010, Congress accepted the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s recommendations, see 2002 Report 104; 2007 Report 8\xe2\x80\x939, and n.\xc2\xa026, and enacted the Fair Sentencing Act into law. The Act increased the drug amounts triggering mandatory minimums for crack trafficking offenses from 5 grams to 28 grams in respect to the 5-year minimum and from 50 grams to 280 grams in respect to the 10-year minimum (while leaving powder at 500 grams and 5,000 grams respectively). \xc2\xa72(a), \n\n124Stat.\n2372. The change had the effect of lowering the 100-to-1 crack-to-powder ratio to 18-to-1. (The Act also eliminated the 5-year mandatory minimum for simple possession of crack. \xc2\xa73, \n\n124Stat.\n2372.)\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFurther, the Fair Sentencing Act instructed the Commission to \xe2\x80\x9cmake such conforming amendments to the Federal sentencing guidelines as the Commission determines necessary to achieve consistency with other guideline provisions and applicable law.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78(2), id., at 2374. And it directed the Commission to \xe2\x80\x9cpromulgate the guidelines, policy statements, or amendments provided for in this Act as soon as practicable, and in any event not later than 90 days\xe2\x80\x9d after the new Act took effect. \xc2\xa78(1), ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Fair Sentencing Act took effect on August 3, 2010. The Commission promulgated conforming emergency Guidelines amendments that became effective on November 1, 2010. 75 Fed. Reg. 66188 (2010). A permanent version of those Guidelines amendments took effect on November 1, 2011. See 76 id., at 24960 (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWith this background in mind, we turn to the relevant facts of the cases before us. Corey Hill, one of the petitioners, unlawfully sold 53 grams of crack in March 2007, before the Fair Sentencing Act became law. App. in No. 11\xe2\x80\x935721, pp. 6, 83 (hereinafter Hill App.). Under the 1986 Drug Act, an offender who sold 53 grams of crack was subject to a 10-year mandatory minimum. \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A)(iii) (2006 ed.). Hill was not sentenced, however, until December 2010, after the Fair Sentencing Act became law and after the new Guidelines amendments had become effective. Hill App. 83\xe2\x80\x9394. Under the Fair Sentencing Act, an offender who sold 53 grams of crack was subject to a 5-year, not a 10-year, minimum. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(B)(iii) (2006 ed., Supp. IV). The sentencing judge stated that, if he thought that the Fair Sentencing Act applied, he would have sentenced Hill to that Act\xe2\x80\x99s 5-year minimum. Id., at 69. But he concluded that the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s lower minimums apply only to those who committed a drug crime after August 3, 2010\xe2\x80\x94the Act\xe2\x80\x99s effective date. Id., at 65, 68. That is to say, he concluded that the new Act\xe2\x80\x99s more lenient sentences did not apply to those who committed a crime before August 3, even if they were sentenced after that date. Hence, the judge sen-tenced Hill to 10 years of imprisonment. Id., at 78. The Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit affirmed. 417 Fed. Appx. 560 (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe second petitioner, Edward Dorsey (who had previously been convicted of a drug felony), unlawfully sold 5.5 grams of crack in August 2008, before the Fair Sentencing Act took effect. App. in No. 5683, pp. 9, 48\xe2\x80\x9349, 57\xe2\x80\x9358 (hereinafter Dorsey App.). Under the 1986 Drug Act, an offender such as Dorsey with a prior drug felony who sold 5.5 grams of crack was subject to a 10-year minimum. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(B)(iii) (2006 ed.). Dorsey was not sentenced, however, until September 2010, after the new Fair Sentencing Act took effect. Id., at 84\xe2\x80\x9395. Under the Fair Sentencing Act, such an offender who sold 5.5 grams of crack was not subject to a mandatory minimum at all, for 5.5 grams is less than the 28 grams that triggers the new Act\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory minimum provisions. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(B)(iii) (2006 ed., Supp. IV). Dorsey asked the judge to apply the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s more lenient statutory penalties. Id., at 54\xe2\x80\x9355.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, as of Dorsey\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing in September 2010, the unrevised Guidelines (reflecting the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s old minimums) were still in effect. The Commission had not yet finished revising the Guidelines to reflect the new, lower statutory minimums. And the basic sentencing statute, the Sentencing Reform Act, provides that a judge shall apply the Guidelines that \xe2\x80\x9care in effect on the date the defendant is sentenced.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a)(4)(A)(ii).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe sentencing judge, however, had the legal authority not to apply the Guidelines at all (for they are advisory). But he also knew that he could not ignore a minimum sentence contained in the applicable statute. Dorsey App. 67\xe2\x80\x9368. The judge noted that, even though he was sentencing Dorsey after the effective date of the Fair Sentencing Act, Dorsey had committed the underlying crime prior to that date. Id., at 69\xe2\x80\x9370. And he concluded that the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s old minimums, not the new Fair Sentencing Act, applied in those circumstances. Ibid. He consequently sentenced Dorsey to the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s 10-year man-datory minimum term. Id., at 80. The Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit affirmed, United States v. Fisher, 635 F.\xc2\xa03d 336 (2011), and denied rehearing en banc, 646 F.\xc2\xa03d 429 (2011) (per\xc2\xa0curiam); see also United States v. Holcomb, 657 F.\xc2\xa03d 445 (CA7 2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Courts of Appeals have come to different conclusions as to whether the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s more lenient mandatory minimums apply to offenders whose unlawful conduct took place before, but whose sentencing took place after, the date that Act took effect, namely, August 3, 2010. Compare United States v. Douglas, 644 F.\xc2\xa03d 39, 42\xe2\x80\x9344 (CA1 2011) (Act applies), and United States v. Dixon, 648 F.\xc2\xa03d 195, 203 (CA3 2011) (same), with 635 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 339\xe2\x80\x93340 (Act does not apply), United States v. Sidney, 648 F.\xc2\xa03d 904, 910 (CA8 2011) (same), and United States v. Tickles, 661 F.\xc2\xa03d 212, 215 (CA5 2011) (per\xc2\xa0curiam) (same). In light of that disagreement, we granted Hill\xe2\x80\x99s and Dorsey\xe2\x80\x99s petitions for certiorari. Since petitioners and the Government both take the position that the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s new minimums do apply in these circumstances, we appointed as amicus curiae Miguel Estrada to argue the contrary position. He has ably discharged his responsibilities.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe timing issue before us is difficult in part because relevant language in different statutes argues in opposite directions. See Appendix A, infra. On the one hand, a federal saving statute, Act of Feb. 25, 1871 (1871 Act), \xc2\xa74, \n\n16Stat.\n432, phrased in general terms, provides that a new criminal statute that \xe2\x80\x9crepeal[s]\xe2\x80\x9d an older criminal statute shall not change the penalties \xe2\x80\x9cincurred\xe2\x80\x9d under that older statute \xe2\x80\x9cunless the repealing Act shall so expressly provide.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7109. Case law makes clear that the word \xe2\x80\x9crepeal\xe2\x80\x9d applies when a new statute simply diminishes the penalties that the older statute set forth. See Warden v. Marrero, \n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 653\n\xe2\x80\x93664 (1974); see also United States v. Tynen, 11 Wall. 88, 92 (1871). Case law also makes clear that penalties are \xe2\x80\x9cincurred\xe2\x80\x9d under the older statute when an offender becomes subject to them, i.e., commits the underlying conduct that makes the offender liable. See United States v. Reisinger, \n\n128 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398, \n401 (1888)\n; Great Northern R. Co. v. United States, \n\n208 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452\n\xe2\x80\x93470 (1908).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn the other hand, the Sentencing Reform Act says that, regardless of when the offender\xe2\x80\x99s conduct occurs, the applicable Guidelines are the ones \xe2\x80\x9cin effect on the date the defendant is sentenced.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a)(4)(A)(ii). And the Fair Sentencing Act requires the Commission to change the Guidelines in the wake of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s new minimums, making them consistent with \xe2\x80\x9cother guideline provisions and applicable law.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78(2), \n\n124Stat.\n2374.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCourts that have held that they must apply the old, higher 1986 Drug Act minimums to all pre-Act offenders, including those sentenced after the Fair Sentencing Act took effect, have emphasized that the 1871 Act requires that result unless the Fair Sentencing Act either expressly says or at least by fair implication implies the contrary. See 635 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 339\xe2\x80\x93340; Sidney, supra, at 906\xe2\x80\x93908; Tickles, supra, at 214\xe2\x80\x93215; see also Holcomb, supra, at 446\xe2\x80\x93448 (opinion of Easterbrook, J.). Courts that have concluded that the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s more lenient penalties apply have found in that Act, together with the Sentencing Reform Act and other related circumstances, indicia of a clear congressional intent to apply the new Act\xe2\x80\x99s minimums. See Douglas, supra, at 42\xe2\x80\x9344; Dixon, supra, at 199\xe2\x80\x93203; see also Holcomb, 657 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 454\xe2\x80\x93457 (Williams, J., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc); id., at 461\xe2\x80\x93463 (Posner, J., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc). We too take the latter view. Six considerations, taken together, convince us that Congress intended the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s more lenient penalties to apply to those offenders whose crimes preceded August 3, 2010, but who are sentenced after that date.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, the 1871 saving statute permits Congress to apply a new Act\xe2\x80\x99s more lenient penalties to pre-Act offenders without expressly saying so in the new Act. It is true that the 1871 Act uses the words \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly provide.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7109. But the Court has long recognized that this saving statute creates what is in effect a less demanding interpretive requirement. That is because statutes en- acted by one Congress cannot bind a later Congress, which remains free to repeal the earlier statute, to exempt the current statute from the earlier statute, to modify the earlier statute, or to apply the earlier statute but as modified. See, e.g., Fletcher v. Peck, 6 Cranch 87, 135 (1810); Reichelderfer v. Quinn, \n\n287 U.\xc2\xa0S. 315, \n318 (1932)\n. And Congress remains free to express any such intention either expressly or by implication as it chooses.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, the Court has said that the 1871 Act \xe2\x80\x9ccannot justify a disregard of the will of Congress as manifested either expressly or by necessary implication in a subsequent enactment.\xe2\x80\x9d Great Northern R.\xc2\xa0Co., supra, at 465 (emphasis added). And in a comparable context the Court has emphasized that the Administrative Procedure Act\xe2\x80\x99s use of the word \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly\xe2\x80\x9d does not require Congress to use any \xe2\x80\x9cmagical passwords\xe2\x80\x9d to exempt a later statute from the provision. Marcello v. Bonds, \n\n349 U.\xc2\xa0S. 302, \n310 (1955)\n. Without requiring an \xe2\x80\x9cexpress\xe2\x80\x9d statement, the Court has described the necessary indicia of congressional intent by the terms \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary implication,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cclear implication,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cfair implication,\xe2\x80\x9d phrases it has used interchangeably. Great Northern R.\xc2\xa0Co., supra, at 465, 466; Hertz v. Woodman, \n\n218 U.\xc2\xa0S. 205, \n218 (1910)\n; Marrero, supra, at 660, n.\xc2\xa010. One Member of the Court has said we should determine whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe plain import of a later statute directly conflicts with an earlier statute,\xe2\x80\x9d and, if so, \xe2\x80\x9cthe later enactment governs, regardless of its compliance with any earlier-enacted requirement of an express reference or other \xe2\x80\x98magical password.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lockhart v. United States, \n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 142, \n149 (2005)\n (Scalia, J., concurring).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHence, the Court has treated the 1871 Act as setting forth an important background principle of interpretation. The Court has also assumed Congress is well aware of the background principle when it enacts new criminal statutes. E.g., Great Northern R.\xc2\xa0Co., supra, at 465; Hertz, supra, at 217; cf. Marcello, supra, at 310. And the prin-ciple requires courts, before interpreting a new criminal statute to apply its new penalties to a set of pre-Act offenders, to assure themselves that ordinary interpretive considerations point clearly in that direction. Words such as \xe2\x80\x9cplain import,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfair implication,\xe2\x80\x9d or the like reflect the need for that assurance. And it is that assurance, which we shall assume is conveyed by the phrases \xe2\x80\x9cplain import\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cfair implication,\xe2\x80\x9d that we must look for here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, the Sentencing Reform Act sets forth a special and different background principle. That statute says that when \xe2\x80\x9cdetermining the particular sentence to be imposed\xe2\x80\x9d in an initial sentencing, the sentencing court \xe2\x80\x9cshall consider,\xe2\x80\x9d among other things, the \xe2\x80\x9csentencing range\xe2\x80\x9d established by the Guidelines that are \xe2\x80\x9cin effect on the date the defendant is sentenced.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a)(4)(A)(ii) (emphasis added). Although the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s Ex Post Facto Clause, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa79, cl.\xc2\xa03, prohibits applying a new Act\xe2\x80\x99s higher penalties to pre-Act conduct, it does not prohibit applying lower penalties. See Calder v. Bull, 3 Dall. 386, 390\xe2\x80\x93391 (1798); Collins v. Youngblood, \n497 U.\xc2\xa0S. 37\n\xe2\x80\x9344 (1990). The Sentencing Commission has consequently instructed sentencing judges to \xe2\x80\x9cuse the Guidelines Manual in effect on the date that the defendant is sentenced,\xe2\x80\x9d regardless of when the defendant committed the offense, unless doing so \xe2\x80\x9cwould violate the ex post facto clause.\xe2\x80\x9d USSG \xc2\xa71B1.11. And therefore when the Commission adopts new, lower Guidelines amendments, those amendments become effective to offenders who committed an offense prior to the adoption of the new amendments but are sentenced thereafter. Just as we assume Congress was aware of the 1871 Act\xe2\x80\x99s background norm, so we assume that Congress was aware of this different background sentencing principle.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThird, language in the Fair Sentencing Act implies that Congress intended to follow the Sentencing Reform Act background principle here. A section of the Fair Sentencing Act entitled \xe2\x80\x9cEmergency Authority for United States Sentencing Commission\xe2\x80\x9d requires the Commission to prom-ulgate \xe2\x80\x9cas soon as practicable\xe2\x80\x9d (and not later than 90 days after August 3, 2010) \xe2\x80\x9cconforming amendments\xe2\x80\x9d to the Guidelines that \xe2\x80\x9cachieve consistency with other guideline provisions and applicable law.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78, \n\n124Stat.\n2374. Read most naturally, \xe2\x80\x9capplicable law\xe2\x80\x9d refers to the law as changed by the Fair Sentencing Act, including the provision reducing the crack mandatory minimums. \xc2\xa72(a), id., at 2372. As the Commission understood this provision, achieving consistency with \xe2\x80\x9cother guideline provisions\xe2\x80\x9d means reducing the base offense levels for all crack amounts proportionally (using the new 18-to-1 ratio), in-cluding the offense levels governing small amounts of crack that did not fall within the scope of the mandatory minimum provisions. 75 Fed. Reg. 66191. And consis-tency with \xe2\x80\x9cother guideline provisions\xe2\x80\x9d and with prior Com-mission practice would require application of the new Guidelines amendments to offenders who committed their offense prior to the new amendments\xe2\x80\x99 effective date but were sentenced thereafter. See USSG \xc2\xa71B1.11(a); e.g., USSG App. C, amdts. 706, 711 (Supp. Nov.\xc2\xa02004\xe2\x80\x93Nov. 2007); see also Memorandum from G. Schmitt, L. Reed, & K. Cohen, USSC, to Chair Hinojosa et\xc2\xa0al., Subject: Analysis of the Impact of the Crack Cocaine Amendment if Made Retroactive 23 (Oct. 3, 2007). Cf. USSG App. C, amdt. 571 (amendment increasing restitution, which may present ex post facto and one-book-rule concerns, would apply only to defendants sentenced for post-amendment offenses), discussed post, at 5 (Scalia, J., dissenting).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFourth, applying the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s old mandatory minimums to the post-August 3 sentencing of pre-August 3 offenders would create disparities of a kind that Congress enacted the Sentencing Reform Act and the Fair Sentencing Act to prevent. Two individuals with the same number of prior offenses who each engaged in the same criminal conduct involving the same amount of crack and were sentenced at the same time would receive radically different sentences. For example, a first-time post-Act offender with five grams of crack, subject to a Guidelines range of 21 to 27 months, could receive two years of imprisonment, while an otherwise identical pre-Act offender would have to receive the 5-year mandatory minimum. Compare USSG \xc2\xa72D1.1(c) (Nov. 2011) with \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(B) (2006 ed.). A first-time post-Act 50-gram offender would be subject to a Guidelines range of less than six years of imprisonment, while his otherwise identical pre-Act counterpart would have to receive the 10-year mandatory minimum. Compare USSG \xc2\xa72D1.1(c) (Nov. 2011) with \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(A) (2006 ed.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, unlike many prechange/postchange discrepancies, the imposition of these disparate sentences involves roughly contemporaneous sentencing, i.e., the same time, the same place, and even the same judge, thereby highlighting a kind of unfairness that modern sentenc- ing statutes typically seek to combat. See, e.g., 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7991(b)(1)(B) (purposes of Guidelines-based sentencing include \xe2\x80\x9cavoiding unwarranted sentencing disparities among defendants with similar records who have been found guilty of similar criminal conduct\xe2\x80\x9d); S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No.\xc2\xa098\xe2\x80\x93223, p.\xc2\xa074 (1983) (explaining rationale for using same, current Guidelines for all roughly contemporaneous sentencings). Further, it would involve imposing upon the pre-Act offender a pre-Act sentence at a time after Congress had specifically found in the Fair Sentencing Act that such a sentence was unfairly long.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, one cannot treat such problems as if they were minor ones. Given the 5-year statute of limitations for federal drug offenses, the 11-month median time between indictment and sentencing for those offenses, and the approximately 5,000 federal crack offenders convicted each year, many pre-Act offenders were not (and will not be) sentenced until after August 3, 2010, when the new, more lenient mandatory minimums took effect. See \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73282(a); Administrative Office of United States Courts, Judicial Business of the United States Courts, p.\xc2\xa0272 (2010) (Table D\xe2\x80\x9310); 2011 Report 191.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFifth, not to apply the Fair Sentencing Act would do more than preserve a disproportionate status quo; it would make matters worse. It would create new anomalies\xe2\x80\x94new sets of disproportionate sentences\xe2\x80\x94not previously present. That is because sentencing courts must apply new Guidelines (consistent with the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s new minimums) to pre-Act offenders, see supra, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314, and the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s old minimums would trump those new Guidelines for some pre-Act offenders but not for all of them\xe2\x80\x94say, pre-Act offenders who possessed crack in small amounts not directly the subject of mandatory minimums.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsider, for example, a first-time offender convicted of possessing with intent to distribute four grams of crack. No mandatory sentence, under the 1986 Drug Act or the Fair Sentencing Act, applies to an offender possessing so small an amount. Yet under the old law, the Commission, charged with creating proportionate sentences, had created a Guidelines range of 41 to 51 months for such an of-fender, a sentence proportional to the 60 months that the 1986 Drug Act required for one who trafficked five grams of crack. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936; USSG \xc2\xa72D1.1(c) (Nov. 2009).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Fair Sentencing Act, however, requires the Commission to write new Guidelines consistent with the new law. The Commission therefore wrote new Guidelines that provide a sentencing range of 21 to 27 months\xe2\x80\x94about two years\xe2\x80\x94for the first-time, 4-gram offender. See USSG \xc2\xa72D1.1(c) (Nov. 2011). And the Sentencing Reform Act requires application of those new Guidelines to all of- fenders (including pre-Act offenders) who are sentenced once those new Guidelines take effect. See \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a)(4)(A)(ii). Those new Guidelines must take effect and apply to a pre-Act 4-gram offender, for such an offender was never subject to a trumping statutory 1986 Drug Act mandatory minimum. However, unless the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s new, more lenient mandatory mini- mums apply to pre-Act offenders, an otherwise identical of-fender who possessed five grams would have to receive a 5-year sentence. See \n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7841(b)(1)(B) (2006 ed., Supp. IV).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor example, imagine that on July 1, 2010, both Smith and Jones commit a crack crime identical but for the fact that Smith possesses with intent to distribute four grams of crack and Jones five grams. Both are sentenced on December 1, 2010, after the Fair Sentencing Act and the new Guidelines take effect. Smith\xe2\x80\x99s Guidelines sentence would be two years, but unless the Fair Sentencing Act applies, Jones\xe2\x80\x99s sentence would have to be five years. The difference of one gram would make a difference, not of only one year as it did before enactment of the Fair Sentencing Act, but instead of three years. Passage of the new Act, designed to have brought about fairer sentences, would here have created a new disparate sentencing \xe2\x80\x9ccliff.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor can one say that the new Act would produce disproportionalities like this in only a few cases. In fiscal year 2010, 17.8 percent of all crack offenders were convicted of- offenses not subject to the 1986 Drug Act\xe2\x80\x99s minimums. 2011 Report 191. And since those minimums apply only to some drug offenders and they apply in different ways, one can find many similar examples of disproportionalities. See Appendix B, infra. Thus, application of the 1986 Drug Act minimums to pre-Act offenders sentenced after the new Guidelines take effect would produce a crazy quilt of sentences, at odds with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 basic efforts to achieve more uniform, more proportionate sentences. Congress, when enacting the Fair Sentencing Act, could not have intended any such result.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSixth, we have found no strong countervailing consideration. Amicus and the dissent argue that one might read much of the statutory language we have discussed as embodying exceptions, permitting the old 1986 Drug Act minimums to apply to pre-Act offenders sentenced after August 3, 2010, when the Fair Sentencing Act took effect. The words \xe2\x80\x9capplicable law\xe2\x80\x9d in the new Act, for example, could, linguistically speaking, encompass the 1986 Drug Act minimums applied to those sentenced after August 3. Post, at 4\xe2\x80\x936 (Scalia, J., dissenting). Moreover, Congress could have insisted that the Commission write new Guidelines with special speed to assure itself that new, post-August 3 offenders\xe2\x80\x94but not old, pre-August 3 offenders\xe2\x80\x94would receive the benefit of the new Act. Post, at 6\xe2\x80\x938. Further, amicus and the dissent note that to apply the new Act\xe2\x80\x99s minimums to the old, pre-August 3 offenders will create a new disparity\xe2\x80\x94one between pre-Act offenders sentenced before August 3 and those sentenced after that date. Post, at 9.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe do not believe that these arguments make a critical difference. Even if the relevant statutory language can be read as amicus and the dissent suggest and even if Congress might have wanted Guidelines written speedily simply in order to apply them quickly to new offenders, there is scant indication that this is what Congress did mean by the language in question nor that such was in fact Congress\xe2\x80\x99 motivation. The considerations we have set forth, supra, at 13\xe2\x80\x9317 and this page, strongly suggest the contrary.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe also recognize that application of the new minimums to pre-Act offenders sentenced after August 3 will create a new set of disparities. But those disparities, reflecting a line-drawing effort, will exist whenever Congress enacts a new law changing sentences (unless Congress intends re-opening sentencing proceedings concluded prior to a new law\xe2\x80\x99s effective date). We have explained how in federal sentencing the ordinary practice is to apply new penalties to defendants not yet sentenced, while withholding that change from defendants already sentenced. Supra, at 13; compare \n\n18 U. S. C. \xc2\xa73553(a)(4)(A)(ii) with \xc2\xa73582(c). And we have explained how, here, continued application of the old 1986 Drug Act minimums to those pre-Act offenders sentenced after August 3 would make matters worse. Supra, at 16\xe2\x80\x9318. We consequently conclude that this particular new disparity (between those pre-Act offenders already sentenced and those not yet sentenced as of August 3) cannot make a critical difference.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor these reasons considered as a whole, we conclude that Congress intended the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s new, lower mandatory minimums to apply to the post-Act sentencing of pre-Act offenders. That is the Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cplain import\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cfair implication.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe add one final point. Several arguments we have discussed involve the language of statutes that determine how new Guidelines take effect. Supra, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. What about those who committed an offense prior to August 3 and were sentenced after August 3 but before November 1, 2010\xe2\x80\x94a period after the new Act\xe2\x80\x99s effective date but before the new Guidelines first took effect? Do the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s new mandatory minimums apply to them?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn our view, the new Act\xe2\x80\x99s lower minimums apply to them as well. Our reason is that the statute simply instructs the Commission to promulgate new Guidelines \xe2\x80\x9cas soon as practicable\xe2\x80\x9d (but no later than 90 days after the Act took effect). \xc2\xa78(1), \n\n124Stat.\n2374. As far as Congress was concerned, the Commission might have (having prepared new Guidelines in advance) promulgated those Guidelines within a few days\xe2\x80\x94perhaps on August 3 itself. At the same time, the Commission possesses ample authority to permit appropriate adjustments to be made in the Guidelines sentences of those sentenced after August 3 but prior to the new Guidelines promulgation. See \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994(u) (power to make Guidelines reductions retroactive); 76 Fed. Reg. 41333\xe2\x80\x9341334 (2011) (amended 18-to-1 Guidelines made retroactive). In any event, courts, treating the Guidelines as advisory, possess authority to sentence in accordance with the new minimums.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor these reasons, if the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s new minimums apply to all of those sentenced after August 3, 2010 (even if the new Guidelines were not yet ready), it is possible to foresee a reasonably smooth transition. On the other hand, it is difficult to foresee such a transition if the new Act\xe2\x80\x99s application is keyed to a later date, thereby leaving the courts unable to take the new Act fully into account, particularly when that circumstance might create additional disparities and uncertainties that courts and the Commission may be helpless to correct. We have no reason to believe Congress would have wanted to impose an unforeseeable, potentially complex application date.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Scalia, with whom The Chief Justice, Justice Thomas, and Justice Alito join, dissenting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the Fair Sentencing Act of 2010, \n\n124Stat.\n2372, Congress increased the threshold quantities of crack co- caine required to trigger the 5- and 10-year mandatory minimum penalties associated with offenses involving the manufacture, distribution, or dispensation of the drug, and eliminated the 5-year mandatory minimum previously associated with simple possession of it. The Act is silent as to whether these changes apply to defendants who committed their offenses before, but whose sentencing proceedings occurred after, its August 3, 2010, effective date. In my view, the general saving statute, \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7109, dictates that the new, more lenient mandatory minimum provisions do not apply to such pre-enactment offenders.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court starts off on the right foot by acknowledging, ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, that the ameliorative amendments at issue here trigger application of the general saving statute. Enacted in 1871 to reverse the common-law rule that the repeal or amendment of a criminal statute would abate all nonfinal convictions under the repealed or amended statute, see Warden v. Marrero, \n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 653, \n660 (1974)\n, the saving statute provides in relevant part:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe repeal of any statute shall not have the effect to release or extinguish any penalty, forfeiture, or liability incurred under such statute, unless the repealing Act shall so expressly provide, and such statute shall be treated as still remaining in force for the purpose of sustaining any proper action or prosecution for the enforcement of such penalty, forfeiture, or liability.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7109.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tBy reducing the statutory penalties for crack cocaine offenses, the Fair Sentencing Act \xe2\x80\x9crepeal[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d the former penalties; for defendants who committed their offenses (and hence \xe2\x80\x9cincurred\xe2\x80\x9d the penalties) while the prior law was in force, \xc2\xa7109 directs that the prior law \xe2\x80\x9cshall be treated as still remaining in force.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough \xc2\xa7109 purports to require that subsequent legislation opting out of its default rule must do so \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court correctly observes, ante, at 12, that express-statement requirements of this sort are ineffective. See Lockhart v. United States, \n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 142\n\xe2\x80\x93150 (2005) (Scalia, J., concurring). Because \xe2\x80\x9cone legis-lature cannot abridge the powers of a succeeding legislature,\xe2\x80\x9d Fletcher v. Peck, 6 Cranch 87, 135 (1810), a statute is \xe2\x80\x9calterable when the legislature shall please to alter it,\xe2\x80\x9d Marbury v. Madison, 1 Cranch 137, 177 (1803). Consequently, the express-statement requirement of \xc2\xa7109 is itself subject to repeal on the same terms as any other statute, which is to say that a repeal may be accomplished by implication. See, e.g., Marrero, supra, at 659\xe2\x80\x93660, n.\xc2\xa010; Great Northern R. Co. v. United States, \n\n208 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452, \n465 (1908)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnderstanding the interpretive problem posed by these cases as one of implied repeal helps to explain the Court\xe2\x80\x99s observation, ante, at 13, that what is required to override \xc2\xa7109\xe2\x80\x99s default rule is a clear demonstration of congressional intent to do so. Admittedly, our cases have not spoken with the utmost clarity on this point. In Marrero, for example, we suggested that a \xe2\x80\x9cfair implication\xe2\x80\x9d from a subsequently enacted statute would suffice, 417 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 660, n.\xc2\xa010, while in Hertz v. Woodman, \n\n218 U.\xc2\xa0S. 205 (1910)\n, we used the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cclear implication,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 218 (emphasis added); see also ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cplain implication\xe2\x80\x9d). In Great Northern R. Co., we split the difference, stating at one point that \xc2\xa7109 controls unless Congress expresses a contrary intention \xe2\x80\x9ceither expressly or by necessary implication in a subsequent enactment,\xe2\x80\x9d 208 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 465 (emphasis added), but suggesting at another point that a \xe2\x80\x9cfair implication,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 466, would do. In my view, the \xe2\x80\x9cfair implication\xe2\x80\x9d formulation understates the burden properly imposed on a defendant who would claim an implicit exception from \xc2\xa7109\xe2\x80\x99s terms. Because the effect of such an exception is to work a pro\xc2\xa0tanto repeal of \xc2\xa7109\xe2\x80\x99s application to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s case, the implication from the subsequently enacted statute must be clear enough to overcome our strong presumption against implied repeals. See, e.g., Matsushita Elec. Industrial Co. v. Epstein, \n\n516 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367, \n381 (1996)\n; Posadas v. National City Bank, \n\n296 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497, \n503 (1936)\n. Thus, we should conclude that Congress has deviated from \xc2\xa7109 (or any similar statute establishing a background interpretive principle) only when the \xe2\x80\x9cplain import of a later statute directly conflicts\xe2\x80\x9d with it. Lockhart, supra, at 149 (Scalia, J., concurring) (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe considerations relied upon by the Court do not come close to satisfying the demanding standard for repeal by implication. As an initial matter, there is no persuasive force whatever to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s observation that continuing to apply the prior mandatory minimums to pre-enactment offenders would \xe2\x80\x9cinvolve imposing upon the pre-Act offender a pre-Act sentence at a time after Congress had specifically found in the Fair Sentencing Act that such a sentence was unfairly long.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. That is true whenever Congress reduces a criminal penalty, and so is a consequence that Congress affirmatively embraced when it said in \xc2\xa7109 that ameliorative amendments to criminal statutes do not apply to pre-enactment conduct. Nor does it matter that Congress has instructed district courts, when applying the Federal Sentencing Guidelines, to ap-ply the version in force on the date of sentencing, with the object of reducing disparities in sentences between similar defendants who are sentenced for the same conduct at the same time. See \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a)(4)(A)(ii). The presumption against implied repeals requires us to give effect, if possible, to both \xc2\xa73553(a)(4)(A)(ii) and \xc2\xa7109. \xe2\x80\x9cThe courts are not at liberty to pick and choose among congressional enactments, and when two statutes are ca-pable of co-existence, it is the duty of the courts, absent a clearly expressed congressional intention to the con-trary, to regard each as effective.\xe2\x80\x9d Morton v. Mancari, \n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 535, \n551 (1974)\n. We may readily do so here by holding that \xc2\xa73553(a)(4)(A)(ii) applies to Guidelines amendments, and \xc2\xa7109 to statutory ones.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court also stresses that the Fair Sentencing Act instructs the Sentencing Commission to promulgate \xe2\x80\x9cas soon as practicable\xe2\x80\x9d (and not later than 90 days after August 3, 2010) \xe2\x80\x9csuch conforming amendments\xe2\x80\x9d to the Sentencing Guidelines \xe2\x80\x9cas the Commission determines necessary to achieve consistency with other guideline provisions and applicable law.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78, \n\n124Stat.\n2374. The argument goes that, because the Commission implemented this directive by reducing the Guidelines ranges for crack cocaine offenses to track the 18-to-1 crack-to-powder ratio reflected in the new mandatory minimums, see 75 Fed. Reg. 66191 (2010), and because the general rule is that a sentencing court should apply the version of the Guidelines in effect at the time of sentencing, see \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a)(4)(A)(ii), Congress must have understood that the new mandatory minimums would apply immediately, since otherwise there would be a mismatch between the statutory penalties and Guidelines ranges.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat conclusion simply does not follow. For one thing, the argument begs the very question presented here: What is the \xe2\x80\x9capplicable law\xe2\x80\x9d relevant to pre-enactment offenders who are sentenced after enactment? The Commission could well have answered this question by concluding that, in light of \xc2\xa7109, the law applicable to such offenders is the pre-Act mandatory minimums. It might therefore have retained, as to those offenders, the existing Guidelines ranges reflecting a higher crack-to-powder ratio. Although rare, it is not unheard of for the Commission to establish Guidelines whose application turns on the date of commission of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s offense. See United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa75E1.1(g)(1) (Nov. 2011) (governing restitution for offenses committed on or after November 1, 1997, and providing that the prior version of the Guideline shall govern all other cases); id., \xc2\xa78B1.1(f)(1) (same for restitution obligations of organizational defendants). Of course, the Commission did not interpret the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s directive in this manner. But the possibility that it could (not to mention the probability that it should) have done so illustrates the folly of basing inferences about what Congress intended when it passed the Fair Sentencing Act on decisions the Commission would not make until several months later.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, even if one takes it as given that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s new crack cocaine Guidelines would apply the lower 18-to-1 ratio to all defendants sentenced after the new Guidelines were put in place, it would not follow that Congress necessarily expected the new mandatory minimums to apply to pre-enactment offenders. The directive to update the Guidelines on an emergency basis is equally consistent with Congress\xe2\x80\x99s seeking to avoid a mismatch between the Guidelines and the statutory penalties for post-enactment offenders sentenced shortly after the Act\xe2\x80\x99s effective date.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners and the Government discount this explanation, noting that because of the lags associated with in-vestigating and prosecuting drug offenses, most of the defendants sentenced on the 91st day after the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s enactment were sure to be pre-Act offenders. If Congress did not expect the new mandatory minimums to apply to such offenders, they say, there would have been no need to ensure that revised Guidelines were in place so quickly. But most is not all, and it would have been entirely sensible for Congress to worry that some post-Act offenders\xe2\x80\x94offenders clearly subject to the new mandatory minimums\xe2\x80\x94would nonetheless be sentenced under outdated Guidelines if the Guidelines were not revised in short order.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe 11-month median time between indictment and sentencing for non-marijuana federal drug offenses, see Administrative Office of United States Courts, Judicial Business of the United States Courts, p.\xc2\xa0272 (2010) (Table D\xe2\x80\x9310), does not establish that prompt issuance of new Guidelines for post-Act offenders could not have been a pressing concern. Because that is a median figure, it shows that half of all drug defendants are sentenced sooner than 11 months after being indicted. And it is only an aggregate figure. For drug possession offenses\xe2\x80\x94relevant here because the Fair Sentencing Act eliminated the mandatory minimum sentence previously applicable to simple possession of crack cocaine, see \xc2\xa73, \n\n124Stat.\n2372\xe2\x80\x94the equivalent figure was just 5.4 months from indictment to sentencing. The pace of criminal cases also varies considerably from district to district. In the Eastern District of Virginia, for instance, the median time from indictment to sentencing for all criminal cases was just 3.6 months. See Judicial Business, supra, at 252 (Table D\xe2\x80\x936). What is more, without the Fair Sentencing Act\xe2\x80\x99s emer-gency directive, amendments to the Guidelines to implement the Act likely would not have been put in place until more than a year after its passage.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In the interim, a great many post-Act offenders might have been sentenced under the outdated Guidelines, even though they were clearly entitled to take advantage of the statutory amendments. Because the emergency authority conferred on the Commission can reasonably be understood as directed at this mismatch problem, it creates no clear implication that Congress expected the new statutory penalties to apply to pre-enactment offenders.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s last argument is that continuing to apply the prior mandatory minimums to pre-enactment offenders would lead to anomalous, disproportionate sentencing results. It is true enough, as the Court notes, ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9318, that applying the prior mandatory minimums in tandem with the new Guidelines provisions\xe2\x80\x94which track the new, more lenient mandatory minimums\xe2\x80\x94leads to a series of \xe2\x80\x9ccliffs\xe2\x80\x9d at the mandatory minimum thresholds. But this does not establish that Congress clearly meant the new mandatory minimums to apply to pre-enactment offenders. As noted above, supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, there is no reason to take the Guidelines amendments ultimately promulgated by the Commission as a given when evaluating what Congress would have understood when the Fair Sentencing Act was enacted. The Commission could have promulgated amendments that ameliorated this problem by retaining the old Guidelines ranges for pre-enactment offenders.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMoreover, although the cliffs produced by the mismatch between Guidelines and statutory penalties are admittedly inconsistent with the premise of the Guidelines system that sentences should vary in proportion to the gravity of the offense and the culpability of the offender, see \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a)(1), (a)(2)(A), the same objection can be lodged against any mandatory minimum that trumps an otherwise applicable Guidelines range. And it is not as though the results of continuing to apply the pre-Act statutory penalties are so senseless as to establish that Congress must not have intended them. Retaining the old mandatory minimums ensures at least rough equivalence in sentences for defendants who committed their crimes at the same time, but were sentenced at different times\xe2\x80\x94even as it leads to disparities for defendants who are sen-tenced at the same time, but committed their offenses at different times. In light of this plausible basis for continuing to apply the prior law to pre-enactment offenders, there is no reason to conclude that Congress necessarily expected the new statutory penalties to apply.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners and the Government press a handful of ad-ditional arguments which require only brief discussion. They first contend that an intention to apply the new mandatory minimums to pre-enactment offenders can be inferred from \xc2\xa710 of the Fair Sentencing Act, \n\n124Stat.\n2375, which instructs the Commission to study the effects of the new law and make a report to Congress within five years. The suggestion is that, if the statutory penalties do not apply to pre-enactment offenders, then the Act would have no effect on many defendants sentenced during the study period, which would in turn undermine Congress\xe2\x80\x99s goal of compiling useful data. This is makeweight. Whether or not the new mandatory minimums are held applicable to pre-enactment offenders, they will be applied to many post-enactment offenders during the study period, and the Commission will have the opportunity to collect useful data. The study provision simply has nothing to say about the question at issue here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government also notes that the Senate bill that ultimately became the Fair Sentencing Act was based on an earlier bill which contained a provision that would have delayed the Act\xe2\x80\x99s effective date until 180 days after passage, and specifically provided that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here shall be no retroactive application of any portion of this Act.\xe2\x80\x9d H. R. 265, 111th Cong., 1st Sess., \xc2\xa711 (2009). Even if one is inclined to base inferences about statutory meaning on unenacted versions of the relevant bill, but see Hamdan v. Rumsfeld, \n\n548 U.\xc2\xa0S. 557, \n668 (2006)\n (Scalia, J., dissenting), this argument from drafting history is unpersuasive. That Congress considered and rejected a proposal that would have delayed application of the Act until 180 days after passage says nothing about whether the version finally enacted applies to defendants whose criminal con-duct pre-dated the Act. Moreover, the same bill would have provided permissive authority for the Commission to promulgate amended Guidelines on an emergency basis, see \xc2\xa78(a), notwithstanding its delayed effective date provision. This point undercuts the argument that emergency amendment authority and immediate application of the new statutory penalties go hand-in-hand.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners finally appeal to the rule of lenity and the canon of constitutional avoidance. But the rule of lenity has no application here, because the background principle supplied by \xc2\xa7109 serves to remove the ambiguity that is a necessary precondition to invocation of the rule. See Deal v. United States, \n\n508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 129, \n135 (1993)\n. The canon of constitutional avoidance also has no application here. Although many observers viewed the 100-to-1 crack-to-powder ratio under the prior law as having a racially disparate impact, see, e.g., United States Sentencing Commission, Special Report to Congress: Cocaine and Federal Sentencing Policy 8 (Apr. 1997), only intentional discrimination may violate the equal protection component of the \n \nFifth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause. See Arlington Heights v. Metropolitan Housing Development Corp., \n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 252\n\xe2\x80\x93265 (1977); Adarand Constructors, Inc. v. Pe\xc3\xb1a, \n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 200, \n217 (1995)\n. There is thus no constitutional doubt triggered by application of the prior mandatory minimums, much less the sort of \xe2\x80\x9cserious constitutional doub[t]\xe2\x80\x9d required to invoke the avoidance canon. Clark v. Martinez, \n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 371, \n381 (2005)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the end, the mischief of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is not the result in this particular case, but rather the unpredictability it injects into the law for the future. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s de-cision is based on \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ix considerations, taken together,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11, and we are not told whether any one of these considerations might have justified the Court\xe2\x80\x99s result in isolation, or even the relative importance of the various considerations. One of them (the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s emergency authority to issue conforming amendments to the Guidelines) is a particular feature of the statute at issue in these cases, but another (the fact that applying the prior statutory penalties alongside the new Guidelines leads to a mismatch) is a general feature of a sentencing scheme that calibrates Guidelines ranges to the statutory mandatory minimums for a given offense. Are we to conclude that, after the Sentencing Reform Act, \xc2\xa7109 has no further application to criminal penalties, at least when statutory amendments lead to modification of the Guidelines? Por-tions of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion could be understood to sug-gest that result, but the Court leaves us in suspense.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat is most unfortunate, because the whole point of \xc2\xa7109, as well as other provisions of the Dictionary Act, see 1 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71\xe2\x80\x938, and the definitional provisions of the federal criminal law, see 18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa75\xe2\x80\x9327 (2006 ed. and Supp. IV), is to provide a stable set of background principles that will promote effective communication between Congress and the courts. In this context, stability is ensured by a healthy respect for our presumption against implied repeals, which demands a clear showing before we conclude that Congress has deviated from one of these background interpretive principles. Because the Court\xe2\x80\x99s result cannot be reconciled with this approach, I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t
