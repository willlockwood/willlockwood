\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

ANTHONY HEDGPETH, WARDEN, PETITIONER v.\nMICHAEL ROBERT PULIDO

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[December 2, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A conviction based on a general verdict is subject to challenge if the jury was instructed on alternative theories of guilt and may have relied on an invalid one. See Stromberg v. California, 283 U. S. 359 (1931); Yates v. United States, 354 U. S. 298 (1957). In this case the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit held that such an error is \xe2\x80\x9cstructural error,\xe2\x80\x9d requiring that the conviction be set aside on collateral review without regard to whether the flaw in the instructions prejudiced the defendant. The parties now agree that the Court of Appeals was wrong to categorize this type of error as \xe2\x80\x9cstructural.\xe2\x80\x9d They further agree that a reviewing court finding such error should ask whether the flaw in the instructions \xe2\x80\x9chad substantial and injurious effect or influence in determining the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict.\xe2\x80\x9d Brecht v. Abrahamson, 507 U. S. 619, 623 (1993) (internal quotation marks omitted). We agree as well and so hold.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Michael Pulido was convicted by a California jury of felony murder. On direct appeal, Pulido sought to vacate his conviction on the ground that the jury instructions were erroneous: They permitted the jury to find him guilty of felony murder if he formed the intent to aid and abet the underlying felony before the murder, but they also permitted the jury to find him guilty if he formed that intent only after the murder. The California Supreme Court agreed with Pulido that the latter theory was invalid under California law, but upheld the conviction on the ground that Pulido was not prejudiced by the error. People v. Pulido, 15 Cal.\xc2\xa04th 713, 727, 936 P.\xc2\xa02d 1235, 1243\xe2\x80\x931244 (1997). Pulido sought federal habeas relief, which the District Court granted after concluding that instructing the jury on the invalid theory had a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98substantial and injurious effect or influence in determining the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Pulido v. Lamarque, No. C\xc2\xa099\xe2\x80\x934933 CW (RR) (ND Cal., Mar. 24, 2005), App. to Pet. for Cert. 65a\xe2\x80\x9366a (quoting Brecht, supra, at 637).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State appealed and the Court of Appeals affirmed. Pulido v. Chrones, 487 F.\xc2\xa03d 669 (2007) (per curiam). On appeal, Pulido argued the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s Brecht analysis was correct, but in the alternative sought to avoid the harmless-error inquiry altogether. In support of that alternative argument, he maintained that when a jury returns a general verdict after being instructed on both a valid and an invalid theory, the conviction must be automatically set aside, without asking whether the invalid instruction was harmless. The Court of Appeals recognized that the Brecht \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial and injurious effect\xe2\x80\x9d standard governs harmless-error analysis on federal habeas, 487 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 673, n.\xc2\xa03 (internal quotation marks omitted), but agreed with Pulido that instructing a jury on multiple theories of guilt, one of which is legally improper, was \xe2\x80\x9cstructural\xe2\x80\x9d error exempting the instructions as a whole from harmless-error review, id., at 675\xe2\x80\x93676. Such error instead required setting aside the conviction on habeas unless the reviewing court could determine with \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute certainty\xe2\x80\x9d that the defendant was convicted under a proper theory. Id., at 676 (citing Lara v. Ryan, 455 F.\xc2\xa03d 1080, 1086 (CA9 2006)). Because the instructions \xe2\x80\x9cle[ft] open the possibility\xe2\x80\x9d that the jury convicted Pulido on the impermissible ground, the court concluded that the verdict must be reversed. 487 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 676. We granted certiorari. 552 U. S. ___ (2008).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit precedent on which the Court of Appeals relied, see Lara v. Ryan, supra, based its structural-error analysis upon a line of our cases beginning with Stromberg. Stromberg addressed the validity of a general verdict that rested on an instruction that the petitioner could be found guilty for displaying a red flag as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a sign, symbol, or emblem of opposition to organized government, or [a]s an invitation or stimulus to anarchistic action, or [a]s [a]n aid to propaganda that is of a seditious character.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 283 U. S., at 363. After holding that the first clause of the instruction proscribed constitutionally protected conduct, we concluded that the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction must be reversed because \xe2\x80\x9cit [wa]s impossible to say under which clause of the [instruction] the conviction was obtained.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 368. In Yates v. United States, supra, we extended this reasoning to a conviction resting on multiple theories of guilt when one of those theories is not unconstitutional, but is otherwise legally flawed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both Stromberg and Yates were decided before we concluded in Chapman v. California, 386 U. S. 18 (1967), that constitutional errors can be harmless. Accordingly, neither Stromberg nor Yates had reason to address whether the instructional errors they identified could be reviewed for harmlessness, or instead required automatic reversal. In a series of post-Chapman cases, however, we concluded that various forms of instructional error are not structural but instead trial errors subject to harmless-error review. See, e.g., Neder v. United States, 527 U. S. 1 (1999) (omission of an element of an offense); California v. Roy, 519 U. S. 2 (1996) (per curiam) (erroneous aider and abettor instruction); Pope v. Illinois, 481 U. S. 497 (1987) (misstatement of an element of an offense); Rose v. Clark, 478 U. S. 570 (1986) (erroneous burden-shifting as to an element of an offense).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although these cases did not arise in the context of a jury instructed on multiple theories of guilt, one of which is improper, nothing in them suggests that a different harmless-error analysis should govern in that particular context. To the contrary, we emphasized in Rose that \xe2\x80\x9cwhile there are some errors to which [harmless-error analysis] does not apply, they are the exception and not the rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 578. And Neder makes clear that harmless-error analysis applies to instructional errors so long as the error at issue does not categorically \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98vitiat[e] all the jury\xe2\x80\x99s findings.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 527 U. S., at 11 (quoting Sullivan v. Louisiana, 508 U. S. 275, 281 (1993) (erroneous reasonable-doubt instructions constitute structural error)). An instructional error arising in the context of multiple theories of guilt no more vitiates all the jury\xe2\x80\x99s findings than does omission or misstatement of an element of the offense when only one theory is submitted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In fact, drawing a distinction between alternative-theory error and the instructional errors in Neder, Roy, Pope, and Rose would be \xe2\x80\x9cpatently illogical,\xe2\x80\x9d given that such a distinction \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reduces to the strange claim that, because the jury \xe2\x80\xa6 received both a \xe2\x80\x9cgood\xe2\x80\x9d charge and a \xe2\x80\x9cbad\xe2\x80\x9d charge on the issue, the error was somehow more pernicious than \xe2\x80\xa6 where the only charge on the critical issue was a mistaken one.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 487 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 677\xe2\x80\x93678 (O\xe2\x80\x99Scannlain, J., concurring specially) (quoting Quigley v. Vose, 834 F.\xc2\xa02d 14, 16 (CA1 1987) (per curiam)); see also Becht v. United States, 403 F.\xc2\xa03d 541, 548 (CA8 2005) (same), cert. denied, 546 U. S. 1177 (2006).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pulido now agrees with the State that the Court of Appeals erred by treating the instructional error in this case as structural, and that the required prejudice analysis should be governed by Brecht\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial and injurious effect\xe2\x80\x9d standard. See Brief for Respondent 17 (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Ninth Circuit was mistaken in its \xe2\x80\x98structural defect\xe2\x80\x99 nomenclature\xe2\x80\x9d); Tr. of Oral Arg. 27 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe acknowledge that this is a trial error and is subject to the Brecht prejudice standard\xe2\x80\x9d). So do we.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pulido nonetheless maintains we should affirm because the Court of Appeals effectively engaged in the Brecht analysis, despite its clear description of the error as \xe2\x80\x9cstructural.\xe2\x80\x9d But despite full briefing on the applicability of Brecht, the Court of Appeals mentioned Brecht only briefly in a footnote, see 487 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 673, n.\xc2\xa03, and then went on to agree with Pulido\xe2\x80\x99s alternative assertion that \xe2\x80\x9cthe instructional error was structural and therefore not subject to harmless error review,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 675\xe2\x80\x93676. The court also stated that the conviction had to be overturned unless the court was \xe2\x80\x9cabsolutely certain\xe2\x80\x9d that the jury relied on a valid ground. Id., at 676. Such a determination would appear to be a finding that no violation had occurred at all, rather than that any error was harmless. In any event, an \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute certainty\xe2\x80\x9d standard is plainly inconsistent with Brecht. Accordingly, we express no view on whether Pulido is entitled to habeas relief, but rather remand to the Court of Appeals for application of Brecht in the first instance.*

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

*\xc2\xa0The dissent maintains the Court of Appeals \xe2\x80\x9cundertook a searching review of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 evidence and the jury instructions to determine the error\xe2\x80\x99s effect on the jury.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6 (opinion of Stevens, J.). But the Court of Appeals reached its conclusion based solely on the existence of a typographical error in the special circumstances instructions, without addressing any of the State\xe2\x80\x99s arguments that the typographical error was harmless in light of the record as a whole. There was no need for that court to address those arguments, of course, because of its mistaken conclusion that the instructional error was structural. Under such circumstances, remand is the appropriate course.

ANTHONY HEDGPETH, WARDEN, PETITIONER v.\nMICHAEL ROBERT PULIDO

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[December 2, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Souter and Justice Ginsburg join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals misused the term \xe2\x80\x9cstructural error\xe2\x80\x9d in its opinion affirming the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order granting Pulido\xe2\x80\x99s application for a writ of habeas corpus. But the court\xe2\x80\x99s misnomer was inconsequential because its decision rested on substantially the same analysis as the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s, which correctly applied the standards set forth in Kotteakos v. United States, 328 U. S. 750 (1946), Brecht v. Abrahamson, 507 U. S. 619 (1993), and O\xe2\x80\x99Neal v. McAninch, 513 U. S. 432 (1995). The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision therefore did not warrant this Court\xe2\x80\x99s review and does not now merit a remand to require that court to repeat its analysis. In my opinion, the interest in expediting the conclusion of this protracted litigation outweighs the interest in correcting a misnomer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Michael Pulido was charged with felony murder for robbing a gas station and killing the attendant. At trial, the State argued that Pulido acted alone. Pulido maintained that his uncle was the principal actor and that he had no knowledge of his uncle\xe2\x80\x99s plan when the two arrived at the gas station. While he was waiting in the car, Pulido claimed, he heard a shot and ran into the store. At that point, his uncle insisted that Pulido help him pry open the stolen cash register and dispose of it, and Pulido reluctantly complied. The jury convicted Pulido of felony murder, but it was unable to reach a verdict on the charges that Pulido personally used a firearm and intentionally inflicted great bodily harm.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a matter of California law, felony-murder liability extends to all persons jointly engaged in the commission of a felony at the time of a killing when one of the joint actors kills in furtherance of the common design. People v. Pulido, 15 Cal. 4th 713, 716, 936 P.\xc2\xa02d 1235, 1236 (1997). But a person is not guilty of felony murder if he is only a \xe2\x80\x9clate-joining\xe2\x80\x9d aider and abettor\xe2\x80\x94i.e., if he did not himself commit the murder and his participation in the underlying felony did not begin until after the victim was killed. Ibid. In this case, the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s instructions erroneously permitted the jury to find Pulido guilty on such a theory, as they did not require the jury to find either that Pulido committed the murder or that he aided and abetted the underlying robbery before the murder was committed. Because the instructions allowed the jury to convict Pulido of felony murder for conduct that does not amount to that offense, their inclusion was constitutional error.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On direct appeal, the California Supreme Court agreed with Pulido that the late-joiner theory was an invalid theory of felony-murder liability. Ibid. It nevertheless held that any error in the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s instructions was harmless. According to the court, the jury found that Pulido had been engaged in the robbery at the time of the killing because the robbery-murder special-circumstance instruction stated that \xe2\x80\x9cthe murder was committed while the defendant was engaged\xe2\x80\x9d in the \xe2\x80\x9ccommission of or attempted commission of [a] robbery.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 727, 936 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 1243 (citing Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa7190.2(a)(17) (West 1992) (internal quotation marks omitted)). Based on that portion of the instruction, the court concluded that the special-circumstance verdict \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrates that the jury did not accept the theory that [Pulido] joined the robbery only after [the victim] was killed,\xe2\x80\x9d and it therefore held that Pulido was not prejudiced by the error. 15 Cal. 4th, at 727, 936 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 1244.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In reaching that conclusion, however, the California Supreme Court failed to take into account the entire special-circumstance instruction. A typographical error in that instruction in fact permitted the jury to find the special circumstance of robbery-murder true if it found either that the murder was committed while the defendant was engaged in the commission of a robbery or that it \xe2\x80\x9cwas committed in order to carry out or advance the commission of the crime of robbery or to facilitate the escape therefrom or to avoid detection\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a finding entirely consistent with the late-joiner theory. App. 14. Thus, as the State concedes, the erroneous instructions made it \xe2\x80\x9creasonably likely\xe2\x80\x9d that the jury convicted Pulido on the impermissible theory. Brief for Petitioner 18.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After exhausting his state postconviction remedies,[Footnote 1] Pulido sought a writ of habeas corpus in Federal District Court. The District Court recognized the erroneous disjunctive in the special-circumstance instruction that the California Supreme Court had overlooked, and it held that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Pulido was not prejudiced by erroneous instructions was \xe2\x80\x9can objectively unreasonable application of clearly established federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 64a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court then considered the effect of that error on the jury. Correctly relying on Brecht, the District Court began its analysis by noting that a federal habeas petitioner \xe2\x80\x9cis not entitled to habeas relief unless the State court\xe2\x80\x99s error resulted in actual prejudice, that is, the error had a \xe2\x80\x98substantial and injurious effect or influence in determining the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 65a (quoting Brecht, 507 U. S., at 637). If an error had a substantial influence, or if \xe2\x80\x9cthe record is so evenly balanced that a conscientious judge is in \xe2\x80\x98grave doubt\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d as to whether it had such an effect, the conviction must be reversed. App. to Pet. for Cert. 65a. (quoting O\xe2\x80\x99Neal, 513 U. S., at 438).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To determine whether the error was harmless under this standard, the District Court scrutinized the record, including the arguments of both parties, the evidence supporting their respective theories of the case, the jury instructions, the jury\xe2\x80\x99s questions to the trial court, and the various parts of the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict. App. to Pet. for Cert. 65a\xe2\x80\x9366a. Throughout this inquiry, the District Court properly avoided substituting its judgment for the jury\xe2\x80\x99s. As we cautioned in Kotteakos, in undertaking harmless-error analysis \xe2\x80\x9cit is not the [reviewing] court\xe2\x80\x99s function to determine guilt or innocence. Nor is it to speculate upon probable reconviction and decide according to how the speculation comes out.\xe2\x80\x9d 328 U. S., at 763 (citations omitted). Thus, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he inquiry cannot be merely whether there was enough to support the result\xe2\x80\x9d in the absence of the error. Id., at 765. Rather, the proper question is \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the error itself had substantial influence. If so, or if one is left in grave doubt, the conviction cannot stand.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; accord, O\xe2\x80\x99Neal, 513 U. S., at 437.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That was precisely the question addressed by the District Court when it sought to ascertain what the jury actually found. The court concluded that, while it is \xe2\x80\x9cpossible\xe2\x80\x9d that the jury found that Pulido aided and abetted the robbery before the victim was killed, the court had \xe2\x80\x9cno way of determining whether this was the case.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 66a. Because that uncertainty left the court with \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98grave doubt as to the likely effect of [the] error on the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d it faithfully applied the standard mandated by Kotteakos and O\xe2\x80\x99Neal and found that the error was not harmless. App. to Pet. for Cert. 66a. (quoting O\xe2\x80\x99Neal, 513 U. S., at 435).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On appeal, Pulido contended that the judgment of the District Court should be affirmed whether the instructional error was viewed as structural error or as trial error. Brief of Appellee and Cross-Appellant in Nos. 05\xe2\x80\x9315916, 05\xe2\x80\x9316308 (CA9), pp. 53\xe2\x80\x9364 (hereinafter Appellee\xe2\x80\x99s Brief). He argued that the error was not harmless under Brecht and O\xe2\x80\x99Neal because the substantial evidence that supported the invalid theory made it likely that the jury convicted him on that basis. Appellee\xe2\x80\x99s Brief 55\xe2\x80\x9364. In particular, Pulido noted that the \xe2\x80\x9cinjurious effect\xe2\x80\x9d of this type of error \xe2\x80\x9cis greatest when the instruction compromises the defense by appearing to extend liability even to the factual scenario suggested by the defense evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d as was true in this case. Id., at 57 (internal quotation marks omitted and emphasis deleted). At oral argument, the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contentions similarly focused on the Brecht standard and the result that harmless-error analysis required.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Less than two months after oral argument, and before the Court of Appeals issued its decision in this case, a different panel of the Ninth Circuit decided Lara v. Ryan, 455 F.\xc2\xa03d 1080 (2006). Lara was convicted of attempted murder by a jury that had been instructed that it could find him guilty under either an express malice theory or an implied malice theory, the second of which is legally invalid. Id., at 1082. The Ninth Circuit described the erroneous instruction as a \xe2\x80\x9cstructural error,\xe2\x80\x9d but it held that such an error does not necessitate reversal when a reviewing court can \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine with absolute certainty\xe2\x80\x9d that the defendant was not convicted under the erroneous theory. Id., at 1086. Because the jury \xe2\x80\x9cmade a specific finding that Lara attempted to murder willfully, deliberately, and with premeditation,\xe2\x80\x9d the court concluded that it necessarily relied on the valid instruction and that reversal was therefore not required. Id., at 1086\xe2\x80\x931087.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In those limited instances in which this Court has found an error \xe2\x80\x9cstructural,\xe2\x80\x9d we have done so because the error defies analysis by harmless-error standards. See Arizona v. Fulminante, 499 U. S. 279, 309 (1991); see also United States v. Gonzalez-Lopez, 548 U. S. 140, 150 (2006) (quoting Sullivan v. Louisiana, 508 U. S. 275, 282 (1993); United States v. Cronic, 466 U. S. 648, 659, and n. 25 (1984)). Indeed, it is because the consequences \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98are necessarily unquantifiable and indeterminate\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that automatic reversal is required when such errors occur. Gonzalez-Lopez, 548 U. S., at 150 (quoting Sullivan, 508 U. S., at 282). That the court in Lara could be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98absolutely certain\xe2\x80\x99 that the jury relied upon the legally correct theory,\xe2\x80\x9d 455 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1085, shows both that the error was susceptible of harmless-error analysis and that the court in fact found the error harmless despite repeatedly referring to it as \xe2\x80\x9cstructural.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Citing Lara, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 per curiam opinion labeled the erroneous instruction in this case a structural error.[Footnote 2] As in Lara, the court then undertook a searching review of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 evidence and the jury instructions to determine the error\xe2\x80\x99s effect on the jury. Noting, among other things, that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he typographical error in the contemporaneity instruction relied upon by the California Supreme Court introduces doubt into any inference to be drawn from the jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding as to the special circumstance,\xe2\x80\x9d the court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury instructions leave open the possibility that the jury convicted Pulido on a legally impermissible theory.\xe2\x80\x9d Pulido v. Chrones, 487 F.\xc2\xa03d 669, 676 (CA9 2007). That possibility of reliance on the erroneous instruction is the \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial and injurious effect\xe2\x80\x9d to which Brecht refers. Thus, although the Court of Appeals called the error in this case by the wrong name, it performed substantially the same analysis and reached the same conclusion as the District Court did when it applied the standard prescribed by Brecht.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Thomas concurred separately both to defend the Lara decision and to demonstrate that harmless-error analysis also supports the panel\xe2\x80\x99s result. 487 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 678\xe2\x80\x93683. Unlike the District Court, Judge Thomas applied the harmless-error standard announced in Chapman v. California, 386 U. S. 18 (1967), instead of looking to Brecht. 487 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 678. But his analysis similarly establishes that at least some jurors very likely relied on the impermissible late-joiner theory. Id., at 679\xe2\x80\x93683.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The record before us clearly supports that conclusion. Indeed, even petitioner admits that the ambiguity in the robbery and murder instructions and the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s confusing answers to the jury\xe2\x80\x99s questions \xe2\x80\x9ccombined to make it reasonably likely that the jury applied the instructions in an unconstitutional way.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 18. That reasonable likelihood is sufficient to support the conclusion that the error was not harmless under Brecht.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis was correct and the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 result was substantially the same, I think this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to remand for the purpose of obtaining a third analysis of the harmless-error issue is a misuse of scarce judicial resources. I would therefore affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.
