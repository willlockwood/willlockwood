SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

DEBORAH K. JOHNSON, WARDEN v. DONNA KAY LEE

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93789.\xe2\x80\x83Decided May 31, 2016

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

Federal habeas courts generally refuse to hear claims \xe2\x80\x9cdefaulted . . . in state court pursuant to an independent and adequate state procedural rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Coleman v. Thompson,\n\n501 U. S. 722,\n750 (1991)\n. State rules count as \xe2\x80\x9cadequate\xe2\x80\x9d if they are \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly established and regularly followed.\xe2\x80\x9d Walker v. Martin,\n\n562 U. S. 307,\n316 (2011)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). Like all States, California requires criminal defendants to raise available claims on direct appeal. Under the so-called \xe2\x80\x9cDixon bar,\xe2\x80\x9d a defendant procedurally defaults a claim raised for the first time on state collateral review if he could have raised it earlier on direct appeal. See In re Dixon, 41 Cal. 2d 756, 759, 264 P. 2d 513, 514 (1953). Yet, in this case, the Ninth Circuit held that the Dixon bar is inadequate to bar fed-eral habeas review. Because California\xe2\x80\x99s procedural bar is longstanding, oft-cited, and shared by habeas courts across the Nation, this Court now summarily reverses the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

I

\n

Respondent Donna Kay Lee and her boyfriend Paul Carasi stabbed to death Carasi\xe2\x80\x99s mother and his ex-girlfriend. A California jury convicted the pair of two counts each of first-degree murder. Carasi received a death sentence, and Lee received a sentence of life without the possibility of parole. In June 1999, Lee unsuccessfully raised four claims on direct appeal. After the California appellate courts affirmed, Lee skipped state postconviction review and filed the federal habeas petition at issue. See\n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa72254(a). The petition raised mostly new claims that Lee failed to raise on direct appeal. Because Lee had not exhausted available state-court remedies, however, the District Court temporarily stayed federal proceedings to allow Lee to pursue her new claims in a state habeas petition. The California Supreme Court denied Lee\xe2\x80\x99s petition in a summary order citing Dixon.

\n

Having exhausted state remedies, Lee returned to federal court to litigate her federal habeas petition. The District Court dismissed her new claims as procedurally defaulted. Then, for the first time on appeal, Lee challenged the Dixon bar\xe2\x80\x99s adequacy. In her brief, Lee presented a small sample of the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s state habeas denials on a single day about six months after her default. Lee claimed that out of the 210 summary denials on December 21, 1999, the court failed to cite Dixon in 9 cases where it should have been applied. The court instead denied the nine petitions without any citation at all. In Lee\xe2\x80\x99s view, these missing citations proved that the California courts inconsistently applied the Dixon bar. Without evaluating this evidence, the Ninth Circuit reversed and remanded \xe2\x80\x9cto permit the Warden to submit evidence to the contrary, and for consideration by the district court in the first instance.\xe2\x80\x9d Lee v. Jacquez, 406 Fed. Appx. 148, 150 (2010).

\n

On remand, the warden submitted a study analyzing more than 4,700 summary habeas denials during a nearly 2-year period around the time of Lee\xe2\x80\x99s procedural default. From August 1998 to June 2000, the study showed, the California Supreme Court cited Dixon in approximately 12% of all denials\xe2\x80\x94more than 500 times. In light of this evidence, the District Court held that the Dixon bar is adequate.

\n

The Ninth Circuit again reversed. Lee v. Jacquez, 788 F. 3d 1124 (2015). Lee\xe2\x80\x99s 1-day sample proved the Dixon bar\xe2\x80\x99s inadequacy, the court held, because the \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to cite Dixon where Dixon applies . . . reflects [its] irregular application.\xe2\x80\x9d 788 F. 3d, at 1130. The general 12% citation rate proved nothing, the court reasoned, because the warden \xe2\x80\x9cd[id] not purport to show to how many cases the Dixon bar should have been applied.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1133. Inthe Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view, without this \xe2\x80\x9cbaseline number\xe2\x80\x9d the warden\xe2\x80\x99s 2-year study was \xe2\x80\x9centirely insufficient\xe2\x80\x9d to prove Dixon\xe2\x80\x99s adequacy. 788 F. 3d, at 1133.

\n

II

\n

The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision profoundly misapprehends what makes a state procedural bar \xe2\x80\x9cadequate.\xe2\x80\x9d That question is a matter of federal law. Lee v. Kemna,\n\n534 U. S. 362,\n375 (2002)\n. \xe2\x80\x9cTo qualify as an \xe2\x80\x98adequate\xe2\x80\x99 procedural ground,\xe2\x80\x9d capable of barring federal habeas review, \xe2\x80\x9ca state rule must be \xe2\x80\x98firmly established and regularly followed.\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d Martin, supra, at 316 (quoting Beard v. Kindler,\n\n558 U. S. 53,\n60 (2009)\n).

\n

California\xe2\x80\x99s Dixon bar satisfies both adequacy criteria. It is \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly established\xe2\x80\x9d because, decades before Lee\xe2\x80\x99s June 1999 procedural default, the California Supreme Court warned defendants in plain terms that, absent \xe2\x80\x9cspecial circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d habeas \xe2\x80\x9cwill not lie where the claimed errors could have been, but were not, raised upon a timely appeal from a judgment of conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d Dixon, supra, at 759, 264 P. 2d, at 514. And the California Supreme Court eliminated any arguable ambiguity surrounding this bar by reaffirming Dixon in two cases decided before Lee\xe2\x80\x99s default. See In re Harris, 5 Cal. 4th 813,825, n. 3, 829\xe2\x80\x93841, 855 P. 2d 391, 395, n. 3, 398\xe2\x80\x93407 (1993); In re Robbins, 18 Cal. 4th 770, 814\xe2\x80\x93815, and n. 34, 959 P. 2d 311, 340\xe2\x80\x93341, and n. 34 (1998).

\n

The California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s repeated Dixon citations also prove that the bar is \xe2\x80\x9cregularly followed.\xe2\x80\x9d Martin recently held that another California procedural bar\xe2\x80\x94a rule requiring prisoners to file state habeas petitions promptly\xe2\x80\x94met that requirement because \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach year, the California Supreme Court summarily denies hundreds of habeas petitions by citing\xe2\x80\x9d the timeliness rule. 562 U. S., at 318. The same goes for Dixon. Nine purportedly missing Dixon citations from Lee\xe2\x80\x99s 1-day sample of summary orders hardly support an inference of inconsistency. See Dugger v. Adams,\n\n489 U. S. 401\n, n. 6 (1989) (holding that the Florida Supreme Court applied its similar procedural bar \xe2\x80\x9cconsistently and regularly\xe2\x80\x9d despite \xe2\x80\x9caddress[ing] the merits in several cases raising [new] claims on postconviction review\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, all nine orders in that sample were denials. None ignored the Dixon bar to grant relief, so there is no sign of inconsistency.

\n

Nor is California\xe2\x80\x99s rule unique. Federal and state ha-beas courts across the country follow the same rule as Dixon. \xe2\x80\x9cThe general rule in federal habeas cases is that a defendant who fails to raise a claim on direct appeal is barred from raising the claim on collateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d Sanchez-Llamas v. Oregon,\n\n548 U. S. 331\n\xe2\x80\x93351 (2006). Likewise, state postconviction remedies generally \xe2\x80\x9cmay not be used to litigate claims which were or could have been raised at trial or on direct appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 D. Wilkes, State Postconviction Remedies and Relief Handbook \xc2\xa71:2, p. 3 (2015\xe2\x80\x932016 ed.). It appears that every State shares this procedural bar in some form. See Brief for State of Alabama et al. as Amici Curiae 1, n. 2 (collecting citations). For such well-established and ubiquitous rules, it takes more than a few outliers to show inadequacy. Federal habeas courts must not lightly \xe2\x80\x9cdisregard state procedural rules that are substantially similar to those to which we give full force in our own courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Kindler, 558 U. S., at 62. And it would be \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ven stranger to do so with respect to rules in place in nearly every State.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Nothing suggests, moreover, that California courts apply the Dixon bar in a way that disfavors federal claims. The Court therefore holds that it qualifies as adequate to bar federal habeas review.

\n

III

\n

The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s contrary reasoning is unpersuasive and inconsistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents. Applying the Dixon bar may be a \xe2\x80\x9cstraightforward\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmechanica[l]\xe2\x80\x9d task for state courts. 788 F. 3d, at 1130. But simplicity does not imply that missing citations reflect state-court inconsistency. To begin with, since the Dixon bar has several exceptions, see Robbins, supra, at 814\xe2\x80\x93815, and n. 34, 959 P. 2d, at 340\xe2\x80\x93341, and n. 34, the California Supreme Court can hardly be faulted for failing to cite Dixon whenever a petitioner raises a claim that he could have raised on direct appeal.

\n

More importantly, California courts need not address procedural default before reaching the merits, so the purportedly missing citations show nothing. Cf. Bell v. Cone,\n\n543 U. S. 447\n, n. 3 (2005) ( per curiam) (declining to address the warden\xe2\x80\x99s procedural-default argument); Lambrix v. Singletary,\n\n520 U. S. 518,\n525 (1997)\n (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9c[ j]udicial economy might counsel\xe2\x80\x9d bypassing a procedural-default question if the merits \xe2\x80\x9cwere easily resolvable against the habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x9d). Ordinarily, \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural default . . . is not a jurisdictional matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Trest v. Cain,\n\n522 U. S. 87,\n89 (1997)\n. As a result, the appropriate order of analysis for each case remains within the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 discretion. Such discretion will often lead to \xe2\x80\x9cseeming inconsistencies.\xe2\x80\x9d Martin, 562 U. S., at 320, and n. 7. But that superficial tension does not make a procedural bar inadequate. \xe2\x80\x9c[A] state procedural bar may count as an adequate and independent ground for denying a federal habeas petition even if the state court had discretion to reach the merits despite the default.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 311; see Kindler, supra, at 60\xe2\x80\x9361.

\n

The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to get around Martin and Kindler fails. The Court of Appeals distinguished those cases on the ground that California\xe2\x80\x99s Dixon bar is \xe2\x80\x9cman-datory\xe2\x80\x9d rather than discretionary because it involves a discretion-free general rule, notwithstanding exceptions that might involve discretion. 788 F. 3d, at 1130. The Court assumes, without deciding, that this description is accurate and the Dixon bar\xe2\x80\x99s exceptions leave some room for discretion. Even so, there is little difference between discretion exercised through an otherwise adequate procedural bar\xe2\x80\x99s exceptions and discretion that is a part of the bar itself. In any event, the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning ignores the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 discretion to assume, without deciding, that a claim is not procedurally defaulted and instead hold that the claim lacks merit.

\n

The Ninth Circuit was accordingly wrong to dismiss the 500-plus summary denials citing Dixon simply because they do not reveal which cases potentially implicate the bar. 788 F. 3d, at 1133. Martin already rejected this precise reasoning. There, the habeas petitioner unsuccessfully argued that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]se of summary denials makes it impossible to tell why the California Supreme Court decides some delayed petitions on the merits and rejects others as untimely.\xe2\x80\x9d 562 U. S., at 319 (internal quotation marks omitted). So too here, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e see no reason to reject California\xe2\x80\x99s [procedural] bar simply because a court may opt to bypass the e see no reason to reject California\xe2\x80\x99s [procedural] bar simply because a court may opt to bypass the [Dixon] assessment and summarily dismiss a petition on the merits, if that is the easier path.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

By treating every missing citation as a sign of inconsistency, the Court of Appeals \xe2\x80\x9cpose[d] an unnecessary dilemma\xe2\x80\x9d for California. Kindler, 558 U. S., at 61. The court forced the State to choose between the \xe2\x80\x9cfinality of [its] judgments\xe2\x80\x9d and a burdensome opinion-writing requirement. Ibid.; see Martin, supra, at 312\xe2\x80\x93313 (noting that the California Supreme Court \xe2\x80\x9crules on a staggering number of habeas petitions each year\xe2\x80\x9d); Harrington v. Richter,\n\n562 U. S. 86,\n99 (2011)\n (discussing the advantages of summary dispositions). \xe2\x80\x9c[F]ederal courts have no authority,\xe2\x80\x9d however, \xe2\x80\x9cto impose mandatory opinion-writing standards on state courts\xe2\x80\x9d as the price of federal respect for their procedural rules. Johnson v. Williams, 568 U. S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 9). The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision is thus fundamentally at odds with the \xe2\x80\x9cfederalism and comity concerns that motivate the adequate state ground doctrine in the habeas context.\xe2\x80\x9d Kindler, supra, at 62.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA State\xe2\x80\x99s procedural rules are of vital importance to the orderly administration of its criminal courts; when a federal court permits them to be readily evaded, it undermines the criminal justice system.\xe2\x80\x9d Lambrix, supra, at 525. Here, the Ninth Circuit permitted California prisoners to evade a well-established procedural bar that is adequate to bar federal habeas review. The petition for a writ of certiorari and respondent\xe2\x80\x99s motion to proceed in forma pauperis are granted. The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.
