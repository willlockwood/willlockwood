\nWRIGHT V. VAN PATTEN
\n552 U. S. ____ (2008)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

RANDALL WRIGHT, SHERIFF, SHAWANO COUNTY, WISCONSIN v. JOSEPH L. VAN PATTEN

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93212.\xe2\x80\x83Decided January 7, 2008

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit held that respondent Joseph Van Patten was entitled to relief under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254, reasoning that his lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s assistance was presumptively ineffective owing to his participation in a plea hearing by speaker phone. Van Patten v. Deppisch, 434 F.\xc2\xa03d 1038 (2006). We granted certiorari, vacated the judgment, and remanded the case for further consideration in light of Carey v. Musladin, 549 U. S. ___ (2006). On remand, the Seventh Circuit adhered to its original decision, concluding that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in Musladin requires that our 2006 opinion be changed.\xe2\x80\x9d Van Patten v. Endicott, 489 F.\xc2\xa03d 827, 828 (2007). We grant the petition for certiorari now before us and this time reverse the judgment of the Seventh Circuit.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Van Patten was charged with first-degree intentional homicide and pleaded no contest to a reduced charge of first-degree reckless homicide. His counsel was not physically present at the plea hearing but was linked to the courtroom by speaker phone. After the state trial court imposed the maximum term of 25 years in prison, Van Patten retained different counsel and moved in the Wisconsin Court of Appeals to withdraw his no-contest plea. The thrust of the motion was that Van Patten\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment right to counsel had been violated by his trial counsel\xe2\x80\x99s physical absence from the plea hearing. The Wisconsin Court of Appeals noted that, under state law, a postconviction motion to withdraw a no-contest plea will be granted only if a defendant establishes \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest injustice\xe2\x80\x9d by clear and convincing evidence. See State v. Van Pattten, No. 96\xe2\x80\x933036\xe2\x80\x93CR (Wis. App., May 28, 1997), App. to Pet. for Cert. A47\xe2\x80\x93A48. While the court acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9cthe violation of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment right to counsel may constitute a manifest injustice,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at A48, it found that the absence of Van Patten\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer from the plea hearing did not violate his right to counsel:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe plea hearing transcript neither indicates any deficiency in the plea colloquy, nor suggests that Van Patten\xe2\x80\x99s attorney\xe2\x80\x99s participation by telephone interfered in any way with [Van Patten\xe2\x80\x99s] ability to communicate with his attorney about his plea. Van Patten confirmed that he had thoroughly discussed his case and plea decision with his attorney and was satisfied with the legal representation he had received. The court gave Van Patten the opportunity to speak privately with his attorney over the phone if he had questions about the plea, but Van Patten declined. Further, when Van Patten exercised his right to allocution at sentencing, in the personal presence of his attorney, he raised no objection to his plea.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at A49\xe2\x80\x93A50.

\n

\nApplying Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668 (1984), the court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he record does not support, nor does Van Patten\xe2\x80\x99s appellate brief include, any argument that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance was deficient or prejudicial,\xe2\x80\x9d No. 96\xe2\x80\x933036\xe2\x80\x93CR, App. to Pet. for Cert. A51, and denied Van Patten\xe2\x80\x99s motion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the Wisconsin Supreme Court declined further review, Van Patten petitioned for a writ of habeas corpus under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254 in Federal District Court. The District Court denied relief, but the Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit reversed. It held that Van Patten\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Amendment claim should have been resolved, not under Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s two-pronged test (which requires a showing of deficient performance and prejudice to the defendant), but under the standard discussed in United States v. Cronic, 466 U. S. 648 (1984) (under which prejudice may be presumed). Although the Seventh Circuit recognized that this case \xe2\x80\x9cpresents [a] novel \xe2\x80\xa6 question,\xe2\x80\x9d Deppisch, 434 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1040, and conceded that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nder Strickland, it seems clear [that] Van Patten would have no viable claim,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1042, the court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cit is clear to us that Van Patten\xe2\x80\x99s case must be resolved under Cronic,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1043. The resolution was in Van Patten\xe2\x80\x99s favor.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While the prison warden\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari was pending, this Court decided Musladin, supra. Musladin had invoked this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases recognizing \xe2\x80\x9cthat certain courtroom practices are so inherently prejudicial that they deprive the defendant of a fair trial,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___ (slip op., at 1). The issue was the significance of these precedents in a case under \xc2\xa72254, which bars relief on any claim \xe2\x80\x9cadjudicated on the merits\xe2\x80\x9d in state court, unless the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision \xe2\x80\x9cwas contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The prejudicial conduct involved in Musladin was courtroom conduct of private actors. We held that the \xe2\x80\x9cinheren[t] prejudic[e]\xe2\x80\x9d test, which we thus far have applied only in cases involving government-sponsored conduct, see, e.g., Estelle v. Williams, 425 U. S. 501 (1976); Holbrook v. Flynn, 475 U. S. 560 (1986), did not clearly extend to the conduct of independently acting courtroom spectators. See Musladin, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 5) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]lthough the Court articulated the test for inherent prejudice that applies to state conduct in Williams and Flynn, we have never applied that test to spectators\xe2\x80\x99 conduct. Indeed, part of the legal test of Williams and Flynn\xe2\x80\x94asking whether the practices furthered an essential state interest\xe2\x80\x94suggests that those cases apply only to state-sponsored practices\xe2\x80\x9d). For that reason, we reversed the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 grant of habeas relief.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Musladin\xe2\x80\x99s explanation of the \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established Federal law\xe2\x80\x9d requirement prompted us to remand Van Patten\xe2\x80\x99s case to the Seventh Circuit for further consideration. A majority of the panel reaffirmed its original judgment, however, on the ground that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nlike Musladin, this case does not concern an open constitutional question,\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Supreme Court has long recognized a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to relief if his defense counsel was actually or constructively absent at a critical stage of the proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d 489 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 828. Judge Coffey disagreed, observing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe United States Supreme Court has never held that an attorney is presumed to be ineffective if he participates in a plea hearing by speaker phone rather than by physical appearance.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis deleted). He found that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Majority Opinion does not comport with Musladin,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and dissented from \xe2\x80\x9cthe court\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous decision to allow\xe2\x80\x9d its original opinion \xe2\x80\x9cto stand as written,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 829. We reach the same conclusion.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Strickland v. Washington, 466 U. S. 668 (1984) ordinarily applies to claims of ineffective assistance of counsel at the plea hearing stage. See Hill v. Lockhart, 474 U. S. 52, 58 (1985) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he two-part Strickland v. Washington test applies to challenges to guilty pleas based on ineffective assistance of counsel\xe2\x80\x9d). And it was in a different context that Cronic \xe2\x80\x9crecognized a narrow exception to Strickland\xe2\x80\x99s holding that a defendant who asserts ineffective assistance of counsel must demonstrate not only that his attorney\xe2\x80\x99s performance was deficient, but also that the deficiency prejudiced the defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Florida v. Nixon, 543 U. S. 175, 190 (2004) (discussing Cronic). Cronic held that a Sixth Amendment violation may be found \xe2\x80\x9cwithout inquiring into counsel\xe2\x80\x99s actual performance or requiring the defendant to show the effect it had on the trial,\xe2\x80\x9d Bell v. Cone, 535 U. S. 685, 695 (2002), when \xe2\x80\x9ccircumstances [exist] that are so likely to prejudice the accused that the cost of litigating their effect in a particular case is unjustified,\xe2\x80\x9d Cronic, supra, at 658. Cronic, not Strickland, applies \xe2\x80\x9cwhen \xe2\x80\xa6 the likelihood that any lawyer, even a fully competent one, could provide effective assistance is so small that a presumption of prejudice is appropriate without inquiry into the actual conduct of the trial,\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 659\xe2\x80\x93660,* and one circumstance warranting the presumption is the \xe2\x80\x9ccomplete denial of counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d that is, when \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel [is] either totally absent, or prevented from assisting the accused during a critical stage of the proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 659, and n.\xc2\xa025.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No decision of this Court, however, squarely addresses the issue in this case, see Deppisch, supra, at 1040 (noting that this case \xe2\x80\x9cpresents [a] novel \xe2\x80\xa6 question\xe2\x80\x9d), or clearly establishes that Cronic should replace Strickland in this novel factual context. Our precedents do not clearly hold that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s participation by speaker phone should be treated as a \xe2\x80\x9ccomplete denial of counsel,\xe2\x80\x9d on par with total absence. Even if we agree with Van Patten that a lawyer physically present will tend to perform better than one on the phone, it does not necessarily follow that mere telephone contact amounted to total absence or \xe2\x80\x9cprevented [counsel] from assisting the accused,\xe2\x80\x9d so as to entail application of Cronic. The question is not whether counsel in those circumstances will perform less well than he otherwise would, but whether the circumstances are likely to result in such poor performance that an inquiry into its effects would not be worth the time. Cf. United States v. Gonzalez-Lopez, 548 U. S. ___, ___ (2006) (slip op., at 7) (Sixth Amendment ensures \xe2\x80\x9ceffective (not mistake-free) representation\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis in original)). Our cases provide no categorical answer to this question, and for that matter the several proceedings in this case hardly point toward one. The Wisconsin Court of Appeals held counsel\xe2\x80\x99s performance by speaker phone to be constitutionally effective; neither the Magistrate Judge, the District Court, nor the Seventh Circuit disputed this conclusion; and the Seventh Circuit itself stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nder Strickland, it seems clear Van Patten would have no viable claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Deppisch, 434 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1042.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because our cases give no clear answer to the question presented, let alone one in Van Patten\xe2\x80\x99s favor, \xe2\x80\x9cit cannot be said that the state court \xe2\x80\x98unreasonabl[y] appli[ed] clearly established Federal law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Musladin, 549 U. S.,\nat ___ (slip op., at 6) (quoting 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1)). Under the explicit terms of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), therefore, relief is unauthorized.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner tells us that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n urging review, [the State] does not condone, recommend, or encourage the practice of defense counsel assisting clients by telephone rather than in person at court proceedings, even in nonadversarial hearings such as the plea hearing in this case,\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. 5, and he acknowledges that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]erhaps, under similar facts in a direct federal appeal, the Seventh Circuit could have properly reached the same result it reached here,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. Our own consideration of the merits of telephone practice, however, is for another day, and this case turns on the recognition that no clearly established law contrary to the state court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion justifies collateral relief.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

*\xc2\xa0Cronic also applies when \xe2\x80\x9cthere [is] a breakdown in the adversarial process,\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 662, such that \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel entirely fails to subject the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s case to meaningful adversarial testing,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 659. We have made clear that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen we spoke in Cronic of the possibility of presuming prejudice based on an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s failure to test the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s case, we indicated that the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s failure must be complete.\xe2\x80\x9d Bell v. Cone, 535 U. S. 685, 696\xe2\x80\x93697 (2002). It is undisputed that this standard has not been met here.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An unfortunate drafting error in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in United States v. Cronic, 466 U. S. 648 (1984), makes it necessary to join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Cronic, this Court explained that some violations of the right to counsel arise in \xe2\x80\x9ccircumstances that are so likely to prejudice the accused that the cost of litigating their effect in a particular case is unjustified.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 658. One such circumstance exists when the accused is \xe2\x80\x9cdenied the presence of counsel at a critical stage of the prosecution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 662. We noted that the \xe2\x80\x9cpresence\xe2\x80\x9d of lawyers \xe2\x80\x9cis essential because they are the means through which the other rights of the person on trial are secured.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 653. Regrettably, Cronic did not \xe2\x80\x9cclearly establish\xe2\x80\x9d the full scope of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right to the presence of an attorney. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals apparently read \xe2\x80\x9cthe presence of counsel\xe2\x80\x9d in Cronic to mean \xe2\x80\x9cthe presence of counsel in open court.\xe2\x80\x9d Initially, all three judges on the panel assumed that the constitutional right at stake was the right to have counsel by one\xe2\x80\x99s side at all critical stages of the proceeding.* See also Van Patten v. Deppisch, No. 04\xe2\x80\x931276, 2006 U. S. App. LEXIS 5147 (CA7, Feb. 27, 2006) (noting that no member of the Seventh Circuit requested a vote on the warden\xe2\x80\x99s petition for rehearing en banc). In my view, this interpretation is correct. The fact that in 1984, when Cronic was decided, neither the parties nor the Court contemplated representation by attorneys who were not present in the flesh explains the author\xe2\x80\x99s failure to add the words \xe2\x80\x9cin open court\xe2\x80\x9d after the word \xe2\x80\x9cpresent.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court explains today, however, the question is not the reasonableness of the federal court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of Cronic, but rather whether the Wisconsin court\xe2\x80\x99s narrower reading of that opinion was \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively unreasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362, 409 (2000). In light of Cronic\xe2\x80\x99s references to the \xe2\x80\x9ccomplete denial of counsel\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctotally absent\xe2\x80\x9d counsel, 466 U. S., at 659, and n. 25, and the opinion\xe2\x80\x99s failure to state more explicitly that the defendant is entitled to \xe2\x80\x9cthe presence of counsel [in open court],\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 662, I acquiesce in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the state-court decision was not an unreasonable application of clearly established federal law. In doing so, however, I emphasize that today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion does not say that the state courts\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of Cronic was correct, or that we would have accepted that reading if the case had come to us on direct review rather than by way of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254. See ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937; see also Williams, 529 U. S., at 410 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n unreasonable application of federal law is different from an incorrect application of federal law\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

*\xc2\xa0In his opinion for a unanimous panel, Judge Evans explained at length why respondent had not had the assistance of counsel at a critical stage of the proceeding\xe2\x80\x94the plea hearing\xe2\x80\x94which resulted in a sentence of imprisonment for 25 years. He wrote, in part:

\n

\n\nThe Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s right-to-counsel guarantee recognizes \xe2\x80\x98the obvious truth that the average defendant does not have the professional legal skill to protect himself when brought before a tribunal with power to take his life or liberty.\xe2\x80\x99 Johnson v. Zerbst, 304 U. S. 458, 462\xe2\x80\x9363 (1938). \xe2\x80\x98Of all the rights that an accused person has, the right to be represented by counsel is by far the most pervasive for it affects his ability to assert any other rights he may have.\xe2\x80\x99 Cronic, 466 U. S. at 654 (citation omitted). Thus, a defendant requires an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98guiding hand\xe2\x80\x99 through every stage of the proceedings against him. Powell v. Alabama, 287 U. S. 45, 53 (1932); Cronic, 466 U. S. at 658. It is well-settled that a court proceeding in which a defendant enters a plea (a guilty plea or, as here, a plea of no contest) is a \xe2\x80\x98critical stage\xe2\x80\x99 where an attorney\xe2\x80\x99s presence is crucial because \xe2\x80\x98defenses may be \xe2\x80\xa6 irretrievably lost, if not then and there asserted.\xe2\x80\x99 Hamilton v. Alabama, 368 U. S. 52, 54 (1961). See also White v. Maryland, 373 U. S. 59, 60 (1963); United States ex rel. Thomas v. O\xe2\x80\x99Leary, 856 F.\xc2\xa02d 1011, 1014 (7th Cir. 1988). Indeed, with plea bargaining the norm and trial the exception, for most criminal defendants a change of plea hearing is the critical stage of their prosecution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In deciding whether to dispense with the two-part Strickland inquiry, a court must evaluate whether the \xe2\x80\x98surrounding circumstances make it unlikely that the defendant could have received the effective assistance of counsel,\xe2\x80\x99 Cronic, 466 U. S. at 666, and thus \xe2\x80\x98justify a presumption that [the] conviction was insufficiently reliable to satisfy the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x99 id. at 662. In this case, although the transcript shows that the state trial judge did his best to conduct the plea colloquy with care, the arrangements made it impossible for Van Patten to have the \xe2\x80\x98assistance of counsel\xe2\x80\x99 in anything but the most perfunctory sense. Van Patten stood alone before judge and prosecutor. Unlike the usual defendant in a criminal case, he could not turn to his lawyer for private legal advice, to clear up misunderstandings, to seek reassurance, or to discuss any last-minute misgivings. Listening over an audio connection, counsel could not detect and respond to cues from his client\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor that might have indicated he did not understand certain aspects of the proceeding, or that he was changing his mind. If Van Patten wished to converse with his attorney, anyone else in the courtroom could effectively eavesdrop. (We assume the district attorney would balk if he were expected to conduct last-minute consultations with his staff via speakerphone in open court, \xe2\x80\x98on the record,\xe2\x80\x99 with the defendant taking in every word.) No advance arrangements had been made for a private line in a private place, and even if one could \xe2\x80\x98perhaps\xe2\x80\x99 have been provided, it would have required a special request by Van Patten and, apparently, a break in the proceedings. In short, this was not an auspicious setting for someone about to waive very valuable constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Van Patten v. Deppisch, 434 F.\xc2\xa03d 1038, 1042\xe2\x80\x931043 (CA7 2006).
