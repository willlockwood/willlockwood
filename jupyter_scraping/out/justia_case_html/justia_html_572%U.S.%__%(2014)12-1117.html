PLUMHOFF et\xc2\xa0al. v. RICKARD, a minor child, individually, and as surviving daughterof RICKARD, DECEASED, by andthrough her mother RICKARD, as parent and next friend

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 12\xe2\x80\x931117.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 4, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 27, 2014

\n

Donald Rickard led police officers on a high-speed car chase that came to a temporary halt when Rickard spun out into a parking lot. Rickard resumed maneuvering his car, and as he continued to use the accelerator even though his bumper was flush against a patrol car, an officer fired three shots into Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s car. Rickard managed to drive away, almost hitting an officer in the process. Officers fired 12 more shots as Rickard sped away, striking him and his passenger, both of whom died from some combination of gunshot wounds and injuries suffered when the car eventually crashed.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Respondent, Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s minor daughter, filed a\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 action, alleging that the officers used excessive force in violation of the Fourth and\n \nFourteenth Amendments. The District Court denied the officers\xe2\x80\x99 motion for summary judgment based on qualified immunity, holding that their conduct violated the\n \nFourth Amendment and was contrary to clearly established law at the time in question. After finding that it had appellate jurisdiction, the Sixth Circuit held that the officers\xe2\x80\x99 conduct violated the\n \nFourth Amendment. It affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order, suggesting that it agreed that the officers violated clearly established law.

\n

Held:

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n 1.\xc2\xa0The Sixth Circuit properly exercised jurisdiction under\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71291, which gives courts of appeals jurisdiction to hear appeals from \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decisions\xe2\x80\x9d of the district courts. The general rule that an order denying a summary judgment motion is not a \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decision[n],\xe2\x80\x9d and thus not immediately appealable, does not apply when it is based on a qualified immunity claim. Johnson v. Jones,\n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304\n. Respondent argues that Johnson forecloses appellate jurisdiction here, but the order in Johnson was not immediately appealable because it merely decided \xe2\x80\x9ca question of \xe2\x80\x98evidence sufficiency,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 313, while here, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 qualified immunity claims raise legal issues quite different from any purely factual issues that might be confronted at trial. Deciding such legal issues is a core responsibility of appellate courts and does not create an undue burden for them. See, e.g., Scott v. Harris,\n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 372\n. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n 2.\xc2\xa0The officers\xe2\x80\x99 conduct did not violate the\n \nFourth Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a) Addressing this question first will be \xe2\x80\x9cbeneficial\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cdevelop[ing] constitutional precedent\xe2\x80\x9d in an area that courts typically consider in cases in which the defendant asserts a qualified immunity defense, Pearson v. Callahan,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 223\n. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s excessive-force argument requires analyzing the totality of the circumstances from the perspective \xe2\x80\x9cof a reasonable officer on the scene.\xe2\x80\x9d Graham v. Connor,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 386\n. Respondent contends that the\n \nFourth Amendment did not allow the officers to use deadly force to terminate the chase, and that, even if they were permitted to fire their weapons, they went too far when they fired as many rounds as they did. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (1)\xc2\xa0The officers acted reasonably in using deadly force. A \xe2\x80\x9cpolice officer\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to terminate a dangerous high-speed car chase that threatens the lives of innocent bystanders does not violate the\n \nFourth Amendment, even when it places the fleeing motorist at risk of serious injury or death.\xe2\x80\x9d Scott, supra, at 385. Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s outrageously reckless driving\xe2\x80\x94which lasted more than five minutes, exceeded 100 miles per hour, and included the passing of more than two dozen other motorists\xe2\x80\x94posed a grave public safety risk, and the record conclusively disproves that the chase was over when Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s car came to a temporary standstill and officers began shooting. Under the circumstances when the shots were fired, all that a reasonable officer could have concluded from Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s conduct was that he was intent on resuming his flight, which would again pose a threat to others on the road. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (2)\xc2\xa0Petitioners did not fire more shots than necessary to end the public safety risk. It makes sense that, if officers are justified in firing at a suspect in order to end a severe threat to public safety, they need not stop shooting until the threat has ended. Here, during the 10-second span when all the shots were fired, Rickard never abandoned his attempt to flee and eventually managed to drive away. A passenger\xe2\x80\x99s presence does not bear on whether officers violated Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFourth Amendment rights, which \xe2\x80\x9care personal rights [that] may not be vicariously asserted.\xe2\x80\x9d Alderman v. United States,\n\n394 U.\xc2\xa0S. 165\n. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n 3.\xc2\xa0Even if the officers\xe2\x80\x99 conduct had violated the\n \nFourth Amendment, petitioners would still be entitled to summary judgment based on qualified immunity. An official sued under \xc2\xa71983 is entitled to qualified immunity unless it is shown that the official violated a statutory or constitutional right that was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98clearly established\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the challenged conduct. Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Brosseau v. Haugen,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 194\n, where an officer shot at a fleeing vehicle to prevent possible harm, makes plain that no clearly established law precluded the officer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct there. Thus, to prevail, respondent must meaningfully distinguish Brosseau or point to any \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolling authority\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9crobust \xe2\x80\x98consensus of cases of persuasive authority,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d al-Kidd, supra, at ___, that emerged between the events there and those here that would alter the qualified-immunity analysis. Respondent has made neither showing. If anything, the facts here are more favorable to the officers than the facts in Brosseau; and respondent points to no cases that could be said to have clearly established the unconstitutionality of using lethal force to end a high-speed car chase. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

509 Fed. Appx. 388, reversed and remanded.

\n

OFFICER VANCE PLUMHOFF, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. WHITNE RICKARD, a Minor Child, Individually, andas Surviving Daughter of DONALD RICKARD, Deceased, By and Through Her Mother SAMANTHA RICKARD, as Parent and Next Friend

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[May 27, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.[1]*

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The courts below denied qualified immunity for police officers who shot the driver of a fleeing vehicle to put an end to a dangerous car chase. We reverse and hold that the officers did not violate the\n \nFourth Amendment. In the alternative, we conclude that the officers were entitled to qualified immunity because they violated no clearly established law.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Because this case arises from the denial of the officers\xe2\x80\x99 motion for summary judgment, we view the facts in the light most favorable to the nonmoving party, the daughter of the driver who attempted to flee. Wilkie v. Robbins,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 537\n, n.\xc2\xa02 (2007). Near midnight on July 18, 2004, Lieutenant Joseph Forthman of the West Memphis, Arkansas, Police Department pulled over a white Honda Accord because the car had only one operating headlight. Donald Rickard was the driver of the Accord, and Kelly Allen was in the passenger seat. Forthman noticed an indentation, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98roughly the size of a head or a basketball\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in the windshield of the car. Estate of Allen v. West Memphis, 2011 WL 197426, *1 (WD Tenn., Jan. 20, 2011). He asked Rickard if he had been drinking, and Rickard responded that he had not. Because Rickard failed to produce his driver\xe2\x80\x99s license upon request and appeared nervous, Forthman asked him to step out of the car. Rather than comply with Forthman\xe2\x80\x99s request, Rickard sped away.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Forthman gave chase and was soon joined by five other police cruisers driven by Sergeant Vance Plumhoff and Officers Jimmy Evans, Lance Ellis, Troy Galtelli, and John Gardner. The officers pursued Rickard east on In-terstate 40 toward Memphis, Tennessee. While on I\xe2\x80\x9340, they attempted to stop Rickard using a \xe2\x80\x9crolling roadblock,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at *2, but they were unsuccessful. The District Court described the vehicles as \xe2\x80\x9cswerving through traffic at high speeds,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at *8, and respondent does not dispute that the cars attained speeds over 100 miles per hour.[2] See Memorandum of Law in Response to Defendants\xe2\x80\x99 Motion for Summary Judgment in No. 2:05\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x932585 (WD Tenn.), p.\xc2\xa016; see also Tr. of Oral Arg. 54:23\xe2\x80\x9355:6. During the chase, Rickard and the officers passed more than two dozen vehicles.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Rickard eventually exited I\xe2\x80\x9340 in Memphis, and shortly afterward he made \xe2\x80\x9ca quick right turn,\xe2\x80\x9d causing \xe2\x80\x9ccontact [to] occu[r]\xe2\x80\x9d between his car and Evans\xe2\x80\x99 cruiser. 2011 WL 197426, *3. As a result of that contact, Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s car spun out into a parking lot and collided with Plumhoff\xe2\x80\x99s cruiser. Now in danger of being cornered, Rickard put his car into reverse \xe2\x80\x9cin an attempt to escape.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As he did so, Evans and Plumhoff got out of their cruisers and approached Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s car, and Evans, gun in hand, pounded on the passenger-side window. At that point, Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s car \xe2\x80\x9cmade contact with\xe2\x80\x9d yet another police cruiser. Ibid. Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s tires started spinning, and his car \xe2\x80\x9cwas rocking back and forth,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., indicating that Rickard was using the accelerator even though his bumper was flush against a police cruiser. At that point, Plumhoff fired three shots into Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s car. Rickard then \xe2\x80\x9creversed in a 180 degree arc\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmaneuvered onto\xe2\x80\x9d another street, forcing Ellis to \xe2\x80\x9cstep to his right to avoid the vehicle.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As Rickard continued \xe2\x80\x9cfleeing down\xe2\x80\x9d that street, ibid., Gardner and Galtelli fired 12 shots toward Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s car, bringing the total number of shots fired during this incident to 15. Rickard then lost control of the car and crashed into a building. Ibid. Rickard and Allen both died from some combination of gunshot wounds and injuries suffered in the crash that ended the chase. See App. 60, 76.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Respondent, Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s surviving daughter, filed this action under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, against the six individual police officers and the mayor and chief of police of West Memphis. She alleged that the officers used excessive force in violation of the Fourth and\n \nFourteenth Amendments.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The officers moved for summary judgment based on qualified immunity, but the District Court denied that motion, holding that the officers\xe2\x80\x99 conduct violated the\n \nFourth Amendment and was contrary to law that was clearly established at the time in question. The officers appealed, but a Sixth Circuit motions panel initially dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction based on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Johnson v. Jones,\n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 304,\n309 (1995)\n. Later, however, that panel granted rehearing, vacated its dismissal order, and left the jurisdictional issue to be decided by a merits panel.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The merits panel then affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision on the merits. Estate of Allen v. West Memphis, 509 Fed. Appx. 388 (CA6 2012). On the issue of appellate jurisdiction, the merits panel began by stating that a \xe2\x80\x9cmotion for qualified immunity denied on the basis of a district court\xe2\x80\x99s determination that there exists a triable issue of fact generally cannot be appealed on an interlocutory basis.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 391. But the panel then noted that the Sixth Circuit had previously interpreted our decision in Scott v. Harris,\n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 372 (2007)\n, as creating an \xe2\x80\x9cexception to this rule\xe2\x80\x9d under which an immediate appeal may be taken to challenge \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98blatantly and demonstrably false\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d factual determinations. 509 Fed. Appx., at 391 (quoting Moldowan v. Warren, 578 F.\xc2\xa03d 351, 370 (CA6 2009)). Concluding that none of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s fac-tual determinations ran afoul of that high standard, and distinguishing the facts of this case from those in Scott, the panel held that the officers\xe2\x80\x99 conduct violated the\n \nFourth Amendment. 509 Fed. Appx., at 392, and n.\xc2\xa03. The panel said nothing about whether the officers violated clearly established law, but since the panel affirmed the order denying the officers\xe2\x80\x99 summary judgment motion,[3] the panel must have decided that issue in respondent\xe2\x80\x99s favor.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We granted certiorari. 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ____ (2013).

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We start with the question whether the Court of Appeals properly exercised jurisdiction under\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71291, which gives the courts of appeals jurisdiction to hear appeals from \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decisions\xe2\x80\x9d of the district courts.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n An order denying a motion for summary judgment is generally not a final decision within the meaning of \xc2\xa71291 and is thus generally not immediately appealable. Johnson, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 309. But that general rule does not apply when the summary judgment motion is based on a claim of qualified immunity. Id., at 311; Mitchell v. Forsyth,\n\n472 U.\xc2\xa0S. 511,\n528 (1985)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c[Q]ualified immunity is \xe2\x80\x98an immunity from suit rather than a mere defense to liability.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Pearson v. Callahan,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 223,\n231 (2009)\n (quoting Mitchell, supra, at 526). As a result, pretrial orders denying qualified immunity generally fall within the collateral order doctrine. See Ashcroft v. Iqbal,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 662\n\xe2\x80\x93672 (2009). This is so because such orders conclusively determine whether the defendant is entitled to immunity from suit; this immunity issue is both important and completely separate from the merits of the action, and this question could not be effectively reviewed on appeal from a final judgment because by that time the immunity from standing trial will have been irretrievably lost. See ibid; Johnson, supra, at 311\xe2\x80\x93312 (citing Mitchell, supra, at 525\xe2\x80\x93527).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Respondent argues that our decision in Johnson, forecloses appellate jurisdiction under the circumstances here, but the order from which the appeal was taken in Johnson was quite different from the order in the present case. In Johnson, the plaintiff brought suit against certain police officers who, he alleged, had beaten him. 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 307. These officers moved for summary judgment, asserting that they were not present at the time of the alleged beating and had nothing to do with it. Id., at 307\xe2\x80\x93308. The District Court determined, however, that the evidence in the summary judgment record was sufficient to support a contrary finding, and the court therefore denied the officers\xe2\x80\x99 motion for summary judgment. Id., at 308. The officers then appealed, arguing that the District Court had not correctly analyzed the relevant evidence. Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This Court held that the Johnson order was not immediately appealable because it merely decided \xe2\x80\x9ca question of \xe2\x80\x98evidence sufficiency,\xe2\x80\x99 i.e., which facts a party may, or may not, be able to prove at trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 313. The Court noted that an order denying summary judgment based on a determination of \xe2\x80\x9cevidence sufficiency\xe2\x80\x9d does not present a legal question in the sense in which the term was used in Mitchell, the decision that first held that a pretrial order rejecting a claim of qualified immunity is immediately appealable. Johnson, 515 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 314. In addition, the Court observed that a determination of evidence sufficiency is closely related to other determinations that the trial court may be required to make at later stages of the case. Id., at 317. The Court also noted that appellate courts have \xe2\x80\x9cno comparative expertise\xe2\x80\x9d over trial courts in making such determinations and that forcing appellate courts to entertain appeals from such orders would impose an undue burden. Id., at 309\xe2\x80\x93310, 316.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The District Court order in this case is nothing like the order in Johnson. Petitioners do not claim that other officers were responsible for shooting Rickard; rather, they contend that their conduct did not violate the\n \nFourth Amendment and, in any event, did not violate clearly established law. Thus, they raise legal issues; these issues are quite different from any purely factual issues that the trial court might confront if the case were tried; deciding legal issues of this sort is a core responsibility of appellate courts, and requiring appellate courts to decide such issues is not an undue burden.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The District Court order here is not materially distinguishable from the District Court order in Scott v. Harris, and in that case we expressed no doubts about the jurisdiction of the Court of Appeals under \xc2\xa71291. Accordingly, here, as in Scott, we hold that the Court of Appeals prop-erly exercised jurisdiction, and we therefore turn to the merits.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Petitioners contend that the decision of the Court of Appeals is wrong for two separate reasons. They maintain that they did not violate Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFourth Amendment rights and that, in any event, their conduct did not violate any\n \nFourth Amendment rule that was clearly established at the time of the events in question. When confronted with such arguments, we held in Saucier v. Katz,\n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 194,\n200 (2001)\n, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe first inquiry must be whether a constitutional right would have been violated on the facts alleged.\xe2\x80\x9d Only after deciding that question, we concluded, may an appellate court turn to the question whether the right at issue was clearly established at the relevant time. Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We subsequently altered this rigid framework in Pearson, declaring that \xe2\x80\x9cSaucier\xe2\x80\x99s procedure should not be regarded as an inflexible requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 227. At the same time, however, we noted that the Saucier procedure \xe2\x80\x9cis often beneficial\xe2\x80\x9d because it \xe2\x80\x9cpromotes the development of constitutional precedent and is especially valuable with respect to questions that do not frequently arise in cases in which a qualified immunity defense is unavailable.\xe2\x80\x9d 555 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 236. Pearson concluded that courts \xe2\x80\x9chave the discretion to decide whether that [Sau-cier] procedure is worthwhile in particular cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 242.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Heeding our guidance in Pearson, we begin in this case with the question whether the officers\xe2\x80\x99 conduct violated the\n \nFourth Amendment. This approach, we believe, will be \xe2\x80\x9cbeneficial\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cdevelop[ing] constitutional precedent\xe2\x80\x9d in an area that courts typically consider in cases in which the defendant asserts a qualified immunity defense. See Pearson, supra, at 236.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A claim that law-enforcement officers used excessive force to effect a seizure is governed by the\n \nFourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9creasonableness\xe2\x80\x9d standard. See Graham v. Connor,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 386 (1989)\n; Tennessee v. Garner,\n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1985)\n. In Graham, we held that determining the objective reasonableness of a particular seizure under the\n \nFourth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9crequires a careful balancing of the nature and quality of the intrusion on the individual\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFourth Amendment interests against the countervailing governmental interests at stake.\xe2\x80\x9d 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 396 (internal quotation marks omitted). The inquiry requires analyzing the totality of the circumstances. See ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We analyze this question from the perspective \xe2\x80\x9cof a reasonable officer on the scene, rather than with the 20/20 vision of hindsight.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We thus \xe2\x80\x9callo[w] for the fact that police officers are often forced to make split-second judgments\xe2\x80\x94in circumstances that are tense, uncertain, and rapidly evolving\xe2\x80\x94about the amount of force that is necessary in a particular situation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 396\xe2\x80\x93397.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In this case, respondent advances two main\n \nFourth Amendment arguments. First, she contends that the\n \nFourth Amendment did not allow petitioners to use deadly force to terminate the chase. See Brief for Respondent 24\xe2\x80\x9335. Second, she argues that the \xe2\x80\x9cdegree of force was excessive,\xe2\x80\x9d that is, that even if the officers were permitted to fire their weapons, they went too far when they fired as many rounds as they did. See id., at 36\xe2\x80\x9338. We address each issue in turn.

\n

1

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In Scott, we considered a claim that a police officer violated the\n \nFourth Amendment when he terminated a high-speed car chase by using a technique that placed a \xe2\x80\x9cfleeing motorist at risk of serious injury or death.\xe2\x80\x9d 550 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 386. The record in that case contained a videotape of the chase, and we found that the events recorded on the tape justified the officer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. We wrote as follows: \xe2\x80\x9cAlthough there is no obvious way to quantify the risks on either side, it is clear from the videotape that respondent posed an actual and imminent threat to the lives of any pedestrians who might have been present, to other civilian motorists, and to the officers involved in the chase.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 383\xe2\x80\x93384. We also wrote:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[R]espondent\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle rac[ed] down narrow, two-lane roads in the dead of night at speeds that are shock-ingly fast. We see it swerve around more than a dozen other cars, cross the double-yellow line, and force cars traveling in both directions to their respective shoulders to avoid being hit. We see it run multiple red lights and travel for considerable periods of time in the occasional center left-turn-only lane, chased by numerous police cars forced to engage in the same hazardous maneuvers just to keep up.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 379\xe2\x80\x93380 (footnote omitted).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In light of those facts, \xe2\x80\x9cwe [thought] it [was] quite clear that [the police officer] did not violate the\n \nFourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 381. We held that a \xe2\x80\x9cpolice officer\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to terminate a dangerous high-speed car chase that threatens the lives of innocent bystanders does not violate the\n \nFourth Amendment, even when it places the fleeing motorist at risk of serious injury or death.\xe2\x80\x9d[4] Id., at 386.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We see no basis for reaching a different conclusion here. As we have explained supra, at ___, the chase in this case exceeded 100 miles per hour and lasted over five minutes. During that chase, Rickard passed more than two dozen other vehicles, several of which were forced to alter course. Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s outrageously reckless driving posed a grave public safety risk. And while it is true that Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s car eventually collided with a police car and came temporarily to a near standstill, that did not end the chase. Less than three seconds later, Rickard resumed maneuvering his car. Just before the shots were fired, when the front bumper of his car was flush with that of one of the police cruisers, Rickard was obviously pushing down on the accelerator because the car\xe2\x80\x99s wheels were spinning, and then Rickard threw the car into reverse \xe2\x80\x9cin an attempt to escape.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, the record conclusively disproves respondent\xe2\x80\x99s claim that the chase in the present case was already over when petitioners began shooting. Under the circumstances at the moment when the shots were fired, all that a reasonable police officer could have concluded was that Rickard was intent on resuming his flight and that, if he was allowed to do so, he would once again pose a deadly threat for others on the road. Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s conduct even after the shots were fired\xe2\x80\x94as noted, he managed to drive away despite the efforts of the police to block his path\xe2\x80\x94underscores the point.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In light of the circumstances we have discussed, it is beyond serious dispute that Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s flight posed a grave public safety risk, and here, as in Scott, the police acted reasonably in using deadly force to end that risk.

\n

2

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We now consider respondent\xe2\x80\x99s contention that, even if the use of deadly force was permissible, petitioners acted unreasonably in firing a total of 15 shots. We reject that argument. It stands to reason that, if police officers are justified in firing at a suspect in order to end a severe threat to public safety, the officers need not stop shooting until the threat has ended. As petitioners noted below, \xe2\x80\x9cif lethal force is justified, officers are taught to keep shooting until the threat is over.\xe2\x80\x9d 509 Fed. Appx., at 392.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Here, during the 10-second span when all the shots were fired, Rickard never abandoned his attempt to flee. Indeed, even after all the shots had been fired, he managed to drive away and to continue driving until he crashed. This would be a different case if petitioners had initiated a second round of shots after an initial round had clearly incapacitated Rickard and had ended any threat of continued flight, or if Rickard had clearly given himself up. But that is not what happened.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In arguing that too many shots were fired, respondent relies in part on the presence of Kelly Allen in the front seat of the car, but we do not think that this factor changes the calculus. Our cases make it clear that \xe2\x80\x9cFourth Amendment rights are personal rights which .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may not be vicariously asserted.\xe2\x80\x9d Alderman v. United States,\n\n394 U. S. 165,\n174 (1969)\n; see also Rakas v. Illinois,\n\n439 U.\xc2\xa0S. 128\n\xe2\x80\x93143 (1978). Thus, the question before us is whether petitioners violated Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFourth Amendment rights, not Allen\xe2\x80\x99s. If a suit were brought on behalf of Allen under either \xc2\xa71983 or state tort law, the risk to Allen would be of central concern.[5] But Allen\xe2\x80\x99s presence in the car cannot enhance Rickard\xe2\x80\x99s\n \nFourth Amendment rights. After all, it was Rickard who put Allen in danger by fleeing and refusing to end the chase, and it would be perverse if his disregard for Allen\xe2\x80\x99s safety worked to his benefit.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We have held that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 conduct did not violate the\n \nFourth Amendment, but even if that were not the case, petitioners would still be entitled to summary judgment based on qualified immunity.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n An official sued under \xc2\xa71983 is entitled to qualified immunity unless it is shown that the official violated a statutory or constitutional right that was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98clearly established\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the challenged conduct. Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 3). And a defendant cannot be said to have violated a clearly established right unless the right\xe2\x80\x99s contours were sufficiently definite that any reasonable official in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s shoes would have understood that he was violating it. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 9). In other words, \xe2\x80\x9cexisting precedent must have placed the statutory or constitutional question\xe2\x80\x9d confronted by the official \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond debate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In addition, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e have repeatedly told courts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not to define clearly established law at a high level of generality,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at ___ (slip op., at 10), since doing so avoids the crucial question whether the official acted reasonably in the particular circumstances that he or she faced. \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We think our decision in Brosseau v. Haugen,\n\n543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 194 (2004)\n (per curiam) squarely demonstrates that no clearly established law precluded petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 conduct at the time in question. In Brosseau, we held that a police officer did not violate clearly established law when she fired at a fleeing vehicle to prevent possible harm to \xe2\x80\x9cother officers on foot who [she] believed were in the immediate area, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. occupied vehicles in [the driver\xe2\x80\x99s] path[,] and .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any other citizens who might be in the area.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 197 (quoting 339 F.\xc2\xa03d 857, 865 (CA9 2003); internal quotation marks omitted). After surveying lower court decisions regarding the reasonableness of lethal force as a response to vehicular flight, we observed that this is an area \xe2\x80\x9cin which the result depends very much on the facts of each case\xe2\x80\x9d and that the cases \xe2\x80\x9cby no means \xe2\x80\x98clearly establish[ed]\xe2\x80\x99 that [the officer\xe2\x80\x99s] conduct violated the\n \nFourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 201. In reaching that conclusion, we held that Garner and Graham, which are \xe2\x80\x9ccast at a high level of generality,\xe2\x80\x9d did not clearly establish that the officer\xe2\x80\x99s decision was unreasonable. 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 199.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Brosseau makes plain that as of February 21, 1999\xe2\x80\x94the date of the events at issue in that case\xe2\x80\x94it was not clearly established that it was unconstitutional to shoot a fleeing driver to protect those whom his flight might endanger. We did not consider later decided cases because they \xe2\x80\x9ccould not have given fair notice to [the officer].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 200, n.\xc2\xa04. To defeat immunity here, then, respondent must show at a minimum either (1)\xc2\xa0that the officers\xe2\x80\x99 conduct in this case was materially different from the conduct in Brosseau or (2)\xc2\xa0that between February 21, 1999, and July 18, 2004, there emerged either \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98controlling authority\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or a \xe2\x80\x9crobust \xe2\x80\x98consensus of cases of persuasive authority,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d al-Kidd, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 10) (quoting Wilson v. Layne,\n\n526 U.\xc2\xa0S. 603,\n617 (1999)\n; some internal quotation marks omitted), that would alter our analysis of the qualified immunity question. Respondent has made neither showing.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n To begin, certain facts here are more favorable to the officers. In Brosseau, an officer on foot fired at a driver who had just begun to flee and who had not yet driven his car in a dangerous manner. In contrast, the officers here shot at Rickard to put an end to what had already been a lengthy, high-speed pursuit that indisputably posed a danger both to the officers involved and to any civilians who happened to be nearby. Indeed, the lone dissenting Justice in Brosseau emphasized that in that case, \xe2\x80\x9cthere was no ongoing or prior high-speed car chase to inform the [constitutional] analysis.\xe2\x80\x9d 543 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 206, n.\xc2\xa04 (opinion of Stevens, J.). Attempting to distinguish Brosseau, respondent focuses on the fact that the officer there fired only 1 shot, whereas here three officers collectively fired 15 shots. But it was certainly not clearly established at the time of the shooting in this case that the number of shots fired, under the circumstances present here, rendered the use of force excessive.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Since respondent cannot meaningfully distinguish Brosseau, her only option is to show that its analysis was out of date by 2004. Yet respondent has not pointed us to any case\xe2\x80\x94let alone a controlling case or a robust consensus of cases\xe2\x80\x94decided between 1999 and 2004 that could be said to have clearly established the unconstitutionality of using lethal force to end a high-speed car chase. And respondent receives no help on this front from the opinions below. The District Court cited only a single case decided between 1999 and 2004 that identified a possible constitutional violation by an officer who shot a fleeing driver, and the facts of that case\xe2\x80\x94where a reasonable jury could have concluded that the suspect merely \xe2\x80\x9caccelerated to eighty to eighty-five miles per hour in a seventy-miles-per-hour zone\xe2\x80\x9d and did not \xe2\x80\x9cengag[e] in any evasive maneuvers,\xe2\x80\x9d Vaughan v. Cox, 343 F.\xc2\xa03d 1323, 1330\xe2\x80\x931331 (CA11 2003)\xe2\x80\x94bear little resemblance to those here.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Under the circumstances present in this case, we hold that the\n \nFourth Amendment did not prohibit petitioners from using the deadly force that they employed to terminate the dangerous car chase that Rickard precipitated. In the alternative, we note that petitioners are entitled to qualified immunity for the conduct at issue because they violated no clearly established law.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
