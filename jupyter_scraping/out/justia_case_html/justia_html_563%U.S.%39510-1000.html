\nBOBBY V. MITTS
\n563 U. S. ____ (2011)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

DAVID BOBBY, WARDEN, PETITIONER v.\nHARRY MITTS

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 10\xe2\x80\x931000.\xe2\x80\x83Decided May 2, 2011

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Per Curiam.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An Ohio jury convicted respondent Harry Mitts on two counts of aggravated murder and two counts of attempted murder. He was sentenced to death. At issue here is part of the jury instructions given during the penalty phase of Mitts\xe2\x80\x99s trial. The instructions, in pertinent part, were as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Y]ou must determine beyond a reasonable doubt whether the aggravating circumstances, which [Mitts] was found guilty of committing in the separate counts, are sufficient to outweigh the mitigating factors you find are present in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cWhen all 12 members of the jury find by proof beyond a reasonable doubt that the aggravating circumstances in each separate count with which [Mitts] has been found guilty of committing outweigh the mitigating factors, if any, then you must return such finding to the Court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cI instruct you as a matter of law that if you make such a finding, then you must recommend to the Court that the sentence of death be imposed on [Mitts].

\n

.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cOn the other hand, [if] after considering all the relevant evidence raised at trial, the evidence and testimony received at this hearing and the arguments of counsel, you find that the state of Ohio failed to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the aggravating circumstances with which [Mitts] was found guilty of committing outweigh the mitigating factors, you will then proceed to determine which of two possible life imprisonment sentences to recommend to the Court.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 352a\xe2\x80\x93353a.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We considered virtually the same Ohio jury instructions last Term in Smith v. Spisak, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 7). See Mitts v. Bagley, 620 F.\xc2\xa03d 650, 652 (CA6 2010) (noting that the \xe2\x80\x9cinstructions in this case are the same Ohio instructions that were given in\xe2\x80\x9d Spisak). That case, like this one, involved review of a federal habeas petition under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA). AEDPA provides, as relevant here, that relief may not be granted unless the state court adjudication \xe2\x80\x9cresulted in a decision that was contrary to . . . clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Spisak, we reversed a Court of Appeals decision that had found these instructions invalid under our decision in Mills v. Maryland, 486 U. S. 367 (1988). See 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 8\xe2\x80\x939). Up until our decision in Spisak, Mitts had also pressed the claim that the instructions were invalid under Mills. After Spisak rejected that claim, the Court of Appeals in this case determined that the instructions were contrary to our decision in Beck v. Alabama, 447 U. S. 625 (1980), and accordingly vacated Mitts\xe2\x80\x99s death sentence. See 620 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 658.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Beck, we held that the death penalty may not be imposed \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the jury was not permitted to consider a verdict of guilt of a lesser included non-capital offense, and when the evidence would have supported such a verdict.\xe2\x80\x9d 447 U. S., at 627 (internal quotation marks omitted). We explained that such a scheme intolerably enhances the \xe2\x80\x9crisk of an unwarranted conviction\xe2\x80\x9d because it \xe2\x80\x9cinterjects irrelevant considerations into the factfinding process, diverting the jury\xe2\x80\x99s attention from the central issue of whether the State has satisfied its burden of proving beyond a reasonable doubt that the defendant is guilty of a capital crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 638, 642. \xe2\x80\x9c[F]orcing the jury to choose between conviction on the capital offense and acquittal,\xe2\x80\x9d we observed, \xe2\x80\x9cmay encourage the jury to convict for an impermissible reason\xe2\x80\x94its belief that the defendant is guilty of some serious crime and should be punished,\xe2\x80\x9d even when there is \xe2\x80\x9csome doubt with respect to an element\xe2\x80\x9d of the capital offense. Id., at 632, 642, 637. Because the scheme in Beck created a danger that the jury would resolve any doubts in favor of conviction, we concluded that it violated due process. See id., at 638, 643.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0According to the Court of Appeals below, the penalty phase instructions given at Mitts\xe2\x80\x99s trial\xe2\x80\x94and the Supreme Court of Ohio decision upholding their use\xe2\x80\x94were \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to\xe2\x80\x9d Beck, because they \xe2\x80\x9cinterposed before the jury the same false choice\xe2\x80\x9d that our holding in Beck prohibits. 620 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 658, 657 (internal quotation marks omitted). Referring to the instructions as \xe2\x80\x9cacquittal-first,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals stated that they impermissibly required the jury to first decide whether to \xe2\x80\x9cacquit\xe2\x80\x9d Mitts of the death penalty before considering \xe2\x80\x9cmercy and some form of life imprisonment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 656\xe2\x80\x93657. Interpreting Beck to stand for the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9ca jury instruction violates due process if it requires a mandatory death penalty sentence that can only be avoided by an acquittal before the jury has an opportunity to consider life imprisonment,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals concluded that the instructions given during the penalty phase of Mitts\xe2\x80\x99s trial unconstitutionally \xe2\x80\x9cdeprived the jury of a meaningful opportunity to consider\xe2\x80\x9d a life sentence. 620 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 658, 657 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The instructions here are surely not invalid under our decision in Beck. The concern addressed in Beck was \xe2\x80\x9cthe risk of an unwarranted conviction\xe2\x80\x9d created when the jury is forced to choose between finding the defendant guilty of a capital offense and declaring him innocent of any wrongdoing. 447 U. S., at 637 (emphasis added); id., at 638; see also Spaziano v. Florida, 468 U. S. 447, 455 (1984) (explaining that the \xe2\x80\x9cgoal of the Beck rule\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cto eliminate the distortion of the factfinding process that is created when the jury is forced into an all-or-nothing choice between capital murder and innocence\xe2\x80\x9d); Schad v. Arizona, 501 U. S. 624, 646 (1991) (\xe2\x80\x9cOur fundamental concern in Beck was that a jury convinced that the defendant had committed some violent crime but not convinced that he was guilty of a capital crime might nonetheless vote for a capital conviction if the only alternative was to set the defendant free with no punishment at all\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question here, however, concerns the penalty phase, not the guilt phase, and we have already concluded that the logic of Beck is not directly applicable to penalty phase proceedings. In California v. Ramos, 463 U. S. 992 (1983), we rejected an argument that Beck prohibited an instruction to \xe2\x80\x9ca capital sentencing jury regarding the Governor\xe2\x80\x99s power to commute a sentence of life without possibility of parole.\xe2\x80\x9d 463 U. S., at 994, 1006\xe2\x80\x931009. In so doing, we noted the \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental difference between the nature of the guilt/innocence determination at issue in Beck and the nature of the life/death choice at the penalty phase.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1007. In light of that critical distinction, we observed that \xe2\x80\x9cthe concern of Beck regarding the risk of an unwarranted conviction is simply not directly translatable to the deliberative process in which the capital jury engages in determining the appropriate penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1009; see also Schad, supra, at 647 (stating that the \xe2\x80\x9ccentral concern of Beck simply is not implicated\xe2\x80\x9d when the \xe2\x80\x9cjury was not faced with an all-or-nothing choice between the offense of conviction (capital murder) and innocence\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jurors in Mitts\xe2\x80\x99s case could not have plausibly thought that if they declined to recommend the death penalty Mitts would \xe2\x80\x9cescape all penalties for his alleged participation in the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Beck, supra, at 629. They had just convicted him on two counts of aggravated murder and two counts of attempted murder. They were specifically instructed that if they did not find that the aggravating factors outweighed the mitigating factors\xe2\x80\x94and therefore did not recommend the death penalty\xe2\x80\x94they would choose from two life sentence options. There is accordingly no reason to believe that the jurors in this case, unlike the jurors in Beck, could have been improperly influenced by a fear that a decision short of death would have resulted in Mitts walking free.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We all but decided the question presented here in Spisak itself. After rejecting the contention that the Ohio instructions were contrary to Mills, we noted that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Court of Appeals found the jury instructions unconstitutional for an additional reason, that the instructions \xe2\x80\x98require[d] the jury to unanimously reject a death sentence before considering other sentencing alternatives.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 9) (quoting Spisak v. Mitchell, 465 F.\xc2\xa03d 684, 709 (CA6 2006)). That is essentially the Beck claim presented here. See 620 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 658 (holding that a \xe2\x80\x9cjury instruction violates due process if it requires a mandatory death penalty sentence that can only be avoided by an acquittal before the jury has an opportunity to consider life imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d). We rejected that claim in Spisak under AEDPA, noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e have not . . . previously held jury instructions unconstitutional for this reason.\xe2\x80\x9d 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 9). Although neither the parties nor the courts below in Spisak had cited Beck, a separate concurrence in Spisak would have struck down the instructions in reliance on that decision. See 558 U. S., at ___ (Stevens, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 3\xe2\x80\x936). The Court nonetheless concluded that whatever the merits of that argument on direct review, \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury instructions at Spisak\xe2\x80\x99s trial were not contrary to \xe2\x80\x98clearly established Federal law\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d under AEDPA. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 9). The same conclusion applies here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The petition for certiorari and the motion for leave to proceed in forma pauperis are granted. The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit is

\n

Reversed.
