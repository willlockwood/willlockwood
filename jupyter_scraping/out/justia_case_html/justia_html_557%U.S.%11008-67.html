YEAGER v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x9367.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 23, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 18, 2009

\n

A federal indictment charged petitioner Yeager with securities and wire fraud for allegedly misleading the public about the virtues of a fiber-optic telecommunications system offered by his employer, a subsidiary of Enron Corp., and with insider trading for selling his Enron stock while in possession of material, nonpublic information about the new system\xe2\x80\x99s performance and value to Enron. The indictment also charged petitioner with money laundering for conducting various transactions with the proceeds of his stock sales. The jury acquitted Yeager on the fraud counts but failed to reach a verdict on the insider-trading and money-laundering counts. After the Government recharged him with some of the insider-trading and money-laundering counts, Yeager moved to dismiss the charges on the ground that the jury, by acquitting him on the fraud counts, had necessarily decided that he did not possess material, nonpublic information about the project\xe2\x80\x99s performance and value, and that the issue-preclusion component of the Double Jeopardy Clause therefore barred a second trial for insider trading and money laundering. The District Court denied the motion, and the Fifth Circuit affirmed, reasoning that the fact that the jury hung on the insider-trading and money-laundering counts\xe2\x80\x94as opposed to acquitting petitioner\xe2\x80\x94cast doubt on whether it had necessarily decided that petitioner did not possess material, nonpublic information. This inconsistency between the acquittals and the hung counts, the Fifth Circuit concluded, meant that the Government could prosecute petitioner anew for insider trading and money laundering.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0An apparent inconsistency between a jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal on some counts and its failure to return a verdict on other counts does not affect the acquittals\xe2\x80\x99 preclusive force under the Double Jeopardy Clause. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0This case is controlled by the reasoning in Ashe v. Swenson, 397 U. S. 436, where the Court squarely held that the Double Jeopardy Clause precludes the Government from relitigating any issue that was necessarily decided by a jury\xe2\x80\x99s acquittal in a prior trial. For double jeopardy purposes, the jury\xe2\x80\x99s inability to reach a verdict on Yeager\xe2\x80\x99s insider-trading and money-laundering counts was a nonevent that should be given no weight in the issue-preclusion analysis. To identify what a jury necessarily determined at trial, courts should scrutinize the jury\xe2\x80\x99s decisions, not its failures to decide. A jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal represents the community\xe2\x80\x99s collective judgment regarding all the evidence and arguments presented to it. Even if the verdict is \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon an egregiously erroneous foundation,\xe2\x80\x9d Fong Foo v. United States, 369 U. S. 141, 143, its finality is unassailable, see, e.g., Arizona v. Washington, 434 U. S. 497, 503. Thus, if the possession of insider information was a critical issue of ultimate fact in all of the charges against Yeager, a jury verdict that necessarily decided that issue in his favor protects him from prosecution for any charge for which that is an essential element. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Neither Richardson v. United States, 468 U. S. 317, nor United States v. Powell, 469 U. S. 57, supports the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument that it can retry Yeager for insider trading or money laundering. Richardson\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that a jury\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfailure \xe2\x80\xa6 to reach a verdict is not an event which terminates jeopardy,\xe2\x80\x9d 468 U. S., at 325, did not open the door to using a hung count to ignore the preclusive effect of a jury\xe2\x80\x99s acquittal, but was simply a rejection of the argument\xe2\x80\x94similar to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s today\xe2\x80\x94that a mistrial is an event of significance. Also rejected is the contention that an acquittal can never preclude retrial on a hung count because it would impute irrationality to the jury in violation of Powell\xe2\x80\x99s rule that issue preclusion is \xe2\x80\x9cpredicated on the assumption that the jury acted rationally,\xe2\x80\x9d 469 U. S., at 68. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal in Powell and in Dunn v. United States, 284 U. S. 390, to impugn the legitimacy of jury verdicts that, on their face, were logically inconsistent shows, a fortiori, that a potentially inconsistent hung count could not command a different result. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The Government has argued that, even if hung counts cannot enter the issue-preclusion analysis, Yeager has failed to show that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s acquittals necessarily resolved in his favor an issue of ultimate fact that must be proved to convict him of insider trading and money laundering. Having granted certiorari on the assumption that the Fifth Circuit ruled correctly that the acquittals meant the jury found that Yeager did not have insider information that contradicted what was presented to the public, this Court declines to engage in a fact-intensive analysis of the voluminous record that is unnecessary to resolve the narrow legal question at issue. If the Court of Appeals chooses, it may revisit its factual analysis in light of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s arguments before this Court. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

521 F.\xc2\xa03d 367, reversed and remanded.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nYEAGER V. UNITED STATES
\n557 U. S. ____ (2009)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 08-67

\n

F. SCOTT YEAGER, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[June 18, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Dunn v. United States, 284 U. S. 390, 393 (1932), the Court, speaking through Justice Holmes, held that a logical inconsistency between a guilty verdict and a verdict of acquittal does not impugn the validity of either verdict. The question presented in this case is whether an apparent inconsistency between a jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal on some counts and its failure to return a verdict on other counts affects the preclusive force of the acquittals under the Double Jeopardy Clause of the Fifth Amendment. We hold that it does not.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1997, Enron Corporation (Enron) acquired a telecommunications business that it expanded and ultimately renamed Enron Broadband Services (EBS). Petitioner F. Scott Yeager served as Senior Vice President of Strategic Development for EBS from October 1, 1998, until his employment was terminated a few months before Enron filed for bankruptcy on December 2, 2001. During his tenure, petitioner played an active role in EBS\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to develop a nationwide fiber-optic telecommunications system called the Enron Intelligent Network (EIN).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the summer of 1999, Enron announced that EBS would become a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98core\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Enron business and a major part of its overall strategy. App. 11. Thereafter, Enron issued press releases touting the advanced capabilities of EIN and claiming that the project was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98lit,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or operational. Id., at 10. On January 20, 2000, at the company\xe2\x80\x99s annual equity analyst conference, petitioner and others allegedly made false and misleading statements about the value and performance of the EIN project. On January 21, 2000, the price of Enron stock rose from $54 to $67. The next day it reached $72. At that point petitioner sold more than 100,000 shares of Enron stock that he had received as part of his compensation. During the next several months petitioner sold an additional 600,000 shares. All told, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s stock sales generated more than $54 million in proceeds and $19 million in personal profit. As for the EIN project, its value turned out to be illusory. The \xe2\x80\x9cintelligent\xe2\x80\x9d network showcased to the public in the press releases and at the analyst conference was riddled with technological problems and never fully developed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On November 5, 2004, a grand jury returned a \xe2\x80\x9cFifth Superseding Indictment\xe2\x80\x9d charging petitioner with 126 counts of five federal offenses: (1) conspiracy to commit securities and wire fraud; (2) securities fraud; (3) wire fraud; (4) insider trading; and (5) money laundering.[Footnote 1] The Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory of prosecution was that petitioner\xe2\x80\x94acting in concert with other Enron executives\xe2\x80\x94purposefully deceived the public about the EIN project in order to inflate the value of Enron\xe2\x80\x99s stock and, ultimately, to enrich himself.[Footnote 2] Id., at 6.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Count 1 of the indictment described in some detail the alleged conspiracy to commit securities fraud and wire fraud and included as overt acts the substantive offenses charged in counts 2 through 6. Count 2, the securities fraud count, alleged that petitioner made false and misleading statements at the January 20, 2000, analyst conference or that he failed to state facts necessary to prevent statements made by others from being misleading. Counts 3 through 6 alleged that petitioner and others committed four acts of wire fraud when they issued four EBS-related press releases in 2000. Counts 27 through 46, the insider trading counts, alleged that petitioner made 20 separate sales of Enron stock \xe2\x80\x9cwhile in the possession of material non-public information regarding the technological capabilities, value, revenue and business performance of [EBS].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 31. And counts 67 through 165, the money laundering counts, described 99 financial transactions involving petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s use of the proceeds of his sales of Enron stock, which the indictment characterized as \xe2\x80\x9ccriminally derived property.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 37. To simplify our discussion, we shall refer to counts 1 through 6 as the \xe2\x80\x9cfraud counts\xe2\x80\x9d and the remaining counts as the \xe2\x80\x9cinsider trading counts.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial lasted 13 weeks. After four days of deliberations, the jury notified the court that it had reached agreement on some counts but had deadlocked on others. The judge then gave the jury an Allen charge, see Allen v. United States, 164 U. S. 492, 501\xe2\x80\x93502 (1896), urging the jurors to reexamine the grounds for their opinions and to continue deliberations \xe2\x80\x9cuntil the end of the day\xe2\x80\x9d to achieve a final verdict on all counts. Tr. 13724 (July 20, 2005). When the jury failed to break the deadlock, the court told the jurors that it would \xe2\x80\x9ctake their verdict\xe2\x80\x9d instead of prolonging deliberations. Id., at 13725. The jury acquitted petitioner on the fraud counts but failed to reach a verdict on the insider trading counts. The court entered judgment on the acquittals and declared a mistrial on the hung counts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On November 9, 2005, the Government obtained a new indictment against petitioner. This \xe2\x80\x9cEighth Superseding Indictment\xe2\x80\x9d recharged petitioner with some, but not all, of the insider trading counts on which the jury had previously hung. App. 188. The new indictment refined the Government\xe2\x80\x99s case: Whereas the earlier indictment had named multiple defendants, the new indictment dealt exclusively with petitioner. And instead of alleging facts implicating a broader fraudulent scheme, the new indictment focused on petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s knowledge of the EIN project and his failure to disclose that information to the public before selling his Enron stock.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner moved to dismiss all counts in the new indictment on the ground that the acquittals on the fraud counts precluded the Government from retrying him on the insider trading counts.[Footnote 3] He argued that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s acquittals had necessarily decided that he did not possess material, nonpublic information about the performance of the EIN project and its value to Enron. In petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s view, because reprosecution for insider trading would require the Government to prove that critical fact, the issue-preclusion component of the Double Jeopardy Clause barred a second trial of that issue and mandated dismissal of all of the insider trading counts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court denied the motion. After reviewing the trial record, the court disagreed with petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s reading of what the jury necessarily decided. In the court\xe2\x80\x99s telling, the jury likely concluded that petitioner \xe2\x80\x9cdid not knowingly and willfully participate in the scheme to defraud described in the conspiracy, securities fraud, and wire fraud counts.\xe2\x80\x9d 446 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 719, 735 (SD Tex. 2006). The court therefore concluded that the question whether petitioner possessed insider information was not necessarily resolved in the first trial and could be litigated anew in a second prosecution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals disagreed with the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of the record, but nevertheless affirmed. It reasoned that petitioner \xe2\x80\x9cdid not dispute\xe2\x80\x9d the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory that he \xe2\x80\x9chelped shape the message\xe2\x80\x9d of the allegedly fraudulent presentations made at the analyst conference, and therefore rejected the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the jury had \xe2\x80\x9cacquitted [petitioner] on the groun[d] that he did not participate in the fraud.\xe2\x80\x9d 521 F.\xc2\xa03d 367, 377 (CA5 2008). Based on its independent review of the record, the Court of Appeals instead concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury must have found when it acquitted [petitioner] that [he] did not have any insider information that contradicted what was presented to the public.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 378. The court acknowledged that this factual determination would normally preclude the Government from retrying petitioner for insider trading or money laundering.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court was nevertheless persuaded that a truly rational jury, having concluded that petitioner did not have any insider information, would have acquitted him on the insider trading counts. That the jury failed to acquit, and instead hung on those counts, was pivotal in the court\xe2\x80\x99s issue-preclusion analysis. Considering \xe2\x80\x9cthe hung counts along with the acquittals,\xe2\x80\x9d the court found it impossible \xe2\x80\x9cto decide with any certainty what the jury necessarily determined.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Relying on Circuit precedent, United States v. Larkin, 605 F.\xc2\xa02d 1360 (1979), the court concluded that the conflict between the acquittals and the hung counts barred the application of issue preclusion in this case. 521 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 378\xe2\x80\x93379.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Several courts have taken the contrary view and have held that a jury\xe2\x80\x99s failure to reach a verdict on some counts should play no role in determining the preclusive effect of an acquittal. See United States v. Ohayon, 483 F.\xc2\xa03d 1281 (CA11 2007); United States v. Romeo, 114 F.\xc2\xa03d 141 (CA9 1997); United States v. Bailin, 977 F.\xc2\xa02d 270 (CA7 1992); United States v. Frazier, 880 F.\xc2\xa02d 878 (CA6 1989). Others have sided with the Court of Appeals. See United States v. Howe, 538 F.\xc2\xa03d 820 (CA8 2008); United States v. Aguilar-Aranceta, 957 F.\xc2\xa02d 18 (CA1 1992); United States v. White, 936 F.\xc2\xa02d 1326 (CADC 1991). We granted certiorari to resolve the conflict, 555 U. S. ___ (2008), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Double Jeopardy Clause of the Fifth Amendment provides: \xe2\x80\x9c[N]or shall any person be subject for the same offence to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While we have decided an exceptionally large number of cases interpreting this provision, see, e.g., United States v. DiFrancesco, 449 U. S. 117, 126\xe2\x80\x93127 (1980) (collecting cases), most of our decisions have found more guidance in the common-law ancestry of the Clause than in its brief text. Thus, for example, while the risk of being fined or imprisoned implicates neither \xe2\x80\x9clife\xe2\x80\x9d nor \xe2\x80\x9climb,\xe2\x80\x9d our early cases held that double jeopardy protection extends to punishments that are not \xe2\x80\x9cpositively covered by the language of [the] amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte Lange, 18 Wall. 163, 170 (1874). As we explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is very clearly the spirit of the instrument to prevent a second punishment under judicial proceedings for the same crime, so far as the common law gave that protection.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our cases have recognized that the Clause embodies two vitally important interests. The first is the \xe2\x80\x9cdeeply ingrained\xe2\x80\x9d principle that \xe2\x80\x9cthe State with all its resources and power should not be allowed to make repeated attempts to convict an individual for an alleged offense, thereby subjecting him to embarrassment, expense and ordeal and compelling him to live in a continuing state of anxiety and insecurity, as well as enhancing the possibility that even though innocent he may be found guilty.\xe2\x80\x9d Green v. United States, 355 U. S. 184, 187\xe2\x80\x93188 (1957); see Benton v. Maryland, 395 U. S. 784, 795\xe2\x80\x93795 (1969); DiFrancesco, 449 U. S., at 127\xe2\x80\x93128. The second interest is the preservation of \xe2\x80\x9cthe finality of judgments.\xe2\x80\x9d Crist v. Bretz, 437 U. S. 28, 33 (1978).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first interest is implicated whenever the State seeks a second trial after its first attempt to obtain a conviction results in a mistrial because the jury has failed to reach a verdict. In these circumstances, however, while the defendant has an interest in avoiding multiple trials, the Clause does not prevent the Government from seeking to reprosecute. Despite the argument\xe2\x80\x99s textual appeal, we have held that the second trial does not place the defendant in jeopardy \xe2\x80\x9ctwice.\xe2\x80\x9d Richardson v. United States, 468 U. S. 317, 323 (1984); see 3 J. Story, Commentaries on the Constitution \xc2\xa71781, pp. 659\xe2\x80\x93660 (1833). Instead, a jury\xe2\x80\x99s inability to reach a decision is the kind of \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest necessity\xe2\x80\x9d that permits the declaration of a mistrial and the continuation of the initial jeopardy that commenced when the jury was first impaneled. See Arizona v. Washington, 434 U. S. 497, 505\xe2\x80\x93506 (1978); United States v. Perez, 9 Wheat. 579, 580 (1824). The \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in giving the prosecution one complete opportunity to convict those who have violated its laws\xe2\x80\x9d justifies treating the jury\xe2\x80\x99s inability to reach a verdict as a nonevent that does not bar retrial. Washington, 434 U. S., at 509.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 While the case before us involves a mistrial on the insider trading counts, the question presented cannot be resolved by asking whether the Government should be given one complete opportunity to convict petitioner on those charges. Rather, the case turns on the second interest at the core of the Clause. We must determine whether the interest in preserving the finality of the jury\xe2\x80\x99s judgment on the fraud counts, including the jury\xe2\x80\x99s finding that petitioner did not possess insider information, bars a retrial on the insider trading counts. This requires us to look beyond the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on being put in jeopardy \xe2\x80\x9ctwice\xe2\x80\x9d; the jury\xe2\x80\x99s acquittals unquestionably terminated petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s jeopardy with respect to the issues finally decided in those counts. The proper question, under the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s text, is whether it is appropriate to treat the insider trading charges as the \xe2\x80\x9csame offence\xe2\x80\x9d as the fraud charges. Our opinion in Ashe v. Swenson, 397 U. S. 436 (1970), provides the basis for our answer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Ashe, we squarely held that the Double Jeopardy Clause precludes the Government from relitigating any issue that was necessarily decided by a jury\xe2\x80\x99s acquittal in a prior trial. In that case, six poker players were robbed by a group of masked men. Ashe was charged with\xe2\x80\x94and acquitted of\xe2\x80\x94robbing Donald Knight, one of the six players. The State sought to retry Ashe for the robbery of another poker player only weeks after the first jury had acquitted him. The second prosecution was successful: Facing \xe2\x80\x9csubstantially stronger\xe2\x80\x9d testimony from \xe2\x80\x9cwitnesses [who] were for the most part the same,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 439\xe2\x80\x93440, Ashe was convicted and sentenced to a 35-year prison term. We concluded that the subsequent prosecution was constitutionally prohibited. Because the only contested issue at the first trial was whether Ashe was one of the robbers, we held that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal collaterally estopped the State from trying him for robbing a different player during the same criminal episode. Id., at 446. We explained that \xe2\x80\x9cwhen an issue of ultimate fact has once been determined by a valid and final judgment\xe2\x80\x9d of acquittal, it \xe2\x80\x9ccannot again be litigated\xe2\x80\x9d in a second trial for a separate offense. Id., at 443.[Footnote 4] To decipher what a jury has necessarily decided, we held that courts should \xe2\x80\x9cexamine the record of a prior proceeding, taking into account the pleadings, evidence, charge, and other relevant matter, and conclude whether a rational jury could have grounded its verdict upon an issue other than that which the defendant seeks to foreclose from consideration.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 444 (internal quotation marks omitted). We explained that the inquiry \xe2\x80\x9cmust be set in a practical frame and viewed with an eye to all the circumstances of the proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Sealfon v. United States, 332 U. S. 575, 579 (1948) (internal quotation marks\nomitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike Ashe, the case before us today entails a trial that included multiple counts rather than a trial for a single offense. And, while Ashe involved an acquittal for that single offense, this case involves an acquittal on some counts and a mistrial declared on others. The reasoning in Ashe is nevertheless controlling because, for double jeopardy purposes, the jury\xe2\x80\x99s inability to reach a verdict on the insider trading counts was a nonevent and the acquittals on the fraud counts are entitled to the same effect as Ashe\xe2\x80\x99s acquittal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As noted above, see supra, at 4, the Court of Appeals reasoned that the hung counts must be considered to determine what issues the jury decided in the first trial. Viewed in isolation, the court explained, the acquittals on the fraud charges would preclude retrial because they appeared to support petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the jury decided he lacked insider information. 521 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 378. Viewed alongside the hung counts, however, the acquittals appeared less decisive. The problem, as the court saw it, was that, if \xe2\x80\x9cthe jury found that [petitioner] did not have insider information, then the jury, acting rationally, would also have acquitted [him] of the insider trading counts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The fact that the jury hung was a logical wrinkle that made it impossible for the court \xe2\x80\x9cto decide with any certainty what the jury necessarily determined.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Because petitioner failed to show what the jury decided, id., at 380, the court refused to find the Government\nprecluded from pursuing the hung counts in a new\nprosecution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 issue-preclusion analysis was in error. A hung count is not a \xe2\x80\x9crelevant\xe2\x80\x9d part of the \xe2\x80\x9crecord of [the] prior proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d See Ashe, 397 U. S., at 444 (internal quotation marks omitted). Because a jury speaks only through its verdict, its failure to reach a verdict cannot\xe2\x80\x94by negative implication\xe2\x80\x94yield a piece of information that helps put together the trial puzzle. A mistried count is therefore nothing like the other forms of record material that Ashe suggested should be part of the preclusion inquiry. Ibid.; see also Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1301 (8th ed. 2004) (defining \xe2\x80\x9crecord\xe2\x80\x9d as the \xe2\x80\x9cofficial report of the proceedings in a case, including the filed papers, verbatim transcript of the trial or hearing (if any), and tangible exhibits\xe2\x80\x9d). Unlike the pleadings, the jury charge, or the evidence introduced by the parties, there is no way to decipher what a hung count represents. Even in the usual sense of \xe2\x80\x9crelevance,\xe2\x80\x9d a hung count hardly \xe2\x80\x9cmake[s] the existence of any fact \xe2\x80\xa6 more probable or less probable.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Evid. 401. A host of reasons\xe2\x80\x94sharp disagreement, confusion about the issues, exhaustion after a long trial, to name but a few\xe2\x80\x94could work alone or in tandem to cause a jury to hang.[Footnote 5] To ascribe meaning to a hung count would presume an ability to identify which factor was at play in the jury room. But that is not reasoned analysis; it is guesswork.[Footnote 6] Such conjecture about possible reasons for a jury\xe2\x80\x99s failure to reach a decision should play no part in assessing the legal consequences of a unanimous verdict that the jurors did return.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A contrary conclusion would require speculation into what transpired in the jury room. Courts properly avoid such explorations into the jury\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign space, see United States v. Powell, 469 U. S. 57, 66 (1984); Fed. Rule Evid. 606(b), and for good reason. The jury\xe2\x80\x99s deliberations are secret and not subject to outside examination. If there is to be an inquiry into what the jury decided, the \xe2\x80\x9cevidence should be confined to the points in controversy on the former trial, to the testimony given by the parties, and to the questions submitted to the jury for their consideration.\xe2\x80\x9d Packet Co. v. Sickles, 5 Wall. 580, 593 (1866); see also Vaise v. Delaval, 99 Eng. Rep. 944 (K. B. 1785) (Lord Mansfield, C.\xc2\xa0J.) (refusing to rely on juror affidavits to impeach a verdict reached by a coin flip); J. Wigmore, Evidence \xc2\xa72349, pp. 681\xe2\x80\x93690, and n.\xc2\xa02 (McNaughton rev. ed. 1961 and Supp. 1991).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, we hold that the consideration of hung counts has no place in the issue-preclusion analysis. Indeed, if it were relevant, the fact that petitioner has already survived one trial should be a factor cutting in favor of, rather than against, applying a double jeopardy bar. To identify what a jury necessarily determined at trial, courts should scrutinize a jury\xe2\x80\x99s decisions, not its failures to decide. A jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal represents the community\xe2\x80\x99s collective judgment regarding all the evidence and arguments presented to it. Even if the verdict is \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon an egregiously erroneous foundation,\xe2\x80\x9d Fong Foo v. United States, 369 U. S. 141, 143 (1962) (per curiam), its finality is unassailable. See, e.g., Washington, 434 U. S., at 503; Sanabria v. United States, 437 U. S. 54, 64 (1978). Thus, if the possession of insider information was a critical issue of ultimate fact in all of the charges against petitioner, a jury verdict that necessarily decided that issue in his favor protects him from prosecution for any charge for which that is an essential element.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government relies heavily on two of our cases, Richardson v. United States, 468 U. S. 317, and United States v. Powell, 469 U. S. 57, to argue that it is entitled to retry petitioner on the insider trading counts. Neither precedent can bear the weight the Government places on it.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Richardson, the defendant was indicted on three counts of narcotics violations. The jury acquitted him on one count but hung on the others. Richardson moved to bar retrial on the hung counts, insisting that reprosecution would place him twice in jeopardy for the same offense. Unlike petitioner in this case, Richardson did not argue that retrial was barred because the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal meant that it necessarily decided an essential fact in his favor. He simply asserted that the hung counts, standing alone, shielded him from reprosecution. We disagreed and held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe protection of the Double Jeopardy Clause by its terms applies only if there has been some event, such as an acquittal, which terminates the original jeopardy.\xe2\x80\x9d 468 U. S., at 325. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he failure of the jury to reach a verdict,\xe2\x80\x9d we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cis not an event which terminates jeopardy.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. From this the Government extrapolates the altogether different principle that retrial is always permitted whenever a jury convicts on some counts and hangs on others. Brief for United States 23\xe2\x80\x9324. But Richardson was not so broad. Rather, our conclusion was a rejection of the argument\xe2\x80\x94similar to the one the Government urges today\xe2\x80\x94that a mistrial is an event of significance. In so holding, we did not open the door to using a mistried count to ignore the preclusive effect of a jury\xe2\x80\x99s acquittal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government next contends that an acquittal can never preclude retrial on a mistried count because it would impute irrationality to the jury in violation of the rule articulated in Powell, 469 U. S. 57. In Powell, the defendant was charged with various drug offenses. The jury acquitted Powell of the substantive drug charges but convicted her of using a telephone in \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98committing and in causing and facilitating\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d those same offenses. Id., at 59\xe2\x80\x9360. Powell attacked the verdicts on appeal as irrationally inconsistent and urged the reversal of her convictions. She insisted that \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral estoppel should apply to verdicts rendered by a single jury, to preclude acceptance of a guilty verdict on a telephone facilitation count where the jury acquits the defendant of the predicate felony.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 64. We rejected this argument, reasoning that issue preclusion is \xe2\x80\x9cpredicated on the assumption that the jury acted rationally.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 68.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Arguing that a jury that acquits on some counts while inexplicably hanging on others is not rational, the Government contends that issue preclusion is as inappropriate in this case as it was in Powell. There are two serious flaws in this line of reasoning. First, it takes Powell\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of inconsistent verdicts and imports it into an entirely different context involving both verdicts and seemingly inconsistent hung counts. But the situations are quite dissimilar. In Powell, respect for the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdicts counseled giving each verdict full effect, however inconsistent. As we explained, the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict \xe2\x80\x9cbrings to the criminal process, in addition to the collective judgment of the community, an element of needed finality.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 67. By comparison, hung counts have never been accorded respect as a matter of law or history, and are not similar to jury verdicts in any relevant sense. By equating them, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument fails. Second, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on Powell assumes that a mistried count can, in context, be evidence of irrationality. But, as we explained above, see supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, the fact that a jury hangs is evidence of nothing\xe2\x80\x94other than, of course, that it has failed to decide anything. By relying on hung counts to question the basis of the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdicts, the Government violates the very assumption of rationality it invokes for support.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At bottom, the Government misreads our cases that have rejected attempts to question the validity of a jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict. In Powell and, before that, in Dunn, 284 U. S. 390, we were faced with jury verdicts that, on their face, were logically inconsistent and yet we refused to impugn the legitimacy of either verdict. In this case, there is merely a suggestion that the jury may have acted irrationally. And instead of resting that suggestion on a verdict, the Government relies on a hung count, the thinnest reed of all. If the Court in Powell and Dunn declined to use a clearly inconsistent verdict to second-guess the soundness of another verdict, then, a fortiori, a potentially inconsistent hung count could not command a different result.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One final matter requires discussion. The Government argues that even if we conclude (as we do) that acquittals can preclude retrial on counts on which the same jury hangs, we should nevertheless affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals because petitioner failed to show that the jury necessarily resolved in his favor an issue of ultimate fact that the Government must prove in order to convict him of insider trading and money laundering. See Brief for United States 41\xe2\x80\x9345. Given the length and complexity of the proceedings, this factual dispute is understandable. The District Court and Court of Appeals each read the record differently, disagreeing as to what the jury necessarily decided in its acquittals. Compare 446 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 735 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he jury necessarily determined that Defendant Yeager did not knowingly and willfully participate or agree to participate in a scheme to defraud in connection with the alleged false statements or material omissions made at the analyst conference and press releases\xe2\x80\x9d), with 521 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 378 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he jury must have found when it acquitted Yeager that Yeager himself did not have any insider information that contradicted what was presented to the public\xe2\x80\x9d). Our grant of certiorari was based on the assumption that the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of the record was correct. We recognize the Government\xe2\x80\x99s right, as the prevailing party in the Court of Appeals, to \xe2\x80\x9cdefend its judgment on any ground properly raised below whether or not that ground was relied upon, rejected, or even considered by the District Court or the Court of Appeals.\xe2\x80\x9d Washington v. Confederated Bands and Tribes of Yakima Nation, 439 U. S. 463, 476, n.\xc2\xa020 (1979). But we decline to engage in a fact-intensive analysis of the voluminous record, an undertaking unnecessary to the resolution of the narrow legal question we granted certiorari to answer. If it chooses, the Court of Appeals may revisit its factual analysis in light of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s arguments before this Court.

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment is reversed, and the case is remanded to the Court of Appeals for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\nIt is so ordered.

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

F. SCOTT YEAGER, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, with whom Justice Thomas and Justice Alito join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Double Jeopardy Clause of the Fifth Amendment provides that no person shall \xe2\x80\x9cbe subject for the same offence to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court today holds that this proscription, as interpreted in Ashe v. Swenson, 397 U. S. 436 (1970), sometimes bars retrial of hung counts if the jury acquits on factually related counts. Because that result neither accords with the original meaning of the Double Jeopardy Clause nor is required by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, I dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion begins with the proclamation that this Court has \xe2\x80\x9cfound more guidance in the common-law ancestry of the [Double Jeopardy] Clause than its brief text.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. Would that it were so. This case would be easy indeed if our cases had adhered to the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s original meaning. The English common-law pleas of auterfoits acquit and auterfoits convict, on which the Clause was based, barred only repeated \xe2\x80\x9cprosecution for the same identical act and crime.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 330 (1769) (emphasis added). See also Grady v. Corbin, 495 U. S. 508, 530\xe2\x80\x93535 (1990) (Scalia, J., dissenting). As described by Sir Matthew Hale, \xe2\x80\x9ca man acquitted for stealing [a] horse\xe2\x80\x9d could be later \xe2\x80\x9carraigned and convict[ed] for stealing the saddle, tho both were done at the same time.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 Pleas of the Crown 246 (1736). Under the common-law pleas, the jury\xe2\x80\x99s acquittal of Yeager on the fraud counts would have posed no bar to further prosecution for the distinct crimes of insider trading and money laundering.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But that is water over the dam. In Ashe the Court departed from the original meaning of the Double Jeopardy Clause, holding that it precludes successive prosecutions on distinct crimes when facts essential to conviction of the second crime have necessarily been resolved in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s favor by a verdict of acquittal of the first crime. 397 U. S., at 445\xe2\x80\x93446.[Footnote 1] Even if I am to adhere to Ashe on stare decisis grounds, cf. Grady, supra, at 528 (Scalia, J., dissenting), today\xe2\x80\x99s holding is an illogical extension of that case. Ashe held only that the Clause sometimes bars successive prosecution of facts found during \xe2\x80\x9ca prior proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d 397 U. S., at 444. But today the Court bars retrial on hung counts after what was not, under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s theory of \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing jeopardy,\xe2\x80\x9d Justices of Boston Municipal Court v. Lydon, 466 U. S. 294, 308 (1984), a prior proceeding but simply an earlier stage of the same proceeding.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As an historical matter, the common-law pleas could be invoked only once \xe2\x80\x9cthere ha[d] been a conviction or an acquittal\xe2\x80\x94after a complete trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Crist v. Bretz, 437 U. S. 28, 33 (1978). This Court has extended the protections of the Double Jeopardy Clause by holding that jeopardy attaches earlier: at the time a jury is empanelled and sworn. Id., at 38. Although one might think that this early attachment would mean that any second trial with a new jury would constitute a second jeopardy, the Court amended its innovation by holding that discharge of a deadlocked jury does not \xe2\x80\x9cterminat[e] the original jeopardy,\xe2\x80\x9d Richardson v. United States, 468 U. S. 317, 325 (1984). Under this continuing-jeopardy principle, retrial after a jury has failed to reach a verdict is not a new trial but part of the same proceeding.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s holding is inconsistent with this principle. It interprets the Double Jeopardy Clause, for the first time, to have effect internally within a single prosecution, even though the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98criminal proceedings against [the] accused have not run their full course.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lydon, supra, at 308 (quoting Price v. Georgia, 398 U. S. 323, 326 (1970)). As a conceptual matter, it makes no sense to say that events occurring within a single prosecution can cause an accused to be \xe2\x80\x9ctwice put in jeopardy.\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Amdt. 5. And our cases, until today, have acknowledged that. Ever since Dunn v. United States, 284 U. S. 390, 393 (1932), we have refused to set aside convictions that were inconsistent with acquittals in the same trial; and we made clear in United States v. Powell, 469 U. S. 57, 64\xe2\x80\x9365 (1984), that Ashe does not mandate a different result. There is no reason to treat perceived inconsistencies between hung counts and acquittals any differently.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Richardson accentuates the point. Under our cases, if an appellate court reverses a conviction for lack of constitutionally sufficient evidence, that determination constitutes an acquittal which, under the Double Jeopardy Clause, precludes further prosecution. Burks v. United States, 437 U. S. 1, 11 (1978). In Richardson, the defendant sought to prevent retrial after a jury failed to reach a verdict, claiming that the case should not have gone to the jury because the Government failed to present sufficient evidence. 468 U. S., at 322\xe2\x80\x93323. The Court held that the Double Jeopardy Clause was inapplicable because there had not been an \xe2\x80\x9cevent, such as an acquittal, which terminate[d] the original jeopardy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 325. I do not see why the Double Jeopardy Clause effect of a jury acquittal on a different count should be any different from the Double Jeopardy Clause effect of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s failure to present a case sufficient to go to the jury on the same count. In both cases, the predicate necessary for Double Jeopardy Clause preclusion of a new prosecution exists: in the former, the factual findings implicit in the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict of acquittal, in the latter, the State\xe2\x80\x99s presentation of a case so weak that it would have demanded a jury verdict of acquittal. In both cases, it seems to me, the Double Jeopardy Clause cannot be invoked because the jeopardy with respect to the retried count has not terminated.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The acquittals here did not, as the majority argues, \xe2\x80\x9cunquestionably terminat[e] [Yeager\xe2\x80\x99s] jeopardy with respect to the issues finally decided\xe2\x80\x9d in those counts. Ante, at 8 (emphasis added). Jeopardy is commenced and terminated charge by charge, not issue by issue. And if the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s failure to present sufficient evidence at a first trial cannot prevent retrial on a hung count because the retrial is considered part of the same proceeding, then there is no basis for invoking Ashe to prevent retrial in the present case. If a conviction can stand with a contradictory acquittal when both are pronounced at the same trial, there is no reason why an acquittal should prevent the State from pressing for a contradictory conviction in the continuation of the prosecution on the hung counts.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s extension of Ashe to these circumstances cannot even be justified based on the rationales underlying that holding. Invoking issue preclusion to bar seriatim prosecutions has the salutary effect of preventing the Government from circumventing acquittals by forcing defendants \xe2\x80\x9cto \xe2\x80\x98run the gantlet\xe2\x80\x99 a second time\xe2\x80\x9d on effectively the same charges. 397 U. S., at 446. In cases where the prosecution merely seeks to get \xe2\x80\x9cone full and fair opportunity to convict\xe2\x80\x9d on all charges brought in an initial indictment, Ohio v. Johnson, 467 U. S. 493, 502 (1984), there is no risk of such gamesmanship. We have said that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the State has made no effort to prosecute the charges seriatim, the considerations of double jeopardy protection implicit in the application of collateral estoppel are inapplicable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 500, n. 9.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, barring retrial when a jury acquits on some counts and hangs on others bears only a tenuous relationship to preserving the finality of \xe2\x80\x9can issue of ultimate fact [actually] determined by a valid and final judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashe, supra, at 443. There is no clear, unanimous jury finding here. In the unusual situation in which a factual finding upon which an acquittal must have been based would also logically require an acquittal on the hung count, all that can be said for certain is that the conflicting dispositions are irrational\xe2\x80\x94the result of \xe2\x80\x9cmistake, compromise, or lenity.\xe2\x80\x9d Powell, 469 U. S., at 65. It is at least as likely that the irrationality consisted of failing to make the factual finding necessary to support the acquittal as it is that the irrationality consisted of failing to adhere to that factual finding with respect to the hung count. While I agree that courts should avoid speculation as to why a jury reached a particular result, ante, at 11, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion steps in the wrong direction by pretending that the acquittals here mean something that they in all probability do not.[Footnote 3] Powell, supra, at 69, concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe best course to take is simply to insulate jury verdicts\xe2\x80\x9d from review on grounds of inconsistency. In my view the same conclusion applies to claims that inconsistency will arise from proceeding to conviction on hung counts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The burdens created by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion today are likely to be substantial. The Ashe inquiry will require courts to \xe2\x80\x9cexamine the record of a prior proceeding, taking into account the pleadings, evidence, charge, and other relevant matter, and conclude whether a rational jury could have grounded its verdict upon an issue other than that which the defendant seeks to foreclose from consideration.\xe2\x80\x9d 397 U. S., at 446 (internal quotation marks omitted). What is more, our holding in Abney v. United States, 431 U. S. 651 (1977), ensures that every defendant in Yeager\xe2\x80\x99s shoes will be entitled to an immediate interlocutory appeal (and petition for certiorari) whenever his Ashe claim is rejected by the trial court. Abney, supra, at 662.

\n

\n*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Until today, this Court has consistently held that retrial after a jury has been unable to reach a verdict is part of the original prosecution and that there can be no second jeopardy where there has been no second prosecution. Because I believe holding that line against this extension of Ashe is more consistent with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases and with the original meaning of the Double Jeopardy Clause, I would affirm the judgment.

\nFootnote 1

\xc2\xa0Because this case arises in federal court, the federal doctrine of issue preclusion might have prevented the Government from retrying Yeager even without Ashe\xe2\x80\x99s innovation. See United States v. Oppenheimer, 242 U. S. 85, 87 (1916). But the District Court held that the jury in this case had not necessarily decided that Yeager lacked inside information (the fact that Yeager claims the Government is barred from relitigating), 446 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 719, 735 (SD Tex. 2006), and jurisdiction for this interlocutory appeal of that holding comes by way of the collateral order doctrine, which encompasses claims of former jeopardy, Abney v. United States, 431 U. S. 651, 662 (1977). We have not accorded the same privilege to litigants asserting issue preclusion.

\nFootnote 2

\xc2\xa0That the Government issued a new indictment after the mistrial in this case does not alter the fact that, for double jeopardy purposes, retrial would have been part of the same, initial proceeding. As a matter of practice, it seems that prosecutors and courts treat retrials after mistrials as part of the same proceeding by filing superseding indictments under the original docket number. See, e.g., Superseding Information in United States v. Pena, Case No. 8:03\xe2\x80\x93cr\xe2\x80\x93476\xe2\x80\x93T\xe2\x80\x9323EAJ (MD Fla., Feb. 17, 2005). The Court implies that the new indictment in this case materially refined the charges, ante, at 4, but the only relevant changes were dropping of the other defendants and elimination of a few counts and related factual allegations. Compare App. 6\xe2\x80\x9371 with App. 188\xe2\x80\x93200.

\nFootnote 3

\xc2\xa0The Court claims that a jury\xe2\x80\x99s failure to reach a verdict is not relevant evidence, ante, at 10, but its justifications for that statement are utterly unpersuasive. It is obvious that a failure to reach a verdict on one count \xe2\x80\x9cmake[s] the existence\xe2\x80\x9d of a factual finding on a necessary predicate for both counts substantially \xe2\x80\x9cless probable,\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Evid. 401; how the Court can believe otherwise is beyond me.

\nYEAGER V. UNITED STATES
\n557 U. S. ____ (2009)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 08-67
\n
\n

F. SCOTT YEAGER, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[June 18, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito, with whom Justice Scalia and Justice Thomas join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s dissenting opinion. When a jury acquits on some counts but cannot reach agreement on others, I do not think that the Double Jeopardy Clause precludes retrial on the \xe2\x80\x9chung\xe2\x80\x9d counts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a result of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, however, the law is now to the contrary, and I write separately to note that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding makes it imperative that the doctrine of issue preclusion be applied with the rigor prescribed in Ashe v. Swenson, 397 U. S. 436 (1970). Loose application of the doctrine will lead to exceedingly complicated and protracted litigation, both in the trial court and on appeal, and may produce unjust results.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ashe made it clear that an acquittal on one charge precludes a subsequent trial on a different charge only if \xe2\x80\x9ca rational jury\xe2\x80\x9d could not have acquitted on the first charge without finding in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s favor on a factual issue that the prosecution would have to prove in order to convict in the later trial. Id., at 444. This is a demanding standard. The second trial is not precluded simply because it is unlikely\xe2\x80\x94or even very unlikely\xe2\x80\x94that the original jury acquitted without finding the fact in question. Only if it would have been irrational for the jury to acquit without finding that fact is the subsequent trial barred. And the defendant has the burden of showing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe issue whose relitigation he seeks to foreclose was actually decided in the first proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Dowling v. United States, 493 U. S. 342, 350 (1990).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The situation presented in a case like the one now before us\xe2\x80\x94where the jury acquits on some counts but cannot reach a verdict on others\xe2\x80\x94calls for special care in the application of the Ashe standard. In such a situation, the conclusion that the not-guilty verdicts preclude retrial on the hung counts necessarily means that the jury did not act rationally. But courts should begin with the presumption that a jury\xe2\x80\x99s actions can rationally be reconciled. In an analogous situation\xe2\x80\x94where it is claimed that a verdict must be set aside on the ground that the findings set out in a jury\xe2\x80\x99s answers to special interrogatories are inconsistent\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cit is the duty of the courts to attempt to harmonize the answers, if it is possible under a fair reading of them: \xe2\x80\x98Where there is a view of the case that makes the jury\xe2\x80\x99s answers to special interrogatories consistent, they must be resolved that way.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Gallick v. Baltimore & Ohio R. Co., 372 U. S. 108, 119 (1963) (quoting Atlantic & Gulf Stevedores, Inc. v. Ellerman Lines, Ltd., 369 U. S. 355, 364 (1962)). A similar approach is appropriate here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the present case, there is reason to question whether the Ashe standard was met. It is clear that the fraud counts required proof of an element not necessary for conviction on the insider trading charge, namely, that petitioner \xe2\x80\x9ccaused\xe2\x80\x9d material misstatements or omissions to be made at the January 20, 2000, analyst conference and in the press releases that formed the basis for the wire fraud counts. See App. 107 (jury instruction on count two (securities fraud)), 118 (jury instruction on counts three through six (wire fraud)). And it is far from apparent that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s not-guilty verdict on the fraud counts could not have rationally been based on a determination that this element\xe2\x80\x94that petitioner caused the material misstatements or omissions\xe2\x80\x94was not proved beyond a reasonable doubt.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court Judge, who was of course familiar with the trial evidence, analyzed this issue as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe theory of the defense, evident in closing argument and the direct testimony of Defendant Yeager, argued that Defendant Yeager did not participate in the crafting of the statements in the press releases; did not participate in the creation of slides or statements presented at the analysts conference; and did not reach an agreement with any other person to make false, misleading, or deceptive statements or material omissions of fact.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 55a.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The record provides support for the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis. In his summation, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s attorney argued that \xe2\x80\x9cScott Yeager had nothing to do with Counts 3 to 6 [the securities and wire fraud counts].\xe2\x80\x9d 80 Tr. 13384. With respect to the January 20, 2000, conference that provided the basis for the securities fraud count, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s attorney emphasized that his client \xe2\x80\x9cdidn\xe2\x80\x99t say anything.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13365. Counsel reiterated that petitioner \xe2\x80\x9cdidn\xe2\x80\x99t make a presentation. He didn\xe2\x80\x99t make a statement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; id., at 13394. Counsel\xe2\x80\x99s summation on this point summarized portions of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s trial testimony in which he minimized his involvement in matters relating to the conference. See 52 id., at 9932\xe2\x80\x939933, 9938\xe2\x80\x939947, and 9953.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With respect to the press releases on which the wire fraud counts were based, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s attorney argued: \xe2\x80\x9cScott Yeager had nothing to do with the press releases.\xe2\x80\x9d 80 id., at 13384. \xe2\x80\x9cWe didn\xe2\x80\x99t make any press releases.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13394. \xe2\x80\x9cShow me the evidence. Show me where Scott participated in a press release.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13406. Again, counsel\xe2\x80\x99s comments in summation tracked petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s testimony denying participation in the press releases. See 52 id., at 9911, 9913; 80 id., at 13384.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The above portions of the record suggest that a rational jury might have found that petitioner did not \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d the misstatements or omissions at the conference or in the press releases. In light of the length and complexity of the trial record, I am not in a position to say with certainty that the Ashe standard was not met in this case, but the brief discussion of this question in the opinion of the Court of Appeals does not satisfactorily show that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis was incorrect. Concluding that the not-guilty verdict on the securities fraud count could not have been based on a finding that respondent did not cause the misstatements or omissions at the conference, the Court of Appeals stated that petitioner \xe2\x80\x9cdid not dispute\xe2\x80\x9d that he \xe2\x80\x9chelped shape the message of the conference presentations.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 20a. But there is surely tension between that statement and the previously mentioned portions of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s trial testimony and the defense summation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the Court of Appeals held that Ashe does not apply when a jury acquits on some counts and hangs on others, that court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of the possible grounds for the jury\xe2\x80\x99s securities fraud verdict was not necessary to support the court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Now that this Court has held that Ashe does govern in this context, a reexamination of\nthe possible grounds for the fraud count acquittals is\nwarranted.
