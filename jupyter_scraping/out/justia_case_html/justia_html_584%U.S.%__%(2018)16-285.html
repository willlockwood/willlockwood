Epic Systems Corp. v. Lewis

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 16\xe2\x80\x93285.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 2, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 21, 2018[1]

\n

In each of these cases, an employer and employee entered into a contract providing for individualized arbitration proceedings to resolve employment disputes between the parties. Each employee nonetheless sought to litigate Fair Labor Standards Act and related state law claims through class or collective actions in federal court. Although the Federal Arbitration Act generally requires courts to enforce arbitration agreements as written, the employees argued that its \xe2\x80\x9csaving clause\xe2\x80\x9d removes this obligation if an arbitration agreement violates some other federal law and that, by requiring individualized proceedings, the agreements here violated the National Labor Relations Act. The employers countered that the Arbitration Act protects agreements requiring arbitration from judicial interference and that neither the saving clause nor the NLRA demands a different conclusion. Until recently, courts as well as the National Labor Relations Board\xe2\x80\x99s general counsel agreed that such arbitration agreements are enforceable. In 2012, however, the Board ruled that the NLRA effectively nullifies the Arbitration Act in cases like these, and since then other courts have either agreed with or deferred to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s position.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Congress has instructed in the Arbitration Act that arbitration agreements providing for individualized proceedings must be enforced, and neither the Arbitration Act\xe2\x80\x99s saving clause nor the NLRA suggests otherwise. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The Arbitration Act requires courts to enforce agreements to arbitrate, including the terms of arbitration the parties select. See 9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72, 3, 4. These emphatic directions would seem to resolve any argument here. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s saving clause\xe2\x80\x94which allows courts to refuse to enforce arbitration agreements \xe2\x80\x9cupon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x94recognizes only \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98generally applicable contract defenses, such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333, 339, not defenses targeting arbitration either by name or by more subtle methods, such as by \xe2\x80\x9cinterfer[ing] with fundamental attributes of arbitration,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 344. By challenging the agreements precisely because they require individualized arbitration instead of class or collective proceedings, the employees seek to interfere with one of these fundamental attributes. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The employees also mistakenly claim that, even if the Arbitration Act normally requires enforcement of arbitration agreements like theirs, the NLRA overrides that guidance and renders their agreements unlawful yet. When confronted with two Acts allegedly touching on the same topic, this Court must strive \xe2\x80\x9cto give effect to both.\xe2\x80\x9d Morton v. Mancari,\n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 535, 551. To prevail, the employees must show a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98clear and manifest\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d congressional intention to displace one Act with another. Ibid. There is a \xe2\x80\x9cstron[g] presum[ption]\xe2\x80\x9d that disfavors repeals by implication and that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress will specifically address\xe2\x80\x9d preexisting law before suspending the law\xe2\x80\x99s normal operations in a later statute. United States v. Fausto,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 439, 452, 453.

\n

The employees ask the Court to infer that class and collective actions are \xe2\x80\x9cconcerted activities\xe2\x80\x9d protected by \xc2\xa77 of the NLRA, which guarantees employees \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to self-organization, to form, join, or assist labor organizations, to bargain collectively .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , and to engage in other concerted activities for the purpose of collective bargaining or other mutual aid or protection,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7157. But \xc2\xa77 focuses on the right to organize unions and bargain collectively. It does not mention class or collective action procedures or even hint at a clear and manifest wish to displace the Arbitration Act. It is unlikely that Congress wished to confer a right to class or collective actions in \xc2\xa77, since those procedures were hardly known when the NLRA was adopted in 1935. Because the catchall term \xe2\x80\x9cother concerted activities for the purpose of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. other mutual aid or protection\xe2\x80\x9d appears at the end of a detailed list of activities, it should be understood to protect the same kind of things, i.e., things employees do for themselves in the course of exercising their right to free association in the workplace.

\n

The NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s structure points to the same conclusion. After speaking of various \xe2\x80\x9cconcerted activities\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa77, the statute establishes a detailed regulatory regime applicable to each item on the list, but gives no hint about what rules should govern the adjudication of class or collective actions in court or arbitration. Nor is it at all obvious what rules should govern on such essential issues as opt-out and opt-in procedures, notice to class members, and class certification standards. Telling too is the fact that Congress has shown that it knows exactly how to specify certain dispute resolution procedures, cf., e.g., 29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7216(b), 626, or to override the Arbitration Act, see, e.g., \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71226(a)(2), but Congress has done nothing like that in the NLRA.

\n

The employees suggest that the NLRA does not discuss class and collective action procedures because it means to confer a right to use existing procedures provided by statute or rule, but the NLRA does not say even that much. And if employees do take existing rules as they find them, they must take them subject to those rules\xe2\x80\x99 inherent limitations, including the principle that parties may depart from them in favor of individualized arbitration.

\n

In another contextual clue, the employees\xe2\x80\x99 underlying causes of action arise not under the NLRA but under the Fair Labor Standards Act, which permits the sort of collective action the employees wish to pursue here. Yet they do not suggest that the FLSA displaces the Arbitration Act, presumably because the Court has held that an identical collective action scheme does not prohibit individualized arbitration proceedings, see Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp.,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 20, 32. The employees\xe2\x80\x99 theory also runs afoul of the rule that Congress \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not alter the fundamental details of a regulatory scheme in vague terms or ancillary provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc.,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 457, 468, as it would allow a catchall term in the NLRA to dictate the particulars of dispute resolution procedures in Article III courts or arbitration proceedings\xe2\x80\x94matters that are usually left to, e.g., the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, the Arbitration Act, and the FLSA. Nor does the employees\xe2\x80\x99 invocation of the Norris-LaGuardia Act, a predecessor of the NLRA, help their argument. That statute declares unenforceable contracts in conflict with its policy of protecting workers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cconcerted activities for the purpose of collective bargaining or other mutual aid or protection,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7102, and just as under the NLRA, that policy does not conflict with Congress\xe2\x80\x99s directions favoring arbitration.

\n

Precedent confirms the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading. The Court has rejected many efforts to manufacture conflicts between the Arbitration Act and other federal statutes, see, e.g. American Express Co. v. Italian Colors Restaurant,\n\n570 U.\xc2\xa0S. 228; and its \xc2\xa77 cases have generally involved efforts related to organizing and collective bargaining in the workplace, not the treatment of class or collective action procedures in court or arbitration, see, e.g., NLRB v. Washington Aluminum Co.,\n\n370 U.\xc2\xa0S. 9.

\n

Finally, the employees cannot expect deference under Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837, because Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s essential premises are missing. The Board sought not to interpret just the NLRA, \xe2\x80\x9cwhich it administers,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 842, but to interpret that statute in a way that limits the work of the Arbitration Act, which the agency does not administer. The Board and the Solicitor General also dispute the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s meaning, articulating no single position on which the Executive Branch might be held \xe2\x80\x9caccountable to the people.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 865. And after \xe2\x80\x9cemploying traditional tools of statutory construction,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 843, n.\xc2\xa09, including the canon against reading conflicts into statutes, there is no unresolved ambiguity for the Board to address. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9321.

Justice Gorsuch delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Should employees and employers be allowed to agree that any disputes between them will be resolved through one-on-one arbitration? Or should employees always be permitted to bring their claims in class or collective actions, no matter what they agreed with their employers?

\n

As a matter of policy these questions are surely debat- able. But as a matter of law the answer is clear. In the Federal Arbitration Act, Congress has instructed federal courts to enforce arbitration agreements according to their terms\xe2\x80\x94including terms providing for individualized proceedings. Nor can we agree with the employees\xe2\x80\x99 suggestion that the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) offers a conflicting command. It is this Court\xe2\x80\x99s duty to interpret Congress\xe2\x80\x99s statutes as a harmonious whole rather than at war with one another. And abiding that duty here leads to an unmistakable conclusion. The NLRA secures to employees rights to organize unions and bargain collectively, but it says nothing about how judges and arbitrators must try legal disputes that leave the workplace and enter the courtroom or arbitral forum. This Court has never read a right to class actions into the NLRA\xe2\x80\x94and for three quarters of a century neither did the National Labor Relations Board. Far from conflicting, the Arbitration Act and the NLRA have long enjoyed separate spheres of influence and neither permits this Court to declare the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreements unlawful.

\n

I

\n

The three cases before us differ in detail but not in substance. Take Ernst & Young LLP v. Morris. There Ernst & Young and one of its junior accountants, Stephen Morris, entered into an agreement providing that they would arbitrate any disputes that might arise between them. The agreement stated that the employee could choose the arbitration provider and that the arbitrator could \xe2\x80\x9cgrant any relief that could be granted by .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a court\xe2\x80\x9d in the relevant jurisdiction. App. in No. 16\xe2\x80\x93300, p.\xc2\xa043. The agreement also specified individualized arbitration, with claims \xe2\x80\x9cpertaining to different [e]mployees [to] be heard in separate proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 44.

\n

After his employment ended, and despite having agreed to arbitrate claims against the firm, Mr. Morris sued Ernst & Young in federal court. He alleged that the firm had misclassified its junior accountants as professional employees and violated the federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) and California law by paying them salaries without overtime pay. Although the arbitration agreement provided for individualized proceedings, Mr. Morris sought to litigate the federal claim on behalf of a nationwide class under the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s collective action provision,\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7216(b). He sought to pursue the state law claim as a class action under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23.

\n

Ernst & Young replied with a motion to compel arbitration. The district court granted the request, but the Ninth Circuit reversed this judgment. 834 F.\xc2\xa03d 975 (2016). The Ninth Circuit recognized that the Arbitration Act gener- ally requires courts to enforce arbitration agreements as written. But the court reasoned that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csaving clause,\xe2\x80\x9d see\n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72, removes this obligation if an arbitration agreement violates some other federal law. And the court concluded that an agreement requiring individualized arbitration proceedings violates the NLRA by barring employees from engaging in the \xe2\x80\x9cconcerted activit[y],\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7157, of pursuing claims as a class or collective action.

\n

Judge Ikuta dissented. In her view, the Arbitration Act protected the arbitration agreement from judicial interference and nothing in the Act\xe2\x80\x99s saving clause suggested otherwise. Neither, she concluded, did the NLRA demand a different result. Rather, that statute focuses on protecting unionization and collective bargaining in the workplace, not on guaranteeing class or collective action procedures in disputes before judges or arbitrators.

\n

Although the Arbitration Act and the NLRA have long coexisted\xe2\x80\x94they date from 1925 and 1935, respectively\xe2\x80\x94the suggestion they might conflict is something quite new. Until a couple of years ago, courts more or less agreed that arbitration agreements like those before us must be enforced according to their terms. See, e.g., Owen v. Bristol Care, Inc., 702 F.\xc2\xa03d 1050 (CA8 2013); Sutherland v. Ernst & Young LLP, 726 F.\xc2\xa03d 290 (CA2 2013); D.\xc2\xa0R. Horton, Inc. v. NLRB, 737 F.\xc2\xa03d 344 (CA5 2013); Iskanian v. CLS Transp. Los Angeles, LLC, 59 Cal. 4th 348, 327 P.\xc2\xa03d 129 (2014); Tallman v. Eighth Jud. Dist. Court, 131 Nev. 71, 359 P.\xc2\xa03d 113 (2015); 808 F.\xc2\xa03d 1013 (CA5 2015) (case below in No. 16\xe2\x80\x93307).

\n

The National Labor Relations Board\xe2\x80\x99s general counsel expressed much the same view in 2010. Remarking that employees and employers \xe2\x80\x9ccan benefit from the relative simplicity and informality of resolving claims before arbitrators,\xe2\x80\x9d the general counsel opined that the validity of such agreements \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not involve consideration of the policies of the National Labor Relations Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Memorandum GC 10\xe2\x80\x9306, pp.\xc2\xa02, 5 (June 16, 2010).

\n

But recently things have shifted. In 2012, the Board\xe2\x80\x94for the first time in the 77 years since the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s adoption\xe2\x80\x94asserted that the NLRA effectively nullifies the Arbitration Act in cases like ours. D.\xc2\xa0R. Horton, Inc., 357 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 2277. Initially, this agency decision received a cool reception in court. See D.\xc2\xa0R. Horton, 737 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 355\xe2\x80\x93362. In the last two years, though, some circuits have either agreed with the Board\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion or thought themselves obliged to defer to it under Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984). See 823 F.\xc2\xa03d 1147 (CA7 2016) (case below in No. 16\xe2\x80\x93285); 834 F.\xc2\xa03d 975 (case below in No. 16\xe2\x80\x93300); NLRB v. Alternative Entertainment, Inc., 858 F.\xc2\xa03d 393 (CA6 2017). More recently still, the disagreement has grown as the Executive has disavowed the Board\xe2\x80\x99s (most recent) position, and the Solicitor General and the Board have offered us battling briefs about the law\xe2\x80\x99s meaning. We granted certiorari to clear the confusion. 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2017).

\n

II

\n

We begin with the Arbitration Act and the question of its saving clause.

\n

Congress adopted the Arbitration Act in 1925 in response to a perception that courts were unduly hostile to arbitration. No doubt there was much to that perception. Before 1925, English and American common law courts routinely refused to enforce agreements to arbitrate disputes. Scherk v. Alberto-Culver Co.,\n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 506, 510, n.\xc2\xa04 (1974). But in Congress\xe2\x80\x99s judgment arbitration had more to offer than courts recognized\xe2\x80\x94not least the promise of quicker, more informal, and often cheaper resolutions for everyone involved. Id., at 511. So Congress directed courts to abandon their hostility and instead treat arbitration agreements as \xe2\x80\x9cvalid, irrevocable, and enforceable.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72. The Act, this Court has said, establishes \xe2\x80\x9ca liberal federal policy favoring arbitration agreements.\xe2\x80\x9d Moses H. Cone Memorial Hospital v. Mercury Constr. Corp.,\n\n460 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, 24 (1983) (citing Prima Paint Corp. v. Flood & Conklin Mfg. Co.,\n\n388 U.\xc2\xa0S. 395 (1967)); see id., at 404 (discussing \xe2\x80\x9cthe plain meaning of the statute\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe unmistakably clear congressional purpose that the arbitration procedure, when selected by the parties to a contract, be speedy and not subject to delay and obstruction in the courts\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Not only did Congress require courts to respect and enforce agreements to arbitrate; it also specifically directed them to respect and enforce the parties\xe2\x80\x99 chosen arbitration procedures. See \xc2\xa73 (providing for a stay of litigation pending arbitration \xe2\x80\x9cin accordance with the terms of the agreement\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa74 (providing for \xe2\x80\x9can order directing that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. arbitration proceed in the manner provided for in such agreement\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, we have often observed that the Arbitration Act requires courts \xe2\x80\x9crigorously\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cenforce arbitration agreements according to their terms, including terms that specify with whom the parties choose to arbitrate their disputes and the rules under which that arbitration will be conducted.\xe2\x80\x9d American Express Co. v. Italian Colors Restaurant,\n\n570 U.\xc2\xa0S. 228, 233 (2013) (some emphasis added; citations, internal quotation marks, and brackets omitted).

\n

On first blush, these emphatic directions would seem to resolve any argument under the Arbitration Act. The parties before us contracted for arbitration. They pro- ceeded to specify the rules that would govern their arbitrations, indicating their intention to use individualized rather than class or collective action procedures. And this much the Arbitration Act seems to protect pretty absolutely. See AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333 (2011); Italian Colors, supra; DIRECTV, Inc. v. Imburgia, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015). You might wonder if the balance Congress struck in 1925 between arbitration and litigation should be revisited in light of more contemporary developments. You might even ask if the Act was good policy when enacted. But all the same you might find it difficult to see how to avoid the statute\xe2\x80\x99s application.

\n

Still, the employees suggest the Arbitration Act\xe2\x80\x99s saving clause creates an exception for cases like theirs. By its terms, the saving clause allows courts to refuse to enforce arbitration agreements \xe2\x80\x9cupon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72. That provision applies here, the employees tell us, because the NLRA renders their particular class and collective action waivers illegal. In their view, illegality under the NLRA is a \xe2\x80\x9cground\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cexists at law .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for the revocation\xe2\x80\x9d of their arbitration agreements, at least to the extent those agreements prohibit class or collective action proceedings.

\n

The problem with this line of argument is fundamental. Put to the side the question whether the saving clause was designed to save not only state law defenses but also defenses allegedly arising from federal statutes. See 834 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 991\xe2\x80\x93992, 997 (Ikuta, J., dissenting). Put to the side the question of what it takes to qualify as a ground for \xe2\x80\x9crevocation\xe2\x80\x9d of a contract. See Concepcion, supra, at 352\xe2\x80\x93355 (Thomas, J., concurring); post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (Thomas, J., concurring). Put to the side for the moment, too, even the question whether the NLRA actually renders class and collective action waivers illegal. Assuming (but not granting) the employees could satisfactorily answer all those questions, the saving clause still can\xe2\x80\x99t save their cause.

\n

It can\xe2\x80\x99t because the saving clause recognizes only defenses that apply to \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d contract. In this way the clause establishes a sort of \xe2\x80\x9cequal-treatment\xe2\x80\x9d rule for arbitration contracts. Kindred Nursing Centers L. P. v. Clark, 581 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2017) (slip op., at 4). The clause \xe2\x80\x9cpermits agreements to arbitrate to be invalidated by \xe2\x80\x98generally applicable contract defenses, such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Concepcion, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 339. At the same time, the clause offers no refuge for \xe2\x80\x9cdefenses that apply only to arbitration or that derive their meaning from the fact that an agreement to arbitrate is at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Under our precedent, this means the saving clause does not save defenses that target arbitration either by name or by more subtle methods, such as by \xe2\x80\x9cinterfer[ing] with fundamental attributes of arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 344; see Kindred Nursing, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 5).

\n

This is where the employees\xe2\x80\x99 argument stumbles. They don\xe2\x80\x99t suggest that their arbitration agreements were extracted, say, by an act of fraud or duress or in some other unconscionable way that would render any contract unenforceable. Instead, they object to their agreements precisely because they require individualized arbitration proceedings instead of class or collective ones. And by attacking (only) the individualized nature of the arbitration proceedings, the employees\xe2\x80\x99 argument seeks to interfere with one of arbitration\xe2\x80\x99s fundamental attributes.

\n

We know this much because of Concepcion. There this Court faced a state law defense that prohibited as unconscionable class action waivers in consumer contracts. The Court readily acknowledged that the defense formally applied in both the litigation and the arbitration context. 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 338, 341. But, the Court held, the defense failed to qualify for protection under the saving clause because it interfered with a fundamental attribute of arbitration all the same. It did so by effectively permitting any party in arbitration to demand classwide proceedings despite the traditionally individualized and informal nature of arbitration. This \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental\xe2\x80\x9d change to the traditional arbitration process, the Court said, would \xe2\x80\x9csacrific[e] the principal advantage of arbitration\xe2\x80\x94its informality\xe2\x80\x94and mak[e] the process slower, more costly, and more likely to generate procedural morass than final judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 347, 348. Not least, Concepcion noted, arbitrators would have to decide whether the named class representatives are sufficiently representative and typical of the class; what kind of notice, opportunity to be heard, and right to opt out absent class members should enjoy; and how discovery should be altered in light of the classwide nature of the proceedings. Ibid. All of which would take much time and effort, and introduce new risks and costs for both sides. Ibid. In the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, the virtues Congress originally saw in arbitration, its speed and simplicity and inexpensiveness, would be shorn away and arbitration would wind up looking like the litigation it was meant to displace.

\n

Of course, Concepcion has its limits. The Court recognized that parties remain free to alter arbitration procedures to suit their tastes, and in recent years some parties have sometimes chosen to arbitrate on a classwide basis. Id., at 351. But Concepcion\xe2\x80\x99s essential insight remains: courts may not allow a contract defense to reshape traditional individualized arbitration by mandating classwide arbitration procedures without the parties\xe2\x80\x99 consent. Id., at 344\xe2\x80\x93351; see also Stolt-Nielsen S.\xc2\xa0A. v. AnimalFeeds Int\xe2\x80\x99l Corp.,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 662, 684\xe2\x80\x93687 (2010). Just as judicial antagonism toward arbitration before the Arbitration Act\xe2\x80\x99s enactment \xe2\x80\x9cmanifested itself in a great variety of devices and formulas declaring arbitration against public policy,\xe2\x80\x9d Concepcion teaches that we must be alert to new devices and formulas that would achieve much the same result today. 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 342 (internal quotation marks omitted). And a rule seeking to declare individualized arbitration proceedings off limits is, the Court held, just such a device.

\n

The employees\xe2\x80\x99 efforts to distinguish Concepcion fall short. They note that their putative NLRA defense would render an agreement \xe2\x80\x9cillegal\xe2\x80\x9d as a matter of federal statutory law rather than \xe2\x80\x9cunconscionable\xe2\x80\x9d as a matter of state common law. But we don\xe2\x80\x99t see how that distinction makes any difference in light of Concepion\xe2\x80\x99s rationale and rule. Illegality, like unconscionability, may be a traditional, generally applicable contract defense in many cases, including arbitration cases. But an argument that a contract is unenforceable just because it requires bilateral arbitration is a different creature. A defense of that kind, Concepcion tells us, is one that impermissibly disfavors arbitration whether it sounds in illegality or unconscion- ability. The law of precedent teaches that like cases should generally be treated alike, and appropriate respect for that principle means the Arbitration Act\xe2\x80\x99s saving clause can no more save the defense at issue in these cases than it did the defense at issue in Concepcion. At the end of our encounter with the Arbitration Act, then, it appears just as it did at the beginning: a congressional command requiring us to enforce, not override, the terms of the arbitration agreements before us.

\n

III

\n

But that\xe2\x80\x99s not the end of it. Even if the Arbitration Act normally requires us to enforce arbitration agreements like theirs, the employees reply that the NLRA overrides that guidance in these cases and commands us to hold their agreements unlawful yet.

\n

This argument faces a stout uphill climb. When confronted with two Acts of Congress allegedly touching on the same topic, this Court is not at \xe2\x80\x9cliberty to pick and choose among congressional enactments\xe2\x80\x9d and must instead strive \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to give effect to both.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Morton v. Mancari,\n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 535, 551 (1974). A party seeking to suggest that two statutes cannot be harmonized, and that one displaces the other, bears the heavy burden of showing \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a clearly expressed congressional intention\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that such a result should follow. Vimar Seguros y Reaseguros, S. A. v. M/V Sky Reefer,\n\n515 U.\xc2\xa0S. 528, 533 (1995). The intention must be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98clear and manifest.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Morton, supra, at 551. And in approaching a claimed conflict, we come armed with the \xe2\x80\x9cstron[g] presum[ption]\xe2\x80\x9d that repeals by implication are \xe2\x80\x9cdisfavored\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress will specifically address\xe2\x80\x9d preexisting law when it wishes to suspend its normal operations in a later statute. United States v. Fausto,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 439, 452, 453 (1988).

\n

These rules exist for good reasons. Respect for Congress as drafter counsels against too easily finding irreconcilable conflicts in its work. More than that, respect for the separation of powers counsels restraint. Allowing judges to pick and choose between statutes risks transforming them from expounders of what the law is into policymakers choosing what the law should be. Our rules aiming for harmony over conflict in statutory interpretation grow from an appreciation that it\xe2\x80\x99s the job of Congress by legislation, not this Court by supposition, both to write the laws and to repeal them.

\n

Seeking to demonstrate an irreconcilable statutory conflict even in light of these demanding standards, the employees point to Section 7 of the NLRA. That provision guarantees workers

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cthe right to self-organization, to form, join, or assist labor organizations, to bargain collectively through representatives of their own choosing, and to engage in other concerted activities for the purpose of collective bargaining or other mutual aid or protection.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7157.

\n

From this language, the employees ask us to infer a clear and manifest congressional command to displace the Arbitration Act and outlaw agreements like theirs.

\n

But that much inference is more than this Court may make. Section 7 focuses on the right to organize unions and bargain collectively. It may permit unions to bargain to prohibit arbitration. Cf. 14 Penn Plaza LLC v. Pyett,\n\n556 U.\xc2\xa0S. 247, 256\xe2\x80\x93260 (2009). But it does not express approval or disapproval of arbitration. It does not mention class or collective action procedures. It does not even hint at a wish to displace the Arbitration Act\xe2\x80\x94let alone accomplish that much clearly and manifestly, as our precedents demand.

\n

Neither should any of this come as a surprise. The notion that Section 7 confers a right to class or collective actions seems pretty unlikely when you recall that procedures like that were hardly known when the NLRA was adopted in 1935. Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23 didn\xe2\x80\x99t create the modern class action until 1966; class arbitration didn\xe2\x80\x99t emerge until later still; and even the Fair Labor Standards Act\xe2\x80\x99s collective action provision postdated Section 7 by years. See Rule 23\xe2\x80\x93Class Actions, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p.\xc2\xa01258 (1964 ed., Supp. II);\n\n52Stat.\n1069; Concepcion, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 349; see also Califano v. Yamasaki,\n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 682, 700\xe2\x80\x93701 (1979) (noting that the \xe2\x80\x9cusual rule\xe2\x80\x9d then was litigation \xe2\x80\x9cconducted by and on behalf of individual named parties only\xe2\x80\x9d). And while some forms of group litigation existed even in 1935, see 823 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1154, Section 7\xe2\x80\x99s failure to mention them only reinforces that the statute doesn\xe2\x80\x99t speak to such procedures.

\n

A close look at the employees\xe2\x80\x99 best evidence of a potential conflict turns out to reveal no conflict at all. The employees direct our attention to the term \xe2\x80\x9cother con- certed activities for the purpose of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. other mutual aid or protection.\xe2\x80\x9d This catchall term, they say, can be read to include class and collective legal actions. But the term appears at the end of a detailed list of activities speaking of \xe2\x80\x9cself-organization,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cform[ing], join[ing], or assist[ing] labor organizations,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cbargain[ing] collectively.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7157. And where, as here, a more general term follows more specific terms in a list, the general term is usually understood to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98embrace only objects similar in nature to those objects enumerated by the preceding specific words.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Circuit City Stores, Inc. v. Adams,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 105, 115 (2001) (discussing ejusdem generis canon); National Assn. of Mfrs. v. Department of Defense, 583 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2018) (slip op., at 10). All of which suggests that the term \xe2\x80\x9cother concerted activities\xe2\x80\x9d should, like the terms that precede it, serve to protect things employees \xe2\x80\x9cjust do\xe2\x80\x9d for themselves in the course of exercising their right to free association in the workplace, rather than \xe2\x80\x9cthe highly regulated, courtroom-bound \xe2\x80\x98activities\xe2\x80\x99 of class and joint litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Alternative Entertainment, 858 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 414\xe2\x80\x93415 (Sutton, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (emphasis deleted). None of the preceding and more specific terms speaks to the procedures judges or arbitrators must apply in disputes that leave the workplace and enter the courtroom or arbitral forum, and there is no textually sound reason to suppose the final catchall term should bear such a radically different object than all its predecessors.

\n

The NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s broader structure underscores the point. After speaking of various \xe2\x80\x9cconcerted activities\xe2\x80\x9d in Section 7, Congress proceeded to establish a regulatory regime applicable to each of them. The NLRA provides rules for the recognition of exclusive bargaining representatives,\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7159, explains employees\xe2\x80\x99 and employers\xe2\x80\x99 obligation to bargain collectively, \xc2\xa7158(d), and conscribes certain labor organization practices, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7158(a)(3), (b). The NLRA also touches on other concerted activities closely related to organization and collective bargaining, such as picketing, \xc2\xa7158(b)(7), and strikes, \xc2\xa7163. It even sets rules for adjudicatory proceedings under the NLRA itself. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7160, 161. Many of these provisions were part of the original NLRA in 1935, see\n\n49Stat.\n449, while others were added later. But missing entirely from this careful regime is any hint about what rules should govern the adjudication of class or collective actions in court or arbitration. Without some comparably specific guidance, it\xe2\x80\x99s not at all obvious what procedures Section 7 might protect. Would opt-out class action procedures suffice? Or would opt-in procedures be necessary? What notice might be owed to absent class members? What standards would govern class certification? Should the same rules always apply or should they vary based on the nature of the suit? Nothing in the NLRA even whispers to us on any of these essential questions. And it is hard to fathom why Congress would take such care to regulate all the other matters mentioned in Section 7 yet remain mute about this matter alone\xe2\x80\x94unless, of course, Section 7 doesn\xe2\x80\x99t speak to class and collective action procedures in the first place.

\n

Telling, too, is the fact that when Congress wants to mandate particular dispute resolution procedures it knows exactly how to do so. Congress has spoken often and clearly to the procedures for resolving \xe2\x80\x9cactions,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cclaims,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccharges,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccases\xe2\x80\x9d in statute after statute. E.g., 29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7216(b), 626; 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(b), (f)(3)\xe2\x80\x93(5). Congress has likewise shown that it knows how to override the Arbitration Act when it wishes\xe2\x80\x94by explaining, for example, that, \xe2\x80\x9c[n]otwithstanding any other provision of law, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. arbitration may be used .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. only if\xe2\x80\x9d certain conditions are met,\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71226(a)(2); or that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o predispute arbitration agreement shall be valid or enforceable\xe2\x80\x9d in other circumstances,\n\n7 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa726(n)(2);\n\n12 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa75567(d)(2); or that requiring a party to arbitrate is \xe2\x80\x9cunlawful\xe2\x80\x9d in other circumstances yet,\n\n10 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7987(e)(3). The fact that we have nothing like that here is further evidence that Section 7 does nothing to address the question of class and collective actions.

\n

In response, the employees offer this slight reply. They suggest that the NLRA doesn\xe2\x80\x99t discuss any particular class and collective action procedures because it merely confers a right to use existing procedures provided by statute or rule, \xe2\x80\x9con the same terms as [they are] made available to everyone else.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent in No. 16\xe2\x80\x93285, p.\xc2\xa053, n.\xc2\xa010. But of course the NLRA doesn\xe2\x80\x99t say even that much. And, besides, if the parties really take existing class and collective action rules as they find them, they surely take them subject to the limitations inherent in those rules\xe2\x80\x94including the principle that parties may (as here) contract to depart from them in favor of individualized arbitration procedures of their own design.

\n

Still another contextual clue yields the same message. The employees\xe2\x80\x99 underlying causes of action involve their wages and arise not under the NLRA but under an en- tirely different statute, the Fair Labor Standards Act. The FLSA allows employees to sue on behalf of \xe2\x80\x9cthemselves and other employees similarly situated,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7216(b), and it\xe2\x80\x99s precisely this sort of collective action the employees before us wish to pursue. Yet they do not offer the seemingly more natural suggestion that the FLSA overcomes the Arbitration Act to permit their class and collective actions. Why not? Presumably because this Court held decades ago that an identical collective action scheme (in fact, one borrowed from the FLSA) does not displace the Arbitration Act or prohibit individualized arbitration proceedings. Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp.,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 20, 32 (1991) (discussing Age Discrimination in Employment Act). In fact, it turns out that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]very circuit to consider the question\xe2\x80\x9d has held that the FLSA allows agreements for individualized arbitration. Alternative Entertainment, 858 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 413 (opinion of Sutton, J.) (collecting cases). Faced with that obstacle, the employees are left to cast about elsewhere for help. And so they have cast in this direction, suggesting that one statute (the NLRA) steps in to dictate the procedures for claims under a different statute (the FLSA), and thereby overrides the commands of yet a third statute (the Arbitration Act). It\xe2\x80\x99s a sort of interpretive triple bank shot, and just stating the theory is enough to raise a judicial eyebrow.

\n

Perhaps worse still, the employees\xe2\x80\x99 theory runs afoul of the usual rule that Congress \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not alter the fundamental details of a regulatory scheme in vague terms or ancillary provisions\xe2\x80\x94it does not, one might say, hide elephants in mouseholes.\xe2\x80\x9d Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc.,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 457, 468 (2001). Union organization and collective bargaining in the workplace are the bread and butter of the NLRA, while the particulars of dispute resolution procedures in Article III courts or arbitration proceedings are usually left to other statutes and rules\xe2\x80\x94not least the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, the Arbitration Act, and the FLSA. It\xe2\x80\x99s more than a little doubtful that Congress would have tucked into the mousehole of Section 7\xe2\x80\x99s catchall term an elephant that tramples the work done by these other laws; flattens the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contracted-for dispute resolution procedures; and seats the Board as supreme superintendent of claims arising under a statute it doesn\xe2\x80\x99t even administer.

\n

Nor does it help to fold yet another statute into the mix. At points, the employees suggest that the Norris-LaGuardia Act, a precursor of the NLRA, also renders their arbitration agreements unenforceable. But the Norris-LaGuardia Act adds nothing here. It declares \xe2\x80\x9c[un]enforceable\xe2\x80\x9d contracts that conflict with its policy of protecting workers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cconcerted activities for the purpose of collective bargaining or other mutual aid or protection.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7102, 103. That is the same policy the NLRA advances and, as we\xe2\x80\x99ve seen, it does not conflict with Congress\xe2\x80\x99s statutory directions favoring arbitration. See also Boys Markets, Inc. v. Retail Clerks,\n\n398 U.\xc2\xa0S. 235 (1970) (holding that the Norris-LaGuardia Act\xe2\x80\x99s anti-injunction provisions do not bar enforcement of arbitration agreements).

\n

What all these textual and contextual clues indicate, our precedents confirm. In many cases over many years, this Court has heard and rejected efforts to conjure conflicts between the Arbitration Act and other federal statutes. In fact, this Court has rejected every such effort to date (save one temporary exception since overruled), with statutes ranging from the Sherman and Clayton Acts to the Age Discrimination in Employment Act, the Credit Repair Organizations Act, the Securities Act of 1933, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, and the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act. Italian Colors,\n\n570 U.\xc2\xa0S. 228; Gilmer,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 20; CompuCredit Corp. v. Greenwood,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 95 (2012); Rodriguez de Quijas v. Shearson/American Express, Inc.,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477 (1989) (over- ruling Wilko v. Swan,\n\n346 U.\xc2\xa0S. 427 (1953)); Shearson/American Express Inc. v. McMahon,\n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220 (1987). Throughout, we have made clear that even a statute\xe2\x80\x99s express provision for collective legal actions does not necessarily mean that it precludes \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98individual attempts at conciliation\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d through arbitration. Gilmer, supra, at 32. And we\xe2\x80\x99ve stressed that the absence of any specific statutory discussion of arbitration or class actions is an important and telling clue that Congress has not displaced the Arbitration Act. CompuCredit, supra, at 103\xe2\x80\x93104; McMahon, supra, at 227; Italian Colors, supra, at 234. Given so much precedent pointing so strongly in one direction, we do not see how we might faithfully turn the other way here.

\n

Consider a few examples. In Italian Colors, this Court refused to find a conflict between the Arbitration Act and the Sherman Act because the Sherman Act (just like the NLRA) made \xe2\x80\x9cno mention of class actions\xe2\x80\x9d and was adopted before Rule 23 introduced its exception to the \xe2\x80\x9cusual rule\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual\xe2\x80\x9d dispute resolution. 570 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 234 (internal quotation marks omitted). In Gilmer, this Court \xe2\x80\x9chad no qualms in enforcing a class waiver in an arbitration agreement even though\xe2\x80\x9d the Age Discrimination in Employment Act \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly permitted collective legal actions.\xe2\x80\x9d Italian Colors, supra, at 237 (citing Gilmer, supra, at 32). And in CompuCredit, this Court refused to find a conflict even though the Credit Repair Organizations Act expressly provided a \xe2\x80\x9cright to sue,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9crepeated[ly]\xe2\x80\x9d used the words \xe2\x80\x9caction\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccourt\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cclass action,\xe2\x80\x9d and even declared \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny waiver\xe2\x80\x9d of the rights it provided to be \xe2\x80\x9cvoid.\xe2\x80\x9d 565 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 99\xe2\x80\x93100 (internal quotation marks omitted). If all the statutes in all those cases did not provide a congressional command sufficient to displace the Arbitration Act, we cannot imagine how we might hold that the NLRA alone and for the first time does so today.

\n

The employees rejoin that our precedential story is complicated by some of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases interpreting Section 7 itself. But, as it turns out, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Section 7 cases have usually involved just what you would expect from the statute\xe2\x80\x99s plain language: efforts by employees related to organizing and collective bargaining in the workplace, not the treatment of class or collective actions in court or arbitration proceedings. See, e.g., NLRB v. Washington Aluminum Co.,\n\n370 U.\xc2\xa0S. 9 (1962) (walkout to protest workplace conditions); NLRB v. Textile Workers,\n\n409 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213 (1972) (resignation from union and refusal to strike); NLRB v. J. Weingarten, Inc.,\n\n420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 251 (1975) (request for union representation at disciplinary interview). Neither do the two cases the employees cite prove otherwise. In Eastex, Inc. v. NLRB,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 556, 558 (1978), we simply addressed the question whether a union\xe2\x80\x99s distribution of a newsletter in the workplace qualified as a protected concerted activity. We held it did, noting that it was \xe2\x80\x9cundisputed that the union undertook the distribution in order to boost its support and improve its bargaining position in upcoming contract negotiations,\xe2\x80\x9d all part of the union\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98continuing organizational efforts.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 575, and n.\xc2\xa024. In NLRB v. City Disposal Systems, Inc.,\n\n465 U.\xc2\xa0S. 822, 831\xe2\x80\x93832 (1984), we held only that an employee\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of a right under a collective bargaining agreement was protected, reasoning that the collective bargaining \xe2\x80\x9cprocess\xe2\x80\x94beginning with the organization of the union, continuing into the negotiation of a collective-bargaining agreement, and extending through the enforcement of the agreement\xe2\x80\x94is a single, collective activ- ity.\xe2\x80\x9d Nothing in our cases indicates that the NLRA guarantees class and collective action procedures, let alone for claims arising under different statutes and despite the express (and entirely unmentioned) teachings of the Arbitration Act.

\n

That leaves the employees to try to make something of our dicta. The employees point to a line in Eastex observing that \xe2\x80\x9cit has been held\xe2\x80\x9d by other courts and the Board \xe2\x80\x9cthat the \xe2\x80\x98mutual aid or protection\xe2\x80\x99 clause protects employees from retaliation by their employers when they seek to improve working conditions through resort to administrative and judicial forums.\xe2\x80\x9d 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 565\xe2\x80\x93566; see also Brief for National Labor Relations Board in No. 16\xe2\x80\x93307, p.\xc2\xa015 (citing similar Board decisions). But even on its own terms, this dicta about the holdings of other bodies does not purport to discuss what procedures an employee might be entitled to in litigation or arbitration. Instead this passage at most suggests only that \xe2\x80\x9cresort to administrative and judicial forums\xe2\x80\x9d isn\xe2\x80\x99t \xe2\x80\x9centirely unprotected.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 566. Indeed, the Court proceeded to explain that it did not intend to \xe2\x80\x9caddress .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the question of what may constitute \xe2\x80\x98concerted\xe2\x80\x99 activities in this [litigation] context.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid., n.\xc2\xa015. So even the employees\xe2\x80\x99 dicta, when viewed fairly and fully, doesn\xe2\x80\x99t suggest that individualized dispute resolution procedures might be insufficient and collective procedures might be mandatory. Neither should this come as a surprise given that not a single one of the lower court or Board decisions Eastex discussed went so far as to hold that Section 7 guarantees a right to class or collective action procedures. As we\xe2\x80\x99ve seen, the Board did not purport to discover that right until 2012, and no federal appellate court accepted it until 2016. See D.\xc2\xa0R. Horton, 357 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 2277; 823 F.\xc2\xa03d 1147 (case below in No. 16\xe2\x80\x93285).

\n

With so much against them in the statute and our precedent, the employees end by seeking shelter in Chevron. Even if this Court doesn\xe2\x80\x99t see what they see in Section 7, the employees say we must rule for them anyway because of the deference this Court owes to an administrative agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the law. To be sure, the employees do not wish us to defer to the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in 2010 that the NLRA and the Arbitration Act coexist peaceably; they wish us to defer instead to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s 2012 opinion suggesting the NLRA displaces the Arbitration Act. No party to these cases has asked us to reconsider Chevron deference. Cf. SAS Institute Inc. v. Iancu, ante, at 11. But even under Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s terms, no deference is due. To show why, it suffices to outline just a few of the most obvious reasons.

\n

The Chevron Court justified deference on the premise that a statutory ambiguity represents an \xe2\x80\x9cimplicit\xe2\x80\x9d delegation to an agency to interpret a \xe2\x80\x9cstatute which it administers.\xe2\x80\x9d 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 841, 844. Here, though, the Board hasn\xe2\x80\x99t just sought to interpret its statute, the NLRA, in isolation; it has sought to interpret this statute in a way that limits the work of a second statute, the Arbitration Act. And on no account might we agree that Congress implicitly delegated to an agency authority to address the meaning of a second statute it does not administer. One of Chevron\xe2\x80\x99s essential premises is simply missing here.

\n

It\xe2\x80\x99s easy, too, to see why the \xe2\x80\x9creconciliation\xe2\x80\x9d of distinct statutory regimes \xe2\x80\x9cis a matter for the courts,\xe2\x80\x9d not agencies. Gordon v. New York Stock Exchange, Inc.,\n\n422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 659, 685\xe2\x80\x93686 (1975). An agency eager to advance its statutory mission, but without any particular interest in or expertise with a second statute, might (as here) seek to diminish the second statute\xe2\x80\x99s scope in favor of a more expansive interpretation of its own\xe2\x80\x94effectively \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98bootstrap[ping] itself into an area in which it has no jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Adams Fruit Co. v. Barrett,\n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 638, 650 (1990). All of which threatens to undo rather than honor legislative intentions. To preserve the balance Congress struck in its statutes, courts must exercise independent interpretive judgment. See Hoffman Plastic Compounds, Inc. v. NLRB,\n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 137, 144 (2002) (noting that this Court has \xe2\x80\x9cnever deferred to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s remedial preferences where such preferences potentially trench upon federal statutes and policies unrelated to the NLRA\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Another justification the Chevron Court offered for deference is that \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy choices\xe2\x80\x9d should be left to Executive Branch officials \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly accountable to the people.\xe2\x80\x9d 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 865. But here the Executive seems of two minds, for we have received competing briefs from the Board and from the United States (through the Solicitor General) disputing the meaning of the NLRA. And whatever argument might be mustered for deferring to the Executive on grounds of political accountability, surely it becomes a garble when the Executive speaks from both sides of its mouth, articulating no single position on which it might be held accountable. See Hemel & Nielson, Chevron Step One-and-a-Half, 84 U. Chi. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 757, 808 (2017) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf the theory undergirding Chevron is that voters should be the judges of the executive branch\xe2\x80\x99s policy choices, then presumably the executive branch should have to take ownership of those policy choices so that voters know whom to blame (and to credit)\xe2\x80\x9d). In these circumstances, we will not defer.

\n

Finally, the Chevron Court explained that deference is not due unless a \xe2\x80\x9ccourt, employing traditional tools of statutory construction,\xe2\x80\x9d is left with an unresolved ambiguity. 467 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 843, n.\xc2\xa09. And that too is missing: the canon against reading conflicts into statutes is a traditional tool of statutory construction and it, along with the other traditional canons we have discussed, is more than up to the job of solving today\xe2\x80\x99s interpretive puzzle. Where, as here, the canons supply an answer, \xe2\x80\x9cChevron leaves the stage.\xe2\x80\x9d Alternative Entertainment, 858 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 417 (opinion of Sutton, J.).

\n

IV

\n

The dissent sees things a little bit differently. In its view, today\xe2\x80\x99s decision ushers us back to the Lochner era when this Court regularly overrode legislative policy judgments. The dissent even suggests we have resur- rected the long-dead \xe2\x80\x9cyellow dog\xe2\x80\x9d contract. Post, at 3\xe2\x80\x9317, 30 (opinion of Ginsburg, J.). But like most apocalyptic warnings, this one proves a false alarm. Cf. L. Tribe, American Constitutional Law 435 (1978) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Lochnerizing\xe2\x80\x99 has become so much an epithet that the very use of the label may obscure attempts at understanding\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Our decision does nothing to override Congress\xe2\x80\x99s policy judgments. As the dissent recognizes, the legislative policy embodied in the NLRA is aimed at \xe2\x80\x9csafeguard[ing], first and foremost, workers\xe2\x80\x99 rights to join unions and to engage in collective bargaining.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 8. Those rights stand every bit as strong today as they did yesterday. And rather than revive \xe2\x80\x9cyellow dog\xe2\x80\x9d contracts against union organizing that the NLRA outlawed back in 1935, today\xe2\x80\x99s decision merely declines to read into the NLRA a novel right to class action procedures that the Board\xe2\x80\x99s own general counsel disclaimed as recently as 2010.

\n

Instead of overriding Congress\xe2\x80\x99s policy judgments, today\xe2\x80\x99s decision seeks to honor them. This much the dissent surely knows. Shortly after invoking the specter of Lochner, it turns around and criticizes the Court for trying too hard to abide the Arbitration Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98liberal federal policy favoring arbitration agreements,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Howsam v. Dean Witter Reynolds, Inc.,\n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 79, 83 (2002), saying we \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98ski\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d too far down the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98slippery slope\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration precedent, post, at 23. But the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s real complaint lies with the mountain of precedent itself. The dissent spends page after page relitigating our Arbitration Act precedents, rehashing arguments this Court has heard and rejected many times in many cases that no party has asked us to revisit. Compare post, at 18\xe2\x80\x9323, 26 (criticizing Mitsubishi Motors Corp. v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth, Inc.,\n\n473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 614 (1985), Gilmer,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 20, Circuit City,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 105, Concepcion,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333, Italian Colors,\n\n570 U.\xc2\xa0S. 228, and CompuCredit,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 95), with Mitsubishi, supra, at 645\xe2\x80\x93650 (Stevens, J., dissenting), Gilmer, supra, at 36, 39\xe2\x80\x9343 (Stevens, J., dissenting), Circuit City, supra, at 124\xe2\x80\x93129 (Stevens, J., dissenting), Concepcion, supra, at 357\xe2\x80\x93367 (Breyer, J., dissenting), Italian Colors, supra, at 240\xe2\x80\x93253 (Kagan, J., dissenting), and CompuCredit, supra, at 116\xe2\x80\x93117 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting).

\n

When at last it reaches the question of applying our precedent, the dissent offers little, and understandably so. Our precedent clearly teaches that a contract defense \xe2\x80\x9cconditioning the enforceability of certain arbitration agreements on the availability of classwide arbitration procedures\xe2\x80\x9d is inconsistent with the Arbitration Act and its saving clause. Concepcion, supra, at 336 (opinion of the Court). And that, of course, is exactly what the employees\xe2\x80\x99 proffered defense seeks to do.

\n

Nor is the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the NLRA any more available to us than its reading of the Arbitration Act. The dissent imposes a vast construction on Section 7\xe2\x80\x99s language. Post, at 9. But a statute\xe2\x80\x99s meaning does not always \xe2\x80\x9cturn solely\xe2\x80\x9d on the broadest imaginable \xe2\x80\x9cdefinitions of its component words.\xe2\x80\x9d Yates v. United States, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (plurality opinion) (slip op., at 7). Linguistic and statutory context also matter. We have offered an extensive explanation why those clues support our reading today. By contrast, the dissent rests its interpretation on legislative history. Post, at 3\xe2\x80\x935; see also post, at 19\xe2\x80\x9321. But legislative history is not the law. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is the business of Congress to sum up its own debates in its legislation,\xe2\x80\x9d and once it enacts a statute \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[w]e do not inquire what the legislature meant; we ask only what the statute means.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Schwegmann Brothers v. Calvert Distillers Corp.,\n\n341 U.\xc2\xa0S. 384, 396, 397 (1951) (Jackson, J., concurring) (quoting Justice Holmes). Besides, when it comes to the legislative history here, it seems Congress \xe2\x80\x9cdid not discuss the right to file class or consolidated claims against employers.\xe2\x80\x9d D.\xc2\xa0R. Horton, 737 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 361. So the dissent seeks instead to divine messages from congressional commentary directed to different questions altogether\xe2\x80\x94a project that threatens to \xe2\x80\x9csubstitute [the Court] for the Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Schwegmann, supra, at 396.

\n

Nor do the problems end there. The dissent proceeds to argue that its expansive reading of the NLRA conflicts with and should prevail over the Arbitration Act. The NLRA leaves the Arbitration Act without force, the dissent says, because it provides the more \xe2\x80\x9cpinpointed\xe2\x80\x9d direction. Post, at 25. Even taken on its own terms, though, this argument quickly faces trouble. The dissent says the NLRA is the more specific provision because it supposedly \xe2\x80\x9cspeaks directly to group action by employees,\xe2\x80\x9d while the Arbitration Act doesn\xe2\x80\x99t speak to such actions. Ibid. But the question before us is whether courts must enforce particular arbitration agreements according to their terms. And it\xe2\x80\x99s the Arbitration Act that speaks directly to the enforceability of arbitration agreements, while the NLRA doesn\xe2\x80\x99t mention arbitration at all. So if forced to choose between the two, we might well say the Arbitration Act offers the more on-point instruction. Of course, there is no need to make that call because, as our precedents demand, we have sought and found a persuasive interpretation that gives effect to all of Congress\xe2\x80\x99s work, not just the parts we might prefer.

\n

Ultimately, the dissent retreats to policy arguments. It argues that we should read a class and collective action right into the NLRA to promote the enforcement of wage and hour laws. Post, at 26\xe2\x80\x9330. But it\xe2\x80\x99s altogether unclear why the dissent expects to find such a right in the NLRA rather than in statutes like the FLSA that actually regulate wages and hours. Or why we should read the NLRA as mandating the availability of class or collective actions when the FLSA expressly authorizes them yet allows parties to contract for bilateral arbitration instead.\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7216(b); Gilmer, supra, at 32. While the dissent is no doubt right that class actions can enhance enforcement by \xe2\x80\x9cspread[ing] the costs of litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 9, it\xe2\x80\x99s also well known that they can unfairly \xe2\x80\x9cplac[e] pressure on the defendant to settle even unmeritorious claims,\xe2\x80\x9d Shady Grove Orthopedic Associates, P.\xc2\xa0A. v. Allstate Ins. Co.,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393, 445, n.\xc2\xa03 (2010) (Ginsburg, J., dissenting). The respective merits of class actions and private arbitration as means of enforcing the law are questions constitution- ally entrusted not to the courts to decide but to the policy- makers in the political branches where those questions remain hotly contested. Just recently, for example, one federal agency banned individualized arbitration agreements it blamed for underenforcement of certain laws, only to see Congress respond by immediately repealing that rule. See 82 Fed. Reg. 33210 (2017) (cited post, at 28, n.\xc2\xa015); Pub. L. 115\xe2\x80\x9374,\n\n131Stat.\n1243. This Court is not free to substitute its preferred economic policies for those chosen by the people\xe2\x80\x99s representatives. That, we had always understood, was Lochner\xe2\x80\x99s sin.

\n

*

\n

The policy may be debatable but the law is clear: Congress has instructed that arbitration agreements like those before us must be enforced as written. While Congress is of course always free to amend this judgment, we see nothing suggesting it did so in the NLRA\xe2\x80\x94much less that it manifested a clear intention to displace the Arbitration Act. Because we can easily read Congress\xe2\x80\x99s statutes to work in harmony, that is where our duty lies. The judgments in Epic, No. 16\xe2\x80\x93285, and Ernst & Young, No. 16\xe2\x80\x93300, are reversed, and the cases are remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion. The judgment in Murphy Oil, No. 16\xe2\x80\x93307, is affirmed.

So ordered.

Justice Ginsburg, with whom Justice Breyer, Justice Sotomayor, and Justice Kagan join, dissenting.

The employees in these cases complain that their employers have underpaid them in violation of the wage and hours prescriptions of the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 (FLSA),29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7201 et seq., and analogous state laws. Individually, their claims are small, scarcely of a size warranting the expense of seeking redress alone. See Ruan, What\xe2\x80\x99s Left To Remedy Wage Theft? How Arbitration Mandates That Bar Class Actions Impact Low-Wage Workers, 2012 Mich. St. L. Rev. 1103, 1118\xe2\x80\x931119 (Ruan). But by joining together with others similarly circumstanced, employees can gain effective redress for wage underpayment commonly experienced. See id., at 1108\xe2\x80\x931111. To block such concerted action, their employers required them to sign, as a condition of employment, arbitration agreements banning collective judicial and arbitral proceedings of any kind. The question presented: Does the Federal Arbitration Act (Arbitration Act or FAA),9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 et\xc2\xa0seq., permit employers to insist that their employees, whenever seeking redress for commonly experienced wage loss, go it alone, never mind the right secured to employees by the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA),29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7151 et\xc2\xa0seq., \xe2\x80\x9cto engage in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. concerted activities\xe2\x80\x9d for their \xe2\x80\x9cmutual aid or protection\xe2\x80\x9d? \xc2\xa7157. The answer should be a resounding \xe2\x80\x9cNo.\xe2\x80\x9d

In the NLRA and its forerunner, the Norris-LaGuardia Act (NLGA),29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7101 et\xc2\xa0seq., Congress acted on an acute awareness: For workers striving to gain from their employers decent terms and conditions of employment, there is strength in numbers. A single employee, Con- gress understood, is disarmed in dealing with an employer. See NLRB v. Jones & Laughlin Steel Corp., 301 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, 33\xe2\x80\x9334 (1937). The Court today subordinates employee-protective labor legislation to the Arbitration Act. In so doing, the Court forgets the labor market imbalance that gave rise to the NLGA and the NLRA, and ignores the destructive consequences of diminishing the right of employees \xe2\x80\x9cto band together in confronting an employer.\xe2\x80\x9d NLRB v. City Disposal Systems, Inc.,465 U.\xc2\xa0S. 822, 835 (1984). Congressional correction of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s elevation of the FAA over workers\xe2\x80\x99 rights to act in concert is urgently in order.

To explain why the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is egregiously wrong, I first refer to the extreme imbalance once prevalent in our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s workplaces, and Congress\xe2\x80\x99 aim in the NLGA and the NLRA to place employers and employees on a more equal footing. I then explain why the Arbitration Act, sensibly read, does not shrink the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s protective sphere.

I

It was once the dominant view of this Court that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he right of a person to sell his labor upon such terms as he deems proper is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the same as the right of the purchaser of labor to prescribe [working] conditions.\xe2\x80\x9d Adair v. United States,208 U.\xc2\xa0S. 161, 174 (1908) (invalidating federal law prohibiting interstate railroad employers from discharging or discriminating against employees based on their membership in labor organizations); accord Coppage v. Kansas,236 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, 26 (1915) (invalidating state law prohibit- ing employers from requiring employees, as a condition of employment, to refrain or withdraw from union membership).

The NLGA and the NLRA operate on a different premise, that employees must have the capacity to act collectively in order to match their employers\xe2\x80\x99 clout in setting terms and conditions of employment. For decades, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions have reflected that understanding. See Jones & Laughlin Steel,301 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (upholding the NLRA against employer assault); cf. United States v. Darby,312 U.\xc2\xa0S. 100 (1941) (upholding the FLSA).

A

The end of the 19th century and beginning of the 20th was a tumultuous era in the history of our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s labor relations. Under economic conditions then prevailing, workers often had to accept employment on whatever terms employers dictated. See 75 Cong. Rec. 4502 (1932). Aiming to secure better pay, shorter workdays, and safer workplaces, workers increasingly sought to band together to make their demands effective. See ibid.; H. Millis & E. Brown, From the Wagner Act to Taft-Hartley: A Study of National Labor Policy and Labor Relations 7\xe2\x80\x938 (1950).

Employers, in turn, engaged in a variety of tactics to hinder workers\xe2\x80\x99 efforts to act in concert for their mutual benefit. See J. Seidman, The Yellow Dog Contract 11 (1932). Notable among such devices was the \xe2\x80\x9cyellow-dog contract.\xe2\x80\x9d Such agreements, which employers required employees to sign as a condition of employment, typically commanded employees to abstain from joining labor unions. See id., at 11, 56. Many of the employer-designed agreements cast an even wider net, \xe2\x80\x9cproscrib[ing] all manner of concerted activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Finkin, The Meaning and Contemporary Vitality of the Norris-LaGuardia Act, 93 Neb. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 6, 16 (2014); see Seidman, supra, at 59\xe2\x80\x9360, 65\xe2\x80\x9366. As a prominent United States Senator observed, contracts of the yellow-dog genre rendered the \xe2\x80\x9claboring man .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. absolutely helpless\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cwaiv[ing] his right .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to free association\xe2\x80\x9d and by requiring that he \xe2\x80\x9csingly present any grievance he has.\xe2\x80\x9d 75 Cong. Rec. 4504 (remarks of Sen. Norris).

Early legislative efforts to protect workers\xe2\x80\x99 rights to band together were unavailing. See, e.g., Coppage, 236 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 26; Frankfurter & Greene, Legislation Affecting Labor Injunctions, 38 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 879, 889\xe2\x80\x93890 (1929). Courts, including this one, invalidated the legislation based on then-ascendant notions about employers\xe2\x80\x99 and employees\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional right to \xe2\x80\x9cliberty of contract.\xe2\x80\x9d See Coppage, 236 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 26; Frankfurter & Greene, supra, at 890\xe2\x80\x93891. While stating that legislatures could curtail contractual \xe2\x80\x9cliberty\xe2\x80\x9d in the interest of public health, safety, and the general welfare, courts placed outside those bounds legislative action to redress the bargaining power imbalance workers faced. See Coppage, 236 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 16\xe2\x80\x9319.

In the 1930\xe2\x80\x99s, legislative efforts to safeguard vulnerable workers found more receptive audiences. As the Great Depression shifted political winds further in favor of worker-protective laws, Congress passed two statutes aimed at protecting employees\xe2\x80\x99 associational rights. First, in 1932, Congress passed the NLGA, which regulates the employer-employee relationship indirectly. Section 2 of the Act declares:

\xe2\x80\x9cWhereas .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the individual unorganized worker is commonly helpless to exercise actual liberty of contract and to protect his freedom of labor, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. it is necessary that he have full freedom of association, self-organization, and designation of representatives of his own choosing, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and that he shall be free from the interference, restraint, or coercion of employers .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the designation of such representatives or in self-organization or in other concerted activities for the purpose of collective bargaining or other mutual aid or protection.\xe2\x80\x9d29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7102.

Section 3 provides that federal courts shall not enforce \xe2\x80\x9cany .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. undertaking or promise in conflict with the public policy declared in [\xc2\xa72].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7103.[1] In adopting these provisions, Congress sought to render ineffective employer-imposed contracts proscribing employees\xe2\x80\x99 concerted activity of any and every kind. See 75 Cong. Rec. 4504\xe2\x80\x934505 (remarks of Sen. Norris) (\xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne of the objects\xe2\x80\x9d of the NLGA was to \xe2\x80\x9coutlaw\xe2\x80\x9d yellow-dog contracts); Finkin, supra, at 16 (contracts prohibiting \xe2\x80\x9call manner of concerted activities apart from union membership or support .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. were understood to be \xe2\x80\x98yellow dog\xe2\x80\x99 contracts\xe2\x80\x9d). While banning court enforcement of contracts proscribing concerted action by employees, the NLGA did not directly prohibit coercive employer practices.

But Congress did so three years later, in 1935, when it enacted the NLRA. Relevant here, \xc2\xa77 of the NLRA guarantees employees \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to self-organization, to form, join, or assist labor organizations, to bargain collectively through representatives of their own choosing, and to engage in other concerted activities for the purpose of collective bargaining or other mutual aid or protection.\xe2\x80\x9d29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7157 (emphasis added). Section 8(a)(1) safeguards those rights by making it an \xe2\x80\x9cunfair labor practice\xe2\x80\x9d for an employer to \xe2\x80\x9cinterfere with, restrain, or coerce employees in the exercise of the rights guaranteed in [\xc2\xa77].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7158(a)(1). To oversee the Act\xe2\x80\x99s guarantees, the Act established the National Labor Relations Board (Board or NLRB), an independent regulatory agency empowered to administer \xe2\x80\x9clabor policy for the Nation.\xe2\x80\x9d San Diego Building Trades Council v. Garmon,359 U.\xc2\xa0S. 236, 242 (1959); see29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7160.

Unlike earlier legislative efforts, the NLGA and the NLRA had staying power. When a case challenging the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality made its way here, the Court, in retreat from its Lochner-era contractual-\xe2\x80\x9cliberty\xe2\x80\x9d decisions, upheld the Act as a permissible exercise of legislative authority. See Jones & Laughlin Steel, 301 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 33\xe2\x80\x9334. The Court recognized that employees have a \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental right\xe2\x80\x9d to join together to advance their common interests and that Congress, in lieu of \xe2\x80\x9cignor[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d that right, had elected to \xe2\x80\x9csafeguard\xe2\x80\x9d it. Ibid.

B

Despite the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s prohibitions, the employers in the cases now before the Court required their employees to sign contracts stipulating to submission of wage and hours claims to binding arbitration, and to do so only one-by-one.[2] When employees subsequently filed wage and hours claims in federal court and sought to invoke the collective-litigation procedures provided for in the FLSA and Federal Rules of Civil Procedure,[3] the employers moved to compel individual arbitration. The Arbitration Act, in their view, requires courts to enforce their take-it-or-leave-it arbitration agreements as written, including the collective-litigation abstinence demanded therein.

In resisting enforcement of the group-action foreclosures, the employees involved in this litigation do not urge that they must have access to a judicial forum.[4] They argue only that the NLRA prohibits their employers from denying them the right to pursue work-related claims in concert in any forum. If they may be stopped by employer-dictated terms from pursuing collective procedures in court, they maintain, they must at least have access to similar procedures in an arbitral forum.

C

Although the NLRA safeguards, first and foremost, workers\xe2\x80\x99 rights to join unions and to engage in collective bargaining, the statute speaks more embracively. In addition to protecting employees\xe2\x80\x99 rights \xe2\x80\x9cto form, join, or assist labor organizations\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cto bargain collectively through representatives of their own choosing,\xe2\x80\x9d the Act protects employees\xe2\x80\x99 rights \xe2\x80\x9cto engage in other concerted activities for the purpose of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. mutual aid or protection.\xe2\x80\x9d29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7157 (emphasis added); see, e.g., NLRB v. Washington Aluminum Co.,370 U.\xc2\xa0S. 9, 14\xe2\x80\x9315 (1962) (\xc2\xa77 protected unorganized employees when they walked off the job to protest cold working conditions). See also 1 J. Higgins, The Developing Labor Law 209 (6th ed. 2012) (\xe2\x80\x9cSection 7 protects not only union-related activity but also \xe2\x80\x98other concerted activities .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for mutual aid or protection.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); 1 N. Lareau, Labor and Employment Law \xc2\xa71.01[1], p. 1\xe2\x80\x932 (2017) (\xe2\x80\x9cSection 7 extended to employees three federally protected rights: (1) the right to form and join unions; (2) the right to bargain collectively (negotiate) with employers about terms and conditions of employment; and (3) the right to work in concert with another employee or employees to achieve employment-related goals.\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

Suits to enforce workplace rights collectively fit comfortably under the umbrella \xe2\x80\x9cconcerted activities for the purpose of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. mutual aid or protection.\xe2\x80\x9d29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7157. \xe2\x80\x9cConcerted\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9c[p]lanned or accomplished together; combined.\xe2\x80\x9d American Heritage Dictionary 381 (5th ed. 2011). \xe2\x80\x9cMutual\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9creciprocal.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1163. When employees meet the requirements for litigation of shared legal claims in joint, collective, and class proceedings, the litigation of their claims is undoubtedly \xe2\x80\x9caccomplished together.\xe2\x80\x9d By joining hands in litigation, workers can spread the costs of litigation and reduce the risk of employer retaliation. See infra, at 27\xe2\x80\x9328.

Recognizing employees\xe2\x80\x99 right to engage in collective employment litigation and shielding that right from employer blockage are firmly rooted in the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s design. Congress expressed its intent, when it enacted the NLRA, to \xe2\x80\x9cprotec[t] the exercise by workers of full freedom of association,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby remedying \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he inequality of bargaining power\xe2\x80\x9d workers faced.29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7151; see, e.g., Eastex, Inc. v. NLRB,437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 556, 567 (1978) (the Act\xe2\x80\x99s policy is \xe2\x80\x9cto protect the right of workers to act together to better their working conditions\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); City Disposal, 465 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 835 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n enacting \xc2\xa77 of the NLRA, Congress sought generally to equalize the bargaining power of the employee with that of his employer by allowing employees to band together in confronting an employer regarding the terms and conditions of their employment.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. There can be no serious doubt that collective litigation is one way workers may associate with one another to improve their lot.

Since the Act\xe2\x80\x99s earliest days, the Board and federal courts have understood \xc2\xa77\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cconcerted activities\xe2\x80\x9d clause to protect myriad ways in which employees may join together to advance their shared interests. For example, the Board and federal courts have affirmed that the Act shields employees from employer interference when they participate in concerted appeals to the media, e.g., NLRB v. Peter Cailler Kohler Swiss Chocolates Co., 130 F.\xc2\xa02d 503, 505\xe2\x80\x93506 (CA2 1942), legislative bodies, e.g., Bethlehem Shipbuilding Corp. v. NLRB, 114 F.\xc2\xa02d 930, 937 (CA1 1940), and government agencies, e.g., Moss Planing Mill Co., 103 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 414, 418\xe2\x80\x93419, enf\xe2\x80\x99d, 206 F.\xc2\xa02d 557 (CA4 1953). \xe2\x80\x9cThe 74th Congress,\xe2\x80\x9d this Court has noted, \xe2\x80\x9cknew well enough that labor\xe2\x80\x99s cause often is advanced on fronts other than collective bargaining and grievance settlement within the immediate employment context.\xe2\x80\x9d Eastex, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 565.

Crucially important here, for over 75 years, the Board has held that the NLRA safeguards employees from employer interference when they pursue joint, collective, and class suits related to the terms and conditions of their employment. See, e.g., Spandsco Oil and Royalty Co., 42 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 942, 948\xe2\x80\x93949 (1942) (three employees\xe2\x80\x99 joint filing of FLSA suit ranked as concerted activity protected by the NLRA); Poultrymen\xe2\x80\x99s Service Corp., 41 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 444, 460\xe2\x80\x93463, and n. 28 (1942) (same with respect to employee\xe2\x80\x99s filing of FLSA suit on behalf of himself and others similarly situated), enf\xe2\x80\x99d, 138 F.\xc2\xa02d 204 (CA3 1943); Sarkes Tarzian, Inc., 149 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 147, 149, 153 (1964) (same with respect to employees\xe2\x80\x99 filing class libel suit); United Parcel Service, Inc., 252 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 1015, 1018 (1980) (same with respect to employee\xe2\x80\x99s filing class action regarding break times), enf\xe2\x80\x99d, 677 F.\xc2\xa02d 421 (CA6 1982); Harco Trucking, LLC, 344 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 478, 478\xe2\x80\x93479 (2005) (same with respect to employee\xe2\x80\x99s maintaining class action regarding wages). For decades, federal courts have endorsed the Board\xe2\x80\x99s view, comprehending that \xe2\x80\x9cthe filing of a labor related civil action by a group of employees is ordinarily a concerted activity protected by \xc2\xa77.\xe2\x80\x9d Leviton Mfg. Co. v. NLRB, 486 F.\xc2\xa02d 686, 689 (CA1 1973); see, e.g., Brady v. National Football League, 644 F.\xc2\xa03d 661, 673 (CA8 2011) (similar).[5] The Court pays scant heed to this longstanding line of decisions.[6]

D

In face of the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s text, history, purposes, and longstanding construction, the Court nevertheless concludes that collective proceedings do not fall within the scope of \xc2\xa77. None of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for diminishing \xc2\xa77 should carry the day.

1

The Court relies principally on the ejusdem generis canon. See ante, at 12. Observing that \xc2\xa77\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cother concerted activities\xe2\x80\x9d clause \xe2\x80\x9cappears at the end of a detailed list of activities,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court says the clause should be read to \xe2\x80\x9cembrace\xe2\x80\x9d only activities \xe2\x80\x9csimilar in nature\xe2\x80\x9d to those set forth first in the list, ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted), i.e., \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98self-organization,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98form[ing], join[ing], or assist[ing] labor organizations,\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98bargain[ing] collectively,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. The Court concludes that \xc2\xa77 should, therefore, be read to protect \xe2\x80\x9cthings employees \xe2\x80\x98just do\xe2\x80\x99 for themselves.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting NLRB v. Alternative Entertainment, Inc., 858 F.\xc2\xa03d 393, 415 (CA6 2017) (Sutton, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part); emphasis deleted). It is far from apparent why joining hands in litigation would not qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cthings employees just do for themselves.\xe2\x80\x9d In any event, there is no sound reason to employ the ejusdem generis canon to narrow \xc2\xa77\xe2\x80\x99s protections in the manner the Court suggests.

The ejusdem generis canon may serve as a useful guide where it is doubtful Congress intended statutory words or phrases to have the broad scope their ordinary meaning conveys. See Russell Motor Car Co. v. United States,261 U.\xc2\xa0S. 514, 519 (1923). Courts must take care, however, not to deploy the canon to undermine Congress\xe2\x80\x99 efforts to draft encompassing legislation. See United States v. Powell,423 U.\xc2\xa0S. 87, 90 (1975) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e would be justified in narrowing the statute only if such a narrow reading was supported by evidence of congressional intent over and above the language of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d). Nothing suggests that Congress envisioned a cramped construction of the NLRA. Quite the opposite, Congress expressed an embracive purpose in enacting the legislation, i.e., to \xe2\x80\x9cprotec[t] the exercise by workers of full freedom of association.\xe2\x80\x9d29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7151; see supra, at 9.

2

In search of a statutory hook to support its application of the ejusdem generis canon, the Court turns to the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstructure.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. Citing a handful of provisions that touch upon unionization, collective bargaining, picketing, and strikes, the Court asserts that the NLRA \xe2\x80\x9cestablish[es] a regulatory regime\xe2\x80\x9d governing each of the activities protected by \xc2\xa77. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. That regime, the Court says, offers \xe2\x80\x9cspecific guidance\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crules\xe2\x80\x9d regulating each protected activity. Ante, at 13. Observing that none of the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s provisions explicitly regulates employees\xe2\x80\x99 resort to collective litigation, the Court insists that \xe2\x80\x9cit is hard to fathom why Congress would take such care to regulate all the other matters mentioned in [\xc2\xa77] yet remain mute about this matter alone\xe2\x80\x94unless, of course, [\xc2\xa77] doesn\xe2\x80\x99t speak to class and collective action procedures in the first place.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

This argument is conspicuously flawed. When Congress enacted the NLRA in 1935, the only \xc2\xa77 activity Congress addressed with any specificity was employees\xe2\x80\x99 selection of collective-bargaining representatives. See49Stat.453. The Act did not offer \xe2\x80\x9cspecific guidance\xe2\x80\x9d about employees\xe2\x80\x99 rights to \xe2\x80\x9cform, join, or assist labor organizations.\xe2\x80\x9d Nor did it set forth \xe2\x80\x9cspecific guidance\xe2\x80\x9d for any activity falling within \xc2\xa77\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cother concerted activities\xe2\x80\x9d clause. The only provision that touched upon an activity falling within that clause stated: \xe2\x80\x9cNothing in this Act shall be construed so as to interfere with or impede or diminish in any way the right to strike.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 457. That provision hardly offered \xe2\x80\x9cspecific guidance\xe2\x80\x9d regarding employees\xe2\x80\x99 right to strike.

Without much in the original Act to support its \xe2\x80\x9cstructure\xe2\x80\x9d argument, the Court cites several provisions that Congress added later, in response to particular concerns. Compare49Stat.449\xe2\x80\x93457 with 61Stat.142\xe2\x80\x93143 (1947) (adding \xc2\xa78(d) to provide guidance regarding employees\xe2\x80\x99 and employers\xe2\x80\x99 collective-bargaining obligations);61Stat.141\xe2\x80\x93142 (amending \xc2\xa78(a) and adding \xc2\xa78(b) to proscribe specified labor organization practices);73Stat.544 (1959) (adding \xc2\xa78(b)(7) to place restrictions on labor organizations\xe2\x80\x99 right to picket employers). It is difficult to comprehend why Congress\xe2\x80\x99 later inclusion of specific guidance regarding some of the activities protected by \xc2\xa77 sheds any light on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 initial conception of \xc2\xa77\xe2\x80\x99s scope.

But even if each of the provisions the Court cites had been included in the original Act, they still would provide little support for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion. For going on 80 years now, the Board and federal courts\xe2\x80\x94including this one\xe2\x80\x94have understood \xc2\xa77 to protect numerous activities for which the Act provides no \xe2\x80\x9cspecific\xe2\x80\x9d regulatory guidance. See supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310.

3

In a related argument, the Court maintains that the NLRA does not \xe2\x80\x9ceven whispe[r]\xe2\x80\x9d about the \xe2\x80\x9crules [that] should govern the adjudication of class or collective actions in court or arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. The employees here involved, of course, do not look to the NLRA for the procedures enabling them to vindicate their employment rights in arbitral or judicial forums. They assert that the Act establishes their right to act in concert using existing, generally available procedures, see supra, at 7, n. 3, and to do so free from employer interference. The FLSA and the Federal Rules on joinder and class actions provide the procedures pursuant to which the employees may ally to pursue shared legal claims. Their employers cannot lawfully cut off their access to those procedures, they urge, without according them access to similar procedures in arbitral forums. See, e.g., American Arbitration Assn., Supplementary Rules for Class Arbitrations (2011).

To the employees\xe2\x80\x99 argument, the Court replies: If the employees \xe2\x80\x9creally take existing class and collective action rules as they find them, they surely take them subject to the limitations inherent in those rules\xe2\x80\x94including the principle that parties may (as here) contract to depart from them in favor of individualized arbitration procedures.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. The freedom to depart asserted by the Court, as already underscored, is entirely one sided. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x935. Once again, the Court ignores the reality that sparked the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s passage: Forced to face their employers without company, employees ordinarily are no match for the enterprise that hires them. Employees gain strength, however, if they can deal with their employers in numbers. That is the very reason why the NLRA secures against employer interference employees\xe2\x80\x99 right to act in concert for their \xe2\x80\x9cmutual aid or protection.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7151, 157, 158.

4

Further attempting to sow doubt about \xc2\xa77\xe2\x80\x99s scope, the Court asserts that class and collective procedures were \xe2\x80\x9chardly known when the NLRA was adopted in 1935.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. In particular, the Court notes, the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s collective-litigation procedure postdated \xc2\xa77 \xe2\x80\x9cby years\xe2\x80\x9d and Rule 23 \xe2\x80\x9cdidn\xe2\x80\x99t create the modern class action until 1966.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

First, one may ask, is there any reason to suppose that Congress intended to protect employees\xe2\x80\x99 right to act in concert using only those procedures and forums available in 1935? Congress framed \xc2\xa77 in broad terms, \xe2\x80\x9centrust[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d the Board with \xe2\x80\x9cresponsibility to adapt the Act to changing patterns of industrial life.\xe2\x80\x9d NLRB v. J. Weingarten, Inc.,420 U.\xc2\xa0S. 251, 266 (1975); see Pennsylvania Dept. of Corrections v. Yeskey,524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 206, 212 (1998) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he fact that a statute can be applied in situations not expressly anticipated by Congress does not demonstrate ambiguity. It demonstrates breadth.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). With fidelity to Congress\xe2\x80\x99 aim, the Board and federal courts have recognized that the NLRA shields employees from employer interference when they, e.g., join together to file complaints with administrative agencies, even if those agencies did not exist in 1935. See, e.g., Wray Electric Contracting, Inc., 210 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 757, 762 (1974) (the NLRA protects concerted filing of complaint with the Occupational Safety and Health Administration).

Moreover, the Court paints an ahistorical picture. As Judge Wood, writing for the Seventh Circuit, cogently explained, the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s collective-litigation procedure and the modern class action were \xe2\x80\x9cnot written on a clean slate.\xe2\x80\x9d 823 F.\xc2\xa03d 1147, 1154 (2016). By 1935, permissive joinder was scarcely uncommon in courts of equity. See 7 C. Wright, A. Miller, & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa71651 (3d ed. 2001). Nor were representative and class suits novelties. Indeed, their origins trace back to medieval times. See S. Yeazell, From Medieval Group Litigation to the Modern Class Action 38 (1987). And beyond question, \xe2\x80\x9c[c]lass suits long have been a part of American jurisprudence.\xe2\x80\x9d 7A Wright, supra, \xc2\xa71751, at 12 (3d ed. 2005); see Supreme Tribe of Ben-Hur v. Cauble,255 U.\xc2\xa0S. 356, 363 (1921). See also Brief for Constitutional Accountability Center as Amicus Curiae 5\xe2\x80\x9316 (describing group litigation\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crich history\xe2\x80\x9d). Early instances of joint proceedings include cases in which employees allied to sue an employer. E.g., Gorley v. Louisville, 23 Ky. 1782, 65 S. W. 844 (1901) (suit to recover wages brought by ten members of city police force on behalf of themselves and other officers); Guiliano v. Daniel O\xe2\x80\x99Connell\xe2\x80\x99s Sons, 105 Conn. 695, 136 A. 677 (1927) (suit by two employees to recover for injuries sustained while residing in housing provided by their employer). It takes no imagination, then, to comprehend that Congress, when it enacted the NLRA, likely meant to protect employees\xe2\x80\x99 joining together to engage in collective litigation.[7]

E

Because I would hold that employees\xe2\x80\x99 \xc2\xa77 rights include the right to pursue collective litigation regarding their wages and hours, I would further hold that the employer-dictated collective-litigation stoppers, i.e., \xe2\x80\x9cwaivers,\xe2\x80\x9d are unlawful. As earlier recounted, see supra, at 6, \xc2\xa78(a)(1) makes it an \xe2\x80\x9cunfair labor practice\xe2\x80\x9d for an employer to \xe2\x80\x9cinterfere with, restrain, or coerce\xe2\x80\x9d employees in the exercise of their \xc2\xa77 rights.29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7158(a)(1). Beyond genuine dispute, an employer \xe2\x80\x9cinterfere[s] with\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crestrain[s]\xe2\x80\x9d employees in the exercise of their \xc2\xa77 rights by mandating that they prospectively renounce those rights in individual employment agreements.[8] The law could hardly be otherwise: Employees\xe2\x80\x99 rights to band together to meet their employers\xe2\x80\x99 superior strength would be worth precious little if employers could condition employment on workers signing away those rights. See National Licorice Co. v. NLRB,309 U.\xc2\xa0S. 350, 364 (1940). Properly assessed, then, the \xe2\x80\x9cwaivers\xe2\x80\x9d rank as unfair labor practices outlawed by the NLRA, and therefore unenforceable in court. See Kaiser Steel Corp. v. Mullins,455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 72, 77 (1982) (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]ur cases leave no doubt that illegal promises will not be enforced in cases controlled by the federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d).[9]

II

Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision rests largely on the Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding in the Arbitration Act \xe2\x80\x9cemphatic directions\xe2\x80\x9d to enforce arbitration agreements according to their terms, including collective-litigation prohibitions. Ante, at 6. Nothing in the FAA or this Court\xe2\x80\x99s case law, however, requires subordination of the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s protections. Before addressing the interaction between the two laws, I briefly recall the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s history and the domain for which that Act was designed.

A

1

Prior to 1925, American courts routinely declined to order specific performance of arbitration agreements. See Cohen & Dayton, The New Federal Arbitration Law, 12 Va. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 265, 270 (1926). Growing backlogs in the courts, which delayed the resolution of commercial disputes, prompted the business community to seek legislation enabling merchants to enter into binding arbitration agreements. See id., at 265. The business community\xe2\x80\x99s aim was to secure to merchants an expeditious, economical means of resolving their disputes. See ibid. The American Bar Association\xe2\x80\x99s Committee on Commerce, Trade and Commercial Law took up the reins in 1921, drafting the legislation Congress enacted, with relatively few changes, four years later. See Committee on Commerce, Trade & Commercial Law, The United States Arbitration Law and Its Application, 11 A.\xc2\xa0B.\xc2\xa0A. J. 153 (1925).

The legislative hearings and debate leading up to the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s passage evidence Congress\xe2\x80\x99 aim to enable merchants of roughly equal bargaining power to enter into binding agreements to arbitrate commercial disputes. See, e.g., 65 Cong. Rec. 11080 (1924) (remarks of Rep. Mills) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis bill provides that where there are commercial contracts and there is disagreement under the contract, the court can [en]force an arbitration agreement in the same way as other portions of the contract.\xe2\x80\x9d); Joint Hearings on S. 1005 and H.\xc2\xa0R. 646 before the Subcommittees of the Committees on the Judiciary, 68th Cong., 1st Sess. (1924) (Joint Hearings) (consistently focusing on the need for binding arbitration of commercial disputes).[10]

The FAA\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history also shows that Congress did not intend the statute to apply to arbitration provisions in employment contracts. In brief, when the legislation was introduced, organized labor voiced concern. See Hearing on S. 4213 and S. 4214 before the Subcommittee of the Senate Committee on the Judiciary, 67th Cong., 4th Sess., 9 (1923) (Hearing). Herbert Hoover, then Secretary of Commerce, suggested that if there were \xe2\x80\x9cobjection[s]\xe2\x80\x9d to including \xe2\x80\x9cworkers\xe2\x80\x99 contracts in the law\xe2\x80\x99s scheme,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress could amend the legislation to say: \xe2\x80\x9cbut nothing herein contained shall apply to contracts of employment of seamen, railroad employees, or any other class of workers engaged in interstate or foreign commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14. Congress adopted Secretary Hoover\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion virtually verbatim in \xc2\xa71 of the Act, see Joint Hearings 2;9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71, and labor expressed no further opposition, see H. R. Rep. No. 96, 68th Cong., 1st Sess., 1 (1924).[11]

Congress, it bears repetition, envisioned application of the Arbitration Act to voluntary, negotiated agreements. See, e.g., 65 Cong. Rec. 1931 (remarks of Rep. Graham) (the FAA provides an \xe2\x80\x9copportunity to enforce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an agreement to arbitrate, when voluntarily placed in the document by the parties to it\xe2\x80\x9d). Congress never endorsed a policy favoring arbitration where one party sets the terms of an agreement while the other is left to \xe2\x80\x9ctake it or leave it.\xe2\x80\x9d Hearing 9 (remarks of Sen. Walsh) (internal quotation marks omitted); see Prima Paint Corp. v. Flood & Conklin Mfg. Co.,388 U.\xc2\xa0S. 395, 403, n. 9 (1967) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe note that categories of contracts otherwise within the Arbitration Act but in which one of the parties characteristically has little bargaining power are expressly excluded from the reach of the Act. See \xc2\xa71.\xe2\x80\x9d).

2

In recent decades, this Court has veered away from Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent simply to afford merchants a speedy and economical means of resolving commercial disputes. See Sternlight, Panacea or Corporate Tool?: Debunking the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s Preference for Binding Arbitration, 74 Wash. U.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0Q. 637, 644\xe2\x80\x93674 (1996) (tracing the Court\xe2\x80\x99s evolving interpretation of the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s scope). In 1983, the Court declared, for the first time in the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s then 58-year history, that the FAA evinces a \xe2\x80\x9cliberal federal policy favoring arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d Moses H. Cone Memorial Hospital v. Mercury Constr. Corp.,460 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, 24 (1983) (involving an arbitration agreement between a hospital and a construction contractor). Soon thereafter, the Court ruled, in a series of cases, that the FAA requires enforcement of agreements to arbitrate not only contract claims, but statutory claims as well. E.g., Mitsubishi Motors Corp. v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth, Inc.,473 U.\xc2\xa0S. 614 (1985); Shearson/American Express Inc. v. McMahon,482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 220 (1987). Further, in 1991, the Court concluded in Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp.,500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 20, 23 (1991), that the FAA requires enforcement of agreements to arbitrate claims arising under the Age Discrimination in Employment Act of 1967, a workplace antidiscrimination statute. Then, in 2001, the Court ruled in Circuit City Stores, Inc. v. Adams,532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 105, 109 (2001), that the Arbitration Act\xe2\x80\x99s exemption for employment contracts should be construed narrowly, to exclude from the Act\xe2\x80\x99s scope only transportation workers\xe2\x80\x99 contracts.

Employers have availed themselves of the opportunity opened by court decisions expansively interpreting the Arbitration Act. Few employers imposed arbitration agreements on their employees in the early 1990\xe2\x80\x99s. After Gilmer and Circuit City, however, employers\xe2\x80\x99 exaction of arbitration clauses in employment contracts grew steadily. See, e.g., Economic Policy Institute (EPI), A. Colvin, The Growing Use of Mandatory Arbitration 1\xe2\x80\x932, 4 (Sept. 27, 2017), available at https://www.epi.org/files/pdf/135056.pdf (All Internet materials as visited May 18, 2018) (data indicate only 2.1% of nonunionized companies imposed mandatory arbitration agreements on their employees in 1992, but 53.9% do today). Moreover, in response to subsequent decisions addressing class arbitration,[12] employers have increasingly included in their arbitration agreements express group-action waivers. See Ruan 1129; Colvin, supra, at 6 (estimating that 23.1% of nonunionized employees are now subject to express class-action waivers in mandatory arbitration agreements). It is, therefore, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s exorbitant application of the FAA\xe2\x80\x94stretching it far beyond contractual disputes between merchants\xe2\x80\x94that led the NLRB to confront, for the first time in 2012, the precise question whether employers can use arbitration agreements to insulate themselves from collective employment litigation. See D. R. Horton, 357 N.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0B. 2277 (2012), enf. denied in relevant part, 737 F.\xc2\xa03d 344 (CA5 2013). Compare ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x934 (suggesting the Board broke new ground in 2012 when it concluded that the NLRA prohibits employer-imposed arbitration agreements that mandate individual arbitration) with supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311 (NLRB decisions recognizing a \xc2\xa77 right to engage in collective employment litigation), and supra, at 17, n. 8 (NLRB decisions finding employer-dictated waivers of \xc2\xa77 rights unlawful).

As I see it, in relatively recent years, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s Arbitration Act decisions have taken many wrong turns. Yet, even accepting the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions as they are, nothing compels the destructive result the Court reaches today. Cf. R. Bork, The Tempting of America 169 (1990) (\xe2\x80\x9cJudges .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. live on the slippery slope of analogies; they are not supposed to ski it to the bottom.\xe2\x80\x9d).

B

Through the Arbitration Act, Congress sought \xe2\x80\x9cto make arbitration agreements as enforceable as other contracts, but not more so.\xe2\x80\x9d Prima Paint, 388 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 404, n. 12. Congress thus provided in \xc2\xa72 of the FAA that the terms of a written arbitration agreement \xe2\x80\x9cshall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.\xe2\x80\x9d9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72 (emphasis added). Pursuant to this \xe2\x80\x9csaving clause,\xe2\x80\x9d arbitration agreements and terms may be invalidated based on \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally applicable contract defenses, such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability.\xe2\x80\x9d Doctor\xe2\x80\x99s Associates, Inc. v. Casarotto,517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 681, 687 (1996); see ante, at 7.

Illegality is a traditional, generally applicable contract defense. See 5 R. Lord, Williston on Contracts \xc2\xa712.1 (4th ed. 2009). \xe2\x80\x9c[A]uthorities from the earliest time to the present unanimously hold that no court will lend its assistance in any way towards carrying out the terms of an illegal contract.\xe2\x80\x9d Kaiser Steel, 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 77 (quoting McMullen v. Hoffman,174 U.\xc2\xa0S. 639, 654 (1899)). For the reasons stated supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x9317, I would hold that the arbitration agreements\xe2\x80\x99 employer-dictated collective-litigation waivers are unlawful. By declining to enforce those adhesive waivers, courts would place them on the same footing as any other contract provision incompatible with controlling federal law. The FAA\xe2\x80\x99s saving clause can thus achieve harmonization of the FAA and the NLRA without undermining federal labor policy.

The Court urges that our case law\xe2\x80\x94most forcibly, AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion,563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333 (2011)\xe2\x80\x94rules out reconciliation of the NLRA and the FAA through the latter\xe2\x80\x99s saving clause. See ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x939. I disagree. True, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s Arbitration Act decisions establish that the saving clause \xe2\x80\x9coffers no refuge\xe2\x80\x9d for defenses that discriminate against arbitration, \xe2\x80\x9ceither by name or by more subtle methods.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. The Court, therefore, has rejected saving clause salvage where state courts have invoked generally applicable contract defenses to discriminate \xe2\x80\x9ccovertly\xe2\x80\x9d against arbitration. Kindred Nursing Centers L.\xc2\xa0P. v. Clark, 581 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2017) (slip op., at 5). In Concepcion, the Court held that the saving clause did not spare the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s invocation of unconscionability doctrine to establish a rule blocking enforcement of class-action waivers in adhesive consumer contracts. 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 341\xe2\x80\x93344, 346\xe2\x80\x93352. Class proceedings, the Court said, would \xe2\x80\x9csacrific[e] the principal advantage of arbitration\xe2\x80\x94its informality\xe2\x80\x94and mak[e] the process slower, more costly, and more likely to generate procedural morass than final judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 348. Accordingly, the Court concluded, the California Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s rule, though derived from unconscionability doctrine, impermissibly disfavored arbitration, and therefore could not stand. Id., at 346\xe2\x80\x93352.

Here, however, the Court is not asked to apply a generally applicable contract defense to generate a rule discriminating against arbitration. At issue is application of the ordinarily superseding rule that \xe2\x80\x9cillegal promises will not be enforced,\xe2\x80\x9d Kaiser Steel, 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 77, to invalidate arbitration provisions at odds with the NLRA, a pathmarking federal statute. That statute neither discriminates against arbitration on its face, nor by covert operation. It requires invalidation of all employer-imposed contractual provisions prospectively waiving employees\xe2\x80\x99 \xc2\xa77 rights. See supra, at 17, and n. 8; cf. Kindred Nursing Centers, 581 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n. 2 (slip op., at 7, n. 2) (States may enforce generally applicable rules so long as they do not \xe2\x80\x9csingle out arbitration\xe2\x80\x9d for disfavored treatment).

C

Even assuming that the FAA and the NLRA were inharmonious, the NLRA should control. Enacted later in time, the NLRA should qualify as \xe2\x80\x9can implied repeal\xe2\x80\x9d of the FAA, to the extent of any genuine conflict. See Posadas v. National City Bank,296 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497, 503 (1936). Moreover, the NLRA should prevail as the more pinpointed, subject-matter specific legislation, given that it speaks directly to group action by employees to improve the terms and conditions of their employment. See Radzanower v. Touche Ross & Co.,426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 148, 153 (1976) (\xe2\x80\x9ca specific statute\xe2\x80\x9d generally \xe2\x80\x9cwill not be controlled or nullified by a general one\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).[13]

Citing statutory examples, the Court asserts that when Congress wants to override the FAA, it does so expressly. See ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. The statutes the Court cites, however, are of recent vintage.[14] Each was enacted during the time this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions increasingly alerted Congress that it would be wise to leave not the slightest room for doubt if it wants to secure access to a judicial forum or to provide a green light for group litigation before an arbitrator or court. See CompuCredit Corp. v. Greenwood,565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 95, 116 (2012) (Ginsburg, J., dissenting). The Congress that drafted the NLRA in 1935 was scarcely on similar alert.

III

The inevitable result of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will be the underenforcement of federal and state statutes designed to advance the well-being of vulnerable workers. See generally Sternlight, Disarming Employees: How American Employers Are Using Mandatory Arbitration To Deprive Workers of Legal Protections, 80 Brooklyn L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1309 (2015).

The probable impact on wage and hours claims of the kind asserted in the cases now before the Court is all too evident. Violations of minimum-wage and overtime laws are widespread. See Ruan 1109\xe2\x80\x931111; A. Bernhardt et\xc2\xa0al., Broken Laws, Unprotected Workers: Violations of Employment and Labor Laws in America\xe2\x80\x99s Cities 11\xe2\x80\x9316, 21\xe2\x80\x9322 (2009). One study estimated that in Chicago, Los Angeles, and New York City alone, low-wage workers lose nearly $3 billion in legally owed wages each year. Id., at 6. The U.\xc2\xa0S. Department of Labor, state labor departments, and state attorneys general can uncover and obtain recoveries for some violations. See EPI, B. Meixell & R. Eisenbrey, An Epidemic of Wage Theft Is Costing Workers Hundreds of Millions of Dollars a Year 2 (2014), available at https://www.epi.org/files/2014/wage-theft.pdf. Because of their limited resources, however, government agencies must rely on private parties to take a lead role in enforcing wage and hours laws. See Brief for State of Maryland et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 29\xe2\x80\x9333; Glover, The Structural Role of Private Enforcement Mechanisms in Public Law, 53 Wm. & Mary L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1137, 1150\xe2\x80\x931151 (2012) (Department of Labor investigates fewer than 1% of FLSA-covered employers each year).

If employers can stave off collective employment litigation aimed at obtaining redress for wage and hours infractions, the enforcement gap is almost certain to widen. Expenses entailed in mounting individual claims will often far outweigh potential recoveries. See id., at 1184\xe2\x80\x931185 (because \xe2\x80\x9cthe FLSA systematically tends to generate low-value claims,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cmechanisms that facilitate the economics of claiming are required\xe2\x80\x9d); Sutherland v. Ernst & Young LLP, 768 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 547, 552 (SDNY 2011) (finding that an employee utilizing Ernst & Young\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration program would likely have to spend $200,000 to recover only $1,867.02 in overtime pay and an equivalent amount in liquidated damages); cf. Resnik, Diffusing Disputes: The Public in the Private of Arbitration, the Private in Courts, and the Erasure of Rights, 124 Yale L. J. 2804, 2904 (2015) (analyzing available data from the consumer context to conclude that \xe2\x80\x9cprivate enforcement of small-value claims depends on collective, rather than individual, action\xe2\x80\x9d); Amchem Products, Inc. v. Windsor,521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 591, 617 (1997) (class actions help \xe2\x80\x9covercome the problem that small recoveries do not provide the incentive for any individual to bring a solo action prosecuting his or her rights\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).[15]

Fear of retaliation may also deter potential claimants from seeking redress alone. See, e.g., Ruan 1119\xe2\x80\x931121; Bernhardt, supra, at 3, 24\xe2\x80\x9325. Further inhibiting single-file claims is the slim relief obtainable, even of the injunctive kind. See Califano v. Yamasaki,442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 682, 702 (1979) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he scope of injunctive relief is dictated by the extent of the violation established.\xe2\x80\x9d). The upshot: Employers, aware that employees will be disinclined to pursue small-value claims when confined to proceeding one-by-one, will no doubt perceive that the cost-benefit balance of underpaying workers tips heavily in favor of skirting legal obligations.

In stark contrast to today\xe2\x80\x99s decision,[16] the Court has repeatedly recognized the centrality of group action to the effective enforcement of antidiscrimination statutes. With Court approbation, concerted legal actions have played a critical role in enforcing prohibitions against workplace discrimination based on race, sex, and other protected characteristics. See, e.g., Griggs v. Duke Power Co.,401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 424 (1971); Automobile Workers v. Johnson Controls, Inc.,499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 187 (1991). In this context, the Court has comprehended that government entities charged with enforcing antidiscrimination statutes are unlikely to be funded at levels that could even begin to compensate for a significant dropoff in private enforcement efforts. See Newman v. Piggie Park Enterprises, Inc.,390 U.\xc2\xa0S. 400, 401 (1968) (per curiam) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen the Civil Rights Act of 1964 was passed, it was evident that enforcement would prove difficult and that the Nation would have to rely in part upon private litigation as a means of securing broad compliance with the law.\xe2\x80\x9d). That reality, as just noted, holds true for enforcement of wage and hours laws. See supra, at 27.

I do not read the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion to place in jeopardy discrimination complaints asserting disparate-impact and pattern-or-practice claims that call for proof on a groupwide basis, see Brief for NAACP Legal Defense & Educational Fund, Inc., et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 19\xe2\x80\x9325, which some courts have concluded cannot be maintained by solo complainants, see, e.g., Chin v. Port Auth. of N.\xc2\xa0Y. & N.\xc2\xa0J., 685 F.\xc2\xa03d 135, 147 (CA2 2012) (pattern-or-practice method of proving race discrimination is unavailable in non-class actions). It would be grossly exorbitant to read the FAA to devastate Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964,42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e et\xc2\xa0seq., and other laws enacted to eliminate, root and branch, class-based employment discrimination, see Albemarle Paper Co. v. Moody,422 U.\xc2\xa0S. 405, 417, 421 (1975). With fidelity to the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s will, the Court could hardly hold otherwise.

I note, finally, that individual arbitration of employee complaints can give rise to anomalous results. Arbitration agreements often include provisions requiring that outcomes be kept confidential or barring arbitrators from giving prior proceedings precedential effect. See, e.g., App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 16\xe2\x80\x93285, p. 34a (Epic\xe2\x80\x99s agreement); App. in No. 16\xe2\x80\x93300, p. 46 (Ernst & Young\xe2\x80\x99s agreement). As a result, arbitrators may render conflicting awards in cases involving similarly situated employees\xe2\x80\x94even employees working for the same employer. Arbitrators may resolve differently such questions as whether certain jobs are exempt from overtime laws. Cf. Encino Motor Cars, LLC v. Navarro, ante, p. ___ (Court divides on whether \xe2\x80\x9cservice advisors\xe2\x80\x9d are exempt from overtime-pay requirements). With confidentiality and no-precedential-value provisions operative, irreconcilable answers would remain unchecked.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

If these untoward consequences stemmed from legislative choices, I would be obliged to accede to them. But the edict that employees with wage and hours claims may seek relief only one-by-one does not come from Congress. It is the result of take-it-or-leave-it labor contracts harking back to the type called \xe2\x80\x9cyellow dog,\xe2\x80\x9d and of the readiness of this Court to enforce those unbargained-for agreements. The FAA demands no such suppression of the right of workers to take concerted action for their \xe2\x80\x9cmutual aid or protection.\xe2\x80\x9d Accordingly, I would reverse the judgment of the Fifth Circuit in No. 16\xe2\x80\x93307 and affirm the judgments of the Seventh and Ninth Circuits in Nos. 16\xe2\x80\x93285 and 16\xe2\x80\x93300.
