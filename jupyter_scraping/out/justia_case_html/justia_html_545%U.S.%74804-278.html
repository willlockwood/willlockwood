TOWN OF CASTLE ROCK, COLORADO v. GONZALES, individually and a next best friend of her deceased minor children, GONZALES et al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the tenth circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x93278.Argued March 21, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 27, 2005

\n

Respondent filed this suit under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 alleging that petitioner violated the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause when its police officers, acting pursuant to official policy or custom, failed to respond to her repeated reports over several hours that her estranged husband had taken their three children in violation of her restraining order against him. Ultimately, the husband murdered the children. The District Court granted the town\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss, but an en banc majority of the Tenth Circuit reversed, finding that respondent had alleged a cognizable procedural due process claim because a Colorado statute established the state legislature\xe2\x80\x99s clear intent to require police to enforce retraining orders, and thus its intent that the order\xe2\x80\x99s recipient have an entitlement to its enforcement. The court therefore ruled, among other things, that respondent had a protected property interest in the enforcement of her restraining order.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Respondent did not, for Due Process Clause purposes, have a property interest in police enforcement of the restraining order against her husband. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Due Process Clause\xe2\x80\x99s procedural component does not protect everything that might be described as a government \xe2\x80\x9cbenefit\xe2\x80\x9d:\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cTo have a property interest in a benefit, a person \xe2\x80\xa6 must \xe2\x80\xa6 have a legitimate claim of entitlement to it.\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Regents of State Colleges v. Roth, 408 U. S. 564, 577. Such entitlements are created by existing rules or understandings stemming from an independent source such as state law. E.g., ibid. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0A benefit is not a protected entitlement if officials have discretion to grant or deny it. See, e.g., Kentucky Dept. of Corrections v. Thompson, 490 U. S. 454, 462\xe2\x80\x93463. It is inappropriate here to defer to the Tenth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s determination that Colorado law gave respondent a right to police enforcement of the restraining order. This Court therefore proceeds to its own analysis. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Colorado law has not created a personal entitlement to enforcement of restraining orders. It does not appear that state law truly made such enforcement mandatory. A well-established tradition of police discretion has long coexisted with apparently mandatory arrest statutes. Cf. Chicago v. Morales, 527 U. S. 41, 47, n.\xc2\xa02, 62, n.\xc2\xa032. Against that backdrop, a true mandate of police action would require some stronger indication than the Colorado statute\xe2\x80\x99s direction to \xe2\x80\x9cuse every reasonable means to enforce a restraining order\xe2\x80\x9d or even to \xe2\x80\x9carrest \xe2\x80\xa6 or \xe2\x80\xa6 seek a warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d A Colorado officer would likely have some discretion to determine that\xe2\x80\x94despite probable cause to believe a restraining order has been violated\xe2\x80\x94the violation\xe2\x80\x99s circumstances or competing duties counsel decisively against enforcement in a particular instance. The practical necessity for discretion is particularly apparent in a case such as this, where the suspected violator is not actually present and his whereabouts are unknown. In such circumstances, the statute does not appear to require officers to arrest but only to seek a warrant. That, however, would be an entitlement to nothing but procedure, which cannot be the basis for a property interest. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Even if the statute could be said to make enforcement \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory,\xe2\x80\x9d that would not necessarily mean that respondent has an entitlement to enforcement. Her alleged interest stems not from common law or contract, but only from a State\xe2\x80\x99s statutory scheme. If she was given a statutory entitlement, the Court would expect to see some indication of that in the statute itself. Although the statute spoke of \xe2\x80\x9cprotected person[s]\xe2\x80\x9d such as respondent, it did so in connection with matters other than a right to enforcement. Most importantly, it spoke directly to the protected person\xe2\x80\x99s power to \xe2\x80\x9cinitiate\xe2\x80\x9d contempt proceedings if the order was issued in a civil action, which contrasts tellingly with its conferral of a power merely to \xe2\x80\x9crequest\xe2\x80\x9d initiation of criminal contempt proceedings\xe2\x80\x94and even more dramatically with its complete silence about any power to \xe2\x80\x9crequest\xe2\x80\x9d (much less demand) that an arrest be made. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(e)\xc2\xa0Even were the Court to think otherwise about Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s creation of an entitlement, it is not clear that an individual entitlement to enforcement of a restraining order could constitute a \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d interest for due process purposes. Such a right would have no ascertainable monetary value and would arise incidentally, not out of some new species of government benefit or service, but out of a function that government actors have always performed\xe2\x80\x94arresting people when they have probable cause. A benefit\xe2\x80\x99s indirect nature was fatal to a due process claim in O\xe2\x80\x99Bannon v. Town Court Nursing Center, 447 U. S. 773, 787. Here, as there, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he simple distinction between government action that directly affects a citizen\xe2\x80\x99s legal rights \xe2\x80\xa6 and action that is directed against a third party and affects the citizen only \xe2\x80\xa6 incidentally, provides a sufficient answer to\xe2\x80\x9d cases finding government-provided services to be entitlements. Id., at 788. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

TOWN OF CASTLE ROCK, COLORADO, PETITIONER v. JESSICA GONZALES, individually and as next\nbest friend of her deceased minor children, REBECCA GONZALES, KATHERYN\nGONZALES, and LESLIE\nGONZALES

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We decide in this case whether an individual who has obtained a state-law restraining order has a constitutionally protected property interest in having the police enforce the restraining order when they have probable cause to believe it has been violated.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The horrible facts of this case are contained in the complaint that respondent Jessica Gonzales filed in Federal District Court. (Because the case comes to us on appeal from a dismissal of the complaint, we assume its allegations are true. See Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.\xc2\xa0A., 534 U. S. 506, 508, n.\xc2\xa01 (2002).) Respondent alleges that petitioner, the town of Castle Rock, Colorado, violated the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution when its police officers, acting pursuant to official policy or custom, failed to respond properly to her repeated reports that her estranged husband was violating the terms of a restraining order.[Footnote 1]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The restraining order had been issued by a state trial court several weeks earlier in conjunction with respondent\xe2\x80\x99s divorce proceedings. The original form order, issued on May 21, 1999, and served on respondent\xe2\x80\x99s husband on June 4, 1999, commanded him not to \xe2\x80\x9cmolest or disturb the peace of [respondent] or of any child,\xe2\x80\x9d and to remain at least 100 yards from the family home at all times. 366 F.\xc2\xa03d 1093, 1143 (CA10 2004) (en banc) (appendix to dissenting opinion of O\xe2\x80\x99Brien, J.). The bottom of the pre-printed form noted that the reverse side contained \xe2\x80\x9cIMPORTANT NOTICES FOR RESTRAINED PARTIES AND LAW ENFORCEMENT OFFICIALS.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis deleted). The preprinted text on the back of the form included the following \xe2\x80\x9cWARNING\xe2\x80\x9d:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA KNOWING VIOLATION OF A RESTRAINING ORDER IS A CRIME\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. A VIOLATION WILL ALSO CONSTITUTE CONTEMPT OF COURT. YOU MAY BE ARRESTED WITHOUT NOTICE IF A LAW ENFORCEMENT OFFICER HAS PROBABLE CAUSE TO BELIEVE THAT YOU HAVE KNOWINGLY VIOLATED THIS ORDER.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1144.

\n

\nThe preprinted text on the back of the form also included a \xe2\x80\x9cNOTICE TO LAW ENFORCEMENT OFFICIALS,\xe2\x80\x9d which read in part:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cYOU SHALL USE EVERY REASONABLE MEANS TO ENFORCE THIS RESTRAINING ORDER. YOU SHALL ARREST, OR, IF AN ARREST WOULD BE IMPRACTICAL UNDER THE CIRCUMSTANCES, SEEK A WARRANT FOR THE ARREST OF THE RESTRAINED PERSON WHEN YOU HAVE INFORMATION AMOUNTING TO PROBABLE CAUSE THAT THE RESTRAINED PERSON HAS VIOLATED OR ATTEMPTED TO VIOLATE ANY PROVISION OF THIS ORDER AND THE RESTRAINED PERSON HAS BEEN PROPERLY SERVED WITH A COPY OF THIS ORDER OR HAS RECEIVED ACTUAL NOTICE OF THE EXISTENCE OF THIS ORDER.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On June 4, 1999, the state trial court modified the terms of the restraining order and made it permanent. The modified order gave respondent\xe2\x80\x99s husband the right to spend time with his three daughters (ages 10, 9, and 7) on alternate weekends, for two weeks during the summer, and, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98upon reasonable notice,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for a mid-week dinner visit \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98arranged by the parties\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d; the modified order also allowed him to visit the home to collect the children for such \xe2\x80\x9cparenting time.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1097 (majority opinion).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0According to the complaint, at about 5 or 5:30 p.m. on Tuesday, June 22, 1999, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s husband took the three daughters while they were playing outside the family home. No advance arrangements had been made for him to see the daughters that evening. When respondent noticed the children were missing, she suspected her husband had taken them. At about 7:30 p.m., she called the Castle Rock Police Department, which dispatched two officers. The complaint continues: \xe2\x80\x9cWhen [the officers] arrived \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, she showed them a copy of the TRO and requested that it be enforced and the three children be returned to her immediately. [The officers] stated that there was nothing they could do about the TRO and suggested that [respondent] call the Police Department again if the three children did not return home by 10:00 p.m.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 126a.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At approximately 8:30 p.m., respondent talked to her husband on his cellular telephone. He told her \xe2\x80\x9che had the three children [at an] amusement park in Denver.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. She called the police again and asked them to \xe2\x80\x9chave someone check for\xe2\x80\x9d her husband or his vehicle at the amusement park and \xe2\x80\x9cput out an [all points bulletin]\xe2\x80\x9d for her husband, but the officer with whom she spoke \xe2\x80\x9crefused to do so,\xe2\x80\x9d again telling her to \xe2\x80\x9cwait until 10:00 p.m. and see if\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d her husband returned the girls. Id., at 126a\xe2\x80\x93127a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At approximately 10:10 p.m., respondent called the police and said her children were still missing, but she was now told to wait until midnight. She called at midnight and told the dispatcher her children were still missing. She went to her husband\xe2\x80\x99s apartment and, finding nobody there, called the police at 12:10 a.m.; she was told to wait for an officer to arrive. When none came, she went to the police station at 12:50 a.m. and submitted an incident report. The officer who took the report \xe2\x80\x9cmade no reasonable effort to enforce the TRO or locate the three children. Instead, he went to dinner.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 127a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At approximately 3:20 a.m., respondent\xe2\x80\x99s husband arrived at the police station and opened fire with a semiautomatic handgun he had purchased earlier that evening. Police shot back, killing him. Inside the cab of his pickup truck, they found the bodies of all three daughters, whom he had already murdered. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the basis of the foregoing factual allegations, respondent brought an action under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, claiming that the town violated the Due Process Clause because its police department had \xe2\x80\x9can official policy or custom of failing to respond properly to complaints of restraining order violations\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctolerate[d] the non-enforcement of restraining orders by its police officers.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 129a.[Footnote 3] The complaint also alleged that the town\xe2\x80\x99s actions \xe2\x80\x9cwere taken either willfully, recklessly or with such gross negligence as to indicate wanton disregard and deliberate indifference to\xe2\x80\x9d respondent\xe2\x80\x99s civil rights. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before answering the complaint, the defendants filed a motion to dismiss under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6). The District Court granted the motion, concluding that, whether construed as making a substantive due process or procedural due process claim, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s complaint failed to state a claim upon which relief could be granted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A panel of the Court of Appeals affirmed the rejection of a substantive due process claim, but found that respondent had alleged a cognizable procedural due process claim. 307 F.\xc2\xa03d 1258 (CA10 2002). On rehearing en banc, a divided court reached the same disposition, concluding that respondent had a \xe2\x80\x9cprotected property interest in the enforcement of the terms of her restraining order\xe2\x80\x9d and that the town had deprived her of due process because \xe2\x80\x9cthe police never \xe2\x80\x98heard\xe2\x80\x99 nor seriously entertained her request to enforce and protect her interests in the restraining order.\xe2\x80\x9d 366 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1101, 1117. We granted certiorari. 543 U. S. ___ (2004).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution provides that a State shall not \xe2\x80\x9cdeprive any person of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Amdt. 14, \xc2\xa71. In 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, Congress has created a federal cause of action for \xe2\x80\x9cthe deprivation of any rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution and laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Respondent claims the benefit of this provision on the ground that she had a property interest in police enforcement of the restraining order against her husband; and that the town deprived her of this property without due process by having a policy that tolerated nonenforcement of restraining orders.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court of Appeals recognized, we left a similar question unanswered in DeShaney v. Winnebago County Dept. of Social Servs., 489 U. S. 189 (1989), another case with \xe2\x80\x9cundeniably tragic\xe2\x80\x9d facts: Local child-protection officials had failed to protect a young boy from beatings by his father that left him severely brain damaged. Id., at 191\xe2\x80\x93193. We held that the so-called \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d component of the Due Process Clause does not \xe2\x80\x9crequir[e] the State to protect the life, liberty, and property of its citizens against invasion by private actors.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 195. We noted, however, that the petitioner had not properly preserved the argument that\xe2\x80\x94and we thus \xe2\x80\x9cdecline[d] to consider\xe2\x80\x9d whether\xe2\x80\x94state \xe2\x80\x9cchild protection statutes gave [him] an \xe2\x80\x98entitlement\xe2\x80\x99 to receive protective services in accordance with the terms of the statute, an entitlement which would enjoy due process protection.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 195, n.\xc2\xa02.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The procedural component of the Due Process Clause does not protect everything that might be described as a \xe2\x80\x9cbenefit\xe2\x80\x9d: \xe2\x80\x9cTo have a property interest in a benefit, a person clearly must have more than an abstract need or desire\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmore than a unilateral expectation of it. He must, instead, have a legitimate claim of entitlement to it.\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Regents of State Colleges v. Roth, 408 U. S. 564, 577 (1972). Such entitlements are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98of course, \xe2\x80\xa6 not created by the Constitution. Rather, they are created and their dimensions are defined by existing rules or understandings that stem from an independent source such as state law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Paul v. Davis, 424 U. S. 693, 709 (1976) (quoting Roth, supra, at 577); see also Phillips v. Washington Legal Foundation, 524 U. S. 156, 164 (1998).

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our cases recognize that a benefit is not a protected entitlement if government officials may grant or deny it in their discretion. See, e.g., Kentucky Dept. of Corrections v. Thompson, 490 U. S. 454, 462\xe2\x80\x93463 (1989). The Court of Appeals in this case determined that Colorado law created an entitlement to enforcement of the restraining order because the \xe2\x80\x9ccourt-issued restraining order \xe2\x80\xa6 specifically dictated that its terms must be enforced\xe2\x80\x9d and a \xe2\x80\x9cstate statute command[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d enforcement of the order when certain objective conditions were met (probable cause to believe that the order had been violated and that the object of the order had received notice of its existence). 366 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1101, n.\xc2\xa05; see also id., at 1100, n.\xc2\xa04; id., at 1104\xe2\x80\x931105, and n.\xc2\xa09. Respondent contends that we are obliged \xe2\x80\x9cto give deference to the Tenth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of Colorado law on\xe2\x80\x9d whether she had an entitlement to enforcement of the restraining order. Tr. of Oral Arg. 52.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We will not, of course, defer to the Tenth Circuit on the ultimate issue: whether what Colorado law has given respondent constitutes a property interest for purposes of the Fourteenth Amendment. That determination, despite its state-law underpinnings, is ultimately one of federal constitutional law. \xe2\x80\x9cAlthough the underlying substantive interest is created by \xe2\x80\x98an independent source such as state law,\xe2\x80\x99 federal constitutional law determines whether that interest rises to the level of a \xe2\x80\x98legitimate claim of entitlement\xe2\x80\x99 protected by the Due Process Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Memphis Light, Gas & Water Div. v. Craft, 436 U. S. 1, 9 (1978) (emphasis added) (quoting Roth, supra, at 577); cf. United States ex rel. TVA v. Powelson, 319 U. S. 266, 279 (1943). Resolution of the federal issue begins, however, with a determination of what it is that state law provides. In the context of the present case, the central state-law question is whether Colorado law gave respondent a right to police enforcement of the restraining order. It is on this point that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s call for deference to the Tenth Circuit is relevant.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have said that a \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption of deference [is] given the views of a federal court as to the law of a State within its jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Phillips, supra, at 167. That presumption can be overcome, however, see Leavitt v. Jane\xc2\xa0L., 518 U. S. 137, 145 (1996) (per curiam), and we think deference inappropriate here. The Tenth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, which reversed the Colorado District Judge, did not draw upon a deep well of state-specific expertise, but consisted primarily of quoting language from the restraining order, the statutory text, and a state-legislative-hearing transcript. See 366 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1103\xe2\x80\x931109. These texts, moreover, say nothing distinctive to Colorado, but use mandatory language that (as we shall discuss) appears in many state and federal statutes. As for case law: the only state-law cases about restraining orders that the Court of Appeals relied upon were decisions of Federal District Courts in Ohio and Pennsylvania and state courts in New Jersey, Oregon, and Tennessee. Id., at 1104\xe2\x80\x931105, n.\xc2\xa09, 1109.[Footnote 4] Moreover, if we were simply to accept the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion, we would necessarily have to decide conclusively a federal constitutional question (i.e., whether such an entitlement constituted property under the Due Process Clause and, if so, whether petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s customs or policies provided too little process to protect it). We proceed, then, to our own analysis of whether Colorado law gave respondent a right to enforcement of the restraining order.[Footnote 5]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The critical language in the restraining order came not from any part of the order itself (which was signed by the state-court trial judge and directed to the restrained party, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s husband), but from the preprinted notice to law-enforcement personnel that appeared on the back of the order. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. That notice effectively restated the statutory provision describing \xe2\x80\x9cpeace officers\xe2\x80\x99 duties\xe2\x80\x9d related to the crime of violation of a restraining order. At the time of the conduct at issue in this case, that provision read as follows:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(a) Whenever a restraining order is issued, the protected person shall be provided with a copy of such order. A peace officer shall use every reasonable means to enforce a restraining order.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(b) A peace officer shall arrest, or, if an arrest would be impractical under the circumstances, seek a warrant for the arrest of a restrained person when the peace officer has information amounting to probable cause that:

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(I) The restrained person has violated or attempted to violate any provision of a restraining order; and

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(II) The restrained person has been properly served with a copy of the restraining order or the restrained person has received actual notice of the existence and substance of such order.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(c) In making the probable cause determination described in paragraph (b) of this subsection (3), a peace officer shall assume that the information received from the registry is accurate. A peace officer shall enforce a valid restraining order whether or not there is a record of the restraining order in the registry.\xe2\x80\x9d Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(3) (Lexis 1999) (emphases added).

\n

\nThe Court of Appeals concluded that this statutory provision\xe2\x80\x94especially taken in conjunction with a statement from its legislative history,[Footnote 6] and with another statute restricting criminal and civil liability for officers making arrests[Footnote 7]\xe2\x80\x94established the Colorado Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s clear intent \xe2\x80\x9cto alter the fact that the police were not enforcing domestic abuse retraining orders,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus its intent \xe2\x80\x9cthat the recipient of a domestic abuse restraining order have an entitlement to its enforcement.\xe2\x80\x9d 366 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1108. Any other result, it said, \xe2\x80\x9cwould render domestic abuse restraining orders utterly valueless.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1109.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This last statement is sheer hyperbole. Whether or not respondent had a right to enforce the restraining order, it rendered certain otherwise lawful conduct by her husband both criminal and in contempt of court. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(2)(a), (7). The creation of grounds on which he could be arrested, criminally prosecuted, and held in contempt was hardly \xe2\x80\x9cvalueless\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94even if the prospect of those sanctions ultimately failed to prevent him from committing three murders and a suicide.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not believe that these provisions of Colorado law truly made enforcement of restraining orders mandatory. A well established tradition of police discretion has long coexisted with apparently mandatory arrest statutes.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIn each and every state there are long-standing statutes that, by their terms, seem to preclude nonenforcement by the police\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. However, for a number of reasons, including their legislative history, insufficient resources, and sheer physical impossibility, it has been recognized that such statutes cannot be interpreted literally\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [T]hey clearly do not mean that a police officer may not lawfully decline to make an arrest. As to third parties in these states, the full-enforcement statutes simply have no effect, and their significance is further diminished.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 ABA Standards for Criminal Justice 1\xe2\x80\x934.5, commentary, pp. 1\xe2\x80\x93124 to 1\xe2\x80\x93125 (2d ed. 1980) (footnotes omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The deep-rooted nature of law-enforcement discretion, even in the presence of seemingly mandatory legislative commands, is illustrated by Chicago v. Morales, 527 U. S. 41 (1999), which involved an ordinance that said a police officer \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98shall order\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d persons to disperse in certain circumstances, id., at 47, n.\xc2\xa02. This Court rejected out of hand the possibility that \xe2\x80\x9cthe mandatory language of the ordinance \xe2\x80\xa6 afford[ed] the police no discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 62, n.\xc2\xa032. It is, the Court proclaimed, simply \xe2\x80\x9ccommon sense that all police officers must use some discretion in deciding when and where to enforce city ordinances.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Against that backdrop, a true mandate of police action would require some stronger indication from the Colorado Legislature than \xe2\x80\x9cshall use every reasonable means to enforce a restraining order\xe2\x80\x9d (or even \xe2\x80\x9cshall arrest \xe2\x80\xa6 or \xe2\x80\xa6 seek a warrant\xe2\x80\x9d), \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(3)(a), (b). That language is not perceptibly more mandatory than the Colorado statute which has long told municipal chiefs of police that they \xe2\x80\x9cshall pursue and arrest any person fleeing from justice in any part of the state\xe2\x80\x9d and that they \xe2\x80\x9cshall apprehend any person in the act of committing any offense \xe2\x80\xa6 and, forthwith and without any warrant, bring such person before a \xe2\x80\xa6 competent authority for examination and trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x934\xe2\x80\x93112 (Lexis 2004). It is hard to imagine that a Colorado peace officer would not have some discretion to determine that\xe2\x80\x94despite probable cause to believe a restraining order has been violated\xe2\x80\x94the circumstances of the violation or the competing duties of that officer or his agency counsel decisively against enforcement in a particular instance.[Footnote 8] The practical necessity for discretion is particularly apparent in a case such as this one, where the suspected violator is not actually present and his whereabouts are unknown. Cf. Donaldson v. Seattle, 65 Wash. App. 661, 671\xe2\x80\x93672, 831 P.\xc2\xa02d 1098, 1104 (1992) (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is a vast difference between a mandatory duty to arrest [a violator who is on the scene] and a mandatory duty to conduct a follow up investigation [to locate an absent violator]\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. A mandatory duty to investigate would be completely open-ended as to priority, duration and intensity\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent correctly points out that, in the specific context of domestic violence, mandatory-arrest statutes have been found in some States to be more mandatory than traditional mandatory-arrest statutes. Post, at 7\xe2\x80\x9313 (opinion of Stevens, J.). The Colorado statute mandating arrest for a domestic-violence offense is different from but related to the one at issue here, and it includes similar though not identical phrasing. See Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.6(1) (Lexis 1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a peace officer determines that there is probable cause to believe that a crime or offense involving domestic violence \xe2\x80\xa6 has been committed, the officer shall, without undue delay, arrest the person suspected of its commission \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). Even in the domestic-violence context, however, it is unclear how the mandatory-arrest paradigm applies to cases in which the offender is not present to be arrested. As the dissent explains, post, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, and n.\xc2\xa08, much of the impetus for mandatory-arrest statutes and policies derived from the idea that it is better for police officers to arrest the aggressor in a domestic-violence incident than to attempt to mediate the dispute or merely to ask the offender to leave the scene. Those other options are only available, of course, when the offender is present at the scene. See Hanna, No Right to Choose: Mandated Victim Participation in Domestic Violence Prosecutions, 109 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1849, 1860 (1996) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he clear trend in police practice is to arrest the batterer at the scene \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As one of the cases cited by the dissent, post, at 12, recognized, \xe2\x80\x9cthere will be situations when no arrest is possible, such as when the alleged abuser is not in the home.\xe2\x80\x9d Donaldson, 65 Wash. App., at 674, 831 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 1105 (emphasis added). That case held that Washington\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory-arrest statute required an arrest only in \xe2\x80\x9ccases where the offender is on the scene,\xe2\x80\x9d and that it \xe2\x80\x9cd[id] not create an on-going mandatory duty to conduct an investigation\xe2\x80\x9d to locate the offender. Id., at 675, 831 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 1105. Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s restraining-order statute appears to contemplate a similar distinction, providing that when arrest is \xe2\x80\x9cimpractical\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94which was likely the case when the whereabouts of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s husband were unknown\xe2\x80\x94the officers\xe2\x80\x99 statutory duty is to \xe2\x80\x9cseek a warrant\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9carrest.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(3)(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent does not specify the precise means of enforcement that the Colorado restraining-order statute assertedly mandated\xe2\x80\x94whether her interest lay in having police arrest her husband, having them seek a warrant for his arrest, or having them \xe2\x80\x9cuse every reasonable means, up to and including arrest, to enforce the order\xe2\x80\x99s terms,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 29\xe2\x80\x9330.[Footnote 9] Such indeterminacy is not the hallmark of a duty that is mandatory. Nor can someone be safely deemed \xe2\x80\x9centitled\xe2\x80\x9d to something when the identity of the alleged entitlement is vague. See Roth, 408 U. S., at 577 (considering whether \xe2\x80\x9ccertain benefits\xe2\x80\x9d were \xe2\x80\x9csecure[d]\xe2\x80\x9d by rule or understandings); cf. Natale v. Ridgefield, 170 F.\xc2\xa03d 258, 263 (CA2 1999) (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is no reason \xe2\x80\xa6 to restrict the \xe2\x80\x98uncertainty\xe2\x80\x99 that will preclude existence of a federally protectable property interest to the uncertainty that inheres in the exercise of discretion\xe2\x80\x9d). The dissent, after suggesting various formulations of the entitlement in question,[Footnote 10] ultimately contends that the obligations under the statute were quite precise: either make an arrest or (if that is impractical) seek an arrest warrant, post, at 14. The problem with this is that the seeking of an arrest warrant would be an entitlement to nothing but procedure\xe2\x80\x94which we have held inadequate even to support standing, see Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, 504 U. S. 555 (1992); much less can it be the basis for a property interest. See post, at 3\xe2\x80\x934 (Souter, J., concurring). After the warrant is sought, it remains within the discretion of a judge whether to grant it, and after it is granted, it remains within the discretion of the police whether and when to execute it.[Footnote 11] Respondent would have been assured nothing but the seeking of a warrant. This is not the sort of \xe2\x80\x9centitlement\xe2\x80\x9d out of which a property interest is created.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if the statute could be said to have made enforcement of restraining orders \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory\xe2\x80\x9d because of the domestic-violence context of the underlying statute, that would not necessarily mean that state law gave respondent an entitlement to enforcement of the mandate. Making the actions of government employees obligatory can serve various legitimate ends other than the conferral of a benefit on a specific class of people. See, e.g., Sandin v. Conner, 515 U. S. 472, 482 (1995) (finding no constitutionally protected liberty interest in prison regulations phrased in mandatory terms, in part because \xe2\x80\x9c[s]uch guidelines are not set forth solely to benefit the prisoner\xe2\x80\x9d). The serving of public rather than private ends is the normal course of the criminal law because criminal acts, \xe2\x80\x9cbesides the injury [they do] to individuals, \xe2\x80\xa6 strike at the very being of society; which cannot possibly subsist, where actions of this sort are suffered to escape with impunity.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 5 (1769); see also Huntington v. Attrill, 146 U. S. 657, 668 (1892). This principle underlies, for example, a Colorado district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to prosecute a domestic assault, even though the victim withdraws her charge. See People v. Cunefare, 102 P.\xc2\xa03d 302, 311\xe2\x80\x93312 (Colo. 2004) (Bender, J., concurring in part, dissenting in part, and dissenting in part to the judgment).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s alleged interest stems only from a State\xe2\x80\x99s statutory scheme\xe2\x80\x94from a restraining order that was authorized by and tracked precisely the statute on which the Court of Appeals relied. She does not assert that she has any common-law or contractual entitlement to enforcement. If she was given a statutory entitlement, we would expect to see some indication of that in the statute itself. Although Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s statute spoke of \xe2\x80\x9cprotected person[s]\xe2\x80\x9d such as respondent, it did so in connection with matters other than a right to enforcement. It said that a \xe2\x80\x9cprotected person shall be provided with a copy of [a restraining] order\xe2\x80\x9d when it is issued, \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(3)(a); that a law enforcement agency \xe2\x80\x9cshall make all reasonable efforts to contact the protected party upon the arrest of the restrained person,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(3)(d); and that the agency \xe2\x80\x9cshall give [to the protected person] a copy\xe2\x80\x9d of the report it submits to the court that issued the order, \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(3)(e). Perhaps most importantly, the statute spoke directly to the protected person\xe2\x80\x99s power to \xe2\x80\x9cinitiate contempt proceedings against the restrained person if the order [was] issued in a civil action or request the prosecuting attorney to initiate contempt proceedings if the order [was] issued in a criminal action.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(7). The protected person\xe2\x80\x99s express power to \xe2\x80\x9cinitiate\xe2\x80\x9d civil contempt proceedings contrasts tellingly with the mere ability to \xe2\x80\x9crequest\xe2\x80\x9d initiation of criminal contempt proceedings\xe2\x80\x94and even more dramatically with the complete silence about any power to \xe2\x80\x9crequest\xe2\x80\x9d (much less demand) that an arrest be made.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The creation of a personal entitlement to something as vague and novel as enforcement of restraining orders cannot \xe2\x80\x9csimply g[o] without saying.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 17, n.\xc2\xa016 (Stevens, J., dissenting). We conclude that Colorado has not created such an entitlement.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if we were to think otherwise concerning the creation of an entitlement by Colorado, it is by no means clear that an individual entitlement to enforcement of a restraining order could constitute a \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d interest for purposes of the Due Process Clause. Such a right would not, of course, resemble any traditional conception of property. Although that alone does not disqualify it from due process protection, as Roth and its progeny show, the right to have a restraining order enforced does not \xe2\x80\x9chave some ascertainable monetary value,\xe2\x80\x9d as even our \xe2\x80\x9cRoth-type property-as-entitlement\xe2\x80\x9d cases have implicitly required. Merrill, The Landscape of Constitutional Property, 86 Va. L. Rev. 885, 964 (2000).[Footnote 12] Perhaps most radically, the alleged property interest here arises incidentally, not out of some new species of government benefit or service, but out of a function that government actors have always performed\xe2\x80\x94to wit, arresting people who they have probable cause to believe have committed a criminal offense.[Footnote 13]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The indirect nature of a benefit was fatal to the due process claim of the nursing-home residents in O\xe2\x80\x99Bannon v. Town Court Nursing Center, 447 U. S. 773 (1980). We held that, while the withdrawal of \xe2\x80\x9cdirect benefits\xe2\x80\x9d (financial payments under Medicaid for certain medical services) triggered due process protections, id., at 786\xe2\x80\x93787, the same was not true for the \xe2\x80\x9cindirect benefit[s]\xe2\x80\x9d conferred on Medicaid patients when the Government enforced \xe2\x80\x9cminimum standards of care\xe2\x80\x9d for nursing-home facilities, id., at 787. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]n indirect and incidental result of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement action \xe2\x80\xa6 does not amount to a deprivation of any interest in life, liberty, or property.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In this case, as in O\xe2\x80\x99Bannon, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he simple distinction between government action that directly affects a citizen\xe2\x80\x99s legal rights \xe2\x80\xa6 and action that is directed against a third party and affects the citizen only indirectly or incidentally, provides a sufficient answer to\xe2\x80\x9d respondent\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on cases that found government-provided services to be entitlements. Id., at 788. The O\xe2\x80\x99Bannon Court expressly noted, ibid., that the distinction between direct and indirect benefits distinguished Memphis Light, Gas & Water Div. v. Craft, 436 U. S. 1 (1978), one of the government-services cases on which the dissent relies, post, at 19.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude, therefore, that respondent did not, for purposes of the Due Process Clause, have a property interest in police enforcement of the restraining order against her husband. It is accordingly unnecessary to address the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 determination (366 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1110\xe2\x80\x931117) that the town\xe2\x80\x99s custom or policy prevented the police from giving her due process when they deprived her of that alleged interest. See American Mfrs. Mut. Ins. Co. v. Sullivan, 526 U. S. 40, 61 (1999).[Footnote 14]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision and that in DeShaney, the benefit that a third party may receive from having someone else arrested for a crime generally does not trigger protections under the Due Process Clause, neither in its procedural nor in its \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive\xe2\x80\x9d manifestations. This result reflects our continuing reluctance to treat the Fourteenth Amendment as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a font of tort law,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Parratt v. Taylor, 451 U. S. 527, 544 (1981) (quoting Paul v. Davis, 424 U. S., at 701), but it does not mean States are powerless to provide victims with personally enforceable remedies. Although the framers of the Fourteenth Amendment and the Civil Rights Act of 1871, 17 Stat. 13 (the original source of \xc2\xa71983), did not create a system by which police departments are generally held financially accountable for crimes that better policing might have prevented, the people of Colorado are free to craft such a system under state law. Cf. DeShaney, 489 U. S., at 203.[Footnote 15]

Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s complaint does allege that the police officers\xe2\x80\x99 actions \xe2\x80\x9cwere taken either willfully, recklessly or with such gross negligence as to indicate wanton disregard and deliberate indifference to\xe2\x80\x9d her civil rights. App. to Pet. for Cert. 128a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The state cases cited by the dissent that afford a cause of action for police failure to enforce restraining orders, post, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312, 14\xe2\x80\x9315, n.\xc2\xa013, vindicate state common-law or statutory tort claims\xe2\x80\x94not procedural due process claims under the Federal Constitution. See Donaldson v. Seattle, 65 Wash. App. 661, 881 P.\xc2\xa02d 1098 (1992) (city could be liable under some circumstances for per se negligence in failing to meet statutory duty to arrest); Matthews v. Pickett County, 996 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa02d 162 (Tenn. 1999) (county could be liable under Tennessee\xe2\x80\x99s Governmental Tort Liability Act where restraining order created a special duty); Campbell v. Campbell, 294 N.\xc2\xa0J. Super. 18, 682 A.\xc2\xa02d 272 (1996) (rejecting four specific defenses under the New Jersey Tort Claims Act in negligence action against individual officers); Sorichetti v. New York, 65 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa02d 461, 482 N.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 70 (1985) (city breached duty of care arising from special relationship between police and victim); Nearing v. \n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that Jessica Gonzales has shown no violation of an interest protected by the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause, and I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. The Court emphasizes the traditional public focus of law enforcement as reason to doubt that these particular legal requirements to provide police services, however unconditional their form, presuppose enforceable individual rights to a certain level of police protection. Ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316. The Court also notes that the terms of the Colorado statute involved here recognize and preserve the traditional discretion afforded law enforcement officers. Ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9315, and n. 8. Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99s claim of a property right thus runs up against police discretion in the face of an individual demand to enforce, and discretion to ignore an individual instruction not to enforce (because, say, of a domestic reconciliation); no one would argue that the beneficiary of a Colorado order like the one here would be authorized to control a court\xe2\x80\x99s contempt power or order the police to refrain from arresting. These considerations argue against inferring any guarantee of a level of protection or safety that could be understood as the object of a \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate claim of entitlement,\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Regents of State Colleges v. Roth, 408 U. S. 564, 577 (1972), in the nature of property arising under Colorado law.* Consequently, the classic predicate for federal due process protection of interests under state law is missing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Gonzales implicitly recognizes this, when she makes the following argument:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cMs. Gonzales alleges that \xe2\x80\xa6 she was denied the process laid out in the statute. The police did not consider her request in a timely fashion, but instead repeatedly required her to call the station over several hours. The statute promised a process by which her restraining order would be given vitality through careful and prompt consideration of an enforcement request \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Denial of that process drained all of the value from her property interest in the restraining order.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 10.

\n

\nThe argument is unconventional because the state-law benefit for which it claims federal procedural protection is itself a variety of procedural regulation, a set of rules to be followed by officers exercising the State\xe2\x80\x99s executive power: use all reasonable means to enforce, arrest upon demonstrable probable cause, get a warrant, and so on, see ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When her argument is understood as unconventional in this sense, a further reason appears for rejecting its call to apply Roth, a reason that would apply even if the statutory mandates to the police were absolute, leaving the police with no discretion when the beneficiary of a protective order insists upon its enforcement. The Due Process Clause extends procedural protection to guard against unfair deprivation by state officials of substantive state-law property rights or entitlements; the federal process protects the property created by state law. But Gonzales claims a property interest in a state-mandated process in and of itself. This argument is at odds with the rule that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]rocess is not an end in itself. Its constitutional purpose is to protect a substantive interest to which the individual has a legitimate claim of entitlement.\xe2\x80\x9d Olim v. Wakinekona, 461 U. S. 238, 250 (1983); see also Doe by Fein v. District of Columbia, 93 F.\xc2\xa03d 861, 868 (CADC 1996) (per curiam); Doe by Nelson v. Milwaukee County, 903 F.\xc2\xa02d 499, 502\xe2\x80\x93503 (CA7 1990). In putting to rest the notion that the scope of an otherwise discernible property interest could be limited by related state-law procedures, this Court observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he categories of substance and procedure are distinct\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98Property\xe2\x80\x99 cannot be defined by the procedures provided for its deprivation.\xe2\x80\x9d Cleveland Bd. of Ed. v. Loudermill, 470 U. S. 532, 541 (1985). Just as a State cannot diminish a property right, once conferred, by attaching less than generous procedure to its deprivation, ibid., neither does a State create a property right merely by ordaining beneficial procedure unconnected to some articulable substantive guarantee. This is not to say that state rules of executive procedure may not provide significant reasons to infer an articulable property right meant to be protected; but it is to say that we have not identified property with procedure as such. State rules of executive procedure, however important, may be nothing more than rules of executive procedure.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, in every instance of property recognized by this Court as calling for federal procedural protection, the property has been distinguishable from the procedural obligations imposed on state officials to protect it. Whether welfare benefits, Goldberg v. Kelly, 397 U. S. 254 (1970), attendance at public schools, Goss v. Lopez, 419 U. S. 565 (1975), utility services, Memphis Light, Gas & Water Div. v. Craft, 436 U. S. 1 (1978), public employment, Perry v. Sindermann, 408 U. S. 593 (1972), professional licenses, Barry v. Barchi, 443 U. S. 55 (1979), and so on, the property interest recognized in our cases has always existed apart from state procedural protection before the Court has recognized a constitutional claim to protection by federal process. To accede to Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99s argument would therefore work a sea change in the scope of federal due process, for she seeks federal process as a substitute simply for state process. (And she seeks damages under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, for denial of process to which she claimed a federal right.) There is no articulable distinction between the object of Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99s asserted entitlement and the process she desires in order to protect her entitlement; both amount to certain steps to be taken by the police to protect her family and herself. Gonzales\xe2\x80\x99s claim would thus take us beyond Roth or any other recognized theory of Fourteenth Amendment due process, by collapsing the distinction between property protected and the process that protects it, and would federalize every mandatory state-law direction to executive officers whose performance on the job can be vitally significant to individuals affected.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The procedural directions involved here are just that. They presuppose no enforceable substantive entitlement, and Roth does not raise them to federally enforceable status in the name of due process.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Ginsburg joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The issue presented to us is much narrower than is suggested by the far-ranging arguments of the parties and their amici. Neither the tragic facts of the case, nor the importance of according proper deference to law enforcement professionals, should divert our attention from that issue. That issue is whether the restraining order entered by the Colorado trial court on June 4, 1999, created a \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d interest that is protected from arbitrary deprivation by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is perfectly clear, on the one hand, that neither the Federal Constitution itself, nor any federal statute, granted respondent or her children any individual entitlement to police protection. See DeShaney v. Winnebago County Dept. of Social Servs., 489 U. S. 189 (1989). Nor, I assume, does any Colorado statute create any such entitlement for the ordinary citizen. On the other hand, it is equally clear that federal law imposes no impediment to the creation of such an entitlement by Colorado law. Respondent certainly could have entered into a contract with a private security firm, obligating the firm to provide protection to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s family; respondent\xe2\x80\x99s interest in such a contract would unquestionably constitute \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the Due Process Clause. If a Colorado statute enacted for her benefit, or a valid order entered by a Colorado judge, created the functional equivalent of such a private contract by granting respondent an entitlement to mandatory individual protection by the local police force, that state-created right would also qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d entitled to constitutional protection.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I do not understand the majority to rule out the foregoing propositions, although it does express doubts. See ante, at 17 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is by no means clear that an individual entitlement to enforcement of a restraining order could constitute a \xe2\x80\x98property\xe2\x80\x99 interest\xe2\x80\x9d). Moreover, the majority does not contest, see ante, at 18, that if respondent did have a cognizable property interest in this case, the deprivation of that interest violated due process. As the Court notes, respondent has alleged that she presented the police with a copy of the restraining order issued by the Colorado court and requested that it be enforced. Ante, at 2, n.\xc2\xa01. In response, she contends, the officers effectively ignored her. If these allegations are true, a federal statute, Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, provides her with a remedy against the petitioner, even if Colorado law does not. See Cleveland Bd. of Ed. v. Loudermill, 470 U. S. 532 (1985).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The central question in this case is therefore whether, as a matter of Colorado law, respondent had a right to police assistance comparable to the right she would have possessed to any other service the government or a private firm might have undertaken to provide. See Board of Regents of State Colleges v. Roth, 408 U. S. 564, 577 (1972) (\xe2\x80\x9cProperty interests, of course, are not created by the Constitution. Rather, they are created and their dimensions are defined by existing rules or understandings that stem from an independent source such as state law\xe2\x80\x94rules or understandings that secure certain benefits and that support claims of entitlement to those benefits\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There was a time when our tradition of judicial restraint would have led this Court to defer to the judgment of more qualified tribunals in seeking the correct answer to that difficult question of Colorado law. Unfortunately, although the majority properly identifies the \xe2\x80\x9ccentral state-law question\xe2\x80\x9d in this case as \xe2\x80\x9cwhether Colorado law gave respondent a right to police enforcement of the restraining order,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8, it has chosen to ignore our settled practice by providing its own answer to that question. Before identifying the flaws in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling on the merits, I shall briefly comment on our past practice.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision to plunge ahead with its own analysis of Colorado law imprudently departs from this Court\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding policy of paying \xe2\x80\x9cdeference [to] the views of a federal court as to the law of a State within its jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Phillips v. Washington Legal Foundation, 524 U. S. 156, 167 (1998); see also Bishop v. Wood, 426 U. S. 341, 346, and n.\xc2\xa010 (1976) (collecting cases). This policy is not only efficient, but it reflects \xe2\x80\x9cour belief that district courts and courts of appeal are better schooled in and more able to interpret the laws of their respective States.\xe2\x80\x9d Brockett v. Spokane Arcades, Inc., 472 U. S. 491, 500\xe2\x80\x93501 (1985); Hillsborough v. Cromwell, 326 U. S. 620, 629\xe2\x80\x93630 (1946) (endorsing \xe2\x80\x9cgreat deference to the views of the judges of those courts \xe2\x80\x98who are familiar with the intricacies and trends of local law and practice\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, we have declined to show deference only in rare cases in which the court of appeal\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of state law was \xe2\x80\x9cclearly wrong\xe2\x80\x9d or otherwise seriously deficient. See Brockett, 472 U. S., at 500, n.\xc2\xa09; accord, Leavitt v. Jane L., 518 U. S. 137, 145 (1996) (per curiam).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unfortunately, the Court does not even attempt to demonstrate that the six-judge en banc majority was \xe2\x80\x9cclearly wrong\xe2\x80\x9d in its interpretation of Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s domestic restraining order statute; nor could such a showing be made. For it is certainly plausible to construe \xe2\x80\x9cshall use every reasonable means to enforce a restraining order\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cshall arrest,\xe2\x80\x9d Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(3)(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b) (Lexis 1999) (emphases added), as conveying mandatory directives to the police, particularly when the same statute, at other times, tellingly employs different language that suggests police discretion, see \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(6)(a) (\xe2\x80\x9cA peace officer is authorized to use every reasonable means to protect \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d; \xe2\x80\x9cSuch peace officer may transport \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d (emphases added)).[Footnote 1] Moreover, unlike today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, the Court of Appeals was attentive to the legislative history of the statute, focusing on a statement by the statute\xe2\x80\x99s sponsor in the Colorado House, ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa06 (quoting statement), which it took to \xe2\x80\x9cemphasiz[e] the importance of the police\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory enforcement of domestic restraining orders.\xe2\x80\x9d 366 F.\xc2\xa03d 1093, 1107 (CA10 2004) (en banc). Far from overlooking the traditional presumption of police discretion, then, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 diligent analysis of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text, purpose, and history led it to conclude that the Colorado Legislature intended precisely to abrogate that presumption in the specific context of domestic restraining orders. That conclusion is eminently reasonable and, I believe, worthy of our deference.[Footnote 2]

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if the Court had good reason to doubt the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 determination of state law, it would, in my judgment, be a far wiser course to certify the question to the Colorado Supreme Court.[Footnote 3] Powerful considerations support certification in this case. First, principles of federalism and comity favor giving a State\xe2\x80\x99s high court the opportunity to answer important questions of state law, particularly when those questions implicate uniquely local matters such as law enforcement and might well require the weighing of policy considerations for their correct resolution.[Footnote 4] See Elkins v. Moreno, 435 U. S. 647, 662, n.\xc2\xa016 (1978) (sua sponte certifying a question of state law because it is \xe2\x80\x9cone in which state governments have the highest interest\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Arizonans for Official English v. Arizona, 520 U. S. 43, 77 (1997) (\xe2\x80\x9cThrough certification of novel or unsettled questions of state law for authoritative answers by a State\xe2\x80\x99s highest court, a federal court may save \xe2\x80\x98time, energy, and resources, and hel[p] build a cooperative judicial federalism\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (brackets in original)).[Footnote 5] Second, by certifying a potentially dispositive state-law issue, the Court would adhere to its wise policy of avoiding the unnecessary adjudication of difficult questions of constitutional law. See Elkins, 435 U. S., at 661\xe2\x80\x93662 (citing constitutional avoidance as a factor supporting certification). Third, certification would promote both judicial economy and fairness to the parties. After all, the Colorado Supreme Court is the ultimate authority on the meaning of Colorado law, and if in later litigation it should disagree with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s provisional state-law holding, our efforts will have been wasted and respondent will have been deprived of the opportunity to have her claims heard under the authoritative view of Colorado law. The unique facts of this case only serve to emphasize the importance of employing a procedure that will provide the correct answer to the central question of state law. See Brockett, 472 U. S., at 510 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cSpeculation by a federal court about the meaning of a state statute in the absence of a prior state court adjudication is particularly gratuitous when, as is the case here, the state courts stand willing to address questions of state law on certification from a federal court\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 6]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Three flaws in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s rather superficial analysis of the merits highlight the unwisdom of its decision to answer the state-law question de novo. First, the Court places undue weight on the various statutes throughout the country that seemingly mandate police enforcement but are generally understood to preserve police discretion. As a result, the Court gives short shrift to the unique case of \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory arrest\xe2\x80\x9d statutes in the domestic violence context; States passed a wave of these statutes in the 1980\xe2\x80\x99s and 1990\xe2\x80\x99s with the unmistakable goal of eliminating police discretion in this area. Second, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s formalistic analysis fails to take seriously the fact that the Colorado statute at issue in this case was enacted for the benefit of the narrow class of persons who are beneficiaries of domestic restraining orders, and that the order at issue in this case was specifically intended to provide protection to respondent and her children. Finally, the Court is simply wrong to assert that a citizen\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the government\xe2\x80\x99s commitment to provide police enforcement in certain defined circumstances does not resemble any \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional conception of property,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 17; in fact, a citizen\xe2\x80\x99s property interest in such a commitment is just as concrete and worthy of protection as her interest in any other important service the government or a private firm has undertaken to provide.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1994, the Colorado General Assembly passed omnibus legislation targeting domestic violence. The part of the legislation at issue in this case mandates enforcement of a domestic restraining order upon probable cause of a violation, \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(3), while another part directs that police officers \xe2\x80\x9cshall, without undue delay, arrest\xe2\x80\x9d a suspect upon \xe2\x80\x9cprobable cause to believe that a crime or offense of domestic violence has been committed,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.6(1).[Footnote 7] In adopting this legislation, the Colorado General Assembly joined a nationwide movement of States that took aim at the crisis of police underenforcement in the domestic violence sphere by implementing \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory arrest\xe2\x80\x9d statutes. The crisis of underenforcement had various causes, not least of which was the perception by police departments and police officers that domestic violence was a private, \xe2\x80\x9cfamily\xe2\x80\x9d matter and that arrest was to be used as a last resort. Sack, Battered Women and the State: The Struggle for the Future of Domestic Violence Policy, 2004 Wis. L. Rev. 1657, 1662\xe2\x80\x931663 (hereinafter Sack); id., at 1663 (\xe2\x80\x9cBecause these cases were considered noncriminal, police assigned domestic violence calls low priority and often did not respond to them for several hours or ignored them altogether\xe2\x80\x9d). In response to these realities, and emboldened by a well-known 1984 experiment by the Minneapolis police department,[Footnote 8] \xe2\x80\x9cmany states enacted mandatory arrest statutes under which a police officer must arrest an abuser when the officer has probable cause to believe that a domestic assault has occurred or that a protection order has been violated.\xe2\x80\x9d Developments in the Law: Legal Responses to Domestic Violence, 106 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1498, 1537 (1993). The purpose of these statutes was precisely to \xe2\x80\x9ccounter police resistance to arrests in domestic violence cases by removing or restricting police officer discretion; mandatory arrest policies would increase police response and reduce batterer recidivism.\xe2\x80\x9d Sack 1670.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, when Colorado passed its statute in 1994, it joined the ranks of 15 States that mandated arrest for domestic violence offenses and 19 States that mandated arrest for domestic restraining order violations. See Developments in the Law, 106 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 1537, n. 68 (noting statutes in 1993); N. Miller, Institute for Law and Justice, A Law Enforcement and Prosecution Perspective 7, and n. 74, 8, and n. 90 (2003), http://www.ilj.org/dv/dvvawa2000.htm (as visited June 24, 2005, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file) (listing Colorado among the many States that currently have mandatory arrest statutes).[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given the specific purpose of these statutes, there can be no doubt that the Colorado Legislature used the term \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d advisedly in its domestic restraining order statute. While \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d is probably best read to mean \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d in other Colorado statutes that seemingly mandate enforcement, cf. Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x934\xe2\x80\x93112 (Lexis 2004) (police \xe2\x80\x9cshall suppress all riots, disturbances or breaches of the peace, shall apprehend all disorderly persons in the city \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d (emphases added)), it is clear that the elimination of police discretion was integral to Colorado and its fellow States\xe2\x80\x99 solution to the problem of underenforcement in domestic violence cases.[Footnote 10] Since the text of Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s statute perfectly captures this legislative purpose, it is hard to imagine what the Court has in mind when it insists on \xe2\x80\x9csome stronger indication from the Colorado Legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While Colorado case law does not speak to the question, it is instructive that other state courts interpreting their analogous statutes have not only held that they eliminate the police\xe2\x80\x99s traditional discretion to refuse enforcement, but have also recognized that they create rights enforceable against the police under state law. For example, in Nearing v. Weaver, 295 Ore. 702, 670 P.\xc2\xa02d 137 (1983) (en banc), the court held that although the common law of negligence did not support a suit against the police for failing to enforce a domestic restraining order, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory directive formed the basis for the suit because it was \xe2\x80\x9ca specific duty imposed by statute for the benefit of individuals previously identified by judicial order.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 707, 670 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 140.[Footnote 11] In Matthews v. Pickett County, 996 S.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 162 (Tenn. 1999) (on certification to the Sixth Circuit), the court confirmed that the statute mandated arrest for violations of domestic restraining orders, and it held that the \xe2\x80\x9cpublic duty\xe2\x80\x9d defense to a negligence action was unavailable to the defendant police officers because the restraining order had created a \xe2\x80\x9cspecial duty\xe2\x80\x9d to protect the plaintiff. Id., at 165. See also Campbell v. Campbell, 294 N.\xc2\xa0J. Super. 18, 24, 682 A.\xc2\xa02d 272, 274 (1996) (domestic restraining order statute \xe2\x80\x9callows no discretion\xe2\x80\x9d with regard to arrest; \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he duty imposed on the police officer is ministerial\xe2\x80\x9d); Donaldson v. Seattle, 65 Wash. App. 661, 670, 831 P.\xc2\xa02d 1098, 1103 (1992) (\xe2\x80\x9cGenerally, where an officer has legal grounds to make an arrest he has considerable discretion to do so. In regard to domestic violence, the rule is the reverse. If the officer has the legal grounds to arrest pursuant to the statute, he has a mandatory duty to make the arrest\xe2\x80\x9d). To what extent the Colorado Supreme Court would agree with the views of these courts is, of course, an open question, but it does seem rather brazen for the majority to assume that the Colorado Supreme Court would repudiate this consistent line of persuasive authority from other States.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, the Court fails to come to terms with the wave of domestic violence statutes that provides the crucial context for understanding Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s law. The Court concedes that, \xe2\x80\x9cin the specific context of domestic violence, mandatory-arrest statutes have been found in some States to be more mandatory than traditional mandatory-arrest statutes,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 13, but that is a serious understatement. The difference is not a matter of degree, but of kind. Before this wave of statutes, the legal rule was one of discretion; as the Court shows, the \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional,\xe2\x80\x9d general mandatory arrest statutes have always been understood to be \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory\xe2\x80\x9d in name only, see ante, at 11. The innovation of the domestic violence statutes was to make police enforcement, not \xe2\x80\x9cmore mandatory,\xe2\x80\x9d but simply mandatory. If, as the Court says, the existence of a protected \xe2\x80\x9centitlement\xe2\x80\x9d turns on whether \xe2\x80\x9cgovernment officials may grant or deny it in their discretion,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7, the new mandatory statutes undeniably create an entitlement to police enforcement of restraining orders.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Perhaps recognizing this point, the Court glosses over the dispositive question\xe2\x80\x94whether the police enjoyed discretion to deny enforcement\xe2\x80\x94and focuses on a different question\xe2\x80\x94which \xe2\x80\x9cprecise means of enforcement,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 14, were called for in this case. But that question is a red herring. The statute directs that, upon probable cause of a violation, \xe2\x80\x9ca peace officer shall arrest, or, if an arrest would be impractical under the circumstances, seek a warrant for the arrest of a restrained person.\xe2\x80\x9d Colo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(3)(b) (Lexis 1999). Regardless of whether the enforcement called for in this case was arrest or the seeking of an arrest warrant (the answer to that question probably changed over the course of the night as the respondent gave the police more information about the husband\xe2\x80\x99s whereabouts), the crucial point is that, under the statute, the police were required to provide enforcement; they lacked the discretion to do nothing.[Footnote 12] The Court suggests that the fact that \xe2\x80\x9cenforcement\xe2\x80\x9d may encompass different acts infects any entitlement to enforcement with \xe2\x80\x9cindeterminacy.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. But this objection is also unfounded. Our cases have never required the object of an entitlement to be some mechanistic, unitary thing. Suppose a State entitled every citizen whose income was under a certain level to receive health care at a state clinic. The provision of health care is not a unitary thing\xe2\x80\x94doctors and administrators must decide what tests are called for and what procedures are required, and these decisions often involve difficult applications of judgment. But it could not credibly be said that a citizen lacks an entitlement to health care simply because the content of that entitlement is not the same in every given situation. Similarly, the enforcement of a restraining order is not some amorphous, indeterminate thing. Under the statute, if the police have probable cause that a violation has occurred, enforcement consists of either making an immediate arrest or seeking a warrant and then executing an arrest\xe2\x80\x94traditional, well-defined tasks that law enforcement officers perform every day.[Footnote 13]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court similarly errs in speculating that the Colorado Legislature may have mandated police enforcement of restraining orders for \xe2\x80\x9cvarious legitimate ends other than the conferral of a benefit on a specific class of people,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15; see also ibid. (noting that the \xe2\x80\x9cserving of public rather than private ends is the normal course of the criminal law\xe2\x80\x9d). While the Court\xe2\x80\x99s concern would have some bite were we faced with a broadly drawn statute directing, for example, that the police \xe2\x80\x9cshall suppress all riots,\xe2\x80\x9d there is little doubt that the statute at issue in this case conferred a benefit \xe2\x80\x9con a specific class of people\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94namely, recipients of domestic restraining orders. Here, respondent applied for and was granted a restraining order from a Colorado trial judge, who found a risk of \xe2\x80\x9cirreparable injury\xe2\x80\x9d and found that \xe2\x80\x9cphysical or emotional harm\xe2\x80\x9d would result if the husband were not excluded from the family home. 366 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1143 (appendix to dissent of O\xe2\x80\x99Brien, J.). As noted earlier, the restraining order required that the husband not \xe2\x80\x9cmolest or disturb\xe2\x80\x9d the peace of respondent and the daughters, and it ordered (with limited exceptions) that the husband stay at least 100 yards away from the family home. Ibid.[Footnote 14] It also directed the police to \xe2\x80\x9cuse every reasonable means to enforce this \xe2\x80\xa6 order,\xe2\x80\x9d and to arrest or seek a warrant upon probable cause of a violation. Id., at 1144. Under the terms of the statute, when the order issued, respondent and her daughters became \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98protected person[s].\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(1.5)(a) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Protected person\xe2\x80\x99 means the person or persons identified in the restraining order as the person or persons for whose benefit the restraining order was issued\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 15] The statute criminalized the knowing violation of the restraining order, \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(1), and, as already discussed, the statute (as well as the order itself) mandated police enforcement, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x93803.5(3)(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b).[Footnote 16]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the statute\xe2\x80\x99s guarantee of police enforcement is triggered by, and operates only in reference to, a judge\xe2\x80\x99s granting of a restraining order in favor of an identified \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98protected person,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d there is simply no room to suggest that such a person has received merely an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98incidental\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98indirect\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d benefit, see ante, at 18. As one state court put it, domestic restraining order statutes \xe2\x80\x9cidentify with precision when, to whom, and under what circumstances police protection must be afforded. The legislative purpose in requiring the police to enforce individual restraining orders clearly is to protect the named persons for whose protection the order is issued, not to protect the community at large by general law enforcement activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Nearing, 295 Ore., at 712, 670 P.\xc2\xa02d, at 143.[Footnote 17] Not only does the Court\xe2\x80\x99s doubt about whether Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s statute created an entitlement in a protected person fail to take seriously the purpose and nature of restraining orders, but it fails to account for the decisions by other state courts, see supra at 11\xe2\x80\x9312, that recognize that such statutes and restraining orders create individual rights to police action.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given that Colorado law has quite clearly eliminated the police\xe2\x80\x99s discretion to deny enforcement, respondent is correct that she had much more than a \xe2\x80\x9cunilateral expectation\xe2\x80\x9d that the restraining order would be enforced; rather, she had a \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate claim of entitlement\xe2\x80\x9d to enforcement. Roth, 408 U. S., at 577. Recognizing respondent\xe2\x80\x99s property interest in the enforcement of her restraining order is fully consistent with our precedent. This Court has \xe2\x80\x9cmade clear that the property interests protected by procedural due process extend well beyond actual ownership of real estate, chattels, or money.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 571\xe2\x80\x93572. The \xe2\x80\x9ctypes of interests protected as \xe2\x80\x98property\xe2\x80\x99 are varied and, as often as not, intangible, \xe2\x80\x98relating to the whole domain of social and economic fact.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Logan v. Zimmerman Brush Co., 455 U. S. 422, 430 (1982); see also Perry v. Sindermann, 408 U. S. 593, 601 (1972) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[P]roperty\xe2\x80\x99 interests subject to procedural due process protection are not limited by a few rigid, technical forms. Rather, \xe2\x80\x98property\xe2\x80\x99 denotes a broad range of interests that are secured by \xe2\x80\x98existing rules or understandings\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, our cases have found \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d interests in a number of state-conferred benefits and services, including welfare benefits, Goldberg v. Kelly, 397 U. S. 254 (1970); disability benefits, Mathews v. Eldridge, 424 U. S. 319 (1976); public education, Goss v. Lopez, 419 U. S. 565 (1975); utility services, Memphis Light, Gas & Water Div. v. Craft, 436 U. S. 1 (1978); government employment, Cleveland Bd. of Ed. v. Loudermill, 470 U. S. 532 (1985); as well as in other entitlements that defy easy categorization, see, e.g., Bell v. Burson, 402 U. S. 535 (1971) (due process requires fair procedures before a driver\xe2\x80\x99s license may be revoked pending the adjudication of an accident claim); Logan, 455 U. S., at 431 (due process prohibits the arbitrary denial of a person\xe2\x80\x99s interest in adjudicating a claim before a state commission).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Police enforcement of a restraining order is a government service that is no less concrete and no less valuable than other government services, such as education.[Footnote 18] The relative novelty of recognizing this type of property interest is explained by the relative novelty of the domestic violence statutes creating a mandatory arrest duty; before this innovation, the unfettered discretion that characterized police enforcement defeated any citizen\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate claim of entitlement\xe2\x80\x9d to this service. Novel or not, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s claim finds strong support in the principles that underlie our due process jurisprudence. In this case, Colorado law guaranteed the provision of a certain service, in certain defined circumstances, to a certain class of beneficiaries, and respondent reasonably relied on that guarantee. As we observed in Roth, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is a purpose of the ancient institution of property to protect those claims upon which people rely in their daily lives, reliance that must not be arbitrarily undermined.\xe2\x80\x9d 408 U. S., at 577. Surely, if respondent had contracted with a private security firm to provide her and her daughters with protection from her husband, it would be apparent that she possessed a property interest in such a contract. Here, Colorado undertook a comparable obligation, and respondent\xe2\x80\x94with restraining order in hand\xe2\x80\x94justifiably relied on that undertaking. Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s claim of entitlement to this promised service is no less legitimate than the other claims our cases have upheld, and no less concrete than a hypothetical agreement with a private firm.[Footnote 19] The fact that it is based on a statutory enactment and a judicial order entered for her special protection, rather than on a formal contract, does not provide a principled basis for refusing to consider it \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d worthy of constitutional protection.[Footnote 20]

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because respondent had a property interest in the enforcement of the restraining order, state officials could not deprive her of that interest without observing fair procedures.[Footnote 21] Her description of the police behavior in this case and the department\xe2\x80\x99s callous policy of failing to respond properly to reports of restraining order violations clearly alleges a due process violation. At the very least, due process requires that the relevant state decisionmaker listen to the claimant and then apply the relevant criteria in reaching his decision.[Footnote 22] The failure to observe these minimal procedural safeguards creates an unacceptable risk of arbitrary and \xe2\x80\x9cerroneous deprivation[s],\xe2\x80\x9d Mathews, 424 U. S., at 335. According to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s complaint\xe2\x80\x94which we must construe liberally at this early stage in the litigation, see Swierkiewicz v. Sorema N.\xc2\xa0A., 534 U. S. 506, 514 (2002)\xe2\x80\x94the process she was afforded by the police constituted nothing more than a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98sham or a pretense.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Joint Anti&nbhyph;Fascist Refugee Comm. v. McGrath, 341 U. S. 123, 164 (1951) (Frankfurter, J., concurring).

\n
