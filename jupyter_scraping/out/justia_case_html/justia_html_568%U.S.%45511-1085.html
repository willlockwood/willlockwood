\n\t\t\t\tAMGEN INC. et\xc2\xa0al. v. CONNECTICUT RETIREMENT PLANS AND TRUST FUNDS\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x931085.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 5, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 27, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tTo recover damages in a private securities-fraud action under \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and Securities and Exchange Commission Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, a plaintiff must prove, among other things, reliance on a material misrepresentation or omission made by the defendant. Matrixx Initiatives, Inc. v. Siracusano, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Requiring proof of direct reliance \xe2\x80\x9cwould place an unnecessarily unrealistic evidentiary burden on [a] plaintiff who has traded on an impersonal market.\xe2\x80\x9d Basic Inc. v. Levinson, \n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 224\n. Thus, this Court has endorsed a \xe2\x80\x9cfraud-on-the-market\xe2\x80\x9d theory, which permits securities-fraud plaintiffs to invoke a rebuttable presumption of reliance on public, material misrepresentations regarding securities traded in an efficient market. Id., at 241\xe2\x80\x93249. The fraud-on-the-market theory facilitates the certification of securities-fraud class actions by permitting reliance to be proved on a classwide basis.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tInvoking the fraud-on-the-market theory, respondent Connecticut Retirement Plans and Trust Funds (Connecticut Retirement) sought certification of a securities-fraud class action under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(3) against biotechnology company Amgen Inc. and several of its officers (collectively, Amgen). The District Court certified the class, and the Ninth Circuit affirmed. The Ninth Circuit rejected Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Connecticut Retirement was required to prove the materiality of Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations and omissions before class certification in order to satisfy Rule 23(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that \xe2\x80\x9cquestions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members.\xe2\x80\x9d The Ninth Circuit also held that the District Court did not err in refusing to consider rebuttal evidence that Amgen had presented on the issue of materiality at the class-certification stage. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Proof of materiality is not a prerequisite to certification of a securities-fraud class action seeking money damages for alleged violations of \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9326.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The pivotal inquiry in this case is whether proof of materiality is needed to ensure that the questions of law or fact common to the class will \xe2\x80\x9cpredominate over any questions affecting only individual members\xe2\x80\x9d as the litigation progresses. For two reasons, the answer to this question is \xe2\x80\x9cno.\xe2\x80\x9d First, because materiality is judged according to an objective standard, it can be proved through evidence common to the class. TSC Industries, Inc. v. Northway, Inc., \n\n426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 438\n. Thus, it is a common question for Rule 23(b)(3) purposes. Second, a failure of proof on the common question of materiality would not result in individual questions predominating. Instead, it would end the case, for materiality is an essential element of a securities-fraud claim. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s arguments to the contrary are unpersuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9324.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0Amgen points to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s statement in Erica P. John Fund, Inc. v. Halliburton Co., 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, that \xe2\x80\x9csecurities fraud plaintiffs must prove certain things in order to invoke Basic\xe2\x80\x99s rebuttable presumption of reliance,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9cthat the alleged misrepresentations were publicly known .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , that the stock traded in an efficient market, and that the relevant transaction took place \xe2\x80\x98between the time the misrepresentations were made and the time the truth was revealed.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d If these fraud-on-the-market predicates must be proved before class certification, Amgen contends, materiality\xe2\x80\x94another fraud-on-the-market predicate\xe2\x80\x94should be treated no differently. The Court disagrees. The requirement that a putative class representative establish that it executed trades \xe2\x80\x9cbetween the time the misrepresentations were made and the time the truth was revealed\xe2\x80\x9d relates primarily to the Rule 23(a)(3) and (a)(4) inquiries into typicality and adequacy of representation, not to the Rule 23(b)(3) predominance inquiry. And unlike materiality, market efficiency and the public nature of the alleged misrepresentations are not indispensable elements of a Rule 10\xe2\x80\x935 claim. While the failure of common, classwide proof of market efficiency or publicity leaves open the prospect of individualized proof of reliance, the failure of common proof on the issue of materiality ends the case for all class members. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0Amgen also contends that \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy considerations\xe2\x80\x9d militate in favor of requiring precertification proof of materiality. Because class certification can exert substantial pressure on the defendant to settle rather than risk ruinous liability, Amgen asserts, materiality may never be addressed by a court if it is not required to be evaluated at the class-certification stage. In this regard, however, materiality does not differ from other essential elements of a Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 claim, notably, the requirements that the statements or omissions on which the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claims are based were false or misleading and that the alleged statements or omissions caused the plaintiff to suffer economic loss. Significantly, while addressing the settlement pressures associated with securities-fraud class actions, Congress has rejected calls to undo the fraud-on-the-market theory. And contrary to Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s argument that requiring proof of materiality before class certification would conserve judicial resources, Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s position would necessitate time and resource intensive mini-trials on materiality at the class-certification stage. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9322.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Also unavailing is Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s claim that the District Court erred by refusing to consider the rebuttal evidence Amgen proffered in opposing Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s class-certification motion. The Ninth Circuit concluded, and Amgen does not contest, that Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s rebuttal evidence aimed to prove that the misrepresentations and omissions alleged in Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s complaint were immaterial. The potential immateriality of Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations and omissions, however, is no barrier to finding that common questions predominate. Just as a plaintiff class\xe2\x80\x99s inability to prove materiality creates no risk that individual questions will predominate, a definitive rebuttal on the issue of materiality would not undermine the predominance of questions common to the class. Pp. 24\xe2\x80\x9326.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case involves a securities-fraud complaint filed by Connecticut Retirement Plans and Trust Funds (Connecticut Retirement) against biotechnology company Amgen Inc. and several of its officers (collectively, Amgen). Seeking class-action certification under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23, Connecticut Retirement invoked the \xe2\x80\x9cfraud-on-the-market\xe2\x80\x9d presumption endorsed by this Court in Basic Inc. v. Levinson, \n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 224 (1988)\n, and recog- nized most recently in Erica P. John Fund, Inc. v. Halliburton Co., 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011). The fraud-on-the-market premise is that the price of a security traded in an efficient market will reflect all publicly available information about a company; accordingly, a buyer of the security may be presumed to have relied on that information in purchasing the security.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAmgen has conceded the efficiency of the market for the securities at issue and has not contested the public character of the allegedly fraudulent statements on which Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s complaint is based. Nor does Amgen here dispute that Connecticut Retirement meets all of the class-action prerequisites stated in Rule 23(a): (1) the alleged class \xe2\x80\x9cis so numerous that joinder of all members is impracticable\xe2\x80\x9d; (2) \xe2\x80\x9cthere are questions of law or fact common to the class\xe2\x80\x9d; (3) Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s claims are \xe2\x80\x9ctypical of the claims .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the class\xe2\x80\x9d; and (4) Connecticut Retirement will \xe2\x80\x9cfairly and adequately protect the interests of the class.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe issue presented concerns the requirement stated in Rule 23(b)(3) that \xe2\x80\x9cthe questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members.\xe2\x80\x9d Amgen contends that to meet the predominance requirement, Connecticut Retirement must do more than plausibly plead that Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations and misleading omissions materially affected Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s stock price. According to Amgen, certification must be denied unless Connecticut Retirement proves materiality, for immaterial misrepresentations or omissions, by definition, would have no impact on Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s stock price in an efficient market.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile Connecticut Retirement certainly must prove materiality to prevail on the merits, we hold that such proof is not a prerequisite to class certification. Rule 23(b)(3) requires a showing that questions common to the class predominate, not that those questions will be answered, on the merits, in favor of the class. Because materiality is judged according to an objective standard, the materiality of Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations and omissions is a question common to all members of the class Connecticut Retirement would represent. The alleged misrepresentations and omissions, whether material or immaterial, would be so equally for all investors composing the class. As vital, the plaintiff class\xe2\x80\x99s inability to prove materiality would not result in individual questions predominating. Instead, a failure of proof on the issue of materiality would end the case, given that materiality is an essential element of the class members\xe2\x80\x99 securities-fraud claims. As to materiality, therefore, the class is entirely cohesive: It will prevail or fail in unison. In no event will the individual circumstances of particular class members bear on the inquiry.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEssentially, Amgen, also the dissenters from today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, would have us put the cart before the horse. To gain certification under Rule 23(b)(3), Amgen and the dissenters urge, Connecticut Retirement must first establish that it will win the fray. But the office of a Rule 23(b)(3) certification ruling is not to adjudicate the case; rather, it is to select the \xe2\x80\x9cmetho[d]\xe2\x80\x9d best suited to adjudication of the controversy \xe2\x80\x9cfairly and efficiently.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case involves the interaction between federal securities-fraud laws and Rule 23\xe2\x80\x99s requirements for class certification. To obtain certification of a class ac- tion for money damages under Rule 23(b)(3), a plaintiff must satisfy Rule 23(a)\xe2\x80\x99s above-mentioned prerequisites of numerosity, commonality, typicality, and adequacy of representation, see supra, at 1\xe2\x80\x932, and must also establish that \xe2\x80\x9cthe questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members, and that a class action is superior to other available methods for fairly and efficiently adjudicating the controversy.\xe2\x80\x9d To recover damages in a private securities-fraud action under \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, \n\n48Stat.\n891, as amended, \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j(b) (2006 ed., Supp. V), and Securities and Exchange Commission Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.10b\xe2\x80\x935 (2011), a plaintiff must prove \xe2\x80\x9c(1) a material misrepresentation or omission by the defendant; (2) scienter; (3) a connection between the misrepresentation or omission and the purchase or sale of a security; (4) reliance upon the misrepresentation or omission; (5) economic loss; and (6) loss causation.\xe2\x80\x9d Matrixx Initiatives, Inc. v. Siracusano, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9) (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cReliance,\xe2\x80\x9d we have explained, \xe2\x80\x9cis an essential element of the \xc2\xa710(b) private cause of action\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cproof of reliance ensures that there is a proper connection between a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentation and a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Halliburton, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 4) (internal quotation marks omitted). \xe2\x80\x9cThe traditional (and most direct) way\xe2\x80\x9d for a plaintiff to demonstrate reliance \xe2\x80\x9cis by showing that he was aware of a company\xe2\x80\x99s statement and engaged in a relevant transaction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. based on that specific misrepresentation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We have recognized, however, that requiring proof of direct reliance \xe2\x80\x9cwould place an unnecessarily unrealistic evidentiary burden on [a] plaintiff who has traded on an impersonal market.\xe2\x80\x9d Basic, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 245. Accordingly, in Basic the Court endorsed the \xe2\x80\x9cfraud-on-the-market\xe2\x80\x9d theory, which permits certain Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 plaintiffs to invoke a rebuttable presumption of reliance on material misrepresentations aired to the general public. Id., at 241\xe2\x80\x93249.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe fraud-on-the-market theory rests on the premise that certain well developed markets are efficient processors of public information. In such markets, the \xe2\x80\x9cmarket price of shares\xe2\x80\x9d will \xe2\x80\x9creflec[t] all publicly available information.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 246. Few investors in such markets, if any, can consistently achieve above-market returns by trading based on publicly available information alone, for if such above-market returns were readily attainable, it would mean that market prices were not efficiently incorporating the full supply of public information. See R. Brealey, S. Myers, & F. Allen, Principles of Corporate Finance 330 (10th ed. 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n an efficient market, there is no way for most investors to achieve consistently superior rates of return.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Basic, we held that if a market is shown to be efficient, courts may presume that investors who traded securities in that market relied on public, material misrepresentations regarding those securities. See 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 245\xe2\x80\x93247. This presumption springs from the very concept of market efficiency. If a market is generally efficient in incorporating publicly available information into a security\xe2\x80\x99s market price, it is reasonable to presume that a particular public, material misrepresentation will be reflected in the security\xe2\x80\x99s price. Furthermore, it is reasonable to presume that most investors\xe2\x80\x94knowing that they have little hope of outperforming the market in the long run based solely on their analysis of publicly available information\xe2\x80\x94will rely on the security\xe2\x80\x99s market price as an unbiased assessment of the security\xe2\x80\x99s value in light of all public information. Thus, courts may presume that investors trading in efficient markets indirectly rely on public, material misrepresentations through their \xe2\x80\x9creliance on the integrity of the price set by the market.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 245. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he presumption,\xe2\x80\x9d however, is \xe2\x80\x9cjust that, and [can] be rebutted by appropriate evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Halliburton, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 5). See also Basic, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 248\xe2\x80\x93249 (providing examples of showings that would rebut the fraud-on-the-market presumption).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough fraud on the market is a substantive doctrine of federal securities-fraud law that can be invoked by any Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 plaintiff, see, e.g., Black v. Finantra Capital, Inc., 418 F.\xc2\xa03d 203, 209 (CA2 2005); Blackie v. Barrack, 524 F.\xc2\xa02d 891, 908 (CA9 1975), the doctrine has particular significance in securities-fraud class actions. Absent the fraud-on-the-market theory, the requirement that Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 plaintiffs establish reliance would ordinarily preclude certification of a class action seeking money dam- ages because individual reliance issues would overwhelm questions common to the class. See Basic, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 242. The fraud-on-the-market theory, however, facilitates class certification by recognizing a rebuttable presumption of classwide reliance on public, material misrepresentations when shares are traded in an efficient market. Ibid.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn its complaint, Connecticut Retirement alleges that Amgen violated \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 through certain misrepresentations and misleading omissions regarding the safety, efficacy, and marketing of two of its flagship drugs.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t According to Connecticut Retirement, these misrepresentations and omissions artificially inflated the price of Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s stock at the time Connecticut Retirement and numerous other securities buyers purchased the stock. When the truth came to light, Connecticut Retirement asserts, Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s stock price declined, resulting in financial losses to those who purchased the stock at the inflated price. In its answer to Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s complaint, Amgen conceded that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t all relevant times, the market for [its] securities,\xe2\x80\x9d which are traded on the NASDAQ stock exchange, \xe2\x80\x9cwas an efficient market\xe2\x80\x9d; thus, \xe2\x80\x9cthe market for Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s securities promptly digested current information regarding Amgen from all publicly available sources and reflected such information in Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s stock price.\xe2\x80\x9d Consolidated Amended Class Action Complaint \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb6199\xe2\x80\x93200 in No. CV\xe2\x80\x9307\xe2\x80\x932536 (CD Cal.); Answer \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb6199\xe2\x80\x93200.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court granted Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s motion to certify a class action under Rule 23(b)(3) on behalf of all investors who purchased Amgen stock between the date of the first alleged misrepresentation and the date of the last alleged corrective disclosure. After granting Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s request to take an interlocutory appeal from the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s class-certification order, see Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa023(f), the Court of Appeals affirmed. See 660 F.\xc2\xa03d 1170 (CA9 2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAmgen raised two arguments on appeal. First, Amgen contended that the District Court erred by certifying the proposed class without first requiring Connecticut Retirement to prove that Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations and omissions were material. Second, Amgen argued that the District Court erred by refusing to consider certain rebuttal evidence that Amgen had proffered in opposition to Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s class-certification motion. This evidence, in Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s view, demonstrated that the market was well aware of the truth regarding its alleged misrepresentations and omissions at the time the class members purchased their shares.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals rejected both contentions. Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s first argument, the Court of Appeals noted, made the uncontroversial point that immaterial misrepresentations and omissions \xe2\x80\x9cby definition [do] not affect .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. stock price[s] in an efficient market.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1175. Thus, where misrepresentations and omissions are not material, there is no basis for presuming classwide reliance on those misrepresentations and omissions through the information-processing mechanism of the market price. \xe2\x80\x9cThe problem with that argument,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals ob-served, is evident: \xe2\x80\x9c[B]ecause materiality is an element of the merits of their securities fraud claim, the plaintiffs cannot both fail to prove materiality yet still have a viable claim for which they would need to prove reliance individually.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court of Appeals thus concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cproof of materiality is not necessary\xe2\x80\x9d to ensure compliance with Rule 23(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that common questions predominate. Id., at 1177.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWith respect to Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s second argument, the Court of Appeals determined that Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s proffered rebuttal evidence was merely \xe2\x80\x9ca method of refuting [the] materi- ality\xe2\x80\x9d of the misrepresentations and omissions alleged in Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s complaint. Ibid. Having al- ready concluded that a securities-fraud plaintiff does not need to prove materiality before class certification, the court similarly held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe district court correctly refused to consider\xe2\x80\x9d Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s rebuttal evidence \xe2\x80\x9cat the class certification stage.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), to resolve a conflict among the Courts of Appeals over whether district courts must require plaintiffs to prove, and must allow defendants to present evidence rebutting, the element of materiality before certifying a class action under \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935. Compare 660 F.\xc2\xa03d 1170 (case below); and Schleicher v. Wendt, 618 F.\xc2\xa03d 679, 687 (CA7 2010) (materiality need not be proved at the class-certification stage), with In\xc2\xa0re Salomon Analyst Metromedia Litigation, 544 F.\xc2\xa03d 474, 484\xe2\x80\x93485, 486, n. 9 (CA2 2008) (plaintiff must prove, and defendant may present evidence rebutting, materiality before class certification). See also In\xc2\xa0re DVI, Inc. Securities Litigation, 639 F.\xc2\xa03d 623, 631\xe2\x80\x93632, 637\xe2\x80\x93638 (CA3 2011) (plaintiff need not prove materiality before class certification, but defendant may present rebuttal evidence on the issue).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe only issue before us in this case is whether Connecticut Retirement has satisfied Rule 23(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that \xe2\x80\x9cquestions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members.\xe2\x80\x9d Although we have cautioned that a court\xe2\x80\x99s class-certification analysis must be \xe2\x80\x9crigorous\xe2\x80\x9d and may \xe2\x80\x9centail some overlap with the merits of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s underlying claim,\xe2\x80\x9d Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 10) (internal quotation marks omitted), Rule 23 grants courts no license to engage in free-ranging merits inquiries at the certification stage. Merits questions may be considered to the extent\xe2\x80\x94but only to the extent\xe2\x80\x94that they are relevant to determining whether the Rule 23 prerequisites for class certification are satisfied. See id., at ___, n.\xc2\xa06 (slip op., at 10, n.\xc2\xa06) (a district court has no \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98authority to conduct a preliminary inquiry into the merits of a suit\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d at class certification unless it is necessary \xe2\x80\x9cto determine the propriety of certification\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Eisen v. Carlisle & Jacquelin, \n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 156, \n177 (1974)\n)); Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s 2003 Note on subd. (c)(1) of Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa023, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 144 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n evaluation of the probable outcome on the merits is not properly part of the certification decision.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBearing firmly in mind that the focus of Rule 23(b)(3) is on the predominance of common questions, we turn to Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the courts below erred by failing to require Connecticut Retirement to prove the material- ity of Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations and omissions before certifying Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s proposed class. As Amgen notes, materiality is not only an element of the Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 cause of action; it is also an essential predicate of the fraud-on-the-market theory. See Basic, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 247 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]here materially misleading statements have been disseminated into an impersonal, well-developed market for securities, the reliance of individual plaintiffs on the integrity of the market price may be presumed.\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). That theory, Amgen correctly observes, is premised on the understanding that in an efficient market, all publicly available information is rapidly incorporated into, and thus transmitted to investors through, the market price. See id., at 246\xe2\x80\x93247. Because immaterial in- formation, by definition, does not affect market price, it cannot be relied upon indirectly by investors who, as the fraud-on-the-market theory presumes, rely on the mar- ket price\xe2\x80\x99s integrity. Therefore, the fraud-on-the-market theory cannot apply absent a material misrepresentation or omission. And without the fraud-on-the-market theory, the element of reliance cannot be proved on a classwide basis through evidence common to the class. See id., at 242. It thus follows, Amgen contends, that materiality must be proved before a securities-fraud class action can be certified.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tContrary to Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s argument, the key question in this case is not whether materiality is an essential predicate of the fraud-on-the-market theory; indisputably it is.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Instead, the pivotal inquiry is whether proof of materiality is needed to ensure that the questions of law or fact common to the class will \xe2\x80\x9cpredominate over any questions affecting only individual members\xe2\x80\x9d as the litigation progresses. Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa023(b)(3). For two reasons, the answer to this question is clearly \xe2\x80\x9cno.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he question of materiality .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is an objective one, involving the significance of an omitted or misrepresented fact to a reasonable investor,\xe2\x80\x9d materiality can be proved through evidence common to the class. TSC Industries, Inc. v. Northway, Inc., \n\n426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 438, \n445 (1976)\n. Consequently, materiality is a \xe2\x80\x9ccommon questio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of Rule 23(b)(3). Basic, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 242 (listing \xe2\x80\x9cmateriality\xe2\x80\x9d as one of the questions common to the Basic class members).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, there is no risk whatever that a failure of proof on the common question of materiality will result in individual questions predominating. Because materiality is an essential element of a Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 claim, see Matrixx Initiatives, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9), Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s failure to present sufficient evidence of materiality to defeat a summary-judgment motion or to prevail at trial would not cause individual reliance questions to overwhelm the questions common to the class. Instead, the failure of proof on the element of materiality would end the case for one and for all; no claim would re- main in which individual reliance issues could potentially predominate.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTotally misapprehending our essential point, Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x99 dissent asserts that our \xe2\x80\x9centire argument is based on the assumption that the fraud-on-the-market presumption need not be shown at certification because it will be proved later on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 11, n.\xc2\xa09. Our position is not so based. We rest, instead, entirely on the text of Rule 23(b)(3), which provides for class certification if \xe2\x80\x9cthe questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members.\xe2\x80\x9d A failure of proof on the common question of materiality ends the litigation and thus will never cause individual questions of reliance or anything else to overwhelm questions common to the class. Therefore, under the plain language of Rule 23(b)(3), plaintiffs are not required to prove materiality at the class-certification stage. In other words, they need not, at that threshold, prove that the predominating question will be answered in their favor.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Thomas urges that a plaintiff seeking class certification \xe2\x80\x9cmust show that the elements of [her] claim are susceptible to classwide proof.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 7. See also post, at 11 (criticizing the Court for failing to focus its analysis on \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the element of reliance is susceptible to classwide proof\xe2\x80\x9d). From this premise, Justice Thomas concludes that Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 plaintiffs must prove material- ity before class certification because (1) \xe2\x80\x9cmateriality is a necessary component of fraud on the market,\xe2\x80\x9d and (2) without fraud on the market, the Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 element of reliance is not \xe2\x80\x9csusceptible of a classwide answer.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6, 10\xe2\x80\x9311. See also post, at 12 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f a plaintiff wishes to use Basic\xe2\x80\x99s presumption to prove that reliance is a common question, he must establish the entire presumption, including materiality, at the class certification stage.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRule 23(b)(3), however, does not require a plaintiff seeking class certification to prove that each \xe2\x80\x9celemen[t] of [her] claim [is] susceptible to classwide proof.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 7. What the rule does require is that common questions \xe2\x80\x9cpredominate over any questions affecting only individual [class] members.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa023(b)(3) (emphasis added). Nowhere does Justice Thomas explain how, in an action invoking the Basic presumption, a plaintiff class\xe2\x80\x99s failure to prove an essential element of its claim for relief will result in individual questions predominating over common ones. Absent proof of materiality, the claim of the Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 class will fail in its entirety; there will be no remaining individual questions to adjudicate.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsequently, proof of materiality is not required to establish that a proposed class is \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently cohesive to warrant adjudication by representation\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the focus of the predominance inquiry under Rule 23(b)(3). Amchem Products, Inc. v. Windsor, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 591, \n623 (1997)\n. No doubt a clever mind could conjure up fantastic scenarios in which an individual investor might rely on immaterial information (think of the superstitious investor who sells her securities based on a CEO\xe2\x80\x99s statement that a black cat crossed the CEO\xe2\x80\x99s path that morning). But such objectively unreasonable reliance does not give rise to a Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 claim. See TSC Industries, 426 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 445 (materiality is judged by an objective standard). Thus, \xe2\x80\x9cthe individualized questions of reliance,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 9, n.\xc2\xa08, that hypothetically might arise when a failure of proof on the issue of materiality dooms the fraud-on-the-market class are far more imaginative than real. Such \xe2\x80\x9cindividualized questions\xe2\x80\x9d do not undermine class cohesion and thus cannot be said to \xe2\x80\x9cpredominate\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of Rule 23(b)(3).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause the question of materiality is common to the class, and because a failure of proof on that issue would not result in questions \xe2\x80\x9caffecting only individual members\xe2\x80\x9d predominating, Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa023(b)(3), Connecticut Retirement was not required to prove the materiality of Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations and omissions at the class-certification stage. This is not a case in which the asserted problem\xe2\x80\x94i.e., that the plaintiff class cannot prove materiality\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cexhibits some fatal dissimilarity\xe2\x80\x9d among class members that would make use of the class-action device inefficient or unfair. Nagareda, Class Certification in the Age of Aggregate Proof, 84 N. Y. U. L. Rev. 97, 107 (2009). Instead, what Amgen alleges is \xe2\x80\x9ca fatal similarity\xe2\x80\x94[an alleged] failure of proof as to an element of the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 cause of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Such a contention is properly addressed at trial or in a ruling on a summary-judgment motion. The allegation should not be resolved in deciding whether to certify a proposed class. Ibid. See also Schleicher, 618 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 687 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hether a statement is materially false is a question common to all class members and therefore may be resolved on a class-wide basis after certification.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tInsisting that materiality must be proved at the class-certification stage, Amgen relies chiefly on two arguments, neither of which we find persuasive.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAmgen points first to our statement in Halliburton that \xe2\x80\x9csecurities fraud plaintiffs must prove certain things in order to invoke Basic\xe2\x80\x99s rebuttable presumption of reliance,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9cthat the alleged misrepresentations were publicly known .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , that the stock traded in an efficient market, and that the relevant transaction took place \xe2\x80\x98between the time the misrepresentations were made and the time the truth was revealed.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 5\xe2\x80\x936) (quoting Basic, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 248, n. 27). See also Dukes, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa06 (slip op., at 11, n.\xc2\xa06) (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]laintiffs seeking 23(b)(3) certification [of a securities-fraud class action] must prove that their shares were traded on an efficient market.\xe2\x80\x9d). If these fraud-on-the-market predicates must be proved before class certification, Amgen contends, materiality\xe2\x80\x94another fraud-on-the-market predicate\xe2\x80\x94should be treated no differently.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe disagree. As an initial matter, the requirement that a putative class representative establish that it executed trades \xe2\x80\x9cbetween the time the misrepresentations were made and the time the truth was revealed\xe2\x80\x9d relates primarily to the Rule 23(a)(3) and (a)(4) inquiries into typicality and adequacy of representation, not to the Rule 23(b)(3) predominance inquiry. Basic, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 248, n. 27.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t A security\xe2\x80\x99s market price cannot be affected by a misrepresentation not yet made, and in an efficient market, a misrepresentation\xe2\x80\x99s impact on market price is quickly nullified once the truth comes to light. Thus, a plaintiff whose relevant transactions were not executed between the time the misrepresentation was made and the time the truth was revealed cannot be said to have indirectly relied on the misrepresentation through its reliance on the integrity of the market price.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Such a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claims, therefore, would not be \xe2\x80\x9ctypical\xe2\x80\x9d of the claims of investors who did trade during the window between misrepresentation and truth revelation. Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa023(a)(3). Nor could a court confidently conclude that such a plaintiff would \xe2\x80\x9cfairly and adequately protect the interests\xe2\x80\x9d of investors who traded during the relevant window. Rule 23(a)(4). The requirement that the fraud-on-the-market theory\xe2\x80\x99s trade-timing predicate be established before class certification thus sheds little light on the question whether materiality must also be proved at the class-certification stage.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAmgen is not aided by Halliburton\xe2\x80\x99s statement that market efficiency and the public nature of the alleged misrepresentations must be proved before a securities-fraud class action can be certified. As Amgen notes, market efficiency, publicity, and materiality can all be proved on a classwide basis. Furthermore, they are all essential predicates of the fraud-on-the-market theory. Unless those predicates are established, there is no basis for presuming that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations were reflected in the security\xe2\x80\x99s market price, and hence no grounding for any contention that investors indirectly relied on those misrepresentations through their reliance on the integrity of the market price. But unlike materiality, market efficiency and publicity are not indispensable elements of a Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 claim. See Matrixx Initiatives, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9) (listing elements of a Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 claim). Thus, where the market for a security is inefficient or the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations were not aired publicly, a plaintiff cannot invoke the fraud-on-the-market presumption. She can, however, attempt to establish reliance through the \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional\xe2\x80\x9d mode of demonstrating that she was personally \xe2\x80\x9caware of [the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] statement and engaged in a relevant transaction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. based on that specific misrepresentation.\xe2\x80\x9d Halliburton, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 4). Individualized reliance issues would predominate in such a lawsuit. See Basic, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 242. The litigation, therefore, could not be certified under Rule 23(b)(3) as a class action, but the initiating plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim would remain live; it would not be \xe2\x80\x9cdead on arrival.\xe2\x80\x9d 660 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1175.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA failure of proof on the issue of materiality, in contrast, not only precludes a plaintiff from invoking the fraud-on-the-market presumption of classwide reliance; it also establishes as a matter of law that the plaintiff cannot prevail on the merits of her Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 claim. Materiality thus differs from the market-efficiency and publicity predicates in this critical respect: While the failure of common, classwide proof on the issues of market efficiency and publicity leaves open the prospect of individualized proof of reliance, the failure of common proof on the issue of materiality ends the case for the class and for all indi- viduals alleged to compose the class. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 20 (\xe2\x80\x9cUnless the failure of common proof gives rise to a need for individualized proof, it does not cast doubt on the propriety of class certification.\xe2\x80\x9d). In short, there can be no actionable reliance, individually or collectively, on immaterial information. Be- cause a failure of proof on the issue of materiality, unlike the issues of market efficiency and publicity, does not give rise to any prospect of individual questions overwhelming common ones, materiality need not be proved prior to Rule 23(b)(3) class certification.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAmgen also contends that certain \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy considerations\xe2\x80\x9d militate in favor of requiring precertification proof of materiality. Brief for Petitioners 28. An order granting class certification, Amgen observes, can exert substantial pressure on a defendant \xe2\x80\x9cto settle rather than incur the costs of defending a class action and run the risk of potentially ruinous liability.\xe2\x80\x9d Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s 1998 Note on subd. (f) of Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa023, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S. C. App., p. 143. See also AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 16) (class actions can entail a \xe2\x80\x9crisk of \xe2\x80\x98in terrorem\xe2\x80\x99 settlements\xe2\x80\x9d). Absent a requirement to evaluate materiality at the class-certification stage, Amgen contends, the issue may never be addressed by a court, for the defendant will surrender and settle soon after a class is certified. Insistence on proof of materiality before certifying a securities-fraud class action, Amgen thus urges, ensures that the issue will be adjudicated and not forgone. See also post, at 4 (Scalia, J., dissenting) (expressing the same concerns).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this regard, however, materiality does not differ from other essential elements of a Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 claim, notably, the requirements that the statements or omissions on which the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claims are based were false or misleading and that the alleged statements or omissions caused the plaintiff to suffer economic loss. See Matrixx Initiatives, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 9). Settlement pressure exerted by class certification may prevent judicial resolution of these issues. Yet this Court has held that loss causation and the falsity or misleading nature of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s alleged statements or omissions are common questions that need not be adjudicated before a class is certified. See Halliburton, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 3) (loss causation need not be proved at the class-certification stage); Basic, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 242 (\xe2\x80\x9cthe falsity or misleading nature of the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. public statements\xe2\x80\x9d allegedly made by the defendant is a \xe2\x80\x9ccommon questio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Schleicher, 618 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 685 (falsity of alleged mis- statements need not be proved before certification of a securities-fraud class action).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress, we count it significant, has addressed the settlement pressures associated with securities-fraud class actions through means other than requiring proof of materiality at the class-certification stage. In enacting the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PSLRA), \n\n109Stat.\n737, Congress recognized that although private securities-fraud litigation furthers important public-policy interests, prime among them, deterring wrongdoing and providing restitution to defrauded investors, such law- suits have also been subject to abuse, including the \xe2\x80\x9cextract[ion]\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cextortionate \xe2\x80\x98settlements\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of frivolous claims. H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x93369, pp. 31\xe2\x80\x9332 (1995). The PSLRA\xe2\x80\x99s response to the perceived abuses was, inter alia, to \xe2\x80\x9cimpos[e] heightened pleading requirements\xe2\x80\x9d for securities-fraud actions, \xe2\x80\x9climit recoverable damages and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, provide a \xe2\x80\x98safe harbor\xe2\x80\x99 for forward-looking statements, impose new restrictions on the selection of (and compensation awarded to) lead plaintiffs, mandate imposition of sanctions for frivolous litigation, and authorize a stay of discovery pending resolution of any motion to dismiss.\xe2\x80\x9d Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith Inc. v. Dabit, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 71\n\xe2\x80\x9382 (2006). See also \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934 (2006 ed. and Supp. V). Congress later fortified the PSLRA by enacting the Securities Litigation Uniform Standards Act of 1998, \n\n112Stat.\n3227, which curtailed plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 ability to evade the PSLRA\xe2\x80\x99s limitations on federal securities-fraud litigation by bringing class-action suits under state rather than federal law. See \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778bb(f)(1) (2006 ed.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile taking these steps to curb abusive securities-fraud lawsuits, Congress rejected calls to undo the fraud-on-the-market presumption of classwide reliance endorsed in Basic. See Langevoort, Basic at Twenty: Rethinking Fraud on the Market, 2009 Wis. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 151, 153, and n. 8 (noting that the initial version of H.\xc2\xa0R. 10, 104th Cong., 1st Sess. (1995), an unenacted bill that, like the PSLRA, was designed to curtail abuses in private securities litigation, \xe2\x80\x9cwould have undone Basic\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Common Sense Legal Reform Act: Hearings before the Subcommittee on Telecommunications and Finance of the House Committee on Commerce, 104th Cong., 1st Sess., 92, 236\xe2\x80\x93237, 251\xe2\x80\x93252, 272 (1995) (witnesses criticized the fraud-on-the-market presumption and expressed support for H.\xc2\xa0R. 10\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that securities-fraud plaintiffs prove direct reliance). Nor did Congress decree that securities-fraud plaintiffs prove each element of their claim before obtaining class certification. Because Congress has homed in on the precise policy concerns raised in Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s brief, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e do not think it appropriate for the judiciary to make its own further adjustments by reinterpreting Rule 23 to make likely success on the merits essential to class certification in securities-fraud suits.\xe2\x80\x9d Schleicher, 618 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 686; cf. Smith v. Bayer Corp., 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress\xe2\x80\x99s decision to address the relitigation concerns associated with class actions through the mechanism of removal provides yet another reason for federal courts to adhere in this context to longstanding principles of preclusion.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition to seeking our aid in warding off \xe2\x80\x9cin terrorem\xe2\x80\x9d settlements, Amgen also argues that requiring proof of materiality before class certification would conserve judicial resources by sparing judges the task of overseeing large class proceedings in which the essential element of reliance cannot be proved on a classwide basis. In reality, however, it is Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s position, not the judgments of the lower courts in this case, that would waste judicial resources. Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s argument, if embraced, would necessitate a mini-trial on the issue of materiality at the class-certification stage. Such preliminary adjudications would entail considerable expenditures of judicial time and resources, costs scarcely anticipated by Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(c)(1)(A), which instructs that the decision whether to certify a class action be made \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t an early practicable time.\xe2\x80\x9d If the class is certified, materiality might have to be shown all over again at trial. And if certification is denied for failure to prove materiality, nonnamed class members would not be bound by that determination. See Smith, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 12\xe2\x80\x9318). They would be free to renew the fray, perhaps in another forum, perhaps with a stronger showing of materiality.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGiven the tenuousness of Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s judicial-economy argument, Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s policy arguments ultimately return to the contention that private securities-fraud actions should be hemmed in to mitigate their potentially \xe2\x80\x9cvexatiou[s]\xe2\x80\x9d character. Blue Chip Stamps v. Manor Drug Stores, \n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 723, \n739 (1975)\n. We have already noted what Congress has done to control exorbitant securities-fraud actions. See supra, at 19\xe2\x80\x9320. Congress, the Executive Branch, and this Court, moreover, have \xe2\x80\x9crecognized that meritorious private actions to enforce federal antifraud securities laws are an essential supplement to criminal prosecutions and civil enforcement actions brought, respectively, by the Department of Justice and the Securities and Exchange Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d Tellabs, Inc. v. Makor Issues & Rights, Ltd., \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 308, \n313 (2007)\n; see H.\xc2\xa0R. Conf. Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x93369, at 31; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 1. See also Amchem, 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 617 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The policy at the very core of the class action mechanism is to overcome the problem that small recoveries do not provide the incentive for any individual to bring a solo action prosecuting his or her rights.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Mace v. Van Ru Credit Corp., 109 F.\xc2\xa03d 338, 344 (CA7 1997))). We have no warrant to encumber securities-fraud litigation by adopting an atextual requirement of precertification proof of materiality that Congress, despite its extensive in- volvement in the securities field, has not sanctioned.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Scalia acknowledges that proof of materiality is not required to satisfy Rule 23(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s predominance requirement. See post, at 1. Nevertheless, he maintains that full satisfaction of Rule 23\xe2\x80\x99s requirements is insufficient to obtain class certification under Basic. In Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s view, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Basic established a special rule: A securities-fraud class action cannot be certified unless all of the prerequisites of the fraud-on-the-market presumption of reliance, including materiality, have first been established. Post, at 2.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe purported rule is Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s invention. It cannot be attributed to anything the Court said in Basic. That decision is best known for its endorsement of the fraud-on-the-market theory. But the opinion also established something more. It stated the proper standard for judging the materiality of misleading statements regarding the existence and status of preliminary merger discussions. See 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 230\xe2\x80\x93241, 250 (\xe2\x80\x9cMateriality in the merger context depends on the probability that the transaction will be consummated, and its significance to the issuer of the securities.\xe2\x80\x9d). The District Court in Basic certified a class of investors whose share prices were allegedly depressed by misleading statements that disguised ongoing merger negotiations. Id., at 228. Postcertification, the court granted summary judgment to the defendants on the ground that the alleged misstatements were immaterial as a matter of law. Id., at 228\xe2\x80\x93229. The Court of Appeals affirmed the class certification but reversed the grant of summary judgment. Id., at 229. This Court, in turn, vacated the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment and remanded for further proceedings on the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 summary-judgment motion in light of the materiality standard set forth in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. Id., at 240\xe2\x80\x93241, 250. Notably, however, we did not disturb the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s class-certification order, which we stated \xe2\x80\x9cwas appropriate when made.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 250.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf Justice Scalia were correct that our decision in Basic demands proof of materiality before class certification, the Court in Basic should have ordered the lower courts to reconsider on remand both the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 entitlement to summary judgment and the propriety of class certification. Instead, the Court expressly endorsed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s class-certification order while at the same time recognizing that further proceedings were necessary to determine whether the plaintiffs had mustered sufficient evidence to satisfy the relatively lenient standard for avoiding summary judgment. See Anderson v. Liberty Lobby, Inc., \n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 242, \n248 (1986)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]ummary judgment will not lie if .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the evidence is such that a reasonable jury could return a verdict for the nonmoving party.\xe2\x80\x9d). Unlike Justice Scalia, we are unwilling to presume that Basic announced a rule requiring precertification proof of materiality when Basic failed to apply any such rule to the very case before it.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAmgen also argues that the District Court erred by refusing to consider the rebuttal evidence Amgen proffered in opposing Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s class-certification motion. This evidence, Amgen contends, showed that \xe2\x80\x9cin light of all the information available to the market,\xe2\x80\x9d its alleged misrepresentations and misleading omissions \xe2\x80\x9ccould not be presumed to have altered the market price because they would not have \xe2\x80\x98significantly altered the total mix of information made available.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 40\xe2\x80\x9341 (quoting Basic, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 232). For example, Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s complaint alleges that an Amgen executive misleadingly downplayed the significance of an upcoming Food and Drug Administration advisory committee meeting by incorrectly stating that the meeting would not focus on one of Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s leading drugs. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 17a. Amgen responded to this allegation by presenting public documents\xe2\x80\x94including the committee\xe2\x80\x99s meeting agenda, which was published in the Federal Register more than a month before the meeting\xe2\x80\x94stating that safety concerns associated with Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s drug would be discussed at the meeting. See id., at 41a\xe2\x80\x9342a. See also 69 Fed. Reg. 16582 (2004).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court did not err, we agree with the Court of Appeals, by disregarding Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s rebuttal evidence in deciding whether Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s proposed class satisfied Rule 23(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s predominance requirement. The Court of Appeals concluded, and Amgen does not contest, that Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s rebuttal evidence aimed to prove that the misrepresentations and omissions alleged in Connecticut Retirement\xe2\x80\x99s complaint were immaterial. 660 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1177 (characterizing Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s rebuttal evidence as an attempt to present a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98truth-on-the-market\xe2\x80\x99 defense,\xe2\x80\x9d which the Court of Appeals explained \xe2\x80\x9cis a method of refuting an alleged misrepresentation\xe2\x80\x99s materiality\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Reply Brief 17 (Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s evidence was offered to rebut the \xe2\x80\x9cmateriality predicate\xe2\x80\x9d of the fraud-on-the-market theory). As explained above, however, the potential immateriality of Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations and omissions is no barrier to finding that common questions predominate. See Part II, supra. If the alleged misrepresentations and omissions are ultimately found immaterial, the fraud-on-the-market presumption of classwide reliance will collapse. But again, as earlier explained, see supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9313, individual reliance questions will not overwhelm questions common to the class, for the class members\xe2\x80\x99 claims will have failed on their merits, thus bringing the litigation to a close. Therefore, just as a plaintiff class\xe2\x80\x99s inability to prove materiality creates no risk that individual questions will predominate, so even a definitive rebuttal on the issue of materiality would not undermine the predominance of questions common to the class.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe recognized as much in Basic itself. A defendant could \xe2\x80\x9crebut the [fraud-on-the-market] presumption of reliance,\xe2\x80\x9d we observed in Basic, by demonstrating that \xe2\x80\x9cnews of the [truth] credibly entered the market and dissipated the effects of [prior] misstatements.\xe2\x80\x9d 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 248\xe2\x80\x93249. We emphasized, however, that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]roof of that sort is a matter for trial\xe2\x80\x9d (and presumably also for a summary-judgment motion under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 56). Id., at 249, n. 29.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t11\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The District Court thus correctly reserved consideration of Amgen\xe2\x80\x99s rebuttal evidence for summary judgment or trial. It was not required to consider the evidence in determining whether common questions predominated under Rule 23(b)(3).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court today allows plaintiffs to obtain certification of securities-fraud class actions without proof that common questions predominate over individualized questions of reliance, in contravention of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(3). The Court does so by all but eliminating materiality as one of the predicates of the fraud-on-the-market theory, which serves as an alternative mode of es-tablishing reliance. See Basic Inc. v. Levinson, \n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 224\n\xe2\x80\x93250 (1988). Without demonstrating materiality at certification, plaintiffs cannot establish Basic\xe2\x80\x99s fraud-on-the-market presumption. Without proof of fraud on the market, plaintiffs cannot show that otherwise individualized questions of reliance will predominate, as required by Rule 23(b)(3). And without satisfying Rule 23(b)(3), class certification is improper. Fraud on the market is thus a condition precedent to class certification, without which individualized questions of reliance will defeat certification.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion depends on the following assumption: Plaintiffs will either (1) establish materiality at the merits stage, in which case class certification was proper because reliance turned out to be a common question, or (2) fail to establish materiality, in which case the claim would fail on the merits, notwithstanding the fact that the class should not have been certified in the first place, because reliance was never a common question. The failure to establish materiality retrospectively confirms that fraud on the market was never established, that questions regarding the element of reliance were not common under Rule 23(b)(3), and, by extension, that certification was never proper. Plaintiffs cannot be excused of their Rule 23 burden to show at certification that questions of reliance are common merely because they might lose later on the merits element of materiality. Because a securities-fraud plaintiff invoking Basic\xe2\x80\x99s fraud-on-the-market presumption to satisfy Rule 23(b)(3) should be required to prove each of the predicates of that theory at certification in order to demonstrate that questions of reliance are common to the class, I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe begin with \xc2\xa710 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j (2006 ed. and Supp. V).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t We \xe2\x80\x9chave implied a private cause of action from the text and purposes of \xc2\xa710(b)\xe2\x80\x9d and Securities and Exchange Commission Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.10b\xe2\x80\x935 (2011).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Matrixx Initiatives, Inc. v. Siracusano, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9). See also Superintendent of Ins. of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. Bankers Life & Casualty Co., \n\n404 U.\xc2\xa0S. 6, \n13, n. \n9 (1971)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is now established that a private right of action is implied under \xc2\xa710(b)\xe2\x80\x9d). The elements of an implied \xc2\xa710(b) cause of action for securities fraud are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98(1) a material misrepresentation or omission by the defendant; (2) scienter; (3) a connection between the misrepresentation or omission and the purchase or sale of a security; (4) reliance upon the misrep-resentation or omission; (5) economic loss; and (6) loss causation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Matrixx, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 9) (quoting Stoneridge Investment Partners, LLC v. Scientific-Atlanta, Inc., \n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 148, \n157 (2008)\n). This case concerns the reliance element of the \xc2\xa710(b) claim and its interaction with Rule 23(b)(3).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo prove reliance, a plaintiff, whether proceeding individually or as a class member, must show that his stock transaction was caused by the specific alleged misstatement. \xe2\x80\x9c[P]roof of reliance ensures that there is a proper \xe2\x80\x98connection between a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentation and a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Erica P. John Fund, Inc. v. Hal-liburton Co., 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 4) (quoting Basic, supra, at 243).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t To satisfy this element, a plaintiff traditionally was required to \xe2\x80\x9csho[w] that he was aware of a company\xe2\x80\x99s statement and engaged in a relevant transaction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. based on that specific misrepresentation.\xe2\x80\x9d Erica P. John Fund, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 4) (emphasis added). In the face-to-face fraud cases from which securities claims historically arose, see, e.g., Dura Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Broudo, \n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 336\n\xe2\x80\x93344 (2005) (discussing common-law roots of securities-fraud actions), this requirement was easily met by showing that the seller made statements directly to the purchaser and that the purchaser bought stock in reliance on those statements. However, in a modern securities market many, if not most, individuals who purchase stock from third parties on an impersonal exchange will be unaware of statements made by the issuer of those securities. As a result, such purchaser-plaintiffs are unable to meet the traditional reliance requirement because they cannot establish that they \xe2\x80\x9cengaged in a relevant transaction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. based on [a] specific misrepresentation.\xe2\x80\x9d Erica P. John Fund, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 4).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis concern was the driving force behind the development of the fraud-on-the-market theory adopted in Basic. Because individuals trading stock on an impersonal market often cannot show reliance even for purposes of an individual securities-fraud action, Basic permitted \xe2\x80\x9cplaintiffs to invoke a rebuttable presumption of reliance.\xe2\x80\x9d Erica P. John Fund, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 5).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Basic presumes that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in an open and developed securities market, the price of a company\xe2\x80\x99s stock is determined by the avail-able material information regarding the company and its business.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 241 (quoting Peil v. Speiser, 806 F.\xc2\xa02d 1154, 1160\xe2\x80\x931161 (CA3 1986); emphasis added).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Misleading statements will therefore defraud purchasers of stock even if the purchasers do not directly rely on the misstatements.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 241\xe2\x80\x93242. As a result, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n investor who buys or sells stock at the price set by the market does so in reliance on the integrity of that price,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9can investor\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on any public material misrepresentations\xe2\x80\x9d may therefore \xe2\x80\x9cbe presumed for purposes of a Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 247 (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf a plaintiff opts to show reliance through fraud on the market, Basic is clear that the plaintiff must show the following predicates in order to prevail: (1) an efficient market, (2) a public statement, (3) that the stock was traded after the statement was made but before the truth was revealed, and (4) the materiality of the statement. Id., at 248, n. 27.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Both the Court and respondent agree that materiality is a necessary component of fraud on the market. See, e.g., ante, at 9 (materiality is \xe2\x80\x9cindisputably\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9can essential predicate of the fraud-on-the-market the-ory\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Respondent 29 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf the statement is not materially false, then no one in the class can establish reliance via the integrity of the market\xe2\x80\x9d). The materiality of a specific statement is, therefore, essential to the fraud-on-the-market presumption, which in turn enables a plaintiff to prove reliance.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBasic\xe2\x80\x99s fraud-on-the-market presumption is highly sig-nificant because it makes securities-fraud class actions possible by converting the inherently individual reliance inquiry into a question common to the class, which is necessary to satisfy the dictates of Rule 23(b)(3).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Rule 23(b)(3) requires the party seeking certification to prove that \xe2\x80\x9cquestions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members.\xe2\x80\x9d A plaintiff seeking class certification is not required to prove the elements of his claim at the certifi-cation stage, but he must show that the elements of the claim are susceptible to classwide proof. See, e.g., Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, n.\xc2\xa06 (2011) (slip op., at 11, n.\xc2\xa06) (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]laintiffs seeking 23(b)(3) certifi-cation must prove that their shares were traded on an ef-ficient market,\xe2\x80\x9d an element of the fraud-on-the-market theory (emphasis added)). Without that proof, there is no justification for certifying a class because there is no \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98capacity of a classwide proceeding to generate common answers apt to drive the resolution of the litigation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310) (quoting Nagareda, Class Certification in the Age of Aggregate Proof, 84 N.\xc2\xa0Y. U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 97, 132 (2009)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf plaintiffs fail to show that reliance is a common question at the time of certification, certification is improper. For if reliance is not a common question, each plaintiff would be required to prove that he in fact relied on a misstatement, a showing which is simply not susceptible to classwide proof. Individuals make stock transactions for divergent, even idiosyncratic, reasons. As the leading pre-Basic fraud-on-the-market case recognized, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] purchaser on the stock exchanges may be either unaware of a specific false representation, or may not directly rely on it; he may purchase because of a favorable price trend, price earnings ratio, or some other factor.\xe2\x80\x9d Blackie v. Barrack, 524 F.\xc2\xa02d 891, 907 (CA9 1975). The inquiry\xe2\x80\x99s inherently individualized nature renders it impossible to generate the common answers necessary for certification under Rule 23(b)(3). See Basic, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 242 (\xe2\x80\x9cRequiring proof of individualized reliance from each member of the proposed plaintiff class effectively would have prevented respondents from proceeding with a class action, since individual issues then would have overwhelmed the common ones\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s solution in Basic was to allow putative class members to prove reliance through the fraud-on-the-market presumption. Id., at 241\xe2\x80\x93250. As the Court today recognizes, failure to establish fraud on the market \xe2\x80\x9cleaves open the prospect of individualized proof of reliance.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17. Notably, the Court and the Ninth Circuit both acknowledge that in order to obtain the benefit of the presumption, plaintiffs must establish two of the fraud-on-the-market predicates at class certification: (1) that the market was generally efficient, and (2) that the alleged misstatement was public. See ante, at 16 (acknowledging \xe2\x80\x9cthat market efficiency and the public nature of the alleged misrepresentations must be proved before a securities-fraud class action can be certified\xe2\x80\x9d); 660 F.\xc2\xa03d 1170, 1175 (CA9 2011) (same). See also Erica P. John Fund, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 5) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is undisputed that securities fraud plaintiffs must prove,\xe2\x80\x9d at certification inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9cthat the alleged misrepresentations were publicly known .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [and] that the stock traded in an efficient market\xe2\x80\x9d). The Court is correct insofar as its statements recognize that fraud on the market is a condition precedent to showing that there are common questions of reliance at the time of class certification.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNevertheless, the Court asserts that materiality\xe2\x80\x94by its own admission an essential predicate to invoking fraud on the market\xe2\x80\x94need not be established at certification because it will ultimately be proved at the merits stage. Ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9318. This assertion is an express admission that parties will not know at certification whether reliance is an individual or common question.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo support its position, the Court transforms the predicate certification inquiry into a novel either-or inquiry occurring much later on the merits. According to the Court, either (1) plaintiffs will prove materiality on the merits, thus demonstrating ex\xc2\xa0post that common questions predominated at certification, or (2) they will fail to prove materiality, at which point we learn ex\xc2\xa0post that certification was inappropriate because reliance was not, in fact, a common question. In the Court\xe2\x80\x99s second scenario, fraud on the market was never established, reliance for each class member was inherently individualized, and Rule 23(b)(3) in fact should have barred certification long ago.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The Court suggests that the problem created by the second scenario is excusable because the plaintiffs will lose anyway on alternative merits grounds, and the case will be over. See ante, at 17 (\xe2\x80\x9c[F]ailure of proof on the issue of materiality [at the merits stage] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not only precludes a plaintiff from invoking the fraud-on-the-market presumption of classwide reliance; it also establishes as a matter of law that the plaintiff cannot prevail on the merits of her Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 claim\xe2\x80\x9d). But nothing in logic or precedent justifies ignoring at certification whether reliance is susceptible to Rule 23(b)(3) classwide proof simply because one predicate of reliance\xe2\x80\x94materiality\xe2\x80\x94will be resolved, if at all, much later in the litigation on an independent merits element.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is the Court, not Amgen, that \xe2\x80\x9cwould have us put the cart before the horse,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 3, by jumping chronologically to the \xc2\xa710(b) merits element of materiality. But Rule 23, as well as common sense, requires class certification issues to be addressed first. See Rule 23(c)(1)(A) (\xe2\x80\x9cAt an early practicable time after a person sues or is sued .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the court must determine by order whether to certify the action as a class action\xe2\x80\x9d). A plaintiff who cannot prove materiality does not simply have a claim that is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98dead on arrival\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d at the merits, ante, at 17 (quoting 660 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1175); he has a class that should never have arrived at the merits at all because it failed Rule 23(b)(3) certification from the outset. Without materiality, there is no fraud- on-the-market presumption, questions of reliance remain individualized, and Rule 23(b)(3) certification is impossible. And the fact that evidence of materiality goes to both fraud on the market at certification and an independent merits element is no issue; Wal-Mart expressly held that a court at certification may inquire into questions that also have later relevance on the merits. See 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311). The Court reverses that inquiry, effectively saying that certification may be put off until later because an adverse merits determination will retroactively wipe out the entire class. However, a plaintiff who cannot prove materiality cannot prove fraud on the market and, thus, cannot demonstrate that the question of reliance is susceptible of a classwide answer.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe fact that a statement may prove to be material at the merits stage does not justify conflating the doctrinally independent (and distinct) elements of materiality and reliance.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The Court\xe2\x80\x99s error occurs when, instead of asking whether the element of reliance is susceptible to classwide proof, the Court focuses on whether materiality is susceptible to classwide proof. Ante, at 10 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he piv-otal inquiry is whether proof of materiality is needed to ensure that the questions of law or fact common to the class will \xe2\x80\x98predominate\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). The result is that the Court effectively equates \xc2\xa710(b) materiality with fraud-on-the-market materiality and elides reliance as a \xc2\xa710(b) element. But a plaintiff seeking certification under Rule 23 bears the burden of proof with regard to all the elements of a \xc2\xa710(b) claim, which includes materiality and reliance. As Wal-Mart explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] party seeking class certification must affirmatively demonstrate his compliance with the Rule\xe2\x80\x94that is, he must be prepared to prove that there are in fact sufficiently numerous parties, common questions of law or fact, etc.\xe2\x80\x9d 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10). If the elements of fraud on the market are not proved at certification, a plaintiff has failed to carry his burden of establishing that questions of individualized reliance will not predominate, without which the plaintiff class cannot obtain certification. Cf. id., at ___ (slip op., at 12) (holding in Rule 23(a)(2) context that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]ithout some glue hold- ing the alleged reasons for all those decisions together, it will be impossible to say that examination of all the class members\xe2\x80\x99 claims for relief will produce a common answer\xe2\x80\x9d). It is only by establishing all of the elements of the fraud-on-the-market presumption that reliance can be proved on a classwide basis. Therefore, if a plaintiff wishes to use Basic\xe2\x80\x99s presumption to prove that reliance is a common question, he must establish the entire presumption, including materiality, at the class certification stage.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor is it relevant, as respondent argues, that requiring plaintiffs to establish all predicates of fraud on the market at certification will make it more difficult to obtain certification. See Brief for Respondent 35\xe2\x80\x9338. In Basic, four Justices of a six-Justice Court created the fraud-on-the-market presumption from a combination of newly minted economic theories, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 250\xe2\x80\x93251, n. 1 (White, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part), and \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderations of fairness, public policy, and probability,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 245 (majority opinion), to allow claims that otherwise would have been barred due to the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 inability to show reliance, id., at 242. Basic is a judicially invented doctrine based on an economic theory adopted to ease the burden on plaintiffs bringing claims under an implied cause of action. There is nothing untoward about requiring plaintiffs to take the steps that the Basic Court created in an effort to save otherwise inadequate claims.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach is, thus, doctrinally incorrect under Basic. Its shortcomings are further highlighted by the role that materiality played in the pre-Basic development of the fraud-on-the-market theory as a condition precedent to showing that there are common questions of reliance in the class-action context. Materiality, at the time of certification, has been a driving force behind the theory from the outset. This fact further supports the need to prove materiality at the time the fraud-on-the-market theory is invoked to show that questions of reliance can be answered on a classwide basis.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBefore Basic, two signposts marked the way for courts applying the fraud-on-the-market theory. Both demonstrate that the materiality of an alleged falsehood was not a mere afterthought but rather one of the primary reasons for allowing traditional proof of reliance to be brushed aside at certification. This fact weighs strongly in favor of the conclusion that materiality must be resolved at certi-fication when the fraud-on-the-market presumption is invoked to show that reliance can be proved on a classwide basis.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe first signpost was the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s 1975 opinion in Blackie, termed by one pre-Basic court the \xe2\x80\x9cseminal fraud on the market case.\xe2\x80\x9d Peil, 806 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1163, n. 16. See also Basic, supra, at 251, n. 1 (White, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe earliest Court of Appeals case adopting this theory cited by the Court is Blackie v. Barrack, 524 F.\xc2\xa02d 891 (CA9 1975), cert. denied, \n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 816 (1976)\n\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBlackie arose from a $90 million loss reported by audio equipment manufacturer Ampex Corp. in its 1972 annual report. 524 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 894.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Ampex\xe2\x80\x99s independent auditors not only refused to certify the 1972 annual report but also withdrew certification of all 1971 financial statements \xe2\x80\x9cbecause of doubts that the loss reported for 1972 was in fact suffered in that year.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In resultant class actions, the defendants argued that reliance stood in the way of class certification under Rule 23(b)(3) because it was not a common question.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Ninth Circuit disagreed. Instead, it relieved plaintiffs from providing traditional proof of reliance, ex-plaining that \xe2\x80\x9ccausation is adequately established in the impersonal stock exchange context by proof of purchase and of the materiality of misrepresentations, without direct proof of reliance.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 906 (emphasis added). The court left no doubt that the materiality of the $90 million shortfall in Ampex\xe2\x80\x99s financial statements was central to its determination that reliance could be presumed. It asserted that \xe2\x80\x9c[m]ateriality circumstantially establishes the reliance of some market traders and hence the inflation in the stock price\xe2\x80\x94when the purchase is made[,] the causational chain between defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct and plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s loss is sufficiently established to make out a prima facie case.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Materiality was not merely an important factor that allowed reliance to be presumed at certification; materiality was the factor. It demonstrated that the defendants had committed a fraud on the market, that all putative class plaintiffs had relied on it in purchasing stock, and, therefore, that questions of reliance would be susceptible to common answers.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t11\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe second fraud-on-the-market signpost prior to Basic was a note in the Harvard Law Review, which described the nascent theory. See Note, The Fraud-on-the-Market Theory, 95 Harv. L. Rev. 1143 (1982) (hereinafter Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. Note). The Sixth Circuit opinion reviewed in Basic termed the Note \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he clearest statement of the theory of presumption of reliance.\xe2\x80\x9d Levinson v. Basic Inc., 786 F.\xc2\xa02d 741, 750 (1986). Indeed, in the briefing for Basic itself, the plaintiffs, the United States, and plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 amicus cited the article repeatedly as an authoritative statement on the subject. See Brief for Respondent 43, n. 18, 46, n. 20 (cited in Peil, supra, at 1160), Brief for Securities and Exchange Commission as Amicus Curiae 22, n. 25, 24, n. 30, 26, n. 32, and Brief for Joseph Harris et\xc2\xa0al. as Amicus Curiae 4, n. 2, in Basic Inc. v. Levinson, O. T. 1987, No. 86\xe2\x80\x93279.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLike Blackie, the Note also hinged the fraud-on-the-market presumption of reliance on proof of materiality. Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. Note 1161 (\xe2\x80\x9cIn developed markets, which are apparently efficient, reliance should be presumed from the materiality of the deception\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Ultimately, in language that will be familiar to anyone who has read Basic, the Note formulated a \xe2\x80\x9cpivotal assumption\xe2\x80\x9d underlying the fraud-on-the-market theory as the belief that:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cmarket prices respond to information disseminated (or not disseminated) concerning the companies whose securities are traded. In such a setting\xe2\x80\x94often described as an \xe2\x80\x98efficient market\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94the reliance of some traders upon a material deception influences market prices and thereby affects even traders who never read or hear of the deception.\xe2\x80\x9d Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. Note 1154 (footnote omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAgain, the materiality of the alleged misstatement was a key component, without which the market could not be presumed to move. As a result, without materiality it is impossible to say that there has been a fraud on the market at all, and if that is not the case there is no reason to believe that the market price at which stock transactions occurred was affected by an alleged misstatement or, by extension, that any market participants relied on it. Materiality should thus be proved when the fraud-on-the-market presumption is invoked, or there is no common-ality with respect to questions of reliance.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor did the importance of materiality diminish in the Sixth Circuit opinion reviewed in Basic. Rather, the court followed the path marked by the signposts discussed above. It excused plaintiffs from offering traditional evidence of reliance, so long as \xe2\x80\x9ca defendant is shown to have made a material public misrepresentation that, if relied on directly, would fraudulently induce an individual to mis-judge the value of the stock.\xe2\x80\x9d Levinson, 786 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 750 (emphasis added). The court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis made clear that materiality should be demonstrated at the time the presumption was invoked: \xe2\x80\x9cIn order to invoke the presumption of reliance based upon the fraud on the market theory, a plaintiff must allege and prove .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the mis-representations were material .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. .\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (citing Blackie, 524 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 906).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the briefing before this Court in Basic itself built upon this framework and the foundational principle that materiality is an integral part of the theory. Criti-cally, the Basic defendants argued that the plaintiffs could not establish fraud on the market at certification even if the theory were valid because the alleged misstatement was immaterial. They \xe2\x80\x9ccontrast[ed] the likely market impact of disclosure of the [$90 million Blackie loss] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. with the disclosure of the information which respondents contend[ed] rendered Basic\xe2\x80\x99s statements materially misleading.\xe2\x80\x9d See Brief for Petitioners in O. T. 1987, No. 86\xe2\x80\x93279, p. 42. The Basic defendants concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe differences between a company\xe2\x80\x99s $90 million loss and a company\xe2\x80\x99s sporadic contacts with a friendly suitor are sub-stantial. .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [T]he fraud on the market theory, if it has vitality, should not be applied in a case such as this.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 43.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn response, the plaintiffs in Basic did not argue that the defendants misunderstood the role of materiality in the fraud-on-the-market theory. They instead advanced a now-foreclosed interpretation of dicta from Eisen v. Carlisle & Jacquelin, \n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 156, \n177 (1974)\n:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 final argument\xe2\x80\x94that respondents will be unable to establish that Basic\xe2\x80\x99s repeated false and misleading statements impacted the price of Basic stock over a fourteen month period\xe2\x80\x94represents an effort to litigate the merits of this case on the motion for class certification. .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. As this Court held in Eisen v. Carlisle & Jacquelin, \n\n417 U. S. 156, \n177 (1974)\n: \xe2\x80\x98We find nothing in either the language or history of Rule 23 that gives a court any authority to conduct a preliminary inquiry into the merits of a suit in order to determine whether it may be maintained as a class action.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents in O. T. 1987, No. 86\xe2\x80\x93279, p. 54.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court rejected this reading of Eisen two Terms ago, explaining that the very language the Basic plaintiffs quoted was \xe2\x80\x9csometimes mistakenly cited\xe2\x80\x9d as prohibiting inquiry into \xe2\x80\x9cthe propriety of certification under Rules 23(a) and (b).\xe2\x80\x9d Wal-Mart Stores, Inc., 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___, n.\xc2\xa06 (slip op., at 10, n.\xc2\xa06). That reading, the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cis the purest dictum and is contradicted by our other cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Basic defendants\xe2\x80\x99 reply is consistent with Wal-Mart:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cPutative class representatives, such as respondents, should not be permitted to invoke the fraud on the market theory while, at the same time, arguing that courts may not make any preliminary inquiry into the claimed impact on the market. See, e.g., Resp. Br., p. 54. By seeking the benefit of the presumption, respondents necessarily invite judicial scrutiny of the circumstances in which it is invoked.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioners in O. T. 1987, No. 86\xe2\x80\x93279, p. 18.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWell said. The history of Basic is worth the volume of argument offered by the majority. Cf. New York Trust Co. v. Eisner, \n\n256 U.\xc2\xa0S. 345, \n349 (1921)\n (majority opinion of Holmes, J.). Materiality was central to the development, analysis, and adoption of the fraud-on-the-market theory both before Basic and in Basic itself. Materiality, therefore, must be demonstrated to prove fraud on the market, and until materiality of an alleged misstatement is shown there is no reason to believe that all market participants have relied equally on it. Otherwise individualized questions of reliance remain. This history confirms that materiality must be proved at the time that the theory is invoked\xe2\x80\x94i.e., at certification.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI join the principal dissent, that of Justice Thomas, except for Part I\xe2\x80\x93B.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe fraud-on-the-market rule says that purchase or sale of a security in a well functioning market establishes reliance on a material misrepresentation known to the market. This rule is to be found nowhere in the United States Code or in the common law of fraud or deception; it was invented by the Court in Basic Inc. v. Levinson, \n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 224 (1988)\n. Today\xe2\x80\x99s Court applies to that rule the principles of Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(3), and thereby concludes (logically enough) that commonality is established at the certification stage even when material-ity has not been shown. That would be a correct procedure if Basic meant the rule it announced to govern only the question of substantive liability\xe2\x80\x94what must be shown in order to prevail. If that were so, the new substantive rule, like the more general substantive rule that reliance must be proved, would be subject, at the certification stage, to the commonality analysis of Rule 23(b)(3). In my view, however, the Basic rule of fraud-on-the-market\xe2\x80\x94a well functioning market plus purchase or sale in the market plus material misrepresentation known to the market establishes a necessary showing of reliance\xe2\x80\x94governs not only the question of substantive liability, but also the question whether certification is proper. All of the elements of that rule, including materiality, must be established if and when it is relied upon to justify certification. The answer to the question before us today is to be found not in Rule 23(b)(3), but in the opinion of Basic.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBasic established a presumption that the misrepresen-tation was relied upon, not a mere presumption that the plaintiffs relied on the market price. And it established that presumption not just for the question of substantive liability but also for the question of certification. \xe2\x80\x9cWe granted certiorari .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to determine whether the courts below properly applied a presumption of reliance in certifying the class, rather than requiring each class member to show direct reliance on Basic\xe2\x80\x99s statements.\xe2\x80\x9d 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 230 (emphasis added). Of course it makes no sense to \xe2\x80\x9cpresume reliance\xe2\x80\x9d on the misrepresentation merely because the plaintiff relied on the market price, unless the alleged misrepresentation would likely have affected the market price\xe2\x80\x94that is, unless it was material. Thus, as Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x99 dissent shows, the Basic opinion is shot through with references to the necessary materiality. The presumption of reliance does not apply, and hence neither substantive liability will attach nor will certification be proper, unless materiality is shown. The necessity of materiality for certification is demonstrated by the last sentence of the Basic opinion, which comes after the Court has decided to remand the case for reconsideration of materiality under the appropriate legal standard: \xe2\x80\x9cThe District Court\xe2\x80\x99s certification of the class here was appropriate when made but is subject on remand to such adjustment, if any, as developing circumstances demand.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 250. Those circumstances are the establishment of facts that rebut the presumption, including of facts that show the misrepresentation was not material, or was not known to the market.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court argues that if materiality were a predicate to certification on a fraud-on-the-market theory, the Basic Court would not have approved the class certification order while remanding for reconsideration of \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the plaintiffs had mustered sufficient evidence to satisfy the relatively lenient standard for avoiding summary judg-ment.\xe2\x80\x9d See ante, at 23\xe2\x80\x9324. The Court manufactures an inconsistency on the basis of doctrine that did not govern class certification at the time of Basic. We recently clarified that \xe2\x80\x9cRule 23 does not set forth a mere pleading standard.\xe2\x80\x9d Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 10). But review of the Basic certification order shows that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s fraud-on-the-market analysis was based exclusively on the pleadings: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he allegations of plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 complaint are sufficient to bring this section 10(b) and Rule 10(b)(5) claim within the so-called \xe2\x80\x98fraud on the market\xe2\x80\x99 theory.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. in Basic Inc. v. Levinson, O. T. 1987, No. 86\xe2\x80\x93279, p.\xc2\xa0115a (emphasis added); see also ibid. (citing complaint paragraphs as establishing fraud on the market). Under a pleadings standard, the District Court found that the plaintiffs had satisfied Rule 23(b)(3) with regard to fraud on the market, including its materiality predicate. See id., at 133a (denial of reconsideration) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis court ruled on December 10 that transaction causation [i.e., reliance] could be established by the following: proof of a material misrepresentation which affected the market price of the stocks with a resulting injury to the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Thus, even if the plaintiffs sufficiently pleaded materiality that the certification order \xe2\x80\x9cwas appropriate when made,\xe2\x80\x9d Basic, supra, at 250, the defendants retained an opportunity on remand to rebut the pleading in order to defeat certification.*\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCertification of the class is often, if not usually, the prelude to a substantial settlement by the defendant because the costs and risks of litigating further are so high. It does an injustice to the Basic Court to presume without clear evidence\xe2\x80\x94and indeed in the face of language to the contrary\xe2\x80\x94that it was establishing a regime in which not only those market class-action suits that have earned the presumption of reliance pass beyond the crucial certification stage, but all market-purchase and market-sale class-action suits do so, no matter what the al-leged misrepresentation. The opinion need not be read this way, and it should not.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe fraud-on-the-market theory approved by Basic en-visions a demonstration of materiality not just for substantive recovery but for certification. Today\xe2\x80\x99s holding does not merely accept what some consider the regrettable consequences of the four-Justice opinion in Basic; it expands those consequences from the arguably regrettable to the unquestionably disastrous.\n\t\t\t
