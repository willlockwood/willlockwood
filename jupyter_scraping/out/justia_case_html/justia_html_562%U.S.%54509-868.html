\nWALL V. KHOLI
\n

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

WALL, DIRECTOR, RHODE ISLAND DEPARTMENT OF CORRECTIONS v. KHOLI

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x93868.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 29, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 7, 2011

\n

Respondent was convicted in Rhode Island Superior Court on 10 counts of first-degree sexual assault and sentenced to consecutive life terms. His conviction became final on direct review on May 29, 1996. In addition to his direct appeal, he filed two relevant state motions. One, a May 16, 1996, motion to reduce his sentence under Rhode Island Superior Court Rule of Criminal Procedure 35, was denied. The State Supreme Court affirmed on January 16, 1998. The second, a state postconviction relief motion, was also denied. That decision was affirmed on December 14, 2006. When respondent filed his federal habeas petition, his conviction had been final for over 11 years. The Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) generally requires a federal petition to be filed within one year of the date on which a judgment became final, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(A), but \xe2\x80\x9ca properly filed application for State post-conviction or other collateral review\xe2\x80\x9d tolls that period, \xc2\xa72244(d)(2). Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s postconviction relief motion tolled the period for over nine years, but his Rule 35 motion must also trigger the tolling provision for his habeas petition to be timely. The District Court dismissed the petition as untimely, adopting the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the Rule 35 motion was not \xe2\x80\x9ca properly filed application for \xe2\x80\xa6 collateral review\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa72244(d)(2). The First Circuit reversed.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa72244(d)(2) means judicial review of a judgment in a proceeding that is not part of direct review. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The parties agree that the answer to the question whether a motion to reduce sentence is an \xe2\x80\x9capplication for State post-conviction or other collateral review\xe2\x80\x9d turns on the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review,\xe2\x80\x9d but they disagree about what that meaning should be. Pp.\n4\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Because \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d is not defined in AEDPA, the Court begins with the ordinary understanding of that phrase. By definition, \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d describes something that is \xe2\x80\x9cindirect,\xe2\x80\x9d not direct. 3 Oxford English Dictionary 473. This suggests that \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d review is not part of direct review. This conclusion is supported by the definition of the related phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral attack\xe2\x80\x9d and by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior use of the term \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d to describe proceedings that are separate from the direct review process. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The term \xe2\x80\x9creview\xe2\x80\x9d is best understood as a \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial reexamination.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 1944. Pp.\n7\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0A Rule 35 motion to reduce sentence under Rhode Island law is an application for \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d that triggers AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s tolling provision. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Rhode Island\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 35 is similar to the version of Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 35 in effect before the federal Sentencing Reform Act of 1984. The Rule permits a court to provide relief, as relevant here, to \xe2\x80\x9creduce any sentence,\xe2\x80\x9d and it is generally addressed to the sound discretion of the sentencing justice. Under the limited review available, an appellate court may disturb the trial justice\xe2\x80\x99s decision if the sentence imposed is without justification and is grossly disparate when compared to sentences for similar offenses. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Keeping these principles in mind, a Rule 35 sentence reduction proceeding is \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral.\xe2\x80\x9d The parties agree that the motion is not part of the direct review process, and both this Court and lower federal courts have described a motion to reduce sentence under old Federal Rule 35 as invoking a \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d remedy. Therefore, it is not difficult to conclude that Rhode Island\xe2\x80\x99s motion to reduce sentence is \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral.\xe2\x80\x9d A Rule 35 motion also calls for \xe2\x80\x9creview\xe2\x80\x9d of the sentence within \xc2\xa72244(d)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s meaning. The decision to reduce a sentence involves judicial reexamination of the sentence to determine whether a more lenient sentence is proper. The trial justice is guided by several sentencing factors in making that decision. And those factors are also used by the State Supreme Court in evaluating the trial justice\xe2\x80\x99s justifications for the sentence. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Rhode Island\xe2\x80\x99s arguments in support of its opposing view that \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d includes only \xe2\x80\x9clegal\xe2\x80\x9d challenges to a conviction or sentence, and thus excludes motions for a discretionary sentence reduction, are unpersuasive. Nor does \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d turn on whether a motion is part of the same criminal case. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

582 F.\xc2\xa03d 147, affirmed.

ASHBEL T. WALL, II, DIRECTOR, RHODE ISLAND\nDEPARTMENT OF CORRECTIONS, PETI-\nTIONER v. KHALIL KHOLI

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the first circuit

\n

[March 7, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), \xe2\x80\x9ca properly filed application for State post-conviction or other collateral review with respect to the pertinent judgment or claim\xe2\x80\x9d tolls the 1-year limitation period for filing a federal habeas petition. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(2). The question in this case is whether a motion to reduce sentence under Rhode Island law tolls the limitation period, thereby rendering respondent Khalil Kholi\xe2\x80\x99s federal habeas petition timely. We hold that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa72244(d)(2) means judicial review of a judgment in a proceeding that is not part of direct review. Because the parties agree that a motion to reduce sentence under Rhode Island law is not part of the direct review process, we hold that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s motion tolled the AEDPA limitation period and that his federal habeas petition was therefore timely.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1993, respondent was convicted in Rhode Island Superior Court on 10 counts of first-degree sexual assault, and he was sentenced to consecutive terms of life im-prisonment. Respondent raised various challenges to \nhis conviction on direct appeal, but the Supreme Court of Rhode Island affirmed his conviction. See State v. Kholi, 672 A.\xc2\xa02d 429, 431 (1996). The parties agree that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s conviction became final on direct review when his time expired for filing a petition for a writ of certiorari in this Court. Brief for Petitioner 7, n.\xc2\xa04; Brief for Respondent 3, n.\xc2\xa01; 582 F.\xc2\xa03d 147, 150 (CA1 2009); see generally Jimenez v. Quarterman, 555 U. S. 113, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 6). That date was May 29, 1996. See this Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rules 13.1, 13.3, 30.1.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to taking a direct appeal, respondent filed two state motions that are relevant to our decision. The first, filed on May 16, 1996, was a motion to reduce sentence under Rule 35 of the Rhode Island Superior Court Rules of Criminal Procedure.[Footnote 1] App. 8. In that motion, respondent asked the trial court to \xe2\x80\x9creconsider its prior determination\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9corder that his life sentences run concurrently.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Kholi, 706 A.\xc2\xa02d 1326 (R.\xc2\xa0I. 1998) (order). Concluding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe sentence imposed was appropriate,\xe2\x80\x9d the hearing justice denied the Rule 35 motion. Ibid. On January 16, 1998, the State Supreme Court affirmed and observed that the facts clearly justified the sentence. Id., at 1326\xe2\x80\x931327.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On May 23, 1997, while the Rule 35 motion was pending, respondent also filed an application for state postconviction relief, see R.\xc2\xa0I. Gen. Laws 10\xe2\x80\x939.1\xe2\x80\x931 et seq. (Lexis 1997) (titled \xe2\x80\x9cPost Conviction Remedy\xe2\x80\x9d), which challenged his conviction. The trial court denied this motion as well, and the State Supreme Court affirmed that decision on December 14, 2006. See Kholi v. Wall, 911 A.\xc2\xa02d 262, 263\xe2\x80\x93264 (R.\xc2\xa0I. 2006).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent filed a federal habeas petition in the District of Rhode Island on September 5, 2007. App. 3. By that time, his conviction had been final for over 11 years. AEDPA generally requires a federal habeas petition to be filed within one year of the date on which the judgment became final by the conclusion of direct review. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(A). But the 1-year limitation period is tolled during the pendency of \xe2\x80\x9ca properly filed application for State post-conviction or other collateral review with respect to the pertinent judgment or claim.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(d)(2).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no dispute that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s application for postconviction relief tolled the limitation period for over nine years\xe2\x80\x94from May 23, 1997, through December 14, 2006. 582 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 151. Even after subtracting that stretch of time from the 11-year period, however, the period between the conclusion of direct review and the filing of the federal habeas petition still exceeds one year. Thus, in order for respondent\xe2\x80\x99s petition to be timely, the Rule 35 motion to reduce sentence must also trigger the tolling provision.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent\xe2\x80\x99s federal habeas petition was referred to a Magistrate Judge for a report and recommendation, and the Magistrate Judge concluded that the Rule 35 motion was not a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98properly filed application for post-conviction or other collateral review\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa72244(d)(2) because it was \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98plea of leniency,\xe2\x80\x99 and not a motion challenging the legal sufficiency of his sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d No. CA 07\xe2\x80\x93346S, 2008 WL 60194, *4 (R.\xc2\xa0I., Jan. 3, 2008). The District Court adopted the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s report and recommendation and therefore dismissed the federal habeas petition as untimely. See id., at *1. On appeal, the First Circuit reversed. 582 F.\xc2\xa03d 147.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Courts of Appeals are divided over the question whether a motion to reduce sentence tolls the period of limitation under \xc2\xa72244(d)(2).[Footnote 2] We granted certiorari to answer this question with respect to a motion to reduce sentence under Rhode Island law. 560 U. S. ___ (2010).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0AEDPA establishes a 1-year period of limitation for a state prisoner to file a federal application for a writ of habeas corpus. \xc2\xa72244(d)(1). This period runs \xe2\x80\x9cfrom the latest of\xe2\x80\x9d four specified dates, including \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the judgment became final by the conclusion of direct review or the expiration of the time for seeking such review.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(A); see also Jimenez, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 6) (explaining when \xe2\x80\x9cthe conclusion of direct review occurs\xe2\x80\x9d). The limitation period is tolled, however, during the pendency of \xe2\x80\x9ca properly filed application for State post-conviction or other collateral review with respect to the pertinent judgment or claim.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(d)(2). The question in this case is whether a motion for reduction of sentence under Rhode Island\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 35 is an \xe2\x80\x9capplication for State post-conviction or other collateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The parties agree that the answer to this question turns on the meaning of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review,\xe2\x80\x9d see Brief for Petitioner 19; Brief for Respondent 12\xe2\x80\x9313, but they disagree about the definition of that term. Rhode Island argues that \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d includes only \xe2\x80\x9clegal\xe2\x80\x9d challenges to a conviction or sentence and thus excludes motions seeking a discretionary sentence reduction. Respondent, on the other hand, maintains that \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9creview other than review of a judgment in the direct appeal process\xe2\x80\x9d and thus includes motions to reduce sentence. Brief for Respondent 17. We agree with respondent\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cCollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d is not defined in AEDPA, and we have never provided a comprehensive definition of that term. See Duncan v. Walker, 533 U. S. 167, 175\xe2\x80\x93178 (2001). We therefore begin by considering the ordinary understanding of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d See Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 420, 431 (2000) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe give the words of a statute their ordinary, contemporary, common meaning, absent an indication Congress intended them to bear some different import\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); see also Carey v. Saffold, 536 U. S. 214, 219 (2002) (considering the ordinary meaning of the word \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa72244(d)(2)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The term \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral,\xe2\x80\x9d in its \xe2\x80\x9ccustomary and preferred sense,\xe2\x80\x9d Williams, supra, at 431, means \xe2\x80\x9c[l]ying aside from the main subject, line of action, issue, purpose, etc.; \xe2\x80\xa6 subordinate, indirect,\xe2\x80\x9d 3 Oxford English Dictionary 473 (2d ed. 1989) (hereinafter OED); see also Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 444 (1993) (hereinafter Webster\xe2\x80\x99s) (\xe2\x80\x9caccompanying as \xe2\x80\xa6 secondary,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cindirect,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cancillary\xe2\x80\x9d). By definition, something that is \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d \nis \xe2\x80\x9cindirect,\xe2\x80\x9d not direct. 3 OED 473. This suggests that \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d review is review that is \xe2\x80\x9c[l]ying aside from the main\xe2\x80\x9d review, i.e., that is not part of direct review. See ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The definition of the related phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral attack\xe2\x80\x9d points in the same direction. A \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral attack\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n attack on a judgment in a proceeding other than a direct appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 298 (9th ed. 2009) (emphasis added); cf. Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa710.73.090(2) (2008) (defining \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral attack\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cany form of postconviction relief other than a direct appeal\xe2\x80\x9d). This usage buttresses the conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d means a form of review that is not part of the direct appeal process.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our prior usage of the term \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d also supports this understanding. We have previously described a variety of proceedings as \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral,\xe2\x80\x9d and all of these proceedings share the characteristic that we have identified, i.e., they stand apart from the process of direct review.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For example, our cases make it clear that habeas corpus is a form of collateral review. We have used the terms habeas corpus and \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d interchangeably, see, e.g., Murray v. Carrier, 477 U. S. 478, 482\xe2\x80\x93483 (1986), and it is well accepted that state petitions for habeas corpus toll the limitation period, e.g., Rhines v. Weber, 544 U. S. 269, 272 (2005) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he 1-year statute of limitations \xe2\x80\xa6 was tolled while Rhines\xe2\x80\x99 state habeas corpus petition was pending\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have also described coram nobis as a means of \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral attack,\xe2\x80\x9d see, e.g., United States v. Morgan, 346 U. S. 502, 510\xe2\x80\x93511 (1954) (internal quotation marks omitted), and we have used the term \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d to describe proceedings under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255 and a prior version of Rule 35 of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure. In United States v. Robinson, 361 U. S. 220 (1960), we distinguished between the process of direct appeal and \xe2\x80\x9ca number of collateral remedies,\xe2\x80\x9d including Federal Rule 35 motions, \xc2\xa72255 motions, and coram nobis. Id., at 230, n.\xc2\xa014. Similarly, in Bartone v. United States, 375 U. S. 52 (1963) (per curiam), we drew a distinction between a \xe2\x80\x9c[d]irect attack\xe2\x80\x9d on a criminal judgment and \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral proceedings,\xe2\x80\x9d such as Rule 35, habeas corpus, and \xc2\xa72255 proceedings. Id., at 53\xe2\x80\x9354.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0All of the proceedings identified in these prior opinions as \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d are separate from the direct review process, and thus our prior usage of the term \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d buttresses the conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d means a form of review that is not direct.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, to trigger the tolling provision, a \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d proceeding must also involve a form of \xe2\x80\x9creview,\xe2\x80\x9d but the meaning of that term seems clear. \xe2\x80\x9cReview\xe2\x80\x9d is best understood as an \xe2\x80\x9cact of inspecting or examining\xe2\x80\x9d or a \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial reexamination.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 1944; see also Black\xe2\x80\x99s, supra, at 1434 (\xe2\x80\x9c[c]onsideration, inspection, or reexamination of a subject or thing\xe2\x80\x9d); 13 OED 831 (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]o submit (a decree, act, etc.) to examination or revision\xe2\x80\x9d). We thus agree with the First Circuit that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98review\xe2\x80\x99 commonly denotes \xe2\x80\x98a looking over or examination with a view to amendment or improvement.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 582 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 153 (quoting Webster\xe2\x80\x99s 1944 (2002)). Viewed as a whole, then, \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d of a judgment or claim means a judicial reexamination of \na judgment or claim in a proceeding outside of the direct review process.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We now apply this definition of \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d to a Rule 35 motion to reduce sentence under Rhode Island law.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rule 35 of the Rhode Island Rules of Criminal Procedure is much like the version of Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 35 that was in force prior to the enactment of the federal Sentencing Reform Act of 1984 and the promulgation of the Federal Sentencing Guidelines. See State v. Byrnes, 456 A.\xc2\xa02d 742, 744 (R.\xc2\xa0I. 1983) (per curiam); Reporter\xe2\x80\x99s Notes following R.\xc2\xa0I. Super. Ct. Rule Crim. Proc. 35, R.\xc2\xa0I. Court Rules Ann., p. 620 (Lexis 2010). Under the Rhode Island Rules, a Rule 35 motion permits a court to provide relief from a sentence in three ways: A court \xe2\x80\x9cmay\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect an illegal sentence,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccorrect a sentence imposed in an illegal manner,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9creduce any sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d R.\xc2\xa0I. Super. Ct. Rule Crim. Proc. 35(a); see n.\xc2\xa01, supra. In this case, respondent filed a motion to reduce his sentence, which permits a trial justice to decide \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9con reflection or on the basis of changed circumstances that the sentence originally imposed was, for any reason, unduly severe.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Ruffner, 5 A. 3d 864, 867 (R.\xc2\xa0I. 2010) (quoting State v. Mendoza, 958 A.\xc2\xa02d 1159, 1161 (R.\xc2\xa0I. 2008)); see also Reporter\xe2\x80\x99s Notes following R.\xc2\xa0I. Super. Ct. Rule Crim. Proc. 35, R.\xc2\xa0I. Court Rules Ann., at 620\xe2\x80\x93621. Rhode Island courts have, at times, referred to such a motion as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98plea for leniency.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ruffner, supra, at 867 (quoting Mendoza, supra, at 1161).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A Rule 35 motion is made in the Superior Court, and it is generally heard by the same trial justice who sentenced the defendant. Byrnes, supra, at 745. The Rhode Island Supreme Court has explained that a motion to reduce sentence is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98addressed to the sound discretion of the trial justice\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and that appellate review of the trial justice\xe2\x80\x99s decision is limited. Ruffner, supra, at 867 (quoting Mendoza, supra, at 1161). An appellate court may nevertheless disturb the trial justice\xe2\x80\x99s decision \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the trial justice has imposed a sentence that is without justification and is grossly disparate from other sentences generally imposed for similar offenses.\xe2\x80\x9d Ruffner, supra, at 867 (quoting State v. Coleman, 984 A.\xc2\xa02d 650, 654 (R.\xc2\xa0I. 2009); internal quotation marks omitted); see also Ruffner, supra, at 867 (asking whether trial justice \xe2\x80\x9cabuse[d] his discretion\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With these principles in mind, we consider whether Rhode Island\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 35 motion to reduce sentence is an application for \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first\xe2\x80\x94and the critical\xe2\x80\x94question is whether a Rhode Island Rule 35 sentence reduction proceeding is \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral.\xe2\x80\x9d Respondent and Rhode Island agree that such a motion is not part of the direct review process. Moreover, we have previously referred to a motion to reduce sentence under old Rule 35 of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure as invoking a \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d remedy, see Robinson, supra, at 230, n.\xc2\xa014, and Rhode Island\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 35 motion to reduce sentence is \xe2\x80\x9csubstantially similar\xe2\x80\x9d to former Federal Rule 35, Byrnes, supra, at 744. Lower courts have also referred to Federal Rule 35 sentence reduction motions as \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Fernandez v. United States, 941 F.\xc2\xa02d 1488, 1492 (CA11 1991) (\xe2\x80\x9cFernandez initiated a collateral attack on his sentence with a Rule 35(b) motion to reduce his sentence\xe2\x80\x9d under the old Federal Rule). We thus have little difficulty concluding that a Rhode Island sentence reduction proceeding is \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Not only is a motion to reduce sentence under Rhode Island law \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral,\xe2\x80\x9d but it also undoubtedly calls for \xe2\x80\x9creview\xe2\x80\x9d of the sentence. The decision to reduce a sentence, while largely within the discretion of the trial justice, involves judicial reexamination of the sentence to determine whether a more lenient sentence is proper.[Footnote 4] When ruling on such a motion, a trial justice is guided by several factors, including \xe2\x80\x9c(1) the severity of the crime, (2) the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s personal, educational, and employment background, (3) the potential for rehabilitation, (4) the element of societal deterrence, and (5) the appropriateness of the punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d State v. Mollicone, 746 A. 2d 135, 138 (R. I. 2000) (per curiam) (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Ruffner, supra, at 867; Coleman, supra, at 655. On appeal from a trial justice\xe2\x80\x99s decision on a motion to reduce sentence, the Supreme Court of Rhode Island evaluates the trial justice\xe2\x80\x99s justifications in light of the relevant sentencing factors to determine whether a sentence is \xe2\x80\x9cwithout justification\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cgrossly disparate from other sentences.\xe2\x80\x9d Ruffner, supra, at 867 (internal quotation marks omitted).[Footnote 5] This process surely qualifies as \xe2\x80\x9creview\xe2\x80\x9d of a sentence within the meaning of \xc2\xa72244(d)(2).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We thus hold that a motion to reduce sentence under Rhode Island law is an application for \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d that triggers AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s tolling provision.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In resisting this interpretation, Rhode Island advances several arguments that we find unpersuasive.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first of these arguments begins by observing that, whenever our opinions have used the precise phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review,\xe2\x80\x9d the proceeding in question was one challenging the \xe2\x80\x9clawfulness\xe2\x80\x9d of a prior judgment, Brief for Petitioner 21\xe2\x80\x9322, such as a \xc2\xa72254 or \xc2\xa72255 action, see id., at 25. Rhode Island argues that Congress, in enacting AEDPA, must be presumed to have been aware of this usage and must have intended the phrase to carry this narrow meaning.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This argument reads far too much into these prior references to \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d While our opinions have used the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d to refer to proceedings that challenge the lawfulness of a prior judgment, we have never suggested that the phrase may properly be used to describe only proceedings of this type. In addition, Rhode Island overlooks opinions describing a motion to reduce sentence as \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral.\xe2\x80\x9d E.g., Robinson, 361 U. S., at 230, n.\xc2\xa014; Fernandez, supra, at 1492; see also 1 D. Wilkes, State Postconviction Remedies and Relief Handbook \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71:2, 1:7, pp. 2, 15 (2010) (characterizing a motion to reduce sentence as a \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpostconviction\xe2\x80\x9d remedy).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a related argument, Rhode Island notes that several other AEDPA provisions use the term \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d to refer to proceedings that involve a challenge to the lawfulness of a state-court judgment, see 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(A),\xc2\xa0(d)(1)(C), 2254(e)(2)(A)(i),[Footnote 6] and Rhode Island reasons that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa72244(d)(2) should be limited to proceedings of this nature. This argument has the same flaw as the argument just discussed. Just because the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d encompasses proceedings that challenge the lawfulness of a prior judgment, it does not follow that other proceedings may not also be described as involving \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, Rhode Island contends that the purpose of the tolling provision is to allow a state prisoner to exhaust state remedies and that this purpose is not served when a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s state application merely seeks sentencing leniency, a matter that cannot be raised in a federal habeas petition. This argument is based on an excessively narrow understanding of \xc2\xa72244(d)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s role.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is certainly true that a purpose\xe2\x80\x94and perhaps the chief purpose\xe2\x80\x94of tolling under \xc2\xa72244(d)(2) is to permit \nthe exhaustion of state remedies, see Duncan, 533 U. S., at 178\xe2\x80\x93179, but that is not \xc2\xa72244(d)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s only role. The tolling provision \xe2\x80\x9cprovides a powerful incentive for litigants to exhaust all available state remedies before proceeding in the lower federal courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 180 (emphasis added). Tolling the limitation period for all \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d motions provides both litigants and States with an opportunity to resolve objections at the state level, potentially obviating the need for a litigant to resort to federal court. If, for example, a litigant obtains relief on state-law grounds, there may be no need for federal habeas. The same dynamic may be present to a degree with respect to motions that do not challenge the lawfulness of a judgment. If a defendant receives relief in state court, the need for federal habeas review may be narrowed or even obviated, and this furthers principles of \xe2\x80\x9ccomity, finality, and federalism.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams, 529 U. S., at 436.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rhode Island\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa72244(d)(2) would also greatly complicate the work of federal habeas courts. Rhode Island would require those courts to separate motions for a reduced sentence into two categories: those that challenge a sentence on legal grounds and those that merely ask for leniency. But this taxonomy is problematic. Even if a jurisdiction allows sentencing judges to exercise a high degree of discretion in selecting a sentence from within a prescribed range, it does not necessarily follow that the judge\xe2\x80\x99s choice is insulated from challenge on legal grounds. \xe2\x80\x9c[D]iscretionary choices are not left to a court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98inclination, but to its judgment; and its judgment is to be guided by sound legal principles.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Albemarle Paper Co. v. Moody, 422 U. S. 405, 416 (1975) (quoting United States v. Burr, 25 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 30, 35 (No.\xc2\xa014,692d) (CC Va. 1807) (Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J.)). If the law of a jurisdiction provides criteria to guide a trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of sentencing discretion, a motion to reduce sentence may argue that a sentence is inconsistent with those criteria. In that sense, the motion argues that the sentence is contrary to sentencing law. See, e.g., Ruffner, 5 A.\xc2\xa03d, at 867 (\xe2\x80\x9cA trial justice considers a number of factors when determining a fair sentence[,] including the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s potential for rehabilitation. The defendant asserts that the trial justice did not consider defendant\xe2\x80\x99s participation in rehabilitative programs\xe2\x80\x9d (citations omitted)). We do not think that \xc2\xa72244(d)(2) was meant to require federal habeas courts to draw the sort of difficult distinction that Rhode Island\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation would demand.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We also reject the argument that the meaning of the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d should turn on whether the motion or application that triggers that review is captioned as a part of the criminal case or as a separate proceeding. See Walkowiak v. Haines, 272 F.\xc2\xa03d 234, 237 (CA4 2001). This interpretation of \xc2\xa72244(d)(2) would produce confusion and inconsistency.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For one thing, some \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d proceedings are often regarded as part of the criminal case. We have said, for example, that a writ of coram nobis \xe2\x80\x9cis a step in the criminal case and not \xe2\x80\xa6 a separate case and record, the beginning of a separate civil proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Morgan, 346 U. S., at 505, n.\xc2\xa04; see also United States v. Denedo, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 8) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n application for the writ is properly viewed as a belated extension of the original proceeding during which the error allegedly transpired\xe2\x80\x9d). But we have nonetheless suggested that coram nobis is a means of \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral attack.\xe2\x80\x9d Morgan, supra, at 510\xe2\x80\x93511 (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Robinson, 361 U. S., at 230, n.\xc2\xa014. Similarly, a motion under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255 (2006 ed., Supp. III) is entered on the docket of the original criminal case and is typically referred to the judge who originally presided over the challenged proceedings, see \xc2\xa72255 Rules 3(b), 4(a), but there is no dispute that \xc2\xa72255 proceedings are \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral,\xe2\x80\x9d see, e.g., Massaro v. United States, 538 U. S. 500, 504 (2003) (describing \xc2\xa72255 proceedings as \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d); Daniels v. United States, 532 U. S. 374, 379 (2001) (same).[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, the methods of filing for postconviction or collateral review vary among the States. In the District of Columbia and fourteen States, the principal postconviction remedy is part of the original case; in other States, it is not. 1 Wilkes, State Postconviction Remedies and Relief Handbook \xc2\xa71:3, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. Given the States\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cdifferent forms of collateral review,\xe2\x80\x9d Duncan, 533 U. S., at 177, the ap-plication of AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s tolling provision should not turn \non such formalities. See ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress may have refrained from exclusive reliance on the term \xe2\x80\x98post-conviction\xe2\x80\x99 so as to leave no doubt that the tolling provision applies \nto all types of state collateral review available after a conviction\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We thus define \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral review\xe2\x80\x9d according to its ordinary meaning: It refers to judicial review that occurs in a proceeding outside of the direct review process.

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.
