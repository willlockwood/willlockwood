\n\t\t\t\tSyllabus\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tMcQUIGGIN, WARDEN v. PERKINS\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 12\xe2\x80\x93126.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 25, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 28, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tRodney Henderson was found stabbed to death after leaving a party in Flint, Michigan, with respondent Floyd Perkins and Damarr Jones. Perkins was charged with murder. Jones, the key prosecution witness, testified that Perkins alone committed the murder while Jones looked on. Perkins, however, testified that Jones and Henderson left him during the evening, and that he later saw Jones with blood on his clothing. Perkins was convicted of first-degree murder and sentenced to life in prison without the possibility of parole. His conviction became final in 1997.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) gives a state prisoner one year to file a federal habeas petition, starting from \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the judgment became final.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(A). But if the petition alleges newly discovered evidence, the filing deadline is one year from \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the factual predicate of the claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. could have been discovered through .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. due diligence.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMore than 11 years after his conviction became final, Perkins filed his federal habeas petition, alleging, inter alia, ineffective assistance of trial counsel. To overcome AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s time limitations, he asserted newly discovered evidence of actual innocence, relying on three affidavits, the most recent dated July 16, 2002, each pointing to Jones as the murderer. The District Court found that, even if the affidavits could be characterized as evidence newly discovered, Perkins had failed to show diligence entitling him to equitable tolling of AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period. Alternatively, the court found, Perkins had not shown that, taking account of all the evidence, no reasonable juror would have convicted him. The Sixth Circuit reversed. Acknowledging that Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 petition was untimely and that he had not diligently pursued his rights, the court held that Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 actual-innocence claim allowed him to present his ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim as if it had been filed on time. In so ruling, the court apparently considered Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 delay irrelevant to appraisal of his actual-innocence claim. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t1.\xc2\xa0Actual innocence, if proved, serves as a gateway through which a petitioner may pass whether the impediment is a procedural bar, as it was in Schlup v. Delo, \n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 298\n, and House v. Bell, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 518\n, or expiration of the AEDPA statute of limitations, as in this case. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Perkins, who waited nearly six years from the date of the 2002 affidavit to file his petition, maintains that an actual-innocence plea can overcome AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s one-year limitations period. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions support his view. The Court has not resolved whether a prisoner may be entitled to habeas relief based on a freestanding actual-innocence claim, Herrera v. Collins, \n\n506 U.\xc2\xa0S. 390\n\xe2\x80\x93405, but it has recognized that a prisoner \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise subject to defenses of abusive or successive use of the writ may have his federal constitutional claim considered on the merits if he makes a proper showing of actual innocence,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 404. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court has applied this \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental miscarriage of justice exception\xe2\x80\x9d to overcome various procedural defaults, including, as most relevant here, failure to observe state procedural rules, such as filing deadlines. See Coleman v. Thompson, \n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 722\n. The exception, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions bear out, survived AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s passage. See, e.g., Calderon v. Thompson, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 538\n; House, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 537\xe2\x80\x93538. These decisions \xe2\x80\x9csee[k] to balance the societal interests in finality, comity, and conservation of scarce judicial resources with the individual interest in justice that arises in the extraordinary case.\xe2\x80\x9d Schlup, 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 324. Sensitivity to the injustice of incarcerating an innocent individual should not abate when the impediment is AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The State urges that recognition of a miscarriage of justice exception would render \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D) superfluous. That is not so, for AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s time limitations apply to the typical case in which no actual-innocence claim is made, while the exception applies to a severely confined category: cases in which new evidence shows \xe2\x80\x9cit is more likely than not that \xe2\x80\x98no reasonable juror\xe2\x80\x99 would have convicted [the petitioner],\xe2\x80\x9d Schlup, 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 329. Many petitions that could not pass through the actual-innocence gateway will be timely or not measured by \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D)\xe2\x80\x99s triggering provision. Nor does Congress\xe2\x80\x99 inclusion of a miscarriage of justice exception in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B) and 2254(e)(2) indicate an intent to preclude courts from applying the exception in \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D) cases. Congress did not simply incorporate the miscarriage of justice exception into \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B) and 2254(e)(2). Rather, Congress constrained the exception\xe2\x80\x99s application with respect to second-or-successive petitions and the holding of evidentiary hearings in federal court. The more rational inference to draw from the incorporation of a modified version of the exception into other provisions of AEDPA is that, in a case not governed by those provisions, the exception survived AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s passage intact and unrestricted. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t2.\xc2\xa0A federal habeas court, faced with an actual-innocence gateway claim, should count unjustifiable delay on a habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s part, not as an absolute barrier to relief, but as a factor in determining whether actual innocence has been reliably shown. A petitioner invoking the miscarriage of justice exception \xe2\x80\x9cmust show that it is more likely than not that no reasonable juror would have convicted him in the light of the new evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Schlup, 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 327. Unexplained delay in presenting new evidence bears on the determination whether the petitioner has made the requisite showing. Taking account of the delay in the context of the merits of a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s actual-innocence claim, rather than treating timeliness as a threshold inquiry, is tuned to the exception\xe2\x80\x99s underlying rationale of ensuring \xe2\x80\x9cthat federal constitutional errors do not result in the incarceration of innocent persons.\xe2\x80\x9d Herrera, 506 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 404. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t3.\xc2\xa0Here, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s appraisal of Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 petition as insufficient to meet Schlup\xe2\x80\x99s actual-innocence standard should be dispositive, absent cause, which this Court does not currently see, for the Sixth Circuit to upset that evaluation. Under Schlup\xe2\x80\x99s demanding standard, the gateway should open only when a petition presents \xe2\x80\x9cevidence of innocence so strong that a court cannot have confidence in the outcome of the trial unless the court is also satisfied that the trial was free of nonharmless constitutional error.\xe2\x80\x9d 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 316. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9317.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t670 F.\xc2\xa03d 665, vacated and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tGREG McQUIGGIN, WARDEN, PETITIONER v. FLOYD PERKINS\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[May 28, 2013]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t This case concerns the \xe2\x80\x9cactual innocence\xe2\x80\x9d gateway to federal habeas review applied in Schlup v. Delo, \n\n513 U. S. 298 (1995)\n, and further explained in House v. Bell, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 518 (2006)\n. In those cases, a convincing showing of actual innocence enabled habeas petitioners to overcome a procedural bar to consideration of the merits of their constitutional claims. Here, the question arises in the context of \n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(1), the statute of limitations on federal habeas petitions prescribed in the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996. Specifically, if the petitioner does not file her federal habeas peti- tion, at the latest, within one year of \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the factual predicate of the claim or claims presented could have been discovered through the exercise of due diligence,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D), can the time bar be overcome by a convincing showing that she committed no crime?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe hold that actual innocence, if proved, serves as a gateway through which a petitioner may pass whether the impediment is a procedural bar, as it was in Schlup and House, or, as in this case, expiration of the statute of limitations. We caution, however, that tenable actual-innocence gateway pleas are rare: \xe2\x80\x9c[A] petitioner does not meet the threshold requirement unless he persuades the district court that, in light of the new evidence, no juror, acting reasonably, would have voted to find him guilty beyond a reasonable doubt.\xe2\x80\x9d Schlup, 513 U. S., at 329; see House, 547 U. S., at 538 (emphasizing that the Schlup standard is \xe2\x80\x9cdemanding\xe2\x80\x9d and seldom met). And in making an assessment of the kind Schlup envisioned, \xe2\x80\x9cthe timing of the [petition]\xe2\x80\x9d is a factor bearing on the \xe2\x80\x9creliability of th[e] evidence\xe2\x80\x9d purporting to show actual innocence. Schlup, 513 U. S., at 332.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the instant case, the Sixth Circuit acknowledged that habeas petitioner Perkins (respondent here) had filed his petition after the statute of limitations ran out, and had \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to diligently pursue his rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Order in No. 09\xe2\x80\x931875, (CA6, Feb. 24, 2010), p.\xc2\xa02 (Certificate of Appealability). Nevertheless, the Court of Appeals reversed the decision of the District Court denying Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 petition, and held that Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 actual-innocence claim allowed him to pursue his habeas petition as if it had been filed on time. 670 F.\xc2\xa03d 665, 670 (2012). The appeals court ap-parently considered a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s delay irrelevant to ap-praisal of an actual-innocence claim. See ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe vacate the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment and remand the case. Our opinion clarifies that a federal habeas court, faced with an actual-innocence gateway claim, should count unjustifiable delay on a habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s part, not as an absolute barrier to relief, but as a factor in determining whether actual innocence has been re- liably shown. See Brief for Respondent 45 (habeas court \xe2\x80\x9ccould .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. hold the unjustified delay against the petitioner when making credibility findings as to whether the [actual-innocence] exception has been met\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn March 4, 1993, respondent Floyd Perkins attended a party in Flint, Michigan, in the company of his friend, Rodney Henderson, and an acquaintance, Damarr Jones. The three men left the party together. Henderson was later discovered on a wooded trail, murdered by stab wounds to his head.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPerkins was charged with the murder of Henderson. At trial, Jones was the key witness for the prosecution. He testified that Perkins alone committed the murder while Jones looked on. App. 55.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChauncey Vaughn, a friend of Perkins and Henderson, testified that, prior to the murder, Perkins had told him he would kill Henderson, id., at 39, and that Perkins later called Vaughn, confessing to his commission of the crime. Id., at 36\xe2\x80\x9338. A third witness, Torriano Player, also a friend of both Perkins and Henderson, testified that Perkins told him, had he known how Player felt about Henderson, he would not have killed Henderson. Id., at 74.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPerkins, testifying in his own defense, offered a different account of the episode. He testified that he left Hender-son and Jones to purchase cigarettes at a convenience store. When he exited the store, Perkins related, Jones and Henderson were gone. Id., at 84. Perkins said that he then visited his girlfriend. Id., at 87. About an hour later, Perkins recalled, he saw Jones standing under a streetlight with blood on his pants, shoes, and plaid coat. Id., at 90.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe jury convicted Perkins of first-degree murder. He was sentenced to life in prison without the possibility of parole on October 27, 1993. The Michigan Court of Appeals affirmed Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 conviction and sentence, and the Michigan Supreme Court denied Perkins leave to appeal on January 31, 1997. Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 conviction became final on May 5, 1997.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), \n\n110Stat.\n1214, a state prisoner ordinarily has one year to file a federal petition for habeas corpus, starting from \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the judgment became final by the conclusion of direct review or the ex-piration of the time for seeking such review.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(A). If the petition alleges newly discovered evidence, however, the filing deadline is one year from \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the factual predicate of the claim or claims presented could have been discovered through the exercise of due diligence.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPerkins filed his federal habeas corpus petition on June 13, 2008, more than 11 years after his conviction became final. He alleged, inter alia, ineffective assistance on the part of his trial attorney, depriving him of his \n \nSixth Amendment right to competent counsel. To overcome AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s time limitations, Perkins asserted newly discovered evidence of actual innocence. He relied on three affidavits, each pointing to Jones, not Perkins, as Henderson\xe2\x80\x99s murderer.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe first affidavit, dated January 30, 1997, was submitted by Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 sister, Ronda Hudson. Hudson stated that she had heard from a third party, Louis Ford, that Jones bragged about stabbing Henderson and had taken his clothes to the cleaners after the murder. App. to Pet. for Cert. 54a\xe2\x80\x9355a. The second affidavit, dated March 16, 1999, was subscribed to by Demond Louis, Chauncey Vaughn\xe2\x80\x99s younger brother. Louis stated that, on the night of the murder, Jones confessed to him that he had just killed Henderson. Louis also described the clothes Jones wore that night, bloodstained orange shoes and orange pants, and a colorful shirt. Id., at 50a\xe2\x80\x9353a. The next day, Louis added, he accompanied Jones, first to a dumpster where Jones disposed of the bloodstained shoes, and then to the cleaners. Finally, Perkins presented the July 16, 2002 affidavit of Linda Fleming, an employee at Pro-Clean Cleaners in 1993. She stated that, on or about March 4, 1993, a man matching Jones\xe2\x80\x99s description entered the shop and asked her whether bloodstains could be removed from the pants and a shirt he brought in. The pants were orange, she recalled, and heavily stained with blood, as was the multicolored shirt left for cleaning along with the pants. Id., at 48a\xe2\x80\x9349a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court found the affidavits insufficient to entitle Perkins to habeas relief. Characterizing the affidavits as newly discovered evidence was \xe2\x80\x9cdubious,\xe2\x80\x9d the District Court observed, in light of what Perkins knew about the underlying facts at the time of trial. Id., at 29a. But even assuming qualification of the affidavits as evidence newly discovered, the District Court next explained, \xe2\x80\x9c[Perkins\xe2\x80\x99] petition [was] untimely under \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D).\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9c[If] the statute of limitations began to run as of the date of the latest of th[e] affidavits, July 16, 2002,\xe2\x80\x9d the District Court noted, then \xe2\x80\x9cabsent tolling, [Perkins] had until July 16, 2003 in which to file his habeas petition.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Perkins, however, did not file until nearly five years later, on June 13, 2008.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder Sixth Circuit precedent, the District Court stated, \xe2\x80\x9ca habeas petitioner who demonstrates a credible claim of actual innocence based on new evidence may, in ex-ceptional circumstances, be entitled to equitable tolling of habeas limitations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 30a. But Perkins had not established exceptional circumstances, the District Court determined. In any event, the District Court observed, equitable tolling requires diligence and Perkins \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] failed utterly to demonstrate the necessary diligence in exercising his rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 31a. Alternatively, the Dis-trict Court found that Perkins had failed to meet the strict standard by which pleas of actual innocence are mea-sured: He had not shown that, taking account of all the evidence, \xe2\x80\x9cit is more likely than not that no reasonable juror would have convicted him,\xe2\x80\x9d or even that the evidence was new. Id., at 30a\xe2\x80\x9331a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPerkins appealed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. Al-though recognizing that AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations had expired and that Perkins had not diligently pursued his rights, the Sixth Circuit granted a certificate of appealability limited to a single question: Is reasonable diligence a precondition to relying on actual innocence as a gateway to adjudication of a federal habeas petition on the merits? Certificate of Appealability 2\xe2\x80\x933.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOn consideration of the certified question, the Court of Appeals reversed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. Adhering to Circuit precedent, Souter v. Jones, 395 F.\xc2\xa03d 577, 597\xe2\x80\x93602 (2005), the Sixth Circuit held that Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 gateway actual-innocence allegations allowed him to present his ineffective-assistance-of-counsel claim as if it were filed on time. On remand, the Court of Appeals instructed, \xe2\x80\x9cthe [D]istrict [C]ourt [should] fully consider whether Perkins assert[ed] a credible claim of actual innocence.\xe2\x80\x9d 670 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 676.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari to resolve a Circuit conflict on whether AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations can be overcome by a showing of actual innocence. 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012). Compare, e.g., San Martin v. McNeil, 633 F.\xc2\xa03d 1257, 1267\xe2\x80\x931268 (CA11 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9cA court . . . may consider an untimely \xc2\xa72254 petition if, by refusing to consider the petition for untimeliness, the court thereby would endorse a \xe2\x80\x98fundamental miscarriage of justice\xe2\x80\x99 because it would require that an individual who is actually innocent remain imprisoned.\xe2\x80\x9d), with, e.g., Escamilla v. Jungwirth, 426 F.\xc2\xa03d 868, 871\xe2\x80\x93872 (CA7 2005) (\xe2\x80\x9cPrisoners claiming to be innocent, like those contending that other events spoil the conviction, must meet the statutory requirement of timely action.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Rivas v. Fischer, 687 F.\xc2\xa03d 514, 548 (CA2 2012) (collecting cases).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Holland v. Florida, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2010), this Court addressed the circumstances in which a federal habeas petitioner could invoke the doctrine of \xe2\x80\x9cequitable tolling.\xe2\x80\x9d Holland held that \xe2\x80\x9ca [habeas] petitioner is entitled to equitable tolling only if he shows (1) that he has been pursuing his rights diligently, and (2) that some extraordinary circumstance stood in his way and prevented timely filing.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 16\xe2\x80\x9317) (internal quotation marks omitted). As the courts below comprehended, Perkins does not qualify for equitable tolling. In possession of all three affidavits by July 2002, he waited nearly six years to seek federal postconviction relief. \xe2\x80\x9cSuch a delay falls far short of demonstrating the . . . diligence\xe2\x80\x9d required to entitle a petitioner to equitable tolling. App. to Pet. for Cert. 31a (District Court opinion). See also Certificate of Appealability 2.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPerkins, however, asserts not an excuse for filing after the statute of limitations has run. Instead, he maintains that a plea of actual innocence can overcome AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s one-year statute of limitations. He thus seeks an equi-table exception to \xc2\xa72244(d)(1), not an extension of the time statutorily prescribed. See Rivas, 687 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 547, n. 42 (distinguishing from \xe2\x80\x9cequitable tolling\xe2\x80\x9d a plea to override the statute of limitations when actual innocence is shown).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDecisions of this Court support Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 view of the significance of a convincing actual-innocence claim. We have not resolved whether a prisoner may be entitled to habeas relief based on a freestanding claim of actual innocence. Herrera v. Collins, \n\n506 U.\xc2\xa0S. 390\n\xe2\x80\x93405 (1993). We have recognized, however, that a prisoner \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise subject to defenses of abusive or successive use of the writ [of habeas corpus] may have his federal constitutional claim considered on the merits if he makes a proper showing of actual innocence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 404 (citing Sawyer v. Whitley, \n\n505 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333 (1992)\n). See also Murray v. Carrier, \n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 478, \n496 (1986)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e think that in an extraordinary case, where a constitutional violation has probably resulted in the conviction of one who is actually innocent, a federal habeas court may grant the writ even in the absence of a showing of cause for the procedural default.\xe2\x80\x9d). In other words, a credible showing of actual innocence may allow a prisoner to pursue his constitu-tional claims (here, ineffective assistance of counsel) on the merits notwithstanding the existence of a procedural bar to relief. \xe2\x80\x9cThis rule, or fundamental miscarriage of justice exception, is grounded in the \xe2\x80\x98equitable discretion\xe2\x80\x99 of habeas courts to see that federal constitutional errors do not result in the incarceration of innocent persons.\xe2\x80\x9d Herrera, 506 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 404.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have applied the miscarriage of justice exception to overcome various procedural defaults. These include \xe2\x80\x9csuccessive\xe2\x80\x9d petitions asserting previously rejected claims, see Kuhlmann v. Wilson, \n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 436, \n454 (1986)\n (plurality opinion), \xe2\x80\x9cabusive\xe2\x80\x9d petitions asserting in a second petition claims that could have been raised in a first petition, see McCleskey v. Zant, \n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 467\n\xe2\x80\x93495 (1991), failure to develop facts in state court, see Keeney v. Tamayo-Reyes, \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9312 (1992), and failure to observe state procedural rules, including filing deadlines, see Coleman v. Thompson, \n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 722, \n750 (1991)\n; Carrier, 477 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 495\xe2\x80\x93496.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe miscarriage of justice exception, our decisions bear out, survived AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s passage. In Calderon v. Thompson, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 538 (1998)\n, we applied the exception to hold that a federal court may, consistent with AEDPA, recall its mandate in order to revisit the merits of a decision. Id., at 558 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe miscarriage of justice standard is altogether consistent . . . with AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s central concern that the merits of concluded criminal proceedings not be revisited in the absence of a strong showing of actual innocence.\xe2\x80\x9d). In Bousley v. United States, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 614, \n622 (1998)\n, we held, in the context of \xc2\xa72255, that actual in-nocence may overcome a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s failure to raise a constitutional objection on direct review. Most recently, in House, we reiterated that a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s proof of actual innocence may provide a gateway for federal habeas review of a procedurally defaulted claim of constitutional error. 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 537\xe2\x80\x93538.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese decisions \xe2\x80\x9csee[k] to balance the societal interests in finality, comity, and conservation of scarce judicial re-sources with the individual interest in justice that arises in the extraordinary case.\xe2\x80\x9d Schlup, 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 324. Sensitivity to the injustice of incarcerating an innocent individual should not abate when the impediment is AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs just noted, see supra, at 8, we have held that the miscarriage of justice exception applies to state procedural rules, including filing deadlines. Coleman, 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 750. A federal court may invoke the miscarriage of justice exception to justify consideration of claims defaulted in state court under state timeliness rules. See ibid. The State\xe2\x80\x99s reading of AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s time prescription would thus accord greater force to a federal deadline than to a simi-larly designed state deadline. It would be passing strange to interpret a statute seeking to promote federalism and comity as requiring stricter enforcement of federal procedural rules than procedural rules established and enforced by the States.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe State ties to \xc2\xa72244(d)\xe2\x80\x99s text its insistence that AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations precludes courts from considering late-filed actual-innocence gateway claims. \xe2\x80\x9cSection 2244(d)(1)(D),\xe2\x80\x9d the State contends, \xe2\x80\x9cforecloses any argument that a habeas petitioner has unlimited time to present new evidence in support of a constitutional claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 17. That is so, the State maintains, because AEDPA prescribes a comprehensive system for determining when its one-year limitations period begins to run. \xe2\x80\x9cIncluded within that system,\xe2\x80\x9d the State observes, \xe2\x80\x9cis a specific trigger for the precise circumstance presented here: a constitutional claim based on new evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Section 2244(d)(1)(D) runs the clock from \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the factual predicate of the claim . . . could have been discovered through the exercise of due diligence.\xe2\x80\x9d In light of that provision, the State urges, \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no need for the courts to act in equity to provide additional time for persons who allege actual innocence as a gateway to their claims of constitutional error.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 request for an equitable exception to the statute of limitations, the State charges, would \xe2\x80\x9crende[r] superfluous this carefully scripted scheme.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 18.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe State\xe2\x80\x99s argument in this regard bears blinders. AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s time limitations apply to the typical case in which no allegation of actual innocence is made. The miscarriage of justice exception, we underscore, applies to a severely confined category: cases in which new evidence shows \xe2\x80\x9cit is more likely than not that no reasonable ju- ror would have convicted [the petitioner].\xe2\x80\x9d Schlup, 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 329 (internal quotation marks omitted). Section 2244(d)(1)(D) is both modestly more stringent (because it requires diligence) and dramatically less stringent (because it requires no showing of innocence). Many petitions that could not pass through the actual-innocence gateway will be timely or not measured by \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D)\xe2\x80\x99s triggering provision. That provision, in short, will hardly be rendered superfluous by recognition of the miscarriage of justice exception.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe State further relies on provisions of AEDPA other than \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D), namely, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B) and 2254(e) (2), to urge that Congress knew how to incorporate the miscarriage of justice exception when it was so minded. Section 2244(b)(2)(B), the State observes, provides that a petitioner whose first federal habeas petition has already been adjudicated when new evidence comes to light may file a second-or-successive petition when, and only when, the facts underlying the new claim would \xe2\x80\x9ces-tablish by clear and convincing evidence that, but for constitutional error, no reasonable factfinder would have found the applicant guilty of the underlying offense.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B)(ii). And \xc2\xa72254(e)(2), which generally bars evidentiary hearings in federal habeas proceedings ini-tiated by state prisoners, includes an exception for pris-oners who present new evidence of their innocence. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(e)(2)(A)(ii), (B) (permitting evidentiary hearings in federal court if \xe2\x80\x9cthe facts underlying the claim would be sufficient to establish by clear and convincing evidence that but for constitutional error, no reasonable factfinder would have found the applicant guilty of the underlying offense\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut Congress did not simply incorporate the miscarriage of justice exception into \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B) and 2254(e)(2). Rather, Congress constrained the application of the exception. Prior to AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, a court could grant relief on a second-or-successive petition, then known as an \xe2\x80\x9cabusive\xe2\x80\x9d petition, if the petitioner could show that \xe2\x80\x9ca fundamental miscarriage of justice would result from a failure to entertain the claim.\xe2\x80\x9d McCleskey, 499 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 495. Section 2244(b)(2)(B) limits the exception to cases in which \xe2\x80\x9cthe factual predicate for the claim could not have been discovered previously through the exercise of due diligence,\xe2\x80\x9d and the petitioner can establish that no reasonable factfinder \xe2\x80\x9cwould have found [her] guilty of the underlying offense\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Congress thus required second-or-successive habeas petitioners attempting to benefit from the miscarriage of justice exception to meet a higher level of proof (\xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing evidence\xe2\x80\x9d) and to satisfy a diligence requirement that did not exist prior to AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s passage.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLikewise, petitioners asserting actual innocence pre-AEDPA could obtain evidentiary hearings in federal court even if they failed to develop facts in state court. See Keeney, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 12 (\xe2\x80\x9cA habeas petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s failure to develop a claim in state-court proceedings will be excused and a hearing mandated if he can show that a fundamental miscarriage of justice would result from failure to hold a federal evidentiary hearing.\xe2\x80\x9d). Under AEDPA, a petitioner seeking an evidentiary hearing must show diligence and, in addition, establish her actual innocence by clear and convincing evidence. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72254(e)(2)(A)(ii), (B).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSections 2244(b)(2)(B) and 2254(e)(2) thus reflect Congress\xe2\x80\x99 will to modify the miscarriage of justice exception with respect to second-or-successive petitions and the hold-ing of evidentiary hearings in federal court. These pro-visions do not demonstrate Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to preclude courts from applying the exception, unmodified, to \xe2\x80\x9cthe type of petition at issue here\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94an untimely first federal habeas petition alleging a gateway actual-innocence claim. House, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 539.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The more rational inference to draw from Congress\xe2\x80\x99 incorporation of a modified version of the miscarriage of justice exception in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B) and 2254(e)(2) is simply this: In a case not governed by those provisions, i.e., a first petition for federal habeas relief, the miscarriage of justice exception survived AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s passage intact and unrestricted.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur reading of the statute is supported by the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in Holland. \xe2\x80\x9c[E]quitable principles have traditionally governed the substantive law of habeas corpus,\xe2\x80\x9d Holland reminded, and affirmed that \xe2\x80\x9cwe will not construe a statute to displace courts\xe2\x80\x99 traditional equitable authority absent the clearest command.\xe2\x80\x9d 560 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 13) (internal quotation marks omitted). The text of \xc2\xa72244(d)(1) contains no clear command countering the courts\xe2\x80\x99 equitable authority to invoke the miscarriage of justice exception to overcome expiration of the statute of limitations governing a first federal habeas petition. As we observed in Holland,\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cAEDPA seeks to eliminate delays in the federal habeas review process. But AEDPA seeks to do so without undermining basic habeas corpus principles and while seeking to harmonize the new statute with prior law .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. When Congress codified new rules governing this previously judicially managed area of law, it did so without losing sight of the fact that the writ of habeas corpus plays a vital role in protecting constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 16) (citations \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t and internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHaving rejected the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument that \xc2\xa72244(d) (1)(D) precludes a court from entertaining an un- timely first federal habeas petition raising a convincing claim of actual innocence, we turn to the State\xe2\x80\x99s further objection to the Sixth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. Even if a habeas petitioner asserting a credible claim of actual innocence may overcome AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations, the State argues, the Court of Appeals erred in finding that no threshold diligence requirement at all applies to Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 petition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile formally distinct from its argument that \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D)\xe2\x80\x99s text forecloses a late-filed claim alleging actual innocence, the State\xe2\x80\x99s contention makes scant sense. Section 2244(d)(1)(D) requires a habeas petitioner to file a claim within one year of the time in which new evidence \xe2\x80\x9ccould have been discovered through the exercise of due diligence.\xe2\x80\x9d It would be bizarre to hold that a habeas \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tpetitioner who asserts a convincing claim of actual innocence may overcome the statutory time bar \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D) erects, yet simultaneously encounter a court-fashioned diligence barrier to pursuit of her petition. See 670 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 673 (\xe2\x80\x9cRequiring reasonable diligence effectively makes the concept of the actual innocence gateway redundant, since petitioners .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. seek [an equitable exception only] when they were not reasonably diligent in complying with \xc2\xa72244(d)(1)(D).\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile we reject the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument that habeas petitioners who assert convincing actual-innocence claims must prove diligence to cross a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s threshold, we hold that the Sixth Circuit erred to the extent that it eliminated timing as a factor relevant in evaluating the reliability of a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proof of innocence. To invoke the miscarriage of justice exception to AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations, we repeat, a petitioner \xe2\x80\x9cmust show that it is more likely than not that no reasonable juror would have convicted him in the light of the new evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Schlup, 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 327. Unexplained delay in presenting new evidence bears on the determination whether the petitioner has made the requisite showing. Perkins so acknowl-edges. See Brief for Respondent 52 (unjustified delay may figure in determining \xe2\x80\x9cwhether a petitioner has made a sufficient showing of innocence\xe2\x80\x9d). As we stated in Schlup, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] court may consider how the timing of the submission and the likely credibility of [a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] affiants bear on the probable reliability of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. evidence [of actual innocence].\xe2\x80\x9d 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 332. See also House, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 537.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConsidering a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s diligence, not discretely, but as part of the assessment whether actual innocence has been convincingly shown, attends to the State\xe2\x80\x99s concern that it will be prejudiced by a prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s untoward delay in proffering new evidence. The State fears that a prisoner might \xe2\x80\x9clie in wait and use stale evidence to collaterally \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tattack his conviction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. when an elderly witness has died and cannot appear at a hearing to rebut new evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 25. The timing of such a petition, however, should seriously undermine the credibility of the actual-innocence claim. Moreover, the deceased witness\xe2\x80\x99 prior testimony, which would have been subject to cross-examination, could be introduced in the event of a new trial. See Crawford v. Washington, \n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 36\n\xe2\x80\x9354 (2004) (recognizing exception to the Confrontation Clause where witness is unavailable and the defendant had a prior opportunity for cross-examination). And frivolous petitions should occasion instant dismissal. See \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254 Rule 4. Focusing on the merits of a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s actual-innocence claim and taking account of delay in that context, rather than treating timeliness as a threshold inquiry, is tuned to the rationale underlying the miscarriage of justice exception\xe2\x80\x94i.e., ensuring \xe2\x80\x9cthat federal constitutional errors do not result in the incarceration of innocent persons.\xe2\x80\x9d Herrera, 506 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 404.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe now return to the case at hand. The District Court proceeded properly in first determining that Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 claim was filed well beyond AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s limitations period and that equitable tolling was unavailable to Perkins because he could demonstrate neither exceptional circumstances nor diligence. See supra, at 5. The District Court then found that Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 alleged newly discovered evidence, i.e., the information contained in the three affidavits, was \xe2\x80\x9csubstantially available to [Perkins] at trial.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tApp. to Pet. for Cert. 31a. Moreover, the proffered evidence, even if \xe2\x80\x9cnew,\xe2\x80\x9d was hardly adequate to show that, had it been presented at trial, no reasonable juror would have convicted Perkins. Id., at 30a\xe2\x80\x9331a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Sixth Circuit granted a certificate of appealability limited to the question whether reasonable diligence is a precondition to reliance on actual innocence as a gateway to adjudication of a federal habeas petition on the merits. We have explained that untimeliness, although not an unyielding ground for dismissal of a petition, does bear on the credibility of evidence proffered to show actual innocence. On remand, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s appraisal of Perkins\xe2\x80\x99 petition as insufficient to meet Schlup\xe2\x80\x99s actual-innocence standard should be dispositive, absent cause, which we do not currently see, for the Sixth Circuit to upset that evaluation. We stress once again that the Schlup standard is demanding. The gateway should open only when a petition presents \xe2\x80\x9cevidence of innocence so strong that a court cannot have confidence in the outcome of the trial unless the court is also satisfied that the trial was free of nonharmless constitutional error.\xe2\x80\x9d 513 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 316.\n\t\t\t

Delo, \n (petitioner \xe2\x80\x9cmust show that it is more likely than not that no reasonable juror would have convicted him in the light of the new evidence\xe2\x80\x9d), with a \xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing\xe2\x80\x9d evidence requirement. 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 539 (internal quotation marks omitted). As here, the State relied on \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B)(ii) and 2254(e)(2) to support its argument. But \xe2\x80\x9c[n]either provision address[ed] the type of petition at issue .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [,] a first federal habeas petition seeking consideration of defaulted claims based on a showing of actual innocence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Consequently, we held inapplicable to first petitions the stricter standard AEDPA prescribed for second-or-successive petitions. Ibid.\n\t\n
\n\n2\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Prior to AEDPA, it is true, this Court had not ruled that a credible claim of actual innocence could supersede a federal statute of limitations. The reason why that is so is evident: Pre-AEDPA, petitions for federal habeas relief were not governed by any statute of limitations. Notably, we said in Coleman v. Thompson, \n, that a petitioner who failed to comply with a timeliness requirement in state court could nevertheless plead her claims on the merits in federal court if she could show that \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to consider the claims [would] result in a fundamental miscarriage of justice.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 750.\n\t\n
\n\n3\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0For eight pages, the dissent stridently insists that federal (although not state) statutes of limitations allow no exceptions not contained in the text. Well, not quite so, the dissent ultimately acknowledges. Post, at 8. Even AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations, the dissent admits, is subject to equitable tolling. But that is because equitable tolling \xe2\x80\x9ccan be seen as a reasonable assumption of genuine legislative intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 9. Why is it not an equally reasonable assumption that Congress would want a limitations period to yield when what is at stake is a State\xe2\x80\x99s incarceration of an individual for a crime, it has become clear, no reasonable person would find he committed? For all its bluster,the dissent agrees with the Court on a crucial point: Congress legis-lates against the backdrop of existing law. Post, at 10. At the timeof AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, multiple decisions of this Court applied the miscarriage of justice exception to overcome various threshold barriers to relief. See supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x939. It is hardly \xe2\x80\x9cunprecedented,\xe2\x80\x9d therefore, to conclude that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress intended or could have anticipated [a miscarriage of justice] exception\xe2\x80\x9d when it enacted AEDPA. Post, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\n
\n\n4\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0We note one caveat: A showing that delay was part of a deliberate attempt to manipulate the case, say by waiting until a key prosecution witness died or was deported, might raise a different ground for withholding equitable relief. No such contention was presented here, however, so we do not discuss the point.\n\t\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Scalia, with whom The Chief Justice and Justice Thomas join, and with whom Justice Alito joins as to Parts I, II, and III, dissenting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) provides that a \xe2\x80\x9c1-year period of limitation shall apply\xe2\x80\x9d to a state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s application for a writ of habeas corpus in federal court. \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(d)(1). The gaping hole in today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion for the Court is its failure to answer the crucial question upon which all else depends: What is the source of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s power to fashion what it concedes is an \xe2\x80\x9cexception\xe2\x80\x9d to this clear statu- tory command?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat question is unanswered because there is no answer. This Court has no such power, and not one of the cases cited by the opinion says otherwise. The Constitution vests legislative power only in Congress, which never enacted the exception the Court creates today. That inconvenient truth resolves this case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cActual innocence\xe2\x80\x9d has, until today, been an exception only to judge-made, prudential barriers to habeas relief, or as a means of channeling judges\xe2\x80\x99 statutorily conferred discretion not to apply a procedural bar. Never before have we applied the exception to circumvent a categorical statutory bar to relief. We have not done so because we have no power to do so. Where Congress has erected a constitutionally valid barrier to habeas relief, a court cannot decline to give it effect.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBefore AEDPA, the Supreme Court had developed an array of doctrines, see, e.g., Wainwright v. Sykes, \n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 72, \n87 (1977)\n (procedural default); McCleskey v. Zant, \n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 467, \n489 (1991)\n (abuse of the writ), to limit the ha- beas practice that it had radically expanded in the early or mid-20th century to include review of the merits of conviction and not merely jurisdiction of the convicting court, see Stone v. Powell, \n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 465\n\xe2\x80\x93478 (1976) (citing Frank v. Mangum, \n\n237 U.\xc2\xa0S. 309 (1915)\n); Brown v. Allen, \n\n344 U.\xc2\xa0S. 443\n\xe2\x80\x93534 (1953) (Jackson, J., concurring in result); Bator, Finality in Criminal Law and Federal Habeas Corpus for State Prisoners, 76 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 441, 483\xe2\x80\x93499 (1963). For example, the doctrine of procedural default holds that a state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s default of his federal claims \xe2\x80\x9cin state court pursuant to an independent and adequate state procedural rule\xe2\x80\x9d bars federal habeas review of those claims. Coleman v. Thompson, \n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 722, \n750 (1991)\n. That doctrine is not a statutory or ju- risdictional command; rather, it is a \xe2\x80\x9cprudential\xe2\x80\x9d rule \xe2\x80\x9cgrounded in \xe2\x80\x98considerations of comity and concerns for the orderly administration of criminal justice.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Dretke v. Haley, \n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 386\n\xe2\x80\x93393 (2004) (quoting Francis v. Henderson, \n\n425 U.\xc2\xa0S. 536\n\xe2\x80\x93539 (1976)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd what courts have created, courts can modify. One judge-made exception to procedural default allows a petitioner to proceed where he can demonstrate \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d for the default and \xe2\x80\x9cprejudice.\xe2\x80\x9d See Coleman, supra, at 750. As relevant here, we have also expressed a willingness to excuse a petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s default, even absent a showing of cause, \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a constitutional violation has probably resulted in the conviction of one who is actually innocent.\xe2\x80\x9d Murray v. Carrier, \n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 478, \n496 (1986)\n; see Schlup v. Delo, \n\n513 U.\xc2\xa0S. 298\n\xe2\x80\x93327 (1995); House v. Bell, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 518\n\xe2\x80\x93537 (2006).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere is nothing inherently inappropriate (as opposed to merely unwise) about judge-created exceptions to judge-made barriers to relief. Procedural default, for example, raises \xe2\x80\x9cno question of a federal district court\xe2\x80\x99s power to entertain an application for a writ of habeas corpus.\xe2\x80\x9d Francis, supra, at 538. Where a petitioner would, but for a judge-made doctrine like procedural default, have a good habeas claim, it offends no command of Congress\xe2\x80\x99s for a federal court to consider the petition. But that free-and-easy approach has no place where a statutory bar to habeas relief is at issue. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he power to award the writ by any of the courts of the United States, must be given by written law,\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte Bollman, 4 Cranch 75, 94 (1807) (Marshall, C.\xc2\xa0J.), and \xe2\x80\x9cjudgments about the proper scope of the writ are \xe2\x80\x98normally for Congress to make,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Felker v. Turpin, \n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 651, \n664 (1996)\n (quoting Lonchar v. Thomas, \n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 314, \n323 (1996)\n). One would have thought it too obvious to mention that this Court is duty bound to enforce AEDPA, not amend it.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause we have no \xe2\x80\x9cequitable\xe2\x80\x9d power to discard statutory barriers to habeas relief, we cannot simply extend judge-made exceptions to judge-made barriers into the statutory realm. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s insupportable leap from judge-made procedural bars to all procedural bars, including statutory bars, does all the work in its opinion\xe2\x80\x94and there is not a whit of precedential support for it. McCleskey v. Zant applied a \xe2\x80\x9cmiscarriage of justice\xe2\x80\x9d exception to the judge-made abuse-of-the-writ doctrine. 499 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 487\xe2\x80\x93489, 495. Coleman v. Thompson and Murray v. Carrier applied it to the judge-made procedural-default doctrine. 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 750; 477 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 496. Keeney v. Tamayo-Reyes, \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1992)\n, applied it to a variant of procedural default: a state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s failure adequately to develop material facts in state court. Id., at 8. Kuhlmann v. Wilson, \n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 436 (1986)\n, a plurality opinion, applied it to a statute that merely said lower federal courts \xe2\x80\x9cneed not\xe2\x80\x9d entertain successive petitions, thus leaving them with \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretion to entertain successive petitions under some circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 449, 451 (emphasis added). Not one of the cases on which the Court relies today supports the extraordinary premise that courts can create out of whole cloth an exception to a statutory bar to relief.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe opinion for the Court also trots out post-AEDPA cases to prove the irrelevant point that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he miscarriage of justice exception .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. survived AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s passage.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. What it ignores, yet again, is that after AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s passage, as before, the exception applied only to nonstatutory obstacles to relief. Bousley v. United States and House v. Bell were applications of the judge-made doctrine of procedural default. See Bousley, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 614, \n623 (1998)\n; id., at 625 (Stevens, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9cI agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s central holding\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0that none of its judge-made rules foreclose petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s collateral attack\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); id., at 630 (Scalia, J., dissenting); House, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 522. Calderon v. Thompson, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 538 (1998)\n, a non-AEDPA case, involved the courts of appeals\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cinherent power to recall their mandates, subject to review for an abuse of discretion,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 549; it stands only for the proposition that the miscarriage-of-justice exception is an appropriate \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98means of channeling\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that discretion, id., at 559 (quoting McCleskey, supra, at 496).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, in its way, acknowledges the dearth of precedential support for its holding. \xe2\x80\x9cPrior to AEDPA,\xe2\x80\x9d it concedes, \xe2\x80\x9cthis Court had not ruled that a credible claim of actual innocence could supersede a federal statute of limitations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13, n.\xc2\xa02. Its explanation for this lack of precedent is that before AEDPA, \xe2\x80\x9cpetitions for federal habeas relief were not governed by any statute of limitations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That is true but utterly unprobative. There are many statutory bars to relief other than statutes of limitations, and we had never (and before today, have never) created an actual-innocence exception to any of them. The reason why is obvious: Judicially amending a validly enacted statute in this way is a flagrant breach of the separation of powers.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court has no qualms about transgressing such a basic principle. It does not even attempt to cloak its act of judicial legislation in the pretense that it is merely construing the statute; indeed, it freely admits that its opinion recognizes an \xe2\x80\x9cexception\xe2\x80\x9d that the statute does not contain. Ante, at 7. And it dismisses, with a series of transparent non sequiturs, Michigan\xe2\x80\x99s overwhelming textual argument that the statute provides no such exception and envisions none.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe key textual point is that two provisions of \xc2\xa72244, working in tandem, provide a comprehensive path to relief for an innocent prisoner who has newly discovered evidence that supports his constitutional claim. Section 2244(d)(1)(D) gives him a fresh year in which to file, starting on \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the factual predicate of the claim or claims presented could have been discovered through the exercise of due diligence,\xe2\x80\x9d while \xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B) lifts the bar on second or successive petitions. Congress clearly anticipated the scenario of a habeas petitioner with a credible innocence claim and addressed it by crafting an exception (and an exception, by the way, more restrictive than the one that pleases the Court today). One cannot assume that Congress left room for other, judge-made applications of the actual-innocence exception, any more than one would add another gear to a Swiss watch on the theory that the watchmaker surely would have included it if he had thought of it. In both cases, the intricate craftsmanship tells us that the designer arranged things just as he wanted them.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s feeble rejoinder is that its (judicially in- vented) version of the \xe2\x80\x9cactual innocence\xe2\x80\x9d exception applies only to a \xe2\x80\x9cseverely confined category\xe2\x80\x9d of cases. Ante, at 10. Since cases qualifying for the actual-innocence exception will be rare, it explains, the statutory path for innocent petitioners will not \xe2\x80\x9cbe rendered superfluous.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That is no answer at all. That the Court\xe2\x80\x99s exception would not entirely frustrate Congress\xe2\x80\x99s design does not weaken the force of the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Congress addressed the issue comprehensively and chose to exclude dilatory prisoners like respondent. By the Court\xe2\x80\x99s logic, a statute banning littering could simply be deemed to contain an exception for cigarette butts; after all, the statute as thus amended would still cover something. That is not how a court respectful of the separation of powers should interpret statutes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven more bizarre is the Court\xe2\x80\x99s concern that applying AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations without recognizing an atextual actual-innocence exception would \xe2\x80\x9caccord greater force to a federal deadline than to a similarly designed state deadline.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9; see also ante, at 13, n.\xc2\xa02. The Court terms that outcome \xe2\x80\x9cpassing strange,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9, but it is not strange at all. Only federal statutes of limitations bind federal habeas courts with the force of law; a state statute of limitations is given effect on federal habeas review only by virtue of the judge-made doctrine of procedural default.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t See Coleman, 501 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 730\xe2\x80\x93731. With its eye firmly fixed on something it likes\xe2\x80\x94a shiny new exception to a statute unloved in the best circles\xe2\x80\x94the Court overlooks this basic distinction, which would not trouble a second-year law student armed with a copy of Hart & Wechsler. The Court simply ignores basic legal principles where they pose an obstacle to its policy-driven, free-form improvisation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s statutory-construction blooper reel does not end there. Congress\xe2\x80\x99s express inclusion of innocence-based exceptions in two neighboring provisions of the Act confirms, one would think, that there is no actual-innocence exception to \xc2\xa72244(d)(1). Section 2244(b)(2)(B), as already noted, lifts the bar on claims presented in second or successive petitions where \xe2\x80\x9cthe factual predicate for the claim could not have been discovered previously through .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. due diligence\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe facts underlying the claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would be sufficient to establish by clear and convincing evidence that, but for constitutional error, no reasonable factfinder would have found\xe2\x80\x9d the petitioner guilty. Section 2254(e)(2) permits a district court to hold an evidentiary hearing where a diligent state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s claim relies on new facts that \xe2\x80\x9cwould be sufficient to establish by clear and convincing evidence that but for constitutional error, no reasonable factfinder would have found\xe2\x80\x9d him guilty. Ordinarily, we would draw from the express enumeration of these two actual-innocence exceptions the inference that no others were intended.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s twisting path to the contrary conclusion is not easy to follow, but I will try. In the Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, the key fact here is that these two provisions of AEDPA codified what had previously been judge-made barriers to relief and applied to them a stricter actual-innocence standard than the courts had been applying. See ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. From this, the Court reasons that Congress made a conscious choice not also to apply the more restrictive actual-innocence standard to the statute of limitations. Ergo, the Court concludes, we are free to apply the more lenient version of the actual-innocence exception. Ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313. That clever account ignores the background against which Congress legislated. Of course Congress did not \xe2\x80\x9cconstrain\xe2\x80\x9d application of the actual-innocence exception to the statute of limitations. It felt no need to do so, because it had no reason whatsoever to suspect that any version of the exception would apply to the statute of limitations. The collective efforts of respondent and the majority have turned up not a single instance where this Court has applied the actual-innocence exception to any statutory barrier to habeas relief, much less to a statute of limitations. See Part I\xe2\x80\x93B, supra. What has been said of equitable tolling applies in spades to non-tolling judi- cial inventions: \xe2\x80\x9cCongress cannot intend to incorporate, by silence, various forms of equitable tolling that were not generally recognized in the common law at the time of enactment.\xe2\x80\x9d Bain & Colella, Interpreting Federal Statutes of Limitations, 37 Creighton L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 493, 503 (2004). The only conceivable relevance of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B) and 2254(e)(2) is (1) as we have said, that no other actual-innocence exception was intended, and (2) that if Congress had anticipated that this Court would amend \xc2\xa72244(d)(1) to add an actual-innocence exception (which it surely did not), it would have desired the more stringent formulation and not the expansive formulation applied today, which it specifically rejected for those other provisions.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThree years ago, in Holland v. Florida, 560 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2010), we held that AEDPA\xe2\x80\x99s statute of limitations is subject to equitable tolling. That holding offers no support for importing a novel actual-innocence exception. Equit- able tolling\xe2\x80\x94extending the deadline for a filing because of an event or circumstance that deprives the filer, through no fault of his own, of the full period accorded by the statute\xe2\x80\x94seeks to vindicate what might be considered the genuine intent of the statute. By contrast, suspending the statute because of a separate policy that the court believes should trump it (\xe2\x80\x9cactual innocence\xe2\x80\x9d) is a blatant over- ruling. Moreover, the doctrine of equitable tolling is centuries old, and dates from a time when the separation of the legislative and judicial powers was incomplete. See, e.g., Bree v. Holbech, 2 Doug. 655, 656 (1781) (Mansfield, J.); South-Sea Co. v. Wymondsell, 24 E. R. 1004, 3 P. Wms. 143, 144 (1732); Booth v. Warrington, 2 E. R. 111, 112\xe2\x80\x93113, 4 Bro. P.\xc2\xa0C. 163, 165\xe2\x80\x93166 (1714); see also Holmberg v. Armbrecht, \n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 392\n\xe2\x80\x93397 (1946); Exploration Co. v. United States, \n\n247 U.\xc2\xa0S. 435\n\xe2\x80\x93447 (1918); Bailey v. Glover, 21 Wall. 342, 348 (1875); Sherwood v. Sutton, 21 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 1303, 1304\xe2\x80\x931305 (No. 12,782) (CCNH 1828) (Story, J.); Jones v. Conoway, 4 Yeates 109 (Pa. 1804). As Professor Manning has explained, until the Glorious Revolution of 1688, the Crown retained \xe2\x80\x9cpretensions to independent legislative authority, and English judges continued to serve as the Crown\xe2\x80\x99s agents, in theory and practice a component of the executive. Given these conditions, which distinguish the old English from the American constitutional context, it is not surprising to find a similarly indistinct line between appropriate legislative and judicial functions in matters of interpretation.\xe2\x80\x9d Manning, Textualism and the Equity of the Statute, 101 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 36\xe2\x80\x9337 (2001) (footnote omitted). Thus, the doctrine of the equity of the statute, of which equitable tolling was an example, was reflected in Blackstone\xe2\x80\x99s Commentaries \xe2\x80\x9ctwo-thirds of the way through the eighteenth century.\xe2\x80\x9d Manning, supra, at 52.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAmerican courts\xe2\x80\x99 later adoption of the English equitable-tolling practice need not be regarded as a violation of the separation of powers, but can be seen as a reasonable assumption of genuine legislative intent. Colonial legislatures would have assumed that equitable tolling would attend any statute of limitations they adopted. In any case, equitable tolling surely represents such a reasonable assumption today. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is hornbook law that limitations periods are customarily subject to equitable tolling, unless tolling would be inconsistent with the text of the relevant statute. United States, \n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 43\n\xe2\x80\x9350 (2002) (internal quotation marks and citations omitted); see Manning, What Divides Textualists from Purposivists? 106 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 70, 81\xe2\x80\x9382, and n. 42 (2006).
