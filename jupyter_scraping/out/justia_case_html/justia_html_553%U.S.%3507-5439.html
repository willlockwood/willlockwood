BAZE et\xc2\xa0al. v. REES, COMMISSIONER, KENTUCKY DEPARTMENT OF CORRECTIONS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of kentucky

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x935439.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 7, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 16, 2008

\n

Lethal injection is used for capital punishment by the Federal Government and 36 States, at least 30 of which (including Kentucky) use the same combination of three drugs:\xc2\xa0The first, sodium thiopental, induces unconsciousness when given in the specified amounts and thereby ensures that the prisoner does not experience any pain associated with the paralysis and cardiac arrest caused by the second and third drugs, pancuronium bromide and potassium chloride. Among other things, Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol reserves to qualified personnel having at least one year\xe2\x80\x99s professional experience the responsibility for inserting the intravenous (IV) catheters into the prisoner, leaving it to others to mix the drugs and load them into syringes; specifies that the warden and deputy warden will remain in the execution chamber to observe the prisoner and watch for any IV problems while the execution team administers the drugs from another room; and mandates that if, as determined by the warden and deputy, the prisoner is not unconscious within 60 seconds after the sodium thiopental\xe2\x80\x99s delivery, a new dose will be given at a secondary injection site before the second and third drugs are administered.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners, convicted murderers sentenced to death in Kentucky state court, filed suit asserting that the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol violates the Eighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s ban on \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments.\xe2\x80\x9d The state trial court held extensive hearings and entered detailed factfindings and conclusions of law, ruling that there was minimal risk of various of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims of improper administration of the protocol, and upholding it as constitutional. The Kentucky Supreme Court affirmed, holding that the protocol does not violate the Eighth Amendment because it does not create a substantial risk of wanton and unnecessary infliction of pain, torture, or lingering death.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is affirmed.

\n

217 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 207, affirmed.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts, joined by Justice Kennedy and Justice Alito, concluded that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol satisfies the Eighth Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9324.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0To constitute cruel and unusual punishment, an execution method must present a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable\xe2\x80\x9d risk of serious harm. A State\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to adopt proffered alternative procedures may violate the Eighth Amendment only where the alternative procedure is feasible, readily implemented, and in fact significantly reduces a substantial risk of severe pain. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0 This Court has upheld capital punishment as constitutional. See Gregg v. Georgia, 428 U. S. 153, 177. Because some risk of pain is inherent in even the most humane execution method, if only from the prospect of error in following the required procedure, the Constitution does not demand the avoidance of all risk of pain. Petitioners contend that the Eighth Amendment prohibits procedures that create an \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary risk\xe2\x80\x9d of pain, while Kentucky urges the Court to approve the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98substantial risk\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d test used below. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0This Court has held that the Eighth Amendment forbids \xe2\x80\x9cpunishments of torture, \xe2\x80\xa6 and all others in the same line of unnecessary cruelty,\xe2\x80\x9d Wilkerson v. Utah, 99 U. S. 130, 136, such as disemboweling, beheading, quartering, dissecting, and burning alive, all of which share the deliberate infliction of pain for the sake of pain, id., at 135. Observing also that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]unishments are cruel when they involve torture or a lingering death[,] \xe2\x80\xa6 something inhuman and barbarous [and] \xe2\x80\xa6 more than the mere extinguishment of life,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court has emphasized that an electrocution statute it was upholding \xe2\x80\x9cwas passed in the effort to devise a more humane method of reaching the result.\xe2\x80\x9d In re Kemmler, 136 U. S. 436, 447. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Although conceding that an execution under Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s procedures would be humane and constitutional if performed properly, petitioners claim that there is a significant risk that the procedures will not be properly followed\xe2\x80\x94particularly, that the sodium thiopental will not be properly administered to achieve its intended effect\xe2\x80\x94resulting in severe pain when the other chemicals are administered. Subjecting individuals to a substantial risk of future harm can be cruel and unusual punishment if the conditions presenting the risk are \xe2\x80\x9csure or very likely to cause serious illness and needless suffering\xe2\x80\x9d and give rise to \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently imminent dangers.\xe2\x80\x9d Helling v. McKinney, 509 U. S. 25, 33, 34\xe2\x80\x9335. To prevail, such a claim must present a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial risk of serious harm,\xe2\x80\x9d an \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable risk of harm.\xe2\x80\x9d Farmer v. Brennan, 511 U. S. 825, 842, 846, and n.\xc2\xa09. For example, the Court has held that an isolated mishap alone does not violate the Eighth Amendment, Louisiana ex rel. Francis v. Resweber, 329 U. S. 459, 463\xe2\x80\x93464, because such an event, while regrettable, does not suggest cruelty or a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial risk of serious harm.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 primary contention is that the risks they have identified can be eliminated by adopting certain alternative procedures. Because allowing a condemned prisoner to challenge a State\xe2\x80\x99s execution method merely by showing a slightly or marginally safer alternative finds no support in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases, would embroil the courts in ongoing scientific controversies beyond their expertise, and would substantially intrude on the role of state legislatures in implementing execution procedures, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 proposed \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary risk\xe2\x80\x9d standard is rejected in favor of Farmer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial risk of serious harm\xe2\x80\x9d test. To effectively address such a substantial risk, a proffered alternative procedure must be feasible, readily implemented, and in fact significantly reduce a substantial risk of severe pain. A State\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to adopt such an alternative in the face of these documented advantages, without a legitimate penological justification for its current execution method, can be viewed as \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual.\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Petitioners have not carried their burden of showing that the risk of pain from maladministration of a concededly humane lethal injection protocol, and the failure to adopt untried and untested alternatives, constitute cruel and unusual punishment. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0It is uncontested that failing a proper dose of sodium thiopental to render the prisoner unconscious, there is a substantial, constitutionally unacceptable risk of suffocation from the administration of pancuronium bromide and of pain from potassium chloride. It is, however, difficult to regard a practice as \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable\xe2\x80\x9d when it is in fact widely tolerated. Probative but not conclusive in this regard is the consensus among the Federal Government and the States that have adopted lethal injection and the specific three-drug combination Kentucky uses. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0In light of the safeguards Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol puts in place, the risks of administering an inadequate sodium thiopental dose identified by petitioners are not so substantial or imminent as to amount to an Eighth Amendment violation. The charge that Kentucky employs untrained personnel unqualified to calculate and mix an adequate dose was answered by the state trial court\xe2\x80\x99s finding, substantiated by expert testimony, that there would be minimal risk of improper mixing if the manufacturers\xe2\x80\x99 thiopental package insert instructions were followed. Likewise, the IV line problems alleged by petitioners do not establish a sufficiently substantial risk because IV team members must have at least one year of relevant professional experience, and the presence of the warden and deputy warden in the execution chamber allows them to watch for IV problems. If an insufficient dose is initially administered through the primary IV site, an additional dose can be given through the secondary site before the last two drugs are injected. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Nor does Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s failure to adopt petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 proposed alternatives demonstrate that the state execution procedure is cruel and unusual. Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s continued use of the three-drug protocol cannot be viewed as posing an \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable risk\xe2\x80\x9d when no other State has adopted the one-drug method and petitioners have proffered no study showing that it is an equally effective manner of imposing a death sentence. Petitioners contend that Kentucky should omit pancuronium bromide because it serves no therapeutic purpose while suppressing muscle movements that could reveal an inadequate administration of sodium thiopental. The state trial court specifically found that thiopental serves two purposes:\xc2\xa0(1) preventing involuntary convulsions or seizures during unconsciousness, thereby preserving the procedure\xe2\x80\x99s dignity, and (2) hastening death. Petitioners assert that their barbiturate-only protocol is used routinely by veterinarians for putting animals to sleep and that 23 States bar veterinarians from using a neuromuscular paralytic agent like pancuronium bromide. These arguments overlook the States\xe2\x80\x99 legitimate interest in providing for a quick, certain death, and in any event, veterinary practice for animals is not an appropriate guide for humane practices for humans. Petitioners charge that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol lacks a systematic mechanism, such as a Bispectral Index monitor, blood pressure cuff, or electrocardiogram, for monitoring the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9canesthetic depth.\xe2\x80\x9d But expert testimony shows both that a proper thiopental does obviates the concern that a prisoner will not be sufficiently sedated, and that each of the proposed alternatives presents its own concerns. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens concluded that instead of ending the controversy, this case will generate debate not only about the constitutionality of the three-drug protocol, and specifically about the justification for the use of pancuronium bromide, but also about the justification for the death penalty itself. States wishing to decrease the risk that future litigation will delay executions or invalidate their protocol would do well to reconsider their continued use of pancuronium bromide. Moreover, although experience demonstrates that imposing that penalty constitutes the pointless and needless extinction of life with only negligible social or public returns, this conclusion does not justify a refusal to respect this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents upholding the death penalty and establishing a framework for evaluating the constitutionality of particular execution methods, under which petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 evidence fails to prove that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol violates the Eighth Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas, joined by Justice Scalia, concluded that the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s formulation of the governing standard finds no support in the original understanding of the Cruel and Unusual Punishments Clause or in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s previous method-of-execution cases; casts constitutional doubt on long-accepted methods of execution; and injects the Court into matters it has no institutional capacity to resolve. The historical practices leading to the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s inclusion in the Bill of Rights, the views of early commentators on the Constitution, and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases, see, e.g., Wilkerson v. Utah, 99 U. S. 130, 135\xe2\x80\x93136, all demonstrate that an execution method violates the Eighth Amendment only if it is deliberately designed to inflict pain. Judged under that standard, this is an easy case: Because it is undisputed that Kentucky adopted its lethal injection protocol in an effort to make capital punishment more humane, not to add elements of terror, pain, or disgrace to the death penalty, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 challenge must fail. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer concluded that there cannot be found, either in the record or in the readily available literature, sufficient grounds to believe that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection method creates a significant risk of unnecessary suffering. Although the death penalty has serious risks\xe2\x80\x94e.g., that the wrong person may be executed, that unwarranted animus about the victims\xe2\x80\x99 race, for example, may play a role, and that those convicted will find themselves on death row for many years\xe2\x80\x94the penalty\xe2\x80\x99s lawfulness is not before the Court. And petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 proof and evidence, while giving rise to legitimate concern, do not show that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s execution method amounts to \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishmen[t].\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x937.

RALPH BAZE and THOMAS C. BOWLING, PETI-\nTIONERS v. JOHN D. REES, COMMISSIONER,\nKENTUCKY DEPARTMENT OF\nCORRECTIONS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

\n

[April 16, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts announced the judgment of the Court and delivered an opinion, in which Justice Kennedy and Justice Alito join.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Like 35 other States and the Federal Government, Kentucky has chosen to impose capital punishment for certain crimes. As is true with respect to each of these States and the Federal Government, Kentucky has altered its method of execution over time to more humane means of carrying out the sentence. That progress has led to the use of lethal injection by every jurisdiction that imposes the death penalty.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners in this case\xe2\x80\x94each convicted of double homicide\xe2\x80\x94acknowledge that the lethal injection procedure, if applied as intended, will result in a humane death. They nevertheless contend that the lethal injection protocol is unconstitutional under the Eighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s ban on \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments,\xe2\x80\x9d because of the risk that the protocol\xe2\x80\x99s terms might not be properly followed, resulting in significant pain. They propose an alternative protocol, one that they concede has not been adopted by any State and has never been tried.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The trial court held extensive hearings and entered detailed Findings of Fact and Conclusions of Law. It recognized that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here are no methods of legal execution that are satisfactory to those who oppose the death penalty on moral, religious, or societal grounds,\xe2\x80\x9d but concluded that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s procedure \xe2\x80\x9ccomplies with the constitutional requirements against cruel and unusual punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 769. The State Supreme Court affirmed. We too agree that petitioners have not carried their burden of showing that the risk of pain from maladministration of a concededly humane lethal injection protocol, and the failure to adopt untried and untested alternatives, constitute cruel and unusual punishment. The judgment below is affirmed.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By the middle of the 19th century, \xe2\x80\x9changing was the \xe2\x80\x98nearly universal form of execution\xe2\x80\x99 in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Campbell v. Wood, 511 U. S. 1119 (1994) (Blackmun, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (quoting State v. Frampton, 95 Wash. 2d 469, 492, 627 P.\xc2\xa02d 922, 934 (1981)); Denno, Getting to Death: Are Executions Constitutional? 82 Iowa L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 319, 364 (1997) (counting 48 States and Territories that employed hanging as a method of execution). In 1888, following the recommendation of a commission empaneled by the Governor to find \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the most humane and practical method known to modern science of carrying into effect the sentence of death,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d New York became the first State to authorize electrocution as a form of capital punishment. Glass v. Louisiana, 471 U. S. 1080, 1082, and n. 4 (1985) (Brennan, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari); Denno, supra, at 373. By 1915, 11 other States had followed suit, motivated by the \xe2\x80\x9cwell-grounded belief that electrocution is less painful and more humane than hanging.\xe2\x80\x9d Malloy v. South Carolina, 237 U. S. 180, 185 (1915).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Electrocution remained the predominant mode of execution for nearly a century, although several methods, including hanging, firing squad, and lethal gas were in use at one time. Brief for Fordham University School of Law et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 5\xe2\x80\x939 (hereinafter Fordham Brief). Following the 9-year hiatus in executions that ended with our decision in Gregg v. Georgia, 428 U. S. 153 (1976), however, state legislatures began responding to public calls to reexamine electrocution as a means of assuring a humane death. See S. Banner, The Death Penalty: An American History 192\xe2\x80\x93193, 296\xe2\x80\x93297 (2002). In 1977, legislators in Oklahoma, after consulting with the head of the anesthesiology department at the University of Oklahoma College of Medicine, introduced the first bill proposing lethal injection as the State\xe2\x80\x99s method of execution. See Brief for Petitioners 4; Fordham Brief 21\xe2\x80\x9322. A total of 36 States have now adopted lethal injection as the exclusive or primary means of implementing the death penalty, making it by far the most prevalent method of execution in the United States.[Footnote 1] It is also the method used by the Federal Government. See 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73591 et seq. (2000 ed. and Supp. V); App. to Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 1a\xe2\x80\x936a (lethal injection protocol used by the Federal Bureau of Prisons).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of these 36 States, at least 30 (including Kentucky) use the same combination of three drugs in their lethal injection protocols. See Workman v. Bredesen, 486 F.\xc2\xa03d 896, 902 (CA6 2007). The first drug, sodium thiopental (also known as Pentathol), is a fast-acting barbiturate sedative that induces a deep, comalike unconsciousness when given in the amounts used for lethal injection. App. 762\xe2\x80\x93763, 631\xe2\x80\x93632. The second drug, pancuronium bromide (also known as Pavulon), is a paralytic agent that inhibits all muscular-skeletal movements and, by paralyzing the diaphragm, stops respiration. Id., at 763. Potassium chloride, the third drug, interferes with the electrical signals that stimulate the contractions of the heart, inducing cardiac arrest. Ibid. The proper administration of the first drug ensures that the prisoner does not experience any pain associated with the paralysis and cardiac arrest caused by the second and third drugs. Id., at 493\xe2\x80\x93494, 541, 558\xe2\x80\x93559.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kentucky replaced electrocution with lethal injection in 1998. 1998 Ky. Acts ch. 220, p. 777. The Kentucky statute does not specify the drugs or categories of drugs to be used during an execution, instead mandating that \xe2\x80\x9cevery death sentence shall be executed by continuous intravenous injection of a substance or combination of substances sufficient to cause death.\xe2\x80\x9d Ky. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7431.220(1)(a) (West 2006). Prisoners sentenced before 1998 have the option of electing either electrocution or lethal injection, but lethal injection is the default if\xe2\x80\x94as is the case with petitioners\xe2\x80\x94the prisoner refuses to make a choice at least 20 days before the scheduled execution. \xc2\xa7431.220(1)(b). If a court invalidates Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection method, Kentucky law provides that the method of execution will revert to electrocution. \xc2\xa7431.223.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Shortly after the adoption of lethal injection, officials working for the Kentucky Department of Corrections set about developing a written protocol to comply with the requirements of \xc2\xa7431.220(1)(a). Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol called for the injection of 2 grams of sodium thiopental, 50 milligrams of pancuronium bromide, and 240 milliequivalents of potassium chloride. In 2004, as a result of this litigation, the department chose to increase the amount of sodium thiopental from 2 grams to 3 grams. App. 762\xe2\x80\x93763, 768. Between injections, members of the execution team flush the intravenous (IV) lines with 25 milligrams of saline to prevent clogging of the lines by precipitates that may form when residual sodium thiopental comes into contact with pancuronium bromide. Id., at 761, 763\xe2\x80\x93764. The protocol reserves responsibility for inserting the IV catheters to qualified personnel having at least one year of professional experience. Id., at 984. Currently, Kentucky uses a certified phlebotomist and an emergency medical technician (EMT) to perform the venipunctures necessary for the catheters. Id., at 761\xe2\x80\x93762. They have up to one hour to establish both primary and secondary peripheral intravenous sites in the arm, hand, leg, or foot of the inmate. Id., at 975\xe2\x80\x93976. Other personnel are responsible for mixing the solutions containing the three drugs and loading them into syringes. Id., at 761.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s execution facilities consist of the execution chamber, a control room separated by a one-way window, and a witness room. Id., at 203. The warden and deputy warden remain in the execution chamber with the prisoner, who is strapped to a gurney. The execution team administers the drugs remotely from the control room through five feet of IV tubing. Id., at 286. If, as determined by the warden and deputy warden through visual inspection, the prisoner is not unconscious within 60 seconds following the delivery of the sodium thiopental to the primary IV site, a new 3-gram dose of thiopental is administered to the secondary site before injecting the pancuronium and potassium chloride. Id., at 978\xe2\x80\x93979. In addition to assuring that the first dose of thiopental is successfully administered, the warden and deputy warden also watch for any problems with the IV catheters and tubing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A physician is present to assist in any effort to revive the prisoner in the event of a last-minute stay of execution. Id., at 764. By statute, however, the physician is prohibited from participating in the \xe2\x80\x9cconduct of an execution,\xe2\x80\x9d except to certify the cause of death. Ky. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7431.220(3). An electrocardiogram (EKG) verifies the death of the prisoner. App. 764. Only one Kentucky prisoner, Eddie Lee Harper, has been executed since the Commonwealth adopted lethal injection. There were no reported problems at Harper\xe2\x80\x99s execution.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners Ralph Baze and Thomas C. Bowling were each convicted of two counts of capital murder and sentenced to death. The Kentucky Supreme Court upheld their convictions and sentences on direct appeal. See Baze v. Commonwealth, 965 S.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 817, 819\xe2\x80\x93820, 826 (1997), cert. denied, 523 U. S. 1083 (1998); Bowling v. Commonwealth, 873 S.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 175, 176\xe2\x80\x93177, 182 (1993), cert. denied, 513 U. S. 862 (1994).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After exhausting their state and federal collateral remedies, Baze and Bowling sued three state officials in the Franklin Circuit Court for the Commonwealth of Kentucky, seeking to have Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol declared unconstitutional. After a 7-day bench trial during which the trial court received the testimony of approximately 20 witnesses, including numerous experts, the court upheld the protocol, finding there to be minimal risk of various claims of improper administration of the protocol. App. 765\xe2\x80\x93769. On appeal, the Kentucky Supreme Court stated that a method of execution violates the Eighth Amendment when it \xe2\x80\x9ccreates a substantial risk of wanton and unnecessary infliction of pain, torture or lingering death.\xe2\x80\x9d 217 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 207, 209 (2006). Applying that standard, the court affirmed. Id., at 212.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to determine whether Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol satisfies the Eighth Amendment. 551 U. S. ___ (2007). We hold that it does.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Eighth Amendment to the Constitution, applicable to the States through the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment, see Robinson v. California, 370 U. S. 660, 666 (1962), provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xcessive bail shall not be required, nor excessive fines imposed, nor cruel and unusual punishments inflicted.\xe2\x80\x9d We begin with the principle, settled by Gregg, that capital punishment is constitutional. See 428 U. S., at 177 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.). It necessarily follows that there must be a means of carrying it out. Some risk of pain is inherent in any method of execution\xe2\x80\x94no matter how humane\xe2\x80\x94if only from the prospect of error in following the required procedure. It is clear, then, that the Constitution does not demand the avoidance of all risk of pain in carrying out executions.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners do not claim that it does. Rather, they contend that the Eighth Amendment prohibits procedures that create an \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary risk\xe2\x80\x9d of pain. Brief for Petitioners 38. Specifically, they argue that courts must evaluate \xe2\x80\x9c(a) the severity of pain risked, (b) the likelihood of that pain occurring, and (c) the extent to which alternative means are feasible, either by modifying existing execution procedures or adopting alternative procedures.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Petitioners envision that the quantum of risk necessary to make out an Eighth Amendment claim will vary according to the severity of the pain and the availability of alternatives, Reply Brief for Petitioners 23\xe2\x80\x9324, n.\xc2\xa09, but that the risk must be \xe2\x80\x9csignificant\xe2\x80\x9d to trigger Eighth Amendment scrutiny, see Brief for Petitioners 39\xe2\x80\x9340; Reply Brief for Petitioners 25\xe2\x80\x9326.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kentucky responds that this \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary risk\xe2\x80\x9d standard is tantamount to a requirement that States adopt the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98least risk\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d alternative in carrying out an execution, a standard the Commonwealth contends will cast recurring constitutional doubt on any procedure adopted by the States. Brief for Respondents 29, 35. Instead, Kentucky urges the Court to approve the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98substantial risk\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d test used by the courts below. Id., at 34\xe2\x80\x9335.

\n

A

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has never invalidated a State\xe2\x80\x99s chosen procedure for carrying out a sentence of death as the infliction of cruel and unusual punishment. In Wilkerson v. Utah, 99 U. S. 130 (1879), we upheld a sentence to death by firing squad imposed by a territorial court, rejecting the argument that such a sentence constituted cruel and unusual punishment. Id., at 134\xe2\x80\x93135. We noted there the difficulty of \xe2\x80\x9cdefin[ing] with exactness the extent of the constitutional provision which provides that cruel and unusual punishments shall not be inflicted.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 135\xe2\x80\x93136. Rather than undertake such an effort, the Wilkerson Court simply noted that \xe2\x80\x9cit is safe to affirm that punishments of torture, . . . and all others in the same line of unnecessary cruelty, are forbidden\xe2\x80\x9d by the Eighth Amendment. Id., at 136. By way of example, the Court cited cases from England in which \xe2\x80\x9cterror, pain, or disgrace were sometimes superadded\xe2\x80\x9d to the sentence, such as where the condemned was \xe2\x80\x9cembowelled alive, beheaded, and quartered,\xe2\x80\x9d or instances of \xe2\x80\x9cpublic dissection in murder, and burning alive.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 135. In contrast, we observed that the firing squad was routinely used as a method of execution for military officers. Id., at 137. What each of the forbidden punishments had in common was the deliberate infliction of pain for the sake of pain\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9csuperadd[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d pain to the death sentence through torture and the like.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We carried these principles further in In re Kemmler, 136 U. S. 436 (1890). There we rejected an opportunity to incorporate the Eighth Amendment against the States in a challenge to the first execution by electrocution, to be carried out by the State of New York. Id., at 449. In passing over that question, however, we observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]unishments are cruel when they involve torture or a lingering death; but the punishment of death is not cruel within the meaning of that word as used in the Constitution. It implies there something inhuman and barbarous, something more than the mere extinguishment of life.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 447. We noted that the New York statute adopting electrocution as a method of execution \xe2\x80\x9cwas passed in the effort to devise a more humane method of reaching the result.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

B

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners do not claim that lethal injection or the proper administration of the particular protocol adopted by Kentucky by themselves constitute the cruel or wanton infliction of pain. Quite the contrary, they concede that \xe2\x80\x9cif performed properly,\xe2\x80\x9d an execution carried out under Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s procedures would be \xe2\x80\x9chumane and constitutional.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 31. That is because, as counsel for petitioners admitted at oral argument, proper administration of the first drug, sodium thiopental, eliminates any meaningful risk that a prisoner would experience pain from the subsequent injections of pancuronium and potassium chloride. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 5; App. 493\xe2\x80\x93494 (testimony of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 expert that, if sodium thiopental is \xe2\x80\x9cproperly administered\xe2\x80\x9d under the protocol, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n virtually every case, then that would be a humane death\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead, petitioners claim that there is a significant risk that the procedures will not be properly followed\xe2\x80\x94in particular, that the sodium thiopental will not be properly administered to achieve its intended effect\xe2\x80\x94resulting in severe pain when the other chemicals are administered. Our cases recognize that subjecting individuals to a risk of future harm\xe2\x80\x94not simply actually inflicting pain\xe2\x80\x94can qualify as cruel and unusual punishment. To establish that such exposure violates the Eighth Amendment, however, the conditions presenting the risk must be \xe2\x80\x9csure or very likely to cause serious illness and needless suffering,\xe2\x80\x9d and give rise to \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently imminent dangers.\xe2\x80\x9d Helling v. McKinney, 509 U. S. 25, 33, 34\xe2\x80\x9335 (1993) (emphasis added). We have explained that to prevail on such a claim there must be a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial risk of serious harm,\xe2\x80\x9d an \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable risk of harm\xe2\x80\x9d that prevents prison officials from pleading that they were \xe2\x80\x9csubjectively blameless for purposes of the Eighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Farmer v. Brennan, 511 U. S. 825, 842, 846, and n.\xc2\xa09 (1994).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Simply because an execution method may result in pain, either by accident or as an inescapable consequence of death, does not establish the sort of \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable risk of harm\xe2\x80\x9d that qualifies as cruel and unusual. In Louisiana ex rel. Francis v. Resweber, 329 U. S. 459 (1947), a plurality of the Court upheld a second attempt at executing a prisoner by electrocution after a mechanical malfunction had interfered with the first attempt. The principal opinion noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ccidents happen for which no man is to blame,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 462, and concluded that such \xe2\x80\x9can accident, with no suggestion of malevolence,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 463, did not give rise to an Eighth Amendment violation, id., at 463\xe2\x80\x93464.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As Justice Frankfurter noted in a separate opinion based on the Due Process Clause, however, \xe2\x80\x9ca hypothetical situation\xe2\x80\x9d involving \xe2\x80\x9ca series of abortive attempts at electrocution\xe2\x80\x9d would present a different case. Id., at 471 (concurring opinion). In terms of our present Eighth Amendment analysis, such a situation\xe2\x80\x94unlike an \xe2\x80\x9cinnocent misadventure,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 470\xe2\x80\x94would demonstrate an \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable risk of harm\xe2\x80\x9d that officials may not ignore. See Farmer, 511 U. S., at 846, and n. 9. In other words, an isolated mishap alone does not give rise to an Eighth Amendment violation, precisely because such an event, while regrettable, does not suggest cruelty, or that the procedure at issue gives rise to a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial risk of serious harm.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 842.

\n

C

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Much of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 case rests on the contention that they have identified a significant risk of harm that can be eliminated by adopting alternative procedures, such as a one-drug protocol that dispenses with the use of pancuronium and potassium chloride, and additional monitoring by trained personnel to ensure that the first dose of sodium thiopental has been adequately delivered. Given what our cases have said about the nature of the risk of harm that is actionable under the Eighth Amendment, a condemned prisoner cannot successfully challenge a State\xe2\x80\x99s method of execution merely by showing a slightly or marginally safer alternative.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Permitting an Eighth Amendment violation to be established on such a showing would threaten to transform courts into boards of inquiry charged with determining \xe2\x80\x9cbest practices\xe2\x80\x9d for executions, with each ruling supplanted by another round of litigation touting a new and improved methodology. Such an approach finds no support in our cases, would embroil the courts in ongoing scientific controversies beyond their expertise, and would substantially intrude on the role of state legislatures in implementing their execution procedures\xe2\x80\x94a role that by all accounts the States have fulfilled with an earnest desire to provide for a progressively more humane manner of death. See Bell v. Wolfish, 441 U. S. 520, 562 (1979) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe wide range of \xe2\x80\x98judgment calls\xe2\x80\x99 that meet constitutional and statutory requirements are confided to officials outside of the Judicial Branch of Government\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, we reject petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 proposed \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary risk\xe2\x80\x9d standard, as well as the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cuntoward\xe2\x80\x9d risk variation. See post, at 2, 11 (opinion of Ginsburg,\xc2\xa0J.).[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Instead, the proffered alternatives must effectively address a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial risk of serious harm.\xe2\x80\x9d Farmer, supra, at 842. To qualify, the alternative procedure must be feasible, readily implemented, and in fact significantly reduce a substantial risk of severe pain. If a State refuses to adopt such an alternative in the face of these documented advantages, without a legitimate penological justification for adhering to its current method of execution, then a State\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to change its method can be viewed as \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual\xe2\x80\x9d under the Eighth Amendment.[Footnote 3]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In applying these standards to the facts of this case, we note at the outset that it is difficult to regard a practice as \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable\xe2\x80\x9d when it is in fact widely tolerated. Thirty-six States that sanction capital punishment have adopted lethal injection as the preferred method of execution. The Federal Government uses lethal injection as well. See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, and n. 1. This broad consensus goes not just to the method of execution, but also to the specific three-drug combination used by Kentucky. Thirty States, as well as the Federal Government, use a series of sodium thiopental, pancuronium bromide, and potassium chloride, in varying amounts. See supra, at 4. No State uses or has ever used the alternative one-drug protocol belatedly urged by petitioners. This consensus is probative but not conclusive with respect to that aspect of the alternatives proposed by petitioners.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In order to meet their \xe2\x80\x9cheavy burden\xe2\x80\x9d of showing that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s procedure is \xe2\x80\x9ccruelly inhumane,\xe2\x80\x9d Gregg, 428 U. S., at 175 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.), petitioners point to numerous aspects of the protocol that they contend create opportunities for error. Their claim hinges on the improper administration of the first drug, sodium thiopental. It is uncontested that, failing a proper dose of sodium thiopental that would render the prisoner unconscious, there is a substantial, constitutionally unacceptable risk of suffocation from the administration of pancuronium bromide and pain from the injection of potassium chloride. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 27. We agree with the state trial court and State Supreme Court, however, that petitioners have not shown that the risk of an inadequate dose of the first drug is substantial. And we reject the argument that the Eighth Amendment requires Kentucky to adopt the untested alternative procedures petitioners have identified.

\n

A

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners contend that there is a risk of improper administration of thiopental because the doses are difficult to mix into solution form and load into syringes; because the protocol fails to establish a rate of injection, which could lead to a failure of the IV; because it is possible that the IV catheters will infiltrate into surrounding tissue, causing an inadequate dose to be delivered to the vein; because of inadequate facilities and training; and because Kentucky has no reliable means of monitoring the anesthetic depth of the prisoner after the sodium thiopental has been administered. Brief for Petitioners 12\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As for the risk that the sodium thiopental would be improperly prepared, petitioners contend that Kentucky employs untrained personnel who are unqualified to calculate and mix an adequate dose, especially in light of the omission of volume and concentration amounts from the written protocol. Id., at 45\xe2\x80\x9346. The state trial court, however, specifically found that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the manufacturers\xe2\x80\x99 instructions for reconstitution of Sodium Thiopental are followed, . . . there would be minimal risk of improper mixing, despite converse testimony that a layperson would have difficulty performing this task.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 761. We cannot say that this finding is clearly erroneous, see Hernandez v. New York, 500 U. S. 352, 366 (1991) (plurality opinion), particularly when that finding is substantiated by expert testimony describing the task of reconstituting powder sodium thiopental into solution form as \xe2\x80\x9c[n]ot difficult at all. \xe2\x80\xa6 You take a liquid, you inject it into a vial with the powder, then you shake it up until the powder dissolves and, you\xe2\x80\x99re done. The instructions are on the package insert.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 Tr. 695 (Apr. 19, 2005).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Likewise, the asserted problems related to the IV lines do not establish a sufficiently substantial risk of harm to meet the requirements of the Eighth Amendment. Kentucky has put in place several important safeguards to ensure that an adequate dose of sodium thiopental is delivered to the condemned prisoner. The most significant of these is the written protocol\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that members of the IV team must have at least one year of professional experience as a certified medical assistant, phlebotomist, EMT, paramedic, or military corpsman. App. 984. Kentucky currently uses a phlebotomist and an EMT, personnel who have daily experience establishing IV catheters for inmates in Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s prison population. Id., at 273\xe2\x80\x93274; Tr. of Oral Arg. 27\xe2\x80\x9328. Moreover, these IV team members, along with the rest of the execution team, participate in at least 10 practice sessions per year. App. 984. These sessions, required by the written protocol, encompass a complete walk-through of the execution procedures, including the siting of IV catheters into volunteers. Ibid. In addition, the protocol calls for the IV team to establish both primary and backup lines and to prepare two sets of the lethal injection drugs before the execution commences. Id., at 975. These redundant measures ensure that if an insufficient dose of sodium thiopental is initially administered through the primary line, an additional dose can be given through the backup line before the last two drugs are injected. Id., at 279\xe2\x80\x93280, 337\xe2\x80\x93338, 978\xe2\x80\x93979.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The IV team has one hour to establish both the primary and backup IVs, a length of time the trial court found to be \xe2\x80\x9cnot excessive but rather necessary,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 762, contrary to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claim that using an IV inserted after any \xe2\x80\x9cmore than ten or fifteen minutes of unsuccessful attempts is dangerous because the IV is almost certain to be unreliable,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 47. And, in any event, merely because the protocol gives the IV team one hour to establish intravenous access does not mean that team members are required to spend the entire hour in a futile attempt to do so. The qualifications of the IV team also substantially reduce the risk of IV infiltration.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, the presence of the warden and deputy warden in the execution chamber with the prisoner allows them to watch for signs of IV problems, including infiltration. Three of the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s medical experts testified that identifying signs of infiltration would be \xe2\x80\x9cvery obvious,\xe2\x80\x9d even to the average person, because of the swelling that would result. App. 385\xe2\x80\x93386. See id., at 353, 600\xe2\x80\x93601. Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol specifically requires the warden to redirect the flow of chemicals to the backup IV site if the prisoner does not lose consciousness within 60 seconds. Id., at 978\xe2\x80\x93979. In light of these safeguards, we cannot say that the risks identified by petitioners are so substantial or imminent as to amount to an Eighth Amendment violation.

\n

B

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s failure to adopt petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 proposed alternatives demonstrate that the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s execution procedure is cruel and unusual.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, petitioners contend that Kentucky could switch from a three-drug protocol to a one-drug protocol by using a single dose of sodium thiopental or other barbiturate. Brief for Petitioners 51\xe2\x80\x9357. That alternative was not proposed to the state courts below.[Footnote 4] As a result, we are left without any findings on the effectiveness of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 barbiturate-only protocol, despite scattered references in the trial testimony to the sole use of sodium thiopental or pentobarbital as a preferred method of execution. See Reply Brief for Petitioners 18, n.\xc2\xa06.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, the Commonwealth\xe2\x80\x99s continued use of the three-drug protocol cannot be viewed as posing an \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable risk\xe2\x80\x9d when no other State has adopted the one-drug method and petitioners proffered no study showing that it is an equally effective manner of imposing a death sentence. See App. 760\xe2\x80\x93761, n. 8 (\xe2\x80\x9cPlaintiffs have not presented any scientific study indicating a better method of execution by lethal injection\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, the State of Tennessee, after reviewing its execution procedures, rejected a proposal to adopt a one-drug protocol using sodium thiopental. The State concluded that the one-drug alternative would take longer than the three-drug method and that the \xe2\x80\x9crequired dosage of sodium thiopental would be less predictable and more variable when it is used as the sole mechanism for producing death . . . .\xe2\x80\x9d Workman, 486 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 919 (Appendix A). We need not endorse the accuracy of those conclusions to note simply that the comparative efficacy of a one-drug method of execution is not so well established that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s failure to adopt it constitutes a violation of the Eighth Amendment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners also contend that Kentucky should omit the second drug, pancuronium bromide, because it serves no therapeutic purpose while suppressing muscle movements that could reveal an inadequate administration of the first drug. The state trial court, however, specifically found that pancuronium serves two purposes. First, it prevents involuntary physical movements during unconsciousness that may accompany the injection of potassium chloride. App. 763. The Commonwealth has an interest in preserving the dignity of the procedure, especially where convulsions or seizures could be misperceived as signs of consciousness or distress. Second, pancuronium stops respiration, hastening death. Ibid. Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s decision to include the drug does not offend the Eighth Amendment.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 barbiturate-only protocol, they contend, is not untested; it is used routinely by veterinarians in putting animals to sleep. Moreover, 23 States, including Kentucky, bar veterinarians from using a neuromuscular paralytic agent like pancuronium bromide, either expressly or, like Kentucky, by specifically directing the use of a drug like sodium pentobarbital. See Brief for Dr. Kevin Concannon et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 18, n.\xc2\xa05. If pancuronium is too cruel for animals, the argument goes, then it must be too cruel for the condemned inmate. Whatever rhetorical force the argument carries, see Workman, supra, at 909 (describing the comparison to animal euthanasia as \xe2\x80\x9cmore of a debater\xe2\x80\x99s point\xe2\x80\x9d), it overlooks the States\xe2\x80\x99 legitimate interest in providing for a quick, certain death. In the Netherlands, for example, where physician-assisted euthanasia is permitted, the Royal Dutch Society for the Advancement of Pharmacy recommends the use of a muscle relaxant (such as pancuronium dibromide) in addition to thiopental in order to prevent a prolonged, undignified death. See Kimsma, Euthanasia and Euthanizing Drugs in The Netherlands, reprinted in Drug Use in Assisted Suicide and Euthanasia 193, 200, 204 (M. Battin & A. Lipman eds. 1996). That concern may be less compelling in the veterinary context, and in any event other methods approved by veterinarians\xe2\x80\x94such as stunning the animal or severing its spinal cord, see 6 Tr. 758\xe2\x80\x93759 (Apr. 20, 2005)\xe2\x80\x94make clear that veterinary practice for animals is not an appropriate guide to humane practices for humans.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners also fault the Kentucky protocol for lacking a systematic mechanism for monitoring the \xe2\x80\x9canesthetic depth\xe2\x80\x9d of the prisoner. Under petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 scheme, qualified personnel would employ monitoring equipment, such as a Bispectral Index (BIS) monitor, blood pressure cuff, or EKG to verify that a prisoner has achieved sufficient unconsciousness before injecting the final two drugs. The visual inspection performed by the warden and deputy warden, they maintain, is an inadequate substitute for the more sophisticated procedures they envision. Brief for Petitioners 19, 58.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the outset, it is important to reemphasize that a proper dose of thiopental obviates the concern that a prisoner will not be sufficiently sedated. All the experts who testified at trial agreed on this point. The risks of failing to adopt additional monitoring procedures are thus even more \xe2\x80\x9cremote\xe2\x80\x9d and attenuated than the risks posed by the alleged inadequacies of Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s procedures designed to ensure the delivery of thiopental. See Hamilton v. Jones, 472 F.\xc2\xa03d 814, 817 (CA10 2007) (per curiam); Taylor v. Crawford, 487 F.\xc2\xa03d 1072, 1084 (CA8 2007).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But more than this, Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s expert testified that a blood pressure cuff would have no utility in assessing the level of the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s unconsciousness following the introduction of sodium thiopental, which depresses circulation. App. 578. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, the medical community has yet to endorse the use of a BIS monitor, which measures brain function, as an indication of anesthetic awareness. American Society of Anesthesiologists, Practice Advisory for Intraoperative Awareness and Brain Function Monitoring, 104 Anesthesiology 847, 855 (Apr. 2006); see Brown v. Beck, 445 F.\xc2\xa03d 752, 754\xe2\x80\x93755 (CA4 2006) (Michael, J., dissenting). The asserted need for a professional anesthesiologist to interpret the BIS monitor readings is nothing more than an argument against the entire procedure, given that both Kentucky law, see Ky. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7431.220(3), and the American Society of Anesthesiologists\xe2\x80\x99 own ethical guidelines, see Brief for American Society of Anesthesiologists as Amicus Curiae 2\xe2\x80\x933, prohibit anesthesiologists from participating in capital punishment. Nor is it pertinent that the use of a blood pressure cuff and EKG is \xe2\x80\x9cthe standard of care in surgery requiring anesthesia,\xe2\x80\x9d as the dissent points out. Post, at 6. Petitioners have not shown that these supplementary procedures, drawn from a different context, are necessary to avoid a substantial risk of suffering.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent believes that rough-and-ready tests for checking consciousness\xe2\x80\x94calling the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s name, brushing his eyelashes, or presenting him with strong, noxious odors\xe2\x80\x94could materially decrease the risk of administering the second and third drugs before the sodium thiopental has taken effect. See ibid. Again, the risk at issue is already attenuated, given the steps Kentucky has taken to ensure the proper administration of the first drug. Moreover, the scenario the dissent posits involves a level of unconsciousness allegedly sufficient to avoid detection of improper administration of the anesthesia under Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s procedure, but not sufficient to prevent pain. See post, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. There is no indication that the basic tests the dissent advocates can make such fine distinctions. If these tests are effective only in determining whether the sodium thiopental has entered the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s bloodstream, see post, at 6, the record confirms that the visual inspection of the IV site under Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s procedure achieves that objective. See supra, at 17.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent would continue the stay of these executions (and presumably the many others held in abeyance pending decision in this case) and send the case back to the lower courts to determine whether such added measures redress an \xe2\x80\x9cuntoward\xe2\x80\x9d risk of pain. Post, at 11. But an inmate cannot succeed on an Eighth Amendment claim simply by showing one more step the State could take as a failsafe for other, independently adequate measures. This approach would serve no meaningful purpose and would frustrate the State\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate interest in carrying out a sentence of death in a timely manner. See Baze v. Parker, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d 310, 317 (CA6 2004) (petitioner Baze sentenced to death in 1994); Bowling v. Parker, 138 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 821, 840 (ED Ky. 2001) (petitioner Bowling sentenced to death in 1991).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens suggests that our opinion leaves the disposition of other cases uncertain, see post, at 1, but the standard we set forth here resolves more challenges than he acknowledges. A stay of execution may not be granted on grounds such as those asserted here unless the condemned prisoner establishes that the State\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol creates a demonstrated risk of severe pain. He must show that the risk is substantial when compared to the known and available alternatives. A State with a lethal injection protocol substantially similar to the protocol we uphold today would not create a risk that meets this standard.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reasonable people of good faith disagree on the morality and efficacy of capital punishment, and for many who oppose it, no method of execution would ever be acceptable. But as Justice Frankfurter stressed in Resweber, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ne must be on guard against finding in personal disapproval a reflection of more or less prevailing condemnation.\xe2\x80\x9d 329 U. S., at 471 (concurring opinion). This Court has ruled that capital punishment is not prohibited under our Constitution, and that the States may enact laws specifying that sanction. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he power of a State to pass laws means little if the State cannot enforce them.\xe2\x80\x9d McCleskey v. Zant, 499 U. S. 467, 491 (1991). State efforts to implement capital punishment must certainly comply with the Eighth Amendment, but what that Amendment prohibits is wanton exposure to \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable risk,\xe2\x80\x9d Farmer, 511 U. S., at 846, and n.\xc2\xa09, not simply the possibility of pain.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kentucky has adopted a method of execution believed to be the most humane available, one it shares with 35 other States. Petitioners agree that, if administered as intended, that procedure will result in a painless death. The risks of maladministration they have suggested\xe2\x80\x94such as improper mixing of chemicals and improper setting of IVs by trained and experienced personnel\xe2\x80\x94cannot remotely be characterized as \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively intolerable.\xe2\x80\x9d Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s decision to adhere to its protocol despite these asserted risks, while adopting safeguards to protect against them, cannot be viewed as probative of the wanton infliction of pain under the Eighth Amendment. Finally, the alternative that petitioners belatedly propose has problems of its own, and has never been tried by a single State.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Throughout our history, whenever a method of execution has been challenged in this Court as cruel and unusual, the Court has rejected the challenge. Our society has nonetheless steadily moved to more humane methods of carrying out capital punishment. The firing squad, hanging, the electric chair, and the gas chamber have each in turn given way to more humane methods, culminating in today\xe2\x80\x99s consensus on lethal injection. Gomez v. United States Dist. Court for Northern Dist. of Cal., 503 U. S. 653, 657 (1992) (Stevens, J., dissenting); App. 755. The broad framework of the Eighth Amendment has accommodated this progress toward more humane methods of execution, and our approval of a particular method in the past has not precluded legislatures from taking the steps they deem appropriate, in light of new developments, to ensure humane capital punishment. There is no reason to suppose that today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will be any different.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment below concluding that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s procedure is consistent with the Eighth Amendment is, accordingly, affirmed.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join the plurality opinion but write separately to explain my view of how the holding should be implemented. The opinion concludes that \xe2\x80\x9ca State\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to change its method [of execution] can be viewed as \xe2\x80\x98cruel and unusual\xe2\x80\x99 under the Eighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d if the State, \xe2\x80\x9cwithout a legitimate penological justification,\xe2\x80\x9d rejects an alternative method that is \xe2\x80\x9cfeasible\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9creadily\xe2\x80\x9d available and that would \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly reduce a substantial risk of severe pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. Properly understood, this standard will not, as Justice Thomas predicts, lead to litigation that enables \xe2\x80\x9cthose seeking to abolish the death penalty \xe2\x80\xa6 to embroil the States in never-ending litigation concerning the adequacy of their execution procedures.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 12 (opinion concurring in judgment).

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the plurality opinion notes, the constitutionality of capital punishment is not before us in this case, and therefore we proceed on the assumption that the death penalty is constitutional. Ante, at 8. From that assumption, it follows that there must be a constitutional means of carrying out a death sentence.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We also proceed in this case on the assumption that lethal injection is a constitutional means of execution. See Gregg v. Georgia, 428 U. S. 153, 175 (1976) (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens,\xc2\xa0JJ.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n assessing a punishment selected by a democratically elected legislature against the constitutional measure, we presume its validity\xe2\x80\x9d). Lethal injection was adopted by the Federal Government and 36 States because it was thought to be the most humane method of execution, and petitioners here do not contend that lethal injection should be abandoned in favor of any of the methods that it replaced\xe2\x80\x94execution by electric chair, the gas chamber, hanging, or a firing squad. Since we assume for present purposes that lethal injection is constitutional, the use of that method by the Federal Government and the States must not be blocked by procedural requirements that cannot practicably be satisfied.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Prominent among the practical constraints that must be taken into account in considering the feasibility and availability of any suggested modification of a lethal injection protocol are the ethical restrictions applicable to medical professionals. The first step in the lethal injection protocols currently in use is the anesthetization of the prisoner. If this step is carried out properly, it is agreed, the prisoner will not experience pain during the remainder of the procedure. Every day, general anesthetics are administered to surgical patients in this country, and if the medical professionals who participate in these surgeries also participated in the anesthetization of prisoners facing execution by lethal injection, the risk of pain would be minimized. But the ethics rules of medical professionals\xe2\x80\x94for reasons that I certainly do not question here\xe2\x80\x94prohibit their participation in executions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Guidelines issued by the American Medical Association (AMA) state that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n individual\xe2\x80\x99s opinion on capital punishment is the personal moral decision of the individual,\xe2\x80\x9d but that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] physician, as a member of a profession dedicated to preserving life when there is hope of doing so, should not be a participant in a legally authorized execution.\xe2\x80\x9d AMA, Code of Medical Ethics, Policy E\xe2\x80\x932.06 Capital Punishment (2000), online at http://www.ama-assn.org/\nama1/pub/upload/mm/369/e206capitalpunish.pdf (all Internet materials as visited Apr. 14, 2008, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). The guidelines explain:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cPhysician participation in an execution includes, but is not limited to, the following actions: prescribing or administering tranquilizers and other psychotropic agents and medications that are part of the execution procedure; monitoring vital signs on site or remotely (including monitoring electrocardiograms); attending or observing an execution as a physician; and rendering of technical advice regarding execution.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The head of ethics at the AMA has reportedly opined that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ven helping to design a more humane protocol would disregard the AMA code.\xe2\x80\x9d Harris, Will Medics\xe2\x80\x99 Qualms Kill the Death Penalty? 441 Nature 8\xe2\x80\x939 (May 4, 2006).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The American Nurses Association (ANA) takes the position that participation in an execution \xe2\x80\x9cis a breach of the ethical traditions of nursing, and the Code for Nurses.\xe2\x80\x9d ANA, Position Statement: Nurses\xe2\x80\x99 Participation in Capital Punishment (1994), online at http://nursingworld.org/Main\nMenuCategories\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0/HealthcareandPolicyIssues\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0/ANAPosition\nStatementsEthicsandHumanRights.aspx. This means, the ANA explains, that a nurse must not \xe2\x80\x9ctake part in assessment, supervision or monitoring of the procedure or the prisoner; procuring, prescribing or preparing medications or solutions; inserting the intravenous catheter; injecting the lethal solution; and attending or witnessing the execution as a nurse.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The National Association of Emergency Medical Technicians (NAEMT) holds that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]articipation in capital punishment is inconsistent with the ethical precepts and goals of the [Emergency Medical Services] profession.\xe2\x80\x9d NAEMT, Position Statement on EMT and Paramedic Participa-\ntion in Capital Punishment (June 9, 2006), online at http://www.naemt.org/aboutNAEMT/capitalpunishment.htm\nThe NAEMT\xe2\x80\x99s Position Statement advises that emergency medical technicians and paramedics should refrain from the same activities outlined in the ANA statement. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Recent litigation in California has demonstrated the effect of such ethics rules. Michael Morales, who was convicted and sentenced to death for a 1981 murder, filed a federal civil rights action challenging California\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol, which, like Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s, calls for the sequential administration of three drugs: sodium pentothal, pancuronium bromide, and potassium chloride. The District Court enjoined the State from proceeding with the execution unless it either (1) used only sodium pentothal or another barbiturate or (2) ensured that an anesthesiologist was present to ensure that Morales remained unconscious throughout the process. Morales v. Hickman, 415 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1037, 1047 (ND Cal. 2006). The Ninth Circuit affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order, Morales v. Hickman, 438 F.\xc2\xa03d 926, 931 (2006), and the State arranged for two anesthesiologists to be present for the execution. However, they subsequently concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthey could not proceed for reasons of medical ethics,\xe2\x80\x9d Morales v. Tilton, 465 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 972, 976 (ND Cal. 2006), and neither Morales nor any other prisoner in California has since been executed, see Denno, The Lethal Injection Quandary: How Medicine Has Dismantled the Death Penalty, 76 Ford. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 49 (2007).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Objections to features of a lethal injection protocol must be considered against the backdrop of the ethics rules of medical professionals and related practical constraints. Assuming, as previously discussed, that lethal injection is not unconstitutional per se, it follows that a suggested modification of a lethal injection protocol cannot be regarded as \xe2\x80\x9cfeasible\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9creadily\xe2\x80\x9d available if the modification would require participation\xe2\x80\x94either in carrying out the execution or in training those who carry out the execution\xe2\x80\x94by persons whose professional ethics rules or traditions impede their participation.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In order to show that a modification of a lethal injection protocol is required by the Eighth Amendment, a prisoner must demonstrate that the modification would \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly reduce a substantial risk of severe pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13 (emphasis added). Showing merely that a modification would result in some reduction in risk is insufficient. Moreover, an inmate should be required to do more than simply offer the testimony of a few experts or a few studies. Instead, an inmate challenging a method of execution should point to a well-established scientific consensus. Only if a State refused to change its method in the face of such evidence would the State\xe2\x80\x99s conduct be comparable to circumstances that the Court has previously held to be in violation of the Eighth Amendment. See Farmer v. Brennan, 511 U. S. 825, 836 (1994).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The present case well illustrates the need for this type of evidence. Although there has been a proliferation of litigation challenging current lethal injection protocols, evidence regarding alleged defects in these protocols and the supposed advantages of alternatives is strikingly haphazard and unreliable. As The Chief Justice and Justice Breyer both note, the much-discussed Lancet article, Koniaris, Zimmers, Lubarsky, & Sheldon, Inadequate Anaesthesia in Lethal Injection for Execution, 365 Lancet 1412 (Apr. 2005), that prompted criticism of the three-drug protocol has now been questioned, see Groner, Inadequate Anaesthesia in Lethal Injection for Execution, 366 Lancet 1073 (Sept. 2005). And the lack of clear guidance in the currently available scientific literature is dramatically illustrated by the conclusions reached by petitioners and by Justice Stevens regarding what they view as superior alternatives to the three-drug protocol.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 chief argument is that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s procedure violates the Eighth Amendment because it does not employ a one-drug protocol involving a lethal dose of an anesthetic. By \xe2\x80\x9crelying \xe2\x80\xa6 on a lethal dose of an anesthetic,\xe2\x80\x9d petitioners contend, Kentucky \xe2\x80\x9cwould virtually eliminate the risk of pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 51. Petitioners point to expert testimony in the trial court that \xe2\x80\x9ca three-gram dose of thiopental would cause death within three minutes to fifteen minutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 54, n.\xc2\xa016.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The accuracy of that testimony is not universally accepted. Indeed, the medical authorities in the Netherlands, where assisted suicide is legal, have recommended against the use of a lethal dose of a barbiturate. An amicus supporting petitioners, Dr. Robert D. Truog, Professor of Medical Ethics and Anesthesiology at Harvard Medical School, has made the following comments about the use of a lethal dose of a barbiturate:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cA number of experts have said that 2 or 3 or 5 g[rams] of pentothal is absolutely going to be lethal. The fact is that, at least in this country, none of us have any experience with this\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cIf we go to Holland, where euthanasia is legal, and we look at a study from 2000 of 535 cases of euthanasia, in 69% of those cases, they used a paralytic agent. Now, what do they know that we haven\xe2\x80\x99t figured out yet? I think what they know is that it\xe2\x80\x99s actually very difficult to kill someone with just a big dose of a barbiturate. And, in fact, they report that in 6% of those cases, there were problems with completion. And in I think five of those, the person actually woke up, came back out of coma.\xe2\x80\x9d Perspective Roundtable: Physicians and Execution\xe2\x80\x94Highlights from a Discussion of Lethal Injection, 358 New England J. Med. 448 (2008).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens does not advocate a one-drug protocol but argues that \xe2\x80\x9cStates wishing to decrease the risk that future litigation will delay executions or invalidate their protocols would do well to reconsider their continued use of pancuronium bromide\xe2\x80\x9d in the second step of the three-drug protocol.* Post, at 8 (opinion concurring in judgment). But this very drug, pancuronium bromide, is recommended by the Royal Dutch Society for the Advancement of Pharmacy as the second of the two drugs to be used in cases of euthanasia. See Kimsma, Euthanasia and Euthanizing Drugs in The Netherlands, reprinted in Drug Use in Assisted Suicide and Euthanasia 193, 200, 204 (M. Battin & A. Lipman eds. 1996).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0My point in citing the Dutch study is not that a multi-drug protocol is in fact better than a one-drug protocol or that it is advisable to use pancuronium bromide. Rather, my point is that public policy on the death penalty, an issue that stirs deep emotions, cannot be dictated by the testimony of an expert or two or by judicial findings of fact based on such testimony.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The seemingly endless proceedings that have characterized capital litigation during the years following Gregg are well documented. In 1989, the Report of the Judicial Conference\xe2\x80\x99s Ad\xc2\xa0Hoc Committee on Federal Habeas Corpus in Capital Cases, chaired by Justice Powell, noted the lengthy delays produced by collateral litigation in death penalty cases. See Committee Report and Proposal 2\xe2\x80\x934. The Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) was designed to address this problem. See, e.g., Woodford v. Garceau, 538 U. S. 202, 206 (2003) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress enacted AEDPA to reduce delays in the execution of state and federal criminal sentences, particularly in capital cases \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362, 386 (2000) (opinion of Stevens, J.))); H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x9323, p. 8 (1995) (stating that AEDPA was \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to curb the abuse of the habeas corpus process, and particularly to address the problem of delay and repetitive litigation in capital cases\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Misinterpretation of the standard set out in the plurality opinion or adoption of the standard favored by the dissent and Justice Breyer would create a grave danger of extended delay. The dissenters and Justice Breyer would hold that the protocol used in carrying out an execution by lethal injection violates the Eighth Amendment if it creates an \xe2\x80\x9cuntoward, readily avoidable risk of inflicting severe and unnecessary pain.\xe2\x80\x9d See post, at 11 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting) (emphasis added); post, at 1 (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment). Determining whether a risk is \xe2\x80\x9cuntoward,\xe2\x80\x9d we are told, requires a weighing of three factors\xe2\x80\x94the severity of the pain that may occur, the likelihood of this pain, and the availability of alternative methods. Post, at 4 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting). We are further informed that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he three factors are interrelated; a strong showing on one reduces the importance of others.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An \xe2\x80\x9cuntoward\xe2\x80\x9d risk is presumably a risk that is \xe2\x80\x9cunfortunate\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmarked by or causing trouble or unhappiness.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 2513 (1971); Random House Dictionary of the English Language 1567 (1967). This vague and malleable standard would open the gates for a flood of litigation that would go a long way toward bringing about the end of the death penalty as a practical matter. While I certainly do not suggest that this is the intent of the Justices who favor this test, the likely consequences are predictable.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The issue presented in this case\xe2\x80\x94the constitutionality of a method of execution\xe2\x80\x94should be kept separate from the controversial issue of the death penalty itself. If the Court wishes to reexamine the latter issue, it should do so directly, as Justice Stevens now suggests. Post, at 12. The Court should not produce a de facto ban on capital punishment by adopting method-of-execution rules that lead to litigation gridlock.

She highlights the relevant question, whether the method creates an untoward, readily avoidable risk of inflicting severe and unnecessary suffering. Post, at 11 (dissenting opinion). I agree that the relevant factors\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9cdegree of risk,\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cmagnitude of pain,\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cavailability of alternatives\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94are interrelated and each must be considered. At the same time, I believe that the legal merits of the kind of claim presented must inevitably turn not so much upon the wording of an intermediate standard of review as upon facts and evidence. \n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In respect to the literature, I have examined the periodical article that seems first to have brought widespread legal attention to the claim that lethal injection might bring about unnecessary suffering. See ante, at 13, n.\xc2\xa02 (plurality opinion); Denno, The Lethal Injection Quandary: How Medicine Has Dismantled the Death Penalty, 76 Ford. See Koniaris, Zimmers, Lubarsky, & Sheldon, Inadequate Anaesthesia in Lethal Injection for Execution, 365 Lancet 1412 (hereinafter Lancet Study). The authors examined \xe2\x80\x9cautopsy toxicology results from 49 executions in Arizona, Georgia, North Carolina, and South Carolina.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1412\xe2\x80\x931413. The study noted that lethal injection usually consists of sequential administration of a barbiturate (sodium thiopental), followed by injection of a paralyzing agent (pancuronium bromide) and a heart-attack-inducing drug (potassium chloride). The study focused on the effectiveness of the first drug in anaesthetizing the inmate. See id., at 1412. It noted that the four States used 2 grams of thiopental. Id., at 1413. (Kentucky follows a similar system but currently uses 3 grams of sodium thiopental. See ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936 (plurality opinion)). Although the sodium thiopental dose (of, say, 2 grams) was several times the dose used in ordinary surgical operations, the authors found that the level of barbiturate present in the bloodstream several hours (or more) after death was lower than the level one might expect to find during an operation. Lancet Study 1413\xe2\x80\x931414. With certain qualifications, they state that \xe2\x80\x9c21 (43%)\xe2\x80\x9d of the examined instances \xe2\x80\x9chad [thiopental] concentrations consistent with consciousness,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1413,\xe2\x80\x94a fact that should create considerable concern given the related likelihood of unexpressed suffering. See Inadequate Anaesthesia in Lethal Injection for Execution, 366 Lancet 1073. And a long pause between death and measurement means that this kind of diffusion likely occurred. See ibid. \n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second correspondents, Mark J.\xc2\xa0S. Heath (petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 expert in their trial below), Donald R. Stanski, and Derrick J. Weisman, of the University of Miami, School of Medicine, and Florida Poison Information Center, said that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]ost-mortem drug concentrations are extremely difficult to interpret and there is substantial variability in results depending on timing, anatomical origin of the specimen, and physical and chemical properties of the drug.\xe2\x80\x9d 366 Lancet, at 1074. Arizona, 552 U. S. ___ (2007) (Breyer, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari). These risks in part explain why that penalty is so controversial. But the lawfulness of the death penalty is not before us. And petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 proof and evidence, while giving rise to legitimate concern, do not show that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s method of applying the death penalty amounts to \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishmen[t].\xe2\x80\x9d

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although I agree that petitioners have failed to establish that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol violates the Eighth Amendment, I write separately because I cannot subscribe to the plurality opinion\xe2\x80\x99s formulation of the governing standard. As I understand it, that opinion would hold that a method of execution violates the Eighth Amendment if it poses a substantial risk of severe pain that could be significantly reduced by adopting readily available alternative procedures. Ante, at 13. This standard\xe2\x80\x94along with petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 proposed \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary risk\xe2\x80\x9d standard and the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cuntoward risk\xe2\x80\x9d standard, post, at 2\xe2\x80\x94finds no support in the original understanding of the Cruel and Unusual Punishments Clause or in our previous method-of-execution cases; casts constitutional doubt on long-accepted methods of execution; and injects the Court into matters it has no institutional capacity to resolve. Because, in my view, a method of execution violates the Eighth Amendment only if it is deliberately designed to inflict pain, I concur only in the judgment.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Eighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on the \xe2\x80\x9cinflict[ion]\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments\xe2\x80\x9d must be understood in light of the historical practices that led the Framers to include it in the Bill of Rights. Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 ruminations notwithstanding, see ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9318 (opinion concurring in judgment), it is clear that the Eighth Amendment does not prohibit the death penalty. That is evident both from the ubiquity of the death penalty in the founding era, see S. Banner, The Death Penalty: An American History 23 (2002) (hereinafter Banner) (noting that, in the late 18th century, the death penalty was \xe2\x80\x9cthe standard penalty for all serious crimes\xe2\x80\x9d), and from the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s express provision for capital punishment, see, e.g., Amdt. 5 (requiring an indictment or presentment of a grand jury to hold a person for \xe2\x80\x9ca capital, or otherwise infamous crime,\xe2\x80\x9d and prohibiting deprivation of \xe2\x80\x9clife\xe2\x80\x9d without due process of law).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That the Constitution permits capital punishment in principle does not, of course, mean that all methods of execution are constitutional. In English and early colonial practice, the death penalty was not a uniform punishment, but rather a range of punishments, some of which the Framers likely regarded as cruel and unusual. Death by hanging was the most common mode of execution both before and after 1791, and there is no doubt that it remained a permissible punishment after enactment of the Eighth Amendment. \xe2\x80\x9cAn ordinary death by hanging was not, however, the harshest penalty at the disposal of the seventeenth- and eighteenth-century state.\xe2\x80\x9d Banner 70. In addition to hanging, which was intended to, and often did, result in a quick and painless death, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]fficials also wielded a set of tools capable of intensifying a death sentence,\xe2\x80\x9d that is, \xe2\x80\x9cways of producing a punishment worse than death.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 54.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One such \xe2\x80\x9ctool\xe2\x80\x9d was burning at the stake. Because burning, unlike hanging, was always painful and destroyed the body, it was considered \xe2\x80\x9ca form of super-capital punishment, worse than death itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 71. Reserved for offenders whose crimes were thought to pose an especially grave threat to the social order\xe2\x80\x94such as slaves who killed their masters and women who killed their husbands\xe2\x80\x94burning a person alive was so dreadful a punishment that sheriffs sometimes hanged the offender first \xe2\x80\x9cas an act of charity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 72.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Other methods of intensifying a death sentence included \xe2\x80\x9cgibbeting,\xe2\x80\x9d or hanging the condemned in an iron cage so that his body would decompose in public view, see id., at 72\xe2\x80\x9374, and \xe2\x80\x9cpublic dissection,\xe2\x80\x9d a punishment Blackstone associated with murder, 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries 376 (1769) (hereinafter Blackstone). But none of these was the worst fate a criminal could meet. That was reserved for the most dangerous and reprobate offenders\xe2\x80\x94traitors. \xe2\x80\x9cThe punishment of high treason,\xe2\x80\x9d Blackstone wrote, was \xe2\x80\x9cvery solemn and terrible,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 92, and involved \xe2\x80\x9cembowelling alive, beheading, and quartering,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 376. Thus, the following death sentence could be pronounced on seven men convicted of high treason in England:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98That you and each of you, be taken to the place from whence you came, and from thence be drawn on a hurdle to the place of execution, where you shall be hanged by the necks, not till you are dead; that you be severally taken down, while yet alive, and your bowels be taken out and burnt before your faces\xe2\x80\x94that your heads be then cut off, and your bodies cut in four quarters, to be at the King\xe2\x80\x99s disposal. And God Almighty have mercy on your souls.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d G. Scott, History of Capital Punishment 179 (1950).*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The principal object of these aggravated forms of capital punishment was to terrorize the criminal, and thereby more effectively deter the crime. Their defining characteristic was that they were purposely designed to inflict pain and suffering beyond that necessary to cause death. As Blackstone put it, \xe2\x80\x9cin very atrocious crimes, other circumstances of terror, pain, or disgrace [were] superadded.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 Blackstone 376. These \xe2\x80\x9csuperadded\xe2\x80\x9d circumstances \xe2\x80\x9cwere carefully handed out to apply terror where it was thought to be most needed,\xe2\x80\x9d and were designed \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure that death would be slow and painful, and thus all the more frightening to contemplate.\xe2\x80\x9d Banner 70.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although the Eighth Amendment was not the subject of extensive discussion during the debates on the Bill of Rights, there is good reason to believe that the Framers viewed such enhancements to the death penalty as falling within the prohibition of the Cruel and Unusual Punishments Clause. By the late 18th century, the more violent modes of execution had \xe2\x80\x9cdwindled away,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 76, and would for that reason have been \xe2\x80\x9cunusual\xe2\x80\x9d in the sense that they were no longer \xe2\x80\x9cregularly or customarily employed,\xe2\x80\x9d Harmelin v. Michigan, 501 U. S. 957, 976 (1991) (opinion of Scalia, J.); see also Weems v. United States, 217 U. S. 349, 395 (1910) (White, J., dissenting) (noting that, \xe2\x80\x9cprior to the formation of the Constitution, the necessity for the protection afforded by the cruel and unusual punishment guarantee of the English bill of rights had ceased to be a matter of concern, because as a rule the cruel bodily punishments of former times were no longer imposed\xe2\x80\x9d). Embellishments upon the death penalty designed to inflict pain for pain\xe2\x80\x99s sake also would have fallen comfortably within the ordinary meaning of the word \xe2\x80\x9ccruel.\xe2\x80\x9d See 1 S. Johnson, A Dictionary of the English Language 459 (1773) (defining \xe2\x80\x9ccruel\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9c[p]leased with hurting others; inhuman; hard-hearted; void of pity; wanting compassion; savage; barbarous; unrelenting\xe2\x80\x9d); 1 N. Webster, An American Dictionary of the English Language 52 (1828) (defining \xe2\x80\x9ccruel\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[d]isposed to give pain to others, in body or mind; willing or pleased to torment, vex or afflict; inhuman; destitute of pity, compassion or kindness\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, the evidence we do have from the debates on the Constitution confirms that the Eighth Amendment was intended to disable Congress from imposing torturous punishments. It was the absence of such a restriction on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power in the Constitution as drafted in Philadelphia in 1787 that led one delegate at the Massachusetts ratifying convention to complain that Congress was \xe2\x80\x9cnowhere restrained from inventing the most cruel and unheard-of punishments, and annexing them to crimes; and there is no constitutional check on them, but that racks and gibbets may be amongst the most mild instruments of their discipline.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 J. Elliot, The Debates in the Several State Conventions on the Adoption of the Federal Constitution 111 (2d ed. 1891). Similarly, during the ratification debate in Virginia, Patrick Henry objected to the lack of a Bill of Rights, in part because there was nothing to prevent Congress from inflicting \xe2\x80\x9ctortures, or cruel and barbarous punishment[s].\xe2\x80\x9d 3 id., at 447\xe2\x80\x93448.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Early commentators on the Constitution likewise interpreted the Cruel and Unusual Punishments Clause as referring to torturous punishments. One commentator viewed the Eighth Amendment as prohibiting \xe2\x80\x9chorrid modes of torture\xe2\x80\x9d:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe prohibition of cruel and unusual punishments, marks the improved spirit of the age, which would not tolerate the use of the rack or the stake, or any of those horrid modes of torture, devised by human ingenuity for the gratification of fiendish passion.\xe2\x80\x9d J. Bayard, A Brief Exposition of the Constitution of the United States 154 (2d ed. 1840).

\n

\nSimilarly, another commentator found \xe2\x80\x9csufficient reasons\xe2\x80\x9d for the Eighth Amendment in the \xe2\x80\x9cbarbarous and cruel punishments\xe2\x80\x9d inflicted in less enlightened countries:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cUnder the [Eighth] amendment the infliction of cruel and unusual punishments, is also prohibited. The various barbarous and cruel punishments inflicted under the laws of some other countries, and which profess not to be behind the most enlightened nations on earth in civilization and refinement, furnish sufficient reasons for this express prohibition. Breaking on the wheel, flaying alive, rending asunder with horses, various species of horrible tortures inflicted in the inquisition, maiming, mutilating and scourging to death, are wholly alien to the spirit of our humane general constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d B. Oliver, The Rights of An American Citizen 186 (1832) (reprint 1970).

\n

\nSo barbaric were the punishments prohibited by the Eighth Amendment that Joseph Story thought the provision \xe2\x80\x9cwholly unnecessary in a free government, since it is scarcely possible, that any department of such a government should authorize, or justify such atrocious conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 J. Story, Commentaries on the Constitution of the United States 750 (1833).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consistent with the original understanding of the Cruel and Unusual Punishments Clause, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases have repeatedly taken the view that the Framers intended to prohibit torturous modes of punishment akin to those that formed the historical backdrop of the Eighth Amendment. See, e.g., Estelle v. Gamble, 429 U. S. 97, 102 (1976) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he primary concern of the drafters was to proscribe \xe2\x80\x98torture[s]\xe2\x80\x99 and other \xe2\x80\x98barbar[ous]\xe2\x80\x99 methods of punishment\xe2\x80\x9d); Weems, 217 U. S., at 390 (White, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t may not be doubted, and indeed is not questioned by any one, that the cruel punishments against which the bill of rights provided were the atrocious, sanguinary and inhuman punishments which had been inflicted in the past upon the persons of criminals\xe2\x80\x9d). That view has permeated our method-of-execution cases. Thrice the Court has considered a challenge to a modern method of execution, and thrice it has rejected the challenge, each time emphasizing that the Eighth Amendment is aimed at methods of execution purposely designed to inflict pain.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the first case, Wilkerson v. Utah, 99 U. S. 130 (1879), the Court rejected the contention that death by firing squad was cruel and unusual. In so doing, it reviewed the various modes of execution catalogued by Blackstone, repeating his observation that \xe2\x80\x9cin very atrocious crimes other circumstances of terror, pain, or disgrace were sometimes superadded.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 135. The Court found it \xe2\x80\x9csafe to affirm that punishments of torture, such as those mentioned by [Blackstone], and all others in the same line of unnecessary cruelty, are forbidden by [the Eighth Amendment].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 136. The unanimous Court had no difficulty concluding that death by firing squad did not \xe2\x80\x9cfal[l] within that category.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, when the Court in In\xc2\xa0re Kemmler, 136 U. S. 436, 446 (1890), unanimously rejected a challenge to electrocution, it interpreted the Eighth Amendment to prohibit punishments that \xe2\x80\x9cwere manifestly cruel and unusual, as burning at the stake, crucifixion, breaking on the wheel, or the like\xe2\x80\x9d:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cPunishments are cruel when they involve torture or a lingering death; but the punishment of death is not cruel, within the meaning of that word as used in the Constitution. It implies there something inhuman and barbarous, something more than the mere extinguishment of life.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 447.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, in Louisiana ex rel. Francis v. Resweber, 329 U. S. 459 (1947), the Court rejected the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the Eighth Amendment prohibited Louisiana from subjecting him to a second attempt at electrocution, the first attempt having failed when \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he executioner threw the switch but, presumably because of some mechanical difficulty, death did not result.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 460 (plurality opinion). Characterizing the abortive attempt as \xe2\x80\x9can accident, with no suggestion of malevolence,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 463, the plurality opinion concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe fact that petitioner ha[d] already been subjected to a current of electricity [did] not make his subsequent execution any more cruel in the constitutional sense than any other execution\xe2\x80\x9d:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe cruelty against which the Constitution protects a convicted man is cruelty inherent in the method of punishment, not the necessary suffering involved in any method employed to extinguish life humanely. The fact that an unforeseeable accident prevented the prompt consummation of the sentence cannot, it seems to us, add an element of cruelty to a subsequent execution. There is no purpose to inflict unnecessary pain nor any unnecessary pain involved in the proposed execution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 464.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of this consistent understanding of the Cruel and Unusual Punishments Clause as forbidding purposely torturous punishments, it is not surprising that even an ardent abolitionist was constrained to acknowledge in 1977 that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n unbroken line of interpreters has held that it was the original understanding and intent of the framers of the Eighth Amendment \xe2\x80\xa6 to proscribe as \xe2\x80\x98cruel and unusual\xe2\x80\x99 only such modes of execution as compound the simple infliction of death with added cruelties or indignities.\xe2\x80\x9d H. Bedau, The Courts, the Constitution, and Capital Punishment 35. What is surprising is the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s willingness to discard this unbroken line of authority in favor of a standard that finds no support in the original understanding of the Eighth Amendment or in our method-of-execution cases and that, disclaimers notwithstanding, \xe2\x80\x9cthreaten[s] to transform courts into boards of inquiry charged with determining \xe2\x80\x98best practices\xe2\x80\x99 for executions, with each ruling supplanted by another round of litigation touting a new and improved methodology.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have never suggested that a method of execution is \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the Eighth Amendment simply because it involves a risk of pain\xe2\x80\x94whether \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cunnecessary,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cuntoward\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that could be reduced by adopting alternative procedures. And for good reason. It strains credulity to suggest that the defining characteristic of burning at the stake, disemboweling, drawing and quartering, beheading, and the like was that they involved risks of pain that could be eliminated by using alternative methods of execution. Quite plainly, what defined these punishments was that they were designed to inflict torture as a way of enhancing a death sentence; they were intended to produce a penalty worse than death, to accomplish something \xe2\x80\x9cmore than the mere extinguishment of life.\xe2\x80\x9d Kemmler, supra, at 447. The evil the Eighth Amendment targets is intentional infliction of gratuitous pain, and that is the standard our method-of-execution cases have explicitly or implicitly invoked.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, the Court did not find it necessary in Wilkerson to conduct a comparative analysis of death by firing squad as opposed to hanging or some other method of execution. Nor did the Court inquire into the precise procedures used to execute an individual by firing squad in order to determine whether they involved risks of pain that could be alleviated by adopting different procedures. It was enough that death by firing squad was well established in military practice, 99 U. S., at 134\xe2\x80\x93135, and plainly did not fall within the \xe2\x80\x9csame line of unnecessary cruelty\xe2\x80\x9d as the punishments described by Blackstone, id., at 136.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The same was true in Kemmler. One searches the opinion in vain for a comparative analysis of electrocution versus other methods of execution. The Court observed that the New York Legislature had adopted electrocution in order to replace hanging with \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the most humane and practical method known to modern science of carrying into effect the sentence of death in capital cases.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 136 U. S., at 444. But there is no suggestion that the Court thought it necessary to sift through the \xe2\x80\x9cvoluminous mass of evidence \xe2\x80\xa6 taken [in the courts below] as to the effect of electricity as an agent of death,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 442, in order to confirm that electrocution in fact involved less substantial risks of pain or lingering death than hanging. The court below had rejected the challenge because the \xe2\x80\x9cact was passed in the effort to devise a more humane method of reaching the result,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccourts were bound to presume that the legislature was possessed of the facts upon which it took action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 447. Treating the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s decision \xe2\x80\x9cas involving an adjudication that the statute was not repugnant to the Federal Constitution,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., the Court found that conclusion \xe2\x80\x9cso plainly right,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., that it had \xe2\x80\x9cno hesitation\xe2\x80\x9d in denying the writ of error, id., at 449.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Likewise in Resweber, the Court was confronted in dramatic fashion with the reality that the electric chair involved risks of error or malfunction that could result in excruciating pain. See 329 U. S., at 480, n.\xc2\xa02 (Burton, J., dissenting) (quoting affidavits from the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s brief recounting that during the unsuccessful first attempt at electrocution, the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98lips puffed out and his body squirmed and tensed and he jumped so that the chair rocked on the floor\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). But absent \xe2\x80\x9cmalevolence\xe2\x80\x9d or a \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose to inflict unnecessary pain,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court concluded that the Constitution did not prohibit Louisiana from subjecting the petitioner to those very risks a second time in order to carry out his death sentence. Id., at 463, 464 (plurality opinion); id., at 471 (Frankfurter, J., concurring); see also Furman v. Georgia, 408 U. S. 238, 326\xe2\x80\x93327 (1972) (Marshall, J., concurring) (describing Resweber as holding \xe2\x80\x9cthat the legislature adopted electrocution for a humane purpose, and that its will should not be thwarted because, in its desire to reduce pain and suffering in most cases, it may have inadvertently increased suffering in one particular case\xe2\x80\x9d). No one suggested that Louisiana was required to implement additional safeguards or alternative procedures in order to reduce the risk of a second malfunction. And it was the dissenters in Resweber who insisted that the absence of an intent to inflict pain was irrelevant. 329 U. S., at 477 (Burton, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe intent of the executioner cannot lessen the torture or excuse the result\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Aside from lacking support in history or precedent, the various risk-based standards proposed in this case suffer from other flaws, not the least of which is that they cast substantial doubt on every method of execution other than lethal injection. It may well be that other methods of execution such as hanging, the firing squad, electrocution, and lethal gas involve risks of pain that could be eliminated by switching to lethal injection. Indeed, they have been attacked as unconstitutional for that very reason. See, e.g., Gomez v. United States Dist. Court for Northern Dist. of Cal., 503 U. S. 653, 654, 656\xe2\x80\x93657 (1992) (Stevens, J., dissenting) (arguing that lethal gas violates the Eighth Amendment because of \xe2\x80\x9cthe availability of more humane and less violent methods of execution,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, lethal injection); Glass v. Louisiana, 471 U. S. 1080, 1093 (1985) (Brennan, J., dissenting from denial of certiorari) (arguing that electrocution violates the Eighth Amendment because it poses risks of pain that could be alleviated by \xe2\x80\x9cother currently available means of execution,\xe2\x80\x9d such as lethal injection); Campbell v. Wood, 18 F.\xc2\xa03d 662, 715 (CA9 1994) (Reinhardt, J., concurring and dissenting) (arguing that hanging violates the Eighth Amendment because it involves risks of pain and mutilation not presented by lethal injection). But the notion that the Eighth Amendment permits only one mode of execution, or that it requires an anesthetized death, cannot be squared with the history of the Constitution.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is not a little ironic\xe2\x80\x94and telling\xe2\x80\x94that lethal injection, hailed just a few years ago as the humane alternative in light of which every other method of execution was deemed an unconstitutional relic of the past, is the subject of today\xe2\x80\x99s challenge. It appears the Constitution is \xe2\x80\x9cevolving\xe2\x80\x9d even faster than I suspected. And it is obvious that, for some who oppose capital punishment on policy grounds, the only acceptable end point of the evolution is for this Court, in an exercise of raw judicial power unsupported by the text or history of the Constitution, or even by a contemporary moral consensus, to strike down the death penalty as cruel and unusual in all circumstances. In the meantime, though, the next best option for those seeking to abolish the death penalty is to embroil the States in never-ending litigation concerning the adequacy of their execution procedures. But far from putting an end to abusive litigation in this area, and thereby vindicating in some small measure the States\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9csignificant interest in meting out a sentence of death in a timely fashion,\xe2\x80\x9d Nelson v. Campbell, 541 U. S. 637, 644 (2004), today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is sure to engender more litigation. At what point does a risk become \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial\xe2\x80\x9d? Which alternative procedures are \xe2\x80\x9cfeasible\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9creadily implemented\xe2\x80\x9d? When is a reduction in risk \xe2\x80\x9csignificant\xe2\x80\x9d? What penological justifications are \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate\xe2\x80\x9d? Such are the questions the lower courts will have to grapple with in the wake of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Needless to say, we have left the States with nothing resembling a bright-line rule.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Which brings me to yet a further problem with comparative-risk standards: They require courts to resolve medical and scientific controversies that are largely beyond judicial ken. Little need be said here, other than to refer to the various opinions filed by my colleagues today. Under the competing risk standards advanced by the plurality opinion and the dissent, for example, the difference between a lethal injection procedure that satisfies the Eighth Amendment and one that does not may well come down to one\xe2\x80\x99s judgment with respect to something as hairsplitting as whether an eyelash stroke is necessary to ensure that the inmate is unconscious, or whether instead other measures have already provided sufficient assurance of unconsciousness. Compare post, at 6 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting) (criticizing Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol because \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o one calls the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s name, shakes him, brushes his eyelashes to test for a reflex, or applies a noxious stimulus to gauge his response\xe2\x80\x9d), with ante, at 22 (rejecting the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s criticisms because \xe2\x80\x9can inmate cannot succeed on an Eighth Amendment claim simply by showing one more step the State could take as a failsafe for other, independently adequate measures\xe2\x80\x9d). We have neither the authority nor the expertise to micromanage the States\xe2\x80\x99 administration of the death penalty in this manner. There is simply no reason to believe that \xe2\x80\x9cunelected\xe2\x80\x9d judges without scientific, medical, or penological training are any better suited to resolve the delicate issues surrounding the administration of the death penalty than are state administrative personnel specifically charged with the task. Cf. ante, at 5 (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment) (criticizing the States\xe2\x80\x99 use of the three-drug protocol because \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n the majority of States that use the three-drug protocol, the drugs were selected by unelected Department of Correction officials with no specialized medical knowledge and without the benefit of expert assistance or guidance\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, I reject as both unprecedented and unworkable any standard that would require the courts to weigh the relative advantages and disadvantages of different methods of execution or of different procedures for implementing a given method of execution. To the extent that there is any comparative element to the inquiry, it should be limited to whether the challenged method inherently inflicts significantly more pain than traditional modes of execution such as hanging and the firing squad. See, e.g., Gray v. Lucas, 463 U. S. 1237, 1239\xe2\x80\x931240 (1983) (Burger, C.\xc2\xa0J., concurring in denial of certiorari) (rejecting an Eighth Amendment challenge to lethal gas because the petitioner had not shown that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the pain and terror resulting from death by cyanide gas is so different in degree or nature from that resulting from other traditional modes of execution as to implicate the eighth amendment right\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Gray v. Lucas, 710 F.\xc2\xa02d 1048, 1061 (CA5 1983))); Hernandez v. State, 43 Ariz. 424, 441, 32 P.\xc2\xa02d 18, 25 (1934) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe fact that [lethal gas] is less painful and more humane than hanging is all that is required to refute completely the charge that it constitutes cruel and unusual punishment within the meaning of this expression as used in [the Eighth Amendment]\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judged under the proper standard, this is an easy case. It is undisputed that Kentucky adopted its lethal injection protocol in an effort to make capital punishment more humane, not to add elements of terror, pain, or disgrace to the death penalty. And it is undisputed that, if administered properly, Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol will result in a swift and painless death. As the Sixth Circuit observed in rejecting a similar challenge to Tennessee\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol, we \xe2\x80\x9cdo not have a situation where the State has any intent (or anything approaching intent) to inflict unnecessary pain; the complaint is that the State\xe2\x80\x99s pain-avoidance procedure may fail because the executioners may make a mistake in implementing it.\xe2\x80\x9d Workman v. Bredesen, 486 F.\xc2\xa03d 896, 907 (2007). But \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he risk of negligence in implementing a death-penalty procedure\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0does not establish a cognizable Eighth Amendment claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 907\xe2\x80\x93908. Because Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol is designed to eliminate pain rather than to inflict it, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 challenge must fail. I accordingly concur in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment affirming the decision below.

\n

*\xc2\xa0As gruesome as these methods of execution were, they were not the worst punishments the Framers would have been acquainted with. After surveying the various \xe2\x80\x9csuperadd[itions]\xe2\x80\x9d to the death penalty in English law, as well as lesser punishments such as \xe2\x80\x9cmutilation or dismembering, by cutting off the hand or ears\xe2\x80\x9d and stigmatizing the offender \xe2\x80\x9cby slitting the nostrils, or branding in the hand or cheek,\xe2\x80\x9d Blackstone was able to congratulate his countrymen on their refinement, in contrast to the barbarism on the Continent: \xe2\x80\x9cDisgusting as this catalogue may seem, it will afford pleasure to an English reader, and do honor to the English law, to compare it with that shocking apparatus of death and torment to be met with in the criminal codes of almost every other nation in Europe.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 Blackstone 377.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When we granted certiorari in this case, I assumed that our decision would bring the debate about lethal injection as a method of execution to a close. It now seems clear that it will not. The question whether a similar three-drug protocol may be used in other States remains open, and may well be answered differently in a future case on the basis of a more complete record. Instead of ending the controversy, I am now convinced that this case will generate debate not only about the constitutionality of the three-drug protocol, and specifically about the justification for the use of the paralytic agent, pancuronium bromide, but also about the justification for the death penalty itself.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because it masks any outward sign of distress, pancuronium bromide creates a risk that the inmate will suffer excruciating pain before death occurs. There is a general understanding among veterinarians that the risk of pain is sufficiently serious that the use of the drug should be proscribed when an animal\xe2\x80\x99s life is being terminated.[Footnote 1] As a result of this understanding among knowledgeable professionals, several States\xe2\x80\x94including Kentucky\xe2\x80\x94have enacted legislation prohibiting use of the drug in animal euthanasia. See 2 Ky. Admin. Regs., tit. 201, ch. 16:090, \xc2\xa75(1) (2004).[Footnote 2] It is unseemly\xe2\x80\x94to say the least\xe2\x80\x94that Kentucky may well kill petitioners using a drug that it would not permit to be used on their pets.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Use of pancuronium bromide is particularly disturbing because\xe2\x80\x94as the trial court specifically found in this case\xe2\x80\x94it serves \xe2\x80\x9cno therapeutic purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 763. The drug\xe2\x80\x99s primary use is to prevent involuntary muscle movements, and its secondary use is to stop respiration. In my view, neither of these purposes is sufficient to justify the risk inherent in the use of the drug.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality believes that preventing involuntary movement is a legitimate justification for using pancuronium bromide because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Commonwealth has an interest in preserving the dignity of the procedure, especially where convulsions or seizures could be misperceived as signs of consciousness or distress.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 19. This is a woefully inadequate justification. Whatever minimal interest there may be in ensuring that a condemned inmate dies a dignified death, and that witnesses to the execution are not made uncomfortable by an incorrect belief (which could easily be corrected) that the inmate is in pain, is vastly outweighed by the risk that the inmate is actually experiencing excruciating pain that no one can detect.[Footnote 3] Nor is there any necessity for pancuronium bromide to be included in the cocktail to inhibit respiration when it is immediately followed by potassium chloride, which causes death quickly by stopping the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s heart.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, there is no nationwide endorsement of the use of pancuronium bromide that merits any special presumption of respect. While state legislatures have approved lethal injection as a humane method of execution, the majority have not enacted legislation specifically approving the use of pancuronium bromide, or any given combination of drugs.[Footnote 4] And when the Colorado Legislature focused on the issue, it specified a one-drug protocol consisting solely of sodium thiopental. See Colo. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa718\xe2\x80\x931.3\xe2\x80\x931202 (2007).[Footnote 5] In the majority of States that use the three-drug protocol, the drugs were selected by unelected Department of Correction officials with no specialized medical knowledge and without the benefit of expert assistance or guidance. As such, their drug selections are not entitled to the kind of deference afforded legislative decisions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor should the failure of other state legislatures, or of Congress, to outlaw the use of the drug on condemned prisoners be viewed as a nationwide endorsement of an unnecessarily dangerous practice. Even in those States where the legislature specifically approved the use of a paralytic agent, review of the decisions that led to the adoption of the three-drug protocol has persuaded me that they are the product of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98administrative convenience\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and a \xe2\x80\x9cstereotyped reaction\xe2\x80\x9d to an issue, rather than a careful analysis of relevant considerations favoring or disfavoring a conclusion. See Mathews v. Lucas, 427 U. S. 495, 519, 520\xe2\x80\x93521 (1976) (Stevens, J., dissenting). Indeed, the trial court found that \xe2\x80\x9cthe various States simply fell in line\xe2\x80\x9d behind Oklahoma, adopting the protocol without any critical analysis of whether it was the best available alternative.[Footnote 6] App. 756; see also post, at 5 (Ginsburg, J.,\ndissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0New Jersey\xe2\x80\x99s experience with the creation of a lethal injection protocol is illustrative. When New Jersey restored the death penalty in 1983, its legislature \xe2\x80\x9cfell in line\xe2\x80\x9d and enacted a statute that called for inmates to be executed by \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuous, intravenous administration until the person is dead of a lethal quantity of an ultrashort-acting barbiturate in combination with a chemical paralytic agent in a quantity sufficient to cause death.\xe2\x80\x9d N.\xc2\xa0J. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa72C:49\xe2\x80\x932 (West 2005). New Jersey Department of Corrections (DOC) officials, including doctors and administrators, immediately expressed concern. The Capital Sentencing Unit\xe2\x80\x99s chief doctor, for example, warned the Assistant Commissioner that he had \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98concerns \xe2\x80\xa6 in regard to the chemical substance classes from which the lethal substances may be selected.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Edwards, New Jersey\xe2\x80\x99s Long Waltz With Death, 170 N.\xc2\xa0J.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0J. 657, 673 (2002).[Footnote 7] Based on these concerns, the former DOC Commissioner lobbied the legislature to amend the lethal injection statute to provide DOC with discretion to select more humane drugs: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[We wanted] a generic statement, like \xe2\x80\x9cdrugs to be determined and identified by the commissioner, or the attorney general, or the Department of Health\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98Who knew what the future was going to bring?\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And these concerns likely motivated the DOC\xe2\x80\x99s decision to adopt a protocol that omitted pancuronium bromide\xe2\x80\x94despite the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s failure to act on the proposed amendment. See Denno, When Legislatures Delegate Death: The Troubling Paradox Behind State Uses of Electrocution and Lethal Injection and What It Says About Us, 63 Ohio St. L.\xc2\xa0J. 63, 117\xe2\x80\x93118, 233 (2002) (explaining that the New Jersey protocol in effect in 2002 called for use of a two-drug cocktail consisting of sodium thiopental and potassium chloride).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, DOC officials seemed to harbor the same concerns when they undertook to revise New Jersey\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol in 2005. At a public hearing on the proposed amendment, the DOC Supervisor of Legal and Legislative Affairs told attendees that the drugs to be used in the lethal injection protocol were undetermined:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThose substances have not been determined at this point because when and if an execution is scheduled the [DOC] will be doing research and determining the state-of-the-art drugs at that point in time \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. We have not made a decision on which specific drugs because we will have several months once we know that somebody is going to be executed and it will give us the opportunity at that point to decide which would be the most humane.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cAnd things change. We understand that the state-of-the-art is changing daily so to say we are going to use something today when something may be more humane becomes known later wouldn\xe2\x80\x99t make sense for us.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Public Hearing on Proposed Amendments to the New Jersey Lethal Injection Protocol 36 (Feb. 4, 2005).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is striking that when this state agency\xe2\x80\x94with some specialized medical knowledge and with the benefit of some expert assistance and guidance\xe2\x80\x94focused on the issue, it disagreed with the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstereotyped reaction,\xe2\x80\x9d Mathews, 427 U. S., at 520, 521 (Stevens, J., dissenting), and specified a two-drug protocol that omitted pancuronium bromide.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, therefore, States wishing to decrease the risk that future litigation will delay executions or invalidate their protocols would do well to reconsider their continued use of pancuronium bromide.[Footnote 9]

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The thoughtful opinions written by The Chief Justice and by Justice Ginsburg have persuaded me that current decisions by state legislatures, by the Congress of the United States, and by this Court to retain the death penalty as a part of our law are the product of habit and inattention rather than an acceptable deliberative process that weighs the costs and risks of administering that penalty against its identifiable benefits, and rest in part on a faulty assumption about the retributive force of the death penalty.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Gregg v. Georgia, 428 U. S. 153 (1976), we explained that unless a criminal sanction serves a legitimate penological function, it constitutes \xe2\x80\x9cgratuitous infliction of suffering\xe2\x80\x9d in violation of the Eighth Amendment. We then identified three societal purposes for death as a sanction: incapacitation, deterrence, and retribution. See id., at 183, and n.\xc2\xa028 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.). In the past three decades, however, each of these rationales has been called into question.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While incapacitation may have been a legitimate rationale in 1976, the recent rise in statutes providing for life imprisonment without the possibility of parole demonstrates that incapacitation is neither a necessary nor a sufficient justification for the death penalty.[Footnote 10] Moreover, a recent poll indicates that support for the death penalty drops significantly when life without the possibility of parole is presented as an alternative option.[Footnote 11] And the available sociological evidence suggests that juries are less likely to impose the death penalty when life without parole is available as a sentence.[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The legitimacy of deterrence as an acceptable justification for the death penalty is also questionable, at best. Despite 30 years of empirical research in the area, there remains no reliable statistical evidence that capital punishment in fact deters potential offenders.[Footnote 13] In the absence of such evidence, deterrence cannot serve as a sufficient penological justification for this uniquely severe and irrevocable punishment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are left, then, with retribution as the primary rationale for imposing the death penalty. And indeed, it is the retribution rationale that animates much of the remaining enthusiasm for the death penalty.[Footnote 14] As Lord Justice Denning argued in 1950, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98some crimes are so outrageous that society insists on adequate punishment, because the wrong-doer deserves it, irrespective of whether it is a deterrent or not.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d See Gregg, 428 U. S., at 184, n.\xc2\xa030. Our Eighth Amendment jurisprudence has narrowed the class of offenders eligible for the death penalty to include only those who have committed outrageous crimes defined by specific aggravating factors. It is the cruel treatment of victims that provides the most persuasive arguments for prosecutors seeking the death penalty. A natural response to such heinous crimes is a thirst for vengeance.[Footnote 15]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the same time, however, as the thoughtful opinions by The Chief Justice and Justice Ginsburg make pellucidly clear, our society has moved away from public and painful retribution towards ever more humane forms of punishment. State-sanctioned killing is therefore becoming more and more anachronistic. In an attempt to bring executions in line with our evolving standards of decency, we have adopted increasingly less painful methods of execution, and then declared previous methods barbaric and archaic. But by requiring that an execution be relatively painless, we necessarily protect the inmate from enduring any punishment that is comparable to the suffering inflicted on his victim.[Footnote 16] This trend, while appropriate and required by the Eighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment, actually undermines the very premise on which public approval of the retribution rationale is based. See, e.g., Kaufman-Osborn, Regulating Death: Capital Punishment and the Late Liberal State, 111 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 681, 704 (2001) (explaining that there is \xe2\x80\x9ca tension between our desire to realize the claims of retribution by killing those who kill, and \xe2\x80\xa6 a method [of execution] that, because it seems to do no harm other than killing, cannot satisfy the intuitive sense of equivalence that informs this conception of justice\xe2\x80\x9d); A. Sarat, When the State Kills: Capital Punishment and the American Condition 60\xe2\x80\x9384 (2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Full recognition of the diminishing force of the principal rationales for retaining the death penalty should lead this Court and legislatures to reexamine the question recently posed by Professor Salinas, a former Texas prosecutor and judge: \xe2\x80\x9cIs it time to Kill the Death Penalty?\xe2\x80\x9d See Salinas, 34 Am. J. Crim. L. 39 (2006). The time for a dispassionate, impartial comparison of the enormous costs that death penalty litigation imposes on society with the benefits that it produces has surely arrived.[Footnote 17]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[A] penalty may be cruel and unusual because it is excessive and serves no valid legislative purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Furman v. Georgia, 408 U. S. 238, 331 (1972) (Marshall, J., concurring); see also id., at 332 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe entire thrust of the Eighth Amendment is, in short, against \xe2\x80\x98that which is excessive\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Our cases holding that certain sanctions are \xe2\x80\x9cexcessive,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore prohibited by the Eighth Amendment, have relied heavily on \xe2\x80\x9cobjective criteria,\xe2\x80\x9d such as legislative enactments. See, e.g., Solem v. Helm, 463 U. S. 277, 292 (1983); Harmelin v. Michigan, 501 U. S. 957 (1991); United States v. Bajakajian, 524 U. S. 321 (1998). In our recent decision in Atkins v. Virginia, 536 U. S. 304 (2002), holding that death is an excessive sanction for a mentally retarded defendant, we also relied heavily on opinions written by Justice White holding that the death penalty is an excessive punishment for the crime of raping a 16-year-old woman, Coker v. Georgia, 433 U. S. 584 (1977), and for a murderer who did not intend to kill, Enmund v. Florida, 458 U. S. 782 (1982). In those opinions we acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9cobjective evidence, though of great importance, did not \xe2\x80\x98wholly determine\xe2\x80\x99 the controversy, \xe2\x80\x98for the Constitution contemplates that in the end our own judgment will be brought to bear on the question of the acceptability of the death penalty under the Eighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Atkins, 536 U. S., at 312 (quoting Coker, 433 U. S., at 597 (plurality opinion)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice White was exercising his own judgment in 1972 when he provided the decisive vote in Furman, the case that led to a nationwide reexamination of the death penalty. His conclusion that death amounted to \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishment in the constitutional sense\xe2\x80\x9d as well as the \xe2\x80\x9cdictionary sense,\xe2\x80\x9d rested on both an uncontroversial legal premise and on a factual premise that he admittedly could not \xe2\x80\x9cprove\xe2\x80\x9d on the basis of objective criteria. 408 U. S., at 312, 313 (concurring opinion). As a matter of law, he correctly stated that the \xe2\x80\x9cneedless extinction of life with only marginal contributions to any discernible social or public purposes \xe2\x80\xa6 would be patently excessive\xe2\x80\x9d and violative of the Eighth Amendment. Id., at 312. As a matter of fact, he stated, \xe2\x80\x9clike my Brethren, I must arrive at judgment; and I can do no more than state a conclusion based on 10 years of almost daily exposure to the facts and circumstances of hundreds and hundreds of federal and state criminal cases involving crimes for which death is the authorized penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 313. I agree with Justice White that there are occasions when a Member of this Court has a duty to make judgments on the basis of data that falls short of absolute proof.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decisions in 1976 upholding the constitutionality of the death penalty relied heavily on our belief that adequate procedures were in place that would avoid the danger of discriminatory application identified by Justice Douglas\xe2\x80\x99 opinion in Furman, id., at 240\xe2\x80\x93257 (concurring opinion), of arbitrary application identified by Justice Stewart, id., at 306 (same), and of excessiveness identified by Justices Brennan and Marshall. In subsequent years a number of our decisions relied on the premise that \xe2\x80\x9cdeath is different\xe2\x80\x9d from every other form of punishment to justify rules minimizing the risk of error in capital cases. See, e.g., Gardner v. Florida, 430 U. S. 349, 357\xe2\x80\x93358 (1977) (plurality opinion). Ironically, however, more recent cases have endorsed procedures that provide less protections to capital defendants than to ordinary offenders.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of special concern to me are rules that deprive the defendant of a trial by jurors representing a fair cross section of the community. Litigation involving both challenges for cause and peremptory challenges has persuaded me that the process of obtaining a \xe2\x80\x9cdeath qualified jury\xe2\x80\x9d is really a procedure that has the purpose and effect of obtaining a jury that is biased in favor of conviction. The prosecutorial concern that death verdicts would rarely be returned by 12 randomly selected jurors should be viewed as objective evidence supporting the conclusion that the penalty is excessive.[Footnote 18]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Another serious concern is that the risk of error in capital cases may be greater than in other cases because the facts are often so disturbing that the interest in making sure the crime does not go unpunished may overcome residual doubt concerning the identity of the offender. Our former emphasis on the importance of ensuring that decisions in death cases be adequately supported by reason rather than emotion, Gardner, 430 U. S. 349, has been undercut by more recent decisions placing a thumb on the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s side of the scales. Thus, in Kansas v. Marsh, 548 U. S. 163 (2006), the Court upheld a state statute that requires imposition of the death penalty when the jury finds that the aggravating and mitigating factors are in equipoise. And in Payne v. Tennessee, 501 U. S. 808 (1991), the Court overruled earlier cases and held that \xe2\x80\x9cvictim impact\xe2\x80\x9d evidence relating to the personal characteristics of the victim and the emotional impact of the crime on the victim\xe2\x80\x99s family is admissible despite the fact that it sheds no light on the question of guilt or innocence or on the moral culpability of the defendant, and thus serves no purpose other than to encourage jurors to make life or death decisions on the basis of emotion rather than reason.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A third significant concern is the risk of discriminatory application of the death penalty. While that risk has been dramatically reduced, the Court has allowed it to continue to play an unacceptable role in capital cases. Thus, in McCleskey v. Kemp, 481 U. S. 279 (1987), the Court upheld a death sentence despite the \xe2\x80\x9cstrong probability that [the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] sentencing jury \xe2\x80\xa6 was influenced by the fact that [he was] black and his victim was white.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 366 (Stevens, J., dissenting); see also Evans v. State, 396 Md. 256, 323, 914 A.\xc2\xa02d 25, 64 (2006), cert. denied, 552 U. S. ___ (2007) (affirming a death sentence despite the existence of a study showing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe death penalty is statistically more likely to be pursued against a black person who murders a white victim than against a defendant in any other racial combination\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, given the real risk of error in this class of cases, the irrevocable nature of the consequences is of decisive importance to me. Whether or not any innocent defendants have actually been executed, abundant evidence accumulated in recent years has resulted in the exoneration of an unacceptable number of defendants found guilty of capital offenses. See Garrett, Judging Innocence, 108 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 55 (2008); Risinger, Innocents Convicted: An Empirically Justified Factual Wrongful Conviction Rate, 97 J. Crim. L. & C. 761 (2007). The risk of executing innocent defendants can be entirely eliminated by treating any penalty more severe than life imprisonment without the possibility of parole as constitutionally excessive.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, just as Justice White ultimately based his conclusion in Furman on his extensive exposure to countless cases for which death is the authorized penalty, I have relied on my own experience in reaching the conclusion that the imposition of the death penalty represents \xe2\x80\x9cthe pointless and needless extinction of life with only marginal contributions to any discernible social or public purposes. A penalty with such negligible returns to the State [is] patently excessive and cruel and unusual punishment violative of the Eighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Furman, 408 U. S., at 312 (White, J., concurring). [Footnote 19]

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The conclusion that I have reached with regard to the constitutionality of the death penalty itself makes my decision in this case particularly difficult. It does not, however, justify a refusal to respect precedents that remain a part of our law. This Court has held that the death penalty is constitutional, and has established a framework for evaluating the constitutionality of particular methods of execution. Under those precedents, whether as interpreted by The Chief Justice or Justice Ginsburg, I am persuaded that the evidence adduced by petitioners fails to prove that Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol violates the Eighth Amendment. Accordingly, I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join the opinion of Justice Thomas concurring in the judgment. I write separately to provide what I think is needed response to Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 separate opinion.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens concludes as follows: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he imposition of the death penalty represents the pointless and needless extinction of life with only marginal contributions to any discernible social or public purposes. A penalty with such negligible returns to the State [is] patently excessive and cruel and unusual punishment violative of the Eighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17 (opinion concurring in judgment) (internal quotation marks omitted; second bracket in original).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This conclusion is insupportable as an interpretation of the Constitution, which generally leaves it to democratically elected legislatures rather than courts to decide what makes significant contribution to social or public purposes. Besides that more general proposition, the very text of the document recognizes that the death penalty is a permissible legislative choice. The Fifth Amendment expressly requires a presentment or indictment of a grand jury to hold a person to answer for \xe2\x80\x9ca capital, or otherwise infamous crime,\xe2\x80\x9d and prohibits deprivation of \xe2\x80\x9clife\xe2\x80\x9d without due process of law. U. S. Const., Amdt. 5. The same Congress that proposed the Eighth Amendment also enacted the Act of April 30, 1790, which made several offenses punishable by death. 1 Stat. 112 (1st Cong., 2d Sess. 1790); see also Gregg v. Georgia, 428 U. S. 153, 176\xe2\x80\x93178 (1976) (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.). Writing in 1976, Professor Hugo Bedau\xe2\x80\x94no friend of the death penalty himself\xe2\x80\x94observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]ntil fifteen years ago, save for a few mavericks, no one gave any credence to the possibility of ending the death penalty by judicial interpretation of constitutional law.\xe2\x80\x9d The Courts, the Constitution, and Capital Punishment 118 (1977). There is simply no legal authority for the proposition that the imposition of death as a criminal penalty is unconstitutional other than the opinions in Furman v. Georgia, 408 U. S. 238 (1972), which established a nationwide moratorium on capital punishment that Justice Stevens had a hand in ending four years later in Gregg.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What prompts Justice Stevens to repudiate his prior view and to adopt the astounding position that a criminal sanction expressly mentioned in the Constitution violates the Constitution? His analysis begins with what he believes to be the \xe2\x80\x9cuncontroversial legal premise\xe2\x80\x9d that the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98extinction of life with only marginal contributions to any discernible social or public purposes \xe2\x80\xa6 would be patently excessive\xe2\x80\x99 and violative of the Eighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14 (quoting in part Furman, supra, at 312 (White, J., concurring)); see also ante, at 9 (citing Gregg, supra, at 183, and n. 28). Even if that were uncontroversial in the abstract (and it is certainly not what occurs to me as the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments\xe2\x80\x9d), it is assuredly controversial (indeed, flat-out wrong) as applied to a mode of punishment that is explicitly sanctioned by the Constitution. As to that, the people have determined whether there is adequate contribution to social or public purposes, and it is no business of unelected judges to set that judgment aside. But even if we grant Justice Stevens his \xe2\x80\x9cuncontroversial premise,\xe2\x80\x9d his application of that premise to the current practice of capital punishment does not meet the \xe2\x80\x9cheavy burden [that] rests on those who would attack the judgment of the representatives of the people.\xe2\x80\x9d Gregg, supra, at 175 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.). That is to say, Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 policy analysis of the constitutionality of capital punishment fails on its own terms.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0According to Justice Stevens, the death penalty promotes none of the purposes of criminal punishment because it neither prevents more crimes than alternative measures nor serves a retributive purpose. Ante, at 9. He argues that \xe2\x80\x9cthe recent rise in statutes providing for life imprisonment without the possibility of parole\xe2\x80\x9d means that States have a ready alternative to the death penalty. Ibid. Moreover, \xe2\x80\x9c[d]espite 30 years of empirical research in the area, there remains no reliable statistical evidence that capital punishment in fact deters potential offenders.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. Taking the points together, Justice Stevens concludes that the availability of alternatives, and what he describes as the unavailability of \xe2\x80\x9creliable statistical evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d renders capital punishment unconstitutional. In his view, the benefits of capital punishment\xe2\x80\x94as compared to other forms of punishment such as life imprisonment\xe2\x80\x94are outweighed by the costs.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These conclusions are not supported by the available data. Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 analysis barely acknowledges the \xe2\x80\x9csignificant body of recent evidence that capital punishment may well have a deterrent effect, possibly a quite powerful one.\xe2\x80\x9d Sunstein & Vermeule, Is Capital Punishment Morally Required? Acts, Omissions, and Life-Life Tradeoffs, 58 Stan. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 703, 706 (2006); see also id., at 706, n.\xc2\xa09 (listing the approximately half a dozen studies supporting this conclusion). According to a \xe2\x80\x9cleading national study,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ceach execution prevents some eighteen murders, on average.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 706. \xe2\x80\x9cIf the current evidence is even roughly correct \xe2\x80\xa6 then a refusal to impose capital punishment will effectively condemn numerous innocent people to death.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, it may well be that the empirical studies establishing that the death penalty has a powerful deterrent effect are incorrect, and some scholars have disputed its deterrent value. See ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa013. But that is not the point. It is simply not our place to choose one set of responsible empirical studies over another in interpreting the Constitution. Nor is it our place to demand that state legislatures support their criminal sanctions with foolproof empirical studies, rather than commonsense predictions about human behavior. \xe2\x80\x9cThe value of capital punishment as a deterrent of crime is a complex factual issue the resolution of which properly rests with the legislatures, which can evaluate the results of statistical studies in terms of their own local conditions and with a flexibility of approach that is not available to the courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Gregg, supra, at 186 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.). Were Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 current view the constitutional test, even his own preferred criminal sanction\xe2\x80\x94life imprisonment without the possibility of parole\xe2\x80\x94may fail constitutional scrutiny, because it is entirely unclear that enough empirical evidence supports that sanction as compared to alternatives such as life with the possibility of parole.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But even if Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 assertion about the deterrent value of the death penalty were correct, the death penalty would yet be constitutional (as he concedes) if it served the appropriate purpose of retribution. I would think it difficult indeed to prove that a criminal sanction fails to serve a retributive purpose\xe2\x80\x94a judgment that strikes me as inherently subjective and insusceptible of judicial review. Justice Stevens, however, concludes that, because the Eighth Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cprotect[s] the inmate from enduring any punishment that is comparable to the suffering inflicted on his victim,\xe2\x80\x9d capital punishment serves no retributive purpose at all. Ante, at 11. The infliction of any pain, according to Justice Stevens, violates the Eighth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition against cruel and unusual punishments, but so too does the imposition of capital punishment without pain because a criminal penalty lacks a retributive purpose unless it inflicts pain commensurate with the pain that the criminal has caused. In other words, if a punishment is not retributive enough, it is not retributive at all. To state this proposition is to refute it, as Justice Stevens once understood. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he decision that capital punishment may be the appropriate sanction in extreme cases is an expression of the community\xe2\x80\x99s belief that certain crimes are themselves so grievous an affront to humanity that the only adequate response may be the penalty of death.\xe2\x80\x9d Gregg, 428 U. S., at 184 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 final refuge in his cost-benefit analysis is a familiar one: There is a risk that an innocent person might be convicted and sentenced to death\xe2\x80\x94though not a risk that Justice Stevens can quantify, because he lacks a single example of a person executed for a crime he did not commit in the current American system. See ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9317. His analysis of this risk is thus a series of sweeping condemnations that, if taken seriously, would prevent any punishment under any criminal justice system. According to him, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he prosecutorial concern that death verdicts would rarely be returned by 12 randomly selected jurors should be viewed as objective evidence supporting the conclusion that the penalty is excessive.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. But prosecutors undoubtedly have a similar concern that any unanimous conviction would rarely be returned by 12 randomly selected jurors. That is why they, like defense counsel, are permitted to use the challenges for cause and peremptory challenges that Justice Stevens finds so troubling, in order to arrive at a jury that both sides believe will be more likely to do justice in a particular case. Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 concern that prosecutors will be inclined to challenge jurors who will not find a person guilty supports not his conclusion, but the separate (and equally erroneous) conclusion that peremptory challenges and challenges for cause are unconstitutional. According to Justice Stevens, \xe2\x80\x9cthe risk of error in capital cases may be greater than in other cases because the facts are often so disturbing that the interest in making sure the crime does not go unpunished may overcome residual doubt concerning the identity of the offender.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. That rationale, however, supports not Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion that the death penalty is unconstitutional, but the more sweeping proposition that any conviction in a case in which facts are disturbing is suspect\xe2\x80\x94including, of course, convictions resulting in life without parole in those States that do not have capital punishment. The same is true of Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 claim that there is a risk of \xe2\x80\x9cdiscriminatory application of the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. The same could be said of any criminal penalty, including life without parole; there is no proof that in this regard the death penalty is distinctive.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But of all Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 criticisms of the death penalty, the hardest to take is his bemoaning of \xe2\x80\x9cthe enormous costs that death penalty litigation imposes on society,\xe2\x80\x9d including the \xe2\x80\x9cburden on the courts and the lack of finality for victim\xe2\x80\x99s families.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12, and n.\xc2\xa017. Those costs, those burdens, and that lack of finality are in large measure the creation of Justice Stevens and other Justices opposed to the death penalty, who have \xe2\x80\x9cencumber[ed] [it] \xe2\x80\xa6 with unwarranted restrictions neither contained in the text of the Constitution nor reflected in two centuries of practice under it\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the product of their policy views \xe2\x80\x9cnot shared by the vast majority of the American people.\xe2\x80\x9d Kansas v. Marsh, 548 U. S. 163, 186 (2006) (Scalia, J., concurring).

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But actually none of this really matters. As Justice Stevens explains, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98objective evidence, though of great importance, [does] not wholly determine the controversy, for the Constitution contemplates that in the end our own judgment will be brought to bear on the question of the acceptability of the death penalty under the Eighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14 (quoting Atkins v. Virginia, 536 U. S. 304, 312 (2002); emphasis added; some internal quotation marks omitted). \xe2\x80\x9cI have relied on my own experience in reaching the conclusion that the imposition of the death penalty\xe2\x80\x9d is unconstitutional. Ante, at 17 (emphasis added).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Purer expression cannot be found of the principle of rule by judicial fiat. In the face of Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 experience, the experience of all others is, it appears, of little consequence. The experience of the state legislatures and the Congress\xe2\x80\x94who retain the death penalty as a form of punishment\xe2\x80\x94is dismissed as \xe2\x80\x9cthe product of habit and inattention rather than an acceptable deliberative process.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. The experience of social scientists whose studies indicate that the death penalty deters crime is relegated to a footnote. Ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa013. The experience of fellow citizens who support the death penalty is described, with only the most thinly veiled condemnation, as stemming from a \xe2\x80\x9cthirst for vengeance.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. It is Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 experience that reigns over all.

\n

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I take no position on the desirability of the death penalty, except to say that its value is eminently debatable and the subject of deeply, indeed passionately, held views\xe2\x80\x94which means, to me, that it is preeminently not a matter to be resolved here. And especially not when it is explicitly permitted by the Constitution.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is undisputed that the second and third drugs used in Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s three-drug lethal injection protocol, pancuronium bromide and potassium chloride, would cause a conscious inmate to suffer excruciating pain. Pancuronium bromide paralyzes the lung muscles and results in slow asphyxiation. App. 435, 437, 625. Potassium chloride causes burning and intense pain as it circulates throughout the body. Id., at 348, 427, 444, 600, 626. Use of pancuronium bromide and potassium chloride on a conscious inmate, the plurality recognizes, would be \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutionally unacceptable.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The constitutionality of Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol therefore turns on whether inmates are adequately anesthetized by the first drug in the protocol, sodium thiopental. Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s system is constitutional, the plurality states, because \xe2\x80\x9cpetitioners have not shown that the risk of an inadequate dose of the first drug is substantial.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. I would not dispose of the case so swiftly given the character of the risk at stake. Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol lacks basic safeguards used by other States to confirm that an inmate is unconscious before injection of the second and third drugs. I would vacate and remand with instructions to consider whether Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s omission of those safeguards poses an untoward, readily avoidable risk of inflicting severe and unnecessary pain.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court has considered the constitutionality of a specific method of execution on only three prior occasions. Those cases, and other decisions cited by the parties and amici, provide little guidance on the standard that should govern petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 challenge to Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s lethal injection protocol.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Wilkerson v. Utah, 99 U. S. 130 (1879), the Court held that death by firing squad did not rank among the \xe2\x80\x9ccruel and unusual punishments\xe2\x80\x9d banned by the Eighth Amendment. In so ruling, the Court did not endeavor \xe2\x80\x9cto define with exactness the extent of the constitutional provision which provides that cruel and unusual punishments shall not be inflicted.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 135\xe2\x80\x93136. But it was \xe2\x80\x9csafe to affirm,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court stated, that \xe2\x80\x9cpunishments of torture \xe2\x80\xa6 , and all others in the same line of unnecessary cruelty, are forbidden.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 136.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Next, in In re Kemmler, 136 U. S. 436 (1890), death by electrocution was the assailed method of execution.[Footnote 1] The Court reiterated that the Eighth Amendment prohibits \xe2\x80\x9ctorture\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9clingering death.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 447. The word \xe2\x80\x9ccruel,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court further observed, \xe2\x80\x9cimplies \xe2\x80\xa6 something inhuman \xe2\x80\xa6 something more than the mere extinguishment of life.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Those statements, however, were made en passant. Kemmler\xe2\x80\x99s actual holding was that the Eighth Amendment does not apply to the States, id., at 448\xe2\x80\x93449,[Footnote 2] a proposition we have since repudiated, see, e.g., Robinson v. California, 370 U. S. 660 (1962).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, in Louisiana ex rel. Francis v. Resweber, 329 U. S. 459 (1947), the Court rejected Eighth and Fourteenth Amendment challenges to a reelectrocution following an earlier attempt that failed to cause death. The plurality opinion in that case first stated: \xe2\x80\x9cThe traditional humanity of modern Anglo-American law forbids the infliction of unnecessary pain in the execution of the death sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 463. But the very next sentence varied the formulation; it referred to the \xe2\x80\x9c[p]rohibition against the wanton infliction of pain.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No clear standard for determining the constitutionality of a method of execution emerges from these decisions. Moreover, the age of the opinions limits their utility as an aid to resolution of the present controversy. The Eighth Amendment, we have held, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98must draw its meaning from the evolving standards of decency that mark the progress of a maturing society.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Atkins v. Virginia, 536 U. S. 304, 311\xe2\x80\x93312 (2002) (quoting Trop v. Dulles, 356 U. S. 86, 101 (1958) (plurality opinion)). Wilkerson was decided 129 years ago, Kemmler 118 years ago, and Resweber 61 years ago. Whatever little light our prior method-of-execution cases might shed is thus dimmed by the passage of time.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Further phrases and tests can be drawn from more recent decisions, for example, Gregg v. Georgia, 428 U. S. 153 (1976). Speaking of capital punishment in the abstract, the lead opinion said that the Eighth Amendment prohibits \xe2\x80\x9cthe unnecessary and wanton infliction of pain,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 173 (joint opinion of Stewart, Powell, and Stevens, JJ.); the same opinion also cautioned that a death sentence cannot \xe2\x80\x9cbe imposed under sentencing procedures that creat[e] a substantial risk that it would be inflicted in an arbitrary and capricious manner,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 188.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Relying on Gregg and our earlier decisions, the Kentucky Supreme Court stated that an execution procedure violates the Eighth Amendment if it \xe2\x80\x9ccreates a substantial risk of wanton and unnecessary infliction of pain, torture or lingering death.\xe2\x80\x9d 217 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 207, 209, 210 (2006). Petitioners respond that courts should consider \xe2\x80\x9c(a) the severity of pain risked, (b) the likelihood of that pain occurring, and (c) the extent to which alternative means are feasible.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 38 (emphasis added). The plurality settles somewhere in between, requiring a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial risk of serious harm\xe2\x80\x9d and considering whether a \xe2\x80\x9cfeasible, readily implemented\xe2\x80\x9d alternative can \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly reduce\xe2\x80\x9d that risk. Ante, at 13 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with petitioners and the plurality that the degree of risk, magnitude of pain, and availability of alternatives must be considered. I part ways with the plurality, however, to the extent its \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial risk\xe2\x80\x9d test sets a fixed threshold for the first factor. The three factors are interrelated; a strong showing on one reduces the importance of the others.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lethal injection as a mode of execution can be expected, in most instances, to result in painless death. Rare though errors may be, the consequences of a mistake about the condemned inmate\xe2\x80\x99s consciousness are horrendous and effectively undetectable after injection of the second drug. Given the opposing tugs of the degree of risk and magnitude of pain, the critical question here, as I see it, is whether a feasible alternative exists. Proof of \xe2\x80\x9ca slightly or marginally safer alternative\xe2\x80\x9d is, as the plurality notes, insufficient. Ante, at 12. But if readily available measures can materially increase the likelihood that the protocol will cause no pain, a State fails to adhere to contemporary standards of decency if it declines to employ those measures.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s Legislature adopted lethal injection as a method of execution in 1998. See 1998 Ky. Acts ch. 220, p. 777, Ky. Rev. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7431.220(1)(a) (West 2006). Lawmakers left the development of the lethal injection protocol to officials in the Department of Corrections. Those officials, the trial court found, were \xe2\x80\x9cgiven the task without the benefit of scientific aid or policy oversight.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 768. \xe2\x80\x9cKentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol,\xe2\x80\x9d that court observed, \xe2\x80\x9cwas copied from other states and accepted without challenge.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Kentucky \xe2\x80\x9cdid not conduct any independent scientific or medical studies or consult any medical professionals concerning the drugs and dosage amounts to be injected into the condemned.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 760. Instead, the trial court noted, Kentucky followed the path taken in other States that \xe2\x80\x9csimply fell in line\xe2\x80\x9d behind the three-drug protocol first developed by Oklahoma in 1977. Id., at 756. See also ante, at 4, n.\xc2\xa01 (plurality opinion).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol begins with a careful measure: Only medical professionals may perform the venipunctures and establish intravenous (IV) access. Members of the IV team must have at least one year\xe2\x80\x99s experience as a certified medical assistant, phlebotomist, emergency medical technician (EMT), paramedic, or military corpsman. App. 984; ante, at 16 (plurality opinion). Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s IV team currently has two members: a phlebotomist with 8 years\xe2\x80\x99 experience and an EMT with 20 years\xe2\x80\x99 experience. App. 273\xe2\x80\x93274. Both members practice siting catheters at ten lethal injection training sessions held annually. Id., at 984.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Other than using qualified and trained personnel to establish IV access, however, Kentucky does little to ensure that the inmate receives an effective dose of sodium thiopental. After siting the catheters, the IV team leaves the execution chamber. Id., at 977. From that point forward, only the warden and deputy warden remain with the inmate. Id., at 276. Neither the warden nor the deputy warden has any medical training.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The warden relies on visual observation to determine whether the inmate \xe2\x80\x9cappears\xe2\x80\x9d unconscious. Id., at 978. In Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s only previous execution by lethal injection, the warden\xe2\x80\x99s position allowed him to see the inmate best from the waist down, with only a peripheral view of the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s face. See id., at 213\xe2\x80\x93214. No other check for consciousness occurs before injection of pancuronium bromide. Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol does not include an automatic pause in the \xe2\x80\x9crapid flow\xe2\x80\x9d of the drugs, id., at 978, or any of the most basic tests to determine whether the sodium thiopental has worked. No one calls the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s name, shakes him, brushes his eyelashes to test for a reflex, or applies a noxious stimulus to gauge his response.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does Kentucky monitor the effectiveness of the sodium thiopental using readily available equipment, even though the inmate is already connected to an electrocardiogram (EKG), id., at 976. A drop in blood pressure or heart rate after injection of sodium thiopental would not prove that the inmate is unconscious, see id., at 579\xe2\x80\x93580; ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9321 (plurality opinion), but would signal that the drug has entered the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s bloodstream, see App. 424, 498, 578, 580; 8 Tr. 1099 (May 2, 2005). Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s own expert testified that the sodium thiopental should \xe2\x80\x9ccause the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s blood pressure to become very, very low,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 578, and that a precipitous drop in blood pressure would \xe2\x80\x9cconfir[m]\xe2\x80\x9d that the drug was having its expected effect, id., at 580. Use of a blood pressure cuff and EKG, the record shows, is the standard of care in surgery requiring anesthesia. Id., at 539.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A consciousness check supplementing the warden\xe2\x80\x99s visual observation before injection of the second drug is easily implemented and can reduce a risk of dreadful pain. Pancuronium bromide is a powerful paralytic that prevents all voluntary muscle movement. Once it is injected, further monitoring of the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s consciousness becomes impractical without sophisticated equipment and training. Even if the inmate were conscious and in excruciating pain, there would be no visible indication.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Recognizing the importance of a window between the first and second drugs, other States have adopted safeguards not contained in Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol. See Brief for Criminal Justice Legal Foundation as Amicus Curiae 19\xe2\x80\x9323.[Footnote 5] Florida pauses between injection of the first and second drugs so the warden can \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine, after consultation, that the inmate is indeed unconscious.\xe2\x80\x9d Lightbourne v. McCollum, 969 So. 2d 326, 346 (Fla. 2007) (per curiam) (internal quotation marks omitted). The warden does so by touching the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s eyelashes, calling his name, and shaking him. Id., at 347.[Footnote 6] If the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s consciousness remains in doubt in Florida, \xe2\x80\x9cthe medical team members will come out from the chemical room and consult in the assessment of the inmate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. During the entire execution, the person who inserted the IV line monitors the IV access point and the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s face on closed circuit television. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Missouri, \xe2\x80\x9cmedical personnel must examine the prisoner physically to confirm that he is unconscious using standard clinical techniques and must inspect the catheter site again.\xe2\x80\x9d Taylor v. Crawford, 487 F.\xc2\xa03d 1072, 1083 (CA8 2007). \xe2\x80\x9cThe second and third chemicals are injected only after confirmation that the prisoner is unconscious and after a period of at least three minutes has elapsed from the first injection of thiopental.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In California, a member of the IV team brushes\nthe inmate\xe2\x80\x99s eyelashes, speaks to him, and shakes him\nat the halfway point and, again, at the completion of\nthe sodium thiopental injection. See State of Califor-\nnia, San Quentin Operational Procedure No. 0\xe2\x80\x93770, Execution by Lethal Injection, \xc2\xa7V(S)(4)(e) (2007), online at http://www.cdcr.ca.gov/News/docs/RevisedProtocol.pdf.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Alabama, a member of the execution team \xe2\x80\x9cbegin[s] by saying the condemned inmate\xe2\x80\x99s name. If there is no response, the team member will gently stroke the condemned inmate\xe2\x80\x99s eyelashes. If there is no response, the team member will then pinch the condemned inmate\xe2\x80\x99s arm.\xe2\x80\x9d Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 Opposition to Callahan\xe2\x80\x99s Application for a Stay of Execution in Callahan v. Allen, O.\xc2\xa0T. 2007, No. 07A630, p.\xc2\xa03 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Indiana, officials inspect the injection site after administration of sodium thiopental, say the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s name, touch him, and use ammonia tablets to test his response to a noxious nasal stimulus. See Tr. of Preliminary Injunction Hearing in 1:06\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x931859 (SD Ind.), pp. 199\xe2\x80\x93200, online at http://www.law.berkeley.edu/clinics/dpclinic/\nLethalInjection/Public/MoralesTaylorAmicus/20.pdf (hereinafter Timberlake Hearing).[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These checks provide a degree of assurance\xe2\x80\x94missing from Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol\xe2\x80\x94that the first drug has been properly administered. They are simple and essentially costless to employ, yet work to lower the risk that the inmate will be subjected to the agony of conscious suffocation caused by pancuronium bromide and the searing pain caused by potassium chloride. The record contains no explanation why Kentucky does not take any of these elementary measures.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The risk that an error administering sodium thiopental would go undetected is minimal, Kentucky urges, because if the drug was mistakenly injected into the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s tissue, not a vein, he \xe2\x80\x9cwould be awake and screaming.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 30\xe2\x80\x9331. See also Brief for Respondents 42; Brief for State of Texas et al. as Amici Curiae 26\xe2\x80\x9327. That argument ignores aspects of Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol that render passive reliance on obvious signs of consciousness, such as screaming, inadequate to determine whether the inmate is experiencing pain.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s use of pancuronium bromide to paralyze the inmate means he will not be able to scream after the second drug is injected, no matter how much pain he is experiencing. Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s argument, therefore, appears to rest on the assertion that sodium thiopental is itself painful when injected into tissue rather than a vein. See App. 601. The trial court made no finding on that point, and Kentucky cites no supporting evidence from executions in which it is known that sodium thiopental was injected into the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s soft tissue. See, e.g., Lightbourne, 969 So. 2d, at 344 (describing execution of Angel Diaz).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the inmate may receive enough sodium thiopental to mask the most obvious signs of consciousness without receiving a dose sufficient to achieve a surgical plane of anesthesia. See 7 Tr. 976 (Apr. 21, 2005). If the drug is injected too quickly, the increase in blood pressure can cause the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s veins to burst after a small amount of sodium thiopental has been administered. Cf. App. 217 (describing risk of \xe2\x80\x9cblowout\xe2\x80\x9d). Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol does not specify the rate at which sodium thiopental should be injected. The executioner, who does not have any medical training, pushes the drug \xe2\x80\x9cby feel\xe2\x80\x9d through five feet of tubing. Id., at 284, 286\xe2\x80\x93287.[Footnote 8] In practice sessions, unlike in an actual execution, there is no resistance on the catheter, see id., at 285; thus the executioner\xe2\x80\x99s training may lead him to push the drugs too fast.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe easiest and most obvious way to ensure that an inmate is unconscious during an execution,\xe2\x80\x9d petitioners argued to the Kentucky Supreme Court, \xe2\x80\x9cis to check for consciousness prior to injecting pancuronium [bromide].\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellants in No. 2005\xe2\x80\x93SC\xe2\x80\x9300543, p. 41. See also App. 30 (Complaint) (alleging Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol does not \xe2\x80\x9crequire the execution team to determine that the condemned inmate is unconscious prior to administering the second and third chemicals\xe2\x80\x9d). The court did not address petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument. I would therefore remand with instructions to consider whether the failure to include readily available safeguards to confirm that the inmate is unconscious after injection of sodium thiopental, in combination with the other elements of Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s protocol, creates an untoward, readily avoidable risk of inflicting severe and unnecessary pain.
