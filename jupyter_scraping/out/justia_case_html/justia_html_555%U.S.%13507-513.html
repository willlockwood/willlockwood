HERRING v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x93513.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 7, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 14, 2009

\n

Officers in Coffee County arrested petitioner Herring based on a warrant listed in neighboring Dale County\xe2\x80\x99s database. A search incident to that arrest yielded drugs and a gun. It was then revealed that the warrant had been recalled months earlier, though this information had never been entered into the database. Herring was indicted on federal gun and drug possession charges and moved to suppress the evidence on the ground that his initial arrest had been illegal. Assuming that there was a Fourth Amendment violation, the District Court concluded that the exclusionary rule did not apply and denied the motion to suppress. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed, finding that the arresting officers were innocent of any wrongdoing, and that Dale County\xe2\x80\x99s failure to update the records was merely negligent. The court therefore concluded that the benefit of suppression would be marginal or nonexistent and that the evidence was admissible under the good-faith rule of United States v. Leon, 468 U. S. 897.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0 When police mistakes leading to an unlawful search are the result of isolated negligence attenuated from the search, rather than systemic error or reckless disregard of constitutional requirements, the exclusionary rule does not apply. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The fact that a search or arrest was unreasonable does not necessarily mean that the exclusionary rule applies. Illinois v. Gates, 462 U. S. 213, 223. The rule is not an individual right and applies only where its deterrent effect outweighs the substantial cost of letting guilty and possibly dangerous defendants go free. Leon, 468 U. S., at 908\xe2\x80\x93909. For example, it does not apply if police acted \xe2\x80\x9cin objectively reasonable reliance\xe2\x80\x9d on an invalid warrant. Id., at 922. In applying Leon\xe2\x80\x99s good-faith rule to police who reasonably relied on mistaken information in a court\xe2\x80\x99s database that an arrest warrant was outstanding, Arizona v. Evans, 514 U. S. 1, 14\xe2\x80\x9315, the Court left unresolved the issue confronted here: whether evidence should be suppressed if the police committed the error, id., at 16, n.\xc2\xa05. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The extent to which the exclusionary rule is justified by its deterrent effect varies with the degree of law enforcement culpability. See, e.g., Leon, supra, at 911. Indeed, the abuses that gave rise to the rule featured intentional conduct that was patently unconstitutional. See, e.g., Weeks v. United States, 232 U. S.383. An error arising from nonrecurring and attenuated negligence is far removed from the core concerns that led to the rule\xe2\x80\x99s adoption. Pp. 7\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0To trigger the exclusionary rule, police conduct must be sufficiently deliberate that exclusion can meaningfully deter it, and sufficiently culpable that such deterrence is worth the price paid by the justice system. The pertinent analysis is objective, not an inquiry into the arresting officers\xe2\x80\x99 subjective awareness. See, e.g., Leon, supra, at 922, n. 23. Pp. 9\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0The conduct here was not so objectively culpable as to require exclusion. The marginal benefits that might follow from suppressing evidence obtained in these circumstances cannot justify the substantial costs of exclusion. Leon, supra, at 922. Pp. 11\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

492 F.\xc2\xa03d 1212, affirmed.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nHERRING V. UNITED STATES
\n555 U. S. ____ (2009)

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 07-513

BENNIE DEAN HERRING, PETITIONER v. UNITED\nSTATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[January 14, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fourth Amendment forbids \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable searches and seizures,\xe2\x80\x9d and this usually requires the police to have probable cause or a warrant before making an arrest. What if an officer reasonably believes there is an outstanding arrest warrant, but that belief turns out to be wrong because of a negligent bookkeeping error by another police employee? The parties here agree that the ensuing arrest is still a violation of the Fourth Amendment, but dispute whether contraband found during a search incident to that arrest must be excluded in a later prosecution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our cases establish that such suppression is not an automatic consequence of a Fourth Amendment violation. Instead, the question turns on the culpability of the police and the potential of exclusion to deter wrongful police conduct. Here the error was the result of isolated negligence attenuated from the arrest. We hold that in these circumstances the jury should not be barred from considering all the evidence.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On July 7, 2004, Investigator Mark Anderson learned that Bennie Dean Herring had driven to the Coffee County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department to retrieve something from his impounded truck. Herring was no stranger to law enforcement, and Anderson asked the county\xe2\x80\x99s warrant clerk, Sandy Pope, to check for any outstanding warrants for Herring\xe2\x80\x99s arrest. When she found none, Anderson asked Pope to check with Sharon Morgan, her counterpart in neighboring Dale County. After checking Dale County\xe2\x80\x99s computer database, Morgan replied that there was an active arrest warrant for Herring\xe2\x80\x99s failure to appear on a felony charge. Pope relayed the information to Anderson and asked Morgan to fax over a copy of the warrant as confirmation. Anderson and a deputy followed Herring as he left the impound lot, pulled him over, and arrested him. A search incident to the arrest revealed methamphetamine in Herring\xe2\x80\x99s pocket, and a pistol (which as a felon he could not possess) in his vehicle. App. 17\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There had, however, been a mistake about the warrant. The Dale County sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s computer records are supposed to correspond to actual arrest warrants, which the office also maintains. But when Morgan went to the files to retrieve the actual warrant to fax to Pope, Morgan was unable to find it. She called a court clerk and learned that the warrant had been recalled five months earlier. Normally when a warrant is recalled the court clerk\xe2\x80\x99s office or a judge\xe2\x80\x99s chambers calls Morgan, who enters the information in the sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s computer database and disposes of the physical copy. For whatever reason, the information about the recall of the warrant for Herring did not appear in the database. Morgan immediately called Pope to alert her to the mixup, and Pope contacted Anderson over a secure radio. This all unfolded in 10 to 15 minutes, but Herring had already been arrested and found with the gun and drugs, just a few hundred yards from the sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s office. Id., at 26, 35\xe2\x80\x9342, 54\xe2\x80\x9355.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Herring was indicted in the District Court for the Middle District of Alabama for illegally possessing the gun and drugs, violations of 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7922(g)(1) and 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7844(a). He moved to suppress the evidence on the ground that his initial arrest had been illegal because the warrant had been rescinded. The Magistrate Judge recommended denying the motion because the arresting officers had acted in a good-faith belief that the warrant was still outstanding. Thus, even if there were a Fourth Amendment violation, there was \xe2\x80\x9cno reason to believe that application of the exclusionary rule here would deter the occurrence of any future mistakes.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 70. The District Court adopted the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s recommendation, 451 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1290 (2005), and the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed, 492 F.\xc2\xa03d 1212 (2007).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Eleventh Circuit found that the arresting officers in Coffee County \xe2\x80\x9cwere entirely innocent of any wrongdoing or carelessness.\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1218. The court assumed that whoever failed to update the Dale County sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s records was also a law enforcement official, but noted that \xe2\x80\x9cthe conduct in question [wa]s a negligent failure to act, not a deliberate or tactical choice to act.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Because the error was merely negligent and attenuated from the arrest, the Eleventh Circuit concluded that the benefit of suppressing the evidence \xe2\x80\x9cwould be marginal or nonexistent,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted), and the evidence was therefore admissible under the good-faith rule of United States v. Leon, 468 U. S. 897 (1984).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Other courts have required exclusion of evidence obtained through similar police errors, e.g., Hoay v. State, 348 Ark. 80, 86\xe2\x80\x9387, 71 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 573, 577 (2002), so we granted Herring\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari to resolve the conflict, 552 U. S. ___ (2008). We now affirm the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When a probable-cause determination was based on reasonable but mistaken assumptions, the person subjected to a search or seizure has not necessarily been the victim of a constitutional violation. The very phrase \xe2\x80\x9cprobable cause\xe2\x80\x9d confirms that the Fourth Amendment does not demand all possible precision. And whether the error can be traced to a mistake by a state actor or some other source may bear on the analysis. For purposes of deciding this case, however, we accept the parties\xe2\x80\x99 assumption that there was a Fourth Amendment violation. The issue is whether the exclusionary rule should be applied.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fourth Amendment protects \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures,\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9ccontains no provision expressly precluding the use of evidence obtained in violation of its commands,\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona v. Evans, 514 U. S. 1, 10 (1995). Nonetheless, our decisions establish an exclusionary rule that, when applicable, forbids the use of improperly obtained evidence at trial. See, e.g., Weeks v. United States, 232 U. S. 383, 398 (1914). We have stated that this judicially created rule is \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to safeguard Fourth Amendment rights generally through its deterrent effect.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Calandra, 414 U. S. 338, 348 (1974).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In analyzing the applicability of the rule, Leon admonished that we must consider the actions of all the police officers involved. 468 U. S., at 923, n. 24 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is necessary to consider the objective reasonableness, not only of the officers who eventually executed a warrant, but also of the officers who originally obtained it or who provided information material to the probable-cause determination\xe2\x80\x9d). The Coffee County officers did nothing improper. Indeed, the error was noticed so quickly because Coffee County requested a faxed confirmation of the warrant.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Eleventh Circuit concluded, however, that somebody in Dale County should have updated the computer database to reflect the recall of the arrest warrant. The court also concluded that this error was negligent, but did not find it to be reckless or deliberate. 492 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1218.[Footnote 1] That fact is crucial to our holding that this error is not enough by itself to require \xe2\x80\x9cthe extreme sanction of exclusion.\xe2\x80\x9d Leon, supra, at 916.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xe2\x80\x82The fact that a Fourth Amendment violation occurred\xe2\x80\x94i.e., that a search or arrest was unreasonable\xe2\x80\x94does not necessarily mean that the exclusionary rule applies. Illinois v. Gates, 462 U. S. 213, 223 (1983). Indeed, exclusion \xe2\x80\x9chas always been our last resort, not our first impulse,\xe2\x80\x9d Hudson v. Michigan, 547 U. S. 586, 591 (2006), and our precedents establish important principles that constrain application of the exclusionary rule.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the exclusionary rule is not an individual right and applies only where it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98result[s] in appreciable deterrence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Leon, supra, at 909 (quoting United States v. Janis, 428 U. S. 433, 454 (1976)). We have repeatedly rejected the argument that exclusion is a necessary consequence of a Fourth Amendment violation. Leon, supra, at 905\xe2\x80\x93906; Evans, supra, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314; Pennsylvania Bd. of Probation and Parole v. Scott, 524 U. S. 357, 363 (1998). Instead we have focused on the efficacy of the rule in deterring Fourth Amendment violations in the future. See Calandra, supra, at 347\xe2\x80\x93355; Stone v. Powell, 428 U. S. 465, 486 (1976).[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, the benefits of deterrence must outweigh the costs. Leon, supra, at 910. \xe2\x80\x9cWe have never suggested that the exclusionary rule must apply in every circumstance in which it might provide marginal deterrence.\xe2\x80\x9d Scott, supra, at 368. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]o the extent that application of the exclusionary rule could provide some incremental deterrent, that possible benefit must be weighed against [its] substantial social costs.\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois v. Krull, 480 U. S. 340, 352\xe2\x80\x93353 (1987) (internal quotation marks omitted). The principal cost of applying the rule is, of course, letting guilty and possibly dangerous defendants go free\xe2\x80\x94something that \xe2\x80\x9coffends basic concepts of the criminal justice system.\xe2\x80\x9d Leon, supra, at 908. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he rule\xe2\x80\x99s costly toll upon truth-seeking and law enforcement objectives presents a high obstacle for those urging [its] application.\xe2\x80\x9d Scott, supra, at 364\xe2\x80\x93365 (internal quotation marks omitted); see also United States v. Havens, 446 U. S. 620, 626\xe2\x80\x93627 (1980); United States v. Payner, 447 U. S. 727, 734 (1980).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These principles are reflected in the holding of Leon: When police act under a warrant that is invalid for lack of probable cause, the exclusionary rule does not apply if the police acted \xe2\x80\x9cin objectively reasonable reliance\xe2\x80\x9d on the subsequently invalidated search warrant. 468 U. S., at 922. We (perhaps confusingly) called this objectively reasonable reliance \xe2\x80\x9cgood faith.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid., n. 23. In a companion case, Massachusetts v. Sheppard, 468 U. S. 981 (1984), we held that the exclusionary rule did not apply when a warrant was invalid because a judge forgot to make \xe2\x80\x9cclerical corrections\xe2\x80\x9d to it. Id., at 991.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Shortly thereafter we extended these holdings to warrantless administrative searches performed in good-faith reliance on a statute later declared unconstitutional. Krull, supra, at 349\xe2\x80\x93350. Finally, in Evans, 514 U. S. 1, we applied this good-faith rule to police who reasonably relied on mistaken information in a court\xe2\x80\x99s database that an arrest warrant was outstanding. We held that a mistake made by a judicial employee could not give rise to exclusion for three reasons: The exclusionary rule was crafted to curb police rather than judicial misconduct; court employees were unlikely to try to subvert the Fourth Amendment; and \xe2\x80\x9cmost important, there [was] no basis for believing that application of the exclusionary rule in [those] circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d would have any significant effect in deterring the errors. Id., at 15. Evans left unresolved \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the evidence should be suppressed if police personnel were responsible for the error,\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 3] an issue not argued by the State in that case, id., at 16, n.\xc2\xa05, but one that we now confront.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xe2\x80\x82The extent to which the exclusionary rule is justified by these deterrence principles varies with the culpability of the law enforcement conduct. As we said in Leon, \xe2\x80\x9can assessment of the flagrancy of the police misconduct constitutes an important step in the calculus\xe2\x80\x9d of applying the exclusionary rule. 468 U. S., at 911. Similarly, in Krull we elaborated that \xe2\x80\x9cevidence should be suppressed \xe2\x80\x98only if it can be said that the law enforcement officer had knowledge, or may properly be charged with knowledge, that the search was unconstitutional under the Fourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 480 U. S., at 348\xe2\x80\x93349 (quoting United States v. Peltier, 422 U. S. 531, 542 (1975)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Anticipating the good-faith exception to the exclusionary rule, Judge Friendly wrote that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he beneficent aim of the exclusionary rule to deter police misconduct can be sufficiently accomplished by a practice . . . outlawing evidence obtained by flagrant or deliberate violation of rights.\xe2\x80\x9d The Bill of Rights as a Code of Criminal Procedure, 53 Calif. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 929, 953 (1965) (footnotes omitted); see also Brown v. Illinois, 422 U. S. 590, 610\xe2\x80\x93611 (1975) (Powell, J., concurring in part) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he deterrent value of the exclusionary rule is most likely to be effective\xe2\x80\x9d when \xe2\x80\x9cofficial conduct was flagrantly abusive of Fourth Amendment rights\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, the abuses that gave rise to the exclusionary rule featured intentional conduct that was patently unconstitutional. In Weeks, 232 U. S. 383, a foundational exclusionary rule case, the officers had broken into the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s home (using a key shown to them by a neighbor), confiscated incriminating papers, then returned again with a U. S. Marshal to confiscate even more. Id., at 386. Not only did they have no search warrant, which the Court held was required, but they could not have gotten one had they tried. They were so lacking in sworn and particularized information that \xe2\x80\x9cnot even an order of court would have justified such procedure.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 393\xe2\x80\x93394. Silverthorne Lumber Co. v. United States, 251 U. S. 385 (1920), on which petitioner repeatedly relies, was similar; federal officials \xe2\x80\x9cwithout a shadow of authority\xe2\x80\x9d went to the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 office and \xe2\x80\x9cmade a clean sweep\xe2\x80\x9d of every paper they could find. Id., at 390. Even the Government seemed to acknowledge that the \xe2\x80\x9cseizure was an outrage.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 391.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Equally flagrant conduct was at issue in Mapp v. Ohio, 367 U. S. 643 (1961), which overruled Wolf v. Colorado, 338 U. S. 25 (1949), and extended the exclusionary rule to the States. Officers forced open a door to Ms. Mapp\xe2\x80\x99s house, kept her lawyer from entering, brandished what the court concluded was a false warrant, then forced her into handcuffs and canvassed the house for obscenity. 367 U. S., at 644\xe2\x80\x93645. See Friendly, supra, at 953, and n. 127 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he situation in Mapp\xe2\x80\x9d featured a \xe2\x80\x9cflagrant or deliberate violation of rights\xe2\x80\x9d). An error that arises from nonrecurring and attenuated negligence is thus far removed from the core concerns that led us to adopt the rule in the first place. And in fact since Leon, we have never applied the rule to exclude evidence obtained in violation of the Fourth Amendment, where the police conduct was no more intentional or culpable than this.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xe2\x80\x82To trigger the exclusionary rule, police conduct must be sufficiently deliberate that exclusion can meaningfully deter it, and sufficiently culpable that such deterrence is worth the price paid by the justice system. As laid out in our cases, the exclusionary rule serves to deter deliberate, reckless, or grossly negligent conduct, or in some circumstances recurring or systemic negligence. The error in this case does not rise to that level.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decision in Franks v. Delaware, 438 U. S. 154 (1978), provides an analogy. Cf. Leon, supra, at 914. In Franks, we held that police negligence in obtaining a warrant did not even rise to the level of a Fourth Amendment violation, let alone meet the more stringent test for triggering the exclusionary rule. We held that the Constitution allowed defendants, in some circumstances, \xe2\x80\x9cto challenge the truthfulness of factual statements made in an affidavit supporting the warrant,\xe2\x80\x9d even after the warrant had issued. 438 U. S., at 155\xe2\x80\x93156. If those false statements were necessary to the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s probable-cause determination, the warrant would be \xe2\x80\x9cvoided.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But we did not find all false statements relevant: \xe2\x80\x9cThere must be allegations of deliberate falsehood or of reckless disregard for the truth,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[a]llegations of negligence or innocent mistake are insufficient.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 171.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both this case and Franks concern false information provided by police. Under Franks, negligent police miscommunications in the course of acquiring a warrant do not provide a basis to rescind a warrant and render a search or arrest invalid. Here, the miscommunications occurred in a different context\xe2\x80\x94after the warrant had been issued and recalled\xe2\x80\x94but that fact should not require excluding the evidence obtained.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The pertinent analysis of deterrence and culpability is objective, not an \xe2\x80\x9cinquiry into the subjective awareness of arresting officers,\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioner 4\xe2\x80\x935. See also post, at 10, n.\xc2\xa07 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting). We have already held that \xe2\x80\x9cour good-faith inquiry is confined to the objectively ascertainable question whether a reasonably well trained officer would have known that the search was illegal\xe2\x80\x9d in light of \xe2\x80\x9call of the circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Leon, 468 U. S., at 922, n.\xc2\xa023. These circumstances frequently include a particular officer\xe2\x80\x99s knowledge and experience, but that does not make the test any more subjective than the one for probable cause, which looks to an officer\xe2\x80\x99s knowledge and experience, Ornelas v. United States, 517 U. S. 690, 699\xe2\x80\x93700 (1996), but not his subjective intent, Whren v. United States, 517 U. S. 806, 812\xe2\x80\x93813 (1996).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa04.\xe2\x80\x82We do not suggest that all recordkeeping errors by the police are immune from the exclusionary rule. In this case, however, the conduct at issue was not so objectively culpable as to require exclusion. In Leon we held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe marginal or nonexistent benefits produced by suppressing evidence obtained in objectively reasonable reliance on a subsequently invalidated search warrant cannot justify the substantial costs of exclusion.\xe2\x80\x9d 468 U. S., at 922. The same is true when evidence is obtained in objectively reasonable reliance on a subsequently recalled warrant.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If the police have been shown to be reckless in maintaining a warrant system, or to have knowingly made false entries to lay the groundwork for future false arrests, exclusion would certainly be justified under our cases should such misconduct cause a Fourth Amendment violation. We said as much in Leon, explaining that an officer could not \xe2\x80\x9cobtain a warrant on the basis of a \xe2\x80\x98bare bones\xe2\x80\x99 affidavit and then rely on colleagues who are ignorant of the circumstances under which the warrant was obtained to conduct the search.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 923, n. 24 (citing Whiteley v. Warden, Wyo. State Penitentiary, 401 U. S. 560, 568 (1971)). Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s fears that our decision will cause police departments to deliberately keep their officers ignorant, Brief for Petitioner 37\xe2\x80\x9339, are thus unfounded.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent also adverts to the possible unreliability of a number of databases not relevant to this case. Post, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. In a case where systemic errors were demonstrated, it might be reckless for officers to rely on an unreliable warrant system. See Evans, 514 U. S., at 17 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cSurely it would not be reasonable for the police to rely . . . on a recordkeeping system . . . that routinely leads to false arrests\xe2\x80\x9d (second emphasis added)); Hudson, 547 U. S., at 604 (Kennedy, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf a widespread pattern of violations were shown \xe2\x80\xa6 there would be reason for grave concern\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). But there is no evidence that errors in Dale County\xe2\x80\x99s system are routine or widespread. Officer Anderson testified that he had never had reason to question information about a Dale County warrant, App. 27, and both Sandy Pope and Sharon Morgan testified that they could remember no similar miscommunication ever happening on their watch, id., at 33, 61\xe2\x80\x9362. That is even less error than in the database at issue in Evans, where we also found reliance on the database to be objectively reasonable. 514 U. S., at 15 (similar error \xe2\x80\x9cevery three or four years\xe2\x80\x9d). Because no such showings were made here, see 451 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1292,[Footnote 5] the Eleventh Circuit was correct to affirm the denial of the motion to suppress.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim that police negligence automatically triggers suppression cannot be squared with the principles underlying the exclusionary rule, as they have been explained in our cases. In light of our repeated holdings that the deterrent effect of suppression must be substantial and outweigh any harm to the justice system, e.g., Leon, 468 U. S., at 909\xe2\x80\x93910, we conclude that when police mistakes are the result of negligence such as that described here, rather than systemic error or reckless disregard of constitutional requirements, any marginal deterrence does not \xe2\x80\x9cpay its way.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 907\xe2\x80\x93908, n.\xc2\xa06 (internal quotation marks omitted). In such a case, the criminal should not \xe2\x80\x9cgo free because the constable has blundered.\xe2\x80\x9d People v. Defore, 242 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 13, 21, 150 N.\xc2\xa0E. 585, 587 (1926) (opinion of the Court by Cardozo, J.).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.

BENNIE DEAN HERRING, PETITIONER v. UNITED\nSTATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[January 14, 2009]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg, with whom Justice Stevens, Justice Souter, and Justice Breyer join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Bennie Dean Herring was arrested, and subjected to a search incident to his arrest, although no warrant was outstanding against him, and the police lacked probable cause to believe he was engaged in criminal activity. The arrest and ensuing search therefore violated Herring\xe2\x80\x99s Fourth Amendment right \xe2\x80\x9cto be secure \xe2\x80\xa6 against unreasonable searches and seizures.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court of Appeals so determined, and the Government does not contend otherwise. The exclusionary rule provides redress for Fourth Amendment violations by placing the government in the position it would have been in had there been no unconstitutional arrest and search. The rule thus strongly encourages police compliance with the Fourth Amendment in the future. The Court, however, holds the rule inapplicable because careless recordkeeping by the police\xe2\x80\x94not flagrant or deliberate misconduct\xe2\x80\x94accounts for Herring\xe2\x80\x99s arrest.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I would not so constrict the domain of the exclusionary rule and would hold the rule dispositive of this case: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]f courts are to have any power to discourage [police] error of [the kind here at issue], it must be through the application of the exclusionary rule.\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona v. Evans, 514 U. S. 1, 22\xe2\x80\x9323 (1995) (Stevens, J., dissenting). The unlawful search in this case was contested in court because the police found methamphetamine in Herring\xe2\x80\x99s pocket and a pistol in his truck. But the \xe2\x80\x9cmost serious impact\xe2\x80\x9d of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding will be on innocent persons \xe2\x80\x9cwrongfully arrested based on erroneous information [carelessly maintained] in a computer data base.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 22.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A warrant for Herring\xe2\x80\x99s arrest was recalled in February 2004, apparently because it had been issued in error. See Brief for Petitioner 3, n.\xc2\xa01 (citing App. 63). The warrant database for the Dale County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department, however, does not automatically update to reflect such changes. App. 39\xe2\x80\x9340, 43, 45. A member of the Dale County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department\xe2\x80\x94whom the parties have not identified\xe2\x80\x94returned the hard copy of the warrant to the County Circuit Clerk\xe2\x80\x99s office, but did not correct the Department\xe2\x80\x99s database to show that the warrant had been recalled. Id., at 60. The erroneous entry for the warrant remained in the database, undetected, for five months.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On a July afternoon in 2004, Herring came to the Coffee County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department to retrieve his belongings from a vehicle impounded in the Department\xe2\x80\x99s lot. Id., at 17. Investigator Mark Anderson, who was at the Department that day, knew Herring from prior interactions: Herring had told the district attorney, among others, of his suspicion that Anderson had been involved in the killing of a local teenager, and Anderson had pursued Herring to get him to drop the accusations. Id., at 63\xe2\x80\x9364. Informed that Herring was in the impoundment lot, Anderson asked the Coffee County warrant clerk whether there was an outstanding warrant for Herring\xe2\x80\x99s arrest. Id., at 18. The clerk, Sandy Pope, found no warrant. Id., at 19.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Anderson then asked Pope to call the neighboring Dale County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department to inquire whether a warrant to arrest Herring was outstanding there. Upon receiving Pope\xe2\x80\x99s phone call, Sharon Morgan, the warrant clerk for the Dale County Department, checked her computer database. As just recounted, that Department\xe2\x80\x99s database preserved an error. Morgan\xe2\x80\x99s check therefore showed\xe2\x80\x94incorrectly\xe2\x80\x94an active warrant for Herring\xe2\x80\x99s arrest. Id., at 41. Morgan gave the misinformation to Pope, ibid., who relayed it to Investigator Anderson, id., at 35. Armed with the report that a warrant existed, Anderson promptly arrested Herring and performed an incident search minutes before detection of the error.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals concluded, and the Government does not contest, that the \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to bring the [Dale County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department] records up to date [was] \xe2\x80\x98at the very least negligent.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 492 F.\xc2\xa03d 1212, 1217 (CA11 2007) (quoting Michigan v. Tucker, 417 U. S. 433, 447 (1974)). And it is uncontested here that Herring\xe2\x80\x99s arrest violated his Fourth Amendment rights. The sole question presented, therefore, is whether evidence the police obtained through the unlawful search should have been suppressed.[Footnote 1] The Court holds that suppression was unwarranted because the exclusionary rule\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccore concerns\xe2\x80\x9d are not raised by an isolated, negligent recordkeeping error attenuated from the arrest. Ante, at 9, 12.[Footnote 2] In my view, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion underestimates the need for a forceful exclusionary rule and the gravity of recordkeeping errors in law enforcement.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court states that the exclusionary rule is not a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s right, ante, at 5; rather, it is simply a remedy applicable only when suppression would result in appreciable deterrence that outweighs the cost to the justice system, ante, at 12. See also ante, at 9 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he exclusionary rule serves to deter deliberate, reckless, or grossly negligent conduct, or in some circumstances recurring or systemic negligence.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s discussion invokes a view of the exclusionary rule famously held by renowned jurists Henry J. Friendly and Benjamin Nathan Cardozo. Over 80 years ago, Cardozo, then seated on the New York Court of Appeals, commented critically on the federal exclusionary rule, which had not yet been applied to the States. He suggested that in at least some cases the rule exacted too high a price from the criminal justice system. See People v. Defore, 242 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 13, 24\xe2\x80\x9325, 150 N.\xc2\xa0E. 585, 588\xe2\x80\x93589 (1926). In words often quoted, Cardozo questioned whether the criminal should \xe2\x80\x9cgo free because the constable has blundered.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 21, 150 N. E., at 587.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Friendly later elaborated on Cardozo\xe2\x80\x99s query. \xe2\x80\x9cThe sole reason for exclusion,\xe2\x80\x9d Friendly wrote, \xe2\x80\x9cis that experience has demonstrated this to be the only effective method for deterring the police from violating the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d The Bill of Rights as a Code of Criminal Procedure, 53 Calif. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 929, 951 (1965). He thought it excessive, in light of the rule\xe2\x80\x99s aim to deter police conduct, to require exclusion when the constable had merely \xe2\x80\x9cblundered\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94when a police officer committed a technical error in an on-the-spot judgment, id., at 952, or made a \xe2\x80\x9cslight and unintentional miscalculation,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 953. As the Court recounts, Judge Friendly suggested that deterrence of police improprieties could be \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently accomplished\xe2\x80\x9d by confining the rule to \xe2\x80\x9cevidence obtained by flagrant or deliberate violation of rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; ante, at 8.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Others have described \xe2\x80\x9ca more majestic conception\xe2\x80\x9d of the Fourth Amendment and its adjunct, the exclusionary rule. Evans, 514 U. S., at 18 (Stevens, J., dissenting). Protective of the fundamental \xe2\x80\x9cright of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects,\xe2\x80\x9d the Amendment \xe2\x80\x9cis a constraint on the power of the sovereign, not merely on some of its agents.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted); see Stewart, The Road to Mapp v. Ohio and Beyond: The Origins, Development and Future of the Exclusionary Rule in Search-and-Seizure Cases, 83 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1365 (1983). I share that vision of the Amendment.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The exclusionary rule is \xe2\x80\x9ca remedy necessary to ensure that\xe2\x80\x9d the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibitions \xe2\x80\x9care observed in fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1389; see Kamisar, Does (Did) (Should) The Exclusionary Rule Rest On A \xe2\x80\x9cPrincipled Basis\xe2\x80\x9d Rather Than An \xe2\x80\x9cEmpirical Proposition\xe2\x80\x9d? 16 Creighton L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 565, 600 (1983). The rule\xe2\x80\x99s service as an essential auxiliary to the Amendment earlier inclined the Court to hold the two inseparable. See Whiteley v. Warden, Wyo. State Penitentiary, 401 U. S. 560, 568\xe2\x80\x93569 (1971). Cf. Olmstead v. United States, 277 U. S. 438, 469\xe2\x80\x93471 (1928) (Holmes, J., dissenting); id., at 477\xe2\x80\x93479, 483\xe2\x80\x93485 (Brandeis, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Beyond doubt, a main objective of the rule \xe2\x80\x9cis to deter\xe2\x80\x94to compel respect for the constitutional guaranty in the only effectively available way\xe2\x80\x94by removing the incentive to disregard it.\xe2\x80\x9d Elkins v. United States, 364 U. S. 206, 217 (1960). But the rule also serves other important purposes: It \xe2\x80\x9cenabl[es] the judiciary to avoid the taint of partnership in official lawlessness,\xe2\x80\x9d and it \xe2\x80\x9cassur[es] the people\xe2\x80\x94all potential victims of unlawful government conduct\xe2\x80\x94that the government would not profit from its lawless behavior, thus minimizing the risk of seriously undermining popular trust in government.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Calandra, 414 U. S. 338, 357 (1974) (Brennan, J., dissenting). See also Terry v. Ohio, 392 U. S. 1, 13 (1968) (\xe2\x80\x9cA rule admitting evidence in a criminal trial, we recognize, has the necessary effect of legitimizing the conduct which produced the evidence, while an application of the exclusionary rule withholds the constitutional imprimatur.\xe2\x80\x9d); Kamisar, supra, at 604 (a principal reason for the exclusionary rule is that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Court\xe2\x80\x99s aid should be denied \xe2\x80\x98in order to maintain respect for law [and] to preserve the judicial process from contamination\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Olmstead, 277 U. S., at 484 (Brandeis, J., dissenting)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The exclusionary rule, it bears emphasis, is often the only remedy effective to redress a Fourth Amendment violation. See Mapp v. Ohio, 367 U. S. 643, 652 (1961) (noting \xe2\x80\x9cthe obvious futility of relegating the Fourth Amendment to the protection of other remedies\xe2\x80\x9d); Amsterdam, Perspectives on the Fourth Amendment, 58 Minn. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 349, 360 (1974) (describing the exclusionary rule as \xe2\x80\x9cthe primary instrument for enforcing the [F]ourth [A]mendment\xe2\x80\x9d). Civil liability will not lie for \xe2\x80\x9cthe vast majority of [F]ourth [A]mendment violations\xe2\x80\x94the frequent infringements motivated by commendable zeal, not condemnable malice.\xe2\x80\x9d Stewart, 83 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev., at 1389. Criminal prosecutions or administrative sanctions against the offending officers and injunctive relief against widespread violations are an even farther cry. See id., at 1386\xe2\x80\x931388.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court maintains that Herring\xe2\x80\x99s case is one in which the exclusionary rule could have scant deterrent effect and therefore would not \xe2\x80\x9cpay its way.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13 (internal quotation marks omitted). I disagree.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The exclusionary rule, the Court suggests, is capable of only marginal deterrence when the misconduct at issue is merely careless, not intentional or reckless. See ante, at 9, 11. The suggestion runs counter to a foundational premise of tort law\xe2\x80\x94that liability for negligence, i.e., lack of due care, creates an incentive to act with greater care. The Government so acknowledges. See Brief for United States 21; cf. Reply Brief 12.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That the mistake here involved the failure to make a computer entry hardly means that application of the exclusionary rule would have minimal value. \xe2\x80\x9cJust as the risk of respondeat superior liability encourages employers to supervise \xe2\x80\xa6 their employees\xe2\x80\x99 conduct [more carefully], so the risk of exclusion of evidence encourages policymakers and systems managers to monitor the performance of the systems they install and the personnel employed to operate those systems.\xe2\x80\x9d Evans, 514 U. S., at 29, n.\xc2\xa05 (Ginsburg, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consider the potential impact of a decision applying the exclusionary rule in this case. As earlier observed, see supra, at 2, the record indicates that there is no electronic connection between the warrant database of the Dale County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department and that of the County Circuit Clerk\xe2\x80\x99s office, which is located in the basement of the same building. App. 39\xe2\x80\x9340, 43, 45. When a warrant is recalled, one of the \xe2\x80\x9cmany different people that have access to th[e] warrants,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 60, must find the hard copy of the warrant in the \xe2\x80\x9ctwo or three different places\xe2\x80\x9d where the department houses warrants, id., at 41, return it to the Clerk\xe2\x80\x99s office, and manually update the Department\xe2\x80\x99s database, see id., at 60. The record reflects no routine practice of checking the database for accuracy, and the failure to remove the entry for Herring\xe2\x80\x99s warrant was not discovered until Investigator Anderson sought to pursue Herring five months later. Is it not altogether obvious that the Department could take further precautions to ensure the integrity of its database? The Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department \xe2\x80\x9cis in a position to remedy the situation and might well do so if the exclusionary rule is there to remove the incentive to do otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 W. LaFave, Search and Seizure \xc2\xa71.8(e), p. 313 (4th ed. 2004). See also Evans, 514 U. S., at 21 (Stevens, J., dissenting).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Is the potential deterrence here worth the costs it imposes? See ante, at 9. In light of the paramount importance of accurate recordkeeping in law enforcement, I would answer yes, and next explain why, as I see it,\nHerring\xe2\x80\x99s motion presents a particularly strong case for suppression.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Electronic databases form the nervous system of contemporary criminal justice operations. In recent years, their breadth and influence have dramatically expanded. Police today can access databases that include not only the updated National Crime Information Center (NCIC), but also terrorist watchlists, the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s employee eligibility system, and various commercial databases. Brief for Electronic Privacy Information Center (EPIC) et\xc2\xa0al. as Amicus Curiae 6. Moreover, States are actively expanding information sharing between jurisdictions. Id., at 8\xe2\x80\x9313. As a result, law enforcement has an increasing supply of information within its easy electronic reach. See Brief for Petitioner 36\xe2\x80\x9337.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The risk of error stemming from these databases is not slim. Herring\xe2\x80\x99s amici warn that law enforcement databases are insufficiently monitored and often out of date. Brief for Amicus EPIC 13\xe2\x80\x9328. Government reports describe, for example, flaws in NCIC databases,[Footnote 3] terrorist watchlist databases,[Footnote 4] and databases associated with the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s employment eligibility verification system.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Inaccuracies in expansive, interconnected collections of electronic information raise grave concerns for individual liberty. \xe2\x80\x9cThe offense to the dignity of the citizen who is arrested, handcuffed, and searched on a public street simply because some bureaucrat has failed to maintain\nan accurate computer data base\xe2\x80\x9d is evocative of the use\nof general warrants that so outraged the authors of our Bill of Rights. Evans, 514 U. S., at 23 (Stevens, J.,\ndissenting).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court assures that \xe2\x80\x9cexclusion would certainly be justified\xe2\x80\x9d if \xe2\x80\x9cthe police have been shown to be reckless in maintaining a warrant system, or to have knowingly\nmade false entries to lay the groundwork for future false arrests.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. This concession provides little\ncomfort.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, by restricting suppression to bookkeeping errors that are deliberate or reckless, the majority leaves Herring, and others like him, with no remedy for violations of their constitutional rights. See supra, at 6. There can be no serious assertion that relief is available under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. The arresting officer would be sheltered by qualified immunity, see Harlow v. Fitzgerald, 457 U. S. 800 (1982), and the police department itself is not liable for the negligent acts of its employees, see Monell v. New York City Dept. of Social Servs., 436 U. S. 658 (1978). Moreover, identifying the department employee who committed the error may be impossible.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, I doubt that police forces already possess sufficient incentives to maintain up-to-date records. The Government argues that police have no desire to send officers out on arrests unnecessarily, because arrests consume resources and place officers in danger. The facts of this case do not fit that description of police motivation. Here the officer wanted to arrest Herring and consulted the Department\xe2\x80\x99s records to legitimate his predisposition. See App. 17\xe2\x80\x9319.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, even when deliberate or reckless conduct is afoot, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s assurance will often be an empty promise: How is an impecunious defendant to make the required showing? If the answer is that a defendant is entitled to discovery (and if necessary, an audit of police databases), see Tr. of Oral Arg. 57\xe2\x80\x9358, then the Court has imposed a considerable administrative burden on courts and law enforcement.[Footnote 7]

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Negligent recordkeeping errors by law enforcement threaten individual liberty, are susceptible to deterrence by the exclusionary rule, and cannot be remedied effectively through other means. Such errors present no occasion to further erode the exclusionary rule. The rule \xe2\x80\x9cis needed to make the Fourth Amendment something real; a guarantee that does not carry with it the exclusion of evidence obtained by its violation is a chimera.\xe2\x80\x9d Calandra, 414 U. S., at 361 (Brennan, J., dissenting). In keeping with the rule\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccore concerns,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9, suppression should have attended the unconstitutional search in this case.

\n

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated, I would reverse the judgment of the Eleventh Circuit.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Souter joins, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with Justice Ginsburg and join her dissent. I write separately to note one additional supporting factor that I believe important. In Arizona v. Evans, 514 U. S. 1 (1995), we held that recordkeeping errors made by a court clerk do not trigger the exclusionary rule, so long as the police reasonably relied upon the court clerk\xe2\x80\x99s recordkeeping. Id., at 14; id., at 16\xe2\x80\x9317 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring). The rationale for our decision was premised on a distinction between judicial errors and police errors, and we gave several reasons for recognizing that distinction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, we noted that \xe2\x80\x9cthe exclusionary rule was historically designed as a means of deterring police misconduct, not mistakes by court employees.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14 (emphasis added). Second, we found \xe2\x80\x9cno evidence that court employees are inclined to ignore or subvert the Fourth Amendment or that lawlessness among these actors requires application of the extreme sanction of exclusion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 14\xe2\x80\x9315. Third, we recognized that there was \xe2\x80\x9cno basis for believing that application of the exclusionary rule\xe2\x80\xa6 [would] have a significant effect on court employees responsible for informing the police that a warrant has been quashed. Because court clerks are not adjuncts to the law enforcement team engaged in the often competitive enterprise of ferreting out crime, they have no stake in the outcome of particular criminal prosecutions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 15 (citation omitted). Taken together, these reasons explain why police recordkeeping errors should be treated differently than judicial ones.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Other cases applying the \xe2\x80\x9cgood faith\xe2\x80\x9d exception to the exclusionary rule have similarly recognized the distinction between police errors and errors made by others, such as judicial officers or legislatures. See United States v. Leon, 468 U. S. 897 (1984) (police reasonably relied on magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s issuance of warrant); Massachusetts v. Sheppard, 468 U. S. 981 (1984) (same); Illinois v. Krull, 480 U. S. 340 (1987) (police reasonably relied on statute\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Distinguishing between police recordkeeping errors and judicial ones not only is consistent with our precedent, but also is far easier for courts to administer than The Chief Justice\xe2\x80\x99s case-by-case, multifactored inquiry into the degree of police culpability. I therefore would apply the exclusionary rule when police personnel are responsible for a recordkeeping error that results in a Fourth Amendment violation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The need for a clear line, and the recognition of such a line in our precedent, are further reasons in support of the outcome that Justice Ginsburg\xe2\x80\x99s dissent would reach.
