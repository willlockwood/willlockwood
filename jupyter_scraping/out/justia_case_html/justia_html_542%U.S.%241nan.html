\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

INTEL CORP. v. ADVANCED MICRO DEVICES, INC.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x93572. Argued April 20, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 21, 2004

\n

In 1964, pursuant to a recommendation by the Commission on International Rules of Judicial Procedure (Rules Commission), and as part of an endeavor to improve judicial assistance between the United States and foreign countries, Congress completely revised 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71782(a). In its current form, \xc2\xa71782(a) provides that a federal district court \xe2\x80\x9cmay order\xe2\x80\x9d a person residing or found in the district to give testimony or produce documents \xe2\x80\x9cfor use in a proceeding in a foreign or international tribunal \xe2\x80\xa6 upon the application of any interested person.\xe2\x80\x9d The 1964 overhaul of \xc2\xa71782(a) deleted the prior law\xe2\x80\x99s words, \xe2\x80\x9cin any judicial proceeding pending in any court in a foreign country.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Advanced Micro Devices, Inc. (AMD), filed an antitrust complaint against petitioner Intel Corporation (Intel) with the Directorate-General for Competition (DG-Competition) of the Commission of the European Communities (Commission), alleging that Intel had violated European competition law. After the DG-Competition declined AMD\xe2\x80\x99s recommendation to seek documents Intel had produced in a private antitrust suit in an Alabama federal court, AMD petitioned the District Court for the Northern District of California under \xc2\xa71782(a) for an order directing Intel to produce those documents. The District Court concluded that \xc2\xa71782(a) did not authorize such discovery. The Ninth Circuit reversed and remanded with instructions to rule on the application\xe2\x80\x99s merits. The appeals court observed that \xc2\xa71782(a) includes matters before bodies of a quasi-judicial or administrative nature, and, since 1964, has contained no limitation to foreign proceedings that are \xe2\x80\x9cpending.\xe2\x80\x9d A proceeding judicial in character, the Ninth Circuit noted, was a likely sequel to the Commission investigation. The Court of Appeals rejected Intel\xe2\x80\x99s argument that \xc2\xa71782(a) called for a threshold showing that the documents AMD sought, if located in the European Union, would have been discoverable in the Commission investigation. Nothing in \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s language or legislative history, the Ninth Circuit said, required a \xe2\x80\x9cforeign-discoverability\xe2\x80\x9d rule of that order.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section 1782(a) authorizes, but does not require, the District Court to provide discovery aid to AMD. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0To provide context, the Court summarizes how the Commission, acting through the DG-Competition, enforces European competition laws. Upon receiving a complaint, or sU. S.onte, the DG-Competition conducts a preliminary investigation into alleged violations of those laws. The DG-Competition may consider information provided by a complainant, and it may seek information from a complaint\xe2\x80\x99s target. The DG-Competition\xe2\x80\x99s investigation results in a formal written decision whether to pursue the complaint. If the DG-Competition decides not to proceed, its decision may be reviewed by the Court of First Instance and, ultimately, the Court of Justice for the European Communities (European Court of Justice). When the DG-Competition pursues a complaint, it typically serves the investigation\xe2\x80\x99s target with a formal \xe2\x80\x9cstatement of objections\xe2\x80\x9d and advises the target of its intention to recommend a decision finding an antitrust violation. The target is entitled to a hearing before an independent officer, who provides a report to the DG-Competition. Once the DG-Competition makes its recommendation, the Commission may dismiss the complaint or issue a decision holding the target liable and imposing penalties. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s final action is subject to review in the Court of First Instance and the European Court of Justice. Lacking formal \xe2\x80\x9clitigant\xe2\x80\x9d status in Commission proceedings, a complainant nonetheless has significant procedural rights. Important here, a complainant may submit relevant information to the DG-Competition and seek judicial review of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s disposition. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Section 1782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s language, confirmed by its context, warrants the conclusion that the provision authorizes, but does not require, a federal district court to provide assistance to a complainant in a Commission proceeding that leads to a dispositive ruling. The Court therefore rejects the categorical limitations Intel would place on the statute\xe2\x80\x99s reach. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0A complainant before the Commission, such as AMD, qualifies as an \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person\xe2\x80\x9d within \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s compass. The Court rejects Intel\xe2\x80\x99s contention that \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person[s]\xe2\x80\x9d does not include complainants, but encompasses only litigants, foreign sovereigns, and a sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s designated agents. To support its reading, Intel highlights \xc2\xa71782\xe2\x80\x99s caption, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ssistance to foreign and international tribunals and to litigants before such tribunals\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added). A statute\xe2\x80\x99s caption, however, cannot undo or limit its text\xe2\x80\x99s plain meaning. Trainmen v. Baltimore & Ohio R. Co., 331 U. S. 519, 529. Section 1782(a) plainly reaches beyond the universe of persons designated \xe2\x80\x9clitigant.\xe2\x80\x9d With significant participation rights in Commission proceedings, the complainant qualifies as an \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person\xe2\x80\x9d within any fair construction of that term. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The assistance AMD seeks meets \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s specification \xe2\x80\x9cfor use in a foreign or international tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d The Commission qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9ctribunal\xe2\x80\x9d when it acts as a first-instance decisionmaker. Both the Court of First Instance and the European Court of Justice are tribunals, but not proof-takers. Their review is limited to the record before the Commission. Hence, AMD could \xe2\x80\x9cuse\xe2\x80\x9d evidence in those reviewing courts only by submitting it to the Commission in the current, investigative stage. In adopting the Rules Commission\xe2\x80\x99s recommended replacement of the term \xe2\x80\x9cany judicial proceeding\xe2\x80\x9d with the words \xe2\x80\x9ca proceeding in a foreign or international tribunal,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress opened the way for judicial assistance in foreign administrative and quasi-judicial proceedings. This Court has no warrant to exclude the Commission, to the extent that it acts as a first-instance decisionmaker, from \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s ambit. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cproceeding\xe2\x80\x9d for which discovery is sought under \xc2\xa71782(a) must be within reasonable contemplation, but need not be \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cimminent.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court rejects Intel\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the Commission investigation launched by AMD\xe2\x80\x99s complaint does not qualify for \xc2\xa71782(a) assistance. Since the 1964 revision, which deleted the prior law\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xe2\x80\x9cpending,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress has not limited judicial assistance under \xc2\xa71782(a) to \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d adjudicative proceedings. This Court presumes that Congress intends its statutory amendments to have real and substantial effect. Stone v. INS, 514 U. S. 386, 397. The 1964 revision\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history corroborates Congress\xe2\x80\x99 recognition that judicial assistance would be available for both foreign proceedings and investigations. A 1996 amendment clarifies that \xc2\xa71782(a) covers \xe2\x80\x9ccriminal investigations conducted before formal accusation.\xe2\x80\x9d Nothing in that amendment, however, suggests that Congress meant to rein in, rather than to confirm, by way of example, the range of discovery \xc2\xa71782(a) authorizes. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Section 1782(a) does not impose a foreign-discoverability requirement. Although \xc2\xa71782(a) expressly shields from discovery matters protected by legally applicable privileges, nothing in \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s text limits a district court\xe2\x80\x99s production-order authority to materials discoverable in the foreign jurisdiction if located there. Nor does the legislative history suggest that Congress intended to impose a blanket foreign-discoverability rule on \xc2\xa71782(a) assistance. The Court rejects two policy concerns raised by Intel in support of a foreign-discoverability limitation on \xc2\xa71782(a) aid\xe2\x80\x94avoiding offense to foreign governments, and maintaining parity between litigants. While comity and parity concerns may be legitimate touchstones for a district court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of discretion in particular cases, they do not warrant construction of \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s text to include a generally applicable foreign-discoverability rule. Moreover, the Court questions whether foreign governments would be offended by a domestic prescription permitting, but not requiring, judicial assistance. A foreign nation may limit discovery within its domain for reasons peculiar to its own legal practices, culture, or traditions; such reasons do not necessarily signal objection to aid from United States federal courts. A foreign tribunal\xe2\x80\x99s reluctance to order production of materials present in the United States similarly may signal no resistance to the receipt of evidence gathered pursuant to \xc2\xa71782(a). When the foreign tribunal would readily accept relevant information discovered in the United States, application of a categorical foreign-discoverability rule would be senseless. Concerns about parity among adversaries in litigation likewise provide no sound basis for a cross-the-board foreign-discoverability rule. When information is sought by an \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person,\xe2\x80\x9d a district court can condition relief upon reciprocal information exchange. Moreover, the foreign tribunal can place conditions on its acceptance of information, thereby maintaining whatever measure of parity it deems appropriate. The Court also rejects Intel\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that a \xc2\xa71782(a) applicant must show that United States law would allow discovery in domestic litigation analogous to the foreign proceeding. Section 1782 is a provision for assistance to tribunals abroad. It does not direct United States courts to engage in comparative analysis to determine whether analogous proceedings exist here. Comparisons of that order can be fraught with danger. For example, the United States has no close analogue to the Commission regime, under which AMD lacks party status and can participate only as a complainant. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0Whether \xc2\xa71782(a) assistance is appropriate in this case is yet unresolved. To guide the District Court on remand, the Court notes factors relevant to that question. First, when the person from whom discovery is sought is a participant in the foreign proceeding, as Intel is here, the need for \xc2\xa71782(a) aid generally is not as apparent as it ordinarily is when evidence is sought from a nonparticipant in the matter arising abroad. A foreign tribunal has jurisdiction over those appearing before it, and can itself order them to produce evidence. In contrast, nonparticipants in foreign proceedings may be outside the foreign tribunal\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional reach; thus, their evidence, available in the United States, may be unobtainable absent \xc2\xa71782(a) aid. Second, a court presented with a \xc2\xa71782(a) request may consider the nature of the foreign tribunal, the character of proceedings underway abroad, and the receptivity of the foreign government, court, or agency to federal-court judicial assistance. Further, the grounds Intel urged for categorical limitations on \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s scope may be relevant in determining whether a discovery order should be granted in a particular case. Specifically, a district court could consider whether the \xc2\xa71782(a) request conceals an attempt to circumvent foreign proof-gathering limits or other policies of a foreign country or the United States. Also, unduly intrusive or burdensome requests may be rejected or trimmed. The Court declines, at this juncture, Intel\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that it exercise its supervisory authority to adopt rules barring \xc2\xa71782(a) discovery here. Any such endeavor should await further experience with \xc2\xa71782(a) applications in the lower courts. Several facets of this case remain largely unexplored. While Intel and its amici are concerned that granting AMD\xe2\x80\x99s application in any part may yield disclosure of confidential information, encourage \xe2\x80\x9cfishing expeditions,\xe2\x80\x9d and undermine the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s program offering prosecutorial leniency for admissions of wrongdoing, no one has suggested that AMD\xe2\x80\x99s complaint to the Commission is pretextual. Nor has it been shown that \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s preservation of legally applicable privileges and the controls on discovery available under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 26(b)(2) and (c) would be ineffective to prevent discovery of Intel\xe2\x80\x99s confidential information. The Court leaves it to the courts below, applying closer scrutiny, to assure an airing adequate to determine what, if any, assistance is appropriate. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

292 F.\xc2\xa03d 664, affirmed.

\n

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns the authority of federal district courts to assist in the production of evidence for use in a foreign or international tribunal. In the matter before us, respondent Advanced Micro Devices, Inc. (AMD) filed an antitrust complaint against petitioner Intel Corporation (Intel) with the Directorate-General for Competition of the Commission of the European Communities (European Commission or Commission). In pursuit of that complaint, AMD applied to the United States District Court for the Northern District of California, invoking 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71782(a), for an order requiring Intel to produce potentially relevant documents. Section 1782(a) provides that a federal district court \xe2\x80\x9cmay order\xe2\x80\x9d a person \xe2\x80\x9cresid[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cfound\xe2\x80\x9d in the district to give testimony or produce documents \xe2\x80\x9cfor use in a proceeding in a foreign or international tribunal \xe2\x80\xa6 upon the application of any interested person.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Concluding that \xc2\xa71782(a) did not authorize the requested discovery, the District Court denied AMD\xe2\x80\x99s application. The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed that determination and remanded the case, instructing the District Court to rule on the merits of AMD\xe2\x80\x99s application. In accord with the Court of Appeals, we hold that the District Court had authority under \xc2\xa71782(a) to entertain AMD\xe2\x80\x99s discovery request. The statute, we rule, does not categorically bar the assistance AMD seeks: (1) A complainant before the European Commission, such as AMD, qualifies as an \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person\xe2\x80\x9d within \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s compass; (2) the Commission is a \xc2\xa71782(a) \xe2\x80\x9ctribunal\xe2\x80\x9d when it acts as a first-instance decisionmaker; (3) the \xe2\x80\x9cproceeding\xe2\x80\x9d for which discovery is sought under \xc2\xa71782(a) must be in reasonable contemplation, but need not be \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cimminent\xe2\x80\x9d; and (4) \xc2\xa71782(a) contains no threshold requirement that evidence sought from a federal district court would be discoverable under the law governing the foreign proceeding. We caution, however, that \xc2\xa71782(a) authorizes, but does not require, a federal district court to provide judicial assistance to foreign or international tribunals or to \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person[s]\xe2\x80\x9d in proceedings abroad. Whether such assistance is appropriate in this case is a question yet unresolved. To guide the District Court on remand, we suggest considerations relevant to the disposition of that question.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 1782 is the product of congressional efforts, over the span of nearly 150 years, to provide federal-court assistance in gathering evidence for use in foreign tribunals. Congress first provided for federal-court aid to foreign tribunals in 1855; requests for aid took the form of letters rogatory forwarded through diplomatic channels. See Act of Mar. 2, 1855, ch. 140, \xc2\xa72, 10 Stat. 630 (circuit court may appoint \xe2\x80\x9ca United States commissioner designated \xe2\x80\xa6 to make the examination of witnesses\xe2\x80\x9d on receipt of a letter rogatory from a foreign court); Act of Mar. 3, 1863, ch. 95, \xc2\xa71, 12 Stat. 769 (authorizing district courts to respond to letters rogatory by compelling witnesses here to provide testimony for use abroad in \xe2\x80\x9csuit[s] for the recovery of money or property\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 1] In 1948, Congress substantially broadened the scope of assistance federal courts could provide for foreign proceedings. That legislation, codified as \xc2\xa71782, eliminated the prior requirement that the government of a foreign country be a party or have an interest in the proceeding. The measure allowed district courts to designate persons to preside at depositions \xe2\x80\x9cto be used in any civil action pending in any court in a foreign country with which the United States is at peace.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of June 25, 1948, ch. 646, \xc2\xa71782, 62 Stat. 949 (emphasis added). The next year, Congress deleted \xe2\x80\x9ccivil action\xe2\x80\x9d from \xc2\xa71782\xe2\x80\x99s text and inserted \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of May 24, 1949, ch. 139, \xc2\xa793, 63 Stat. 103. See generally, Jones, International Judicial Assistance: Procedural Chaos and a Program for Reform, 62 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 515 (1953).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1958, prompted by the growth of international commerce, Congress created a Commission on International Rules of Judicial Procedure (Rules Commission) to \xe2\x80\x9cinvestigate and study existing practices of judicial assistance and cooperation between the United States and foreign countries with a view to achieving improvements.\xe2\x80\x9d Act of Sept. 2, Pub. L. 85\xe2\x80\x93906, \xc2\xa72, 72 Stat. 1743; S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 2392, 85th Cong., 2d Sess., p.\xc2\xa03 (1958); Smit, International Litigation under the United States Code, 65 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1015\xe2\x80\x931016 (1965) (hereinafter Smit, International Litigation). Six years later, in 1964, Congress unanimously adopted legislation recommended by the Rules Commission;[Footnote 2] the legislation included a complete revision of \xc2\xa71782. See Act of Oct. 3, Pub. L. 88\xe2\x80\x93619, \xc2\xa79, 78 Stat. 997; Smit, International Litigation 1026\xe2\x80\x931035.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As recast in 1964, \xc2\xa71782 provided for assistance in obtaining documentary and other tangible evidence as well as testimony. Notably, Congress deleted the words \xe2\x80\x9cin any judicial proceeding pending in any court in a foreign country,\xe2\x80\x9d and replaced them with the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin a proceeding in a foreign or international tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 6, 4a\xe2\x80\x935a (emphasis added). While the accompanying Senate Report does not account discretely for the deletion of the word \xe2\x80\x9cpending,\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 3] it explains that Congress introduced the word \xe2\x80\x9ctribunal\xe2\x80\x9d to ensure that \xe2\x80\x9cassistance is not confined to proceedings before conventional courts,\xe2\x80\x9d but extends also to \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative and quasi-judicial proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 1580, 88th Cong., 2d Sess., p.\xc2\xa07 (1964); see H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 1052, 88th Cong., 1st Sess., p.\xc2\xa09 (1963) (same). Congress further amended \xc2\xa71782(a) in 1996 to add, after the reference to \xe2\x80\x9cforeign or international tribunal,\xe2\x80\x9d the words \xe2\x80\x9cincluding criminal investigations conducted before formal accusation.\xe2\x80\x9d National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 1996, Pub. L. 104\xe2\x80\x93106, \xc2\xa71342(b), 110 Stat. 486. Section 1782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s current text reads:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe district court of the district in which a person resides or is found may order him to give his testimony or statement or to produce a document or other thing for use in a proceeding in a foreign or international tribunal, including criminal investigations conducted before formal accusation. The order may be made pursuant to a letter rogatory issued, or request made, by a foreign or international tribunal or upon the application of any interested person \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. The order may prescribe the practice and procedure, which may be in whole or part the practice and procedure of the foreign country or the international tribunal, for taking the testimony or statement or producing the document or other thing \xe2\x80\xa6 [or may be] the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cA person may not be compelled to give his testimony or statement or to produce a document or other thing in violation of any legally applicable privilege.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0AMD and Intel are \xe2\x80\x9cworldwide competitors in the microprocessor industry.\xe2\x80\x9d 292 F.\xc2\xa03d 664, 665 (CA9 2002). In October 2000, AMD filed an antitrust complaint with the Directorate-General for Competition (DG-Competition) of the European Commission. Ibid.; App. 41. \xe2\x80\x9cThe European Commission is the executive and administrative organ of the European Communities.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Commission of European Communities as Amicus Curiae 1 (hereinafter European Commission Amicus Curiae). The Commission exercises responsibility over the wide range of subject areas covered by the European Union treaty; those areas include the treaty provisions, and regulations thereunder, governing competition. See ibid.; Consolidated Versions of Treaty on European Union and Treaty Establishing European Community, Arts. 81 and 82, 2002 O. J. (C 325) 33, 64\xe2\x80\x9365, 67 (hereinafter EC Treaty). The DG-Competition, operating under the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s aegis, is the European Union\xe2\x80\x99s primary antitrust law enforcer. European Commission Amicus Curiae 2. Within the DG-Competition\xe2\x80\x99s domain are anticompetitive agreements (Art. 81) and abuse of dominant market position (Art. 82). Ibid.; EC Treaty 64\xe2\x80\x9365.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0AMD\xe2\x80\x99s complaint alleged that Intel, in violation of European competition law, had abused its dominant position in the European market through loyalty rebates, exclusive purchasing agreements with manufacturers and retailers, price discrimination, and standard-setting cartels. App. 40\xe2\x80\x9343; Brief for Petitioner 13. AMD recommended that the DG-Competition seek discovery of documents Intel had produced in a private antitrust suit, titled Intergraph Corp. v. Intel Corp., brought in a Federal District Court in Alabama. 3 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1255 (ND Ala. 1998), vacated 195 F.\xc2\xa03d 1346 (CA Fed. 1999), remanded, 88 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1288 (ND Ala. 2000), aff\xe2\x80\x99d 253 F.\xc2\xa03d 695 (CA Fed. 2001); App. 111; App. to Pet. for Cert. 13a\xe2\x80\x9314a.[Footnote 4] After the DG-Competition declined to seek judicial assistance in the United States, AMD, pursuant to \xc2\xa71782(a), petitioned the District Court for the Northern District of California[Footnote 5] for an order directing Intel to produce documents discovered in the Intergraph litigation and on file in the federal court in Alabama. App. to Pet. for Cert. 13a\xe2\x80\x9314a. AMD asserted that it sought the materials in connection with the complaint it had filed with the European Commission. Ibid.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court denied the application as \xe2\x80\x9c[un]supported by applicable authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 15a. Reversing that determination, the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit remanded the case for disposition on the merits. 292 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 669. The Court of Appeals noted two points significant to its decision: \xc2\xa71782(a) includes matters before \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98bodies of a quasi-judicial or administrative nature,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 667 (quoting In\xc2\xa0re Letters Rogatory from Tokyo District, 539 F.\xc2\xa02d 1216, 1218\xe2\x80\x931219 (CA9 1976)); and, since 1964, the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text has contained \xe2\x80\x9c[no] requirement that the proceeding be \xe2\x80\x98pending,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (quoting United States v. Sealed 1, Letter of Request for Legal Assistance from the Deputy Prosecutor Gen. of Russian Federation, 235 F.\xc2\xa03d 1200, 1204 (CA9 2000)); see supra, at 4. A proceeding judicial in character, the Ninth Circuit further observed, was a likely sequel to the European Commission\xe2\x80\x99s investigation: \xe2\x80\x9c[The European Commission is] a body authorized to enforce the EC Treaty with written, binding decisions, enforceable through fines and penalties. [The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s] decisions are appealable to the Court of First Instance and then to the [European] Court of Justice. Thus, the proceeding for which discovery is sought is, at minimum, one leading to quasi-judicial proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d 292 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 667; see infra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9311 (presenting synopsis of Commission proceedings and judicial review of Commission decisions).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals rejected Intel\xe2\x80\x99s argument that \xc2\xa71782(a) called for a threshold showing that the documents AMD sought in the California federal court would have been discoverable by AMD in the European Commission investigation had those documents been located within the Union. 292 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 668. Acknowledging that other Courts of Appeals had construed \xc2\xa71782(a) to include a \xe2\x80\x9cforeign-discoverability\xe2\x80\x9d rule, the Ninth Circuit found \xe2\x80\x9cnothing in the plain language or legislative history of Section 1782, including its 1964 and 1996 amendments, to require a threshold showing [by] the party seeking discovery that what is sought be discoverable in the foreign proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 669. A foreign-discoverability threshold, the Court of Appeals added, would disserve \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s twin aims of \xe2\x80\x9cproviding efficient assistance to participants in international litigation and encouraging foreign countries by example to provide similar assistance to our courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On remand, a Magistrate Judge found AMD\xe2\x80\x99s application \xe2\x80\x9coverbroad,\xe2\x80\x9d and recommended an order directing AMD to submit a more specific discovery request confined to documents directly relevant to the European Commission investigation. App. to Brief in Opposition 1a\xe2\x80\x936a; Brief for Petitioner 15, n.\xc2\xa09. The District Court has stayed further proceedings pending disposition of the questions presented by Intel\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari. Ibid.; see Order Vacating Hearing Date, No. C 01\xe2\x80\x937033 MISC JW (ND Cal., Nov. 30, 2003) (stating \xe2\x80\x9cIntel may renotice its motion for de novo review of the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision after the Supreme Court issues its ruling\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 540 U. S. 1003 (2003), in view of the division among the Circuits on the question whether \xc2\xa71782(a) contains a foreign-discoverability requirement.[Footnote 7] We now hold that \xc2\xa71782(a) does not impose such a requirement. We also granted review on two other questions. First, does \xc2\xa71782(a) make discovery available to complainants, such as AMD, who do not have the status of private \xe2\x80\x9clitigants\xe2\x80\x9d and are not sovereign agents? See Pet. for Cert. (i). Second, must a \xe2\x80\x9cproceeding\xe2\x80\x9d before a foreign \xe2\x80\x9ctribunal\xe2\x80\x9d be \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d or at least \xe2\x80\x9cimminent\xe2\x80\x9d for an applicant to invoke \xc2\xa71782(a) successfully? Compare In\xc2\xa0re Letter of Request from Crown Prosecution Serv. of United Kingdom, 870 F.\xc2\xa02d 686, 691 (CADC 1989) (proceeding must be \xe2\x80\x9cwithin reasonable contemplation\xe2\x80\x9d), with In\xc2\xa0re Ishihari Chemical Co., 251 F.\xc2\xa03d 120, 125 (CA2 2001) (proceeding must be \xe2\x80\x9cimminent\xe2\x80\x94very likely to occur and very soon to occur\xe2\x80\x9d); In\xc2\xa0re International Judicial Assistance (Letter Rogatory) for Federative Republic of Brazil, 936 F.\xc2\xa02d 702, 706 (CA2 1991) (same). Answering \xe2\x80\x9cyes\xe2\x80\x9d to the first question and \xe2\x80\x9cno\xe2\x80\x9d to the second, we affirm the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To place this case in context, we sketch briefly how the European Commission, acting through the DG-Competition, enforces European competition laws and regulations. The DG-Competition\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9coverriding responsibility\xe2\x80\x9d is to conduct investigations into alleged violations of the European Union\xe2\x80\x99s competition prescriptions. See European Commission Amicus Curiae 6. On receipt of a complaint or sua sponte, the DG-Competition conducts a preliminary investigation. Ibid. In that investigation, the DG-Competition \xe2\x80\x9cmay take into account information provided by a complainant, and it may seek information directly from the target of the complaint.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cUltimately, DG Competition\xe2\x80\x99s preliminary investigation results in a formal written decision whether to pursue the complaint. If [the DG-Competition] declines to proceed, that decision is subject to judicial review\xe2\x80\x9d by the Court of First Instance and, ultimately, by the court of last resort for European Union matters, the Court of Justice for the European Communities (European Court of Justice). Id., at 7; App. 50; see, e.g., case T\xe2\x80\x93241/97, Stork Amsterdam BV v. Commission, 2000 E. C. R. II\xe2\x80\x93309, [2000] 5 C.\xc2\xa0M.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0R. 31 (Ct. 1st Instance 2000) (annulling Commission\xe2\x80\x99s rejection of a complaint).[Footnote 8]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If the DG-Competition decides to pursue the complaint, it typically serves the target of the investigation with a formal \xe2\x80\x9cstatement of objections\xe2\x80\x9d and advises the target of its intention to recommend a decision finding that the target has violated European competition law. European Commission Amicus Curiae 7. The target is entitled to a hearing before an independent officer, who provides a report to the DG-Competition. Ibid.; App. 18\xe2\x80\x9327. Once the DG-Competition has made its recommendation, the European Commission may \xe2\x80\x9cdismis[s] the complaint, or issu[e] a decision finding infringement and imposing penalties.\xe2\x80\x9d European Commission Amicus Curiae 7. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s final action dismissing the complaint or holding the target liable is subject to review in the Court of First Instance and the European Court of Justice. Ibid.; App. 52\xe2\x80\x9353, 89\xe2\x80\x9390.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although lacking formal \xe2\x80\x9cparty\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9clitigant\xe2\x80\x9d status in Commission proceedings, the complainant has significant procedural rights. Most prominently, the complainant may submit to the DG-Competition information in support of its allegations, and may seek judicial review of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s disposition of a complaint. See European Commission Amicus Curiae 7\xe2\x80\x938, and n.\xc2\xa05; Stork Amsterdam, [2000] E.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0R. II, at 328\xe2\x80\x93329, \xc2\xb6 ;\xc2\xb6 ; 51\xe2\x80\x9353.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As \xe2\x80\x9cin all statutory construction cases, we begin [our examination of \xc2\xa71782] with the language of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Barnhart v. Sigmon Coal Co., 534 U. S. 438, 450 (2002). The language of \xc2\xa71782(a), confirmed by its context, our examination satisfies us, warrants this conclusion: The statute authorizes, but does not require, a federal district court to provide assistance to a complainant in a European Commission proceeding that leads to a dispositive ruling, i.e., a final administrative action both responsive to the complaint and reviewable in court.[Footnote 9] Accordingly, we reject the categorical limitations Intel would place on the statute\xe2\x80\x99s reach.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn first to Intel\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the catalog of \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person[s]\xe2\x80\x9d authorized to apply for judicial assistance under \xc2\xa71782(a) includes only \xe2\x80\x9clitigants, foreign sovereigns, and the designated agents of those sovereigns,\xe2\x80\x9d and excludes AMD, a mere complainant before the Commission, accorded only \xe2\x80\x9climited rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 10\xe2\x80\x9311, 24, 26\xe2\x80\x9327. Highlighting \xc2\xa71782\xe2\x80\x99s caption, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ssistance to foreign and international tribunals and to litigants before such tribunals,\xe2\x80\x9d Intel urges that the statutory phrase \xe2\x80\x9cany interested person\xe2\x80\x9d should be read, correspondingly, to reach only \xe2\x80\x9clitigants.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 24 (internal quotation marks omitted, emphasis in original).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The caption of a statute, this Court has cautioned, \xe2\x80\x9ccannot undo or limit that which the [statute\xe2\x80\x99s] text makes plain.\xe2\x80\x9d Trainmen v. Baltimore & Ohio R. Co., 331 U. S. 519, 529 (1947). The text of \xc2\xa71782(a), \xe2\x80\x9cupon the application of any interested person,\xe2\x80\x9d plainly reaches beyond the universe of persons designated \xe2\x80\x9clitigant.\xe2\x80\x9d No doubt litigants are included among, and may be the most common example of, the \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person[s]\xe2\x80\x9d who may invoke \xc2\xa71782; we read \xc2\xa71782\xe2\x80\x99s caption to convey no more. See, e.g., Whitman v. American Trucking Assns., Inc., 531 U. S. 457, 482\xe2\x80\x93483 (2001) (rejecting narrow reading of 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77511(a) based on caption in light of \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically\xe2\x80\x9d broader coverage of provision\xe2\x80\x99s text).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The complainant who triggers a European Commission investigation has a significant role in the process. As earlier observed, see supra, at 11, in addition to prompting an investigation, the complainant has the right to submit information for the DG-Competition\xe2\x80\x99s consideration, and may proceed to court if the Commission discontinues the investigation or dismisses the complaint. App. 52\xe2\x80\x9353. Given these participation rights, a complainant \xe2\x80\x9cpossess[es] a reasonable interest in obtaining [judicial] assistance,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore qualifies as an \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person\xe2\x80\x9d within any fair construction of that term. See Smit, International Litigation 1027 (\xe2\x80\x9cany interested person\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cintended to include not only litigants before foreign or international tribunals, but also foreign and international officials as well as any other person whether he be designated by foreign law or international convention or merely possess a reasonable interest in obtaining the assistance\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 10]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We next consider whether the assistance in obtaining documents here sought by an \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person\xe2\x80\x9d meets the specification \xe2\x80\x9cfor use in a foreign or international tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d Beyond question the reviewing authorities, both the Court of First Instance and the European Court of Justice, qualify as tribunals. But those courts are not proof-taking instances. Their review is limited to the record before the Commission. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 17. Hence, AMD could \xe2\x80\x9cuse\xe2\x80\x9d evidence in the reviewing courts only by submitting it to the Commission in the current, investigative stage.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, when Congress established the Commission on International Rules of Judicial Procedure in 1958, see supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, it instructed the Rules Commission to recommend procedural revisions \xe2\x80\x9cfor the rendering of assistance to foreign courts and quasi-judicial agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72, 72 Stat. 1743 (emphasis added). Section 1782 had previously referred to \xe2\x80\x9cany judicial proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d The Rules Commission\xe2\x80\x99s draft, which Congress adopted, replaced that term with \xe2\x80\x9ca proceeding in a foreign or international tribunal.\xe2\x80\x9d See supra, at 4. Congress understood that change to \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[e] the possibility of U. S. judicial assistance in connection with [administrative and quasi-judicial proceedings abroad].\xe2\x80\x9d S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 1580, at 7\xe2\x80\x938; see Smit, International Litigation 1026\xe2\x80\x931027, and nn. 71, 73 (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]he term \xe2\x80\x98tribunal\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 includes investigating magistrates, administrative and arbitral tribunals, and quasi-judicial agencies, as well as conventional civil, commercial, criminal, and administrative courts\xe2\x80\x9d; in addition to affording assistance in cases before the European Court of Justice, \xc2\xa71782, as revised in 1964, \xe2\x80\x9cpermits the rendition of proper aid in proceedings before the [European] Commission in which the Commission exercises quasi-judicial powers\xe2\x80\x9d). See also European Commission Amicus Curiae 9 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen the Commission acts on DG Competition\xe2\x80\x99s final recommendation \xe2\x80\xa6 the investigative function blur[s] into decisionmaking.\xe2\x80\x9d). We have no warrant to exclude the European Commission, to the extent that it acts as a first-instance decisionmaker, from \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s ambit. See 292 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 667; supra, at 11, n.\xc2\xa09.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Intel also urges that AMD\xe2\x80\x99s complaint has not progressed beyond the investigative stage; therefore, no adjudicative action is currently or even imminently on the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s agenda. Brief for Petitioner 27\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 1782(a) does not limit the provision of judicial assistance to \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d adjudicative proceedings. In 1964, when Congress eliminated the requirement that a proceeding be \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress also deleted the requirement that a proceeding be \xe2\x80\x9cpending.\xe2\x80\x9d See supra, at 4. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen Congress acts to amend a statute, we presume it intends its amendment to have real and substantial effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Stone v. INS, 514 U. S. 386, 397 (1995). The legislative history of the 1964 revision is in sync; it reflects Congress\xe2\x80\x99 recognition that judicial assistance would be available \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the foreign or international proceeding or investigation is of a criminal, civil, administrative, or other nature.\xe2\x80\x9d S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 1580, at 9 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1996, Congress amended \xc2\xa71782(a) to clarify that the statute covers \xe2\x80\x9ccriminal investigations conducted before formal accusation.\xe2\x80\x9d See \xc2\xa71342(b), 110 Stat. 486; supra, at 4. Nothing suggests that this amendment was an endeavor to rein in, rather than to confirm, by way of example, the broad range of discovery authorized in 1964. See S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 1580, at 7 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he [district] court[s] have discretion to grant assistance when proceedings are pending before investigating magistrates in foreign countries.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, we reject the view, expressed in In\xc2\xa0re Ishihara Chemical Co., that \xc2\xa71782 comes into play only when adjudicative proceedings are \xe2\x80\x9cpending\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cimminent.\xe2\x80\x9d See 251 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 125 (proceeding must be \xe2\x80\x9cimminent\xe2\x80\x94very likely to occur and very soon to occur\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Instead, we hold that \xc2\xa71782(a) requires only that a dispositive ruling by the Commission, reviewable by the European courts, be within reasonable contemplation. See Crown Prosecution Serv. of United Kingdom, 870 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 691; In\xc2\xa0re Request for Assistance from Ministry of Legal Affairs of Trinidad and Tobago, 848 F.\xc2\xa02d 1151, 1155, and n.\xc2\xa09 (CA11 1988); Smit, International Litigation 1026 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is not necessary \xe2\x80\xa6 for the [adjudicative] proceeding to be pending at the time the evidence is sought, but only that the evidence is eventually to be used in such a proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We take up next the foreign-discoverability rule on which lower courts have divided: Does \xc2\xa71782(a) categorically bar a district court from ordering production of documents when the foreign tribunal or the \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person\xe2\x80\x9d would not be able to obtain the documents if they were located in the foreign jurisdiction? See supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x939, and n.\xc2\xa07.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We note at the outset, and count it significant, that \xc2\xa71782(a) expressly shields privileged material: \xe2\x80\x9cA person may not be compelled to give his testimony or statement or to produce a document or other thing in violation of any legally applicable privilege.\xe2\x80\x9d See S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 1580, at 9 (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]o person shall be required under the provisions of [\xc2\xa71782] to produce any evidence in violation of an applicable privilege.\xe2\x80\x9d). Beyond shielding material safeguarded by an applicable privilege, however, nothing in the text of \xc2\xa71782 limits a district court\xe2\x80\x99s production-order authority to materials that could be discovered in the foreign jurisdiction if the materials were located there. \xe2\x80\x9cIf Congress had intended to impose such a sweeping restriction on the district court\xe2\x80\x99s discretion, at a time when it was enacting liberalizing amendments to the statute, it would have included statutory language to that effect.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Application of Gianoli Aldunate, 3 F.\xc2\xa03d 54, 59 (CA2 1993); accord Four Pillars Enterprises Co. v. Avery Dennison Corp., 308 F.\xc2\xa03d 1075, 1080 (CA9 2002); 292 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 669 (case below); In\xc2\xa0re Bayer AG, 146 F. 3d 188, 193\xe2\x80\x93194 (CA3 1998).[Footnote 11]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s legislative history suggest that Congress intended to impose a blanket foreign-discoverability rule on the provision of assistance under \xc2\xa71782(a). The Senate Report observes in this regard that \xc2\xa71782(a) \xe2\x80\x9cleaves the issuance of an appropriate order to the discretion of the court which, in proper cases, may refuse to issue an order or may impose conditions it deems desirable.\xe2\x80\x9d S. Rep. No. 1580, at 7.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Intel raises two policy concerns in support of a foreign-discoverability limitation on \xc2\xa71782(a) aid\xe2\x80\x94avoiding offense to foreign governments, and maintaining parity between litigants. Brief for Petitioner 23\xe2\x80\x9324; Reply Brief 5, 13\xe2\x80\x9314; see In\xc2\xa0re Application of Asta Medica, S.\xc2\xa0A., 981 F.\xc2\xa02d 1, 6 (CA1 1992) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress did not seek to place itself on a collision course with foreign tribunals and legislatures, which have carefully chosen the procedures and laws best suited to their concepts of litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d). While comity and parity concerns may be important as touchstones for a district court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of discretion in particular cases, they do not permit our insertion of a generally applicable foreign-discoverability rule into the text of \xc2\xa71782(a).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We question whether foreign governments would in fact be offended by a domestic prescription permitting, but not requiring, judicial assistance. A foreign nation may limit discovery within its domain for reasons peculiar to its own legal practices, culture, or traditions\xe2\x80\x94reasons that do not necessarily signal objection to aid from United States federal courts. See Bayer, 146 F. 3d, at 194 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here is no reason to assume that because a country has not adopted a particular discovery procedure, it would take offense at its use.\xe2\x80\x9d); Smit, Recent Developments in International Litigation, 35 S. Tex. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 215, 235\xe2\x80\x93236 (1994) (hereinafter Smit, Recent Developments) (same).[Footnote 12] A foreign tribunal\xe2\x80\x99s reluctance to order production of materials present in the United States similarly may signal no resistance to the receipt of evidence gathered pursuant to \xc2\xa71782(a). See South Carolina Ins. Co. v. Assurantie Maatschappij \xe2\x80\x9cDe Zeven Provincien\xe2\x80\x9d N. V., [1987] 1 App. Cas. 24 (House of Lords ruled that nondiscoverability under English law did not stand in the way of a litigant in English proceedings seeking assistance in the United States under \xc2\xa71782).[Footnote 13] When the foreign tribunal would readily accept relevant information discovered in the United States, application of a foreign-discoverability rule would be senseless. The rule in that situation would serve only to thwart \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s objective to assist foreign tribunals in obtaining relevant information that the tribunals may find useful but, for reasons having no bearing on international comity, they cannot obtain under their own laws.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Concerns about maintaining parity among adversaries in litigation likewise do not provide a sound basis for a cross-the-board foreign-discoverability rule. When information is sought by an \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person,\xe2\x80\x9d a district court could condition relief upon that person\xe2\x80\x99s reciprocal exchange of information. See Euromepa, S. A. v. R. Esmerian, Inc., 51 F.\xc2\xa03d 1095, 1102 (CA2 1995); Smit, Recent Developments 237. Moreover, the foreign tribunal can place conditions on its acceptance of the information to maintain whatever measure of parity it concludes is appropriate. See Euromepa, 51 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1101.[Footnote 14]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We also reject Intel\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that a \xc2\xa71782(a) applicant must show that United States law would allow discovery in domestic litigation analogous to the foreign proceeding. Brief for Petitioner 19\xe2\x80\x9320 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f AMD were pursuing this matter in the United States, U. S. law would preclude it from obtaining discovery of Intel\xe2\x80\x99s documents.\xe2\x80\x9d). Section 1782 is a provision for assistance to tribunals abroad. It does not direct United States courts to engage in comparative analysis to determine whether analogous proceedings exist here. Comparisons of that order can be fraught with danger.[Footnote 15] For example, we have in the United States no close analogue to the European Commission regime under which AMD is not free to mount its own case in the Court of First Instance or the European Court of Justice, but can participate only as complainant, an \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person,\xe2\x80\x9d in Commission-steered proceedings. See L. Ritter, W. Braun, & F. Rawlinson, European Competition Law: A Practitioner\xe2\x80\x99s Guide 824\xe2\x80\x93826 (2d ed. 2000) (describing a complaint as a potentially \xe2\x80\x9cmore certain (and cheaper) alternative to private enforcement through the [European Union\xe2\x80\x99s member states\xe2\x80\x99] courts\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 16]

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As earlier emphasized, see supra, at 17, a district court is not required to grant a \xc2\xa71782(a) discovery application simply because it has the authority to do so. See United Kingdom v. United States, 238 F.\xc2\xa03d 1312, 1319 (CA11 2001) (\xe2\x80\x9ca district court\xe2\x80\x99s compliance with a \xc2\xa71782 request is not mandatory\xe2\x80\x9d). We note below factors that bear consideration in ruling on a \xc2\xa71782(a) request.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, when the person from whom discovery is sought is a participant in the foreign proceeding (as Intel is here), the need for \xc2\xa71782(a) aid generally is not as apparent as it ordinarily is when evidence is sought from a nonparticipant in the matter arising abroad. A foreign tribunal has jurisdiction over those appearing before it, and can itself order them to produce evidence. App. to Reply Brief 4a (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen th[e] person [who is to produce the evidence] is a party to the foreign proceedings, the foreign or international tribunal can exercise its own jurisdiction to order production of the evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Decl. of H. Smit in In\xc2\xa0re: Application of Ishihara Chemical Co., Ltd., For order to take discovery of Shipley Company, L.L.C., Pursuant to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71782, Misc. 99\xe2\x80\x93232 (FB) (EDNY, May 18, 2000)). In contrast, nonparticipants in the foreign proceeding may be outside the foreign tribunal\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional reach; hence, their evidence, available in the United States, may be unobtainable absent \xc2\xa71782(a) aid. See App. to Reply Brief 4a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, as the 1964 Senate Report suggests, a court presented with a \xc2\xa71782(a) request may take into account the nature of the foreign tribunal, the character of the proceedings underway abroad, and the receptivity of the foreign government or the court or agency abroad to U. S. federal-court judicial assistance. See S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 1580, at 7. Further, the grounds Intel urged for categorical limitations on \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s scope may be relevant in determining whether a discovery order should be granted in a particular case. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 23. Specifically, a district court could consider whether the \xc2\xa71782(a) request conceals an attempt to circumvent foreign proof-gathering restrictions or other policies of a foreign country or the United States. See id., at 27. Also, unduly intrusive or burdensome requests may be rejected or trimmed. See Bayer, 146 F. 3d, at 196 (remanding for district-court consideration of \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate measures, if needed, to protect the confidentiality of materials\xe2\x80\x9d); In\xc2\xa0re Application of Esses, 101 F.\xc2\xa03d 873, 876 (CA2 1996) (affirming limited discovery that is neither \xe2\x80\x9cburdensome [n]or duplicative\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Intel maintains that, if we do not accept the categorical limitations it proposes, then, at least, we should exercise our supervisory authority to adopt rules barring \xc2\xa71782(a) discovery here. Brief for Petitioner 34\xe2\x80\x9336; cf. Thomas v. Arn, 474 U. S. 140, 146\xe2\x80\x93147 (1985) (this Court can establish rules of \xe2\x80\x9csound judicial practice\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). We decline, at this juncture, to adopt supervisory rules. Any such endeavor at least should await further experience with \xc2\xa71782(a) applications in the lower courts.[Footnote 17] The European Commission has stated in amicus curiae briefs to this Court that it does not need or want the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s assistance. See European Commission Amicus Curiae 11\xe2\x80\x9316; Brief for European Commission as Amicus Curiae in Support of Pet. for Cert. 4\xe2\x80\x938. It is not altogether clear, however, whether the Commission, which may itself invoke \xc2\xa71782(a) aid, means to say \xe2\x80\x9cnever\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9chardly ever\xe2\x80\x9d to judicial assistance from United States courts. Nor do we know whether the European Commission\xe2\x80\x99s views on \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s utility are widely shared in the international community by entities with similarly blended adjudicative and prosecutorial functions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Several facets of this case remain largely unexplored. Intel and its amici have expressed concerns that AMD\xe2\x80\x99s application, if granted in any part, may yield disclosure of confidential information, encourage \xe2\x80\x9cfishing expeditions,\xe2\x80\x9d and undermine the European Commission\xe2\x80\x99s Leniency Program. See Brief for Petitioner 37; European Commission Amicus Curiae 11\xe2\x80\x9316.[Footnote 18] Yet no one has suggested that AMD\xe2\x80\x99s complaint to the Commission is pretextual. Nor has it been shown that \xc2\xa71782(a)\xe2\x80\x99s preservation of legally applicable privileges, see supra, at 16, and the controls on discovery available to the District Court, see, e.g., Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 26(b)(2) and (c), would be ineffective to prevent discovery of Intel\xe2\x80\x99s business secrets and other confidential information.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the merits, this case bears closer scrutiny than it has received to date. Having held that \xc2\xa71782(a) authorizes, but does not require, discovery assistance, we leave it to the courts below to assure an airing adequate to determine what, if any, assistance is appropriate.[Footnote 19]

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court reads the scope of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71782 to extend beyond what I believe Congress might reasonably have intended. Some countries allow a private citizen to ask a court to review a criminal prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to prosecute. On the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading, that foreign private citizen could ask an American court to help the citizen obtain information, even if the foreign prosecutor were indifferent or unreceptive. See, e.g., Mann, Criminal Procedure, in Introduction to the Law of Israel 278 (A. Shapira & K. DeWitt-Arar eds. 1995). Many countries allow court review of decisions made by any of a wide variety of nonprosecutorial, nonadjudicative bodies. On the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading, a British developer, hoping to persuade the British Housing Corporation to grant it funding to build a low-income housing development, could ask an American court to demand that an American firm produce information designed to help the developer obtain the British grant. Cf., e.g., Mayer, The Housing Corporation: Multiple Lines of Accountability, in Quangos, Accountability and Reform: The Politics of Quasi-Government 111, 114 (M. Flinders & M. Smith eds. 1999). This case itself suggests that an American firm, hoping to obtain information from a competitor, might file an antitrust complaint with the European antitrust authorities, thereby opening up the possibility of broad American discovery\xe2\x80\x94contrary to the antitrust authorities\xe2\x80\x99 desires.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One might ask why it is wrong to read the statute as permitting the use of America\xe2\x80\x99s court processes to obtain information in such circumstances. One might also ask why American courts should not deal case by case with any problems of the sort mentioned. The answer to both of these questions is that discovery and discovery-related judicial proceedings take time, they are expensive, and cost and delay, or threats of cost and delay, can themselves force parties to settle underlying disputes. See The Brookings Institution, Justice For All: Reducing Costs and Delay in Civil Litigation, Report of a Task Force 6\xe2\x80\x937 (1989) (lawyers surveyed estimated that 60% of litigation costs in a typical federal case are attributable to discovery and agreed that high litigation costs are often attributable to abuse of the discovery process); Federal Judicial Center, T. Willging, J. Shapard, D. Stienstra, & D. Milfich, Discovery and Disclosure Practice, Problems, and Proposals for Change 1\xe2\x80\x932, 4, 8, 14\xe2\x80\x9316 (Tables 3\xe2\x80\x935) (1997) (study outlining costs of discovery). To the extent that expensive, time-consuming battles about discovery proliferate, they deflect the attention of foreign authorities from other matters those authorities consider more important; they can lead to results contrary to those that foreign authorities desire; and they can promote disharmony among national and international authorities, rather than the harmony that \xc2\xa71782 seeks to achieve. They also use up domestic judicial resources and crowd our dockets.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That is why I believe the statute, while granting district courts broad authority to order discovery, nonetheless must be read as subject to some categorical limits, at least at the outer bounds\xe2\x80\x94a matter that today\xe2\x80\x99s decision makes even more important. Those limits should rule out instances in which it is virtually certain that discovery (if considered case by case) would prove unjustified.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case does not require us to find a comprehensive set of limits. But it does suggest two categorical limitations, which I would adopt. First, when a foreign entity possesses few tribunal-like characteristics, so that the applicability of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s word \xe2\x80\x9ctribunal\xe2\x80\x9d is in serious doubt, then a court should pay close attention to the foreign entity\xe2\x80\x99s own view of its \xe2\x80\x9ctribunal\xe2\x80\x9d-like or non-\xe2\x80\x9ctribunal\xe2\x80\x9d-like status. By paying particular attention to the views of the very foreign nations that Congress sought to help, courts would better achieve Congress\xe2\x80\x99 basic cooperative objectives in enacting the statute. See Act of Sept. 2, 1958, Pub. L. 85\xe2\x80\x93906, \xc2\xa72, 72 Stat. 1743 (creating Commission on International Rules of Judicial Procedure to investigate and improve judicial \xe2\x80\x9ccooperation\xe2\x80\x9d between the United States and other countries).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The concept of paying special attention to administrative views is well established in American law. Cf. Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837, 843 (1984); Skidmore v. Swift & Co., 323 U. S. 134, 140 (1944). Like American administrators, foreign administrators are likely to understand better than American courts their own job and, for example, how discovery rights might affect their ability to carry out their responsibilities. I can think of no reason why Congress would have intended a court to pay less attention to the foreign entity\xe2\x80\x99s view of the matter than courts ordinarily pay to a domestic agency\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the workings of its own statute.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, a court should not permit discovery where both of the following are true: (1) A private person seeking discovery would not be entitled to that discovery under foreign law, and (2) the discovery would not be available under domestic law in analogous circumstances. The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, for example, make only limited provisions for nonlitigants to obtain certain discovery. See Fed. Rule Civ. Proc.\xc2\xa027. The limitations contained in the Rules help to avoid discovery battles launched by firms simply seeking information from competitors. Where there is benefit in permitting such discovery, and the benefit outweighs the cost of allowing it, one would expect either domestic law or foreign law to authorize it. If, notwithstanding the fact that it would not be allowed under either domestic or foreign law, there is some special need for the discovery in a particular instance, one would expect to find foreign governmental or intergovernmental authorities making the case for that need. Where none of these circumstances is present, what benefit could offset the obvious costs to the competitor and to our courts? I cannot think of any.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Application of either of these limiting principles would require dismissal of this discovery proceeding. First, the Commission of the European Communities\xe2\x80\x99 (Commission) antitrust authority\xe2\x80\x99s status as a \xe2\x80\x9ctribunal\xe2\x80\x9d is questionable. In many respects, the Commission more closely resembles a prosecuting authority, say, the Department of Justice\xe2\x80\x99s Antitrust Division, than an administrative agency that adjudicates cases, say, the Federal Trade Commission. To my knowledge, those who decide whether to bring an antitrust prosecution on the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s behalf are not judges. See App. 96; Wils, The Combination of the Investigative and Prosecutorial Function and the Adjudicative Function in EC Antitrust Enforcement: A Legal and Economic Analysis, 27 World Competition Law and Economics Review 201, 207 (June 2004) (explaining, in an article written by a member of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s Legal Service, that \xe2\x80\x9cin European Commission proceedings there is no independent initial adjudicator . . . and the Commissioners do not sit as judges hearing directly both sides of the case\xe2\x80\x9d). They do not adjudicate adversary proceedings on the basis of proofs and argument. Id., at 207. Nor, as the majority appears to recognize, does the later availability of a reviewing court matter where \xe2\x80\x9creview is limited to the record before the Commission,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cAMD could \xe2\x80\x98use\xe2\x80\x99 evidence in the reviewing courts only by submitting it to the Commission in the current, investigative stage.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. At a minimum, then, the question whether the Commission is a \xe2\x80\x9ctribunal\xe2\x80\x9d is unclear. See Wils, supra, at 207\xe2\x80\x93209 (noting the scholarly and legal debate as to whether the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s antitrust investigation and enforcement activities qualify it as an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98independent and impartial tribunal\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of the European Convention on Human Rights).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the same time, the Commission has told this Court that it is not a \xe2\x80\x9ctribunal\xe2\x80\x9d under the Act. It has added that, should it be considered, against its will, a \xe2\x80\x9ctribunal,\xe2\x80\x9d its \xe2\x80\x9cability to carry out its governmental responsibilities\xe2\x80\x9d will be seriously threatened. Brief for Commission of the European Communities as Amicus Curiae 2. Given the potential need for the Commission to respond when a private firm (including an American company) files a complaint with the Commission and seeks discovery in an American court (say, from a competitor), its concerns are understandable.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of its own functions is, in my view, entitled to deference. The majority disregards the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s opinion and states categorically that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Commission is a \xc2\xa71782(a) \xe2\x80\x98tribunal\xe2\x80\x99 when it acts as a first-instance decisionmaker.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2. In so ignoring the Commission, the majority undermines the comity interests \xc2\xa71782 was designed to serve and disregards the maxim that we construe statutes so as to \xe2\x80\x9chel[p] the potentially conflicting laws of different nations work together in harmony\xe2\x80\x94a harmony particularly needed in today\xe2\x80\x99s highly interdependent commercial world.\xe2\x80\x9d F.\xc2\xa0Hoffmann-LaRoche Ltd. v. Empagran S.\xc2\xa0A., ante, at __ (slip op., at 8).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second limiting factor is also present. Neither AMD nor any comparable private party would be able to obtain the kind of discovery AMD seeks, either in Europe or in the United States. In respect to Europe, the Commission has told us that any person in the world is free to file a complaint with the Commission, but it is the Commission that then investigates. The private complainant lacks any authority to obtain discovery of business secrets and commercial information. See Brief for Commission of the European Communities as Amicus Curiae 13, and n.\xc2\xa015. In respect to the United States, AMD is a nonlitigant, apart from this discovery proceeding. Conditions under which a nonlitigant may obtain discovery are limited. AMD does not suggest that it meets those conditions, or that it is comparable in any other way to one who might obtain discovery under roughly analogous circumstances. In addition, the material it seeks is under a protective order. See ante, at 6, n. 4.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What is the legal source of these limiting principles? In my view, they, and perhaps others, are implicit in the statute itself, given its purpose and use of the terms \xe2\x80\x9ctribunal\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cinterested person.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71782(a). But even if they are not, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csupervisory powers . . . permit, at the least, the promulgation of procedural rules governing the management of litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d not to mention \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98procedures deemed desirable from the viewpoint of sound judicial practice although in nowise commanded by statute or by the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Thomas v. Arn, 474 U. S. 140, 146\xe2\x80\x93147 (1985) (quoting Cupp v. Naughten, 414 U. S. 141, 146 (1973)). See also Dickerson v. United States, 530 U. S. 428, 437 (2000) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis Court has supervisory authority over the federal courts, and we may use that authority to prescribe rules of evidence and procedure that are binding in those tribunals\xe2\x80\x9d). Intel Corp. has asked us to exercise those powers in this case. Brief for Petitioner 34\xe2\x80\x9338. We should do so along the lines that I suggest; consequently, we should reverse the judgment below and order the complaint in this case dismissed.
