YARBOROUGH, WARDEN v. ALVARADO

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 02\xe2\x80\x931684. Argued March 1, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 1, 2004

\n

Respondent Alvarado helped Paul Soto try to steal a truck, leading to the death of the truck\xe2\x80\x99s owner. Alvarado was called in for an interview with Los Angeles detective Comstock. Alvarado was 17 years old at the time, and his parents brought him to the station and waited in the lobby during the interview. Comstock took Alvarado to a small room where only the two of them were present. The interview lasted about two hours, and Alvarado was not given a warning under Miranda v. Arizona, 334 U. S. 436. Although he at first denied being present at the shooting, Alvarado slowly began to change his story, finally admitting that he had helped Soto try to steal the victim\xe2\x80\x99s truck and to hide the gun after the murder. Comstock twice asked Alvarado if he needed a break and, when the interview was over, returned him to his parents, who drove him home. After California charged Alvarado with murder and attempted robbery, the trial court denied his motion to suppress his interview statements on Miranda grounds. In affirming Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, the District Court of Appeal (hereinafter state court) ruled that a Miranda warning was not required because Alvarado had not been in custody during the interview under the test articulated in Thompson v. Keohane, 516 U. S. 99, 112, which requires a court to consider the circumstances surrounding the interrogation and then determine whether a reasonable person would have felt at liberty to leave. The Federal District Court agreed with the state court on habeas review, but the Ninth Circuit reversed, holding that the state court erred in failing to account for Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s youth and inexperience when evaluating whether a reasonable person in his position would have felt free to leave the interview. Noting that this Court has considered a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s juvenile status in other criminal law contexts, see, e.g., Haley v. Ohio, 332 U. S. 596, 599, the Court of Appeals held that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s error warranted habeas relief under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) because it \xe2\x80\x9cresulted in a decision that \xe2\x80\xa6 involved an unreasonable application of \xe2\x80\xa6 clearly established Federal law, as determined by [this] Court,\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1).

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The state court considered the proper factors and reached a reasonable conclusion that Alvarado was not in custody for Miranda purposes during his police interview. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0AEDPA requires federal courts to consider whether the state-court decision involved an unreasonable application of clearly established law. Clearly established law \xe2\x80\x9crefers to the holdings, as opposed to the dicta, of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions as of the time of the relevant state-court decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362, 412. The Miranda custody test is an objective test. Two discrete inquiries are essential:\xc2\xa0(1) the circumstances surrounding the interrogation, and (2) given those circumstances, whether a reasonable person would have felt free to terminate the interrogation and leave. \xe2\x80\x9cOnce the \xe2\x80\xa6 players\xe2\x80\x99 lines and actions are reconstructed, the court must apply an objective test to resolve the ultimate inquiry: was there a formal arrest or restraint on freedom of movement of the degree associated with a formal arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d Thompson, 516 U. S., at 112. Pp. 7\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The state-court adjudication did not involve an unreasonable application of clearly established law when it concluded that Alvarado was not in custody. The meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d can depend in part on the specificity of the relevant legal rule. If a rule is specific, the range of reasonable judgment may be narrow. Applications of the rule may be plainly correct or incorrect. Other rules are more general, and their meaning must emerge in application over time. The more general the rule, the more leeway courts have in reaching outcomes in case by case determinations. Cf. Wright v. West, 505 U. S. 277, 308\xe2\x80\x93309. Fair-minded jurists could disagree over whether Alvarado was in custody. The custody test is general, and the state court\xe2\x80\x99s application of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s law fits within the matrix of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior decisions. Certain facts weigh against a finding that Alvarado was in custody. The police did not transport him to the station or require him to appear at a particular time, cf. Oregon v. Mathiason, 429 U. S. 492, 495; they did not threaten him or suggest he would be placed under arrest, ibid.; his parents remained in the lobby during the interview, suggesting that the interview would be brief, see Berkemer v. McCarty, 468 U. S. 420, 441\xe2\x80\x93442; Comstock appealed to Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s interest in telling the truth and being helpful to a police officer, cf. Mathiason, 429 U. S., at 495; Comstock twice asked Alvarado if he wanted to take a break; and, at the end of the interview, Alvarado went home, ibid. Other facts point in the opposite direction. Comstock interviewed Alvarado at the police station; the interview lasted 4 times longer than the 30-minute interview in Mathiason; Comstock did not tell Alvarado that he was free to leave; he was brought to the station by his legal guardians rather than arriving on his own accord; and his parents allegedly asked to be present at the interview but were rebuffed. Given these differing indications, the state court\xe2\x80\x99s application of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s custody standard was reasonable. Indeed, a number of the facts echo those in Mathiason, a per curiam summary reversal in which we found it clear that the suspect was not in custody. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The state court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to consider Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s age and inexperience does not provide a proper basis for finding that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was an unreasonable application of clearly established law. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinions applying the Miranda custody test have not mentioned the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s age, much less mandated its consideration. The only indications in those opinions relevant to a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s experience with law enforcement have rejected reliance on such factors. See, e.g., Berkemer, supra, at 442, n.\xc2\xa035, 430\xe2\x80\x93432. It was therefore improper for the Court of Appeals to grant relief on the basis of the state court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to consider them. There is an important conceptual difference between the Miranda test and the line of cases from other contexts considering age and experience. The Miranda custody inquiry is an objective test, see Thompson, supra, at 112, that furthers \xe2\x80\x9cthe clarity of [Miranda\xe2\x80\x99s] rule,\xe2\x80\x9d Berkemer, 468 U. S., at 430, ensuring that the police need not \xe2\x80\x9cgues[s] as to [the circumstances] at issue before deciding how they may interrogate the suspect,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 431. This objective inquiry could reasonably be viewed as different from doctrinal tests that depend on the actual mindset of a particular suspect, where the Court does consider a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s age and experience. In concluding that such factors should also apply to the Miranda custody inquiry, the Ninth Circuit ignored the argument that that inquiry states an objective rule designed to give clear guidance to the police, while consideration of a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s individual characteristics\xe2\x80\x94including his age\xe2\x80\x94could be viewed as creating a subjective inquiry, cf. Mathiason, supra, at 495\xe2\x80\x93496. Reliance on Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s prior history with law enforcement was improper not only under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s deferential standard, but also as a de novo matter. In most cases, the police will not know a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s interrogation history. See Berkemer, supra, at 430\xe2\x80\x93431. Even if they do, the relationship between a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s experiences and the likelihood a reasonable person with that experience would feel free to leave often will be speculative. Officers should not be asked to consider these contingent psychological factors when deciding when suspects should be advised of Miranda rights. See Berkemer, supra, at 431\xe2\x80\x93432. Pp. 12\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

316 F.\xc2\xa03d 841, reversed.

MICHAEL YARBOROUGH, WARDEN, PETITIONER v. MICHAEL ALVARADO

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 1, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA), 110 Stat. 1214, a federal court can grant an application for a writ of habeas corpus on behalf of a person held pursuant to a state-court judgment if the state-court adjudication \xe2\x80\x9cresulted in a decision that was contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by the Supreme Court of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit ruled that a state court unreasonably applied clearly established law when it held that the respondent was not in custody for Miranda purposes. Alvarado v. Hickman, 316 F.\xc2\xa03d 841 (2002). We disagree and\nreverse.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Paul Soto and respondent Michael Alvarado attempted to steal a truck in the parking lot of a shopping mall in Santa\xc2\xa0Fe Springs, California. Soto and Alvarado were part of a larger group of teenagers at the mall that night. Soto decided to steal the truck, and Alvarado agreed to help. Soto pulled out a .357 Magnum and approached the driver, Francisco Castaneda, who was standing near the truck emptying trash into a dumpster. Soto demanded money and the ignition keys from Castaneda. Alvarado, then five months short of his 18th birthday, approached the passenger side door of the truck and crouched down. When Castaneda refused to comply with Soto\xe2\x80\x99s demands, Soto shot Castaneda, killing him. Alvarado then helped hide Soto\xe2\x80\x99s gun.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Los Angeles County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s detective Cheryl Comstock led the investigation into the circumstances of Castaneda\xe2\x80\x99s death. About a month after the shooting, Comstock left word at Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s house and also contacted Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s mother at work with the message that she wished to speak with Alvarado. Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s parents brought him to the Pico Rivera Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Station to be interviewed around lunchtime. They waited in the lobby while Alvarado went with Comstock to be interviewed. Alvarado contends that his parents asked to be present during the interview but were rebuffed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Comstock brought Alvarado to a small interview room and began interviewing him at about 12:30 p.m. The interview lasted about two hours, and was recorded by Comstock with Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s knowledge. Only Comstock and Alvarado were present. Alvarado was not given a warning under Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U. S. 436 (1965). Comstock began the interview by asking Alvarado to recount the events on the night of the shooting. On that night, Alvarado explained, he had been drinking alcohol at a friend\xe2\x80\x99s house with some other friends and acquaintances. After a few hours, part of the group went home and the rest walked to a nearby mall to use its public telephones. In Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s initial telling, that was the end of it. The group went back to the friend\xe2\x80\x99s home and \xe2\x80\x9cjust went to bed.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 101.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unpersuaded, Comstock pressed on:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Okay. We did real good up until this point and everything you\xe2\x80\x99ve said it\xe2\x80\x99s pretty accurate till this point, except for you left out the shooting.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cA.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The shooting?

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Uh huh, the shooting.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cA.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Well I had never seen no shooting.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Well I\xe2\x80\x99m afraid you did.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cA.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I had never seen no shooting.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Well I beg to differ with you. I\xe2\x80\x99ve been told quite the opposite and we have witnesses that are saying quite the opposite.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cA.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That I had seen the shooting?

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9cQ.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0So why don\xe2\x80\x99t you take a deep breath, like I told you before, the very best thing is to be honest\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. You can\xe2\x80\x99t have that many people get involved in a murder and expect that some of them aren\xe2\x80\x99t going to tell the truth, okay? Now granted if it was maybe one person, you might be able to keep your fingers crossed and say, god I hope he doesn\xe2\x80\x99t tell the truth, but the problem is is that they have to tell the truth, okay? Now all I\xe2\x80\x99m simply doing is giving you the opportunity to tell the truth and when we got that many people telling a story and all of a sudden you tell something way far fetched different.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 101\xe2\x80\x93102 (punctuation added).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At this point, Alvarado slowly began to change his story. First he acknowledged being present when the carjacking occurred but claimed that he did not know what happened or who had a gun. When he hesitated to say more, Comstock tried to encourage Alvarado to discuss what happened by appealing to his sense of honesty and the need to bring the man who shot Castaneda to justice. See, e.g., id., at 106 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hat I\xe2\x80\x99m looking for is to see if you\xe2\x80\x99ll tell the truth\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 105\xe2\x80\x93106 (\xe2\x80\x9cI know it\xe2\x80\x99s very difficult when it comes time to \xe2\x80\x98drop the dime\xe2\x80\x99 on somebody[,]\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0[but] if that had been your parent, your mother, or your brother, or your sister, you would darn well want [the killer] to go to jail \xe2\x80\x98cause no one has the right to take someone\xe2\x80\x99s life like that\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). Alvarado then admitted he had helped the other man try to steal the truck by standing near the passenger side door. Next he admitted that the other man was Paul Soto, that he knew Soto was armed, and that he had helped hide the gun after the murder. Alvarado explained that he had expected Soto to scare the driver with the gun, but that he did not expect Soto to kill anyone. Id., at 127. Toward the end of the interview, Comstock twice asked Alvarado if he needed to take a break. Alvarado declined. When the interview was over, Comstock returned with Alvarado to the lobby of the sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s station where his parents were waiting. Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s father drove him home.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A few months later, the State of California charged Soto and Alvarado with first-degree murder and attempted robbery. Citing Miranda, supra, Alvarado moved to suppress his statements from the Comstock interview. The trial court denied the motion on the ground that the interview was noncustodial. App. 196. Alvarado and Soto were tried together, and Alvarado testified in his own defense. He offered an innocent explanation for his conduct, testifying that he happened to be standing in the parking lot of the mall when a gun went off nearby. The government\xe2\x80\x99s cross-examination relied on Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s statement to Comstock. Alvarado admitted having made some of the statements but denied others. When Alvarado denied particular statements, the prosecution countered by playing excerpts from the audio recording of the interview.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0During cross-examination, Alvarado agreed that the interview with Comstock \xe2\x80\x9cwas a pretty friendly conversation,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 438, that there was \xe2\x80\x9csort of a free flow between [Alvarado] and Detective Comstock,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 439, and that Alvarado did not \xe2\x80\x9cfeel coerced or threatened in any way\xe2\x80\x9d during the interview, ibid. The jury convicted Soto and Alvarado of first-degree murder and attempted robbery. The trial judge later reduced Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s conviction to second-degree murder for his comparatively minor role in the offense. The judge sentenced Soto to life in prison and Alvarado to 15-years-to-life.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On direct appeal, the Second Appellate District Court of Appeal (hereinafter state court) affirmed. People v. Soto, 74 Cal. App. 4th 1099, 88 Cal. Rptr. 688 (1999) (unpublished in relevant part). The state court rejected Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s contention that his statements to Comstock should have been excluded at trial because no Miranda warnings were given. The court ruled Alvarado had not been in custody during the interview, so no warning was required. The state court relied upon the custody test articulated in Thompson v. Keohane, 516 U. S. 99, 112 (1995), which requires a court to consider the circumstances surrounding the interrogation and then determine whether a reasonable person would have felt at liberty to leave. The state court reviewed the facts of the Comstock interview and concluded Alvarado was not in custody. App. to Pet. for Cert. C\xe2\x80\x9317. The court emphasized the absence of any intense or aggressive tactics and noted that Comstock had not told Alvarado that he could not leave. The California Supreme Court denied discretionary review.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Alvarado filed a petition for a writ of habeas corpus in the United States District Court for the Central District of California. The District Court agreed with the state court that Alvarado was not in custody for Miranda purposes during the interview. Alvarado v. Hickman, No. ED CV\xe2\x80\x9300\xe2\x80\x93326\xe2\x80\x93VAP(E) (2000), App. to Pet. for Cert. B1\xe2\x80\x93B10. \xe2\x80\x9cAt a minimum,\xe2\x80\x9d the District Court added, the deferential standard of review provided by 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d) foreclosed relief. App. to Pet. for Cert. B\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed. Alvarado v. Hickman, 316 F.\xc2\xa03d 841 (2002). First, the Court of Appeals held that the state court erred in failing to account for Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s youth and inexperience when evaluating whether a reasonable person in his position would have felt free to leave. It noted that this Court has considered a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s juvenile status when evaluating the voluntariness of confessions and the waiver of the privilege against self-incrimination. See id., at 843 (citing, inter alia, Haley v. Ohio, 332 U. S. 596, 599\xe2\x80\x93601 (1948), and In re Gault, 387 U. S. 1, 45 (1967)). The Court of Appeals held that in light of these authorities, Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s age and experience must be a factor in the Miranda custody inquiry. 316 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 843. A minor with no criminal record would be more likely to feel coerced by police tactics and conclude he is under arrest than would an experienced adult, the Court of Appeals reasoned. This required extra \xe2\x80\x9csafeguards \xe2\x80\xa6 commensurate with the age and circumstances of a juvenile defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 850. According to the Court of Appeals, the effect of Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s age and inexperience was so substantial that it turned the interview into a custodial interrogation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals next considered whether Alvarado could obtain relief in light of the deference a federal court must give to a state-court determination on habeas review. The deference required by AEDPA did not bar relief, the Court of Appeals held, because the relevance of juvenile status in Supreme Court case law as a whole compelled the \xe2\x80\x9cextension of the principle that juvenile status is relevant\xe2\x80\x9d to the context of Miranda custody determinations. 316 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 853. In light of the clearly established law considering juvenile status, it was \xe2\x80\x9csimply unreasonable to conclude that a reasonable 17-year-old, with no prior history of arrest or police interviews, would have felt that he was at liberty to terminate the interrogation and leave.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 854\xe2\x80\x93855 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari. 539 U. S. 986 (2003).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We begin by determining the relevant clearly established law. For purposes of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), clearly established law as determined by this Court \xe2\x80\x9crefers to the holdings, as opposed to the dicta, of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions as of the time of the relevant state-court decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Williams v. Taylor, 529 U. S. 362, 412 (2000). We look for \xe2\x80\x9cthe governing legal principle or principles set forth by the Supreme Court at the time the state court renders its decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Lockyer v. Andrade, 538 U. S. 62, 71, 72 (2003).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Miranda itself held that preinterrogation warnings are required in the context of custodial interrogations given \xe2\x80\x9cthe compulsion inherent in custodial surroundings.\xe2\x80\x9d 384 U. S., at 458. The Court explained that \xe2\x80\x9ccustodial interrogation\xe2\x80\x9d meant \xe2\x80\x9cquestioning initiated by law enforcement officers after a person has been taken into custody or otherwise deprived of his freedom of action in any significant way.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 444. The Miranda decision did not provide the Court with an opportunity to apply that test to a set of facts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After Miranda, the Court first applied the custody test in Oregon v. Mathiason, 429 U. S. 492 (1977) (per curiam). In Mathiason, a police officer contacted the suspect after a burglary victim identified him. The officer arranged to meet the suspect at a nearby police station. At the outset of the questioning, the officer stated his belief that the suspect was involved in the burglary but that he was not under arrest. During the 30-minute interview, the suspect admitted his guilt. He was then allowed to leave. The Court held that the questioning was not custodial because there was \xe2\x80\x9cno indication that the questioning took place in a context where [the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s] freedom to depart was restricted in any way.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 495. The Court noted that the suspect had come voluntarily to the police station, that he was informed that he was not under arrest, and that he was allowed to leave at the end of the interview. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In California v. Beheler, 463 U. S. 1121 (1983) (per curiam), the Court reached the same result in a case with facts similar to those in Mathiason. In Beheler, the state court had distinguished Mathiason based on what it described as differences in the totality of the circumstances. The police interviewed Beheler shortly after the crime occurred; Beheler had been drinking earlier in the day; he was emotionally distraught; he was well known to the police; and he was a parolee who knew it was necessary for him to cooperate with the police. 463 U. S., at 1124\xe2\x80\x931125. The Court agreed that \xe2\x80\x9cthe circumstances of each case must certainly influence\xe2\x80\x9d the custody determination, but reemphasized that \xe2\x80\x9cthe ultimate inquiry is simply whether there is a formal arrest or restraint on freedom of movement of the degree associated with a formal arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1125 (internal quotation marks omitted). The Court found the case indistinguishable from Mathiason. It noted that how much the police knew about the suspect and how much time had elapsed after the crime occurred were irrelevant to the custody inquiry. 463 U. S., at 1125.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our more recent cases instruct that custody must be determined based on a how a reasonable person in the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s situation would perceive his circumstances. In Berkemer v. McCarty, 468 U. S. 420 (1984), a police officer stopped a suspected drunk driver and asked him some questions. Although the officer reached the decision to arrest the driver at the beginning of the traffic stop, he did not do so until the driver failed a sobriety test and acknowledged that he had been drinking beer and smoking marijuana. The Court held the traffic stop noncustodial despite the officer\xe2\x80\x99s intent to arrest because he had not communicated that intent to the driver. \xe2\x80\x9cA policeman\xe2\x80\x99s unarticulated plan has no bearing on the question whether a suspect was \xe2\x80\x98in custody\xe2\x80\x99 at a particular time,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court explained. Id., at 442. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he only relevant inquiry is how a reasonable man in the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s position would have understood his situation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In a footnote, the Court cited a New York state case for the view that an objective test was preferable to a subjective test in part because it does not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98place upon the police the burden of anticipating the frailties or idiosyncrasies of every person whom they question.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 442, n. 35 (quoting People v. P., 21 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 2d 1, 9\xe2\x80\x9310, 233 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 255, 260 (1967)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Stansbury v. California, 511 U. S. 318 (1994) (per curiam), confirmed this analytical framework. Stansbury explained that \xe2\x80\x9cthe initial determination of custody depends on the objective circumstances of the interrogation, not on the subjective views harbored by either the interrogating officers or the person being questioned.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 323. Courts must examine \xe2\x80\x9call of the circumstances surrounding the interrogation\xe2\x80\x9d and determine \xe2\x80\x9chow a reasonable person in the position of the individual being questioned would gauge the breadth of his or her freedom of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 322, 325 (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, in Thompson v. Keohane, 516 U. S. 99 (1995), the Court offered the following description of the Miranda custody test:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cTwo discrete inquiries are essential to the determination: first, what were the circumstances surrounding the interrogation; and second, given those circumstances, would a reasonable person have felt he or she was not at liberty to terminate the interrogation and leave. Once the scene is set and the players\xe2\x80\x99 lines and actions are reconstructed, the court must apply an objective test to resolve the ultimate inquiry: was there a formal arrest or restraint on freedom of movement of the degree associated with a formal arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d 516 U. S., at 112 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn now to the case before us and ask if the state-court adjudication of the claim \xe2\x80\x9cinvolved an unreasonable application\xe2\x80\x9d of clearly established law when it concluded that Alvarado was not in custody. 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1). See Williams, 529 U. S., at 413 (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder the \xe2\x80\x98unreasonable application\xe2\x80\x99 clause, a federal habeas court may grant the writ if the state court identifies the correct governing principle from this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions but unreasonably applies that principle to the facts of the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s case\xe2\x80\x9d). The term \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9ca common term in the legal world and, accordingly, federal judges are familiar with its meaning.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 410. At the same time, the range of reasonable judgment can depend in part on the nature of the relevant rule. If a legal rule is specific, the range may be narrow. Applications of the rule may be plainly correct or incorrect. Other rules are more general, and their meaning must emerge in application over the course of time. Applying a general standard to a specific case can demand a substantial element of judgment. As a result, evaluating whether a rule application was unreasonable requires considering the rule\xe2\x80\x99s specificity. The more general the rule, the more leeway courts have in reaching outcomes in case by case determinations. Cf. Wright v. West, 505 U. S. 277, 308\xe2\x80\x93309 (1992) (Kennedy,\xc2\xa0J., concurring in judgment).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Based on these principles, we conclude that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s application of our clearly established law was reasonable. Ignoring the deferential standard of \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) for the moment, it can be said that fair-minded jurists could disagree over whether Alvarado was in custody. On one hand, certain facts weigh against a finding that Alvarado was in custody. The police did not transport Alvarado to the station or require him to appear at a particular time. Cf. Mathiason, 429 U. S., at 495. They did not threaten him or suggest he would be placed under arrest. Ibid. Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s parents remained in the lobby during the interview, suggesting that the interview would be brief. See Berkemer, 468 U. S., at 441\xe2\x80\x93442. In fact, according to trial counsel for Alvarado, he and his parents were told that the interview was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98not going to be long.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. 186. During the interview, Comstock focused on Soto\xe2\x80\x99s crimes rather than Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s. Instead of pressuring Alvarado with the threat of arrest and prosecution, she appealed to his interest in telling the truth and being helpful to a police officer. Cf. Mathiason, 429 U. S., at 495. In addition, Comstock twice asked Alvarado if he wanted to take a break. At the end of the interview, Alvarado went home. Ibid. All of these objective facts are consistent with an interrogation environment in which a reasonable person would have felt free to terminate the interview and leave. Indeed, a number of the facts echo those of Mathiason, a per curiam summary reversal in which we found it \xe2\x80\x9cclear from these facts\xe2\x80\x9d that the suspect was not in custody. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Other facts point in the opposite direction. Comstock interviewed Alvarado at the police station. The interview lasted two hours, four times longer than the 30-minute interview in Mathiason. Unlike the officer in Mathiason, Comstock did not tell Alvarado that he was free to leave. Alvarado was brought to the police station by his legal guardians rather than arriving on his own accord, making the extent of his control over his presence unclear. Counsel for Alvarado alleges that Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s parents asked to be present at the interview but were rebuffed, a fact that\xe2\x80\x94if known to Alvarado\xe2\x80\x94might reasonably have led someone in Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s position to feel more restricted than otherwise. These facts weigh in favor of the view that Alvarado was in custody.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These differing indications lead us to hold that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s application of our custody standard was reasonable. The Court of Appeals was nowhere close to the mark when it concluded otherwise. Although the question of what an \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable application\xe2\x80\x9d of law might be difficult in some cases, it is not difficult here. The custody test is general, and the state court\xe2\x80\x99s application of our law fits within the matrix of our prior decisions. We cannot grant relief under AEDPA by conducting our own independent inquiry into whether the state court was correct as a de novo matter. \xe2\x80\x9c[A] federal habeas court may not issue the writ simply because that court concludes in its independent judgment that the state-court decision applied [the law] incorrectly.\xe2\x80\x9d Woodford v. Visciotti, 537 U. S. 19, 24\xe2\x80\x9325 (2002) (per curiam). Relief is available under \xc2\xa72254(d)(1) only if the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is objectively unreasonable. See Williams, supra, at 410; Andrade, 538 U. S., at 75. Under that standard, relief cannot be granted.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals reached the opposite result by placing considerable reliance on Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s age and inexperience with law enforcement. Our Court has not stated that a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s age or experience is relevant to the Miranda custody analysis, and counsel for Alvarado did not press the importance of either factor on direct appeal or in habeas proceedings. According to the Court of Appeals, however, our Court\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on juvenile status in other contexts demanded consideration of Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s age and inexperience here. The Court of Appeals viewed the state court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to \xe2\x80\x9cextend a clearly established legal principle [of the relevance of juvenile status] to a new context\xe2\x80\x9d as objectively unreasonable in this case, requiring issuance of the writ. 316 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 853 (quoting Anthony v. Cambra, 236 F.\xc2\xa03d 568, 578 (CA9 2000)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The petitioner contends that if a habeas court must extend a rationale before it can apply to the facts at hand then the rationale cannot be clearly established at the time of the state-court decision. Brief for Petitioner 10\xe2\x80\x9324. See also Hawkins v. Alabama, 318 F.\xc2\xa03d 1302, 1306, n.\xc2\xa03 (CA11 2003) (asserting a similar argument). There is force to this argument. Section 2254(d)(1) would be undermined if habeas courts introduced rules not clearly established under the guise of extensions to existing law. Cf. Teague v. Lane, 489 U. S. 288 (1989). At the same time, the difference between applying a rule and extending it is not always clear. Certain principles are fundamental enough that when new factual permutations arise, the necessity to apply the earlier rule will be beyond doubt.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is not such a case, however. Our opinions applying the Miranda custody test have not mentioned the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s age, much less mandated its consideration. The only indications in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinions relevant to a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s experience with law enforcement have rejected reliance on such factors. See Beheler, 463 U. S., at 1125 (rejecting a lower court\xe2\x80\x99s view that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s prior interview with the police was relevant to the custody inquiry); Berkemer, supra, at 442, n.\xc2\xa035 (citing People v. P., 21 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 2d, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, 233 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 260, which noted the difficulties of a subjective test that would require police to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98anticipat[e] the frailties or idiosyncrasies of every person whom they question\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); 468 U. S., at 430\xe2\x80\x93432 (describing a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s criminal past and police record as a circumstance \xe2\x80\x9cunknowable to the police\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is an important conceptual difference between the Miranda custody test and the line of cases from other contexts considering age and experience. The Miranda custody inquiry is an objective test. As we stated in Keohane, \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nce the scene is set and the players\xe2\x80\x99 lines and actions are reconstructed, the court must apply an objective test to resolve the ultimate inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d 516 U. S., at 112 (internal quotation marks omitted). The objective test furthers \xe2\x80\x9cthe clarity of [Miranda\xe2\x80\x99s] rule,\xe2\x80\x9d Berkemer, 468 U. S., at 430, ensuring that the police do not need \xe2\x80\x9cto make guesses as to [the circumstances] at issue before deciding how they may interrogate the suspect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 431. To be sure, the line between permissible objective facts and impermissible subjective experiences can be indistinct in some cases. It is possible to subsume a subjective factor into an objective test by making the latter more specific in its formulation. Thus the Court of Appeals styled its inquiry as an objective test by considering what a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable 17-year-old, with no prior history of arrest or police interviews\xe2\x80\x9d would perceive. 316 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 854\xe2\x80\x93855 (case below).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the same time, the objective Miranda custody inquiry could reasonably be viewed as different from doctrinal tests that depend on the actual mindset of a particular suspect, where we do consider a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s age and experience. For example, the voluntariness of a statement is often said to depend on whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe defendant\xe2\x80\x99s will was overborne,\xe2\x80\x9d Lynumn v. Illinois, 372 U. S. 528, 534 (1963), a question that logically can depend on \xe2\x80\x9cthe characteristics of the accused.\xe2\x80\x9d Schneckloth v. Bustamonte, 412 U. S. 218, 226 (1973). The characteristics of the accused can include the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s age, education, and intelligence, see ibid., as well as a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s prior experience with law enforcement, see Lynumn, supra, at 534. In concluding that there was \xe2\x80\x9cno principled reason\xe2\x80\x9d why such factors should not also apply to the Miranda custody inquiry, 316 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 850, the Court of Appeals ignored the argument that the custody inquiry states an objective rule designed to give clear guidance to the police, while consideration of a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s individual characteristics\xe2\x80\x94including his age\xe2\x80\x94could be viewed as creating a subjective inquiry. Cf. Mathiason, 429 U. S., at 495\xe2\x80\x93496 (noting that facts arguably relevant to whether an environment is coercive may have \xe2\x80\x9cnothing to do with whether respondent was in custody for purposes of the Miranda rule\xe2\x80\x9d). For these reasons, the state court\xe2\x80\x99s failure to consider Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s age does not provide a proper basis for finding that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was an unreasonable application of clearly established law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, reliance on Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s prior history with law enforcement was improper not only under the deferential standard of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1), but also as a de novo matter. In most cases, police officers will not know a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s interrogation history. See Berkemer, supra, at 430\xe2\x80\x93431. Even if they do, the relationship between a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s past experiences and the likelihood a reasonable person with that experience would feel free to leave often will be speculative. True, suspects with prior law enforcement experience may understand police procedures and reasonably feel free to leave unless told otherwise. On the other hand, they may view past as prologue and expect another in a string of arrests. We do not ask police officers to consider these contingent psychological factors when deciding when suspects should be advised of their Miranda rights. See Berkemer, supra, at 431\xe2\x80\x93432. The inquiry turns too much on the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s subjective state of mind and not enough on the \xe2\x80\x9cobjective circumstances of the interrogation.\xe2\x80\x9d Stansbury, 511 U. S., at 323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The state court considered the proper factors and reached a reasonable conclusion. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is

\n

Reversed.

\nBREYER, J., DISSENTING
\nYARBOROUGH V. ALVARADO
\n541 U. S. ____ (2004)
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 02-1684

MICHAEL YARBOROUGH, WARDEN, PETITIONER v. MICHAEL ALVARADO

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 1, 2004]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Stevens, Justice Souter, and Justice Ginsburg join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, Michael Alvarado clearly was \xe2\x80\x9cin custody\xe2\x80\x9d when the police questioned him (without Miranda warnings) about the murder of Francisco Castaneda. To put the question in terms of federal law\xe2\x80\x99s well-established legal standards: Would a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable person\xe2\x80\x9d in Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cposition\xe2\x80\x9d have felt he was \xe2\x80\x9cat liberty to terminate the interrogation and leave\xe2\x80\x9d? Thompson v. Keohane, 516 U. S. 99, 112 (1995); Stansbury v. California 511 U. S. 318, 325 (1994) (per curiam). A court must answer this question in light of \xe2\x80\x9call of the circumstances surrounding the interrogation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 322. And the obvious answer here is \xe2\x80\x9cno.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The law in this case asks judges to apply, not arcane or complex legal directives, but ordinary common sense. Would a reasonable person in Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s position have felt free simply to get up and walk out of the small room in the station house at will during his 2-hour police interrogation? I ask the reader to put himself, or herself, in Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s circumstances and then answer that question: Alvarado hears from his parents that he is needed for police questioning. His parents take him to the station. On arrival, a police officer separates him from his parents. His parents ask to come along, but the officer says they may not. App. 185\xe2\x80\x93186. Another officer says, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98What do we have here; we are going to question a suspect.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 189.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The police take Alvarado to a small interrogation room, away from the station\xe2\x80\x99s public area. A single officer begins to question him, making clear in the process that the police have evidence that he participated in an attempted carjacking connected with a murder. When he says that he never saw any shooting, the officer suggests that he is lying, while adding that she is \xe2\x80\x9cgiving [him] the opportunity to tell the truth\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctak[e] care of [him]self.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 102, 105. Toward the end of the questioning, the officer gives him permission to take a bathroom or water break. After two hours, by which time he has admitted he was involved in the attempted theft, knew about the gun, and helped to hide it, the questioning ends.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What reasonable person in the circumstances\xe2\x80\x94brought to a police station by his parents at police request, put in a small interrogation room, questioned for a solid two hours, and confronted with claims that there is strong evidence that he participated in a serious crime, could have thought to himself, \xe2\x80\x9cWell, anytime I want to leave I can just get up and walk out\xe2\x80\x9d? If the person harbored any doubts, would he still think he might be free to leave once he recalls that the police officer has just refused to let his parents remain with him during questioning? Would he still think that he, rather than the officer, controls the situation?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is only one possible answer to these questions. A reasonable person would not have thought he was free simply to pick up and leave in the middle of the interrogation. I believe the California courts were clearly wrong to hold the contrary, and the Ninth Circuit was right in concluding that those state courts unreasonably applied clearly established federal law. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254(d)(1).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What about the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view that \xe2\x80\x9cfair-minded jurists could disagree over whether Alvarado was in custody\xe2\x80\x9d? Ante, at 10. Consider each of the facts it says \xe2\x80\x9cweigh against a finding\xe2\x80\x9d of custody:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1) \xe2\x80\x9cThe police did not transport Alvarado to the station or require him to appear at a particular time.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. True. His parents brought him to the station at police request. But why does that matter? The relevant question is whether Alvarado came to the station of his own free will or submitted to questioning voluntarily. Cf. Oregon v. Mathiason, 429 U. S. 492, 493\xe2\x80\x93495 (1977) (per curiam); California v. Beheler, 463 U. S. 1121, 1122\xe2\x80\x931123 (1983) (per curiam); Thompson, supra, at 118 (Thomas, J., dissenting). And the involvement of Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s parents suggests involuntary, not voluntary, behavior on Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s part.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2) \xe2\x80\x9cAlvarado\xe2\x80\x99s parents remained in the lobby during the interview, suggesting that the interview would be brief. In fact, [Alvarado] and his parents were told that the interview \xe2\x80\x98was not going to be long.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311 (citation omitted). Whatever was communicated to Alvarado before the questioning began, the fact is that the interview was not brief, nor, after the first half hour or so, would Alvarado have expected it to be brief. And those are the relevant considerations. See Berkemer v. McCarty, 468 U. S. 420, 441 (1984).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(3) \xe2\x80\x9cAt the end of the interview, Alvarado went home.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. As the majority acknowledges, our recent case law makes clear that the relevant question is how a reasonable person would have gauged his freedom to leave during, not after, the interview. See ante, at 9 (citing Stansbury, supra, at 325).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(4) \xe2\x80\x9cDuring the interview, [Officer] Comstock focused on Soto\xe2\x80\x99s crimes rather than Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. In fact, the police officer characterized Soto as the ringleader, while making clear that she knew Alvarado had participated in the attempted carjacking during which Castaneda was killed. See App. 102\xe2\x80\x93103, 109. Her questioning would have reinforced, not diminished, Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s fear that he was not simply a witness, but also suspected of having been involved in a serious crime. See Stansbury, 511 U. S., at 325.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(5) \xe2\x80\x9c[The officer did not] pressur[e] Alvarado with the threat of arrest and prosecution . . . [but instead] appealed to his interest in telling the truth and being helpful to a police officer.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. This factor might be highly significant were the question one of \xe2\x80\x9ccoercion.\xe2\x80\x9d But it is not. The question is whether Alvarado would have felt free to terminate the interrogation and leave. In respect to that question, police politeness, while commendable, does not significantly help the majority.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(6) \xe2\x80\x9cComstock twice asked Alvarado if he wanted to take a break.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This circumstance, emphasizing the officer\xe2\x80\x99s control of Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s movements, makes it less likely, not more likely, that Alvarado would have thought he was free to leave at will.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The facts to which the majority points make clear what the police did not do, for example, come to Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s house, tell him he was under arrest, handcuff him, place him in a locked cell, threaten him, or tell him explicitly that he was not free to leave. But what is important here is what the police did do\xe2\x80\x94namely, have Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s parents bring him to the station, put him with a single officer in a small room, keep his parents out, let him know that he was a suspect, and question him for two hours. These latter facts compel a single conclusion: A reasonable person in Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s circumstances would not have felt free to terminate the interrogation and leave.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What about Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s youth? The fact that Alvarado was 17 helps to show that he was unlikely to have felt free to ignore his parents\xe2\x80\x99 request to come to the station. See Schall v. Martin, 467 U. S. 253, 265 (1984) (juveniles assumed \xe2\x80\x9cto be subject to the control of their parents\xe2\x80\x9d). And a 17-year-old is more likely than, say, a 35-year-old, to take a police officer\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of authority to keep parents outside the room as an assertion of authority to keep their child inside as well.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority suggests that the law might prevent a judge from taking account of the fact that Alvarado was 17. See ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. I can find nothing in the law that supports that conclusion. Our cases do instruct lower courts to apply a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable person\xe2\x80\x9d standard. But the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable person\xe2\x80\x9d standard does not require a court to pretend that Alvarado was a 35-year-old with aging parents whose middle-aged children do what their parents ask only out of respect. Nor does it say that a court should pretend that Alvarado was the statistically determined \xe2\x80\x9caverage person\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a working, married, 35-year-old white female with a high school degree. See U. S. Dept. of Commerce, Bureau of Census, Statistical Abstract of the United States: 2003 (123d ed.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rather, the precise legal definition of \xe2\x80\x9creasonable person\xe2\x80\x9d may, depending on legal context, appropriately account for certain personal characteristics. In negligence suits, for example, the question is what would a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable person\xe2\x80\x9d do \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98under the same or similar circumstances.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d In answering that question, courts enjoy \xe2\x80\x9clatitude\xe2\x80\x9d and may make \xe2\x80\x9callowance not only for external facts, but sometimes for certain characteristics of the actor himself,\xe2\x80\x9d including physical disability, youth, or advanced age. W. Keeton, D. Dobbs, R. Keeton, & D. Owen, Prosser and Keeton on Law of Torts \xc2\xa732, pp. 174\xe2\x80\x93179 (5th ed. 1984); see id., at 179\xe2\x80\x93181; see also Restatement (Third) of Torts \xc2\xa710, Comment b, pp. 128\xe2\x80\x93130 (Tent. Draft No. 1, Mar. 28, 2001) (all American jurisdictions count a person\xe2\x80\x99s childhood as a \xe2\x80\x9crelevant circumstance\xe2\x80\x9d in negligence determinations). This allowance makes sense in light of the tort standard\xe2\x80\x99s recognized purpose: deterrence. Given that purpose, why pretend that a child is an adult or that a blind man can see? See O. Holmes, The Common Law 85\xe2\x80\x9389 (M. Howe ed. 1963).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the present context, that of Miranda\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cin custody\xe2\x80\x9d inquiry, the law has introduced the concept of a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable person\xe2\x80\x9d to avoid judicial inquiry into subjective states of mind, and to focus the inquiry instead upon objective circumstances that are known to both the officer and the suspect and that are likely relevant to the way a person would understand his situation. See Stansbury, supra, at 323\xe2\x80\x93325; Berkemer, 468 U. S., at 442, and n. 35. This focus helps to keep Miranda a workable rule. See Berkemer, supra, at 430\xe2\x80\x93431.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s youth is an objective circumstance that was known to the police. It is not a special quality, but rather a widely shared characteristic that generates commonsense conclusions about behavior and perception. To focus on the circumstance of age in a case like this does not complicate the \xe2\x80\x9cin custody\xe2\x80\x9d inquiry. And to say that courts should ignore widely shared, objective characteristics, like age, on the ground that only a (large) minority of the population possesses them would produce absurd results, the present instance being a case in point. I am not surprised that the majority points to no case suggesting any such limitation. Cf. Alvarado v. Hickman, 316 F.\xc2\xa03d 841, 848, 851, n. 5 (CA9 2002) (case below) (listing 12 cases from 12 different jurisdictions suggesting the contrary).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor am I surprised that the majority makes no real argument at all explaining why any court would believe that the objective fact of a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s age could never be relevant. But see ante, at 1 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cThere may be cases in which a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s age will be relevant to the Miranda \xe2\x80\x98custody\xe2\x80\x99 inquiry\xe2\x80\x9d). The majority does discuss a suspect\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9chistory with law enforcement,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x94a bright red herring in the present context where Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s youth (an objective fact) simply helps to show (with the help of a legal presumption) that his appearance at the police station was not voluntary. See supra, at 5.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As I have said, the law in this case is clear. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases establish that, even if the police do not tell a suspect he is under arrest, do not handcuff him, do not lock him in a cell, and do not threaten him, he may nonetheless reasonably believe he is not free to leave the place of questioning\xe2\x80\x94and thus be in custody for Miranda purposes. See Stansbury, 511 U. S., at 325\xe2\x80\x93326; Berkemer, supra, at 440.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our cases also make clear that to determine how a suspect would have \xe2\x80\x9cgaug[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d his \xe2\x80\x9cfreedom of movement,\xe2\x80\x9d a court must carefully examine \xe2\x80\x9call of the circumstances surrounding the interrogation,\xe2\x80\x9d Stansbury, supra, at 322, 325 (internal quotation marks omitted), including, for example, how long the interrogation lasted (brief and routine or protracted?), see, e.g., Berkemer, supra, at 441; how the suspect came to be questioned (voluntarily or against his will?), see, e.g., Mathiason, 429 U. S., at 495; where the questioning took place (at a police station or in public?), see, e.g., Berkemer, supra, at 438\xe2\x80\x93439; and what the officer communicated to the individual during the interrogation (that he was a suspect? that he was under arrest? that he was free to leave at will?) see, e.g., Stansbury, supra, at 325. In the present case, every one of these factors argues\xe2\x80\x94and argues strongly\xe2\x80\x94that Alvarado was in custody for Miranda purposes when the police questioned him.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Common sense, and an understanding of the law\xe2\x80\x99s basic purpose in this area, are enough to make clear that Alvarado\xe2\x80\x99s age\xe2\x80\x94an objective, widely shared characteristic about which the police plainly knew\xe2\x80\x94is also relevant to the inquiry. Cf. Kaupp v. Texas, 538 U. S. 626, 629\xe2\x80\x93631 (2003) (per curiam). Unless one is prepared to pretend that Alvarado is someone he is not, a middle-aged gentleman, well-versed in police practices, it seems to me clear that the California courts made a serious mistake. I agree with the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s similar conclusions. Consequently, I dissent.
