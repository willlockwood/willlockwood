CHADBOURNE & PARKE LLP v. TROICE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 12\xe2\x80\x9379.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 7, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 26, 2014[1]

\n

The Securities Litigation Uniform Standards Act of 1998 (Litigation Act or Act) forbids the bringing of large securities class actions \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon the statutory or common law of any State\xe2\x80\x9d in which the plaintiffs allege \xe2\x80\x9ca misrepresentation or omission of a material fact in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778bb(f)(1). The Act defines \xe2\x80\x9ccovered security\xe2\x80\x9d to include, as relevant here, only securities traded on a national exchange. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778bb(f)(5)(E), 77r(b)(1).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n \n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Four sets of plaintiffs, respondents here, filed civil class actions under state law, contending that the defendants, petitioners here, helped Allen Stanford and his companies perpetrate a Ponzi scheme by falsely representing that uncovered securities (certificates of deposit in Stanford International Bank) that plaintiffs were purchasing were backed by covered securities. The District Court dismissed each case under the Litigation Act. Although the certificates of deposit were not covered securities, the court concluded, the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentation that its holdings in covered securities made investments in its uncovered securities more secure provided the requisite \xe2\x80\x9cconnection\xe2\x80\x9d (under the Litigation Act) between the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 state-law actions and transactions in covered securities. The Fifth Circuit reversed, concluding that the falsehoods about the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s holdings in covered securities were too tangentially related to the fraud to trigger the Litigation Act.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The Litigation Act does not preclude the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 state-law class actions. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0Several factors support the conclusion that the scope of \xc2\xa778bb(f)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x99s phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmisrepresentation or omission of a material fact in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security\xe2\x80\x9d does not extend further than misrepresentations that are material to the decision by one or more individuals (other than the fraudster) to purchase or sell a covered security. First, this interpretation is consistent with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s basic focus on transactions in covered, not uncovered, securities. Second, the interpretation is supported by the Act\xe2\x80\x99s language. The phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial fact in connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d suggests a connection that matters. And a connection matters where the misrepresentation makes a significant difference to someone\xe2\x80\x99s decision to purchase or to sell a covered security, not an uncovered one, something about which the Act expresses no concern. See Matrixx Initiatives, Inc. v. Siracusano, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. Further, for the connection to matter, the \xe2\x80\x9csomeone\xe2\x80\x9d making the decision to purchase or sell a covered security must be a party other than the fraudster. Third, the securities cases in which this Court has found a fraud to be \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d a purchase or sale of a security, under both the Litigation Act and Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 (which also uses the \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d phrase), have involved victims who took, who tried to take, who divested themselves of, who tried to divest themselves of, or who maintained an ownership interest in financial instruments that fall within the relevant statutory definition. See, e.g., Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith Inc. v. Dabit,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 71\n. Fourth, this Court reads the Litigation Act in light of and consistent with the language and purpose of the underlying regulatory statutes, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and the Securities Act of 1933, which refer to persons engaged in securities transactions that lead to the taking or dissolving of ownership positions, and which make it illegal to deceive a person when he or she is doing so. The basic purpose of the 1934 and 1933 regulatory statutes is to protect investor confidence in the securities markets. Nothing in those statutes, or in the Litigation Act, suggests their object is to protect persons whose connection with the statutorily defined securities is more remote than buying or selling. Fifth, a broader interpretation of the necessary statutory \xe2\x80\x9cconnection\xe2\x80\x9d would interfere with state efforts to provide remedies for victims of ordinary state-law frauds, despite the fact that the Litigation Act purposefully seeks to avoid such results by maintaining States\xe2\x80\x99 legal authority over matters that are primarily of state concern, see, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778bb(f)(4). Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0Respondents and the Government make two important, but unavailing, counterarguments. First, they point to this Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestions that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d should be given a broad interpretation. But every case in which this Court interpreted the phrase to cover a fraud involved a false statement (or the like) that was \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial\xe2\x80\x9d to another individual\xe2\x80\x99s decision to \xe2\x80\x9cpurchase or s[ell]\xe2\x80\x9d a statutorily defined \xe2\x80\x9csecurity\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccovered security,\xe2\x80\x9d e.g., Dabit, supra, at 75\xe2\x80\x9377, and where the transaction was by or on behalf of someone other than the fraudster. Second, the Government warns that a narrow interpretation would curtail the Securities and Exchange Commission\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement powers under \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act, which uses the same \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d phrase. To the contrary, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is perfectly consistent with past SEC practice. The authority of the SEC and the Department of Justice extends to all \xe2\x80\x9csecurities\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa710(b), not just to those traded on national exchanges.\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778c(a)(10). The SEC has accordingly brought successful enforcement actions against Stanford and his associates, based on the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s fraudulent sales of certificates of deposit\xe2\x80\x94products that are \xe2\x80\x9csecurities\xe2\x80\x9d even if not \xe2\x80\x9ccovered securities.\xe2\x80\x9d Neither the Government nor the dissent has pointed to an example of any prior SEC enforcement action that the instant holding would have prevented the SEC from bringing. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (c)\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 complaints do not allege, for Litigation Act purposes, misrepresentations or omissions of material fact \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cpurchase or sale of a covered security.\xe2\x80\x9d At most, they allege misrepresentations about the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s ownership of covered securities. But the Bank is the fraudster, not the fraudster\xe2\x80\x99s victim; nor is it some other person transacting in covered securities. Thus, there is not the necessary \xe2\x80\x9cconnection\xe2\x80\x9d between the materiality of the misstatements and the statutorily required \xe2\x80\x9cpurchase or sale of a covered security.\xe2\x80\x9d In addition, while the District Court found that one plaintiff acquired Bank certificates with proceeds from the sale of covered securities, the plaintiffs did not allege that the sale of these covered securities constituted any part of the fraudulent scheme or that Stanford or his associates were interested in how the plaintiffs obtained the funds to purchase the certificates. Thus, those sales were only incidental to the fraud. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Securities Litigation Uniform Standards Act of 1998 (which we shall refer to as the \xe2\x80\x9cLitigation Act\xe2\x80\x9d) for-bids the bringing of large securities class actions based upon violations of state law. It says that plaintiffs may not maintain a class action \xe2\x80\x9cbased upon the statutory or common law of any State\xe2\x80\x9d in which the plaintiffs allege \xe2\x80\x9ca misrepresentation or omission of a material fact in con-nection with the purchase or sale of a covered security.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778bb(f)(1) (emphasis added). The Act defines \xe2\x80\x9cclass actions\xe2\x80\x9d as those involving more than 50 members. See \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(5). It defines \xe2\x80\x9ccovered security\xe2\x80\x9d narrowly to include only securities traded on a national exchange (or, here irrelevant, those issued by investment companies). \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(5)(E), 77r(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The question before us is whether the Litigation Act encompasses a class action in which the plaintiffs allege (1) that they \xe2\x80\x9cpurchase[d]\xe2\x80\x9d uncovered securities (certificates of deposit that are not traded on any national exchange), but (2) that the defendants falsely told the victims that the uncovered securities were backed by covered securities. We note that the plaintiffs do not allege that the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 misrepresentations led anyone to buy or to sell (or to maintain positions in) covered securities. Under these circumstances, we conclude the Act does not apply.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In light of the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of our holding, post, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312 (opinion of Kennedy, j.)\xe2\x80\x94which we believe is incorrect\xe2\x80\x94we specify at the outset that this holding does not limit the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s authority to prosecute \xe2\x80\x9cfrauds like the one here.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 11. The Federal Government has in fact brought successful prosecutions against the fraudsters at the heart of this litigation, see infra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, and we fail to understand thedissent\xe2\x80\x99s repeated suggestions to the contrary, post, at 3, 4,11, 12, 17. Rather, as we shall explain, we believe the basic consequence of our holding is that, without limiting the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s prosecution power in any sig-nificant way, it will permit victims of this (and similar) frauds to recover damages under state law. See infra, at 15\xe2\x80\x9317. Under the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approach, they would have no such ability.

I

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The relevant statutory framework has four parts:

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (1) Section 10(b) of the underlying regulatory statute, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934. \n\n48Stat.\n891, as amended, \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j (2012 ed.). This well-known statutory provision forbids the \xe2\x80\x9cuse\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cemploy[ment]\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cany manipulative or deceptive device or contrivance\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale of any security.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778j(b).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Securities and Exchange Commission Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 similarly forbids the use of any \xe2\x80\x9cdevice, scheme, or artifice to defraud\xe2\x80\x9d (including the making of \xe2\x80\x9cany untrue statement of a material fact\xe2\x80\x9d or any similar \xe2\x80\x9comi[ssion]\xe2\x80\x9d) \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale of any security.\xe2\x80\x9d 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.10b\xe2\x80\x935 (2013).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For purposes of these provisions, the Securities Exchange Act defines \xe2\x80\x9csecurity\xe2\x80\x9d broadly to include not just things traded on national exchanges, but also \xe2\x80\x9cany note, stock, treasury stock, security future, security-based swap, bond, debenture .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [or] certificate of deposit for a security.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778c(a)(10). See also \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa777b(a)(1), 80a\xe2\x80\x932(a)(36), 80b\xe2\x80\x932(a)(18) (providing virtually identical defini-tions of \xe2\x80\x9csecurity\xe2\x80\x9d for the Securities Act of 1933, the Investment Company Act of 1940, and the InvestmentAdvisers Act of 1940).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (2) A statute-based private right of action. The Court has read \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 as providing injured persons with a private right of action to sue for damages suffered through those provisions\xe2\x80\x99 violation. See, e.g., Blue Chip Stamps v. Manor Drug Stores,\n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 723,\n730 (1975)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The scope of the private right of action is more limited than the scope of the statutes upon which it is based. See Stoneridge Investment Partners, LLC v. Scientific-Atlanta, Inc.,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 148,\n153,\n155,\n166 (2008)\n (private right does not cover suits against \xe2\x80\x9csecondary actors\xe2\x80\x9d who had no \xe2\x80\x9crole in preparing or disseminating\xe2\x80\x9d a stock issuer\xe2\x80\x99s fraudulent \xe2\x80\x9cfinancial statements\xe2\x80\x9d); Central Bank of Denver, N. A. v. First Interstate Bank of Denver, N. A.,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 164\n (1994) (private right does not extend to actions against \xe2\x80\x9caiders and abettors\xe2\x80\x9d of securities fraud); Blue Chip Stamps, supra, at 737 (private right extends only to purchasers and sellers, not to holders, of securities).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (3) The Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995 (PSLRA). \n\n109Stat.\n737,\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa777z\xe2\x80\x931, 78u\xe2\x80\x934. This law imposes procedural and substantive limitations upon the scope of the private right of action available under \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935. It requires plaintiffs to meet heightened pleading standards. It permits defendants to obtain automatic stays of discovery. It limits recoverable damages and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. And it creates a new \xe2\x80\x9csafe harbor\xe2\x80\x9d for forward-looking statements. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778u\xe2\x80\x934, 78u\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (4) The Securities Litigation Uniform Standards Act.\n\n112Stat.\n3227,\n\n15 U. S. C. \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(1)(A). As we said at the outset, this 1998 law forbids any

\n

\xe2\x80\x9ccovered class action based upon the statutory or common law of any State .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by any private partyalleging\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(A) a misrepresentation or omission of a material fact in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security; or

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(B) that the defendant used or employed any manipulative or deceptive device or contrivance in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x93(B).

\n

The law defines \xe2\x80\x9ccovered security\xe2\x80\x9d narrowly. It is a security that \xe2\x80\x9csatisfies the standards for a covered security specified in paragraph (1) or (2) of section 18(b) of the Securities Act of 1933.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(5)(E). And the relevant paragraphs of \xc2\xa718(b) of the 1933 Act define a \xe2\x80\x9ccovered security\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[a security] listed, or authorized for listing, on a national securities exchange,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa777r(b)(1) (or, though not relevant here, as a security issued by an \xe2\x80\x9cinvestment company,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa777r(b)(2)). The Litigation Act also specifies that a \xe2\x80\x9ccovered security\xe2\x80\x9d must be listed or authorized for listing on a national exchange \xe2\x80\x9cat the time during which it is alleged that the misrepresentation, omission, or manipulative or deceptive conduct occurred.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(5)(E).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Litigation Act sets forth exceptions. It does not apply to class actions with fewer than 51 \xe2\x80\x9cpersons or prospective class members.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(5)(B). It does not apply to actions brought on behalf of a State itself. \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(3)(B)(i). It does not apply to class actions based on the law \xe2\x80\x9cof the State in which the issuer is incorporated.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(3)(A)(i). And it reserves the authority of state securities commissions \xe2\x80\x9cto investigate and bring enforcement actions.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(4).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We are here primarily interested in the Litigation Act\xe2\x80\x99s phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmisrepresentation or omission of a material factin connection with the purchase or sale of a covered secu-rity.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(1)(A). Unless this phrase applies to the class actions before us, the plaintiffs may maintain their state-law-based class actions, and they may do so either in federal or state court. Otherwise, their class actions are precluded altogether. See \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(2) (providing for the removal from state to federal court of class actions that meet the specifications of paragraph 1, and for the dismissal of such suits by the district court).

\n

B

\n

1

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The plaintiffs in these actions (respondents here) say that Allen Stanford and several of his companies ran a multibillion dollar Ponzi scheme. Essentially, Stanford and his companies sold the plaintiffs certificates of deposit in Stanford International Bank. Those certificates \xe2\x80\x9cwere debt assets that promised a fixed rate of return.\xe2\x80\x9d Roland v. Green, 675 F. 3d 503, 522 (CA5 2012). The plaintiffs expected that Stanford International Bank would use the money it received to buy highly lucrative assets. But instead, Stanford and his associates used the money provided by new investors to repay old investors, to finance an elaborate lifestyle, and to finance speculative real estate ventures.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Department of Justice brought related criminal charges against Allen Stanford. A jury convicted Stanford of mail fraud, wire fraud, conspiracy to commit money laundering, and obstruction of a Securities and Exchange Commission investigation. Stanford was sentenced to prison and required to forfeit $6 billion. The SEC, noting that the Bank certificates of deposit fell within the 1934 Securities Exchange Act\xe2\x80\x99s broad definition of \xe2\x80\x9csecurity,\xe2\x80\x9d filed a \xc2\xa710(b) civil case against Allen Stanford, the Stanford International Bank, and related Stanford companies and associates. The SEC won the civil action, and the court imposed a civil penalty of $6 billion.

\n

2

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The plaintiffs in each of the four civil class actions are private investors who bought the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s certificates of deposit. Two groups of plaintiffs filed their actions in Louisiana state court against firms and individuals who helped sell the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s certificates by working as \xe2\x80\x9cinvestment advisers\xe2\x80\x9d affiliated with Stanford, or who provided Stanford-related companies with trust, insurance, accounting, or reporting services. (The defendants included a respondent here, SEI Investments Company.) The plaintiffs claimed that the defendants helped the Bank perpetrate the fraud, thereby violating Louisiana state law.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Two other groups of plaintiffs filed their actions in federal court for the Northern District of Texas. One group sued Willis of Colorado (and related Willis companies) and Bowen, Miclette & Britt, two insurance brokers; the other group sued Proskauer Rose and Chadbourne & Parke, two law firms. Both groups claimed that the defendants helped the Bank (and Allen Stanford) perpetrate the fraud or conceal it from regulators, thereby violating Texas securities law.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Louisiana state-court defendants removed their cases to federal court, and the Judicial Panel on Multi-District Litigation moved the Louisiana cases to the Northern District of Texas. A single federal judge heard all four class actions.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The defendants in each of the cases moved to dismiss the complaints. The District Court concluded that the Litigation Act required dismissal. The court recognized that the certificates of deposit themselves were not \xe2\x80\x9ccovered securities\xe2\x80\x9d under the Litigation Act, for they were not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98traded nationally [or] listed on a regulated national exchange.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 12\xe2\x80\x9386, p. 62.But each complaint in one way or another alleged thatthe fraud included misrepresentations that the Bank maintained significant holdings in \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98highly marketable se-curities issued by stable governments [and] strong mul-tinational companies,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and that the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s ownership of these \xe2\x80\x9ccovered\xe2\x80\x9d securities made investments in the uncovered certificates more secure. Id., at 66. The court concluded that this circumstance provided the requisite statutory \xe2\x80\x9cconnection\xe2\x80\x9d between (1) the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 state-law fraud claims, and (2) \xe2\x80\x9ctransactions in covered securities.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 64, 66\xe2\x80\x9367. Hence, the court dismissed the class actions under the Litigation Act. Id., at 75. See also 675 F. 3d,at 511.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n All four sets of plaintiffs appealed. The Fifth Circuit reversed. It agreed with the District Court that the complaints described misrepresentations about the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s investments in nationally traded securities. Still, the \xe2\x80\x9cheart, crux, and gravamen of\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9callegedly fraudulent scheme was representing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the [uncovered] CDs were a \xe2\x80\x98safe and secure\xe2\x80\x99 investment that was preferable to other investments for many reasons.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 522. The court held that the falsehoods about the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s holdings in covered securities were too \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98tangentially related\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to the \xe2\x80\x9ccrux\xe2\x80\x9d of the fraud to trigger the Litigation Act. Id., at 520, 522 (quoting Madden v. Cowen & Co., 576 F. 3d 957, 965\xe2\x80\x93966 (CA9 2009)). \xe2\x80\x9cThat the CDs were marketed with some vague references to [the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s] portfolio containing instruments that might be [covered by the Litigation Act] seems tangential to the schemes,\xe2\x80\x9d to the point where the complaints fall outside the scope of that Act. 675 F.\xc2\xa03d,at 522.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Defendants in the four class actions sought certiorari. We granted their petitions.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The question before us concerns the scope of the Litigation Act\xe2\x80\x99s phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmisrepresentation or omission of a material fact in connection with the purchase or sale ofa covered security.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(1)(A). How broad is that scope? Does it extend further than misrepresentations that are material to the purchase or sale of a covered security?

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In our view, the scope of this language does not extend further. To put the matter more specifically: A fraudulent misrepresentation or omission is not made \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d such a \xe2\x80\x9cpurchase or sale of a covered security\xe2\x80\x9d unless it is material to a decision by one or more individuals (other than the fraudster) to buy or to sell a \xe2\x80\x9ccovered security.\xe2\x80\x9d We add that in Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith Inc. v. Dabit,\n\n547 U. S. 71 (2006)\n, we held that the Litigation Act precluded a suit where the plaintiffs alleged a \xe2\x80\x9cfraudulent manipulation of stock prices\xe2\x80\x9d that was material to and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98concide[d]\xe2\x80\x99 with\xe2\x80\x9d third-party securities transactions, while also inducing the plaintiffs to \xe2\x80\x9chold their stocks long beyond the point when, had the truth been known, they would have sold.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 75, 85, 89 (citing United States v. O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan,\n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 642,\n651 (1997)\n). We do not here modify Dabit.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We reach this interpretation of the Litigation Act for several reasons. First, the Act focuses upon transactions in covered securities, not upon transactions in uncovered securities. An interpretation that insists upon a material connection with a transaction in a covered security is consistent with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s basic focus.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Second, a natural reading of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s language supports our interpretation. The language requires the dismissal of a state-law-based class action where a private party alleges a \xe2\x80\x9cmisrepresentation or omission of a material fact\xe2\x80\x9d (or engages in other forms of deception, not relevant here) \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale of a covered secu-rity.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778bb(f)(1). The phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial fact in connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d suggests a connection that matters. And for present purposes, a connection matters where the misrepresentation makes a significant difference to someone\xe2\x80\x99s decision to purchase or to sell a covered security, not to purchase or to sell an uncovered security, something about which the Act expresses no concern. See generally Matrixx Initiatives, Inc. v. Siracusano, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9312) (a misrepresentation or omission is \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial\xe2\x80\x9d if a reasonable investor would have considered the information significant when contemplating a statutorily relevant investment decision). Further, the \xe2\x80\x9csomeone\xe2\x80\x9d making that decision to purchase or sell must be a party other than the fraudster. If the only party who decides to buy or sell a covered security as a result of a lie is the liar, that is not a \xe2\x80\x9cconnection\xe2\x80\x9d that matters.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Third, prior case law supports our interpretation. As far as we are aware, every securities case in which this Court has found a fraud to be \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d a purchase or sale of a security has involved victims who took, who tried to take, who divested themselves of, who tried to divest themselves of, or who maintained an ownership interest in financial instruments that fall within the relevant statutory definition. See, e.g., Dabit, supra, at 77 (Litigation Act: victims were \xe2\x80\x9cholders\xe2\x80\x9d of covered securities that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s fraud caused to become overvalued); SEC v. Zandford,\n\n535 U. S. 813,\n822 (2002)\n (\xc2\xa710(b): victims were \xe2\x80\x9cduped into believing\xe2\x80\x9d that the defendant would \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98invest\xe2\x80\x99 their assets in the stock market\xe2\x80\x9d); Wharf (Holdings) Ltd. v. United Int\xe2\x80\x99l Holdings, Inc.,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 588,\n592 (2001)\n (\xc2\xa710(b): victim purchased an oral option to buy 10% of a company\xe2\x80\x99s stock); O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, supra, at 655\xe2\x80\x93656 (\xc2\xa710(b): victims were \xe2\x80\x9cmembers of the investing public\xe2\x80\x9d harmed by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cgain[ing of an] advantageous market position\xe2\x80\x9d through insider trading); Superintendent of Ins. of N. Y. v. Bankers Life & Casualty Co.,\n\n404 U. S. 6,\n10 (1971)\n (\xc2\xa710(b): victim was \xe2\x80\x9cinjured as an investor\xe2\x80\x9d when the fraud deprived it of \xe2\x80\x9ccompensation for the sale of its valuable block of securities\xe2\x80\x9d). We have found no Court case involving a fraud \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d the purchase or sale of a statutorily defined security in which the victims did not fit one of these descriptions. And the dissent apparently has not either.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Although the dissent characterizes our approach as \xe2\x80\x9cnew,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 3, and tries to describe several of our prior cases, such as Zanford or Dabit, in a different way, post, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315, it cannot escape the fact that every case it cites involved a victim who took, tried to take, or maintained an ownership position in the statutorily relevant securities through \xe2\x80\x9cpurchases\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9csales\xe2\x80\x9d induced by the fraud. E.g., Zandford, supra, at 815, 820 (fraudster told customershe would \xe2\x80\x9c \xe2\x80\x98conservatively invest\xe2\x80\x99 their money\xe2\x80\x9d in the stock market and made sales of \xe2\x80\x9chis customer\xe2\x80\x99s securities,\xe2\x80\x9dbut pocketed the proceeds (emphasis added)); Dabit, supra, at 76, 85, 89 (the \xe2\x80\x9cmisrepresentations and manipulative tactics caused [the plaintiffs] to hold onto overvalued securities\xe2\x80\x9d while also inducing third parties to trade them); In\xc2\xa0re Orlando Joseph Jett, 82 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa0C. Docket 1211, 1236\xe2\x80\x931237 (2004) (trader\xe2\x80\x99s scheme \xe2\x80\x9cgreatly inflated the reporting trading profits\xe2\x80\x9d that his firm \xe2\x80\x9cused to determine . . . the amount of capital he was permitted to commit on the firm\xe2\x80\x99s behalf\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Fourth, we read the Litigation Act in light of and consistent with the underlying regulatory statutes, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and the Securities Act of 1933. The regulatory statutes refer to persons engaged in securities transactions that lead to the taking or dissolving of ownership positions. And they make it illegal to deceive a person when he or she is doing so. Section 5 of the 1933 Act, for example, makes it unlawful to \xe2\x80\x9coffer to sell or offer to buy .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any security, unless a registration statement has been filed as to such security.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n15 U. S. C. \xc2\xa777e(c). Section 17 of the 1933 Act makes it unlawful \xe2\x80\x9cin the offer or sale of any securities .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to employ any device, scheme, or artifice to defraud, or to obtain money or propertyby means of any untrue statement of a material fact.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa777q(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2). And \xc2\xa710(b) of the 1934 Act makes it unlawful to \xe2\x80\x9cuse or employ, in connection with the purchase or sale of any security .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any manipulative or de-ceptive device or contrivance.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa778j(b).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Not only language but also purpose suggests a statutory focus upon transactions involving the statutorily relevant securities. The basic purpose of the 1934 and 1933 regulatory statutes is \xe2\x80\x9cto insure honest securities markets and thereby promote investor confidence.\xe2\x80\x9d See O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, su-pra, at 658. Nothing in the regulatory statutes suggests their object is to protect persons whose connection with the statutorily defined securities is more remote than words such as \xe2\x80\x9cbuy,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9csell,\xe2\x80\x9d and the like, indicate. Nor does anything in the Litigation Act provide us with reasons for interpreting its similar language more broadly.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The dissent correctly points out that the federal securities laws have another purpose, beyond protecting investors. Namely, they also seek to protect securities issuers, as well as the investment advisers, accountants, and brokers who help them sell financial products, from abusive class-action lawsuits. Post, at 5. Both the PSLRA and the Litigation Act were enacted in service of that goal. By imposing heightened pleading standards, limiting damages, and pre-empting state-law suits where the claims pertained to covered securities, Congress sought to reduce frivolous suits and mitigate legal costs for firms and investment professionals that participate in the market for nationally traded securities.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We fail to see, however, how our decision today undermines that objective. The dissent worries our approach will \xe2\x80\x9csubject many persons and entities whose profession it is to give advice, counsel, and assistance in investing in the securities markets to complex and costly state-law litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 4. To the contrary, the only issuers, investment advisers, or accountants that today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will continue to subject to state-law liability are those who do not sell or participate in selling securities traded on U.\xc2\xa0S. national exchanges. We concede that this means a bank, chartered in Antigua and whose sole product is a fixed-rate debt instrument not traded on a U.\xc2\xa0S. exchange, will not be able to claim the benefit of preclusion under the Litigation Act. But it is difficult to see why the federal securities laws would be\xe2\x80\x94or should be\xe2\x80\x94concerned with shielding such entities from lawsuits.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Fifth, to interpret the necessary statutory \xe2\x80\x9cconnection\xe2\x80\x9d more broadly than we do here would interfere with state efforts to provide remedies for victims of ordinary state-law frauds. A broader interpretation would allow the Litigation Act to cover, and thereby to prohibit, a lawsuit brought by creditors of a small business that falsely represented it was creditworthy, in part because it owns or intends to own exchange-traded stock. It could prohibit a lawsuit brought by homeowners against a mortgage broker for lying about the interest rates on their mortgages\xe2\x80\x94if, say, the broker (not the homeowners) later sold the mortgages to a bank which then securitized them in a pool and sold off pieces as \xe2\x80\x9ccovered securities.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Sixteen Law Professors as Amici Curiae 24.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The dissent all but admits this. Its proposed rule is that whenever \xe2\x80\x9cthe purchase or sale of the securities [including by the fraudster] is what enables the fraud,\xe2\x80\x9d the Litigation Act pre-empts the suit. Post, at 12. In other words, any time one person convinces another to loan him money, by pretending he owns nationally traded securities or will acquire them for himself in the future, the action constitutes federal securities fraud, is subject to federal enforcement, and is also precluded by the Litigation Act if it qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9ccovered class action\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(5)(B) (e.g., involves more than 50 members). Leaving aside whether this would work a significant expansion of the scope of liability under the federal securities laws, it unquestionably would limit the scope of protection under state laws that seek to provide remedies to victims of garden-variety fraud.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The text of the Litigation Act reflects congressional care to avoid such results. Under numerous provisions, it purposefully maintains state legal authority, especially over matters that are primarily of state concern. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x93(B) (limiting preclusion to lawsuits in-volving \xe2\x80\x9ccovered,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., nationally traded, securities); \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(4) (providing that the \xe2\x80\x9csecurities commission .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of any State shall retain jurisdiction under the laws of such State to investigate and bring enforcement actions\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa778bb(f\xc2\xa0)(3)(B) (preserving States\xe2\x80\x99 authority to bring suits of the kind forbidden to private class-action plaintiffs). See also\n\n112Stat.\n3227 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress finds that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. it is appropriate to enact national standards for securities class action lawsuits involving nationally traded securities, while preserving the appropriate enforcement powers of State securities regulators\xe2\x80\x9d). A broad interpretation of the Litigation Act works at cross-purposes with this state-oriented concern. Cf. Zandford, 535 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 820 (warning against \xe2\x80\x9cconstru[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cso broadly as to convert any common-law fraud that happens to involve securities into a violation of \xc2\xa710(b)\xe2\x80\x9d); Wharf (Holdings) Ltd., 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 596 (recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9cordinary state breach-of-contract claims\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9cactions that lie outside the [Securities Exchange] Act\xe2\x80\x99s basic objectives\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Respondents and the Government make two important counterarguments. Respondents point to statements we have made suggesting we should give the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d a broad interpretation. In Dabit, for example, we said that the Court has consistently \xe2\x80\x9cespoused a broad interpretation\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d in the context of \xc2\xa710(b) and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, and we added that the Litigation Act language similarly warranted a \xe2\x80\x9cbroad construction.\xe2\x80\x9d 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 85\xe2\x80\x9386. In Bankers Life, we said that, if a deceptive practice \xe2\x80\x9ctouch[es]\xe2\x80\x9d a securities transaction, it meets \xc2\xa710(b)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d requirement, 404 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 12, and in O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, we said the fraud and the purchase or sale of a security must simply \xe2\x80\x9ccoincide.\xe2\x80\x9d 521 U. S., at 656. The idea, we explained in Zandford, is that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cshould be \xe2\x80\x98construed not technically and restrictively, but flexibly to effectuate its remedial purposes.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 535 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 819 (quoting Affiliated Ute Citizens ofUtah v. United States,\n\n406 U.\xc2\xa0S. 128,\n151 (1972)\n).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Every one of these cases, however, concerned a false statement (or the like) that was \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial\xe2\x80\x9d to another individual\xe2\x80\x99s decision to \xe2\x80\x9cpurchase or s[ell]\xe2\x80\x9d a statutorily defined \xe2\x80\x9csecurity\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccovered security.\xe2\x80\x9d Dabit, supra, at 75\xe2\x80\x9377; Zandford, supra, at 822; Wharf (Holdings) Ltd., supra, at 590\xe2\x80\x93592; O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, supra, at 655\xe2\x80\x93657; Bankers Life, supra, at 10. And the relevant statements or omissions were material to a transaction in the relevant securities by or on behalf of someone other than the fraudster.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Second, the Government points out that \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act also uses the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale of any security.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778j(b). And the Government warns that a narrow interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d here threatens a simi-larly narrow interpretation there, which could limit the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement capabilities. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 28.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We do not understand, however, how our interpretation could significantly curtail the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement powers. As far as the Government has explained the matter, our interpretation seems perfectly consistent with past SEC practice. For one thing, we have cast no doubt on the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s ability to bring enforcement actions against Stanford and Stanford International Bank. The SEC has already done so successfully. As we have repeatedly pointed out, the term \xe2\x80\x9csecurity\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa710(b) covers a wide range of financial products beyond those traded on national exchanges, apparently including the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s certificatesof deposit at issue in these cases. No one here denies that, for \xc2\xa710(b) purposes, the \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial\xe2\x80\x9d misrepresentations by Stanford and his associates were made \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cpurchases\xe2\x80\x9d of those certificates.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n We find it surprising that the dissent worries that our decision will \xe2\x80\x9cnarro[w] and constric[t] essential protection for our national securities market,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 3, and put \xe2\x80\x9cfrauds like the one here .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. not within the reach of fed-eral regulation,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 11. That would be news to Allen Stanford, who was sentenced to 110 years in federal prison after a successful federal prosecution, and to Stanford International Bank, which was ordered to pay billions in federal fines, after the same. Frauds like the one here\xe2\x80\x94including this fraud itself\xe2\x80\x94will continue to be within the reach of federal regulation because the authority of the SEC and Department of Justice extends to all \xe2\x80\x9csecurities,\xe2\x80\x9d not just to those traded on national exchanges.\n\n15 U. S. C. \xc2\xa778c(a)(10); accord, \xc2\xa777b(a)(1), \xc2\xa780a\xe2\x80\x932(a)(36), \xc2\xa780b\xe2\x80\x932(a)(18). When the fraudster peddles an uncovered secu-rity like the CDs here, the Federal Government will have the full scope of its usual powers to act. The only difference between our approach and that of the dissent, is that we also preserve the ability for investors to obtain relief under state laws when the fraud bears so remote a connection to the national securities market that no person actually believed he was taking an ownership position in that market.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Thus, despite the Government\xe2\x80\x99s and the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s hand wringing, neither has been able to point to an example of any prior SEC enforcement action brought during the past 80 years that our holding today would have prevented the SEC from bringing. At oral argument, the Government referred to an administrative proceeding, In\xc2\xa0re Richard Line, 62 S.\xc2\xa0E. C. Docket 2879 (1996), as its best example. Our examination of the report of that case, however, indicates that the defendant was a fraudster to whom the fraud\xe2\x80\x99s victims had loaned money, expecting that he would purchase securities on their behalf. Id., at 2880 (\xe2\x80\x9cLine represented to investors that he would invest their non-admitted assets in various securities, including U.\xc2\xa0S. Treasury notes, mutual fund shares, and collateralized debt obligations\xe2\x80\x9d); ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9c[He] fabricated account statements which falsely recited that securities had beenpurchased on behalf of certain investors\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Government\xe2\x80\x99s brief refers to two other proceedings as demonstrating the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s broad \xc2\xa710(b) enforcement powers. Each, however, involved defrauded investors who had tried to take an ownership interest in the relevant securities. Jett, 82 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa0C. Docket, at 1251 (involving a \xc2\xa710(b) action where a defrauded trading firm\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdecision to purchase or \xe2\x80\x98invest\xe2\x80\x99 in strips or bonds .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. stemmed directly from the activity that constituted the fraud\xe2\x80\x9d); In re D. S. Waddy & Co., 30 S. E. C. 367, 368 (1949) (involving a \xc2\xa710(b) action where a broker \xe2\x80\x9cappropriated to his own use money paid to him by customers for securities purchases\xe2\x80\x9d). We have examined SEC records without finding any further examples.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For these reasons, the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s warning that our de-cision will \xe2\x80\x9cinhibit\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9clitigants from using federal law to police frauds\xe2\x80\x9d and will \xe2\x80\x9cundermine the primacy of federal law in policing abuses in the securities markets\xe2\x80\x9d rings hollow. Post, at 4, 5. The dissent cannot point to one example of a federal securities action\xe2\x80\x94public or private\xe2\x80\x94that would have been permissible in the past but that our approach will disallow in the future. And the irony of the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s position is that federal law would have precluded private recovery in these very suits, because \xc2\xa710(b) does not create a private right of action for investors vis-\xc3\xa0-vis \xe2\x80\x9csecondary actors\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9caiders and abettors\xe2\x80\x9d of securities fraud. Stoneridge Investment Partners, 552 U. S., at 152, 155; Central Bank of Denver, 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 180; accord, Brief for Petitioners in No. 12\xe2\x80\x9386, p. 46 (\xe2\x80\x9cAny federal securities action against Petitioners would clearly run afoul of Central Bank and Stoneridge\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Respon-dents 48 (same); Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 28 (same).

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 complaints specify that their claims rest upon their purchases of uncovered, not of covered, securities. Our search for allegations that might bring their allegations within the scope of the Litigation Act reveals the following:

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (1) The first set of Texas plaintiffs alleged that they bought certificates of deposit from Stanford International Bank because they were told \xe2\x80\x9cthe CDs issued by SIBwere safer even than U.\xc2\xa0S. bank-issued CDs\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccould be redeemed at any time,\xe2\x80\x9d given that the Bank \xe2\x80\x9conly invested the money [i.e., the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s money obtained from its certificate sale proceeds] in safe, secure, and liquid assets.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 433. They claimed Stanford \xe2\x80\x9ctouted the high quality of SIB\xe2\x80\x99s investment portfolio,\xe2\x80\x9d and such falsehoods were material to their decision to purchase the uncovered certificates. Id., at 444.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (2) The second set of Texas plaintiffs contended that they, too, purchased the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s certificates on the belief \xe2\x80\x9cthat their money was being invested in safe, liquid investments.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 715. They alleged that the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s marketing materials stated it devoted \xe2\x80\x9cthe greater part of its assets\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cfirst grade investment bonds (AAA, AA+, AA) and shares of stock (of great reputation, liquidity, and credibility).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 744 (emphasis deleted).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (3) Both groups of Louisiana plaintiffs alleged that they were induced to purchase the certificates based on misrepresentations that the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s assets were \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98invested in a well-diversified portfolio of highly marketable securities issued by stable governments, strong multinational companies and major international banks.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 253, 345. And they claimed the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98liquidity/marketability of SIB\xe2\x80\x99s invested assets\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9cthe most important factor to provide security to SIB clients.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 254.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n These statements do not allege, for Litigation Act purposes, misrepresentations or omissions of material fact \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cpurchase or sale of a covered secu-rity.\xe2\x80\x9d At most, the complaints allege misrepresentations about the Bank\xe2\x80\x99s ownership of covered securities\xe2\x80\x94fraudulent assurances that the Bank owned, would own, or would use the victims\xe2\x80\x99 money to buy for itself shares of covered securities. But the Bank is an entity that made the misrepresentations. The Bank is the fraudster, not the fraudster\xe2\x80\x99s victim. Nor is the Bank some other person transacting (or refraining from transacting, see Dabit, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 75\xe2\x80\x9377) in covered securities. And consequently, there is not the necessary \xe2\x80\x9cconnection\xe2\x80\x9d between the materiality of the misstatements and the statutorily required \xe2\x80\x9cpurchase or sale of a covered security.\xe2\x80\x9d See supra, at 8.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A final point: The District Court found that one of the plaintiffs acquired Bank certificates \xe2\x80\x9cwith the proceeds of selling\xe2\x80\x9d covered securities contained in his IRA portfolio. App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 12\xe2\x80\x9386, p. 70. The plaintiffs, however, did not allege that the sale of these covered securities (which were used to finance the purchase of the certificates) constituted any part of the fraudulent scheme. Nor did the complaints allege that Stanford or his associates were at all interested in how the plaintiffs obtained the funds they needed to purchase the certificates. Thus, we agree with the Court of Appeals that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nlike Bankers Life and Zandford, where the entirety of the fraud depended upon the tortfeasor convincing the victims of those fraudulent schemes to sell their covered securities in order for the fraud to be accomplished, the allegations here are not so tied with the sale of covered securities.\xe2\x80\x9d 675 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 523. In our view, like that of the Court of Appeals, these sales constituted no relevant part of the fraud but were rather incidental to it.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For these reasons the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Kennedy, with whom Justice Alito joins, dissenting.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A number of investors purchased certificates of deposit (CDs) in the Stanford International Bank (SIB). For pur-poses of this litigation all accept the premise that Allen Stanford and SIB induced the investors to purchase the CDs by fraudulent representations. In various state and federal courts the investors filed state-law suits against persons and entities, including attorneys, accountants, brokers, and investment advisers, alleging that they participated in or enabled the fraud. The defendants in the state-court suits removed the actions to federal court, where they were consolidated with the federal-court suits. The defendants contended that the state-law suits are precluded under the terms of the Securities Litigation Uniform Standards Act of 1998 (SLUSA or Act),\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778bb(f)(1). As the investors prevailed in the Court of Appeals, they are the respondents here. The persons and entities who were defendants in the state-law actions are the petitioners. The investors contend the state-law suits are not precluded by SLUSA, and the petitioners contend the suits are precluded.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For purposes of determining SLUSA\xe2\x80\x99s reach, all can agree that the CD purchases would not have been, without more, transactions regulated by that Act; for the CDs were not themselves covered securities. As a result, in determining whether the Act must be invoked, a further circumstance must be considered: The investors purchased the CDs based on the misrepresentations that the CDs were, or would be, backed by investments in, among other assets, covered securities.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n What must be resolved, to determine whether the Act precludes the state-law suits at issue, is whether the misrepresentations regarding covered securities and the ensuing failure to invest in those securities were so related to the purchase of the CDs that the misrepresentations were \xe2\x80\x9cmisrepresentation[s] or omission[s] of a material fact in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778bb(f)(1)(A).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The opinion for the Court, it seems fair to say, adopts this beginning framework, and it is quite correct to do so. The Court is further correct to view this litigation as in-volving a fraud of a type, scale, and perhaps sophistica-tion that has not yet been addressed in its precedents with respect to the applicability of the federal securities laws.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is the premise of this dissent that the more simple frauds addressed in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, where the Court did find fraud \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale,\xe2\x80\x9d are applicable here. In those cases, as here, the immediate cause of loss to the victim of the fraud was not simply a purchase or sale but rather a fraud that depended on the purchase or sale of securities or the promise todo so. It is submitted that this litigation should not come out differently simply because the fraud here was so widespread that many investors were misled by misrepresentations respecting investments, or promised investments, in regulated securities in the markets. And it is necessary to caution that, in holding otherwise, the Court adopts a new approach, an approach which departs from the rules established in the earlier, albeit simpler, cases. And, as a consequence, today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, to a serious degree, narrows and constricts essential protection for our national securities markets, protection vital for their strength and in-tegrity. The result will be a lessened confidence in the market, a force for instability that should otherwise be countered by the proper interpretation of federal securities laws and regulations. Though the reasons supporting the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion are set forth with care and clarity, this respectful dissent submits that established principles do not support its holding.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It must be determined whether the misrepresentations to the investors\xe2\x80\x94misrepresentations that led them to buy CDs in the belief they could rely on the expertise and sophistication of Stanford and SIB in the national securities markets\xe2\x80\x94were \xe2\x80\x9cmisrepresentation[s] or omission[s] of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. material fact[s] in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778bb(f)(1). This is the central provision of SLUSA for purposes of this litigation. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents instruct that this language has broad application and must be construed flexibly in order to encompass new and ever more ingenious fraudulent schemes. Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith Inc. v. Dabit,\n\n547 U. S. 71,\n85 (2006)\n; SEC v. Zandford, 535 U. S. 813, 819 (2002). The Court has held that a material misrepresentation is made \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of a security when the \xe2\x80\x9cfraud coincided with the sales [or purchases] themselves.\xe2\x80\x9d Zandford, supra, at 820.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This significant language must apply here in order to implement two of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 purposes in passing SLUSA. First, SLUSA seeks to preclude a broad range of state-law securities claims in order to protect those who advise, counsel, and otherwise assist investors from abusive and multiplicitous class actions designed to extract settlements from defendants vulnerable to litigation costs. This,in turn, protects the integrity of the markets. Second, even as the Act cuts back on the availability of state-law securities claims, a fair interpretation of its language ensures robust federal regulation of the national securities markets. That is because, in designing SLUSA, Congress \xe2\x80\x9cimported the key phrase\xe2\x80\x9d from \xc2\xa710(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, which provide a private cause of action, as well as SEC enforcement authority, for securities fraud. Dabit, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 79, 85. As a result, that language must be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98presumed to have the same meaning\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in SLUSA as it does in those contexts. Id., at 86.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s narrow interpretation of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s language will inhibit the SEC and litigants from using federal law to police frauds and abuses that undermine confidence in the national securities markets. Throughout the country, then, it will subject many persons and entities whose profession it is to give advice, counsel, and assistance in investing in the securities markets to complex and costly state-law litigation based on allegations of aiding or participating in transactions that are in fact regulated by the federal securities laws.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Congress enacted SLUSA and its predecessor, the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act of 1995, to reform \xe2\x80\x9cperceived abuses of the class-action vehicle in litigation involving nationally traded securities.\xe2\x80\x9d Dabit, 547 U. S., at 81. Congress found that these abuses were being used \xe2\x80\x9cto injure \xe2\x80\x98the entire U.\xc2\xa0S. economy.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Act and its predecessor together addressed these problems by limiting damages, imposing heightened pleading standards, and, as most relevant here, precluding state-law claims involving nationally traded securities.\n\n112Stat.\n3227; see S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 104\xe2\x80\x9398, pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9320 (1995); H. R. Rep. No. 105\xe2\x80\x93640, p. 10 (1998); S. Rep. No. 105\xe2\x80\x93182, pp. 3\xe2\x80\x934 (1998).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In light of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s objectives, the Act must be givena \xe2\x80\x9cbroad construction,\xe2\x80\x9d because a \xe2\x80\x9cnarrow reading of the statute would undercut the effectiveness\xe2\x80\x9d of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 reforms. Dabit, supra, at 86. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision does not heed that principle. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s narrow reading of the statute will permit proliferation of state-law class actions, forcing defendants to defend against multiple suits in various state fora. This state-law litigation will drive up legal costs for market participants and the secondary actors, such as lawyers, accountants, brokers, and advisers, who seek to rely on the stability that results from a national securities market regulated by federal law. See Central Bank of Denver, N. A. v. First Interstate Bank of Denver, N. A.,\n\n511 U. S. 164,\n189 (1994)\n. This is a serious burden to put on attorneys, accountants, brokers, and investment advisers nationwide; and that burden itself will make the national securities markets more costly and difficult to enter. The purpose of the Act is to preclude just these suits. By permitting the very state-law claims Congress intended to prohibit, the Court will undermine the primacy of federal law in policing abuses in the securities markets.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court casts its rule as allowing victims to recover against secondary actors under state law when they would not be able to recover under federal law due to Central Bank. Ante, at 12, 17. But in Dabit a unanimous Court rejected that conception of SLUSA. A federal-law claim was not available to the plaintiffs in Dabit because Blue Chip Stamps v. Manor Drug Stores,\n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 723 (1975)\n, limited the Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 private right of action to purchasers and sellers, not holders. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Second Circuit held that SLUSA only pre-empts state-law class action claims brought by plaintiffs who have a private remedy under federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 74. The Court held the opposite, \xe2\x80\x9cconcluding that SLUSA pre-empts state-law holder class-action claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 87. \xe2\x80\x9cIt would be odd, to say the least,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court reasoned, \xe2\x80\x9cif SLUSA exempted that particularly troublesome subset of class actions from its pre-emptive sweep.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 86. The Court in Dabit also noted that SLUSA preclusion does not leave victims with \xe2\x80\x9cno\xe2\x80\x9d ability to \xe2\x80\x9crecover damages under state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2. Rather, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t simply denies plaintiffs the right to use the class-action device to vindicate certain claims.\xe2\x80\x9d 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 87. The Court in Dabit precluded the suit at issue in order to effect the purpose of Blue Chip. By following the opposite course today, the Court revisits Dabit\xe2\x80\x99s logic and undermines Central Bank.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Congress intended to make \xe2\x80\x9cfederal law, not state law, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the principal vehicle for asserting class-action securities fraud claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Dabit, supra, at 88. And a broad construction of the \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d language found in both SLUSA and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 ensures an efficient and effective federal regulatory regime, one equal to the task of deterring and punishing fraud and providing compensation for victims.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In undertaking regulation of the national markets during the Great Depression, Congress sought to eliminate the \xe2\x80\x9cabuses which were found to have contributed to the stock market crash of 1929 and the depression of the 1930\xe2\x80\x99s.\xe2\x80\x9d SEC v. Capital Gains Research Bureau, Inc.,\n\n375 U. S. 180,\n186 (1963)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98It requires but little appreciation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of what happened in this country during the 1920\xe2\x80\x99s and 1930\xe2\x80\x99s to realize how essential it is that the highest ethical standards prevail\xe2\x80\x99 in every facet of the securities industry.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 186\xe2\x80\x93187 (quoting Silver v. New York Stock Exchange,\n\n373 U. S. 341,\n366 (1963)\n). In the Securities Exchange Act, Congress sought \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to achieve a high standard of business ethics in the securities industry\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9dby \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98substitut[ing] a philosophy of full disclosure for the philosophy of caveat emptor.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Affiliated Ute Citizens of Utah v. United States,\n\n406 U. S. 128,\n151 (1972)\n. To that end, Congress enacted \xc2\xa710(b) \xe2\x80\x9cto insure honest securities markets and thereby promote investor confidence.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan,\n\n521 U. S. 642,\n658 (1997)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Investor confidence indicates fair dealing and integrity in the markets. See Dabit, supra, at 78; O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, supra, at 658; see also Central Bank, supra, at 188. It also is critical to achieving an efficient market. The corollary to the principle that insider trading and other frauds have an \xe2\x80\x9cinhibiting impact on market participation\xe2\x80\x9d is that investor confidence in strong federal regulation to prevent these abuses inspires participation in the market. See O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, supra, at 659. Widespread market participation in turn facilitates efficient allocation of capital to the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s companies. See also Central Bank, supra, at 188.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Mindful of the ends of both SLUSA and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents interpret the key phrase in both laws to mean that a \xe2\x80\x9cmisrepresentation or omission of a material fact\xe2\x80\x9d is made \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of a security when the \xe2\x80\x9cfraud coincided with the sales [or purchases] themselves.\xe2\x80\x9d Zandford, 535 U. S., at 820; see also Dabit, supra, at 85.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n This litigation is very similar to Zandford and satisfies the coincides test it sets forth, and for similar reasons. In Zandford, the SEC brought a civil action against a broker, who, over a period of time, gained control of an investment account, sold its securities, and then pocketed the proceeds. 535 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 815\xe2\x80\x93816. The broker argued that \xe2\x80\x9cthe sales themselves were perfectly lawful and that the sub-equent misappropriation of the proceeds, though fraud-lent, is not properly viewed as having the requisiteconnection with the sales.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 820. The Court rejected that argument. Although the transactions were lawful and separate from the misappropriations, the two were \xe2\x80\x9cnot independent events.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Rather, the fraud \xe2\x80\x9ccoincided with the sales,\xe2\x80\x9d in part because the sales \xe2\x80\x9cfurther[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d the fraud. Ibid.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court likened the broker\xe2\x80\x99s fraud to that in Superintendent of Ins. of N. Y. v. Bankers Life & Casualty Co.,\n\n404 U. S. 6,\n10 (1971)\n, where the fraud victims were misled to believe that they \xe2\x80\x9cwould receive the proceeds of thesale\xe2\x80\x9d of securities. Zandford, 535 U. S., at 821. Like the victims in Bankers Life, the victims in Zandford \xe2\x80\x9cwere injured as investors through [the broker]\xe2\x80\x99s deceptions\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hey were duped into believing that [the broker] would \xe2\x80\x98conservatively invest\xe2\x80\x99 their assets in the stock market and that any transactions made on their behalf would be for their benefit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 822. Both suffered losses because they were victims of dishonest intermediaries or fiduciaries. See also In re Richard J. Line, 62S.E.C. Docket 2879 (1996) (broker who induced parents to transfer funds to him to invest in securities so as to temporarily hide them during the college financial aid application process, but then failed to return the money, violated Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Here, just as in Zandford, the victims parted with their money based on a fraudster\xe2\x80\x99s promise to invest it on their behalf by purchases and sales in the securities markets. The investors had\xe2\x80\x94or were led to believe they could have\xe2\x80\x94the advantages of Stanford\xe2\x80\x99s and SIB\xe2\x80\x99s expertisein investments in the national market. So here, as in Zandford, the success of the fraud turned on the promise to trade in regulated securities. According to the complaints, SIB represented that it would \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98re-inves[t]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 money on their behalf in \xe2\x80\x9ca well-diversified portfolio of highly marketable securities issued by stable national governments, strong multinational companies, and major international banks\xe2\x80\x9d to ensure a \xe2\x80\x9csafe, liquid,\xe2\x80\x9d and above-market return. See App. 244, 249, 250, 253, 336, 342, 345, 444, 470, 480, 628. The misrepresentation was about nationally traded securities and lent credence to SIB\xe2\x80\x99s promise that the CDs were a liquid investment that \xe2\x80\x9ccould be redeemed with just a few days\xe2\x80\x99 notice.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 253, 345, 445, 628. The CDs, SIB explained, would be backed by nationally traded securities. As a result, according to the complaints, the misrepresentation was \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 244\xe2\x80\x93245, 336\xe2\x80\x93338, 480, 715. The fraud could not have succeeded without the misrepresentation: The investors gave SIB money because they expected it to be invested in the national securities markets. The connection between the promised purchases and the misrepresentation is more direct than in Zandford, because the misrepresentation was essential to the fraud.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Here, and again just as in Zandford, the fraud was not complete until the representation about securities transactions became untrue, just as Stanford intended all along. Instead of purchasing covered securities, SIB purchased some but fewer covered securities than it promised\xe2\x80\x94only 10% of its portfolio, according to an affidavit attached to a complaint\xe2\x80\x94and primarily speculated in Caribbean real estate. Brief for Respondents 37; App. 594; but see Tr. of Oral Arg. 43\xe2\x80\x9344 (suggesting SIB did not purchase securities). It was not until SIB rendered the CDs illiquid by failing to make substantial investments in the nationally traded securities it promised that the fraud was consummated. At that point, SIB blocked the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 access to the market. The fraud and SIB\xe2\x80\x99s failure to purchase all that it promised were not independent events. Rather, the false promises to invest in covered securities enabled and furthered the CD fraud. Without the false promise, there would have been no money to purchase the covered securities. On these facts, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s controlling precedents instruct that these misrepresentations were made \xe2\x80\x9cin con-ection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of regulated securi-ties; and, as a result, state-law claims concerning them should be precluded.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Dabit provides further support for this conclusion. There, the Court held that an investment bank that deceived brokers into advising their clients to hold covered securities made misrepresentations \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cUnder our precedents,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cit is enough that the fraud alleged \xe2\x80\x98coincide\xe2\x80\x99 with a securities transaction\xe2\x80\x94whether by the plaintiff or by someone else.\xe2\x80\x9d 547 U. S., at 85. It did not matter that the plaintiffs did not purchase or sell securities, because they were participants in the national markets: \xe2\x80\x9cThe requisite showing, in other words,\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98deception \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale of any security,\xe2\x80\x9d not deception of an identifiable purchaser or seller.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, 521 U. S., at 658). Here, for like reasons, it does not matter that the fraud victims, as opposed to Stanford and SIB, were not the ones to fail to invest in the market. The very essence of the fraud was to induce purchase of the CDs on the (false) promise that investors should rely on SIB\xe2\x80\x99s special skills and expertise in making market investments in covered securities on their behalf. If promises related to covered securities are integral to the fraud in this direct way, federal regula-tion is necessary if confidence in the market is to bemaintained.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n That interest is at stake here. Because confidence in the ability to act as an investor without diversion of funds by intermediaries and insiders is critical, it does not matter if the victim of a fraud does not purchase or sell a security, Dabit, supra, at 85; or if the sale or purchase does not occur at the same time as the deception, Bankers Life, 404 U. S., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313; or if no party to the actual transaction is deceived by the fraud, O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, supra, at 656; or if the misrepresentation has nothing to do with the value of a covered security, Zandford, 535 U. S., at 820. An investor\xe2\x80\x99s confidence in the market, and willingness to participate in it, may be severely undermined if frauds like the one here are not within the reach of federal regulation. Frauds like this one undermine investor confidence in attorneys, accountants, brokers, and investment advisers, the intermediaries on whom investors depend to gain access to the market. And when frauds are as widespread as this one, the market as a whole is weakened because investors, including sophisticated ones, are misled as to the amount of funds committed to the market and its consequent stability and resilience.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The rule that SLUSA applies when a misrepresentation about the market is coincident to the fraud is, then, essential to the framework of the Act and to federal securities regulation. Fraudulent practices \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98constantly vary,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98practices legitimate for some purposes may be turned to illegitimate and fraudulent means.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bankers Life, supra, at 12. That is why the key language \xe2\x80\x9cshould be construed not technically and restrictively, but flexibly to effectuate its remedial purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Zandford, supra, at 819 (internal quotation marks omitted); see Affiliated Ute, 406 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 151. The language merits a \xe2\x80\x9cbroad interpretation\xe2\x80\x9d because it is part of a residuary provision that must be able to accommodate evolving methods of fraud by intermediaries and insiders in ever more complicated securities markets. Central Bank, 511 U. S., at 174. Its interpretation should not privilege fraudsters who devise ever more devious methods of committing fraud involving covered securities.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n At the same time, the submitted interpretation is not so broad as to \xe2\x80\x9cconvert every common-law fraud that happens to involve securities into a violation of \xc2\xa710(b)\xe2\x80\x9d or preclude all state tort claims that involve securities in a tangential way. Zandford, supra, at 820. So, for example, the statutory language does not extend to cover a thief who steals money from a store to buy securities or to a fraudster who defrauds a bank for a loan that he uses to buy securities. See O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, supra, at 656. The victims in those cases are not concerned about their ability to act as investors but rather about their duties as a store clerk or a loan officer. Those frauds involve securities transactions only as happenstance. As a result, the interpretation submitted in this dissent strikes the balance that Congress intended between forbidding frauds by intermediaries in the market without reaching frauds that touch the markets in only tangential ways.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The key question is whether the misrepresentation coincides with the purchase or sale of a covered securityor the purchase or sale of the securities is what enables the fraud. Stanford\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentation did so. Stanford promised to purchase covered securities for investors, using his special expertise, thus allowing investors to rely on his skill to participate in the national securities markets. The entire scheme rested on investors falling for the trick. When covered securities are so integral to the fraud, the false promise is incident to the purchase or sale of regulated securities because it coincides with it, and the misrepresentation respecting national securities enabled the fraud.

\n

D

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court interprets the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cmisrepresentation or omission of a material fact in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered security\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the key phrase in SLUSA and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x94in a different manner. The result, it is submitted, is inconsistent with the statutory scheme Congress enacted and casts doubt on the applicability of federal securities law to cases of serious securities fraud.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court construes the text of SLUSA and Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 to require a misrepresentation that \xe2\x80\x9cis material to a decision by one or more individuals (other than the fraudster) to buy or sell a \xe2\x80\x98covered security.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. The Act simply does not say that the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x94or the promise to make a purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x94must be by one other than the fraudster. Rather the Act states that there must be \xe2\x80\x9ca misrepresentation or omission of a material fact in connection with the purchase or sale of a covered secu-rity.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778bb(f)(1)(A). See 17 CFR \xc2\xa7240.10b\xe2\x80\x935 (2013) (requiring an \xe2\x80\x9cuntrue statement of a material fact\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale of any security\xe2\x80\x9d). The Court narrows the statute Congress wrote in two ways. It excises the important \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d language, resulting in a confined reading inconsistent with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s purpose, structure, and operation. And, by requiring the purchase or sale be made by someone \xe2\x80\x9cother than the fraudster,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court inserts a limiting phrase that nowhere appears in the language of the provisions. In litigation like this, this new rule has it upside down. When the violation that adversely affects the securities market is done by the fraudster himself, that is all the more reason for applying federal law. This is not a case where Congress has limited its coverage to a certain subset of purchasers. Congress enacted such a limit two subsections later in SLUSA when detailing which ac-tions are not precluded. See\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778bb(f)(3)(A)(ii)(I) (\xe2\x80\x9cthe purchase or sale of securities by the issuer or an affiliate of the issuer exclusively from or to holders of equity securities of the issuer\xe2\x80\x9d). But it did not do so in the provision at issue.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reconstruction of the language of the provisions also casts doubt on the applicability of federal securities law to three established instances of federal securities fraud and one instance of preclusion under the Act as adjudged by the Court and the SEC in earlier cases.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n First, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation necessarily suggests that Zandford is incorrect and that dishonest brokers need not fear Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 liability. The deceit in Zandford was that the broker would act as the victim\xe2\x80\x99s fiduciary when in fact he planned on selling (and did sell) the investor\xe2\x80\x99s securities for his own benefit. 535 U. S., at 820; see also Line, 62 S.E.C. Docket 2879 (broker\xe2\x80\x99s deceit was false promise to buy). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s rule that liability must rest on a finding that someone other than the fraudster purchased or sold securities is inconsistent with Zandford, where the recipient of the misrepresentation did not buy or sell. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion disregards the hazards to the market when the fraudster is the one acting in the market and frustrates the investment objectives of his victims.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Second, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is difficult to reconcile with liability for insider trading. In O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan, the Court held that the \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d element \xe2\x80\x9cis satisfied because the fiduciary\xe2\x80\x99s fraud is consummated, not when the fiduciary gains the confidential information, but when, without disclosure to his principal, he uses the information to purchase or sell securities,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ceven though the person or entity defrauded is not the other party to the trade.\xe2\x80\x9d 521 U. S., at 656. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that someone other than the fraudster purchase or sell a security is hard to square with O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Third, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is difficult to square with the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s position in In re Orlando Joseph Jett, 82 S.E.C. Docket 1211 (2004). There, the SEC held liable a trader who fabricated complex trades to supplement the returns of his real trades, so as to increase his standingin his company. The SEC likened Jett to \xe2\x80\x9cgarden-variety securities fraud cases in which a broker-dealer or investment adviser engages in unsuccessful securities trades for a client and then hides the losses or inflates the profits by sending out false account statements.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1253. The decision of the Court today would require that Jett\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentation led to the purchase or sale of securities by someone other than Jett. But the SEC found Jett\xe2\x80\x99s own purchases and sales to be sufficient to come within the securities laws.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Finally, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis is inconsistent with the unanimous opinion in Dabit, which interpreted the same statutory language at issue in this litigation. Dabit squarely rejected the view that \xe2\x80\x9can alleged fraud is \xe2\x80\x98in connection with\xe2\x80\x99 a purchase or sale of securities only when the plaintiff himself was defrauded into purchasing or selling particular securities.\xe2\x80\x9d 547 U. S., at 85. Instead,it approved the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation that a broker who \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98sells customer securities with intent to misappropriate the proceeds\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d satisfies the \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d requirement. Ibid., n. 10. Dabit cannot be reconciled with today\xe2\x80\x99s decision to require someone other than the fraudster buy or sell a security.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is correct that there is no case precisely standing for the proposition that a victim does not have to take an ownership position. However, O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan supports that view. O\xe2\x80\x99Hagan clearly states that in insider trading cases \xe2\x80\x9cthe person or entity defrauded is not the other party to the trade.\xe2\x80\x9d 521 U. S., at 656. And in Zandford a fraudster told customers he would invest \xe2\x80\x9ctheir money\xe2\x80\x9d in securities and then sold those securities. 535 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 815. Here the fraudster told plaintiffs that he would \xe2\x80\x9cre-invest\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ctheir\xe2\x80\x9d money in securities and then bought different securities. App. 250, 470, 715. The only difference is that there the fraudster sold and here he bought. Federal regulation should not turn on whether a fraudster arrives before or after an investor makes his first purchase.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation also introduces confusion into securities law by not defining what it means for someone \xe2\x80\x9cother than the fraudster to buy or sell\xe2\x80\x9d a security, a rule that it derives from its view that the precedents all involve victims who had an ownership interest in securities. Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9310. The precedents the Court cites involve what the parties have called direct ownership, where the victim buys or sells an entire equity. By using the term ownership interest instead of ownership, the Court also appears to accept the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 concession that indirect ownership, where the victim buys or sells shares in a defendant fund that itself owns equities, is sufficient in certain circumstances, such as when a victim has \xe2\x80\x9csome interest in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s supposed portfolio.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 16.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n An ownership rule distinguishing between different types of indirect ownership is unworkable. Indirect ownership is a common type of investment. See M. Fink, The Rise of Mutual Funds 1 (2008) (U.\xc2\xa0S. mutual funds have over 88 million American shareholders and over $11 trillion in assets). Yet whether indirect ownership involves an interest in the underlying equities is a complex question of corporation, LLC, or partnership law. See In re Bernard L. Madoff Inv. Securities LLC, 708 F. 3d 422, 427 (CA2 2013). Congress likely did not intend preclusion of state-law suits to depend on the complexities of the Delaware Code.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s ownership approach also casts doubt on the scope of Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935. Under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, \xc2\xa710(b) applies to fraudulent mutual or hedge funds not because those funds invest in securities but because investments in the funds are securities. But not all such investments are securities. 2 L. Ribstein & R. Keatinge, Limited Liability Companies \xc2\xa714:2 (2010) (discussing test for a security from SEC v. W. J. Howey Co.,\n\n328 U. S. 293 (1946)\n); 1 H. Bloomenthal & S. Wolff, Securities Law Handbook \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72:3 to 2:4 (2010). For those that are not, the Court seems to envision liability only when the investment confers an ownership interest in the fund\xe2\x80\x99s securities. And the general rule for investments in funds organized as LLPs and LLCs is that they do not convey such claims. 1 Ribstein & Keatinge, supra, \xc2\xa77:11; see In re Herald, 730 F. 3d 112 (CA2 2013). As a result, in important instances Rule 10b\xe2\x80\x935 may not extend to mutual and hedge funds under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It is true that the SEC pursued the fraudster with success here. But that is because the CDs are securities. See Order Denying Motion to Dismiss in SEC v. Stanford International Bank, No. 3\xe2\x80\x9309\xe2\x80\x93CV\xe2\x80\x930298\xe2\x80\x93N (ND Tex., Nov. 30, 2011), pp. 5\xe2\x80\x9310. This aspect of Stanford\xe2\x80\x99s fraud is not a necessary feature of all frauds involving funds similarto SIB.

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The fraudster in this litigation misrepresented that he would purchase nationally traded securities. That misrepresentation was made \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with the purchase or sale\xe2\x80\x9d of the promised securities because it coincided with them. The fraud turned on the misrepresentation. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s contrary interpretation excises the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d from the Act, a phrase that the Court in earlier cases held to require a broad and flexible meaning. At the same time, by holding that the purchase or sale of securities be made by someone other than the fraudster, the Court engrafts a limitation that does not appear in the text. The result is to constrict the application of federal securities regulation in instances where dishonest brokers, insider traders, and lying employees purchase or sell securities, or promise to do so, as part of the fraud. Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision introduces confusion in the enforcement of securities laws.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n For these reasons, it is submitted that the judgment of the Court of Appeals should be reversed.
