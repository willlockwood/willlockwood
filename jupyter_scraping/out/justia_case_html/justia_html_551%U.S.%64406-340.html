\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

NATIONAL ASSOCIATION OF HOME BUILDERS et\xc2\xa0al. v. DEFENDERS OF WILDLIFE et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x93340.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 17, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 25, 2007

\n

Under the Clean Water Act (CWA), petitioner Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) initially administers each State\xe2\x80\x99s National Pollution Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permitting program, but CWA \xc2\xa7402(b) provides that the EPA \xe2\x80\x9cshall approve\xe2\x80\x9d transfer of permitting authority to a State upon application and a showing that the State has met nine specified criteria. Section 7(a)(2) of the Endangered Species Act of 1973 (ESA) requires federal agencies to consult with agencies designated by the Secretaries of Commerce and the Interior to \xe2\x80\x9cinsure\xe2\x80\x9d that a proposed agency action is unlikely to jeopardize an endangered or threatened species. The Fish and Wildlife Service (FWS) and the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) administer the ESA. Once a consultation process is complete, a written biological opinion is issued, which may suggest alternative actions to protect a jeopardized species or its critical habitat. When Arizona officials sought EPA authorization to administer the State\xe2\x80\x99s NPDES program, the EPA initiated consultation with the FWS to determine whether the transfer would adversely affect any listed species. The FWS regional office wanted potential impacts taken into account, but the EPA disagreed, finding that \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s mandatory nature stripped it of authority to disapprove a transfer based on any other considerations. The dispute was referred to the agencies\xe2\x80\x99 national offices for resolution. The FWS\xe2\x80\x99s biological opinion concluded that the requested transfer would not jeopardize listed species. The EPA concluded that Arizona had met each of \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s nine criteria and approved the transfer, noting that the biological opinion had concluded the consultation \xe2\x80\x9crequired\xe2\x80\x9d by ESA \xc2\xa77(a)(2). Respondents sought review in the Ninth Circuit, petitioner National Association of Home Builders intervened, and part of respondent Defenders of Wildlife\xe2\x80\x99s separate action was consolidated with the suit. The court held that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s transfer approval was arbitrary and capricious because the EPA had relied on contradictory positions regarding its \xc2\xa77(a)(2) responsibilities during the administrative process. Rather than remanding the case for the agency to explain its decision, however, the court reviewed the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s substantive construction of the statutes. It did not dispute that Arizona had met CWA \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s nine criteria, but nevertheless concluded that ESA \xc2\xa77(a)(2) required the EPA to determine whether its transfer decision would jeopardize listed species, in effect adding a tenth criterion. The court dismissed the argument that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s approval was not subject to \xc2\xa77(a)(2) because it was not a \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary action\xe2\x80\x9d under 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03, \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s interpretative regulation. The court thus vacated the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s transfer decision.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s determination that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s action was arbitrary and capricious is not fairly supported by the record. This Court will not vacate an agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision under the arbitrary and capricious standard unless the agency \xe2\x80\x9crelied on factors which Congress had not intended it to consider, entirely failed to consider an important aspect of the problem, offered an explanation for its decision that runs counter to the evidence before the agency, or is so implausible that it could not be ascribed to a difference in view or the product of agency expertise.\xe2\x80\x9d Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Assn. of United States, Inc. v. State Farm Mut. Automobile Ins. Co., 463 U. S. 29, 43. Here, the Ninth Circuit concluded that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s decision was internally inconsistent in its statements during the review process. Federal courts ordinarily are empowered to review only an agency\xe2\x80\x99s final action, and the fact that a local agency representative\xe2\x80\x99s preliminary determination is later overruled at a higher agency level does not render the decisionmaking process arbitrary and capricious. The EPA\xe2\x80\x99s final approval notice stating that \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s required consultation process had been concluded may be inconsistent with its previously expressed position\xe2\x80\x94and position in this litigation\xe2\x80\x94that \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s consultation requirement is not triggered by a \xc2\xa7402 transfer application, but that is not the type of error requiring a remand. By the time the statement was issued, the EPA and FWS had already consulted, and the question whether that consultation had been required was not germane to the final agency decision. Thus, this Court need not further delay the permitting authority transfer by remanding to the agency for clarification. Respondents suggest that the EPA nullified their right to participate in the application proceedings by altering its legal position during the pendency of the transfer decision and its associated litigation, but they do not suggest that they were deprived of their right to comment during the comment period made available under the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s regulations. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Because \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s no-jeopardy duty covers only discretionary agency actions, it does not attach to actions (like the NPDES permitting transfer authorization) that an agency is required by statute to undertake once certain specified triggering events have occurred. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0At first glance the legislative commands here are irreconcilable. Section 402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cshall approve\xe2\x80\x9d language is mandatory and its list exclusive; if the nine specified criteria are satisfied, the EPA does not have the discretion to deny a transfer application. Section 7(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s similarly imperative language would literally add a tenth criterion to \xc2\xa7402(b). Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0While a later enacted statute (such as the ESA) can sometimes operate to amend or even repeal an earlier statutory provision (such as the CWA), \xe2\x80\x9crepeals by implication are not favored\xe2\x80\x9d and will not be presumed unless the legislature\xe2\x80\x99s intention \xe2\x80\x9cto repeal [is] clear and manifest.\xe2\x80\x9d Watt v. Alaska, 451 U. S. 259, 267. Statutory repeal will not be inferred \xe2\x80\x9cunless the later statute \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cexpressly contradict[s] the original act\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 or such a construction \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cis absolutely necessary [to give the later statute\xe2\x80\x99s words] any meaning at all.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Traynor v. Turnage, 485 U. S. 535, 548. Otherwise, \xe2\x80\x9ca statute dealing with a narrow, precise, and specific subject is not submerged by a later enacted statute covering a more generalized spectrum.\xe2\x80\x9d Radzanower v. Touche Ross & Co., 426 U. S. 148, 153. The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) would effectively repeal \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s mandate that the EPA \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d issue a permit whenever all nine exclusive statutory prerequisites are met. Section 402(b) does not just set minimum requirements; it affirmatively mandates a transfer\xe2\x80\x99s approval, thus operating as a ceiling as well as a floor. By adding an additional criterion, the Ninth Circuit raises that floor and alters the statute\xe2\x80\x99s command. Read broadly, the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s construction would also partially override every federal statute mandating agency action by subjecting such action to the further condition that it not jeopardize listed species. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Title 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03, promulgated by the NMFS and FWS and applying \xc2\xa77(a)(2) \xe2\x80\x9cto all actions in which there is discretionary Federal involvement or control\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added), harmonizes the CWA and ESA by giving effect to the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s no-jeopardy mandate whenever an agency has discretion to do so, but not when the agency is forbidden from considering such extrastatutory factors. The Court owes \xe2\x80\x9csome degree of deference to the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretation\xe2\x80\x9d of the ESA, Babbitt v. Sweet Home Chapter, Communities for Great Ore., 515 U. S. 687, 703. Deference is not due if Congress has made its intent \xe2\x80\x9cclear\xe2\x80\x9d in the statutory text, Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837, 842, but \xe2\x80\x9cif the statute is silent or ambiguous \xe2\x80\xa6 the question \xe2\x80\xa6 is whether the agency\xe2\x80\x99s answer is based on a permissible construction of the statute,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 843. Because the \xe2\x80\x9cmeaning\xe2\x80\x94or ambiguity\xe2\x80\x94of certain words or phrases may only become evident \xe2\x80\xa6 in context,\xe2\x80\x9d FDA v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., 529 U. S. 120, 132, \xc2\xa77(a)(2) must be read against the statutory backdrop of the many mandatory agency directives whose operation it would implicitly abrogate or repeal were it construed as broadly as the Ninth Circuit did below. Such a reading leaves a fundamental ambiguity. An agency cannot simultaneously obey the differing mandates of ESA \xc2\xa77(a)(2) and CWA \xc2\xa7402(b), and consequently the statutory language\xe2\x80\x94read in light of the canon against implied repeals\xe2\x80\x94does not itself provide clear guidance as to which command must give way. Thus, it is appropriate to look to the implementing agency\xe2\x80\x99s expert interpretation, which harmonizes the statutes by applying \xc2\xa77(a)(2) to guide agencies\xe2\x80\x99 existing discretionary authority, but not reading it to override express statutory mandates. This interpretation is reasonable in light of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and the overall statutory scheme and is therefore entitled to Chevron deference. The regulation\xe2\x80\x99s focus on \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d actions accords with the commonsense conclusion that, when an agency is required to do something by statute, it simply lacks the power to \xe2\x80\x9cinsure\xe2\x80\x9d that such action will not jeopardize listed species. The basic principle of Department of Transportation v. Public Citizen, 541 U. S. 752\xe2\x80\x94that an agency cannot be considered the legal \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d of an action that it has no statutory discretion not to take, id., at 770\xe2\x80\x94supports the reasonableness of the FWS\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 contrary position is not supported by TVA v. Hill, 437 U. S. 153, which had no occasion to answer the question presented in these cases. Pp. 22\xe2\x80\x9324.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(e)\xc2\xa0Also unavailing is the argument that EPA\xe2\x80\x99s decision to transfer NPDES permitting authority to Arizona represented a \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d agency action. While the EPA may exercise some judgment in determining whether a State has shown that it can carry out \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated criteria, the statute clearly does not grant it the discretion to add another entirely separate prerequisite to that list. Nothing in \xc2\xa7402(b) authorizes the EPA to consider the protection of listed species as an end in itself when evaluating a transfer application. And to the extent that some of \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s criteria may result in environmental benefits to marine species, Arizona has satisfied each of those criteria. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 argument has also been disclaimed by the FWS and the NMFS, the agencies primarily charged with administering \xc2\xa77(a)(2) and the drafters of the regulations implementing that section. Pp. 24\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

420 F.\xc2\xa03d 946, reversed and remanded.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These cases concern the interplay between two federal environmental statutes. Section 402(b) of the Clean Water Act requires that the Environmental Protection Agency transfer certain permitting powers to state authorities upon an application and a showing that nine specified criteria have been met. Section 7(a)(2) of the Endangered Species Act of 1973 provides that a federal agency must consult with agencies designated by the Secretaries of Commerce and the Interior in order to \xe2\x80\x9cinsure that any action authorized, funded, or carried out by such agency \xe2\x80\xa6 is not likely to jeopardize the continued existence of any endangered species or threatened species.\xe2\x80\x9d The question presented is whether \xc2\xa77(a)(2) effectively operates as a tenth criterion on which the transfer of permitting power under the first statute must be conditioned. We conclude that it does not. The transfer of permitting authority to state authorities\xe2\x80\x94who will exercise that authority under continuing federal oversight to ensure compliance with relevant mandates of the Endangered Species Act and other federal environmental protection statutes\xe2\x80\x94was proper. We therefore reverse the judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\n1

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Clean Water Act of 1972 (CWA), 86 Stat. 816, 33 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71251 et seq., established a National Pollution Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) that is designed to prevent harmful discharges into the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s waters. The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) initially administers the NPDES permitting system for each State, but a State may apply for a transfer of permitting authority to state officials. See 33 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71342; see also \xc2\xa71251(b) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is the policy of Congress that the Stat[e] \xe2\x80\xa6 implement the permit progra[m] under sectio[n] 1342 \xe2\x80\xa6 of this title\xe2\x80\x9d). If authority is transferred, then state officials\xe2\x80\x94not the federal EPA\xe2\x80\x94have the primary responsibility for reviewing and approving NPDES discharge permits, albeit with continuing EPA oversight.[Footnote 1]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Governor of each State desiring to administer its own permit program for discharges into navigable waters within its jurisdiction may submit to [the EPA] a full and complete description of the program it proposes to establish and administer under State law or under an interstate compact,\xe2\x80\x9d as well as a certification \xe2\x80\x9cthat the laws of such State \xe2\x80\xa6 provide adequate authority to carry out the described program.\xe2\x80\x9d 33 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71342(b). The same section provides that the EPA \xe2\x80\x9cshall approve each submitted program\xe2\x80\x9d for transfer of permitting authority to a State \xe2\x80\x9cunless [it] determines that adequate authority does not exist\xe2\x80\x9d to ensure that nine specified criteria are satisfied. Ibid. These criteria all relate to whether the state agency that will be responsible for permitting has the requisite authority under state law to administer the NPDES program.[Footnote 2] If the criteria are met, the transfer must be approved.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Endangered Species Act of 1973 (ESA), 87 Stat. 884, as amended, 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71531 et seq., is intended to protect and conserve endangered and threatened species and their habitats. Section 4 of the ESA directs the Secretaries of Commerce and the Interior to list threatened and endangered species and to designate their critical habitats. \xc2\xa71533. The Fish and Wildlife Service (FWS) administers the ESA with respect to species under the jurisdiction of the Secretary of the Interior, while the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) administers the ESA with respect to species under the jurisdiction of the Secretary of Commerce. See 50 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa717.11, 222.101(a), 223.102, 402.01(b) (2006).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 7 of the ESA prescribes the steps that federal agencies must take to ensure that their actions do not jeopardize endangered wildlife and flora. Section 7(a)(2) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach Federal agency shall, in consultation with and with the assistance of the Secretary [of Commerce or the Interior], insure that any action authorized, funded, or carried out by such agency (hereinafter in this section referred to as an \xe2\x80\x98agency action\xe2\x80\x99) is not likely to jeopardize the continued existence of any endangered species or threatened species.\xe2\x80\x9d 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71536(a)(2).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once the consultation process contemplated by \xc2\xa77(a)(2) has been completed, the Secretary is required to give the agency a written biological opinion \xe2\x80\x9csetting forth the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, and a summary of the information on which the opinion is based, detailing how the agency action affects the species or its critical habitat.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71536(b)(3)(A); see also 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.14(h). If the Secretary concludes that the agency action would place the listed species in jeopardy or adversely modify its critical habitat, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Secretary shall suggest those reasonable and prudent alternatives which he believes would not violate [\xc2\xa77(a)(2)] and can be taken by the Federal agency \xe2\x80\xa6 in implementing the agency action.\xe2\x80\x9d 16 U. S.\xe2\x80\x82C. \xc2\xa71536(b)(3)(A); see also 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.14(h)(3). Regulations promulgated jointly by the Secretaries of Commerce and the Interior provide that, in order to qualify as a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable and prudent alternative,\xe2\x80\x9d an alternative course of action must be able to be implemented in a way \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent with the scope of the Federal agency\xe2\x80\x99s legal authority and jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7402.02. Following the issuance of a \xe2\x80\x9cjeopardy\xe2\x80\x9d opinion, the agency must either terminate the action, implement the proposed alternative, or seek an exemption from the Cabinet-level Endangered Species Committee pursuant to 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71536(e). The regulations also provide that \xe2\x80\x9cSection 7 and the requirements of this part apply to all actions in which there is discretionary Federal involvement or control.\xe2\x80\x9d 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03.

\n

B

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In February 2002, Arizona officials applied for EPA authorization to administer that State\xe2\x80\x99s NPDES program.[Footnote 3] The EPA initiated consultation with the FWS to determine whether the transfer of permitting authority would adversely affect any listed species.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The FWS regional office concluded that the transfer of authority would not cause any direct impact on water quality that would adversely affect listed species. App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 06\xe2\x80\x93340, p. 564. However, the FWS office was concerned that the transfer could result in the issuance of more discharge permits, which would lead to more development, which in turn could have an indirect adverse effect on the habitat of certain upland species, such as the cactus ferruginous pygmy-owl and the Pima pineapple cactus. Specifically, the FWS feared that, because \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s consultation requirement does not apply to permitting decisions by state authorities,[Footnote 4] the transfer of authority would empower Arizona officials to issue individual permits without considering and mitigating their indirect impact on these upland species. Id., at 565\xe2\x80\x93566. The FWS regional office therefore urged that, in considering the proposed transfer of permitting authority, those involved in the consultation process should take these potential indirect impacts into account.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The EPA disagreed, maintaining that \xe2\x80\x9cits approval action, which is an administrative transfer of authority, [would not be] the cause of future non-discharge-related impacts on endangered species from projects requiring State NPDES permits.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 564. As a factual matter, the EPA believed that the link between the transfer of permitting authority and the potential harm that could result from increased development was too attenuated. Id., at 654. And as a legal matter, the EPA concluded that the mandatory nature of CWA \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x94which directs that the EPA \xe2\x80\x9cshall approve\xe2\x80\x9d a transfer request if that section\xe2\x80\x99s nine statutory criteria are met\xe2\x80\x94stripped it of authority to disapprove a transfer based on any other considerations. Id., at 654\xe2\x80\x93655.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pursuant to procedures set forth in a memorandum of understanding between the agencies, the dispute was referred to the agencies\xe2\x80\x99 national offices for resolution. In December 2002, the FWS issued its biological opinion, which concluded that the requested transfer would not cause jeopardy to listed species. The opinion reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9cthe loss of section 7-related conservation benefits . . . is not an indirect effect of the authorization action,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 117, because

\n

\xe2\x80\x9closs of any conservation benefit is not caused by EPA\xe2\x80\x99s decision to approve the State of Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s program. Rather, the absence of the section 7 process that exists with respect to Federal NPDES permits reflects Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to grant States the right to administer these programs under state law provided the State\xe2\x80\x99s program meets the requirements of [\xc2\xa7]402(b) of the Clean Water Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 114.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, the FWS opined that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s continuing oversight of Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s permitting program, along with other statutory protections, would adequately protect listed species and their habitats following the transfer. Id., at 101\xe2\x80\x93107.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The EPA concluded that Arizona had met each of the nine statutory criteria listed in \xc2\xa7402(b) and approved the transfer of permitting authority. In the notice announcing the approval of the transfer, the EPA noted that the issuance of the FWS\xe2\x80\x99s biological opinion had \xe2\x80\x9cconclude[d] the consultation process required by ESA section 7(a)(2) and reflects the [FWS\xe2\x80\x99] agreement with EPA that the approval of the State program meets the substantive requirements of the ESA.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 73.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On April 2, 2003, respondents filed a petition in the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit seeking review of the transfer pursuant to 33 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71369(b)(1)(D), which allows private parties to seek direct review of the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s determinations regarding state permitting programs in the federal courts of appeals. The court granted petitioner National Association of Homebuilders leave to intervene as a respondent in that case. Respondent Defenders of Wildlife also filed a separate action in the United States District Court for the District of Arizona, alleging, among other things, that the biological opinion issued by the FWS in support of the proposed transfer did not comply with the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s standards. The District Court severed that claim and transferred it to the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, which consolidated the case with the suit challenging the EPA transfer. See 420 F.\xc2\xa03d 946 (2005).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A divided panel of the Ninth Circuit held that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s approval of the transfer was arbitrary and capricious because the EPA \xe2\x80\x9crelied during the administrative proceedings on legally contradictory positions regarding its section 7 obligations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 959. The court concluded that the EPA \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ed] to understand its own authority under section 7(a)(2) to act on behalf of listed species and their habitat,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 977, because \xe2\x80\x9cthe two propositions that underlie the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s action\xe2\x80\x94that (1) it must, under the [ESA], consult concerning transfers of CWA permitting authority, but (2) it is not permitted, as a matter of law, to take into account the impact on listed species in making the transfer decision\xe2\x80\x94cannot both be true,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 961. The court therefore concluded that it was required to \xe2\x80\x9cremand to the agency for a plausible explanation of its decision, based on a single, coherent interpretation of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 962.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The panel majority, however, did not follow this course of action. Rather, the panel went on to review the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s substantive construction of the statutes at issue and held that the ESA granted the EPA both the power and the duty to determine whether its transfer decision would jeopardize threatened or endangered species. The panel did not dispute that Arizona had met the nine criteria set forth in \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA, but the panel nevertheless concluded that \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the ESA provided an \xe2\x80\x9caffirmative grant of authority to attend to [the] protection of listed species,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 965, in effect adding a tenth criterion to those specified in \xc2\xa7402(b). The panel dismissed the argument that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s approval of the transfer application was not subject to \xc2\xa77(a)(2) because it was not a \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary action\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03 (interpreting \xc2\xa77(a)(2) to apply only to agency actions \xe2\x80\x9cin which there is discretionary Federal involvement and control\xe2\x80\x9d). 420 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 967\xe2\x80\x93969. It viewed the FWS\xe2\x80\x99s regulation as merely \xe2\x80\x9ccoterminous\xe2\x80\x9d with the express statutory language encompassing all agency actions that are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98authorized, funded, or carried out\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by the agency. Id., at 969 (quoting 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71536(a)(2)). On these grounds, the court granted the petition and vacated the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s transfer decision.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In dissent, Judge Thompson explained that the transfer decision was not a \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary action\xe2\x80\x9d under 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03 because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Clean Water Act, by its very terms, permits the EPA to consider only the nine specified factors. If a state\xe2\x80\x99s proposed permitting program meets the enumerated requirements,\xe2\x80\x9d he reasoned, \xe2\x80\x9cthe EPA administrator \xe2\x80\x98shall approve\xe2\x80\x99 the program. 33 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71342(b). This [c]ongressional directive does not permit the EPA to impose additional conditions.\xe2\x80\x9d 420 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 980.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit denied rehearing and rehearing en banc. 450 F.\xc2\xa03d 394 (2006). Writing for the six judges who dissented from the denial of rehearing en banc, Judge Kozinski disagreed with the panel\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s analysis was so internally inconsistent as to be arbitrary and capricious. He further noted that, if the panel was correct on this point, the proper resolution would have been to remand to the EPA for further explanation. Id., at 396\xe2\x80\x93398. On the statutory question, Judge Kozinski echoed Judge Thompson\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that once the nine criteria set forth in \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA are satisfied, a transfer is mandatory and nondiscretionary. Id., at 397\xe2\x80\x93399. He rejected the panel majority\xe2\x80\x99s broad construction of ESA \xc2\xa77(a)(2), concluding that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the ESA were as powerful as the majority contends, it would modify not only the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s obligation under the CWA, but every categorical mandate applicable to every federal agency.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 399, n.\xc2\xa04.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) is at odds with that of other Courts of Appeals. Compare 420 F.\xc2\xa03d 946 (case below), with Platte River Whooping Crane Critical Habitat Maintenance Trust v. FERC, 962 F.\xc2\xa02d 27, 33\xe2\x80\x9334 (CADC 1992), and American Forest & Paper Association v. EPA, 137 F.\xc2\xa03d 291, 298\xe2\x80\x93299 (CA5 1998). We granted certiorari to resolve this conflict, 549 U. S. ___ (2007), and we now reverse.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before addressing this question of statutory interpretation, however, we first consider whether the Court of Appeals erred in holding that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s transfer decision was arbitrary and capricious because, in that court\xe2\x80\x99s words, the agencies involved in the decision \xe2\x80\x9crelied \xe2\x80\xa6 on legally contradictory positions regarding [their] section 7 obligations.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 06\xe2\x80\x93340, at 23.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As an initial matter, we note that if the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s action was arbitrary and capricious, as the Ninth Circuit held, the proper course would have been to remand to the agency for clarification of its reasons. See Gonzales v. Thomas, 547 U. S. 183 (2006) (per curiam). Indeed, the court below expressly recognized that this finding required it to \xe2\x80\x9cremand to the agency for a plausible explanation of its decision, based on a single, coherent interpretation of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 06\xe2\x80\x93340, at 28. But the Ninth Circuit did not take this course; instead, it jumped ahead to resolve the merits of the dispute. In so doing, it erroneously deprived the agency of its usual administrative avenue for explaining and reconciling the arguably contradictory rationales that sometimes appear in the course of lengthy and complex administrative decisions. We need not examine this question further, however, because we conclude that the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s determination that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s action was arbitrary and capricious is not fairly supported by the record.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Review under the arbitrary and capricious standard is deferential; we will not vacate an agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision unless it

\n

\xe2\x80\x9chas relied on factors which Congress had not intended it to consider, entirely failed to consider an important aspect of the problem, offered an explanation for its decision that runs counter to the evidence before the agency, or is so implausible that it could not be ascribed to a difference in view or the product of agency expertise.\xe2\x80\x9d Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Assn. of United States, Inc. v. State Farm Mut. Automobile Ins. Co., 463 U. S. 29, 43 (1983).

\n

\n\xe2\x80\x9cWe will, however, \xe2\x80\x98uphold a decision of less than ideal clarity if the agency\xe2\x80\x99s path may reasonably be discerned.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Bowman Transp., Inc. v. Arkansas-Best Freight System, Inc., 419 U. S. 281, 286 (1974)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals concluded that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s decision was \xe2\x80\x9cinternally inconsistent\xe2\x80\x9d because, in its view, the agency stated\xe2\x80\x94both during preliminary review of Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s transfer application and in the Federal Register notice memorializing its final action\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthat section 7 requires consultation regarding the effect of a permitting transfer on listed species.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 06\xe2\x80\x93340, at 23.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With regard to the various statements made by the involved agencies\xe2\x80\x99 regional offices during the early stages of consideration, the only \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistency\xe2\x80\x9d respondents can point to is the fact that the agencies changed their minds\xe2\x80\x94something that, as long as the proper procedures were followed, they were fully entitled to do. The federal courts ordinarily are empowered to review only an agency\xe2\x80\x99s final action, see 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7704, and the fact that a preliminary determination by a local agency representative is later overruled at a higher level within the agency does not render the decisionmaking process arbitrary and capricious.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents also point to the final Federal Register notice memorializing the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s approval of Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s transfer application. This notice stated that the FWS\xe2\x80\x99s issuance of its biological opinion had \xe2\x80\x9cconclude[d] the consultation process required by ESA section 7(a)(2).\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 06\xe2\x80\x93340, at 73. Respondents contend that this statement is inconsistent with the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s previously expressed position\xe2\x80\x94and their position throughout this litigation\xe2\x80\x94that \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s consultation requirement is not triggered by a transfer application under \xc2\xa7402 of the CWA.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are not persuaded that this statement constitutes the type of error that requires a remand. By the time the Federal Register statement was issued, the EPA had already consulted with the FWS about the Arizona application, and the question whether that consultation had been required, as opposed to voluntarily undertaken by the agency, was simply not germane to the final agency transfer decision. The Federal Register statement, in short, was dictum, and it had no bearing on the final agency action that respondents challenge. Mindful of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 admonition that in reviewing agency action, \xe2\x80\x9cdue account shall be taken of the rule of prejudicial error,\xe2\x80\x9d 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7706, we do not believe that this stray statement, which could have had no effect on the underlying agency action being challenged, requires that we further delay the transfer of permitting authority to Arizona by remanding to the agency for clarification. See also PDK Labs., Inc. v. United States Drug Enforcement Admin., 362 F.\xc2\xa03d 786, 799 (CADC 2004) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn administrative law, as in federal civil and criminal litigation, there is a harmless error rule\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We further disagree with respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suggestion that, by allegedly altering its legal position while the Arizona transfer decision and its associated litigation was pending, the \xe2\x80\x9cEPA is effectively nullifying respondents\xe2\x80\x99 rights to participate in administrative proceedings concerning Arizona\xe2\x80\x99s application, and particularly respondents\xe2\x80\x99 rights under EPA\xe2\x80\x99s own regulations to comment on NPDES transfer applications.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 28 (citing 40 CFR \xc2\xa7123.61(b); emphasis deleted). Consistent with EPA regulations, the agency made available \xe2\x80\x9ca comment period of not less than 45 days during which interested members of the public [could] express their views on the State program.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7123.61(a)(1). Respondents do not suggest that they were deprived of their right to comment during this period.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents also contend that if the case were remanded to the EPA, they would raise additional challenges\xe2\x80\x94including, for example, a challenge to the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s provision of financial assistance to Arizona for the administration of its NPDES program. However, as explained below, any such agency action is separate and independent of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision to authorize the transfer of permitting authority pursuant to \xc2\xa7402(b). See n.\xc2\xa011, infra. We express no opinion as to the viability of a separate administrative or legal challenge to such actions.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We turn now to the substantive statutory question raised by the petitions, a question that requires us to mediate a clash of seemingly categorical\xe2\x80\x94and, at first glance, irreconcilable\xe2\x80\x94legislative commands. Section 402(b) of the CWA provides, without qualification, that the EPA \xe2\x80\x9cshall approve\xe2\x80\x9d a transfer application unless it determines that the State lacks adequate authority to perform the nine functions specified in the section. 33 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71342(b). By its terms, the statutory language is mandatory and the list exclusive; if the nine specified criteria are satisfied, the EPA does not have the discretion to deny a transfer application. Cf. Lopez v. Davis, 531 U. S. 230, 241 (2001) (noting Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cuse of a mandatory \xe2\x80\x98shall\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 to impose discretionless obligations\xe2\x80\x9d); Lexecon Inc. v. Milberg Weiss Bershad Hynes & Lerach, 523 U. S. 26, 35 (1998) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he mandatory \xe2\x80\x98shall\xe2\x80\x99 .\xe2\x80\x82.\xe2\x80\x82.\xe2\x80\x82normally creates an obligation impervious to judicial discretion\xe2\x80\x9d); Association of Civil Technicians v. FLRA, 22 F.\xc2\xa03d 1150, 1153 (CADC 1994) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe word \xe2\x80\x98shall\xe2\x80\x99 generally indicates a command that admits of no discretion on the part of the person instructed to carry out the directive\xe2\x80\x9d); Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1375 (6th ed. 1990) (\xe2\x80\x9cAs used in statutes \xe2\x80\xa6 this word is generally imperative or mandatory\xe2\x80\x9d). Neither respondents nor the Ninth Circuit has ever disputed that Arizona satisfied each of these nine criteria. See 420 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 963, n.\xc2\xa011; Brief for Respondents 19, n.\xc2\xa08.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The language of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the ESA is similarly imperative: it provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach Federal agency shall, in consultation with and with the assistance of the Secretary, insure that any action authorized, funded, or carried out by such agency . . . is not likely to jeopardize\xe2\x80\x9d endangered or threatened species or their habitats. 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71536(a)(2). This mandate is to be carried out through consultation and may require the agency to adopt an alternative course of action. As the author of the panel opinion below recognized, applying this language literally would \xe2\x80\x9cad[d] one [additional] requirement to the list of considerations under the Clean Water Act permitting transfer provision.\xe2\x80\x9d 450 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 404, n.\xc2\xa02 (Berzon, J., concurring in denial of rehearing en banc) (emphasis in original). That is, it would effectively repeal the mandatory and exclusive list of criteria set forth in \xc2\xa7402(b), and replace it with a new, expanded list that includes \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s no-jeopardy requirement.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While a later enacted statute (such as the ESA) can sometimes operate to amend or even repeal an earlier statutory provision (such as the CWA), \xe2\x80\x9crepeals by implication are not favored\xe2\x80\x9d and will not be presumed unless the \xe2\x80\x9cintention of the legislature to repeal [is] clear and manifest.\xe2\x80\x9d Watt v. Alaska, 451 U. S. 259, 267 (1981) (internal quotation marks omitted). We will not infer a statutory repeal \xe2\x80\x9cunless the later statute \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cexpressly contradict[s] the original act\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 or unless such a construction \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cis absolutely necessary . . . in order that [the] words [of the later statute] shall have any meaning at all.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Traynor v. Turnage, 485 U. S. 535, 548 (1988) (quoting Radzanower v. Touche Ross & Co., 426 U. S. 148, 153 (1976), in turn quoting T. Sedgwick, The Interpretation and Construction of Statutory and Constitutional Law 98 (2d ed. 1874)); see also Branch v. Smith, 538 U. S. 254, 273 (2003) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn implied repeal will only be found where provisions in two statutes are in \xe2\x80\x98irreconcilable conflict,\xe2\x80\x99 or where the latter Act covers the whole subject of the earlier one and \xe2\x80\x98is clearly intended as a substitute\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Posadas v. National City Bank, 296 U. S. 497, 503 (1936) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he intention of the legislature to repeal must be clear and manifest\xe2\x80\x9d). Outside these limited circumstances, \xe2\x80\x9ca statute dealing with a narrow, precise, and specific subject is not submerged by a later enacted statute covering a more generalized spectrum.\xe2\x80\x9d Radzanower, supra, at 153.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, reading \xc2\xa77(a)(2) as the Court of Appeals did would effectively repeal \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s statutory mandate by engrafting a tenth criterion onto the CWA.[Footnote 7] Section 402(b) of the CWA commands that the EPA \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d issue a permit whenever all nine exclusive statutory prerequisites are met. Thus, \xc2\xa7402(b) does not just set forth minimum requirements for the transfer of permitting authority; it affirmatively mandates that the transfer \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d be approved if the specified criteria are met. The provision operates as a ceiling as well as a floor. By adding an additional criterion, the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) raises that floor and alters \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s statutory command.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) would not only abrogate \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s statutory mandate, but also result in the implicit repeal of many additional otherwise categorical statutory commands. Section 7(a)(2) by its terms applies to \xe2\x80\x9cany action authorized, funded, or carried out by\xe2\x80\x9d a federal agency\xe2\x80\x94covering, in effect, almost anything that an agency might do. Reading the provision broadly would thus partially override every federal statute mandating agency action by subjecting such action to the further condition that it pose no jeopardy to endangered species. See, e.g., Platte River Whooping Crane Critical Habitat Maintenance Trust v. FERC, 962 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 33\xe2\x80\x9334 (considering whether \xc2\xa77(a)(2) overrides the Federal Power Act\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on amending annual power licenses). While the language of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) does not explicitly repeal any provision of the CWA (or any other statute), reading it for all that it might be worth runs foursquare into our presumption against implied repeals.

\n

C

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The agencies charged with implementing the ESA have attempted to resolve this tension through regulations implementing \xc2\xa77(a)(2). The NMFS and FWS, acting jointly on behalf of the Secretaries of Commerce and the Interior and following notice-and-comment rulemaking procedures, have promulgated a regulation stating that \xe2\x80\x9cSection 7 and the requirements of this part apply to all actions in which there is discretionary Federal involvement or control.\xe2\x80\x9d 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03 (emphasis added). Pursuant to this regulation, \xc2\xa77(a)(2) would not be read as impliedly repealing nondiscretionary statutory mandates, even when they might result in some agency action. Rather, the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s requirements would come into play only when an action results from the exercise of agency discretion. This interpretation harmonizes the statutes by giving effect to the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s no-jeopardy mandate whenever an agency has discretion to do so, but not when the agency is forbidden from considering such extrastatutory factors.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have recognized that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he latitude the ESA gives the Secretary in enforcing the statute, together with the degree of regulatory expertise necessary to its enforcement, establishes that we owe some degree of deference to the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretation\xe2\x80\x9d of the statutory scheme. Babbitt v. Sweet Home Chapter, Communities for Great Ore., 515 U. S. 687, 703 (1995). But such deference is appropriate only where \xe2\x80\x9cCongress has not directly addressed the precise question at issue\xe2\x80\x9d through the statutory text. Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837, 843 (1984).

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf the intent of Congress is clear, that is the end of the matter; for the court, as well as the agency, must give effect to the unambiguously expressed intent of Congress\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [However,] if the statute is silent or ambiguous with respect to the specific issue, the question for the court is whether the agency\xe2\x80\x99s answer is based on a permissible construction of the statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 842\xe2\x80\x93843 (footnotes omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In making the threshold determination under Chevron, \xe2\x80\x9ca reviewing court should not confine itself to examining a particular statutory provision in isolation.\xe2\x80\x9d FDA v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp., 529 U. S. 120, 132 (2000). Rather, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he meaning\xe2\x80\x94or ambiguity\xe2\x80\x94of certain words or phrases may only become evident when placed in context\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. It is a \xe2\x80\x98fundamental canon of statutory construction that the words of a statute must be read in their context and with a view to their place in the overall statutory scheme.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 132\xe2\x80\x93133 (quoting Davis v. Michigan Dept. of Treasury, 489 U. S. 803, 809 (1989)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We must therefore read \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the ESA against the statutory backdrop of the many mandatory agency directives whose operation it would implicitly abrogate or repeal if it were construed as broadly as the Ninth Circuit did below. When \xc2\xa77(a)(2) is read this way, we are left with a fundamental ambiguity that is not resolved by the statutory text. An agency cannot simultaneously obey the differing mandates set forth in \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the ESA and \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA, and consequently the statutory language\xe2\x80\x94read in light of the canon against implied repeals\xe2\x80\x94does not itself provide clear guidance as to which command must give way.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this situation, it is appropriate to look to the implementing agency\xe2\x80\x99s expert interpretation, which cabins \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s application to \xe2\x80\x9cactions in which there is discretionary Federal involvement or control.\xe2\x80\x9d 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03. This reading harmonizes the statutes by applying \xc2\xa77(a)(2) to guide agencies\xe2\x80\x99 existing discretionary authority, but not reading it to override express statutory mandates.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude that this interpretation is reasonable in light of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and the overall statutory scheme, and that it is therefore entitled to deference under Chevron. Section 7(a)(2) requires that an agency \xe2\x80\x9cinsure\xe2\x80\x9d that the actions it authorizes, funds, or carries out are not likely to jeopardize listed species or their habitats. To \xe2\x80\x9cinsure\xe2\x80\x9d something\xe2\x80\x94as the court below recognized\xe2\x80\x94means \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]o make certain, to secure, to guarantee (some thing, event, etc.).\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 420 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 963 (quoting 7 Oxford English Dictionary 1059 (2d ed. 1989)). The regulation\xe2\x80\x99s focus on \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d actions accords with the commonsense conclusion that, when an agency is required to do something by statute, it simply lacks the power to \xe2\x80\x9cinsure\xe2\x80\x9d that such action will not jeopardize endangered species.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This reasoning is supported by our decision in Department of Transportation v. Public Citizen, 541 U. S. 752 (2004). That case concerned safety regulations that were promulgated by the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) and had the effect of triggering a Presidential directive allowing Mexican trucks to ply their trade on United States roads. The Court held that the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) did not require the agency to assess the environmental effects of allowing the trucks entry because \xe2\x80\x9cthe legally relevant cause of the entry of the Mexican trucks is not FMCSA\xe2\x80\x99s action, but instead the actions of the President in lifting the moratorium and those of Congress in granting the President this authority while simultaneously limiting FMCSA\xe2\x80\x99s discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 769 (emphasis in original). The Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere an agency has no ability to prevent a certain effect due to its limited statutory authority over the relevant actions, the agency cannot be considered a legally relevant \xe2\x80\x98cause\xe2\x80\x99 of the effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 770.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not suggest that Public Citizen controls the outcome here; \xc2\xa77(a)(2), unlike NEPA, imposes a substantive (and not just a procedural) statutory requirement, and these cases involve agency action more directly related to environmental concerns than the FMCSA\xe2\x80\x99s truck safety regulations. But the basic principle announced in Public Citizen\xe2\x80\x94that an agency cannot be considered the legal \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d of an action that it has no statutory discretion not to take\xe2\x80\x94supports the reasonableness of the FWS\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) as reaching only discretionary agency actions. See also California v. United States, 438 U. S. 645, 668, n. 21 (1978) (holding that a statutory requirement that federal operating agencies conform to state water usage rules applied only to the extent that it was not \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with other congressional directives\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court below simply disregarded \xc2\xa7402.03\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s reach, dismissing \xe2\x80\x9cthe regulation\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xe2\x80\x98discretionary \xe2\x80\xa6 involvement\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d as merely \xe2\x80\x9ccongruent with the statutory reference to actions \xe2\x80\x98authorized, funded, or carried out\xe2\x80\x99 by the agency.\xe2\x80\x9d 420 F.\xc2\xa03d, 968. But this reading cannot be right. Agency discretion presumes that an agency can exercise \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment\xe2\x80\x9d in connection with a particular action. See Citizens to Preserve Overton Park, Inc. v. Volpe, 401 U. S. 402, 415\xe2\x80\x93416 (1971); see also Random House Dictionary of the English Language 411 (unabridged ed. 1967) (\xe2\x80\x9cdiscretion\xe2\x80\x9d defined as \xe2\x80\x9cthe power or right to decide or act according to one\xe2\x80\x99s own judgment; freedom of judgment or choice\xe2\x80\x9d). As the mandatory language of \xc2\xa7402(b) itself illustrates, not every action authorized, funded, or carried out by a federal agency is a product of that agency\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of discretion.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa7402.03 is similarly implausible. The dissent would read the regulation as simply clarifying that discretionary agency actions are included within the scope of \xc2\xa77(a)(2), but not confining the statute\xe2\x80\x99s reach to such actions. See post, at 7\xe2\x80\x9311. But this reading would render the regulation entirely superfluous. Nothing in either \xc2\xa77(a)(2) or the other agency regulations interpreting that section, see \xc2\xa7402.02, suggests that discretionary actions are excluded from the scope of the ESA, and there is thus no need for a separate regulation to bring them within the statute\xe2\x80\x99s scope. On the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reading, \xc2\xa7402.03\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d federal involvement is mere surplusage, and we have cautioned against reading a text in a way that makes part of it redundant. See, e.g., TRW Inc. v. Andrews, 534 U. S. 19, 31 (2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This history of the regulation also supports the reading to which we defer today. As the dissent itself points out, the proposed version of \xc2\xa7402.03 initially stated that \xe2\x80\x9cSection 7 and the requirements of this Part apply to all actions in which there is Federal involvement or control,\xe2\x80\x9d 48 Fed. Reg. 29999 (1983) (emphasis added); the Secretary of the Interior modified this language to provide (as adopted in the Final Rule now at issue) that the statuory requirements apply to \xe2\x80\x9call actions in which there is discretionary Federal involvement or control,\xe2\x80\x9d 51 Fed. Reg. 19958 (1986) (emphasis added). The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reading would rob the word \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d of any effect, and substitute the earlier, proposed version of the regulation for the text that was actually adopted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, we read \xc2\xa7402.03 to mean what it says: that \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s no-jeopardy duty covers only discretionary agency actions and does not attach to actions (like the NPDES permitting transfer authorization) that an agency is required by statute to undertake once certain specified triggering events have occurred. This reading not only is reasonable, inasmuch as it gives effect to the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s provision, but also comports with the canon against implied repeals because it stays \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s mandate where it would effectively override otherwise mandatory statutory duties.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents argue that our opinion in TVA v. Hill, 437 U. S. 153 (1978), supports their contrary position. In that case, we held that the ESA prohibited the Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA) from putting into operation the Tellico Dam\xe2\x80\x94despite the fact that the agency had already spent over $100 million on the nearly completed project\xe2\x80\x94because doing so would have threatened the critical habitat of the endangered snail darter. In language on which respondents rely, the Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe ordinary meaning\xe2\x80\x9d of \xc2\xa77 of the ESA contained \xe2\x80\x9cno exemptions\xe2\x80\x9d and reflected \xe2\x80\x9ca conscious decision by Congress to give endangered species priority over the \xe2\x80\x98primary missions\xe2\x80\x99 of federal agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 173, 185, 188.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0TVA v. Hill, however, had no occasion to answer the question presented in these cases. That case was decided almost a decade before the adoption in 1986 of the regulations contained in 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03. And in any event, the construction project at issue in TVA v. Hill, while expensive, was also discretionary. The TVA argued that by continuing to make lump-sum appropriations to the TVA, some of which were informally earmarked for the Tellico Dam project, Congress had implicitly repealed \xc2\xa77\xe2\x80\x99s no-jeopardy requirement as it applied to that project. See 437 U. S., at 189\xe2\x80\x93193. The Court rejected this argument, concluding that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Appropriations Acts did not themselves identify the projects for which the sums had been appropriated\xe2\x80\x9d and that reports by congressional committees allegedly directing the TVA to complete the project lacked the force of law. Id., at 189, n. 35. Central to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was the conclusion that Congress did not mandate that the TVA put the dam into operation; there was no statutory command to that effect; and there was therefore no basis for contending that applying the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s no-jeopardy requirement would implicitly repeal another affirmative congressional directive.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0TVA v. Hill thus supports the position, expressed in \xc2\xa7402.03, that the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s no-jeopardy mandate applies to every discretionary agency action\xe2\x80\x94regardless of the expense or burden its application might impose. But that case did not speak to the question whether \xc2\xa77(a)(2) applies to non-discretionary actions, like the one at issue here. The regulation set forth in 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03 addressed that question, and we defer to its reasonable interpretation.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, respondents and their amici argue that, even if \xc2\xa77(a)(2) is read to apply only to \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d agency actions, the decision to transfer NPDES permitting authority to Arizona represented such an exercise of discretion. They contend that the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s decision to authorize a transfer is not entirely mechanical; that it involves some exercise of judgment as to whether a State has met the criteria set forth in \xc2\xa7402(b); and that these criteria incorporate references to wildlife conservation that bring consideration of \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s no-jeopardy mandate properly within the agency\xe2\x80\x99s discretion.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The argument is unavailing. While the EPA may exercise some judgment in determining whether a State has demonstrated that it has the authority to carry out \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated statutory criteria, the statute clearly does not grant it the discretion to add another entirely separate prerequisite to that list. Nothing in the text of \xc2\xa7402(b) authorizes the EPA to consider the protection of threatened or endangered species as an end in itself when evaluating a transfer application. And to the extent that some of the \xc2\xa7402(b) criteria may result in environmental benefits to marine species,[Footnote 10] there is no dispute that Arizona has satisfied each of those statutory criteria.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 argument has been disclaimed not only by the EPA, but also by the FWS and the NMFS, the two agencies primarily charged with administering \xc2\xa77(a)(2) and the drafters of the regulations implementing that section. Each agency recently issued a formal letter concluding that the authorization of an NPDES permitting transfer is not the kind of discretionary agency action that is covered by \xc2\xa7402.03. See App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 06\xe2\x80\x93549, at 103a\xe2\x80\x93116a. An agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the meaning of its own regulations is entitled to deference \xe2\x80\x9cunless plainly erroneous or inconsistent with the regulation,\xe2\x80\x9d Auer v. Robbins, 519 U. S. 452, 461 (1997) (internal quotation marks omitted), and that deferential standard is plainly met here.[Footnote 11]

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying Chevron, we defer to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable interpretation of ESA \xc2\xa77(a)(2) as applying only to \xe2\x80\x9cactions in which there is discretionary Federal involvement or control.\xe2\x80\x9d 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03. Since the transfer of NPDES permitting authority is not discretionary, but rather is mandated once a State has met the criteria set forth in \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA, it follows that a transfer of NPDES permitting authority does not trigger \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s consultation and no-jeopardy requirements. Accordingly, the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is reversed, and these cases are remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These cases present a problem of conflicting \xe2\x80\x9cshalls.\xe2\x80\x9d On the one hand, \xc2\xa7402(b) of the Clean Water Act (CWA) provides that the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d approve a State\xe2\x80\x99s application to administer a National Pollution Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permitting program unless it determines that nine criteria are not satisfied. 33 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71342(b). On the other hand, shortly after the passage of the CWA, Congress enacted \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the Endangered Species Act of 1973 (ESA), which commands that federal agencies \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d insure that their actions do not jeopardize endangered species. 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71536(a)(2).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When faced with competing statutory mandates, it is our duty to give full effect to both if at all possible. See, e.g., Morton v. Mancari, 417 U. S. 535, 551 (1974) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen two statutes are capable of co-existence, it is the duty of the courts, absent a clearly expressed congressional intention to the contrary, to regard each as effective\xe2\x80\x9d). The Court fails at this task. Its opinion unsuccessfully tries to reconcile the CWA and ESA by relying on a federal regulation, 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03 (2006), which it reads as limiting the reach of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) to only discretionary federal actions, see ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9319. Not only is this reading inconsistent with the text and history of \xc2\xa7402.03, but it is fundamentally inconsistent with the ESA itself.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the celebrated \xe2\x80\x9csnail darter\xe2\x80\x9d case, TVA v. Hill, 437 U. S. 153 (1978), we held that the ESA \xe2\x80\x9creveals a conscious decision by Congress to give endangered species priority over the \xe2\x80\x98primary missions\xe2\x80\x99 of federal agencies,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 185. Consistent with that intent, Chief Justice Burger\xe2\x80\x99s exceptionally thorough and admirable opinion explained that \xc2\xa77 \xe2\x80\x9cadmits of no exception.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 173. Creating precisely such an exception by exempting nondiscretionary federal actions from the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s coverage, the Court whittles away at Congress\xe2\x80\x99 comprehensive effort to protect endangered species from the risk of extinction and fails to give the Act its intended effect. After first giving Hill the attention it deserves, I will comment further on the irrelevance of \xc2\xa7402.03 to these cases and offer other available ways to give effect to both CWA and the ESA. Having done so, I conclude by explaining why these cases should be remanded to the EPA for further proceedings.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Hill, we were presented with two separate questions: (1) whether the ESA required a court to enjoin the operation of the nearly completed Tellico Dam and Reservoir Project because the Secretary of the Interior had determined that its operation would eradicate a small endangered fish known as a snail darter; and (2) whether post-1973 congressional appropriations for the completion of the Tellico Dam constituted an implied repeal of the ESA, at least insofar as it applied to the Dam. 437 U. S., at 156. More than 30 pages of our opinion explain our affirmative answer to the first question, see id., at 156\xe2\x80\x93188, but just over four pages sufficed to explain our negative answer to the second, see id., at 189\xe2\x80\x93193. While it is our ruling on the first question that is relevant to the cases before us, it is our refusal to hold that the ESA itself had been impliedly repealed that the majority strangely deems most significant. See ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In answering Hill\xe2\x80\x99s first question, we did not discuss implied repeals. On the contrary, that portion of the opinion contained our definitive interpretation of the ESA, in which we concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe language, history, and structure of the [ESA] indicates beyond doubt that Congress intended endangered species to be afforded the highest of priorities.\xe2\x80\x9d 437 U. S., at 174; see also id., at 177 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The dominant theme pervading all Congressional discussion of the proposed [ESA] was the overriding need to devote whatever effort and resources were necessary to avoid further diminution of national and worldwide wildlife resources\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Coggins, Conserving Wildlife Resources: An Overview of the Endangered Species Act of 1973, 51 N. D. L. Rev. 315, 321 (1975) (emphasis added in Hill))). With respect to \xc2\xa77 in particular, our opinion could not have been any clearer. We plainly held that it \xe2\x80\x9cadmits of no exception.\xe2\x80\x9d 437 U. S., at 173 (emphasis added).[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our opinion in Hill explained at length why \xc2\xa77 imposed obligations on \xe2\x80\x9call federal agencies\xe2\x80\x9d to ensure that \xe2\x80\x9cactions authorized, funded, or carried out by them do not jeopardize the continued existence of endangered species.\xe2\x80\x9d 437 U. S., at 173 (emphasis deleted; internal quotation marks omitted). Not a word in the opinion stated or suggested that \xc2\xa77 obligations are inapplicable to mandatory agency actions that would threaten the eradication of an endangered species. Nor did the opinion describe the Tennessee Valley Authority\xe2\x80\x99s (TVA) attempted completion of the Tellico Dam as a discretionary act. How could it? After all, if the Secretary of the Interior had not declared the snail darter an endangered species whose critical habitat would be destroyed by operation of the Tellico Dam, the TVA surely would have been obligated to spend the additional funds that Congress appropriated to complete the project.[Footnote 2] Unconcerned with whether an agency action was mandatory or discretionary, we simply held that \xc2\xa77 of the ESA

\n

\xe2\x80\x9creveals an explicit congressional decision to require agencies to afford first priority to the declared national policy of saving endangered species. The pointed omission of the type of qualifying language previously included in endangered species legislation reveals a conscious decision by Congress to give endangered species priority over the \xe2\x80\x98primary missions\xe2\x80\x99 of federal agencies. \xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 185 (emphasis added).[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The fact that we also concluded that the post-1973 congressional appropriations did not impliedly repeal the ESA provides no support for the majority\xe2\x80\x99s contention that the obligations imposed by \xc2\xa77(a)(2) may be limited to discretionary acts. A few passages from the relevant parts of Hill belie that suggestion. After noting the oddity of holding that the interest in protecting the survival of a relatively small number of 3-inch fish \xe2\x80\x9cwould require the permanent halting of a virtually completed dam for which Congress has expended more than $100 million,\xe2\x80\x9d we found \xe2\x80\x9cthat the explicit provisions of the Endangered Species Act require precisely that result.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 172, 173. We then continued:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cOne would be hard pressed to find a statutory provision whose terms were any plainer than those in \xc2\xa77 of the Endangered Species Act. Its very words affirmatively command all federal agencies \xe2\x80\x98to insure that actions authorized, funded, or carried out by them do not jeopardize the continued existence\xe2\x80\x99 of an endangered species or \xe2\x80\x98result in the destruction or modification of habitat of such species \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 173 (quoting 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71536 (1976 ed.) (emphasis added in Hill)).

\n

\nWe also reviewed the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s history to identify a variety of exceptions that had been included in earlier legislation and unenacted proposals but were omitted from the final version of the 1973 statute. We explained that earlier endangered species legislation \xe2\x80\x9cqualified the obligation of federal agencies,\xe2\x80\x9d but the 1973 Act purposefully omitted \xe2\x80\x9call phrases which might have qualified an agency\xe2\x80\x99s responsibilities.\xe2\x80\x9d 437 U. S., at 181, 182. Moreover, after observing that the ESA creates only a limited number of \xe2\x80\x9chardship exemptions,\xe2\x80\x9d see 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71539\xe2\x80\x94none of which would apply to federal agencies\xe2\x80\x94we applied the maxim expressio unius est expression alterius to conclude that \xe2\x80\x9cthere are no exemptions in the Endangered Species Act for federal agencies,\xe2\x80\x9d437 U. S., at 188.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today, however, the Court countenances such an exemption. It erroneously concludes that the ESA contains an unmentioned exception for nondiscretionary agency action and that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s command to enjoin the completion of the Tellico Dam depended on the unmentioned fact that the TVA was attempting to perform a discretionary act. But both the text of the ESA and our opinion in Hill compel the contrary determination that Congress intended the ESA to apply to \xe2\x80\x9call federal agencies\xe2\x80\x9d and to all \xe2\x80\x9cactions authorized, funded, or carried out by them.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 173 (emphasis deleted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A transfer of NPDES permitting authority under \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA is undoubtedly one of those \xe2\x80\x9cactions\xe2\x80\x9d that is \xe2\x80\x9cauthorized\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccarried out\xe2\x80\x9d by a federal agency. See 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71536(b); 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.02 (defining \xe2\x80\x9caction\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9call activities or programs of any kind authorized, funded, or carried out, in whole or in part, by Federal agencies in the United States or upon the high seas. Examples include, but are not limited to . . . actions directly or indirectly causing modifications to the land, water, or air\xe2\x80\x9d). It follows from Hill that \xc2\xa77(a)(2) applies to such NPDES transfers\xe2\x80\x94whether they are mandatory or discretionary.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given our unequivocal holding in Hill that the ESA has \xe2\x80\x9cfirst priority\xe2\x80\x9d over all other federal action, 437 U. S., at 185, if any statute should yield, it should be the CWA. But no statute must yield unless it is truly incapable of coexistence. See, e.g., Morton, 417 U. S., at 551. Therefore, assuming that \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA contains its own mandatory command, we should first try to harmonize that provision with the mandatory requirements of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the ESA.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s solution is to rely on 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.03, which states that \xe2\x80\x9cSection 7 and the requirements of this part apply to all actions in which there is discretionary Federal involvement or control.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court explains that this regulation \xe2\x80\x9charmonizes the statutes by giving effect to the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s no-jeopardy mandate whenever an agency has discretion to do so, but by lifting that mandate when the agency is forbidden from considering such extrastatutory factors.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17. This is not harmony, and it certainly isn\xe2\x80\x99t effect. Rather than giving genuine effect to \xc2\xa77(a)(2), the Court permits a wholesale limitation on the reach of the ESA. Its interpretation of \xc2\xa7402.03 conflicts with the text and history of the regulation, as well as our interpretation of \xc2\xa77 in the \xe2\x80\x9csnail darter\xe2\x80\x9d case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To begin with, the plain language of \xc2\xa7402.03 does not state that its coverage is limited to discretionary actions. Quite the opposite, the most natural reading of the text is that it confirms the broad construction of \xc2\xa77 endorsed by our opinion in Hill. Indeed, the only way to read \xc2\xa7402.03 in accordance with the facts of the case and our holding that \xc2\xa77 \xe2\x80\x9cadmits of no exception[s],\xe2\x80\x9d 437 U. S., at 173, is that it eliminates any possible argument that the ESA does not extend to situations in which the discretionary federal involvement is only marginal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is simply mistaken when it says that it reads \xc2\xa7402.03 \xe2\x80\x9cto mean what it says: that \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s no-jeopardy duty covers only discretionary agency actions\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21 (emphasis added). That is not, in fact, what \xc2\xa7402.03 \xe2\x80\x9csays.\xe2\x80\x9d The word \xe2\x80\x9conly\xe2\x80\x9d is the Court\xe2\x80\x99s addition to the text, not the agency\xe2\x80\x99s. Moreover, that text surely does not go on to say (as the Court does) that the duty \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not attach to actions (like the NPDES permitting transfer authorization) that an agency is required by statute to undertake once certain specified triggering events have occurred.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. If the drafters of the regulation had intended such a far-reaching change in the law, surely they would have said so by using language similar to that which the Court uses today.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nothing in the proceedings that led to the promulgation of the regulation suggests any reason for limiting the pre-existing understanding of the scope of \xc2\xa77\xe2\x80\x99s coverage. EPA codified the current version of \xc2\xa7402.03 in 1986 as part of a general redrafting of ESA regulations. In the 1983 Notice of Proposed Rulemaking, the proposed version of \xc2\xa7402.03 stated that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa77 and the requirements of this Part apply to all actions in which there is Federal involvement or control.\xe2\x80\x9d 48 Fed. Reg. 29999 (1983). Without any explanation, the final rule inserted the word \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d before \xe2\x80\x9cFederal involvement or control.\xe2\x80\x9d 51 Fed. Reg. 19958 (1986).[Footnote 4] Clearly, if the Secretary of the Interior meant to limit the pre-existing understanding of the scope of the coverage of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) by promulgating this regulation, that intent would have been mentioned somewhere in the text of the regulations or in contemporaneous comment about them. See National Cable & Telecommunications Assn. v. Brand X Internet Services, 545 U. S. 967, 1001 (2005) (holding that an agency is free within \xe2\x80\x9cthe limits of reasoned interpretation to change course\xe2\x80\x9d only if it \xe2\x80\x9cadequately justifies the change\xe2\x80\x9d); Motor Vehicle Mfrs. Assn. of United States, Inc. v. State Farm Mut. Automobile Ins. Co., 463 U. S. 29, 48 (1983) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe have frequently reiterated that an agency must cogently explain why it has exercised its discretion in a given manner\xe2\x80\x9d). Yet, the final rule said nothing about limiting the reach of \xc2\xa77 or our decision in Hill. Nor did it mention the change from the notice of proposed rulemaking. I can only assume, then, that the regulation does mean what both it and the notice of proposed rulemaking says: Section 7(a)(2) applies to discretionary federal action, but not only to discretionary action.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The only explanation the agency provided for \xc2\xa7402.03 was the following:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThis section, which explains the applicability of section 7, implicitly covers Federal activities within the territorial jurisdiction of the United States and upon the high seas as a result of the definition of \xe2\x80\x98action\xe2\x80\x99 in \xc2\xa7402.02. The explanation for the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x98action\xe2\x80\x99 is provided in the discussion under \xc2\xa7402.01 above.\xe2\x80\x9d 51 Fed. Reg. 19937.

\n

\nThis statement directs us to two sources: the definition of \xe2\x80\x9caction\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7402.02 and the \xe2\x80\x9cexplanation for the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x98action\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7402.01. Ibid. Both confirm that there was no intent to draw a distinction between discretionary and nondiscretionary actions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 402.02 provides in relevant part:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cAction means all activities or programs of any kind authorized, funded, or carried out, in whole or in part, by Federal agencies in the United States or upon the high seas. Examples include, but are not limited to:

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(a)\xe2\x80\x82actions intended to conserve listed species or their habitat;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(b)\xe2\x80\x82the promulgation of regulations . . . \xe2\x80\x9d (second and third emphases added.)

\n

\nActions in either of the described sub-categories are sometimes mandatory and sometimes discretionary. Likewise, as the italicized portions indicate, the term \xe2\x80\x9caction\xe2\x80\x9d expressly refers to \xe2\x80\x9call\xe2\x80\x9d agency activities or programs \xe2\x80\x9cof any kind,\xe2\x80\x9d regardless of whether they are discretionary or mandatory. By reading the term \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d as a limitation on \xe2\x80\x9caction,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court creates a contradiction in the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s own regulation.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As for the final rule\xe2\x80\x99s explanation for the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x99action\xe2\x80\x99 in \xc2\xa7402.01, that too is fully consistent with my interpretation of \xc2\xa7402.03. That explanation plainly states that \xe2\x80\x9call Federal actions including \xe2\x80\x98conservations programs\xe2\x80\x99 are subject to the consultation requirements of section 7(a)(2) if they \xe2\x80\x98may affect\xe2\x80\x99 listed species or their critical habitats.\xe2\x80\x9d 51 Fed. Reg. 19929 (emphasis added). The regulation does not say all \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d federal actions, nor does it evince an intent to limit the scope of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) in any way. Rather, it just restates that the ESA applies to \xe2\x80\x9call\xe2\x80\x9d federal actions, just as the notice of proposed rulemaking did. This explanation of the scope of the word \xe2\x80\x9caction\xe2\x80\x9d is therefore a strong indication that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary\xe2\x80\x9d is contrary to its intended meaning.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An even stronger indication is the fact that at no point in the administrative proceedings in these cases did EPA even mention it.[Footnote 6] As an initial matter, it is worth emphasizing that even if EPA had relied on \xc2\xa7402.03, its interpretation of the ESA would not be entitled to deference under Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., 467 U. S. 837 (1984), because it is not charged with administering that statute, id., at 844 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe have long recognized that considerable weight should be accorded to an executive department\xe2\x80\x99s construction of a statutory scheme it is entrusted to administer.\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); Department of Treasury v. FLRA, 837 F.\xc2\xa02d 1163, 1167 (CADC 1988) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen an agency interprets a statute other than that which it has been entrusted to administer, its interpretation is not entitled to deference\xe2\x80\x9d). The Departments of the Interior and Commerce, not EPA, are charged with administering the ESA. See Babbitt v. Sweet Home Chapter, Communities for Great Ore., 515 U. S., 687, 703\xe2\x80\x93704 (1995). And EPA has conceded that the Department of the Interior\xe2\x80\x99s biological opinion \xe2\x80\x9cdid not discuss 50 CFR. 402.03, and it did not address the question whether the consultation that produced the [biological opinion] was required by the ESA.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. in No.\xc2\xa006\xe2\x80\x93549, p.\xc2\xa024; see App. 77\xe2\x80\x93124 (never mentioning \xc2\xa7402.03). Left with this unfavorable administrative record, EPA can only lean on the fact that the Department of the Interior has recently \xe2\x80\x9cclarified\xe2\x80\x9d its position regarding \xc2\xa7402.03 in a different administrative proceeding. See Pet. for Cert. in No. 06\xe2\x80\x93549, pp. 24\xe2\x80\x9325; id., at 26 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe recent F[ish and Wildlife Service] and N[ational Marine Fisheries Service] communications regarding Alaska\xe2\x80\x99s pending transfer application reflect those agencies\xe2\x80\x99 considered interpretations . . . of [50 CFR] 402.03\xe2\x80\x9d); App. to Pet. for Cert. in No. 06\xe2\x80\x93340, pp. 103a\xe2\x80\x93116a; see also ante, at 12 n.\xc2\xa05. We have long held, however, that courts may not affirm an agency action on grounds other than those adopted by the agency in the administrative proceedings. See SEC v. Chenery Corp., 318 U. S. 80, 87 (1943). The majority ignores this hoary principle of administrative law and substitutes a post-hoc interpretation of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) and \xc2\xa7402.03 for that of the relevant agency. For that reason alone, these cases should be remanded to the agency. And for the other reasons I have given, \xc2\xa7402.03 cannot be used to harmonize the CWA and the ESA.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There are at least two ways in which the CWA and the ESA can be given full effect without privileging one statute over the other.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The text of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) itself provides the first possible way of reconciling that provision with \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA. The subsection reads:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cEach Federal agency shall, in consultation with and with the assistance of the Secretary, insure that any action authorized, funded, or carried out by such agency (hereinafter in this section referred to as an \xe2\x80\x98agency action\xe2\x80\x99) is not likely to jeopardize the continued existence of any endangered species or threatened species or result in the destruction or adverse modification of habitat of such species which is determined by the Secretary, after consultation as appropriate with affected States, to be critical, unless such agency has been granted an exemption for such action by the Committee pursuant to subsection (h) of this section.\xe2\x80\x9d 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71536(a)(2) (emphasis added).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is certainly correct that the use of the word \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa77(a)(2) imposes a mandatory requirement on the federal agencies. See ante, at 14. It is also correct that the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmandate is to be carried out through consultation and may require the agency to adopt an alternative course of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 15. The Court is too quick to conclude, however, that this consultation requirement creates an irreconcilable conflict between this provision and \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA. It rushes to this flawed judgment because of a basic conceptual error\xe2\x80\x94an error that is revealed as early as the first paragraph of its opinion. Rather than attempting to find a way to give effect to \xc2\xa77(a)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s consultation requirement, the Court frames the question presented as \xe2\x80\x9cwhether \xc2\xa77(a)(2) effectively operates as a tenth criterion on which the transfer of permitting power under the first statute must be conditioned. \xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x932. The Court is not alone in this. The author of the Ninth Circuit opinion below also stated that the ESA \xe2\x80\x9cadds one requirement to the list of considerations under the Clean Water Act permitting transfer provision.\xe2\x80\x9d 450 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 404 n.\xc2\xa02 (2006) (Berzon, J., concurring in denial of rehearing en banc) (emphasis in original). But while the ESA does mandate that the relevant agencies \xe2\x80\x9cconsul[t]\xe2\x80\x9d with the Interior Department, that consultation process also provides a way for the agencies to give effect to both statutes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first step in the statutory consultation process is to identify whether any endangered species will be affected by an agency action. An agency proposing a particular action, such as an NPDES transfer, will typically ask the Secretary of the Interior whether any listed species may be present in the area of the proposed action and whether that action will \xe2\x80\x9caffect\xe2\x80\x9d those species. See 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71536(c). It is entirely possible that no listed species will be affected, and any anticipated conflict between the ESA and another statute will have been avoided at this threshold stage. If, however, the Secretary determines that a proposed action may affect an endangered species or its critical habitat, the agency must formally consult with the Secretary. This consultation culminates in the issuance of a \xe2\x80\x9cbiological opinion,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9cdetail[s] how the agency action affects the species or its critical habitat.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71536(b)(3)(A); see also 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.14(h). Even at this stage, it is still possible that formal consultation will reveal that the agency action will not jeopardize any species. See, e.g., 63 Fed. Reg. 51199 (1998) (noting that FWS rendered a \xe2\x80\x9cno jeopardy\xe2\x80\x9d finding with respect to the transfer of permitting authority to Texas).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If the biological opinion concludes that the agency action would put a listed species in jeopardy, however, the ESA contains a process for resolving the competing demands of agency action and species protection. The ESA provides that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Secretary shall suggest those reasonable and prudent alternatives which he believes would not violate subsection (a)(2) and can be taken by the Federal agency or applicant in implementing the agency action.\xe2\x80\x9d 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71536(b)(3)(A); see also 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.14(h)(3). The agency\xe2\x80\x99s regulations define \xe2\x80\x9c[r]easonable and prudent alternatives\xe2\x80\x9d as

\n

\xe2\x80\x9calternative actions identified during formal consultation that can be implemented in a manner consistent with the intended purpose of the action, that can be implemented consistent with the scope of the Federal agency\xe2\x80\x99s legal authority and jurisdiction, that is economically and technologically feasible, and that the Director [of FWS] believes would avoid the likelihood of jeopardizing the continued existence of listed species or resulting in the destruction or adverse modification of critical habitat. \xe2\x80\x9d 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.02.

\n

\nThus, in the face of any conflict between the ESA and another federal statute, the ESA and its implementing regulations encourage federal agencies to work out a reasonable alternative that would let the proposed action move forward \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent with [its] intended purpose\xe2\x80\x9d and the agency\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9clegal authority,\xe2\x80\x9d while also avoiding any violation of \xc2\xa77(a)(2).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When applied to the NPDES transfer program, the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable and prudent alternatives\xe2\x80\x9d process would enable EPA and the Department of the Interior to develop a substitute that would allow a transfer of permitting authority and would not jeopardize endangered species. Stated differently, the consultation process would generate an alternative course of action whereby the transfer could still take place\xe2\x80\x94as required by \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA\xe2\x80\x94but in such a way that would honor the mandatory requirements of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the ESA. This should come as no surprise to EPA, as it has engaged in pre-transfer consultations at least six times in the past and has stated that it is not barred from doing so by the CWA.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, for the rare case in which no \xe2\x80\x9creasonable and prudent alternative\xe2\x80\x9d can be found, Congress has provided yet another mechanism for resolving any conflicts between the ESA and a proposed agency action. In 1978, shortly after our decision in Hill, Congress amended the ESA to create the \xe2\x80\x9cEndangered Species Committee,\xe2\x80\x9d which it authorized to grant exemptions from \xc2\xa77(a)(2). 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71536(e). Because it has the authority to approve the extinction of an endangered species, the Endangered Species Committee is colloquially described as the \xe2\x80\x9cGod Squad\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cGod Committee.\xe2\x80\x9d In light of this weighty responsibility, Congress carefully laid out requirements for the God Committee\xe2\x80\x99s membership,[Footnote 8] procedures,[Footnote 9] and the factors it must consider in deciding whether to grant an exemption.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the final arbiter in situations in which the ESA conflicts with a proposed agency action, the God Committee embodies the primacy of the ESA\xe2\x80\x99s mandate and serves as the final mechanism for harmonizing that Act with other federal statutes. By creating this Committee, Congress recognized that some conflicts with the ESA may not be capable of resolution without having to forever sacrifice some endangered species. At the same time, the creation of this last line of defense reflects Congress\xe2\x80\x99 view that the ESA should not yield to another federal action except as a final resort and except when authorized by high level officials after serious consideration. In short, when all else has failed and two federal statutes are incapable of resolution, Congress left the choice to the Committee\xe2\x80\x94not to this Court; it did not limit the ESA in the way the majority does today.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0EPA\xe2\x80\x99s regulations offer a second way to harmonize the CWA with the ESA. After EPA has transferred NPDES permitting authority to a State, the agency continues to oversee the State\xe2\x80\x99s permitting program. See Arkansas v. Oklahoma, 503 U. S. 91, 105 (1992) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress preserved for the Administrator broad authority to oversee state permit programs\xe2\x80\x9d). If a state permit is \xe2\x80\x9coutside the guidelines and the requirements\xe2\x80\x9d of the CWA, EPA may object to it and block its issuance. See 33 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71342(d)(2); 66 Fed. Reg. 11206 (2001). Given these ongoing responsibilities, EPA has enacted a regulation that requires a State to enter into a Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) that sets forth the particulars of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s oversight duties. See 40 CFR \xc2\xa7123.24(a) (2006).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The regulation governing MOAs contains several detailed requirements. For instance, the regulation states that an MOA must contain \xe2\x80\x9c[p]rovisions specifying classes and categories of permit applications, draft permits and proposed permits that the State will send to the [EPA] Regional Administrator for review, comment and, where applicable, objection,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7123.24(b)(2); \xe2\x80\x9c[p]rovisions specifying the frequency and content of reports, documents and other information which the State is required to submit to the EPA,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7123.24(b)(3); and \xe2\x80\x9c[p]rovisions for coordination of compliance monitoring activities by the State and by EPA,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7123.24(b)(4)(i). More generally, the regulation provides that an MOA \xe2\x80\x9cmay include other terms, conditions, or agreements\xe2\x80\x9d that are \xe2\x80\x9crelevant to the administration and enforcement of the State\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory program.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7123.24(a). Under the MOA regulation, furthermore, EPA will not approve any MOA that restricts its statutory oversight responsibility. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Like the \xc2\xa77(a)(2) consultation process described above, MOAs provide a potential mechanism for giving effect to \xc2\xa77 of the ESA while also allowing the transfer of permitting authority to a State. It is important to remember that EPA must approve an MOA prior to the transfer of NPDES authority. As such, EPA can use\xe2\x80\x94and in fact has used\xe2\x80\x94the MOA process to structure its later oversight in a way that will allow it to protect endangered species in accordance with \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the ESA. EPA might negotiate a provision in the MOA that would require a State to abide by the ESA requirements when issuing pollution permits. See Brief for American Fisheries Society et al. as Amici Curiae 28. (\xe2\x80\x9cIn the Maine MOA, for example, EPA and the state agreed that state permits would protect ESA-listed species by ensuring compliance with state water quality standards\xe2\x80\x9d). Alternatively, \xe2\x80\x9cEPA could require the state to provide copies of draft permits for discharges in particularly sensitive habitats such as those of ESA-listed species or for discharges that contain a pollutant that threatens ESA-listed wildlife.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 10. Or the MOA might be drafted in a way that would allow the agency to object to state permits that would jeopardize any and all endangered species. See id., at 28 (explaining that the Maine MOA includes a provision allowing EPA to \xe2\x80\x9cobject to any state permit that risks harm to a listed species by threatening water quality\xe2\x80\x9d). These are just three of many possibilities. I need not identify other ways EPA could use the MOA process to comply with the ESA; it is enough to observe that MOAs provide a straightforward way to give the ESA its full effect without restricting \xc2\xa77(a)(2) in the way the Court does.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As discussed above, I believe that the Court incorrectly restricts the reach of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) to discretionary federal actions. See Part II, supra. Even if such a limitation were permissible, however, it is clear that EPA\xe2\x80\x99s authority to transfer permitting authority under \xc2\xa7402(b) is discretionary.[Footnote 11]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The EPA Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s authority to approve state permit programs pursuant to \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA does not even fit within the Court\xe2\x80\x99s description of the category of mandatory actions that the Court holds are covered by the ESA. In the Court\xe2\x80\x99s words, that category includes actions \xe2\x80\x9cthat an agency is required by statute to undertake once certain specified triggering events have occurred.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 22. The \xe2\x80\x9ctriggering event\xe2\x80\x9d for EPA\xe2\x80\x99s approval is simply the filing of a satisfactory description of the State\xe2\x80\x99s proposed program. See 33 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71342(b). The statute then commands that the EPA Administrator \xe2\x80\x9cshall approve\xe2\x80\x9d the submitted program unless he determines that state law does not satisfy nine specified conditions. Those conditions are not \xe2\x80\x9ctriggering events\xe2\x80\x9d; they are potential objections to the exercise of the Administrator\xe2\x80\x99s authority.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What is more, \xc2\xa7402(b) is a perfect example of why our analysis should not end simply because a statute uses the word \xe2\x80\x9cshall.\xe2\x80\x9d Instead, we must look more closely at its listed criteria to determine whether they allow for discretion, despite the use of \xe2\x80\x9cshall.\xe2\x80\x9d After all, as then-Justice Rehnquist\xe2\x80\x99s dissenting opinion in the \xe2\x80\x9csnail darter\xe2\x80\x9d case explains, a federal statute using the word \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d will sometimes allow room for discretion. See Hill, 437 U. S. at 211\xe2\x80\x93212 (Rehnquist, J., dissenting).[Footnote 12] In these cases, there is significant room for discretion in EPA\xe2\x80\x99s evaluation of \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s nine conditions. The first criterion, for example, requires the EPA Administrator to examine five other statutes and ensure that the State has adequate authority to comply with each. 33 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71342(b)(1)(A). One of those five statutes, in turn, expressly directs the Administrator to exercise his \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71312. Even the Court acknowledges that EPA must exercise \xe2\x80\x9csome judgment in determining whether a State has demonstrated that it has the authority to carry out \xc2\xa7402(b)\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated statutory criteria.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 24. However, in the very same breath, the Court states that the dispositive fact is that \xe2\x80\x9cthe statute clearly does not grant it the discretion to add another entirely separate prerequisite to that list.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This reasoning flouts the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own logic. Under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa7402.03, \xc2\xa77(a)(2) applies to discretionary federal actions of any kind. The Court plainly acknowledges that EPA exercises discretion when deciding whether to transfer permitting authority to a State. If we are to take the Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach seriously, once any discretion has been identified\xe2\x80\x94as it has here\xe2\x80\x94\xc2\xa77(a)(2) must apply.[Footnote 13]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The MOA regulation described in Part III\xe2\x80\x93B, supra, also demonstrates that an NPDES transfer is not as ministerial a task as the Court would suggest. The agency retains significant discretion under \xc2\xa7123.24 over the content of an MOA, which of course must be approved prior to a transfer. For instance, EPA may require a State to file reports on a weekly basis or a monthly basis. It may require a State to submit only certain classes and categories of permit applications. And it may include any additional terms and conditions that are relevant to the enforcement of the NPDES program. There is ample room for judgment in all of these areas, and EPA has exercised such judgment in the past when approving MOAs from many States. See, e.g., Approval of Application by Maine to Administer the NPDES Program, 66 Fed. Reg. 12791, (2001); Approval of Application by Maine to Administer the NPDES Program; Texas, 63 Fed. Reg. 51165 (1998).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In fact, in an earlier case raising a question similar to this one, see American Forest & Paper Assn. v. EPA, 137 F.\xc2\xa03d 291, 298\xe2\x80\x93299 (CA5 1998), EPA itself explained how 40 CFR \xc2\xa7123.24 gives it discretion over the approval of a State pollution control program, see Brief for EPA in No. 96\xe2\x80\x9360874 (CA5). Arguing that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]ndicia of discretionary involvement or control abound in [its] regulations,\xe2\x80\x9d the agency listed its MOA regulation as a prime example.[Footnote 14] Again, because EPA\xe2\x80\x99s approval of a State application to administer an NPDES program entails significant\xe2\x80\x94indeed, abounding\xe2\x80\x94discretion, I would find that \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the ESA applies even under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own flawed theory of these cases.

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Mindful that judges must always remain faithful to the intent of the legislature, Chief Justice Burger closed his opinion in the \xe2\x80\x9csnail darter\xe2\x80\x9d case with a reminder that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nce the meaning of an enactment is discerned and its constitutionality determined, the judicial process comes to an end.\xe2\x80\x9d Hill, 437 U. S., at 194. This Court offered a definitive interpretation of the Endangered Species Act nearly 30 years ago in that very case. Today the Court turns its back on our decision in Hill and places a great number of endangered species in jeopardy, including the cactus ferruginous pygmy-owl and Pima pineapple cactus at issue here. At the risk of plagiarizing Chief Justice Burger\xe2\x80\x99s fine opinion, I think it is appropriate to end my opinion just as he did\xe2\x80\x94with a quotation attributed to Sir Thomas More that has as much relevance today as it did three decades ago. This quotation illustrates not only the fundamental character of the rule of law embodied in \xc2\xa77 of the ESA but also the pernicious consequences of official disobedience of such a rule. Repetition of that literary allusion is especially appropriate today:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe law, Roper, the law. I know what\xe2\x80\x99s legal, not what\xe2\x80\x99s right. And I\xe2\x80\x99ll stick to what\xe2\x80\x99s legal. . . . I\xe2\x80\x99m not God. The currents and eddies of right and wrong, which you find such plain-sailing, I can\xe2\x80\x99t navigate, I\xe2\x80\x99m no voyager. But in the thickets of the law, oh there I\xe2\x80\x99m a forester. . . . What would you do? Cut a great road through the law to get after the Devil? . . . And when the last law was down, and the Devil turned round on you\xe2\x80\x94where would you hide, Roper, the laws all being flat? . . . This country\'s planted thick with laws from coast to coast\xe2\x80\x94Man\xe2\x80\x99s laws, not God\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x94and if you cut them down . . . d\xe2\x80\x99you really think you could stand upright in the winds that would blow then? . . . Yes, I\xe2\x80\x99d give the Devil benefit of law, for my own safety\xe2\x80\x99s sake.\xe2\x80\x9d R. Bolt, A Man for All Seasons, Act I, p. 147 (Three Plays, Heinemann ed. 1967) (quoted in Hill, 437 U. S., at 195).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although its reasons have shifted over time, at both the administrative level and in the federal courts, EPA has insisted that the requirements of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the ESA do not apply to its decision to transfer permitting authority under \xc2\xa7402(b) of the CWA. See App. 114; Brief for Petitioner EPA 16, 42. As I have explained above, that conclusion is contrary to the text of \xc2\xa77(a)(2), our decision in the TVA v. Hill, and the regulation on which the agency has since relied and upon which the Court relies on today. Accordingly, I would hold that EPA\xe2\x80\x99s decision was arbitrary and capricious under the Administrative Procedure Act, see 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7706(2)(A), and would remand to the agency for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I respectfully dissent.

S. 752 (2004),\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthat an agency cannot be considered the legal \xe2\x80\x98cause\xe2\x80\x99 of an action that it has no statutory discretion not to take\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94supports its reliance on \xc2\xa7402.03. First of all, the Court itself recognizes that it must distance itself from that case, ibid., because Public Citizen dealt with a procedural requirement under the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA), not a substantive requirement like that imposed by \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the ESA, see TVA v. What the Court does not recognize, however, is that what it views as the \xe2\x80\x9cbasic principle\xe2\x80\x9d of Public Citizen is stated too broadly and therefore inapplicable to this case. Ante, at 20.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decision in Public Citizen turned on what we called \xe2\x80\x9ca critical feature of the case\xe2\x80\x9d: that the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) had \xe2\x80\x9cno ability to countermand\xe2\x80\x9d the President\xe2\x80\x99s lifting a moratorium that prohibited certain motor carriers from obtaining authority to operate within the United States. 541 U. S., at 766. Once the President decided to lift that moratorium, and once the relevant vehicles had entered the United States, FMCSA was required by statute to register the vehicles if certain conditions were met. Ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder FMCSA\xe2\x80\x99s entirely reasonable reading of this provision, it must certify any motor carrier that can show that it is willing and able to comply with the various substantive requirements for safety and financial responsibility contained in DOT regulations; only the moratorium prevented it from doing so for Mexican motor carriers before 2001\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis deleted)). Therefore, any potential NEPA concerns were generated by another decisionmaker, the President, and not the FMCSA. Here, by contrast, EPA is not required to act ministerially once another person or agency has made a decision. Instead, EPA must exercise its own judgment when considering the transfer of NPDES authority to a State; it also has its own authority to deny such a transfer. Any effect on endangered species will be caused, even if indirectly, by the agency\xe2\x80\x99s own decision to transfer NPDES authority. Cf. 50 CFR \xc2\xa7402.02 (providing that the ESA will apply to all agency activities that \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly or indirectly caus[e] modifications to the land, water, or air\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 dissent, while reserving judgment as to whether \xc2\xa77(a)(2) of the Endangered Species Act of 1973, 16 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71536(a)(2), really covers every possible agency action even of totally unrelated agencies\xe2\x80\x94such as, say, a discretionary determination by the Internal Revenue Service whether to prosecute or settle a particular tax liability, see 26 U. S. C. \xc2\xa77121.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the same time I add one additional consideration in support of his (and my own) dissenting views. The Court emphasizes that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]y its terms, the statutory language [of \xc2\xa7402(b) of the Clean Water Act, 33 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71342(b)] is mandatory and the list exclusive; if the nine specified criteria are satisfied, the EPA does not have the discretion to deny a transfer application.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14 (emphasis added). My own understanding of agency action leads me to believe that the majority cannot possibly be correct in concluding that the structure of \xc2\xa7402(b) precludes application of \xc2\xa77(a)(2) to the EPA\xe2\x80\x99s discretionary action. See ante, at 19\xe2\x80\x9321 (Stevens, J., dissenting). That is because grants of discretionary authority always come with some implicit limits attached. See L. Jaffe, Judicial Control of Administrative Action 359 (1965) (discretion is \xe2\x80\x9ca power to make a choice\xe2\x80\x9d from a \xe2\x80\x9cpermissible class of actions\xe2\x80\x9d). And there are likely numerous instances in which, prior to, but not after, the enactment of \xc2\xa77(a)(2), the statute might have implicitly placed \xe2\x80\x9cspecies preservation\xe2\x80\x9d outside those limits.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To take one example, consider the statute that once granted the old Federal Power Commission (FPC) the authority to grant a \xe2\x80\x9ccertificate of public convenience and necessity\xe2\x80\x9d to permit a natural gas company to operate a new pipeline. See 15 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7717f(c)(1)(A). It says that \xe2\x80\x9ca certificate shall be issued to any qualified applicant therefor . . . if it is found that the applicant is able and willing properly to do the acts and to perform the service proposed . . . and that the proposed service . . . is or will be required by the present or future public convenience and necessity.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7717f(e).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before enactment of the Endangered Species Act of 1973, 87 Stat. 884, it is at least uncertain whether the FPC could have withheld a certificate simply because a natural gas pipeline might threaten an endangered animal, for given the Act\xe2\x80\x99s language and history, species preservation does not naturally fall within its terms. But we have held that the Endangered Species Act changed the regulatory landscape, \xe2\x80\x9cindicat[ing] beyond doubt that Congress intended endangered species to be afforded the highest of priorities.\xe2\x80\x9d TVA v. Hill, 437 U. S. 153, 174 (1978) (emphasis added). Indeed, the Endangered Species Act demonstrated \xe2\x80\x9ca conscious decision by Congress to give endangered species priority over the \xe2\x80\x98primary missions\xe2\x80\x99 of federal agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 185. And given a new pipeline\xe2\x80\x99s potential effect upon habitat and landscape, it seems reasonable to believe, once Congress enacted the new law, the FPC\xe2\x80\x99s successor (the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission) would act within its authority in taking species-endangering effects into account.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To take another example, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has, by statute, an \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive\xe2\x80\x9d list of criteria to consider in reviewing applications for approval of a new drug. See 21 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7355(d) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf the Secretary finds . . . [e.g.,] the investigations . . . do not include adequate tests by all methods reasonably applicable to show whether or not such drug is safe . . . he shall issue an order refusing to approve the application\xe2\x80\x9d). Preservation of endangered species is not on this \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive\xe2\x80\x9d list of criteria. Yet I imagine that the FDA now should take account, when it grants or denies drug approval, of the effect of manufacture and marketing of a new drug upon the preservation or destruction of an endangered species.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The only meaningful difference between the provision now before us, \xc2\xa7402(b) of the Clean Water Act, and the energy- and drug-related statutes that I have mentioned is that the very purpose of the former is to preserve the state of our natural environment\xe2\x80\x94a purpose that the Endangered Species Act shares. That shared purpose shows that \xc2\xa77(a)(2) must apply to the Clean Water Act a fortiori.
