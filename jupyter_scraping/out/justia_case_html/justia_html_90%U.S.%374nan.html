piece silks, ribbons, and silk velvets, or velvets of which silk is the component material of chief value, 60 percentum ad valorem."

\n

"Now, had this statute of July 30, 1864, been a statute which purported to cover the whole subject -- if it purported to be an entire revision of the whole law relating to duties on imports, and had, either by express words or by implication, repealed previously existing laws, there would have been no room to doubt that \'not otherwise provided for\' meant not otherwise provided for in this act. MR. JUSTICE SWAYNE delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

A thing may be within the letter of a statute and not within its meaning, and within its meaning, though not within its letter. [Footnote 6] The intention of the lawmaker is the law.

\n

Revenue laws are to be construed liberally to carry out the purposes of their enactment. Their penal provisions are not penal in the sense that requires a rigidly strict construction. [Footnote 7] Where doubt exists as to the meaning of a statute, the title may be looked to for aid in its construction. [Footnote 8] The preexisting law, and the reason and purpose of the new enactment are also considerations of great weight. [Footnote 9]

\n

Upon the trial of this case, the learned circuit judge held that silk neckties were within the last clause of the eighth section of the Act of July 30th, 1864, unless the words "not otherwise provided for" excluded them from it, and brought them within the acts of 1861 and 1862. Such he instructed the jury was the effect of this negation.

\n

In his view, those words referred not to the preceding part of the section in which they are found, but to the prior acts specified.

\n

We agree with him as to the comprehensive character of the previous part of the sentence, if unqualified, but we dissent from his second proposition. To the latter we think there is a conclusive answer.

\n

The object of the statute was to increase the duties before imposed upon the things which it embraces. The title and the context alike show this. The preceding part of the section

Page 90 U. S. 381

contains a very full enumeration of articles of silk, both manufactured and unmanufactured. It was evidently intended to be exhaustive. The last clause seems to have been added, as it is not unusual in such cases, out of abundant caution, that nothing might escape. Hence the phrase "not otherwise provided for," was interposed, and meant to apply, not to preceding acts which may not have been present to the mind of the draftsman, and to which there was no necessity to recur, but to the preceding enumeration in the same section -- which it supplemented.

\n

The section, thus construing this clause, covers the whole subject of silk, in all its variety of forms. It was complete in itself. There was no need to refer generally or specially to any prior act. If there was conflict, the prior legislation yielded, necessarily, ipso facto to the later.

\n

All the manufactured articles enumerated in this section of the Act of 1864 were subjected to a duty of 60 percent

\n

The duty imposed by the acts of 1861 and 1862 is 35 percent

\n

Why leave the nonenumerated articles, covered by the Act of 1864, subject only to this lower rate of duty? Why this distinction? Such a result would, we think, be a solecism, and contrary to the spirit and purpose of the act. It cannot reasonably be supposed that such was the intent of the clause in question.

\n

This view of the subject fixes the duty upon silk neckties at 50 percent ad valorem.

\n

If we had not come to this conclusion, we should hold that the case is controlled by the twentieth section of the Act of 1842. The provisions of that section first appeared in the second section of the Act of September 11th, 1841. [Footnote 10] They were reenacted in the Act of 1842, and were a permanent part of the customs duty system of the country. They were unaffected by any of the later tariff acts, and were in force when the duty in question was collected. [Footnote 11] Under that section, as applied to the Act of 1864, the rate of duty would be

Page 90 U. S. 382

60 percent instead of 50. But as we hold that the clause we have considered, of the eighth section of the Act of 1864, applies, it excludes the operation of the earlier statute.

\n

The construction we have indicated of these statutes is that given to them in their practical administration by the Treasury Department ever since their enactment. This, though not controlling, is not without weight, and is entitled to respectful consideration. [Footnote 12]

\n

Our views as to the amount of duty chargeable on the neckties in question are corroborated by the following further considerations:

\n

In the Revised Code of the United States, of June 22, 1874, [Footnote 13] the similitude clause of the Act of 1842, and the eighth section of the Act of 1864, are reproduced without change. The provision as to "articles worn by men," &c., is also reproduced, but as follows:

\n

"Articles worn by men, women, or children, of whatever material composed, except silk and linen, made up or made wholly or in part by hand, not otherwise provided for, 35 percent ad valorem. [Footnote 14]"

\n

The exceptions mentioned were here for the first time expressly interposed, but it was a legislative declaration that such was the state of the law on the 1st of December, 1873, without the exceptions, and it is necessarily a construction of the last clause of the eighth section of the Act of 1864, in accordance with that which we have given to it. It was the declared purpose of Congress to collate all the statutes as they were at that date, and not to make any change in their provisions. Obviously these exceptions were intended to remove doubts and misconstruction, which were known to have prevailed to some extent.

\n

The question whether these neckties were either "scarfs" or "ready-made clothing," was submitted to the jury, and

Page 90 U. S. 383

they must have found the negative as to both. We have proceeded upon the assumption that this finding was correct. We have no power to review it in this proceeding.

\n

Judgment reversed and the cause remanded to the circuit court with directions to award a venire de novo and proceed in conformity to this opinion.
