\n\t\t\t\tSyllabus\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tFILARSKY v. DELIA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 10\xe2\x80\x931018.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 17, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 17, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tRespondent Delia, a firefighter employed by the City of Rialto, California, missed work after becoming ill on the job. Suspicious of Delia\xe2\x80\x99s extended absence, the City hired a private investigation firm to conduct surveillance on him. When Delia was seen buying fiberglass insulation and other building supplies, the City initiated an internal affairs investigation. It hired petitioner Filarsky, a private attorney, to interview Delia. At the interview, which Delia\xe2\x80\x99s attorney and two fire department officials also attended, Delia acknowledged buying the supplies, but denied having done any work on his home. To verify Delia\xe2\x80\x99s claim, Filarsky asked Delia to allow a fire department official to enter his home and view the unused materials. When Delia refused, Filarsky ordered him to bring the materials out of his home for the official to see. This prompted Delia\xe2\x80\x99s attorney to threaten a civil rights action against the City and Filarsky. Nonetheless, after the interview concluded, officials followed Delia to his home, where he produced the materials. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDelia brought an action under \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 against the City, the Fire Department, Filarsky, and other individuals, alleging that the order to produce the building materials violated his Fourth and \n \nFourteenth Amendment rights. The District Court granted summary judgment to the individual defendants on the basis of qualified immunity. The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed with respect to all individual defendants except Filarsky, concluding that he was not entitled to seek qualified immunity because he was a private attorney, not a City employee.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0A private individual temporarily retained by the government to carry out its work is entitled to seek qualified immunity from suit under \xc2\xa71983. Pp. 4\xe2\x88\x9216.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0In determining whether the Court of Appeals made a valid distinction between City employees and Filarsky for qualified immunity purposes, this Court looks to the general principles of tort immunities and defenses applicable at common law, and the reasons the Court has afforded protection from suit under \xc2\xa71983. See Imbler v. Pachtman, \n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 409\n. The common law as it existed in 1871, when Congress enacted \xc2\xa71983, did not draw a distinction between full-time public servants and private individuals engaged in public service in according protection to those carrying out government responsibilities. Government at that time was smaller in both size and reach, had fewer responsibilities, and operated primarily at the local level. Government work was carried out to a significant extent by individ- uals who did not devote all their time to public duties, but instead pur- sued private callings as well. In according protection from suit to individuals doing the government\xe2\x80\x99s work, the common law did not draw distinctions based on the nature of a worker\xe2\x80\x99s engagement with the government. Indeed, examples of individuals receiving immunity for actions taken while engaged in public service on a temporary or occasional basis are as varied as the reach of government itself. Common law principles of immunity were incorporated into \xc2\xa71983 and should not be abrogated absent clear legislative intent. See Pulliam v. Allen, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522\n. Immunity under \xc2\xa71983 therefore should not vary depending on whether an individual working for the government does so as a permanent or full-time employee, or on some other basis. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Nothing about the reasons this Court has given for recognizing immunity under \xc2\xa71983 counsels against carrying forward the common law rule. First, the government interest in avoiding \xe2\x80\x9cunwarranted timidity\xe2\x80\x9d on the part of those engaged in the public\xe2\x80\x99s business\xe2\x80\x94 which has been called \xe2\x80\x9cthe most important special government immunity-producing concern,\xe2\x80\x9d Richardson v. McKnight, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399\n\xe2\x80\x94is equally implicated regardless of whether the individual sued as a state actor works for the government full-time or on some other basis. Second, affording immunity to those acting on the government\xe2\x80\x99s behalf serves to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98ensure that talented candidates [are] not deterred by the threat of damages suits from entering public service.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 408. The government, in need of specialized knowledge or expertise, may look outside its permanent workforce to secure the services of private individuals. But because those individuals are free to choose other work that would not expose them to liability for government actions, the most talented candidates might decline public engagements if they did not receive the same immunity enjoyed by their public employee counterparts. Third, the public interest in ensuring performance of government duties free from the distractions that can accompany lawsuits is implicated whether those duties are discharged by private individuals or permanent government employees. Finally, distinguishing among those who carry out the public\xe2\x80\x99s business based on their particular relationship with the government creates significant line-drawing problems and can deprive state actors of the ability to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonably anticipate when their conduct may give rise to liability for damages,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Creighton, \n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635\n. Pp. 11\xe2\x88\x9213.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0This conclusion is not contrary to Wyatt v. Cole, \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 158\n, or Richardson v. McKnight, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399\n. Wyatt did not implicate the reasons underlying recognition of qualified immunity because the defendant in that case had no connection to government and pursued purely private ends. Richardson involved the unusual circumstances of prison guards employed by a private company who worked in a privately run prison facility. Nothing of the sort is involved here, or in the typical case of an individual hired by the government to assist in carrying out its work. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x88\x9215.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t621 F.\xc2\xa03d 1069, reversed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tSteve A. Filarsky, PETITIONER v. Nicholas B. Delia\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[April 17, 2012]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 1983 provides a cause of action against state actors who violate an individual\xe2\x80\x99s rights under federal law. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. At common law, those who carried out the work of government enjoyed various protections from liability when doing so, in order to allow them to serve the government without undue fear of personal exposure. Our decisions have looked to these common law protections in affording either absolute or qualified immunity to individuals sued under \xc2\xa71983. The question in this case is whether an individual hired by the government to do its work is prohibited from seeking such immunity, solely be- cause he works for the government on something other than a permanent or full-time basis.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNicholas Delia, a firefighter employed by the City of Rialto, California, became ill while responding to a toxic spill in August 2006. Under a doctor\xe2\x80\x99s orders, Delia missed three weeks of work. The City became suspicious of Delia\xe2\x80\x99s extended absence, and hired a private investi- gation firm to conduct surveillance on him. The private investigators observed Delia purchasing building supplies\xe2\x80\x94 including several rolls of fiberglass insulation\xe2\x80\x94from a home improvement store. The City surmised that Delia was missing work to do construction on his home rather than because of illness, and it initiated a formal internal affairs investigation of him.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDelia was ordered to appear for an administrative in- vestigation interview. The City hired Steve Filarsky to conduct the interview. Filarsky was an experienced employ- ment lawyer who had previously represented the City in several investigations. Delia and his attorney attended the interview, along with Filarsky and two fire department officials, Mike Peel and Frank Bekker. During the interview, Filarsky questioned Delia about the building sup- plies. Delia acknowledged that he had purchased the supplies, but claimed that he had not yet done the work on his home.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDuring a break, Filarsky met with Peel, Bekker, and Fire Chief Stephen Wells. Filarsky proposed resolving the investigation by verifying Delia\xe2\x80\x99s claim that he had not done any work on his home. To do so, Filarsky recommended asking Delia to produce the building materials. Chief Wells approved the plan.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen the meeting resumed, Filarsky requested permission for Peel to enter Delia\xe2\x80\x99s home to view the materials. On the advice of counsel, Delia refused. Filarsky then asked Delia if he would be willing to bring the materials out onto his lawn, so that Peel could observe them without entering his home. Delia again refused to consent. Un- able to obtain Delia\xe2\x80\x99s cooperation, Filarsky ordered him to produce the materials for inspection.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDelia\xe2\x80\x99s counsel objected to the order, asserting that it would violate the \n \nFourth Amendment. When that objection proved unavailing, Delia\xe2\x80\x99s counsel threatened to sue the City. He went on to tell Filarsky that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e might quite possibly find a way to figure if we can name you Mr. Filarsky.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. If you want to take that chance, you go right ahead.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 131\xe2\x80\x93132. The threat was repeated over and over: \xe2\x80\x9c[E]verybody is going to get named, and they are going to sweat it out as to whether or not they have individual liability .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[Y]ou order him and you will be named and that is not an idle threat.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cWhoever issues that order is going to be named in the lawsuit.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[W]e will seek any and all damages including individual liability .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [W]e are coming if you order this.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[M]ake sure the spell- ing is clear [in the order] so we know who to sue.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 134\xe2\x80\x93136, 148\xe2\x80\x93149. Despite these threats, Filarsky prepared an order directing Delia to produce the materials, which Chief Wells signed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs soon as the interview concluded, Peel and Bekker followed Delia to his home. Once there, Delia, his attorney, and a union representative went into Delia\xe2\x80\x99s house, brought out the four rolls of insulation, and placed them on Delia\xe2\x80\x99s lawn. Peel and Bekker, who remained in their car during this process, thanked Delia for showing them the insulation and drove off.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDelia brought an action under \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 against the City, its Fire Department, Chief Wells, Peel, Bekker, Filarsky, and ten unidentified individuals, alleging that the order to produce the building materials violated his rights under the Fourth and \n \nFourteenth Amendments. The District Court granted summary judgment to all the individual defendants, concluding that they were pro- tected by qualified immunity. The court held that Delia had \xe2\x80\x9cnot demonstrated a violation of a clearly established constitutional right,\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9cDelia was not threatened with insubordination or termination if he did not comply with any order given and none of these defendants entered [his] house.\xe2\x80\x9d Delia v. Rialto, No. CV 08\xe2\x80\x9303359 (CD Cal., Mar. 9, 2009), App. to Pet. for Cert. 42, 48.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed with respect to all defendants except Filarsky. The Court of Appeals concluded that the order violated the \n \nFourth Amendment, but agreed with the District Court that Delia \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] not demonstrated that a constitutional right was clearly established as of the date of Chief Wells\xe2\x80\x99s order, such that defendants would have known that their actions were unlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d Delia v. Rialto, 621 F.\xc2\xa03d 1069, 1079 (2010). As to Filarsky, however, the court concluded that because he was a private attorney and not a City employee, he was not entitled to seek the protection of qualified immunity. Id., at 1080\xe2\x80\x931081. The court noted that its decision conflicted with a decision of the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit, see Cullinan v. Abramson, 128 F.\xc2\xa03d 301, 310 (1997), but considered itself bound by Circuit precedent and therefore \xe2\x80\x9cnot free to follow the Cullinan decision.\xe2\x80\x9d 621 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1080 (citing Gonzalez v. Spencer, 336 F. 3d 832 (CA9 2003)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFilarsky filed a petition for certiorari, which we granted. 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 1983 provides a cause of action against any person who deprives an individual of federally guaranteed rights \xe2\x80\x9cunder color\xe2\x80\x9d of state law. \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. Anyone whose conduct is \xe2\x80\x9cfairly attributable to the state\xe2\x80\x9d can be sued as a state actor under \xc2\xa71983. See Lugar v. Edmondson Oil Co., \n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 922, \n937 (1982)\n. At common law, government actors were afforded certain protections from liability, based on the reasoning that \xe2\x80\x9cthe public good can best be secured by allowing officers charged with the duty of deciding upon the rights of others, to act upon their own free, unbiased convictions, uninfluenced by any apprehensions.\xe2\x80\x9d Wasson v. Mitchell, 18 Iowa 153, 155\xe2\x80\x93156 (1864) (internal quotation marks omitted); see also W. Prosser, Law of Torts \xc2\xa725, p. 150 (1941) (common law protections derived from the need to avoid the \xe2\x80\x9cimpossible burden [that] would fall upon all our agencies of government\xe2\x80\x9d if those acting on behalf of the government were \xe2\x80\x9cunduly hampered and intimidated in the discharge of their duties\xe2\x80\x9d by a fear of personal liability). Our decisions have recognized similar immunities under \xc2\xa71983, reasoning that common law protections \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98well grounded in his- tory and reason\xe2\x80\x99 had not been abrogated \xe2\x80\x98by covert inclusion in the general language\xe2\x80\x99 of \xc2\xa71983.\xe2\x80\x9d Imbler v. Pachtman, \n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 409, \n418 (1976)\n (quoting Tenney v. Brandhove, \n\n341 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367, \n376 (1951)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, there is no dispute that qualified immunity is available for the sort of investigative activities at issue. See Pearson v. Callahan, \n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 223\n\xe2\x80\x93244 (2009). The Court of Appeals granted this protection to Chief Wells, Peel, and Bekker, but denied it to Filarsky, because he was not a public employee but was instead a private individual \xe2\x80\x9cretained by the City to participate in internal affairs investigations.\xe2\x80\x9d 621 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1079\xe2\x80\x931080. In determining whether this distinction is valid, we look to the \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral principles of tort immunities and defenses\xe2\x80\x9d applicable at common law, and the reasons we have afforded protection from suit under \xc2\xa71983. Imbler, supra, at 418.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder our precedent, the inquiry begins with the common law as it existed when Congress passed \xc2\xa71983 in 1871. Tower v. Glover, \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 914, \n920 (1984)\n. Understanding the protections the common law afforded to those exercising government power in 1871 requires an appreciation of the nature of government at that time. In the mid-nineteenth century, government was smaller in both size and reach. It had fewer responsibilities, and operated primarily at the local level. Local governments faced tight budget constraints, and generally had neither the need nor the ability to maintain an established bureaucracy staffed by professionals. See B. Campbell, The Growth of American Government: Governance From the Cleveland Era to the Present 14\xe2\x80\x9316, 20\xe2\x80\x9321 (1995); id., at 20 (noting that in the 1880s \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he governor\xe2\x80\x99s office staff in Wisconsin .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. totaled five workers if we count the lieutenant governor and the janitor\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs one commentator has observed, there was at that time \xe2\x80\x9cno very clear conception of a professional office, that is, an office the incumbent of which devotes his entire time to the discharge of public functions, who has no other occupation, and who receives a sufficiently large compensation to enable him to live without resorting to other means.\xe2\x80\x9d F. Goodnow, Principles of the Administrative Law of the United States 227 (1905). Instead, to a significant extent, government was \xe2\x80\x9cadministered by members of society who temporarily or occasionally discharge[d] public functions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 228. Whether government relied primarily upon professionals or occasional workers obviously varied across the country and across different government functions. But even at the turn of the twentieth century, a public servant was often one who \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not devote his entire time to his public duties, but is, at the same time that he is holding public office, permitted to carry on some other regular business, and as a matter of fact finds his main means of support in such business or in his private means since he receives from his office a compensation insufficient to support him.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 227.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPrivate citizens were actively involved in government work, especially where the work most directly touched the lives of the people. It was not unusual, for example, to see the owner of the local general store step behind a window in his shop to don his postman\xe2\x80\x99s hat. See, e.g., Stole Stamps, Maysville, KY, The Evening Bulletin, p.\xc2\xa01, Sept. 25, 1895 (reporting that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he post office and general store at Mount Hope was broken into,\xe2\x80\x9d resulting in the loss of $400 worth of cutlery and stamps). Nor would it have been a surprise to find, on a trip to the docks, the local ferryman collecting harbor fees as public wharfmaster. See 3 E. Johnson, A History of Kentucky and Kentuckians 1346 (1912).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven such a core government activity as criminal prosecution was often carried out by a mixture of public employees and private individuals temporarily serving the public. At the time \xc2\xa71983 was enacted, private lawyers were regularly engaged to conduct criminal prosecutions on behalf of the State. See, e.g., Commonwealth v. Gibbs, 70 Mass. 146 (1855); White v. Polk County, 17 Iowa 413 (1864). Abraham Lincoln himself accepted several such appointments. See, e.g., An Awful Crime and Speedy Punishment, Springfield Daily Register, May 14, 1853 (reporting that \xe2\x80\x9cA. Lincoln, esq. was appointed prosecutor\xe2\x80\x9d in a rape case). In addition, private lawyers often assisted public prosecutors in significant cases. See, e.g., Commonwealth v. Knapp, 10 Mass. 477, 490\xe2\x80\x93491 (1830); Chambers v. State, 22 Tenn. 237 (1842). And public prosecutors themselves continued to represent private clients while in office\xe2\x80\x94sometimes creating odd conflicts of interest. See People v. Bussey, 82 Mich. 49, 46 N.\xc2\xa0W. 97, 98 (1890) (public prosecutor employed as private counsel by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s wife in several civil suits against the defendant); Phillip v. Waller, 5 Haw. 609, 617 (1886) (public prosecutor represented plaintiff in a suit for malicious prosecution); Oliver v. Pate, 43 Ind. 132, 139 (1873) (public prosecutor who conducted a state prosecution against a defendant later served as counsel for the defendant in a malicious prosecution suit against the complaining witness).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis mixture of public responsibility and private pursuits extended even to the highest levels of government. Until the position became full-time in 1853, for example, the Attorney General of the United States was expected to and did maintain an active private law practice. To cite a notable illustration, in Hayburn\xe2\x80\x99s Case, 2 Dall. 409 (1792), the first Attorney General, Edmund Randolph, sought a writ of mandamus from this Court to compel a lower court to hear William Hayburn\xe2\x80\x99s petition to be put on the pension list. When this Court did not allow the Attorney General to seek the writ in his official capacity, Randolph readily solved the problem by arguing the case as Hayburn\xe2\x80\x99s private lawyer. Ibid.; see also Letter from Edmund Randolph to James Madison (Aug. 12, 1792), reprinted in 14 The Papers of James Madison 348, 349 (R. Rutland, T. Mason, R. Brugger, J. Sisson, & F. Teute eds. 1983); Bloch, The Early Role of the Attorney General in Our Constitutional Scheme: In the Beginning There Was Pragmatism, 1989 Duke L. J. 561, 598\xe2\x80\x93599, n. 121, 619.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGiven all this, it should come as no surprise that the common law did not draw a distinction between public servants and private individuals engaged in public service in according protection to those carrying out government responsibilities. Government actors involved in adjudicative activities, for example, were protected by an absolute immunity from suit. See Bradley v. Fisher, 13 Wall. 335, 347\xe2\x80\x93348 (1872); J. Bishop, Commentaries on the Non-Contract Law \xc2\xa7781 (1889). This immunity applied equally to \xe2\x80\x9cthe highest judge in the State or nation\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe lowest officer who sits as a court and tries petty causes,\xe2\x80\x9d T. Cooley, Law of Torts 409 (1879), including those who served as judges on a part-time or episodic basis. Justices of the peace, for example, often maintained active private law practices (or even had nonlegal livelihoods), and generally served in a judicial capacity only part-time. See Hubbell v. Harbeck, 54 Hun. 147, 7 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0S. 243 (1889); Ingraham v. Leland, 19 Vt. 304 (1847). In fact, justices of the peace were not even paid a salary by the government, but instead received compensation through fees payable by the parties that came before them. See W. Murfee, The Justice of the Peace \xc2\xa71145 (1886). Yet the common law extended the same immunity \xe2\x80\x9cto a justice of the peace as to any other judicial officer.\xe2\x80\x9d Pratt v. Gardner, 56 Mass. 63, 70 (1848); see also Mangold v. Thorpe, 33 N.\xc2\xa0J.\xc2\xa0L. 134, 137\xe2\x80\x93138 (1868).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe common law also extended certain protections to individuals engaged in law enforcement activities, such as sheriffs and constables. At the time \xc2\xa71983 was enacted, however, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he line between public and private policing was frequently hazy. Private detectives and privately em- ployed patrol personnel often were publicly appointed as special policemen, and the means and objects of detective work, in particular, made it difficult to distinguish between those on the public payroll and private detectives.\xe2\x80\x9d Sklansky, The Private Police, 46 UCLA L. Rev. 1165, 1210 (1999) (footnotes and internal quotation marks omitted). The protections provided by the common law did not turn on whether someone we today would call a police officer worked for the government full-time or instead for both public and private employers. Rather, at common law, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] special constable, duly appointed according to law, ha[d] all the powers of a regular constable so far as may be necessary for the proper discharge of the special duties intrusted to him, and in the lawful discharge of those duties, [was] as fully protected as any other officer.\xe2\x80\x9d W. Murfee, A Treatise on the Law of Sheriffs and Other Ministerial Officers \xc2\xa71121, p. 609 (1884).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSheriffs executing a warrant were empowered by the common law to enlist the aid of the able-bodied men of the community in doing so. See 1 W. Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England 332 (1765); In\xc2\xa0re Quarles, \n\n158 U.\xc2\xa0S. 532, \n535 (1895)\n. While serving as part of this \xe2\x80\x9cposse comitatus,\xe2\x80\x9d a private individual had the same authority as the sheriff, and was protected to the same extent. See, e.g., Robinson v. State, 93 Ga. 77, 18 S.\xc2\xa0E. 1018, 1019 (1893) (\xe2\x80\x9cA member of a posse comitatus summoned by the sheriff to aid in the execution of a warrant for a felony in the sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s hands is entitled to the same protection in the discharge of his duties as the sheriff himself\xe2\x80\x9d); State v. Mooring, 115 N.\xc2\xa0C. 709, 20 S.\xc2\xa0E. 182 (1894) (considering it \xe2\x80\x9cwell settled by the courts\xe2\x80\x9d that a sheriff may break open the doors of a house to execute a search warrant and that \xe2\x80\x9cif he act in good faith in doing so, both he and his posse comitatus will be protected\xe2\x80\x9d); North Carolina v. Gosnell, 74 F. 734, 738\xe2\x80\x93739 (CC WDNC 1896) (\xe2\x80\x9cBoth judicial and ministerial officers, in the execution of the duties of their office, are under the strong protection of the law; and their legally summoned assistants, for such time as in service, are officers of the law\xe2\x80\x9d); Reed v. Rice, 25 Ky. 44, 46\xe2\x80\x9347 (App. 1829) (private individuals summoned by a constable to execute a search warrant were protected from a suit based on the invalidity of the warrant).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, examples of individuals receiving immunity for actions taken while engaged in public service on a temporary or occasional basis are as varied as the reach of government itself. See, e.g., Gregory v. Brooks, 37 Conn. 365, 372 (1870) (public wharfmaster not liable for ordering re- moval of a vessel unless the order was issued maliciously); Henderson v. Smith, 26 W.\xc2\xa0Va. 829, 836\xe2\x80\x93838 (1885) (notaries public given immunity for discretionary acts taken in good faith); Chamberlain v. Clayton, 56 Iowa 331, 9 N.\xc2\xa0W. 237 (1881) (trustees of a public institution for the disabled not liable absent a showing of malice); McCormick v. Burt, 95 Ill. 263, 265\xe2\x80\x93266 (1880) (school board members not liable for suspending a student in good faith); Donohue v. Richards, 38 Me. 379, 392 (1854) (same); Downer v. Lent, 6 Cal. 94, 95 (1856) (members of a Board of Pilot Commissioners given immunity for official acts); Rail v. Potts & Baker, 27 Tenn. 225, 228\xe2\x80\x93230 (1847) (private individuals appointed by the sheriff to serve as judges of an election were not liable for refusing a voter absent a showing of malice); Jenkins v. Waldron, 11 Johns. 114, 120\xe2\x80\x93121 (NY Sup. Ct. 1814) (same).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe read \xc2\xa71983 \xe2\x80\x9cin harmony with general principles of tort immunities and defenses.\xe2\x80\x9d Imbler, 424 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 418. And we \xe2\x80\x9cproceed[ ] on the assumption that common-law principles of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. immunity were incorporated into our judicial system and that they should not be abrogated absent clear legislative intent to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d Pulliam v. Allen, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 522, \n529 (1984)\n. Under this assumption, immu- nity under \xc2\xa71983 should not vary depending on whether an individual working for the government does so as a full-time employee, or on some other basis.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNothing about the reasons we have given for recognizing immunity under \xc2\xa71983 counsels against carrying forward the common law rule. As we have explained, such immu- nity \xe2\x80\x9cprotect[s] government\xe2\x80\x99s ability to perform its traditional functions.\xe2\x80\x9d Wyatt v. Cole, \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 158, \n167 (1992)\n. It does so by helping to avoid \xe2\x80\x9cunwarranted timidity\xe2\x80\x9d in performance of public duties, ensuring that talented candidates are not deterred from public service, and preventing the harmful distractions from carrying out the work of government that can often accompany damages suits. Richardson v. McKnight, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399\n\xe2\x80\x93411 (1997).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have called the government interest in avoiding \xe2\x80\x9cunwarranted timidity\xe2\x80\x9d on the part of those engaged in the public\xe2\x80\x99s business \xe2\x80\x9cthe most important special government immunity-producing concern.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 409. Ensuring that those who serve the government do so \xe2\x80\x9cwith the decisiveness and the judgment required by the public good,\xe2\x80\x9d Scheuer v. Rhodes, \n\n416 U.\xc2\xa0S. 232, \n240 (1974)\n, is of vital importance regardless whether the individual sued as a state actor works full-time or on some other basis.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAffording immunity not only to public employees but also to others acting on behalf of the government similarly serves to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98ensure that talented candidates [are] not deterred by the threat of damages suits from entering public service.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Richardson, supra, at 408 (quoting Wyatt, supra, at 167). The government\xe2\x80\x99s need to attract talented individuals is not limited to full-time public employees. Indeed, it is often when there is a particular need for specialized knowledge or expertise that the government must look outside its permanent work force to secure the services of private individuals. This case is a good example: Filarsky had 29 years of specialized experience as an attorney in labor, employment, and personnel matters, with particular expertise in conducting internal affairs investigations. App. to Pet. for Cert. 59, 89; App. 156. The City of Rialto certainly had no permanent employee with anything approaching those qualifications. To the extent such private individuals do not depend on the government for their livelihood, they have freedom to select other work\xe2\x80\x94work that will not expose them to liability for government actions. This makes it more likely that the most talented candidates will decline public en- gagements if they do not receive the same immunity enjoyed by their public employee counterparts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSometimes, as in this case, private individuals will work in close coordination with public employees, and face threatened legal action for the same conduct. See App. 134 (Delia\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer: \xe2\x80\x9ceverybody is going to get named\xe2\x80\x9d in threatened suit). Because government employees will often be protected from suit by some form of immunity, those working alongside them could be left holding the bag\xe2\x80\x94facing full liability for actions taken in conjunction with government employees who enjoy immunity for the same activity. Under such circumstances, any private individual with a choice might think twice before accepting a government assignment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe public interest in ensuring performance of government duties free from the distractions that can accompany even routine lawsuits is also implicated when individuals other than permanent government employees discharge these duties. See Richardson, supra, at 411. Not only will such individuals\xe2\x80\x99 performance of any ongoing government responsibilities suffer from the distraction of lawsuits, but such distractions will also often affect any public employees with whom they work by embroiling those employees in litigation. This case is again a good example: If the suit against Filarsky moves forward, it is highly likely that Chief Wells, Bekker, and Peel will all be required to tes- tify, given their roles in the dispute. Allowing suit under \xc2\xa71983 against private individuals assisting the government will substantially undermine an important reason immunity is accorded public employees in the first place.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDistinguishing among those who carry out the public\xe2\x80\x99s business based on the nature of their particular relationship with the government also creates significant line-drawing problems. It is unclear, for example, how Fil- arsky would be categorized if he regularly spent half his time working for the City, or worked exclusively on one City project for an entire year. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 34\xe2\x80\x9336. Such questions deprive state actors of the ability to \xe2\x80\x9creasonably anticipate when their conduct may give rise to liability for damages,\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Creighton, \n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635, \n646 (1987)\n (alteration and internal quotation marks omitted), frustrating the purposes immunity is meant to serve. An uncertain immunity is little better than no immunity at all.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur decisions in Wyatt v. Cole, \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 158 (1992)\n, and Richardson v. McKnight, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399 (1997)\n, are not to the contrary. In Wyatt, we held that individuals who used a state replevin law to compel the local sheriff to seize disputed property from a former business partner were not entitled to seek qualified immunity. Cf. Lugar, \n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 922\n (holding that an individual who uses a state replevin, garnishment, or attachment statute later declared to be unconstitutional acts under color of state law for purposes of \xc2\xa71983). We explained that the reasons underlying recognition of qualified immunity did not sup- port its extension to individuals who had no connection to government and pursued purely private ends. Because such individuals \xe2\x80\x9chold no office requiring them to exercise discretion; nor are they principally concerned with enhancing the public good,\xe2\x80\x9d we concluded that extending immunity to them would \xe2\x80\x9chave no bearing on whether public officials are able to act forcefully and decisively in their jobs or on whether qualified applicants enter public service.\xe2\x80\x9d 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 168.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWyatt is plainly not implicated by the circumstances of this case. Unlike the defendants in Wyatt, who were us- ing the mechanisms of government to achieve their own ends, individuals working for the government in pursuit of government objectives are \xe2\x80\x9cprincipally concerned with en- hancing the public good.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Whether such individ- uals have assurance that they will be able to seek protection if sued under \xc2\xa71983 directly affects the government\xe2\x80\x99s ability to achieve its objectives through their public service. Put simply, Wyatt involved no government agents, no government interests, and no government need for immunity.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Richardson, we considered whether guards employed by a privately run prison facility could seek the protection of qualified immunity. Although the Court had previously determined that public-employee prison guards were entitled to qualified immunity, see Procunier v. Navarette, \n\n434 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555 (1978)\n, it determined that prison guards employed by a private company and working in a privately run prison facility did not enjoy the same protection. We explained that the various incentives characteristic of the private market in that case ensured that the guards would not perform their public duties with unwarranted timidity or be deterred from entering that line of work. 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 410\xe2\x80\x93411.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRichardson was a self-consciously \xe2\x80\x9cnarrow[ ]\xe2\x80\x9d decision. Id., at 413 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e have answered the immunity question narrowly, in the context in which it arose\xe2\x80\x9d). The Court made clear that its holding was not meant to foreclose all claims of immunity by private individuals. Ibid. Instead, the Court emphasized that the particular circumstances of that case\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ca private firm, systematically organized to assume a major lengthy administrative task (managing an institution) with limited direct supervision by the government, undertak[ing] that task for profit and potentially in competition with other firms\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94combined sufficiently to mitigate the concerns underlying recognition of governmental immunity under \xc2\xa71983. Ibid. Nothing of the sort is involved here, or in the typical case of an individual hired by the government to assist in carrying out its work.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA straightforward application of the rule set out above is sufficient to resolve this case. Though not a public em- ployee, Filarsky was retained by the City to assist in conducting an official investigation into potential wrongdoing. There is no dispute that government employees performing such work are entitled to seek the protection of qualified immunity. The Court of Appeals rejected Filarsky\xe2\x80\x99s claim to the protection accorded Wells, Bekker, and Peel solely because he was not a permanent, full-time employee of the City. The common law, however, did not draw such distinctions, and we see no justification for doing so under \xc2\xa71983.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNew York City has a Department of Investigation staffed by full-time public employees who investigate city personnel, and the resources to pay for it. The City of Rialto has neither, and so must rely on the occasional services of private individuals such as Mr. Filarsky. There is no reason Rialto\xe2\x80\x99s internal affairs investigator should be denied the qualified immunity enjoyed by the ones who work for New York.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn light of the foregoing, the judgment of the Court of Appeals denying qualified immunity to Filarsky is reversed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIt is so ordered.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Ginsburg, concurring.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court addresses a sole question in this case: Is a private attorney retained by a municipality to investigate a personnel matter eligible for qualified immunity in a suit under \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 alleging a constitutional violation committed in the course of the investigation? I agree that the answer is yes and that the judgment of the Court of Appeals holding private attorney Filarsky categorically ineligible for qualified immunity must be re- versed. Qualified immunity may be overcome, however, if the defendant knew or should have known that his conduct violated a right \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of the episode in suit. See Harlow v. Fitzgerald, \n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800, \n818 (1982)\n. Because the Ninth Circuit did not con- sider the application of that standard to Filarsky, the matter, as I see it, may be pursued on remand.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFilarsky was retained by the City of Rialto to investigate whether city firefighter Delia was taking time off from work under the false pretense of a disabling physical condition. In pursuit of the investigation, Filarsky asked Delia to consent to a search of his home to determine what Delia had done with several rolls of insulation he had recently purchased at a home improvement store. When Delia, on counsel\xe2\x80\x99s advice, refused to consent to the search, Filarsky \xe2\x80\x9chatch[ed] a plan\xe2\x80\x9d to overcome Delia\xe2\x80\x99s resistance. Delia v. Rialto, 621 F.\xc2\xa03d 1069, 1077 (CA9 2010). \xe2\x80\x9c[W]e will do it a different way,\xe2\x80\x9d Filarsky informed Delia. App. 129; see 621 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1077 (\xe2\x80\x9cUnable to obtain Delia\xe2\x80\x99s consent to a warrantless search of his house . . . , Filarsky tried a different tactic.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFollowing Filarsky\xe2\x80\x99s advice, Fire Chief Wells ordered Delia to bring the insulation out of his house and place the rolls on his lawn for inspection. App. 158. Filarsky recommended this course, the Ninth Circuit observed, mindful that \xe2\x80\x9can individual does not have an expectation of privacy in items exposed to the public, thereby eliminating the need for a search warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d 621 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1077. Delia complied with Chief Wells\xe2\x80\x99s order by producing the rolls, all of them unused, App. 78, 85, after which the investigation into the legitimacy of Delia\xe2\x80\x99s absence from work apparently ended.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn explaining why the individual defendants other than Filarsky were entitled to summary judgment on their qualified immunity pleas, the Ninth Circuit stated that \xe2\x80\x9cno . . . threat to [Delia\xe2\x80\x99s] employment\xe2\x80\x9d attended Fire Chief Wells\xe2\x80\x99s order. 621 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1079. The District Court similarly stated that \xe2\x80\x9cDelia was not threatened with insubordination or termination if he did not comply with [the] order.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 48.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThese statements are at odds with the facts, as re- counted by the Court of Appeals. \xe2\x80\x9cAt the onset of the in- terview,\xe2\x80\x9d the Ninth Circuit stressed, \xe2\x80\x9cFilarsky warned Delia that he was obligated to fully cooperate,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f at any time it is deemed you are not cooperating then you can be held to be insubordinate and subject to disciplinary action, up to and including termination.\xe2\x80\x9d 621 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1072 (internal quotation marks omitted). Continuing in this vein, the Court of Appeals concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cDelia\xe2\x80\x99s actions were involuntary and coerced by the direct threat of sanctions including loss of his firefighter position.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1077; see id., at 1085 (\xe2\x80\x9cDelia\xe2\x80\x99s actions were involun- tary and occurred as a result of the direct threat of sanctions[.]\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn further proceedings upon return of this case to the Court of Appeals, these questions bear attention. \n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals denied qualified immunity to Filarsky solely because, as retained outside counsel, he was not a formal employee of the City of Rialto. I agree with and join today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion holding that this distinction is not a sound basis on which to deny immunity.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI add only that it does not follow that every private individual who works for the government in some capacity necessarily may claim qualified immunity when sued under \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. Such individuals must satisfy our usual test for conferring immunity. As the Court explains, that test \xe2\x80\x9clook[s] to the \xe2\x80\x98general principles of tort immunities and defenses\xe2\x80\x99 applicable at common law, and the reasons we have afforded protection from suit under \xc2\xa71983.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5 (quoting Imbler v. Pachtman, \n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 409, \n418 (1976)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus in Richardson v. McKnight, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399 (1997)\n, we denied qualified immunity to prison guards who were privately employed, despite their quintessentially public function. We did so because we found \xe2\x80\x9cno special reasons significantly favoring an extension of governmental immunity\xe2\x80\x9d in that context. Id., at 412. We left open, how-ever, the question whether immunity would be appropriate for \xe2\x80\x9ca private individual briefly associated with a government body, serving as an adjunct to government in an essential governmental activity, or acting under close of- icial supervision.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 413.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFilarsky, supported by the United States as amicus curiae, contends that he fits into this coda because he worked in close coordination with and under the supervision of City employees. Whether Filarsky was supervised by those employees, and did not himself do the supervising, is unclear. But there is no doubt that Filarsky worked alongside the employees in investigating Delia. In such circumstances, I agree that Filarsky should be allowed to claim qualified immunity from a \xc2\xa71983 suit. As the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion persuasively explains, there is a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98firmly rooted\xe2\x80\x99 tradition of immunity\xe2\x80\x9d applicable to individuals who perform government work in capacities other than as formal employees. Id., at 404; see ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x9311. And conferring qualified immunity on individuals like Filarsky helps \xe2\x80\x9cprotec[t] government\xe2\x80\x99s ability to perform its traditional functions,\xe2\x80\x9d and thereby helps \xe2\x80\x9cprotect the public at large.\xe2\x80\x9d Wyatt v. Cole, \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 158\n\xe2\x80\x93168 (1992). When a private individual works closely with immune government employees, there is a real risk that the individual will be intimidated from performing his duties fully if he, and he alone, may bear the price of liability for collective conduct. See ante, at 12; see also ante, at 13 (noting distraction caused to immune public employees by \xc2\xa71983 litigation brought against nonimmune associates).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis does not mean that a private individual may assert qualified immunity only when working in close coordination with government employees. For example, Richardson\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that immunity is also appropriate for individuals \xe2\x80\x9cserving as an adjunct to government in an essential governmental activity,\xe2\x80\x9d 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 413, would seem to encompass modern-day special prosecutors and comparable individuals hired for their independence. There may yet be other circumstances in which immunity is warranted for private actors. The point is simply that such cases should be decided as they arise, as is our longstanding practice in the field of immunity law.\n\t\t\t
