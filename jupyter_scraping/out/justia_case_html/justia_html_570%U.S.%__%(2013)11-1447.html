\n\t\t\t\tKOONTZ v. ST.\xc2\xa0JOHNS RIVER WATER MANAGEMENT DISTRICT\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the supreme court of florida\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x931447.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 15, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 25, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tCoy Koontz, Sr., whose estate is represented here by petitioner, sought permits to develop a section of his property from respondent St. Johns River Water Management District (District), which, consistent with Florida law, requires permit applicants wishing to build on wetlands to offset the resulting environmental damage. Koontz offered to mitigate the environmental effects of his development proposal by deeding to the District a conservation easement on nearly three-quarters of his property. The District rejected Koontz\xe2\x80\x99s proposal and informed him that it would approve construction only if he (1) reduced the size of his development and, inter alia, deeded to the District a conservation easement on the resulting larger remainder of his property or (2) hired contractors to make improvements to District-owned wetlands several miles away. Believing the District\xe2\x80\x99s demands to be excessive in light of the environmental effects his proposal would have caused, Koontz filed suit under a state law that provides money damages for agency action that is an \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable exercise of the state\xe2\x80\x99s police power constituting a taking without just compensation.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe trial court found the District\xe2\x80\x99s actions unlawful because they failed the requirements of Nollan v. California Coastal Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, \n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825\n, and Dolan v. City of Tigard, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374\n. Those cases held that the government may not condition the approval of a land-use permit on the owner\xe2\x80\x99s relinquishment of a portion of his property unless there is a nexus and rough proportionality between the government\xe2\x80\x99s demand and the effects of the proposed land use. The District Court of Appeal affirmed, but the State Supreme Court reversed on two grounds. First, it held that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim failed because, unlike in Nollan or Dolan, the District denied the application. Second, the State Supreme Court held that a demand for money cannot give rise to a claim under Nollan and Dolan.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t1.\xc2\xa0The government\xe2\x80\x99s demand for property from a land-use permit applicant must satisfy the Nollan/Dolan requirements even when it denies the permit. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The unconstitutional conditions doctrine vindicates the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated rights by preventing the government from coercing people into giving them up, and Nollan and Dolan represent a special application of this doctrine that protects the \n \nFifth Amendment right to just compensation for property the government takes when owners apply for land-use permits. The standard set out in Nollan and Dolan reflects the danger of governmental coercion in this context while accommodating the government\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate need to offset the public costs of development through land use exactions. Dolan, supra, at 391; Nollan, supra, at 837. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The principles that undergird Nollan and Dolan do not change depending on whether the government approves a permit on the condition that the applicant turn over property or denies a permit because the applicant refuses to do so. Recognizing such a distinction would enable the government to evade the Nollan/Dolan limitations simply by phrasing its demands for property as conditions precedent to permit approval. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s unconstitutional conditions cases have long refused to attach significance to the distinction between conditions precedent and conditions subsequent. See, e.g., Frost & Frost Trucking Co. v. Railroad Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Cal., \n\n271 U.\xc2\xa0S. 583\n\xe2\x80\x93593. It makes no difference that no property was actually taken in this case. Extortionate demands for property in the land-use permitting context run afoul of the Takings Clause not because they take property but because they impermissibly burden the right not to have property taken without just compensation. Nor does it matter that the District might have been able to deny Koontz\xe2\x80\x99s application outright without giving him the option of securing a permit by agreeing to spend money improving public lands. It is settled that the unconstitutional conditions doctrine applies even when the government threatens to withhold a gratuitous benefit. See e.g., United States v. American Library Assn., Inc., \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 194\n. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0The District concedes that the denial of a permit could give rise to a valid Nollan/Dolan claim, but urges that this Court should not review this particular denial because Koontz sued in the wrong court, for the wrong remedy, and at the wrong time. Most of its arguments raise questions of state law. But to the extent that respondent alleges a federal obstacle to adjudication of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim, the Florida courts can consider respondent\xe2\x80\x99s arguments in the first instance on remand. Finally, the District errs in arguing that because it gave Koontz another avenue to obtain permit approval, this Court need not decide whether its demand for offsite improvements satisfied Nollan and Dolan. Had Koontz been offered at least one alternative that satisfied Nollan and Dolan, he would not have been subjected to an unconstitutional condition. But the District\xe2\x80\x99s offer to approve a less ambitious project does not obviate the need to apply Nollan and Dolan to the conditions it imposed on its approval of the project Koontz actually proposed. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t2.\xc2\xa0The government\xe2\x80\x99s demand for property from a land-use permit applicant must satisfy the Nollan/Dolan requirements even when its demand is for money. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9322.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Contrary to respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument, Eastern Enterprises v. Apfel, \n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 498\n, where five Justices concluded that the Takings Clause does not apply to government-imposed financial obligations that \xe2\x80\x9cd[o] not operate upon or alter an identified property interest,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 540 (Kennedy, J., concurring in judgment and dissenting in part), does not control here, where the demand for money did burden the ownership of a specific parcel of land. Because of the direct link between the government\xe2\x80\x99s demand and a specific parcel of real property, this case implicates the central concern of Nollan and Dolan: the risk that the government may deploy its substantial power and discretion in land-use permitting to pursue governmental ends that lack an essential nexus and rough proportionality to the effects of the proposed use of the property at issue. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The District argues that if monetary exactions are subject to Nollan/Dolan scrutiny, then there will be no principled way of distinguishing impermissible land-use exactions from property taxes. But the District exaggerates both the extent to which that problem is unique to the land-use permitting context and the practical difficulty of distinguishing between the power to tax and the power to take by eminent domain. It is beyond dispute that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]axes and user fees .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are not \xe2\x80\x98takings,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brown v. Legal Foundation of Wash., \n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 216\n, n.\xc2\xa02, yet this Court has repeatedly found takings where the government, by confiscating financial obligations, achieved a result that could have been obtained through taxation, e.g., id., at 232. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9321.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding that monetary exactions are subject to scrutiny under Nollan and Dolan will not work a revolution in land use law or unduly limit the discretion of local authorities to implement sensible land use regulations. The rule that Nollan and Dolan apply to monetary exactions has been the settled law in some of our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s most populous States for many years, and the protections of those cases are often redundant with the requirements of state law. Pp.\xc2\xa021\xe2\x80\x9322.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t77 So. 3d 1220, reversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tCOY A. KOONTZ, Jr., PETITIONER v. ST. JOHNS RIVER WATER MANAGEMENT DISTRICT\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the supreme court of florida\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[June 25, 2013]\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Alito delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur decisions in Nollan v. California Coastal Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, \n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825 (1987)\n, and Dolan v. City of Tigard, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374 (1994)\n, provide important protection against the misuse of the power of land-use regulation. In those cases, we held that a unit of government may not condition the approval of a land-use permit on the owner\xe2\x80\x99s relinquishment of a portion of his property unless there is a \xe2\x80\x9cnexus\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crough proportionality\xe2\x80\x9d between the government\xe2\x80\x99s demand and the effects of the proposed land use. In this case, the St. Johns River Water Management District (District) believes that it circumvented Nollan and Dolan because of the way in which it structured its handling of a permit application submitted by Coy Koontz, Sr., whose estate is represented in this Court by Coy Koontz, Jr.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The District did not approve his application on the condition that he surrender an interest in his land. Instead, the District, after suggesting that he could obtain approval by signing over such an interest, denied his application because he refused to yield. The Florida Supreme Court blessed this maneuver and thus effectively interred those important decisions. Because we conclude that Nollan and Dolan cannot be evaded in this way, the Florida Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision must be reversed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1972, petitioner purchased an undeveloped 14.9-acre tract of land on the south side of Florida State Road 50, a divided four-lane highway east of Orlando. The property is located less than 1,000 feet from that road\xe2\x80\x99s intersection with Florida State Road 408, a tolled expressway that is one of Orlando\xe2\x80\x99s major thoroughfares.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA drainage ditch runs along the property\xe2\x80\x99s western edge, and high-voltage power lines bisect it into northern and southern sections. The combined effect of the ditch, a 100foot wide area kept clear for the power lines, the highways, and other construction on nearby parcels is to isolate the northern section of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s property from any other undeveloped land. Although largely classified as wetlands by the State, the northern section drains well; the most significant standing water forms in ruts in an unpaved road used to access the power lines. The natural topography of the property\xe2\x80\x99s southern section is somewhat more diverse, with a small creek, forested uplands, and wetlands that sometimes have water as much as a foot deep. A wildlife survey found evidence of animals that often frequent developed areas: raccoons, rabbits, several species of bird, and a turtle. The record also indicates that the land may be a suitable habitat for opossums.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe same year that petitioner purchased his property, Florida enacted the Water Resources Act, which divided the State into five water management districts and authorized each district to regulate \xe2\x80\x9cconstruction that connects to, draws water from, drains water into, or is placed in or across the waters in the state.\xe2\x80\x9d 1972 Fla. Laws ch. 72\xe2\x80\x93299, pt. IV, \xc2\xa71(5), pp. 1115, 1116 (codified as amended at Fla. Stat. \xc2\xa7373.403(5) (2010)). Under the Act, a landowner wishing to undertake such construction must obtain from the relevant district a Management and Storage of Surface Water (MSSW) permit, which may impose \xe2\x80\x9csuch reasonable conditions\xe2\x80\x9d on the permit as are \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary to assure\xe2\x80\x9d that construction will \xe2\x80\x9cnot be harmful to the water resources of the district.\xe2\x80\x9d 1972 Fla. Laws \xc2\xa74(1), at 1118 (codified as amended at Fla. Stat. \xc2\xa7373.413(1)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1984, in an effort to protect the State\xe2\x80\x99s rapidly diminishing wetlands, the Florida Legislature passed the Warren S. Henderson Wetlands Protection Act, which made it illegal for anyone to \xe2\x80\x9cdredge or fill in, on, or over sur- face waters\xe2\x80\x9d without a Wetlands Resource Management (WRM) permit. 1984 Fla. Laws ch. 84\xe2\x80\x9379, pt. VIII, \xc2\xa7403.905(1), pp. 204\xe2\x80\x93205. Under the Henderson Act, permit applicants are required to provide \xe2\x80\x9creasonable assurance\xe2\x80\x9d that proposed construction on wetlands is \xe2\x80\x9cnot contrary to the public interest,\xe2\x80\x9d as defined by an enumerated list of criteria. See Fla. Stat. \xc2\xa7373.414(1). Consistent with the Henderson Act, the St. Johns River Water Management District, the district with jurisdiction over petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s land, requires that permit applicants wishing to build on wetlands offset the resulting environmental damage by creating, enhancing, or preserving wetlands elsewhere.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner decided to develop the 3.7-acre northern section of his property, and in 1994 he applied to the District for MSSW and WRM permits. Under his proposal, petitioner would have raised the elevation of the northernmost section of his land to make it suitable for a building, graded the land from the southern edge of the building site down to the elevation of the high-voltage electrical lines, and installed a dry-bed pond for retaining and gradually releasing stormwater runoff from the building and its parking lot. To mitigate the environmental effects of his proposal, petitioner offered to foreclose any possible future development of the approximately 11acre southern section of his land by deeding to the District a conservation easement on that portion of his property.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District considered the 11acre conservation easement to be inadequate, and it informed petitioner that it would approve construction only if he agreed to one of two concessions. First, the District proposed that petitioner reduce the size of his development to 1 acre and deed to the District a conservation easement on the remaining 13.9 acres. To reduce the development area, the District suggested that petitioner could eliminate the dry-bed pond from his proposal and instead install a more costly subsurface stormwater management system beneath the building site. The District also suggested that petitioner install retaining walls rather than gradually sloping the land from the building site down to the elevation of the rest of his property to the south.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the alternative, the District told petitioner that he could proceed with the development as proposed, build- ing on 3.7 acres and deeding a conservation easement to the government on the remainder of the property, if he also agreed to hire contractors to make improvements to District-owned land several miles away. Specifically, peti- tioner could pay to replace culverts on one parcel or fill in ditches on another. Either of those projects would have enhanced approximately 50 acres of District-owned wetlands. When the District asks permit applicants to fund offsite mitigation work, its policy is never to require any particular offsite project, and it did not do so here. Instead, the District said that it \xe2\x80\x9cwould also favorably consider\xe2\x80\x9d alternatives to its suggested offsite mitigation projects if petitioner proposed something \xe2\x80\x9cequivalent.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 75.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBelieving the District\xe2\x80\x99s demands for mitigation to be excessive in light of the environmental effects that his building proposal would have caused, petitioner filed suit in state court. Among other claims, he argued that he was entitled to relief under Fla. Stat. \xc2\xa7373.617(2), which allows owners to recover \xe2\x80\x9cmonetary damages\xe2\x80\x9d if a state agency\xe2\x80\x99s action is \xe2\x80\x9can unreasonable exercise of the state\xe2\x80\x99s police power constituting a taking without just compensation.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Florida Circuit Court granted the District\xe2\x80\x99s mo- tion to dismiss on the ground that petitioner had not ade- quately exhausted his state-administrative remedies, but the Florida District Court of Appeal for the Fifth Circuit re- versed. On remand, the State Circuit Court held a 2-day bench trial. After considering testimony from several ex- perts who examined petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s property, the trial court found that the property\xe2\x80\x99s northern section had already been \xe2\x80\x9cseriously degraded\xe2\x80\x9d by extensive construction on the surrounding parcels. App. to Pet. for Cert. D\xe2\x80\x933. In light of this finding and petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s offer to dedicate nearly three-quarters of his land to the District, the trial court concluded that any further mitigation in the form of payment for offsite improvements to District property lacked both a nexus and rough proportionality to the environmental impact of the proposed construction. Id., at D\xe2\x80\x9311. It accordingly held the District\xe2\x80\x99s actions unlawful under our decisions in Nollan and Dolan.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Florida District Court affirmed, 5 So.\xc2\xa03d 8 (2009), but the State Supreme Court reversed, 77 So.\xc2\xa03d 1220 (2011). A majority of that court distinguished Nollan and Dolan on two grounds. First, the majority thought it significant that in this case, unlike Nollan or Dolan, the District did not approve petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s application on the condition that he accede to the District\xe2\x80\x99s demands; in- stead, the District denied his application because he refused to make concessions. 77 So.\xc2\xa03d, at 1230. Second, the majority drew a distinction between a demand for an interest in real property (what happened in Nollan and Dolan) and a demand for money. 77 So.\xc2\xa03d, at 1229\xe2\x80\x93 1230. The majority acknowledged a division of authority over whether a demand for money can give rise to a claim under Nollan and Dolan, and sided with those courts that have said it cannot. 77 So.\xc2\xa03d, at 1229\xe2\x80\x931230. Compare, e.g., McClung v. Sumner, 548 F.\xc2\xa03d 1219, 1228 (CA9 2008), with Ehrlich v. Culver City, 12 Cal. 4th 854, 876, 911 P.\xc2\xa02d 429, 444 (1996); Flower Mound v. Stafford Estates Ltd. Partnership, 135 S.\xc2\xa0W.\xc2\xa03d 620, 640\xe2\x80\x93641 (Tex. 2004). Two justices concurred in the result, arguing that petitioner had failed to exhaust his administrative remedies as re- quired by state law before bringing an inverse condem- nation suit that challenges the propriety of an agency action. 77 So.\xc2\xa03d, at 1231\xe2\x80\x931232; see Key Haven Associated Enterprises, Inc. v. Board of Trustees of Internal Improvement Trust Fund, 427 So.\xc2\xa02d 153, 159 (Fla. 1982).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRecognizing that the majority opinion rested on a question of federal constitutional law on which the lower courts are divided, we granted the petition for a writ of certiorari, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012), and now reverse.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have said in a variety of contexts that \xe2\x80\x9cthe government may not deny a benefit to a person because he ex- ercises a constitutional right.\xe2\x80\x9d Regan v. Taxation With Representation of Wash., \n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 540, \n545 (1983)\n. See also, e.g., Rumsfeld v. Forum for Academic and Institutional Rights, Inc., \n\n547 U. S. 47\n\xe2\x80\x9360 (2006); Rutan v. Republican Party of Ill., \n\n497 U.\xc2\xa0S. 62, \n78 (1990)\n. In Perry v. Sindermann, \n\n408 U.\xc2\xa0S. 593 (1972)\n, for example, we held that a public college would violate a professor\xe2\x80\x99s freedom of speech if it declined to renew his contract because he was an outspoken critic of the college\xe2\x80\x99s administration. And in Memorial Hospital v. Maricopa County, \n\n415 U.\xc2\xa0S. 250 (1974)\n, we concluded that a county impermissibly burdened the right to travel by extending healthcare benefits only to those indigent sick who had been residents of the county for at least one year. Those cases reflect an overarching principle, known as the unconstitutional condi- tions doctrine, that vindicates the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated rights by preventing the government from coercing people into giving them up.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNollan and Dolan \xe2\x80\x9cinvolve a special application\xe2\x80\x9d of this doctrine that protects the \n \nFifth Amendment right to just compensation for property the government takes when owners apply for land-use permits. Lingle v. Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc., \n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 528, \n547 (2005)\n; Dolan, 512 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 385 (invoking \xe2\x80\x9cthe well-settled doctrine of \xe2\x80\x98unconstitutional conditions\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Our decisions in those cases reflect two realities of the permitting process. The first is that land-use permit applicants are especially vulnerable to the type of coercion that the unconstitutional conditions doctrine prohibits because the government often has broad discretion to deny a permit that is worth far more than property it would like to take. By conditioning a building permit on the owner\xe2\x80\x99s deeding over a public right-of- way, for example, the government can pressure an owner into voluntarily giving up property for which the \n \nFifth Amendment would otherwise require just compensation. See id., at 384; Nollan, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 831. So long as the building permit is more valuable than any just compensation the owner could hope to receive for the right-of-way, the owner is likely to accede to the government\xe2\x80\x99s demand, no matter how unreasonable. Extortionate demands of this sort frustrate the \n \nFifth Amendment right to just compensation, and the unconstitutional conditions doctrine prohibits them.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA second reality of the permitting process is that many proposed land uses threaten to impose costs on the public that dedications of property can offset. Where a building proposal would substantially increase traffic congestion, for example, officials might condition permit approval on the owner\xe2\x80\x99s agreement to deed over the land needed to widen a public road. Respondent argues that a similar rationale justifies the exaction at issue here: petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed construction project, it submits, would destroy wetlands on his property, and in order to compensate for this loss, respondent demands that he enhance wet- lands elsewhere. Insisting that landowners internalize the negative externalities of their conduct is a hallmark of responsible land-use policy, and we have long sustained such regulations against constitutional attack. See Village of Euclid v. Ambler Realty Co., \n\n272 U.\xc2\xa0S. 365 (1926)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNollan and Dolan accommodate both realities by allowing the government to condition approval of a permit on the dedication of property to the public so long as there is a \xe2\x80\x9cnexus\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crough proportionality\xe2\x80\x9d between the prop- erty that the government demands and the social costs of the applicant\xe2\x80\x99s proposal. Dolan, supra, at 391; Nollan, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 837. Our precedents thus enable permitting authorities to insist that applicants bear the full costs of their proposals while still forbidding the government from engaging in \xe2\x80\x9cout-and-out .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. extortion\xe2\x80\x9d that would thwart the \n \nFifth Amendment right to just compensation. Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). Under Nollan and Dolan the government may choose whether and how a per- mit applicant is required to mitigate the impacts of a proposed development, but it may not leverage its legitimate interest in mitigation to pursue governmental ends that lack an essential nexus and rough proportionality to those impacts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe principles that undergird our decisions in Nollan and Dolan do not change depending on whether the government approves a permit on the condition that the applicant turn over property or denies a permit because the applicant refuses to do so. We have often concluded that denials of governmental benefits were impermissible under the unconstitutional conditions doctrine. See, e.g., Perry, 408 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 597 (explaining that the government \xe2\x80\x9cmay not deny a benefit to a person on a basis that infringes his constitutionally protected interests\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); Memorial Hospital, \n\n415 U.\xc2\xa0S. 250\n (finding unconstitutional condition where government denied healthcare benefits). In so holding, we have recognized that regardless of whether the government ultimately succeeds in pressuring someone into forfeiting a constitutional right, the unconstitutional conditions doctrine forbids burdening the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s enumerated rights by coercively withholding benefits from those who exercise them.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA contrary rule would be especially untenable in this case because it would enable the government to evade the limitations of Nollan and Dolan simply by phrasing its demands for property as conditions precedent to permit approval. Under the Florida Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach, a government order stating that a permit is \xe2\x80\x9capproved if\xe2\x80\x9d the owner turns over property would be subject to Nollan and Dolan, but an identical order that uses the words \xe2\x80\x9cdenied until\xe2\x80\x9d would not. Our unconstitutional condi- tions cases have long refused to attach significance to the distinction between conditions precedent and conditions subsequent. See Frost & Frost Trucking Co. v. Railroad Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Cal., \n\n271 U.\xc2\xa0S. 583\n\xe2\x80\x93593 (1926) (invalidating regulation that required the petitioner to give up a constitutional right \xe2\x80\x9cas a condition precedent to the enjoyment of a privilege\xe2\x80\x9d); Southern Pacific Co. v. Denton, \n\n146 U.\xc2\xa0S. 202, \n207 (1892)\n (invalidating statute \xe2\x80\x9crequiring the corporation, as a condition precedent to obtaining a per- mit to do business within the State, to surrender a right and privilege secured to it by the Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Flower Mound, 135 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 639 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe government cannot sidestep constitutional protections merely by rephrasing its decision from \xe2\x80\x98only if\xe2\x80\x99 to \xe2\x80\x98not unless\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). To do so here would effectively render Nollan and Dolan a dead letter.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Florida Supreme Court puzzled over how the government\xe2\x80\x99s demand for property can violate the Takings Clause even though \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98no property of any kind was ever taken,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 77 So.\xc2\xa03d, at 1225 (quoting 5 So.\xc2\xa03d, at 20 (Griffin, J., dissenting)); see also 77 So.\xc2\xa03d, at 1229\xe2\x80\x931230, but the unconstitutional conditions doctrine provides a ready answer. Extortionate demands for property in the land-use permitting context run afoul of the Takings Clause not because they take property but because they impermis- sibly burden the right not to have property taken without just compensation. As in other unconstitutional condi- tions cases in which someone refuses to cede a constitutional right in the face of coercive pressure, the impermissible denial of a governmental benefit is a constitutionally cog- nizable injury.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNor does it make a difference, as respondent suggests, that the government might have been able to deny petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s application outright without giving him the option of securing a permit by agreeing to spend money to improve public lands. See Penn Central Transp. Co. v. New York City, \n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104 (1978)\n. Virtually all of our unconstitutional conditions cases involve a gratuitous governmental benefit of some kind. See, e.g., Regan, \n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 540\n (tax benefits); Memorial Hospital, \n\n415 U.\xc2\xa0S. 250\n (healthcare); Perry, \n\n408 U.\xc2\xa0S. 593\n (public employment); United States v. Butler, \n\n297 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n71 (1936)\n (crop payments); Frost, supra (business license). Yet we have repeatedly rejected the argument that if the government need not confer a benefit at all, it can withhold the benefit because someone refuses to give up constitutional rights. E.g., United States v. American Library Assn., Inc., \n\n539 U.\xc2\xa0S. 194, \n210 (2003)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he government may not deny a benefit to a person on a basis that infringes his constitutionally protected .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. freedom of speech even if he has no entitlement to that benefit\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added and inter- nal quotation marks omitted)); Wieman v. Updegraff, \n\n344 U.\xc2\xa0S. 183, \n191 (1952)\n (explaining in unconstitutional conditions case that to focus on \xe2\x80\x9cthe facile generalization that there is no constitutionally protected right to public employment is to obscure the issue\xe2\x80\x9d). Even if respondent would have been entirely within its rights in denying the permit for some other reason, that greater authority does not imply a lesser power to condition permit approval on petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s forfeiture of his constitutional rights. See Nollan, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 836\xe2\x80\x93837 (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he evident constitutional propriety\xe2\x80\x9d of prohibiting a land use \xe2\x80\x9cdisappears .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. if the condition substituted for the prohibition utterly fails to further the end advanced as the justification for the prohibition\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat is not to say, however, that there is no relevant difference between a consummated taking and the denial of a permit based on an unconstitutionally extortionate demand. Where the permit is denied and the condition is never imposed, nothing has been taken. While the un- constitutional conditions doctrine recognizes that this burdens a constitutional right, the \n \nFifth Amendment man- dates a particular remedy\xe2\x80\x94just compensation\xe2\x80\x94only for takings. In cases where there is an excessive demand but no taking, whether money damages are available is not a question of federal constitutional law but of the cause of action\xe2\x80\x94whether state or federal\xe2\x80\x94on which the landowner relies. Because petitioner brought his claim pursuant to a state law cause of action, the Court has no occasion to discuss what remedies might be available for a Nollan/Dolan unconstitutional conditions violation either here or in other cases.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt oral argument, respondent conceded that the denial of a permit could give rise to a valid claim under Nollan and Dolan, Tr. of Oral Arg. 33\xe2\x80\x9334, but it urged that we should not review the particular denial at issue here because petitioner sued in the wrong court, for the wrong remedy, and at the wrong time. Most of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s objections to the posture of this case raise questions of Florida procedure that are not ours to decide. See Mullaney v. Wilbur, \n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 684, \n691 (1975)\n; Murdock v. Memphis, 20 Wall. 590, 626 (1875). But to the extent that respondent suggests that the posture of this case creates some federal obstacle to adjudicating petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s unconstitutional conditions claim, we remand for the Florida courts to consider that argument in the first instance.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent argues that we should affirm because, rather than suing for damages in the Florida trial court as authorized by Fla. Stat. \xc2\xa7373.617, petitioner should have first sought judicial review of the denial of his permit in the Florida appellate court under the State\xe2\x80\x99s Administrative Procedure Act, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7120.68(1), (2) (2010). The Flor-ida Supreme Court has said that the appellate court is the \xe2\x80\x9cproper forum to resolve\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cclaim that an agency has applied a .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. statute or rule in such a way that the aggrieved party\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional rights have been violated,\xe2\x80\x9d Key Haven Associated Enterprises, 427 So.\xc2\xa02d, at 158, and respondent has argued throughout this litigation that petitioner brought his unconstitutional conditions claim in the wrong forum. Two members of the Florida Supreme Court credited respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument, 77 So.\xc2\xa03d, at 1231\xe2\x80\x931232, but four others refused to address it. We decline respondent\xe2\x80\x99s invitation to second-guess a State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of its own procedural law.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent also contends that we should affirm because petitioner sued for damages but is at most entitled to an injunction ordering that his permit issue without any conditions. But we need not decide whether federal law authorizes plaintiffs to recover damages for unconstitutional conditions claims predicated on the Takings Clause because petitioner brought his claim under state law. Florida law allows property owners to sue for \xe2\x80\x9cdamages\xe2\x80\x9d whenever a state agency\xe2\x80\x99s action is \xe2\x80\x9can unreasonable ex- ercise of the state\xe2\x80\x99s police power constituting a taking without just compensation.\xe2\x80\x9d Fla. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7373.617. Whether that provision covers an unconstitutional conditions claim like the one at issue here is a question of state law that the Florida Supreme Court did not address and on which we will not opine.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor similar reasons, we decline to reach respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument that its demands for property were too indefinite to give rise to liability under Nollan and Dolan. The Florida Supreme Court did not reach the question whether respondent issued a demand of sufficient concreteness to trigger the special protections of Nollan and Dolan. It relied instead on the Florida District Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 characterization of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s behavior as a demand for Nollan/Dolan purposes. See 77 So. 3d, at 1224 (quoting 5 So. 3d, at 10). Whether that characterization is correct is beyond the scope of the questions the Court agreed to take up for review. If preserved, the issue remains open on remand for the Florida Supreme Court to address. This Court therefore has no occasion to consider how concrete and specific a demand must be to give rise to liability un- der Nollan and Dolan.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, respondent argues that we need not decide whether its demand for offsite improvements satisfied Nollan and Dolan because it gave petitioner another avenue for obtaining permit approval. Specifically, respondent said that it would have approved a revised permit application that reduced the footprint of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s proposed construction site from 3.7 acres to 1 acre and placed a conservation easement on the remaining 13.9 acres of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s land. Respondent argues that regardless of whether its demands for offsite mitigation satisfied Nollan and Dolan, we must separately consider each of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s options, one of which did not require any of the offsite work the trial court found objectionable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument is flawed because the option to which it points\xe2\x80\x94developing only 1 acre of the site and granting a conservation easement on the rest\xe2\x80\x94involves the same issue as the option to build on 3.7 acres and perform offsite mitigation. We agree with respondent that, so long as a permitting authority offers the landowner at least one alternative that would satisfy Nollan and Dolan, the landowner has not been subjected to an unconstitutional condition. But respondent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that we should treat its offer to let petitioner build on 1 acre as an alternative to offsite mitigation misapprehends the gov- ernmental benefit that petitioner was denied. Petitioner sought to develop 3.7 acres, but respondent in effect told petitioner that it would not allow him to build on 2.7 of those acres unless he agreed to spend money improving public lands. Petitioner claims that he was wrongfully denied a permit to build on those 2.7 acres. For that reason, respondent\xe2\x80\x99s offer to approve a less ambitious building project does not obviate the need to determine whether the demand for offsite mitigation satisfied Nollan and Dolan.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe turn to the Florida Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s alternative holding that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim fails because respondent asked him to spend money rather than give up an easement on his land. A predicate for any unconstitutional conditions claim is that the government could not have constitutionally ordered the person asserting the claim to do what it attempted to pressure that person into doing. See Rumsfeld, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 59\xe2\x80\x9360. For that reason, we began our analysis in both Nollan and Dolan by observing that if the government had directly seized the easements it sought to obtain through the permitting process, it would have committed a per se taking. See Dolan, 512 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 384; Nollan, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 831. The Florida Su- preme Court held that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim fails at this first step because the subject of the exaction at issue here was money rather than a more tangible interest in real prop- erty. 77 So.\xc2\xa03d, at 1230. Respondent and the dissent take the same position, citing the concurring and dissenting opinions in Eastern Enterprises v. Apfel, \n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 498 (1998)\n, for the proposition that an obligation to spend money can never provide the basis for a takings claim. See post, at 5\xe2\x80\x938 (opinion of Kagan, J.).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe note as an initial matter that if we accepted this argument it would be very easy for land-use permitting officials to evade the limitations of Nollan and Dolan. Because the government need only provide a permit applicant with one alternative that satisfies the nexus and rough proportionality standards, a permitting authority wishing to exact an easement could simply give the owner a choice of either surrendering an easement or making a payment equal to the easement\xe2\x80\x99s value. Such so-called \xe2\x80\x9cin lieu of\xe2\x80\x9d fees are utterly commonplace, Rosenberg, The Changing Culture of American Land Use Regulation: Paying for Growth with Impact Fees, 59 S.\xc2\xa0M.\xc2\xa0U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 177, 202\xe2\x80\x93203 (2006), and they are functionally equivalent to other types of land use exactions. For that reason and those that follow, we reject respondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument and hold that so-called \xe2\x80\x9cmonetary exactions\xe2\x80\x9d must satisfy the nexus and rough proportionality requirements of Nollan and Dolan.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Eastern Enterprises, supra, the United States retroactively imposed on a former mining company an obligation to pay for the medical benefits of retired miners and their families. A four-Justice plurality concluded that the statute\xe2\x80\x99s imposition of retroactive financial liability was so arbitrary that it violated the Takings Clause. Id., at 529\xe2\x80\x93537. Although Justice Kennedy concurred in the result on due process grounds, he joined four other Justices in dissent in arguing that the Takings Clause does not apply to government-imposed financial obligations that \xe2\x80\x9cd[o] not operate upon or alter an identified property interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 540 (opinion concurring in judgment and dissenting in part); see id., at 554\xe2\x80\x93556 (Breyer, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe \xe2\x80\x98private property\xe2\x80\x99 upon which the [Takings] Clause traditionally has focused is a specific interest in physical or intellectual property\xe2\x80\x9d). Relying on the concurrence and dissent in Eastern Enterprises, respondent argues that a requirement that petitioner spend money improving public lands could not give rise to a taking.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent\xe2\x80\x99s argument rests on a mistaken premise. Unlike the financial obligation in Eastern Enterprises, the demand for money at issue here did \xe2\x80\x9coperate upon .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an identified property interest\xe2\x80\x9d by directing the owner of a particular piece of property to make a monetary payment. Id., at 540 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). In this case, unlike Eastern Enterprises, the monetary obligation burdened petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s ownership of a specific parcel of land. In that sense, this case bears resemblance to our cases holding that the government must pay just compensation when it takes a lien\xe2\x80\x94a right to receive money that is secured by a particular piece of property. See Armstrong v. United States, \n\n364 U.\xc2\xa0S. 40\n\xe2\x80\x9349 (1960); Louisville Joint Stock Land Bank v. Radford, \n\n295 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555\n\xe2\x80\x93602 (1935); United States v. Security Industrial Bank, \n\n459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 70\n\xe2\x80\x9378 (1982); see also Palm Beach Cty. v. Cove Club Investors Ltd., 734 So.\xc2\xa02d 379, 383\xe2\x80\x93384 (1999) (the right to receive income from land is an interest in real property under Florida law). The fulcrum this case turns on is the direct link between the government\xe2\x80\x99s demand and a spe- cific parcel of real property.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Because of that direct link, this case implicates the central concern of Nollan and Dolan: the risk that the government may use its substantial power and discretion in land-use permitting to pursue governmental ends that lack an essential nexus and rough proportionality to the effects of the proposed new use of the specific property at issue, thereby diminishing without justification the value of the property.\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, moreover, petitioner does not ask us to hold that the government can commit a regulatory taking by directing someone to spend money. As a result, we need not apply Penn Central\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cessentially ad\xc2\xa0hoc, factual inquir[y],\xe2\x80\x9d 438 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 124, at all, much less extend that \xe2\x80\x9calready difficult and uncertain rule\xe2\x80\x9d to the \xe2\x80\x9cvast category of cases\xe2\x80\x9d in which someone believes that a regulation is too costly. Eastern Enterprises, 524 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 542 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). Instead, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim rests on the more limited proposition that when the government commands the relinquishment of funds linked to a specific, identifiable property interest such as a bank account or parcel of real property, a \xe2\x80\x9cper\xc2\xa0se [takings] approach\xe2\x80\x9d is the proper mode of analysis under the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent. Brown v. Legal Foundation of Wash., \n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 216, \n235 (2003)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, it bears emphasis that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim does not implicate \xe2\x80\x9cnormative considerations about the wisdom of government decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Eastern Enterprises, 524 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 545 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). We are not here concerned with whether it would be \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary or unfair\xe2\x80\x9d for respondent to order a landowner to make improvements to public lands that are nearby. Id., at 554 (Breyer, J., dissenting). Whatever the wisdom of such a policy, it would transfer an interest in property from the landowner to the government. For that reason, any such demand would amount to a per\xc2\xa0se taking similar to the taking of an easement or a lien. Cf. Dolan, 512 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 384; Nollan, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 831.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent and the dissent argue that if monetary exactions are made subject to scrutiny under Nollan and Dolan, then there will be no principled way of distinguishing impermissible land-use exactions from property taxes. See post, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. We think they exaggerate both the extent to which that problem is unique to the land-use permitting context and the practical difficulty of distinguishing between the power to tax and the power to take by eminent domain.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is beyond dispute that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]axes and user fees .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are not \xe2\x80\x98takings.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brown, supra, at 243, n.\xc2\xa02 (Scalia, J., dissenting). We said as much in County of Mobile v. Kimball, \n\n102 U.\xc2\xa0S. 691, \n703 (1881)\n, and our cases have been clear on that point ever since. United States v. Sperry Corp., \n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 52\n, n.\xc2\xa09 (1989); see A. Magnano Co. v. Hamilton, \n\n292 U.\xc2\xa0S. 40, \n44 (1934)\n; Dane v. Jackson, \n\n256 U.\xc2\xa0S. 589, \n599 (1921)\n; Henderson Bridge Co. v. Henderson City, \n\n173 U.\xc2\xa0S. 592\n\xe2\x80\x93615 (1899). This case therefore does not affect the ability of governments to impose property taxes, user fees, and similar laws and regulations that may impose financial burdens on property owners.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt the same time, we have repeatedly found takings where the government, by confiscating financial obligations, achieved a result that could have been obtained by imposing a tax. Most recently, in Brown, supra, at 232, we were unanimous in concluding that a State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s seizure of the interest on client funds held in escrow was a taking despite the unquestionable constitutional propriety of a tax that would have raised exactly the same revenue. Our holding in Brown followed from Phillips v. Washington Legal Foundation, \n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 156 (1998)\n, and Webb\xe2\x80\x99s Fabulous Pharmacies, Inc. v. Beckwith, \n\n449 U.\xc2\xa0S. 155 (1980)\n, two earlier cases in which we treated confiscations of money as takings despite their functional similarity to a tax. Perhaps most closely analogous to the present case, we have repeatedly held that the government takes property when it seizes liens, and in so ruling we have never considered whether the government could have achieved an economically equivalent result through taxation. Armstrong, \n\n364 U.\xc2\xa0S. 40\n; Louisville Joint Stock Land Bank, \n\n295 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTwo facts emerge from those cases. The first is that the need to distinguish taxes from takings is not a creature of our holding today that monetary exactions are subject to scrutiny under Nollan and Dolan. Rather, the problem is inherent in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s long-settled view that property the government could constitutionally demand through its taxing power can also be taken by eminent domain.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, our cases show that teasing out the difference between taxes and takings is more difficult in theory than in practice. Brown is illustrative. Similar to respondent in this case, the respondents in Brown argued that extending the protections of the Takings Clause to a bank account would open a Pandora\xe2\x80\x99s Box of constitutional chal- lenges to taxes. Brief for Respondents Washington Legal Foundation et\xc2\xa0al. 32 and Brief for Respondent Justices of the Washington Supreme Court 22, in Brown v. Legal Foundation of Wash., O. T. 2002, No. 01\xe2\x80\x931325. But also like respondent here, the Brown respondents never claimed that they were exercising their power to levy taxes when they took the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 property. Any such argument would have been implausible under state law; in Washington, taxes are levied by the legislature, not the courts. See 538 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 242, n.\xc2\xa02 (Scalia, J., dissenting).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe same dynamic is at work in this case because Flor- ida law greatly circumscribes respondent\xe2\x80\x99s power to tax. See Fla. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa7373.503 (authorizing respondent to impose ad\xc2\xa0valorem tax on properties within its jurisdiction); \xc2\xa7373.109 (authorizing respondent to charge permit application fees but providing that such fees \xe2\x80\x9cshall not exceed the cost .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for processing, monitoring, and inspecting for compliance with the permit\xe2\x80\x9d). If respondent had argued that its demand for money was a tax, it would have effectively conceded that its denial of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s permit was improper under Florida law. Far from making that concession, respondent has maintained throughout this litigation that it considered petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s money to be a substitute for his deeding to the public a conservation easement on a larger parcel of undeveloped land.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case does not require us to say more. We need not decide at precisely what point a land-use permitting charge denominated by the government as a \xe2\x80\x9ctax\xe2\x80\x9d becomes \xe2\x80\x9cso arbitrary .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that it was not the exertion of taxation but a confiscation of property.\xe2\x80\x9d Brushaber v. Union Pacific R.\xc2\xa0Co., \n\n240 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9325 (1916). For present purposes, it suffices to say that despite having long recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthe power of taxation should not be confused with the power of eminent domain,\xe2\x80\x9d Houck v. Little River Drainage Dist., \n\n239 U.\xc2\xa0S. 254, \n264 (1915)\n, we have had little trouble distinguishing between the two.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, we disagree with the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s forecast that our decision will work a revolution in land use law by depriving local governments of the ability to charge reasonable permitting fees. Post, at 8. Numerous courts\xe2\x80\x94including courts in many of our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s most populous States\xe2\x80\x94have confronted constitutional challenges to monetary exactions over the last two decades and applied the standard from Nollan and Dolan or something like it. See, e.g., Northern Ill. Home Builders Assn. v. County of Du Page, 165 Ill. 2d. 25, 31\xe2\x80\x9332, 649 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 384, 388\xe2\x80\x93389 (1995); Home Builders Assn. v. Beavercreek, 89 Ohio St. 3d 121, 128, 729 N.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 349, 356 (2000); Flower Mound, 135 S. W.\xc2\xa03d, at 640\xe2\x80\x93641. Yet the \xe2\x80\x9csignificant practical harm\xe2\x80\x9d the dissent predicts has not come to pass. Post, at 8. That is hardly surprising, for the dissent is correct that state law normally provides an independent check on excessive land use permitting fees. Post, at 11.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe dissent criticizes the notion that the Federal Constitution places any meaningful limits on \xe2\x80\x9cwhether one town is overcharging for sewage, or another is setting the price to sell liquor too high.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 9. But only two pages later, it identifies three constraints on land use permitting fees that it says the Federal Constitution imposes and suggests that the additional protections of Nollan and Dolan are not needed. Post, at 11. In any event, the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s argument that land use permit applicants need no further protection when the government demands money is really an argument for overruling Nollan and Dolan. After all, the Due Process Clause protected the Nollans from an unfair allocation of public burdens, and they too could have argued that the government\xe2\x80\x99s demand for property amounted to a taking under the Penn Central framework. See Nollan, 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 838. We have repeatedly rejected the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s contention that other constitutional doctrines leave no room for the nexus and rough proportionality requirements of Nollan and Dolan. Mindful of the special vulnerability of land use permit applicants to extortionate demands for money, we do so again today.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe hold that the government\xe2\x80\x99s demand for property from a land-use permit applicant must satisfy the requirements of Nollan and Dolan even when the government denies the permit and even when its demand is for money. The Court expresses no view on the merits of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s claim that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s actions here failed to comply with the principles set forth in this opinion and those two cases. The Florida Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment is reversed, and this case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tCOY A. KOONTZ, Jr., PETITIONER v. ST. JOHNS RIVER WATER MANAGEMENT DISTRICT\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the supreme court of florida\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t[June 25, 2013]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Kagan, with whom Justice Ginsburg, Justice Breyer, and Justice Sotomayor join, dissenting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the paradigmatic case triggering review under Nollan v. California Coastal Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, \n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 825 (1987)\n, and Dolan v. City of Tigard, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374 (1994)\n, the government approves a building permit on the condition that the landowner relinquish an interest in real property, like an easement. The significant legal questions that the Court resolves today are whether Nollan and Dolan also apply when that case is varied in two ways. First, what if the government does not approve the permit, but instead demands that the condition be fulfilled before it will do so? Second, what if the condition entails not transferring real property, but simply paying money? This case also raises other, more fact-specific issues I will address: whether the government here imposed any condition at all, and whether petitioner Coy Koontz suffered any compensable injury.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI think the Court gets the first question it addresses right. The Nollan-Dolan standard applies not only when the government approves a development permit conditioned on the owner\xe2\x80\x99s conveyance of a property interest (i.e., imposes a condition subsequent), but also when the government denies a permit until the owner meets the condition (i.e., imposes a condition precedent). That means an owner may challenge the denial of a permit on the ground that the government\xe2\x80\x99s condition lacks the \xe2\x80\x9cnexus\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crough proportionality\xe2\x80\x9d to the development\xe2\x80\x99s social costs that Nollan and Dolan require. Still, the condition-subsequent and condition-precedent situations differ in an important way. When the government grants a permit subject to the relinquishment of real property, and that condition does not satisfy Nollan and Dolan, then the government has taken the property and must pay just compensation under the \n \nFifth Amendment. But when the government denies a permit because an owner has refused to accede to that same demand, nothing has actually been taken. The owner is entitled to have the improper condition removed; and he may be entitled to a monetary remedy created by state law for imposing such a condition; but he cannot be entitled to constitutional compensation for a taking of property. So far, we all agree.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur core disagreement concerns the second question the Court addresses. The majority extends Nollan and Dolan to cases in which the government conditions a permit not on the transfer of real property, but instead on the payment or expenditure of money. That runs roughshod over Eastern Enterprises v. Apfel, \n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 498 (1998)\n, which held that the government may impose ordinary financial obligations without triggering the Takings Clause\xe2\x80\x99s protections. The boundaries of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s new rule are uncertain. But it threatens to subject a vast array of land-use regulations, applied daily in States and localities throughout the country, to heightened constitutional scrutiny. I would not embark on so unwise an adventure, and would affirm the Florida Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI also would affirm for two independent reasons establishing that Koontz cannot get the money damages he seeks. First, respondent St. Johns River Water Management District (District) never demanded anything (including money) in exchange for a permit; the Nollan-Dolan standard therefore does not come into play (even assuming that test applies to demands for money). Second, no taking occurred in this case because Koontz never acceded to a demand (even had there been one), and so no property changed hands; as just noted, Koontz therefore cannot claim just compensation under the \n \nFifth Amendment. The majority does not take issue with my first conclusion, and affirmatively agrees with my second. But the majority thinks Koontz might still be entitled to money damages, and remands to the Florida Supreme Court on that question. I do not see how, and expect that court will so rule.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tClaims that government regulations violate the Takings Clause by unduly restricting the use of property are generally \xe2\x80\x9cgoverned by the standards set forth in Penn Central Transp. Co. v. New York City, \n\n438 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104 (1978)\n.\xe2\x80\x9d Lingle v. Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc., \n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 528, \n538 (2005)\n. Under Penn Central, courts examine a regulation\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccharacter\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ceconomic impact,\xe2\x80\x9d asking whether the action goes beyond \xe2\x80\x9cadjusting the benefits and burdens of economic life to promote the common good\xe2\x80\x9d and whether it \xe2\x80\x9cinterfere[s] with distinct investment-backed expectations.\xe2\x80\x9d Penn Central, 438 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 124. That multi-factor test balances the government\xe2\x80\x99s manifest need to pass laws and regulations \xe2\x80\x9cadversely affect[ing].\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. economic values,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., with our longstanding recognition that some regulation \xe2\x80\x9cgoes too far,\xe2\x80\x9d Pennsylvania Coal Co. v. Mahon, \n\n260 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393, \n415 (1922)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur decisions in Nollan and Dolan are different: They provide an independent layer of protection in \xe2\x80\x9cthe special context of land-use exactions.\xe2\x80\x9d Lingle, 544 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 538. In that situation, the \xe2\x80\x9cgovernment demands that a landowner dedicate an easement\xe2\x80\x9d or surrender a piece of real property \xe2\x80\x9cas a condition of obtaining a development permit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 546. If the government appropriated such a property interest outside the permitting process, its action would constitute a taking, necessitating just compensation. Id., at 547. Nollan and Dolan prevent the government from exploiting the landowner\xe2\x80\x99s permit application to evade the constitutional obligation to pay for the property. They do so, as the majority explains, by subjecting the government\xe2\x80\x99s demand to heightened scrutiny: The government may condition a land-use permit on the relinquishment of real property only if it shows a \xe2\x80\x9cnexus\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9crough proportionality\xe2\x80\x9d between the demand made and \xe2\x80\x9cthe impact of the proposed development.\xe2\x80\x9d Dolan, 512 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 386, 391; see ante, at 8. Nollan and Dolan thus serve not to address excessive regulatory burdens on land use (the function of Penn Central), but instead to stop the government from imposing an \xe2\x80\x9cunconstitutional condition\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a requirement that a person give up his constitutional right to receive just compensation \xe2\x80\x9cin exchange for a discretionary benefit\xe2\x80\x9d having \xe2\x80\x9clittle or no relationship\xe2\x80\x9d to the property taken. Lingle, 544 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 547.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAccordingly, the Nollan-Dolan test applies only when the property the government demands during the permitting process is the kind it otherwise would have to pay for\xe2\x80\x94or, put differently, when the appropriation of that property, outside the permitting process, would constitute a taking. That is why Nollan began by stating that \xe2\x80\x9c[h]ad California simply required the Nollans to make an easement across their beachfront available to the public .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , rather than conditioning their permit to rebuild their house on their agreeing to do so, we have no doubt there would have been a taking\xe2\x80\x9d requiring just compensation. 483 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 831. And it is why Dolan started by maintaining that \xe2\x80\x9chad the city simply required petitioner to dedicate a strip of land .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for public use, rather than conditioning the grant of her permit to [d]evelop her property on such a dedication, a taking would have occurred.\xe2\x80\x9d 512 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 384. Even the majority acknowledges this basic point about Nollan and Dolan: It too notes that those cases rest on the premise that \xe2\x80\x9cif the government had directly seized the easements it sought to obtain through the permitting process, it would have committed a per se taking.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315. Only if that is true could the government\xe2\x80\x99s demand for the property force a landowner to relinquish his constitutional right to just compensation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHere, Koontz claims that the District demanded that he spend money to improve public wetlands, not that he hand over a real property interest. I assume for now that the District made that demand (although I think it did not, see infra, at 12\xe2\x80\x9316.) The key question then is: Independent of the permitting process, does requiring a person to pay money to the government, or spend money on its behalf, constitute a taking requiring just compensation? Only if the answer is yes does the Nollan-Dolan test apply.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut we have already answered that question no. Eastern Enterprises v. Apfel, \n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 498\n, as the Court describes, involved a federal statute requiring a former mining company to pay a large sum of money for the health benefits of retired employees. Five Members of the Court determined that the law did not effect a taking, distinguishing between the appropriation of a specific property interest and the imposition of an order to pay money. Justice Kennedy acknowledged in his controlling opinion that the statute \xe2\x80\x9cimpose[d] a staggering financial burden\xe2\x80\x9d (which influenced his conclusion that it violated due process). Id., at 540 (opinion concurring in judgment and dissenting in part). Still, Justice Kennedy explained, the law did not effect a taking because it did not \xe2\x80\x9coperate upon or alter\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cspecific and identified propert[y] or property right[\xc2\xa0].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 540\xe2\x80\x93541. Instead, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he law simply imposes an obligation to perform an act, the payment of benefits. The statute is indifferent as to how the regulated entity elects to comply or the property it uses to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 540. Justice Breyer, writing for four more Justices, agreed. He stated that the Takings Clause applies only when the government appropriates a \xe2\x80\x9cspecific interest in physical or intellectual property\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ca specific, separately identifiable fund of money\xe2\x80\x9d; by contrast, the Clause has no bearing when the government imposes \xe2\x80\x9can ordinary liability to pay money.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 554\xe2\x80\x93555 (dissenting opinion).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, a requirement that a person pay money to repair public wetlands is not a taking. Such an order does not affect a \xe2\x80\x9cspecific and identified propert[y] or property right[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d; it simply \xe2\x80\x9cimposes an obligation to perform an act\xe2\x80\x9d (the improvement of wetlands) that costs money. Id., at 540\xe2\x80\x93541 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). To be sure, when a person spends money on the government\xe2\x80\x99s behalf, or pays money directly to the government, it \xe2\x80\x9cwill reduce [his] net worth\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94but that \xe2\x80\x9ccan be said of any law which has an adverse economic effect\xe2\x80\x9d on someone. Id., at 543. Because the government is merely imposing a \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral liability\xe2\x80\x9d to pay money, id., at 555 (Breyer, J., dissenting)\xe2\x80\x94and therefore is \xe2\x80\x9cindifferent as to how the regulated entity elects to comply or the property it uses to do so,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 540 (opinion of Kennedy, J.)\xe2\x80\x94the order to repair wetlands, viewed independent of the permitting process, does not constitute a taking. And that means the order does not trigger the Nollan-Dolan test, because it does not force Koontz to relinquish a constitutional right.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority tries to distinguish Apfel by asserting that the District\xe2\x80\x99s demand here was \xe2\x80\x9cclosely analogous\xe2\x80\x9d (and \xe2\x80\x9cbears resemblance\xe2\x80\x9d) to the seizure of a lien on property or an income stream from a parcel of land. Ante, at 16, 19. The majority thus seeks support from decisions like Armstrong v. United States, \n\n364 U.\xc2\xa0S. 40 (1960)\n, where this Court held that the government effected a taking when it extinguished a lien on several ships, and Palm Beach Cty. v. Cove Club Investors Ltd., 734 So.\xc2\xa02d 379 (1999), where the Florida Supreme Court held that the government committed a taking when it terminated a covenant entit-ling the beneficiary to an income stream from a piece of land.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut the majority\xe2\x80\x99s citations succeed only in showing what this case is not. When the government dissolves a lien, or appropriates a determinate income stream from a piece of property\xe2\x80\x94or, for that matter, seizes a particular \xe2\x80\x9cbank account or [the] accrued interest\xe2\x80\x9d on it\xe2\x80\x94the government indeed takes a \xe2\x80\x9cspecific\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cidentified prop- erty interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Apfel, 524 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 540\xe2\x80\x93541 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). But nothing like that occurred here. The District did not demand any particular lien, or bank account, or income stream from property. It just ordered Koontz to spend or pay money (again, assuming it ordered anything at all). Koontz\xe2\x80\x99s liability would have been the same whether his property produced income or not\xe2\x80\x94e.g., even if all he wanted to build was a family home. And similarly, Koontz could meet that obligation from what-ever source he chose\xe2\x80\x94a checking account, shares of stock, a wealthy uncle; the District was \xe2\x80\x9cindifferent as to how [he] elect[ed] to [pay] or the property [he] use[d] to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 540. No more than in Apfel, then, was the (supposed) demand here for a \xe2\x80\x9cspecific and identified\xe2\x80\x9d piece of property, which the government could not take without paying for it. Id., at 541.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority thus falls back on the sole way the District\xe2\x80\x99s alleged demand related to a property interest: The demand arose out of the permitting process for Koontz\xe2\x80\x99s land. See ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317. But under the analytic framework that Nollan and Dolan established, that connection alone is insufficient to trigger heightened scrutiny. As I have described, the heightened standard of Nollan and Dolan is not a freestanding protection for land-use permit applicants; rather, it is \xe2\x80\x9ca special application of the doctrine of unconstitutional conditions, which provides that the government may not require a person to give up a constitutional right\xe2\x80\x94here the right to receive just compensation when property is taken\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94in exchange for a land-use permit. Lingle, 544 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 547 (internal quotation marks omitted); see supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x935. As such, Nollan and Dolan apply only if the demand at issue would have violated the Constitution independent of that proposed exchange. Or put otherwise, those cases apply only if the demand would have constituted a taking when executed outside the permitting process. And here, under Apfel, it would not.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach, on top of its analytic flaws, threatens significant practical harm. By applying Nollan and Dolan to permit conditions requiring monetary payments\xe2\x80\x94with no express limitation except as to taxes\xe2\x80\x94the majority extends the Takings Clause, with its notoriously \xe2\x80\x9cdifficult\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cperplexing\xe2\x80\x9d standards, into the very heart of local land-use regulation and service delivery. 524 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 541. Cities and towns across the nation impose many kinds of permitting fees every day. Some enable a government to mitigate a new development\xe2\x80\x99s impact on the community, like increased traffic or pollution\xe2\x80\x94or destruction of wetlands. See, e.g., Olympia v. Drebick, 156 Wash. 2d 289, 305, 126 P.\xc2\xa03d 802, 809 (2006). Others cover the direct costs of providing services like sewage or water to the development. See, e.g., Krupp v. Breckenridge Sanitation Dist., 19 P.\xc2\xa03d 687, 691 (Colo. 2001). Still others are meant to limit the number of landowners who engage in a certain activity, as fees for liquor licenses do. See, e.g., Phillips v. Mobile, \n\n208 U.\xc2\xa0S. 472, \n479 (1908)\n; BHA Investments, Inc. v. Idaho, 138 Idaho 348, 63 P.\xc2\xa03d 474 (2003). All now must meet Nollan and Dolan\xe2\x80\x99s nexus and proportionality tests. The Federal Constitution thus will decide whether one town is overcharging for sewage, or another is setting the price to sell liquor too high. And the flexibility of state and local governments to take the most routine actions to enhance their communities will diminish accordingly.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat problem becomes still worse because the majority\xe2\x80\x99s distinction between monetary \xe2\x80\x9cexactions\xe2\x80\x9d and taxes is so hard to apply. Ante, at 18. The majority acknowledges, as it must, that taxes are not takings. See ibid. (This case \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not affect the ability of governments to impose property taxes, user fees, and similar laws and regulations that may impose financial burdens on property owners\xe2\x80\x9d). But once the majority decides that a simple demand to pay money\xe2\x80\x94the sort of thing often viewed as a tax\xe2\x80\x94can count as an impermissible \xe2\x80\x9cexaction,\xe2\x80\x9d how is anyone to tell the two apart? The question, as Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in Apfel noted, \xe2\x80\x9cbristles with conceptual difficulties.\xe2\x80\x9d 524 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 556. And practical ones, too: How to separate orders to pay money from .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. well, orders to pay money, so that a locality knows what it can (and cannot) do. State courts sometimes must confront the same question, as they enforce restrictions on localities\xe2\x80\x99 taxing power. And their decisions\xe2\x80\x94contrary to the majority\xe2\x80\x99s blithe assertion, see ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9321\xe2\x80\x94struggle to draw a coherent boundary. Because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no set rule\xe2\x80\x9d by which to determine \xe2\x80\x9cin which category a particular\xe2\x80\x9d action belongs, Eastern Diversified Properties, Inc. v. Montgomery Cty., 319 Md. 45, 53, 570 A.\xc2\xa02d 850, 854 (1990), courts often reach opposite conclusions about classifying nearly identical fees. Compare, e.g., Coulter v. Rawlins, 662 P.\xc2\xa02d 888, 901\xe2\x80\x93904 (Wyo. 1983) (holding that a fee to enhance parks, imposed as a permit condition, was a regulatory exaction), with Home Builders Assn. v. West Des Moines, 644 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 339, 350 (Iowa 2002) (rejecting Coulter and holding that a nearly identical fee was a tax).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Nor does the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion provide any help with that issue: Perhaps its most striking feature is its refusal to say even a word about how to make the distinction that will now determine whether a given fee is subject to heightened scrutiny.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPerhaps the Court means in the future to curb the intrusion into local affairs that its holding will accomplish; the Court claims, after all, that its opinion is intended to have only limited impact on localities\xe2\x80\x99 land-use authority. See ante, at 8, 21. The majority might, for example, approve the rule, adopted in several States, that Nollan and Dolan apply only to permitting fees that are imposed ad\xc2\xa0hoc, and not to fees that are generally applicable. See, e.g., Ehrlich v. Culver City, 12 Cal. 4th 854, 911 P. 2d 429 (1996). Dolan itself suggested that limitation by underscoring that there \xe2\x80\x9cthe city made an adjudicative decision to condition petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s application for a building permit on an individual parcel,\xe2\x80\x9d instead of imposing an \xe2\x80\x9cessen-tially legislative determination[\xc2\xa0] classifying entire areas of the city.\xe2\x80\x9d 512 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 385. Maybe today\xe2\x80\x99s majority accepts that distinction; or then again, maybe not. At the least, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s refusal \xe2\x80\x9cto say more\xe2\x80\x9d about the scope of its new rule now casts a cloud on every decision by every local government to require a person seeking a permit to pay or spend money. Ante, at 20.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt bottom, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s analysis seems to grow out of a yen for a prophylactic rule: Unless Nollan and Dolan apply to monetary demands, the majority worries, \xe2\x80\x9cland-use permitting officials\xe2\x80\x9d could easily \xe2\x80\x9cevade the limitations\xe2\x80\x9d on exaction of real property interests that those decisions impose. Ante, at 15. But that is a prophylaxis in search of a problem. No one has presented evidence that in the many States declining to apply heightened scrutiny to permitting fees, local officials routinely short-circuit Nollan and Dolan to extort the surrender of real property interests having no relation to a development\xe2\x80\x99s costs. See, e.g., Krupp v. Breckenridge Sanitation Dist., 19 P.\xc2\xa03d, at 697; Home Builders Assn. of Central Arizona v. Scottsdale, 187 Ariz. 479, 486, 930 P.\xc2\xa02d 993, 1000 (1997); McCarthy v. Leawood, 257 Kan. 566, 579, 894 P.\xc2\xa02d 836, 845 (1995). And if officials were to impose a fee as a contrivance to take an easement (or other real property right), then a court could indeed apply Nollan and Dolan. See, e.g., Norwood v. Baker, \n\n172 U.\xc2\xa0S. 269 (1898)\n (preventing circumvention of the Takings Clause by prohibiting the government from imposing a special assessment for the full value of a property in advance of condemning it). That situation does not call for a rule extending, as the majority\xe2\x80\x99s does, to all monetary exactions. Finally, a court can use the Penn Central framework, the Due Process Clause, and (in many places) state law to protect against monetary demands, whether or not imposed to evade Nollan and Dolan, that simply \xe2\x80\x9cgo[\xc2\xa0] too far.\xe2\x80\x9d Mahon, 260 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 415; see supra, at 3.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, Nollan and Dolan restrain governments from using the permitting process to do what the Takings Clause would otherwise prevent\xe2\x80\x94i.e., take a specific property interest without just compensation. Those cases have no application when governments impose a general financial obligation as part of the permitting process, because under Apfel such an action does not otherwise trigger the Takings Clause\xe2\x80\x99s protections. By extending Nollan and Dolan\xe2\x80\x99s heightened scrutiny to a simple payment demand, the majority threatens the heartland of local land-use regulation and service delivery, at a bare minimum depriving state and local governments of \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary predictability.\xe2\x80\x9d Apfel, 524 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 542 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). That decision is unwarranted\xe2\x80\x94and deeply unwise. I would keep Nollan and Dolan in their intended sphere and affirm the Florida Supreme Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI also would affirm the judgment below for two independent reasons, even assuming that a demand for money can trigger Nollan and Dolan. First, the District never demanded that Koontz give up anything (including money) as a condition for granting him a permit.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t And second, because (as everyone agrees) no actual taking occurred, Koontz cannot claim just compensation even had the District made a demand. The majority nonetheless remands this case on the theory that Koontz might still be entitled to money damages. I cannot see how, and so would spare the Florida courts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNollan and Dolan apply only when the government makes a \xe2\x80\x9cdemand[\xc2\xa0]\xe2\x80\x9d that a landowner turn over property in exchange for a permit. Lingle, 544 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 546. I understand the majority to agree with that proposition: After all, the entire unconstitutional conditions doctrine, as the majority notes, rests on the fear that the government may use its control over benefits (like permits) to \xe2\x80\x9ccoerc[e]\xe2\x80\x9d a person into giving up a constitutional right. Ante, at 7; see ante, at 13. A Nollan-Dolan claim therefore depends on a showing of government coercion, not relevant in an ordinary challenge to a permit denial. See Monterey v. Del Monte Dunes at Monterey, Ltd., \n\n526 U.\xc2\xa0S. 687, \n703 (1999)\n (Nollan and Dolan were \xe2\x80\x9cnot designed to address, and [are] not readily applicable to,\xe2\x80\x9d a claim based on the mere \xe2\x80\x9cdenial of [a] development\xe2\x80\x9d permit). Before applying Nollan and Dolan, a court must find that the permit denial occurred because the government made a demand of the landowner, which he rebuffed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd unless Nollan and Dolan are to wreck land-use permitting throughout the country\xe2\x80\x94to the detriment of both communities and property owners\xe2\x80\x94that demand must be unequivocal. If a local government risked a lawsuit every time it made a suggestion to an applicant about how to meet permitting criteria, it would cease to do so; indeed, the government might desist altogether from communicating with applicants. That hazard is to some extent baked into Nollan and Dolan; observers have wondered whether those decisions have inclined some local governments to deny permit applications outright, rather than negotiate agreements that could work to both sides\xe2\x80\x99 advantage. See W. Fischel, Regulatory Takings 346 (1995). But that danger would rise exponentially if something less than a clear condition\xe2\x80\x94if each idea or proposal offered in the back-and-forth of reconciling diverse interests\xe2\x80\x94triggered Nollan-Dolan scrutiny. At that point, no local government official with a decent lawyer would have a conversation with a developer. Hence the need to reserve Nollan and Dolan, as we always have, for reviewing only what an official demands, not all he says in negotiations.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWith that as backdrop, consider how this case arose. To arrest the loss of the State\xe2\x80\x99s rapidly diminishing wetlands, Florida law prevents landowners from filling or draining any such property without two permits. See ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933. Koontz\xe2\x80\x99s property qualifies as a wetland, and he therefore needed the permits to embark on development. His applications, however, failed the District\xe2\x80\x99s preliminary review: The District found that they did not preserve wetlands or protect fish and wildlife to the extent Florida law required. See App. Exh. 19\xe2\x80\x9320, 47. At that point, the District could simply have denied the applications; had it done so, the Penn Central test\xe2\x80\x94not Nollan and Dolan\xe2\x80\x94would have governed any takings claim Koontz might have brought. See Del Monte Dunes, 526 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 702\xe2\x80\x93703.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRather than reject the applications, however, the District suggested to Koontz ways he could modify them to meet legal requirements. The District proposed reducing the development\xe2\x80\x99s size or modifying its design to lessen the impact on wetlands. See App. Exh. 87\xe2\x80\x9388, 91\xe2\x80\x9392. Alternatively, the District raised several options for \xe2\x80\x9coff-site mitigation\xe2\x80\x9d that Koontz could undertake in a nearby nature preserve, thus compensating for the loss of wetlands his project would cause. Id., at 90\xe2\x80\x9391. The District never made any particular demand respecting an off-site project (or anything else); as Koontz testified at trial, that possibility was presented only in broad strokes, \xe2\x80\x9c[n]ot in any great detail.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 103. And the District made clear that it welcomed additional proposals from Koontz to mitigate his project\xe2\x80\x99s damage to wetlands. See id., at 75. Even at the final hearing on his applications, the District asked Koontz if he would \xe2\x80\x9cbe willing to go back with the staff over the next month and renegotiate this thing and try to come up with\xe2\x80\x9d a solution. Id., at 37. But Koontz refused, saying (through his lawyer) that the proposal he submitted was \xe2\x80\x9cas good as it can get.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 41. The District therefore denied the applications, consistent with its original view that they failed to satisfy Florida law.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn short, the District never made a demand or set a condition\xe2\x80\x94not to cede an identifiable property interest, not to undertake a particular mitigation project, not even to write a check to the government. Instead, the District suggested to Koontz several non-exclusive ways to make his applications conform to state law. The District\xe2\x80\x99s only hard-and-fast requirement was that Koontz do something\xe2\x80\x94anything\xe2\x80\x94to satisfy the relevant permitting criteria. Koontz\xe2\x80\x99s failure to obtain the permits therefore did not result from his refusal to accede to an allegedly extortionate demand or condition; rather, it arose from the legal deficien-cies of his applications, combined with his unwillingness to correct them by any means. Nollan and Dolan were never meant to address such a run-of-the-mill denial of a land-use permit. As applications of the unconstitutional conditions doctrine, those decisions require a condition; and here, there was none.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIndeed, this case well illustrates the danger of extending Nollan and Dolan beyond their proper compass. Consider the matter from the standpoint of the District\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer. The District, she learns, has found that Koontz\xe2\x80\x99s permit applications do not satisfy legal requirements. It can deny the permits on that basis; or it can suggest ways for Koontz to bring his applications into compliance. If every suggestion could become the subject of a lawsuit under Nollan and Dolan, the lawyer can give but one recommendation: Deny the permits, without giving Koontz any advice\xe2\x80\x94even if he asks for guidance. As the Florida Supreme Court observed of this case: Were Nollan and Dolan to apply, the District would \xe2\x80\x9copt to simply deny permits outright without discussion or negotiation rather than risk the crushing costs of litigation\xe2\x80\x9d; and property owners like Koontz then would \xe2\x80\x9chave no opportunity to amend their applications or discuss mitigation options.\xe2\x80\x9d 77 So. 3d 1220, 1231 (2011). Nothing in the Takings Clause requires that folly. I would therefore hold that the District did not impose an unconstitutional condition\xe2\x80\x94because it did not impose a condition at all.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd finally, a third difficulty: Even if (1) money counted as \xe2\x80\x9cspecific and identified propert[y]\xe2\x80\x9d under Apfel (though it doesn\xe2\x80\x99t), and (2) the District made a demand for it (though it didn\xe2\x80\x99t), (3) Koontz never paid a cent, so the District took nothing from him. As I have explained, that third point does not prevent Koontz from suing to invalidate the purported demand as an unconstitutional condition. See supra, at 1\xe2\x80\x932. But it does mean, as the majority agrees, that Koontz is not entitled to just compensation under the Takings Clause. See ante, at 11. He may obtain monetary relief under the Florida statute he invoked only if it authorizes damages beyond just compensation for a taking.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority remands that question to the Florida Supreme Court, and given how it disposes of the other issues here, I can understand why. As the majority indicates, a State could decide to create a damages remedy not only for a taking, but also for an unconstitutional conditions claim predicated on the Takings Clause. And that question is one of state law, which we usually do well to leave to state courts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut as I look to the Florida statute here, I cannot help but see yet another reason why the Florida Supreme Court got this case right. That statute authorizes damages only for \xe2\x80\x9can unreasonable exercise of the state\xe2\x80\x99s police power constituting a taking without just compensation.\xe2\x80\x9d Fla. Stat. \xc2\xa7373.617 (2010); see ante, at 12. In what legal universe could a law authorizing damages only for a \xe2\x80\x9ctaking\xe2\x80\x9d also provide damages when (as all agree) no taking has occurred? I doubt that inside-out, upside-down universe is the State of Florida. Certainly, none of the Florida courts in this case suggested that the majority\xe2\x80\x99s hypothesized remedy actually exists; rather, the trial and appellate courts imposed a damages remedy on the mistaken theory that there had been a taking (although of exactly what neither was clear). See App. to Pet. for Cert. C\xe2\x80\x932; 5 So. 3d 8, 8 (2009). So I would, once more, affirm the Florida Supreme Court, not make it say again what it has already said\xe2\x80\x94that Koontz is not entitled to money damages.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNollan and Dolan are important decisions, designed to curb governments from using their power over land-use permitting to extract for free what the Takings Clause would otherwise require them to pay for. But for no fewer than three independent reasons, this case does not present that problem. First and foremost, the government commits a taking only when it appropriates a specific property interest, not when it requires a person to pay or spend money. Here, the District never took or threatened such an interest; it tried to extract from Koontz solely a commitment to spend money to repair public wetlands. Second, Nollan and Dolan can operate only when the government makes a demand of the permit applicant; the decisions\xe2\x80\x99 prerequisite, in other words, is a condition. Here, the District never made such a demand: It informed Koontz that his applications did not meet legal requirements; it offered suggestions for bringing those applications into compliance; and it solicited further proposals from Koontz to achieve the same end. That is not the stuff of which an unconstitutional condition is made. And third, the Florida statute at issue here does not, in any event, offer a damages remedy for imposing such a condition. It provides relief only for a consummated taking, which did not occur here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority\xe2\x80\x99s errors here are consequential. The majority turns a broad array of local land-use regulations into federal constitutional questions. It deprives state and local governments of the flexibility they need to enhance their communities\xe2\x80\x94to ensure environmentally sound and economically productive development. It places courts smack in the middle of the most everyday local government activity. As those consequences play out across the country, I believe the Court will rue today\xe2\x80\x99s decision. I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t
