\n\t\t\t\tSEKHAR v. UNITED STATES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 12\xe2\x80\x93357.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 23, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 26, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tInvestments for the employee pension fund of the State of New York and its local governments are chosen by the fund\xe2\x80\x99s sole trustee, the State Comptroller. After the Comptroller\xe2\x80\x99s general counsel recommended against investing in a fund managed by FA Technology Ventures, the general counsel received anonymous e-mails demanding that he recommend the investment and threatening, if he did not, to disclose information about the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s alleged affair to his wife, government officials, and the media. Some of the e-mails were traced to the home computer of petitioner Sekhar, a managing partner of FA Technology Ventures. Petitioner was convicted of attempted extortion, in violation of the Hobbs Act, \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71951(a), which defines \xe2\x80\x9cextortion\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cthe obtaining of property from another, with his consent, induced by wrongful use of actual or threatened force, violence, or fear, or under color of official right,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71951(b)(2). The jury specified that the property petitioner attempted to extort was the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s recommendation to approve the investment. The Second Circuit affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Attempting to compel a person to recommend that his employer approve an investment does not constitute \xe2\x80\x9cthe obtaining of property from another\xe2\x80\x9d under the Hobbs Act. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x939.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Absent other indication, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress intends to incorporate the well-settled meaning of the common-law terms it uses.\xe2\x80\x9d Neder v. United States, \n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n. As far as is known, no case predating the Hobbs Act\xe2\x80\x94English, federal, or state\xe2\x80\x94ever identified conduct such as that charged here as extortionate. Extortion required the obtaining of items of value, typically cash, from the victim. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s text confirms that obtaining property requires \xe2\x80\x9cnot only the deprivation but also the acquisition of property.\xe2\x80\x9d Scheidler v. National Organization for Women, Inc., \n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393\n. The property extorted must therefore be transferable\xe2\x80\x94that is, capable of passing from one person to another, a defining feature lacking in the alleged property here. The genesis of the Hobbs Act reinforces that conclusion. Congress borrowed nearly verbatim the definition of extortion from a 1909 New York statute but did not copy the coercion provision of that statute. And in 1946, the time of the borrowing, New York courts had consistently held that the sort of interference with rights that occurred here was coercion. Finally, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s own precedent demands reversal of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s convictions. See id., at 404\xe2\x80\x93405. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s defense of the theory of conviction is unpersuasive. No fluent speaker of English would say that \xe2\x80\x9cpetitioner obtained and exercised the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s right to make a recommendation,\xe2\x80\x9d any more than he would say that a person \xe2\x80\x9cobtained and exercised another\xe2\x80\x99s right to free speech.\xe2\x80\x9d He would say that \xe2\x80\x9cpetitioner forced the general counsel to make a particular recommendation,\xe2\x80\x9d just as he would say that a person \xe2\x80\x9cforced another to make a statement.\xe2\x80\x9d Adopting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory here would not only make nonsense of words; it would collapse the longstanding distinction between extortion and coercion and ignore Congress\xe2\x80\x99s choice to penalize one but not the other. See Scheidler, supra, at 409. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t683 F.\xc2\xa03d 436, reversed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe consider whether attempting to compel a person to recommend that his employer approve an investment con- stitutes \xe2\x80\x9cthe obtaining of property from another\xe2\x80\x9d under \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71951(b)(2).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNew York\xe2\x80\x99s Common Retirement Fund is an employee pension fund for the State of New York and its local governments. As sole trustee of the Fund, the State Comptroller chooses Fund investments. When the Comptroller decides to approve an investment he issues a \xe2\x80\x9cCommitment.\xe2\x80\x9d A Commitment, however, does not actually bind the Fund. For that to happen, the Fund and the recipient of the investment must enter into a limited partnership agreement. 683 F.\xc2\xa03d 436, 438 (CA2 2012).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Giridhar Sekhar was a managing partner of FA Technology Ventures. In October 2009, the Comptroller\xe2\x80\x99s office was considering whether to invest in a fund managed by that firm. The office\xe2\x80\x99s general counsel made a written recommendation to the Comptroller not to invest in the fund, after learning that the Office of the New York Attorney General was investigating another fund managed by the firm. The Comptroller decided not to issue a Commitment and notified a partner of FA Technology Ventures. That partner had previously heard rumors that the general counsel was having an extramarital affair.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe general counsel then received a series of anony- mous e-mails demanding that he recommend moving for- ward with the investment and threatening, if he did not, to disclose information about his alleged affair to his wife, government officials, and the media. App. 59\xe2\x80\x9361. The general counsel contacted law enforcement, which traced some of the e-mails to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s home computer and other e-mails to offices of FA Technology Ventures.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner was indicted for, and a jury convicted him of, attempted extortion, in violation of the Hobbs Act, \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71951(a). That Act subjects a person to criminal liability if he \xe2\x80\x9cin any way or degree obstructs, delays, or affects commerce or the movement of any article or commodity in commerce, by robbery or extortion or attempts or conspires so to do.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71951(a). The Act defines \xe2\x80\x9cextortion\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cthe obtaining of property from another, with his consent, induced by wrongful use of actual or threatened force, violence, or fear, or under color of official right.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71951(b)(2).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t On the verdict form, the jury was asked to specify the property that petitioner attempted to extort: (1) \xe2\x80\x9cthe Commitment\xe2\x80\x9d; (2) \xe2\x80\x9cthe Comptroller\xe2\x80\x99s approval of the Commitment\xe2\x80\x9d; or (3) \xe2\x80\x9cthe General Counsel\xe2\x80\x99s recommendation to approve the Commitment.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 141\xe2\x80\x93142. The jury chose only the third option.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit affirmed the conviction. The court held that the general counsel \xe2\x80\x9chad a property right in rendering sound legal advice to the Comptroller and, specifically, to recommend\xe2\x80\x94free from threats\xe2\x80\x94whether the Comptroller should issue a Commitment for [the funds].\xe2\x80\x9d 683 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 441. The court concluded that petitioner not only attempted to deprive the general counsel of his \xe2\x80\x9cproperty right,\xe2\x80\x9d but that petitioner also \xe2\x80\x9cattempted to exercise that right by forcing the General Counsel to make a recommendation determined by [petitioner].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 442.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari. 568 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhether viewed from the standpoint of the common law, the text and genesis of the statute at issue here, or the jurisprudence of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior cases, what was charged in this case was not extortion.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is a settled principle of interpretation that, absent other indication, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress intends to incorporate the well-settled meaning of the common-law terms it uses.\xe2\x80\x9d Neder v. United States, \n\n527 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n23 (1999)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[W]here Congress borrows terms of art in which are accumulated the legal tradition and meaning of centuries of practice, it presumably knows and adopts the cluster of ideas that were attached to each borrowed word in the body of learning from which it was taken and the meaning its use will convey to the judicial mind unless otherwise instructed.\xe2\x80\x9d Morissette v. United States, \n\n342 U. S. 246, \n263 (1952)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tOr as Justice Frankfurter colorfully put it, \xe2\x80\x9cif a word is obviously transplanted from another legal source, whether the common law or other legislation, it brings the old soil with it.\xe2\x80\x9d Some Reflections on the Reading of Statutes, 47 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 527, 537 (1947).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Hobbs Act punishes \xe2\x80\x9cextortion,\xe2\x80\x9d one of the oldest crimes in our legal tradition, see E. Coke, The Third Part of the Institutes of the Laws of England 148\xe2\x80\x93150 (1648) (reprint 2008). The crime originally applied only to extortionate action by public officials, but was later extended by statute to private extortion. See 4 C. Torcia, Wharton\xe2\x80\x99s Criminal Law \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7695, 699 (14th ed. 1981). As far as is known, no case predating the Hobbs Act\xe2\x80\x94English, federal, or state\xe2\x80\x94ever identified conduct such as that charged here as extortionate. Extortion required the obtaining of items of value, typically cash, from the victim. See, e.g., People v. Whaley, 6 Cow. 661 (N.\xc2\xa0Y. Sup. Ct. 1827) (justice of the peace properly indicted for extorting money); Commonwealth v. Bagley, 24 Mass. 279 (1828) (officer properly convicted for demanding a fee for letting a man out of prison); Commonwealth v. Mitchell, 66 Ky. 25 (1867) (jailer properly indicted for extorting money from pris- oner); Queen v. Woodward, 11 Mod. 137, 88 Eng. Rep. 949 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1707) (upholding indictment for extorting \xe2\x80\x9cmoney and a note\xe2\x80\x9d). It did not cover mere coercion to act, or to refrain from acting. See, e.g., King v. Burdett, 1 Ld. Raym. 149, 91 Eng. Rep. 996 (K.\xc2\xa0B. 1696) (dictum) (extortion consisted of the \xe2\x80\x9ctaking of money for the use of the stalls,\xe2\x80\x9d not the deprivation of \xe2\x80\x9cfree liberty to sell [one\xe2\x80\x99s] wares in the market according to law\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe text of the statute at issue confirms that the alleged property here cannot be extorted. Enacted in 1946, the Hobbs Act defines its crime of \xe2\x80\x9cextortion\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe ob- taining of property from another, with his consent, induced by wrongful use of actual or threatened force, violence, or fear, or under color of official right.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71951(b)(2) (emphasis added). Obtaining property requires \xe2\x80\x9cnot only the deprivation but also the acquisition of property.\xe2\x80\x9d Scheidler v. National Organization for Women, Inc., \n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393, \n404 (2003)\n (citing United States v. Enmons, \n\n410 U.\xc2\xa0S. 396, \n400 (1973)\n). That is, it requires that the victim \xe2\x80\x9cpart with\xe2\x80\x9d his property, R. Perkins & R. Boyce, Criminal Law 451 (3d ed. 1982), and that the extortionist \xe2\x80\x9cgain possession\xe2\x80\x9d of it, Scheidler, supra, at 403, n.\xc2\xa08; see also Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 1682 (2d ed. 1949) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cobtain\xe2\x80\x9d); Murray, Note, Protesters, Extortion, and Coercion: Preventing RICO from Chilling \n \nFirst Amendment Freedoms, 75 Notre Dame L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 691, 706 (1999) (Murray). The property extorted must therefore be transferable\xe2\x80\x94that is, capable of passing from one person to another. The alleged property here lacks that defining feature.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe genesis of the Hobbs Act reinforces that conclusion. The Act was modeled after \xc2\xa7850 of the New York Penal Law (1909), which was derived from the famous Field Code, a 19th-century model penal code, see 4 Commissioners of the Code, Penal Code of the State of New York \xc2\xa7613, p.\xc2\xa0220 (1865) (reprint 1998). Congress borrowed, nearly verbatim, the New York statute\xe2\x80\x99s definition of extortion. See Scheidler, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 403. The New York statute contained, in addition to the felony crime of extortion, a new (that is to say, nonexistent at common law) misdemeanor crime of coercion. Whereas the former required, as we have said, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the criminal acquisition of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. property,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., the latter required merely the use of threats \xe2\x80\x9cto compel another person to do or to abstain from doing an act which such other such person has a legal right to do or to abstain from doing.\xe2\x80\x9d N.\xc2\xa0Y. Penal Law \xc2\xa7530 (1909), earlier codified in N.\xc2\xa0Y. Penal Code \xc2\xa7653 (1881). Congress did not copy the coercion provision. The omission must have been deliberate, since it was perfectly clear that extortion did not include coercion. At the time of the borrowing (1946), New York courts had consistently held that the sort of interference with rights that occurred here was coercion. See, e.g., People v. Ginsberg, 262 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 556, 188 N.\xc2\xa0E. 62 (1933) (per curiam) (compelling store owner to become a member of a trade association and to remove advertisements); People v. Scotti, 266 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 480, 195 N.\xc2\xa0E. 162 (App. Div. 1934) (compelling victim to enter into agreement with union); People v. Kaplan, 240 App. Div. 72, 74\xe2\x80\x9375, 269 N.\xc2\xa0Y.\xc2\xa0S. 161, 163\xe2\x80\x93164, aff\xe2\x80\x99d, 264 N.\xc2\xa0Y. 675, 191 N.\xc2\xa0E. 621 (1934) (compelling union members to drop lawsuits against union leadership).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAnd finally, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s own precedent similarly demands reversal of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s convictions. In Scheidler, we held that protesters did not commit extortion under the Hobbs Act, even though they \xe2\x80\x9cinterfered with, disrupted, and in some instances completely deprived\xe2\x80\x9d abortion clinics of their ability to run their business. 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 404\xe2\x80\x93405. We reasoned that the protesters may have deprived the clinics of an \xe2\x80\x9calleged property right,\xe2\x80\x9d but they did not pursue or receive \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98something of value from\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the clinics that they could then \xe2\x80\x9cexercise, transfer, or sell\xe2\x80\x9d themselves. Id., at 405. The opinion supported its holding by citing the three New York coercion cases discussed above. See id., at 405\xe2\x80\x93406.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case is easier than Scheidler, where one might at least have said that physical occupation of property amounted to obtaining that property. The deprivation alleged here is far more abstract. Scheidler rested its decision, as we do, on the term \xe2\x80\x9cobtaining.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 402, n.\xc2\xa06. The principle announced there\xe2\x80\x94that a defendant must pursue something of value from the victim that can be exercised, transferred, or sold\xe2\x80\x94applies with equal force here.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Whether one considers the personal right at issue to be \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d in a broad sense or not, it certainly was not obtainable property under the Hobbs Act.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government\xe2\x80\x99s shifting and imprecise characterization of the alleged property at issue betrays the weakness of its case. According to the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict form, the \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d that petitioner attempted to extort was \xe2\x80\x9cthe General Counsel\xe2\x80\x99s recommendation to approve the Commitment.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 142. But the Government expends minuscule effort in defending that theory of conviction. And for good reason\xe2\x80\x94to wit, our decision in Cleveland v. United States, \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 12 (2000)\n, which reversed a business owner\xe2\x80\x99s mail-fraud conviction for \xe2\x80\x9cobtaining money or property\xe2\x80\x9d through misrepresentations made in an application for a video-poker license issued by the State. We held that a \xe2\x80\x9clicense\xe2\x80\x9d is not \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d while in the State\xe2\x80\x99s hands and so cannot be \xe2\x80\x9cobtained\xe2\x80\x9d from the State. Id., at 20\xe2\x80\x9322. Even less so can an employee\xe2\x80\x99s yet-to-be-issued recommendation be called obtainable property, and less so still a yet-to-be-issued recommendation that would merely ap- prove (but not effect) a particular investment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tHence the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on an alternative, more sophisticated (and sophistic) description of the property. Instead of defending the jury\xe2\x80\x99s description, the Gov- ernment hinges its case on the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cintangible property right to give his disinterested legal opinion to his client free of improper outside interference.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 39. But what, exactly, would the petitioner have obtained for himself? A right to give his own disinterested legal opinion to his own client free of improper interference? Or perhaps, a right to give the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s disinterested legal opinion to the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s client?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEither formulation sounds absurd, because it is. Clearly, petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s goal was not to acquire the general coun- sel\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cintangible property right to give disinterested legal advice.\xe2\x80\x9d It was to force the general counsel to offer advice that accorded with petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s wishes. But again, that is coercion, not extortion. See Murray 721\xe2\x80\x93722. No fluent speaker of English would say that \xe2\x80\x9cpetitioner obtained and exercised the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s right to make a recommendation,\xe2\x80\x9d any more than he would say that a person \xe2\x80\x9cobtained and exercised another\xe2\x80\x99s right to free speech.\xe2\x80\x9d He would say that \xe2\x80\x9cpetitioner forced the general counsel to make a particular recommendation,\xe2\x80\x9d just as he would say that a person \xe2\x80\x9cforced another to make a statement.\xe2\x80\x9d Adopting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory here would not only make nonsense of words; it would collapse the longstanding distinction between extortion and coercion and ignore Congress\xe2\x80\x99s choice to penalize one but not the other. See Scheidler, supra, at 409. That we cannot do. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit is reversed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIt is so ordered.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Alito, with whom Justice Kennedy and Jus-tice Sotomayor join, concurring in the judgment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe question that we must decide in this case is whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe General Counsel\xe2\x80\x99s recommendation to approve the Commitment,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 142\xe2\x80\x94or his right to make that recommendation\xe2\x80\x94is property that is capable of being ex-torted under the Hobbs Act, \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71951. In my view, they are not.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe jury in this case returned a special verdict form and stated that the property that petitioner attempted to extort was \xe2\x80\x9cthe General Counsel\xe2\x80\x99s recommendation to approve the Commitment.\xe2\x80\x9d What the jury obviously meant by this was the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s internal suggestion to his superior that the state government issue a nonbinding commitment to invest in a fund managed by FA Technology Ventures. We must therefore decide whether this nonbinding internal recommendation by a salaried state employee constitutes \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of the Hobbs Act, which defines \xe2\x80\x9cextortion\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe obtaining of property from another, with his con- sent, induced by wrongful use of actual or threatened force, violence, or fear, or under color of official right.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71951(b)(2).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Hobbs Act does not define the term \xe2\x80\x9cproperty,\xe2\x80\x9d but even at common law the offense of extortion was understood to include the obtaining of any thing of value. 2 E. Coke, The First Part of the Institutes of the Laws of England 368b (18th English ed. 1823) (\xe2\x80\x9cExtortion .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is a great misprison, by wresting or unlawfully taking by any officer, by colour of his office, any money or valuable thing of or from any man\xe2\x80\x9d); 4 W. Blackstone, Commentaries *141 (extortion is \xe2\x80\x9can abuse of public, justice which consists in any officer\xe2\x80\x99s unlawfully taking, by colour of his office, from any man, any money or thing of value\xe2\x80\x9d). See also 2 J. Bishop, Criminal Law \xc2\xa7401, pp. 331\xe2\x80\x93332 (9th ed. 1923) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn most cases, the thing obtained is money.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. But probably anything of value will suffice\xe2\x80\x9d); 3 F. Wharton, A Treatise on Criminal Law \xc2\xa71898, p. 2095 (11th ed. 1912) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is enough if any valuable thing is received\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt the time Congress enacted the Hobbs Act, the contemporary edition of Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary included an expansive definition of the term. See Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1446 (3d ed. 1933). It stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he term is said to extend to every species of valuable right and interest.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. The word is also commonly used to denote everything which is the subject of ownership, corporeal or incorporeal, tangible or intangible, visible or invisible, real or personal; everything that has an exchangeable value or which goes to make up wealth or estate.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1446\xe2\x80\x931447. And the lower courts have long given the term a similarly expansive construction. See, e.g., United States v. Tropiano, 418 F. 2d 1069, 1075 (CA2 1969) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe concept of prop-erty under the Hobbs Act .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. includes, in a broad sense, any valuable right considered as a source or element of wealth\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tDespite the breadth of some of these formulations, however, the term \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d plainly does not reach everything that a person may hold dear; nor does it extend to everything that might in some indirect way portend the possibility of future economic gain. I do not suggest that the current lower court case law is necessarily correct, but it seems clear that the case now before us is an outlier and that the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict stretches the concept of property beyond the breaking point.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is not customary to refer to an internal recommendation to make a government decision as a form of property. It would seem strange to say that the government or its employees have a property interest in their internal recommendations regarding such things as the issuance of a building permit, the content of an environmental impact statement, the approval of a new drug, or the indictment of an individual or a corporation. And it would be even stranger to say that a private party who might be affected by the government\xe2\x80\x99s decision can obtain a property interest in a recommendation to make the decision. See, e.g., Doyle v. University of Alabama, 680 F.\xc2\xa02d 1323, 1326 (CA11 1982) (\xe2\x80\x9cDoyle had no protected property interest in the mere recommendation for a raise; thus she was not entitled to due process safeguards when the recommended raise was disapproved by the University\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur decision in Cleveland v. United States, \n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 12 (2000)\n, supports the conclusion that internal recommendations regarding government decisions are not property. In Cleveland, we vacated a business owner\xe2\x80\x99s conviction under the federal mail fraud statute, \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341, for \xe2\x80\x9cobtaining money or property\xe2\x80\x9d through misrepresentations made in an application for a video poker license issued by the State. We held that a video poker license is not property in the hands of the State. Cleveland, supra, at 15. I do not suggest that the concepts of property under the mail fraud statute and the Hobbs Act are necessarily the same. But surely a video poker license has a stronger claim to be classified as property than a mere internal recommendation that a state government take an initial step that might lead eventually to an investment that would be beneficial to private parties.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government has not cited any Hobbs Act case holding that an internal recommendation regarding a gov-ernment decision constitutes property. Nor has the Government cited any other example of the use of the term \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d in this sense.*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Second Circuit recharacterized the property that petitioner attempted to obtain as the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cright to make a recommendation consistent with his legal judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d 683 F.\xc2\xa03d 436, 442 (2012). And the Government also presses that theory in this Court. Brief for United States 15, 34\xe2\x80\x9345. According to the Government, the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s property interest in his recommendation encompasses the right to make the recommendation. Id., at 35\xe2\x80\x9336. But this argument assumes that the recommendation itself is property. See id., at 35 (the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98recommendation\xe2\x80\x99 and his \xe2\x80\x98right to make the recommendation\xe2\x80\x99 are merely different expressions of the same property\xe2\x80\x9d). If an internal recommendation regarding a government decision does not constitute property, then surely a government employee\xe2\x80\x99s right to make such a recommendation is not property either (nor could it be deemed a property right).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government argues that the recommendation was the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s personal property because it was inextricably related to his right to pursue his profession as an attorney. See id., at 34\xe2\x80\x9335. But that argument is clearly wrong: If the general counsel had left the State\xe2\x80\x99s employ before submitting the recommendation, he could not have taken the recommendation with him, and he certainly could not have given it or sold it to someone else. Therefore, it is obvious that the recommendation (and the right to make it) were inextricably related to the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s position with the government, and not to his broader personal right to pursue the practice of law.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s job surely had economic value to him, as did his labor as a lawyer, his law license, and his reputation as an attorney. But the indictment did not allege, and the jury did not find, that petitioner attempted to obtain those things. Nor would such a theory make sense in the context of this case. Petitioner did not, for example, seek the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s legal advice or demand that the general counsel represent him in a legal proceeding. Cf. United States v. Thompson, 647 F.\xc2\xa03d 180, 186\xe2\x80\x93187 (CA5 2011) (a person\xe2\x80\x99s labor is property capable of being extorted). Nor did petitioner attempt to enhance his own ability to compete with the general counsel for legal work by threatening to do something that would, say, tarnish the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s reputation or cause his law license to be revoked. Cf. Tropiano, 418 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 1071\xe2\x80\x931072, 1075\xe2\x80\x931077 (threats to competitor in order to obtain customers constitute extortion); United States v. Zemek, 634 F.\xc2\xa02d 1159, 1173\xe2\x80\x931174 (CA9 1980) (same); United States v. Coffey, 361 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 102, 108\xe2\x80\x93109 (EDNY 2005) (the right to pursue a lawful business is extortable property under the Hobbs Act).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court holds that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s conduct does not amount to attempted extortion, but for a different reason: According to the Court, the alleged property that petitioner pursued was not transferrable and therefore is not capable of being \xe2\x80\x9cobtained.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, 7\xe2\x80\x938. Because I do not believe that the item in question constitutes prop-erty, it is unnecessary for me to determine whether or not petitioner sought to obtain it.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf Congress had wanted to classify internal recommendations pertaining to government decisions as property, I think it would have spoken more clearly than it did in the Hobbs Act. But even if the Hobbs Act were ambiguous on this point, the rule of lenity would counsel in favor of an interpretation of the statute that does not reach so broadly, see Scheidler v. National Organization for Women, Inc., \n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393, \n409 (2003)\n. This is not to say that the Government could not have prosecuted petitioner for ex-tortion on these same facts under some other theory. The question before us is whether the general counsel\xe2\x80\x99s recommendation\xe2\x80\x94or the right to make it\xe2\x80\x94constitutes property under the Hobbs Act. In my view, they do not.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor these reasons, I concur in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.\n\t\t\t
