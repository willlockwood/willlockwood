\nMEDELLIN V. TEXAS

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

MEDELLIN v. TEXAS

\n

certiorari to the court of criminal appeals of texas

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x93984.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 10, 2007\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 25, 2008

\n

In the Case Concerning Avena and Other Mexican Nationals (Mex. v. U. S.), 2004 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0J. 12 (Avena), the International Court of Justice (ICJ) held that the United States had violated Article 36(1)(b) of the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations (Vienna Convention or Convention) by failing to inform 51 named Mexican nationals, including petitioner Medell\xc3\xadn, of their Vienna Convention rights. The ICJ found that those named individuals were entitled to review and reconsideration of their U. S. state-court convictions and sentences regardless of their failure to comply with generally applicable state rules governing challenges to criminal convictions. In Sanchez-Llamas v. Oregon, 548 U. S. 331\xe2\x80\x94issued after Avena but involving individuals who were not named in the Avena judgment\xe2\x80\x94this Court held, contrary to the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s determination, that the Convention did not preclude the application of state default rules. The President then issued a memorandum (President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum or Memorandum) stating that the United States would \xe2\x80\x9cdischarge its international obligations\xe2\x80\x9d under Avena \xe2\x80\x9cby having State courts give effect to the decision.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Relying on Avena and the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum, Medell\xc3\xadn filed a second Texas state-court habeas application challenging his state capital murder conviction and death sentence on the ground that he had not been informed of his Vienna Convention rights. The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals dismissed Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s application as an abuse of the writ, concluding that neither Avena nor the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum was binding federal law that could displace the State\xe2\x80\x99s limitations on filing successive habeas applications.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Neither Avena nor the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum constitutes directly enforceable federal law that pre-empts state limitations on the filing of successive habeas petitions. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9337.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The Avena judgment is not directly enforceable as domestic law in state court. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9327.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0While a treaty may constitute an international commitment, it is not binding domestic law unless Congress has enacted statutes implementing it or the treaty itself conveys an intention that it be \xe2\x80\x9cself-executing\xe2\x80\x9d and is ratified on that basis. See, e.g., Foster v. Neilson, 2 Pet. 253, 314. The Avena judgment creates an international law obligation on the part of the United States, but it is not automatically binding domestic law because none of the relevant treaty sources\xe2\x80\x94the Optional Protocol, the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter, or the ICJ Statute\xe2\x80\x94creates binding federal law in the absence of implementing legislation, and no such legislation has been enacted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The most natural reading of the Optional Protocol is that it is a bare grant of jurisdiction. The Protocol says nothing about the effect of an ICJ decision, does not commit signatories to comply therewith, and is silent as to any enforcement mechanism. The obligation to comply with ICJ judgments is derived from Article 94 of the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter, which provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach \xe2\x80\xa6 Member \xe2\x80\xa6 undertakes to comply with the [ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s] decision \xe2\x80\xa6 in any case to which it is a party.\xe2\x80\x9d The phrase \xe2\x80\x9cundertakes to comply\xe2\x80\x9d is simply a commitment by member states to take future action through their political branches. That language does not indicate that the Senate, in ratifying the Optional Protocol, intended to vest ICJ decisions with immediate legal effect in domestic courts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This reading is confirmed by Article 94(2)\xe2\x80\x94the enforcement provision\xe2\x80\x94which provides the sole remedy for noncompliance: referral to the U.\xc2\xa0N. Security Council by an aggrieved state. The provision of an express diplomatic rather than judicial remedy is itself evidence that ICJ judgments were not meant to be enforceable in domestic courts. See Sanchez-Llamas, 548 U. S., at 347. Even this \xe2\x80\x9cquintessentially international remed[y],\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 355, is not absolute. It requires a Security Council resolution, and the President and Senate were undoubtedly aware that the United States retained the unqualified right to exercise its veto of any such resolution. Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s construction would eliminate the option of noncompliance contemplated by Article 94(2), undermining the ability of the political branches to determine whether and how to comply with an ICJ judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The ICJ Statute, by limiting disputes to those involving nations, not individuals, and by specifying that ICJ decisions have no binding force except between those nations, provides further evidence that the Avena judgment does not automatically constitute federal law enforceable in U. S. courts. Medell\xc3\xadn, an individual, cannot be considered a party to the Avena decision. Finally, the United States\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of a treaty \xe2\x80\x9cis entitled to great weight,\xe2\x80\x9d Sumitomo Shoji America, Inc. v. Avagliano, 457 U. S., at 184\xe2\x80\x93185, and the Executive Branch has unfailingly adhered to its view that the relevant treaties do not create domestically enforceable federal law. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9317. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The foregoing interpretive approach\xe2\x80\x94parsing a treaty\xe2\x80\x99s text to determine if it is self-executing\xe2\x80\x94is hardly novel. This Court has long looked to the language of a treaty to determine whether the President who negotiated it and the Senate that ratified it intended for the treaty to automatically create domestically enforceable federal law. See, e.g., Foster, supra. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Avena does not by itself constitute binding federal law is confirmed by the \xe2\x80\x9cpostratification understanding\xe2\x80\x9d of signatory countries. See Zicherman v. Korean Air Lines Co., 516 U. S. 217, 226. There are currently 47 nations that are parties to the Optional Protocol and 171 nations that are parties to the Vienna Convention. Yet neither Medell\xc3\xadn nor his amici have identified a single nation that treats ICJ judgments as binding in domestic courts. The lack of any basis for supposing that any other country would treat ICJ judgments as directly enforceable as a matter of their domestic law strongly suggests that the treaty should not be so viewed in our courts. See Sanchez-Llamas, 548 U. S., at 343\xe2\x80\x93344, and n.\xc2\xa03.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion is further supported by general principles of interpretation. Given that the forum state\xe2\x80\x99s procedural rules govern a treaty\xe2\x80\x99s implementation absent a clear and express statement to the contrary, see e.g., id., at 351, one would expect the ratifying parties to the relevant treaties to have clearly stated any intent to give ICJ judgments such effect. There is no statement in the Optional Protocol, the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter, or the ICJ Statute that supports this notion. Moreover, the consequences of Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s argument give pause: neither Texas nor this Court may look behind an ICJ decision and quarrel with its reasoning or result, despite this Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in Sanchez-Llamas that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in the [ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s] structure or purpose \xe2\x80\xa6 suggests that its interpretations were intended to be conclusive on our courts.\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 354. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9324.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding does not call into question the ordinary enforcement of foreign judgments. An agreement to abide by the result of an international adjudication can be a treaty obligation like any other, so long as the agreement is consistent with the Constitution. In addition, Congress is up to the task of implementing non-self-executing treaties, even those involving complex commercial disputes. Medell\xc3\xadn contends that domestic courts generally give effect to foreign judgments, but the judgment Medell\xc3\xadn asks us to enforce is hardly typical: It would enjoin the operation of state law and force the State to take action to \xe2\x80\x9creview and reconside[r]\xe2\x80\x9d his case. Foreign judgments awarding injunctive relief against private parties, let alone sovereign States, \xe2\x80\x9care not generally entitled to enforcement.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Third) of Foreign Relations Law of the United States \xc2\xa7481, Comment b, p. 595 (1986). Pp. 24\xe2\x80\x9327.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum does not independently require the States to provide review and reconsideration of the claims of the 51 Mexican nationals named in Avena without regard to state procedural default rules. Pp. 27\xe2\x80\x9337.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The President seeks to vindicate plainly compelling interests in ensuring the reciprocal observance of the Vienna Convention, protecting relations with foreign governments, and demonstrating commitment to the role of international law. But those interests do not allow the Court to set aside first principles. The President\xe2\x80\x99s authority to act, as with the exercise of any governmental power, \xe2\x80\x9cmust stem either from an act of Congress or from the Constitution itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer, 343 U. S. 579, 585.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Jackson\xe2\x80\x99s familiar tripartite scheme provides the accepted framework for evaluating executive action in this area. First, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen the President acts pursuant to an express or implied authorization of Congress, his authority is at its maximum, for it includes all that he possesses in his own right plus all that Congress can delegate.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown, 343 U. S., at 635 (Jackson, J., concurring). Second, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen the President acts in absence of either a congressional grant or denial of authority, he can only rely upon his own independent powers, but there is a zone of twilight in which he and Congress may have concurrent authority, or in which its distribution is uncertain.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 637. In such a circumstance, Presidential authority can derive support from \xe2\x80\x9ccongressional inertia, indifference or quiescence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Finally, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen the President takes measures incompatible with the expressed or implied will of Congress, his power is at its lowest ebb,\xe2\x80\x9d and the Court can sustain his actions \xe2\x80\x9conly by disabling the Congress from acting upon the subject.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 637\xe2\x80\x93638. Pp.\xc2\xa028\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The United States marshals two principal arguments in favor of the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority to establish binding rules of decision that preempt contrary state law. The United States argues that the relevant treaties give the President the authority to implement the Avena judgment and that Congress has acquiesced in the exercise of such authority. The United States also relies upon an \xe2\x80\x9cindependent\xe2\x80\x9d international dispute-resolution power. We find these arguments, as well as Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s additional argument that the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum is a valid exercise of his \xe2\x80\x9cTake Care\xe2\x80\x9d power, unpersuasive. Pp.\xc2\xa029\xe2\x80\x9337.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(i)\xc2\xa0The United States maintains that the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum is implicitly authorized by the Optional Protocol and the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter. But the responsibility for transforming an international obligation arising from a non-self-executing treaty into domestic law falls to Congress, not the Executive. Foster, 2 Pet., at 315. It is a fundamental constitutional principle that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]he power to make the necessary laws is in Congress; the power to execute in the President.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hamdan v. Rumsfeld, 548 U. S. 557, 591. A non-self-executing treaty, by definition, is one that was ratified with the understanding that it is not to have domestic effect of its own force. That understanding precludes the assertion that Congress has implicitly authorized the President\xe2\x80\x94acting on his own\xe2\x80\x94to achieve precisely the same result. Accordingly, the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum does not fall within the first category of the Youngstown framework. Indeed, because the non-self-executing character of the relevant treaties not only refutes the notion that the ratifying parties vested the President with the authority to unilaterally make treaty obligations binding on domestic courts, but also implicitly prohibits him from doing so, the President\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of authority is within Youngstown\xe2\x80\x99s third category, not the first or even the second.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States maintains that congressional acquiescence requires that the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum be given effect as domestic law. But such acquiescence is pertinent when the President\xe2\x80\x99s action falls within the second Youngstown category, not the third. In any event, congressional acquiescence does not exist here. Congress\xe2\x80\x99 failure to act following the President\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of prior ICJ controversies does not demonstrate acquiescence because in none of those prior controversies did the President assert the authority to transform an international obligation into domestic law and thereby displace state law. The United States\xe2\x80\x99 reliance on the President\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crelated\xe2\x80\x9d statutory responsibilities and on his \xe2\x80\x9cestablished role\xe2\x80\x9d in litigating foreign policy concerns is also misplaced. The President\xe2\x80\x99s statutory authorization to represent the United States before the U.\xc2\xa0N., the ICJ, and the U.\xc2\xa0N. Security Council speaks to his international responsibilities, not to any unilateral authority to create domestic law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The combination of a non-self-executing treaty and the lack of implementing legislation does not preclude the President from acting to comply with an international treaty obligation by other means, so long as those means are consistent with the Constitution. But the President may not rely upon a non-self-executing treaty to establish binding rules of decision that pre-empt contrary state law. Pp.\xc2\xa030\xe2\x80\x9335.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(ii)\xc2\xa0The United States also claims that\xe2\x80\x94independent of the United States\xe2\x80\x99 treaty obligations\xe2\x80\x94the Memorandum is a valid exercise of the President\xe2\x80\x99s foreign affairs authority to resolve claims disputes. See, e.g., American Ins. Assn. v. Garamendi, 539 U. S. 396, 415. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s claims-settlement cases involve a narrow set of circumstances: the making of executive agreements to settle civil claims between American citizens and foreign governments or foreign nationals. They are based on the view that \xe2\x80\x9ca systematic, unbroken, executive practice, long pursued to the knowledge of the Congress and never before questioned,\xe2\x80\x9d can \xe2\x80\x9craise a presumption that the [action] had been [taken] in pursuance of its consent.\xe2\x80\x9d Dames & Moore v. Regan, 453 U. S. 654, 668. But \xe2\x80\x9c[p]ast practice does not, by itself, create power.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum\xe2\x80\x94a directive issued to state courts that would compel those courts to reopen final criminal judgments and set aside neutrally applicable state laws\xe2\x80\x94is not supported by a \xe2\x80\x9cparticularly longstanding practice.\xe2\x80\x9d The Executive\xe2\x80\x99s limited authority to settle international claims disputes pursuant to an executive agreement cannot stretch so far. Pp.\xc2\xa035\xe2\x80\x9337.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(iii)\xc2\xa0Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum is a valid exercise of his power to \xe2\x80\x9cTake Care\xe2\x80\x9d that the laws be faithfully executed, U. S. Const., Art. II, \xc2\xa73, fails because the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Avena is not domestic law. P.\xc2\xa037.

JOSE ERNESTO MEDELLIN, PETITIONER v. TEXAS

\n

on writ of certiorari to the court of criminal appeals of texas

\n

[March 25, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The International Court of Justice (ICJ), located in the Hague, is a tribunal established pursuant to the United Nations Charter to adjudicate disputes between member states. In the Case Concerning Avena and Other Mexican Nationals (Mex. v. U. S.), 2004 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0J. 12 (Judgment of Mar. 31) (Avena), that tribunal considered a claim brought by Mexico against the United States. The ICJ held that, based on violations of the Vienna Convention, 51 named Mexican nationals were entitled to review and reconsideration of their state-court convictions and sentences in the United States. This was so regardless of any forfeiture of the right to raise Vienna Convention claims because of a failure to comply with generally applicable state rules governing challenges to criminal convictions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Sanchez-Llamas v. Oregon, 548 U. S. 331 (2006)\xe2\x80\x94issued after Avena but involving individuals who were not named in the Avena judgment\xe2\x80\x94we held that, contrary to the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s determination, the Vienna Convention did not preclude the application of state default rules. After the Avena decision, President George W. Bush determined, through a Memorandum to the Attorney General (Feb. 28, 2005), App. to Pet. for Cert. 187a (Memorandum or President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum), that the United States would \xe2\x80\x9cdischarge its international obligations\xe2\x80\x9d under Avena \xe2\x80\x9cby having State courts give effect to the decision.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Jos\xc3\xa9 Ernesto Medell\xc3\xadn, who had been convicted and sentenced in Texas state court for murder, is one of the 51 Mexican nationals named in the Avena decision. Relying on the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s decision and the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum, Medell\xc3\xadn filed an application for a writ of habeas corpus in state court. The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals dismissed Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s application as an abuse of the writ under state law, given Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s failure to raise his Vienna Convention claim in a timely manner under state law. We granted certiorari to decide two questions. First, is the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in Avena directly enforceable as domestic law in a state court in the United States? Second, does the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum independently require the States to provide review and reconsideration of the claims of the 51 Mexican nationals named in Avena without regard to state procedural default rules? We conclude that neither Avena nor the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum constitutes directly enforceable federal law that pre-empts state limitations on the filing of successive habeas petitions. We therefore affirm the decision below.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1969, the United States, upon the advice and consent of the Senate, ratified the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations (Vienna Convention or Convention), Apr. 24, 1963, [1970] 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0T. 77, T.\xc2\xa0I.\xc2\xa0A.\xc2\xa0S. No. 6820, and the Optional Protocol Concerning the Compulsory Settlement of Disputes to the Vienna Convention (Optional Protocol or Protocol), Apr. 24, 1963, [1970] 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0T. 325, T.\xc2\xa0I.\xc2\xa0A.\xc2\xa0S. No. 6820. The preamble to the Convention provides that its purpose is to \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute to the development of friendly relations among nations.\xe2\x80\x9d 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0T., at 79; Sanchez-Llamas, supra, at 337. Toward that end, Article 36 of the Convention was drafted to \xe2\x80\x9cfacilitat[e] the exercise of consular functions.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. 36(1), 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0T., at 100. It provides that if a person detained by a foreign country \xe2\x80\x9cso requests, the competent authorities of the receiving State shall, without delay, inform the consular post of the sending State\xe2\x80\x9d of such detention, and \xe2\x80\x9cinform the [detainee] of his righ[t]\xe2\x80\x9d to request assistance from the consul of his own state. Art. 36(1)(b), id., at 101.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Optional Protocol provides a venue for the resolution of disputes arising out of the interpretation or application of the Vienna Convention. Art.\xc2\xa0I, 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0T., at 326. Under the Protocol, such disputes \xe2\x80\x9cshall lie within the compulsory jurisdiction of the International Court of Justice\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmay accordingly be brought before the [ICJ] \xe2\x80\xa6 by any party to the dispute being a Party to the present Protocol.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The ICJ is \xe2\x80\x9cthe principal judicial organ of the United Nations.\xe2\x80\x9d United Nations Charter, Art. 92, 59 Stat. 1051, T.\xc2\xa0S. No. 993 (1945). It was established in 1945 pursuant to the United Nations Charter. The ICJ Statute\xe2\x80\x94annexed to the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter\xe2\x80\x94provides the organizational framework and governing procedures for cases brought before the ICJ. Statute of the International Court of Justice (ICJ Statute), 59 Stat. 1055, T.\xc2\xa0S. No. 993 (1945).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under Article 94(1) of the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter, \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach Member of the United Nations undertakes to comply with the decision of the [ICJ] in any case to which it is a party.\xe2\x80\x9d 59 Stat. 1051. The ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction in any particular case, however, is dependent upon the consent of the parties. See Art. 36, 59 Stat. 1060. The ICJ Statute delineates two ways in which a nation may consent to ICJ jurisdiction: It may consent generally to jurisdiction on any question arising under a treaty or general international law, Art. 36(2), ibid., or it may consent specifically to jurisdiction over a particular category of cases or disputes pursuant to a separate treaty, Art. 36(1), ibid. The United States originally consented to the general jurisdiction of the ICJ when it filed a declaration recognizing compulsory jurisdiction under Art. 36(2) in 1946. The United States withdrew from general ICJ jurisdiction in 1985. See U. S. Dept. of State Letter and Statement Concerning Termination of Acceptance of ICJ Compulsory Jurisdiction (Oct. 7, 1985), reprinted in 24 I.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0M. 1742 (1985). By ratifying the Optional Protocol to the Vienna Convention, the United States consented to the specific jurisdiction of the ICJ with respect to claims arising out of the Vienna Convention. On March 7, 2005, subsequent to the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in Avena, the United States gave notice of withdrawal from the Optional Protocol to the Vienna Convention. Letter from Condoleezza Rice, Secretary of State, to Kofi A. Annan, Secretary-General of the United Nations.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Jos\xc3\xa9 Ernesto Medell\xc3\xadn, a Mexican national, has lived in the United States since preschool. A member of the \xe2\x80\x9cBlack and Whites\xe2\x80\x9d gang, Medell\xc3\xadn was convicted of capital murder and sentenced to death in Texas for the gang rape and brutal murders of two Houston teenagers.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On June 24, 1993, 14-year-old Jennifer Ertman and 16-year-old Elizabeth Pena were walking home when they encountered Medell\xc3\xadn and several fellow gang members. Medell\xc3\xadn attempted to engage Elizabeth in conversation. When she tried to run, petitioner threw her to the ground. Jennifer was grabbed by other gang members when she, in response to her friend\xe2\x80\x99s cries, ran back to help. The gang members raped both girls for over an hour. Then, to prevent their victims from identifying them, Medell\xc3\xadn and his fellow gang members murdered the girls and discarded their bodies in a wooded area. Medell\xc3\xadn was personally responsible for strangling at least one of the girls with her own shoelace.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Medell\xc3\xadn was arrested at approximately 4 a.m. on June 29, 1993. A few hours later, between 5:54 and 7:23 a.m., Medell\xc3\xadn was given Miranda warnings; he then signed a written waiver and gave a detailed written confession. App. to Brief for Respondent 32\xe2\x80\x9336. Local law enforcement officers did not, however, inform Medell\xc3\xadn of his Vienna Convention right to notify the Mexican consulate of his detention. Brief for Petitioner 6\xe2\x80\x937. Medell\xc3\xadn was convicted of capital murder and sentenced to death; his conviction and sentence were affirmed on appeal. Medell\xc3\xadn v. State, No. 71,997 (Tex. Crim. App., May 16, 1997), App. to Brief for Respondent 2\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Medell\xc3\xadn first raised his Vienna Convention claim in his first application for state postconviction relief. The state trial court held that the claim was procedurally defaulted because Medell\xc3\xadn had failed to raise it at trial or on direct review. The trial court also rejected the Vienna Convention claim on the merits, finding that Medell\xc3\xadn had \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ed] to show that any non-notification of the Mexican authorities impacted on the validity of his conviction or punishment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 62.[Footnote 1] The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals affirmed. Id., at 64\xe2\x80\x9365.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Medell\xc3\xadn then filed a habeas petition in Federal District Court. The District Court denied relief, holding that Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s Vienna Convention claim was procedurally defaulted and that Medell\xc3\xadn had failed to show prejudice arising from the Vienna Convention violation. See Medell\xc3\xadn v. Cockrell, Civ. Action No. H\xe2\x80\x9301\xe2\x80\x934078 (SD Tex., June 26, 2003), App. to Brief for Respondent 86\xe2\x80\x9392.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s application for a certificate of appealability was pending in the Fifth Circuit, the ICJ issued its decision in Avena. The ICJ held that the United States had violated Article 36(1)(b) of the Vienna Convention by failing to inform the 51 named Mexican nationals, including Medell\xc3\xadn, of their Vienna Convention rights. 2004 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0J., at 53\xe2\x80\x9355. In the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s determination, the United States was obligated \xe2\x80\x9cto provide, by means of its own choosing, review and reconsideration of the convictions and sentences of the [affected] Mexican nationals.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 72. The ICJ indicated that such review was required without regard to state procedural default rules. Id., at 56\xe2\x80\x9357.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Fifth Circuit denied a certificate of appealability. Medell\xc3\xadn v. Dretke, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d 270, 281 (2004). The court concluded that the Vienna Convention did not confer individually enforceable rights. Id., at 280. The court further ruled that it was in any event bound by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Breard v. Greene, 523 U. S. 371, 375 (1998) (per curiam), which held that Vienna Convention claims are subject to procedural default rules, rather than by the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s contrary decision in Avena. 371 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 280.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court granted certiorari. Medell\xc3\xadn v. Dretke, 544 U. S. 660, 661 (2005) (per curiam) (Medell\xc3\xadn I). Before we heard oral argument, however, President George W. Bush issued his Memorandum to the United States Attorney General, providing:

\n

I have determined, pursuant to the authority vested in me as President by the Constitution and the laws of the United States of America, that the United States will discharge its international obligations under the decision of the International Court of Justice in [Avena], by having State courts give effect to the decision in accordance with general principles of comity in cases filed by the 51 Mexican nationals addressed in that decision. App. to Pet. for Cert. 187a.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Medell\xc3\xadn, relying on the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum and the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Avena, filed a second application for habeas relief in state court. Ex parte Medell\xc3\xadn, 223 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 315, 322\xe2\x80\x93323 (Tex. Crim. App. 2006). Because the state-court proceedings might have provided Medell\xc3\xadn with the review and reconsideration he requested, and because his claim for federal relief might otherwise have been barred, we dismissed his petition for certiorari as improvidently granted. Medell\xc3\xadn I, supra, at 664.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Texas Court of Criminal Appeals subsequently dismissed Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s second state habeas application as an abuse of the writ. 223 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d, at 352. In the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, neither the Avena decision nor the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum was \xe2\x80\x9cbinding federal law\xe2\x80\x9d that could displace the State\xe2\x80\x99s limitations on the filing of successive habeas applications. Ibid. We again granted certiorari. 550 U. S. ___ (2007).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Medell\xc3\xadn first contends that the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in Avena constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9cbinding\xe2\x80\x9d obligation on the state and federal courts of the United States. He argues that \xe2\x80\x9cby virtue of the Supremacy Clause, the treaties requiring compliance with the Avena judgment are already the \xe2\x80\x98Law of the Land\xe2\x80\x99 by which all state and federal courts in this country are \xe2\x80\x98bound.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioner 1. Accordingly, Medell\xc3\xadn argues, Avena is a binding federal rule of decision that pre-empts contrary state limitations on successive habeas petitions.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No one disputes that the Avena decision\xe2\x80\x94a decision that flows from the treaties through which the United States submitted to ICJ jurisdiction with respect to Vienna Convention disputes\xe2\x80\x94constitutes an international law obligation on the part of the United States. But not all international law obligations automatically constitute binding federal law enforceable in United States courts. The question we confront here is whether the Avena judgment has automatic domestic legal effect such that the judgment of its own force applies in state and federal courts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has long recognized the distinction between treaties that automatically have effect as domestic law, and those that\xe2\x80\x94while they constitute international law commitments\xe2\x80\x94do not by themselves function as binding federal law. The distinction was well explained by Chief Justice Marshall\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in Foster v. Neilson, 2 Pet. 253, 315 (1829), overruled on other grounds, United States v. Percheman, 7 Pet. 51 (1833), which held that a treaty is \xe2\x80\x9cequivalent to an act of the legislature,\xe2\x80\x9d and hence self-executing, when it \xe2\x80\x9coperates of itself without the aid of any legislative provision.\xe2\x80\x9d Foster, supra, at 314. When, in contrast, \xe2\x80\x9c[treaty] stipulations are not self-executing they can only be enforced pursuant to legislation to carry them into effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Whitney v. Robertson, 124 U. S. 190, 194 (1888). In sum, while treaties \xe2\x80\x9cmay comprise international commitments . . . they are not domestic law unless Congress has either enacted implementing statutes or the treaty itself conveys an intention that it be \xe2\x80\x98self-executing\xe2\x80\x99 and is ratified on these terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Igart\xc3\xbaa-De La Rosa v. United States, 417 F.\xc2\xa03d 145, 150 (CA1 2005) (en banc) (Boudin, C.\xc2\xa0J.).[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A treaty is, of course, \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily a compact between independent nations.\xe2\x80\x9d Head Money Cases, 112 U. S. 580, 598 (1884). It ordinarily \xe2\x80\x9cdepends for the enforcement of its provisions on the interest and the honor of the governments which are parties to it.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also The Federalist No. 33, p. 207 (J. Cooke ed. 1961) (A. Hamilton) (comparing laws that individuals are \xe2\x80\x9cbound to observe\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe supreme law of the land\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9ca mere treaty, dependent on the good faith of the parties\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9cIf these [interests] fail, its infraction becomes the subject of international negotiations and reclamations \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. It is obvious that with all this the judicial courts have nothing to do and can give no redress.\xe2\x80\x9d Head Money Cases, supra, at 598. Only \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f the treaty contains stipulations which are self-executing, that is, require no legislation to make them operative, [will] they have the force and effect of a legislative enactment.\xe2\x80\x9d Whitney, supra, at 194.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Medell\xc3\xadn and his amici nonetheless contend that the Optional Protocol, United Nations Charter, and ICJ Statute supply the \xe2\x80\x9crelevant obligation\xe2\x80\x9d to give the Avena judgment binding effect in the domestic courts of the United States. Reply Brief for Petitioner 5\xe2\x80\x936.[Footnote 4] Because none of these treaty sources creates binding federal law in the absence of implementing legislation, and because it is uncontested that no such legislation exists, we conclude that the Avena judgment is not automatically binding domestic law.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The interpretation of a treaty, like the interpretation of a statute, begins with its text. Air France v. Saks, 470 U. S. 392, 396\xe2\x80\x93397 (1985). Because a treaty ratified by the United States is \xe2\x80\x9can agreement among sovereign powers,\xe2\x80\x9d we have also considered as \xe2\x80\x9caids to its interpretation\xe2\x80\x9d the negotiation and drafting history of the treaty as well as \xe2\x80\x9cthe postratification understanding\xe2\x80\x9d of signatory nations. Zicherman v. Korean Air Lines Co., 516 U. S. 217, 226 (1996); see also United States v. Stuart, 489 U. S. 353, 365\xe2\x80\x93366 (1989); Choctaw Nation v. United States, 318 U. S. 423, 431\xe2\x80\x93432 (1943).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a signatory to the Optional Protocol, the United States agreed to submit disputes arising out of the Vienna Convention to the ICJ. The Protocol provides: \xe2\x80\x9cDisputes arising out of the interpretation or application of the [Vienna] Convention shall lie within the compulsory jurisdiction of the International Court of Justice.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0T., at 326. Of course, submitting to jurisdiction and agreeing to be bound are two different things. A party could, for example, agree to compulsory nonbinding arbitration. Such an agreement would require the party to appear before the arbitral tribunal without obligating the party to treat the tribunal\xe2\x80\x99s decision as binding. See, e.g., North American Free Trade Agreement, U. S.-Can.-Mex., Art. 2018(1), Dec. 17, 1992, 32 I.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0M. 605, 697 (1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cOn receipt of the final report of [the arbitral panel requested by a Party to the agreement], the disputing Parties shall agree on the resolution of the dispute, which normally shall conform with the determinations and recommendations of the panel\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The most natural reading of the Optional Protocol is as a bare grant of jurisdiction. It provides only that \xe2\x80\x9c[d]isputes arising out of the interpretation or application of the [Vienna] Convention shall lie within the compulsory jurisdiction of the International Court of Justice\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmay accordingly be brought before the [ICJ] . . . by any party to the dispute being a Party to the present Protocol.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0T., at 326. The Protocol says nothing about the effect of an ICJ decision and does not itself commit signatories to comply with an ICJ judgment. The Protocol is similarly silent as to any enforcement mechanism.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The obligation on the part of signatory nations to comply with ICJ judgments derives not from the Optional Protocol, but rather from Article 94 of the United Nations Charter\xe2\x80\x94the provision that specifically addresses the effect of ICJ decisions. Article 94(1) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach Member of the United Nations undertakes to comply with the decision of the [ICJ] in any case to which it is a party.\xe2\x80\x9d 59 Stat. 1051 (emphasis added). The Executive Branch contends that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cundertakes to comply\xe2\x80\x9d is not \xe2\x80\x9can acknowledgement that an ICJ decision will have immediate legal effect in the courts of U.\xc2\xa0N. members,\xe2\x80\x9d but rather \xe2\x80\x9ca commitment on the part of U.\xc2\xa0N. Members to take future action through their political branches to comply with an ICJ decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae in Medell\xc3\xadn I, O.\xc2\xa0T. 2004, No. 04\xe2\x80\x935928, p.\xc2\xa034.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We agree with this construction of Article 94. The Article is not a directive to domestic courts. It does not provide that the United States \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmust\xe2\x80\x9d comply with an ICJ decision, nor indicate that the Senate that ratified the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter intended to vest ICJ decisions with immediate legal effect in domestic courts. Instead, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he words of Article 94 . . . call upon governments to take certain action.\xe2\x80\x9d Committee of United States Citizens Living in Nicaragua v. Reagan, 859 F.\xc2\xa02d 929, 938 (CADC 1988) (quoting Diggs v. Richardson, 555 F.\xc2\xa02d 848, 851 (CADC 1976); internal quotation marks omitted). See also Foster, 2 Pet., at 314, 315 (holding a treaty non-self-executing because its text\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98all . . . grants of land . . . shall be ratified and confirmed\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94did not \xe2\x80\x9cact directly on the grants\xe2\x80\x9d but rather \xe2\x80\x9cpledge[d] the faith of the United States to pass acts which shall ratify and confirm them\xe2\x80\x9d). In other words, the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter reads like \xe2\x80\x9ca compact between independent nations\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cdepends for the enforcement of its provisions on the interest and the honor of the governments which are parties to it.\xe2\x80\x9d Head Money Cases, 112 U. S., at 598.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The remainder of Article 94 confirms that the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter does not contemplate the automatic enforceability of ICJ decisions in domestic courts.[Footnote 6] Article 94(2)\xe2\x80\x94the enforcement provision\xe2\x80\x94provides the sole remedy for noncompliance: referral to the United Nations Security Council by an aggrieved state. 59 Stat. 1051.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter\xe2\x80\x99s provision of an express diplomatic\xe2\x80\x94that is, nonjudicial\xe2\x80\x94remedy is itself evidence that ICJ judgments were not meant to be enforceable in domestic courts. See Sanchez-Llamas, 548 U. S., at 347. And even this \xe2\x80\x9cquintessentially international remed[y],\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 355, is not absolute. First, the Security Council must \xe2\x80\x9cdee[m] necessary\xe2\x80\x9d the issuance of a recommendation or measure to effectuate the judgment. Art. 94(2), 59 Stat. 1051. Second, as the President and Senate were undoubtedly aware in subscribing to the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter and Optional Protocol, the United States retained the unqualified right to exercise its veto of any Security Council resolution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This was the understanding of the Executive Branch when the President agreed to the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter and the declaration accepting general compulsory ICJ jurisdiction. See, e.g., The Charter of the United Nations for the Maintenance of International Peace and Security: Hearings before the Senate Committee on Foreign Relations, 79th Cong., 1st Sess., 124\xe2\x80\x93125 (1945) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f a state fails to perform its obligations under a judgment of the [ICJ], the other party may have recourse to the Security Council\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 286 (statement of Leo Paslovsky, Special Assistant to the Secretary of State for International Organizations and Security Affairs) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen the Court has rendered a judgment and one of the parties refuses to accept it, then the dispute becomes political rather than legal. It is as a political dispute that the matter is referred to the Security Council\xe2\x80\x9d); A Resolution Proposing Acceptance of Compulsory Jurisdiction of International Court of Justice: Hearings on S. Res. 196 before the Subcommittee of the Senate Committee on Foreign Relations, 79th Cong., 2d Sess., 142 (1946) (statement of Charles Fahy, State Dept. Legal Adviser) (while parties that accept ICJ jurisdiction have \xe2\x80\x9ca moral obligation\xe2\x80\x9d to comply with ICJ decisions, Article 94(2) provides the exclusive means of enforcement).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If ICJ judgments were instead regarded as automatically enforceable domestic law, they would be immediately and directly binding on state and federal courts pursuant to the Supremacy Clause. Mexico or the ICJ would have no need to proceed to the Security Council to enforce the judgment in this case. Noncompliance with an ICJ judgment through exercise of the Security Council veto\xe2\x80\x94always regarded as an option by the Executive and ratifying Senate during and after consideration of the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter, Optional Protocol, and ICJ Statute\xe2\x80\x94would no longer be a viable alternative. There would be nothing to veto. In light of the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter\xe2\x80\x99s remedial scheme, there is no reason to believe that the President and Senate signed up for such a result.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s view that ICJ decisions are automatically enforceable as domestic law is fatally undermined by the enforcement structure established by Article 94. His construction would eliminate the option of noncompliance contemplated by Article 94(2), undermining the ability of the political branches to determine whether and how to comply with an ICJ judgment. Those sensitive foreign policy decisions would instead be transferred to state and federal courts charged with applying an ICJ judgment directly as domestic law. And those courts would not be empowered to decide whether to comply with the judgment\xe2\x80\x94again, always regarded as an option by the political branches\xe2\x80\x94any more than courts may consider whether to comply with any other species of domestic law. This result would be particularly anomalous in light of the principle that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he conduct of the foreign relations of our Government is committed by the Constitution to the Executive and Legislative\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x98the political\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94Departments.\xe2\x80\x9d Oetjen v. Central Leather Co., 246 U. S. 297, 302 (1918).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The ICJ Statute, incorporated into the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter, provides further evidence that the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in Avena does not automatically constitute federal law judicially enforceable in United States courts. Art. 59, 59 Stat. 1062. To begin with, the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal purpose\xe2\x80\x9d is said to be to \xe2\x80\x9carbitrate particular disputes between national governments.\xe2\x80\x9d Sanchez-Llamas, supra, at 355 (citing 59 Stat. 1055). Accordingly, the ICJ can hear disputes only between nations, not individuals. Art. 34(1), 59 Stat. 1059 (\xe2\x80\x9cOnly states [i.e., countries] may be parties in cases before the [ICJ]\xe2\x80\x9d). More important, Article 59 of the statute provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he decision of the [ICJ] has no binding force except between the parties and in respect of that particular case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1062 (emphasis added).[Footnote 7] The dissent does not explain how Medell\xc3\xadn, an individual, can be a party to the ICJ proceeding.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Medell\xc3\xadn argues that because the Avena case involves him, it is clear that he\xe2\x80\x94and the 50 other Mexican nationals named in the Avena decision\xe2\x80\x94should be regarded as parties to the Avena judgment. Brief for Petitioner 21\xe2\x80\x9322. But cases before the ICJ are often precipitated by disputes involving particular persons or entities, disputes that a nation elects to take up as its own. See, e.g., Case Concerning the Barcelona Traction, Light & Power Co. (Belg. v. Spain), 1970 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0J. 3 (Judgment of Feb. 5) (claim brought by Belgium on behalf of Belgian nationals and shareholders); Case Concerning the Protection of French Nationals and Protected Persons in Egypt (Fr. v. Egypt), 1950 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0J. 59 (Order of Mar. 29) (claim brought by France on behalf of French nationals and protected persons in Egypt); Anglo-Iranian Oil Co. Case (U.\xc2\xa0K. v. Iran), 1952 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0J. 93, 112 (Judgment of July 22) (claim brought by the United Kingdom on behalf of the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company). That has never been understood to alter the express and established rules that only nation-states may be parties before the ICJ, Art. 34, 59 Stat. 1059, and\xe2\x80\x94contrary to the position of the dissent, post, at 23\xe2\x80\x94that ICJ judgments are binding only between those parties, Art. 59, id., at 1062.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is, moreover, well settled that the United States\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of a treaty \xe2\x80\x9cis entitled to great weight.\xe2\x80\x9d Sumitomo Shoji America, Inc. v. Avagliano, 457 U. S. 176, 184\xe2\x80\x93185 (1982); see also El Al Israel Airlines, Ltd. v. Tsui Yuan Tseng, 525 U. S. 155, 168 (1999). The Executive Branch has unfailingly adhered to its view that the relevant treaties do not create domestically enforceable federal law. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 4, 27\xe2\x80\x9329.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The pertinent international agreements, therefore, do not provide for implementation of ICJ judgments through direct enforcement in domestic courts, and \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a treaty does not provide a particular remedy, either expressly or implicitly, it is not for the federal courts to impose one on the States through lawmaking of their own.\xe2\x80\x9d Sanchez-Llamas, 548 U. S., at 347.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent faults our analysis because it \xe2\x80\x9clooks for the wrong thing (explicit textual expression about self-execution) using the wrong standard (clarity) in the wrong place (the treaty language).\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 26. Given our obligation to interpret treaty provisions to determine whether they are self-executing, we have to confess that we do think it rather important to look to the treaty language to see what it has to say about the issue. That is after all what the Senate looks to in deciding whether to approve the treaty.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The interpretive approach employed by the Court today\xe2\x80\x94resorting to the text\xe2\x80\x94is hardly novel. In two early cases involving an 1819 land-grant treaty between Spain and the United States, Chief Justice Marshall found the language of the treaty dispositive. In Foster, after distinguishing between self-executing treaties (those \xe2\x80\x9cequivalent to an act of the legislature\xe2\x80\x9d) and non-self-executing treaties (those \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislature must execute\xe2\x80\x9d), Chief Justice Marshall held that the 1819 treaty was non-self-executing. 2 Pet., at 314. Four years later, the Supreme Court considered another claim under the same treaty, but concluded that the treaty was self-executing. See Percheman, 7 Pet., at 87. The reason was not because the treaty was sometimes self-executing and sometimes not, but because \xe2\x80\x9cthe language of\xe2\x80\x9d the Spanish translation (brought to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s attention for the first time) indicated the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent to ratify and confirm the land-grant \xe2\x80\x9cby force of the instrument itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 89.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As against this time-honored textual approach, the dissent proposes a multifactor, judgment-by-judgment analysis that would \xe2\x80\x9cjettiso[n] relative predictability for the open-ended rough-and-tumble of factors.\xe2\x80\x9d Jerome B. Grubart, Inc. v. Great Lakes Dredge & Dock Co., 513 U. S. 527, 547 (1995). The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s novel approach to deciding which (or, more accurately, when) treaties give rise to directly enforceable federal law is arrestingly indeterminate. Treaty language is barely probative. Post, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he absence or presence of language in a treaty about a provision\xe2\x80\x99s self-execution proves nothing at all\xe2\x80\x9d). Determining whether treaties themselves create federal law is sometimes committed to the political branches and sometimes to the judiciary. Post, at 13. Of those committed to the judiciary, the courts pick and choose which shall be binding United States law\xe2\x80\x94trumping not only state but other federal law as well\xe2\x80\x94and which shall not. Post, at 13\xe2\x80\x9327. They do this on the basis of a multifactor, \xe2\x80\x9ccontext-specific\xe2\x80\x9d inquiry. Post, at 13. Even then, the same treaty sometimes gives rise to United States law and sometimes does not, again depending on an ad hoc judicial assessment. Post, at 13\xe2\x80\x9327.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our Framers established a careful set of procedures that must be followed before federal law can be created under the Constitution\xe2\x80\x94vesting that decision in the political branches, subject to checks and balances. U. S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa77. They also recognized that treaties could create federal law, but again through the political branches, with the President making the treaty and the Senate approving it. Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72. The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the treaty route, depending on an ad hoc judgment of the judiciary without looking to the treaty language\xe2\x80\x94the very language negotiated by the President and approved by the Senate\xe2\x80\x94cannot readily be ascribed to those same Framers.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approach risks the United States\xe2\x80\x99 involvement in international agreements. It is hard to believe that the United States would enter into treaties that are sometimes enforceable and sometimes not. Such a treaty would be the equivalent of writing a blank check to the judiciary. Senators could never be quite sure what the treaties on which they were voting meant. Only a judge could say for sure and only at some future date. This uncertainty could hobble the United States\xe2\x80\x99 efforts to negotiate and sign international agreements.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the dissent\xe2\x80\x94for a grab bag of no less than seven reasons\xe2\x80\x94would tell us that this particular ICJ judgment is federal law. Post, at 13\xe2\x80\x9327. That is no sort of guidance. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor is it any answer to say that the federal courts will diligently police international agreements and enforce the decisions of international tribunals only when they should be enforced. Ibid. The point of a non-self-executing treaty is that it \xe2\x80\x9caddresses itself to the political, not the judicial department; and the legislature must execute the contract before it can become a rule for the Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Foster, supra, at 314 (emphasis added); Whitney, 124 U. S., at 195. See also Foster, supra, at 307 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe judiciary is not that department of the government, to which the assertion of its interests against foreign powers is confided\xe2\x80\x9d). The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s contrary approach would assign to the courts\xe2\x80\x94not the political branches\xe2\x80\x94the primary role in deciding when and how international agreements will be enforced. To read a treaty so that it sometimes has the effect of domestic law and sometimes does not is tantamount to vesting with the judiciary the power not only to interpret but also to create the law.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our conclusion that Avena does not by itself constitute binding federal law is confirmed by the \xe2\x80\x9cpostratification understanding\xe2\x80\x9d of signatory nations. See Zicherman, 516 U. S., at 226. There are currently 47 nations that are parties to the Optional Protocol and 171 nations that are parties to the Vienna Convention. Yet neither Medell\xc3\xadn nor his amici have identified a single nation that treats ICJ judgments as binding in domestic courts.[Footnote 10] In determining that the Vienna Convention did not require certain relief in United States courts in Sanchez-Llamas, we found it pertinent that the requested relief would not be available under the treaty in any other signatory country. See 548 U. S., at 343\xe2\x80\x93344, and n.\xc2\xa03. So too here the lack of any basis for supposing that any other country would treat ICJ judgments as directly enforceable as a matter of their domestic law strongly suggests that the treaty should not be so viewed in our courts.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our conclusion is further supported by general principles of interpretation. To begin with, we reiterated in Sanchez-Llamas what we held in Breard, that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98absent a clear and express statement to the contrary, the procedural rules of the forum State govern the implementation of the treaty in that State.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 548 U. S., at 351 (quoting Breard, 523 U. S., at 375). Given that ICJ judgments may interfere with state procedural rules, one would expect the ratifying parties to the relevant treaties to have clearly stated their intent to give those judgments domestic effect, if they had so intended. Here there is no statement in the Optional Protocol, the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter, or the ICJ Statute that supports the notion that ICJ judgments displace state procedural rules.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, the consequences of Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s argument give pause. An ICJ judgment, the argument goes, is not only binding domestic law but is also unassailable. As a result, neither Texas nor this Court may look behind a judgment and quarrel with its reasoning or result. (We already know, from Sanchez-Llamas, that this Court disagrees with both the reasoning and result in Avena.) Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation would allow ICJ judgments to override otherwise binding state law; there is nothing in his logic that would exempt contrary federal law from the same fate. See, e.g., Cook v. United States, 288 U. S. 102, 119 (1933) (later-in-time self-executing treaty supersedes a federal statue if there is a conflict). And there is nothing to prevent the ICJ from ordering state courts to annul criminal convictions and sentences, for any reason deemed sufficient by the ICJ. Indeed, that is precisely the relief Mexico requested. Avena, 2004 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0J., at 58\xe2\x80\x9359.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even the dissent flinches at reading the relevant treaties to give rise to self-executing ICJ judgments in all cases. It admits that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress is unlikely to authorize automatic judicial enforceability of all ICJ judgments, for that could include some politically sensitive judgments and others better suited for enforcement by other branches.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 24. Our point precisely. But the lesson to draw from that insight is hardly that the judiciary should decide which judgments are politically sensitive and which are not.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, and as we observed in Sanchez-Llamas, \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in the structure or purpose of the ICJ suggests that its interpretations were intended to be conclusive on our courts.\xe2\x80\x9d 548 U. S., at 354. Given that holding, it is difficult to see how that same structure and purpose can establish, as Medell\xc3\xadn argues, that judgments of the ICJ nonetheless were intended to be conclusive on our courts. A judgment is binding only if there is a rule of law that makes it so. And the question whether ICJ judgments can bind domestic courts depends upon the same analysis undertaken in Sanchez-Llamas and set forth above.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our prior decisions identified by the dissent as holding a number of treaties to be self-executing, see post, at 8\xe2\x80\x939, Appendix A, stand only for the unremarkable proposition that some international agreements are self-executing and others are not.\xc2\xa0 It is well settled that the \xe2\x80\x9c[i]nterpretation of [a treaty] . . . must, of course, begin with the language of the Treaty itself.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0 Sumitomo Shoji America, Inc., 457 U. S., at 180. As a result, we have held treaties to be self-executing when the textual provisions indicate that the President and Senate intended for the agreement to have domestic effect.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Medell\xc3\xadn and the dissent cite Comegys v. Vasse, 1 Pet. 193 (1828), for the proposition that the judgments of international tribunals are automatically binding on domestic courts. See post, at 9; Reply Brief for Petitioner 2; Brief for Petitioner 19\xe2\x80\x9320. That case, of course, involved a different treaty than the ones at issue here; it stands only for the modest principle that the terms of a treaty control the outcome of a case.[Footnote 11] We do not suggest that treaties can never afford binding domestic effect to international tribunal judgments\xe2\x80\x94only that the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter, the Optional Protocol, and the ICJ Statute do not do so. And whether the treaties underlying a judgment are self-executing so that the judgment is directly enforceable as domestic law in our courts is, of course, a matter for this Court to decide. See Sanchez-Llamas, supra, at 353\xe2\x80\x93354.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our holding does not call into question the ordinary enforcement of foreign judgments or international arbitral agreements. Indeed, we agree with Medell\xc3\xadn that, as a general matter, \xe2\x80\x9can agreement to abide by the result\xe2\x80\x9d of an international adjudication\xe2\x80\x94or what he really means, an agreement to give the result of such adjudication domestic legal effect\xe2\x80\x94can be a treaty obligation like any other, so long as the agreement is consistent with the Constitution. See Brief for Petitioner 20. The point is that the particular treaty obligations on which Medell\xc3\xadn relies do not of their own force create domestic law.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent worries that our decision casts doubt on some 70-odd treaties under which the United States has agreed to submit disputes to the ICJ according to \xe2\x80\x9croughly similar\xe2\x80\x9d provisions. See post, at 4, 16\xe2\x80\x9317. Again, under our established precedent, some treaties are self-executing and some are not, depending on the treaty. That the judgment of an international tribunal might not automatically become domestic law hardly means the underlying treaty is \xe2\x80\x9cuseless.\xe2\x80\x9d See post, at 17; cf. post, at 11 (describing the British system in which treaties \xe2\x80\x9cvirtually always requir[e] parliamentary legislation\xe2\x80\x9d). Such judgments would still constitute international obligations, the proper subject of political and diplomatic negotiations. See Head Money Cases, 112 U. S., at 598. And Congress could elect to give them wholesale effect (rather than the judgment-by-judgment approach hypothesized by the dissent, post, at 24) through implementing legislation, as it regularly has. See, e.g., Foreign Affairs Reform and Restructuring Act of 1998, Pub. L. 105\xe2\x80\x93277, div. G, \xc2\xa72242, 112 Stat. 2681\xe2\x80\x93822, note following 8 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71231 (directing the \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate agencies\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cprescribe regulations to implement the obligations of the United States under Article 3\xe2\x80\x9d of the Convention Against Torture and Other Forms of Cruel, Inhuman or Degrading Treatment or Punishment); see also infra, at 25\xe2\x80\x9326 (listing examples of legislation implementing international obligations).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Further, that an ICJ judgment may not be automatically enforceable in domestic courts does not mean the particular underlying treaty is not. Indeed, we have held that a number of the \xe2\x80\x9cFriendship, Commerce, and Navigation\xe2\x80\x9d Treaties cited by the dissent, see post, Appendix B, are self-executing\xe2\x80\x94based on \xe2\x80\x9cthe language of the[se] Treat[ies].\xe2\x80\x9d See Sumitomo Shoji America, Inc., supra, at 180, 189\xe2\x80\x93190. In Kolovrat v. Oregon, 366 U. S. 187, 191, 196 (1961), for example, the Court found that Yugoslavian claimants denied inheritance under Oregon law were entitled to inherit personal property pursuant to an 1881 Treaty of Friendship, Navigation, and Commerce between the United States and Serbia. See also Clark v. Allen, 331 U. S. 503, 507\xe2\x80\x93511, 517\xe2\x80\x93518 (1947) (finding that the right to inherit real property granted German aliens under the Treaty of Friendship, Commerce, and Consular Rights with Germany prevailed over California law). Contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, see post, at 11, neither our approach nor our cases require that a treaty provide for self-execution in so many talismanic words; that is a caricature of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. Our cases simply require courts to decide whether a treaty\xe2\x80\x99s terms reflect a determination by the President who negotiated it and the Senate that confirmed it that the treaty has domestic effect.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, Congress is up to the task of implementing non-self-executing treaties, even those involving complex commercial disputes. Cf. post, at 24 (Breyer,\xc2\xa0J., dissenting). The judgments of a number of international tribunals enjoy a different status because of implementing legislation enacted by Congress. See, e.g., 22 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71650a(a) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn award of an arbitral tribunal rendered pursuant to chapter IV of the [Convention on the Settlement of Investment Disputes] shall create a right arising under a treaty of the United States. The pecuniary obligations imposed by such an award shall be enforced and shall be given the same full faith and credit as if the award were a final judgment of a court of general jurisdiction of one of the several States\xe2\x80\x9d); 9 U. S.C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7201\xe2\x80\x93208 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe [U.\xc2\xa0N.] Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards of June 10, 1958, shall be enforced in United States courts in accordance with this chapter,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7201). Such language demonstrates that Congress knows how to accord domestic effect to international obligations when it desires such a result.[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Further, Medell\xc3\xadn frames his argument as though giving the Avena judgment binding effect in domestic courts simply conforms to the proposition that domestic courts generally give effect to foreign judgments. But Medell\xc3\xadn does not ask us to enforce a foreign-court judgment settling a typical commercial or property dispute. See, e.g., Hilton v. Guyot, 159 U. S. 113 (1895); United States v. Arredondo, 6 Pet. 691 (1832); see also Uniform Foreign Money-Judgments Recognition Act \xc2\xa71(2), 13 U. L. A., pt. 2, p. 44 (2002) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[F]oreign judgment\xe2\x80\x99 means any judgment of a foreign state granting or denying recovery of a sum of money\xe2\x80\x9d). Rather, Medell\xc3\xadn argues that the Avena judgment has the effect of enjoining the operation of state law. What is more, on Medell\xc3\xadn\xe2\x80\x99s view, the judgment would force the State to take action to \xe2\x80\x9creview and reconside[r]\xe2\x80\x9d his case. The general rule, however, is that judgments of foreign courts awarding injunctive relief, even as to private parties, let alone sovereign States, \xe2\x80\x9care not generally entitled to enforcement.\xe2\x80\x9d See 2 Restatement \xc2\xa7481, Comment b, at 595.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, while the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in Avena creates an international law obligation on the part of the United States, it does not of its own force constitute binding federal law that pre-empts state restrictions on the filing of successive habeas petitions. As we noted in Sanchez-Llamas, a contrary conclusion would be extraordinary, given that basic rights guaranteed by our own Constitution do not have the effect of displacing state procedural rules. See 548 U. S., at 360. Nothing in the text, background, negotiating and drafting history, or practice among signatory nations suggests that the President or Senate intended the improbable result of giving the judgments of an international tribunal a higher status than that enjoyed by \xe2\x80\x9cmany of our most fundamental constitutional protections.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Medell\xc3\xadn next argues that the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in Avena is binding on state courts by virtue of the President\xe2\x80\x99s February 28, 2005 Memorandum. The United States contends that while the Avena judgment does not of its own force require domestic courts to set aside ordinary rules of procedural default, that judgment became the law of the land with precisely that effect pursuant to the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum and his power \xe2\x80\x9cto establish binding rules of decision that preempt contrary state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 5. Accordingly, we must decide whether the President\xe2\x80\x99s declaration alters our conclusion that the Avena judgment is not a rule of domestic law binding in state and federal courts.[Footnote 13]

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States maintains that the President\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional role \xe2\x80\x9cuniquely qualifies\xe2\x80\x9d him to resolve the sensitive foreign policy decisions that bear on compliance with an ICJ decision and \xe2\x80\x9cto do so expeditiously.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 11, 12. We do not question these propositions. See, e.g., First Nat. City Bank v. Banco Nacional de Cuba, 406 U. S. 759, 767 (1972) (plurality opinion) (The President has \xe2\x80\x9cthe lead role . . . in foreign policy\xe2\x80\x9d); American Ins. Assn. v. Garamendi, 539 U. S. 396, 414 (2003) (Article II of the Constitution places with the President the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98vast share of responsibility for the conduct of our foreign relations\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer, 343 U. S. 579, 610\xe2\x80\x93611 (1952) (Frankfurter, J., concurring)). In this case, the President seeks to vindicate United States interests in ensuring the reciprocal observance of the Vienna Convention, protecting relations with foreign governments, and demonstrating commitment to the role of international law. These interests are plainly compelling.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Such considerations, however, do not allow us to set aside first principles. The President\xe2\x80\x99s authority to act, as with the exercise of any governmental power, \xe2\x80\x9cmust stem either from an act of Congress or from the Constitution itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown, supra, at 585; Dames & Moore v. Regan, 453 U. S. 654, 668 (1981).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Jackson\xe2\x80\x99s familiar tripartite scheme provides the accepted framework for evaluating executive action in this area. First, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen the President acts pursuant to an express or implied authorization of Congress, his authority is at its maximum, for it includes all that he possesses in his own right plus all that Congress can delegate.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown, 343 U. S., at 635 (Jackson, J., concurring). Second, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen the President acts in absence of either a congressional grant or denial of authority, he can only rely upon his own independent powers, but there is a zone of twilight in which he and Congress may have concurrent authority, or in which its distribution is uncertain.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 637. In this circumstance, Presidential authority can derive support from \xe2\x80\x9ccongressional inertia, indifference or quiescence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Finally, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen the President takes measures incompatible with the expressed or implied will of Congress, his power is at its lowest ebb,\xe2\x80\x9d and the Court can sustain his actions \xe2\x80\x9conly by disabling the Congress from acting upon the subject.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 637\xe2\x80\x93638.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States marshals two principal arguments in favor of the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority \xe2\x80\x9cto establish binding rules of decision that preempt contrary state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 5. The Solicitor General first argues that the relevant treaties give the President the authority to implement the Avena judgment and that Congress has acquiesced in the exercise of such authority. The United States also relies upon an \xe2\x80\x9cindependent\xe2\x80\x9d international dispute-resolution power wholly apart from the asserted authority based on the pertinent treaties. Medell\xc3\xadn adds the additional argument that the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum is a valid exercise of his power to take care that the laws be faithfully executed.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States maintains that the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum is authorized by the Optional Protocol and the U.\xc2\xa0N. Charter. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 9. That is, because the relevant treaties \xe2\x80\x9ccreate an obligation to comply with Avena,\xe2\x80\x9d they \xe2\x80\x9cimplicitly give the President authority to implement that treaty-based obligation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 11 (emphasis added). As a result, the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum is well grounded in the first category of the Youngstown framework.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We disagree. The President has an array of political and diplomatic means available to enforce international obligations, but unilaterally converting a non-self-executing treaty into a self-executing one is not among them. The responsibility for transforming an international obligation arising from a non-self-executing treaty into domestic law falls to Congress. Foster, 2 Pet., at 315; Whitney, 124 U. S., at 194; Igart\xc3\xbaa-De La Rosa, 417 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 150. As this Court has explained, when treaty stipulations are \xe2\x80\x9cnot self-executing they can only be enforced pursuant to legislation to carry them into effect.\xe2\x80\x9d Whitney, supra, at 194. Moreover, \xe2\x80\x9c[u]ntil such act shall be passed, the Court is not at liberty to disregard the existing laws on the subject.\xe2\x80\x9d Foster, supra, at 315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The requirement that Congress, rather than the President, implement a non-self-executing treaty derives from the text of the Constitution, which divides the treaty-making power between the President and the Senate. The Constitution vests the President with the authority to \xe2\x80\x9cmake\xe2\x80\x9d a treaty. Art. II, \xc2\xa72. If the Executive determines that a treaty should have domestic effect of its own force, that determination may be implemented \xe2\x80\x9cin mak[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d the treaty, by ensuring that it contains language plainly providing for domestic enforceability. If the treaty is to be self-executing in this respect, the Senate must consent to the treaty by the requisite two-thirds vote, ibid., consistent with all other constitutional restraints.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once a treaty is ratified without provisions clearly according it domestic effect, however, whether the treaty will ever have such effect is governed by the fundamental constitutional principle that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]he power to make the necessary laws is in Congress; the power to execute in the President.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Hamdan v. Rumsfeld, 548 U. S. 557, 591 (2006) (quoting Ex parte Milligan, 4 Wall. 2, 139 (1866) (opinion of Chase, C.\xc2\xa0J.)); see U. S. Const., Art. I, \xc2\xa71 (\xe2\x80\x9cAll legislative Powers herein granted shall be vested in a Congress of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d). As already noted, the terms of a non-self-executing treaty can become domestic law only in the same way as any other law\xe2\x80\x94through passage of legislation by both Houses of Congress, combined with either the President\xe2\x80\x99s signature or a congressional override of a Presidential veto. See Art. I, \xc2\xa77. Indeed, \xe2\x80\x9cthe President\xe2\x80\x99s power to see that the laws are faithfully executed refutes the idea that he is to be a lawmaker.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown, 343 U. S., at 587.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A non-self-executing treaty, by definition, is one that was ratified with the understanding that it is not to have domestic effect of its own force. That understanding precludes the assertion that Congress has implicitly authorized the President\xe2\x80\x94acting on his own\xe2\x80\x94to achieve precisely the same result. We therefore conclude, given the absence of congressional legislation, that the non-self-executing treaties at issue here did not \xe2\x80\x9cexpress[ly] or implied[ly]\xe2\x80\x9d vest the President with the unilateral authority to make them self-executing. See id., at 635 (Jackson, J., concurring). Accordingly, the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum does not fall within the first category of the Youngstown framework.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, the preceding discussion should make clear that the non-self-executing character of the relevant treaties not only refutes the notion that the ratifying parties vested the President with the authority to unilaterally make treaty obligations binding on domestic courts, but also implicitly prohibits him from doing so. When the President asserts the power to \xe2\x80\x9cenforce\xe2\x80\x9d a non-self-executing treaty by unilaterally creating domestic law, he acts in conflict with the implicit understanding of the ratifying Senate. His assertion of authority, insofar as it is based on the pertinent non-self-executing treaties, is therefore within Justice Jackson\xe2\x80\x99s third category, not the first or even the second. See id., at 637\xe2\x80\x93638.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Each of the two means described above for giving domestic effect to an international treaty obligation under the Constitution\xe2\x80\x94for making law\xe2\x80\x94requires joint action by the Executive and Legislative Branches: The Senate can ratify a self-executing treaty \xe2\x80\x9cma[de]\xe2\x80\x9d by the Executive, or, if the ratified treaty is not self-executing, Congress can enact implementing legislation approved by the President. It should not be surprising that our Constitution does not contemplate vesting such power in the Executive alone. As Madison explained in The Federalist No. 47, under our constitutional system of checks and balances, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he magistrate in whom the whole executive power resides cannot of himself make a law.\xe2\x80\x9d J. Cooke ed., p. 326 (1961). That would, however, seem an apt description of the asserted executive authority unilaterally to give the effect of domestic law to obligations under a non-self-executing treaty.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States nonetheless maintains that the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum should be given effect as domestic law because \xe2\x80\x9cthis case involves a valid Presidential action in the context of Congressional \xe2\x80\x98acquiescence\xe2\x80\x99.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 11, n. 2. Under the Youngstown tripartite framework, congressional acquiescence is pertinent when the President\xe2\x80\x99s action falls within the second category\xe2\x80\x94that is, when he \xe2\x80\x9cacts in absence of either a congressional grant or denial of authority.\xe2\x80\x9d 343 U. S., at 637 (Jackson, J., concurring). Here, however, as we have explained, the President\xe2\x80\x99s effort to accord domestic effect to the Avena judgment does not meet that prerequisite.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, even if we were persuaded that congressional acquiescence could support the President\xe2\x80\x99s asserted authority to create domestic law pursuant to a non-self-executing treaty, such acquiescence does not exist here. The United States first locates congressional acquiescence in Congress\xe2\x80\x99s failure to act following the President\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of prior ICJ controversies. A review of the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s actions in those prior cases, however, cannot support the claim that Congress acquiesced in this particular exercise of Presidential authority, for none of them remotely involved transforming an international obligation into domestic law and thereby displacing state law.[Footnote 14]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States also directs us to the President\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crelated\xe2\x80\x9d statutory responsibilities and to his \xe2\x80\x9cestablished role\xe2\x80\x9d in litigating foreign policy concerns as support for the President\xe2\x80\x99s asserted authority to give the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Avena the force of domestic law. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 16\xe2\x80\x9319. Congress has indeed authorized the President to represent the United States before the United Nations, the ICJ, and the Security Council, 22 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7287, but the authority of the President to represent the United States before such bodies speaks to the President\xe2\x80\x99s international responsibilities, not any unilateral authority to create domestic law. The authority expressly conferred by Congress in the international realm cannot be said to \xe2\x80\x9cinvite\xe2\x80\x9d the Presidential action at issue here. See Youngstown, supra, at 637 (Jackson, J., concurring). At bottom, none of the sources of authority identified by the United States supports the President\xe2\x80\x99s claim that Congress has acquiesced in his asserted power to establish on his own federal law or to override state law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0None of this is to say, however, that the combination of a non-self-executing treaty and the lack of implementing legislation precludes the President from acting to comply with an international treaty obligation. It is only to say that the Executive cannot unilaterally execute a non-self-executing treaty by giving it domestic effect. That is, the non-self-executing character of a treaty constrains the President\xe2\x80\x99s ability to comply with treaty commitments by unilaterally making the treaty binding on domestic courts. The President may comply with the treaty\xe2\x80\x99s obligations by some other means, so long as they are consistent with the Constitution. But he may not rely upon a non-self-executing treaty to \xe2\x80\x9cestablish binding rules of decision that preempt contrary state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 5.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We thus turn to the United States\xe2\x80\x99 claim that\xe2\x80\x94independent of the United States\xe2\x80\x99 treaty obligations\xe2\x80\x94the Memorandum is a valid exercise of the President\xe2\x80\x99s foreign affairs authority to resolve claims disputes with foreign nations. Id., at 12\xe2\x80\x9316. The United States relies on a series of cases in which this Court has upheld the authority of the President to settle foreign claims pursuant to an executive agreement. See Garamendi, 539 U. S., at 415; Dames & Moore, 453 U. S., at 679\xe2\x80\x93680; United States v. Pink, 315 U. S. 203, 229 (1942); United States v. Belmont, 301 U. S. 324, 330 (1937). In these cases this Court has explained that, if pervasive enough, a history of congressional acquiescence can be treated as a \xe2\x80\x9cgloss on \xe2\x80\x98Executive Power\xe2\x80\x99 vested in the President by \xc2\xa71 of Art.\xc2\xa0II.\xe2\x80\x9d Dames & Moore, supra, at 686 (some internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This argument is of a different nature than the one rejected above. Rather than relying on the United States\xe2\x80\x99 treaty obligations, the President relies on an independent source of authority in ordering Texas to put aside its procedural bar to successive habeas petitions. Nevertheless, we find that our claims-settlement cases do not support the authority that the President asserts in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The claims-settlement cases involve a narrow set of circumstances: the making of executive agreements to settle civil claims between American citizens and foreign governments or foreign nationals. See, e.g., Belmont, supra, at 327. They are based on the view that \xe2\x80\x9ca systematic, unbroken, executive practice, long pursued to the knowledge of the Congress and never before questioned,\xe2\x80\x9d can \xe2\x80\x9craise a presumption that the [action] had been [taken] in pursuance of its consent.\xe2\x80\x9d Dames & Moore, supra, at 686 (some internal quotation marks omitted). As this Court explained in Garamendi,

\n

Making executive agreements to settle claims of American nationals against foreign governments is a particularly longstanding practice \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Given the fact that the practice goes back over 200 years, and has received congressional acquiescence throughout its history, the conclusion that the President\xe2\x80\x99s control of foreign relations includes the settlement of claims is indisputable. 539 U. S., at 415 (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted).

\n

\nEven still, the limitations on this source of executive power are clearly set forth and the Court has been careful to note that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]ast practice does not, by itself, create power.\xe2\x80\x9d Dames & Moore, supra, at 686.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum is not supported by a \xe2\x80\x9cparticularly longstanding practice\xe2\x80\x9d of congressional acquiescence, see Garamendi, supra, at 415, but rather is what the United States itself has described as \xe2\x80\x9cunprecedented action,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae in Sanchez-Llamas, O.\xc2\xa0T. 2005, Nos. 05\xe2\x80\x9351 and 04\xe2\x80\x9310566, pp.\xc2\xa029\xe2\x80\x9330. Indeed, the Government has not identified a single instance in which the President has attempted (or Congress has acquiesced in) a Presidential directive issued to state courts, much less one that reaches deep into the heart of the State\xe2\x80\x99s police powers and compels state courts to reopen final criminal judgments and set aside neutrally applicable state laws. Cf. Brecht v. Abrahamson, 507 U. S. 619, 635 (1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cStates possess primary authority for defining and enforcing the criminal law\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Engle v. Isaac, 456 U. S. 107, 128 (1982); internal quotation marks omitted). The Executive\xe2\x80\x99s narrow and strictly limited authority to settle international claims disputes pursuant to an executive agreement cannot stretch so far as to support the current Presidential Memorandum.

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Medell\xc3\xadn argues that the President\xe2\x80\x99s Memorandum is a valid exercise of his \xe2\x80\x9cTake Care\xe2\x80\x9d power. Brief for Petitioner 28. The United States, however, does not rely upon the President\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility to \xe2\x80\x9ctake Care that the Laws be faithfully executed.\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Art. II, \xc2\xa73. We think this a wise concession. This authority allows the President to execute the laws, not make them. For the reasons we have stated, the Avena judgment is not domestic law; accordingly, the President cannot rely on his Take Care powers here.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Texas Court of Criminal Appeals is affirmed.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is a great deal of wisdom in Justice Breyer\xe2\x80\x99s dissent. I agree that the text and history of the Supremacy Clause, as well as this Court\xe2\x80\x99s treaty-related cases, do not support a presumption against self-execution. See post, at 5\xe2\x80\x9310. I also endorse the proposition that the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations, Apr. 24, 1963, [1970] 21 U. S. T. 77, T.\xc2\xa0I.\xc2\xa0A.\xc2\xa0S. No. 6820, \xe2\x80\x9cis itself self-executing and judicially enforceable.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 19. Moreover, I think this case presents a closer question than the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion allows. In the end, however, I am persuaded that the relevant treaties do not authorize this Court to enforce the judgment of the International Court of Justice (ICJ) in Case Concerning Avena and Other Mexican Nationals (Mex. v. U. S.), 2004 I.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0J. 12 (Judgment of Mar. 31) (Avena).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The source of the United States\xe2\x80\x99 obligation to comply with judgments of the ICJ is found in Article 94(1) of the United Nations Charter, which was ratified in 1945. Article 94(1) provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ach Member of the United Nations undertakes to comply with the decision of the [ICJ] in any case to which it is a party.\xe2\x80\x9d 59 Stat. 1051, T.\xc2\xa0S. No. 993 (emphasis added). In my view, the words \xe2\x80\x9cundertakes to comply\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94while not the model of either a self-executing or a non-self-executing commitment\xe2\x80\x94are most naturally read as a promise to take additional steps to enforce ICJ judgments.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike the text of some other treaties, the terms of the United Nations Charter do not necessarily incorporate international judgments into domestic law. Cf., e.g., United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, Annex VI, Art. 39, Dec. 10, 1982, S. Treaty Doc. No. 103\xe2\x80\x9339, 1833 U.\xc2\xa0N.\xc2\xa0T.\xc2\xa0S. 570 (\xe2\x80\x9c[D]ecisions of the [Seabed Disputes] Chamber shall be enforceable in the territories of the States Parties in the same manner as judgments or orders of the highest court of the State Party in whose territory the enforcement is sought\xe2\x80\x9d). Moreover, Congress has passed implementing legislation to ensure the enforcement of other international judgments, even when the operative treaty provisions use far more mandatory language than \xe2\x80\x9cundertakes to comply.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the other hand Article 94(1) does not contain the kind of unambiguous language foreclosing self-execution that is found in other treaties. The obligation to undertake to comply with ICJ decisions is more consistent with self-execution than, for example, an obligation to enact legislation. Cf., e.g., International Plant Protection Convention, Art. I, Dec. 6, 1951, [1972] 23 U. S.\xc2\xa0T. 2770, T.\xc2\xa0I.\xc2\xa0A.\xc2\xa0S. No. 7465 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he contracting Governments undertake to adopt the legislative, technical and administrative measures specified in this Convention\xe2\x80\x9d). Furthermore, whereas the Senate has issued declarations of non-self-execution when ratifying some treaties, it did not do so with respect to the United Nations Charter.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Absent a presumption one way or the other, the best reading of the words \xe2\x80\x9cundertakes to comply\xe2\x80\x9d is, in my judgment, one that contemplates future action by the political branches. I agree with the dissenters that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress is unlikely to authorize automatic judicial enforceability of all ICJ judgments, for that could include some politically sensitive judgments and others better suited for enforcement by other branches.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 24. But this concern counsels in favor of reading any ambiguity in Article 94(1) as leaving the choice of whether to comply with ICJ judgments, and in what manner, \xe2\x80\x9cto the political, not the judicial department.\xe2\x80\x9d Foster v. Neilson, 2 Pet. 253, 314 (1829).[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The additional treaty provisions cited by the dissent do not suggest otherwise. In an annex to the United Nations Charter, the Statute of the International Court of Justice (ICJ Statute) states that a decision of the ICJ \xe2\x80\x9chas no binding force except between the parties and in respect of that particular case.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. 59, 59 Stat. 1062. Because I read that provision as confining, not expanding, the effect of ICJ judgments, it does not make the undertaking to comply with such judgments any more enforceable than the terms of Article 94(1) itself. That the judgment is \xe2\x80\x9cbinding\xe2\x80\x9d as a matter of international law says nothing about its domestic legal effect. Nor in my opinion does the reference to \xe2\x80\x9ccompulsory jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d in the Optional Protocol Concerning the Compulsory Settlement of Disputes to the Vienna Convention, Art. I, Apr. 24, 1963, [1970] 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0T. 325, T.\xc2\xa0I.\xc2\xa0A.\xc2\xa0S. No. 6820, shed any light on the matter. This provision merely secures the consent of signatory nations to the specific jurisdiction of the ICJ with respect to claims arising out of the Vienna Convention. See ICJ Statute, Art. 36(1), 59 Stat. 1060 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe jurisdiction of the Court comprises . . . all matters specially provided for . . . in treaties and conventions in force\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even though the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in Avena is not \xe2\x80\x9cthe supreme Law of the Land,\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0VI, cl. 2, no one disputes that it constitutes an international law obligation on the part of the United States. Ante, at 8. By issuing a memorandum declaring that state courts should give effect to the judgment in Avena, the President made a commendable attempt to induce the States to discharge the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s obligation. I agree with the Texas judges and the majority of this Court that the President\xe2\x80\x99s memorandum is not binding law. Nonetheless, the fact that the President cannot legislate unilaterally does not absolve the United States from its promise to take action necessary to comply with the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under the express terms of the Supremacy Clause, the United States\xe2\x80\x99 obligation to \xe2\x80\x9cundertak[e] to comply\xe2\x80\x9d with the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s decision falls on each of the States as well as the Federal Government. One consequence of our form of government is that sometimes States must shoulder the primary responsibility for protecting the honor and integrity of the Nation. Texas\xe2\x80\x99 duty in this respect is all the greater since it was Texas that\xe2\x80\x94by failing to provide consular notice in accordance with the Vienna Convention\xe2\x80\x94ensnared the United States in the current controversy. Having already put the Nation in breach of one treaty, it is now up to Texas to prevent the breach of another.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The decision in Avena merely obligates the United States \xe2\x80\x9cto provide, by means of its own choosing, review and reconsideration of the convictions and sentences of the [affected] Mexican nationals,\xe2\x80\x9d 2004 I. C. J., at 72, \xc2\xb6153(9), \xe2\x80\x9cwith a view to ascertaining\xe2\x80\x9d whether the failure to provide proper notice to consular officials \xe2\x80\x9ccaused actual prejudice to the defendant in the process of administration of criminal justice,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 60, \xc2\xb6121. The cost to Texas of complying with Avena would be minimal, particularly given the remote likelihood that the violation of the Vienna Convention actually prejudiced Jos\xc3\xa9 Ernesto Medell\xc3\xadn. See ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x936, and n.\xc2\xa01. It is a cost that the State of Oklahoma unhesitatingly assumed.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the other hand, the costs of refusing to respect the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s judgment are significant. The entire Court and the President agree that breach will jeopardize the United States\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cplainly compelling\xe2\x80\x9d interests in \xe2\x80\x9censuring the reciprocal observance of the Vienna Convention, protecting relations with foreign governments, and demonstrating commitment to the role of international law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 28. When the honor of the Nation is balanced against the modest cost of compliance, Texas would do well to recognize that more is at stake than whether judgments of the ICJ, and the principled admonitions of the President of the United States, trump state procedural rules in the absence of implementing legislation.

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Medell\xc3\xadn v. Texas, 552 U. Id., at ___. In reaching this conclusion the majority, as well as the dissent, recognized that, without the further hearing that the ICJ found necessary, the execution would violate our international treaty commitments. See id., at ___.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner, who is scheduled to be executed this evening, now asks us to delay the execution in order to give Congress an opportunity to act to cure the legal defect that the Court found in Medell\xc3\xadn. In my view, several factors counsel in favor of delay. First, since this Court handed down Medell\xc3\xadn, Mexico has returned to the ICJ requesting this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s compliance with its international obligations; and the ICJ has asked that the United States \xe2\x80\x9ctake all measures necessary to ensure that [the Mexican nationals] are not executed\xe2\x80\x9d unless and until they \xe2\x80\x9creceive review and reconsideration consistent\xe2\x80\x9d with the ICJ\xe2\x80\x99s earlier Avena decision. \n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, legislation has been introduced in Congress seeking to provide the legislative approval necessary to transform our international legal obligations into binding domestic law. \n
