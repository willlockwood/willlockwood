SAMSUNG ELECTRONICS CO., LTD., et\xc2\xa0al. v.APPLE INC.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93777.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 11, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided December 6, 2016

\n

Section 289 of the Patent Act makes it unlawful to manufacture or sell an \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d to which a patented design or a colorable imitation thereof has been applied and makes an infringer liable to the patent holder \xe2\x80\x9cto the extent of his total profit.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7289. As relevant here, a jury found that various smartphones manufactured by petitioners (collectively, Samsung) infringed design patents owned by respondent Apple Inc. that covered a rectangular front face with rounded edges and a grid of colorful icons on a black screen. Apple was awarded $399 million in damages\xe2\x80\x94Samsung\xe2\x80\x99s entire profit from the sale of its infringing smartphones. The Federal Circuit affirmed the damages award, rejecting Samsung\xe2\x80\x99s argument that damages should be limited because the relevant articles of manufacture were the front face or screen rather than the entire smartphone. The court reasoned that such a limit was not required because the components of Samsung\xe2\x80\x99s smartphones were not sold separately to ordinary consumers and thus were not distinct articles of manufacture.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0In the case of a multicomponent product, the relevant \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d for arriving at a \xc2\xa7289 damages award need not be the end product sold to the consumer but may be only a component of that product. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The statutory text resolves the issue here. An \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture,\xe2\x80\x9d which is simply a thing made by hand or machine, encompasses both a product sold to a consumer and a component of that product. This reading is consistent with \xc2\xa7171(a) of the Patent Act, which makes certain \xe2\x80\x9cdesign[s] for an article of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d eligible for design patent protection, and which has been understood by the Patent Office and the courts to permit a design patent that extends to only a component of a multicomponent product, see, e.g., Ex parte Adams, 84 Off. Gaz. Pat. Office 311; Application of Zahn, 617 F.\xc2\xa02d 261, 268 (CCPA). This reading is also consistent with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the term \xe2\x80\x9cmanufacture\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7101, which makes \xe2\x80\x9cany new and useful .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d eligible for utility patent protection. See Diamond v. Chakrabarty,\n\n447 U.\xc2\xa0S. 303\n. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Because the term \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d is broad enough to embrace both a product sold to a consumer and a component of that product, whether sold separately or not, the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s narrower reading cannot be squared with \xc2\xa7289\xe2\x80\x99s text. Absent adequate briefing by the parties, this Court declines to resolve whether the relevant article of manufacture for each design patent at issue here is the smartphone or a particular smartphone component. Doing so is not necessary to resolve the question presented, and the Federal Circuit may address any remaining issues on remand. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

786 F.\xc2\xa03d 983, reversed and remanded.

\n

Sotomayor, J., delivered the opinion for a unanimous Court.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93777

\n

SAMSUNG ELECTRONICS CO., LTD., et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. APPLE INC.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

[December 6, 2016]

\n

Justice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Section 289 of the Patent Act provides a damages rem-edy specific to design patent infringement. A person who manufactures or sells \xe2\x80\x9cany article of manufacture to which [a patented] design or colorable imitation has been applied shall be liable to the owner to the extent of his totalprofit.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7289. In the case of a design for a single-component product, such as a dinner plate, the product is the \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d to which the design has been applied. In the case of a design for a multicomponent product, such as a kitchen oven, identifying the \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d to which the design has been applied is a more difficult task.

\n

This case involves the infringement of designs for smartphones. The United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit identified the entire smartphone as the only permissible \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d for the purpose of calculating \xc2\xa7289 damages because consumers could not separately purchase components of the smartphones. The question before us is whether that reading is consistent with \xc2\xa7289. We hold that it is not.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

The federal patent laws have long permitted those who invent designs for manufactured articles to patent their designs. See Patent Act of 1842, \xc2\xa73,\n\n5Stat.\n543\xe2\x80\x93544. Patent protection is available for a \xe2\x80\x9cnew, original and ornamental design for an article of manufacture.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7171(a). A patentable design \xe2\x80\x9cgives a peculiar or distinctive appearance to the manufacture, or article to which it may be applied, or to which it gives form.\xe2\x80\x9d Gorham Co. v. White, 14 Wall. 511, 525 (1872). This Court has explained that a design patent is infringed \xe2\x80\x9cif, in the eye of an ordinary observer, giving such attention as a purchaser usually gives, two designs are substantially the same.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 528.

\n

In 1885, this Court limited the damages available for design patent infringement. The statute in effect at the time allowed a holder of a design patent to recover \xe2\x80\x9cthe actual damages sustained\xe2\x80\x9d from infringement. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa74919. In Dobson v. Hartford Carpet Co.,\n\n114 U.\xc2\xa0S. 439 (1885)\n, the lower courts had awarded the holders of design patents on carpets damages in the amount of \xe2\x80\x9cthe entire profit to the [patent holders], per yard, in the manufacture and sale of carpets of the patented designs, and not merely the value which the designs contributed to the carpets.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 443. This Court reversed the damages award and construed the statute to require proof that the profits were \xe2\x80\x9cdue to\xe2\x80\x9d the design rather than other aspects of the carpets. Id., at 444; see also Dobson v. Dornan,\n\n118 U.\xc2\xa0S. 10,\n17 (1886)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe plaintiff must show what profits or damages are attributable to the use of the infringing design\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

In 1887, in response to the Dobson cases, Congress enacted a specific damages remedy for design patent infringement. See S. Rep. No. 206, 49th Cong., 1st Sess., 1\xe2\x80\x932 (1886); H. R. Rep. No. 1966, 49th Cong., 1st Sess., 1\xe2\x80\x932 (1886). The new provision made it unlawful to manufacture or sell an article of manufacture to which a patented design or a colorable imitation thereof had been applied. An act to amend the law relating to patents, trademarks, and copyright, \xc2\xa71,\n\n24Stat.\n387. It went on to make a design patent infringer \xe2\x80\x9cliable in the amount of\xe2\x80\x9d $250 or \xe2\x80\x9cthe total profit made by him from the manufacture or sale .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the article or articles to which the design, or color-able imitation thereof, has been applied.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The Patent Act of 1952 codified this provision in \xc2\xa7289.\n\n66Stat.\n813. That codified language now reads, in relevant part:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cWhoever during the term of a patent for a design, without license of the owner, (1) applies the patented design, or any colorable imitation thereof, to any article of manufacture for the purpose of sale, or (2) sells or exposes for sale any article of manufacture to which such design or colorable imitation has been applied shall be liable to the owner to the extent of his total profit, but not less than $250 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7289.

\n

B

\n

Apple Inc. released its first-generation iPhone in 2007. The iPhone is a smartphone, a \xe2\x80\x9ccell phone with a broad range of other functions based on advanced computing capability, large storage capacity, and Internet connectiv-ity.\xe2\x80\x9d Riley v. California, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 2). Apple secured many design patents in connection with the release. Among those patents were the D618,677 patent, covering a black rectangular front face with rounded corners, the D593,087 patent, covering a rectangular front face with rounded corners and a raised rim, and the D604,305 patent, covering a grid of 16 colorful icons on a black screen. App. 530\xe2\x80\x93578.

\n

Samsung Electronics Co., Samsung Electronics America, Inc., and Samsung Telecommunications America, LLC (Samsung), also manufacture smartphones. After Apple released its iPhone, Samsung released a series of smartphones that resembled the iPhone. Id., at 357\xe2\x80\x93358.

\n

Apple sued Samsung in 2011, alleging, as relevant here, that various Samsung smartphones infringed Apple\xe2\x80\x99s D593,087, D618,677, and D604,305 design patents. A jury found that several Samsung smartphones did infringe those patents. See id., at 273\xe2\x80\x93276. All told, Apple was awarded $399 million in damages for Samsung\xe2\x80\x99s design patent infringement, the entire profit Samsung made from its sales of the infringing smartphones. See id., at 277\xe2\x80\x93280, 348\xe2\x80\x93350.

\n

The Federal Circuit affirmed the design patent infringement damages award.[1] In doing so, it rejected Samsung\xe2\x80\x99s argument \xe2\x80\x9cthat the profits awarded should have been limited to the infringing \xe2\x80\x98article of manufacture\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94for example, the screen or case of the smartphone\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cnot the entire infringing product\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the smartphone. 786 F.\xc2\xa03d 983, 1002 (2015). It reasoned that \xe2\x80\x9climit[ing] the dam-ages\xe2\x80\x9d award was not required because the \xe2\x80\x9cinnards of Samsung\xe2\x80\x99s smartphones were not sold separately from their shells as distinct articles of manufacture to ordinary purchasers.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

We granted certiorari, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016), and now reverse and remand.

\n

II

\n

Section 289 allows a patent holder to recover the total profit an infringer makes from the infringement. It does so by first prohibiting the unlicensed \xe2\x80\x9cappli[cation]\xe2\x80\x9d of a \xe2\x80\x9cpatented design, or any colorable imitation thereof, to any article of manufacture for the purpose of sale\xe2\x80\x9d or the unlicensed sale or exposure to sale of \xe2\x80\x9cany article of manufacture to which [a patented] design or colorable imitation has been applied.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7289. It then makes a person who violates that prohibition \xe2\x80\x9cliable to the owner to the extent of his total profit, but not less than $250.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cTotal,\xe2\x80\x9d of course, means all. See American Heritage Dictionary 1836 (5th ed. 2011) (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]he whole amount of something; the entirety\xe2\x80\x9d). The \xe2\x80\x9ctotal profit\xe2\x80\x9d for which \xc2\xa7289 makes an infringer liable is thus all of the profit made from the prohibited conduct, that is, from the manufacture or sale of the \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture to which [the patented] design or colorable imitation has been applied.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Arriving at a damages award under \xc2\xa7289 thus involves two steps. First, identify the \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d to which the infringed design has been applied. Second, calculate the infringer\xe2\x80\x99s total profit made on that article of manufacture.

\n

This case requires us to address a threshold matter: the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture.\xe2\x80\x9d The only question we resolve today is whether, in the case of a multicomponent product, the relevant \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d must always be the end product sold to the consumer or whether it can also be a component of that product. Under the former interpretation, a patent holder will always be entitled to the infringer\xe2\x80\x99s total profit from the end product. Under the latter interpretation, a patent holder will sometimes be entitled to the infringer\xe2\x80\x99s total profit from a component of the end product.[2]

\n

A

\n

The text resolves this case. The term \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture,\xe2\x80\x9d as used in \xc2\xa7289, encompasses both a product sold to a consumer and a component of that product.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cArticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d has a broad meaning. An \xe2\x80\x9carticle\xe2\x80\x9d is just \xe2\x80\x9ca particular thing.\xe2\x80\x9d J. Stormonth, A Dictionary of the English Language 53 (1885) (Stormonth); see also American Heritage Dictionary, at 101 (\xe2\x80\x9c[a]n individual thing or element of a class; a particular object or item\xe2\x80\x9d). And \xe2\x80\x9cmanufacture\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9cthe conversion of raw materials by the hand, or by machinery, into articles suitable for the use of man\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthe articles so made.\xe2\x80\x9d Stormonth 589; see also American Heritage Dictionary, at 1070 (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]he act, craft, or process of manufacturing products, especially on a large scale\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c[a] product that is manufactured\xe2\x80\x9d). An article of manufacture, then, is sim-ply a thing made by hand or machine.

\n

So understood, the term \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d is broad enough to encompass both a product sold to a consumer as well as a component of that product. A component of a product, no less than the product itself, is a thing made by hand or machine. That a component may be integrated into a larger product, in other words, does not put it outside the category of articles of manufacture.

\n

This reading of article of manufacture in \xc2\xa7289 is consistent with\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7171(a), which makes \xe2\x80\x9cnew, original and ornamental design[s] for an article of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d eligible for design patent protection.[3] The Patent Office and the courts have understood \xc2\xa7171 to permit a design patent for a design extending to only a component of a multicomponent product. See, e.g., Ex parte Adams, 84 Off. Gaz. Pat. Office 311 (1898) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe several articles of manufacture of peculiar shape which when combined produce a machine or structure having movable parts may each separately be patented as a design .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Application of Zahn, 617 F.\xc2\xa02d 261, 268 (CCPA 1980) (\xe2\x80\x9cSection 171 authorizes patents on ornamental designs for articles of manufacture. While the design must be embodied in some articles, the statute is not limited to designs for complete articles, or \xe2\x80\x98discrete\xe2\x80\x99 articles, and certainly not to articles separately sold .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

This reading is also consistent with\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7101, which makes \xe2\x80\x9cany new and useful .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. manufacture .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. or any new and useful improvement thereof\xe2\x80\x9d eligible for utility patent protection. Cf. 8 D. Chisum, Patents \xc2\xa723.03[2], pp. 23\xe2\x80\x9312 to 23\xe2\x80\x9313 (2014) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7171 includes \xe2\x80\x9cwhat would be considered a \xe2\x80\x98manufacture\xe2\x80\x99 within the meaning of Section 101\xe2\x80\x9d). \xe2\x80\x9c[T]his Court has read the term \xe2\x80\x98manufacture\xe2\x80\x99 in \xc2\xa7101 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to mean \xe2\x80\x98the production of articles for use from raw or prepared materials by giving to these materials new forms, qualities, properties, or combinations, whether by hand-labor or by machinery.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Diamond v. Chakrabarty,\n\n447 U.\xc2\xa0S. 303,\n308 (1980)\n (quoting American Fruit Growers, Inc. v. Brogdex Co.,\n\n283 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n11 (1931)\n). The broad term includes \xe2\x80\x9cthe parts of a machine considered sepa-rately from the machine itself.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 W. Robinson, The Law of Patents for Useful Inventions \xc2\xa7183, p. 270 (1890).

\n

B

\n

The Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s narrower reading of \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d cannot be squared with the text of \xc2\xa7289. The Federal Circuit found that components of the infringing smartphones could not be the relevant article of manufacture because consumers could not purchase those components separately from the smartphones. See 786 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1002 (declining to limit a \xc2\xa7289 award to a component of the smartphone because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he innards of Samsung\xe2\x80\x99s smartphones were not sold separately from their shells as distinct articles of manufacture to ordinary purchasers\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Nordock, Inc. v. Systems Inc., 803 F.\xc2\xa03d 1344, 1355 (CA Fed. 2015) (declining to limit a \xc2\xa7289 award to a design for a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98lip and hinge plate\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d because it was \xe2\x80\x9cwelded together\xe2\x80\x9d with a leveler and \xe2\x80\x9cthere was no evidence\xe2\x80\x9d it was sold \xe2\x80\x9cseparate[ly] from the leveler as a complete unit\xe2\x80\x9d). But, for the reasons given above, the term \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d is broad enough to embrace both a product sold to a consumer and a component of that product, whether sold separately or not. Thus, reading \xe2\x80\x9carticle of manufacture\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7289 to cover only an end product sold to a consumer gives too narrow a meaning to the phrase.

\n

The parties ask us to go further and resolve whether, for each of the design patents at issue here, the relevant article of manufacture is the smartphone, or a particular smartphone component. Doing so would require us to set out a test for identifying the relevant article of manufacture at the first step of the \xc2\xa7289 damages inquiry and to parse the record to apply that test in this case. The United States as amicus curiae suggested a test, see Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 27\xe2\x80\x9329, but Samsung and Apple did not brief the issue. We decline to lay out a test for the first step of the \xc2\xa7289 damages inquiry in the absence of adequate briefing by the parties. Doing so is not necessary to resolve the question presented in this case, and the Federal Circuit may address any remaining issues on remand.

\n

III

\n

The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit is therefore reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
