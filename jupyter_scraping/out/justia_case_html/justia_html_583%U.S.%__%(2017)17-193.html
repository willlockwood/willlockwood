SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

JEFFERSON DUNN, COMMISSIONER, ALABAMA DEPARTMENT OF CORRECTIONS v. VERNON MADISON

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 17\xe2\x80\x93193.\xe2\x80\x83Decided November 6, 2017

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

More than 30 years ago, Vernon Madison crept up behind police officer Julius Schulte and shot him twice in the head at close range. An Alabama jury found Madison guilty of capital murder. The trial court sentenced him to death. See Ex\xc2\xa0parte Madison, 718 So.\xc2\xa02d 104, 105\xe2\x80\x93106 (1998).

\n

In 2016, as Madison\xe2\x80\x99s execution neared, he petitioned the trial court for a suspension of his death sentence. He argued that, due to several recent strokes, he has become incompetent to be executed. The court held a hearing to receive testimony from two psychologists who had examined Madison and prepared reports concerning his competence. The court\xe2\x80\x99s appointed psychologist, Dr. Karl Kirkland, reported that, although Madison may have \xe2\x80\x9csuffered a significant decline post-stroke, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [he] understands the exact posture of his case at this point,\xe2\x80\x9d and appears to have a \xe2\x80\x9crational understanding of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the results or effects\xe2\x80\x9d of his death sentence. App. to Pet. for Cert. 75a (internal quotation marks omitted); Madison v. Commissioner, Ala. Dept. of Corrections, 851 F.\xc2\xa03d 1173, 1193 (CA11 2017) (internal quotation marks omitted). Asked at the hearing whether Madison understands that Alabama is seeking retribution against him for his criminal act, Dr. Kirkland answered, \xe2\x80\x9cCertainly.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1180 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Dr. John Goff, a psychologist hired by Madison\xe2\x80\x99s counsel, reported that Madison\xe2\x80\x99s strokes have rendered him unable to remember \xe2\x80\x9cnumerous events that have occurred over the past thirty years or more.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 77a. Nevertheless, Dr. Goff found that Madison \xe2\x80\x9cis able to understand the nature of the pending proceeding and he has an understanding of what he was tried for\xe2\x80\x9d; that he knows he is \xe2\x80\x9cin prison .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. because of \xe2\x80\x98murder\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d; that he \xe2\x80\x9cunderstands that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [Alabama is] seeking retribution\xe2\x80\x9d for that crime; and that he \xe2\x80\x9cunderstands the sentence, specifically the meaning of a death sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 76a\xe2\x80\x9378a (some internal quotation marks omitted). In Dr. Goff\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, however, Madison does not \xe2\x80\x9cunderstan[d] the act that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. he is being punished for\xe2\x80\x9d because he cannot recall \xe2\x80\x9cthe sequence of events from the offense to his arrest to the trial or any of those details\xe2\x80\x9d and believes that he \xe2\x80\x9cnever went around killing folks.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

The trial court denied Madison\xe2\x80\x99s petition. It held that, under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions in Ford v. Wainwright,\n\n477 U.\xc2\xa0S. 399 (1986)\n, and Panetti v. Quarterman,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 930 (2007)\n, Madison was entitled to relief if he could show that he \xe2\x80\x9csuffers from a mental illness which deprives [him] of the mental capacity to rationally understand that he is being executed as a punishment for a crime.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 74a. The court concluded that Madison had failed to make that showing. Specifically, it found that Madison understands \xe2\x80\x9cthat he is going to be executed because of the murder he committed[,] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the State is seeking retribution[,] and that he will die when he is executed.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 82a.

\n

Madison then filed a petition for a writ of habeas corpus in Federal District Court. As a state prisoner, Madison is entitled to federal habeas relief under the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) only if the state trial court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication of his incompetence claim \xe2\x80\x9cwas contrary to, or involved an unreasonable application of, clearly established Federal law, as determined by\xe2\x80\x9d this Court, or else was \xe2\x80\x9cbased on an unreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence presented\xe2\x80\x9d in state court. 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa02254(d). A habeas petitioner meets this demanding standard only when he shows that the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision was \xe2\x80\x9cso lacking in justification that there was an error well understood and comprehended in existing law beyond any possibility for fairminded disagreement.\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington v. Richter,\n\n562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 86,\n103 (2011)\n. The District Court denied Madison\xe2\x80\x99s petition after concluding that the state court \xe2\x80\x9ccorrectly applied Ford and Panetti\xe2\x80\x9d and did not make an \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable determination of the facts in light of the evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 67a.

\n

The Eleventh Circuit granted a certificate of appealability and, on appeal, reversed over Judge Jordan\xe2\x80\x99s dissent. In the majority\xe2\x80\x99s view, given the undisputed fact that Madison \xe2\x80\x9chas no memory of his capital offense,\xe2\x80\x9d it inescapably follows that he \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not rationally understand the connection between his crime and his execution.\xe2\x80\x9d 851 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1185\xe2\x80\x931186. On that basis, the Eleventh Circuit held that the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that Madison is competent to be executed was \xe2\x80\x9cplainly unreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccannot be reconciled with any reasonable application of Panetti.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1187\xe2\x80\x931188 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

We disagree. In Panetti, this Court addressed the question whether the\n \nEighth Amendment forbids the execution of a prisoner who lacks \xe2\x80\x9cthe mental capacity to understand that [he] is being executed as a punishment for a crime.\xe2\x80\x9d 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 954 (internal quotation marks omitted). We noted that the retributive purpose of capital punishment is not well served where \xe2\x80\x9cthe prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s mental state is so distorted by a mental illness that his awareness of the crime and punishment has little or no relation to the understanding of those concepts shared by the community as a whole.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 958\xe2\x80\x93959. Similarly, in Ford, we questioned the \xe2\x80\x9cretributive value of executing a person who has no comprehension of why he has been singled out.\xe2\x80\x9d 477 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 409. Neither Panetti nor Ford \xe2\x80\x9cclearly established\xe2\x80\x9d that a prisoner is incompetent to be executed because of a failure to remember his commission of the crime, as distinct from a failure to rationally comprehend the concepts of crime and punishment as applied in his case. The state court did not unreasonably apply Panetti and Ford when it determined that Madison is competent to be executed because\xe2\x80\x94notwithstanding his memory loss\xe2\x80\x94he recognizes that he will be put to death as punishment for the murder he was found to have committed.

\n

Nor was the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision founded on an unreasonable assessment of the evidence before it. Testimony from each of the psychologists who examined Madison supported the court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that Madison understands both that he was tried and imprisoned for murder and that Alabama will put him to death as punishment for that crime.

\n

In short, the state court\xe2\x80\x99s determinations of law and fact were not \xe2\x80\x9cso lacking in justification\xe2\x80\x9d as to give rise to error \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond any possibility for fairminded disagreement.\xe2\x80\x9d Richter, supra, at 103. Under that deferential standard, Madison\xe2\x80\x99s claim to federal habeas relief must fail. We express no view on the merits of the underlying question outside of the AEDPA context.

\n

The petition for a writ of certiorari and respondent\xe2\x80\x99s motion to proceed in forma pauperis are granted, and the judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

Justice Breyer, concurring.

\n

I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s per curiam disposition of this case for the reason set forth in Justice Ginsburg\xe2\x80\x99s concurrence (which I also join). I write separately to underline the fact that this case illustrates one of the basic problems with the administration of the death penalty itself. That problem concerns the unconscionably long periods of time that prisoners often spend on death row awaiting execution. See Glossip v. Gross, 576 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2015) (Breyer, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 2, 17\xe2\x80\x9333).

\n

As I have previously noted, this Court once said that delays in execution can produce uncertainty amounting to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98one of the most horrible feelings to which\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d a prisoner \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98can be subjected.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 20) (quoting In re Medley,\n\n134 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160,\n172 (1890)\n). Justice Stevens later observed that the delay in Medley was a delay of four weeks. Lackey v. Texas, \n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1045,\n1046 (1995)\n (memorandum respecting denial of certiorari). And he wrote that the Medley description \xe2\x80\x9cshould apply with even greater force in the case of delays that last for many years.\xe2\x80\x9d 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 1046.

\n

In light of those statements, consider the present case. The respondent, Vernon Madison, was convicted of a murder that took place in April 1985. He was sentenced to death and transferred to Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s William C. Holman Correctional Facility in September 1985. Mr. Madison is now 67 years old. He has lived nearly half of his life on death row. During that time, he has suffered severe strokes, which caused vascular dementia and numerous other significant physical and mental problems. He is legally blind. His speech is slurred. He cannot walk independently. He is incontinent. His disability leaves him without a memory of his commission of a capital offense.

\n

Moreover, Mr. Madison is one among a growing number of aging prisoners who remain on death row in this country for ever longer periods of time. In 1987, the average period of imprisonment between death sentence and execution was just over seven years. See Dept. of Justice, Bureau of Justice Statistics, T. Snell, Capital Punishment, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Statistical Tables 14 (rev. Dec. 19, 2014) (Table 10). A decade later, in 1997, the average delay was about 11 years. Ibid. In 2007, the average delay rose to a little less than 13 years. Ibid. In 2017, the 21 individuals who have been executed were on death row on average for more than 19 years. See Death Penalty Information Center, Execution List 2017, online at https://deathpenaltyinfo.org/execution-list-2017 (as last visited Nov. 3, 2017). Alabama has executed three individuals this year, including Thomas Arthur, who spent 34 years on death row before hisexecution on May 26, 2017, at the age of 75; Robert Melson, who spent 21 years on death row before his execution on June 8, 2017; and Torrey McNabb, who spent nearly two decades on death row before his execution on October 19, 2017.

\n

Given this trend, we may face ever more instances of state efforts to execute prisoners suffering the diseases and infirmities of old age. And we may well have to consider the ways in which lengthy periods of imprisonment between death sentence and execution can deepen the cruelty of the death penalty while at the same time undermining its penological rationale. Glossip, supra, at ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (Breyer, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 17\xe2\x80\x9318) (recognizing the inevitability of delays in light of constitutional requirements needed to ensure procedural and substantive validity of death sentences); see ante, at 1 (Ginsburg, J., concurring).

\n

Rather than develop a constitutional jurisprudence that focuses upon the special circumstances of the aged, however, I believe it would be wiser to reconsider the root cause of the problem\xe2\x80\x94the constitutionality of the death penalty itself. Glossip, supra, at ___ (Breyer, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 1).

Justice Ginsburg, with whom Justice Breyer and Justice Sotomayor join, concurring.

\n

The issue whether a State may administer the death penalty to a person whose disability leaves him without memory of his commission of a capital offense is a substantial question not yet addressed by the Court. Appropriately presented, the issue would warrant full airing. But in this case, the restraints imposed by the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996, I agree, preclude consideration of the question. With that understanding, I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s per curiam disposition of this case.
