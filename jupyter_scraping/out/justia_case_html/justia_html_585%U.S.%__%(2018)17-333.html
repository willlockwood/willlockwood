O. JOHN BENISEK, et\xc2\xa0al., APPELLANTS v. LINDA H. LAMONE, ADMINISTRATOR, MARYLAND STATE BOARD OF ELECTIONS, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on appeal from the united states district court for the district of maryland

\n

[June 18, 2018]

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

This appeal arises from the denial of a motion for a preliminary injunction in the District Court. Appellants are several Republican voters, plaintiffs below, who allege that Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s Sixth Congressional District was gerrymandered in 2011 for the purpose of retaliating against them for their political views.

\n

In May 2017, six years after the Maryland General Assembly redrew the Sixth District, plaintiffs moved the District Court to enjoin Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s election officials from holding congressional elections under the 2011 map. They asserted that \xe2\x80\x9cextend[ing] this constitutional offense\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the alleged gerrymander\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cinto the 2018 election would be a manifest and irreparable injury.\xe2\x80\x9d Record in No. 1:13\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x933233, Doc. 177\xe2\x80\x931, p.\xc2\xa03. In order to allow time for the creation of a new districting map, plaintiffs urged the District Court to enter a preliminary injunction by August 18, 2017. Id., at 32.

\n

On August 24, 2017, the District Court denied plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 motion and stayed further proceedings pending this Court\xe2\x80\x99s disposition of partisan gerrymandering claims in Gill v. Whitford, No. 16\xe2\x80\x931161. 266 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa03d 799. The District Court found that plaintiffs had failed to show a likelihood of success on the merits sufficient to warrant a preliminary injunction. Id., at 808\xe2\x80\x93814. The District Court also held that it was \xe2\x80\x9cin no position to award [p]laintiffs the remedy they .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. requested on the timetable they .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. demanded.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 815. The court explained that, notwithstanding its \xe2\x80\x9cdiligence in ruling on the pending preliminary injunction motion (which has been a priority for each member of this panel),\xe2\x80\x9d plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 proposed August deadline for injunctive relief had \xe2\x80\x9calready come and gone.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

In addition, the District Court emphasized that it was concerned about \xe2\x80\x9cmeasuring the legality and constitutionality of any redistricting plan in Maryland .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. according to the proper legal standard.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 816. In the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, it would be \xe2\x80\x9cbetter equipped to make that legal determination and to chart a wise course for further proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d after this Court issued a decision in Gill. Ibid. Plaintiffs ask this Court to vacate the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order and remand for further consideration of whether a preliminary injunction is appropriate.

\n

We now note our jurisdiction and review the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision for an abuse of discretion, keeping in mind that a preliminary injunction is \xe2\x80\x9can extraordinary remedy never awarded as of right.\xe2\x80\x9d Winter v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 7, 24 (2008). As a matter of equitable discretion, a preliminary injunction does not follow as a matter of course from a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s showing of a likelihood of success on the merits. See id., at 32. Rather, a court must also consider whether the mov-ant has shown \xe2\x80\x9cthat he is likely to suffer irreparable harm in the absence of preliminary relief, that the balance of equities tips in his favor, and that an injunction is in the public interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 20.

\n

Plaintiffs made no such showing below. Even if we assume\xe2\x80\x94contrary to the findings of the District Court\xe2\x80\x94that plaintiffs were likely to succeed on the merits of their claims, the balance of equities and the public interest tilted against their request for a preliminary injunction.

\n

First, a party requesting a preliminary injunction must generally show reasonable diligence. Cf. Holmberg v. Armbrecht,\n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 392, 396 (1946). That is as true in election law cases as elsewhere. See Lucas v. Townsend,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1301, 1305 (1988) (Kennedy, J., in chambers); Fishman v. Schaffer,\n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1325, 1330 (1976) (Marshall, J., in chambers). In this case, appellants did not move for a preliminary injunction in the District Court until six years, and three general elections, after the 2011 map was adopted, and over three years after the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 first complaint was filed.

\n

Plaintiffs argue that they have nevertheless pursued their claims diligently, and they attribute their delay in seeking a preliminary injunction to the \xe2\x80\x9cconvoluted procedural history of the case\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cdogged refusal to cooperate in discovery\xe2\x80\x9d by state officials. Reply Brief 22. Yet the record suggests that the delay largely arose from a circumstance within plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 control: namely, their failure to plead the claims giving rise to their request for preliminary injunctive relief until 2016. Although one of the seven plaintiffs before us filed a complaint in 2013 alleging that Maryland\xe2\x80\x99s congressional map was an unconstitutional gerrymander, that initial complaint did not present the retaliation theory asserted here. See Amended Complaint, Doc. 11, p.\xc2\xa03 (Dec. 2, 2013) (explaining that the gerrymandering claim did not turn upon \xe2\x80\x9cthe reason or intent of the legislature\xe2\x80\x9d in adopting the map).

\n

It was not until 2016 that the remaining plaintiffs joined the case and filed an amended complaint alleging that Maryland officials intentionally retaliated against them because of their political views. See 3 App. 640\xe2\x80\x93643. Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 newly presented claims\xe2\x80\x94unlike the gerrymandering claim presented in the 2013 complaint\xe2\x80\x94required discovery into the motives of the officials who produced the 2011 congressional map. See, e.g., Memorandum of Law in Support of Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 Motion to Compel, Doc. 111\xe2\x80\x931, p.\xc2\xa03 (Jan. 4, 2017) (describing plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 demand that various state officials \xe2\x80\x9ctestify .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and answer questions concerning legislative intent\xe2\x80\x9d). It is true that the assertion of legislative privilege by those officials delayed the completion of that discovery. See Joint Motion To Extend Deadlines for Completion of Fact Discovery and Expert Witness Disclosures, Doc. 161, pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x932 (Mar. 3, 2017); Joint Motion To Extend Deadlines for Completion of Fact Discovery and Expert Witness Disclosures, Doc. 170, pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x932 (Mar. 27, 2017). But that does not change the fact that plaintiffs could have sought a preliminary injunction much earlier. See Fishman, supra, at 1330. In considering the balance of equities among the parties, we think that plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 unnecessary, years-long delay in asking for preliminary injunctive relief weighed against their request.

\n

Second, a due regard for the public interest in orderly elections supported the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s discretionary decision to deny a preliminary injunction and to stay the proceedings. See Purcell v. Gonzalez,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, 4\xe2\x80\x935 (2006) (per curiam). Plaintiffs themselves represented to the District Court that any injunctive relief would have to be granted by August 18, 2017, to ensure the timely completion of a new districting scheme in advance of the 2018 election season. Despite the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s undisputedly diligent efforts, however, that date had \xe2\x80\x9calready come and gone\xe2\x80\x9d by the time the court ruled on plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 motion. 266 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa03d, at 815. (Such deadline has also, of course, long since passed for purposes of entering a preliminary injunction on remand from this Court.)

\n

On top of this time constraint was the legal uncertainty surrounding any potential remedy for the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 asserted injury. At the time the District Court made its decision, the appeal in Gill was pending before this Court. The District Court recognized that our decision in Gill had the potential to \xe2\x80\x9cshed light on critical questions in this case\xe2\x80\x9d and to set forth a \xe2\x80\x9cframework\xe2\x80\x9d by which plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claims could be decided and, potentially, remedied. 266 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa03d, at 815\xe2\x80\x93816. In the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, \xe2\x80\x9ccharging ahead\xe2\x80\x9d and adjudicating the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claims in that fluctuating legal environment, when firmer guidance from this Court might have been forthcoming, would have been a mistake. Id., at 816. Such a determination was within the sound discretion of the District Court. Given the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to wait for this Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling in Gill before further adjudicating plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claims, the court reasonably could have concluded that a preliminary injunction would have been against the public interest, as an injunction might have worked a needlessly \xe2\x80\x9cchaotic and disruptive effect upon the electoral process,\xe2\x80\x9d Fishman, supra, at 1330, and because the \xe2\x80\x9cpurpose of a preliminary injunction is merely to preserve the relative positions of the parties until a trial on the merits can be held,\xe2\x80\x9d University of Tex. v. Camenisch,\n\n451 U.\xc2\xa0S. 390, 395 (1981). In these particular circumstances, we conclude that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision denying a preliminary injunction cannot be regarded as an abuse of discretion.

\n

The order of the District Court is

\n

Affirmed.
