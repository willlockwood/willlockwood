KUCANA v. HOLDER, ATTORNEY GENERAL

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the seventh circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x93911.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 10, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 20, 2010

\n

The Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996 (IIRIRA) amended the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA or Act), codifying certain rules, earlier prescribed by the Attorney General, that govern the process of reopening removal proceedings. IIRIRA also added a provision stating that no court has jurisdiction to review any action of the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cthe authority for which is specified under this subchapter to be in the discretion of the Attorney General.\xe2\x80\x9d 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii). A regulation, amended just months before IIRIRA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he decision to grant or deny a motion to reopen \xe2\x80\xa6 is within the discretion of the [Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA)],\xe2\x80\x9d 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2(a). As adjudicator in immigration cases, the BIA exercises authority delegated by the Attorney General.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Kucana moved to reopen his removal proceedings, asserting new evidence in support of his plea for asylum. An Immigration Judge denied the motion, and the BIA sustained that ruling. The Seventh Circuit concluded that it lacked jurisdiction to review the administrative determination, holding that \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) bars judicial review not only of administrative decisions made discretionary by statute, but also of those made discretionary by regulation.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section 1252(a)(2)(B)\xe2\x80\x99s proscription of judicial review applies only to Attorney General determinations made discretionary by statute, not to determinations declared discretionary by the Attorney General himself through regulation. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The motion to reopen is an \xe2\x80\x9cimportant safeguard\xe2\x80\x9d intended \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure a proper and lawful disposition\xe2\x80\x9d of immigration proceedings. Dada v. Mukasey, 554 U. S. 1, ___. Federal-court review of administrative decisions denying motions to reopen removal proceedings dates back to at least 1916, with the courts employing a deferential abuse-of-discretion standard of review. While the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s regulation in point, 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2(a), places the reopening decision within the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s discretion, the statute does not codify that prescription or otherwise \xe2\x80\x9cspecif[y]\xe2\x80\x9d that such decisions are in the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s discretion. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Section 1252(a)(2)(B) does not proscribe judicial review of denials of motions to reopen. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0The amicus defending the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment urges that regulations suffice to trigger \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii)\xe2\x80\x99s proscription. She comprehends \xe2\x80\x9cunder\xe2\x80\x9d in \xe2\x80\x9cauthority \xe2\x80\xa6 specified under this subchapter\xe2\x80\x9d to mean, e.g., \xe2\x80\x9cpursuant to,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9csubordinate to.\xe2\x80\x9d Administrative regulations count for \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B) purposes, she submits, because they are issued \xe2\x80\x9cpursuant to,\xe2\x80\x9d and are measures \xe2\x80\x9csubordinate to,\xe2\x80\x9d the legislation they serve to implement. On that reading, \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) would bar judicial review of any decision that an executive regulation places within the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s discretion, including the decision to deny a motion to reopen. The parties, on the other hand, read the statutory language to mean \xe2\x80\x9cspecified in,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cspecified by,\xe2\x80\x9d the subchapter. On their reading, \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) precludes judicial review only when the statute itself specifies the discretionary character of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s authority. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0The word \xe2\x80\x9cunder\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9chas many dictionary definitions and must draw its meaning from its context.\xe2\x80\x9d Ardestani v. INS, 502 U. S. 129, 135. Examining the provision at issue in statutory context, the parties\xe2\x80\x99 position stands on firmer ground. Section 1252(a)(2)(B)(ii) is far from IIRIRA\xe2\x80\x99s only jurisdictional limitation. It is sandwiched between two subsections, \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(A) and \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(C), both dependent on statutory provisions, not on any regulation, to define their scope. Given \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)\xe2\x80\x99s statutory placement, one would expect that it, too, would cover statutory provisions alone. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(3)\xc2\xa0Section 1252(a)(2)(B)(i) places within the no-judicial-review category \xe2\x80\x9cany judgment regarding the granting of relief under section 1182(h), 1182(i), 1229b, 1229c, or 1255.\xe2\x80\x9d Each of the referenced statutory provisions addresses a different form of discretionary relief from removal and contains language indicating that the decision is entrusted to the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s discretion. Clause (i) does not refer to any regulatory provision. The proximity of clause (i) and the clause (ii) catchall, and the words linking them\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cany other decision\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94suggests that Congress had in mind decisions of the same genre, i.e., those made discretionary by legislation. Read harmoniously, both clauses convey that Congress barred court review of discretionary decisions only when Congress itself set out the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s discretionary authority in the statute. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(4)\xc2\xa0Also significant is the character of the decisions insulated from judicial review in \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(i). The listed determinations are substantive decisions the Executive makes involving whether or not aliens can stay in the country. Other decisions specified by statute \xe2\x80\x9cto be in the discretion of the Attorney General,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore shielded from court oversight by \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii), are of a like kind. See, e.g., \xc2\xa71157(c)(1). Decisions on reopening motions made discretionary by regulation, in contrast, are adjunct rulings. A court decision reversing the denial of a motion to reopen does not direct the Executive to afford the alien substantive relief; ordinarily, it touches and concerns only the question whether the alien\xe2\x80\x99s claims have been accorded a reasonable hearing. Had Congress wanted the jurisdictional bar to encompass decisions specified as discretionary by regulation as well as by statute, moreover, Congress could easily have said so, as it did in provisions enacted simultaneously with \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii). See, e.g., IIRIRA, \xc2\xa7213, 110 Stat. 3009\xe2\x80\x93572. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(5)\xc2\xa0The history of the relevant statutory provisions corroborates this determination. Attorney General regulations have long addressed reopening requests. In enacting IIRIRA, Congress simultaneously codified the process for filing motions to reopen and acted to bar judicial review of a number of executive decisions regarding removal. But Congress did not codify the regulation delegating to the BIA discretion to grant or deny reopening motions. This legislative silence indicates that Congress left the matter where it was pre-IIRIRA: The BIA has broad discretion, conferred by the Attorney General, \xe2\x80\x9cto grant or deny a motion to reopen,\xe2\x80\x9d 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2(a), but courts retain jurisdiction to review the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s decision. It is unsurprising that Congress would leave in place judicial oversight of this \xe2\x80\x9cimportant [procedural] safeguard,\xe2\x80\x9d Dada, 554 U. S., at ___, where, as here, the alien\xe2\x80\x99s underlying asylum claim would itself be reviewable. The REAL ID Act of 2005, which further amended the INA by adding or reformulating provisions on asylum, protection from removal, and even judicial review, did not disturb the unbroken line of decisions upholding court review of administrative denials of motions to reopen. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Any lingering doubt about \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii)\xe2\x80\x99s proper interpretation would be dispelled by a familiar statutory construction principle: the presumption favoring judicial review of administrative action. When a statute is \xe2\x80\x9creasonably susceptible to divergent interpretation,\xe2\x80\x9d this Court adopts the reading \xe2\x80\x9cthat executive determinations generally are subject to judicial review.\xe2\x80\x9d Gutierrez de Martinez v. Lamagno, 515 U. S. 417, 434. The Court has consistently applied this interpretive guide to legislation regarding immigration, and particularly to questions concerning the preservation of federal-court jurisdiction. See, e.g., Reno v. Catholic Social Services, Inc., 509 U. S. 43, 63\xe2\x80\x9364. Because this presumption is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98well-settled,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., the Court assumes that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress legislates with knowledge of\xe2\x80\x9d it, McNary v. Haitian Refugee Center, Inc., 498 U. S. 479, 496. It therefore takes \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing evidence\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to dislodge the presumption. Catholic Social Services, Inc., 509 U. S., at 64. There is no such evidence here. Finally, reading \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) to apply to matters where discretion is conferred on the BIA by regulation would ignore Congress\xe2\x80\x99 design to retain for itself control over federal-court jurisdiction. The Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s construction would free the Executive to shelter its own decisions from abuse-of-discretion appellate court review simply by issuing a regulation declaring those decisions \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary.\xe2\x80\x9d Such an extraordinary delegation of authority cannot be extracted from the statute Congress enacted. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

533 F.\xc2\xa03d 534, reversed and remanded.

AGRON KUCANA, PETITIONER v. ERIC H. HOLDER,\nJr., ATTORNEY GENERAL

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the seventh circuit

\n

[January 20, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Agron Kucana moved to reopen his removal proceedings, asserting new evidence in support of his plea for asylum. An Immigration Judge (IJ) denied the motion, the Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA or Board) sustained the IJ\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, and the U. S. Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit concluded that it lacked jurisdiction to review the administrative determination. For that conclusion, the court relied on a provision added to the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA or Act), 66 Stat. 166, 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101 et seq., by the Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996 (IIRIRA), 110 Stat. 3009\xe2\x80\x93546. The provision found dispositive by the Seventh Circuit, 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B), states that no court shall have jurisdiction to review any action of the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cthe authority for which is specified under this subchapter to be in the discretion of the Attorney General,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to decide whether the proscription of judicial review stated in \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B) applies not only to Attorney General determinations made discretionary by statute, but also to determinations declared discretionary by the Attorney General himself through regulation. We hold that the key words \xe2\x80\x9cspecified under this subchapter\xe2\x80\x9d refer to statutory, but not to regulatory, specifications. We so rule based on the longstanding exercise of judicial review of administrative rulings on reopening motions, the text and context of \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B), and the history of the relevant statutory provisions. We take account, as well, of the \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption favoring interpretations of statutes [to] allow judicial review of administrative action.\xe2\x80\x9d Reno v. Catholic Social Services, Inc., 509 U. S. 43, 63\xe2\x80\x9364 (1993) (quoting McNary v. Haitian Refugee Center, Inc., 498 U. S. 479, 496 (1991)). Separation-of-powers concerns, moreover, caution us against reading legislation, absent clear statement, to place in executive hands authority to remove cases from the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s domain.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In IIRIRA, Congress for the first time codified certain rules, earlier prescribed by the Attorney General, governing the reopening process. The amended Act instructs that reopening motions \xe2\x80\x9cshall state the new facts that will be proven at a hearing to be held if the motion is granted, and shall be supported by affidavits or other evidentiary material.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71229a(c)(7)(B). Congress also prescribed that \xe2\x80\x9cthe motion to reopen shall be filed within 90 days of the date of entry of a final administrative order of removal.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71229a(c)(7)(C)(i). Among matters excepted from the 90-day limitation are motions to reopen asylum applications because of changed conditions in the country of nationality or removal. \xc2\xa71229a(c)(7)(C)(ii).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 1252(a)(2), captioned \xe2\x80\x9cMatters not subject to judicial review,\xe2\x80\x9d contains the provision on which this case turns. Subparagraph (B) of that paragraph, headed \xe2\x80\x9cDenials of discretionary relief,\xe2\x80\x9d states:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cNotwithstanding any other provision of law (statutory or nonstatutory), \xe2\x80\xa6 except as provided in subparagraph (D),[[Footnote 1]] and regardless of whether the judgment, decision, or action is made in removal proceedings, no court shall have jurisdiction to review\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(i) any judgment regarding the granting of relief under section 1182(h), 1182(i), 1229b, 1229c, or 1255 of this title,[[Footnote 2]] or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(ii) any other decision or action of the Attorney General \xe2\x80\xa6 the authority for which is specified under this subchapter[[Footnote 3]] to be in the discretion of the Attorney General \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, other than the granting of relief under section 1158(a) of this title.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 4]

\n

\nA regulation, amended in 1996, just months before Congress enacted IIRIRA, 61 Fed. Reg. 18904, Pt. 3, \xc2\xa73.2(a), states that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he decision to grant or deny a motion to reopen \xe2\x80\xa6 is within the discretion of the Board.\xe2\x80\x9d 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2(a) (2009). As adjudicator in immigration cases, the Board exercises authority delegated by the Attorney General. See 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71103(g)(2); 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.1. See also 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.23(b)(3) (governing motions to reopen filed with an IJ).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kucana, a citizen of Albania, entered the United States on a business visa in 1995 and remained after the visa expired. Alleging that he would be persecuted based on his political beliefs if returned to Albania, Kucana applied for asylum and withholding of removal in 1996. An IJ determined that Kucana was removable and scheduled a hearing to evaluate his eligibility for asylum. When Kucana failed to appear for the hearing, the IJ immediately ordered his removal in absentia. Kucana filed a motion to reopen, explaining that he had missed his hearing because he had overslept. The IJ denied the motion, and the BIA affirmed in 2002. Kucana did not seek judicial review, nor did he leave the United States.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Kucana filed a second motion to reopen his removal proceedings in 2006, contending that conditions in Albania had worsened.[Footnote 5] The BIA denied relief; it concluded that conditions in Albania had actually improved since 1997. Arguing that the BIA had abused its discretion in denying his motion, Kucana filed a petition for review in the Seventh Circuit.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a fractured decision, the Seventh Circuit dismissed the petition for lack of jurisdiction. Kucana v. Mukasey, 533 F.\xc2\xa03d 534, 539 (2008). The court held that 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) bars judicial review not only of administrative decisions made discretionary by statute, but also \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the agency\xe2\x80\x99s discretion is specified by a regulation rather than a statute.\xe2\x80\x9d 533 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 536.[Footnote 6] In so ruling, the Seventh Circuit created a split between itself and other Courts of Appeals, all of them holding that denials of reopening motions are reviewable in court.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Ripple concurred dubitante. He acknowledged that the court was following an earlier decision, Ali v. Gonzales, 502 F.\xc2\xa03d 659 (CA7 2007),[Footnote 8] but \xe2\x80\x9csuggest[ed] that, had Congress intended to deprive th[e] court of jurisdiction \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, it would have done so explicitly, as it did in 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(i).\xe2\x80\x9d 533 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 540. The court, he concluded, should revisit both Ali and Kucana and \xe2\x80\x9cchart a course \xe2\x80\xa6 more closely adher[ing] to the statutory language chosen and enacted by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d 533 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 540.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Cudahy dissented. Given the absence of \xe2\x80\x9cspecific [statutory] language entrusting the decision on a motion to reopen to the discretion of the Attorney General,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted), he saw no impediment to the exercise of jurisdiction over Kucana\xe2\x80\x99s petition. In support of his position, Judge Cudahy invoked the \xe2\x80\x9cstrong presumption that Congress intends judicial review of administrative action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 541 (quoting Traynor v. Turnage, 485 U. S. 535, 542 (1988)). With four judges dissenting, the Seventh Circuit denied Kucana\xe2\x80\x99s petition for rehearing en banc. See 533 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 541\xe2\x80\x93542 (dissenting statement of Ripple, J., joined by Rovner, Wood, and Williams, JJ.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 556 U. S. ___ (2009), to re-\nsolve the Circuit conflict. As it did before the Seventh\nCircuit, the Government agrees with Kucana that \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) does not remove federal-court jurisdiction to review the denial of a reopening motion. We appointed Amanda C. Leiter to brief and argue the case, as amicus curiae, in support of the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment. 557 U. S. ___ (2009). Ms. Leiter has ably discharged her assigned responsibilities.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The motion to reopen is an \xe2\x80\x9cimportant safeguard\xe2\x80\x9d intended \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure a proper and lawful disposition\xe2\x80\x9d of immigration proceedings. Dada v. Mukasey, 554 U. S. 1, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316); cf. Stone v. INS, 514 U. S. 386, 401 (1995) (analogizing motions to reconsider immigration decisions to motions for relief from a judgment under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 60(b)). Federal-court review of administrative decisions denying motions to reopen removal proceedings dates back to at least 1916. See Dada, 554 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310) (citing cases). This Court has ultimately reviewed reopening decisions on numerous occasions. See, e.g., INS v. Doherty, 502 U. S. 314, 322\xe2\x80\x93324 (1992); INS v. Abudu, 485 U. S. 94, 104\xe2\x80\x93111 (1988); INS v. Rios-Pineda, 471 U. S. 444, 449\xe2\x80\x93452 (1985); INS v. Jong Ha Wang, 450 U. S. 139, 141\xe2\x80\x93146 (1981) (per curiam). Mindful of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cbroad discretion\xe2\x80\x9d in such matters, however, courts have employed a deferential, abuse-of-discretion standard of review. See Doherty, 502 U. S., at 323 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Seventh Circuit held that Congress removed the authority long exercised by federal courts to review denials of an alien\xe2\x80\x99s reopening request. Congress did so, the Court of Appeals said, in \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii), which removes jurisdiction to review a decision of the Attorney General \xe2\x80\x9cthe authority for which is specified under this subchapter to be in the discretion of the Attorney General.\xe2\x80\x9d All agree that the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s regulation, 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2(a), places \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he decision to grant or deny a motion to reopen \xe2\x80\xa6 within the discretion of the Board.\xe2\x80\x9d But the statute does not codify that prescription,[Footnote 9] and does not otherwise \xe2\x80\x9cspecif[y]\xe2\x80\x9d that reopening decisions are \xe2\x80\x9cin the discretion of the Attorney General.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 10]

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\n1

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Board\xe2\x80\x99s discretionary authority to act on a motion to reopen, we have thus far explained, is \xe2\x80\x9cspecified\xe2\x80\x9d not in a statute, but only in the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s regulation, which instructs: \xe2\x80\x9cThe decision to grant or deny a motion to reopen \xe2\x80\xa6 is within the discretion of the Board, subject to the restrictions of this section. The Board has discretion to deny a motion to reopen even if the party moving has made out a prima facie case for relief.\xe2\x80\x9d 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2(a). Nevertheless, in defense of the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, amicus urges that regulations suffice to trigger 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii)\xe2\x80\x99s proscription of judicial review.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The jurisdiction-stripping provision, amicus reminds, refers to \xe2\x80\x9cauthority \xe2\x80\xa6 specified under this subchapter.\xe2\x80\x9d As she reads that formulation, the word \xe2\x80\x9cunder\xe2\x80\x9d is key. She comprehends \xe2\x80\x9cunder\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cpursuant to,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9csubordinate to,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cbelow or lower than,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cinferior \xe2\x80\xa6 in rank or importance,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cby reason of the authority of.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Court-Appointed Amicus Curiae in Support of Judgment Below 15, 17 (citing, inter alia, Florida Dept. of Revenue v. Piccadilly Cafeterias, Inc., 554 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 5); Ardestani v. INS, 502 U. S. 129, 135 (1991)). Administrative regulations count for \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B) purposes, she urges, because they are issued \xe2\x80\x9cpursuant to,\xe2\x80\x9d and are measures \xe2\x80\x9csubordinate to,\xe2\x80\x9d the legislation they serve to implement. The parties, on the other hand, read \xe2\x80\x9cspecified under this subchapter\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cspecified in,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cspecified by,\xe2\x80\x9d the subchapter.[Footnote 11]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the reading amicus advances, \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) would bar judicial review of any decision that an executive regulation places within the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s discretion, including the decision to deny a motion to reopen. On the parties\xe2\x80\x99 reading, however, \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) precludes judicial review only when the statute itself specifies the discretionary character of the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s authority.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the parties and amicus recognize, their diverse renderings of \xe2\x80\x9cunder,\xe2\x80\x9d standing alone, do not equip us to resolve this case. The word \xe2\x80\x9cunder\xe2\x80\x9d is chameleon; it \xe2\x80\x9chas many dictionary definitions and must draw its meaning from its context.\xe2\x80\x9d Ardestani, 502 U. S., at 135.[Footnote 12] Examining, in statutory context, the provision in which the word \xe2\x80\x9cunder\xe2\x80\x9d is embedded, we conclude that the parties\xe2\x80\x99 position stands on firmer ground.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 1252(a)(2)(B)(ii), the provision at issue here, is far from the only jurisdictional limitation in IIRIRA. See Dada, 554 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 13) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn reading a statute we must not look merely to a particular clause, but consider in connection with it the whole statute.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Davis v. Michigan Dept. of Treasury, 489 U. S. 803, 809 (1989) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he words of a statute must be read in their context and with a view to their place in the overall statutory scheme.\xe2\x80\x9d). Section 1252(a)(2), titled \xe2\x80\x9cMatters not subject to judicial review,\xe2\x80\x9d lists a variety of agency determinations the federal courts lack jurisdiction to review. Those determinations divide into three categories. The first, \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(A), concerns immigration officers\xe2\x80\x99 determinations whether aliens applying for admission are admissible. Next in statutory order is the provision before us, \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B), which involves denials of discretionary relief. The last category, \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(C), concerns final orders of removal entered against criminal aliens.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(A) and \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(C) depend on statutory provisions, not on any regulation, to define their scope. The latter provision, the criminal alien bar, precludes judicial review of \xe2\x80\x9cany final order of removal against an alien who is removable by reason of having committed a criminal offense covered in\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71182(a)(2), \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(A)(iii), (B), (C), or (D), or certain offenses covered in \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(A)(ii). All the defining references are statutory; none invokes a regulation. The same holds for the admissibility bar in \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(A). Given \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)\xe2\x80\x99s statutory placement, sandwiched between subsections (a)(2)(A) and (a)(2)(C), one would expect that it, too, would cover statutory provisions alone.

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Focusing on \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B), we note the lead line serving to introduce both of the subparagraph\xe2\x80\x99s two clauses: \xe2\x80\x9c[N]o court shall have jurisdiction to review \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Clause (i) then places within the no-judicial-review category \xe2\x80\x9cany judgment regarding the granting of relief under section 1182(h), 1182(i), 1229b, 1229c, or 1255.\xe2\x80\x9d Each of the statutory provisions referenced in clause (i) addresses a different form of discretionary relief from removal, see supra, at 3, n.\xc2\xa02, and each contains language indicating that the decision is entrusted to the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s discretion. See, e.g., \xc2\xa71182(h) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Attorney General may, in his discretion, waive [inadmissibility based on certain criminal offenses].\xe2\x80\x9d). Clause (i) does not refer to any regulatory provision.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To the clause (i) enumeration of administrative judgments that are insulated from judicial review, Congress added in clause (ii) a catchall provision covering \xe2\x80\x9cany other decision \xe2\x80\xa6 the authority for which is specified under this subchapter.\xe2\x80\x9d The proximity of clauses (i) and (ii), and the words linking them\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cany other decision\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94suggests that Congress had in mind decisions of the same genre, i.e., those made discretionary by legislation.[Footnote 13] The clause (i) enumeration, we find, is instructive in determining the meaning of the clause (ii) catchall. Read harmoniously, both clauses convey that Congress barred court review of discretionary decisions only when Congress itself set out the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s discretionary authority in the statute. See Hall Street Associates, L.\xc2\xa0L.\xc2\xa0C. v. Mattel, Inc., 552 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 9) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen a statute sets out a series of specific items ending with a general term, that general term is confined to covering subjects comparable to the specifics it follows.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 14]

\n

4

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We also find significant the character of the decisions Congress enumerated in \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(i), thereby insulating them from judicial review. As the Government explained at oral argument, the determinations there listed are \xe2\x80\x9csubstantive decisions \xe2\x80\xa6 made by the Executive in the immigration context as a matter of grace, things that involve whether aliens can stay in the country or not.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 14.[Footnote 15] They include waivers of inadmissibility based on certain criminal offenses, \xc2\xa71182(h), or based on fraud or misrepresentation, \xc2\xa71182(i); cancellation of removal, \xc2\xa71229b; permission for voluntary departure, \xc2\xa71229c; and adjustment of status, \xc2\xa71255.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Other decisions specified by statute \xe2\x80\x9cto be in the discretion of the Attorney General,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore shielded from court oversight by \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii), are of a like kind. See, e.g., \xc2\xa71157(c)(1) (discretion to admit refugees \xe2\x80\x9cdetermined to be of special humanitarian concern to the United States\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa71181(b) (discretion to waive requirement of documentation for readmission); \xc2\xa71182(a)(3)(D)(iv) (discretion to waive, in certain cases, inadmissibility of aliens who have affiliated with a totalitarian party). Decisions on reopening motions made discretionary by regulation, in contrast, are adjunct rulings: The motion to reopen is a procedural device serving to ensure \xe2\x80\x9cthat aliens [a]re getting a fair chance to have their claims heard.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 17. A court decision reversing the denial of a motion to reopen does not direct the Executive to afford the alien substantive relief; ordinarily, it touches and concerns only the question whether the alien\xe2\x80\x99s claims have been accorded a reasonable hearing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If Congress wanted the jurisdictional bar to encompass decisions specified as discretionary by regulation along with those made discretionary by statute, moreover, Congress could easily have said so. In other provisions enacted simultaneously with \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii), Congress expressed precisely that meaning. See IIRIRA, \xc2\xa7213, 110 Stat. 3009\xe2\x80\x93572 (\xe2\x80\x9cimmigration benefits pursuant to this Act; or the regulations promulgated thereunder\xe2\x80\x9d), codified at 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71324c(e)(2); IIRIRA, \xc2\xa7372, 110 Stat. 3009\xe2\x80\x93646 (\xe2\x80\x9cany of the powers, privileges, or duties conferred or imposed by this Act or regulations issued thereunder\xe2\x80\x9d), codified at 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71103(a)(10). \xe2\x80\x9c[W]here Congress includes particular language in one section of a statute but omits it in another section of the same Act, it is generally presumed that Congress acts intentionally and purposely in the disparate inclusion or exclusion.\xe2\x80\x9d Nken v. Holder, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 11) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The history of the relevant statutory provisions corroborates our determination that \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) does not proscribe judicial review of denials of motions to reopen. Attorney General regulations have long addressed reopening requests. See 6 Fed. Reg. 71\xe2\x80\x9372 (1941). The current regulations, adopted in 1996, 61 Fed. Reg. 18904\xe2\x80\x9318906, derive from rules published in 1958, see 23 Fed. Reg. 9118\xe2\x80\x939119; Dada, 554 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 10).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Enacting IIRIRA in 1996, Congress \xe2\x80\x9ctransform[ed] the motion to reopen from a regulatory procedure to a statutory form of relief available to the alien.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 12). IIRIRA largely codified the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s directions on filing reopening motions. See \xc2\xa71229a(c)(7) (guaranteeing right to file one motion, prescribing contents, and setting deadlines).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the same legislation, Congress amended the INA aggressively to expedite removal of aliens lacking a legal basis to remain in the United States. See Reno v. American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Comm., 525 U. S. 471, 475 (1999). Among IIRIRA\xe2\x80\x99s several proscriptions of judicial review is the one here at issue, \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii), barring review of administrative decisions Congress placed within the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s discretion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress thus simultaneously codified the process for filing motions to reopen and acted to bar judicial review of a number of executive decisions regarding removal. But Congress did not codify the regulation delegating to the BIA discretion to grant or deny motions to reopen. See 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2(a) (reopening may be entertained not only on application; Board \xe2\x80\x9cmay at any time reopen \xe2\x80\xa6 on its own motion any case in which it has rendered a decision\xe2\x80\x9d). Had Congress elected to insulate denials of motions to reopen from judicial review, it could have so specified together with its codification of directions on filing reopening motions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From the Legislature\xe2\x80\x99s silence on the discretion of the Attorney General (or his delegate, the Board) over reopening motions, see supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, n.\xc2\xa010, we take it that Congress left the matter where it was pre-IIRIRA: The BIA has broad discretion, conferred by the Attorney General, \xe2\x80\x9cto grant or deny a motion to reopen,\xe2\x80\x9d 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2(a), but courts retain jurisdiction to review, with due respect, the Board\xe2\x80\x99s decision.[Footnote 16] It is unsurprising that Congress would leave in place judicial oversight of this \xe2\x80\x9cimportant [procedural] safeguard\xe2\x80\x9d designed \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure a proper and lawful disposition\xe2\x80\x9d of immigration proceedings, Dada, 554 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316), where, as here, the alien\xe2\x80\x99s underlying claim (for asylum) would itself be reviewable.[Footnote 17]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the REAL ID Act, Congress further amended the INA. By 2005, two Courts of Appeals had already ruled that 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) did not preclude them from reviewing denials of motions to reopen, see Infanzon v. Ashcroft, 386 F.\xc2\xa03d 1359, 1361\xe2\x80\x931362 (CA10 2004); Medina-Morales v. Ashcroft, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d 520, 528\xe2\x80\x93529 (CA9 2004), and no court had reached a contrary result. Although adding or reformulating provisions on asylum, \xc2\xa7101(a), (b), 119 Stat. 302\xe2\x80\x93303, protection from removal, \xc2\xa7101(c), (d), id., at 303\xe2\x80\x93305, even judicial review, \xc2\xa7106, id., at 310\xe2\x80\x93311, the REAL ID Act did not disturb the unbroken line of decisions upholding court review of administrative denials of motions to reopen. See supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937; supra, at 3, n.\xc2\xa01.[Footnote 18]

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Any lingering doubt about the proper interpretation of 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) would be dispelled by a familiar principle of statutory construction: the presumption favoring judicial review of administrative action. When a statute is \xe2\x80\x9creasonably susceptible to divergent interpretation, we adopt the reading that accords with traditional understandings and basic principles: that executive determinations generally are subject to judicial review.\xe2\x80\x9d Gutierrez de Martinez v. Lamagno, 515 U. S. 417, 434 (1995). We have consistently applied that interpretive guide to legislation regarding immigration, and particularly to questions concerning the preservation of federal-court jurisdiction. See, e.g., INS v. St. Cyr, 533 U. S. 289, 298 (2001); Catholic Social Services, Inc., 509 U. S., at 63\xe2\x80\x9364; McNary, 498 U. S., at 496. Because the \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption favoring interpretations of statutes [to] allow judicial review of administrative action\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cwell-settled,\xe2\x80\x9d Catholic Social Services, Inc., 509 U. S., at 63\xe2\x80\x9364 (quoting McNary, 498 U. S., at 496), the Court assumes that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress legislates with knowledge of\xe2\x80\x9d the presumption, id., at 496. It therefore takes \xe2\x80\x9cclear and convincing evidence\xe2\x80\x9d to dislodge the presumption. Catholic Social Services, Inc., 509 U. S., at 64 (internal quotation marks omitted). There is no such evidence here.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, we stress a paramount factor in the decision we render today. By defining the various jurisdictional bars by reference to other provisions in the INA itself, Congress ensured that it, and only it, would limit the federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 jurisdiction. To read \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) to apply to matters where discretion is conferred on the Board by regulation, rather than on the Attorney General by statute, would ignore that congressional design. If the Seventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s construction of \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii) were to prevail, the Executive would have a free hand to shelter its own decisions from abuse-of-discretion appellate court review simply by issuing a regulation declaring those decisions \xe2\x80\x9cdiscretionary.\xe2\x80\x9d Such an extraordinary delegation of authority cannot be extracted from the statute Congress enacted.

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A statute affecting federal jurisdiction \xe2\x80\x9cmust be construed both with precision and with fidelity to the terms by which Congress has expressed its wishes.\xe2\x80\x9d Cheng Fan Kwok v. INS, 392 U. S. 206, 212 (1968). As we have noted, see supra, at 14, and as amicus emphasizes, \xe2\x80\x9cmany provisions of IIRIRA [we]re aimed at protecting [from court review exercises of] the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d American-Arab Anti-Discrimination Comm., 525 U. S., at 486 (emphasis deleted). But \xe2\x80\x9cno law pursues its purpose at all costs, and \xe2\x80\xa6 the textual limitations upon a law\xe2\x80\x99s scope are no less a part of its \xe2\x80\x98purpose\xe2\x80\x99 than its substantive authorizations.\xe2\x80\x9d Rapanos v. United States, 547 U. S. 715, 752 (2006) (plurality opinion). While Congress pared back judicial review in IIRIRA, it did not delegate to the Executive authority to do so. Action on motions to reopen, made discretionary by the Attorney General only, therefore remain subject to judicial review.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito, concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree that the Court of Appeals had jurisdiction to review the denial of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion to reopen his removal proceeding, but I would decide this case on narrower grounds. The controlling statutory provision, 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252(a)(2)(B)(ii), states that \xe2\x80\x9cno court shall have jurisdiction to review \xe2\x80\xa6 any \xe2\x80\xa6 decision \xe2\x80\xa6 of the Attorney General \xe2\x80\xa6 the authority for which is specified under this subchapter to be in the discretion of the Attorney General.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) The phrase \xe2\x80\x9cunder this subchapter\xe2\x80\x9d refers to Subchapter II of Chapter 12 of Title 8, 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71151\xe2\x80\x931381, see ante, at 3, n.\xc2\xa03, and, as the Court notes, no provision of Subchapter II confers discretionary authority on the Attorney General to decide motions to reopen. See ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, 14\xe2\x80\x9315. The Court of Appeals, however, held that the Attorney General\xe2\x80\x99s decision in this case was unreviewable because a regulation, 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2(a) (2009), made that decision discretionary.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If this regulation had been promulgated pursuant to authority conferred by a provision of Subchapter II, we would have to confront the question that the opinion of the Court addresses. But it seems clear that 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2, at least insofar as it gave the Attorney General the discretionary authority that he exercised in this case, is grounded on authority conferred under Subchapter I of Chapter 12 of Title 8, 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71101\xe2\x80\x931107. See 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71103(a) (1994 ed.) (giving the Attorney General the authority to \xe2\x80\x9cestablish such regulations \xe2\x80\xa6 as he deems necessary for carrying out his authority under [Chapter 12 of Title 8 of the U. S. Code]\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The amicus curiae whom we appointed to defend the decision of the Court of Appeals has attempted to link 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2 to Subchapter II. She notes that the Attorney General, in promulgating that regulation, cited not only 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71103(a), but also a provision of Subchapter II, 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252b (1994 ed.). See 61 Fed. Reg. 18900, 18904 (1996).[Footnote 1] This latter statutory provision[Footnote 2] conferred the authority to reopen a narrow set of deportation orders, i.e., those issued after the alien failed to appear at the deportation hearing. Although this statutory provision does not apply to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s motion to reopen, amicus argues that \xe2\x80\x9cthe section\xe2\x80\x99s brief allusion to motions to reopen clearly presupposed that the Attorney General had in place a more general procedure for reviewing all motions to reopen removal proceedings.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Court-Appointed Amicus Curiae in Support of Judgment Below 41\xe2\x80\x9342.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Amicus\xe2\x80\x99 argument is ingenious but ultimately unpersuasive. At most, 8 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71252b (1994 ed.) may be read as implicitly authorizing the promulgation of a regulation giving the Attorney General the discretion to reopen certain deportation orders that were issued in absentia. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s second motion to reopen, however, seeks reopening on grounds outside of \xc2\xa71252b, and therefore 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.2, insofar as it applies to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s case, was not issued pursuant to Subchapter II and does not implement any provision of that Subchapter.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, this case can and should be decided on the narrow ground that, even if some regulations can render a decision of the Attorney General unreviewable, the regulation at issue in this case does not have that effect.
