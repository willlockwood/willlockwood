SALAZAR, SECRETARY OF THE INTERIOR, et\xc2\xa0al. v. BUONO

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x93472.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 7, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 28, 2010

\n

In 1934, members of the Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW) placed a Latin cross on federal land in the Mojave National Preserve (Preserve) to honor American soldiers who died in World War I. Claiming to be offended by a religious symbol\xe2\x80\x99s presence on federal land, respondent Buono, a regular visitor to the Preserve, filed this suit alleging a violation of the First Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Establishment Clause and seeking an injunction requiring the Government to remove the cross. In the litigation\xe2\x80\x99s first stage (Buono I), the District Court found that Buono had standing to sue and, concluding that the presence of the cross on federal land conveyed an impression of governmental endorsement of religion, see Lemon v. Kurtzman, 403 U. S. 602, 612\xe2\x80\x93613, it granted Buono\xe2\x80\x99s requested injunctive relief (2002 injunction). The District Court did not consider whether the Government\xe2\x80\x99s actions regarding the cross had a secular purpose or caused entanglement with religion. While the Government\xe2\x80\x99s appeal was pending, Congress passed the Department of Defense Appropriations Act, 2004, \xc2\xa78121(a) of which directed the Secretary of the Interior to transfer the cross and the land on which it stands to the VFW in exchange for privately owned land elsewhere in the Preserve (land-transfer statute). Affirming the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment both as to standing and on the merits, the Ninth Circuit declined to address the statute\xe2\x80\x99s effect on Buono\xe2\x80\x99s suit or the statute\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality (Buono II). Because the Government did not seek review by this Court, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment became final. Buono then returned to the District Court seeking injunctive relief against the land transfer, either through enforcement or modification of the 2002 injunction. In 2005, that court rejected the Government\xe2\x80\x99s claim that the transfer was a bona fide attempt to comply with the injunction, concluding, instead, that it was actually an invalid attempt to keep the cross on display. The court granted Buono\xe2\x80\x99s motion to enforce the 2002 injunction; denied as moot his motion to amend it; and permanently enjoined the Government from implementing the land-transfer statute (Buono III). The Ninth Circuit again affirmed, largely following the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is reversed, and the case is remanded.

\n

502 F.\xc2\xa03d 1069 and 527 F.\xc2\xa03d 758, reversed and remanded.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy, joined in full by The Chief Justice and in part by Justice Alito, concluded:

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0Buono has standing to maintain this action. Whatever the validity of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Buono\xe2\x80\x99s asserted injury\xe2\x80\x94offense at a religious symbol\xe2\x80\x99s presence on federal land\xe2\x80\x94is not personal to him and so does not confer Article III standing, that argument is not available at this stage of the litigation. The District Court rejected the argument in Buono I, the Ninth Circuit affirmed in Buono II, and the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment became final and unreviewable upon the expiration of the 90-day deadline for filing a certiorari petition, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72101(c). Moreover, Buono had standing in Buono III to seek application of the injunction against the land-transfer statute. A party that obtains a judgment in its favor acquires a \xe2\x80\x9cjudicially cognizable\xe2\x80\x9d interest in ensuring compliance with that judgment. See Allen v. Wright, 468 U. S. 737. Buono\xe2\x80\x99s entitlement to an injunction having been established in Buono I and II, he sought in Buono III to prevent the Government from frustrating or evading that injunction. His interests in doing so were sufficiently personal and concrete to support his standing, given the rights he obtained under the earlier decree against the same party as to the same cross and the same land. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s contention that Buono sought to extend, rather than to enforce, the 2002 injunction is not an argument about standing, but about the merits of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The District Court erred in enjoining the Government from implementing the land-transfer statute on the premise that the relief was necessary to protect Buono\xe2\x80\x99s rights under the 2002 injunction. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0A court may order an injunction only after taking into account all the circumstances bearing on the need for prospective relief. See, e.g., United States v. Swift & Co., 286 U. S. 106, 114. Here, the District Court did not engage in the appropriate inquiry. The land-transfer statute was a substantial change in circumstances bearing on the propriety of the requested relief. By dismissing as illicit the motives of Congress in passing it, the District Court took insufficient account of the context in which the statute was enacted and the reasons for its passage. Placement of the cross on federal land by private persons was not an attempt to set the state\xe2\x80\x99s imprimatur on a particular creed. Rather, the intent was simply to honor fallen soldiers. Moreover, the cross stood for nearly seven decades before the statute was enacted, by which time the cross and the cause it commemorated had become entwined in the public consciousness. The 2002 injunction thus presented the Government with a dilemma. It could not maintain the cross without violating the injunction, but it could not remove the cross without conveying disrespect for those the cross was seen as honoring. Deeming neither alternative satisfactory, Congress enacted the land-transfer statute. The statute embodied a legislative judgment that this dispute is best resolved through a framework and policy of accommodation. The statute should not have been dismissed as an evasion, for it brought about a change of law and a congressional statement of policy applicable to the case. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Where legislative action undermines the basis for previous relief, the relevant question is whether an ongoing exercise of the court\xe2\x80\x99s equitable authority is supported by the prior showing of illegality, judged against the claim that changed circumstances render prospective relief inappropriate. The District Court granted the 2002 injunction based solely on its conclusion that the presence of the cross on federal land conveyed an impression of governmental endorsement of religion, and the Ninth Circuit affirmed on the same grounds. Neither court considered whether the Government had acted based on an improper purpose. Given this sole reliance on perception, any further relief grounded on the injunction should have rested on the same basis. But the District Court used an injunction granted for one reason (perceived governmental endorsement) as the basis for enjoining conduct that was alleged to be objectionable for a different reason (an illicit governmental purpose). Ordering relief under such circumstances was improper. The court failed to consider whether the change in law and circumstances effected by the land-transfer statute had rendered the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable observer\xe2\x80\x9d standard inappropriate to resolve the dispute. Nor did the court attempt to reassess Buono I\xe2\x80\x99s findings in light of the accommodation policy embraced by Congress. Rather, it concentrated solely on the religious aspects of the cross, divorced from its background and context. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The same respect for a coordinate branch of Government that forbids striking down an Act of Congress except upon a clear showing of unconstitutionality, see, e.g., United States v. Morrison, 529 U. S. 598, 607, requires that a congressional command be given effect unless no legal alternative exists. Even if, contrary to the congressional judgment, the land transfer were thought an insufficient accommodation in light of the earlier endorsement finding, it was incumbent upon the District Court to consider less drastic relief than complete invalidation of the statute. See, e.g., Ayotte v. Planned Parenthood of Northern New Eng., 546 U. S. 320, 329. On remand, that court should conduct a proper inquiry into the continued necessity for injunctive relief in light of the statute. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito concluded that this case should not be remanded for the lower courts to decide whether implementation of the land-transfer statute would violate the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s injunction or the Establishment Clause. Rather, because the factual record has been sufficiently developed to permit resolution of these questions, he would decide them and hold that the statute may be implemented. The case\xe2\x80\x99s singular circumstances presented Congress with a delicate problem. Its solution was an approach designed to eliminate any perception of religious sponsorship stemming from the location of the cross on federally owned land, while avoiding the disturbing symbolism that some would associate with the destruction of this historic monument. The mechanism Congress selected is quite common in the West, a \xe2\x80\x9cland exchange,\xe2\x80\x9d whereby ownership of the land on which the cross is located would be transferred to the VFW in exchange for another nearby parcel of equal value. The land transfer would not violate the District Court injunction, the obvious meaning of which was simply that the Government could not allow the cross to remain on federal land. Nor would the statute\xe2\x80\x99s implementation constitute an endorsement of religion in violation of the Establishment Clause. The so-called \xe2\x80\x9cendorsement test\xe2\x80\x9d views a challenged religious display through the eyes of a hypothetical reasonable observer aware of the history and all other pertinent facts relating to the display. Here, therefore, this observer would be familiar with the monument\xe2\x80\x99s origin and history and thereby appreciate that the transfer represents an effort by Congress to address a unique situation and to find a solution that best accommodates conflicting concerns. Finally, the statute was not enacted for the illicit purpose of embracing the monument\xe2\x80\x99s religious message but to commemorate the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s war dead and to avoid the disturbing symbolism that would have been created by the monument\xe2\x80\x99s destruction. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, joined by Justice Thomas, concluded that this Court need not\xe2\x80\x94indeed, cannot\xe2\x80\x94decide this case\xe2\x80\x99s merits because Buono lacks Article III standing to pursue the relief he seeks, which is not enforcement of the original injunction but expansion of it. By enjoining the Government from implementing the statute at issue, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s 2005 order went well beyond the original injunction\xe2\x80\x99s proscription of the cross\xe2\x80\x99s display on public property. Because Buono seeks new relief, he must show that he has standing to pursue that relief by demonstrating that blocking the land transfer will \xe2\x80\x9credress or prevent an actual or imminently threatened injury to [him] caused by private or official violation of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Summers v. Earth Island Institute, 555 U. S. ___, ___. He has failed, however, to allege any such injury. Even assuming that being offended by a religious display constitutes a cognizable injury, it is merely speculative whether the cross will remain in place, and in any event Buono has made clear, by admitting he has no objection to Christian symbols on private property, that he will not be offended. Neither district courts\xe2\x80\x99 discretion to expand injunctions they have issued nor this District Court\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of its 2005 order as merely enforcing the existing injunction makes any difference. If in fact a court awards new relief, it must have Article III jurisdiction to do so. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x937.

\nOPINION OF KENNEDY, J.
\nSALAZAR V. BUONO
\n559 U. S. ____ (2010)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 08-472

KEN L. SALAZAR, SECRETARY OF THE INTERIOR,\net\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. FRANK BUONO

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[April 28, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy announced the judgment of the Court and delivered an opinion, in which The Chief Justice joins and Justice Alito joins in part.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1934, private citizens placed a Latin cross on a rock outcropping in a remote section of the Mojave Desert. Their purpose and intent was to honor American soldiers who fell in World War I. The original cross deteriorated over time, but a reconstructed one now stands at the same place. It is on federal land.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is asked to consider a challenge, not to the first placement of the cross or its continued presence on federal land, but to a statute that would transfer the cross and the land on which it stands to a private party. Department of Defense Appropriations Act, 2004, Pub. L. 108\xe2\x80\x9387, \xc2\xa78121(a), 117 Stat. 1100. The District Court permanently enjoined the Government from implementing the statute. The Court of Appeals affirmed. We conclude that its judgment was in error.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Mojave National Preserve (Preserve) spans approximately 1.6 million acres in southeastern California. The Preserve is nestled within the Mojave Desert, whose picturesque but rugged territory comprises 25,000 square miles, exceeding in size the combined area of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s five smallest States. See Merriam-Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Geographical Dictionary 755, 1228\xe2\x80\x931230 (3d ed. 1997). Just over 90 percent of the land in the Preserve is federally owned, with the rest owned either by the State of California or by private parties. The National Park Service, a division of the Department of the Interior, administers the Preserve as part of the National Park System. 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7410aaa\xe2\x80\x9341 and 410aaa\xe2\x80\x9346.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sunrise Rock is a granite outcropping located within the Preserve. Sunrise Rock and the area in its immediate vicinity are federal land, but two private ranches are located less than two miles away. The record does not indicate whether fencing is used to mark the boundary of these ranches. In 1934, members of the Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW) mounted a Latin cross on the rock as a memorial to soldiers who died in World War I. A Latin cross consists of two bars\xe2\x80\x94a vertical one and a shorter, horizontal one. The cross has been replaced or repaired at various times over the years, most recently in 1998 by Henry Sandoz. Sandoz is a private citizen who owns land elsewhere in the Preserve, a portion of which he is prepared to transfer to the Government in return for its conveyance to the VFW of the land on which the cross stands, all pursuant to the statute now under review.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The cross, as built by Sandoz, consists of 4-inch diameter metal pipes painted white. The vertical bar is less than eight feet tall. It cannot be seen from the nearest highway, which lies more than 10 miles away. It is visible, however, from Cima Road, a narrow stretch of blacktop that comes within 100 feet of Sunrise Rock.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The cross has been a gathering place for Easter services since it was first put in place; and Sunrise Rock and its immediate area continue to be used as a campsite. At one time the cross was accompanied by wooden signs stating \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The Cross, Erected in Memory of the Dead of All Wars,\xe2\x80\x99 and \xe2\x80\x98Erected 1934 by Members of Veterans of Foregin [sic] Wars, Death Valley post 2884.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Buono v. Kempthorne, 527 F.\xc2\xa03d 758, 769 (CA9 2008). The signs have since disappeared, and the cross now stands unmarked.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Frank Buono, respondent here, is a retired Park Service employee who makes regular visits to the Preserve. Buono claims to be offended by the presence of a religious symbol on federal land. He filed suit in the United States District Court for the Central District of California. He alleged a violation of the Establishment Clause of the First Amendment and sought an injunction requiring the Government to remove the cross.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The litigation proceeded in what can be described as four stages. In the first, the District Court ruled in Buono\xe2\x80\x99s favor on opposing motions for summary judgment. Buono v. Norton, 212 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1202 (CD Cal. 2002) (Buono I). As an initial matter, the court found that Buono had standing to maintain his Establishment Clause challenge. Id., at 1210\xe2\x80\x931214. On the merits, the parties agreed that the dispute should be governed by the so-called Lemon test, which the District Court formulated as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA government religious practice or symbol will survive an Establishment Clause challenge when it (1)\xc2\xa0has a secular purpose, (2) has a primary effect that neither advances nor inhibits religion, and (3) does not foster excessive state entanglement with religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Buono I, supra, at 1214\xe2\x80\x931215 (citing Lemon v. Kurtzman, 403 U. S. 602, 612\xe2\x80\x93613 (1971)).

\n

\nThe court expressly declined to consider whether the Government\xe2\x80\x99s actions regarding the cross had a secular purpose, 212 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1214\xe2\x80\x931215, or whether they caused excessive entanglement with religion, id., at 1217, n.\xc2\xa09. Instead, the court evaluated the primary effect of the cross by asking how it would be viewed by a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable observer.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1216. Concluding that presence of the cross on federal land conveyed an impression of governmental endorsement of religion, the court granted Buono\xe2\x80\x99s request for injunctive relief. The court\xe2\x80\x99s order in Buono I (2002 injunction) permanently forbade the Government \xe2\x80\x9cfrom permitting the display of the Latin cross in the area of Sunrise Rock in the Mojave National Preserve.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 146a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit stayed the 2002 injunction to the extent that it required the cross to be removed or dismantled but did not forbid alternative methods of complying with the order. The Government covered the cross, first with a tarpaulin and later with a plywood box.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On appeal, the judgment of the District Court was affirmed, both as to standing and on the merits of Buono\xe2\x80\x99s Establishment Clause challenge. Buono v. Norton, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d 543 (CA9 2004) (Buono II). Like the District Court, the Court of Appeals did not decide whether the Government\xe2\x80\x99s action, or nonaction, with respect to the cross had been motivated by a secular purpose. Id., at 550. Its ruling was based instead on the conclusion that a reasonable observer would perceive a cross on federal land as governmental endorsement of religion. Id., at 549\xe2\x80\x93550. The Government did not seek review by this Court, so that the judgment of the Court of Appeals in Buono II became final.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0During the relevant proceedings, Congress enacted certain statutes related to the cross:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1) Before Buono I was filed, Congress passed an appropriations bill that included a provision forbidding the use of governmental funds to remove the cross. Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2001, Pub. L. 106\xe2\x80\x93554, \xc2\xa7133, 114 Stat. 2763A\xe2\x80\x93230.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2) While Buono I was pending before the District Court, Congress designated the cross and its adjoining land \xe2\x80\x9cas a national memorial commemorating United States participation in World War I and honoring the American veterans of that war.\xe2\x80\x9d Department of Defense Appropriations Act, 2002, Pub. L. 107\xe2\x80\x93117, \xc2\xa78137(a), 115 Stat. 2278. The Secretary of the Interior was directed to expend up to $10,000 to acquire a replica of the original cross and its memorial plaque and to install the plaque at a suitable nearby location. \xc2\xa78137(c).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(3) Three months after Buono I was decided, Congress again prohibited the spending of governmental funds to remove the cross. Department of Defense Appropriations Act, 2003, Pub. L. 107\xe2\x80\x93248, \xc2\xa78065(b), 116 Stat. 1551.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(4) While the Government\xe2\x80\x99s appeal in Buono II was pending, Congress passed a statute (land-transfer statute) directing the Secretary of the Interior to transfer to the VFW the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the land that had been designated a national memorial. Department of Defense Appropriations Act, 2004, Pub. L. 108\xe2\x80\x9387, \xc2\xa78121(a), 117 Stat. 1100. In exchange, the Government was to receive land elsewhere in the preserve from Henry Sandoz and his wife. Ibid. Any difference in value between the two parcels would be equalized through a cash payment. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa78121(c), (d). The land-transfer statute provided that the property would revert to the Government if not maintained \xe2\x80\x9cas a memorial commemorating United States participation in World War I and honoring the American veterans of that war.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78121(e), 117 Stat. 1100. The statute presents a central issue in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals in Buono II did not address the effect on the suit of a potential land transfer under the statute. The court noted that the transfer might \xe2\x80\x9ctake as long as two years to complete,\xe2\x80\x9d 371 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 545, and that its effect was not yet known, id., at 545\xe2\x80\x93546. The court thus \xe2\x80\x9cexpress[ed] no view as to whether a transfer completed under [the statute] would pass constitutional muster.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 546.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the Court of Appeals affirmed in Buono II, Buono returned to the District Court seeking to prevent the land transfer. He sought injunctive relief against the transfer, either through enforcement or modification of the 2002 injunction. In evaluating his request the trial court described the relevant question as whether the land transfer was a bona fide attempt to comply with the injunction (as the Government claimed), or a sham aimed at keeping the cross in place (as Buono claimed). Buono v. Norton, 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1175, 1178 (CD Cal. 2005) (Buono III). In Buono III, the court did not consider whether the transfer itself was an \xe2\x80\x9cindependent violation of the Establishment Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1182, n.\xc2\xa08. The court nevertheless concluded that the transfer was an attempt by the Government to keep the cross atop Sunrise Rock and so was invalid. The court granted Buono\xe2\x80\x99s motion to enforce the 2002 injunction; denied as moot his motion to amend it; and permanently enjoined the Government from implementing the land-transfer statute. Id., at 1182.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals again affirmed, largely following the reasoning of the District Court. Buono v. Kempthorne, 502 F.\xc2\xa03d 1069 (CA9 2007). The Government\xe2\x80\x99s motion for rehearing en banc was denied over a dissent by Judge O\xe2\x80\x99Scannlain, 527 F.\xc2\xa03d 758, and this Court granted certiorari, 555 U. S. ___ (2009).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before considering the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order on the merits, the first inquiry must be with respect to Buono\xe2\x80\x99s standing to maintain this action. To demonstrate standing, a plaintiff must have \xe2\x80\x9calleged such a personal stake in the outcome of the controversy as to warrant his invocation of federal-court jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Horne v. Flores, 557 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 8) (internal quotation marks omitted). The Government argues that Buono\xe2\x80\x99s asserted injury is not personal to him and so does not confer Article III standing. As noted above, Buono does not find the cross itself objectionable but instead takes offense at the presence of a religious symbol on federal land. Buono does not claim that, as a personal matter, he has been made to feel excluded or coerced, and so, the Government contends, he cannot object to the presence of the cross. Brief for Petitioners 12\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whatever the validity of the objection to Buono\xe2\x80\x99s standing, that argument is not available to the Government at this stage of the litigation. When Buono moved the District Court in Buono I for an injunction requiring the removal of the cross, the Government raised the same standing objections it proffers now. Rejecting the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position, the District Court entered a judgment in Buono\xe2\x80\x99s favor, which the Court of Appeals affirmed in Buono II. The Government did not seek review in this Court. The judgment became final and unreviewable upon the expiration of the 90-day deadline under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72101(c) for filing a petition for certiorari. Toledo Scale Co. v. Computing Scale Co., 261 U. S. 399, 418 (1923); see Missouri v. Jenkins, 495 U. S. 33, 45 (1990) (90-day deadline is \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory and jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d). The Government cannot now contest Buono\xe2\x80\x99s standing to obtain the final judgment in Buono I.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, even though the Court may not reconsider whether Buono had standing to seek the 2002 injunction, it is still necessary to evaluate his standing in Buono III to seek application of the injunction against the land-transfer statute. That measure of relief is embodied in the judgment upon which we granted review.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This was a measure of relief that Buono had standing to seek. A party that obtains a judgment in its favor acquires a \xe2\x80\x9cjudicially cognizable\xe2\x80\x9d interest in ensuring compliance with that judgment. See Allen v. Wright, 468 U. S. 737, 763 (1984) (plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 right to enforce a desegregation decree to which they were parties is \xe2\x80\x9ca personal interest, created by law, in having the State refrain from taking specific actions\xe2\x80\x9d). Having obtained a final judgment granting relief on his claims, Buono had standing to seek its vindication.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government does not deny this proposition as a general matter. Instead, it argues that Buono was not seeking to vindicate\xe2\x80\x94but rather to extend\xe2\x80\x94the 2002 injunction. The first injunction forbade the Government from maintaining the cross on Sunrise Rock; yet in Buono III he sought to preclude the land transfer, a different governmental action. The Government contends that Buono lacked standing to seek this additional relief. Reply Brief for Petitioners 5.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument, however, is properly addressed to the relief granted by the judgment below, not to Buono\xe2\x80\x99s standing to seek that relief. The Government has challenged whether appropriate relief was granted in Buono III in light of the relevant considerations and legal principles, and we shall consider these questions. The standing inquiry, by contrast, turns on the alleged injury that prompted the plaintiff to invoke the court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction in the first place. Buono\xe2\x80\x99s entitlement to an injunction having been established in Buono I and II, he sought in Buono III to prevent the Government from frustrating or evading that injunction. Based on the rights he obtained under the earlier decree\xe2\x80\x94against the same party, regarding the same cross and the same land\xe2\x80\x94his interests in doing so were sufficiently personal and concrete to support his standing. Although Buono also argued that the land transfer should be prohibited as an \xe2\x80\x9cindependent\xe2\x80\x9d Establishment Clause violation, the District Court did not address or order relief on that claim, which is not before us. Buono III, 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1182, n.\xc2\xa08. This is not a case in which a party seeks to import a previous standing determination into a wholly different dispute.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In arguing that Buono sought to extend, rather than to enforce, the 2002 injunction, the Government in essence contends that the injunction did not provide a basis for\nthe District Court to invalidate the land transfer. This is not an argument about standing but about the merits of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order. Those points now must be addressed.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The procedural history of this litigation must be considered to identify the issues now subject to review. The District Court granted the 2002 injunction after concluding that a cross on federal land violated the Establishment Clause. The Government unsuccessfully challenged that conclusion on appeal, and the judgment became final upon completion of direct review. At that point, the judgment \xe2\x80\x9cbecame res judicata to the parties and those in privity with them, not only as to every matter which was offered and received to sustain or defeat the claim or demand, but as to any other admissible matter which might have been offered for that purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Travelers Indemnity Co. v. Bailey, 557 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 14) (internal quotation marks omitted). The Government therefore does not\xe2\x80\x94and could not\xe2\x80\x94ask this Court to reconsider the propriety of the 2002 injunction or the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for granting it.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question now before the Court is whether the District Court properly enjoined the Government from implementing the land-transfer statute. The District Court did not consider whether the statute, in isolation, would have violated the Establishment Clause, and it did not forbid the land transfer as an independent constitutional violation. Buono III, supra, at 1182, n.\xc2\xa08. Rather, the court enjoined compliance with the statute on the premise that the relief was necessary to protect the rights Buono had secured through the 2002 injunction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An injunction is an exercise of a court\xe2\x80\x99s equitable authority, to be ordered only after taking into account all of the circumstances that bear on the need for prospective relief. See United States v. Swift & Co., 286 U. S. 106, 114 (1932). See also Weinberger v. Romero-Barcelo, 456 U. S. 305, 312 (1982); Hecht Co. v. Bowles, 321 U. S. 321, 329 (1944); 11A C. Wright, A. Miller, & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa72942, pp.\xc2\xa039\xe2\x80\x9342 (2d ed. 1995) (hereinafter Wright & Miller). Equitable relief is not granted as a matter of course, see Weinberger, 456 U. S., at 311\xe2\x80\x93312, and a court should be particularly cautious when contemplating relief that implicates public interests, see id., at 312 (\xe2\x80\x9cIn exercising their sound discretion, courts of equity should pay particular regard for the public consequences in employing the extraordinary remedy of injunction\xe2\x80\x9d); Harrisonville v. W. S. Dickey Clay Mfg. Co., 289 U. S. 334, 338 (1933) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhere an important public interest would be prejudiced, the reasons for denying the injunction may be compelling\xe2\x80\x9d). Because injunctive relief \xe2\x80\x9cis drafted in light of what the court believes will be the future course of events, \xe2\x80\xa6 a court must never ignore significant changes in the law or circumstances underlying an injunction lest the decree be turned into an \xe2\x80\x98instrument of wrong.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Wright & Miller \xc2\xa72961, at 393\xe2\x80\x93394 (quoting Swift & Co., supra, at 115).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, the District Court did not engage in the appropriate inquiry. The land-transfer statute was a substantial change in circumstances bearing on the propriety of the requested relief. The court, however, did not acknowledge the statute\xe2\x80\x99s significance. It examined the events that led to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s enactment and found an intent to prevent removal of the cross. Deeming this intent illegitimate, the court concluded that nothing of moment had changed. This was error. Even assuming that the land-transfer statute was an attempt to prevent removal of the cross, it does not follow that an injunction against its implementation was appropriate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By dismissing Congress\xe2\x80\x99s motives as illicit, the District Court took insufficient account of the context in which the statute was enacted and the reasons for its passage. Private citizens put the cross on Sunrise Rock to commemorate American servicemen who had died in World War I. Although certainly a Christian symbol, the cross was not emplaced on Sunrise Rock to promote a Christian message. Cf. County of Allegheny v. American Civil Liberties Union, Greater Pittsburgh Chapter, 492 U. S. 573, 661 (1989) (Kennedy, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he [Establishment] Clause forbids a city to permit the permanent erection of a large Latin cross on the roof of city hall \xe2\x80\xa6 because such an obtrusive year-round religious display would place the government\xe2\x80\x99s weight behind an obvious effort to proselytize on behalf of a particular religion\xe2\x80\x9d). Placement of the cross on Government-owned land was not an attempt to set the imprimatur of the state on a particular creed. Rather, those who erected the cross intended simply to honor our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s fallen soldiers. See Brief for Veterans of Foreign Wars of the United States et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 15 (noting that the plaque accompanying the cross \xe2\x80\x9cwas decorated with VFW decals\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Time also has played its role. The cross had stood on Sunrise Rock for nearly seven decades before the statute was enacted. By then, the cross and the cause it commemorated had become entwined in the public consciousness. See ibid. Members of the public gathered regularly at Sunrise Rock to pay their respects. Rather than let the cross deteriorate, community members repeatedly took it upon themselves to replace it. Congress ultimately designated the cross as a national memorial, ranking it among those monuments honoring the noble sacrifices that constitute our national heritage. See note following 16 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7431 (listing officially designated national memorials, including the National D-Day Memorial and the Vietnam Veterans Memorial). Research discloses no other national memorial honoring American soldiers\xe2\x80\x94more than 300,000 of them\xe2\x80\x94who were killed or wounded in World War I. See generally A. Leland & M. Oboroceanu, Congressional Research Service Report for Congress, American War and Military Operations Casualties: Lists and Statistics 2 (2009). It is reasonable to interpret the congressional designation as giving recognition to the historical meaning that the cross had attained. Cf. Van Orden v. Perry, 545 U. S. 677, 702\xe2\x80\x93703 (2005) (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9c40 years\xe2\x80\x9d without legal challenge to a Ten Commandments display \xe2\x80\x9csuggest that the public visiting the [surrounding] grounds has considered the religious aspect of the tablets\xe2\x80\x99 message as part of what is a broader moral and historical message reflective of a cultural heritage\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The 2002 injunction thus presented the Government with a dilemma. It could not maintain the cross without violating the injunction, but it could not remove the cross without conveying disrespect for those the cross was seen as honoring. Cf. id., at 704 (to invalidate a longstanding Ten Commandments display might \xe2\x80\x9ccreate the very kind of religiously based divisiveness that the Establishment Clause seeks to avoid\xe2\x80\x9d). Deeming neither alternative to be satisfactory, Congress enacted the statute here at issue. Congress, of course, may not use its legislative powers to reopen final judgments. See Plaut v. Spendthrift Farm, Inc., 514 U. S. 211, 225\xe2\x80\x93226 (1995). That principle, however, was not a bar to this statute. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s right to transfer the land was not adjudicated in Buono I or compromised by the 2002 injunction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In belittling the Government\xe2\x80\x99s efforts as an attempt to \xe2\x80\x9cevade\xe2\x80\x9d the injunction, Buono III, 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1182, the District Court had things backwards. Congress\xe2\x80\x99s prerogative to balance opposing interests and its institutional competence to do so provide one of the principal reasons for deference to its policy determinations. See Patsy v. Board of Regents of Fla., 457 U. S. 496, 513 (1982). Here, Congress adopted a policy with respect to land it now owns in order to resolve a specific controversy. Congress, the Executive, and the Judiciary all have a duty to support and defend the Constitution. See United States v. Nixon, 418 U. S. 683, 703 (1974) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn the performance of assigned constitutional duties each branch of the Government must initially interpret the Constitution, and the interpretation of its powers by any branch is due great respect from the others\xe2\x80\x9d). The land-transfer statute embodies Congress\xe2\x80\x99s legislative judgment that this dispute is best resolved through a framework and policy of accommodation for a symbol that, while challenged under the Establishment Clause, has complex meaning beyond the expression of religious views. That judgment should not have been dismissed as an evasion, for the statute brought about a change of law and a congressional statement of policy applicable to the case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Buono maintains that any governmental interest in keeping the cross up must cede to the constitutional concerns on which the 2002 injunction was based. He argues that the land transfer would be \xe2\x80\x9can incomplete remedy\xe2\x80\x9d to the constitutional violation underlying the injunction and that the transfer would make achieving a proper remedy more difficult. Brief for Respondent 54.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A court must find prospective relief that fits the remedy to the wrong or injury that has been established. See Swift & Co., 286 U. S., at 114 (\xe2\x80\x9cA continuing decree of injunction directed to events to come is subject always to adaptation as events may shape the need\xe2\x80\x9d). See also United States v. United Shoe Machinery Corp., 391 U. S. 244, 249 (1968). Where legislative action has undermined the basis upon which relief has previously been granted, a court must consider whether the original finding of wrongdoing continues to justify the court\xe2\x80\x99s intervention. See Railway Employees v. Wright, 364 U. S. 642, 648\xe2\x80\x93649 (1961); Pennsylvania v. Wheeling & Belmont Bridge Co., 18 How. 421, 430\xe2\x80\x93432 (1856). The relevant question is whether an ongoing exercise of the court\xe2\x80\x99s equitable authority is supported by the prior showing of illegality, judged against the claim that changed circumstances have rendered prospective relief inappropriate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court granted the 2002 injunction based solely on its conclusion that presence of the cross on federal land conveyed an impression of governmental endorsement of religion. The court expressly disavowed any inquiry into whether the Government\xe2\x80\x99s actions had a secular purpose or caused excessive entanglement. Buono I, 212 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1215, 1217, n. 9. The Court of Appeals affirmed the injunction on the same grounds, similarly eschewing any scrutiny of governmental purpose. Buono II, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 550.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although, for purposes of the opinion, the propriety of the 2002 injunction may be assumed, the following discussion should not be read to suggest this Court\xe2\x80\x99s agreement with that judgment, some aspects of which may be questionable. The goal of avoiding governmental endorsement does not require eradication of all religious symbols in the public realm. A cross by the side of a public highway marking, for instance, the place where a state trooper perished need not be taken as a statement of governmental support for sectarian beliefs. The Constitution does not oblige government to avoid any public acknowledgment of religion\xe2\x80\x99s role in society. See Lee v. Weisman, 505 U. S. 577, 598 (1992) (\xe2\x80\x9cA relentless and all-pervasive attempt to exclude religion from every aspect of public life could itself become inconsistent with the Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Corporation of Presiding Bishop of Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints v. Amos, 483 U. S. 327, 334 (1987) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis Court has long recognized that the government may (and sometimes must) accommodate religious practices and that it may do so without violating the Establishment Clause\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Rather, it leaves room to accommodate divergent values within a constitutionally permissible framework.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Even assuming the propriety of the original relief, however, the question before the District Court in Buono III was whether to invalidate the land transfer. Given the sole reliance on perception as a basis for the 2002 injunction, one would expect that any relief grounded on that decree would have rested on the same basis. But the District Court enjoined the land transfer on an entirely different basis: its suspicion of an illicit governmental purpose. See Buono III, 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1182. The court made no inquiry into the effect that knowledge of the transfer of the land to private ownership would have had on any perceived governmental endorsement of religion, the harm to which the 2002 injunction was addressed. The District Court thus used an injunction granted for one reason as the basis for enjoining conduct that was alleged to be objectionable for a different reason. Ordering relief under such circumstances was improper\xe2\x80\x94absent a finding that the relief was necessary to address an independent wrong. See ibid., n.\xc2\xa08 (noting that the court \xe2\x80\x9cneed not consider [Buono\xe2\x80\x99s] other contention that the land transfer itself is an independent violation of the Establishment Clause\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court should have evaluated Buono\xe2\x80\x99s modification request in light of the objectives of the 2002 injunction. The injunction was issued to address the impression conveyed by the cross on federal, not private, land. Even if its purpose were characterized more generally as avoiding the perception of governmental endorsement, that purpose would favor\xe2\x80\x94or at least not oppose\xe2\x80\x94ownership of the cross by a private party rather than by the Government. Cf. Pleasant Grove City v. Summum, 555 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 8) (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]ersons who observe donated monuments routinely\xe2\x80\x94and reasonably\xe2\x80\x94interpret them as conveying some message on the property owner\xe2\x80\x99s behalf\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Buono argues that the cross would continue to stand on Sunrise Rock, which has no visual differentiation from the rest of the primarily federally owned Preserve. He also points to the reversionary clause in the land-transfer statute requiring that the land be returned to the Government if not maintained as a World War I memorial. Finally, he notes that the cross remains designated a national memorial by an Act of Congress, which arguably would prevent the VFW from dismantling the cross even if it wanted to do so. Brief for Respondent 37\xe2\x80\x9348.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court failed to consider whether, in light of the change in law and circumstances effected by the land-transfer statute, the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable observer\xe2\x80\x9d standard continued to be the appropriate framework through which to consider the Establishment Clause concerns invoked to justify the requested relief. As a general matter, courts considering Establishment Clause challenges do not inquire into \xe2\x80\x9creasonable observer\xe2\x80\x9d perceptions with respect to objects on private land. Even if, however, this standard were the appropriate one, but see County of Allegheny, 492 U. S., at 668 (Kennedy, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part) (criticizing the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable observer\xe2\x80\x9d test); Capitol Square Review and Advisory Bd. v. Pinette, 515 U. S. 753 763\xe2\x80\x93768 (1995) (plurality opinion) (criticizing reliance on \xe2\x80\x9cperceived endorsement\xe2\x80\x9d), it is not clear that Buono\xe2\x80\x99s claim is meritorious. That test requires the hypothetical construct of an objective observer who knows all of the pertinent facts and circumstances surrounding the symbol and its placement. See id., at 780 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). But see id., at 767\xe2\x80\x93768 (plurality opinion) (doubting the workability of the reasonable observer test). Applying this test here, the message conveyed by the cross would be assessed in the context of all relevant factors. See Van Orden, 545 U. S., at 700 (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment) (the Establishment Clause inquiry \xe2\x80\x9cmust take account of context and consequences\xe2\x80\x9d); Lee, supra, at 597 (\xe2\x80\x9cOur Establishment Clause jurisprudence remains a delicate and fact-sensitive one\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court did not attempt to reassess the findings in Buono I in light of the policy of accommodation that Congress had embraced. Rather, the District Court concentrated solely on the religious aspects of the cross, divorced from its background and context. But a Latin cross is not merely a reaffirmation of Christian beliefs. It is a symbol often used to honor and respect those whose heroic acts, noble contributions, and patient striving help secure an honored place in history for this Nation and its people. Here, one Latin cross in the desert evokes far more than religion. It evokes thousands of small crosses in foreign fields marking the graves of Americans who fell in battles, battles whose tragedies are compounded if the fallen are forgotten.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respect for a coordinate branch of Government forbids striking down an Act of Congress except upon a clear showing of unconstitutionality. See United States v. Morrison, 529 U. S. 598, 607 (2000); El Paso & Northeastern R. Co. v. Gutierrez, 215 U. S. 87, 96 (1909). The same respect requires that a congressional command be given effect unless no legal alternative exists. Even if, contrary to the congressional judgment, the land transfer were thought an insufficient accommodation in light of the earlier finding of religious endorsement, it was incumbent upon the District Court to consider less drastic relief than complete invalidation of the land-transfer statute. See Ayotte v. Planned Parenthood of Northern New Eng., 546 U. S. 320, 329 (2006) (in granting relief, \xe2\x80\x9cwe try not to nullify more of a legislature\xe2\x80\x99s work than is necessary, for we know that [a] ruling of unconstitutionality frustrates the intent of the elected representatives of the people\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted; alteration in original)); Alaska Airlines, Inc. v. Brock, 480 U. S. 678, 684 (1987). For instance, if there is to be a conveyance, the question might arise regarding the necessity of further action, such as signs to indicate the VFW\xe2\x80\x99s ownership of the land. As we have noted, Congress directed the Secretary of the Interior to install near the cross a replica of its original memorial plaque. One of the signs that appears in early photographs of the cross specifically identifies the VFW as the group that erected it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Noting the possibility of specific remedies, however, is not an indication of agreement about the continued necessity for injunctive relief. The land-transfer statute\xe2\x80\x99s bearing on this dispute must first be determined. To date, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisprudence in this area has refrained from making sweeping pronouncements, and this case is ill suited for announcing categorical rules. In light of the finding of unconstitutionality in Buono I, and the highly fact-specific nature of the inquiry, it is best left to the District Court to undertake the analysis in the first instance. On remand, if Buono continues to challenge implementation of the statute, the District Court should conduct a proper inquiry as described above.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings.

\n

It is so ordered.

KEN L. SALAZAR, SECRETARY OF THE INTERIOR,\net\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. FRANK BUONO

\n

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito, concurring in part and concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join Justice Kennedy\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in all respects but one: I would not remand this case for the lower courts to decide whether implementation of the land-transfer statute enacted by Congress in 2003, Department of Defense Appropriations Act, 2004, \xc2\xa78121, would violate the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s injunction or the Establishment Clause. The factual record has been sufficiently developed to permit resolution of these questions, and I would therefore decide them and hold that the statute may be implemented.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The singular circumstances surrounding the monument on Sunrise Rock presented Congress with a delicate problem, and the solution that Congress devised is true to the spirit of practical accommodation that has made the United States a Nation of unparalleled pluralism and religious tolerance. In brief, the situation that Congress faced was as follows.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After service in the First World War, a group of veterans moved to the Mojave Desert, in some cases for health reasons.[Footnote 1] They joined the Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW), Death Valley Post 2884, and in 1934, they raised a simple white cross on an outcropping called Sunrise Rock to honor fallen American soldiers.[Footnote 2] These veterans selected Sunrise Rock \xe2\x80\x9cin part because they believed there was a color shading on the Rock in the shape of an American soldier or \xe2\x80\x98doughboy.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One of these men was John Riley Bembry, a miner who had served as a medic and had thus presumably witnessed the carnage of the war firsthand.[Footnote 4] It is said that Mr. Bembry was not a particularly religious man, but he nevertheless agreed to look after the cross and did so for some years.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sunrise Rock monument was located on land belonging to the Federal Government, but in this part of the country, where much of the land is federally owned, boundaries between Government and private land are often not marked,[Footnote 6] and private citizens are permitted to go on and to use federal land for a variety of purposes.[Footnote 7] Although Sunrise Rock was federally owned, Mr. Bembry and his fellow veterans took it upon themselves to place their monument on that spot, apparently without obtaining approval from any federal officials, and this use of federal land seems to have gone largely unnoticed for many years, in all likelihood due to the spot\xe2\x80\x99s remote and rugged location.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sunrise Rock is situated far from any major population center; temperatures often exceed 100 degrees Fahrenheit in the summer; and visitors are warned of the dangers of traveling in the area.[Footnote 8] As a result, at least until this litigation, it is likely that the cross was seen by more rattlesnakes than humans.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Those humans who made the trip to see the monument appear to have viewed it as conveying at least two significantly different messages. See Pleasant Grove City, Utah v. Summum, 555 U. S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2009) (slip op., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe meaning conveyed by a monument is generally not a simple one,\xe2\x80\x9d and a monument may be \xe2\x80\x9cinterpreted by different observers, in a variety of ways\xe2\x80\x9d). The cross is of course the preeminent symbol of Christianity, and Easter services have long been held on Sunrise Rock, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d 543, 548 (CA9 2004). But, as noted, the original reason for the placement of the cross was to commemorate American war dead and, particularly for those with searing memories of The Great War, the symbol that was selected, a plain unadorned white cross, no doubt evoked the unforgettable image of the white crosses, row on row, that marked the final resting places of so many American soldiers who fell in that conflict.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is roughly how things stood until the plaintiff in this case, an employee of the National Park Service who sometimes viewed the cross during the performance of his duties and claims to have been offended by its presence on federally owned land, brought this suit and obtained an injunction restraining the Federal Government from \xe2\x80\x9cpermitting the display of the Latin cross in the area of Sunrise Rock.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 146a. After the Ninth Circuit affirmed that decision, and the Government elected not to seek review by this Court, Congress faced a problem.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If Congress had done nothing, the Government would have been required to take down the cross, which had stood on Sunrise Rock for nearly 70 years, and this removal would have been viewed by many as a sign of disrespect for the brave soldiers whom the cross was meant to honor. The demolition of this venerable if unsophisticated, monument would also have been interpreted by some as an arresting symbol of a Government that is not neutral but hostile on matters of religion and is bent on eliminating from all public places and symbols any trace of our country\xe2\x80\x99s religious heritage. Cf. Van Orden v. Perry, 545 U. S. 677, 704 (2005) (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One possible solution would have been to supplement the monument on Sunrise Rock so that it appropriately recognized the religious diversity of the American soldiers who gave their lives in the First World War. In American military cemeteries overseas, the graves of soldiers who perished in that war were marked with either a white cross or a white Star of David.[Footnote 9] More than 3,500 Jewish soldiers gave their lives for the United States in World War I,[Footnote 10] and Congress might have chosen to place a Star of David on Sunrise Rock so that the monument would duplicate those two types of headstones. But Congress may well have thought\xe2\x80\x94not without reason\xe2\x80\x94that the addition of yet another religious symbol would have been unlikely to satisfy the plaintiff, his attorneys, or the lower courts that had found the existing monument to be uncon-stitutional on the ground that it impermissibly endorsed religion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress chose an alternative approach that was designed to eliminate any perception of religious sponsorship stemming from the location of the cross on federally owned land, while at the same time avoiding the disturbing symbolism associated with the destruction of the historic monument. The mechanism that Congress selected is one that is quite common in the West, a \xe2\x80\x9cland exchange.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 11] Congress enacted a law under which ownership of the parcel of land on which Sunrise Rock is located would be transferred to the VFW in exchange for another nearby parcel of equal value. Congress required that the Sunrise Rock parcel be used for a war memorial, \xc2\xa78121(a), 117 Stat. 1100, but Congress did not prevent the VFW from supplementing the existing monument or replacing it with a war memorial of a different design. Although Justice Stevens characterizes this land exchange as one that endorses \xe2\x80\x9ca particular religious view,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 26 (dissenting opinion), it is noteworthy that Congress, in which our country\xe2\x80\x99s religious diversity is well represented, passed this law by overwhelming majorities: 95\xe2\x80\x930 in the Senate and 407\xe2\x80\x9315 in the House. See 149 Cong. Rec. H8793 (Sept. 24, 2003); id., at S11943 (Sept. 25, 2003). In my view, there is no legal ground for blocking the implementation of this law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent contends that the land transfer would violate the District Court injunction, but that argument, for the reasons explained in Justice Scalia\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, see ante, at 2 (concurring in judgment), is plainly unsound. The obvious meaning of the injunction was simply that the Government could not allow the cross to remain on federal land.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is also no merit in Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 contention that implementation of the statute would constitute an endorsement of Christianity and would thus violate the Establishment Clause. Assuming that it is appropriate to apply the so-called \xe2\x80\x9cendorsement test,\xe2\x80\x9d this test would not be violated by the land exchange. The endorsement test views a challenged display through the eyes of a hypothetical reasonable observer who is deemed to be aware of the history and all other pertinent facts relating to a challenged display. See ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317 (opinion of Kennedy, J.). Here, therefore, this observer would be familiar with the origin and history of the monument and would also know both that the land on which the monument is located is privately owned and that the new owner is under no obligation to preserve the monument\xe2\x80\x99s present design. With this knowledge, a reasonable observer would not view the land exchange as the equivalent of the construction of an official World War I memorial on the National Mall. Cf. post, at 26. Rather, a well-informed observer would appreciate that the transfer represents an effort by Congress to address a unique situation and\nto find a solution that best accommodates conflicting concerns.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, I reject Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 suggestion that the enactment of the land-transfer law was motivated by an illicit purpose. Id. at 24. I would not be \xe2\x80\x9cso dismissive of Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens United v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 70) (Stevens, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). Congress has shown notable solicitude for the rights of religious minorities. See, e.g., Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000bb et seq.; Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act of 2000, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc et seq. I would not jump to the conclusion that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 aim in enacting the land-transfer law was to embrace the religious message of the cross; rather, I see no reason to doubt that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 consistent goal, in legislating with regard to the Sunrise Rock monument, has been to commemorate our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s war dead and to avoid the disturbing symbolism that would have been created by the destruction of the monument.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, I would reverse the decision below and remand with instructions to vacate the order prohibiting the implementation of the land-transfer statute.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the plurality that the Court of Appeals erred in affirming the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order enjoining the transfer of the memorial to the Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW). My reason, however, is quite different: In my view we need not\xe2\x80\x94indeed, cannot\xe2\x80\x94decide the merits of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 dispute, because Frank Buono lacks Article III standing to pursue the relief he seeks. The District Court had no power to award the requested relief, and our authority is limited to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98announcing the fact and dismissing the cause.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U. S. 83, 94 (1998) (quoting Ex parte McCardle, 7 Wall. 506, 514 (1869)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality is correct that Buono\xe2\x80\x99s standing to obtain the original injunction is not before us. See ante, at 7.[Footnote 1] Nor is Buono\xe2\x80\x99s standing to request enforcement of the original injunction at issue. If he sought only to compel compliance with the existing order, Article III would not stand in his way.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the plurality all but admits, however, the relief Buono requests and the District Court awarded in this proceeding is not enforcement of the original injunction but expansion of it. See ante, at 15. The only reasonable reading of the original injunction, in context, is that it proscribed the cross\xe2\x80\x99s display on federal land. Buono\xe2\x80\x99s alleged injuries arose from the cross\xe2\x80\x99s presence on public property, see App. 50, 59, and the injunction accordingly prohibited the Government, its \xe2\x80\x9cemployees, agents, and those in active concert with [them] \xe2\x80\xa6 from permitting the display of the Latin cross in the area of Sunrise Rock in the Mojave National Preserve.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 146a. Barring the Government from \xe2\x80\x9cpermitting\xe2\x80\x9d the cross\xe2\x80\x99s display at a particular location makes sense only if the Government owns the location. As the proprietor, it can remove the cross that private parties have erected and deny permission to erect another. But if the land is privately owned, the Government can prevent the cross\xe2\x80\x99s display only by making it illegal. Prohibitory legislation does not consist of a mere refusal to \xe2\x80\x9cpermi[t],\xe2\x80\x9d nor is the enactment of legislation what the injunction commanded (a command that would raise serious First Amendment and separation-of-powers questions).[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s 2005 order purporting to \xe2\x80\x9cenforce\xe2\x80\x9d the earlier injunction went well beyond barring the display of the cross on public property. Id., at 98a. At Buono\xe2\x80\x99s request, the court enjoined certain Government officials and \xe2\x80\x9canyone acting in concert with them\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0from implementing the provisions of Section 8121 of Public Law 108\xe2\x80\x9387,\xe2\x80\x9d the statutory provision enacted after the original injunction that directs the Executive Branch to transfer the memorial to the VFW. Id., at 99a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because Buono seeks new relief, he must show (and the District Court should have ensured) that he has standing to pursue it. As the party invoking federal-court jurisdiction, Buono \xe2\x80\x9cbears the burden of showing that he has standing for each type of relief sought,\xe2\x80\x9d Summers v. Earth Island Institute, 555 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 4); see Los Angeles v. Lyons, 461 U. S. 95, 105 (1983). A plaintiff cannot sidestep Article III\xe2\x80\x99s requirements by combining a request for injunctive relief for which he has standing with a request for injunctive relief for which he lacks standing. And for the same reason, a plaintiff cannot ask a court to expand an existing injunction unless he has standing to seek the additional relief.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Buono must therefore demonstrate that the additional relief he sought\xe2\x80\x94blocking the transfer of the memorial to a private party\xe2\x80\x94will \xe2\x80\x9credress or prevent actual or imminently threatened injury to [him] caused by private or official violation of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Summers, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 4). He has failed, however, to allege any actual or imminent injury. To begin with, the predicate for any injury he might assert\xe2\x80\x94that the VFW, after taking possession of the land, will continue to display the cross\xe2\x80\x94is at this stage merely speculative.[Footnote 3] Nothing in the statutes compels the VFW (or any future proprietor) to keep it up. The land reverts back to the Government only if \xe2\x80\x9cthe conveyed property is no longer being maintained as a war memorial,\xe2\x80\x9d Pub. L. 108\xe2\x80\x9387, \xc2\xa78121(e), 117 Stat. 1100, which does not depend on whether the cross remains.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, Buono has not alleged, much less established, that he will be harmed if the VFW does decide to keep the cross. To the contrary, his amended complaint averred that \xe2\x80\x9che is deeply offended by the display of a Latin Cross on government-owned property\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9chas no objection to Christian symbols on private property.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 50. In a subsequent deposition he agreed with the statement that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he only thing that\xe2\x80\x99s offensive about this cross is that [he has] discovered that it\xe2\x80\x99s located on federal land.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 85. And in a signed declaration several months later, he reiterated that although the \xe2\x80\x9cpresence of the cross on federally owned land in the Preserve deeply offends [him] and impairs [his] enjoyment of the Preserve,\xe2\x80\x9d he \xe2\x80\x9cha[s] no objection to Christian symbols on private property.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 64\xe2\x80\x9365. In short, even assuming that being \xe2\x80\x9cdeeply offended\xe2\x80\x9d by a religious display (and taking steps to avoid seeing it) constitutes a cognizable injury, Buono has made clear that he will not be offended.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These same considerations bear upon the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that Buono has standing to \xe2\x80\x9cprevent the Government from frustrating or evading\xe2\x80\x9d the original injunction, ante, at 8. If this refers to frustration or evasion in a narrow sense, the injunction is in no need of\xe2\x80\x94indeed, is insusceptible of\xe2\x80\x94protection. It was issued to remedy the sole complaint that Buono had brought forward: erection of a cross on public land. And it was entirely effective in remedying that complaint, having induced Congress to abandon public ownership of the land. If meant in this narrow sense, the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of a need to prevent frustration or evasion by the Government ignores the reality that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s 2005 order awarded new relief beyond the scope of the original injunction. The revised injunction is directed at Buono\xe2\x80\x99s new complaint that the manner of abandoning public ownership and the nature of the new private ownership violate the Establishment Clause. Now it may be that a court has subject-matter jurisdiction to prevent frustration or evasion of its prior injunction in a broader sense\xe2\x80\x94that is, to eliminate an unconstitutional manner of satisfying that prior injunction. But it surely cannot do so unless it has before it someone who has standing to complain of that unconstitutional manner. If preventing frustration or evasion of an injunction includes expanding it to cover additional actions that produce no concrete harm to the original plaintiff, our standing law in this area will make no sense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is no answer that a district court has discretion to expand an injunction it has issued if it finds the existing terms are not fulfilling the original purpose. Doubtless it can do that, and is in that sense the master of its own injunctions. But whether the District Court abused that discretion by enlarging the injunction is beside the point. What matters is that it granted relief beyond the existing order, and that Buono must have had standing to seek the extension.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It also makes no difference that the District Court said it was merely enforcing its original injunction. The question is whether in fact the new order goes beyond the old one. If so, the court must satisfy itself of jurisdiction to award the additional relief\xe2\x80\x94which includes making certain the plaintiff has standing. See Steel Co., 523 U. S., at 94. That is true whether the court revisits the injunction at a party\xe2\x80\x99s request or on its own initiative; Article III\xe2\x80\x99s case-or-controversy requirement is not merely a prerequisite to relief, but a restraint on judicial power. See Summers, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 4).[Footnote 6]

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Keeping within the bounds of our constitutional authority often comes at a cost. Here, the litigants have lost considerable time and money disputing the merits, and we are forced to forgo an opportunity to clarify the law. But adhering to Article III\xe2\x80\x99s limits upon our jurisdiction respects the authority of those whom the people have chosen to make and carry out the laws. In this case Congress has determined that transferring the memorial to private hands best serves the public interest and complies with the Constitution, and the Executive defends that decision and seeks to carry it out. Federal courts have no warrant to revisit that decision\xe2\x80\x94and to risk replacing the people\xe2\x80\x99s judgment with their own\xe2\x80\x94unless and until a proper case has been brought before them. This is not it.

Subsequently, Government authorities covered the cross with a plywood box so that it could not be seen. Congress then enacted a statute directing the Secretary of the Interior to convey to a private entity approximately one acre of land upon which the cross stood, presumably so that the cross could be displayed uncovered. The plaintiff, returning to the District Court, asked that court to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98hold that the transfer violates the \xe2\x80\xa6 injunction.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Buono v. Norton, 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1175, 1177 (CD Cal. 2005) (quoting plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s motion). The court held that it did. Id., at 1182. And the question before us is whether the law permits the District Court so to interpret its injunction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To answer this question we need not address any significant issue of Establishment Clause law. Because the Government has already lost the case, taken an appeal, and lost the appeal, we must take as a given the lower court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of the Establishment Clause question before the land transfer. That is to say, as the plurality points out, ante, at 9, we must here assume that the original display of the cross violated the Constitution because \xe2\x80\x9cthe presence of the cross on federal land conveys a message\xe2\x80\x9d to a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable observer\xe2\x80\x9d of governmental \xe2\x80\x9cendorsement of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Buono v. Norton, 212 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1202, 1216\xe2\x80\x931217 (CD Cal. 2002). See Travelers Indemnity Co. v. Bailey, 557 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 14) (once orders become final on direct review, they are res judicata to the parties). For the same reason, we must here assume that the plaintiff originally had standing to bring the lawsuit. Ante, at 7; see also ante, at 1 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment); Travelers, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 14) (orders are no less preclusive when the collateral attack is jurisdictional). And, as the plurality also points out, the plaintiff consequently has standing now to seek enforcement of the injunction. Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939; see also Allen v. Wright, 468 U. S. 737, 763 (1984).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, we are not faced with the question whether changed circumstances require modification of the injunction. The Government did not ask the District Court to modify it. In fact, the Government did not ask the District Court for any relief at all. Rather, it was the plaintiff who asked the District Court either (1) to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98hold that the [land] transfer violates the current injunction,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or (2) to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98modify that injunction to prohibit the land transfer because it violates the Establishment Clause.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1177 (quoting plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s motion). The District Court did the former, i.e., it interpreted the injunction as prohibiting the Government from transferring the land for purposes of displaying the cross. And having granted the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s request to enforce the injunction, it dismissed the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s alternative request to modify the injunction as moot.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, as I said at the outset, the only question before us is whether the law permits the District Court to hold that the land transfer (presumably along with the subsequent public display of the cross) falls within the scope of its original injunctive order, an order that says the Government must not \xe2\x80\x9cpermi[t] the display of the Latin cross in the area of Sunrise Rock in the Mojave National Preserve.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 39. In my view the law authorizes the District Court to do so.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The legal principles that answer the question presented are found not in the Constitution but in cases that concern the law of injunctions. First, the law of injunctions grants a district court considerable leeway to interpret the meaning and application of its own injunctive order. Members of this Court have written that the \xe2\x80\x9cconstruction given to\xe2\x80\x9d an \xe2\x80\x9cinjunction by the issuing judge \xe2\x80\xa6 is entitled to great weight.\xe2\x80\x9d Madsen v. Women\xe2\x80\x99s Health Center, Inc., 512 U. S. 753, 795 (1994) (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part). And the Courts of Appeals have consistently held that district courts have considerable discretion in interpreting and applying their own injunctive decrees. See, e.g., JTH Tax, Inc. v. H\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0R Block Eastern Tax Servs., Inc., 359 F.\xc2\xa03d 699, 705 (CA4 2004); Alabama Nursing Home Assn v. Harris, 617 F.\xc2\xa02d 385, 388 (CA5 1980). This principle is longstanding and well established, as reflected in a prominent treatise writer\xe2\x80\x99s summary of the case law: \xe2\x80\x9cThe court granting the injunction is necessarily invested with large discretion in enforcing obedience to its mandate, and \xe2\x80\xa6 courts of appellate powers are exceedingly averse to interfering with the exercise of such judgment and discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 J. High, Law of Injunctions \xc2\xa71458, pp. 1467\xe2\x80\x931468 (4th ed. 1905).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, a court should construe the scope of an injunction in light of its purpose and history, in other words, \xe2\x80\x9cwhat the decree was really designed to accomplish.\xe2\x80\x9d Mayor of Vicksburg v. Henson, 231 U. S. 259, 273 (1913). Courts have long looked to \xe2\x80\x9cthe objects for which the [injunctive] relief was granted, as well as the circumstances attending it,\xe2\x80\x9d in deciding whether an enjoined party has complied with an injunction. 2 High, supra, \xc2\xa71446, at 1455, and n.\xc2\xa068 (citing cases); see also John B. Stetson Co. v. Stephen L. Stetson Co., 128 F.\xc2\xa02d 981, 983 (CA2 1942). And they have long refused to \xe2\x80\x9cpermit defendants to evade responsibility for violating an injunction, by doing through subterfuge a thing which is not in terms a violation, yet produces the same effect by accomplishing substantially that which they were enjoined from doing.\xe2\x80\x9d Stodder v. Rosen Talking Mach. Co., 247 Mass. 60, 68, 141 N.\xc2\xa0E. 569, 571 (1923).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These two principles adequately support the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation and application of the injunctive language at issue here. As an initial matter, the plain text of the injunction is reasonably read to prohibit the transfer. Right now, the cross is covered with a plywood box; after the transfer, the box will be removed and the cross will be displayed. The transfer thus \xe2\x80\x9cpermits\xe2\x80\x9d the public \xe2\x80\x9cdisplay\xe2\x80\x9d of the cross. Indeed, that is the statute\xe2\x80\x99s\nobjective.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consideration of the injunction\xe2\x80\x99s purpose points in the same direction. The injunction rested upon the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s determination that the display of the cross \xe2\x80\x9cconveys a message of endorsement of religion\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable observer\xe2\x80\x9d in violation of the Establishment Clause. 212 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1216\xe2\x80\x931217. (As I have said, for present purposes we must assume that this is so.) The purpose of the injunction is to prevent the conveyance of such a message to the reasonable observer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With that purpose in mind, consider the following facts that confronted the District Court when the plaintiff asked it to enforce the decree:

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government had designated the \xe2\x80\x9cwhite cross \xe2\x80\xa6 as well as a limited amount of adjoining [land]\xe2\x80\x9d as a national memorial. Pub. L. 107\xe2\x80\x93117, \xc2\xa78137(a), 115 Stat. 2278\xe2\x80\x932279.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The new statute directed the transfer of the \xe2\x80\x9cproperty \xe2\x80\xa6 designated \xe2\x80\xa6 as a national memorial\xe2\x80\x9d to a private entity with an interest in maintaining the cross in its current location, in exchange for a parcel of land located elsewhere in the Preserve owned by private individuals who have taken a similar interest in the cross. Pub. L. 108\xe2\x80\x9387, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa78121(a) and (b), 117 Stat. 1100.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The transfer was made \xe2\x80\x9csubject to the condition that the recipient maintain the conveyed property as a memorial,\xe2\x80\x9d and the property reverts to the United States if the Secretary determines that the recipient has failed to do so. \xc2\xa78121(e), id., at 1100.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the transfer, the cross would sit on 1 acre of privately owned land in a 1.6 million acre national preserve, over 90% of which is federally owned. 212 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1205.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress had previously prevented the use of federal funds to remove the cross from its present location. Pub. L. 107\xe2\x80\x93248, \xc2\xa78065(b), 116 Stat. 1551; Pub. L. 106\xe2\x80\x93554, \xc2\xa7133, 114 Stat. 2763A\xe2\x80\x93230.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court considered the facts before it through the lens of the injunction\xe2\x80\x99s original purpose. See 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1180 (explaining that the \xe2\x80\x9c[G]overnment\xe2\x80\x99s continuing control over the Latin cross\xe2\x80\x9d and the involvement of private parties who \xe2\x80\x9cdesir[e] its continued presence in the Preserve,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrat[e]\xe2\x80\x9d that the transfer would not end \xe2\x80\x9cthe [G]overnment\xe2\x80\x99s apparent endorsement of a particular religion\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 1182 (considering the historical context of the transfer statute); see also Buono v. Kempthorne, 527 F.\xc2\xa03d 758, 783 (CA9 2008) (\xe2\x80\x9ccarving out a tiny parcel of property in the midst of this vast Preserve \xe2\x80\xa6 will do nothing to minimize the impermissible governmental endorsement\xe2\x80\x9d perceived by the reasonable observer). And it concluded that the land transfer would frustrate that purpose. See 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1182 (the transfer would \xe2\x80\x9ckeep the Latin cross atop Sunrise Rock without actually curing the continuing Establishment Clause violation\xe2\x80\x9d); see also 527 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 783 (finding that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in the present posture of the case alters \xe2\x80\xa6 earlier conclusions\xe2\x80\x9d regarding what a reasonable observer would perceive). In my view, this is a reasonable\nconclusion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The injunction forbids the Government to permit the display of the cross on Sunrise Rock, and its basic purpose was to prevent a reasonable observer from believing that the Government had endorsed the cross. Under the circumstances presented to the District Court, the transfer would have resulted in such a display and might well have conveyed such a message. Consequently, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision that the land transfer violated the injunction as written and intended was not an abuse of discretion. And that is what the Ninth Circuit properly held on appeal. What the Establishment Clause implications of the changed circumstances may be is a matter not before us. Cf. Frew v. Hawkins, 540 U. S. 431, 442 (2004) (\xe2\x80\x9cIf the [enjoined defendant] establishes reason to modify the decree, the court should make the necessary changes; where it has not done so, however, the decree should be enforced according to its terms\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because my conclusion rests primarily upon the law of injunctions, because that law is fairly clear, and because we cannot properly reach beyond that law to consider the underlying Establishment Clause and standing questions, I can find no federal question of general significance in this case. I believe we should not have granted the petition for certiorari. Having granted it, the Court should now dismiss the writ as improvidently granted. Since the Court has not done so, however, I believe that we should simply affirm the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With respect, I dissent.

KEN L. SALAZAR, SECRETARY OF THE INTERIOR,\net\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. FRANK BUONO

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Ginsburg and Justice Sotomayor join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 2002 Congress designated a \xe2\x80\x9cfive-foot-tall white cross\xe2\x80\x9d located in the Mojave National Preserve \xe2\x80\x9cas a national memorial commemorating United States participation in World War I and honoring the American veterans of that war.\xe2\x80\x9d Department of Defense Appropriations Act, Pub. L. 107\xe2\x80\x93117, \xc2\xa78137(a), 115 Stat. 2278. Later that year, in a judgment not open to question, the District Court determined that the display of that cross violated the Establishment Clause because it \xe2\x80\x9cconvey[ed] a message of endorsement of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Buono v. Norton, 212 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1202, 1217 (CD Cal. 2002) (Buono I). The question in this case is whether Congress\xe2\x80\x99 subsequent decision to transfer ownership of the property underlying the cross cured that violation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe Establishment Clause, if nothing else, prohibits government from \xe2\x80\x98specifying details upon which men and women who believe in a benevolent, omnipotent Creator and Ruler of the world are known to differ.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Van Orden v. Perry, 545 U. S. 677, 718 (2005) (Stevens, J., dissenting) (quoting Lee v. Weisman, 505 U. S. 577, 641 (1992) (Scalia, J., dissenting)). A Latin cross necessarily symbolizes one of the most important tenets upon which believers in a benevolent Creator, as well as nonbelievers, are known to differ. In my view, the District Court was right to enforce its prior judgment by enjoining Congress\xe2\x80\x99 proposed remedy\xe2\x80\x94a remedy that was engineered to leave the cross intact and that did not alter its basic meaning. I certainly agree that the Nation should memorialize the service of those who fought and died in World War I, but it cannot lawfully do so by continued endorsement of a starkly sectarian message.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the history recounted by the plurality indicates, this case comes to us in a procedural posture that significantly narrows the question presented to the Court. In the first stage of this litigation, the District Court and the Court of Appeals ruled that the Government violated the Establishment Clause by permitting the display of a single white Latin cross at Sunrise Rock. Those courts further ruled that the appropriate remedy was an injunction prohibiting the Government from \xe2\x80\x9cpermitting the display of the Latin cross in the area of Sunrise Rock in the Mojave National Preserve.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 39. The Government declined to seek a writ of certiorari following those rulings. Accordingly, for the purpose of this case, it is settled that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Sunrise Rock cross will project a message of government endorsement [of religion] to a reasonable observer,\xe2\x80\x9d Buono v. Norton, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d 543, 549 (CA9 2004) (Buono II), and that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s remedy for that endorsement was proper.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are, however, faced with an additional fact: Congress has enacted a statute directing the Secretary of the Interior to transfer a 1-acre parcel of land containing the cross to the Veterans of Foreign Wars (VFW), subject to certain conditions, in exchange for a 5-acre parcel of land elsewhere in the Preserve. See Department of Defense Appropriations Act, 2004, Pub. L. 108\xe2\x80\x9387, \xc2\xa78121, 117 Stat. 1100. The District Court found that the land transfer under \xc2\xa78121 \xe2\x80\x9cviolate[d] [the] court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment ordering a permanent injunction\xe2\x80\x9d and did not \xe2\x80\x9cactually cur[e] the continuing Establishment Clause violation.\xe2\x80\x9d Buono v. Norton, 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1175, 1182 (CD Cal. 2005) (Buono III). The District Court therefore enforced its 2002 judgment by enjoining the transfer, without considering whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe land transfer itself is an independent violation of the Establishment Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid., n.\xc2\xa08. Because the District Court did not base its decision upon an independent Establishment Clause violation, the constitutionality of the land-transfer statute is not before us. See ante, at 10. Instead, the question we confront is whether the District Court properly enforced its 2002 judgment by enjoining the transfer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In answering that question we, like the District Court, must first consider whether the transfer would violate the 2002 injunction. We must then consider whether changed circumstances nonetheless rendered enforcement of that judgment inappropriate; or conversely whether they made it necessary for the District Court to bar the transfer, even if the transfer is not expressly prohibited by the prior injunction, in order to achieve the intended objective of the injunction. The plurality correctly notes that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a court must never ignore significant changes in the law or circumstances underlying an injunction,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10 (quoting 11A C. Wright, A. Miller, & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa72961, pp. 393\xe2\x80\x93394 (2d ed. 1995) (hereinafter Wright & Miller)), and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[a] continuing decree of injunction directed to events to come is subject always to adaptation as events may shape the need,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 14 (quoting United States v. Swift & Co., 286 U. S. 106, 114 (1932)).[Footnote 1] At the same time, it is axiomatic that when a party seeks to enforce or modify an injunction, the only circumstances that matter are changed circumstances. See Swift, 286 U. S., at 119 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe injunction, whether right or wrong, is not subject to impeachment in its application to the conditions that existed at its making\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I further accept that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s task was to evaluate the changed circumstances \xe2\x80\x9cin light of the objectives of the 2002 injunction.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. This case does not simply pit a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprior showing of illegality\xe2\x80\x9d against a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s claim that \xe2\x80\x9cchanged circumstances have rendered prospective relief inappropriate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14. That formulation implies that the changed circumstances all cut in one direction, against prospective relief, and that the defendant has asked the court to alleviate its obligations. But it is important to note that in this case, the Government did not move to \xe2\x80\x9calleviate or eliminate conditions or restrictions imposed by the original decree\xe2\x80\x9d so as to permit the transfer. Wright & Miller \xc2\xa72961, at 397. Rather, it was the beneficiary of the original injunction who went back into court seeking its enforcement or modification in light of the transfer. Plainly, respondent had standing to seek enforcement of a decree in his favor.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent argued that such action was necessary, either to enforce the plain terms of the 2002 injunction or to \xe2\x80\x9cachieve the purposes of the provisions of the decree,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. United Shoe Machinery Corp., 391 U. S. 244, 249 (1968); see Wright & Miller \xc2\xa72961, at 393 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] court must continually be willing to redraft the order at the request of the party who obtained equitable relief in order to insure that the decree accomplishes its intended result\xe2\x80\x9d). Only at that point did the Government argue that changed circumstances made prospective relief unnecessary. This difference in focus is a subtle one, but it is important to emphasize that the question that was before the District Court\xe2\x80\x94and that is now before us\xe2\x80\x94is whether enjoining the transfer was necessary to effectuate the letter or logic of the 2002 judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although I agree with the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s basic framework, I disagree with its decision to remand the case to the District Court. The District Court already \xe2\x80\x9cengage[d] in the appropriate inquiry,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10, and it was well within its rights to enforce the 2002 judgment. First, the District Court properly recognized that the transfer was a means of \xe2\x80\x9cpermitting\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94indeed, encouraging\xe2\x80\x94the display of the cross. The transfer therefore would violate the terms of the court\xe2\x80\x99s original injunction. Second, even if the transfer would not violate the terms of the 2002 injunction, the District Court properly took into account events that transpired since 2002 and determined that barring the transfer was necessary to achieve the intended result of the 2002 decree, as the transfer would not eliminate government endorsement of religion.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first step in the analysis is straightforward: The District Court had to ask whether the transfer of the property would violate the extant injunction. Under the terms of that injunction, the answer was yes.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The 2002 injunction barred the Government from \xe2\x80\x9cpermitting the display of the Latin cross in the area of Sunrise Rock in the Mojave National Preserve.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 39. The land-transfer statute mandated transfer of the land to an organization that has announced its intention to maintain the cross on Sunrise Rock. That action surely \xe2\x80\x9cpermit[s]\xe2\x80\x9d the display of the cross. See 11 Oxford English Dictionary 578 (2d ed. 1989) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cpermit\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o admit or allow the doing or occurrence of; to give leave or opportunity for\xe2\x80\x9d). True, the Government would no longer exert direct control over the cross. But the transfer itself would be an act permitting its display.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I therefore disagree with Justice Scalia that the \xe2\x80\x9conly reasonable reading of the original injunction . .\xc2\xa0. is that it proscribed the cross\xe2\x80\x99s display on federal land.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2 (opinion concurring in judgment). If the land were already privately owned, Justice Scalia may be correct that the cross\xe2\x80\x99 display on Sunrise Rock would not violate the injunction because the Government would not have to do anything to allow the cross to stand, and the Government could try to prevent its display only by making such a display illegal. But the Government does own this land, and the transfer statute requires the Executive Branch to take an affirmative act (transfer to private ownership) designed to keep the cross in place. In evaluating a claim that the Government would impermissibly \xe2\x80\x9cpermit\xe2\x80\x9d the cross\xe2\x80\x99 display by effecting a transfer, a court cannot\nstart from a baseline in which the cross has already been\ntransferred.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, \xc2\xa78121 was designed specifically to foster the display of the cross. Regardless of why the Government wanted to \xe2\x80\x9caccommodat[e]\xe2\x80\x9d the interests associated with its display, ante, at 13 (plurality opinion), it was not only foreseeable but also intended that the cross would remain standing. Indeed, so far as the record indicates, the Government had no other purpose for turning over this land to private hands. It was therefore proper for the District Court to find that the transfer would violate its 2002 injunction and to enforce that injunction against the transfer.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As already noted, it was respondent, the beneficiary of the injunction, who moved the District Court for relief. When the beneficiary of an injunction seeks relief \xe2\x80\x9cto achieve the purposes of the provisions of the decree,\xe2\x80\x9d United Shoe Machinery Corp., 391 U. S., at 249, a district court has the authority to \xe2\x80\x9cmodify the decree so as to achieve the required result with all appropriate expedition,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 252. Thus, regardless of whether the transfer was prohibited by the plain terms of the 2002 judgment, the District Court properly inquired into whether enjoining the transfer was necessary to achieve the objective of that judgment. The Government faces a high burden in arguing the District Court exceeded its authority. A decree \xe2\x80\x9cmay not be changed in the interests of the defendants if the purposes of the litigation \xe2\x80\xa6 have not been fully achieved.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 248 (emphasis deleted). And contrary to the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position, the changed circumstances in this case support, rather than count against, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement decision.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The objective of the 2002 judgment, as the plurality grudgingly allows, was to \xe2\x80\x9cavoi[d] the perception of governmental endorsement\xe2\x80\x9d of religion. Ante, at 16; see Buono III, 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1178 (analyzing \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98whether government action endorsing religion has actually ceased\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d in light of the transfer). The parties do not disagree on this point; rather, they dispute whether the transfer would end government endorsement of the cross. Compare Brief for Petitioners 21 (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress\xe2\x80\x99s transfer of the land \xe2\x80\xa6 ends any governmental endorsement of the cross\xe2\x80\x9d) with Brief for Respondent 34 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he government\xe2\x80\x99s endorsement of the Christian cross is not remedied\xe2\x80\x9d by the land transfer). The District Court rightly found that the transfer would not end government endorsement of the cross.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A government practice violates the Establishment Clause if it \xe2\x80\x9ceither has the purpose or effect of \xe2\x80\x98endorsing\xe2\x80\x99 religion.\xe2\x80\x9d County of Allegheny v. American Civil Liberties Union, Greater Pittsburgh Chapter, 492 U. S. 573, 592 (1989). \xe2\x80\x9cWhether the key word is \xe2\x80\x98endorsement,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98favoritism,\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98promotion,\xe2\x80\x99 the essential principle remains the same. The Establishment Clause, at the very least, prohibits government from appearing to take a position on questions of religious belief or from \xe2\x80\x98making adherence to a religion relevant in any way to a person\xe2\x80\x99s standing\nin the political community.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 593\xe2\x80\x93594 (quoting\nLynch v. Donnelly, 465 U. S. 668, 687 (1984) (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J.,\nconcurring)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The 2002 injunction was based on a finding that display of the cross had the effect of endorsing religion. That is, \xe2\x80\x9cthe Sunrise Rock cross \xe2\x80\xa6 project[s] a message of government endorsement [of religion] to a reasonable observer.\xe2\x80\x9d Buono II, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 549. The determination that the Government had endorsed religion necessarily rested on two premises: first, that the Government endorsed the cross, and second, that the cross \xe2\x80\x9ctake[s] a position on questions of religious belief\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98mak[es] adherence to religion relevant \xe2\x80\xa6 to a person\xe2\x80\x99s standing in the political community,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d County of Allegheny, 492 U. S., at 594. Taking the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s 2002 finding of an Establishment Clause violation as res judicata, as we must, the land transfer has the potential to dislodge only the first of those premises, in that the transfer might change the Government\xe2\x80\x99s endorsing relationship with the cross. As I explain below, I disagree that the transfer ordered by \xc2\xa78121 would in fact have this result. But it is also worth noting at the outset that the transfer statute could not (and does not) dislodge the second premise\xe2\x80\x94that the cross conveys a religious message. Continuing government endorsement of the cross is thus continuing government endorsement of religion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, the transfer ordered by \xc2\xa78121 would not end government endorsement of the cross for two independently sufficient reasons. First, after the transfer it would continue to appear to any reasonable observer that the Government has endorsed the cross, notwithstanding that the name has changed on the title to a small patch of underlying land. This is particularly true because the Government has designated the cross as a national memorial, and that endorsement continues regardless of whether the cross sits on public or private land. Second, the transfer continues the existing government endorsement of the cross because the purpose of the transfer is to preserve its display. Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to preserve the display of the cross maintains the Government\xe2\x80\x99s endorsement of the cross.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality does not conclude to the contrary; that is, it does not decide that the transfer would end government endorsement of the cross and the religious message it conveys. Rather, the plurality concludes that the District Court did not conduct an appropriate analysis, and it remands the case for a do-over. I take up each of the purported faults the plurality finds in the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis in my examination of the reasons why the transfer does not cure the existing Establishment Clause\nviolation.

\n

\nPerception of the Cross Post-Transfer

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The 2002 injunction was based upon a finding of impermissible effect: The \xe2\x80\x9cSunrise Rock cross \xe2\x80\xa6 project[s] a message of government endorsement [of religion] to a reasonable observer.\xe2\x80\x9d Buono II, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 549. The transfer would not end that impermissible state of affairs because the cross, post-transfer, would still have \xe2\x80\x9cthe effect of communicating a message of government endorsement \xe2\x80\xa6 of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Lynch, 465 U. S., at 692 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring). As the Court of Appeals correctly found, \xe2\x80\x9c[n]othing in the present posture of the case alters\xe2\x80\x9d the conclusion that a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable observer would perceive governmental endorsement of the message\xe2\x80\x9d the cross conveys. Buono v. Kempthorne, 527 F.\xc2\xa03d 758, 783 (CA9 2008) (Buono IV).[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In its original judgment, the Court of Appeals found that a well-informed reasonable observer would perceive government endorsement of religion, notwithstanding the cross\xe2\x80\x99 initial \xe2\x80\x9cplacement by private individuals,\xe2\x80\x9d based upon the following facts: \xe2\x80\x9cthat the cross rests on public land[,] \xe2\x80\xa6 that Congress has designated the cross as a war memorial and prohibited the use of funds to remove it, and that the Park Service has denied similar access for expression by an adherent of the\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6 Buddhist faith.\xe2\x80\x9d Buono II, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 550. After the transfer, a well-informed observer would know that the cross was no longer on public land, but would additionally be aware of the following facts: The cross was once on public land, the Government was enjoined from permitting its display, Congress transferred it to a specific purchaser in order to preserve its display in the same location, and the Government maintained a reversionary interest in the land. From this chain of events, in addition to the factors that remain the same after the transfer, he would perceive government endorsement of the cross.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Particularly important to this analysis is that although the transfer might remove the implicit endorsement that presence on public land signifies, see Capitol Square Review and Advisory Bd. v. Pinette, 515 U. S. 753, 801 (1995) (Stevens, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe very fact that a sign is installed on public property implies official recognition and reinforcement of its message\xe2\x80\x9d), it would not change the fact that the Government has taken several explicit actions to endorse this cross. In its decision upholding the initial entry of the injunction, the Court of Appeals found those actions contributed to a reasonable observer\xe2\x80\x99s perception of government endorsement. Buono II, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 550. Their significance does not depend upon the ownership of the land.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 2000, and again after the District Court had entered its initial injunction, Congress passed legislation prohibiting the use of any federal funds to remove the cross from its location on federal property. See Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2001, Pub. L. 106\xe2\x80\x93554, App. D, \xc2\xa7133, 114 Stat. 2763A\xe2\x80\x93230; Department of Defense Appropriations Act, 2003, Pub. L. 107\xe2\x80\x93248, \xc2\xa78065(b), 116 Stat. 1551. Thus, beyond merely acquiescing in the continued presence of a cross on federal property, Congress singled out that cross for special treatment, and it affirmatively commanded that the cross must remain.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress also made a more dramatic intervention. Without the benefit of any committee hearings or floor debate in either the Senate or the House of Representatives\xe2\x80\x94indeed, without a moment of discussion in any official forum\xe2\x80\x94Congress passed legislation officially designating the \xe2\x80\x9cfive-foot-tall white cross\xe2\x80\x9d in the Mojave Desert \xe2\x80\x9cas a national memorial commemorating United States participation in World War I and honoring the American veterans of that war.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa78137(a), 115 Stat. 2278. Thereafter, the cross was no longer just a local artifact; it acquired a formal national status of the highest order. Once that momentous step was taken, changing the identity of the owner of the underlying land could no longer change the public or private character of the cross. The Government has expressly adopted the cross as its own.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even though Congress recognized this cross for its military associations, the solitary cross conveys an inescapably sectarian message. See Separation of Church and State Comm. v. Eugene, 93 F.\xc2\xa03d 617, 626 (CA9 1996) (O\xe2\x80\x99Scannlain, J., concurring in result) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he City\xe2\x80\x99s use of a cross to memorialize the war dead may lead observers to believe that the City has chosen to honor only Christian veterans\xe2\x80\x9d). As the District Court observed, it is undisputed that the \xe2\x80\x9c[L]atin cross is the preeminent symbol of Christianity. It is exclusively a Christian symbol, and not a symbol of any other religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Buono I, 212 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1205. We have recognized the significance of the Latin cross as a sectarian symbol,[Footnote 6] and no participant in this litigation denies that the cross bears that social meaning. Making a plain, unadorned Latin cross a war memorial does not make the cross secular. It makes the war memorial sectarian.[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More fundamentally, however, the message conveyed by the cross is not open to reconsideration given the posture of this case. The plurality employs a revealing turn of phrase when it characterizes the cross as \xe2\x80\x9ca symbol that, while challenged under the Establishment Clause, has complex meaning beyond the expression of religious views.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. The days of considering the cross itself as challenged under the Establishment Clause are over; it is settled that the Government is not permitted to endorse the cross. However complex the meaning of the cross, the Court of Appeals in 2004 considered and rejected the argument that its dual symbolism as a war memorial meant that government endorsement of the cross did not amount to endorsement of religion. See Buono II, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 549, n.\xc2\xa05. All we are debating at this juncture is whether the shift from public to private ownership of the land sufficiently distanced the Government from the cross; we are no longer debating the message the cross conveys to a reasonable observer. In arguing that Congress can legitimately favor the cross because of its purported double meaning, the plurality implicitly tries to reopen what is closed.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality also poses a different objection to consideration of whether the transfer would change a reasonable observer\xe2\x80\x99s perception of the cross. The plurality suggests that the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonable observer\xe2\x80\x99 standard\xe2\x80\x9d may not \xe2\x80\x9cbe the appropriate framework\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9ccourts considering Establishment Clause challenges do not,\xe2\x80\x9d as a general matter, \xe2\x80\x9cinquire into \xe2\x80\x98reasonable observer\xe2\x80\x99 perceptions with respect to objects on private land.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. Once again, the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s approach fails to pay heed to the posture of this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the risk of stating the obvious, respondent is not simply challenging a private object on private land. Although \xe2\x80\x9can Establishment Clause violation must be moored in government action of some sort,\xe2\x80\x9d Pinette, 515 U. S., at 779 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment), respondent\xe2\x80\x99s objection to the transfer easily meets that test for two reasons. First, he is currently challenging official legislation, taken in response to an identified Establishment Clause violation. That legislation would transfer public land to a particular private party, with the proviso that the transferee must use the land to fulfill a specific public function or else the land reverts back to the Government. Second, even once the transfer is complete, the cross would remain a national memorial. The cross is therefore not a purely \xe2\x80\x9cprivate\xe2\x80\x9d object in any meaningful sense.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notwithstanding these facts, the plurality appears to conclude that the transfer might render the cross purely private speech. It relies in part on the plurality opinion in Pinette for its suggestion that the reasonable observer standard may not be apposite, and Pinette addressed a privately owned cross displayed in a public forum. The Pinette plurality would have rejected the idea that \xe2\x80\x9ca neutrally behaving government\xe2\x80\x9d can ever endorse \xe2\x80\x9cprivate religious expression,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 764, even if a reasonable observer would perceive government endorsement, id., at 768. But the Pinette plurality acknowledged that government favoritism of private religious speech is unconstitutional, as when a government \xe2\x80\x9cgiv[es] sectarian religious speech preferential access to a forum close to the seat of government (or anywhere else for that matter).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 766. And in this case, the Government is not acting neutrally: The transfer statute and the government actions preceding it have all favored the cross.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, even assuming (wrongly) that the cross would be purely private speech after the transfer, and even assuming (quite implausibly) that the transfer statute is neutral with respect to the cross, it would still be appropriate for the District Court to apply the reasonable observer standard. The majority of the Pinette Court rejected the per se rule proposed by the plurality. Instead, the relevant standard provides that the Establishment Clause is violated whenever \xe2\x80\x9cthe State\xe2\x80\x99s own actions \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, and their relationship to the private speech at issue, actually convey a message of endorsement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 777 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). Moreover, the Establishment Clause \xe2\x80\x9cimposes affirmative obligations that may require a State, in some situations, to take steps to avoid being perceived as supporting or endorsing a private religious message.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. It is particularly appropriate in this context\xe2\x80\x94when the issue is whether the transfer cures an already identified Establishment Clause violation\xe2\x80\x94for the District Court to consider whether the Government, by complying with \xc2\xa78121, would have taken sufficient steps to avoid being perceived as endorsing the cross.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As I explained at the outset of this section, the answer to that inquiry is surely no. The reasonable observer \xe2\x80\x9cwho knows all of the pertinent facts and circumstances surrounding the symbol and its placement,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 17, would perceive that the Government has endorsed the cross: It prohibited the use of federal funds to take down the cross, designated the cross as a national memorial, and engaged in \xe2\x80\x9cherculean efforts to preserve the Latin cross\xe2\x80\x9d following the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s initial injunction, Buono III, 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1182. Those efforts include a transfer statute designed to keep the cross in place. Changing the ownership status of the underlying land in the manner required by \xc2\xa78121 would not change the fact that the cross conveys a message of government endorsement of religion.

\n

\nPurpose in Enacting the Transfer Statute

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even setting aside that the effect of the post-transfer cross would still be to convey a message of government endorsement of religion, the District Court was correct to conclude that \xc2\xa78121 would not cure the Establishment Clause violation because the very purpose of the transfer was to preserve the display of the cross. That evident purpose maintains government endorsement of the cross. The plurality does not really contest that this was Congress\xe2\x80\x99 purpose, ante, at 11, so I need not review the evidence in great detail. Suffice it to say that the record provides ample support. The land-transfer statute authorizes a conveyance to the particular recipient that has expressed an intent to preserve the cross. See Brief for Veterans of Foreign Wars of the United States et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 4 (transfer recipient \xe2\x80\x9cintends to maintain and preserve the Veterans Memorial\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 7 (identifying Veterans Memorial as the \xe2\x80\x9ccross and plaque\xe2\x80\x9d). And it conveys the particular land that has already been designated \xe2\x80\x9cas a national memorial\xe2\x80\x9d commemorating the veterans of World War I, \xc2\xa78121(a), 117 Stat. 1100, subject to a reversionary clause requiring that a memorial \xe2\x80\x9ccommemorating United States participation in World War I and honoring the American veterans of that war\xe2\x80\x9d be maintained, \xc2\xa78121(e). If it does not categorically require the new owner of the property to display the existing memorial meeting that description (the cross), see \xc2\xa78137, 115 Stat. 2278, the statute most certainly encourages this result. Indeed, the Government concedes that Congress sought to \xe2\x80\x9cpreserve a longstanding war memorial\xe2\x80\x9d at the site, Brief for Petitioners 28 (emphasis added), and the only memorial that could be \xe2\x80\x9cpreserved\xe2\x80\x9d at Sunrise Rock is the cross itself.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality insists, however, that even assuming the purpose of the land transfer was to preserve the display of the cross, enjoining the transfer was not necessarily appropriate. It contends the District Court failed to give adequate consideration to \xe2\x80\x9cthe context in which the [land-transfer] statute was enacted and the reasons for its passage,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11, and it directs the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s attention to three factors: the message intended by the private citizens who first erected the cross, ibid.; the time the cross stood on Sunrise Rock and its historical meaning, ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312; and Congress\xe2\x80\x99 balancing of \xe2\x80\x9copposing interests\xe2\x80\x9d and selection of a \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy of accommodation,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 13; see also ante, at 17.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first two of these factors are red herrings. The District Court, in its enforcement decision, had no occasion to consider anew either the private message intended by those who erected the cross or how long the cross had stood atop Sunrise Rock. Neither of these factors constituted a novel or changed circumstance since the entry of the 2002 injunction. Whatever message those who initially erected the cross intended\xe2\x80\x94and I think we have to presume it was a Christian one, at least in part, for the simple reason that those who erected the cross chose to commemorate American veterans in an explicitly Christian manner\xe2\x80\x94that historical fact did not change between 2002 and 2005. I grant that the amount of time the cross had stood on Sunrise Rock did change, from 68 years to 71 years, but no one can seriously maintain that \xe2\x80\x9cthe historical meaning that the cross had attained,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12, was materially transformed in that 3-year increment.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This brings us to the final factor identified by the plurality: Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy of accommodation\xe2\x80\x9d for the cross.[Footnote 10] Of course, the District Court did consider Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy\xe2\x80\x9d in the sense that it considered the result Congress was trying to achieve with respect to the cross, i.e., to keep it in place. See Buono III, 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1182 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he proposed transfer of the subject property can only be viewed as an attempt to keep the Latin cross atop Sunrise Rock without actually curing the continuing Establishment Clause violation\xe2\x80\x9d). But I understand the plurality to be faulting the District Court for failing to inquire into a deeper level of motivation: If the purpose of the transfer was to keep the cross in place, what was the purpose of keeping the cross in place?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I do not see why it was incumbent upon the District Court to examine this second-order purpose when determining whether the transfer violated the 2002 injunction. As discussed in Part II, supra, the injunction barred the Government from permitting the display of the cross, which fairly encompasses any act providing an opportunity for the cross\xe2\x80\x99 display. It was entirely appropriate for the District Court to characterize a transfer with the purpose of preserving the cross as an attempt to evade that injunction, and to find that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s purpose to preserve the cross maintains government endorsement of the cross.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality would have the District Court revise its entire analysis of whether the transfer would end government endorsement, in light of the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s view of the land-transfer statute\xe2\x80\x99s putative second-order purpose. That analysis ignores the procedural posture of the case. If the question before the Court were whether \xc2\xa78121 itself violated the Establishment Clause, then this argument might have merit. But we are instead examining whether action taken with the purpose of preserving the display of the cross cures or continues government endorsement. In my view, that purpose continues the impermissible endorsement of\xe2\x80\x94indeed, favoritism toward\xe2\x80\x94the cross, regardless of why Congress chose to intervene as it did.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 second-order purpose does little for the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s position. Without relying on any legislative history or findings\xe2\x80\x94there are none\xe2\x80\x94the plurality opines that Congress wanted to keep the cross in place in order to accommodate those who might view removal as \xe2\x80\x9cconveying disrespect for those the cross was seen as honoring,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 12, and it suggests that this decision was an acceptable method of \xe2\x80\x9cbalanc[ing] opposing interests\xe2\x80\x9d because the cross \xe2\x80\x9chas complex meaning beyond the expression of religious views,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 13. As I have already explained, the meaning of the cross (complex or otherwise) is no longer before us, and the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on a \xe2\x80\x9ccongressional statement of policy,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., as negating any government endorsement of religion finds no support in logic or precedent. The cross cannot take on a nonsectarian character by congressional (or judicial) fiat, and the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s evaluation of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 actions is divorced from the methodology prescribed by our doctrine.[Footnote 11]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our precedent provides that we evaluate purpose based upon what the objective indicia of intent would reveal to a reasonable observer. See McCreary County v. American Civil Liberties Union of Ky., 545 U. S. 844, 862 (2005) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe eyes that look to purpose belong to an objective observer, one who takes account of the traditional external signs that show up in the text, legislative history, and implementation of the statute, or comparable official act\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). \xe2\x80\x9c[R]easonable observers have reasonable memories, and our precedents sensibly forbid an observer \xe2\x80\x98to turn a blind eye to the context in which [the] policy arose.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 866 (quoting Santa\xc2\xa0Fe Independent School Dist. v. Doe, 530 U. S. 290, 315 (2000)). The plurality nowhere engages with how a reasonable observer would view Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy of accommodation\xe2\x80\x9d for this cross. Instead, the plurality insists that deference is owed because of \xe2\x80\x9cCongress\xe2\x80\x99s prerogative to balance opposing interests and its institutional competence to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The proper remedy for an Establishment Clause violation is a legal judgment, which is not the sort of issue for which Congress \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98has both wisdom and experience \xe2\x80\xa6 that is far superior to ours.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens United v. FEC, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (Stevens, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 71) (quoting Colorado Republican Federal Campaign Comm. v. FEC, 518 U. S. 604, 650 (1996) (Stevens, J., dissenting)). Moreover, the inference that Congress has exercised its institutional competence\xe2\x80\x94or even its considered judgment\xe2\x80\x94is significantly weaker in a case such as this, when the legislative action was \xe2\x80\x9cburied in a defense appropriations bill,\xe2\x80\x9d Buono III, 364 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1181, and, so far as the record shows, undertaken without any deliberation whatsoever. I am not dismissive of Congress, see ante, at 7 (opinion of Alito, J.), but \xc2\xa78121 presents no factual findings, reasoning, or long history of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98careful legislative adjustment,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens United, 558 U. S., at ___ (Stevens, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 71) (quoting FEC v. Beaumont, 539 U. S. 146, 162, n.\xc2\xa09 (2003)), to which I could possibly defer. Congress did not devote \xe2\x80\x9cyears of careful study\xe2\x80\x9d to \xc2\xa78121, Citizens United, 558 U. S., at ___ (Stevens, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 73), nor did it develop a record of any kind, much less an exhaustive one, see id., at ___ (slip op., at 20) (noting the legislative record for the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act of 2002 spanned 100,000 pages). The concurrence\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to draw an equivalence between a provision tucked silently into an appropriations bill and a major statute debated and developed over many years is, to say the least, not persuasive. All legislative acts are not fungible.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, in the Establishment Clause context, we do not accord any special deference to the legislature on account of its generic advantages as a policymaking body, and the purpose test is not \xe2\x80\x9csatisfied so long as any secular purpose for the government action is apparent,\xe2\x80\x9d McCreary County, 545 U. S., at 865, n.\xc2\xa013 (emphasis added). Nor can the Government pursue a secular aim through religious means. See Van Orden, 545 U. S., at 715 (Stevens, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThough the State of Texas may genuinely wish to combat juvenile delinquency, and may rightly want to honor the Eagles for their efforts, it cannot effectuate these admirable purposes through an explicitly religious medium\xe2\x80\x9d). It is odd that the plurality ignores all of these well-settled principles in exalting this particular legislative determination.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A reasonable observer, considering the nature of this symbol, the timing and the substance of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 efforts, and the history of the Sunrise Rock site, could conclude that Congress chose to preserve the cross primarily because of its salience as a cross. Cf. McCreary County, 545 U. S., at 873 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf the observer had not thrown up his hands, he would probably suspect that the Counties were simply reaching for any way to keep a religious document on the walls \xe2\x80\xa6\xe2\x80\x9d). But no such conclusion is necessary to find for respondent.[Footnote 12] The religious meaning of the cross was settled by the 2002 judgment; the only question before us is whether the Government has sufficiently distanced itself from the cross to end government endorsement of it. At the least, I stress again, a reasonable observer would conclude that the Government\xe2\x80\x99s purpose in transferring the underlying land did not sufficiently distance the Government from the cross. Indeed, \xc2\xa78121 evidenced concern for whether the cross would be displayed. The District Court was therefore correct to find that the transfer would not end government endorsement of religion.

\n

IV

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, I conclude that the transfer ordered by \xc2\xa78121 will not end the pre-existing government endorsement of the cross, and to the contrary may accentuate the problem in some respects. Because the transfer would perpetuate the Establishment Clause violation at issue in the 2002 injunction, I further conclude that enjoining the transfer was necessary to secure relief. Given the transfer statute\xe2\x80\x99s fundamental inadequacy as a remedy, there was\xe2\x80\x94and is\xe2\x80\x94no need for the District Court to consider \xe2\x80\x9cless drastic relief than complete invalidation of the \xe2\x80\xa6 statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 18. Allowing the transfer to go forward would interfere with the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to enforce its judgment and deprive the District Court of the ability to ensure a complete remedy. Nor could allowing the transfer to go forward be made a complete remedy with add-on measures, such as signs or fences indicating the ownership of the land. Such measures would not completely end the government endorsement of this cross, as the land would have been transferred in a manner favoring the cross and the cross would remain designated as a national memorial. Enjoining compliance with \xc2\xa78121 was therefore a proper exercise of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to enforce the 2002 judgment.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congressional action, taken after due deliberation, that honors our fallen soldiers merits our highest respect. As far as I can tell, however, it is unprecedented in the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s history to designate a bare, unadorned cross as the national war memorial for a particular group of veterans. Neither the Korean War Memorial, the Vietnam War Memorial, nor the World War II Memorial commemorates our veterans\xe2\x80\x99 sacrifice in sectarian or predominantly religious ways. Each of these impressive structures pays equal respect to all members of the Armed Forces who perished in the service of our Country in those conflicts. In this case, by contrast, a sectarian symbol is the memorial. And because Congress has established no other national monument to the veterans of the Great War, this solitary cross in the middle of the desert is the national World War I memorial. The sequence of legislative decisions made to designate and preserve a solitary Latin cross at an isolated location in the desert as a memorial for those who fought and died in World War I not only failed to cure the Establishment Clause violation but also, in my view, resulted in a dramatically inadequate and inappropriate tribute.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I believe that most judges would find it to be a clear Establishment Clause violation if Congress had simply directed that a solitary Latin cross be erected on the Mall in the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s Capital to serve as a World War I Memorial. Congress did not erect this cross, but it commanded that the cross remain in place, and it gave the cross the imprimatur of Government. Transferring the land pursuant to \xc2\xa78121 would perpetuate rather than cure that unambiguous endorsement of a sectarian message.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Mojave Desert is a remote location, far from the seat of our Government. But the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interest in honoring all those who have rendered heroic public service regardless of creed, as well as its constitutional responsibility to avoid endorsement of a particular religious view, should control wherever national memorials speak on behalf of our entire country.
