\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

FREE ENTERPRISE FUND et\xc2\xa0al. v. PUBLIC COMPANY ACCOUNTING OVERSIGHT BOARD et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x93861.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 7, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 28, 2010

\n

Respondent, the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board, was created as part of a series of accounting reforms in the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002. The Board is composed of five members appointed by the Securities and Exchange Commission. It was modeled on private self-regulatory organizations in the securities industry\xe2\x80\x94such as the New York Stock Exchange\xe2\x80\x94that investigate and discipline their own members subject to Commission oversight. Unlike these organizations, the Board is a Government-created entity with expansive powers to govern an entire industry. Every accounting firm that audits public companies under the securities laws must register with the Board, pay it an annual fee, and comply with its rules and oversight. The Board may inspect registered firms, initiate formal investigations, and issue severe sanctions in its disciplinary proceedings. The parties agree that the Board is \xe2\x80\x9cpart of the Government\xe2\x80\x9d for constitutional purposes, Lebron v. National Railroad Passenger Corporation, 513 U. S. 374, 397, and that its members are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Officers of the United States\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d who \xe2\x80\x9cexercis[e] significant authority pursuant to the laws of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d Buckley v. Valeo, 424 U. S. 1, 125\xe2\x80\x93126. While the SEC has oversight of the Board, it cannot remove Board members at will, but only \xe2\x80\x9cfor good cause shown,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cin accordance with\xe2\x80\x9d specified procedures. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77211(e)(6), 7217(d)(3). The parties also agree that the Commissioners, in turn, cannot themselves be removed by the President except for \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor v. United States, 295 U. S. 602, 620.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Board inspected petitioner accounting firm, released a report critical of its auditing procedures, and began a formal investigation. The firm and petitioner Free Enterprise Fund, a nonprofit organization of which the firm is a member, sued the Board and its members, seeking, inter alia, a declaratory judgment that the Board is unconstitutional and an injunction preventing the Board from exercising its powers. Petitioners argued that the Sarbanes-Oxley Act contravened the separation of powers by conferring executive power on Board members without subjecting them to Presidential control. The basis for petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 challenge was that Board members were insulated from Presidential control by two layers of tenure protection: Board members could only be removed by the Commission for good cause, and the Commissioners could in turn only be removed by the President for good cause. Petitioners also challenged the Board\xe2\x80\x99s appointment as violating the Appointments Clause, which requires officers to be appointed by the President with the Senate\xe2\x80\x99s advice and consent, or\xe2\x80\x94in the case of \xe2\x80\x9cinferior Officers\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94by \xe2\x80\x9cthe President alone, \xe2\x80\xa6 the Courts of Law, or \xe2\x80\xa6 the Heads of Departments,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02. The United States intervened to defend the statute. The District Court found it had jurisdiction and granted summary judgment to respondents. The Court of Appeals affirmed. It first agreed that the District Court had jurisdiction. It then ruled that the dual restraints on Board members\xe2\x80\x99 removal are permissible, and that Board members are inferior officers whose appointment is consistent with the Appointments Clause.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The District Court had jurisdiction over these claims. The Commission may review any Board rule or sanction, and an aggrieved party may challenge the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfinal order\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9crule\xe2\x80\x9d in a court of appeals under 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778y. The Government reads \xc2\xa778y as an exclusive route to review, but the text does not expressly or implicitly limit the jurisdiction that other statutes confer on district courts. It is presumed that Congress does not intend to limit jurisdiction if \xe2\x80\x9ca finding of preclusion could foreclose all meaningful judicial review\xe2\x80\x9d; if the suit is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98wholly \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 to a statute\xe2\x80\x99s review provisions\xe2\x80\x9d; and if the claims are \xe2\x80\x9coutside the agency\xe2\x80\x99s expertise.\xe2\x80\x9d Thunder Basin Coal Co. v. Reich, 510 U. S. 200, 212\xe2\x80\x93213.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These considerations point against any limitation on review here. Section 78y provides only for review of Commission action, and petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 challenge is \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d to any Commission orders or rules from which review might be sought. The Government advises petitioners to raise their claims by appealing a Board sanction, but petitioners have not been sanctioned, and it is no \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningful\xe2\x80\x9d avenue of relief, Thunder Basin, supra, at 212, to require a plaintiff to incur a sanction in order to test a law\xe2\x80\x99s validity, MedImmune, Inc. v. Genentech, Inc., 549 U. S. 118, 129. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims are also outside the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s competence and expertise, and the statutory questions involved do not require technical considerations of agency policy. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The dual for-cause limitations on the removal of Board members contravene the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s separation of powers. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9327.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Constitution provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he executive Power shall be vested in a President of the United States of America.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa71, cl.\xc2\xa01. Since 1789, the Constitution has been understood to empower the President to keep executive officers accountable\xe2\x80\x94by removing them from office, if necessary. See generally Myers v. United States, 272 U. S. 52. This Court has determined that this authority is not without limit. In Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, supra, this Court held that Congress can, under certain circumstances, create independent agencies run by principal officers appointed by the President, whom the President may not remove at will but only for good cause. And in United States v. Perkins, 116 U. S. 483, and Morrison v. Olson, 487 U. S. 654, the Court sustained similar restrictions on the power of principal executive officers\xe2\x80\x94themselves responsible to the President\xe2\x80\x94to remove their own inferiors. However, this Court has not addressed the consequences of more than one level of good-cause tenure. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Where this Court has upheld limited restrictions on the President\xe2\x80\x99s removal power, only one level of protected tenure separated the President from an officer exercising executive power. The President\xe2\x80\x94or a subordinate he could remove at will\xe2\x80\x94decided whether the officer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct merited removal under the good-cause standard. Here, the Act not only protects Board members from removal except for good cause, but withdraws from the President any decision on whether that good cause exists. That decision is vested in other tenured officers\xe2\x80\x94the Commissioners\xe2\x80\x94who are not subject to the President\xe2\x80\x99s direct control. Because the Commission cannot remove a Board member at will, the President cannot hold the Commission fully accountable for the Board\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. He can only review the Commissioner\xe2\x80\x99s determination of whether the Act\xe2\x80\x99s rigorous good-cause standard is met. And if the President disagrees with that determination, he is powerless to intervene\xe2\x80\x94unless the determination is so unreasonable as to constitute \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, supra, at 620.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This arrangement contradicts Article II\xe2\x80\x99s vesting of the executive power in the President. Without the ability to oversee the Board, or to attribute the Board\xe2\x80\x99s failings to those whom he can oversee, the President is no longer the judge of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. He can neither ensure that the laws are faithfully executed, nor be held responsible for a Board member\xe2\x80\x99s breach of faith. If this dispersion of responsibility were allowed to stand, Congress could multiply it further by adding still more layers of good-cause tenure. Such diffusion of power carries with it a diffusion of accountability; without a clear and effective chain of command, the public cannot determine where the blame for a pernicious measure should fall. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s restrictions are therefore incompatible with the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s separation of powers. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fact that a given law or procedure is efficient, convenient, and useful in facilitating functions of government, standing alone, will not save it if it is contrary to the Constitution.\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bowsher v. Synar, 478 U. S. 714, 736. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s multilevel tenure protections provide a blueprint for the extensive expansion of legislative power. Congress controls the salary, duties, and existence of executive offices, and only Presidential oversight can counter its influence. The Framers created a structure in which \xe2\x80\x9c[a] dependence on the people\xe2\x80\x9d would be the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary controul on the government,\xe2\x80\x9d and that dependence is maintained by giving each branch \xe2\x80\x9cthe necessary constitutional means and personal motives to resist encroachments of the others.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 51, p. 349. A key \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional means\xe2\x80\x9d vested in the President was \xe2\x80\x9cthe power of appointing, overseeing, and controlling those who execute the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Annals of Congress 463. While a government of \xe2\x80\x9copposite and rival interests\xe2\x80\x9d may sometimes inhibit the smooth functioning of administration, The Federalist No. 51, at 349, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Framers recognized that, in the long term, structural protections against abuse of power were critical to preserving liberty.\xe2\x80\x9d Bowsher, supra, at 730. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0The Government errs in arguing that, even if some constraints on the removal of inferior executive officers might violate the Constitution, the restrictions here do not. There is no construction of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s good-cause removal power that is broad enough to avoid invalidation. Nor is the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s broad power over Board functions the equivalent of a power to remove Board members. Altering the Board\xe2\x80\x99s budget or powers is not a meaningful way to control an inferior officer; the Commission cannot supervise individual Board members if it must destroy the Board in order to fix it. Moreover, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s power over the Board is hardly plenary, as the Board may take significant enforcement actions largely independently of the Commission. Enacting new SEC rules through the required notice and comment procedures would be a poor means of micromanaging the Board, and without certain findings, the Act forbids any general rule requiring SEC preapproval of Board actions. Finally, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act is highly unusual in committing substantial executive authority to officers protected by two layers of good-cause removal. Pp.\xc2\xa021\xe2\x80\x9327.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0The unconstitutional tenure provisions are severable from the remainder of the statute. Because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he unconstitutionality of a part of an Act does not necessarily defeat or affect the validity of its remaining provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d Champlin Refining Co. v. Corporation Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Okla., 286 U. S. 210, 234, the \xe2\x80\x9cnormal rule\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cthat partial \xe2\x80\xa6 invalidation is the required course,\xe2\x80\x9d Brockett v. Spokane Arcades, Inc., 472 U. S. 491, 504. The Board\xe2\x80\x99s existence does not violate the separation of powers, but the substantive removal restrictions imposed by \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77211(e)(6) and 7217(d)(3) do. Concluding that the removal restrictions here are invalid leaves the Board removable by the Commission at will. With the tenure restrictions excised, the Act remains \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fully operative as a law,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d New York v. United States, 505 U. S. 144, 186, and nothing in the Act\xe2\x80\x99s text or historical context makes it \xe2\x80\x9cevident\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress would have preferred no Board at all to a Board whose members are removable at will, Alaska Airlines, Inc. v. Brock, 480 U. S. 678, 684. The consequence is that the Board may continue to function as before, but its members may be removed at will by the Commission. Pp.\xc2\xa027\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa04.\xc2\xa0The Board\xe2\x80\x99s appointment is consistent with the Appointments Clause. Pp. 29\xe2\x80\x9333.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Board members are inferior officers whose appointment Congress may permissibly vest in a \xe2\x80\x9cHea[d] of Departmen[t].\xe2\x80\x9d Inferior officers \xe2\x80\x9care officers whose work is directed and supervised at some level\xe2\x80\x9d by superiors appointed by the President with the Senate\xe2\x80\x99s consent. Edmond v. United States, 520 U. S. 651, 662\xe2\x80\x93663. Because the good-cause restrictions discussed above are unconstitutional and void, the Commission possesses the power to remove Board members at will, in addition to its other oversight authority. Board members are therefore directed and supervised by the Commission. Pp. 29\xe2\x80\x9330.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Commission is a \xe2\x80\x9cDepartmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d under the Appointments Clause. Freytag v. Commissioner, 501 U. S. 868, 887, n. 4, specifically reserved the question whether a \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal agenc[y], such as\xe2\x80\x9d the SEC, is a \xe2\x80\x9cDepartmen[t].\xe2\x80\x9d The Court now adopts the reasoning of the concurring Justices in Freytag, who would have concluded that the SEC is such a \xe2\x80\x9cDepartmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d because it is a freestanding component of the Executive Branch not subordinate to or contained within any other such component. This reading is consistent with the common, near-contemporary definition of a \xe2\x80\x9cdepartment\xe2\x80\x9d; with the early practice of Congress, see \xc2\xa73, 1 Stat. 234; and with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases, which have never invalidated an appointment made by the head of such an establishment. Pp. 30\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The several Commissioners, and not the Chairman, are the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cHea[d].\xe2\x80\x9d The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s powers are generally vested in the Commissioners jointly, not the Chairman alone. The Commissioners do not report to the Chairman, who exercises administrative functions subject to the full Commission\xe2\x80\x99s policies. There is no reason why a multimember body may not be the \xe2\x80\x9cHea[d]\xe2\x80\x9d of a \xe2\x80\x9cDepartmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d that it governs. The Appointments Clause necessarily contemplates collective appointments by the \xe2\x80\x9cCourts of Law,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02, and each House of Congress appoints its officers collectively, see, e.g., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa05. Practice has also sanctioned the appointment of inferior officers by multimember agencies. Pp.\xc2\xa031\xe2\x80\x9333.

\n

FREE ENTERPRISE FUND and BECKSTEAD AND\nWATTS, LLP, PETITIONERS v. PUBLIC COMPANY\nACCOUNTING OVERSIGHT BOARD et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

[June 28, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our Constitution divided the \xe2\x80\x9cpowers of the new Federal Government into three defined categories, Legislative, Executive, and Judicial.\xe2\x80\x9d INS v. Chadha, 462 U. S. 919, 951 (1983). Article II vests \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he executive Power \xe2\x80\xa6 in a President of the United States of America,\xe2\x80\x9d who must \xe2\x80\x9ctake Care that the Laws be faithfully executed.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. II, \xc2\xa71, cl.\xc2\xa01; id., \xc2\xa73. In light of \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he impossibility that one man should be able to perform all the great business of the State,\xe2\x80\x9d the Constitution provides for executive officers to \xe2\x80\x9cassist the supreme Magistrate in discharging the duties of his trust.\xe2\x80\x9d 30 Writings of George Washington 334 (J. Fitzpatrick ed. 1939).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since 1789, the Constitution has been understood to empower the President to keep these officers accountable\xe2\x80\x94by removing them from office, if necessary. See generally Myers v. United States, 272 U. S. 52 (1926). This Court has determined, however, that this authority is not without limit. In Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor v. United States, 295 U. S. 602 (1935), we held that Congress can, under certain circumstances, create independent agencies run by principal officers appointed by the President, whom the President may not remove at will but only for good cause. Likewise, in United States v. Perkins, 116 U. S. 483 (1886), and Morrison v. Olson, 487 U. S. 654 (1988), the Court sustained similar restrictions on the power of principal executive officers\xe2\x80\x94themselves responsible to the President\xe2\x80\x94to remove their own inferiors. The parties do not ask us to reexamine any of these precedents, and we do not do so.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are asked, however, to consider a new situation not yet encountered by the Court. The question is whether these separate layers of protection may be combined. May the President be restricted in his ability to remove a principal officer, who is in turn restricted in his ability to remove an inferior officer, even though that inferior officer determines the policy and enforces the laws of the United States?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that such multilevel protection from removal is contrary to Article II\xe2\x80\x99s vesting of the executive power in the President. The President cannot \xe2\x80\x9ctake Care that the Laws be faithfully executed\xe2\x80\x9d if he cannot oversee the faithfulness of the officers who execute them. Here the President cannot remove an officer who enjoys more than one level of good-cause protection, even if the President determines that the officer is neglecting his duties or discharging them improperly. That judgment is instead committed to another officer, who may or may not agree with the President\xe2\x80\x99s determination, and whom the President cannot remove simply because that officer disagrees with him. This contravenes the President\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional obligation to ensure the faithful execution of the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 693.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After a series of celebrated accounting debacles, Congress enacted the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (or Act), 116 Stat. 745. Among other measures, the Act introduced tighter regulation of the accounting industry under a new Public Company Accounting Oversight Board. The Board is composed of five members, appointed to staggered 5-year terms by the Securities and Exchange Commission. It was modeled on private self-regulatory organizations in the securities industry\xe2\x80\x94such as the New York Stock Exchange\xe2\x80\x94that investigate and discipline their own members subject to Commission oversight. Congress created the Board as a private \xe2\x80\x9cnonprofit corporation,\xe2\x80\x9d and Board members and employees are not considered Government \xe2\x80\x9cofficer[s] or employee[s]\xe2\x80\x9d for statutory purposes. 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77211(a), (b). The Board can thus recruit its members and employees from the private sector by paying salaries far above the standard Government pay scale. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77211(f)(4), 7219.[Footnote 1]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike the self-regulatory organizations, however, the Board is a Government-created, Government-appointed entity, with expansive powers to govern an entire industry. Every accounting firm\xe2\x80\x94both foreign and domestic\xe2\x80\x94that participates in auditing public companies under the securities laws must register with the Board, pay it an annual fee, and comply with its rules and oversight. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77211(a), 7212(a), (f), 7213, 7216(a)(1). The Board is charged with enforcing the Sarbanes-Oxley Act, the securities laws, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s rules, its own rules, and professional accounting standards. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77215(b)(1), (c)(4). To this end, the Board may regulate every detail of an accounting firm\xe2\x80\x99s practice, including hiring and professional development, promotion, supervision of audit work, the acceptance of new business and the continuation of old, internal inspection procedures, professional ethics rules, and \xe2\x80\x9csuch other requirements as the Board may prescribe.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77213(a)(2)(B).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Board promulgates auditing and ethics standards, performs routine inspections of all accounting firms, demands documents and testimony, and initiates formal investigations and disciplinary proceedings. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77213\xe2\x80\x937215 (2006 ed. and Supp. II). The willful violation of any Board rule is treated as a willful violation of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, 48 Stat. 881, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778a et seq.\xe2\x80\x94a federal crime punishable by up to 20 years\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment or $25 million in fines ($5 million for a natural person). \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778ff(a), 7202(b)(1) (2006 ed.). And the Board itself can issue severe sanctions in its disciplinary proceedings, up to and including the permanent revocation of a firm\xe2\x80\x99s registration, a permanent ban on a person\xe2\x80\x99s associating with any registered firm, and money penalties of $15 million ($750,000 for a natural person). \xc2\xa77215(c)(4). Despite the provisions specifying that Board members are not Government officials for statutory purposes, the parties agree that the Board is \xe2\x80\x9cpart of the Government\xe2\x80\x9d for constitutional purposes, Lebron v. National Railroad Passenger Corporation, 513 U. S. 374, 397 (1995), and that its members are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Officers of the United States\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d who \xe2\x80\x9cexercis[e] significant authority pursuant to the laws of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d Buckley v. Valeo, 424 U. S. 1, 125\xe2\x80\x93126 (1976) (per curiam) (quoting Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02); cf. Brief for Petitioners 9, n.\xc2\xa01; Brief for United States 29, n.\xc2\xa08.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Act places the Board under the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s oversight, particularly with respect to the issuance of rules or the imposition of sanctions (both of which are subject to Commission approval and alteration). \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77217(b)\xe2\x80\x93(c). But the individual members of the Board\xe2\x80\x94like the officers and directors of the self-regulatory organizations\xe2\x80\x94are substantially insulated from the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s control. The Commission cannot remove Board members at will, but only \xe2\x80\x9cfor good cause shown,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cin accordance with\xe2\x80\x9d certain procedures. \xc2\xa77211(e)(6).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Those procedures require a Commission finding, \xe2\x80\x9con the record\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cafter notice and opportunity for a hearing,\xe2\x80\x9d that the Board member

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c(A)\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0has willfully violated any provision of th[e] Act, the rules of the Board, or the securities laws;

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c(B)\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0has willfully abused the authority of that member; or

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c(C)\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0without reasonable justification or excuse, has failed to enforce compliance with any such provision or rule, or any professional standard by any registered public accounting firm or any associated person thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77217(d)(3).

\n

\nRemoval of a Board member requires a formal Commission order and is subject to judicial review. See 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7554(a), 556(a), 557(a), (c)(B); 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778y(a)(1). Similar procedures govern the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s removal of officers and directors of the private self-regulatory organizations. See \xc2\xa778s(h)(4). The parties agree that the Commissioners cannot themselves be removed by the President except under the Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor standard of \xe2\x80\x9cinefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office,\xe2\x80\x9d 295 U. B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Beckstead and Watts, LLP, is a Nevada accounting firm registered with the Board. The Board inspected the firm, released a report critical of its auditing procedures, and began a formal investigation. Beckstead and Watts and the Free Enterprise Fund, a nonprofit organization of which the firm is a member, then sued the Board and its members, seeking (among other things) a declaratory judgment that the Board is unconstitutional and an injunction preventing the Board from exercising its powers. App. 71.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before the District Court, petitioners argued that the Sarbanes-Oxley Act contravened the separation of powers by conferring wide-ranging executive power on Board members without subjecting them to Presidential control. Id., at 67\xe2\x80\x9368. Petitioners also challenged the Act under the Appointments Clause, which requires \xe2\x80\x9cOfficers of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d to be appointed by the President with the Senate\xe2\x80\x99s advice and consent. Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02. The Clause provides an exception for \xe2\x80\x9cinferior Officers,\xe2\x80\x9d whose appointment Congress may choose to vest \xe2\x80\x9cin the President alone, in the Courts of Law, or in the Heads of Departments.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Because the Board is appointed by the SEC, petitioners argued that (1) Board members are not \xe2\x80\x9cinferior Officers\xe2\x80\x9d who may be appointed by \xe2\x80\x9cHeads of Departments\xe2\x80\x9d; (2) even if they are, the Commission is not a \xe2\x80\x9cDepartmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d; and (3) even if it is, the several Commissioners (as opposed to the Chairman) are not its \xe2\x80\x9cHea[d].\xe2\x80\x9d See App. 68\xe2\x80\x9370. The United States intervened to defend the Act\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality. Both sides moved for summary judgment; the District Court determined that it had jurisdiction and granted summary judgment to respondents. App. to Pet. for Cert. 110a\xe2\x80\x93117a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A divided Court of Appeals affirmed. 537 F.\xc2\xa03d 667 (CADC 2008). It agreed that the District Court had jurisdiction over petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims. Id., at 671. On the merits, the Court of Appeals recognized that the removal issue was \xe2\x80\x9ca question of first impression,\xe2\x80\x9d as neither that court nor this one \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] considered a situation where a restriction on removal passes through two levels of control.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 679. It ruled that the dual restraints on Board members\xe2\x80\x99 removal are permissible because they do not \xe2\x80\x9crender the President unable to perform his constitutional duties.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 683. The majority reasoned that although the President \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not directly select or supervise the Board\xe2\x80\x99s members,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 681, the Board is subject to the comprehensive control of the Commission, and thus the President\xe2\x80\x99s influence over the Commission implies a constitutionally sufficient influence over the Board as well. Id., at 682\xe2\x80\x93683. The majority also held that Board members are inferior officers subject to the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s direction and supervision, id., at 672\xe2\x80\x93676, and that their appointment is otherwise consistent with the Appointments Clause, id., at 676\xe2\x80\x93678.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Kavanaugh dissented. He agreed that the case was one of first impression, id., at 698, but argued that \xe2\x80\x9cthe double for-cause removal provisions in the [Act] \xe2\x80\xa6 combine to eliminate any meaningful Presidential control over the [Board],\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 697. Judge Kavanaugh also argued that Board members are not effectively supervised by the Commission and thus cannot be inferior officers under the Appointments Clause. Id., at 709\xe2\x80\x93712.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We first consider whether the District Court had jurisdiction. We agree with both courts below that the statutes providing for judicial review of Commission action did not prevent the District Court from considering petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sarbanes-Oxley Act empowers the Commission to review any Board rule or sanction. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77217(b)(2)\xe2\x80\x93(4), (c)(2). Once the Commission has acted, aggrieved parties may challenge \xe2\x80\x9ca final order of the Commission\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ca rule of the Commission\xe2\x80\x9d in a court of appeals under \xc2\xa778y, and \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o objection \xe2\x80\xa6 may be considered by the court unless it was urged before the Commission or there was reasonable ground for failure to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778y(a)(1), (b)(1), (c)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government reads \xc2\xa778y as an exclusive route to review. But the text does not expressly limit the jurisdiction that other statutes confer on district courts. See, e.g., 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71331, 2201. Nor does it do so implicitly. Provisions for agency review do not restrict judicial review unless the \xe2\x80\x9cstatutory scheme\xe2\x80\x9d displays a \xe2\x80\x9cfairly discernible\xe2\x80\x9d intent to limit jurisdiction, and the claims at issue \xe2\x80\x9care of the type Congress intended to be reviewed within th[e] statutory structure.\xe2\x80\x9d Thunder Basin Coal Co. v. Reich, 510 U. S. 200, 207, 212 (1994) (internal quotation marks omitted). Generally, when Congress creates procedures \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to permit agency expertise to be brought to bear on particular problems,\xe2\x80\x9d those procedures \xe2\x80\x9care to be exclusive.\xe2\x80\x9d Whitney Nat. Bank in Jefferson Parish v. Bank of New Orleans & Trust Co., 379 U. S. 411, 420 (1965). But we presume that Congress does not intend to limit jurisdiction if \xe2\x80\x9ca finding of preclusion could foreclose all meaningful judicial review\xe2\x80\x9d; if the suit is \xe2\x80\x9cwholly collateral to a statute\xe2\x80\x99s review provisions\xe2\x80\x9d; and if the claims are \xe2\x80\x9coutside the agency\xe2\x80\x99s expertise.\xe2\x80\x9d Thunder Basin, supra, at 212\xe2\x80\x93213 (internal quotation marks omitted). These considerations point against any limitation on review here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not see how petitioners could meaningfully pursue their constitutional claims under the Government\xe2\x80\x99s theory. Section 78y provides only for judicial review of Commission action, and not every Board action is encapsulated in a final Commission order or rule.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government suggests that petitioners could first have sought Commission review of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cauditing standards, registration requirements, or other rules.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 16. But petitioners object to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s existence, not to any of its auditing standards. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 general challenge to the Board is \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral\xe2\x80\x9d to any Commission orders or rules from which review might be sought. Cf. McNary v. Haitian Refugee Center, Inc., 498 U. S. 479, 491\xe2\x80\x93492 (1991). Requiring petitioners to select and challenge a Board rule at random is an odd procedure for Congress to choose, especially because only new rules, and not existing ones, are subject to challenge. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778s(b)(2), 78y(a)(1), 7217(b)(4).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Alternatively, the Government advises petitioners to raise their claims by appealing a Board sanction. Brief for United States 16\xe2\x80\x9317. But the investigation of Beckstead and Watts produced no sanction, see id., at 7, n.\xc2\xa05; Reply Brief for Petitioners 29, n.\xc2\xa011 (hereinafter Reply Brief), and an uncomplimentary inspection report is not subject to judicial review, see \xc2\xa77214(h)(2). So the Government proposes that Beckstead and Watts incur a sanction (such as a sizable fine) by ignoring Board requests for documents and testimony. Brief for United States 17. If the Commission then affirms, the firm will win access to a court of appeals\xe2\x80\x94and severe punishment should its challenge fail. We normally do not require plaintiffs to \xe2\x80\x9cbet the farm \xe2\x80\xa6 by taking the violative action\xe2\x80\x9d before \xe2\x80\x9ctesting the validity of the law,\xe2\x80\x9d MedImmune, Inc. v. Genentech, Inc., 549 U. S. 118, 129 (2007); accord, Ex parte Young, 209 U. S. 123 (1908), and we do not consider this a \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningful\xe2\x80\x9d avenue of relief. Thunder Basin, 510 U. S., at 212.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims are also outside the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s competence and expertise. In Thunder Basin, the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s primary claims were statutory; \xe2\x80\x9cat root \xe2\x80\xa6 [they] ar[o]se under the Mine Act and f[e]ll squarely within the [agency\xe2\x80\x99s] expertise,\xe2\x80\x9d given that the agency had \xe2\x80\x9cextensive experience\xe2\x80\x9d on the issue and had \xe2\x80\x9crecently addressed the precise \xe2\x80\xa6 claims presented.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 214\xe2\x80\x93215. Likewise, in United States v. Ruzicka, 329 U. S. 287 (1946), on which the Government relies, we reserved for the agency fact-bound inquiries that, even if \xe2\x80\x9cformulated in constitutional terms,\xe2\x80\x9d rested ultimately on \xe2\x80\x9cfactors that call for [an] understanding of the milk industry,\xe2\x80\x9d to which the Court made no pretensions. Id., at 294. No similar expertise is required here, and the statutory questions involved do not require \xe2\x80\x9ctechnical considerations of [agency] policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Johnson v. Robison, 415 U. S. 361, 373 (1974). They are instead standard questions of administrative law, which the courts are at no disadvantage in answering.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We therefore conclude that \xc2\xa778y did not strip the District Court of jurisdiction over these claims, which are properly presented for our review.[Footnote 2]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that the dual for-cause limitations on the removal of Board members contravene the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s separation of powers.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Constitution provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he executive Power shall be vested in a President of the United States of America.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa71, cl.\xc2\xa01. As Madison stated on the floor of the First Congress, \xe2\x80\x9cif any power whatsoever is in its nature Executive, it is the power of appointing, overseeing, and controlling those who execute the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Annals of Cong. 463 (1789).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The removal of executive officers was discussed extensively in Congress when the first executive departments were created. The view that \xe2\x80\x9cprevailed, as most consonant to the text of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cto the requisite responsibility and harmony in the Executive Department,\xe2\x80\x9d was that the executive power included a power to oversee executive officers through removal; because that traditional executive power was not \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly taken away, it remained with the President.\xe2\x80\x9d Letter from James Madison to Thomas Jefferson (June 30, 1789), 16 Documentary History of the First Federal Congress 893 (2004). \xe2\x80\x9cThis Decision of 1789 provides contemporaneous and weighty evidence of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s meaning since many of the Members of the First Congress had taken part in framing that instrument.\xe2\x80\x9d Bowsher v. Synar, 478 U. S. 714, 723\xe2\x80\x93724 (1986) (internal quotation marks omitted). And it soon became the \xe2\x80\x9csettled and well understood construction of the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte Hennen, 13 Pet. 230, 259 (1839).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The landmark case of Myers v. United States reaffirmed the principle that Article II confers on the President \xe2\x80\x9cthe general administrative control of those executing the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d 272 U. S., at 164. It is his responsibility to take care that the laws be faithfully executed. The buck stops with the President, in Harry Truman\xe2\x80\x99s famous phrase. As we explained in Myers, the President therefore must have some \xe2\x80\x9cpower of removing those for whom he can not continue to be responsible.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 117.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nearly a decade later in Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, this Court held that Myers did not prevent Congress from conferring good-cause tenure on the principal officers of certain independent agencies. That case concerned the members of the Federal Trade Commission, who held 7-year terms and could not be removed by the President except for \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 295 U. S., at 620 (quoting 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa741). The Court distinguished Myers on the ground that Myers concerned \xe2\x80\x9can officer [who] is merely one of the units in the executive department and, hence, inherently subject to the exclusive and illimitable power of removal by the Chief Executive, whose subordinate and aid he is.\xe2\x80\x9d 295 U. S., at 627. By contrast, the Court characterized the FTC as \xe2\x80\x9cquasi-legislative and quasi-judicial\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cpurely executive,\xe2\x80\x9d and held that Congress could require it \xe2\x80\x9cto act \xe2\x80\xa6 independently of executive control.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 627\xe2\x80\x93629. Because \xe2\x80\x9cone who holds his office only during the pleasure of another, cannot be depended upon to maintain an attitude of independence against the latter\xe2\x80\x99s will,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court held that Congress had power to \xe2\x80\x9cfix the period during which [the Commissioners] shall continue in office, and to forbid their removal except for cause in the meantime.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 629.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor did not address the removal of inferior officers, whose appointment Congress may vest in heads of departments. If Congress does so, it is ordinarily the department head, rather than the President, who enjoys the power of removal. See Myers, supra, at 119, 127; Hennen, supra, at 259\xe2\x80\x93260. This Court has upheld for-cause limitations on that power as well.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Perkins, a naval cadet-engineer was honorably discharged from the Navy because his services were no longer required. 116 U. S. 483. He brought a claim for his salary under statutes barring his peacetime discharge except by a court-martial or by the Secretary of the Navy \xe2\x80\x9cfor misconduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71229, 1525. This Court adopted verbatim the reasoning of the Court of Claims, which had held that when Congress \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98vests the appointment of inferior officers in the heads of Departments[,] it may limit and restrict the power of removal as it deems best for the public interest.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 116 U. S., at 485. Because Perkins had not been \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98dismissed for misconduct \xe2\x80\xa6 [or upon] the sentence of a court-martial,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the Court agreed that he was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98still in office and \xe2\x80\xa6 entitled to [his] pay.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We again considered the status of inferior officers in Morrison. That case concerned the Ethics in Government Act, which provided for an independent counsel to investigate allegations of crime by high executive officers. The counsel was appointed by a special court, wielded the full powers of a prosecutor, and was removable by the Attorney General only \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98for good cause.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 487 U. S., at 663 (quoting 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7596(a)(1)). We recognized that the independent counsel was undoubtedly an executive officer, rather than \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98quasi-legislative\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98quasi-judicial,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but we stated as \xe2\x80\x9cour present considered view\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress had power to impose good-cause restrictions on her removal. 487 U. S., at 689\xe2\x80\x93691. The Court noted that the statute \xe2\x80\x9cg[a]ve the Attorney General,\xe2\x80\x9d an officer directly responsible to the President and \xe2\x80\x9cthrough [whom]\xe2\x80\x9d the President could act, \xe2\x80\x9cseveral means of supervising or controlling\xe2\x80\x9d the independent counsel\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c[m]ost importantly \xe2\x80\xa6 the power to remove the counsel for good cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 695\xe2\x80\x93696 (internal quotation marks omitted). Under those circumstances, the Court sustained the statute. Morrison did not, however, address the consequences of more than one level of good-cause tenure\xe2\x80\x94leaving the issue, as both the court and dissent below recognized, \xe2\x80\x9ca question of first impression\xe2\x80\x9d in this Court. 537 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 679; see id., at 698 (dissenting opinion).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As explained, we have previously upheld limited restrictions on the President\xe2\x80\x99s removal power. In those cases, however, only one level of protected tenure separated the President from an officer exercising executive power. It was the President\xe2\x80\x94or a subordinate he could remove at will\xe2\x80\x94who decided whether the officer\xe2\x80\x99s conduct merited removal under the good-cause standard.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Act before us does something quite different. It not only protects Board members from removal except for good cause, but withdraws from the President any decision on whether that good cause exists. That decision is vested instead in other tenured officers\xe2\x80\x94the Commissioners\xe2\x80\x94none of whom is subject to the President\xe2\x80\x99s direct control. The result is a Board that is not accountable to the President, and a President who is not responsible for the Board.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The added layer of tenure protection makes a difference. Without a layer of insulation between the Commission and the Board, the Commission could remove a Board member at any time, and therefore would be fully responsible for what the Board does. The President could then hold the Commission to account for its supervision of the Board, to the same extent that he may hold the Commission to account for everything else it does.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A second level of tenure protection changes the nature of the President\xe2\x80\x99s review. Now the Commission cannot remove a Board member at will. The President therefore cannot hold the Commission fully accountable for the Board\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, to the same extent that he may hold the Commission accountable for everything else that it does. The Commissioners are not responsible for the Board\xe2\x80\x99s actions. They are only responsible for their own determination of whether the Act\xe2\x80\x99s rigorous good-cause standard is met. And even if the President disagrees with their determination, he is powerless to intervene\xe2\x80\x94unless that determination is so unreasonable as to constitute \xe2\x80\x9cinefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office.\xe2\x80\x9d Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, 295 U. S., at 620 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This novel structure does not merely add to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s independence, but transforms it. Neither the President, nor anyone directly responsible to him, nor even an officer whose conduct he may review only for good cause, has full control over the Board. The President is stripped of the power our precedents have preserved, and his ability to execute the laws\xe2\x80\x94by holding his subordinates accountable for their conduct\xe2\x80\x94is impaired.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That arrangement is contrary to Article II\xe2\x80\x99s vesting of the executive power in the President. Without the ability to oversee the Board, or to attribute the Board\xe2\x80\x99s failings to those whom he can oversee, the President is no longer the judge of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. He is not the one who decides whether Board members are abusing their offices or neglecting their duties. He can neither ensure that the laws are faithfully executed, nor be held responsible for a Board member\xe2\x80\x99s breach of faith. This violates the basic principle that the President \xe2\x80\x9ccannot delegate ultimate responsibility or the active obligation to supervise that goes with it,\xe2\x80\x9d because Article II \xe2\x80\x9cmakes a single President responsible for the actions of the Executive Branch.\xe2\x80\x9d Clinton v. Jones, 520 U. S. 681, 712\xe2\x80\x93713 (1997) (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment).[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, if allowed to stand, this dispersion of responsibility could be multiplied. If Congress can shelter the bureaucracy behind two layers of good-cause tenure, why not a third? At oral argument, the Government was unwilling to concede that even five layers between the President and the Board would be too many. Tr. of Oral Arg. 47\xe2\x80\x9348. The officers of such an agency\xe2\x80\x94safely encased within a Matryoshka doll of tenure protections\xe2\x80\x94would be immune from Presidential oversight, even as they exercised power in the people\xe2\x80\x99s name.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Perhaps an individual President might find advantages in tying his own hands. But the separation of powers does not depend on the views of individual Presidents, see Freytag v. Commissioner, 501 U. S. 868, 879\xe2\x80\x93880 (1991), nor on whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe encroached-upon branch approves the encroachment,\xe2\x80\x9d New York v. United States, 505 U. S. 144, 182 (1992). The President can always choose to restrain himself in his dealings with subordinates. He cannot, however, choose to bind his successors by diminishing their powers, nor can he escape responsibility for his choices by pretending that they are not his own.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The diffusion of power carries with it a diffusion of accountability. The people do not vote for the \xe2\x80\x9cOfficers of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02. They instead look to the President to guide the \xe2\x80\x9cassistants or deputies \xe2\x80\xa6 subject to his superintendence.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 72, p. 487 (J. Cooke ed. 1961) (A. Hamilton). Without a clear and effective chain of command, the public cannot \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine on whom the blame or the punishment of a pernicious measure, or series of pernicious measures ought really to fall.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., No. 70, at 476 (same). That is why the Framers sought to ensure that \xe2\x80\x9cthose who are employed in the execution of the law will be in their proper situation, and the chain of dependence be preserved; the lowest officers, the middle grade, and the highest, will depend, as they ought, on the President, and the President on the community.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Annals of Cong., at 499 (J. Madison).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By granting the Board executive power without the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s oversight, this Act subverts the President\xe2\x80\x99s ability to ensure that the laws are faithfully executed\xe2\x80\x94as well as the public\xe2\x80\x99s ability to pass judgment on his efforts. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s restrictions are incompatible with the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s separation of powers.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents and the dissent resist this conclusion, portraying the Board as \xe2\x80\x9cthe kind of practical accommodation between the Legislature and the Executive that should be permitted in a \xe2\x80\x98workable government.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Metropolitan Washington Airports Authority v. Citizens for Abatement of Aircraft Noise, Inc., 501 U. S. 252, 276 (1991) (MWAA) (quoting Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer, 343 U. S. 579, 635 (1952) (Jackson, J., concurring)); see, e.g., post, at 6 (opinion of Breyer, J.). According to the dissent, Congress may impose multiple levels of for-cause tenure between the President and his subordinates when it \xe2\x80\x9crests agency independence upon the need for technical expertise.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 18. The Board\xe2\x80\x99s mission is said to demand both \xe2\x80\x9ctechnical competence\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9capolitical expertise,\xe2\x80\x9d and its powers may only be exercised by \xe2\x80\x9ctechnical professional experts.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 18 (internal quotation marks omitted). In this respect the statute creating the Board is, we are told, simply one example of the \xe2\x80\x9cvast numbers of statutes governing vast numbers of subjects, concerned with vast numbers of different problems, [that] provide for, or foresee, their execution or administration through the work of administrators organized within many different kinds of administrative structures, exercising different kinds of administrative authority, to achieve their legislatively mandated objectives.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 8.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No one doubts Congress\xe2\x80\x99s power to create a vast and varied federal bureaucracy. But where, in all this, is the role for oversight by an elected President? The Constitution requires that a President chosen by the entire Nation oversee the execution of the laws. And the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fact that a given law or procedure is efficient, convenient, and useful in facilitating functions of government, standing alone, will not save it if it is contrary to the Constitution,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[c]onvenience and efficiency are not the primary objectives\xe2\x80\x94or the hallmarks\xe2\x80\x94of democratic government.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bowsher, 478 U. S., at 736 (quoting Chadha, 462 U. S., at 944).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One can have a government that functions without being ruled by functionaries, and a government that benefits from expertise without being ruled by experts. Our Constitution was adopted to enable the people to govern themselves, through their elected leaders. The growth of the Executive Branch, which now wields vast power and touches almost every aspect of daily life, heightens the concern that it may slip from the Executive\xe2\x80\x99s control, and thus from that of the people. This concern is largely absent from the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s paean to the administrative state.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For example, the dissent dismisses the importance of removal as a tool of supervision, concluding that the President\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cpower to get something done\xe2\x80\x9d more often depends on \xe2\x80\x9cwho controls the agency\xe2\x80\x99s budget requests and funding, the relationships between one agency or department and another, \xe2\x80\xa6 purely political factors (including Congress\xe2\x80\x99 ability to assert influence),\xe2\x80\x9d and indeed whether particular unelected officials support or \xe2\x80\x9cresist\xe2\x80\x9d the President\xe2\x80\x99s policies. Post, at 11, 13 (emphasis deleted). The Framers did not rest our liberties on such bureaucratic minutiae. As we said in Bowsher, supra, at 730, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he separated powers of our Government cannot be permitted to turn on judicial assessment of whether an officer exercising executive power is on good terms with Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In fact, the multilevel protection that the dissent endorses \xe2\x80\x9cprovides a blueprint for extensive expansion of the legislative power.\xe2\x80\x9d MWAA, supra, at 277. In a system of checks and balances, \xe2\x80\x9c[p]ower abhors a vacuum,\xe2\x80\x9d and one branch\xe2\x80\x99s handicap is another\xe2\x80\x99s strength. 537 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 695, n.\xc2\xa04 (Kavanaugh, J., dissenting) (internal quotation marks omitted). \xe2\x80\x9cEven when a branch does not arrogate power to itself,\xe2\x80\x9d therefore, it must not \xe2\x80\x9cimpair another in the performance of its constitutional duties.\xe2\x80\x9d Loving v. United States, 517 U. S. 748, 757 (1996).[Footnote 5] Congress has plenary control over the salary, duties, and even existence of executive offices. Only Presidential oversight can counter its influence. That is why the Constitution vests certain powers in the President that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Legislature has no right to diminish or modify.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Annals of Cong., at 463 (J. Madison).[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Framers created a structure in which \xe2\x80\x9c[a] dependence on the people\xe2\x80\x9d would be the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary controul on the government.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 51, at 349 (J. Madison). That dependence is maintained, not just by \xe2\x80\x9cparchment barriers,\xe2\x80\x9d id., No. 48, at 333 (same), but by letting \xe2\x80\x9c[a]mbition \xe2\x80\xa6 counteract ambition,\xe2\x80\x9d giving each branch \xe2\x80\x9cthe necessary constitutional means, and personal motives, to resist encroachments of the others,\xe2\x80\x9d id., No. 51, at 349. A key \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional means\xe2\x80\x9d vested in the President\xe2\x80\x94perhaps the key means\xe2\x80\x94was \xe2\x80\x9cthe power of appointing, overseeing, and controlling those who execute the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Annals of Cong., at 463. And while a government of \xe2\x80\x9copposite and rival interests\xe2\x80\x9d may sometimes inhibit the smooth functioning of administration, The Federalist No. 51, at 349, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Framers recognized that, in the long term, structural protections against abuse of power were critical to preserving liberty.\xe2\x80\x9d Bowsher, supra, at 730.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Calls to abandon those protections in light of \xe2\x80\x9cthe era\xe2\x80\x99s perceived necessity,\xe2\x80\x9d New York, 505 U. S., at 187, are not unusual. Nor is the argument from bureaucratic expertise limited only to the field of accounting. The failures of accounting regulation may be a \xe2\x80\x9cpressing national problem,\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9ca judiciary that licensed extraconstitutional government with each issue of comparable gravity would, in the long run, be far worse.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 187\xe2\x80\x93188. Neither respondents nor the dissent explains why the Board\xe2\x80\x99s task, unlike so many others, requires more than one layer of insulation from the President\xe2\x80\x94or, for that matter, why only two. The point is not to take issue with for-cause limitations in general; we do not do that. The question here is far more modest. We deal with the unusual situation, never before addressed by the Court, of two layers of for-cause tenure. And though it may be criticized as \xe2\x80\x9celementary arithmetical logic,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 23, two layers are not the same as one.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The President has been given the power to oversee executive officers; he is not limited, as in Harry Truman\xe2\x80\x99s lament, to \xe2\x80\x9cpersuad[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d his unelected subordinates \xe2\x80\x9cto do what they ought to do without persuasion.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 11 (internal quotation marks omitted). In its pursuit of a \xe2\x80\x9cworkable government,\xe2\x80\x9d Congress cannot reduce the Chief Magistrate to a cajoler-in-chief.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States concedes that some constraints on the removal of inferior executive officers might violate the Constitution. See Brief for United States 47. It contends, however, that the removal restrictions at issue here do not.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To begin with, the Government argues that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s removal power over the Board is \xe2\x80\x9cbroad,\xe2\x80\x9d and could be construed as broader still, if necessary to avoid invalidation. See, e.g., id., at 51, and n.\xc2\xa019; cf. PCAOB Brief 22\xe2\x80\x9323. But the Government does not contend that simple disagreement with the Board\xe2\x80\x99s policies or priorities could constitute \xe2\x80\x9cgood cause\xe2\x80\x9d for its removal. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 41\xe2\x80\x9343, 45\xe2\x80\x9346. Nor do our precedents suggest as much. Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, for example, rejected a removal premised on a lack of agreement \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98on either the policies or the administering of the Federal Trade Commission,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d because the FTC was designed to be \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98independent in character,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfree from \xe2\x80\x98political domination or control,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98subject to anybody in the government\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to the orders of the President.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 295 U. S., at 619, 625. Accord, Morrison, 487 U. S., at 693 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cthe congressional determination to limit the removal power of the Attorney General was essential . . . to establish the necessary independence of the office\xe2\x80\x9d); Wiener v. United States, 357 U. S. 349, 356 (1958) (describing for-cause removal as \xe2\x80\x9cinvolving the rectitude\xe2\x80\x9d of an officer). And here there is judicial review of any effort to remove Board members, see 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778y(a)(1), so the Commission will not have the final word on the propriety of its own removal orders. The removal restrictions set forth in the statute mean what they say.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, this case presents an even more serious threat to executive control than an \xe2\x80\x9cordinary\xe2\x80\x9d dual for-cause standard. Congress enacted an unusually high standard that must be met before Board members may be removed. A Board member cannot be removed except for willful violations of the Act, Board rules, or the securities laws; willful abuse of authority; or unreasonable failure to enforce compliance\xe2\x80\x94as determined in a formal Commission order, rendered on the record and after notice and an opportunity for a hearing. \xc2\xa77217(d)(3); see \xc2\xa778y(a). The Act does not even give the Commission power to fire Board members for violations of other laws that do not relate to the Act, the securities laws, or the Board\xe2\x80\x99s authority. The President might have less than full confidence in, say, a Board member who cheats on his taxes; but that discovery is not listed among the grounds for removal under \xc2\xa77217(d)(3).[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The rigorous standard that must be met before a Board member may be removed was drawn from statutes concerning private organizations like the New York Stock Exchange. Cf. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778s(h)(4), 7217(d)(3). While we need not decide the question here, a removal standard appropriate for limiting Government control over private bodies may be inappropriate for officers wielding the executive power of the United States.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Alternatively, respondents portray the Act\xe2\x80\x99s limitations on removal as irrelevant, because\xe2\x80\x94as the Court of Appeals held\xe2\x80\x94the Commission wields \xe2\x80\x9cat-will removal power over Board functions if not Board members.\xe2\x80\x9d 537 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 683 (emphasis added); accord, Brief for United States 27\xe2\x80\x9328; PCAOB Brief 48. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s general \xe2\x80\x9coversight and enforcement authority over the Board,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77217(a), is said to \xe2\x80\x9cblun[t] the constitutional impact of for-cause removal,\xe2\x80\x9d 537 F. 3d, at 683, and to leave the President no worse off than \xe2\x80\x9cif Congress had lodged the Board\xe2\x80\x99s functions in the SEC\xe2\x80\x99s own staff,\xe2\x80\x9d PCAOB Brief 15.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Broad power over Board functions is not equivalent to the power to remove Board members. The Commission may, for example, approve the Board\xe2\x80\x99s budget, \xc2\xa77219(b), issue binding regulations, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77202(a), 7217(b)(5), relieve the Board of authority, \xc2\xa77217(d)(1), amend Board sanctions, \xc2\xa77217(c), or enforce Board rules on its own, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77202(b)(1), (c). But altering the budget or powers of an agency as a whole is a problematic way to control an inferior officer. The Commission cannot wield a free hand to supervise individual members if it must destroy the Board in order to fix it.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if Commission power over Board activities could substitute for authority over its members, we would still reject respondents\xe2\x80\x99 premise that the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s power in this regard is plenary. As described above, the Board is empowered to take significant enforcement actions, and does so largely independently of the Commission. See supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. Its powers are, of course, subject to some latent Commission control. See supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. But the Act nowhere gives the Commission effective power to start, stop, or alter individual Board investigations, executive activities typically carried out by officials within the Executive Branch.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government and the dissent suggest that the Commission could govern and direct the Board\xe2\x80\x99s daily exercise of prosecutorial discretion by promulgating new SEC rules, or by amending those of the Board. Brief for United States 27; post, at 15. Enacting general rules through the required notice and comment procedures is obviously a poor means of micromanaging the Board\xe2\x80\x99s affairs. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa778s(c), 7215(b)(1), 7217(b)(5); cf. 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7553, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77202(a), PCAOB Brief 24, n.\xc2\xa06.[Footnote 8] So the Government offers another proposal, that the Commission require the Board by rule to \xe2\x80\x9csecure SEC approval for any actions that it now may take itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 27. That would surely constitute one of the \xe2\x80\x9climitations upon the activities, functions, and operations of the Board\xe2\x80\x9d that the Act forbids, at least without Commission findings equivalent to those required to fire the Board instead. \xc2\xa77217(d)(2). The Board thus has significant independence in determining its priorities and intervening in the affairs of regulated firms (and the lives of their associated persons) without Commission preapproval or direction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, respondents suggest that our conclusion is contradicted by the past practice of Congress. But the Sarbanes-Oxley Act is highly unusual in committing substantial executive authority to officers protected by two layers of for-cause removal\xe2\x80\x94including at one level a sharply circumscribed definition of what constitutes \xe2\x80\x9cgood cause,\xe2\x80\x9d and rigorous procedures that must be followed prior to removal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The parties have identified only a handful of isolated positions in which inferior officers might be protected by two levels of good-cause tenure. See, e.g., PCAOB Brief 43. As Judge Kavanaugh noted in dissent below:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cPerhaps the most telling indication of the severe constitutional problem with the PCAOB is the lack of historical precedent for this entity. Neither the majority opinion nor the PCAOB nor the United States as intervenor has located any historical analogues for this novel structure. They have not identified any independent agency other than the PCAOB that is appointed by and removable only for cause by another independent agency.\xe2\x80\x9d 537 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 669.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent here suggests that other such positions might exist, and complains that we do not resolve their status in this opinion. Post, at 23\xe2\x80\x9331. The dissent itself, however, stresses the very size and variety of the Federal Government, see post, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, and those features discourage general pronouncements on matters neither briefed nor argued here. In any event, the dissent fails to support its premonitions of doom; none of the positions it identifies are similarly situated to the Board. See post, at 28\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For example, many civil servants within independent agencies would not qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cOfficers of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d who \xe2\x80\x9cexercis[e] significant authority pursuant to the laws of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d Buckley, 424 U. S., at 126.[Footnote 9] The parties here concede that Board members are executive \xe2\x80\x9cOfficers,\xe2\x80\x9d as that term is used in the Constitution. See supra, at 4; see also Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02. We do not decide the status of other Government employees, nor do we decide whether \xe2\x80\x9clesser functionaries subordinate to officers of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d must be subject to the same sort of control as those who exercise \xe2\x80\x9csignificant authority pursuant to the laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Buckley, supra, at 126, and n.\xc2\xa0162.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor do the employees referenced by the dissent enjoy the same significant and unusual protections from Presidential oversight as members of the Board. Senior or policymaking positions in government may be excepted from the competitive service to ensure Presidential control, see 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72302(a)(2)(B), 3302, 7511(b)(2), and members of the Senior Executive Service may be reassigned or reviewed by agency heads (and entire agencies may be excluded from that Service by the President), see, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73132(c), 3395(a), 4312(d), 4314(b)(3), (c)(3); cf. \xc2\xa72302(a)(2)(B)(ii). While the full extent of that authority is not before us, any such authority is of course wholly absent with respect to the Board. Nothing in our opinion, therefore, should be read to cast doubt on the use of what is colloquially known as the civil service system within independent agencies.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the dissent wanders far afield when it suggests that today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion might increase the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority to remove military officers. Without expressing any view whatever on the scope of that authority, it is enough to note that we see little analogy between our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s armed services and the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board. Military officers are broadly subject to Presidential control through the chain of command and through the President\xe2\x80\x99s powers as Commander in Chief. Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa01; see, e.g., 10 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7162, 164(g). The President and his subordinates may also convene boards of inquiry or courts-martial to hear claims of misconduct or poor performance by those officers. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7822(a)(1), 823(a)(1), 892(3), 933\xe2\x80\x93934, 1181\xe2\x80\x931185. Here, by contrast, the President has no authority to initiate a Board member\xe2\x80\x99s removal for cause.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no reason for us to address whether these positions identified by the dissent, or any others not at issue in this case, are so structured as to infringe the President\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional authority. Nor is there any substance to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s concern that the \xe2\x80\x9cwork of all these various officials\xe2\x80\x9d will \xe2\x80\x9cbe put on hold.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 31. As the judgment in this case demonstrates, restricting certain officers to a single level of insulation from the President affects the conditions under which those officers might some day be removed, and would have no effect, absent a congressional determination to the contrary, on the validity of any officer\xe2\x80\x99s continuance in office. The only issue in this case is whether Congress may deprive the President of adequate control over the Board, which is the regulator of first resort and the primary law enforcement authority for a vital sector of our economy. We hold that it cannot.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 complaint argued that the Board\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfreedom from Presidential oversight and control\xe2\x80\x9d rendered it \xe2\x80\x9cand all power and authority exercised by it\xe2\x80\x9d in violation of the Constitution. App. 46. We reject such a broad holding. Instead, we agree with the Government that the unconstitutional tenure provisions are severable from the remainder of the statute.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cGenerally speaking, when confronting a constitutional flaw in a statute, we try to limit the solution to the problem,\xe2\x80\x9d severing any \xe2\x80\x9cproblematic portions while leaving the remainder intact.\xe2\x80\x9d Ayotte v. Planned Parenthood of Northern New Eng., 546 U. S. 320, 328\xe2\x80\x93329 (2006). Because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he unconstitutionality of a part of an Act does not necessarily defeat or affect the validity of its remaining provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d Champlin Refining Co. v. Corporation Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Okla., 286 U. S. 210, 234 (1932), the \xe2\x80\x9cnormal rule\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cthat partial, rather than facial, invalidation is the required course,\xe2\x80\x9d Brockett v. Spokane Arcades, Inc., 472 U. S. 491, 504 (1985). Putting to one side petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 Appointments Clause challenges (addressed below), the existence of the Board does not violate the separation of powers, but the substantive removal restrictions imposed by \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77211(e)(6) and 7217(d)(3) do. Under the traditional default rule, removal is incident to the power of appointment. See, e.g., Sampson v. Murray, 415 U. S. 61, 70, n.\xc2\xa017 (1974); Myers, 272 U. S., at 119; Ex parte Hennen, 13 Pet., at 259\xe2\x80\x93260. Concluding that the removal restrictions are invalid leaves the Board removable by the Commission at will, and leaves the President separated from Board members by only a single level of good-cause tenure. The Commission is then fully responsible for the Board\xe2\x80\x99s actions, which are no less subject than the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s own functions to Presidential oversight.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sarbanes-Oxley Act remains \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fully operative as a law\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d with these tenure restrictions excised. New York, 505 U. S., at 186 (quoting Alaska Airlines, Inc. v. Brock, 480 U. S. 678, 684 (1987)). We therefore must sustain its remaining provisions \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nless it is evident that the Legislature would not have enacted those provisions \xe2\x80\xa6 independently of that which is [invalid].\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). Though this inquiry can sometimes be \xe2\x80\x9celusive,\xe2\x80\x9d Chadha, 462 U. S., at 932, the answer here seems clear: The remaining provisions are not \xe2\x80\x9cincapable of functioning independently,\xe2\x80\x9d Alaska Airlines, 480 U. S., at 684, and nothing in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text or historical context makes it \xe2\x80\x9cevident\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress, faced with the limitations imposed by the Constitution, would have preferred no Board at all to a Board whose members are removable at will. Ibid.; see also Ayotte, supra, at 330.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true that the language providing for good-cause removal is only one of a number of statutory provisions that, working together, produce a constitutional violation. In theory, perhaps, the Court might blue-pencil a sufficient number of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s responsibilities so that its members would no longer be \xe2\x80\x9cOfficers of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Or we could restrict the Board\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement powers, so that it would be a purely recommendatory panel. Or the Board members could in future be made removable by the President, for good cause or at will. But such editorial freedom\xe2\x80\x94far more extensive than our holding today\xe2\x80\x94belongs to the Legislature, not the Judiciary. Congress of course remains free to pursue any of these options going forward.

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners raise three more challenges to the Board under the Appointments Clause. None has merit.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, petitioners argue that Board members are principal officers requiring Presidential appointment with the Senate\xe2\x80\x99s advice and consent. We held in Edmond v. United States, 520 U. S. 651, 662\xe2\x80\x93663 (1997), that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether one is an \xe2\x80\x98inferior\xe2\x80\x99 officer depends on whether he has a superior,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98inferior officers\xe2\x80\x99 are officers whose work is directed and supervised at some level\xe2\x80\x9d by other officers appointed by the President with the Senate\xe2\x80\x99s consent. In particular, we noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he power to remove officers\xe2\x80\x9d at will and without cause \xe2\x80\x9cis a powerful tool for control\xe2\x80\x9d of an inferior. Id., at 664. As explained above, the statutory restrictions on the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s power to remove Board members are unconstitutional and void. Given that the Commission is properly viewed, under the Constitution, as possessing the power to remove Board members at will, and given the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s other oversight authority, we have no hesitation in concluding that under Edmond the Board members are inferior officers whose appointment Congress may permissibly vest in a \xe2\x80\x9cHea[d] of Departmen[t].\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But, petitioners argue, the Commission is not a \xe2\x80\x9cDepartmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d like the \xe2\x80\x9cExecutive departments\xe2\x80\x9d (e.g., State, Treasury, Defense) listed in 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7101. In Freytag, 501 U. S., at 887, n. 4, we specifically reserved the question whether a \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal agenc[y], such as \xe2\x80\xa6 the Securities and Exchange Commission,\xe2\x80\x9d is a \xe2\x80\x9cDepartmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d under the Appointments Clause. Four Justices, however, would have concluded that the Commission is indeed such a \xe2\x80\x9cDepartmen[t],\xe2\x80\x9d see id., at 918 (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment), because it is a \xe2\x80\x9cfree-standing, self-contained entity in the Executive Branch,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 915.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents urge us to adopt this reasoning as to those entities not addressed by our opinion in Freytag, see Brief for United States 37\xe2\x80\x9339; PCAOB Brief 30\xe2\x80\x9333, and we do. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 reading of the Appointments Clause is consistent with the common, near-contemporary definition of a \xe2\x80\x9cdepartment\xe2\x80\x9d as a \xe2\x80\x9cseparate allotment or part of business; a distinct province, in which a class of duties are allotted to a particular person.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 N. Webster, American Dictionary of the English Language (1828) (def. 2) (1995 facsimile ed.). It is also consistent with the early practice of Congress, which in 1792 authorized the Postmaster General to appoint \xe2\x80\x9can assistant, and deputy postmasters, at all places where such shall be found necessary,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73, 1 Stat. 234\xe2\x80\x94thus treating him as the \xe2\x80\x9cHea[d] of [a] Departmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d without the title of Secretary or any role in the President\xe2\x80\x99s Cabinet. And it is consistent with our prior cases, which have never invalidated an appointment made by the head of such an establishment. See Freytag, supra, at 917; cf. Burnap v. United States, 252 U. S. 512, 515 (1920); United States v. Germaine, 99 U. S. 508, 511 (1879). Because the Commission is a freestanding component of the Executive Branch, not subordinate to or contained within any other such component, it constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9cDepartmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d for the purposes of the Appointments Clause.[Footnote 11]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But petitioners are not done yet. They argue that the full Commission cannot constitutionally appoint Board members, because only the Chairman of the Commission is the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cHea[d].\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 12] The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s powers, however, are generally vested in the Commissioners jointly, not the Chairman alone. See, e.g., 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa777s, 77t, 78u, 78w. The Commissioners do not report to the Chairman, who exercises administrative and executive functions subject to the full Commission\xe2\x80\x99s policies. See Reorg. Plan No. 10 of 1950, \xc2\xa71(b)(1), 64 Stat. 1265. The Chairman is also appointed from among the Commissioners by the President alone, id., \xc2\xa73, at 1266, which means that he cannot be regarded as \xe2\x80\x9cthe head of an agency\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of the Reorganization Act. See 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7904. (The Commission as a whole, on the other hand, does meet the requirements of the Act, including its provision that \xe2\x80\x9cthe head of an agency [may] be an individual or a commission or board with more than one member.\xe2\x80\x9d)[Footnote 13]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As a constitutional matter, we see no reason why a multimember body may not be the \xe2\x80\x9cHea[d]\xe2\x80\x9d of a \xe2\x80\x9cDepartmen[t]\xe2\x80\x9d that it governs. The Appointments Clause necessarily contemplates collective appointments by the \xe2\x80\x9cCourts of Law,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa02, and each House of Congress, too, appoints its officers collectively, see Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa05; id., \xc2\xa73, cl.\xc2\xa05. Petitioners argue that the Framers vested the nomination of principal officers in the President to avoid the perceived evils of collective appointments, but they reveal no similar concern with respect to inferior officers, whose appointments may be vested elsewhere, including in multimember bodies. Practice has also sanctioned the appointment of inferior officers by multimember agencies. See Freytag, supra, at 918 (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment); see also Classification Act of 1923, ch. 265, \xc2\xa72, 42 Stat. 1488 (defining \xe2\x80\x9cthe head of the department\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cthe officer or group of officers \xe2\x80\xa6 who are not subordinate or responsible to any other officer of the department\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); 37 Op. Atty. Gen. 227, 231 (1933) (endorsing collective appointment by the Civil Service Commission). We conclude that the Board members have been validly appointed by the full Commission.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of the foregoing, petitioners are not entitled to broad injunctive relief against the Board\xe2\x80\x99s continued operations. But they are entitled to declaratory relief sufficient to ensure that the reporting requirements and auditing standards to which they are subject will be enforced only by a constitutional agency accountable to the Executive. See Bowsher, 478 U. S., at 727, n.\xc2\xa05 (concluding that a separation of powers violation may create a \xe2\x80\x9chere-and-now\xe2\x80\x9d injury that can be remedied by a court (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Constitution that makes the President accountable to the people for executing the laws also gives him the power to do so. That power includes, as a general matter, the authority to remove those who assist him in carrying out his duties. Without such power, the President could not be held fully accountable for discharging his own responsibilities; the buck would stop somewhere else. Such diffusion of authority \xe2\x80\x9cwould greatly diminish the intended and necessary responsibility of the chief magistrate himself.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 70, at 478.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While we have sustained in certain cases limits on the President\xe2\x80\x99s removal power, the Act before us imposes a new type of restriction\xe2\x80\x94two levels of protection from removal for those who nonetheless exercise significant executive power. Congress cannot limit the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority in this way.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit is affirmed in part and reversed in part, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court holds unconstitutional a statute providing that the Securities and Exchange Commission can remove members of the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board from office only for cause. It argues that granting the \xe2\x80\x9cinferior officer[s]\xe2\x80\x9d on the Accounting Board \xe2\x80\x9cmore than one level of good-cause protection \xe2\x80\xa6 contravenes the President\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98constitutional obligation to ensure the faithful execution of the laws.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2. I agree that the Accounting Board members are inferior officers. See ante, at 28\xe2\x80\x9329. But in my view the statute does not significantly interfere with the President\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. II, \xc2\xa71. It violates no separation-of-powers principle. And the Court\xe2\x80\x99s contrary holding threatens to disrupt severely the fair and efficient administration of the laws. I consequently dissent.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The legal question before us arises at the intersection of two general constitutional principles. On the one hand, Congress has broad power to enact statutes \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary and proper\xe2\x80\x9d to the exercise of its specifically enumerated constitutional authority. Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl. 18. As Chief Justice Marshall wrote for the Court nearly 200 years ago, the Necessary and Proper Clause reflects the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 efforts to create a Constitution that would \xe2\x80\x9cendure for ages to come.\xe2\x80\x9d McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 415 (1819). It embodies their recognition that it would be \xe2\x80\x9cunwise\xe2\x80\x9d to prescribe \xe2\x80\x9cthe means by which government should, in all future time, execute its powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Such \xe2\x80\x9cimmutable rules\xe2\x80\x9d would deprive the Government of the needed flexibility to respond to future \xe2\x80\x9cexigencies which, if foreseen at all, must have been seen dimly.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Thus the Necessary and Proper Clause affords Congress broad authority to \xe2\x80\x9ccreate\xe2\x80\x9d governmental \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98offices\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and to structure those offices \xe2\x80\x9cas it chooses.\xe2\x80\x9d Buckley v. Valeo, 424 U. S. 1, 138 (1976) (per curiam); cf. Lottery Case, 188 U. S. 321, 355 (1903). And Congress has drawn on that power over the past century to create numerous federal agencies in response to \xe2\x80\x9cvarious crises of human affairs\xe2\x80\x9d as they have arisen. McCulloch, supra, at 415 (emphasis deleted). Cf. Wong Yang Sung v. McGrath, 339 U. S. 33, 36\xe2\x80\x9337 (1950).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the other hand, the opening sections of Articles I, II, and III of the Constitution separately and respectively vest \xe2\x80\x9call legislative Powers\xe2\x80\x9d in Congress, the \xe2\x80\x9cexecutive Power\xe2\x80\x9d in the President, and the \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial Power\xe2\x80\x9d in the Supreme Court (and such \xe2\x80\x9cinferior Courts as Congress may from time to time ordain and establish\xe2\x80\x9d). In doing so, these provisions imply a structural separation-of-powers principle. See, e.g., Miller v. French, 530 U. S. 327, 341\xe2\x80\x93342 (2000). And that principle, along with the instruction in Article II, \xc2\xa73 that the President \xe2\x80\x9cshall take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed,\xe2\x80\x9d limits Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power to structure the Federal Government. See, e.g., INS v. Chadha, 462 U. S. 919, 946 (1983); Freytag v. Commissioner, 501 U. S. 868, 878 (1991); Northern Pipeline Constr. Co. v. Marathon Pipe Line Co., 458 U. S. 50, 64 (1982); Commodity Futures Trading Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Schor, 478 U. S. 833, 859\xe2\x80\x93860 (1986). Indeed, this Court has held that the separation-of-powers principle guarantees the President the authority to dismiss certain Executive Branch officials at will. Myers v. United States, 272 U. S. 52 (1926).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But neither of these two principles is absolute in its application to removal cases. The Necessary and Proper Clause does not grant Congress power to free all Executive Branch officials from dismissal at the will of the President. Ibid. Nor does the separation-of-powers principle grant the President an absolute authority to remove any and all Executive Branch officials at will. Rather, depending on, say, the nature of the office, its function, or its subject matter, Congress sometimes may, consistent with the Constitution, limit the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority to remove an officer from his post. See Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor v. United States, 295 U. S. 602 (1935), overruling in part Myers, supra; Morrison v. Olson, 487 U. S. 654 (1988). And we must here decide whether the circumstances surrounding the statute at issue justify such a limitation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In answering the question presented, we cannot look to more specific constitutional text, such as the text of the Appointments Clause or the Presentment Clause, upon which the Court has relied in other separation-of-powers cases. See, e.g., Chadha, supra, at 946; Buckley, supra, at 124\xe2\x80\x93125. That is because, with the exception of the general \xe2\x80\x9cvesting\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ctake care\xe2\x80\x9d language, the Constitution is completely \xe2\x80\x9csilent with respect to the power of removal from office.\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte Hennen, 13 Pet. 230, 258 (1839); see also Morrison, supra, at 723 (Scalia, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is, of course, no provision in the Constitution stating who may remove executive officers \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does history offer significant help. The President\xe2\x80\x99s power to remove Executive Branch officers \xe2\x80\x9cwas not discussed in the Constitutional Convention.\xe2\x80\x9d Myers, supra, at 109\xe2\x80\x93110. The First Congress enacted federal statutes that limited the President\xe2\x80\x99s ability to oversee Executive Branch officials, including the Comptroller of the United States, federal district attorneys (precursors to today\xe2\x80\x99s United States Attorneys), and, to a lesser extent, the Secretary of the Treasury. See, e.g., Lessig, Readings By Our Unitary Executive, 15 Cardozo L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 175, 183\xe2\x80\x93184 (1993); Teifer, The Constitutionality of Independent Officers as Checks on Abuses of Executive Power, 63 B.\xc2\xa0U. L. Rev. 59, 74\xe2\x80\x9375 (1983); Casper, An Essay in Separation of Powers: Some Early Versions and Practices, 30 Wm. & Mary L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 211, 240\xe2\x80\x93241 (1989) (hereinafter Casper); H. Bruff, Balance of Forces: Separation of Powers in the Administrative State 414\xe2\x80\x93417 (2006). But those statutes did not directly limit the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority to remove any of those officials\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9ca subject\xe2\x80\x9d that was \xe2\x80\x9cmuch disputed\xe2\x80\x9d during \xe2\x80\x9cthe early history of this government,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cand upon which a great diversity of opinion was entertained.\xe2\x80\x9d Hennen, supra, at 259; see also United States ex rel. Goodrich v. Guthrie, 17 How. 284, 306 (1855) (McLean, J., dissenting); Casper 233\xe2\x80\x93237 (recounting the Debate of 1789). Scholars, like Members of this Court, have continued to disagree, not only about the inferences that should be drawn from the inconclusive historical record, but also about the nature of the original disagreement. Compare ante, at 11; Myers, supra, at 114 (majority opinion of Taft, C.\xc2\xa0J.); and Prakash, New Light on the Decision of 1789, 91 Cornell L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1021 (2006), with, e.g., Myers, supra, at 194 (McReynolds, J., dissenting); Corwin, Tenure of Office and the Removal Power Under the Constitution, 27 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 353, 369 (1927); Lessig & Sunstein, The President and the Administration, 94 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 25\xe2\x80\x9326 (1994) (hereinafter Lessig & Sunstein); and L. Fisher, President and Congress: Power and Policy 86\xe2\x80\x9389 (1972).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent fully answer the question presented. At least it does not clearly invalidate the provision in dispute. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93C, infra. In Myers, supra, the Court invalidated\xe2\x80\x94for the first and only time\xe2\x80\x94a congressional statute on the ground that it unduly limited the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority to remove an Executive Branch official. But soon thereafter the Court expressly disapproved most of Myers\xe2\x80\x99 broad reasoning. See Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, 295 U. S., at 626\xe2\x80\x93627, overruling in part Myers, supra; Wiener v. United States, 357 U. S. 349, 352 (1958) (stating that Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor \xe2\x80\x9cexplicitly \xe2\x80\x98disapproved\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of much of the reasoning in Myers). Moreover, the Court has since said that \xe2\x80\x9cthe essence of the decision in Myers was the judgment that the Constitution prevents Congress from \xe2\x80\x98draw[ing] to itself \xe2\x80\xa6 the power to remove or the right to participate in the exercise of that power.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison, supra, at 686 (emphasis added). And that feature of the statute\xe2\x80\x94a feature that would aggrandize the power of Congress\xe2\x80\x94is not present here. Congress has not granted itself any role in removing the members of the Accounting Board. Cf. Freytag, 501 U. S., at 878 (\xe2\x80\x9cseparation-of-powers jurisprudence generally focuses on the danger of one branch\xe2\x80\x99s aggrandizing its power at the expense of another branch\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); Buckley, 424 U. S., at 129 (same); Schor, 478 U. S., at 856 (same); Bowsher v. Synar, 478 U. S. 714, 727 (1986) (same). Compare Myers, supra, (striking down statute where Congress granted itself removal authority over Executive Branch official), with Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, supra, (upholding statute where such aggrandizing was absent); Wiener, supra (same); Morrison, supra (same).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, the question presented lies at the intersection of two sets of conflicting, broadly framed constitutional principles. And no text, no history, perhaps no precedent provides any clear answer. Cf. Chicago v. Morales, 527 U. S. 41, 106 (1999) (Thomas, J., joined by Rehnquist, C.\xc2\xa0J., and Scalia, J., dissenting) (expressing the view that \xe2\x80\x9cthis Court\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cmost vulnerable\xe2\x80\x9d when \xe2\x80\x9cit deals with judge-made constitutional law\xe2\x80\x9d that lacks \xe2\x80\x9croots in the language\xe2\x80\x9d of the Constitution (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When previously deciding this kind of nontextual question, the Court has emphasized the importance of examining how a particular provision, taken in context, is likely to function. Thus, in Crowell v. Benson, 285 U. S. 22, 53 (1932), a foundational separation-of-powers case, the Court said that \xe2\x80\x9cregard must be had, as in other cases where constitutional limits are invoked, not to mere matters of form, but to the substance of what is required.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court repeated this injunction in Schor and again in Morrison. See Schor, supra, at 854 (stating that the Court must look \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98beyond form to the substance of what\xe2\x80\x99 Congress has done\xe2\x80\x9d); Morrison, 487 U. S., at 689\xe2\x80\x93690 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe analysis contained in our removal cases is designed not to define rigid categories of those officials who may or may not be removed at will by the President,\xe2\x80\x9d but rather asks whether, given the \xe2\x80\x9cfunctions of the officials in question,\xe2\x80\x9d a removal provision \xe2\x80\x9cinterfere[s] with the President\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of the \xe2\x80\x98executive power\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). The Court has thereby written into law Justice Jackson\xe2\x80\x99s wise perception that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Constitution \xe2\x80\xa6 contemplates that practice will integrate the dispersed powers into a workable government.\xe2\x80\x9d Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer, 343 U. S. 579, 635 (1952) (opinion concurring in judgment) (emphasis added). See also ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cThe actual art of governing under our Constitution does not and cannot conform to judicial definitions of the power of any of its branches based on isolated clauses or even single Articles torn from context\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is not surprising that the Court in these circumstances has looked to function and context, and not to bright-line rules. For one thing, that approach embodies the intent of the Framers. As Chief Justice Marshall long ago observed, our Constitution is fashioned so as to allow the three coordinate branches, including this Court, to exercise practical judgment in response to changing conditions and \xe2\x80\x9cexigencies,\xe2\x80\x9d which at the time of the founding could be seen only \xe2\x80\x9cdimly,\xe2\x80\x9d and perhaps not at all. McCulloch, 4 Wheat., at 415.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For another, a functional approach permits Congress and the President the flexibility needed to adapt statutory law to changing circumstances. That is why the \xe2\x80\x9cpowers conferred upon the Federal Government by the Constitution were phrased in language broad enough to allow for the expansion of the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s role\xe2\x80\x9d over time. New York v. United States, 505 U. S. 144, 157 (1992). Indeed, the Federal Government at the time of the founding consisted of about 2,000 employees and served a population of about 4 million. See Kaufman, The Growth of the Federal Personnel System, in The Federal Government Service 7, 8 (W.\xc2\xa0Sayre 2d ed. 1965); Dept. of Commerce, Census Bureau, Historical Statistics of the United States: Colonial Times to 1970, pt. 1, p. 8 (1975). Today, however, the Federal Government employs about 4.4 million workers who serve a Nation of more than 310 million people living in a society characterized by rapid technological, economic, and social change. See Office of Management and Budget, Analytical Perspectives, Budget of the U. S. Government, Fiscal Year 2010, p.\xc2\xa0368 (2009).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Federal statutes now require or permit Government officials to provide, regulate, or otherwise administer, not only foreign affairs and defense, but also a wide variety of such subjects as taxes, welfare, social security, medicine, pharmaceutical drugs, education, highways, railroads, electricity, natural gas, nuclear power, financial instruments, banking, medical care, public health and safety, the environment, fair employment practices, consumer protection and much else besides. Those statutes create a host of different organizational structures. Sometimes they delegate administrative authority to the President directly, e.g., 10 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72031(a)(1); 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa75192(c); sometimes they place authority in a long-established Cabinet department, e.g., 7 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71637b(c)(1); 12 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa75221(b)(2) (2006 ed., Supp. II); sometimes they delegate authority to an independent commission or board, e.g., 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa74404(b); 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7994; sometimes they place authority directly in the hands of a single senior administrator, e.g., 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7657d(c)(4); 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7421; sometimes they place it in a sub-cabinet bureau, office, division or other agency, e.g., 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa74048; sometimes they vest it in multimember or multiagency task groups, e.g. 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7593\xe2\x80\x93594; 50 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7402 (2006 ed. and Supp. II); sometimes they vest it in commissions or advisory committees made up of members of more than one branch, e.g., 20 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa742(a); 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7991(a) (2006 ed., Supp. II); 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71975; sometimes they divide it among groups of departments, commissions, bureaus, divisions, and administrators, e.g., 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa79902(a) (2006 ed., Supp. II); 7 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7136i\xe2\x80\x931(g); and sometimes they permit state or local governments to participate as well, e.g., 7 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72009aa\xe2\x80\x931(a). Statutes similarly grant administrators a wide variety of powers\xe2\x80\x94for example, the power to make rules, develop informal practices, investigate, adjudicate, impose sanctions, grant licenses, and provide goods, services, advice, and so forth. See generally 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7500 et seq.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The upshot is that today vast numbers of statutes governing vast numbers of subjects, concerned with vast numbers of different problems, provide for, or foresee, their execution or administration through the work of administrators organized within many different kinds of administrative structures, exercising different kinds of administrative authority, to achieve their legislatively mandated objectives. And, given the nature of the Government\xe2\x80\x99s work, it is not surprising that administrative units come in many different shapes and sizes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The functional approach required by our precedents recognizes this administrative complexity and, more importantly, recognizes the various ways presidential power operates within this context\xe2\x80\x94and the various ways in which a removal provision might affect that power. As human beings have known ever since Ulysses tied himself to the mast so as safely to hear the Sirens\xe2\x80\x99 song, sometimes it is necessary to disable oneself in order to achieve a broader objective. Thus, legally enforceable commitments\xe2\x80\x94such as contracts, statutes that cannot instantly be changed, and, as in the case before us, the establishment of independent administrative institutions\xe2\x80\x94hold the potential to empower precisely because of their ability to constrain. If the President seeks to regulate through impartial adjudication, then insulation of the adjudicator from removal at will can help him achieve that goal. And to free a technical decisionmaker from the fear of removal without cause can similarly help create legitimacy with respect to that official\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory actions by helping to insulate his technical decisions from nontechnical political pressure.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither is power always susceptible to the equations of elementary arithmetic. A rule that takes power from a President\xe2\x80\x99s friends and allies may weaken him. But a rule that takes power from the President\xe2\x80\x99s opponents may strengthen him. And what if the rule takes power from a functionally neutral independent authority? In that case, it is difficult to predict how the President\xe2\x80\x99s power is affected in the abstract.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These practical reasons not only support our precedents\xe2\x80\x99 determination that cases such as this should examine the specific functions and context at issue; they also indicate that judges should hesitate before second-guessing a \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d decision made by the other branches. See, e.g., Chadha, 462 U. S., at 944 (applying a \xe2\x80\x9cpresumption that the challenged statute is valid\xe2\x80\x9d); Bowsher, 478 U. S., at 736 (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment). Compared to Congress and the President, the Judiciary possesses an inferior understanding of the realities of administration, and the manner in which power, including and most especially political power, operates in context.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no indication that the two comparatively more expert branches were divided in their support for the \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provision at issue here. In this case, the Act embodying the provision was passed by a vote of 423 to 3 in the House of Representatives and a by vote of 99 to 0 in the Senate. 148 Cong. Rec. 14458, 14505 (2002). The creation of the Accounting Board was discussed at great length in both bodies without anyone finding in its structure any constitutional problem. See id., at 12035\xe2\x80\x9312037, 12112\xe2\x80\x9312132, 12315\xe2\x80\x9312323, 12372\xe2\x80\x9312377, 12488\xe2\x80\x9312508, 12529\xe2\x80\x9312534, 12612\xe2\x80\x9312618, 12673\xe2\x80\x9312680, 12734\xe2\x80\x9312751, 12915\xe2\x80\x9312960, 13347\xe2\x80\x9313354, 14439\xe2\x80\x9314458, 14487\xe2\x80\x9314506. The President signed the Act. And, when he did so, he issued a signing statement that critiqued multiple provisions of the Act but did not express any separation-of-powers concerns. See President\xe2\x80\x99s Statement on Signing the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, 30 Weekly Comp. of Pres. Doc. 1286 (2002). Cf. ABA, Report of Task Force on Presidential Signing Statements and the Separation of Powers Doctrine 15 (2006), online at http://www.signingstatementsaba_final_signing_\nstatements_recommendations-report_7-24-06.pdf (all Inter-\nnet materials as visited June 24, 2010, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file) (noting that President Bush asserted \xe2\x80\x9cover 500\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cconstitutional objections\xe2\x80\x9d through signing statements \xe2\x80\x9cin his first term,\xe2\x80\x9d including 82 \xe2\x80\x9crelated to his theory of the \xe2\x80\x98unitary executive\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, here, as in similar cases, we should decide the constitutional question in light of the provision\xe2\x80\x99s practical functioning in context. And our decision should take account of the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s comparative lack of institutional expertise.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To what extent then is the Act\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provision likely, as a practical matter, to limit the President\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of executive authority? In practical terms no \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provision can, in isolation, define the full measure of executive power. This is because a legislative decision to place ultimate administrative authority in, say, the Secretary of Agriculture rather than the President, the way in which the statute defines the scope of the power the relevant administrator can exercise, the decision as to who controls the agency\xe2\x80\x99s budget requests and funding, the relationships between one agency or department and another, as well as more purely political factors (including Congress\xe2\x80\x99 ability to assert influence) are more likely to affect the President\xe2\x80\x99s power to get something done. That is why President Truman complained that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the powers of the President amount to\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d bringing \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98people in and try[ing] to persuade them to do what they ought to do without persuasion.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d C. Rossiter, The American Presidency 154 (2d rev. ed. 1960). And that is why scholars have written that the President \xe2\x80\x9cis neither dominant nor powerless\xe2\x80\x9d in his relationships with many Government entities, \xe2\x80\x9cwhether denominated executive or independent.\xe2\x80\x9d Strauss, The Place of Agencies in Government: Separation of Powers and the Fourth Branch, 84 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 573, 583 (1984) (hereinafter Strauss). Those entities \xe2\x80\x9care all subject to presidential direction in significant aspects of their functioning, and [are each] able to resist presidential direction in others.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, notwithstanding the majority\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that the removal authority is \xe2\x80\x9cthe key\xe2\x80\x9d mechanism by which the President oversees inferior officers in the independent agencies, ante, at 20, it appears that no President has ever actually sought to exercise that power by testing the scope of a \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provision. See Bruff, Bringing the Independent Agencies in from the Cold, 62 Vanderbilt L.\xc2\xa0Rev. En Banc 63, 68 (2009), online at http://vanderbiltlawreview.\norg/articles/2009/11/Bruff-62-Vand-L-Rev-En-Banc-63.pdf (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cPresidents do not test the limits of their power by removing commissioners \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Lessig & Sunstein 110\xe2\x80\x93112 (noting that courts have not had occasion to define what constitutes \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d because Presidents rarely test removal provisions).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But even if we put all these other matters to the side, we should still conclude that the \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d restriction before us will not restrict presidential power significantly. For one thing, the restriction directly limits, not the President\xe2\x80\x99s power, but the power of an already independent agency. The Court seems to have forgotten that fact when it identifies its central constitutional problem: According to the Court, the President \xe2\x80\x9cis powerless to intervene\xe2\x80\x9d if he has determined that the Board members\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cconduct merit[s] removal\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hat decision is vested instead in other tenured officers\xe2\x80\x94the Commissioners\xe2\x80\x94none of whom is subject to the President\xe2\x80\x99s direct control.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315. But so long as the President is legitimately foreclosed from removing the Commissioners except for cause (as the majority assumes), nullifying the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s power to remove Board members only for cause will not resolve the problem the Court has identified: The President will still be \xe2\x80\x9cpowerless to intervene\xe2\x80\x9d by removing the Board members if the Commission reasonably decides not to do so.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In other words, the Court fails to show why two layers of \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d protection\xe2\x80\x94Layer One insulating the Commissioners from the President, and Layer Two insulating the Board from the Commissioners\xe2\x80\x94impose any more serious limitation upon the President\xe2\x80\x99s powers than one layer. Consider the four scenarios that might arise:

\n

\n1.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The President and the Commission both want to keep a Board member in office. Neither layer is relevant.

\n

\n2.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The President and the Commission both want to dismiss a Board member. Layer Two stops them both from doing so without cause. The President\xe2\x80\x99s ability to remove the Commission (Layer One) is irrelevant, for he and the Commission are in agreement.

\n

\n3.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The President wants to dismiss a Board member, but the Commission wants to keep the member. Layer One allows the Commission to make that determination notwithstanding the President\xe2\x80\x99s contrary view. Layer Two is irrelevant because the Commission does not seek to remove the Board member.

\n

\n4.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The President wants to keep a Board member, but the Commission wants to dismiss the Board member. Here, Layer Two helps the President, for it hinders the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s ability to dismiss a Board member whom the President wants to keep in place.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision to eliminate only Layer Two accomplishes virtually nothing. And that is because a removal restriction\xe2\x80\x99s effect upon presidential power depends not on the presence of a \xe2\x80\x9cdouble-layer\xe2\x80\x9d of for-cause removal, as the majority pretends, but rather on the real-world nature of the President\xe2\x80\x99s relationship with the Commission. If the President confronts a Commission that seeks to resist his policy preferences\xe2\x80\x94a distinct possibility when, as here, a Commission\xe2\x80\x99s membership must reflect both political parties, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778d(a)\xe2\x80\x94the restriction on the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s ability to remove a Board member is either irrelevant (as in scenario 3) or may actually help the President (as in scenario 4). And if the President faces a Commission that seeks to implement his policy preferences, Layer One is irrelevant, for the President and Commission see eye to eye.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In order to avoid this elementary logic, the Court creates two alternative scenarios. In the first, the Commission and the President both want to remove a Board member, but have varying judgments as to whether they have good \xe2\x80\x9ccause\xe2\x80\x9d to do so\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the President and the Commission both conclude that a Board member should be removed, but disagree as to whether that conclusion (which they have both reached) is reasonable. Ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315. In the second, the President wants to remove a Board member and the Commission disagrees; but, notwithstanding its freedom to make reasonable decisions independent of the President (afforded by Layer One), the Commission (while apparently telling the President that it agrees with him and would like to remove the Board member) uses Layer Two as an \xe2\x80\x9cexcuse\xe2\x80\x9d to pursue its actual aims\xe2\x80\x94an excuse which, given Layer One, it does not need. Ante, at 15, n.\xc2\xa04.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Both of these circumstances seem unusual. I do not know if they have ever occurred. But I do not deny their logical possibility. I simply doubt their importance. And the fact that, with respect to the President\xe2\x80\x99s power, the double layer of for-cause removal sometimes might help, sometimes might hurt, leads me to conclude that its overall effect is at most indeterminate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But once we leave the realm of hypothetical logic and view the removal provision at issue in the context of the entire Act, its lack of practical effect becomes readily apparent. That is because the statute provides the Commission with full authority and virtually comprehensive control over all of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s functions. Those who created the Accounting Board modeled it, in terms of structure and authority, upon the semiprivate regulatory bodies prevalent in the area of financial regulation, such as the New York Stock Exchange and other similar self-regulating organizations. See generally Brief for Former Chairmen of the SEC as Amici Curiae (hereinafter Brief for Former SEC Chairmen). And those organizations\xe2\x80\x94which rely on private financing and on officers drawn from the private sector\xe2\x80\x94exercise rulemaking and adjudicatory authority that is pervasively controlled by, and is indeed \xe2\x80\x9centirely derivative\xe2\x80\x9d of, the SEC. See National Assn. of Securities Dealers, Inc. v. SEC, 431 F.\xc2\xa03d 803, 806 (CADC 2005).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Adhering to that model, the statute here gives the Accounting Board the power to adopt rules and standards \xe2\x80\x9crelating to the preparation of audit reports\xe2\x80\x9d; to adjudicate disciplinary proceedings involving accounting firms that fail to follow these rules; to impose sanctions; and to engage in other related activities, such as conducting inspections of accounting firms registered as the law requires and investigations to monitor compliance with the rules and related legal obligations. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77211\xe2\x80\x937216. But, at the same time,

\n

\n\xe2\x80\xa2\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0No Accounting Board rule takes effect unless and until the Commission approves it, \xc2\xa77217(b)(2);

\n

\n\xe2\x80\xa2\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission may \xe2\x80\x9cabrogat[e], delet[e] or ad[d] to\xe2\x80\x9d any rule or any portion of a rule promulgated by the Accounting Board whenever, in the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s view, doing so \xe2\x80\x9cfurther[s] the purposes\xe2\x80\x9d of the securities and accounting-oversight laws, \xc2\xa77217(b)(5);

\n

\n\xe2\x80\xa2\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission may review any sanction the Board imposes and \xe2\x80\x9cenhance, modify, cancel, reduce, or require the remission of\xe2\x80\x9d that sanction if it find\xe2\x80\x99s the Board\xe2\x80\x99s action not \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77215(e), 7217(c)(3);

\n

\n\xe2\x80\xa2\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission may promulgate rules restricting or directing the Accounting Board\xe2\x80\x99s conduct of all inspections and investigations, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77211(c)(3), 7214(h), 7215(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(4);

\n

\n\xe2\x80\xa2\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission may itself initiate any investigation or promulgate any rule within the Accounting Board\xe2\x80\x99s purview, \xc2\xa77202, and may also remove any Accounting Board member who has unreasonably \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to enforce compliance with\xe2\x80\x9d the relevant \xe2\x80\x9crule[s], or any professional standard,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77217(d)(3)(C) (emphasis added);

\n

\n\xe2\x80\xa2\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Commission may at any time \xe2\x80\x9crelieve the Board of any responsibility to enforce compliance with any provision\xe2\x80\x9d of the Act, the rules, or professional standards if, in the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s view, doing so is in \xe2\x80\x9cthe public interest,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77217(d)(1) (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As these statutory provisions make clear, the Court is simply wrong when it says that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Act nowhere gives the Commission effective power to start, stop, or alter\xe2\x80\x9d Board investigations. Ante, at 23\xe2\x80\x9324. On the contrary, the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s control over the Board\xe2\x80\x99s investigatory and legal functions is virtually absolute. Moreover, the Commission has general supervisory powers over the Accounting Board itself: It controls the Board\xe2\x80\x99s budget, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77219(b), (d)(1); it can assign to the Board any \xe2\x80\x9cduties or functions\xe2\x80\x9d that it \xe2\x80\x9cdetermines are necessary or appropriate,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77211(c)(5); it has full \xe2\x80\x9coversight and enforcement authority over the Board,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa77217(a), including the authority to inspect the Board\xe2\x80\x99s activities whenever it believes it \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate\xe2\x80\x9d to do so, \xc2\xa77217(d)(2) (emphasis added). And it can censure the Board or its members, as well as remove the members from office, if the members, for example, fail to enforce the Act, violate any provisions of the Act, or abuse the authority granted to them under the Act, \xc2\xa77217(d)(3). Cf. Shurtleff v. United States, 189 U. S. 311, 314\xe2\x80\x93319 (1903) (holding that removal authority is not always \xe2\x80\x9crestricted to a removal for th[e] causes\xe2\x80\x9d set forth by statute); Bowsher, 478 U. S., at 729 (rejecting the \xe2\x80\x9carguable premis[e]\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthat the enumeration of certain specified causes of removal excludes the possibility of removal for other causes\xe2\x80\x9d). Contra, ante, at 22, n.\xc2\xa07. See generally Pildes, Putting Power Back into Separation of Powers Analysis: Why the SEC-PCAOB Structure is Constitutional, 62 Vand. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. En Banc 85 (2009), online at http://vanderbiltlawreview.org1/articles/2009/11/Pildes-62-Vand-L-Rev-En-Banc-85.pdf (explaining further the comprehensive nature of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s powers).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What is left? The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s inability to remove a Board member whose perfectly reasonable actions cause the Commission to overrule him with great frequency? What is the practical likelihood of that occurring, or, if it does, of the President\xe2\x80\x99s serious concern about such a matter? Everyone concedes that the President\xe2\x80\x99s control over the Commission is constitutionally sufficient. See Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, 295 U. S. 602; Wiener, 357 U. S. 349; ante, at 1\xe2\x80\x932. And if the President\xe2\x80\x99s control over the Commission is sufficient, and the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s control over the Board is virtually absolute, then, as a practical matter, the President\xe2\x80\x99s control over the Board should prove sufficient as well.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the same time, Congress and the President had good reason for enacting the challenged \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provision. First and foremost, the Board adjudicates cases. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77215. This Court has long recognized the appropriateness of using \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provisions to protect the personal independence of those who even only sometimes engage in adjudicatory functions. Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, supra, at 623\xe2\x80\x93628; see also Wiener, supra, at 355\xe2\x80\x93356; Morrison, 487 U. S., at 690\xe2\x80\x93691, and n. 30; McAllister v. United States, 141 U. S. 174, 191\xe2\x80\x93201 (1891) (Field, J., dissenting). Indeed, as early as 1789 James Madison stated that \xe2\x80\x9cthere may be strong reasons why an\xe2\x80\x9d executive \xe2\x80\x9cofficer\xe2\x80\x9d such as the Comptroller of the United States \xe2\x80\x9cshould not hold his office at the pleasure of the Executive branch\xe2\x80\x9d if one of his \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal dut[ies]\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cpartakes strongly of the judicial character.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Annals of Congress 611\xe2\x80\x93612; cf. ante, at 19, n.\xc2\xa06 (noting that the statute Congress ultimately enacted limited Presidential control over the Comptroller in a different fashion); see supra, at 4. The Court, however, all but ignores the Board\xe2\x80\x99s adjudicatory functions when conducting its analysis. See, e.g., ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. And when it finally does address that central function (in a footnote), it simply asserts that the Board does not \xe2\x80\x9cperform adjudicative \xe2\x80\xa6 functions,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 26, n.\xc2\xa010 (emphasis added), an assertion that is inconsistent with the terms of the statute. See \xc2\xa77215(c)(1) (governing \xe2\x80\x9cproceeding[s] by the Board to determine whether a registered public accounting firm, or an associated person thereof, should be disciplined\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, in addition to their adjudicative functions, the Accounting Board members supervise, and are themselves, technical professional experts. See \xc2\xa77211(e)(1) (requiring that Board members \xe2\x80\x9chave a demonstrated\xe2\x80\x9d technical \xe2\x80\x9cunderstanding of the responsibilities\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cobligations of accountants with respect to the preparation and issuance of audit reports\xe2\x80\x9d). This Court has recognized that the \xe2\x80\x9cdifficulties involved in the preparation of\xe2\x80\x9d sound auditing reports require the application of \xe2\x80\x9cscientific accounting principles.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Anderson, 269 U. S. 422, 440 (1926). And this Court has recognized the constitutional legitimacy of a justification that rests agency independence upon the need for technical expertise. See Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, supra, at 624\xe2\x80\x93626; see also Breger & Edles, Established by Practice: The Theory and Operation of Independent Federal Agencies, 52 Admin. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1111, 1131\xe2\x80\x931133 (2000) (explaining how the need for administrators with \xe2\x80\x9ctechnical competence,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9capolitical expertise,\xe2\x80\x9d and skill in \xe2\x80\x9cscientific management\xe2\x80\x9d led to original creation of independent agencies) (hereinafter Breger & Edles); J. Landis, The Administrative Process 23 (1938) (similar); Woodrow Wilson, Democracy and Efficiency, 87 Atlantic Monthly 289, 299 (1901) (describing need for insulation of experts from political influences).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, the justification for insulating the \xe2\x80\x9ctechnical experts\xe2\x80\x9d on the Board from fear of losing their jobs due to political influence is particularly strong. Congress deliberately sought to provide that kind of protection. See, e.g., 148 Cong. Rec. 12036, 12115, 13352\xe2\x80\x9313355. It did so for good reason. See ante, at 3 (noting that the Accounting Board was created in response to \xe2\x80\x9ca series of celebrated accounting debacles\xe2\x80\x9d); H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 107\xe2\x80\x93414, pp. 18\xe2\x80\x9319 (2002) (same); Brief for Former SEC Chairmen 8\xe2\x80\x939. And historically, this regulatory subject matter\xe2\x80\x94financial regulation\xe2\x80\x94has been thought to exhibit a particular need for independence. See e.g., 51 Cong. Rec. 8857 (1914) (remarks of Sen. Morgan upon creation of the Federal Trade Commission) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is unsafe for an \xe2\x80\xa6 administrative officer representing a great political party \xe2\x80\xa6 to hold the power of life and death over the great business interests of this country\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. That is \xe2\x80\xa6 why I believe in \xe2\x80\xa6 taking these business matters out of politics\xe2\x80\x9d). And Congress, by, for example, providing the Board with a revenue stream independent of the congressional appropriations process, \xc2\xa77219, helped insulate the Board from congressional, as well as other, political influences. See, e.g., 148 Cong. Rec. 12036 (statement of Sen. Stabenow).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, Congress and the President could reasonably have thought it prudent to insulate the adjudicative Board members from fear of purely politically based removal. Cf. Civil Service Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. Letter Carriers, 413 U. S. 548, 565 (1973) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is not only important that the Government and its employees in fact avoid practicing political justice, but it is also critical that they appear to the public to be avoiding it, if confidence in the system of representative Government is not to be eroded to a disastrous extent\xe2\x80\x9d). And in a world in which we count on the Federal Government to regulate matters as complex as, say, nuclear-power production, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that we should simply learn to get by \xe2\x80\x9cwithout being\xe2\x80\x9d regulated \xe2\x80\x9cby experts\xe2\x80\x9d is, at best, unrealistic\xe2\x80\x94at worst, dangerously so. Ante, at 18.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Where a \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provision is so unlikely to restrict presidential power and so likely to further a legitimate institutional need, precedent strongly supports its constitutionality. First, in considering a related issue in Nixon v. Administrator of General Services, 433 U. S. 425 (1977), the Court made clear that when \xe2\x80\x9cdetermining whether the Act disrupts the proper balance between the coordinate branches, the proper inquiry focuses on the extent to which it prevents the Executive Branch from accomplishing its constitutionally assigned functions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 443. The Court said the same in Morrison, where it upheld a restriction on the President\xe2\x80\x99s removal power. 487 U. S., at 691 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he real question is whether the removal restrictions are of such a nature that they impede the President\xe2\x80\x99s ability to perform his constitutional duty, and the functions of the officials in question must be analyzed in that light\xe2\x80\x9d). Here, the removal restriction may somewhat diminish the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s ability to control the Board, but it will have little, if any, negative effect in respect to the President\xe2\x80\x99s ability to control the Board, let alone to coordinate the Executive Branch. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93A, supra. Indeed, given Morrison, where the Court upheld a restriction that significantly interfered with the President\xe2\x80\x99s important historic power to control criminal prosecutions, a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98purely executive\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d function, 487 U. S., at 687\xe2\x80\x93689, the constitutionality of the present restriction would seem to follow a fortiori.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, as previously pointed out, this Court has repeatedly upheld \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provisions where they restrict the President\xe2\x80\x99s power to remove an officer with adjudicatory responsibilities. Compare Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, 295 U. S., at 623\xe2\x80\x93628; Wiener, 357 U. S., at 355; Schor, 478 U. S., at 854; Morrison, supra, at 691, n. 30, with ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318 (ignoring these precedents). And we have also upheld such restrictions when they relate to officials with technical responsibilities that warrant a degree of special independence. E.g., Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, supra, at 624. The Accounting Board\xe2\x80\x99s functions involve both kinds of responsibility. And, accordingly, the Accounting Board\xe2\x80\x99s adjudicatory responsibilities, the technical nature of its job, the need to attract experts to that job, and the importance of demonstrating the nonpolitical nature of the job to the public strongly justify a statute that assures that Board members need not fear for their jobs when competently carrying out their tasks, while still maintaining the Commission as the ultimate authority over Board policies and actions. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, supra.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, consider how several cases fit together in a way that logically compels a holding of constitutionality here. In Perkins, 116 U. S., at 483, 484\xe2\x80\x94which was reaffirmed in Myers, 272 U. S., at 127 and in Morrison, supra, at 689, n. 27\xe2\x80\x94the Court upheld a removal restriction limiting the authority of the Secretary of the Navy to remove a \xe2\x80\x9ccadet-engineer,\xe2\x80\x9d whom the Court explicitly defined as an \xe2\x80\x9cinferior officer.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court said,

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cWe have no doubt that when Congress, by law, vests the appointment of inferior officers in the heads of Departments it may limit and restrict the power of removal as it deems best for the public interest. The constitutional authority in Congress to thus vest the appointment implies authority to limit, restrict, and regulate the removal by such laws as Congress may enact in relation to the officers so appointed.\xe2\x80\x9d Perkins, supra, at 485 (emphasis added; internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\nSee also Morrison, supra, at 723\xe2\x80\x93724 (Scalia, J., dissenting) (agreeing that the power to remove an \xe2\x80\x9cinferior officer\xe2\x80\x9d who is appointed by a department head can be restricted). Cf. ante, at 30\xe2\x80\x9333 (holding that SEC Commissioners are \xe2\x80\x9cHeads of Departments\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, the Court held that Congress may constitutionally limit the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority to remove certain principal officers, including heads of departments. 295 U. S., at 627\xe2\x80\x93629. And the Court has consistently recognized the validity of that holding. See Wiener, supra; United States v. Nixon, 418 U. S. 683, 706 (1974); Buckley, 424 U. S., at 133\xe2\x80\x93136; Chadha, 462 U. S., at 953, n.\xc2\xa016; Bowsher, 478 U. S., at 725\xe2\x80\x93726; Morrison, supra, at 686\xe2\x80\x93693; Mistretta v. United States, 488 U. S. 361, 410\xe2\x80\x93411 (1989).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And in Freytag, 501 U. S., at 921, Justice Scalia stated in a concurring opinion written for four Justices, including Justice Kennedy, that \xe2\x80\x9cadjusting the remainder of the Constitution to compensate for Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor is a fruitless endeavor.\xe2\x80\x9d In these Justices\xe2\x80\x99 view, the Court should not create a separate constitutional jurisprudence for the \xe2\x80\x9cindependent agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d That being so, the law should treat their heads as it treats other Executive Branch heads of departments. Consequently, as the Court held in Perkins, Congress may constitutionally \xe2\x80\x9climit and restrict\xe2\x80\x9d the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s power to remove those whom they appoint (e.g, the Accounting Board members).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fourth, the Court has said that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]ur separation-of-powers jurisprudence generally focuses on the danger of one branch\xe2\x80\x99s aggrandizing its power at the expense of another branch.\xe2\x80\x9d Freytag, supra, at 878 (emphasis added); accord, Buckley, supra, at 129; Schor, supra, at 856; Morrison, supra, at 686; cf. Bowsher, supra. Indeed, it has added that \xe2\x80\x9cthe essence of the decision in Myers,\xe2\x80\x9d which is the only one of our cases to have struck down a \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d removal restriction, \xe2\x80\x9cwas the judgment that the Constitution prevents Congress from \xe2\x80\x98draw[ing] to itself \xe2\x80\xa6 the power to remove.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Morrison, supra, at 686 (quoting Myers, supra, at 161; emphasis added). Congress here has \xe2\x80\x9cdrawn\xe2\x80\x9d no power to itself to remove the Board members. It has instead sought to limit its own power, by, for example, providing the Accounting Board with a revenue stream independent of the congressional appropriations process. See supra, at 19; see also Brief for Former SEC Chairmen 16. And this case thereby falls outside the ambit of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s most serious constitutional concern.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior cases impose functional criteria that are readily met here. Once one goes beyond the Court\xe2\x80\x99s elementary arithmetical logic (i.e., \xe2\x80\x9cone plus one is greater than one\xe2\x80\x9d) our precedent virtually dictates a holding that the challenged \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provision is constitutional.

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We should ask one further question. Even if the \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provision before us does not itself significantly interfere with the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority or aggrandize Congress\xe2\x80\x99 power, is it nonetheless necessary to adopt a bright-line rule forbidding the provision lest, through a series of such provisions, each itself upheld as reasonable, Congress might undercut the President\xe2\x80\x99s central constitutional role? Cf. Strauss 625\xe2\x80\x93626. The answer to this question is that no such need has been shown. Moreover, insofar as the Court seeks to create such a rule, it fails. And in failing it threatens a harm that is far more serious than any imaginable harm this \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provision might bring about.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court fails to create a bright-line rule because of considerable uncertainty about the scope of its holding\xe2\x80\x94an uncertainty that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion both reflects and generates. The Court suggests, for example, that its rule may not apply where an inferior officer \xe2\x80\x9cperform[s] adjudicative \xe2\x80\xa6 functions.\xe2\x80\x9d Cf. ante, at 26, n.\xc2\xa010. But the Accounting Board performs adjudicative functions. See supra, at 17\xe2\x80\x9318. What, then, are we to make of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s potential exception? And would such an exception apply to an administrative law judge who also has important administrative duties beyond pure adjudication? See, e.g., 8 CFR \xc2\xa71003.9, 34 CFR \xc2\xa781.4 (2009). The Court elsewhere suggests that its rule may be limited to removal statutes that provide for \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial review of a[n] effort to remove\xe2\x80\x9d an official for cause. Ante, at 22; ante, at 25. But we have previously stated that all officers protected by a for-cause removal provision and later subject to termination are entitled to \xe2\x80\x9cnotice and [a] hearing\xe2\x80\x9d in the \xe2\x80\x9ccourts,\xe2\x80\x9d as without such review \xe2\x80\x9cthe appointing power\xe2\x80\x9d otherwise \xe2\x80\x9ccould remove at pleasure or for such cause as [only] it deemed sufficient.\xe2\x80\x9d Reagan v. United States, 182 U. S. 419, 425 (1901); Shurtleff, 189 U. S., at 314; cf. Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, supra (entertaining civil suit challenging removal). But cf. Bowsher, supra, at 729. What weight, then, should be given to this hint of an exception?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court further seems to suggest that its holding may not apply to inferior officers who have a different relationship to their appointing agents than the relationship between the Commission and the Board. See ante, at 22, 24\xe2\x80\x9326. But the only characteristic of the \xe2\x80\x9crelationship\xe2\x80\x9d between the Commission and the Board that the Court apparently deems relevant is that the relationship includes two layers of for-cause removal. See, e.g., ante, at 23 (\xe2\x80\x9cBroad power over Board functions is not equivalent to the power to remove Board members\xe2\x80\x9d). Why then would any different relationship that also includes two layers of for-cause removal survive where this one has not? Cf. Part II\xe2\x80\x93A, supra (describing the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s near absolute control over the Board). In a word, what differences are relevant? If the Court means to state that its holding in fact applies only where Congress has \xe2\x80\x9cenacted an unusually high standard\xe2\x80\x9d of for-cause removal\xe2\x80\x94and does not otherwise render two layers of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98ordinary\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d for-cause removal unconstitutional\xe2\x80\x94I should welcome the statement. Ante, at 22 (emphasis added); see also ante, at 24\xe2\x80\x9325, 15, 22, (underscoring this statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csharply circumscribed definition of what constitutes \xe2\x80\x98good cause\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and its \xe2\x80\x9crigorous,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9csignificant and unusual [removal] protections\xe2\x80\x9d). But much of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion appears to avoid so narrow a holding in favor of a broad, basically mechanical rule\xe2\x80\x94a rule that, as I have said, is divorced from the context of the case at hand. Compare Parts III\xe2\x80\x93A, III\xe2\x80\x93B, III\xe2\x80\x93C, ante, with Parts II\xe2\x80\x93A, II\xe2\x80\x93B, II\xe2\x80\x93C, supra. And such a mechanical rule cannot be cabined simply by saying that, perhaps, the rule does not apply to instances that, at least at first blush, seem highly similar. A judicial holding by its very nature is not \xe2\x80\x9ca restricted railroad ticket, good for\xe2\x80\x9d one \xe2\x80\x9cday and train only.\xe2\x80\x9d Smith v. Allwright, 321 U. S. 649, 669 (1944) (Roberts, J., dissenting).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court begins to reveal the practical problems inherent in its double for-cause rule when it suggests that its rule may not apply to \xe2\x80\x9cthe civil service.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 26. The \xe2\x80\x9ccivil service\xe2\x80\x9d is defined by statute to include \xe2\x80\x9call appointive positions in \xe2\x80\xa6 the Government of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d excluding the military, but including all civil \xe2\x80\x9cofficer[s]\xe2\x80\x9d up to and including those who are subject to Senate confirmation. 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72101, 2102(a)(1)(B), 2104. The civil service thus includes many officers indistinguishable from the members of both the Commission and the Accounting Board. Indeed, as this Court recognized in Myers, the \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitive service\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the class within the broader civil service that enjoys the most robust career protection\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cincludes a vast majority of all the civil officers\xe2\x80\x9d in the United States. 272 U. S., at 173 (emphasis added); 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72102(c).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But even if I assume that the majority categorically excludes the competitive service from the scope of its new rule, cf. ante, at 26 (leaving this question open), the exclusion would be insufficient. This is because the Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdouble for-cause\xe2\x80\x9d rule applies to appointees who are \xe2\x80\x9cinferior officer[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2. And who are they? Courts and scholars have struggled for more than a century to define the constitutional term \xe2\x80\x9cinferior officers,\xe2\x80\x9d without much success. See 2 J. Story, Commentaries on the Constitution \xc2\xa71536, pp. 397\xe2\x80\x93398 (3d ed. 1858) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here does not seem to have been any exact line drawn, who are and who are not to be deemed inferior officers, in the sense of the constitution\xe2\x80\x9d); Edmond v. United States, 520 U. S. 651, 661 (1997) (\xe2\x80\x9cOur cases have not set forth an exclusive criterion for [defining] inferior officers\xe2\x80\x9d); Memorandum from Steven G. Bradbury, Acting Assistant Attorney General, Office of Legal Counsel, to the General Counsels of the Executive Branch: Officers of the United States Within the Meaning of the Appointments Clause, p.\xc2\xa03 (Apr. 16, 2007) (hereinafter OLC Memo), online at http://www.justice.gov/olc/2007/appointmentsclausev10.pdf (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Supreme Court has not articulated the precise scope and application of the [Inferior Officer] Clause\xe2\x80\x99s requirements\xe2\x80\x9d); Konecke, The Appointments Clause and Military Judges: Inferior Appointment to a Principal Office, 5 Seton Hall Const. L.\xc2\xa0J. 489, 492 (1995) (same); Burkoff, Appointment and Removal Under the Federal Constitution: The Impact of Buckley v. Valeo, 22 Wayne L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1335, 1347, 1364 (1976) (describing our early precedent as \xe2\x80\x9ccircular\xe2\x80\x9d and our later law as \xe2\x80\x9cnot particularly useful\xe2\x80\x9d). The Court does not clarify the concept. But without defining who is an inferior officer, to whom the majority\xe2\x80\x99s new rule applies, we cannot know the scope or the coherence of the legal rule that the Court creates. I understand the virtues of a common-law case-by-case approach. But here that kind of approach (when applied without more specificity than I can find in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion) threatens serious harm.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The problem is not simply that the term \xe2\x80\x9cinferior officer\xe2\x80\x9d is indefinite but also that efforts to define it inevitably conclude that the term\xe2\x80\x99s sweep is unusually broad. Consider the Court\xe2\x80\x99s definitions: Inferior officers are, inter alia, (1) those charged with \xe2\x80\x9cthe administration and enforcement of the public law,\xe2\x80\x9d Buckley, 424 U. S., at 139; ante, at 2; (2) those granted \xe2\x80\x9csignificant authority,\xe2\x80\x9d 424 U. S., at 126; ante, at 25; (3) those with \xe2\x80\x9cresponsibility for conducting civil litigation in the courts of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d 424 U. S., at 140; and (4) those \xe2\x80\x9cwho can be said to hold an office,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Germaine, 99 U. S. 508, 510 (1879), that has been created either by \xe2\x80\x9cregulations\xe2\x80\x9d or by \xe2\x80\x9cstatute,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Mouat, 124 U. S. 303, 307\xe2\x80\x93308 (1888).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consider the definitional conclusion that the Department of Justice more recently reached: An \xe2\x80\x9cinferior officer\xe2\x80\x9d is anyone who holds a \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuing\xe2\x80\x9d position and who is \xe2\x80\x9cinvested by legal authority with a portion of the sovereign powers of the federal Government,\xe2\x80\x9d including, inter alia, the power to \xe2\x80\x9carrest criminals,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cseize persons or property,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cissue regulations,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cissue \xe2\x80\xa6 authoritative legal opinions,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cconduc[t] civil litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccollec[t] revenue,\xe2\x80\x9d represent \xe2\x80\x9cthe United States to foreign nations,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccommand\xe2\x80\x9d military force, or enter into \xe2\x80\x9ccontracts\xe2\x80\x9d on behalf \xe2\x80\x9cof the nation.\xe2\x80\x9d OLC Memo 1, 4, 12\xe2\x80\x9313, 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (internal quotation marks omitted; emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And consider the fact that those whom this Court has held to be \xe2\x80\x9cofficers\xe2\x80\x9d include: (1) a district court clerk, Hennen, 13 Pet., at 258; (2) \xe2\x80\x9cthousands of clerks in the Departments of the Treasury, Interior and the othe[r]\xe2\x80\x9d departments, Germaine, supra, at 511, who are responsible for \xe2\x80\x9cthe records, books, and papers appertaining to the office,\xe2\x80\x9d Hennen, supra, at 259; (3) a clerk to \xe2\x80\x9cthe assistant treasurer\xe2\x80\x9d stationed \xe2\x80\x9cat Boston,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Hartwell, 6 Wall. 385, 392 (1868); (4 & 5) an \xe2\x80\x9cassistant-surgeon\xe2\x80\x9d and a \xe2\x80\x9ccadet-engineer\xe2\x80\x9d appointed by the Secretary of the Navy, United States v. Moore, 95 U. S. 760, 762 (1878); Perkins, 116 U. S., at 484; (6) election monitors, Ex parte Siebold, 100 U. S. 371, 397\xe2\x80\x93399 (1880); (7) United States attorneys, Myers, supra, at 159; (8) federal marshals, Sieblod, supra, at 397; Morrison, 487 U. S., at 676; (9) military judges, Weiss v. United States, 510 U. S., 163, 170 (1994); (10) judges in Article I courts, Freytag, 501 U. S., at 880\xe2\x80\x93881; and (11) the general counsel of the Department of Transportation, Edmond v. United States, 520 U. S. 651 (1997). Individual Members of the Court would add to the list the Federal Communication Commission\xe2\x80\x99s managing director, the Federal Trade Commission\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csecretary,\xe2\x80\x9d the general counsel of the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, and more generally, bureau chiefs, general counsels, and administrative law judges, see Freytag, supra, at 918\xe2\x80\x93920 (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment), as well as \xe2\x80\x9cordinary commissioned military officers,\xe2\x80\x9d Weiss, supra, at 182 (Souter, J., concurring).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Reading the criteria above as stringently as possible, I still see no way to avoid sweeping hundreds, perhaps thousands of high level government officials within the scope of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding, putting their job security and their administrative actions and decisions constitutionally at risk. To make even a conservative estimate, one would have to begin by listing federal departments, offices, bureaus and other agencies whose heads are by statute removable only \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause.\xe2\x80\x9d I have found 48 such agencies, which I have listed in Appendix A, infra. Then it would be necessary to identify the senior officials in those agencies (just below the top) who themselves are removable only \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause.\xe2\x80\x9d I have identified 573 such high-ranking officials, whom I have listed in Appendix B, infra. They include most of the leadership of the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (including that agency\xe2\x80\x99s executive director as well as the directors of its Office of Nuclear Reactor Regulation and Office of Enforcement), virtually all of the leadership of the Social Security Administration, the executive directors of the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission and the Federal Trade Commission, as well as the general counsels of the Chemical Safety Board, the Federal Mine Safety and Health Review Commission, and the National Mediation Board.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This list is a conservative estimate because it consists only of career appointees in the Senior Executive Service (SES), see 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72101a, 3132(a)(2), a group of high-ranking officials distinct from the \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitive service,\xe2\x80\x9d see \xc2\xa72101(a)(1)(C), who \xe2\x80\x9cserve in the key positions just below the top Presidential appointees,\xe2\x80\x9d Office of Personnel Management, About the Senior Executive Service, online at http://www.opm.gov/ses/about_ses/index.asp; \xc2\xa72102(a)(1)(C), and who are, without exception, subject to \xe2\x80\x9cremoval\xe2\x80\x9d only for cause. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77542\xe2\x80\x937543; see also \xc2\xa72302(a)(2) (substantially limiting conditions under which \xe2\x80\x9ca career appointee in the Senior Executive Service\xe2\x80\x9d may be \xe2\x80\x9ctransfer[red], or reassign[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d). SES officials include, for example, the Director of the Bureau of Prisons, the Director of the National Drug Intelligence Center, and the Director of the Office of International Monetary Policy in the Treasury Department. See Senate Committee on Homeland Security and Government Affairs, United States Government Policy and Supporting Positions (2008), pp. 99, 103, 129 (hereinafter Plum Book). And by virtually any definition, essentially all SES officials qualify as \xe2\x80\x9cinferior officers,\xe2\x80\x9d for their duties, as defined by statute, require them to \xe2\x80\x9cdirec[t] the work of an organizational unit,\xe2\x80\x9d carry out high-level managerial functions, or \xe2\x80\x9cotherwise exercis[e] important policy-making, policy-determining, or other executive functions.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73132(a)(2) (emphasis added). Cf. ante, at 2 (describing an \xe2\x80\x9cinferior officer\xe2\x80\x9d as someone who \xe2\x80\x9cdetermines the policy and enforces the laws of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d); ante, at 26 (acknowledging that career SES appointees in independent agencies may be rendered unconstitutional in future cases). Is the SES exempt from today\xe2\x80\x99s rule or is it not? The Court, after listing reasons why the SES may be different, simply says that it will not \xe2\x80\x9caddres[s]\xe2\x80\x9d the matter. Ante, at 27. Perhaps it does not do so because it cannot do so without revealing the difficulty of distinguishing the SES from the Accounting Board and thereby also revealing the inherent instability of the legal rule it creates.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The potential list of those whom today\xe2\x80\x99s decision affects is yet larger. As Justice Scalia has observed, administrative law judges (ALJs) \xe2\x80\x9care all executive officers.\xe2\x80\x9d Freytag, 501 U. S., at 910 (opinion concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (emphasis deleted); see also, e.g., id., at 881 (majority opinion) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] [tax-court] special trial judge is an \xe2\x80\x98inferior Officer\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); Edmond, supra, at 654 (\xe2\x80\x9c[M]ilitary trial and appellate judges are [inferior] officers\xe2\x80\x9d). But cf. ante, at 26, n.\xc2\xa010. And ALJs are each removable \xe2\x80\x9conly for good cause established and determined by the Merit Systems Protection Board,\xe2\x80\x9d 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77521(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b). But the members of the Merit Systems Protection Board are themselves protected from removal by the President absent good cause. \xc2\xa71202(d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0My research reflects that the Federal Government relies on 1,584 ALJs to adjudicate administrative matters in over 25 agencies. See Appendix C, infra; see also Memorandum of Juanita Love, Office of Personnel Management, to Supreme Court Library (May 28, 2010) (available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). These ALJs adjudicate Social Security benefits, employment disputes, and other matters highly important to individuals. Does every losing party before an ALJ now have grounds to appeal on the basis that the decision entered against him is unconstitutional? Cf. ante, at 26, n. 10 (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]ur holding also does not address\xe2\x80\x9d this question).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And what about the military? Commissioned military officers \xe2\x80\x9care \xe2\x80\x98inferior officers.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Weiss, 510 U. S., at 182 (Souter, J., concurring); id., at 169\xe2\x80\x93170 (majority opinion). There are over 210,000 active-duty commissioned officers currently serving in the armed forces. See Dept. of Defense, Active Duty Military Personnel by Rank (Apr. 30, 2010), online at http://siadapp.dmdc.osd.mil/personnel/MILITARY/\nrg1004.pdf. Numerous statutory provisions provide that such officers may not be removed from office except for cause (at least in peacetime). See, e.g., 10 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7629\xe2\x80\x93632, 804, 1161, 1181\xe2\x80\x931185. And such officers can generally be so removed only by other commissioned officers, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7612, 825, 1187, who themselves enjoy the same career protections.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority might simply say that the military is different. But it will have to explain how it is different. It is difficult to see why the Constitution would provide a President who is the military\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccommander-in-chief,\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0II, \xc2\xa72, cl.\xc2\xa01, with less authority to remove \xe2\x80\x9cinferior\xe2\x80\x9d military \xe2\x80\x9cofficers\xe2\x80\x9d than to remove comparable civil officials. See Barron & Lederman, The Commander in Chief at the Lowest Ebb\xe2\x80\x94A Constitutional History, 121 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 941, 1102\xe2\x80\x931106 (2008) (describing President\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csuperintendence prerogative\xe2\x80\x9d over the military). Cf. ante, at 26\xe2\x80\x9327 (not \xe2\x80\x9cexpressing any view whatever\xe2\x80\x9d as to whether military officers\xe2\x80\x99 authority is now unconstitutional).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority sees \xe2\x80\x9cno reason \xe2\x80\xa6 to address whether\xe2\x80\x9d any of \xe2\x80\x9cthese positions,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cor any others,\xe2\x80\x9d might be deemed unconstitutional under its new rule, preferring instead to leave these matters for a future case. Ante, at 27. But what is to happen in the meantime? Is the work of all these various officials to be put on hold while the courts of appeals determine whether today\xe2\x80\x99s ruling applies to them? Will Congress have to act to remove the \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provisions? Cf. Buckley, 424 U. S., at 142\xe2\x80\x93143. Can the President then restore them via executive order? And, still, what about the military? A clearer line would help avoid these practical difficulties.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority asserts that its opinion will not affect the Government\xe2\x80\x99s ability to function while these many questions are litigated in the lower courts because the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding concerns only \xe2\x80\x9cthe conditions under which th[e]se officers might some day be removed.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 27. But this case was not brought by federal officials challenging their potential removal. It was brought by private individuals who were subject to regulation \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98here-and-now\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and who \xe2\x80\x9cobject to the\xe2\x80\x9d very \xe2\x80\x9cexistence\xe2\x80\x9d of the regulators themselves. Ante, at 33, 8 (emphasis added). And those private individuals have prevailed. Thus, any person similarly regulated by a federal official who is potentially subject to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s amorphous new rule will be able to bring an \xe2\x80\x9cimplied private right of action directly under the Constitution\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cseeking \xe2\x80\xa6 a declaratory judgment that\xe2\x80\x9d the official\xe2\x80\x99s actions are \xe2\x80\x9cunconstitutional and an injunction preventing the\xe2\x80\x9d official \xe2\x80\x9cfrom exercising [his] powers.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa02, 6; cf., e.g., Legal Services Corporation v. Velazquez, 531 U. S. 533, 546 (2001) (affirming grant of preliminary injunction to cure, inter alia, a separation-of-powers violation); Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co., 343 U. S. 579 (same). Such a plaintiff need not even first exhaust his administrative remedies. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor is it clear that courts will always be able to cure such a constitutional defect merely by severing an offending removal provision. For a court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cability to devise [such] a judicial remedy \xe2\x80\xa6 often depends on how clearly\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cbackground constitutional rules at issue\xe2\x80\x9d have been \xe2\x80\x9carticulated\xe2\x80\x9d; severance will be unavailable \xe2\x80\x9cin a murky constitutional context,\xe2\x80\x9d which is precisely the context that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new rule creates. Ayotte v. Planned Parenthood of Northern New Eng., 546 U. S. 320, 329, 330 (2006). Moreover, \xe2\x80\x9cthe touchstone\xe2\x80\x9d of the severability analysis \xe2\x80\x9cis legislative intent,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 330, and Congress has repeatedly expressed its judgment \xe2\x80\x9cover the last century that it is in the best interest of the country, indeed essential, that federal service should depend upon meritorious performance rather than political service,\xe2\x80\x9d Civil Service Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 413 U. S., at 557; see also Bush v. Lucas, 462 U. S. 367, 380\xe2\x80\x93388 (1983) (describing the history of \xe2\x80\x9cCongressional attention to the problem of politically-motivated removals\xe2\x80\x9d). And so it may well be that courts called upon to resolve the many questions the majority\xe2\x80\x99s opinion raises will not only apply the Court\xe2\x80\x99s new rule to its logical conclusion, but will also determine that the only available remedy to certain double for-cause problems is to invalidate entire agencies.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, notwithstanding the majority\xe2\x80\x99s assertions to the contrary, the potential consequences of today\xe2\x80\x99s holding are worrying. The upshot, I believe, is a legal dilemma. To interpret the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision as applicable only in a few circumstances will make the rule less harmful but arbitrary. To interpret the rule more broadly will make the rule more rational, but destructive.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One last question: How can the Court simply assume without deciding that the SEC Commissioners themselves are removable only \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause?\xe2\x80\x9d See ante, at 5 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e decide the case with th[e] understanding\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthat the Commissioners cannot themselves be removed by the President except\xe2\x80\x9d for cause (emphasis added)). Unless the Commissioners themselves are in fact protected by a \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d requirement, the Accounting Board statute, on the Court\xe2\x80\x99s own reasoning, is not constitutionally defective. I am not aware of any other instance in which the Court has similarly (on its own or through stipulation) created a constitutional defect in a statute and then relied on that defect to strike a statute down as unconstitutional. Cf. Alabama v. North Carolina, 560 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (opinion for the Court by Scalia, J.) (slip op., at 20) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe do not\xe2\x80\x94we cannot\xe2\x80\x94add provisions to a federal statute \xe2\x80\xa6 especially [if] \xe2\x80\xa6 separation-of-powers concerns \xe2\x80\xa6 would [thereby] arise\xe2\x80\x9d); The Anaconda v. American Sugar Refining Co., 322 U. S. 42, 46 (1944) (describing parties\xe2\x80\x99 inability to \xe2\x80\x9cstipulate away\xe2\x80\x9d what \xe2\x80\x9cthe legislation declares\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is certainly not obvious that the SEC Commissioners enjoy \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d protection. Unlike the statutes establishing the 48 federal agencies listed in Appendix A, infra, the statue that established the Commission says nothing about removal. It is silent on the question. As far as its text is concerned, the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority to remove the Commissioners is no different from his authority to remove the Secretary of State or the Attorney General. See Shurtleff, 189 U. S., at 315 (\xe2\x80\x9cTo take away th[e] power of removal \xe2\x80\xa6 would require very clear and explicit language. It should not be held to be taken away by mere inference or implication\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Memorandum from David J. Barron, Acting Assistant Attorney General, Office of Legal Counsel, to the Principal Deputy Counsel to the President: Removability of the Federal Coordinator for Alaska Natural Gas Transportation Projects, p.\xc2\xa02 (Oct. 23, 2009), online at http://justice.gov/olc/2009/gas-transport-\nproject.pdf (\xe2\x80\x9c[Where] Congress did not explicitly provide tenure protection \xe2\x80\xa6 the President, consistent with \xe2\x80\xa6 settled principles, may remove \xe2\x80\xa6 without cause\xe2\x80\x9d); The Constitutional Separation of Powers Between the President and Congress, 20 Op. Legal Counsel 124, 170 (1996) (same).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor is the absence of a \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provision in the statute that created the Commission likely to have been inadvertent. Congress created the Commission during the 9-year period after this Court decided Myers, and thereby cast serious doubt on the constitutionality of all \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d removal provisions, but before it decided Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor, which removed any doubt in respect to the constitutionality of making commissioners of independent agencies removable only for cause. In other words, Congress created the SEC at a time when, under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, it would have been unconstitutional to make the Commissioners removable only for cause. And, during that 9-year period, Congress created at least three major federal agencies without making any of their officers removable for cause. See 48 Stat. 885, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa778d (Securities and Exchange Commission), 48 Stat. 1066, 47 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7154 (Federal Communications Commission); 46 Stat. 797 (Federal Power Commission) (reformed post-Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor as the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission with \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d protection, 91 Stat. 582, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77171). By way of contrast, only one month after Humphrey\xe2\x80\x99s Executor was decided, Congress returned to its pre-Myers practice of including such provisions in statutes creating independent commissions. See \xc2\xa73, 49 Stat. 451, 29 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7153 (establishing National Labor Relations Board with an explicit removal limitation).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The fact that Congress did not make the SEC Commissioners removable \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d does not mean it intended to create a dependent, rather than an independent agency. Agency independence is a function of several different factors, of which \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d protection is only one. Those factors include, inter alia, an agency\xe2\x80\x99s separate (rather than presidentially dependent) budgeting authority, its separate litigating authority, its composition as a multimember bipartisan board, the use of the word \xe2\x80\x9cindependent\xe2\x80\x9d in its authorizing statute, and, above all, a political environment, reflecting tradition and function, that would impose a heavy political cost upon any President who tried to remove a commissioner of the agency without cause. See generally Breger & Edles 1135\xe2\x80\x931155.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The absence of a \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d provision is thus not fatal to agency independence. Indeed, a \xe2\x80\x9cCongressional Research Service official suggests that there are at least 13 \xe2\x80\x98independent\xe2\x80\x99 agencies without a removal provision in their statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1143, n. 161 (emphasis added) (citing congressional testimony). But it does draw the majority\xe2\x80\x99s rule into further confusion. For not only are we left without a definition of an \xe2\x80\x9cinferior officer,\xe2\x80\x9d but we are also left to guess which department heads will be deemed by the majority to be subject to for-cause removal notwithstanding statutes containing no such provision. If any agency deemed \xe2\x80\x9cindependent\xe2\x80\x9d will be similarly treated, the scope of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s holding is even broader still. See Appendix D, infra (listing agencies potentially affected).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court then, by assumption, reads into the statute books a \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause removal\xe2\x80\x9d phrase that does not appear in the relevant statute and which Congress probably did not intend to write. And it does so in order to strike down, not to uphold, another statute. This is not a statutory construction that seeks to avoid a constitutional question, but its opposite. See Ashwander v. TVA, 297 U. S. 288, 347 (1936) (Brandeis, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is not the habit of the Court to decide questions of a constitutional nature unless absolutely necessary to a decision of the case\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); NLRB v. Catholic Bishop of Chicago, 440 U. S. 490, 500 (1979) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n Act of Congress ought not to be construed to violate the Constitution if any other possible construction remains available\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I do not need to decide whether the Commissioners are in fact removable only \xe2\x80\x9cfor cause\xe2\x80\x9d because I would uphold the Accounting Board\xe2\x80\x99s removal provision as constitutional regardless. But were that not so, a determination that the silent SEC statute means no more than it says would properly avoid the determination of unconstitutionality that the Court now makes.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision is wrong\xe2\x80\x94very wrong. As Parts II\xe2\x80\x93A, II\xe2\x80\x93B, and II\xe2\x80\x93C of this opinion make clear, if the Court were to look to the proper functional and contextual considerations, it would find the Accounting Board provision constitutional. As Part II\xe2\x80\x93D shows, insofar as the Court instead tries to create a bright-line rule, it fails to do so. Its rule of decision is both imprecise and overly broad. In light of the present imprecision, it must either narrow its rule arbitrarily, leaving it to apply virtually alone to the Accounting Board, or it will have to leave in place a broader rule of decision applicable to many other \xe2\x80\x9cinferior officers\xe2\x80\x9d as well. In doing the latter, it will undermine the President\xe2\x80\x99s authority. And it will create an obstacle, indeed pose a serious threat, to the proper functioning of that workable Government that the Constitution seeks to create\xe2\x80\x94in provisions this Court is sworn to uphold.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With respect I dissent.
