SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

\n

ONEOK, INC., et\xc2\xa0al. v. LEARJET, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93271.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 12, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 21, 2015

\n

Respondents, a group of manufacturers, hospitals, and other institutions that buy natural gas directly from interstate pipelines, sued petitioner interstate pipelines, claiming that the pipelines had engaged in behavior that violated state antitrust laws. In particular, respondents alleged that petitioners reported false information to the natural-gas indices on which respondents\xe2\x80\x99 natural-gas contracts were based. The indices affected not only retail natural-gas prices, but also wholesale natural-gas prices.

\n

\n \n \n \n After removing the cases to federal court, the petitioner pipelines sought summary judgment on the ground that the Natural Gas Act pre-empted respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law claims. That Act gives the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) the authority to determine whether rates charged by natural-gas companies or practices affecting such rates are unreasonable.\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7717d(a). But it also limits FERC\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction to the transportation of natural gas in interstate commerce, the sale in interstate commerce of natural gas for resale, and natural-gas companies engaged in such transportation or sale. \xc2\xa7717(b). The Act leaves regulation of other portions of the industry\xe2\x80\x94such as retail sales\xe2\x80\x94to the States. Ibid.

\n

\n \n \n \n The District Court granted petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 motion for summary judgment, reasoning that because petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 challenged practices directly affected wholesale as well as retail prices, they were pre-empted by the Act. The Ninth Circuit reversed. While acknowledging that the pipelines\xe2\x80\x99 index manipulation increased wholesale prices as well as retail prices, it held that the state-law claims were not pre-empted because they were aimed at obtaining damages only for excessively high retail prices.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law antitrust claims are not within the field of matters pre-empted by the Natural Gas Act. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n \n (a)\xc2\xa0The Act \xe2\x80\x9cwas drawn with meticulous regard for the continued exercise of state power.\xe2\x80\x9d Panhandle Eastern Pipe Line Co. v. Public Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Ind.,\n\n332 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507\n\xe2\x80\x93518. Where, as here, a practice affects nonjurisdictional as well as jurisdictional sales, pre-emption can be found only where a detailed examination convincingly demonstrates that a matter falls within the pre-empted field as defined by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents. Those precedents emphasize the importance of considering the target at which the state-law claims aim. See, e.g., Northern Natural Gas Co. v. State Corporation Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Kan., \n\n372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 84\n; Northwest Central Pipeline Corp. v. State Corporation Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Kan.,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 493\n. Here, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims are aimed at practices affecting retail prices, a matter \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly on the States\xe2\x80\x99 side of [the] dividing line.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 514.

\n

\nSchneidewind v. ANR Pipeline Co.,\n\n485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 293\n, is not to the contrary. That opinion explains that the Act does not pre-empt \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional\xe2\x80\x9d state regulation, such as blue sky laws. Id., at 308, n.\xc2\xa011. Antitrust laws, like blue sky laws, are not aimed at natural-gas companies in particular, but rather all businesses in the marketplace. The broad applicability of state antitrust laws supports a finding of no pre-emption here.

\n

\n \n So, too, does the fact that States have long provided \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-law and statutory remedies against monopolies and unfair business practices,\xe2\x80\x9d California v. ARC America Corp.,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 93\n. As noted earlier, the Act circumscribes FERC\xe2\x80\x99s powers and preserves traditional areas of state authority. \xc2\xa7717(b). Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n \n (b)\xc2\xa0Neither Mississippi Power & Light Co. v. Mississippi ex rel. Moore,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 354\n, nor FPC v. Louisiana Power & Light Co.,\n\n406 U.\xc2\xa0S. 621\n, supports petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position. Mississippi Power is best read as a conflict pre-emption case, not a field pre-emption case. In any event, the state inquiry in Mississippi Power was pre-empted because it was directed at jurisdictional sales in a way that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state antitrust suits are not. Louisiana Power is also a conflict pre-emption case, and thus does not significantly help petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 field pre-emption argument. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n \n (c)\xc2\xa0Because the parties have not argued conflict pre-emption, questions involving conflicts between state antitrust proceedings and the federal rate-setting process are left for the lower courts to resolve in the first instance. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n \n (d)\xc2\xa0While petitioners and the Government argue that this Court should defer to FERC\xe2\x80\x99s determination that field pre-emption bars respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims, they fail to point to a specific FERC determination that state antitrust claims fall within the field pre-empted by the Natural Gas Act. Thus, this Court need not consider what legal effect such a determination might have. P.\xc2\xa016.

\n

715 F.\xc2\xa03d 716, affirmed.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

_________________

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93271

_________________

ONEOK, INC., et\xc2\xa0al. PETITIONERS v. LEARJET, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

[April 21, 2015]

Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

In this case, a group of manufacturers, hospitals, and other institutions that buy natural gas directly from interstate pipelines sued the pipelines, claiming that they engaged in behavior that violated state antitrust laws. The pipelines\xe2\x80\x99 behavior affected both federally regulated wholesale natural-gas prices and nonfederally regulated retail natural-gas prices. The question is whether the federal Natural Gas Act pre-empts these lawsuits. We have said that, in passing the Act, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress occupied the field of matters relating to wholesale sales and transportation of natural gas in interstate commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d Schneidewind v. ANR Pipeline Co.,485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 293,305 (1988). Nevertheless, for the reasons given below, we conclude that the Act does not pre-empt the state-law antitrust suits at issue here.

I

A

The Supremacy Clause provides that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Laws of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d (as well as treaties and the Constitution itself\xc2\xa0) \xe2\x80\x9cshall be the supreme Law of the Land .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any Thing in the Constitution or Laws of any state to the Contrary notwithstanding.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0VI, cl.\xc2\xa02. Congress may consequently pre-empt, i.e., invalidate, a state law through federal legislation. It may do so through express language in a statute. But even where, as here, a statute does not refer expressly to pre-emption, Congress may implicitly pre-empt a state law, rule, or other state action. See Sprietsma v. Mercury Marine,537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 51,64 (2002).

It may do so either through \xe2\x80\x9cfield\xe2\x80\x9d pre-emption or \xe2\x80\x9cconflict\xe2\x80\x9d pre-emption. As to the former, Congress may have intended \xe2\x80\x9cto foreclose any state regulation in the area,\xe2\x80\x9d irrespective of whether state law is consistent or inconsistent with \xe2\x80\x9cfederal standards.\xe2\x80\x9d Arizona v. United States, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 10) (emphasis added). In such situations, Congress has forbidden the State to take action in the field that the federal statute pre-empts.

By contrast, conflict pre-emption exists where \xe2\x80\x9ccompliance with both state and federal law is impossible,\xe2\x80\x9d or where \xe2\x80\x9cthe state law \xe2\x80\x98stands as an obstacle to the accomplishment and execution of the full purposes and objectives of Congress.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d California v. ARC America Corp.,490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 93,100,101 (1989). In either situation, federal law must prevail.

No one here claims that any relevant federal statute expressly pre-empts state antitrust lawsuits. Nor have the parties argued at any length that these state suits conflict with federal law. Rather, the interstate pipeline companies (petitioners here) argue that Congress implic-itly \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98occupied the field of matters relating to wholesale sales and transportation of natural gas in interstate commerce.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 18 (quoting Schneidewind, supra, at 305 (emphasis added)). And they contend that the state antitrust claims advanced by their direct-sales customers (respondents here) fall within that field. The United States, supporting the pipelines, argues similarly. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 15. Since the parties have argued this case almost exclusively in terms of field pre-emption, we consider only the field pre-emption question.

B

1

Federal regulation of the natural-gas industry began at a time when the industry was divided into three segments. See 1 Regulation of the Natural Gas Industry \xc2\xa71.01 (W. Mogel ed. 2008) (hereinafter Mogel); General Motors Corp. v. Tracy,519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 278,283 (1997). First, natural-gas producers sunk wells in large oil and gas fields (such as the Permian Basin in Texas and New Mexico). They gathered the gas, brought it to transportation points, and left it to interstate gas pipelines to transport the gas to distant markets. Second, interstate pipelines shipped the gas from the field to cities and towns across the Nation. Third, local gas distributors bought the gas from the interstate pipelines and resold it to business and residential customers within their localities.

Originally, the States regulated all three segments of the industry. See 1 Mogel \xc2\xa71.03. But in the early 20th century, this Court held that the Commerce Clause forbids the States to regulate the second part of the business\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the interstate shipment and sale of gas to local distributors for resale. See, e.g., Public Util. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of R.\xc2\xa0I. v. Attleboro Steam & Elec. Co.,273 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83\xe2\x80\x9390 (1927); Missouri ex\xc2\xa0rel. Barrett v. Kansas Natural Gas Co.,265 U.\xc2\xa0S. 298\xe2\x80\x93308 (1924). These holdings left a regula-tory gap. Congress enacted the Natural Gas Act,52Stat.821, to fill it. See Phillips Petroleum Co. v. Wisconsin,347 U.\xc2\xa0S. 672\xe2\x80\x93684, n. 13 (1954) (citing H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 709, 75th Cong., 1st Sess., 1\xe2\x80\x932 (1937); S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 1162, 75th Cong., 1st Sess., 1\xe2\x80\x932 (1937)).

The Act, in \xc2\xa75(a), gives rate-setting authority to the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC, formerly the Federal Power Commission (FPC)). That authority allows FERC to determine whether \xe2\x80\x9cany rate, charge, or classification .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. collected by any natural-gas company in connection with any transportation or sale of natural gas, subject to the jurisdiction of [FERC],\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cany rule, regulation, practice, or contract affecting such rate, charge, or classification is unjust, unreasonable, unduly discriminatory, or preferential.\xe2\x80\x9d15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7717d(a) (emphasis added). As the italicized words make clear, \xc2\xa75(a) limits the scope of FERC\xe2\x80\x99s authority to activities \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with any transportation or sale of natural gas, subject to the jurisdiction of the Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasisadded). And the Act, in \xc2\xa71(b), limits FERC\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d to (1) \xe2\x80\x9cthe transportation of natural gas in interstate commerce,\xe2\x80\x9d (2) \xe2\x80\x9cthe sale in interstate commerce of natural gas for resale,\xe2\x80\x9d and (3) \xe2\x80\x9cnatural-gas companies engaged in such transportation or sale.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7717(b). The Act leaves regulation of other portions of the industry\xe2\x80\x94such as production, local distribution facilities, and direct sales\xe2\x80\x94to the States. See Northwest Central Pipeline Corp. v. State Corporation Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Kan.,489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 493,507 (1989) (Section 1(b) of the Act \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly\xe2\x80\x9d provides that \xe2\x80\x9cStates retain jurisdiction over intrastate transportation, local distribution, and distribution facilities, and over \xe2\x80\x98the production or gathering of natural gas\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d).

To simplify our discussion, we shall describe the firms that engage in interstate transportation as \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional sellers\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cinterstate pipelines\xe2\x80\x9d (though various brokers and others may also fall within the Act\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional scope). Similarly, we shall refer to the sales over which FERC has jurisdiction as \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional sales\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cwholesale sales.\xe2\x80\x9d

2

Until the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s, natural-gas regulation roughly tracked the industry model we described above. Interstate pipelines would typically buy gas from field producers and resell it to local distribution companies for resale. See Tracy, supra, at 283. FERC (or FPC), acting under the authority of the Natural Gas Act, would set interstate pipeline wholesale rates using classical \xe2\x80\x9ccost-of-service\xe2\x80\x9d ratemaking methods. See Public Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of N.\xc2\xa0Y. v. Mid-Louisiana Gas Co.,463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 319,328 (1983). That is, FERC would determine a pipeline\xe2\x80\x99s revenue requirement by calculating the costs of providing its services, including operating and maintenance expenses, depreciation expenses, taxes, and a reasonable profit. See FERC, Cost-of-Service Rates Manual 6 (June 1999). FERC would then set wholesale rates at a level designed to meet the pipeline\xe2\x80\x99s revenue requirement.

Deregulation of the natural-gas industry, however, brought about changes in FERC\xe2\x80\x99s approach. In the 1950\xe2\x80\x99s, this Court had held that the Natural Gas Act required regulation of prices at the interstate pipelines\xe2\x80\x99 buying end\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the prices at which field producers sold natural gas to interstate pipelines. Phillips Petroleum Co., supra, at 682, 685. By the 1970\xe2\x80\x99s, many in Congress thought that such efforts to regulate field prices had jeopardizednatural-gas supplies in an industry already dependent \xe2\x80\x9con the caprice of nature.\xe2\x80\x9d FPC v. Hope Natural Gas Co.,320 U.\xc2\xa0S. 591,630 (1944) (opinion of Jackson, J.); see id., at 629 (recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe wealth of Midas and the wit of man cannot produce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a natural gas field\xe2\x80\x9d). Hoping to avoid future shortages, Congress enacted forms of field price deregulation designed to rely upon competition, rather than regulation, to keep field prices low. See, e.g., Natural Gas Policy Act of 1978,92Stat.3409, codified in part at15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73301 et\xc2\xa0seq. (phasing out regulation of wellhead prices charged by producers of natural gas); Natural Gas Wellhead Decontrol Act of 1989,103Stat.157 (removing price controls on wellhead sales as of January 1993).

FERC promulgated new regulations designed to further this process of deregulation. See, e.g., Regulation of Natural Gas Pipelines after Partial Wellhead Decontrol, 50 Fed. Reg. 42408 (1985) (allowing \xe2\x80\x9copen access\xe2\x80\x9d to pipelines so that consumers could pay to ship their own gas). Most important here, FERC adopted an approach that relied on the competitive marketplace, rather than classical regulatory rate-setting, as the main mechanism for keeping wholesale natural-gas rates at a reasonable level. Order No. 636, issued in 1992, allowed FERC to issue blanket certificates that permitted jurisdictional sellers (typically interstate pipelines) to charge market-based rates for gas, provided that FERC had first determined that the sellers lacked market power. See 57 Fed. Reg. 57957\xe2\x80\x9357958 (1992); id., at 13270.

After the issuance of this order, FERC\xe2\x80\x99s oversight of the natural-gas market largely consisted of (1) ex ante examinations of jurisdictional sellers\xe2\x80\x99 market power, and (2) the availability of a complaint process under \xc2\xa7717d(a). See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 4. The new system also led many large gas consumers\xe2\x80\x94such as industrial and commercial users\xe2\x80\x94to buy their own gas directly from gas producers, and to arrange (and often pay separately) for transportation from the field to the place of consumption. See Tracy, 519 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 284. Insofar as interstate pipelines sold gas to such consumers, they sold it for direct consumption rather than resale.

3

The free-market system for setting interstate pipeline rates turned out to be less than perfect. Interstate pipelines, distributing companies, and many of the customers who bought directly from the pipelines found that they had to rely on privately published price indices to determine appropriate prices for their natural-gas contracts. These indices listed the prices at which natural gas was being sold in different (presumably competitive) markets across the country. The information on which these in-dices were based was voluntarily reported by natural-gas traders.

In 2003, FERC found that the indices were inaccurate, in part because much of the information that natural-gas traders reported had been false. See FERC, Final Report on Price Manipulation in Western Markets (Mar. 2003), App. 88\xe2\x80\x9389. FERC found that false reporting had involved \xe2\x80\x9cinflating the volume of trades, omitting trades, and adjusting the price of trades.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 88. That is, sometimes those who reported information simply fabricated it. Other times, the information reported reflected \xe2\x80\x9cwash trades,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., \xe2\x80\x9cprearranged pair[s] of trades of the same good between the same parties, involving no economic risk and no net change in beneficial ownership.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 215. FERC concluded that these \xe2\x80\x9cefforts to manipulate price indices compiled by trade publications\xe2\x80\x9d had helped raise \xe2\x80\x9cto extraordinary levels\xe2\x80\x9d the prices of both jurisdictional sales (that is, interstate pipeline sales for resale) and nonjurisdictional direct sales to ultimate consumers. Id., at 86, 85.

After issuing its final report on price manipulation in western markets, FERC issued a Code of Conduct. That code amended all blanket certificates to prohibit jurisdictional sellers \xe2\x80\x9cfrom engaging in actions without a legitimate business purpose that manipulate or attempt to manipulate market conditions, including wash trades and collusion.\xe2\x80\x9d 68 Fed. Reg. 66324 (2003). The code also required jurisdictional companies, when they provided information to natural-gas index publishers, to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide accurate and factual information, and not knowingly submit false or misleading information or omit material information to any such publisher.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 66337. At the same time, FERC issued a policy statement setting forth \xe2\x80\x9cminimum standards for creation and publication of any energy price index,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cfor reporting transaction data to index developers.\xe2\x80\x9d Price Discovery in Natural Gas and Elec. Markets, 104 FERC \xc2\xb661,121, pp. 61,407, 61,408 (2003). Finally, FERC, after finding that certain jurisdictional sellers had \xe2\x80\x9cengaged in wash trading .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that resulted in the manipulation of [natural-gas] prices,\xe2\x80\x9d terminated those sellers\xe2\x80\x99 blanket marketing certificates. Enron Power Marketing, Inc., 103 FERC \xc2\xb661,343, p. 62,303 (2003).

Congress also took steps to address these problems. In particular, it passed the Energy Policy Act of 2005,119Stat.594, which gives FERC the authority to issue rules and regulations to prevent \xe2\x80\x9cany manipulative or deceptive device or contrivance\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cany entity .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in connection with the purchase or sale of natural gas or the purchase or sale of transportation services subject to the jurisdiction of\xe2\x80\x9d FERC,15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7717c\xe2\x80\x931.

C

We now turn to the cases before us. Respondents, as we have said, bought large quantities of natural gas directly from interstate pipelines for their own consumption. They believe that they overpaid in these transactions due to the interstate pipelines\xe2\x80\x99 manipulation of the natural-gas indices. Based on this belief, they filed state-law antitrust suits against petitioners in state and federal courts. See App. 244\xe2\x80\x93246 (alleging violations of Wis. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7133.03, 133.14, 133.18); see also App. 430\xe2\x80\x93433 (same); id., at 519\xe2\x80\x93521 (same); id., at 362\xe2\x80\x93364 (alleging violations of Kansas Restraint of Trade Act, Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa750\xe2\x80\x93101 et seq.); App. 417\xe2\x80\x93419 (alleging violations of Missouri Antitrust Law, Mo. Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7416.011\xe2\x80\x93416.161). The pipelines removed all the state cases to federal court, where they were consolidated and sent for pretrial proceedings to the Federal District Court for the District of Nevada. See28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71407.

The pipelines then moved for summary judgment on the ground that the Natural Gas Act pre-empted respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law antitrust claims. The District Court granted their motion. It concluded that the pipelines were \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional sellers,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., \xe2\x80\x9cnatural gas companies engaged in\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9ctransportation of natural gas in interstate commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d Order in No. 03\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x931431 (D Nev., July 18, 2011), pp.\xc2\xa04, 11. And it held that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims, which were \xe2\x80\x9caimed at\xe2\x80\x9d these sellers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9calleged practices of false price reporting, wash trades, and anticompetitive collusive behavior\xe2\x80\x9d were pre-empted because \xe2\x80\x9csuch practices,\xe2\x80\x9d not only affected nonjurisdictional direct-sale prices but also \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly affect[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d jurisdictional (i.e., wholesale) rates. Id., at 36\xe2\x80\x9337.

The Ninth Circuit reversed. It emphasized that the price-manipulation of which respondents complained affected not only jurisdictional (i.e., wholesale) sales, but also nonjurisdictional (i.e., retail) sales. The court construed the Natural Gas Act\xe2\x80\x99s pre-emptive scope narrowly in light of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent\xe2\x80\x94manifested in \xc2\xa71(b) of the Act\xe2\x80\x94to preserve for the States the authority to regulate nonjurisdictional sales. And it held that the Act did not pre-empt state-law claims aimed at obtaining damages for excessively high retail natural-gas prices stemming from interstate pipelines\xe2\x80\x99 price manipulation, even if the manipulation raised wholesale rates as well. See In\xc2\xa0re Western States Wholesale Natural Gas Antitrust Litigation, 715 F.\xc2\xa03d 716, 729\xe2\x80\x93736 (2013).

The pipelines sought certiorari. They asked us to resolve confusion in the lower courts as to whether the Natural Gas Act pre-empts retail customers\xe2\x80\x99 state antitrust law challenges to practices that also affect wholesale rates. Compare id., at 729\xe2\x80\x93736, with Leggett v. Duke Energy Corp., 308 S.\xc2\xa0W. 3d 843 (Tenn. 2010). We granted the petition.

II

Petitioners, supported by the United States, argue that their customers\xe2\x80\x99 state antitrust lawsuits are within the field that the Natural Gas Act pre-empts. See Brief for Petitioners 18 (citing Schneidewind, 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 305); Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 13 (same). They point out that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 antitrust claims target anticompetitive activities that affected wholesale (as well as retail) rates. See Brief for Petitioners 2. They add that the Natural Gas Act expressly grants FERC authority to keep wholesale rates at reasonable levels. See ibid. (citing 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7717(b), 717d(a)). In exercising this authority, FERC has prohibited the very kind of anticompetitive conduct that the state actions attack. See Part I\xe2\x80\x93B\xe2\x80\x933, supra. And, petitioners contend, letting these actions proceed will permit state antitrust courts to reach conclusions about that conduct that differ from those that FERC might reach or has already reached. Accordingly, petitioners argue, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law antitrust suits fall within the pre-empted field.

A

Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arguments are forceful, but we cannot accept their conclusion. As we have repeatedly stressed, the Natural Gas Act \xe2\x80\x9cwas drawn with meticulous regard for the continued exercise of state power, not to handicap or dilute it in any way.\xe2\x80\x9d Panhandle Eastern Pipe Line Co. v. Public Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Ind.,332 U.\xc2\xa0S. 507\xe2\x80\x93518 (1947); see also Northwest Central, 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 511 (the \xe2\x80\x9clegislative history of the [Act] is replete with assurances that the Act \xe2\x80\x98takes nothing from the State [regulatory] commissions\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting 81 Cong. Rec. 6721 (1937))). Accordingly, where (as here) a state law can be applied to nonjurisdictional as well as jurisdictional sales, we must proceed cautiously, finding pre-emption only where detailed examination convinces us that a matter falls within the pre-empted field as defined by our precedents. See Panhandle Eastern, supra, at 516\xe2\x80\x93518; Interstate Natural Gas Co. v. FPC,331 U.\xc2\xa0S. 682\xe2\x80\x93693 (1947).

Those precedents emphasize the importance of considering the target at which the state law aims in determining whether that law is pre-empted. For example, in Northern Natural Gas Co. v. State Corporation Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Kan.,372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 84 (1963), the Court said that it had \xe2\x80\x9cconsistently recognized\xe2\x80\x9d that the \xe2\x80\x9csignificant distinction\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of pre-emption in the natural-gas context is the distinction between \xe2\x80\x9cmeasures aimed directly at interstate purchasers and wholesales for resale, and those aimed at\xe2\x80\x9d subjects left to the States to regulate. Id., at 94 (emphasis added). And, in Northwest Central, the Court found that the Natural Gas Act did not pre-empt a state regulation concerning the timing of gas production from a gas field within the State, even though the regulation might have affected the costs of and the prices of interstate wholesale sales, i.e., jurisdictional sales. 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 514. In reaching this conclusion, the Court explained that the state regulation aimed primarily at \xe2\x80\x9cprotect[ing] producers\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. rights\xe2\x80\x94a matter firmly on the States\xe2\x80\x99 side of that dividing line.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Court contrasted this state regulation with the state orders at issue in Northern Natural, which \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98inva-lidly invade[d] the federal agency\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive domain\xe2\x80\x99 pre-cisely because\xe2\x80\x9d they were \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unmistakably and unambiguously directed at purchasers.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 513 (quoting Northern Natural, supra, at 92; emphasis added). Here, too, the lawsuits are directed at practices affecting retail rates\xe2\x80\x93which are \xe2\x80\x9cfirmly on the States\xe2\x80\x99 side of that dividing line.\xe2\x80\x9d

Petitioners argue that Schneidewind constitutes con-trary authority. In that case, the Court found pre-empted a state law that required public utilities, such as interstate pipelines crossing the State, to obtain state approval before issuing long-term securities. 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 306\xe2\x80\x93309. But the Court there thought that the State\xe2\x80\x99s securities regulation was aimed directly at interstate pipelines. It wrote that the state law was designed to keep \xe2\x80\x9ca natural gas company from raising its equity levels above a certain point\xe2\x80\x9d in order to keep the company\xe2\x80\x99s revenue requirement low, thereby ensuring lower wholesale rates. Id., at 307\xe2\x80\x93308. Indeed, the Court expressly said that the state law was pre-empted because it was \xe2\x80\x9cdirected at .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the control of rates and facilities of natural gas companies,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cprecisely the things over which FERC has comprehensive author-ity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 308 (emphasis added).

The dissent rejects the notion that the proper test for purposes of pre-emption in the natural gas context is whether the challenged measures are \xe2\x80\x9caimed directly at interstate purchasers and wholesales for resale\xe2\x80\x9d or not. Northern Natural, supra, at 94. It argues that this approach is \xe2\x80\x9cunprecedented,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s focus should be on \xe2\x80\x9cwhat the State seeks to regulate .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. , not why the State seeks to regulate it.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6 (opinion of Scalia, J.). But the \xe2\x80\x9ctarget\xe2\x80\x9d to which our cases refer must mean more than just the physical activity that a State regulates. After all, a single physical action, such as reporting a price to a specialized journal, could be the subject of many different laws\xe2\x80\x94including tax laws, disclosure laws, and others. To repeat the point we made above, no one could claim that FERC\xe2\x80\x99s regulation of this physical activity for purposes of wholesale rates forecloses every other form of state regulation that affects those rates.

Indeed, although the dissent argues that Schneidwind created a definitive test for pre-emption in the natural gas context that turns on whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe matter on which the State asserts the right to act is in any way regulated by the Federal Act,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 3 (quoting 485 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 310, n.\xc2\xa013), Schneidewind could not mean this statement as an absolute test. It goes on to explain that the Natural Gas Act does not pre-empt \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional\xe2\x80\x9d state regulation, such as state blue sky laws (which, of course, raise wholesale\xe2\x80\x94as well as retail\xe2\x80\x94investment costs). Id., at 308, n.\xc2\xa011.

Antitrust laws, like blue sky laws, are not aimed at natural-gas companies in particular, but rather all businesses in the marketplace. See ibid. They are far broader in their application than, for example, the regulations at issue in Northern Natural, which applied only to entities buying gas from fields within the State. See 372 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 85\xe2\x80\x9386, n.\xc2\xa01; contra, post, at 5\xe2\x80\x936 (stating that Northern Natural concerned \xe2\x80\x9cbackground market conditions\xe2\x80\x9d). This broad applicability of state antitrust law supports a finding of no pre-emption here.

Petitioners and the dissent argue that there is, or should be, a clear division between areas of state and federal authority in natural-gas regulation. See Brief for Petitioners 18; post, at 7. But that Platonic ideal does not describe the natural gas regulatory world. Suppose FERC, when setting wholesale rates in the former cost-of-service rate-making days, had denied cost recovery for pipelines\xe2\x80\x99 failure to recycle. Would that fact deny States the power to enact and apply recycling laws? These state laws might well raise pipelines\xe2\x80\x99 operating costs, and thus the costs of wholesale natural gas transportation. But in Northwest Central we said that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o find field pre-emption of [state] regulation merely because purchasers\xe2\x80\x99 costs and hence rates might be affected would be largely to nullify .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xc2\xa71(b).\xe2\x80\x9d 489 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 514.

The dissent barely mentions the limitations on FERC\xe2\x80\x99s powers in \xc2\xa71(b), but the enumeration of FERC\xe2\x80\x99s powers in \xc2\xa75(a) is circumscribed by a reference back to the limitations in \xc2\xa71(b). See post, at 1\xe2\x80\x933. As we explained above, see Part I\xe2\x80\x93B\xe2\x80\x931, supra, those limits are key to understanding the careful balance between federal and state regulation that Congress struck when it passed the Natural Gas Act. That Act \xe2\x80\x9cwas drawn with meticulous regard for the continued exercise of state power, not to handicap or dilute it in any way.\xe2\x80\x9d Panhandle Eastern, 332 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 517\xe2\x80\x93518. Contra, post, at 8. States have a \xe2\x80\x9clong history of\xe2\x80\x9d providing \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-law and statutory remedies against monopolies and unfair business practices.\xe2\x80\x9d ARC America, 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 101; see also Watson v. Buck,313 U.\xc2\xa0S. 387,404 (1941) (noting the States\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9clong-recognized power to regulate combinations in restraint of trade\xe2\x80\x9d). Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law antitrust suits relied on this well established state power.

B

Petitioners point to two other cases that they believe support their position. The first is Mississippi Power & Light Co. v. Mississippi ex rel. Moore,487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 354 (1988). There, the Court held that the Federal Power Act\xe2\x80\x94which gives FERC the authority to determine whether rates charged by public utilities in electric energy sales are \xe2\x80\x9cjust and reasonable,\xe2\x80\x9d16 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7824d(a)\xe2\x80\x94pre-empted a state inquiry into the reasonableness of FERC-approved prices for the sale of nuclear power to wholesalers of electricity (which led to higher retail electricity rates). 487 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 373\xe2\x80\x93377. Petitioners argue that this case shows that state regulation of similar sales here\xe2\x80\x94i.e., by a pipeline to a direct consumer\xe2\x80\x94must also be pre-empted. See Reply Brief 11\xe2\x80\x9312. Mississippi Power, however, is best read as a conflict pre-emption case, not a field pre-emption case. See 487 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 377 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] state agency\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98efforts to regulate commerce must fall when they conflict with or interfere with federal authority over the same activity\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Chicago & North Western Transp. Co. v. Kalo Brick & Tile Co.,450 U.\xc2\xa0S. 311\xe2\x80\x93319 (1981))).

Regardless, the state inquiry in Mississippi Power was pre-empted because it was directed at jurisdictional sales in a way that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state antitrust lawsuits are not. Mississippi\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry into the reasonableness of FERC-approved purchases was effectively an attempt to \xe2\x80\x9cregulate in areas where FERC has properly exercised its jurisdiction to determine just and reasonable wholesale rates.\xe2\x80\x9d 487 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 374. By contrast, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state antitrust lawsuits do not seek to challenge the reason-ableness of any rates expressly approved by FERC. Rather, they seek to challenge the background marketplace conditions that affected both jurisdictional and nonjurisdic-tional rates.

Petitioners additionally point to FPC v. Louisiana Power & Light Co.,406 U.\xc2\xa0S. 621 (1972). In that case, the Court held that federal law gave FPC the authority to allocate natural gas during shortages by ordering interstate pipelines to curtail gas deliveries to all customers, including retail customers. This latter fact, the pipelines argue, shows that FERC has authority to regulate index manipulation insofar as that manipulation affects retail (as well as wholesale) sales. Brief for Petitioners 26. Accordingly, they contend that state laws that aim at this same subject are pre-empted.

This argument, however, makes too much of too little. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding of pre-emption in Louisiana Power rested on its belief that the state laws in question con-flicted with federal law. The Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cFPC has authority to effect orderly curtailment plans involving both direct sales and sales for resale,\xe2\x80\x9d 406 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 631, because otherwise there would be \xe2\x80\x9cunavoidable conflict between\xe2\x80\x9d state regulation of direct sales and the \xe2\x80\x9cuniform federal regulation\xe2\x80\x9d that the Natural Gas Act foresees, id., at 633\xe2\x80\x93635. Conflict pre-emption may, of course, invalidate a state law even though field pre-emption does not. Because petitioners have not argued this case as a conflict pre-emption case, Louisiana Power does not offer them significant help.

C

To the extent any conflicts arise between state antitrust law proceedings and the federal rate-setting process, the doctrine of conflict pre-emption should prove sufficient to address them. But as we have noted, see Part I\xe2\x80\x93A, supra, the parties have not argued conflict pre-emption. See also, e.g., Tr. of Oral Arg. 24 (Solicitor General agrees that he has not \xe2\x80\x9canalyzed this [case] under a conflict preemption regime\xe2\x80\x9d). We consequently leave conflict pre-emption questions for the lower courts to resolve in the firstinstance.

D

We note that petitioners and the Solicitor General have argued that we should defer to FERC\xe2\x80\x99s determination that field pre-emption bars the respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims. See Brief for Petitioners 22 (citing Arlington v. FCC, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2013) (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9314); Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 32 (same). But they have not pointed to a specific FERC determination that state antitrust claims fall within the field pre-empted by the Natural Gas Act. Rather, they point only to the fact that FERC has promulgated detailed rules governing manipulation of price indices. Because there is no determination by FERC that its regulation pre-empts the field into which respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law antitrust suits fall, we need not consider what legal effect such a determination might have. And we conclude that the detailed federal regulations here do not offset the other considerations that weigh against a finding of pre-emption in this context.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

For these reasons, the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is affirmed.

It is so ordered.

ONEOK, INC., et\xc2\xa0al. PETITIONERS v. LEARJET, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

[April 21, 2015]

Justice Thomas, concurring in part and concurring in the judgment.

I agree with much of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s application of our precedents governing pre-emption under the Natural Gas Act. I write separately to reiterate my view that \xe2\x80\x9cimplied pre-emption doctrines that wander far from the statutory text are inconsistent with the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Wyeth v. Levine,555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555,583 (2009) (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). The Supremacy Clause of our Constitution \xe2\x80\x9cgives \xe2\x80\x98supreme\xe2\x80\x99 status only to those [federal laws] that are \xe2\x80\x98made in Pursuance\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of it. Id., at 585 (quoting Art.\xc2\xa0VI, cl. 2). And to be \xe2\x80\x9cmade in Pursuance\xe2\x80\x9d of the Constitution, a law must fall within one of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 enumerated powers and be promulgated in accordance with the lawmaking procedures set forth in that document. Id., at 585\xe2\x80\x93586. \xe2\x80\x9cThe Supremacy Clause thus requires that pre-emptive effect be given only to those federal standards and policies that are set forth in, or necessarily follow from, the statutory text that was produced through the constitutionally required bicameral and presentment procedures.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 586.

In light of this constitutional requirement, I have doubts about the legitimacy of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents concern-ing the pre-emptive scope of the Natural Gas Act, see, e.g., Northern Natural Gas Co. v. State Corporation Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Kan.,372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 84\xe2\x80\x9392 (1963) (defining the pre-empted field in light of the \xe2\x80\x9cobjective[s]\xe2\x80\x9d of the Act). Neither party, however, has asked us to overrule these longstanding precedents or \xe2\x80\x9cto overcome the presumption of stare decisis that attaches to\xe2\x80\x9d them. Kurns v. Railroad Friction Products Corp., 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 7). And even under these precedents, the challenged state antitrust laws fall outside the pre-empted field. Because the Court today avoids extending its earlier questionable precedents, I concur in its judgment and join all but Part I\xe2\x80\x93A of its opinion.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93271

_________________

ONEOK, INC., et\xc2\xa0al. PETITIONERS v. LEARJET, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

[April 21, 2015]

Justice Scalia, with whom The Chief Justice joins, dissenting.

The Natural Gas Act divides responsibility over trade in natural gas between federal and state regulators. The Act and our cases interpreting it draw a firm line between national and local authority over this trade: If the Federal Government may regulate a subject, the States may not. Today the Court smudges this line. It holds that States may use their antitrust laws to regulate practices already regulated by the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission whenever \xe2\x80\x9cother considerations .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. weigh against a finding of pre-emption.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s make-it-up-as-you-go-along approach to preemption has no basis in the Act, contradicts our cases, and will prove unworkable in practice.

I

Trade in natural gas consists of three parts. A drilling company collects gas from the earth; a pipeline company then carries the gas to its destination and sells it at wholesale to a local distributor; and the local distributor sells the gas at retail to industries and households. See ante, at 3. The Natural Gas Act empowers the Commission to regulate the middle of this three-leg journey\xe2\x80\x94interstate transportation and wholesale sales. 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7717 et seq. But it does not empower the Commission to regulate the opening and closing phases\xe2\x80\x94production at one end, retail sales at the other\xe2\x80\x94thus leaving those matters to the States. \xc2\xa7717(b). (Like the Court, I will for simplicity\xe2\x80\x99s sake call the sales controlled by the Commission wholesale sales, and the companies controlled by the Commission pipelines. See ante, at 4.)

Over 70 years ago, the Court concluded that the Act confers \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive jurisdiction upon the federal regulatory agency.\xe2\x80\x9d Public Util. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Ohio v. United Fuel Gas Co.,317 U.\xc2\xa0S. 456,469 (1943). The Court thought it \xe2\x80\x9cclear\xe2\x80\x9d that the Act contemplates \xe2\x80\x9ca harmonious, dual system of regulation of the natural gas industry\xe2\x80\x94federal and state regulatory bodies operating side by side, each active in its own sphere,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cwithout any confusion of functions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 467. The Court drew this inference from the law\xe2\x80\x99s purpose and legislative history, though it could just as easily have relied on the law\xe2\x80\x99s terms and structure. The Act grants the Commission a wide range of powers over wholesale sales and transportation, but qualifies only some of these powers with reservations of state authority over the same subject. See \xc2\xa7717g(a) (concurrent authority over recordkeeping); \xc2\xa7717h(a) (concurrent authority over depreciation and amortization rates). Congress\xe2\x80\x99s decision to include express reservations of state power alongside these grants of authority, but to omit them alongside other grants of authority, suggests that the other grants are exclusive. Right or wrong, in any event, our inference of exclusivity is now settled beyond debate.

United Fuel rejected a State\xe2\x80\x99s regulation of wholesale rates. Id., at 468. But our later holdings establish that the Act makes exclusive the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s powers in general, not just its rate-setting power in particular. We have again and again set aside state laws\xe2\x80\x94even those that do not purport to fix wholesale rates\xe2\x80\x94for regulating a matter already subject to regulation by the Commission. See, e.g., Northern Natural Gas Co. v. State Corporation Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Kan.,372 U.\xc2\xa0S. 84,89 (1963) (state regulation of pipelines\xe2\x80\x99 gas purchases preempted because it \xe2\x80\x9cinvade[s] the exclusive jurisdiction which the Natural Gas Act has conferred upon the [Commission]\xe2\x80\x9d); Exxon Corp. v. Eagerton,462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 176,185 (1983) (state law prohibiting producers from passing on production taxes preempted because it \xe2\x80\x9ctrespasse[s] upon FERC\xe2\x80\x99s authority\xe2\x80\x9d); Schneidewind v. ANR Pipeline Co.,485 U.\xc2\xa0S. 293,309 (1988) (state securities regulation directly affecting wholesale rates and gas transportation facilities preempted because it regulates \xe2\x80\x9cmatters that Congress intended FERC to regulate\xe2\x80\x9d). The test for preemption in this setting, the Court has confirmed, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98is whether the matter on which the State asserts the right to act is in any way regulated by the Federal Act.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 310, n.\xc2\xa013.

Straightforward application of these precedents would make short work of the case at hand. The Natural Gas Act empowers the Commission to regulate \xe2\x80\x9cpractice[s] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. affecting [wholesale] rate[s].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7717d. Nothing in the Act suggests that the States share power to regulate these practices. The Commission has reasonably determined that this power allows it to regulate the behavior involved in this case, pipelines\xe2\x80\x99 use of sham trades and false reports to manipulate gas price indices. Because the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive authority extends to the conduct challenged here, state antitrust regulation of that conduct is preempted.

II

The Court agrees that the Commission may regulate index manipulation, but upholds state antitrust regulation of this practice anyway on account of \xe2\x80\x9cother considerations that weigh against a finding of pre-emption in this context.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 16. That is an unprecedented decision. The Court does not identify a single case\xe2\x80\x94not one\xe2\x80\x94in which we have sustained state regulation of behavior already regulated by the Commission. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s justifications for its novel approach do not persuade.

A

The Court begins by considering \xe2\x80\x9cthe target at which the state law aims.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. It reasons that because this case involves a practice that affects both wholesale and retail rates, the Act tolerates state regulation that takes aim at the practice\xe2\x80\x99s retail-stage effects. Ibid.

This analysis misunderstands how the Natural Gas Act divides responsibilities between national and local regulators. The Act does not give the Commission the power to aim at particular effects; it gives it the power to regulate particular activities. When the Commission regulates those activities, it may consider their effects on all parts of the gas trade, not just on wholesale sales. It may, for example, set wholesale rates with the aim of encouraging producers to conserve gas supplies\xe2\x80\x94even though production is a state-regulated activity. See Colorado Interstate Gas Co. v. FPC,324 U.\xc2\xa0S. 581\xe2\x80\x93603 (1945); id., at 609\xe2\x80\x93610 (Jackson, J., concurring). Or it may regulate wholesale sales with an eye toward blunting the sales\xe2\x80\x99 anticompetitive effects in the retail market\xe2\x80\x94even though retail prices are controlled by the States. See FPC v. Conway Corp.,426 U.\xc2\xa0S. 271\xe2\x80\x93280 (1976). The Court\xe2\x80\x99s ad\xc2\xa0hoc partition of authority over index manipulation\xe2\x80\x94leaving it to the Commission to control the practice\xe2\x80\x99s consequences for wholesale sales, but allowing the States to target its consequences for retail sales\xe2\x80\x94thus clashes with the design of the Act.

To justify its fixation on aims, the Court stresses that this case involves regulation of \xe2\x80\x9cbackground marketplace conditions\xe2\x80\x9d rather than regulation of wholesale rates or sales themselves. Ante, at 15. But the Natural Gas Act empowers the Commission to regulate wholesale rates and \xe2\x80\x9cbackground\xe2\x80\x9d practices affecting such rates. It grants both powers in the same clause: \xe2\x80\x9cWhenever the Commission .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. find[s] that a [wholesale] rate, charge, or classification .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [or] any rule, regulation, practice, or contract affecting such rate, charge, or classification is unjust [or] unreasonable, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the Commission shall determine the just and reasonable rate, charge, classification, rule, regulation, practice, or contract to be thereafter observed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7717d(a) (emphasis added). Nothing in this provision, and for that matter nothing in the Act, suggests that federal authority over practices is a second-class power, somehow less exclusive than the authority over rates.

The Court persists that the background conditions in this case affect both wholesale and retail sales. Ante, at 15. This observation adds atmosphere, but nothing more. The Court concedes that index manipulation\xe2\x80\x99s dual effect does not weaken the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s power to regulate it. Ante, at 10. So too should the Court have seen that this simultaneous effect does not strengthen the claims of the States. It is not at all unusual for an activity controlled by the Commission to have effects in the States\xe2\x80\x99 field; production, wholesale, and retail are after all interdependent stages of a single trade. We have never suggested that the rules of field preemption change in such situations. For example, producers\xe2\x80\x99 ability to pass production taxes on to pipelines no doubt affects both producers and pipelines. Yet we had no trouble concluding that a state law restricting producers\xe2\x80\x99 ability to pass these taxes impermissibly attempted to manage \xe2\x80\x9ca matter within the sphere of FERC\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Exxon, supra, at 185\xe2\x80\x93186.

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach makes a snarl of our precedents. In Northern Natural, the Court held that the Act preempts state regulations requiring pipelines to buy gas ratably from gas wells. 372 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 90. The regulations in that case shared each of the principal features emphasized by the Court today. They governed background market conditions, not wholesale prices. Id., at 90\xe2\x80\x9391. The background conditions in question, pipelines\xe2\x80\x99 purchases from gas wells, affected both the federal field of wholesale sales and the state field of gas production. Id., at 92\xe2\x80\x9393. And the regulations took aim at the purchases\xe2\x80\x99 effects on production; they sought to promote conservation of natural resources by limiting how much gas pipelines could take from each well. Id., at 93. No matter; the Court still concluded that the regulations \xe2\x80\x9cinvade[d] the federal agency\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive domain.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 92. The factors that made no difference in Northern Natural should make no difference today.

Contrast Northern Natural with Northwest Central Pipeline Corp. v. State Corporation Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Kan.,489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 493 (1989), which involved state regulations that restricted the times when producers could take gas from wells. On this occasion the Court upheld the regulations\xe2\x80\x94not because the law aimed at the objective of gas conservation, but because the State pursued this end by regulating \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the physical ac[t] of drawing gas from the earth.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 510. Our precedents demand, in other words, that the Court focus in the present case upon what the State seeks to regulate (a pipeline practice that is subject to regulation by the Commission), not why the State seeks to regulate it (to curb the practice\xe2\x80\x99s effects on retail rates).

Trying to turn liabilities into assets, the Court brandishes statements from Northern Natural and Northwest Central that (in its view) discuss where state law was \xe2\x80\x9caimed\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cdirected.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. But read in context, these statements refer to the entity or activity that the state law regulates, not to which of the activity\xe2\x80\x99s effects the law seeks to control by regulating it. See, e.g., Northern Natural, supra, at 94 (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]ur cases have consistently recognized a significant distinction .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. between conservation measures aimed directly at interstate purchasers and wholesales .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, and those aimed at producers and production\xe2\x80\x9d); Northwest Central, supra, at 512 (\xe2\x80\x9c[This regulation] is directed to the behavior of gas producers\xe2\x80\x9d). The lawsuits at hand target pipelines (entities regulated by the Commission) for their manipulation of indices (behavior regulated by the Commission). That should have sufficed to establish preemption.

B

The Court also tallies several features of state antitrust law that, it believes, weigh against preemption. Ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. Once again the Court seems to have forgotten its precedents. We have said before that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Congress meant to draw a bright line easily ascertained, between state and federal jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d over the gas trade. Nantahala Power & Light Co. v. Thornburg,476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 953,966 (1986) (quoting FPC v. Southern Cal. Edison Co.,376 U.\xc2\xa0S. 205\xe2\x80\x93216 (1964)). Our decisions have therefore \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98squarely rejected\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d the theory, endorsed by the Court today, that the boundary between national and local authority turns on \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a case-by-case analysis of the impact of state regulation upon the national interest.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

State antitrust law, the Court begins, applies to \xe2\x80\x9call businesses in the marketplace\xe2\x80\x9d rather than just \xe2\x80\x9cnatural-gas companies in particular.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. So what? No principle of our natural-gas preemption jurisprudence distinguishes particularized state laws from state laws of general applicability. We have never suggested, for example, that a State may use general price-gouging laws to fix wholesale rates, or general laws about unfair trade practices to control wholesale contracts, or general common-carrier laws to administer interstate pipelines. The Court in any event could not have chosen a worse setting in which to attempt a distinction between general and particular laws. Like their federal counterpart, state antitrust laws tend to use the rule of reason to judge the lawfulness of challenged practices. Legal Aspects of Buying and Selling \xc2\xa710:12 (P. Zeidman ed. 2014\xe2\x80\x932015). This amorphous standard requires the reviewing court to consider \xe2\x80\x9ca variety of factors, including specific information about the relevant business, its condition before and after the restraint was imposed, and the restraint\xe2\x80\x99s history, nature, and effect.\xe2\x80\x9d State Oil Co. v. Khan,522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 3,10 (1997). Far from authorizing across-the-board application of a uniform requirement, therefore, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision will invite state antitrust courts to engage in targeted regulation of the natural-gas industry.

The Court also stresses the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98long history\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of state antitrust regulation. Ante, at 14. Again, quite beside the point. States have long regulated public utilities, yet the Natural Gas Act precludes them from using that established power to fix gas wholesale prices. United Fuel, 317 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 468. States also have long enacted laws to conserve natural resources, yet the Act precludes them from deploying that power to control purchases made by gas pipelines. Northern Natural, 372 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 93\xe2\x80\x9394. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s invocation of the pedigree of state antitrust law rests on air.

One need not launch this unbounded inquiry into the features of state law in order to preserve the States\xe2\x80\x99 authority to apply \xe2\x80\x9ctax laws,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cdisclosure laws,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cblue sky laws\xe2\x80\x9d to natural-gas companies, ante, at 12. One need only stand by the principle that if the Commission has authority over a subject, the States lack authority over that subject. The Commission\xe2\x80\x99s authority to regulate gas pipelines \xe2\x80\x9cin the public interest,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7717a, is a power to address matters that are traditionally the concern of utility regulators, not \xe2\x80\x9ca broad license to promote the general public welfare,\xe2\x80\x9d NAACP v. FPC,425 U.\xc2\xa0S. 662,669 (1976). We have explained that the Commission does not, for example, have power to superintend \xe2\x80\x9cemployment discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cunfair labor practices.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 670\xe2\x80\x93671. So the Act does not preempt state employment discrimination or labor laws. But the Commission does have power to consider, say, \xe2\x80\x9cconservation, environmental, and antitrust questions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 670, n.\xc2\xa06 (emphasis added). So the Act does preempt state antitrust laws.

C

At bottom, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision turns on its perception that the Natural Gas Act \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98was drawn with meticulous regard for the continued exercise of state power.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. No doubt the Act protects state authority in a variety of ways. It gives the Commission authority over only some parts of the gas trade. \xc2\xa7717(b). It establishes procedures under which the Commission may consult, collaborate, or share information with States. \xc2\xa7717p. It even provides that the Commission may regulate practices affecting wholesale rates \xe2\x80\x9cupon its own motion or upon complaint of any State.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7717d(a) (emphasis added). It should have gone without saying, however, that no law pursues its purposes at all costs. Nothing in the Act and nothing in our cases suggests that Congress protected state power in the way imagined by today\xe2\x80\x99s decision: by licensing state sorties into the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s domain whenever judges conclude that an incursion would not be too disruptive.

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s preoccupation with the purpose of preserving state authority is all the more inexpiable because that is not the Act\xe2\x80\x99s only purpose. The Act also has competing purposes, the most important of which is promoting \xe2\x80\x9cuniformity of regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Northern Natural, supra, at 91. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision impairs that objective. Before today, interstate pipelines knew that their practices relating to price indices had to comply with one set of regulations promulgated by the Commission. From now on, however, pipelines will have to ensure that their behavior conforms to the discordant regulations of 50 States\xe2\x80\x94or more accurately, to the discordant verdicts of untold state antitrust juries. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reassurance that pipelines may still invoke conflict preemption, see ante, at 15\xe2\x80\x9316, provides little comfort on this front. Conflict preemption will resolve only discrepancies between state and federal regulations, not the discrepancies among differing state regulations to which today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion subjects the industry.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\xe2\x80\x9cThe Natural Gas Act was designed . . . to produce a harmonious and comprehensive regulation of the industry. Neither state nor federal regulatory body was to encroach upon the jurisdiction of the other.\xe2\x80\x9d FPC v. Panhandle Eastern Pipe Line Co.,337 U.\xc2\xa0S. 498,513 (1949) (footnote omitted). Today, however, the Court allows the States to encroach. Worse still, it leaves pipelines guessing about when States will be allowed to encroach again. May States aim at retail rates under laws that share none of the features of antitrust law advertised today? Under laws that share only some of those features? May States apply their antitrust laws to pipelines without aiming at retail rates? But that is just the start. Who knows what other \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderations that weigh against a finding of pre-emption\xe2\x80\x9d remain to be unearthed in future cases? The Court\xe2\x80\x99s all-things-considered test does not make for a stable background against which to carry on the natural gas trade.

I would stand by the more principled and more workable line traced by our precedents. The Commission may regulate the practices alleged in this case; the States therefore may not. I respectfully dissent.
