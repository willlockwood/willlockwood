Henry Schein, Inc., et al. v. Archer & White Sales, Inc.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 17\xe2\x80\x931272.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 29, 2018\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 8, 2019

\n

Respondent Archer & White Sales, Inc., sued petitioner Henry Schein, Inc., alleging violations of federal and state antitrust law and seeking both money damages and injunctive relief. The relevant contract between the parties provided for arbitration of any dispute arising under or related to the agreement, except for, among other things, actions seeking injunctive relief. Invoking the Federal Arbitration Act, Schein asked the District Court to refer the matter to arbitration, but Archer & White argued that the dispute was not subject to arbitration because its complaint sought injunctive relief, at least in part. Schein contended that because the rules governing the contract provide that arbitrators have the power to resolve arbitrability questions, an arbitrator\xe2\x80\x94not the court\xe2\x80\x94should decide whether the arbitration agreement applied. Archer & White countered that Schein\xe2\x80\x99s argument for arbitration was wholly groundless, so the District Court could resolve the threshold arbitrability question. The District Court agreed with Archer & White and denied Schein\xe2\x80\x99s motion to compel arbitration. The Fifth Circuit affirmed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception to arbitrability is inconsistent with the Federal Arbitration Act and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent. Under the Act, arbitration is a matter of contract, and courts must enforce arbitration contracts according to their terms. Rent-A-Center, West, Inc. v. Jackson,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 63, 67. The parties to such a contract may agree to have an arbitrator decide not only the merits of a particular dispute, but also \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98gateway\xe2\x80\x99 questions of \xe2\x80\x98arbitrability.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 68\xe2\x80\x9369. Therefore, when the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract delegates the arbitrability question to an arbitrator, a court may not override the contract, even if the court thinks that the arbitrability claim is wholly groundless. That conclusion follows also from this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent. See AT&T Technologies, Inc. v. Communications Workers,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 643, 649\xe2\x80\x93650.

\n

Archer & White\xe2\x80\x99s counterarguments are unpersuasive. First, its argument that \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73 and 4 of the Act should be interpreted to mean that a court must always resolve questions of arbitrability has already been addressed and rejected by this Court. See, e.g., First Options of Chicago, Inc. v. Kaplan,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 938, 944. Second, its argument that \xc2\xa710 of the Act\xe2\x80\x94which provides for back-end judicial review of an arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s decision if an arbitrator has \xe2\x80\x9cexceeded\xe2\x80\x9d his or her \xe2\x80\x9cpowers\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94supports the conclusion that the court at the front end should also be able to say that the underlying issue is not arbitrable is inconsistent with the way Congress designed the Act. And it is not this Court\xe2\x80\x99s proper role to redesign the Act. Third, its argument that it would be a waste of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 time and money to send wholly groundless arbitrability questions to an arbitrator ignores the fact that the Act contains no \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception. This Court may not engraft its own exceptions onto the statutory text. Nor is it likely that the exception would save time and money systemically even if it might do so in some individual cases. Fourth, its argument that the exception is necessary to deter frivolous motions to compel arbitration overstates the potential problem. Arbitrators are already capable of efficiently disposing of frivolous cases and deterring frivolous motions, and such motions do not appear to have caused a substantial problem in those Circuits that have not recognized a \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception.

\n

The Fifth Circuit may address the question whether the contract at issue in fact delegated the arbitrability question to an arbitrator, as well as other properly preserved arguments, on remand. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

878 F.\xc2\xa03d 488, vacated and remanded.

HENRY SCHEIN, INC., et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. ARCHER AND WHITE SALES, INC.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

[January 8, 2019]

\n

\n

Justice Kavanaugh delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Under the Federal Arbitration Act, parties to a contract may agree that an arbitrator rather than a court will resolve disputes arising out of the contract. When a dispute arises, the parties sometimes may disagree not only about the merits of the dispute but also about the threshold arbitrability question\xe2\x80\x94that is, whether their arbitration agreement applies to the particular dispute. Who decides that threshold arbitrability question? Under the Act and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases, the question of who decides arbitrability is itself a question of contract. The Act allows parties to agree by contract that an arbitrator, rather than a court, will resolve threshold arbitrability questions as well as underlying merits disputes. Rent-A-Center, West, Inc. v. Jackson,\n\n561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 63, 68\xe2\x88\x9270 (2010); First Options of Chicago, Inc. v. Kaplan,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 938, 943\xe2\x88\x92944 (1995).

\n

Even when a contract delegates the arbitrability question to an arbitrator, some federal courts nonetheless will short-circuit the process and decide the arbitrability question themselves if the argument that the arbitration agreement applies to the particular dispute is \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless.\xe2\x80\x9d The question presented in this case is whether the \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception is consistent with the Federal Arbitration Act. We conclude that it is not. The Act does not contain a \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception, and we are not at liberty to rewrite the statute passed by Congress and signed by the President. When the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract delegates the arbitrability question to an arbitrator, the courts must respect the parties\xe2\x80\x99 decision as embodied in the contract. We vacate the contrary judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

I

\n

Archer and White is a small business that distributes dental equipment. Archer and White entered into a contract with Pelton and Crane, a dental equipment manufacturer, to distribute Pelton and Crane\xe2\x80\x99s equipment. The relationship eventually soured. As relevant here, Archer and White sued Pelton and Crane\xe2\x80\x99s successor-in-interest and Henry Schein, Inc. (collectively, Schein) in Federal District Court in Texas. Archer and White\xe2\x80\x99s complaint alleged violations of federal and state antitrust law, and sought both money damages and injunctive relief.

\n

The relevant contract between the parties provided:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cDisputes. This Agreement shall be governed by the laws of the State of North Carolina. Any dispute arising under or related to this Agreement (except for actions seeking injunctive relief and disputes related to trademarks, trade secrets, or other intellectual property of [Schein]), shall be resolved by binding arbitration in accordance with the arbitration rules of the American Arbitration Association [(AAA)]. The place of arbitration shall be in Charlotte, North Carolina.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 3a.

\n

After Archer and White sued, Schein invoked the Federal Arbitration Act and asked the District Court to refer the parties\xe2\x80\x99 antitrust dispute to arbitration. Archer and White objected, arguing that the dispute was not subject to arbitration because Archer and White\xe2\x80\x99s complaint sought injunctive relief, at least in part. According to Archer and White, the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract barred arbitration of disputes when the plaintiff sought injunctive relief, even if only in part.

\n

The question then became: Who decides whether the antitrust dispute is subject to arbitration? The rules of the American Arbitration Association provide that arbitrators have the power to resolve arbitrability questions. Schein contended that the contract\xe2\x80\x99s express incorporation of the American Arbitration Association\xe2\x80\x99s rules meant that an arbitrator\xe2\x80\x94not the court\xe2\x80\x94had to decide whether the arbitration agreement applied to this particular dispute. Archer and White responded that in cases where the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s argument for arbitration is wholly groundless\xe2\x80\x94as Archer and White argued was the case here\xe2\x80\x94the District Court itself may resolve the threshold question of arbitrability.

\n

Relying on Fifth Circuit precedent, the District Court agreed with Archer and White about the existence of a \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception, and ruled that Schein\xe2\x80\x99s argument for arbitration was wholly groundless. The District Court therefore denied Schein\xe2\x80\x99s motion to compel arbitration. The Fifth Circuit affirmed.

\n

In light of disagreement in the Courts of Appeals over whether the \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception is consistent with the Federal Arbitration Act, we granted certiorari, 585 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2018). Compare 878 F.\xc2\xa03d 488 (CA5 2017) (case below); Simply Wireless, Inc. v. T-Mobile US, Inc., 877 F.\xc2\xa03d 522 (CA4 2017); Douglas v. Regions Bank, 757 F.\xc2\xa03d 460 (CA5 2014); Turi v. Main Street Adoption Servs., LLP, 633 F.\xc2\xa03d 496 (CA6 2011); Qualcomm, Inc. v. Nokia Corp., 466 F.\xc2\xa03d 1366 (CA Fed. 2006), with Belnap v. Iasis Healthcare, 844 F.\xc2\xa03d 1272 (CA10 2017); Jones v. Waffle House, Inc., 866 F.\xc2\xa03d 1257 (CA11 2017); Douglas, 757 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 464 (Dennis, J., dissenting).

\n

II

\n

In 1925, Congress passed and President Coolidge signed the Federal Arbitration Act. As relevant here, the Act provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA written provision in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a contract evidencing a transaction involving commerce to settle by arbitration a controversy thereafter arising out of such contract .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72.

\n

Under the Act, arbitration is a matter of contract, and courts must enforce arbitration contracts according to their terms. Rent-A-Center, 561 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 67. Applying the Act, we have held that parties may agree to have an arbitrator decide not only the merits of a particular dispute but also \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98gateway\xe2\x80\x99 questions of \xe2\x80\x98arbitrability,\xe2\x80\x99 such as whether the parties have agreed to arbitrate or whether their agreement covers a particular controversy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 68\xe2\x80\x9369; see also First Options, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 943. We have explained that an \xe2\x80\x9cagreement to arbitrate a gateway issue is simply an additional, antecedent agreement the party seeking arbitration asks the federal court to enforce, and the FAA operates on this additional arbitration agree- ment just as it does on any other.\xe2\x80\x9d Rent-A-Center, 561 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 70.

\n

Even when the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract delegates the threshold arbitrability question to an arbitrator, the Fifth Circuit and some other Courts of Appeals have determined that the court rather than an arbitrator should decide the threshold arbitrability question if, under the contract, the argument for arbitration is wholly groundless. Those courts have reasoned that the \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception enables courts to block frivolous attempts to transfer disputes from the court system to arbitration.

\n

We conclude that the \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception is inconsistent with the text of the Act and with our precedent.

\n

We must interpret the Act as written, and the Act in turn requires that we interpret the contract as written. When the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract delegates the arbitrability question to an arbitrator, a court may not override the contract. In those circumstances, a court possesses no power to decide the arbitrability issue. That is true even if the court thinks that the argument that the arbitration agreement applies to a particular dispute is wholly groundless.

\n

That conclusion follows not only from the text of the Act but also from precedent. We have held that a court may not \xe2\x80\x9crule on the potential merits of the underlying\xe2\x80\x9d claim that is assigned by contract to an arbitrator, \xe2\x80\x9ceven if it appears to the court to be frivolous.\xe2\x80\x9d AT&T Technologies, Inc. v. Communications Workers,\n\n475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 643, 649\xe2\x80\x93650 (1986). A court has \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98no business weighing the merits of the grievance\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d because the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98agreement is to submit all grievances to arbitration, not merely those which the court will deem meritorious.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 650 (quoting Steelworkers v. American Mfg. Co.,\n\n363 U.\xc2\xa0S. 564, 568 (1960)).

\n

That AT&T Technologies principle applies with equal force to the threshold issue of arbitrability. Just as a court may not decide a merits question that the parties have delegated to an arbitrator, a court may not decide an arbitrability question that the parties have delegated to an arbitrator.

\n

In an attempt to overcome the statutory text and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases, Archer and White advances four main arguments. None is persuasive.

\n

First, Archer and White points to \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa73 and 4 of the Federal Arbitration Act. Section 3 provides that a court must stay litigation \xe2\x80\x9cupon being satisfied that the issue\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9creferable to arbitration\xe2\x80\x9d under the \xe2\x80\x9cagreement.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 4 says that a court, in response to a motion by an aggrieved party, must compel arbitration \xe2\x80\x9cin accordance with the terms of the agreement\xe2\x80\x9d when the court is \xe2\x80\x9csatisfied that the making of the agreement for arbitration or the failure to comply therewith is not in issue.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Archer and White interprets those provisions to mean, in essence, that a court must always resolve questions of arbitrability and that an arbitrator never may do so. But that ship has sailed. This Court has consistently held that parties may delegate threshold arbitrability questions to the arbitrator, so long as the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement does so by \xe2\x80\x9cclear and unmistakable\xe2\x80\x9d evidence. First Options, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 944 (alterations omitted); see also Rent-A-Center, 561 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 69, n. 1. To be sure, before referring a dispute to an arbitrator, the court determines whether a valid arbitration agreement exists. See\n\n9 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72. But if a valid agreement exists, and if the agreement delegates the arbitrability issue to an arbitrator, a court may not decide the arbitrability issue.

\n

Second, Archer and White cites \xc2\xa710 of the Act, which provides for back-end judicial review of an arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s decision if an arbitrator has \xe2\x80\x9cexceeded\xe2\x80\x9d his or her \xe2\x80\x9cpowers.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710(a)(4). According to Archer and White, if a court at the back end can say that the underlying issue was not arbitrable, the court at the front end should also be able to say that the underlying issue is not arbitrable. The dispositive answer to Archer and White\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa710 argument is that Congress designed the Act in a specific way, and it is not our proper role to redesign the statute. Archer and White\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa710 argument would mean, moreover, that courts presumably also should decide frivolous merits questions that have been delegated to an arbitrator. Yet we have already rejected that argument: When the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract assigns a matter to arbitration, a court may not resolve the merits of the dispute even if the court thinks that a party\xe2\x80\x99s claim on the merits is frivolous. AT&T Technologies, 475 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 649\xe2\x88\x92650. So, too, with arbitrability.

\n

Third, Archer and White says that, as a practical and policy matter, it would be a waste of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 time and money to send the arbitrability question to an arbitrator if the argument for arbitration is wholly groundless. In cases like this, as Archer and White sees it, the arbitrator will inevitably conclude that the dispute is not arbitrable and then send the case back to the district court. So why waste the time and money? The short answer is that the Act contains no \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception, and we may not engraft our own exceptions onto the statutory text. See Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Allapattah Services, Inc.,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 546, 556\xe2\x88\x92557 (2005).

\n

In addition, contrary to Archer and White\xe2\x80\x99s claim, it is doubtful that the \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception would save time and money systemically even if it might do so in some individual cases. Archer and White assumes that it is easy to tell when an argument for arbitration of a particular dispute is wholly groundless. We are dubious. The exception would inevitably spark collateral litigation (with briefing, argument, and opinion writing) over whether a seemingly unmeritorious argument for arbitration is wholly groundless, as opposed to groundless. We see no reason to create such a time-consuming sideshow.

\n

Archer and White further assumes that an arbitrator would inevitably reject arbitration in those cases where a judge would conclude that the argument for arbitration is wholly groundless. Not always. After all, an arbitrator might hold a different view of the arbitrability issue than a court does, even if the court finds the answer obvious. It is not unheard-of for one fair-minded adjudicator to think a decision is obvious in one direction but for another fair-minded adjudicator to decide the matter the other way.

\n

Fourth, Archer and White asserts another policy argument: that the \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception is necessary to deter frivolous motions to compel arbitration. Again, we may not rewrite the statute simply to accommodate that policy concern. In any event, Archer and White overstates the potential problem. Arbitrators can efficiently dispose of frivolous cases by quickly ruling that a claim is not in fact arbitrable. And under certain circumstances, arbitrators may be able to respond to frivolous arguments for arbitration by imposing fee-shifting and cost-shifting sanctions, which in turn will help deter and remedy frivolous motions to compel arbitration. We are not aware that frivolous motions to compel arbitration have caused a substantial problem in those Circuits that have not recognized a \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception.

\n

In sum, we reject the \xe2\x80\x9cwholly groundless\xe2\x80\x9d exception. The exception is inconsistent with the statutory text and with our precedent. It confuses the question of who decides arbitrability with the separate question of who prevails on arbitrability. When the parties\xe2\x80\x99 contract delegates the arbitrability question to an arbitrator, the courts must respect the parties\xe2\x80\x99 decision as embodied in the contract.

\n

We express no view about whether the contract at issue in this case in fact delegated the arbitrability question to an arbitrator. The Court of Appeals did not decide that issue. Under our cases, courts \xe2\x80\x9cshould not assume that the parties agreed to arbitrate arbitrability unless there is clear and unmistakable evidence that they did so.\xe2\x80\x9d First Options, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 944 (alterations omitted). On remand, the Court of Appeals may address that issue in the first instance, as well as other arguments that Archer and White has properly preserved.

\n

The judgment of the Court of Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
