\nGOODYEAR DUNLOP TIRES OPERATIONS, S.&NBSP;A.V. BROWN
\n

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

GOODYEAR DUNLOP TIRES OPERATIONS, S.\xc2\xa0A., et\xc2\xa0al. v. BROWN et ux., co-administrators of\nthe ESTATE OF BROWN, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the court of appeals of north carolina

\n

No. 10\xe2\x80\x9376.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 11, 2011\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 27, 2011

\n

Respondents, North Carolina residents whose sons died in a bus accident outside Paris, France, filed a suit for wrongful-death damages in North Carolina state court. Alleging that the accident was caused by tire failure, they named as defendants Goodyear USA, an Ohio corporation, and petitioners, three Goodyear USA subsidiaries, organized and operating, respectively, in Luxembourg, Turkey, and France. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 tires are manufactured primarily for European and Asian markets and differ in size and construction from tires ordinarily sold in the United States. Petitioners are not registered to do business in North Carolina; have no place of business, employees, or bank accounts in the State; do not design, manufacture, or advertise their products in the State; and do not solicit business in the State or sell or ship tires to North Carolina customers. Even so, a small percentage of their tires were distributed in North Carolina by other Goodyear USA affiliates. The trial court denied petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 motion to dismiss the claims against them for want of personal jurisdiction. The North Carolina Court of Appeals affirmed, concluding that the North Carolina courts had general jurisdiction over petitioners, whose tires had reached the State through \xe2\x80\x9cthe stream of commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Petitioners were not amenable to suit in North Carolina on claims unrelated to any activity of petitioners in the forum State. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause sets the outer boundaries of a state tribunal\xe2\x80\x99s authority to proceed against a defendant. The pathmarking decision of International Shoe Co. v. Washington, 326 U. S. 310, provides that state courts may exercise personal jurisdiction over an out-of-state defendant who has \xe2\x80\x9ccertain minimum contacts with [the State] such that the maintenance of the suit does not offend \xe2\x80\x98traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 316. Endeavoring to give specific content to the \xe2\x80\x9cfair play and substantial justice\xe2\x80\x9d concept, the Court in International Shoe classified cases involving out-of-state corporate defendants. First, the Court recognized that jurisdiction could be asserted where the corporation\xe2\x80\x99s in-state activity is \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuous and systematic\xe2\x80\x9d and gave rise to the episode-in-suit. Id., at 317. It also observed that the commission of \xe2\x80\x9csingle or occasional acts\xe2\x80\x9d in a State may be sufficient to render a corporation answerable in that State with respect to those acts, though not with respect to matters unrelated to the forum connections. Id., at 318. These two categories compose what is now known as \xe2\x80\x9cspecific jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Helicopteros Nacionales de Colombia, S. A. v. Hall, 466 U. S. 408, 414, n.\xc2\xa08. International Shoe distinguished from cases that fit within the \xe2\x80\x9cspecific jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d categories, \xe2\x80\x9cinstances in which the continuous corporate operations within a state [are] so substantial and of such a nature as to justify suit against it on causes of action arising from dealings entirely distinct from those activities.\xe2\x80\x9d 326 U. S., at 318. Adjudicatory authority so grounded is now called \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Helicopteros, 466 U. S., at 414, n.\xc2\xa09. Since International Shoe, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions have elaborated primarily on circumstances that warrant the exercise of specific jurisdiction. In only two decisions postdating International Shoe has this Court considered whether an out-of-state corporate defendant\xe2\x80\x99s in-state contacts were sufficiently \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuous and systematic\xe2\x80\x9d to justify the exercise of general jurisdiction over claims unrelated to those contacts: Perkins v. Benguet Consol. Mining Co., 342 U. S. 437; and Helicopteros, 466 U. S. 408. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Petitioners lack \xe2\x80\x9cthe kind of continuous and systematic general business contacts\xe2\x80\x9d necessary to allow North Carolina to entertain a suit against them unrelated to anything that connects them to the State. Helicopteros, 466 U. S., at 416. The stream-of-commerce cases on which the North Carolina court relied relate to exercises of specific jurisdiction in products liability actions, in which a nonresident defendant, acting outside the forum, places in the stream of commerce a product that ultimately causes harm inside the forum. Many state long-arm statutes authorize courts to exercise specific jurisdiction over manufacturers when the events in suit, or some of them, occurred within the forum State. The North Carolina court\xe2\x80\x99s stream-of-commerce analysis elided the essential difference between case-specific and general jurisdiction. Flow of a manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s products into the forum may bolster an affiliation germane to specific jurisdiction, see, e.g., World-Wide Volkswagen Corp. v. Woodson, 444 U. S. 286, 297; but ties serving to bolster the exercise of specific jurisdiction do not warrant a determination that, based on those ties, the forum has general jurisdiction over a defendant. A corporation\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuous activity of some sorts within a state,\xe2\x80\x9d International Shoe instructed, \xe2\x80\x9cis not enough to support the demand that the corporation be amenable to suits unrelated to that activity.\xe2\x80\x9d 326 U. S., at 318.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Measured against Helicopteros and Perkins, North Carolina is not a forum in which it would be permissible to subject petitioners to general jurisdiction. In the 1952 Perkins case, general jurisdiction was appropriately exercised over a Philippine corporation sued in Ohio, where the company\xe2\x80\x99s affairs were overseen during World War II. In Helicopteros, however, the survivors of U. S. citizens killed when a helicopter owned by a Colombian corporation crashed in Peru could not maintain wrongful-death actions against that corporation in Texas, where the company\xe2\x80\x99s contacts \xe2\x80\x9cconsisted of sending its chief executive officer to Houston for a contract-negotiation session; accepting into its New York bank account checks drawn on a Houston bank; purchasing helicopters, equipment, and training services from [a Texas enterprise]; and sending personnel to [Texas] for training.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 416. These links to Texas did not \xe2\x80\x9cconstitute the kind of continuous and systematic general business contacts \xe2\x80\xa6 found to exist in Perkins,\xe2\x80\x9d and were insufficient to support the exercise of jurisdiction over a claim that neither \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98ar[o]se out of\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 no[r] related to\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s activities in Texas. Id., at 415\xe2\x80\x93416. This Court sees no reason to differentiate from the ties to Texas held insufficient in Helicopteros, the sales of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 tires sporadically made in North Carolina through intermediaries. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Neither below nor in their brief in opposition to the petition for certiorari did respondents urge disregard of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 discrete status as subsidiaries and treatment of all Goodyear entities as a \xe2\x80\x9cunitary business,\xe2\x80\x9d so that jurisdiction over the parent would draw in the subsidiaries as well. Respondents have therefore forfeited this contention. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

199 N.\xc2\xa0C. App. 50, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 382, reversed.

GOODYEAR DUNLOP TIRES OPERATIONS, S.\xc2\xa0A., et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. EDGAR D. BROWN,\n et ux., co-administrators of the ESTATE\nOF JULIAN DAVID BROWN, et\xc2\xa0al.

on writ of certiorari to the court of appeals of north carolina

\n

[June 27, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns the jurisdiction of state courts over corporations organized and operating abroad. We address, in particular, this question: Are foreign subsidiaries of a United States parent corporation amenable to suit in state court on claims unrelated to any activity of the subsidiaries in the forum State?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A bus accident outside Paris that took the lives of two 13-year-old boys from North Carolina gave rise to the liti-gation we here consider. Attributing the accident to a defective tire manufactured in Turkey at the plant of a foreign subsidiary of The Goodyear Tire and Rubber Company (Goodyear USA), the boys\xe2\x80\x99 parents commenced an action for damages in a North Carolina state court; they named as defendants Goodyear USA, an Ohio corporation, and three of its subsidiaries, organized and operating, respectively, in Turkey, France, and Luxembourg. Goodyear USA, which had plants in North Carolina and regularly engaged in commercial activity there, did not contest the North Carolina court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction over it; Goodyear USA\xe2\x80\x99s foreign subsidiaries, however, maintained that North Carolina lacked adjudicatory authority over them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A state court\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of jurisdiction exposes defendants to the State\xe2\x80\x99s coercive power, and is therefore subject to review for compatibility with the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Due Process Clause. International Shoe\nCo. v. Washington, 326 U. S. 310, 316 (1945) (assertion of jurisdiction over out-of-state corporation must comply with \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Milliken v. Meyer, 311 U. S. 457, 463 (1940))). Opinions in the wake of the pathmarking International Shoe decision have differentiated between general or all-purpose jurisdiction, and specific or case-linked jurisdiction. Helicopteros Nacionales de Colombia, S. A. v. Hall, 466 U. S. 408, 414, nn. 8, 9 (1984).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A court may assert general jurisdiction over foreign (sister-state or foreign-country) corporations to hear any and all claims against them when their affiliations with the State are so \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuous and systematic\xe2\x80\x9d as to render them essentially at home in the forum State. See International Shoe, 326 U. S., at 317. Specific jurisdiction, on the other hand, depends on an \xe2\x80\x9caffiliatio[n] between the forum and the underlying controversy,\xe2\x80\x9d principally, activity or an occurrence that takes place in the forum State and is therefore subject to the State\xe2\x80\x99s regulation. von Mehren & Trautman, Jurisdiction to Adjudicate: A Suggested Analysis, 79 Harv. L. Rev. 1121, 1136 (1966) (hereinafter von Mehren & Trautman); see Brilmayer et al., A General Look at General Jurisdiction, 66 Texas L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 721, 782 (1988) (hereinafter Brilmayer). In contrast to general, all-purpose jurisdiction, specific jurisdiction is confined to adjudication of \xe2\x80\x9cissues deriving from, or connected with, the very controversy that establishes jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d von Mehren & Trautman 1136.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the episode-in-suit, the bus accident, occurred in France, and the tire alleged to have caused the accident was manufactured and sold abroad, North Carolina courts lacked specific jurisdiction to adjudicate the controversy. The North Carolina Court of Appeals so acknowledged. Brown v. Meter, 199 N.\xc2\xa0C. App. 50, 57\xe2\x80\x9358, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 382, 388 (2009). Were the foreign subsidiaries nonetheless amenable to general jurisdiction in North Carolina courts? Confusing or blending general and specific jurisdictional inquiries, the North Carolina courts answered yes. Some of the tires made abroad by Goodyear\xe2\x80\x99s foreign subsidiaries, the North Carolina Court of Appeals stressed, had reached North Carolina through \xe2\x80\x9cthe stream of commerce\xe2\x80\x9d; that connection, the Court of Appeals believed, gave North Carolina courts the handle needed for the exercise of general jurisdiction over the foreign corporations. Id., at 67\xe2\x80\x9368, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 394\xe2\x80\x93395.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A connection so limited between the forum and the for-eign corporation, we hold, is an inadequate basis for the exercise of general jurisdiction. Such a connection does not establish the \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuous and systematic\xe2\x80\x9d affiliation necessary to empower North Carolina courts to entertain claims unrelated to the foreign corporation\xe2\x80\x99s contacts with the State.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On April 18, 2004, a bus destined for Charles de Gaulle Airport overturned on a road outside Paris, France. Passengers on the bus were young soccer players from North Carolina beginning their journey home. Two 13-year-olds, Julian Brown and Matthew Helms, sustained fatal injuries. The boys\xe2\x80\x99 parents, respondents in this Court, filed a suit for wrongful-death damages in the Superior Court of Onslow County, North Carolina, in their capacity as administrators of the boys\xe2\x80\x99 estates. Attributing the accident to a tire that failed when its plies separated, the parents alleged negligence in the \xe2\x80\x9cdesign, construction, testing, and inspection\xe2\x80\x9d of the tire. 199 N.\xc2\xa0C. App., at 51, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 384 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Goodyear Luxembourg Tires, SA (Goodyear Luxembourg), Goodyear Lastikleri T.\xc2\xa0A.\xc2\xa0S. (Goodyear Turkey), and Goodyear Dunlop Tires France, SA (Goodyear France), petitioners here, were named as defendants. Incorporated in Luxembourg, Turkey, and France, respectively, petitioners are indirect subsidiaries of Goodyear USA, an Ohio corporation also named as a defendant in the suit. Petitioners manufacture tires primarily for sale in European and Asian markets. Their tires differ in size and construction from tires ordinarily sold in the United States. They are designed to carry significantly heavier loads, and to serve under road conditions and speed limits in the manufacturers\xe2\x80\x99 primary markets.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In contrast to the parent company, Goodyear USA, which does not contest the North Carolina courts\xe2\x80\x99 personal jurisdiction over it, petitioners are not registered to do business in North Carolina. They have no place of business, employees, or bank accounts in North Carolina. They do not design, manufacture, or advertise their products in North Carolina. And they do not solicit business in North Carolina or themselves sell or ship tires to North Carolina customers. Even so, a small percentage of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 tires (tens of thousands out of tens of millions manufactured between 2004 and 2007) were distributed within North Carolina by other Goodyear USA affiliates. These tires were typically custom ordered to equip specialized vehicles such as cement mixers, waste haulers, and boat and horse trailers. Petitioners state, and respondents do not here deny, that the type of tire involved in the accident, a Goodyear Regional RHS tire manufactured\nby Goodyear Turkey, was never distributed in North Carolina.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners moved to dismiss the claims against them for want of personal jurisdiction. The trial court denied the motion, and the North Carolina Court of Appeals af-firmed. Acknowledging that the claims neither \xe2\x80\x9crelated\nto, nor \xe2\x80\xa6 ar[o]se from, [petitioners\xe2\x80\x99] contacts with North Carolina,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court of Appeals confined its analysis to \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral rather than specific jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d which the court recognized required a \xe2\x80\x9chigher threshold\xe2\x80\x9d showing: A defendant must have \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuous and systematic contacts\xe2\x80\x9d with the forum. Id., at 58, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 388 (internal quotation marks omitted). That threshold was crossed, the court determined, when petitioners placed their tires \xe2\x80\x9cin the stream of interstate commerce without any limitation on the extent to which those tires could be sold in North Carolina.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 67, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 394.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nothing in the record, the court observed, indicated\nthat petitioners \xe2\x80\x9ctook any affirmative action to cause tires which they had manufactured to be shipped into North Carolina.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 64, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 392. The court found, however, that tires made by petitioners reached North Carolina as a consequence of a \xe2\x80\x9chighly-organized distribution process\xe2\x80\x9d involving other Goodyear USA subsidiaries. Id., at 67, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 394. Petitioners, the court noted, made \xe2\x80\x9cno attempt to keep these tires from reaching the North Carolina market.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 66, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 393. Indeed, the very tire involved in the accident, the court observed, conformed to tire standards established by the U. S. Department of Transportation and bore markings required for sale in the United States. Ibid.[Footnote 2] As further support, the court invoked North Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in providing a forum in which its citizens are able to seek redress for [their] injuries,\xe2\x80\x9d and noted the hardship North Carolina plaintiffs would experience \xe2\x80\x9c[were they] required to litigate their claims in France,\xe2\x80\x9d a country to which they have no ties. Id., at 68, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 394. The North Carolina Supreme Court denied discretionary review. Brown v. Meter, 364 N.\xc2\xa0C. 128, 695 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 756 (2010).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to decide whether the general jurisdiction the North Carolina courts asserted over petitioners is consistent with the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. 561 U. S. ___ (2010).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment sets the outer boundaries of a state tribunal\xe2\x80\x99s authority to proceed against a defendant. Shaffer v. Heitner, 433 U. S. 186, 207 (1977). The canonical opinion in this area remains International Shoe, 326 U. S. 310, in which we held that a State may authorize its courts to exercise personal jurisdiction over an out-of-state defendant if the defendant has \xe2\x80\x9ccertain minimum contacts with [the State] such that the maintenance of the suit does not offend \xe2\x80\x98traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 316 (quoting Meyer, 311 U. S., at 463).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Endeavoring to give specific content to the \xe2\x80\x9cfair play and substantial justice\xe2\x80\x9d concept, the Court in International Shoe classified cases involving out-of-state corporate defendants. First, as in International Shoe itself, jurisdiction unquestionably could be asserted where the corporation\xe2\x80\x99s in-state activity is \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuous and systematic\xe2\x80\x9d and that activity gave rise to the episode-in-suit. 326 U. S., at 317. Further, the Court observed, the commission of certain \xe2\x80\x9csingle or occasional acts\xe2\x80\x9d in a State may be sufficient to render a corporation answerable in that State with respect to those acts, though not with respect to matters unrelated to the forum connections. Id., at 318. The heading courts today use to encompass these two International Shoe categories is \xe2\x80\x9cspecific jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d See von Mehren & Trautman 1144\xe2\x80\x931163. Adjudicatory authority is \xe2\x80\x9cspecific\xe2\x80\x9d when the suit \xe2\x80\x9caris[es] out of or relate[s] to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s contacts with the forum.\xe2\x80\x9d Helicopteros, 466 U. S., at 414, n.\xc2\xa08.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0International Shoe distinguished from cases that fit within the \xe2\x80\x9cspecific jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d categories, \xe2\x80\x9cinstances in which the continuous corporate operations within a state [are] so substantial and of such a nature as to justify suit against it on causes of action arising from dealings entirely distinct from those activities.\xe2\x80\x9d 326 U. S., at 318. Adjudicatory authority so grounded is today called \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Helicopteros, 466 U. S., at 414, n.\xc2\xa09. For an individual, the paradigm forum for the exercise of general jurisdiction is the individual\xe2\x80\x99s domicile; for a corporation, it is an equivalent place, one in which the corporation is fairly regarded as at home. See Brilmayer 728 (identifying domicile, place of incorporation, and principal place of business as \xe2\x80\x9cparadig[m]\xe2\x80\x9d bases for the exercise of general jurisdiction).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since International Shoe, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions have elaborated primarily on circumstances that warrant the exercise of specific jurisdiction, particularly in cases involving \xe2\x80\x9csingle or occasional acts\xe2\x80\x9d occurring or having their impact within the forum State. As a rule in these cases, this Court has inquired whether there was \xe2\x80\x9csome act by which the defendant purposefully avail[ed] itself of the privilege of conducting activities within the forum State, thus invoking the benefits and protections of its laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Hanson v. Denckla, 357 U. S. 235, 253 (1958). See, e.g., World-Wide Volkswagen Corp. v. Woodson, 444 U. S. 286, 287, 297 (1980) (Oklahoma court may not exercise personal jurisdiction \xe2\x80\x9cover a nonresident automobile retailer and its wholesale distributor in a products-liability action, when the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 only connection with Oklahoma is the fact that an automobile sold in New York to New York residents became involved in an accident in Oklahoma\xe2\x80\x9d); Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz, 471 U. S. 462, 474\xe2\x80\x93475 (1985) (franchisor headquartered in Florida may maintain breach-of-contract action in Florida against Michigan franchisees, where agreement contemplated on-going interactions between franchisees and franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s headquarters); Asahi Metal Industry Co. v. Superior Court of Cal., Solano Cty., 480 U. S. 102, 105 (1987) (Taiwanese tire manufacturer settled product liability action brought in California and sought indemnification there from Japanese valve assembly manufacturer; Japanese company\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmere awareness \xe2\x80\xa6 that the components it manufactured, sold, and delivered outside the United States would reach the forum State in the stream of commerce\xe2\x80\x9d held insufficient to permit California court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudication of Taiwanese company\xe2\x80\x99s cross-complaint); id., at 109 (opinion of O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J.); id., at 116\xe2\x80\x93117 (Brennan, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). See also Twitchell, The Myth of General Jurisdiction, 101 Harv. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 610, 628 (1988) (in the wake of International Shoe, \xe2\x80\x9cspecific jurisdiction has become the centerpiece of modern jurisdiction theory, while general jurisdiction plays a reduced role\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In only two decisions postdating International Shoe, discussed infra, at 11\xe2\x80\x9313, has this Court considered whether an out-of-state corporate defendant\xe2\x80\x99s in-state contacts were sufficiently \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuous and systematic\xe2\x80\x9d to justify the exercise of general jurisdiction over claims unrelated to those contacts: Perkins v. Benguet Consol. Mining Co., 342 U. S. 437 (1952) (general jurisdiction appropriately exercised over Philippine corporation sued in Ohio, where the company\xe2\x80\x99s affairs were overseen during World War II); and Helicopteros, 466 U. S. 408 (helicopter owned by Colombian corporation crashed in Peru; survivors of U. S. citizens who died in the crash, the Court\nheld, could not maintain wrongful-death actions against the Colombian corporation in Texas, for the corporation\xe2\x80\x99s helicopter purchases and purchase-linked activity in Texas were insufficient to subject it to Texas court\xe2\x80\x99s general jurisdiction).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To justify the exercise of general jurisdiction over petitioners, the North Carolina courts relied on the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 placement of their tires in the \xe2\x80\x9cstream of commerce.\xe2\x80\x9d See supra, at 5. The stream-of-commerce metaphor has been invoked frequently in lower court decisions permitting \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction in products liability cases in which the product has traveled through an extensive chain of distribution before reaching the ultimate consumer.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 W. Fletcher, Cyclopedia of the Law of Corporations \xc2\xa78640.40, p. 133 (rev. ed. 2007). Typically, in such cases, a nonresident defendant, acting outside the forum, places in the stream of commerce a product that ultimately causes harm inside the forum. See generally Dayton, Personal Jurisdiction and the Stream of Commerce, 7 Rev. Litigation 239, 262\xe2\x80\x93268 (1988) (discussing origins and evolution of the stream-of-commerce doctrine).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Many States have enacted long-arm statutes authorizing courts to exercise specific jurisdiction over manufacturers when the events in suit, or some of them, occurred within the forum state. For example, the \xe2\x80\x9cLocal Injury; Foreign Act\xe2\x80\x9d subsection of North Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s long-arm statute authorizes North Carolina courts to exercise personal jurisdiction in \xe2\x80\x9cany action claiming injury to person or property within this State arising out of [the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s] act or omission outside this State,\xe2\x80\x9d if, \xe2\x80\x9cin addition[,] at or about the time of the injury,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9d[p]roducts \xe2\x80\xa6 manufactured by the defendant were used or consumed, within this State in the ordinary course of trade.\xe2\x80\x9d N.\xc2\xa0C. Gen. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa71\xe2\x80\x9375.4(4)(b) (Lexis 2009).[Footnote 3] As the North Carolina Court of Appeals recognized, this provision of the State\xe2\x80\x99s long-arm statute \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not apply to this case,\xe2\x80\x9d for both the act alleged to have caused injury (the fabrication of the allegedly defective tire) and its impact (the accident) occurred outside the forum. See 199 N.\xc2\xa0C. App., at 61, n.\xc2\xa06, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 390, n.\xc2\xa06.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The North Carolina court\xe2\x80\x99s stream-of-commerce analysis elided the essential difference between case-specific and all-purpose (general) jurisdiction. Flow of a manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s products into the forum, we have explained, may bolster an affiliation germane to specific jurisdiction. See, e.g., World-Wide Volkswagen, 444 U. S., at 297 (where \xe2\x80\x9cthe sale of a product \xe2\x80\xa6 is not simply an isolated occurrence, but arises from the efforts of the manufacturer or distributor to serve \xe2\x80\xa6 the market for its product in [several] States, it is not unreasonable to subject it to suit in one\nof those States if its allegedly defective merchandise has there been the source of injury to its owner or to others\xe2\x80\x9d (em-phasis added)). But ties serving to bolster the exercise\nof specific jurisdiction do not warrant a determination that, based on those ties, the forum has general jurisdiction over a defendant. See, e.g., Stabilisierungsfonds Fur Wein v. Kaiser Stuhl Wine Distributors Pty. Ltd., 647 F.\xc2\xa02d 200, 203, n.\xc2\xa05 (CADC 1981) (defendants\xe2\x80\x99 marketing arrangements, although \xe2\x80\x9cadequate to permit litigation of claims relating to [their] introduction of \xe2\x80\xa6 wine into\nthe United States stream of commerce, \xe2\x80\xa6 would not be adequate to support general, \xe2\x80\x98all purpose\xe2\x80\x99 adjudicatory authority\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A corporation\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9ccontinuous activity of some sorts within a state,\xe2\x80\x9d International Shoe instructed, \xe2\x80\x9cis not enough to support the demand that the corporation be amenable to suits unrelated to that activity.\xe2\x80\x9d 326 U. S., at 318. Our 1952 decision in Perkins v. Benguet Consol. Mining Co. remains \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he textbook case of general jurisdiction appropriately exercised over a foreign corporation that has not consented to suit in the forum.\xe2\x80\x9d Donahue v. Far Eastern Air Transport Corp., 652 F.\xc2\xa02d 1032, 1037 (CADC 1981).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sued in Ohio, the defendant in Perkins was a Philippine mining corporation that had ceased activities in the Philippines during World War II. To the extent that the company was conducting any business during and immediately after the Japanese occupation of the Philippines, it was doing so in Ohio: the corporation\xe2\x80\x99s president maintained his office there, kept the company files in that office, and supervised from the Ohio office \xe2\x80\x9cthe necessarily limited wartime activities of the company.\xe2\x80\x9d Perkins, 342 U. S., at 447\xe2\x80\x93448. Although the claim-in-suit did not arise in Ohio, this Court ruled that it would not violate due process for Ohio to adjudicate the controversy. Ibid.; see Keeton v. Hustler Magazine, Inc., 465 U. S. 770, 779\xe2\x80\x93780, n. 11 (1984) (Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of general jurisdiction was permissible in Perkins because \xe2\x80\x9cOhio was the corporation\xe2\x80\x99s principal, if temporary, place of business\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We next addressed the exercise of general jurisdiction over an out-of-state corporation over three decades later, in Helicopteros. In that case, survivors of United States citizens who died in a helicopter crash in Peru instituted wrongful-death actions in a Texas state court against the owner and operator of the helicopter, a Colombian corporation. The Colombian corporation had no place of business in Texas and was not licensed to do business there. \xe2\x80\x9cBasically, [the company\xe2\x80\x99s] contacts with Texas consisted of sending its chief executive officer to Houston for a\ncontract-negotiation session; accepting into its New York bank account checks drawn on a Houston bank; purchasing helicopters, equipment, and training services from [a Texas enterprise] for substantial sums; and sending personnel to [Texas] for training.\xe2\x80\x9d 466 U. S., at 416. These links to Texas, we determined, did not \xe2\x80\x9cconstitute the kind of continuous and systematic general business contacts \xe2\x80\xa6 found to exist in Perkins,\xe2\x80\x9d and were insufficient to support the exercise of jurisdiction over a claim that neither \xe2\x80\x9car[o]se out of \xe2\x80\xa6 no[r] related to\xe2\x80\x9d the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s activities in Texas. Id., at 415\xe2\x80\x93416 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Helicopteros concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cmere purchases [made in the forum State], even if occurring at regular intervals, are not enough to warrant a State\xe2\x80\x99s assertion of [general] jurisdiction over a nonresident corporation in a cause of action not related to those purchase transactions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 418. We see no reason to differentiate from the ties to Texas held insufficient in Helicopteros, the sales of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 tires sporadically made in North Carolina through intermediaries. Under the sprawling view of general jurisdiction urged by respondents and embraced by the North Carolina Court of Appeals, any substantial manufacturer or seller of goods would be amenable to suit, on any claim for relief, wherever its products are distributed. But cf. World-Wide Volkswagen, 444 U. S., at 296 (every seller of chattels does not, by virtue of the sale, \xe2\x80\x9cappoint the chattel his agent for service of process\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Measured against Helicopteros and Perkins, North Caro-lina is not a forum in which it would be permissible to subject petitioners to general jurisdiction. Unlike the defendant in Perkins, whose sole wartime business activity was conducted in Ohio, petitioners are in no sense at home in North Carolina. Their attenuated connections to the State, see supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935, fall far short of the \xe2\x80\x9cthe continuous and systematic general business contacts\xe2\x80\x9d necessary to empower North Carolina to entertain suit against them on claims unrelated to anything that connects them to the State. Helicopteros, 466 U. S., at 416.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents belatedly assert a \xe2\x80\x9csingle enterprise\xe2\x80\x9d theory, asking us to consolidate petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 ties to North Carolina with those of Goodyear USA and other Goodyear entities. See Brief for Respondents 44\xe2\x80\x9350. In effect, respondents would have us pierce Goodyear corporate veils, at least for jurisdictional purposes. See Brilmayer & Paisley, Personal Jurisdiction and Substantive Legal Relations: Corporations, Conspiracies, and Agency, 74 Cal. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1, 14, 29\xe2\x80\x9330 (1986) (merging parent and subsidiary for jurisdictional purposes requires an inquiry \xe2\x80\x9ccomparable to the corporate law question of piercing the corporate veil\xe2\x80\x9d). But see 199 N.\xc2\xa0C. App., at 64, 681 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 392 (North Carolina Court of Appeals understood that petitioners are \xe2\x80\x9cseparate corporate entities \xe2\x80\xa6 not directly re-sponsible for the presence in North Carolina of tires that they had manufactured\xe2\x80\x9d). Neither below nor in their brief in opposition to the petition for certiorari did respondents urge disregard of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 discrete status as subsidiaries and treatment of all Goodyear entities as a \xe2\x80\x9cunitary business,\xe2\x80\x9d so that jurisdiction over the parent would draw in the subsidiaries as well.[Footnote 6] Brief for Respondents 44. Respondents have therefore forfeited this contention, and we do not address it. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 15.2; Granite Rock Co. v. Teamsters, 561 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 16).
