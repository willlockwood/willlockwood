\n\t\t\t\tLEXMARK INTERNATIONAL, INC. v. STATIC CONTROL COMPONENTS, INC.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 12\xe2\x80\x93873.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 3, 2013\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 25, 2014\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tPetitioner Lexmark sells the only style of toner cartridges that work with the company\xe2\x80\x99s laser printers, but \xe2\x80\x9cremanufacturers\xe2\x80\x9d acquire and refurbish used Lexmark cartridges to sell in competition with Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s own new and refurbished ones. Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cPrebate\xe2\x80\x9d program gives customers a discount on new cartridges if they agree to return empty cartridges to the company. Each Prebate cartridge has a microchip that disables the empty cartridge unless Lexmark replaces the chip. Respondent Static Control, a maker and seller of components for the remanufacture of Lexmark cartridges, developed a microchip that mimicked Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s. Lexmark sued for copyright infringement, but Static Control counterclaimed, alleging that Lexmark engaged in false or misleading advertising in violation of \xc2\xa743(a) of the Lanham Act, \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71125(a), and that its misrepresentations had caused Static Control lost sales and damage to its business reputation. The District Court held that Static Control lacked \xe2\x80\x9cprudential standing\xe2\x80\x9d to bring the Lanham Act claim, applying a multifactor balancing test the court attributed to Associated Gen. Contractors of Cal., Inc. v. Carpenters, \n\n459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519\n. In reversing, the Sixth Circuit relied on the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9creasonable interest\xe2\x80\x9d test.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Static Control has adequately pleaded the elements of a Lanham Act cause of action for false advertising. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9322.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The question here is whether Static Control falls within the class of plaintiffs that Congress authorized to sue under \xc2\xa71125(a). To decide that question, this Court must determine the provision\xe2\x80\x99s meaning, using traditional principles of statutory interpretation. It is misleading to label this a \xe2\x80\x9cprudential standing\xe2\x80\x9d question. Lexmark bases its \xe2\x80\x9cprudential standing\xe2\x80\x9d arguments on Associated General Contractors, but that case rested on statutory considerations: The Court sought to \xe2\x80\x9cascertain,\xe2\x80\x9d as a statutory-interpretation matter, the \xe2\x80\x9cscope of the private remedy created by\xe2\x80\x9d Congress in \xc2\xa74 of the Clayton Act, and the \xe2\x80\x9cclass of persons who [could] maintain a private damages action under\xe2\x80\x9d that legislatively conferred cause of action, 459 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 529, 532. And while this Court may have placed the \xe2\x80\x9czone of interests\xe2\x80\x9d test that Static Control relies on under the \xe2\x80\x9cprudential\xe2\x80\x9d rubric in the past, see, e.g., Elk Grove Unified School Dist. v. Newdow, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n, it does not belong there any more than Associated General Contractors does. Rather, whether a plaintiff comes within the zone of interests requires the Court to determine, using traditional statutory-interpretation tools, whether a legislatively conferred cause of action encompasses a particular plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim. See, e. g., Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83\n, and n.\xc2\xa02. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The \xc2\xa71125(a) cause of action extends to plaintiffs who fall within the zone of interests protected by that statute and whose injury was proximately caused by a violation of that statute. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0A statutory cause of action is presumed to extend only to plaintiffs whose interests \xe2\x80\x9cfall within the zone of interests protected by the law invoked.\xe2\x80\x9d Allen v. Wright, \n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 737\n. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he breadth of [that] zone .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. varies according to the provisions of law at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Bennett v. Spear, \n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 154\n. The Lanham Act includes a detailed statement of its purposes, including, as relevant here, \xe2\x80\x9cprotect[ing] persons engaged in [commerce within the control of Congress] against unfair competition,\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71127; and \xe2\x80\x9cunfair competition\xe2\x80\x9d was understood at common law to be concerned with injuries to business reputation and present and future sales. Thus, to come within the zone of interests in a \xc2\xa71125(a) false-advertising suit, a plaintiff must allege an injury to a commercial interest in reputation or sales. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0A statutory cause of action is also presumed to be limited to plaintiffs whose injuries are proximately caused by violations of the statute. See, e.g., Holmes v. Securities Investor Protection Corporation, \n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 258\n\xe2\x80\x93270. This requirement generally bars suits for alleged harm that is \xe2\x80\x9ctoo remote\xe2\x80\x9d from the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s unlawful conduct, such as when the harm is purely derivative of \xe2\x80\x9cmisfortunes visited upon a third person by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s acts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 268\xe2\x80\x93269. In a sense, all commercial injuries from false advertising are derivative of those suffered by consumers deceived by the advertising. But since the Lanham Act authorizes suit only for commercial injuries, the intervening consumer-deception step is not fatal to the proximate-cause showing the statute requires. Cf. Bridge v. Phoenix Bond & Indemnity Co., \n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 639\n. Thus, a plaintiff suing under \xc2\xa71125(a) ordinarily must show that its economic or reputational injury flows directly from the deception wrought by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s advertising; and that occurs when deception of consumers causes them to withhold trade from the plaintiff. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9315.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(3)\xc2\xa0Direct application of the zone-of-interests test and the proximate-cause requirement supplies the relevant limits on who may sue under \xc2\xa71125(a). These principles provide better guidance than the multifactor balancing test urged by Lexmark, the direct-competitor test, or the reasonable-interest test applied by the Sixth Circuit. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Under these principles, Static Control comes within the class of plaintiffs authorized to sue under \xc2\xa71125(a). Its alleged injuries\xe2\x80\x94lost sales and damage to its business reputation\xe2\x80\x94fall within the zone of interests protected by the Act, and Static Control sufficiently alleged that its injuries were proximately caused by Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentations. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9322.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t697 F.\xc2\xa03d 387, affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case requires us to decide whether respondent, Static Control Components, Inc., may sue petitioner, Lex- mark International, Inc., for false advertising under the Lanham Act, \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71125(a).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI. Background\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLexmark manufactures and sells laser printers. It also sells toner cartridges for those printers (toner being the powdery ink that laser printers use to create images on paper). Lexmark designs its printers to work only with its own style of cartridges, and it therefore dominates the market for cartridges compatible with its printers. That market, however, is not devoid of competitors. Other businesses, called \xe2\x80\x9cremanufacturers,\xe2\x80\x9d acquire used Lex- mark toner cartridges, refurbish them, and sell them in competition with new and refurbished cartridges sold by Lexmark.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLexmark would prefer that its customers return their empty cartridges to it for refurbishment and resale, rather than sell those cartridges to a remanufacturer. So Lexmark introduced what it called a \xe2\x80\x9cPrebate\xe2\x80\x9d program, which enabled customers to purchase new toner cartridges at a 20-percent discount if they would agree to return the cartridge to Lexmark once it was empty. Those terms were communicated to consumers through notices printed on the toner-cartridge boxes, which advised the consumer that opening the box would indicate assent to the terms\xe2\x80\x94a practice commonly known as \xe2\x80\x9cshrinkwrap licensing,\xe2\x80\x9d see, e.g., ProCD, Inc. v. Zeidenberg, 86 F.\xc2\xa03d 1447, 1449 (CA7 1996). To enforce the Prebate terms, Lexmark included a microchip in each Prebate cartridge that would disable the cartridge after it ran out of toner; for the cartridge to be used again, the microchip would have to be replaced by Lexmark.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStatic Control is not itself a manufacturer or remanufacturer of toner cartridges. It is, rather, \xe2\x80\x9cthe market leader [in] making and selling the components necessary to remanufacture Lexmark cartridges.\xe2\x80\x9d 697 F.\xc2\xa03d 387, 396 (CA6 2012) (case below). In addition to supplying remanufacturers with toner and various replacement parts, Static Control developed a microchip that could mimic the microchip in Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s Prebate cartridges. By purchasing Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s microchips and using them to replace the Lexmark microchip, remanufacturers were able to refurbish and resell used Prebate cartridges.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLexmark did not take kindly to that development. In 2002, it sued Static Control, alleging that Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s microchips violated both the Copyright Act of 1976, \n\n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7101 et\xc2\xa0seq., and the Digital Millennium Copyright Act, \n\n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71201 et\xc2\xa0seq. Static Control counterclaimed, alleging, among other things, violations of \xc2\xa743(a) of the Lanham Act, \n\n60Stat.\n441, codified at \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71125(a). Section 1125(a) provides:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(1) Any person who, on or in connection with any goods or services, or any container for goods, uses in commerce any word, term, name, symbol, or device, or any combination thereof, or any false designation of origin, false or misleading description of fact, or false or misleading representation of fact, which\xe2\x80\x94\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(A)\xc2\xa0is likely to cause confusion, or to cause mistake, or to deceive as to the affiliation, connection, or association of such person with another person, or as to the origin, sponsorship, or approval of his or her goods, services, or commercial activities by another person, or\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(B)\xc2\xa0in commercial advertising or promotion, misrepresents the nature, characteristics, qualities, or geo- graphic origin of his or her or another person\xe2\x80\x99s goods, services, or commercial activities,\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cshall be liable in a civil action by any person who believes that he or she is or is likely to be damaged by such act.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tSection 1125(a) thus creates two distinct bases of liability: false association, \xc2\xa71125(a)(1)(A), and false advertising, \xc2\xa71125(a)(1)(B). See Waits v. Frito-Lay, Inc., 978 F.\xc2\xa02d 1093, 1108 (CA9 1992). Static Control alleged only false advertising.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs relevant to its Lanham Act claim, Static Control alleged two types of false or misleading conduct by Lexmark. First, it alleged that through its Prebate program Lexmark \xe2\x80\x9cpurposefully misleads end-users\xe2\x80\x9d to believe that they are legally bound by the Prebate terms and are thus required to return the Prebate-labeled cartridge to Lexmark after a single use. App. 31, \xc2\xb639. Second, it alleged that upon introducing the Prebate program, Lexmark \xe2\x80\x9csent letters to most of the companies in the toner cartridge remanufacturing business\xe2\x80\x9d falsely advising those companies that it was illegal to sell refurbished Prebate cartridges and, in particular, that it was illegal to use Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s products to refurbish those cartridges. Id., at 29, \xc2\xb635. Static Control asserted that by those statements, Lexmark had materially misrepresented \xe2\x80\x9cthe nature, characteristics, and qualities\xe2\x80\x9d of both its own products and Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s products. Id., at 43\xe2\x80\x9344, \xc2\xb685. It further maintained that Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentations had \xe2\x80\x9cproximately caused and [we]re likely to cause injury to [Static Control] by diverting sales from [Static Control] to Lexmark,\xe2\x80\x9d and had \xe2\x80\x9csubstantially injured [its] business reputation\xe2\x80\x9d by \xe2\x80\x9cleading consumers and others in the trade to believe that [Static Control] is engaged in illegal conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 44, \xc2\xb688. Static Control sought treble damages, attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and costs, and injunctive relief.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court granted Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s motion to dismiss Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s Lanham Act claim. It held that Static Control lacked \xe2\x80\x9cprudential standing\xe2\x80\x9d to bring that claim, App. to Pet. for Cert. 83, relying on a multifactor balancing test it attributed to Associated Gen. Contractors of Cal., Inc. v. Carpenters, \n\n459 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519 (1983)\n. The court emphasized that there were \xe2\x80\x9cmore direct plaintiffs in the form of remanufacturers of Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s cartridges\xe2\x80\x9d; that Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s injury was \xe2\x80\x9cremot[e]\xe2\x80\x9d because it was a mere \xe2\x80\x9cbyproduct of the supposed manipulation of consumers\xe2\x80\x99 relationships with remanufacturers\xe2\x80\x9d; and that Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9calleged intent [was] to dry up spent cartridge supplies at the remanufacturing level, rather than at [Static Control]\xe2\x80\x99s supply level, making remanufacturers Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s alleged intended target.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 83.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Sixth Circuit reversed the dismissal of Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s Lanham Act claim. 697 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 423. Taking the lay of the land, it identified three competing approaches to determining whether a plaintiff has standing to sue under the Lanham Act. It observed that the Third, Fifth, Eighth, and Eleventh Circuits all refer to \xe2\x80\x9cantitrust standing or the [Associated General Contractors] factors in deciding Lanham Act standing,\xe2\x80\x9d as the District Court had done. Id., at 410 (citing Conte Bros. Automotive, Inc. v. Quaker State-Slick 50, Inc., 165 F.\xc2\xa03d 221, 233\xe2\x80\x93234 (CA3 1998); Procter & Gamble Co. v. Amway Corp., 242 F.\xc2\xa03d 539, 562\xe2\x80\x93563 (CA5 2001); Gilbert/Robinson, Inc. v. Carrie Beverage-Missouri, Inc., 989 F.\xc2\xa02d 985, 990\xe2\x80\x93991 (CA8 1993); Phoenix of Broward, Inc. v. McDonald\xe2\x80\x99s Corp., 489 F.\xc2\xa03d 1156, 1162\xe2\x80\x931164 (CA11 2007)). By contrast, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Seventh, Ninth, and Tenth [Circuits] use a categorical test, permitting Lanham Act suits only by an actual competitor.\xe2\x80\x9d 697 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 410 (citing L. S. Heath & Son, Inc. v. AT&T Information Systems, Inc., 9 F.\xc2\xa03d 561, 575 (CA7 1993); Waits, supra, at 1108\xe2\x80\x931109; Stanfield v. Osborne Industries, Inc., 52 F.\xc2\xa03d 867, 873 (CA10 1995)). And the Second Circuit applies a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonable interest\xe2\x80\x99 approach,\xe2\x80\x9d under which a Lanham Act plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9chas standing if the claimant can demonstrate \xe2\x80\x98(1)\xc2\xa0a reasonable interest to be protected against the alleged false advertising and (2)\xc2\xa0a reasonable basis for believing that the interest is likely to be damaged by the alleged false advertising.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 697 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 410 (quoting Famous Horse, Inc. v. 5th Avenue Photo Inc., 624 F.\xc2\xa03d 106, 113 (CA2 2010)). The Sixth Circuit applied the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable-interest test and concluded that Static Control had standing because it \xe2\x80\x9calleged a cognizable interest in its business reputation and sales to remanufacturers and sufficiently alleged that th[o]se interests were harmed by Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s statements to the remanufacturers that Static Control was engaging in illegal conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d 697 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 411.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari to decide \xe2\x80\x9cthe appropriate ana- lytical framework for determining a party\xe2\x80\x99s standing to maintain an action for false advertising under the Lanham Act.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i; 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ____ (2013).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII. \xe2\x80\x9cPrudential Standing\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe parties\xe2\x80\x99 briefs treat the question on which we granted certiorari as one of \xe2\x80\x9cprudential standing.\xe2\x80\x9d Because we think that label misleading, we begin by clarifying the nature of the question at issue in this case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFrom Article III\xe2\x80\x99s limitation of the judicial power to resolving \xe2\x80\x9cCases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cControversies,\xe2\x80\x9d and the separation-of-powers principles underlying that limitation, we have deduced a set of requirements that together make up the \xe2\x80\x9cirreducible constitutional minimum of standing.\xe2\x80\x9d Lujan v. Defenders of Wildlife, \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555, \n560 (1992)\n. The plaintiff must have suffered or be imminently threatened with a concrete and particularized \xe2\x80\x9cinjury in fact\xe2\x80\x9d that is fairly traceable to the challenged action of the defendant and likely to be redressed by a favorable judicial decision. Ibid. Lexmark does not deny that Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s alle- gations of lost sales and damage to its business reputa- tion give it standing under Article III to press its false-advertising claim, and we are satisfied that they do.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s claim thus presents a case or controversy that is properly within federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 Article III jurisdiction, Lexmark urges that we should decline to adjudicate Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s claim on grounds that are \xe2\x80\x9cprudential,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than constitutional. That request is in some tension with our recent reaffirmation of the principle that \xe2\x80\x9ca federal court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98obligation\xe2\x80\x99 to hear and decide\xe2\x80\x9d cases within its jurisdiction \xe2\x80\x9cis \xe2\x80\x98virtually unflagging.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Sprint Communications, Inc. v. Jacobs, 571 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 6) (quoting Colorado River Water Con-servation Dist. v. United States, \n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800, \n817 (1976)\n). In recent decades, however, we have adverted to a \xe2\x80\x9cprudential\xe2\x80\x9d branch of standing, a doctrine not derived from Article III and \xe2\x80\x9cnot exhaustively defined\xe2\x80\x9d but encompassing (we have said) at least three broad principles: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the general prohibition on a litigant\xe2\x80\x99s raising another person\xe2\x80\x99s legal rights, the rule barring adjudication of generalized grievances more appropriately addressed in the representative branches, and the requirement that a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s complaint fall within the zone of interests protected by the law invoked.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Elk Grove Unified School Dist. v. Newdow, \n\n542 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n12 (2004)\n (quoting Allen v. Wright, \n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 737, \n751 (1984)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLexmark bases its \xe2\x80\x9cprudential standing\xe2\x80\x9d arguments chiefly on Associated General Contractors, but we did not describe our analysis in that case in those terms. Rather, we sought to \xe2\x80\x9cascertain,\xe2\x80\x9d as a matter of statutory interpretation, the \xe2\x80\x9cscope of the private remedy created by\xe2\x80\x9d Congress in \xc2\xa74 of the Clayton Act, and the \xe2\x80\x9cclass of persons who [could] maintain a private damages action under\xe2\x80\x9d that legislatively conferred cause of action. 459 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 529, 532. We held that the statute limited the class to plaintiffs whose injuries were proximately caused by a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s antitrust violations. Id., at 532\xe2\x80\x93533. Later decisions confirm that Associated General Contractors rested on statutory, not \xe2\x80\x9cprudential,\xe2\x80\x9d considerations. See, e.g., Holmes v. Securities Investor Protection Corporation, \n\n503 U.\xc2\xa0S. 258\n\xe2\x80\x93268 (1992) (relying on Associated General Contractors in finding a proximate-cause requirement in the cause of action created by the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (RICO), \n\n18 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71964(c)); Anza v. Ideal Steel Supply Corp., \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 451, \n456 (2006)\n (affirming that Holmes \xe2\x80\x9crelied on a careful interpretation of \xc2\xa71964(c)\xe2\x80\x9d). Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s arguments thus do not deserve the \xe2\x80\x9cprudential\xe2\x80\x9d label.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStatic Control, on the other hand, argues that we should measure its \xe2\x80\x9cprudential standing\xe2\x80\x9d by using the zone-of-interests test. Although we admittedly have placed that test under the \xe2\x80\x9cprudential\xe2\x80\x9d rubric in the past, see, e.g., Elk Grove, supra, at 12, it does not belong there any more than Associated General Contractors does. Whether a plain- tiff comes within \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98zone of interests\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is an issue that requires us to determine, using traditional tools of statutory interpretation, whether a legislatively conferred cause of action encompasses a particular plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim. See Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, \n\n523 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83\n, and n.\xc2\xa02 (1998); Clarke v. Securities Industry Assn., \n\n479 U.\xc2\xa0S. 388\n\xe2\x80\x93395 (1987); Holmes, supra, at 288 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment). As Judge Silberman of the D.\xc2\xa0C. Circuit recently observed, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98prudential standing\xe2\x80\x99 is a misnomer\xe2\x80\x9d as applied to the zone-of-interests analysis, which asks whether \xe2\x80\x9cthis particular class of persons ha[s] a right to sue under this substantive statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Association of Battery Recyclers, Inc. v. EPA, 716 F.\xc2\xa03d 667, 675\xe2\x80\x93676 (2013) (concurring opinion).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, the question this case presents is whether Static Control falls within the class of plaintiffs whom Congress has authorized to sue under \xc2\xa71125(a). In other words, we ask whether Static Control has a cause of action under the statute.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t That question requires us to determine the meaning of the congressionally enacted provision creating a cause of action. In doing so, we apply traditional principles of statutory interpretation. We do not ask whether in our judgment Congress should have authorized Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s suit, but whether Congress in fact did so. Just as a court cannot apply its independent policy judgment to recognize a cause of action that Congress has denied, see Alexander v. Sandoval, \n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 275\n\xe2\x80\x93287 (2001), it cannot limit a cause of action that Congress has created merely because \xe2\x80\x9cprudence\xe2\x80\x9d dictates.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII. Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s Right To Sue Under \xc2\xa71125(a)\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThus, this case presents a straightforward question of statutory interpretation: Does the cause of action in \xc2\xa71125(a) extend to plaintiffs like Static Control? The statute authorizes suit by \xe2\x80\x9cany person who believes that he or she is likely to be damaged\xe2\x80\x9d by a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s false advertising. \xc2\xa71125(a)(1). Read literally, that broad language might suggest that an action is available to anyone who can satisfy the minimum requirements of Article III. No party makes that argument, however, and the \xe2\x80\x9cunlikelihood that Congress meant to allow all factually injured plaintiffs to recover persuades us that [\xc2\xa71125(a)] should not get such an expansive reading.\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 266 (footnote omitted). We reach that conclusion in light of two relevant background principles already mentioned: zone of interests and proximate causality.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA. Zone of Interests\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, we presume that a statutory cause of action extends only to plaintiffs whose interests \xe2\x80\x9cfall within the zone of interests protected by the law invoked.\xe2\x80\x9d Allen, 468 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 751. The modern \xe2\x80\x9czone of interests\xe2\x80\x9d formulation originated in Association of Data Processing Service Organizations, Inc. v. Camp, \n\n397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 150 (1970)\n, as a limitation on the cause of action for judicial review conferred by the Administrative Procedure Act (APA). We have since made clear, however, that it applies to all statutorily created causes of action; that it is a \xe2\x80\x9crequirement of general application\xe2\x80\x9d; and that Congress is presumed to \xe2\x80\x9clegislat[e] against the background of\xe2\x80\x9d the zone-of-interests limitation, \xe2\x80\x9cwhich applies unless it is expressly negated.\xe2\x80\x9d Bennett v. Spear, \n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 154, \n163 (1997)\n; see also Holmes, supra, at 287\xe2\x80\x93288 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment). It is \xe2\x80\x9cperhaps more accurat[e],\xe2\x80\x9d though not very different as a practical matter, to say that the limitation always applies and is never negated, but that our analysis of certain statutes will show that they protect a more-than-usually \xe2\x80\x9cexpan[sive]\xe2\x80\x9d range of interests. Bennett, supra, at 164. The zone-of-interests test is therefore an appropriate tool for determining who may invoke the cause of action in \xc2\xa71125(a).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have said, in the APA context, that the test is not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98especially demanding,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Match-E-Be-Nash-She-Wish Band of Pottawatomi Indians v. Patchak, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 15). In that context we have often \xe2\x80\x9cconspicuously included the word \xe2\x80\x98arguably\xe2\x80\x99 in the test to indicate that the benefit of any doubt goes to the plaintiff,\xe2\x80\x9d and have said that the test \xe2\x80\x9cforecloses suit only when a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98interests are so marginally related to or inconsistent with the purposes implicit in the statute that it cannot reasonably be assumed that\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Congress authorized that plaintiff to sue. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316). That lenient approach is an appropriate means of preserving the flexibility of the APA\xe2\x80\x99s omnibus judicial-review provision, which permits suit for violations of numerous statutes of varying character that do not themselves include causes of action for judicial review. \xe2\x80\x9cWe have made clear, however, that the breadth of the zone of interests varies according to the provisions of law at issue, so that what comes within the zone of interests of a statute for purposes of obtaining judicial review of administrative action under the \xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cgenerous review provisions\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 of the APA may not do so for other purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Bennett, supra, at 163 (quot- ing Clarke, 479 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 400, n.\xc2\xa016, in turn quoting Data Processing, supra, at 156).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIdentifying the interests protected by the Lanham Act, however, requires no guesswork, since the Act includes an \xe2\x80\x9cunusual, and extraordinarily helpful,\xe2\x80\x9d detailed statement of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s purposes. H.\xc2\xa0B. Halicki Productions v. United Artists Communications, Inc., 812 F.\xc2\xa02d 1213, 1214 (CA9 1987). Section 45 of the Act, codified at \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71127, provides:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cThe intent of this chapter is to regulate commerce within the control of Congress by making actionable the deceptive and misleading use of marks in such commerce; to protect registered marks used in such commerce from interference by State, or territorial legislation; to protect persons engaged in such commerce against unfair competition; to prevent fraud and deception in such commerce by the use of reproductions, copies, counterfeits, or colorable imitations of registered marks; and to provide rights and remedies stipulated by treaties and conventions respect- ing trademarks, trade names, and unfair competition entered into between the United States and foreign nations.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tMost of the enumerated purposes are relevant to false-association cases; a typical false-advertising case will implicate only the Act\xe2\x80\x99s goal of \xe2\x80\x9cprotect[ing] persons engaged in [commerce within the control of Congress] against unfair competition.\xe2\x80\x9d Although \xe2\x80\x9cunfair competition\xe2\x80\x9d was a \xe2\x80\x9cplastic\xe2\x80\x9d concept at common law, Ely-Norris Safe Co. v. Mosler Safe Co., 7 F.\xc2\xa02d 603, 604 (CA2 1925) (L. Hand, J.), it was understood to be concerned with injuries to business reputation and present and future sales. See Rogers, Book Review, 39 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J. 297, 299 (1929); see generally 3 Restatement of Torts, ch. 35, Introductory Note, pp. 536\xe2\x80\x93537 (1938).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe thus hold that to come within the zone of interests in a suit for false advertising under \xc2\xa71125(a), a plaintiff must allege an injury to a commercial interest in reputation or sales. A consumer who is hoodwinked into purchasing a disappointing product may well have an injury-in-fact cognizable under Article III, but he cannot invoke the protection of the Lanham Act\xe2\x80\x94a conclusion reached by every Circuit to consider the question. See Colligan v. Activities Club of N.\xc2\xa0Y., Ltd., 442 F.\xc2\xa02d 686, 691\xe2\x80\x93692 (CA2 1971); Serbin v. Ziebart Int\xe2\x80\x99l Corp., 11 F.\xc2\xa03d 1163, 1177 (CA3 1993); Made in the USA Foundation v. Phillips Foods, Inc., 365 F.\xc2\xa03d 278, 281 (CA4 2004); Procter & Gamble Co., 242 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 563\xe2\x80\x93564; Barrus v. Sylvania, 55 F.\xc2\xa03d 468, 470 (CA9 1995); Phoenix of Broward, 489 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1170. Even a business misled by a supplier into purchasing an inferior product is, like consumers generally, not under the Act\xe2\x80\x99s aegis.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB. Proximate Cause\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, we generally presume that a statutory cause of action is limited to plaintiffs whose injuries are proxi- mately caused by violations of the statute. For centuries, it has been \xe2\x80\x9ca well established principle of [the common] law, that in all cases of loss, we are to attribute it to the proximate cause, and not to any remote cause.\xe2\x80\x9d Waters v. Merchants\xe2\x80\x99 Louisville Ins. Co., 11 Pet. 213, 223 (1837); see Holmes, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 287 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment). That venerable principle reflects the reality that \xe2\x80\x9cthe judicial remedy cannot encompass every conceivable harm that can be traced to alleged wrongdoing.\xe2\x80\x9d Associ- ated Gen. Contractors, 459 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 536. Congress, we assume, is familiar with the common-law rule and does not mean to displace it sub silentio. We have thus construed federal causes of action in a variety of contexts to incorporate a requirement of proximate causation. See, e.g., Dura Pharmaceuticals, Inc. v. Broudo, \n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 336, \n346 (2005)\n (securities fraud); Holmes, supra, at 268\xe2\x80\x93270 (RICO); Associated Gen. Contractors, supra, at 529\xe2\x80\x93535 (Clayton Act). No party disputes that it is proper to read \xc2\xa71125(a) as containing such a requirement, its broad language notwithstanding.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe proximate-cause inquiry is not easy to define, and over the years it has taken various forms; but courts have a great deal of experience applying it, and there is a wealth of precedent for them to draw upon in doing so. See Exxon Co., U. S. A. v. Sofec, Inc., \n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 830\n\xe2\x80\x93839 (1996); Pacific Operators Offshore, LLP v. Valladolid, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2012) (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (slip op., at 3). Proximate-cause analysis is controlled by the nature of the statutory cause of action. The question it presents is whether the harm alleged has a sufficiently close connection to the conduct the statute prohibits.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPut differently, the proximate-cause requirement generally bars suits for alleged harm that is \xe2\x80\x9ctoo remote\xe2\x80\x9d from the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s unlawful conduct. That is ordinarily the case if the harm is purely derivative of \xe2\x80\x9cmisfortunes visited upon a third person by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s acts.\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes, supra, at 268\xe2\x80\x93269; see, e.g., Hemi Group, LLC v. City of New York, \n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9311 (2010). In a sense, of course, all commercial injuries from false advertising are derivative of those suffered by consumers who are deceived by the advertising; but since the Lanham Act authorizes suit only for commercial injuries, the intervening step of consumer deception is not fatal to the showing of proximate causation required by the statute. See Harold H. Huggins Realty, Inc. v. FNC, Inc., 634 F.\xc2\xa03d 787, 800\xe2\x80\x93801 (CA5 2011). That is consistent with our recognition that under common-law principles, a plaintiff can be directly injured by a misrepresentation even where \xe2\x80\x9ca third party, and not the plaintiff, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. relied on\xe2\x80\x9d it. Bridge v. Phoenix Bond & Indemnity Co., \n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 639, \n656 (2008)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe thus hold that a plaintiff suing under \xc2\xa71125(a) ordinarily must show economic or reputational injury flowing directly from the deception wrought by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s advertising; and that that occurs when deception of consumers causes them to withhold trade from the plaintiff. That showing is generally not made when the deception produces injuries to a fellow commercial actor that in turn affect the plaintiff. For example, while a competitor who is forced out of business by a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s false advertising generally will be able to sue for its losses, the same is not true of the competitor\xe2\x80\x99s landlord, its electric company, and other commercial parties who suffer merely as a result of the competitor\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinability to meet [its] financial obligations.\xe2\x80\x9d Anza, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 458.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC. Proposed Tests\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt oral argument, Lexmark agreed that the zone of in- terests and proximate causation supply the relevant background limitations on suit under \xc2\xa71125(a). See Tr. of Oral Arg. 4\xe2\x80\x935, 11\xe2\x80\x9312, 17\xe2\x80\x9318. But it urges us to adopt, as the optimal formulation of those principles, a multifactor balancing test derived from Associated General Contrac-tors. In the alternative, it asks that we adopt a categorical test permitting only direct competitors to sue for false advertising. And although neither party urges adoption of the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable interest\xe2\x80\x9d test applied below, several amici do so. While none of those tests is wholly without merit, we decline to adopt any of them. We hold instead that a direct application of the zone-of-interests test and the proximate-cause requirement supplies the relevant limits on who may sue.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe balancing test Lexmark advocates was first articulated by the Third Circuit in Conte Bros. and later adopted by several other Circuits. Conte Bros. identified five relevant considerations:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(1)\xc2\xa0The nature of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s alleged injury: Is the injury of a type that Congress sought to redress in providing a private remedy for violations of the [Lanham Act]?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(2)\xc2\xa0The directness or indirectness of the asserted injury.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(3)\xc2\xa0The proximity or remoteness of the party to the alleged injurious conduct.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(4)\xc2\xa0The speculativeness of the damages claim.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c(5)\xc2\xa0The risk of duplicative damages or complexity in apportioning damages.\xe2\x80\x9d 165 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 233 (citations and internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThis approach reflects a commendable effort to give content to an otherwise nebulous inquiry, but we think it slightly off the mark. The first factor can be read as requiring that the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury be within the relevant zone of interests and the second and third as requiring (somewhat redundantly) proximate causation; but it is not correct to treat those requirements, which must be met in every case, as mere factors to be weighed in a balance. And the fourth and fifth factors are themselves problem- atic. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he difficulty that can arise when a court attempts to ascertain the damages caused by some remote action\xe2\x80\x9d is a \xe2\x80\x9cmotivating principle\xe2\x80\x9d behind the proximate-cause requirement, Anza, supra, at 457\xe2\x80\x93458; but potential diffi- culty in ascertaining and apportioning damages is not, as Conte Bros. might suggest, an independent basis for denying standing where it is adequately alleged that a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct has proximately injured an interest of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s that the statute protects. Even when a plaintiff cannot quantify its losses with sufficient certainty to re- cover damages, it may still be entitled to injunctive re- lief under \xc2\xa71116(a) (assuming it can prove a likelihood of future injury) or disgorgement of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s ill-gotten profits under \xc2\xa71117(a). See TrafficSchool.com, Inc. v. Edriver Inc., 653 F.\xc2\xa03d 820, 831 (CA9 2011); Johnson & Johnson v. Carter-Wallace, Inc., 631 F.\xc2\xa02d 186, 190 (CA2 1980). Finally, experience has shown that the Conte Bros. approach, like other open-ended balancing tests, can yield unpredictable and at times arbitrary results. See, e.g., Tushnet, Running the Gamut from A to B: Federal Trademark and False Advertising Law, 159 U.\xc2\xa0Pa. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1305, 1376\xe2\x80\x931379 (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn contrast to the multifactor balancing approach, the direct-competitor test provides a bright-line rule; but it does so at the expense of distorting the statutory language. To be sure, a plaintiff who does not compete with the defendant will often have a harder time establishing proximate causation. But a rule categorically prohibiting all suits by noncompetitors would read too much into the Act\xe2\x80\x99s reference to \xe2\x80\x9cunfair competition\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa71127. By the time the Lanham Act was adopted, the common-law tort of unfair competition was understood not to be limited to actions between competitors. One leading authority in the field wrote that \xe2\x80\x9cthere need be no competition in unfair competition,\xe2\x80\x9d just as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no soda in soda water, no grapes in grape fruit, no bread in bread fruit, and a clothes horse is not a horse but is good enough to hang things on.\xe2\x80\x9d Rogers, 39 Yale L.\xc2\xa0J., at 299; accord, Vogue Co. v. Thompson-Hudson Co., 300 F. 509, 512 (CA6 1924); 1 H. Nims, The Law of Unfair Competition and Trade-Marks, p. vi (4th ed. 1947); 2 id., at 1194\xe2\x80\x931205. It is thus a mistake to infer that because the Lanham Act treats false advertising as a form of unfair competition, it can protect only the false-advertiser\xe2\x80\x99s direct competitors.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, there is the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable interest\xe2\x80\x9d test applied by the Sixth Circuit in this case. As typically formulated, it requires a commercial plaintiff to \xe2\x80\x9cdemonstrate \xe2\x80\x98(1)\xc2\xa0a reasonable interest to be protected against the alleged false advertising and (2)\xc2\xa0a reasonable basis for believing that the interest is likely to be damaged by the alleged false advertising.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 697 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 410 (quoting Famous Horse, 624 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 113). A purely practical objection to the test is that it lends itself to widely divergent appli- cation. Indeed, its vague language can be understood as requiring only the bare minimum of Article III standing. The popularity of the multifactor balancing test reflects its appeal to courts tired of \xe2\x80\x9cgrappl[ing] with defining\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonable interest\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d test \xe2\x80\x9cwith greater precision.\xe2\x80\x9d Conte Bros., 165 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 231. The theoretical difficulties with the test are even more substantial: The relevant question is not whether the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s interest is \xe2\x80\x9creasonable,\xe2\x80\x9d but whether it is one the Lanham Act protects; and not whether there is a \xe2\x80\x9creasonable basis\xe2\x80\x9d for the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim of harm, but whether the harm alleged is proximately tied to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. In short, we think the principles set forth above will provide clearer and more accurate guidance than the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable interest\xe2\x80\x9d test.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV. Application\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApplying those principles to Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s false-advertising claim, we conclude that Static Control comes within the class of plaintiffs whom Congress authorized to sue under \xc2\xa71125(a).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo begin, Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s alleged injuries\xe2\x80\x94lost sales and damage to its business reputation\xe2\x80\x94are injuries to precisely the sorts of commercial interests the Act protects. Static Control is suing not as a deceived consumer, but as a \xe2\x80\x9cperso[n] engaged in\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ccommerce within the control of Congress\xe2\x80\x9d whose position in the marketplace has been damaged by Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s false advertising. \xc2\xa71127. There is no doubt that it is within the zone of interests protected by the statute.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tStatic Control also sufficiently alleged that its injuries were proximately caused by Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentations. This case, it is true, does not present the \xe2\x80\x9cclassic Lanham Act false-advertising claim\xe2\x80\x9d in which \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98one competito[r] directly injur[es] another by making false statements about his own goods [or the competitor\xe2\x80\x99s goods] and thus inducing customers to switch.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Harold H. Huggins Realty, 634 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 799, n. 24. But although diversion of sales to a direct competitor may be the paradigmatic direct injury from false advertising, it is not the only type of injury cognizable under \xc2\xa71125(a). For at least two reasons, Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s allegations satisfy the requirement of proximate causation.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, Static Control alleged that Lexmark disparaged its business and products by asserting that Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s business was illegal. See 697 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 411, n.\xc2\xa010 (noting allegation that Lexmark \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly target[ed] Static Control\xe2\x80\x9d when it \xe2\x80\x9cfalsely advertised that Static Control infringed Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s patents\xe2\x80\x9d). When a defendant harms a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s reputation by casting aspersions on its business, the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s injury flows directly from the audience\xe2\x80\x99s belief in the disparaging statements. Courts have therefore afforded relief under \xc2\xa71125(a) not only where a defendant denigrates a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s product by name, see, e.g., McNeilab, Inc. v. American Home Prods. Corp., 848 F.\xc2\xa02d 34, 38 (CA2 1988), but also where the defendant damages the product\xe2\x80\x99s reputation by, for example, equat-ing it with an inferior product, see, e.g., Camel Hair and Cashmere Inst. of Am., Inc. v. Associated Dry Goods Corp., 799 F.\xc2\xa02d 6, 7\xe2\x80\x938, 11\xe2\x80\x9312 (CA1 1986); PPX Enterprises, Inc. v. Audiofidelity, Inc., 746 F.\xc2\xa02d 120, 122, 125 (CA2 1984). Traditional proximate-causation principles support those results: As we have observed, a defendant who \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98seeks to promote his own interests by telling a known falsehood to or about the plaintiff or his product\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d may be said to have proximately caused the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s harm. Bridge, 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 657 (quoting Restatement (Second) of Torts \xc2\xa7870, Comment h (1977); emphasis added in Bridge).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court emphasized that Lexmark and Static Control are not direct competitors. But when a party claims reputational injury from disparagement, competition is not required for proximate cause; and that is true even if the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s aim was to harm its immediate competitors, and the plaintiff merely suffered collateral damage. Consider two rival carmakers who purchase airbags for their cars from different third-party manufacturers. If the first carmaker, hoping to divert sales from the second, falsely proclaims that the airbags used by the second carmaker are defective, both the second carmaker and its airbag supplier may suffer reputational injury, and their sales may decline as a result. In those circumstances, there is no reason to regard either party\xe2\x80\x99s injury as de- rivative of the other\xe2\x80\x99s; each is directly and independently harmed by the attack on its merchandise.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn addition, Static Control adequately alleged proximate causation by alleging that it designed, manufactured, and sold microchips that both (1)\xc2\xa0were necessary for, and (2)\xc2\xa0had no other use than, refurbishing Lexmark toner cartridges. See App. 13, \xc2\xb631; id., at 37, \xc2\xb654.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t It follows from that allegation that any false advertising that reduced the remanufacturers\xe2\x80\x99 business necessarily injured Static Control as well. Taking Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s assertions at face value, there is likely to be something very close to a 1:1 relationship between the number of refurbished Prebate cartridges sold (or not sold) by the remanufacturers and the number of Prebate microchips sold (or not sold) by Static Control. \xe2\x80\x9cWhere the injury alleged is so integral an aspect of the [violation] alleged, there can be no question\xe2\x80\x9d that proximate cause is satisfied. Blue Shield of Va. v. McCready, \n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 465, \n479 (1982)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo be sure, on this view, the causal chain linking Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s injuries to consumer confusion is not direct, but includes the intervening link of injury to the remanufacturers. Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s allegations therefore might not support standing under a strict application of the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cgeneral tendency\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d not to stretch proximate causation \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cbeyond the first step.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Holmes, 503 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 271. But the reason for that general tendency is that there ordinarily is a \xe2\x80\x9cdiscontinuity\xe2\x80\x9d between the injury to the direct victim and the injury to the indirect victim, so that the latter is not surely attributable to the former (and thus also to the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct), but might instead have resulted from \xe2\x80\x9cany number of [other] reasons.\xe2\x80\x9d Anza, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 458\xe2\x80\x93459. That is not the case here. Static Control\xe2\x80\x99s allegations suggest that if the remanufacturers sold 10,000 fewer refurbished cartridges because of Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s false advertising, then it would follow more or less automatically that Static Control sold 10,000 fewer microchips for the same reason, without the need for any \xe2\x80\x9cspeculative .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cintricate, uncertain inquiries.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 459\xe2\x80\x93460. In these relatively unique circumstances, the remanufacturers are not \xe2\x80\x9cmore immediate victim[s]\xe2\x80\x9d than Static Control. Bridge, supra, at 658.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough we conclude that Static Control has alleged an adequate basis to proceed under \xc2\xa71125(a), it cannot obtain relief without evidence of injury proximately caused by Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s alleged misrepresentations. We hold only that Static Control is entitled to a chance to prove its case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo invoke the Lanham Act\xe2\x80\x99s cause of action for false advertising, a plaintiff must plead (and ultimately prove) an injury to a commercial interest in sales or business reputation proximately caused by the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s mis- representations. Static Control has adequately pleaded both elements. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.\n\t\t\t
