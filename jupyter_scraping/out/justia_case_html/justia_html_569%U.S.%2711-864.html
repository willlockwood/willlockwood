\n\t\t\t\tCOMCAST CORP. et\xc2\xa0al. v. BEHREND et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x93864.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 5, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 27, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners, Comcast Corporation and its subsidiaries, allegedly \xe2\x80\x9ccluster\xe2\x80\x9d their cable television operations within a particular region by swapping their systems outside the region for competitor systems inside the region. Respondents, named plaintiffs in this class-action antitrust suit, claim that they and other Comcast subscribers in the Philadelphia \xe2\x80\x9ccluster\xe2\x80\x9d are harmed because Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s strategy lessens competition and leads to supra-competitive prices. They sought class certification under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(3), which requires that \xe2\x80\x9cquestions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members.\xe2\x80\x9d The District Court required them to show (1) that the \xe2\x80\x9cantitrust impact\xe2\x80\x9d of the violation could be proved at trial through evidence common to the class and (2) that the damages were measurable on a classwide basis through a \xe2\x80\x9ccommon methodology.\xe2\x80\x9d The court accepted only one of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 four proposed theories of antitrust impact: that Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s actions lessened competition from \xe2\x80\x9coverbuilders,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., companies that build competing networks in areas where an incumbent cable company already operates. It then certified the class, finding that the damages from overbuilder deterrence could be calculated on a classwide basis, even though respondents\xe2\x80\x99 expert acknowledged that his regression model did not isolate damages resulting from any one of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 theories. In affirming, the Third Circuit refused to consider petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that the model failed to attribute damages to overbuilder deterrence because doing so would require reaching the merits of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claims at the class certification stage.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 class action was improperly certified under Rule 23(b)(3). Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0A party seeking to maintain a class action must be prepared to show that Rule 23(a)\xe2\x80\x99s numerosity, commonality, typicality, and adequacy-of-representation requirements have been met, Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, and must satisfy through evidentiary proof at least one of Rule 23(b)\xe2\x80\x99s provisions. The same analytical principles govern certification under both Rule 23(a) and Rule 23(b). Courts may have to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98probe behind the pleadings before coming to rest on the certification question,\xe2\x80\x99 and [a] certification is proper only if \xe2\x80\x98the trial court is satisfied, after a rigorous analysis, that [Rule 23\xe2\x80\x99s] prerequisites .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. have been satisfied.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The analysis will frequently \xe2\x80\x9coverlap with the merits of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s underlying claim\xe2\x80\x9d because a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98class determination generally involves considerations that are enmeshed in the factual and legal issues comprising the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s cause of action.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The Third Circuit ran afoul of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents when it refused to entertain arguments against respondents\xe2\x80\x99 damages model that bore on the propriety of class certification simply because they would also be pertinent to the merits determination. If they prevail, respondents would be entitled only to damages resulting from reduced overbuilder competition. A model that does not attempt to measure only those damages attributable to that theory cannot establish that damages are susceptible of measurement across the entire class for Rule 23(b)(3) purposes. The lower courts\xe2\x80\x99 contrary reasoning flatly contradicts this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases, which require a determination that Rule 23 is satisfied, even when that requires inquiry into the merits of the claim. Wal-Mart, supra, at ___, and n.\xc2\xa06. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x938.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Under the proper standard for evaluating certification, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 model falls far short of establishing that damages can be measured classwide. The figure respondents\xe2\x80\x99 expert used was calculated assuming the validity of all four theories of antitrust impact initially advanced by respondents. Because the model cannot bridge the differences between supra-competitive prices in general and supra-competitive prices attributable to overbuilder deterrence, Rule 23(b)(3) cannot authorize treating subscribers in the Philadelphia cluster as members of a single class. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the third circuit\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court and the Court of Appeals approved certification of a class of more than 2 million current and former Comcast subscribers who seek damages for al- leged violations of the federal antitrust laws. We consider whether certification was appropriate under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(3).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tComcast Corporation and its subsidiaries, petitioners here, provide cable-television services to residential and commercial customers. From 1998 to 2007, petitioners engaged in a series of transactions that the parties have described as \xe2\x80\x9cclustering,\xe2\x80\x9d a strategy of concentrating op- erations within a particular region. The region at issue here, which the parties have referred to as the Philadel- phia \xe2\x80\x9ccluster\xe2\x80\x9d or the Philadelphia \xe2\x80\x9cDesignated Market Area\xe2\x80\x9d (DMA), includes 16 counties located in Pennsylvania, Delaware, and New Jersey.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Petitioners pursued their clustering strategy by acquiring competitor cable providers in the region and swapping their own systems outside the region for competitor systems located in the region. For instance, in 2001, petitioners obtained Adelphia Com- munications\xe2\x80\x99 cable systems in the Philadelphia DMA, along with its 464,000 subscribers; in exchange, petitioners sold to Adelphia their systems in Palm Beach, Florida, and Los Angeles, California. As a result of nine cluster- ing transactions, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 share of subscribers in the re- gion allegedly increased from 23.9 percent in 1998 to 69.5 percent in 2007. See 264 F. R. D. 150, 156, n.\xc2\xa08, 160 (ED Pa. 2010).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe named plaintiffs, respondents here, are subscribers to Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s cable-television services. They filed a class-action antitrust suit against petitioners, claiming that petitioners entered into unlawful swap agreements, in violation of \xc2\xa71 of the Sherman Act, and monopolized or at- tempted to monopolize services in the cluster, in viola- tion of \xc2\xa72. Ch. 647, \n\n26Stat.\n209, as amended, 15 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71, 2. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 clustering scheme, respondents contended, harmed subscribers in the Philadelphia cluster by eliminating competition and holding prices for cable services above competitive levels.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondents sought to certify a class under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(3). That provision permits certification only if \xe2\x80\x9cthe court finds that the questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members.\xe2\x80\x9d The District Court held, and it is uncontested here, that to meet the predominance requirement respondents had to show (1) that the existence of individual injury resulting from the alleged antitrust violation (referred to as \xe2\x80\x9cantitrust impact\xe2\x80\x9d) was \xe2\x80\x9ccapable of proof at trial through evidence that [was] common to the class rather than individual to its members\xe2\x80\x9d; and (2) that the damages resulting from that injury were measurable \xe2\x80\x9con a class-wide basis\xe2\x80\x9d through use of a \xe2\x80\x9ccommon methodology.\xe2\x80\x9d 264 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D., at 154.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondents proposed four theories of antitrust impact: First, Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s clustering made it profitable for Comcast to withhold local sports programming from its competi- tors, resulting in decreased market penetration by direct broadcast satellite providers. Second, Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s activities reduced the level of competition from \xe2\x80\x9coverbuilders,\xe2\x80\x9d companies that build competing cable networks in areas where an incumbent cable company already operates. Third, Comcast reduced the level of \xe2\x80\x9cbenchmark\xe2\x80\x9d competition on which cable customers rely to compare prices. Fourth, clustering increased Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s bargaining power relative to content providers. Each of these forms of impact, respondents alleged, increased cable subscription rates throughout the Philadelphia DMA.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court accepted the overbuilder theory of antitrust impact as capable of classwide proof and rejected the rest. Id., at 165, 174, 178, 181. Accordingly, in its certification order, the District Court limited respondents\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cproof of antitrust impact\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cthe theory that Comcast engaged in anticompetitive clustering conduct, the effect of which was to deter the entry of overbuilders in the Philadelphia DMA.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 192a\xe2\x80\x93193a.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court further found that the damages resulting from overbuilder-deterrence impact could be calculated on a classwide basis. To establish such dam- ages, respondents had relied solely on the testimony of Dr. James McClave. Dr. McClave designed a regression model comparing actual cable prices in the Philadelphia DMA with hypothetical prices that would have prevailed but for petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 allegedly anticompetitive activities. The model calculated damages of $875,576,662 for the entire class. App. 1388a (sealed). As Dr. McClave acknowledged, however, the model did not isolate damages resulting from any one theory of antitrust impact. Id., at 189a\xe2\x80\x93190a. The District Court nevertheless certified the class.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA divided panel of the Court of Appeals affirmed. On appeal, petitioners contended the class was improperly certified because the model, among other shortcomings, failed to attribute damages resulting from overbuilder deterrence, the only theory of injury remaining in the case. The court refused to consider the argument because, in its view, such an \xe2\x80\x9cattac[k] on the merits of the methodology [had] no place in the class certification inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d 655 F. 3d 182, 207 (CA3 2011). The court emphasized that, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t the class certification stage,\xe2\x80\x9d respondents were not required to \xe2\x80\x9ctie each theory of antitrust impact to an exact calculation of damages.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 206. According to the court, it had \xe2\x80\x9cnot reached the stage of determining on the merits whether the methodology is a just and reasonable inference or speculative.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Rather, the court said, respondents must \xe2\x80\x9cassure us that if they can prove antitrust impact, the resulting damages are capable of measurement and will not require labyrinthine individual calculations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, that burden was met because respondents\xe2\x80\x99 model calculated \xe2\x80\x9csupra-competitive prices regardless of the type of anticompetitive conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 205.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari. 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe class action is \xe2\x80\x9can exception to the usual rule that litigation is conducted by and on behalf of the individual named parties only.\xe2\x80\x9d Califano v. Yamasaki, \n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 682\n\xe2\x80\x93701 (1979). To come within the exception, a party seeking to maintain a class action \xe2\x80\x9cmust affirmatively demonstrate his compliance\xe2\x80\x9d with Rule 23. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Dukes, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 10). The Rule \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not set forth a mere pleading standard.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Rather, a party must not only \xe2\x80\x9cbe prepared to prove that there are in fact sufficiently numerous parties, common questions of law or fact,\xe2\x80\x9d typicality of claims or defenses, and adequacy of representation, as required by Rule 23(a). Ibid. The party must also satisfy through evidentiary proof at least one of the provisions of Rule 23(b). The provision at issue here is Rule 23(b)(3), which requires a court to find that \xe2\x80\x9cthe questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRepeatedly, we have emphasized that it \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98may be necessary for the court to probe behind the pleadings before coming to rest on the certification question,\xe2\x80\x99 and that certification is proper only if \xe2\x80\x98the trial court is satisfied, after a rigorous analysis, that the prerequisites of Rule 23(a) have been satisfied.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting General Telephone Co. of Southwest v. Falcon, \n\n457 U.\xc2\xa0S. 147\n\xe2\x80\x93161 (1982)). Such an analysis will frequently entail \xe2\x80\x9coverlap with the merits of the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s underlying claim.\xe2\x80\x9d 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 10). That is so because the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98class determination generally involves considerations that are enmeshed in the factual and legal issues comprising the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s cause of action.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Falcon, supra, at 160).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe same analytical principles govern Rule 23(b). If anything, Rule 23(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s predominance criterion is even more demanding than Rule 23(a). Amchem Products, Inc. v. Windsor, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 591\n\xe2\x80\x93624 (1997). Rule 23(b)(3), as an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98adventuresome innovation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is designed for situations \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in which \xe2\x80\x9cclass-action treatment is not as clearly called for.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Wal-Mart, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 22) (quoting Amchem, 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 614\xe2\x80\x93615). That explains Congress\xe2\x80\x99s addition of procedural safeguards for (b)(3) class members beyond those provided for (b)(1) or (b)(2) class members (e.g., an opportunity to opt out), and the court\xe2\x80\x99s duty to take a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98close look\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d at whether common questions predominate over individual ones. Id., at 615.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondents\xe2\x80\x99 class action was improperly certified un- der Rule 23(b)(3). By refusing to entertain arguments against respondents\xe2\x80\x99 damages model that bore on the propriety of class certification, simply because those arguments would also be pertinent to the merits determination, the Court of Appeals ran afoul of our precedents requiring precisely that inquiry. And it is clear that, under the proper standard for evaluating certification, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 model falls far short of establishing that damages are capable of measurement on a classwide basis. Without presenting another methodology, respondents cannot show Rule 23(b)(3) predominance: Questions of individual damage calculations will inevitably overwhelm questions common to the class. This case thus turns on the straightforward application of class-certification principles; it provides no occasion for the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s extended discussion, post, at 5\xe2\x80\x9311 (Ginsburg and Breyer, JJ., dissenting), of substantive antitrust law.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe start with an unremarkable premise. If respondents prevail on their claims, they would be entitled only to damages resulting from reduced overbuilder competition, since that is the only theory of antitrust impact accepted for class-action treatment by the District Court. It follows that a model purporting to serve as evidence of damages in this class action must measure only those damages attributable to that theory. If the model does not even attempt to do that, it cannot possibly establish that damages are susceptible of measurement across the entire class for purposes of Rule 23(b)(3). Calculations need not be exact, see Story Parchment Co. v. Paterson Parchment Paper Co., \n\n282 U.\xc2\xa0S. 555, \n563 (1931)\n, but at the class-certification stage (as at trial), any model supporting a \xe2\x80\x9cplaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s damages case must be consistent with its liability case, particularly with respect to the alleged anticompetitive effect of the violation.\xe2\x80\x9d ABA Section of Antitrust Law, Proving Antitrust Damages: Legal and Economic Issues 57, 62 (2d ed. 2010); see, e.g., Image Tech. Servs. v. Eastman Kodak Co., 125 F.\xc2\xa03d 1195, 1224 (CA9 1997). And for purposes of Rule 23, courts must conduct a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98rigorous analysis\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to determine whether that is so. Wal-Mart, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 10). \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court and the Court of Appeals saw no need for respondents to \xe2\x80\x9ctie each theory of antitrust impact\xe2\x80\x9d to a calculation of damages. 655 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 206. That, they said, would involve consideration of the \xe2\x80\x9cmerits\xe2\x80\x9d having \xe2\x80\x9cno place in the class certification inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 206\xe2\x80\x93207. That reasoning flatly contradicts our cases requiring a determination that Rule 23 is satisfied, even when that requires inquiry into the merits of the claim. Wal-Mart, supra, at ___, and n.\xc2\xa06 (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311, and n.\xc2\xa06). The Court of Appeals simply concluded that respondents \xe2\x80\x9cprovided a method to measure and quantify damages on a classwide basis,\xe2\x80\x9d finding it unnecessary to decide \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the methodology [was] a just and reasonable inference or speculative.\xe2\x80\x9d 655 F. 3d, at 206. Under that logic, at the class-certification stage any method of measurement is acceptable so long as it can be applied classwide, no matter how arbitrary the measurements may be. Such a proposition would reduce Rule 23(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x99s predominance requirement to a nullity.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere is no question that the model failed to measure damages resulting from the particular antitrust injury on which petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 liability in this action is premised.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The scheme devised by respondents\xe2\x80\x99 expert, Dr. McClave, sought to establish a \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d baseline\xe2\x80\x94a figure that would show what the competitive prices would have been if there had been no antitrust violations. Damages would then be determined by comparing to that baseline what the actual prices were during the charged period. The \xe2\x80\x9cbut for\xe2\x80\x9d figure was calculated, however, by assuming a market that contained none of the four distortions that respondents attributed to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 actions. In other words, the model assumed the validity of all four theories of antitrust impact initially advanced by respondents: decreased penetration by satellite providers, overbuilder deterrence, lack of benchmark competition, and increased bargaining power. At the evidentiary hearing, Dr. McClave expressly admitted that the model calculated damages resulting from \xe2\x80\x9cthe alleged anticompetitive conduct as a whole\xe2\x80\x9d and did not attribute damages to any one particular theory of anticompetitive impact. App. 189a\xe2\x80\x93190a, 208a. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis methodology might have been sound, and might have produced commonality of damages, if all four of those alleged distortions remained in the case. But as Judge Jordan\xe2\x80\x99s partial dissent pointed out:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[B]ecause the only surviving theory of antitrust impact is that clustering reduced overbuilding, for Dr. McClave\xe2\x80\x99s comparison to be relevant, his benchmark counties must reflect the conditions that would have prevailed in the Philadelphia DMA but for the alleged reduction in overbuilding. In all respects unrelated to reduced overbuilding, the benchmark counties should reflect the actual conditions in the Philadelphia DMA, or else the model will identify \xe2\x80\x98damages\xe2\x80\x99 that are not the result of reduced overbuilding, or, in other words, that are not the certain result of the wrong.\xe2\x80\x9d 655 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 216 (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority\xe2\x80\x99s only response to this was that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t the class certification stage we do not require that Plaintiffs tie each theory of antitrust impact to an exact calculation of damages, but instead that they assure us that if they can prove antitrust impact, the resulting damages are capable of measurement and will not require labyrinthine individual calculations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 206. But such assurance is not provided by a methodology that identifies damages that are not the result of the wrong. For all we know, cable subscribers in Gloucester County may have been overcharged because of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 alleged elimination of satellite competition (a theory of liability that is not ca- pable of classwide proof); while subscribers in Camden County may have paid elevated prices because of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 increased bargaining power vis-\xc3\xa0-vis content providers (another theory that is not capable of classwide proof); while yet other subscribers in Montgomery County may have paid rates produced by the combined effects of multiple forms of alleged antitrust harm; and so on. The permutations involving four theories of liability and 2 million subscribers located in 16 counties are nearly endless.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn light of the model\xe2\x80\x99s inability to bridge the differences between supra-competitive prices in general and supra-competitive prices attributable to the deterrence of overbuilding, Rule 23(b)(3) cannot authorize treating subscribers within the Philadelphia cluster as members of a single class.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Prices whose level above what an expert deems \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitive\xe2\x80\x9d has been caused by factors unrelated to an accepted theory of antitrust harm are not \xe2\x80\x9canticompetitive\xe2\x80\x9d in any sense relevant here. \xe2\x80\x9cThe first step in a damages study is the translation of the legal theory of the harmful event into an analysis of the economic impact of that event.\xe2\x80\x9d Federal Judicial Center, Reference Manual on Scientific Evidence 432 (3d ed. 2011) (emphasis added). The District Court and the Court of Appeals ignored that first step entirely.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Third Cir- cuit is reversed.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIt is so ordered.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tToday the Court reaches out to decide a case hardly fit for our consideration. On both procedural and substantive grounds, we dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case comes to the Court infected by our misguided reformulation of the question presented. For that reason alone, we would dismiss the writ of certiorari as improvidently granted.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tComcast sought review of the following question: \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hether a district court may certify a class action without resolving \xe2\x80\x98merits arguments\xe2\x80\x99 that bear on [Federal Rule of Civil Procedure] 23\xe2\x80\x99s prerequisites for certifica-tion, including whether purportedly common issues predominate over individual ones under Rule 23(b)(3).\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Cert. i. We granted review of a different question: \xe2\x80\x9cWhether a district court may certify a class action without resolving whether the plaintiff class has introduced admissible evidence, including expert testimony, to show that the case is susceptible to awarding damages on a class-wide basis.\xe2\x80\x9d 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012) (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur rephrasing shifted the focus of the dispute from the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s Rule 23(b)(3) analysis to its attention (or lack thereof) to the admissibility of expert testimony. The parties, responsively, devoted much of their briefing to the question whether the standards for admissibility of expert evidence set out in Federal Rule of Evidence 702 and Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc., \n\n509 U.\xc2\xa0S. 579 (1993)\n, apply in class certification proceedings. See Brief for Petitioners 35\xe2\x80\x9349; Brief for Respondents 24\xe2\x80\x9337. Indeed, respondents confirmed at oral argument that they understood our rewritten question to center on admissibility, not Rule 23(b)(3). See, e.g., Tr. of Oral Arg. 25.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs it turns out, our reformulated question was inapt. To preserve a claim of error in the admission of evidence, a party must timely object to or move to strike the evidence. Fed. Rule Evid. 103(a)(1). In the months preceding the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s class certification order, Comcast did not object to the admission of Dr. McClave\xe2\x80\x99s damages model under Rule 702 or Daubert. Nor did Comcast move to strike his testimony and expert report. Consequently, Comcast forfeited any objection to the admission of Dr. McClave\xe2\x80\x99s model at the certification stage. At this late date, Comcast may no longer argue that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 damages evidence was inadmissible.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tComcast\xe2\x80\x99s forfeiture of the question on which we granted review is reason enough to dismiss the writ as improvidently granted. See Rogers v. United States, \n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 252, \n259 (1998)\n (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in result) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e ought not to decide the question if it has not been cleanly presented.\xe2\x80\x9d); The Monrosa v. Carbon Black Export, Inc., \n\n359 U.\xc2\xa0S. 180, \n183 (1959)\n (dismissal appropriate in light of \xe2\x80\x9ccircumstances . . . not fully apprehended at the time certiorari was granted\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). The Court, however, elects to evaluate whether re-spondents \xe2\x80\x9cfailed to show that the case is susceptible to awarding damages on a class-wide basis.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5, n.\xc2\xa04 (internal quotation marks omitted). To justify this second revision of the question presented, the Court observes that Comcast \xe2\x80\x9cargued below, and continue[s] to argue here, that certification was improper because respondents had failed to establish that damages could be measured on a classwide basis.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And so Comcast did, in addition to endeavoring to address the question on which we granted review. By treating the first part of our reformulated question as though it did not exist, the Court is hardly fair to respondents.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAbandoning the question we instructed the parties to brief does \xe2\x80\x9cnot reflect well on the processes of the Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Redrup v. New York, \n\n386 U.\xc2\xa0S. 767, \n772 (1967)\n (Harlan, J., dissenting). Taking their cue from our order, respondents did not train their energies on defending the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding of predominance in their briefing or at oral argument. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s newly revised question, focused on predominance, phrased only after briefing was done, left respondents without an unclouded opportunity to air the issue the Court today decides against them. And by resolving a complex and fact-intensive question without the benefit of full briefing, the Court invites the error into which it has fallen. See infra, at 5\xe2\x80\x9311.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhile the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to review the merits of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s certification order is both unwise and un-fair to respondents, the opinion breaks no new ground on the standard for certifying a class action under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(b)(3). In particular, the decision should not be read to require, as a prerequisite to certification, that damages attributable to a classwide injury be measurable \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98on a class-wide basis.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d See ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (acknowledging Court\xe2\x80\x99s dependence on the absence of contest on the matter in this case); Tr. of Oral Arg. 41.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo gain class-action certification under Rule 23(b)(3), the named plaintiff must demonstrate, and the District Court must find, \xe2\x80\x9cthat the questions of law or fact common to class members predominate over any questions affecting only individual members.\xe2\x80\x9d This predominance requirement is meant to \xe2\x80\x9ctes[t] whether proposed classes are sufficiently cohesive to warrant adjudication by representation,\xe2\x80\x9d Amchem Products, Inc. v. Windsor, \n\n521 U.\xc2\xa0S. 591, \n623 (1997)\n, but it scarcely demands commonality as to all questions. See 7AA C. Wright, A. Miller, & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa71778, p. 121 (3d ed. 2005) (hereinafter Wright, Miller, & Kane). In particular, when adjudication of questions of liability common to the class will achieve economies of time and expense, the predominance standard is generally satisfied even if damages are not provable in the aggregate. See Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s 1966 Notes on Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 23, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 141 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] fraud perpetrated on numerous persons by the use of similar misrepresentations may be an appealing situation for a class action, and it may remain so despite the need, if liability is found, for separate determination of the damages suffered by individuals within the class.\xe2\x80\x9d); 7AA Wright, Miller, & Kane \xc2\xa71781, at 235\xe2\x80\x93237.*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRecognition that individual damages calculations do not preclude class certification under Rule 23(b)(3) is well nigh universal. See 2 W. Rubenstein, Newberg on Class Actions \xc2\xa74:54, p. 205 (5th ed. 2012) (ordinarily, \xe2\x80\x9cindividual damage[s] calculations should not scuttle class certification under Rule 23(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x9d). Legions of appellate decisions across a range of substantive claims are illustrative. See, e.g., Tardiff v. Knox County, 365 F.\xc2\xa03d 1, 6 (CA1 2004) (\n \nFourth Amendment); Chiang v. Veneman, 385 F.\xc2\xa03d 256, 273 (CA3 2004) (Equal Credit Opportunity Act); Bertulli v. Independent Assn. of Continental Pilots, 242 F.\xc2\xa03d 290, 298 (CA5 2001) (Labor-Management Reporting and Disclosure Act and Railway Labor Act); Beattie v. CenturyTel, Inc., 511 F.\xc2\xa03d 554, 564\xe2\x80\x93566 (CA6 2007) (Federal Communications Act); Arreola v. Godinez, 546 F.\xc2\xa03d 788, 801 (CA7 2008) (\n \nEighth Amendment). Antitrust cases, which typically involve common allegations of antitrust violation, antitrust impact, and the fact of damages, are classic examples. See In\xc2\xa0re Visa Check/MasterMoney Antitrust Litigation, 280 F.\xc2\xa03d 124, 139\xe2\x80\x93140 (CA2 2001). See also 2A P. Areeda, H. Hovenkamp, R. Blair, & C. Durrance, Antitrust Law \xc2\xb6331, p. 56 (3d ed. 2007) (hereinafter Areeda & Hovenkamp); 6 A. Conte & H. Newberg, Newberg on Class Actions \xc2\xa718:27, p. 91 (4th ed. 2002). As this Court has rightly observed, \xe2\x80\x9c[p]redominance is a test readily met\xe2\x80\x9d in actions alleging \xe2\x80\x9cviolations of the antitrust laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Amchem, 521 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 625.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe oddity of this case, in which the need to prove damages on a classwide basis through a common methodology was never challenged by respondents, see Brief for Plaintiffs-Appellees in No. 10\xe2\x80\x932865 (CA3), pp. 39\xe2\x80\x9340, is a further reason to dismiss the writ as improvidently granted. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling is good for this day and case only. In the mine run of cases, it remains the \xe2\x80\x9cblack letter rule\xe2\x80\x9d that a class may obtain certification under Rule 23(b)(3) when liability questions common to the class predominate over damages questions unique to class members. 2 Rubenstein, supra, \xc2\xa74:54, at 208.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIncautiously entering the fray at this interlocutory stage, the Court sets forth a profoundly mistaken view of antitrust law. And in doing so, it relies on its own version of the facts, a version inconsistent with factual findings made by the District Court and affirmed by the Court of Appeals.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo understand the antitrust problem, some (simplified) background discussion is necessary. Plaintiffs below, re-spondents here, alleged that Comcast violated \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71 and 2 of the Sherman Act. See 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71, 2. For present purposes, the \xc2\xa72 claim provides the better illustration. A firm is guilty of monopolization under \xc2\xa72 if the plaintiff proves (1)\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cthe possession of monopoly power in the relevant market\xe2\x80\x9d and (2)\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cthe willful acquisition or maintenance of that power[,] as distinguished from growth or development as a consequence of a superior product, business acumen, or historic accident.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Grinnell Corp., \n\n384 U.\xc2\xa0S. 563\n\xe2\x80\x93571 (1966). A private plaintiff seeking damages must also show that (3)\xc2\xa0the monopolization caused \xe2\x80\x9cinjur[y].\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa715. We have said that antitrust injuries must be \xe2\x80\x9cof the type the antitrust laws were intended to prevent and that flo[w] from that which makes defendants\xe2\x80\x99 acts unlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d Atlantic Richfield Co. v. USA Petroleum Co., \n\n495 U.\xc2\xa0S. 328, \n334 (1990)\n (quoting Brunswick Corp. v. Pueblo Bowl-O-Mat, Inc., \n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477, \n489 (1977)\n). See 2A Areeda & Ho-venkamp \xc2\xb6391a, at 320 (To prove antitrust injury, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] private plaintiff must identify the economic rationale for a business practice\xe2\x80\x99s illegality under the antitrust laws and show that its harm flows from whatever it is that makes the practice unlawful.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs plaintiffs below, respondents attempted to meet these requirements by showing that (1) Comcast obtained a 60% or greater share of the Philadelphia market, and that its share provides it with monopoly power; (2) Comcast acquired its share through exclusionary conduct consisting of a series of mergers with competitors and \xe2\x80\x9cswaps\xe2\x80\x9d of customers and locations; and (3)\xc2\xa0Comcast consequently injured respondents by charging them supra-competitive prices.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIf, as respondents contend, Philadelphia is a separate well-defined market, and the alleged exclusionary conduct permitted Comcast to obtain a market share of at least 60%, then proving the \xc2\xa72 violation may not be arduous. As a point of comparison, the government considers a market shared by four firms, each of which has 25% market share, to be \xe2\x80\x9chighly concentrated.\xe2\x80\x9d Dept. of Justice & Federal Trade Commission, Horizontal Merger Guidelines \xc2\xa75.3, p. 19 (2010). A market, such as the one alleged by respondents, where one firm controls 60% is far worse. See id., \xc2\xa75.3, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319, and n.\xc2\xa09 (using a concentration index that determines a market\xe2\x80\x99s concentration level by summing the squares of each firm\xe2\x80\x99s market share, one firm with 100% yielding 10,000, five firms with 20% each yielding 2000, while a market where one firm accounts for 60% yields an index number of at least 3,600). The Guidelines, and any standard antitrust treatise, explain why firms in highly concentrated markets normally have the power to raise prices significantly above competitive levels. See, e.g., 2B Areeda & Hovenkamp \xc2\xb6503, at 115.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSo far there is agreement. But consider the last matter respondents must prove: Can they show that Comcast injured them by charging higher prices? After all, a firm with monopoly power will not necessarily exercise that power by charging higher prices. It could instead act less competitively in other ways, such as by leading the quiet life. See J. Hicks, Annual Survey of Economic Theory: The Theory of Monopoly, 3 Econometrica 1, 8 (1935) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe best of all monopoly profits is a quiet life.\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIt is at this point that Dr. McClave\xe2\x80\x99s model enters the scene. His model first selects a group of comparable outside-Philadelphia \xe2\x80\x9cbenchmark\xe2\x80\x9d counties, where Comcast enjoyed a lower market share (and where satellite broadcasting accounted for more of the local business). Using multiple regression analysis, McClave\xe2\x80\x99s model measures the effect of the anticompetitive conduct by comparing the class counties to the benchmark counties. The model concludes that the prices Philadelphia area consumers would have paid had the Philadelphia counties shared the properties of the benchmark counties (including a diminished Comcast market share), would have been 13.1% lower than those they actually paid. Thus, the model provides evidence that Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s anticompetitive conduct, which led to a 60% market share, caused the class to suffer injuriously higher prices.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe special antitrust-related difficulty present here stems from the manner in which respondents attempted to prove their antitrust injuries. They proffered four \xe2\x80\x9cnon-exclusive mechanisms\xe2\x80\x9d that allegedly \xe2\x80\x9ccause[d] the high prices\xe2\x80\x9d in the Philadelphia area. App. 403a. Those four theories posit that (1) due to Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s acquisitions of competitors, customers found it more difficult to compare prices; (2)\xc2\xa0one set of potential competitors, namely Direct Broadcast Satellite companies, found it more difficult to obtain access to local sports broadcasts and consequently decided not to enter the Philadelphia market; (3) Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s ability to obtain programming material at lower prices permitted it to raise prices; and (4) a number of potential competitors (called \xe2\x80\x9coverbuilders\xe2\x80\x9d), whose presence in the market would have limited Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s power to raise prices, were ready to enter some parts of the market but decided not to do so in light of Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s anticompetitive conduct. 264 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D. 150, 161\xe2\x80\x93162 (ED Pa. 2010).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor reasons not here relevant, the District Court found the first three theories inapplicable and limited the liability-phase proof to the \xe2\x80\x9coverbuilder\xe2\x80\x9d theory. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 192a\xe2\x80\x93193a. It then asked the parties to brief whether doing so had any impact on the viability of McClave\xe2\x80\x99s model as a measure of classwide damages. See 264 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D., at 190. After considering the parties\xe2\x80\x99 arguments, the District Court found that striking the three theories \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not impeach Dr. McClave\xe2\x80\x99s damages model\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny anticompetitive conduct is reflected in the [higher Philadelphia] price [which Dr. McClave\xe2\x80\x99s model determines], not in the [the model\xe2\x80\x99s] selection of the comparison counties, [i.e., the lower-price \xe2\x80\x98benchmark counties\xe2\x80\x99 with which the Philadelphia area prices were compared].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 190\xe2\x80\x93191. The court explained that \xe2\x80\x9cwhether or not we accepted all [four] . . . theories . . . is inapposite to Dr. McClave\xe2\x80\x99s methods of choosing benchmarks.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. On appeal, the Third Circuit held that this finding was not an abuse of discretion. 655 F.\xc2\xa03d 182, 207 (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court, however, concludes that \xe2\x80\x9cthe model failed to measure damages resulting from the particular antitrust injury on which petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 liability in this action is premised.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. To reach this conclusion the Court must consider fact-based matters, namely what this econometric multiple-regression model is about, what it proves, and how it does so. And it must overturn two lower courts\xe2\x80\x99 related factual findings to the contrary.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe are normally \xe2\x80\x9creluctant to disturb findings of fact in which two courts below have concurred.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Doe, \n\n465 U. S. 605, \n614 (1984)\n. See also United States v. Virginia, \n\n518 U.\xc2\xa0S. 515\n, n.\xc2\xa05 (1996) (Scalia, J., dissenting) (noting \xe2\x80\x9cour well-settled rule that we will not \xe2\x80\x98undertake to review concurrent findings of fact by two courts below in the absence of a very obvious and exceptional showing of error\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Graver Tank & Mfg. Co. v. Linde Air Products Co., \n\n336 U.\xc2\xa0S. 271, \n275 (1949)\n)). Here, the District Court found McClave\xe2\x80\x99s econometric model capable of measuring damages on a classwide basis, even after striking three of the injury theories. 264 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D., at 190\xe2\x80\x93191. Contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s characterization, see ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939, n.\xc2\xa05, this was not a legal conclusion about what the model proved; it was a factual finding about how the model worked. Under our typical practice, we should leave that finding alone.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn any event, as far as we can tell, the lower courts were right. On the basis of the record as we understand it, the District Court did not abuse its discretion in finding that McClave\xe2\x80\x99s model could measure damages suffered by the class\xe2\x80\x94even if the damages were limited to those caused by deterred overbuilding. That is because respondents alleged that Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s anticompetitive conduct increased Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s market share (and market power) by deterring potential entrants, in particular, overbuilders, from entering the Philadelphia area market. See App. 43a\xe2\x80\x9366a. By showing that this was so, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 proof tends to show the same in respect to other entrants. The overbuilders\xe2\x80\x99 failure to enter deprives the market of the price discipline that their entry would have provided in other parts via threat of the overbuilders\xe2\x80\x99 expansion or that of others potentially led on by their example. Indeed, in the District Court, Comcast argued that the three other theories, i.e., the three rejected theories, had no impact on prices. See 264 F.\xc2\xa0R.\xc2\xa0D., at 166, 176, 180\xe2\x80\x93181. If Comcast was right, then the damages McClave\xe2\x80\x99s model found must have stemmed exclusively from conduct that deterred new entry, say from \xe2\x80\x9coverbuilders.\xe2\x80\x9d Not surprisingly, the Court offers no support at all for its contrary conclusion, namely, that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98obvious[ly] and exceptional[ly]\xe2\x80\x99 erroneous.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939, n.\xc2\xa05 (quoting Virginia, 518 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 589, n.\xc2\xa05 (Scalia, J., dissenting)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe are particularly concerned about the matter because the Court, in reaching its contrary conclusion, makes broad statements about antitrust law that it could not mean to apply in other cases. The Court begins with what it calls an \xe2\x80\x9cunremarkable premise\xe2\x80\x9d that respondents could be \xe2\x80\x9centitled only to damages resulting from reduced overbuilder competition.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at\xc2\xa07. In most \xc2\xa72 cases, how-ever, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s starting place would seem remarkable, not \xe2\x80\x9cunremarkable.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSuppose in a different case a plaintiff were to prove that Widget, Inc. has obtained, through anticompetitive means, a 90% share of the California widget market. Suppose the plaintiff also proves that the two small remaining firms\xe2\x80\x94one in Ukiah, the other in San Diego\xe2\x80\x94lack the capacity to expand their widget output to the point where that pos-sibility could deter Widget, Inc. from raising its prices. Suppose further that the plaintiff introduces a model that shows California widget prices are now twice those in every other State, which, the model concludes is (after accounting for other possible reasons) the result of lack of competition in the California widget market. Why would a court hearing that case restrict damages solely to customers in the vicinity of Ukiah and San Diego?\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLike the model in this example, Dr. McClave\xe2\x80\x99s model does not purport to show precisely how Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s conduct led to higher prices in the Philadelphia area. It simply shows that Comcast\xe2\x80\x99s conduct brought about higher prices. And it measures the amount of subsequent harm.\n\t\t\t

The need for focused argument is particularly strong here where, as we have said, the underlying considerations are detailed, technical, and fact-based. The Court departs from our ordinary practice, risks inaccurate judicial decisionmaking, and is unfair to respondents and the courts below. For these rea-sons, we would not disturb the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment and, instead, would dismiss the writ as improvidently granted.\n\t\t\t
