Syllabus

\n

heien v. north carolina

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of north carolina

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x93604.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 6, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided December 15, 2014

\n

Following a suspicious vehicle, Sergeant Matt Darisse noticed that only one of the vehicle\xe2\x80\x99s brake lights was working and pulled the driver over. While issuing a warning ticket for the broken brake light, Darisse became suspicious of the actions of the two occupants and their answers to his questions. Petitioner Nicholas Brady Heien, the car\xe2\x80\x99s owner, gave Darisse consent to search the vehicle. Darisse found cocaine, and Heien was arrested and charged with attempted trafficking. The trial court denied Heien\xe2\x80\x99s motion to suppress the seized evidence on\n \nFourth Amendment grounds, concluding that the vehicle\xe2\x80\x99s faulty brake light gave Darisse reasonable suspicion to initiate the stop. The North Carolina Court of Appeals reversed, holding that the relevant code provision, which requires that a car be \xe2\x80\x9cequipped with a stop lamp,\xe2\x80\x9d N.\xc2\xa0C. Gen. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x93129(g), requires only a single lamp\xe2\x80\x94which Heien\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle had\xe2\x80\x94and therefore the justification for the stop was objectively unreasonable. Reversing in turn, the State Supreme Court held that, even assuming no violation of the state law had occurred, Darisse\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken understanding of the law was reasonable, and thus the stop was valid.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Because Darisse\xe2\x80\x99s mistake of law was reasonable, there was reasonable suspicion justifying the stop under the\n \nFourth Amendment. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n \n (a)\xc2\xa0The\n \nFourth Amendment requires government officials to act reasonably, not perfectly, and gives those officials \xe2\x80\x9cfair leeway for enforcing the law,\xe2\x80\x9d Brinegar v. United States,\n\n338 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160\n. Searches and seizures based on mistakes of fact may be reasonable. See, e.g., Illinois v. Rodriguez,\n\n497 U.\xc2\xa0S. 177\n\xe2\x80\x93186. The limiting factor is that \xe2\x80\x9cthe mistakes must be those of reasonable men.\xe2\x80\x9d Brinegar, supra, at 176. Mistakes of law are no less compatible with the concept of reasonable suspicion, which arises from an understanding of both the facts and the relevant law. Whether an officer is reasonably mistaken about the one or the other, the result is the same: the facts are outside the scope of the law. And neither the\n \nFourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s text nor this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents offer any reason why that result should not be acceptable when reached by a reasonable mistake of law.

\n

\n \n More than two centuries ago, this Court held that reasonable mistakes of law, like those of fact, could justify a certificate of probable cause. United States v. Riddle, 5 Cranch 311, 313. That holding was reiterated in numerous 19th-century decisions. Although Riddle was not a\n \nFourth Amendment case, it explained the concept of probable cause, which this Court has said carried the same \xe2\x80\x9cfixed and well known meaning\xe2\x80\x9d in the\n \nFourth Amendment, Brinegar, supra, at 175, and n.\xc2\xa014, and no subsequent decision of this Court has undermined that understanding. The contrary conclusion would be hard to reconcile with the more recent precedent of Michigan v. DeFillippo,\n\n443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 31\n, where the Court, addressing the validity of an arrest made under a criminal law later declared unconstitutional, held that the officers\xe2\x80\x99 reasonable assumption that the law was valid gave them \xe2\x80\x9cabundant probable cause\xe2\x80\x9d to make the arrest, id., at 37. Heien attempts to recast DeFillippo as a case solely about the exclusionary rule, not the\n \nFourth Amendment itself, but DeFillippo\xe2\x80\x99s express holding is that the arrest was constitutionally valid because the officers had probable cause. See id., at 40. Heien misplaces his reliance on cases such as Davis v. United States, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, where any consideration of reasonableness was limited to the separate matter of remedy, not whether there was a\n \nFourth Amendment violation in the first place.

\n

\n \n Heien contends that the rationale that permits reasonable errors of fact does not extend to reasonable errors of law, arguing that officers in the field deserve a margin of error when making factual assessments on the fly. An officer may, however, also be suddenly confronted with a situation requiring application of an unclear statute. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding does not discourage officers from learning the law. Because the\n \nFourth Amendment tolerates only objectively reasonable mistakes, cf. Whren v. United States,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 806\n, an officer can gain no advantage through poor study. Finally, while the maxim \xe2\x80\x9cIgnorance of the law is no excuse\xe2\x80\x9d correctly implies that the State cannot impose punishment based on a mistake of law, it does not mean a reasonable mistake of law cannot justify an investigatory stop. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n \n (b)\xc2\xa0There is little difficulty in concluding that Officer Darisse\xe2\x80\x99s error of law was reasonable. The North Carolina vehicle code that requires \xe2\x80\x9ca stop lamp\xe2\x80\x9d also provides that the lamp \xe2\x80\x9cmay be incorporated into a unit with one or more other rear lamps,\xe2\x80\x9d N. C. Gen. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x93129(g), and that \xe2\x80\x9call originally equipped rear lamps\xe2\x80\x9d must be \xe2\x80\x9cin good working order,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x93129(d). Although the State Court of Appeals held that \xe2\x80\x9crear lamps\xe2\x80\x9d do not include brake lights, the word \xe2\x80\x9cother,\xe2\x80\x9d coupled with the lack of state-court precedent interpreting the provision, made it objectively reasonable to think that a faulty brake light constituted a violation. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

367 N.\xc2\xa0C. 163, 749 S. E. 2d 278, affirmed.

\n

NICHOLAS BRADY HEIEN, PETITIONER v. NORTH CAROLINA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of north carolina

\n

[December 15, 2014]

\n

Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n \n The\n \nFourth Amendment prohibits \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonable searches and seizures.\xe2\x80\x9d Under this standard, a search or seizure may be permissible even though the justification for the action includes a reasonable factual mistake. An officer might, for example, stop a motorist for traveling alone in a high-occupancy vehicle lane, only to discover upon approaching the car that two children are slumped over asleep in the back seat. The driver has not violated the law, but neither has the officer violated the\n \nFourth Amendment.

\n

\n \n But what if the police officer\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable mistake is not one of fact but of law? In this case, an officer stopped a vehicle because one of its two brake lights was out, but a court later determined that a single working brake light was all the law required. The question presented is whether such a mistake of law can nonetheless give rise to the reasonable suspicion necessary to uphold the seizure under the\n \nFourth Amendment. We hold that it can. Because the officer\xe2\x80\x99s mistake about the brake-light law was reasonable, the stop in this case was lawful under the\n \nFourth Amendment.

\n

I

\n

\n \n On the morning of April 29, 2009, Sergeant Matt Da-risse of the Surry County Sheriff\xe2\x80\x99s Department sat in his patrol car near Dobson, North Carolina, observing northbound traffic on Interstate 77. Shortly before 8 a.m., a Ford Escort passed by. Darisse thought the driver looked \xe2\x80\x9cvery stiff and nervous,\xe2\x80\x9d so he pulled onto the interstate and began following the Escort. A few miles down the road, the Escort braked as it approached a slower vehicle, but only the left brake light came on. Noting the faulty right brake light, Darisse activated his vehicle\xe2\x80\x99s lights and pulled the Escort over. App. 4\xe2\x80\x937, 15\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n \n Two men were in the car: Maynor Javier Vasquez sat behind the wheel, and petitioner Nicholas Brady Heien lay across the rear seat. Sergeant Darisse explained to Vasquez that as long as his license and registration checked out, he would receive only a warning ticket for the broken brake light. A records check revealed no problems with the documents, and Darisse gave Vasquez the warning ticket. But Darisse had become suspicious during the course of the stop\xe2\x80\x94Vasquez appeared nervous, Heien remained lying down the entire time, and the two gave inconsistent answers about their destination. Darisse asked Vasquez if he would be willing to answer some questions. Vasquez assented, and Darisse asked whether the men were transporting various types of contraband. Told no, Darisse asked whether he could search the Escort. Vasquez said he had no objection, but told Darisse he should ask Heien, because Heien owned the car. Heien gave his consent, and Darisse, aided by a fellow officer who had since arrived, began a thorough search of the vehicle. In the side compartment of a duffle bag, Darisse found a sandwich bag containing cocaine. The officers arrested both men. 366 N.\xc2\xa0C. 271, 272\xe2\x80\x93273, 737 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 351, 352\xe2\x80\x93353 (2012); App. 5\xe2\x80\x936, 25, 37.

\n

\n \n The State charged Heien with attempted trafficking in cocaine. Heien moved to suppress the evidence seized from the car, contending that the stop and search had violated the\n \nFourth Amendment of the United States Constitution. After a hearing at which both officers testified and the State played a video recording of the stop, the trial court denied the suppression motion, concluding that the faulty brake light had given Sergeant Darisse reasonable suspicion to initiate the stop, and that Heien\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent consent to the search was valid. Heien pleaded guilty but reserved his right to appeal the suppression decision. App. 1, 7\xe2\x80\x9310, 12, 29, 43\xe2\x80\x9344.

\n

\n \n The North Carolina Court of Appeals reversed. 214 N.\xc2\xa0C. App. 515, 714 S. E. 2d 827 (2011). The initial stop was not valid, the court held, because driving with only one working brake light was not actually a violation of North Carolina law. The relevant provision of the vehicle code provides that a car must be

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cequipped with a stop lamp on the rear of the vehicle. The stop lamp shall display a red or amber light visible from a distance of not less than 100 feet to the rear in normal sunlight, and shall be actuated upon application of the service (foot) brake. The stop lamp may be incorporated into a unit with one or moreother rear lamps.\xe2\x80\x9d N.\xc2\xa0C. Gen. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x93129(g) (2007).

\n

\n \n Focusing on the statute\xe2\x80\x99s references to \xe2\x80\x9ca stop lamp\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he stop lamp\xe2\x80\x9d in the singular, the court concluded that a vehicle is required to have only one working brake light\xe2\x80\x94which Heien\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle indisputably did. The justification for the stop was therefore \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively unreason-able,\xe2\x80\x9d and the stop violated the\n \nFourth Amendment. 214 N.\xc2\xa0C. App., at 518\xe2\x80\x93522, 714 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 829\xe2\x80\x93831.

\n

\n \n The State appealed, and the North Carolina Supreme Court reversed. 366 N.\xc2\xa0C. 271, 737 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 351. Noting that the State had chosen not to seek review of the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of the vehicle code, the North Carolina Supreme Court assumed for purposes of its decision that the faulty brake light was not a violation. Id., at 275, 737 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 354. But the court concluded that, for several reasons, Sergeant Darisse could have reasonably, even if mistakenly, read the vehicle code to require that both brake lights be in good working order. Most notably, a nearby code provision requires that \xe2\x80\x9call originally equipped rear lamps\xe2\x80\x9d be functional. Id., at 282\xe2\x80\x93283, 737 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 358\xe2\x80\x93359 (quoting N.\xc2\xa0C. Gen. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x93129(d)). Because Sergeant Darisse\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken understanding of the vehicle code was reasonable, the stop was valid. \xe2\x80\x9cAn officer may make a mistake, including a mistake of law, yet still act reasonably under the circumstances. . . . [W]hen an officer acts reasonably under the circumstances, he is not violating the\n \nFourth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 279, 737 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 356.

\n

\n \n The North Carolina Supreme Court remanded to the Court of Appeals to address Heien\xe2\x80\x99s other arguments for suppression (which are not at issue here). Id., at 283, 737 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 359. The Court of Appeals rejected those arguments and affirmed the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of his motion to suppress. ___ N.\xc2\xa0C. App. ___, 741 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 1 (2013). The North Carolina Supreme Court affirmed in turn. 367 N.\xc2\xa0C. 163, 749 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 278 (2013). We granted certiorari. 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014).

\n

II

\n

\n \n The\n \nFourth Amendment provides:

\n

\n \n \xe2\x80\x9cThe right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreason-able searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by Oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n \n A traffic stop for a suspected violation of law is a \xe2\x80\x9cseizure\xe2\x80\x9d of the occupants of the vehicle and therefore must be conducted in accordance with the\n \nFourth Amendment. Brendlin v. California,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 249\n\xe2\x80\x93259 (2007). All parties agree that to justify this type of seizure, officers need only \xe2\x80\x9creasonable suspicion\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, \xe2\x80\x9ca particularized and objective basis for suspecting the particular person stopped\xe2\x80\x9d of breaking the law. Prado Navarette v. California, 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 3) (internal quotation marks omitted). The question here is whether reasonable suspicion can rest on a mistaken understanding of the scope of a legal prohibition. We hold that it can.

\n

\n \n As the text indicates and we have repeatedly affirmed, \xe2\x80\x9cthe ultimate touchstone of the\n \nFourth Amendment is \xe2\x80\x98reasonableness.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Riley v. California, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 5) (some internal quotation marks omitted). To be reasonable is not to be perfect, and so the\n \nFourth Amendment allows for some mistakes on the part of government officials, giving them \xe2\x80\x9cfair leeway for enforcing the law in the community\xe2\x80\x99s protection.\xe2\x80\x9d Brinegar v. United States,\n\n338 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160,\n176 (1949)\n. We have recognized that searches and seizures based on mistakes of fact can be reasonable. The warrantless search of a home, for instance, is reasonable if undertaken with the consent of a resident, and remains lawful when officers obtain the consent of someone who reasonably appears to be but is not in fact a resident. See Illinois v. Rodriguez,\n\n497 U.\xc2\xa0S. 177\n\xe2\x80\x93186 (1990). By the same token, if officers with probable cause to arrest a suspect mistakenly arrest an individual matching the suspect\xe2\x80\x99s description, neither the seizure nor an accompanying search of the arrestee would be unlawful. See Hill v. California,\n\n401 U.\xc2\xa0S. 797\n\xe2\x80\x93805 (1971). The limit is that \xe2\x80\x9cthe mistakes must be those of reasonable men.\xe2\x80\x9d Brinegar, supra, at 176.

\n

\n \n But reasonable men make mistakes of law, too, and such mistakes are no less compatible with the concept of reasonable suspicion. Reasonable suspicion arises from the combination of an officer\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the facts and his understanding of the relevant law. The officer may be reasonably mistaken on either ground. Whether the facts turn out to be not what was thought, or the law turns out to be not what was thought, the result is the same: the facts are outside the scope of the law. There is no reason, under the text of the\n \nFourth Amendment or our precedents, why this same result should be acceptable when reached by way of a reasonable mistake of fact, but not when reached by way of a similarly reasonable mistake of law.

\n

\n \n The dissent counters that our cases discussing probable cause and reasonable suspicion, most notably Ornelas v. United States,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 690\n\xe2\x80\x93697 (1996), have contained \xe2\x80\x9cscarcely a peep\xe2\x80\x9d about mistakes of law. Post, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (opinion of Sotomayor, J.). It would have been surprising, of course, if they had, since none of those cases involved a mistake of law.

\n

\n \n Although such recent cases did not address mistakes of law, older precedents did. In fact, cases dating back two centuries support treating legal and factual errors alike in this context. Customs statutes enacted by Congress not long after the founding authorized courts to issue certificates indemnifying customs officers against damages suits premised on unlawful seizures. See, e.g., Act of Mar. 2, 1799, ch. 22, \xc2\xa789,\n\n1Stat.\n695\xe2\x80\x93696. Courts were to issue such certificates on a showing that the officer had \xe2\x80\x9creasonable cause\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a synonym for \xe2\x80\x9cprobable cause\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94for the challenged seizure. Ibid.; see Stacey v. Emery, 97 U.\xc2\xa0S. 642, 646 (1878); United States v. Riddle, 5 Cranch 311 (1809). In United States v. Riddle, a customs officer seized goods on the ground that the English shipper had violated the customs laws by preparing an invoice that undervalued the merchandise, even though the American consignee declared the true value to the customs collector. Chief Justice Marshall held that there had been no violation of the customs law because, whatever the shipper\xe2\x80\x99s intention, the consignee had not actually attempted to defraud the Government. Nevertheless, because \xe2\x80\x9cthe construction of the law was liable to some question,\xe2\x80\x9d he affirmed the issuance of a certificate of probable cause: \xe2\x80\x9cA doubt as to the true construction of the law is as reasonable a cause for seizure as a doubt respecting the fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 313.

\n

\n \n This holding\xe2\x80\x94that reasonable mistakes of law, like those of fact, would justify certificates of probable cause\xe2\x80\x94was reiterated in a number of 19th-century decisions. See, e.g., The Friendship, 9 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 825, 826 (No. 5,125) (CC Mass. 1812) (Story, J.); United States v. The Reindeer, 27 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 758, 768 (No. 16,145) (CC RI 1848); United States v. The Recorder, 27 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 723 (No. 16,130) (CC SDNY 1849). By the Civil War, there had been \xe2\x80\x9cnumerous cases in which [a] captured vessel was in no fault, and had not, under a true construction of the law, presented even ground of suspicion, and yet the captor was exonerated because he acted under an honest mistake of the law.\xe2\x80\x9d The La Manche, 14 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 965, 972 (No. 8,004) (D Mass. 1863).

\n

\nRiddle and its progeny are not directly on point. Chief Justice Marshall was not construing the\n \nFourth Amendment, and a certificate of probable cause functioned much like a modern-day finding of qualified immunity, which depends on an inquiry distinct from whether an officer has committed a constitutional violation. See, e.g., Carroll v. Carman, ante, at 7 (per curiam). But Chief Justice Marshall was nevertheless explaining the concept of probable cause, which, he noted elsewhere, \xe2\x80\x9cin all cases of seizure, has a fixed and well known meaning. It imports a seizure made under circumstances which warrant suspicion.\xe2\x80\x9d Locke v. United States, 7 Cranch 339, 348 (1813). We have said the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cprobable cause\xe2\x80\x9d bore this \xe2\x80\x9cfixed and well known meaning\xe2\x80\x9d in the\n \nFourth Amendment, see Brinegar, supra, at 175, and n.\xc2\xa014, and Riddle illustrates that it encompassed suspicion based on reasonable mistakes of both fact and law. No decision of this Court in the two centuries since has undermined that understanding.[1]

\n

\n \n The contrary conclusion would be hard to reconcile with a much more recent precedent. In Michigan v. DeFillippo,\n\n443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 31 (1979)\n, we addressed the validity of an arrest made under a criminal law later declared unconstitu-tional. A Detroit ordinance that authorized police officers to stop and question individuals suspected of criminal activ-ity also made it an offense for such an individual \xe2\x80\x9cto refuse to identify himself and produce evidence of his identity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 33. Detroit police officers sent to investigate a report of public intoxication arrested Gary DeFillippo after he failed to identify himself. A search incident to arrest uncovered drugs, and DeFillippo was charged with possession of a controlled substance. The Michigan Court of Appeals ordered the suppression of the drugs, concluding that the identification ordinance was unconstitutionally vague and that DeFillippo\xe2\x80\x99s arrest was therefore invalid. Id., at 34\xe2\x80\x9335.

\n

\n \n Accepting the unconstitutionality of the ordinance as a given, we nonetheless reversed. At the time the officers arrested DeFillippo, we explained, \xe2\x80\x9cthere was no controlling precedent that this ordinance was or was not constitutional, and hence the conduct observed violated a presumptively valid ordinance.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 37. Acknowledging that the outcome might have been different had the ordinance been \xe2\x80\x9cgrossly and flagrantly unconstitutional,\xe2\x80\x9d we concluded that under the circumstances \xe2\x80\x9cthere was abundant probable cause to satisfy the constitutional prerequisite for an arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 37\xe2\x80\x9338.

\n

\n \n The officers were wrong in concluding that DeFillippo was guilty of a criminal offense when he declined to iden-tify himself. That a court only later declared the ordinance unconstitutional does not change the fact that DeFillippo\xe2\x80\x99s conduct was lawful when the officers observed it. See Danforth v. Minnesota,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 264,\n271 (2008)\n. But the officers\xe2\x80\x99 assumption that the law was valid was reason-able, and their observations gave them \xe2\x80\x9cabundant probable cause\xe2\x80\x9d to arrest DeFillippo. 443 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 37. Although DeFillippo could not be prosecuted under the identifica-tion ordinance, the search that turned up the drugs was constitutional.

\n

\n \n Heien struggles to recast DeFillippo as a case solely about the exclusionary rule, not the\n \nFourth Amendment itself. In his view, the officers\xe2\x80\x99 mistake of law resulted in a violation the\n \nFourth Amendment, but suppression of the drugs was not the proper remedy. We did say in a footnote that suppression of the evidence found on DeFillippo would serve none of the purposes of the exclusionary rule. See id., at 38, n.\xc2\xa03. But that literally marginal discussion does not displace our express holding that the arrest was constitutionally valid because the officers had probable cause. See id., at 40. Nor, contrary to Heien\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, did either United States v. Leon,\n\n468 U.\xc2\xa0S. 897 (1984)\n, or Illinois v. Gates,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213 (1983)\n, somehow erase that holding and transform DeFillippo into an exclusionary rule decision. See Brief for Petitioner 28\xe2\x80\x9329. In Leon, we said DeFillippo paid \xe2\x80\x9cattention to the purposes underlying the exclusionary rule,\xe2\x80\x9d but we also clarified that it did \xe2\x80\x9cnot involv[e] the scope of the rule itself.\xe2\x80\x9d 468 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 911\xe2\x80\x93912. As for Gates, only Justice White\xe2\x80\x99s separate opinion (joined by no other Justice) discussed DeFillippo, and it acknowledged that \xe2\x80\x9cDeFillippo did not modify the exclusionary rule itself\xe2\x80\x9d but instead \xe2\x80\x9cupheld the validity of an arrest.\xe2\x80\x9d 462 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 256, n.\xc2\xa012 (opinion concurring in judgment).

\n

\n \n Heien is correct that in a number of decisions we have looked to the reasonableness of an officer\xe2\x80\x99s legal error in the course of considering the appropriate remedy for a constitutional violation, instead of whether there was a violation at all. See, e.g., Davis v. United States, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 11) (exclusionary rule); Illinois v. Krull,\n\n480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 340\n\xe2\x80\x93360 (1987) (exclusionary rule); Wilson v. Layne,\n\n526 U.\xc2\xa0S. 603,\n615 (1999)\n (qualified immunity); Anderson v. Creighton,\n\n483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635,\n641 (1987)\n (qualified immunity). In those cases, however, we had already found or assumed a\n \nFourth Amendment violation. An officer\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken view that the conduct at issue did not give rise to such a violation\xe2\x80\x94no matter how reason-able\xe2\x80\x94could not change that ultimate conclusion. See Brief for Respondent 29\xe2\x80\x9331; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 30, n.\xc2\xa03. Any consideration of the reasonableness of an officer\xe2\x80\x99s mistake was therefore limited to the separate matter of remedy.

\n

\n \n Here, by contrast, the mistake of law relates to the antecedent question of whether it was reasonable for an officer to suspect that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct was illegal. If so, there was no violation of the\n \nFourth Amendment in the first place. None of the cases Heien or the dissent cites precludes a court from considering a reasonable mistake of law in addressing that question. Cf. Herring v. United States,\n\n555 U.\xc2\xa0S. 135,\n139 (2009)\n (assuming a\n \nFourth Amendment violation while rejecting application of the exclusionary rule, but noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a probable-cause determination was based on reasonable but mis-taken assumptions, the person subjected to a search or seizure has not necessarily been the victim of a constitutional violation\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n \n Heien also contends that the reasons the\n \nFourth Amendment allows some errors of fact do not extend to errors of law. Officers in the field must make factual assessments on the fly, Heien notes, and so deserve a margin of error. In Heien\xe2\x80\x99s view, no such margin is appropriate for questions of law: The statute here either requires one working brake light or two, and the answer does not turn on anything \xe2\x80\x9can officer might suddenly confront in the field.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 21. But Heien\xe2\x80\x99s point does not consider the reality that an officer may \xe2\x80\x9csuddenly confront\xe2\x80\x9d a situation in the field as to which the application of a statute is unclear\xe2\x80\x94however clear it may later become. A law prohibiting \xe2\x80\x9cvehicles\xe2\x80\x9d in the park either covers Segways or not, see A. Scalia & B. Garner, Reading Law: The Interpretation of Legal Texts 36\xe2\x80\x9338 (2012), but an officer will nevertheless have to make a quick decision on the law the first time one whizzes by.

\n

\n \n Contrary to the suggestion of Heien and amici, our decision does not discourage officers from learning the law. The\n \nFourth Amendment tolerates only reasonable mistakes, and those mistakes\xe2\x80\x94whether of fact or of law\xe2\x80\x94must be objectively reasonable. We do not examine the subjective understanding of the particular officer involved. Cf. Whren v. United States,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 806,\n813 (1996)\n. And the inquiry is not as forgiving as the one employed in the distinct context of deciding whether an officer is entitled to qualified immunity for a constitutional or statutory violation. Thus, an officer can gain no\n \nFourth Amendment advantage through a sloppy study of the laws he is duty-bound to enforce.

\n

\n \n Finally, Heien and amici point to the well-known maxim, \xe2\x80\x9cIgnorance of the law is no excuse,\xe2\x80\x9d and contend that itis fundamentally unfair to let police officers get away with mistakes of law when the citizenry is accorded no such leeway. Though this argument has a certain rhetorical appeal, it misconceives the implication of the maxim. The true symmetry is this: Just as an individual generally cannot escape criminal liability based on a mistaken understanding of the law, so too the government cannot impose criminal liability based on a mistaken understanding of the law. If the law required two working brake lights, Heien could not escape a ticket by claiming he reasonably thought he needed only one; if the law required only one, Sergeant Darisse could not issue a valid ticket by claiming he reasonably thought drivers needed two. But just because mistakes of law cannot justify either the imposition or the avoidance of criminal liability, it does not follow that they cannot justify an investigatory stop. And Heien is not appealing a brake-light ticket; he is appealing a cocaine-trafficking conviction as to which there is no asserted mistake of fact or law.

\n

III

\n

\n \n Here we have little difficulty concluding that the officer\xe2\x80\x99s error of law was reasonable. Although the North Carolina statute at issue refers to \xe2\x80\x9ca stop lamp,\xe2\x80\x9d suggesting the need for only a single working brake light, it also provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he stop lamp may be incorporated into a unit with one or more other rear lamps.\xe2\x80\x9d N.\xc2\xa0C. Gen. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x93129(g) (emphasis added). The use of \xe2\x80\x9cother\xe2\x80\x9d suggests to the everyday reader of English that a \xe2\x80\x9cstop lamp\xe2\x80\x9d is a type of \xe2\x80\x9crear lamp.\xe2\x80\x9d And another subsection of the same provision requires that vehicles \xe2\x80\x9chave all originally equipped rear lamps or the equivalent in good working order,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x93129(d), arguably indicating that if a vehicle has multiple \xe2\x80\x9cstop lamp[s],\xe2\x80\x9d all must be functional.

\n

\n \n The North Carolina Court of Appeals concluded that the \xe2\x80\x9crear lamps\xe2\x80\x9d discussed in subsection (d) do not include brake lights, but, given the \xe2\x80\x9cother,\xe2\x80\x9d it would at least have been reasonable to think they did. Both the majority and the dissent in the North Carolina Supreme Court so concluded, and we agree. See 366 N.\xc2\xa0C., at 282\xe2\x80\x93283, 737 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 358\xe2\x80\x93359; id., at 283, 737 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 359 (Hudson, J., dissenting) (calling the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision \xe2\x80\x9csurprising\xe2\x80\x9d). This \xe2\x80\x9cstop lamp\xe2\x80\x9d provision, moreover, had never been previously construed by North Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s appellate courts. See id., at 283, 737 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 359 (majority opinion). It was thus objectively reasonable for an officer in Sergeant Darisse\xe2\x80\x99s position to think that Heien\xe2\x80\x99s faulty right brake light was a violation of North Carolina law. And because the mistake of law was reasonable, there was reasonable suspicion justifying the stop.

\n

\n \n The judgment of the Supreme Court of North Carolina is

\n

Affirmed.

NICHOLAS BRADY HEIEN, PETITIONER v. NORTH CAROLINA

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of north carolina

[December 15, 2014]

Justice Kagan, with whom Justice Ginsburg joins, concurring.

I concur in full in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion, which explains why certain mistakes of law can support the reasonable suspicion needed to stop a vehicle under the Fourth Amendment. In doing so, the Court correctly emphasizes that the \xe2\x80\x9c Fourth Amendment tolerates only .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. objectively reasonable\xe2\x80\x9d mistakes of law. Ante, at 11. And the Court makes clear that the inquiry into whether an officer\xe2\x80\x99s mistake of law counts as objectively reasonable \xe2\x80\x9cis not as forgiving as the one employed in the distinct context of deciding whether an officer is entitled to qualified immunity.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. I write separately to elaborate briefly on those important limitations.[1]

First, an officer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csubjective understanding\xe2\x80\x9d is irrelevant: As the Court notes, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e do not examine\xe2\x80\x9d it at all. Ibid. That means the government cannot defend an officer\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken legal interpretation on the ground that the officer was unaware of or untrained in the law. And it means that, contrary to the dissenting opinion in the court below, an officer\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on \xe2\x80\x9can incorrect memo or training program from the police department\xe2\x80\x9d makes no difference to the analysis. 366 N.\xc2\xa0C. 271, 284, 737 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 351, 360 (2012) (Hudson, J., dissenting). Those considerations pertain to the officer\xe2\x80\x99s subjective understanding of the law and thus cannot help to justify a seizure.

Second, the inquiry the Court permits today is more demanding than the one courts undertake before awarding qualified immunity. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 51 (Solicitor General stating that the two tests \xe2\x80\x9crequire essentially the opposite\xe2\x80\x9d showings); Brief for Respondent 31\xe2\x80\x9332 (making a similar point). Our modern qualified immunity doctrine protects \xe2\x80\x9call but the plainly incompetent or those who knowingly violate the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 12) (quoting Malley v. Briggs,475 U.\xc2\xa0S. 335,341 (1986)). By contrast, Justice Story\xe2\x80\x99s opinion in The Friendship, 9 F.\xc2\xa0Cas. 825, 826 (No. 5,125) (CC Mass. 1812) (cited ante, at 7), suggests the appropriate standard for deciding when a legal error can support a seizure: when an officer takes a reasonable view of a \xe2\x80\x9cvexata questio\xe2\x80\x9d on which different judges \xe2\x80\x9ch[o]ld opposite opinions.\xe2\x80\x9d See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 26 (invoking that language). Or to make the same point without the Latin, the test is satisfied when the law at issue is \xe2\x80\x9cso doubtful in construction\xe2\x80\x9d that a reasonable judge could agree with the officer\xe2\x80\x99s view. The Friendship, 9 F.\xc2\xa0Cas., at 826.

A court tasked with deciding whether an officer\xe2\x80\x99s mistake of law can support a seizure thus faces a straightforward question of statutory construction. If the statute is genuinely ambiguous, such that overturning the officer\xe2\x80\x99s judgment requires hard interpretive work, then the officer has made a reasonable mistake. But if not, not. As the Solicitor General made the point at oral argument, the statute must pose a \xe2\x80\x9creally difficult\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cvery hard question of statutory interpretation.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 50. And indeed, both North Carolina and the Solicitor General agreed that such cases will be \xe2\x80\x9cexceedingly rare.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 17; Tr. of Oral Arg. 48.

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of Sergeant Darisse\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the North Carolina law at issue here appropriately reflects these principles. As the Court explains, see ante, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313, the statute requires every car on the highway to have \xe2\x80\x9ca stop lamp,\xe2\x80\x9d in the singular. N.\xc2\xa0C. Gen. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x93129(g) (2007). But the statute goes on to state that a stop lamp (or, in more modern terminology, brake light) \xe2\x80\x9cmay be incorporated into a unit with one or more other rear lamps,\xe2\x80\x9d suggesting that a stop lamp itself qualifies as a rear lamp. Ibid. (emphasis added). And the statute further mandates that every car have \xe2\x80\x9call originally equipped rear lamps .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in good working order.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa720\xe2\x80\x93129(d) (emphasis added). The North Carolina Court of Appeals dealt with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s conflicting signals in one way (deciding that a brake light is not a rear lamp, and so only one needs to work); but a court could easily take the officer\xe2\x80\x99s view (deciding that a brake light is a rear lamp, and if a car comes equipped with more than one, as modern cars do, all must be in working order). The critical point is that the statute poses a quite difficult question of interpretation, and Sergeant Darisse\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, although overturned, had much to recommend it. I therefore agree with the Court that the traffic stop he conducted did not violate the Fourth Amendment.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

_________________

NICHOLAS BRADY HEIEN, PETITIONER v. NORTH CAROLINA

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of north carolina

[December 15, 2014]

Justice Sotomayor, dissenting.

The Court is, of course, correct that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the ultimate touchstone of the Fourth Amendment is \xe2\x80\x9creasonableness.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Riley v. California, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2014) (slip op., at 5). But this broad statement simply sets the standard a court is to apply when it conducts its inquiry into whether the Fourth Amendment has been violated. It does not define the categories of inputs that courts are to consider when assessing the reasonableness of a search or seizure, each of which must be independently justified. What this case requires us to decide is whether a police officer\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the law is an input into the reasonableness inquiry, or whether this inquiry instead takes the law as a given and assesses an officer\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the facts against a fixed legal yardstick.

I would hold that determining whether a search or seizure is reasonable requires evaluating an officer\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the facts against the actual state of the law. I would accordingly reverse the judgment of the North Carolina Supreme Court, and I respectfully dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s contrary holding.

I

It is common ground that Heien was seized within the meaning of the Fourth Amendment. Such a seizure comports with the Constitution only if the officers had articulable and reasonable suspicion that Heien was breaking the law. In Ornelas v. United States,517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 690,696 (1996), we explained that the \xe2\x80\x9cprincipal components\xe2\x80\x9d of that determination \xe2\x80\x9cwill be the events which occurred leading up to the stop or search, and then the decision whether these historical facts, viewed from the standpoint of an objectively reasonable police officer, amount to reasonable suspicion or to probable cause.\xe2\x80\x9d We described this kind of determination as \xe2\x80\x9ca mixed question of law and fact\xe2\x80\x9d: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[T]he issue is whether the facts satisfy the [relevant] statutory [or constitutional] standard, or to put it another way, whether the rule of law as applied to the established facts is or is not violated.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 696\xe2\x80\x93697 (quoting Pullman-Standard v. Swint, 456 U.\xc2\xa0S 273, 289, n.\xc2\xa019 (1982)). What matters, we said, are the facts as viewed by an objectively reasonable officer, and the rule of law\xe2\x80\x94not an officer\xe2\x80\x99s conception of the rule of law, and not even an officer\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable misunderstanding about the law, but the law.

As a result, when we have talked about the leeway that officers have in making probable-cause determinations, we have focused on their assessments of facts. See, e.g., Terry v. Ohio,392 U. S. 1\xe2\x80\x9322 (1968) (framing the question as whether the \xe2\x80\x9cfacts\xe2\x80\x9d give rise to reasonable suspicion). We have conceded that an arresting officer\xe2\x80\x99s state of mind does not factor into the probable-cause inquiry, \xe2\x80\x9cexcept for the facts that he knows.\xe2\x80\x9d Devenpeck v. Alford,543 U.\xc2\xa0S. 146,153 (2004) (emphasis added). And we have said that, to satisfy the reasonableness requirement, \xe2\x80\x9cwhat is generally demanded of the many factual determinations that must regularly be made by agents of the government .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is not that they always be correct, but that they always be reasonable.\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois v. Rodriguez,497 U.\xc2\xa0S. 177,185 (1990) (emphasis added). There is scarcely a peep in these cases to suggest that an officer\xe2\x80\x99s understanding or conception of anything other than the facts is relevant.

This framing of the reasonableness inquiry has not only been focused on officers\xe2\x80\x99 understanding of the facts, it has been justified in large part based on the recognition that officers are generally in a superior position, relative to courts, to evaluate those facts and their significance as they unfold. In other words, the leeway we afford officers\xe2\x80\x99 factual assessments is rooted not only in our recognition that police officers operating in the field have to make quick decisions, see id., at 186, but also in our understanding that police officers have the expertise to \xe2\x80\x9cdra[w] inferences and mak[e] deductions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that might well elude an untrained person.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Cortez,449 U.\xc2\xa0S. 411,418 (1981). When officers evaluate unfolding circumstances, they deploy that expertise to draw \xe2\x80\x9cconclusions about human behavior\xe2\x80\x9d much in the way that \xe2\x80\x9cjurors [do] as factfinders.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).

The same cannot be said about legal exegesis. After all, the meaning of the law is not probabilistic in the same way that factual determinations are. Rather, \xe2\x80\x9cthe notion that the law is definite and knowable\xe2\x80\x9d sits at the foundation of our legal system. Cheek v. United States,498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 192,199 (1991). And it is courts, not officers, that are in the best position to interpret the laws.

Both our enunciation of the reasonableness inquiry and our justification for it thus have always turned on an officer\xe2\x80\x99s factual conclusions and an officer\xe2\x80\x99s expertise with respect to those factual conclusions. Neither has hinted at taking into account an officer\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the law, reasonable or otherwise.

II

Departing from this tradition means further eroding the Fourth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s protection of civil liberties in a context where that protection has already been worn down. Traffic stops like those at issue here can be \xe2\x80\x9cannoying, frightening, and perhaps humiliating.\xe2\x80\x9d Terry, 392 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 25; see Delaware v. Prouse,440 U.\xc2\xa0S. 648,657 (1979). We have nevertheless held that an officer\xe2\x80\x99s subjective motivations do not render a traffic stop unlawful. Whren v. United States,517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 806 (1996). But we assumed in Whren that when an officer acts on pretext, at least that pretext would be the violation of an actual law. See id., at 810 (discussing the three provisions of the District of Columbia traffic code that the parties accepted the officer had probable cause to believe had been vio-lated). Giving officers license to effect seizures so long as they can attach to their reasonable view of the facts some reasonable legal interpretation (or misinterpretation) that suggests a law has been violated significantly expands this authority. Cf. Barlow v. United States, 7 Pet. 404, 411 (1833) (Story, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is scarcely any law which does not admit of some ingenious doubt\xe2\x80\x9d). One wonders how a citizen seeking to be law-abiding and to structure his or her behavior to avoid these invasive, frightening, and humiliating encounters could do so.

In addition to these human consequences\xe2\x80\x94including those for communities and for their relationships with the police\xe2\x80\x94permitting mistakes of law to justify seizures has the perverse effect of preventing or delaying the clarification of the law. Under such an approach, courts need not interpret statutory language but can instead simply decide whether an officer\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation was reasonable. Indeed, had this very case arisen after the North Carolina Supreme Court announced its rule, the North Carolina Court of Appeals would not have had the occasion to interpret the statute at issue. Similarly, courts in the Eighth Circuit, which has been the only Circuit to include police mistakes of law in the reasonableness inquiry, have observed that they need not decide interpretive questions under their approach. See, e.g., United States v. Rodriguez-Lopez, 444 F. 3d 1020, 1022\xe2\x80\x931023 (CA8 2006).[1] This result is bad for citizens, who need to know their rights and responsibilities, and it is bad for police, who would benefit from clearer direction. Cf. Camreta v. Greene, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2011) (slip op., at 10\xe2\x80\x9311) (recognizing the importance of clarifying the law).

Of course, if the law enforcement system could not function without permitting mistakes of law to justify seizures, one could at least argue that permitting as much is a necessary evil. But I have not seen any persuasive argument that law enforcement will be unduly hampered by a rule that precludes consideration of mistakes of law in the reasonableness inquiry. After all, there is no indication that excluding an officer\xe2\x80\x99s mistake of law from the reasonableness inquiry has created a problem for law enforcement in the overwhelming number of Circuits which have adopted that approach. If an officer makes a stop in good faith but it turns out that, as in this case, the officer was wrong about what the law proscribed or required, I know of no penalty that the officer would suffer. See 366 N.\xc2\xa0C. 271, 286\xe2\x80\x93288, 737 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 351, 361\xe2\x80\x93362 (2012) (Hudson, J., dissenting) (observing that \xe2\x80\x9cofficers (rightfully) face no punishment for a stop based on a mistake of law\xe2\x80\x9d). Moreover, such an officer would likely have a defense to any civil suit on the basis of qualified immunity. See Ashcroft v. al-Kidd, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 12) (\xe2\x80\x9cQualified immunity gives government officials breathing room to make reasonable but mistaken judgments about open legal questions\xe2\x80\x9d).

Nor will it often be the case that any evidence that may be seized during the stop will be suppressed, thanks to the exception to the exclusionary rule for good-faith police errors. See, e.g., Davis v. United States, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2011) (slip op., at 8\xe2\x80\x939). It is true that, unlike most States, North Carolina does not provide a good-faith exception as a matter of state law, see State v. Carter, 322 N.\xc2\xa0C. 709, 721\xe2\x80\x93724, 370 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 553, 560\xe2\x80\x93562 (1988), but North Carolina recognizes that it may solve any remedial problems it may perceive on its own, see id., at 724, 370 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 562; N. C. Gen. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa715A\xe2\x80\x93974 (2013) (statutory good-faith exception).[2] More fundamentally, that is a remedial concern, and the protections offered by the Fourth Amendment are not meant to yield to accommodate remedial concerns. Our jurisprudence draws a sharp \xe2\x80\x9canalytica[l] distinct[ion]\xe2\x80\x9d between the existence of a Fourth Amendment violation and the remedy for that violation. Davis, 564 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 14).

In short, there is nothing in our case law requiring us to hold that a reasonable mistake of law can justify a seizure under the Fourth Amendment, and quite a bit suggesting just the opposite. I also see nothing to be gained from such a holding, and much to be lost.

III

In reaching the contrary conclusion, the Court makes both serious legal and practical errors. On the legal side, the Court barely addresses Ornelas and the other cases that frame the reasonableness inquiry around factual determinations. Instead, in support of its conclusion that reasonable suspicion \xe2\x80\x9carises from the combination of an officer\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the facts and his understanding of the relevant law,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6 (emphasis added), the Court first reaches to founding-era customs statutes and cases applying those statutes. It concedes, however, that these cases are \xe2\x80\x9cnot directly on point\xe2\x80\x9d because they say nothing about the scope of the Fourth Amendment and are instead equivalents of our modern-day qualified immunity jurisprudence for civil damages. Ante, at 7.

The only link in the tenuous chain the Court constructs between those cases and this one that has anything to say about the Fourth Amendment is Brinegar v. United States,338 U.\xc2\xa0S. 160 (1949). See ante, at 8. But all that our opinion in Brinegar actually says is that probable cause exists where \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the facts and circumstances within [the officers\xe2\x80\x99] knowledge and of which they had reasonably trustworthy information [are] sufficient in themselves to warrant a man of reasonable caution in the belief that\xe2\x80\x99 an offense has been or is being committed.\xe2\x80\x9d 338 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 175\xe2\x80\x93176 (quoting Carroll v. United States,267 U.\xc2\xa0S. 132,162 (1925)). It thus states the uncontroversial proposition that the probable-cause inquiry looks to the reasonableness of an officer\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of the facts. Indeed, Brinegar is an odd case for the Court to rely on given that, like the cases I discussed above, it subsequently emphasizes that \xe2\x80\x9cthe mistakes must be those of reasonable men, acting on facts leading sensibly to their conclusions of probability.\xe2\x80\x9d 338 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 176 (emphasis added). Again, reasonable understandings of the facts, not reasonable understandings of what the law says.[3]

Further, the Court looks to our decision in Michigan v. DeFillippo,443 U.\xc2\xa0S. 31 (1979). This is a Fourth Amendment case, but the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of it imagines a holding that is not rooted in the logic of the opinion. We held in DeFillippo that an officer had probable cause to support an arrest even though the ordinance that had allegedly been violated was later held by the Michigan Court of Appeals to be unconstitutional. This was so, we explained, because the officer conducted an arrest after having observed conduct that was criminalized by a presumptively valid law at the time of that conduct. See id., at 37 (\xe2\x80\x9cAt th[e] time [of the arrest], of course, there was no controlling precedent that this ordinance was or was not constitutional, and hence the conduct observed violated a presumptively valid ordinance\xe2\x80\x9d). We noted that it would have been wrong for that officer not to enforce the law in that sit-uation. See id., at 38 (\xe2\x80\x9cPolice are charged to enforce laws until and unless they are declared unconstitutional.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Society would be ill-served if its police officers tookit upon themselves to determine which laws are and which are not constitutionally entitled to enforcement\xe2\x80\x9d).

DeFillippo thus did not involve any police \xe2\x80\x9cmistake\xe2\x80\x9d at all. Rather, DeFillippo involved a police officer correctly applying the law that was then in existence and that carried with it a presumption of validity. Here, by contrast, police stopped Heien on suspicion of committing an offense that never actually existed. Given that our holding in DeFillippo relied so squarely on the existence of a law criminalizing the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, and on the presumption of validity that attends actual laws, it can hardly be said to control where, as here, no law ever actually criminalized Heien\xe2\x80\x99s conduct.

On the practical side, the Court primarily contends that an officer may confront \xe2\x80\x9ca situation in the field as to which the application of a statute is unclear.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. One is left to wonder, however, why an innocent citizen should be made to shoulder the burden of being seized whenever the law may be susceptible to an interpretive question. Moreover, the Court fails to reconcile its belief that the Fourth Amendment gives officers leeway to address situations where the application of a criminal statute may be unclear with our prior assumption that the Fourth Amendment does not give officers such leeway where they rely on a statute that authorizes police conduct that may violate the Fourth Amendment. See Illinois v. Krull,480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 340, n.\xc2\xa012, 359 (1987). Nor does it engage with the analytic consequences of North Carolina\xe2\x80\x99s similar concession that it does not mean to claim \xe2\x80\x9cthat an officer\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken understanding of the Fourth Amendment itself can support a seizure if that understanding was reason-able.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 29. It is not clear why an officer\xe2\x80\x99s mistaken understanding of other laws should be viewed differently.

While I appreciate that the Court has endeavored to set some bounds on the types of mistakes of law that it thinks will qualify as reasonable, and while I think that the set of reasonable mistakes of law ought to be narrowly circumscribed if they are to be countenanced at all, I am not at all convinced that the Court has done so in a clear way. It seems to me that the difference between qualified immunity\xe2\x80\x99s reasonableness standard\xe2\x80\x94which the Court insists without elaboration does not apply here\xe2\x80\x94and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conception of reasonableness in this context\xe2\x80\x94which remains undefined\xe2\x80\x94will prove murky in application. See ante, at 11. I fear the Court\xe2\x80\x99s unwillingness to sketch a fuller view of what makes a mistake of law reasonable only presages the likely difficulty that courts will have applying the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in this case.

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

To my mind, the more administrable approach\xe2\x80\x94and the one more consistent with our precedents and principles\xe2\x80\x94would be to hold that an officer\xe2\x80\x99s mistake of law, no matter how reasonable, cannot support the individualized suspicion necessary to justify a seizure under the Fourth Amendment. I respectfully dissent.
