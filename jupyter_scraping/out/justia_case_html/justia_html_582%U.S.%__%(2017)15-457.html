Syllabus

\n

MICROSOFT CORP. v. BAKER et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93457.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 21, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 12, 2017

\n

Orders granting or denying class certification are inherently interlocutory, hence not immediately reviewable under\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71291, which empowers federal courts of appeals to review only \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decisions of the district courts.\xe2\x80\x9d In Coopers & Lybrand v. Livesay,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 463\n, a 1978 decision, this Court held that the death-knell doctrine\xe2\x80\x94which rested on courts\xe2\x80\x99 recognition that a denial of class certification would sometimes end a lawsuit for all practical purposes\xe2\x80\x94did not warrant mandatory appellate jurisdiction of certification orders. Id., at 470, 477. Although the death-knell theory likely \xe2\x80\x9cenhanced the quality of justice afforded a few litigants,\xe2\x80\x9d it did so at a heavy cost to \xc2\xa71291\xe2\x80\x99s finality requirement. Id., at 473. First, the potential for multiple interlocutory appeals inhered in the doctrine. See id., at 474. Second, the death-knell theory forced appellate courts indiscriminately into the trial process, circumventing the two-tiered \xe2\x80\x9cscreening procedure\xe2\x80\x9d Congress established for interlocutory appeals in\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71292(b). Id., at 474, 476. Finally, the doctrine \xe2\x80\x9coperat[ed] only in favor of plaintiffs,\xe2\x80\x9d even though the class-certification question may be critically important to defendants as well. Id., at 476.

\n

Two decades later, in 1998, after Congress amended the Rules Enabling Act,\n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa72071 et seq., to empower this Court to promulgate rules providing for interlocutory appeal of orders \xe2\x80\x9cnot otherwise provided for [in \xc2\xa71292],\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71292(e), this Court approved Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(f). Rule 23(f) authorizes \xe2\x80\x9cpermissive interlocutory appeal\xe2\x80\x9d from adverse class-certification orders in \xe2\x80\x9cthe sole discretion of the court of appeals.\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 815. This discretionary arrangement was the product of careful calibration on the part of the rulemakers.

\n

Respondents, owners of Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s videogame console, the Xbox 360, filed this putative class action alleging a design defect in the device. The District Court struck respondents\xe2\x80\x99 class allegations from the complaint, and the Court of Appeals denied respondents permission to appeal that order under Rule 23(f). Instead of pursuing their individual claims to final judgment on the merits, respondents stipulated to a voluntary dismissal of their claims with prejudice, but reserved the right to revive their claims should the Court of Appeals reverse the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s certification denial. Respondents then appealed, challenging only the interlocutory order striking their class allegations. The Ninth Circuit held it had jurisdiction to entertain the appeal under \xc2\xa71291. It then held that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s rationale for striking respondents\xe2\x80\x99 class allegations was an impermissible one, but refused to opine on whether class certification was inappropriate for a different reason, leaving that question for the District Court on remand.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Federal courts of appeals lack jurisdiction under \xc2\xa71291 to review an order denying class certification (or, as here, an order striking class allegations) after the named plaintiffs have voluntarily dismissed their claims with prejudice. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Section 1291\xe2\x80\x99s final-judgment rule preserves the proper balance between trial and appellate courts, minimizes the harassment and delay that would result from repeated interlocutory appeals, and promotes the efficient administration of justice. This Court has resisted efforts to stretch \xc2\xa71291 to permit appeals of right that would erode the finality principle and disserve its objectives. See, e.g., Mohawk Industries, Inc. v. Carpenter,\n\n558 U.\xc2\xa0S. 100\n. Attempts to secure appeal as of right from adverse class certification orders fit that bill. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 voluntary-dismissal tactic, even more than the death-knell theory, invites protracted litigation and piecemeal appeals. Under the death-knell doctrine, a court of appeals could decline to hear an appeal if it determined that the plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] adequate incentive to continue\xe2\x80\x9d despite the denial of class certification. Coopers & Lybrand, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 471. Under respondents\xe2\x80\x99 theory, however, the decision whether an immediate appeal will lie resides exclusively with the plaintiff, who need only dismiss her claims with prejudice in order to appeal the district court\xe2\x80\x99s order denying class certification. And she may exercise that option more than once, interrupting district court proceedings with an interlocutory appeal again, should the court deny class certification on a different ground.

\n

Respondents contend that their position promotes efficiency, observing that after dismissal with prejudice the case is over if the plaintiff loses on appeal. But plaintiffs with weak merits claims may readily assume that risk, mindful that class certification often leads to a hefty settlement. And the same argument was evident in the death-knell context, yet this Court determined that the potential for piecemeal litigation was \xe2\x80\x9capparent and serious.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 474. That potential is greater still under respondents\xe2\x80\x99 theory, where plaintiffs alone determine whether and when to appeal an adverse certification ruling. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Also like the death-knell doctrine, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 theory allows indiscriminate appellate review of interlocutory orders. Beyond disturbing the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98appropriate relationship between the respective courts,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Coopers & Lybrand, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 476, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 dismissal tactic undercuts Rule 23(f)\xe2\x80\x99s discretionary regime. This consideration is \xe2\x80\x9c[o]f prime significance to the jurisdictional issue\xe2\x80\x9d in this case, Swint v. Chambers County Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n514 U. S. 35\n, because Congress has established rulemaking as the means for determining when a decision is final for purposes of \xc2\xa71291 and for providing for appellate review of interlocutory orders not covered by statute, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72072(c) and 1292(e).

\n

Respondents maintain that Rule 23(f) is irrelevant, for it concerns interlocutory orders, whereas this case involves an actual final judgment. Yet permitting respondents\xe2\x80\x99 voluntary-dismissal tactic to yield an appeal of right would seriously undermine Rule 23(f)\xe2\x80\x99s careful calibration, as well as Congress\xe2\x80\x99 designation of rulemaking \xe2\x80\x9cas the preferred means for determining whether and when prejudgment orders should be immediately appealable,\xe2\x80\x9d Mohawk Industries, 558 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 113. Plaintiffs in putative class actions cannot transform a tentative interlocutory order into a final judgment within the meaning of \xc2\xa71291 simply by dismissing their claims with prejudice. Finality \xe2\x80\x9cis not a technical concept of temporal or physical termination.\xe2\x80\x9d Cobbledick v. United States,\n\n309 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323\n. It is one \xe2\x80\x9cmeans [geared to] achieving a healthy legal system,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and its contours are determined accordingly. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0The one-sidedness of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 voluntary-dismissal device reinforces the conclusion that it does not support mandatory appellate jurisdiction of refusals to grant class certification. The tactic permits only plaintiffs, never defendants, to force an immediate appeal of an adverse certification ruling. Yet the \xe2\x80\x9cclass issue\xe2\x80\x9d may be just as important to defendants, Coopers & Lybrand, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 476, for class certification may force a defendant to settle rather than run the risk of ruinous liability. P.\xc2\xa017.

\n

797 F.\xc2\xa03d 607, reversed and remanded.

\n

MICROSOFT CORPORATION, PETITIONER v.SETH BAKER, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 12, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case concerns options open to plaintiffs, when denied class-action certification by a district court, to gain appellate review of the district court\xe2\x80\x99s order. Orders granting or denying class certification, this Court has held, are \xe2\x80\x9cinherently interlocutory,\xe2\x80\x9d Coopers & Lybrand v. Livesay,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 463,\n470 (1978)\n, hence not immediately reviewable under\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71291, which provides for appeals from \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Pursuant to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(f), promulgated in 1998, however, orders denying or granting class certification may be appealed immediately if the court of appeals so permits. Absent such permission, plaintiffs may pursue their individual claims on the merits to final judgment, at which point the denial of class-action certification becomes ripe for review.

\n

The plaintiffs in the instant case, respondents here, were denied Rule 23(f) permission to appeal the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to grant class certification. Instead of pursuing their individual claims to final judgment on the merits, respondents stipulated to a voluntary dismissal of their claims \xe2\x80\x9cwith prejudice,\xe2\x80\x9d but reserved the right to revive their claims should the Court of Appeals reverse the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s certification denial.

\n

We hold that the voluntary dismissal essayed by respondents does not qualify as a \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decision\xe2\x80\x9d within the compass of \xc2\xa71291. The tactic would undermine \xc2\xa71291\xe2\x80\x99s firm finality principle, designed to guard against piecemeal appeals, and subvert the balanced solution Rule 23(f\xc2\xa0) put in place for immediate review of class-action orders.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Under \xc2\xa71291 of the Judicial Code, federal courts of appeals are empowered to review only \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decisions of the district courts.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71291.[1] Two guides, our decision in Coopers & Lybrand v. Livesay,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 463 (1978)\n, and Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(f), control our application of that finality rule here.

\n

1

\n

In Coopers & Lybrand, this Court considered whether a plaintiff in a putative class action may, under certain circumstances, appeal as of right a district court order striking class allegations or denying a motion for class certification. We held unanimously that the so-called \xe2\x80\x9cdeath-knell\xe2\x80\x9d doctrine did not warrant mandatory appellate jurisdiction of such \xe2\x80\x9cinherently interlocutory\xe2\x80\x9d orders. 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 470, 477. Courts of Appeals employing the doctrine \xe2\x80\x9cregarded [their] jurisdiction as depending on whether [rejection of class-action status] had sounded the \xe2\x80\x98death knell\xe2\x80\x99 of the action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 466. These courts asked whether the refusal to certify a class would end a lawsuit for all practical purposes because the value of the named plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s individual claims made it \xe2\x80\x9ceconomically imprudent to pursue his lawsuit to a final judgment and [only] then seek appellate review of [the] adverse class determination.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 469\xe2\x80\x93470. If, in the court of appeals\xe2\x80\x99 view, the order would terminate the litigation, the court deemed the order an appealable final decision under \xc2\xa71291. Id., at 471. If, instead, the court determined that the plaintiff had \xe2\x80\x9cadequate incentive to continue [litigating], the order [was] considered interlocutory.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Consequently, immediate appeal would be denied.

\n

The death-knell theory likely \xe2\x80\x9cenhance[d] the quality of justice afforded a few litigants,\xe2\x80\x9d we recognized. Id., at 473. But the theory did so, we observed, at a heavy cost to \xc2\xa71291\xe2\x80\x99s finality requirement, and therefore to \xe2\x80\x9cthe judicial system\xe2\x80\x99s overall capacity to administer justice.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 473; see id., at 471 (Section 1291 \xe2\x80\x9cevinces a legislative judgment that \xe2\x80\x98restricting appellate review to final decisions prevents the debilitating effect on judicial administration caused by piecemeal appeal disposition.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Eisen v. Carlisle & Jacquelin,\n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 156,\n170 (1974)\n (alterations and internal quotation marks omitted))). First, the potential for multiple interlocutory appeals inhered in the doctrine: When a ruling denying class certification on one ground was reversed on appeal, a death-knell plaintiff might again claim \xe2\x80\x9centitle[ment] to an appeal as a matter of right\xe2\x80\x9d if, on remand, the district court denied class certification on a different ground. Coopers & Lybrand, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 474.

\n

Second, the doctrine forced appellate courts indiscriminately into the trial process, thereby defeating a \xe2\x80\x9cvital purpose of the final-judgment rule\xe2\x80\x94that of maintaining the appropriate relationship between the respective courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 476 (internal quotation marks omitted); see id., at 474. The Interlocutory Appeals Act of 1958,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71292(b), we explained, had created a two-tiered \xe2\x80\x9cscreening procedure\xe2\x80\x9d to preserve this relationship and to restrict the availability of interlocutory review to \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate cases.\xe2\x80\x9d 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 474. For a party to obtain review under \xc2\xa71292(b), the district court must certify that the interlocutory order \xe2\x80\x9cinvolves a controlling question of law as to which there is substantial ground for difference of opinion and that an immediate appeal from the order may materially advance the ultimate termination of the litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d The court of appeals may then, \xe2\x80\x9cin its discretion, permit an appeal to be taken from such order.\xe2\x80\x9d The death-knell doctrine, we stressed, \xe2\x80\x9ccircumvent[ed] [\xc2\xa71292(b)\xe2\x80\x99s] restrictions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 475.

\n

Finally, we observed, the doctrine was one sided: It \xe2\x80\x9coperate[d] only in favor of plaintiffs,\xe2\x80\x9d even though the class-certification question is often \xe2\x80\x9cof critical importance to defendants as well.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 476. Just as a denial of class certification may sound the death knell for plaintiffs, \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ertification of a large class may so increase the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s potential damages liability and litigation costs that he may find it economically prudent to settle and to abandon a meritorious defense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[2]

\n

In view of these concerns, the Court reached this conclusion in Coopers & Lybrand: \xe2\x80\x9cThe fact that an interlocutory order may induce a party to abandon his claim before final judgment is not a sufficient reason for considering [the order] a \xe2\x80\x98final decision\xe2\x80\x99 within the meaning of \xc2\xa71291.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 477.[3]

\n

2

\n

After Coopers & Lybrand, a party seeking immediate review of an adverse class-certification order had no easy recourse. The Federal Rules of Civil Procedure did not then \xe2\x80\x9ccontain any unique provisions governing appeals\xe2\x80\x9d in class actions, id., at 470, so parties had to survive \xc2\xa71292(b)\xe2\x80\x99s two-level inspection, see id., at 474\xe2\x80\x93475, and n.\xc2\xa027; supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934, or satisfy the extraordinary-circumstances test applicable to writs of mandamus, see Will v. United States,\n\n389 U.\xc2\xa0S. 90,\n108 (1967)\n (Black, J., concurring) (\xe2\x80\x9c[In] extraordinary circumstances, mandamus may be used to review an interlocutory order which is by no means \xe2\x80\x98final\xe2\x80\x99 and thus appealable under federal statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Coopers & Lybrand, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 466, n.\xc2\xa06.

\n

Another avenue opened in 1998 when this Court approved Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(f). Seen as a response to Coopers & Lybrand, see, e.g., Blair v. Equifax Check Services, Inc., 181 F.\xc2\xa03d 832, 834 (CA7 1999); Solimine & Hines, Deciding To Decide: Class Action Certification and Interlocutory Review by the United States Courts of Appeals Under Rule 23(f), 41 Wm. & Mary L. Rev. 1531, 1568 (2000), Rule 23(f) authorizes \xe2\x80\x9cpermissive interlocutory appeal\xe2\x80\x9d from adverse class-certification orders in the discretion of the court of appeals, Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s 1998 Note on subd. (f) of Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 23, 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 815 (hereinafter Committee Note on Rule 23(f)). The Rule was adopted pursuant to \xc2\xa71292(e), see Committee Note on Rule 23(f), which empowers this Court, in accordance with the Rules Enabling Act,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72072, to promulgate rules \xe2\x80\x9cto provide for an appeal of an interlocutory decision to the courts of appeals that is not otherwise provided for [in \xc2\xa71292].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71292(e).[4] Rule 23(f) reads:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA court of appeals may permit an appeal from an order granting or denying class-action certification .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. if a petition for permission to appeal is filed with the circuit clerk within 14 days after the order is entered. An appeal does not stay proceedings in the district court unless the district judge or the court of appeals so orders.\xe2\x80\x9d[5]

\n

Courts of appeals wield \xe2\x80\x9cunfettered discretion\xe2\x80\x9d under Rule 23(f), akin to the discretion afforded circuit courts under \xc2\xa71292(b). Committee Note on Rule 23(f). But Rule 23(f) otherwise \xe2\x80\x9cdeparts from the \xc2\xa71292(b) model,\xe2\x80\x9d for it requires neither district court certification nor adherence to \xc2\xa71292(b)\xe2\x80\x99s other \xe2\x80\x9climiting requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d Committee Note on Rule 23(f); see supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

This resolution was the product of careful calibration. By \xe2\x80\x9c[r]emoving the power of the district court to defeat any opportunity to appeal,\xe2\x80\x9d the drafters of Rule 23(f) sought to provide \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly greater protection against improvident certification decisions than \xc2\xa71292(b)\xe2\x80\x9d alone offered. Judicial Conference of the United States, Advisory Committee on Civil Rules, Minutes of November 9\xe2\x80\x9310, 1995. But the drafters declined to go further and provide for appeal as a matter of right. \xe2\x80\x9c[A] right to appeal would lead to abuse\xe2\x80\x9d on the part of plaintiffs and defendants alike, the drafters apprehended, \xe2\x80\x9cincreas[ing] delay and expense\xe2\x80\x9d over \xe2\x80\x9croutine class certification decisions\xe2\x80\x9d unworthy of immediate appeal. Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). See also Brief for Civil Procedure Scholars as Amici Curiae 6\xe2\x80\x937, 11\xe2\x80\x9314 (\xe2\x80\x9cRule 23(f) was crafted to balance the benefits of immediate review against the costs of interlocutory appeals.\xe2\x80\x9d (capitalization omitted)). Rule 23(f\xc2\xa0) therefore commits the decision whether to permit interlocutory appeal from an adverse certification decision to \xe2\x80\x9cthe sole discretion of the court of appeals.\xe2\x80\x9d Committee Note on Rule 23(f); see Federal Judicial Center, T. Willging, L. Hooper, & R. Niemic, Empirical Study of Class Actions in Four Federal District Courts: Final Report to the Advisory Committee on Civil Rules 86 (1996) (hereinafter Federal Judicial Center Study) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe discretionary nature of the proposed rule .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is designed to be a guard against abuse of the appellate process.\xe2\x80\x9d).[6]

\n

The Rules Committee offered some guidance to courts of appeals considering whether to authorize appeal under Rule 23(f). \xe2\x80\x9cPermission is most likely to be granted,\xe2\x80\x9d the Committee Note states, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen the certification decision turns on a novel or unsettled question of law,\xe2\x80\x9d or when \xe2\x80\x9cthe decision on certification is likely dispositive of the litigation,\xe2\x80\x9d as in a death-knell or reverse death-knell situation. Committee Note on Rule 23(f); see supra, at 4, and n.\xc2\xa02. Even so, the Rule allows courts of appeals to grant or deny review \xe2\x80\x9con the basis of any consideration.\xe2\x80\x9d Committee Note on Rule 23(f) (emphasis added).

\n

B

\n

With this background in mind, we turn to the putative class action underlying our jurisdictional inquiry. The lawsuit is not the first of its kind. A few years after petitioner Microsoft Corporation released its popular videogame console, the Xbox 360, a group of Xbox owners brought a putative class action against Microsoft based on an alleged design defect in the device. See In\xc2\xa0re Microsoft Xbox 360 Scratched Disc Litigation, 2009 WL 10219350, *1 (WD Wash., Oct. 5, 2009). The named plaintiffs, advised by some of the same counsel representing respondents in this case, asserted that the Xbox scratched (and thus destroyed) game discs during normal game-playing conditions. See ibid. The District Court denied class certification, holding that individual issues of damages and causation predominated over common issues. See id., at *6\xe2\x80\x93*7. The plaintiffs petitioned the Ninth Circuit under Rule 23(f) for leave to appeal the class-certification denial, but the Ninth Circuit denied the request. See 851 F.\xc2\xa0Supp.\xc2\xa02d 1274, 1276 (WD Wash. 2012). Thereafter, the Scratched Disc plaintiffs settled their claims individually. 851 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1276.

\n

Two years later, in 2011, respondents filed this lawsuit in the same Federal District Court. They proposed a nationwide class of Xbox owners based on the same design defect alleged in Scratched Disc Litigation. See 851 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1275\xe2\x80\x931276. The class-certification analysis in the earlier case did not control, respondents urged, because an intervening Ninth Circuit decision constituted a change in law sufficient to overcome the deference ordinarily due, as a matter of comity, the previous certification denial. Id., at 1277\xe2\x80\x931278. The District Court disagreed. Concluding that the relevant Circuit decision had not undermined Scratched Disc Litigation\xe2\x80\x99s causation analysis, the court determined that comity required adherence to the earlier certification denial and therefore struck respondents\xe2\x80\x99 class allegations. 851 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1280\xe2\x80\x931281.

\n

Invoking Rule 23(f), respondents petitioned the Ninth Circuit for permission to appeal that ruling.[7] Interlocu-tory review was appropriate in this case, they argued, be-cause the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order striking the class allegations created a \xe2\x80\x9cdeath-knell situation\xe2\x80\x9d: The \xe2\x80\x9csmall size of [their] claims ma[de] it economically irrational to bear the cost of litigating th[e] case to final judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d they asserted, so the order would \xe2\x80\x9ceffectively kil[l] the case.\xe2\x80\x9d Pet. for Permission To Appeal Under Rule 23(f) in No. 12\xe2\x80\x9380085 (CA9), App. 118. The Ninth Circuit denied the petition. Order in No. 12\xe2\x80\x9380085 (CA9, June 12, 2012), App. 121.

\n

Respondents then had several options. They could have settled their individual claims like their Scratched Disc predecessors or petitioned the District Court, pursuant to \xc2\xa71292(b), to certify the interlocutory order for appeal, see supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934. They could also have proceeded to litigate their case, mindful that the District Court could later reverse course and certify the proposed class. See Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 23(c)(1)(C) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn order that grants or denies class certification may be altered or amended before final judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d); Coopers & Lybrand, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 469 (a certification order \xe2\x80\x9cis subject to revision in the District Court\xe2\x80\x9d). Or, in the event the District Court did not change course, respondents could have litigated the case to final judgment and then appealed. Id., at 469 (\xe2\x80\x9can order denying class certification is subject to effective review after final judgment at the behest of the named plaintiff\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Instead of taking one of those routes, respondents moved to dismiss their case with prejudice. \xe2\x80\x9cAfter the [c]ourt has entered a final order and judgment,\xe2\x80\x9d respondents explained, they would \xe2\x80\x9cappeal the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. order striking [their] class allegations.\xe2\x80\x9d Motion To Dismiss in No. 11\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9300722 (WD Wash., Sept. 25, 2012), App. 122\xe2\x80\x93123. In respondents\xe2\x80\x99 view, the voluntary dismissal enabled them \xe2\x80\x9cto pursue their individual claims or to pursue relief solely on behalf of the class, should the certification decision be reversed.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 15. Microsoft stipulated to the dismissal, but maintained that respondents would have \xe2\x80\x9cno right to appeal\xe2\x80\x9d the order striking the class allegations after thus dismissing their claims. App. to Pet. for Cert. 35a\xe2\x80\x9336a. The District Court granted the stipulated motion to dismiss, id., at 39a, and respondents appealed. They challenged only the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s interlocutory order striking their class allegations, not the dismissal order which they invited. See Brief for Plaintiffs-Appellants in No. 12\xe2\x80\x9335946 (CA9).

\n

The Ninth Circuit held it had jurisdiction to entertain the appeal under \xc2\xa71291. 797 F.\xc2\xa03d 607, 612 (2015). The Court of Appeals rejected Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s argument that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 voluntary dismissal, explicitly engineered to appeal the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s interlocutory order striking the class allegations, impermissibly circumvented Rule 23(f). Ibid., n.\xc2\xa03. Because the stipulated dismissal \xe2\x80\x9cdid not involve a settlement,\xe2\x80\x9d the court reasoned, it was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a sufficiently adverse\xe2\x80\x94and thus appealable\xe2\x80\x94final decision\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa71291. Id., at 612 (quoting Berger v. Home Depot USA, Inc., 741 F.\xc2\xa03d 1061, 1065 (CA9 2014)); see id., at 1065 (relying on 7B C. Wright, A. Miller, & M. Kane, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa71802, pp. 297\xe2\x80\x93298 (3d ed. 2005), for the proposition \xe2\x80\x9cthat finality for appeal purposes can be achieved in this manner\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Satisfied of its jurisdiction, the Ninth Circuit held that the District Court had abused its discretion in striking respondents\xe2\x80\x99 class allegations. 797 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 615. The Court of Appeals \xe2\x80\x9cexpress[ed] no opinion on whether\xe2\x80\x9d respondents \xe2\x80\x9cshould prevail on a motion for class certification,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., concluding only that the District Court had misread recent Circuit precedent, see id., at 613\xe2\x80\x93615, and therefore misapplied the comity doctrine, id., at 615. Whether a class should be certified, the court said, was a question for remand, \xe2\x80\x9cbetter addressed if and when [respondents] move[d] for class certification.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

We granted certiorari to resolve a Circuit conflict over this question: Do federal courts of appeals have jurisdiction under \xc2\xa71291 and Article III of the Constitution to review an order denying class certification (or, as here, an order striking class allegations) after the named plaintiffs have voluntarily dismissed their claims with prejudice?[8] 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016). Because we hold that \xc2\xa71291 does not countenance jurisdiction by these means, we do not reach the constitutional question, and therefore do not address the arguments and analysis discussed in the opinion concurring in the judgment.

\n

II

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cFrom the very foundation of our judicial system,\xe2\x80\x9d the general rule has been that \xe2\x80\x9cthe whole case and every matter in controversy in it [must be] decided in a single appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d McLish v. Roff,\n\n141 U.\xc2\xa0S. 661\n\xe2\x80\x93666 (1891). This final-judgment rule, now codified in \xc2\xa71291, preserves the proper balance between trial and appellate courts, minimizes the harassment and delay that would result from repeated interlocutory appeals, and promotes the efficient administration of justice. See Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. v. Risjord,\n\n449 U.\xc2\xa0S. 368,\n374 (1981)\n.

\n

Construing \xc2\xa71291 in line with these reasons for the rule, we have recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cfinality is to be given a practical rather than a technical construction.\xe2\x80\x9d Eisen, 417 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 171 (internal quotation marks omitted). Repeatedly we have resisted efforts to stretch \xc2\xa71291 to permit appeals of right that would erode the finality principle and disserve its objectives. See, e.g., Mohawk Industries, Inc. v. Carpenter,\n\n558 U.\xc2\xa0S. 100,\n112 (2009)\n; Digital Equipment Corp. v. Desktop Direct, Inc.,\n\n511 U.\xc2\xa0S. 863\n\xe2\x80\x93879, 884 (1994); Cobbledick v. United States,\n\n309 U.\xc2\xa0S. 323\n\xe2\x80\x93325, 330 (1940) (construing \xc2\xa71291\xe2\x80\x99s predecessor statute). Attempts to secure appeal as of right from adverse class-certification orders fit that bill. See supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x934. Because respondents\xe2\x80\x99 dismissal device subverts the final-judgment rule and the process Congress has established for refining that rule and for determining when nonfinal orders may be immediately appealed, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72072(c) and 1292(e), the tactic does not give rise to a \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decisio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa71291.

\n

A

\n

Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 voluntary-dismissal tactic, even more than the death-knell theory, invites protracted litigation and piecemeal appeals. Under the death-knell doctrine, a court of appeals could decline to hear an appeal if it determined that the plaintiff \xe2\x80\x9cha[d] adequate incentive to continue\xe2\x80\x9d despite the denial of class certification. Coopers & Lybrand, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 471. Appellate courts lack even that authority under respondents\xe2\x80\x99 theory. Instead, the decision whether an immediate appeal will lie resides exclusively with the plaintiff; she need only dismiss her claims with prejudice, whereupon she may appeal the district court\xe2\x80\x99s order denying class certification. And, as under the death-knell doctrine, she may exercise that option more than once, stopping and starting the district court proceedings with repeated interlocutory appeals. See id., at 474 (death-knell doctrine offered \xe2\x80\x9cno assurance that the trial process [would] not again be disrupted by interlocutory review\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

Consider this case. The Ninth Circuit reviewed and rejected only the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s application of comity as a basis for striking respondents\xe2\x80\x99 class allegations. 797 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 615. The appeals court declined to reach Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s other arguments against class certification. See ibid. It remained open to the District Court, in the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 view, to deny class certification on a differ-ent ground, and respondents would be free, under their theory, to force appellate review of any new order denying certification by again dismissing their claims. In designing Rule 23(f)\xe2\x80\x99s provision for discretionary review, the Rules Committee sought to prevent such disruption and delay. See supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x938.[9]

\n

Respondents nevertheless maintain that their position promotes efficiency, observing that after dismissal with prejudice the case is over if the plaintiff loses on appeal. Brief for Respondents 38\xe2\x80\x9339. Their way, they say, means prompt resolution of many lawsuits and infrequent use of the voluntary-dismissal tactic, for \xe2\x80\x9cmost appeals lose\xe2\x80\x9d and few plaintiffs will \xe2\x80\x9ctake th[e] risk\xe2\x80\x9d of losing their claims for good. Id., at 35\xe2\x80\x9336. Respondents overlook the prospect that plaintiffs with weak merits claims may readily assume that risk, mindful that class certification often leads to a hefty settlement. See Coopers & Lybrand, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 476 (defendant facing the specter of classwide liability may \xe2\x80\x9cabandon a meritorious defense\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, the same argument\xe2\x80\x94that the case was over if the plaintiff lost on appeal\xe2\x80\x94was evident in the death-knell context, yet this Court determined that the potential for piecemeal litigation was \xe2\x80\x9capparent and serious.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 474.[10] And that potential is greater still under respondents\xe2\x80\x99 theory, where plaintiffs alone determine whether and when to appeal an adverse certification ruling.

\n

B

\n

Another vice respondents\xe2\x80\x99 theory shares with the death-knell doctrine, both allow indiscriminate appellate review of interlocutory orders. Ibid. Beyond disturbing the \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate relationship between the respective courts,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 476 (internal quotation marks omitted), respondents\xe2\x80\x99 dismissal tactic undercuts Rule 23(f)\xe2\x80\x99s discretionary regime. This consideration is \xe2\x80\x9c[o]f prime significance to the jurisdictional issue before us.\xe2\x80\x9d Swint v. Chambers County Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 35,\n46 (1995)\n (pendent appellate jurisdiction in collateral-order context would undermine \xc2\xa71292(b)); see supra, at 3\xe2\x80\x934 (death-knell doctrine impermissibly circumvented \xc2\xa71292(b)).

\n

In the Rules Enabling Act, as earlier recounted, Congress authorized this Court to determine when a decision is final for purposes of \xc2\xa71291, and to provide for appellate review of interlocutory orders not covered by statute. See supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, and n.\xc2\xa04. These changes are to come from rulemaking, however, not judicial decisions in particular controversies or inventive litigation ploys. See Swint, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 48. In this case, the rulemaking process has dealt with the matter, yielding a \xe2\x80\x9cmeasured, practical solutio[n]\xe2\x80\x9d to the questions whether and when adverse certification orders may be immediately appealed. Mohawk Industries, 558 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 114. Over years the Advisory Committee on the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure studied the data on class-certification rulings and appeals, weighed various proposals, received public comment, and refined the draft rule and Committee Note. See Solimine & Hines, 41 Wm. & Mary L. Rev., at 1564\xe2\x80\x931566, and nn. 178\xe2\x80\x93189; Federal Judicial Center Study 80\xe2\x80\x9387. Rule 23(f) reflects the rulemakers\xe2\x80\x99 informed assessment, permitting, as explained supra, at 5\xe2\x80\x937, interlocutory appeals of adverse certification orders, whether sought by plaintiffs or defendants, solely in the discretion of the courts of appeals. That assessment \xe2\x80\x9cwarrants the Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99s full respect.\xe2\x80\x9d Swint, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 48; see Mohawk Industries, 558 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 118\xe2\x80\x93119 (Thomas, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment).

\n

Here, however, the Ninth Circuit, after denying respondents permission to appeal under Rule 23(f), nevertheless assumed jurisdiction of their appeal challenging only the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order striking the class allegations. See supra, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310. According to respondents, even plaintiffs who altogether bypass Rule 23(f) may force an appeal by dismissing their claims with prejudice. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 34. Rule 23(f), respondents say, is irrelevant, for it \xe2\x80\x9caddress[es] interlocutory orders,\xe2\x80\x9d whereas this case involves \xe2\x80\x9can actual final judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 26, 28.

\n

We are not persuaded. If respondents\xe2\x80\x99 voluntary-dismissal tactic could yield an appeal of right, Rule 23(f)\xe2\x80\x99s careful calibration\xe2\x80\x94as well as Congress\xe2\x80\x99 designation of rulemaking \xe2\x80\x9cas the preferred means for determining whether and when prejudgment orders should be immediately appealable,\xe2\x80\x9d Mohawk Industries, 558 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 113 (majority opinion)\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cwould be severely undermined,\xe2\x80\x9d Swint, 514 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 47. Respondents, after all, \xe2\x80\x9c[sought] review of only the [inherently interlocutory] orde[r]\xe2\x80\x9d striking their class allegations; they \xe2\x80\x9cd[id] not complain of the \xe2\x80\x98final\xe2\x80\x99 orde[r] that dismissed their cas[e].\xe2\x80\x9d Camesi v. University of Pittsburgh Medical Center, 729 F.\xc2\xa03d 239, 244 (CA3 2013).

\n

Plaintiffs in putative class actions cannot transform a tentative interlocutory order, see supra, at 9, into a final judgment within the meaning of \xc2\xa71291 simply by dismissing their claims with prejudice\xe2\x80\x94subject, no less, to the right to \xe2\x80\x9crevive\xe2\x80\x9d those claims if the denial of class certification is reversed on appeal, see Brief for Respondents 45; Tr. of Oral Arg. 31 (assertion by respondents\xe2\x80\x99 counsel that, if the appeal succeeds, \xe2\x80\x9ceverything would spring back to life\xe2\x80\x9d on remand). Were respondents\xe2\x80\x99 reasoning embraced by this Court, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress[\xe2\x80\x99] final decision rule would end up a pretty puny one.\xe2\x80\x9d Digital Equipment Corp., 511 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 872. Contrary to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 argument, \xc2\xa71291\xe2\x80\x99s firm final-judgment rule is not satisfied whenever a litigant persuades a district court to issue an order purporting to end the litigation. Finality, we have long cautioned, \xe2\x80\x9cis not a technical concept of temporal or physical termination.\xe2\x80\x9d Cobbledick, 309 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 326. It is one \xe2\x80\x9cmeans [geared to] achieving a healthy legal system,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., and its contours are determined accordingly, see supra, at 12.[11]

\n

C

\n

The one-sidedness of respondents\xe2\x80\x99 voluntary-dismissal device \xe2\x80\x9creinforce[s] our conclusion that [it] does not support appellate jurisdiction of prejudgment orders denying class certification.\xe2\x80\x9d Coopers & Lybrand, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 476; see supra, at 4. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 theory permits plaintiffs only, never defendants, to force an immediate appeal of an adverse certification ruling. Yet the \xe2\x80\x9cclass issue\xe2\x80\x9d may be just as important to defendants, Coopers & Lybrand, 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 476, for \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n order granting certification .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. may force a defendant to settle rather than .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. run the risk of potentially ruinous liability,\xe2\x80\x9d Committee Note on Rule 23(f); see supra, at 4, and n.\xc2\xa02 (defendants may face a \xe2\x80\x9creverse death knell\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, we recognized in Coopers & Lybrand that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hatever similarities or differences there are between plaintiffs and defendants in this context involve questions of policy for Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d 437 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 476. Congress chose the rulemaking process to settle the matter, and the rulemakers did so by adopting Rule 23(f)\xe2\x80\x99s evenhanded prescription. It is not the prerogative of litigants or federal courts to disturb that settlement. See supra, at 14\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

For the reasons stated, the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.

MICROSOFT CORPORATION, PETITIONER v.SETH BAKER, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 12, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, with whom The Chief Justice and Justice Alito join, concurring in the judgment.

\n

I agree with the Court that the Court of Appeals lacked jurisdiction over respondents\xe2\x80\x99 appeal, but I would ground that conclusion in Article III of the Constitution instead of\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71291. I therefore concur only in the judgment.

\n

The plaintiffs in this case, respondents here, sued Microsoft, petitioner here, to recover damages after they purchased allegedly faulty video game consoles that Microsoft manufactured. The plaintiffs brought claims for themselves (individual claims) and on behalf of a putative class of similarly situated consumers (class allegations). Early in the litigation, the District Court granted Microsoft\xe2\x80\x99s motion to strike the class allegations, effectively declining to certify the class. The Court of Appeals denied permission to appeal that decision under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 23(f), which requires a party to obtain permission from the court of appeals before appealing a decision regarding class certification.

\n

The plaintiffs decided not to pursue their individual claims, instead stipulating to a voluntary dismissal of those claims with prejudice. They then filed a notice of appeal from the voluntary dismissal order. On appeal, they did not ask the Court of Appeals to reverse the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s dismissal of their individual claims. They instead asked the Court of Appeals to reverse the order striking their class allegations. The question presented in this case is whether the Court of Appeals had jurisdiction to hear the appeal under both \xc2\xa71291, which grants appellate jurisdiction to the courts of appeals over \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decisions\xe2\x80\x9d by district courts, and under Article III of the Constitution, which limits the jurisdiction of federal courts to \xe2\x80\x9ccases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversies.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The Court today holds that the Court of Appeals lacked jurisdiction under \xc2\xa71291 because the voluntary dismissal with prejudice did not result in a \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decision.\xe2\x80\x9d I dis-agree with that holding. A decision is \xe2\x80\x9cfinal\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of \xc2\xa71291 if it \xe2\x80\x9cends the litigation on the merits and leaves nothing for the court to do but execute the judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Catlin v. United States,\n\n324 U.\xc2\xa0S. 229,\n233 (1945)\n. The order here dismissed all of the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claims with prejudice and left nothing for the District Court to do but execute the judgment. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 39a (\xe2\x80\x9cdirect[ing] the Clerk to enter Judgment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and close th[e] case\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The Court reaches the opposite conclusion, relying not on the text of \xc2\xa71291 or this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents about finality, but on Rule 23(f). Rule 23(f) makes interlocutory orders regarding class certification appealable only with the permission of the court of appeals. The Court concludes that the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cvoluntary dismissal\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not qualify as a \xe2\x80\x98final decision\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d because allowing the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 appeal would \xe2\x80\x9csubvert the balanced solution Rule 23(f) put in place for immediate review of class-action orders.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2.

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion does not follow from its reasoning. Whether a dismissal with prejudice is \xe2\x80\x9cfinal\xe2\x80\x9d depends on the meaning of \xc2\xa71291, not Rule 23(f). Rule 23(f) says nothing about finality, much less about the finality of an order dismissing individual claims with prejudice. I agree with the Court that the plaintiffs are trying to avoid the requirements for interlocutory appeals under Rule 23(f), but our view of the balance struck in that rule should not warp our understanding of finality under \xc2\xa71291.

\n

Although I disagree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xa71291, I agree that the plaintiffs could not appeal in these circumstances. In my view, they could not appeal because the Court of Appeals lacked jurisdiction under Article III of the Constitution. The \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial Power\xe2\x80\x9d of the United States extends only to \xe2\x80\x9cCases\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cControversies.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa72. This requirement limits the jurisdiction of the federal courts to issues presented \xe2\x80\x9cin an adversary context,\xe2\x80\x9d Flast v. Cohen,\n\n392 U.\xc2\xa0S. 83,\n95 (1968)\n, in which the parties maintain an \xe2\x80\x9cactual\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cconcrete\xe2\x80\x9d interest, Campbell-Ewald Co. v. Gomez, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2016) (slip op., at 6) (internal quotation marks omitted). Put another way, \xe2\x80\x9cArticle III denies federal courts the power to decide questions that cannot affect the rights of litigants in the case before them, and confines them to resolving real and substantial controversies admitting of specific relief through a decree of a conclusive character.\xe2\x80\x9d Lewis v. Continental Bank Corp.,\n\n494 U.\xc2\xa0S. 472,\n477 (1990)\n (internal quotation marks, citation, and alteration omitted).

\n

The plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 appeal from their voluntary dismissal did not satisfy this jurisdictional requirement. When the plaintiffs asked the District Court to dismiss their claims, they consented to the judgment against them and dis-avowed any right to relief from Microsoft. The parties thus were no longer adverse to each other on any claims, and the Court of Appeals could not \xe2\x80\x9caffect the[ir] rights\xe2\x80\x9d in any legally cognizable manner. Ibid. Indeed, it has long been the rule that a party may not appeal from the voluntary dismissal of a claim, since the party consented to the judgment against it. See, e.g., Evans v. Phillips, 4 Wheat. 73 (1819); Lord v. Veazie, 8 How. 251, 255\xe2\x80\x93256 (1850); United States v. Babbitt,\n\n104 U.\xc2\xa0S. 767 (1882)\n; Deakins v. Monaghan,\n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193\n\xe2\x80\x93200 (1988).

\n

The plaintiffs contend that their interest in reversing the order striking their class allegations is sufficient to satisfy Article III\xe2\x80\x99s case-or-controversy requirement, but they misunderstand the status of putative class actions. Class allegations, without an underlying individual claim, do not give rise to a \xe2\x80\x9ccase\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccontroversy.\xe2\x80\x9d Those allegations are simply the means of invoking a procedural mechanism that enables a plaintiff to litigate his individual claims on behalf of a class. See Shady Grove Orthopedic Associates, P.\xc2\xa0A. v. Allstate Ins. Co.,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 393,\n408 (2010)\n (plurality opinion). Thus, because the Court of Appeals lacked Article III jurisdiction to adjudicate the individual claims, it could not hear the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 appeal of the order striking their class allegations.

\n

Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 representation that they hope to \xe2\x80\x9crevive their [individual] claims should they prevail\xe2\x80\x9d on the appeal of the order striking their class allegations does not undermine this conclusion. Brief for Respondents 45. This Court has interpreted Article III \xe2\x80\x9cto demand that an ac-tual controversy be extant at all stages of review, not merely at the time the complaint is filed.\xe2\x80\x9d Campbell Ewald Co., supra, at ___ (slip op., at 6) (internal quotation marks and alterations omitted). And in any event, a favorable ruling on class certification would not \xe2\x80\x9crevive\xe2\x80\x9d their individual claims: A court\xe2\x80\x99s decision about class allegations \xe2\x80\x9cin no way touch[es] the merits\xe2\x80\x9d of those claims. Gardner v. Westinghouse Broadcasting Co.,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 478,\n482 (1978)\n.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Because I would hold that the Court of Appeals lacked jurisdiction under Article III to consider respondents\xe2\x80\x99 appeal, I concur in the judgment.
