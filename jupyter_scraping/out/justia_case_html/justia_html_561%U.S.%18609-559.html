\nOCTOBER TERM, 2009
\nDOE V. REED
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

DOE et\xc2\xa0al. v. REED, WASHINGTON SECRETARY OF STATE, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x93559.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 28, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 24, 2010

\n

The Washington Constitution allows citizens to challenge state laws by referendum. To initiate a referendum, proponents must file a petition with the secretary of state that contains valid signatures of registered Washington voters equal to or exceeding four percent of the votes cast for the office of Governor at the last gubernatorial election. A valid submission requires not only a signature, but also the signer\xe2\x80\x99s address and the county in which he is registered to vote.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In May 2009, Washington Governor Christine Gregoire signed into law Senate Bill 5688, which expanded the rights and responsibilities of state-registered domestic partners, including same-sex domestic partners. That same month, Protect Marriage Washington, one of the petitioners here, was organized as a \xe2\x80\x9cState Political Committee\xe2\x80\x9d for the purpose of collecting the petition signatures necessary to place a referendum challenging SB 5688 on the ballot. If the referendum made it onto the ballot, Protect Marriage Washington planned to encourage voters to reject SB 5688. Protect Marriage Washington submitted the petition with more than 137,000 signatures to the secretary of state, and after conducting the verification and canvassing process required by state law, the secretary determined that the petition contained sufficient signatures to qualify the referendum (R\xe2\x80\x9371) for the ballot. Respondent intervenors invoked the Washington Public Records Act (PRA) to obtain copies of the petition, which contained the signers\xe2\x80\x99 names and addresses.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The R\xe2\x80\x9371 petition sponsor and certain signers filed a complaint and a motion for injunctive relief in Federal District Court, seeking to enjoin the public release of the petition. Count I alleges that the PRA \xe2\x80\x9cis unconstitutional as applied to referendum petitions,\xe2\x80\x9d and Count II alleges that the PRA \xe2\x80\x9cis unconstitutional as applied to the Referendum 71 petition because there is a reasonable probability that the signatories \xe2\x80\xa6 will be subjected to threats, harassment, and reprisals.\xe2\x80\x9d Determining that the PRA burdened core political speech, the District Court held that plaintiffs were likely to succeed on the merits of Count I and granted a preliminary injunction preventing release of the signatory information. Reviewing only Count I, the Ninth Circuit held that plaintiffs were unlikely to succeed on their claim that the PRA is unconstitutional as applied to referendum petitions in general, and therefore reversed.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Disclosure of referendum petitions does not as a general matter violate the First Amendment. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Because plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 Count I claim and the relief that would follow\xe2\x80\x94an injunction barring the secretary of state from releasing referendum petitions to the public\xe2\x80\x94reach beyond the particular circumstances of these plaintiffs, they must satisfy this Court\xe2\x80\x99s standards for a facial challenge to the extent of that reach. See United States v. Stevens, 559 U. S. ___, ___. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The compelled disclosure of signatory information on referendum petitions is subject to review under the First Amendment. In most cases, the individual\xe2\x80\x99s signature will express the view that the law subject to the petition should be overturned. Even if the signer is agnostic as to the merits of the underlying law, his signature still expresses the political view that the question should be considered \xe2\x80\x9cby the whole electorate.\xe2\x80\x9d Meyer v. Grant, 486 U. S. 414, 421. In either case, the expression of a political view implicates a First Amendment right.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petition signing remains expressive even when it has legal effect in the electoral process. But that does not mean that the electoral context is irrelevant to the nature of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s First Amendment review. States have significant flexibility in implementing their own voting systems. To the extent a regulation concerns the legal effect of a particular activity in that process, the government is afforded substantial latitude to enforce that regulation. Also pertinent is the fact that the PRA is not a prohibition on speech, but a disclosure requirement that may burden \xe2\x80\x9cthe ability to speak, but [does] \xe2\x80\x98not prevent anyone from speaking.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens United v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 558 U. S. ___, ___. This Court has reviewed First Amendment challenges to disclosure requirements in the electoral context under an \xe2\x80\x9cexacting scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d standard, requiring \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98substantial relation\xe2\x80\x99 between the disclosure requirement and a \xe2\x80\x98sufficiently important\xe2\x80\x99 governmental interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___. To withstand this scrutiny, \xe2\x80\x9cthe strength of the governmental interest must reflect the seriousness of the actual burden on First Amendment rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 554 U. S. ___, ___. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in preserving the integrity of the electoral process suffices to defeat the argument that the PRA is unconstitutional with respect to referendum petitions in general. That interest is particularly strong with respect to efforts to root out fraud. But the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest is not limited to combating fraud; it extends to efforts to ferret out invalid signatures caused not by fraud but by simple mistake, such as duplicate signatures or signatures of individuals who are not registered to vote in the State. The State\xe2\x80\x99s interest also extends more generally to promoting transparency and accountability in the electoral process.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs contend that disclosure is not sufficiently related to the interest of protecting the integrity of the electoral process to withstand First Amendment scrutiny. They argue that disclosure is not necessary because the secretary of state is already charged with verifying and canvassing the names on a petition, a measure\xe2\x80\x99s advocates and opponents can observe that process, any citizen can challenge the secretary\xe2\x80\x99s actions in court, and criminal penalties reduce the danger of fraud in the petition process. But the secretary\xe2\x80\x99s verification and canvassing will not catch all the invalid signatures, and public disclosure can help cure the inadequacies of the secretary\xe2\x80\x99s process. Disclosure also helps prevent difficult-to-detect fraud such as outright forgery and \xe2\x80\x9cbait and switch\xe2\x80\x9d fraud, in which an individual signs the petition based on a misrepresentation of the underlying issue. And disclosure promotes transparency and accountability in the electoral process to an extent other measures cannot. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 main objection is that \xe2\x80\x9cthe strength of the governmental interest\xe2\x80\x9d does not \xe2\x80\x9creflect the seriousness of the actual burden on First Amendment rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis, supra, at ___. According to plaintiffs, the objective of those seeking disclosure is not to prevent fraud, but to publicly identify signatories and broadcast their political views on the subject of the petition. Plaintiffs allege, for example, that several groups plan to post the petitions in searchable form on the Internet, and then encourage other citizens to seek out R\xe2\x80\x9371 petition signers. That, plaintiffs argue, would subject them to threats, harassment, and reprisals.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The problem for plaintiffs is that their argument rests almost entirely on the specific harm that would attend the disclosure of information on the R\xe2\x80\x9371 petition. But the question before the Court at this stage of the litigation is whether disclosure of referendum petitions in general violates the First Amendment. Faced with the State\xe2\x80\x99s unrebutted arguments that only modest burdens attend the disclosure of a typical petition, plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 broad challenge to the PRA must be rejected. But upholding the PRA against a broad-based challenge does not foreclose success on plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 narrower challenge in Count II, which is pending before the District Court. See Buckley v. Valeo, 424 U. S. 1, 74. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

JOHN DOE #1, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. SAM REED,\nWASHINGTON SECRETARY OF STATE, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 24, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State of Washington allows its citizens to challenge state laws by referendum. Roughly four percent of Washington voters must sign a petition to place such a referendum on the ballot. That petition, which by law must include the names and addresses of the signers, is then submitted to the government for verification and canvassing, to ensure that only lawful signatures are counted. The Washington Public Records Act (PRA) authorizes private parties to obtain copies of government documents, and the State construes the PRA to cover submitted referendum petitions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case arises out of a state law extending certain benefits to same-sex couples, and a corresponding referendum petition to put that law to a popular vote. Respondent intervenors invoked the PRA to obtain copies of the petition, with the names and addresses of the signers. Certain petition signers and the petition sponsor objected, arguing that such public disclosure would violate their rights under the First Amendment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The course of this litigation, however, has framed the legal question before us more broadly. The issue at this stage of the case is not whether disclosure of this particular petition would violate the First Amendment, but whether disclosure of referendum petitions in general would do so. We conclude that such disclosure does not as a general matter violate the First Amendment, and we therefore affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals. We leave it to the lower courts to consider in the first instance the signers\xe2\x80\x99 more focused claim concerning disclosure of the information on this particular petition, which is pending before the District Court.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Washington Constitution reserves to the people the power to reject any bill, with a few limited exceptions not relevant here, through the referendum process. Wash. Const., Art. II, \xc2\xa71(b). To initiate a referendum, proponents must file a petition with the secretary of state that contains valid signatures of registered Washington voters equal to or exceeding four percent of the votes cast for the office of Governor at the last gubernatorial election. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71(b), (d). A valid submission requires not only a signature, but also the signer\xe2\x80\x99s address and the county in which he is registered to vote. Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa729A.72.130 (2008).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In May 2009, Washington Governor Christine Gregoire signed into law Senate Bill 5688, which \xe2\x80\x9cexpand[ed] the rights and responsibilities\xe2\x80\x9d of state-registered domestic partners, including same-sex domestic partners. 586 F.\xc2\xa03d 671, 675 (CA9 2009). That same month, Protect Marriage Washington, one of the petitioners here, was organized as a \xe2\x80\x9cState Political Committee\xe2\x80\x9d for the purpose of collecting the petition signatures necessary to place a referendum on the ballot, which would give the voters themselves an opportunity to vote on SB 5688. App. 8\xe2\x80\x939. If the referendum made it onto the ballot, Protect Marriage Washington planned to encourage voters to reject SB 5688. Id., at 7, 9.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On July 25, 2009, Protect Marriage Washington submitted to the secretary of state a petition containing over 137,000 signatures. See 586 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 675; Brief for Respondent Washington Families Standing Together 6. The secretary of state then began the verification and canvassing process, as required by Washington law, to ensure that only legal signatures were counted. Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa729A.72.230. Some 120,000 valid signatures were required to place the referendum on the ballot. Sam Reed, Washington Secretary of State, Certification of Referendum 71 (Sept. 2, 2009). The secretary of state determined that the petition contained a sufficient number of valid signatures, and the referendum (R\xe2\x80\x9371) appeared on the November 2009 ballot. The voters approved SB 5688 by a margin of 53% to 47%.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The PRA, Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa742.56.001 et seq., makes all \xe2\x80\x9cpublic records\xe2\x80\x9d available for public inspection and copying. \xc2\xa742.56.070(1) (2008). The Act defines \xe2\x80\x9c[p]ublic record\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cany writing containing information relating to the conduct of government or the performance of any governmental or proprietary function prepared, owned, used, or retained by any state or local agency.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa742.56.010(2). Washington takes the position that referendum petitions are \xe2\x80\x9cpublic records.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent Reed 5.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By August 20, 2009, the secretary had received requests for copies of the R\xe2\x80\x9371 petition from an individual and four entities, including Washington Coalition for Open Government (WCOG) and Washington Families Standing Together (WFST), two of the respondents here. 586 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 675. Two entities, WhoSigned.org and KnowThyNeighbor.org, issued a joint press release stating their intention to post the names of the R\xe2\x80\x9371 petition signers online, in a searchable format. See App. 11; 586 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 675.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The referendum petition sponsor and certain signers filed a complaint and a motion for a preliminary injunction in the United States District Court for the Western District of Washington, seeking to enjoin the secretary of state from publicly releasing any documents that would reveal the names and contact information of the R\xe2\x80\x9371 petition signers. App. 4. Count I of the complaint alleges that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Public Records Act is unconstitutional as applied to referendum petitions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 16. Count II of the complaint alleges that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Public Records Act is unconstitutional as applied to the Referendum 71 petition because there is a reasonable probability that the signatories of the Referendum 71 petition will be subjected to threats, harassment, and reprisals.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 17. Determining that the PRA burdened core political speech, the District Court held that plaintiffs were likely to succeed on the merits of Count I and granted them a preliminary injunction on that count, enjoining release of the information on the petition. 661 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1194, 1205\xe2\x80\x931206 (WD Wash. 2009).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed. Reviewing only Count I of the complaint, the Court of Appeals held that plaintiffs were unlikely to succeed on their claim that the PRA is unconstitutional as applied to referendum petitions generally. It therefore reversed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s grant of the preliminary injunction. 586 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 681. We granted certiorari. 558 U. S. ___ (2010).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is important at the outset to define the scope of the challenge before us. As noted, Count I of the complaint contends that the PRA \xe2\x80\x9cviolates the First Amendment as applied to referendum petitions.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 16. Count II asserts that the PRA \xe2\x80\x9cis unconstitutional as applied to the Referendum 71 petition.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 17. The District Court decision was based solely on Count I; the Court of Appeals decision reversing the District Court was similarly limited. 586 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 676, n.\xc2\xa06. Neither court addressed Count II.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The parties disagree about whether Count I is properly viewed as a facial or as-applied challenge. Compare Reply Brief for Petitioners 8 (\xe2\x80\x9cCount I expressly made an as-applied challenge\xe2\x80\x9d), with Brief for Respondent Reed 1 (\xe2\x80\x9cThis is a facial challenge to Washington\xe2\x80\x99s Public Records Act\xe2\x80\x9d). It obviously has characteristics of both: The claim is \xe2\x80\x9cas applied\xe2\x80\x9d in the sense that it does not seek to strike the PRA in all its applications, but only to the extent it covers referendum petitions. The claim is \xe2\x80\x9cfacial\xe2\x80\x9d in that it is not limited to plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 particular case, but challenges application of the law more broadly to all referendum petitions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The label is not what matters. The important point is that plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claim and the relief that would follow\xe2\x80\x94an injunction barring the secretary of state \xe2\x80\x9cfrom making referendum petitions available to the public,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 16 (Complaint Count I)\xe2\x80\x94reach beyond the particular circumstances of these plaintiffs. They must therefore satisfy our standards for a facial challenge to the extent of that reach. See United States v. Stevens, 559 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 10).

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The compelled disclosure of signatory information on referendum petitions is subject to review under the First Amendment. An individual expresses a view on a political matter when he signs a petition under Washington\xe2\x80\x99s referendum procedure. In most cases, the individual\xe2\x80\x99s signature will express the view that the law subject to the petition should be overturned. Even if the signer is agnostic as to the merits of the underlying law, his signature still expresses the political view that the question should be considered \xe2\x80\x9cby the whole electorate.\xe2\x80\x9d Meyer v. Grant, 486 U. S. 414, 421 (1988). In either case, the expression of a political view implicates a First Amendment right. The State, having \xe2\x80\x9ccho[sen] to tap the energy and the legitimizing power of the democratic process, \xe2\x80\xa6 must accord the participants in that process the First Amendment rights that attach to their roles.\xe2\x80\x9d Republican Party of Minn. v. White, 536 U. S. 765, 788 (2002) (internal quotation marks and ellipsis omitted).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents counter that signing a petition is a legally operative legislative act and therefore \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not involve any significant expressive element.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent Reed 31. It is true that signing a referendum petition may ultimately have the legal consequence of requiring the secretary of state to place the referendum on the ballot. But we do not see how adding such legal effect to an expressive activity somehow deprives that activity of its expressive component, taking it outside the scope of the First Amendment. Respondents themselves implicitly recognize that the signature expresses a particular viewpoint, arguing that one purpose served by disclosure is to allow the public to engage signers in a debate on the merits of the underlying law. See, e.g., id., at 45; Brief for Respondent WCOG 49; Brief for Respondent WFST 58.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petition signing remains expressive even when it has legal effect in the electoral process. But that is not to say that the electoral context is irrelevant to the nature of our First Amendment review. We allow States significant flexibility in implementing their own voting systems. See Burdick v. Takushi, 504 U. S. 428, 433\xe2\x80\x93434 (1992). To the extent a regulation concerns the legal effect of a particular activity in that process, the government will be afforded substantial latitude to enforce that regulation. Also pertinent to our analysis is the fact that the PRA is not a prohibition on speech, but instead a disclosure requirement. \xe2\x80\x9c[D]isclosure requirements may burden the ability to speak, but they \xe2\x80\xa6 do not prevent anyone from speaking.\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens United v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 51) (internal quotation marks\nomitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have a series of precedents considering First Amendment challenges to disclosure requirements in the electoral context. These precedents have reviewed such challenges under what has been termed \xe2\x80\x9cexacting scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Buckley v. Valeo, 424 U. S. 1, 64 (1976) (per curiam) (\xe2\x80\x9cSince NAACP v. Alabama [357 U. S. 449 (1958),] we have required that the subordinating interests of the State [offered to justify compelled disclosure] survive exacting scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d); Citizens United, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 51) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court has subjected [disclosure] requirements to \xe2\x80\x98exacting scrutiny\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Buckley, supra, at 64)); Davis v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 554 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 18) (governmental interest in disclosure \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98must survive exacting scrutiny\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Buckley, supra, at 64)); Buckley v. American Constitutional Law Foundation, Inc., 525 U. S. 182, 204 (1999) (ACLF) (finding that disclosure rules \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ed] exacting scrutiny\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That standard \xe2\x80\x9crequires a \xe2\x80\x98substantial relation\xe2\x80\x99 between the disclosure requirement and a \xe2\x80\x98sufficiently important\xe2\x80\x99 governmental interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens United, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 51) (quoting Buckley, supra, at 64, 66). To withstand this scrutiny, \xe2\x80\x9cthe strength of the governmental interest must reflect the seriousness of the actual burden on First Amendment rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 18) (citing Buckley, supra, at 68, 71).[Footnote 1]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents assert two interests to justify the burdens of compelled disclosure under the PRA on First Amendment rights: (1) preserving the integrity of the electoral process by combating fraud, detecting invalid signatures, and fostering government transparency and accountability; and (2) providing information to the electorate about who supports the petition. See, e.g., Brief for Respondent Reed 39\xe2\x80\x9342, 44\xe2\x80\x9345. Because we determine that the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in preserving the integrity of the electoral process suffices to defeat the argument that the PRA is unconstitutional with respect to referendum petitions in general, we need not, and do not, address the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cinformational\xe2\x80\x9d interest.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in preserving the integrity of the electoral process is undoubtedly important. \xe2\x80\x9cStates allowing ballot initiatives have considerable leeway to protect the integrity and reliability of the initiative process, as they have with respect to election processes generally.\xe2\x80\x9d ACLF, 525 U. S., at 191. The State\xe2\x80\x99s interest is particularly strong with respect to efforts to root out fraud, which not only may produce fraudulent outcomes, but has a systemic effect as well: It \xe2\x80\x9cdrives honest citizens out of the democratic process and breeds distrust of our government.\xe2\x80\x9d Purcell v. Gonzalez, 549 U. S. 1, 4 (2006) (per curiam); see also Crawford v. Marion County Election Bd., 553 U. S. 181, 196 (2008) (opinion of Stevens, J.). The threat of fraud in this context is not merely hypothetical; respondents and their amici cite a number of cases of petition-related fraud across the country to support the point. See Brief for Respondent Reed 43; Brief for State of Ohio et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 22\xe2\x80\x9324.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in preserving electoral integrity is not limited to combating fraud. That interest extends to efforts to ferret out invalid signatures caused not by fraud but by simple mistake, such as duplicate signatures or signatures of individuals who are not registered to vote in the State. See Brief for Respondent Reed 42. That interest also extends more generally to promoting transparency and accountability in the electoral process, which the State argues is \xe2\x80\x9cessential to the proper functioning of a democracy.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 39.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs contend that the disclosure requirements of the PRA are not \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently related\xe2\x80\x9d to the interest of protecting the integrity of the electoral process. Brief for Petitioners 51. They argue that disclosure is not necessary because the secretary of state is already charged with verifying and canvassing the names on a petition, advocates and opponents of a measure can observe that process, and any citizen can challenge the secretary\xe2\x80\x99s actions in court. See Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa729A.72.230, 29A.72.240. They also stress that existing criminal penalties reduce the danger of fraud in the petition process. See Brief for Petitioners 50; \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa729A.84.210, 29A.84.230, 29A.84.250.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But the secretary\xe2\x80\x99s verification and canvassing will not catch all invalid signatures: The job is large and difficult (the secretary ordinarily checks \xe2\x80\x9conly 3 to 5% of signatures,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent WFST 54), and the secretary can make mistakes, too, see Brief for Respondent Reed 42. Public disclosure can help cure the inadequacies of the verification and canvassing process.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Disclosure also helps prevent certain types of petition fraud otherwise difficult to detect, such as outright forgery and \xe2\x80\x9cbait and switch\xe2\x80\x9d fraud, in which an individual signs the petition based on a misrepresentation of the underlying issue. See Brief for Respondent WFST 9\xe2\x80\x9311, 53\xe2\x80\x9354; cf. Brief for Massachusetts Gay and Lesbian Political Caucus et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 18\xe2\x80\x9322 (detailing \xe2\x80\x9cbait and switch\xe2\x80\x9d fraud in a petition drive in Massachusetts). The signer is in the best position to detect these types of fraud, and public disclosure can bring the issue to the signer\xe2\x80\x99s attention.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Public disclosure thus helps ensure that the only signatures counted are those that should be, and that the only referenda placed on the ballot are those that garner enough valid signatures. Public disclosure also promotes transparency and accountability in the electoral process to an extent other measures cannot. In light of the foregoing, we reject plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 argument and conclude that public disclosure of referendum petitions in general is substantially related to the important interest of preserving the integrity of the electoral process.[Footnote 2]

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 more significant objection is that \xe2\x80\x9cthe strength of the governmental interest\xe2\x80\x9d does not \xe2\x80\x9creflect the seriousness of the actual burden on First Amendment rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis, 554 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 18) (citing Buckley, 424 U. S., at 68, 71); see, e.g., Brief for Petitioners 12\xe2\x80\x9313, 30. According to plaintiffs, the objective of those seeking disclosure of the R\xe2\x80\x9371 petition is not to prevent fraud, but to publicly identify those who had validly signed and to broadcast the signers\xe2\x80\x99 political views on the subject of the petition. Plaintiffs allege, for example, that several groups plan to post the petitions in searchable form on the Internet, and then encourage other citizens to seek out the R\xe2\x80\x9371 signers. See App. 11; Brief for Petitioners 8, 46\xe2\x80\x9347.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs explain that once on the Internet, the petition signers\xe2\x80\x99 names and addresses \xe2\x80\x9ccan be combined with publicly available phone numbers and maps,\xe2\x80\x9d in what will effectively become a blueprint for harassment and intimidation. Id., at 46. To support their claim that they will be subject to reprisals, plaintiffs cite examples from the history of a similar proposition in California, see, e.g., id., at 2\xe2\x80\x936, 31\xe2\x80\x9332, and from the experience of one of the petition sponsors in this case, see App. 9.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In related contexts, we have explained that those resisting disclosure can prevail under the First Amendment if they can show \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable probability that the compelled disclosure [of personal information] will subject them to threats, harassment, or reprisals from either Government officials or private parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Buckley, supra, at 74; see also Citizens United, 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 52). The question before us, however, is not whether PRA disclosure violates the First Amendment with respect to those who signed the R\xe2\x80\x9371 petition, or other particularly controversial petitions. The question instead is whether such disclosure in general violates the First Amendment rights of those who sign referendum petitions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The problem for plaintiffs is that their argument rests almost entirely on the specific harm they say would attend disclosure of the information on the R\xe2\x80\x9371 petition, or on similarly controversial ones. See, e.g., Brief for Petitioners 10, 26\xe2\x80\x9329, 46, 56. But typical referendum petitions \xe2\x80\x9cconcern tax policy, revenue, budget, or other state law issues.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent WFST 36 (listing referenda); see also App. 26 (stating that in recent years the State has received PRA requests for petitions supporting initiatives concerning limiting motor vehicle charges; government regulation of private property; energy resource use by certain electric utilities; long-term care services for the elderly and persons with disabilities; and state, county, and city revenue); id., at 26\xe2\x80\x9327 (stating that in the past 20 years, referendum measures that have qualified for the ballot in the State concerned land-use regulation; unemployment insurance; charter public schools; and insurance coverage and benefits). Voters care about such issues, some quite deeply\xe2\x80\x94but there is no reason to assume that any burdens imposed by disclosure of typical referendum petitions would be remotely like the burdens plaintiffs fear in this case.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs have offered little in response. They have provided us scant evidence or argument beyond the burdens they assert disclosure would impose on R\xe2\x80\x9371 petition signers or the signers of other similarly controversial petitions. Indeed, what little plaintiffs do offer with respect to typical petitions in Washington hurts, not helps: Several other petitions in the State \xe2\x80\x9chave been subject to release in recent years,\xe2\x80\x9d plaintiffs tell us, Brief for Petitioners 50, but apparently that release has come without incident. Cf. Citizens United, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 55) (\xe2\x80\x9cCitizens United has been disclosing its donors for years and has identified no instance of harassment or retaliation\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Faced with the State\xe2\x80\x99s unrebutted arguments that only modest burdens attend the disclosure of a typical petition, we must reject plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 broad challenge to the PRA. In doing so, we note\xe2\x80\x94as we have in other election law disclosure cases\xe2\x80\x94that upholding the law against a broad-based challenge does not foreclose a litigant\xe2\x80\x99s success in a narrower one. See Buckley, supra, at 74 (\xe2\x80\x9cminor parties\xe2\x80\x9d may be exempt from disclosure requirements if they can show \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable probability that the compelled disclosure of a party\xe2\x80\x99s contributors\xe2\x80\x99 names will subject them to threats, harassment, or reprisals from either Government officials or private parties\xe2\x80\x9d); Citizens United, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 54) (disclosure \xe2\x80\x9cwould be unconstitutional as applied to an organization if there were a reasonable probability that the group\xe2\x80\x99s members would face threats, harassment, or reprisals if their names were disclosed\xe2\x80\x9d (citing McConnell v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 540 U. S. 93, 198 (2003)). The secretary of state acknowledges that plaintiffs may press the narrower challenge in Count II of their complaint in proceedings pending before the District Court. Brief for Respondent Reed 17.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude that disclosure under the PRA would not violate the First Amendment with respect to referendum petitions in general and therefore affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs claim the First Amendment, as applied to the States through the Fourteenth Amendment, forbids the State of Washington to release to the public signed referendum petitions, which they submitted to the State in order to suspend operation of a law and put it to a popular vote. I doubt whether signing a petition that has the effect of suspending a law fits within \xe2\x80\x9cthe freedom of speech\xe2\x80\x9d at all. But even if, as the Court concludes, ante, at 5, it does, a long history of practice shows that the First Amendment does not prohibit public disclosure.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We should not repeat and extend the mistake of McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 514 U. S. 334 (1995). There, with neither textual support nor precedents requiring the result, the Court invalidated a form of election regulation that had been widely used by the States since the end of the 19th century. Id., at 371 (Scalia, J., dissenting). The Court held that an Ohio statute prohibiting the distribution of anonymous campaign literature violated the First and Fourteenth Amendments.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Mrs. McIntyre sought a general right to \xe2\x80\x9cspeak\xe2\x80\x9d anonymously about a referendum. Here, plaintiffs go one step further\xe2\x80\x94they seek a general right to participate anonymously in the referendum itself.[Footnote 1] Referendum petitions are subject to public disclosure under the Public Records Act (PRA), Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa742.56.001 et seq., which requires government agencies to \xe2\x80\x9cmake available for public inspection and copying all public records,\xe2\x80\x9d subject to certain exemptions not relevant here. \xc2\xa742.56.070(1) (2008). Plaintiffs contend that disclosure of the names, and other personal information included on the petitions, of those who took this legislative action violates their First Amendment right to anonymity.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion acknowledges such a right, finding that it can be denied here only because of the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in \xe2\x80\x9cpreserving the integrity of the electoral process,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8. In my view this is not a matter for judicial interest-balancing. Our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s longstanding traditions of legislating and voting in public refute the claim that the First Amendment accords a right to anonymity in the performance of an act with governmental effect. \xe2\x80\x9cA governmental practice that has become general throughout the United States, and particularly one that has the validation of long, accepted usage, bears a strong presumption of con-\nstitutionality.\xe2\x80\x9d McIntyre, supra, at 375 (Scalia, J.,\ndissenting).

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When a Washington voter signs a referendum petition subject to the PRA, he is acting as a legislator. The Washington Constitution vests \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he legislative authority\xe2\x80\x9d of the State in the legislature, but \xe2\x80\x9cthe people reserve to themselves the power . . . to approve or reject at the polls any act, item, section, or part of any bill, act, or law passed by the legislature.\xe2\x80\x9d Art. 2, \xc2\xa71. This \xe2\x80\x9creferendum\xe2\x80\x9d power of popular legislation is exercised by submitting a petition, in accordance with certain specifications, to the Washington secretary of state with valid signatures of registered voters in number equal to or exceeding four percent of the votes cast in the last gubernatorial election. \xc2\xa71(b); Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa729A.72.100, 130, 140, 150, 160 (2008).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The filing of a referendum petition that satisfies these requirements has two legal effects: (1) It requires the secretary to place the measure referred to the people on the ballot at the next general election; and (2) it suspends operation of the measure, causing it only to have effect 30 days after it is approved during that election. Art. 2, \xc2\xa71(d). See Brief for Respondent Sam Reed, Secretary of State of Washington 2\xe2\x80\x936. A voter who signs a referendum petition is therefore exercising legislative power because his signature, somewhat like a vote for or against a bill in the legislature, seeks to affect the legal force of the measure at issue.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs point to no precedent from this Court holding that legislating is protected by the First Amendment.[Footnote 3] Nor do they identify historical evidence demonstrating that \xe2\x80\x9cthe freedom of speech\xe2\x80\x9d the First Amendment codified encompassed a right to legislate without public disclosure. This should come as no surprise; the exercise of lawmaking power in the United States has traditionally been public.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The public nature of federal lawmaking is constitutionally required. Article I, \xc2\xa75, cl.\xc2\xa03 requires Congress to legislate in public: \xe2\x80\x9cEach House shall keep a Journal of its Proceedings, and from time to time publish the same, excepting such Parts as may in their Judgment require Secrecy; and the Yeas and Nays of the Members of either House on any question shall, at the Desire of one fifth of those Present, be entered on the Journal.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 4] State constitutions enacted around the time of the founding had similar provisions. See, e.g., Ky. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa720 (1792); Ga. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa715 (1798). The desirability of public accountability was obvious. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]s to the votes of representatives and senators in Congress, no man has yet been bold enough to vindicate a secret or ballot vote, as either more safe or more wise, more promotive of independence in the members, or more beneficial to their constituents.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 J. Story, Commentaries on the Constitution \xc2\xa7841, p.\xe2\x80\x82591 (4th ed. 1873).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, even when the people asked Congress for legislative changes\xe2\x80\x94by exercising their constitutional right to \xe2\x80\x9cto petition the Government for a redress of grievances,\xe2\x80\x9d U. S. Const., Amdt. 1\xe2\x80\x94they did so publicly. The petition was read aloud in Congress. Mazzone, Freedom\xe2\x80\x99s Associations, 77 Wash. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 639, 726 (2002). The petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s name (when large groups were not involved), his request, and what action Congress had taken on the petition were consistently recorded in the House and Senate Journals. See, e.g., Journal of the Senate, June 18, 1790, 1st Cong., 1st Sess., 163; Journal of the House of Representatives, Nov. 24, 1820, 16th Cong., 2d Sess., 32. Even when the people exercised legislative power directly, they did so not anonymously, but openly in town hall meetings. See generally J. Zimmerman, The New England Town Meeting (1999).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioning the government and participating in the traditional town meeting were precursors of the modern initiative and referendum. Those innovations were modeled after similar devices used by the Swiss democracy in the 1800\xe2\x80\x99s, and were first used in the United States by South Dakota in 1898. See S. Piott, Giving Voters a Voice 1\xe2\x80\x933, 16 (2003). The most influential advocate of the initiative and referendum in the United States analogized the Swiss practice to the town meeting, because both \xe2\x80\x9crequired open conduct of political affairs and free expression of opinions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 5 (discussing J.\xe2\x80\x82W. Sullivan, Direct Legislation by the Citizenship through the Initiative and Referendum (1892)). Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 argument implies that the public nature of these practices, so longstanding and unquestioned, violated the freedom of speech. There is no historical support for such a claim.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Legislating was not the only governmental act that was public in America. Voting was public until 1888 when the States began to adopt the Australian secret ballot. See Burson v. Freeman, 504 U. S. 191, 203 (1992) (plurality opinion). We have acknowledged the existence of a First Amendment interest in voting, see, e.g., Burdick v. Takushi, 504 U. S. 428 (1992), but we have never said that it includes the right to vote anonymously. The history of voting in the United States completely undermines that claim.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Initially, the Colonies mostly continued the English traditions of voting by a show of hands or by voice\xe2\x80\x94viva voce voting. Burson, supra, at 200; E. Evans, A History of the Australian Ballot System in the United States 1\xe2\x80\x936 (1917) (Evans). One scholar described the viva voce system as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98The election judges, who were magistrates, sat upon a bench with their clerks before them. Where practicable, it was customary for the candidates to be present in person, and to occupy a seat at the side of the judges. As the voter appeared, his name was called out in a loud voice. The judges inquired, \xe2\x80\x9cJohn Jones (or Smith), for whom do you vote?\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94for governor, or whatever was the office to be filled. He replied by proclaiming the name of his favorite. Then the clerks enrolled the vote, and the judges announced it as enrolled. The representative of the candidate for whom he voted arose, bowed, and thanked him aloud; and his partisans often applauded.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 5 (quoting J.\xc2\xa0Wise, The End of An Era 55\xe2\x80\x9356 (1899)).

\n

\nSee also R. Dinkin, A Study of Elections in the Original Thirteen States, 1776\xe2\x80\x931789, p.\xe2\x80\x82101 (1982) (Dinkin).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although there was variation, the election official would ordinarily compile a poll with the name and residence of each voter, and the name of the candidate for whom he voted. See C. Bishop, History of Elections in the American Colonies 160\xe2\x80\x9364 (1893) (Bishop); P. Argersinger, Structure, Process, and Party: Essays in American Political History 47 (1992) (Argersinger). To prevent fraud, the Colonies in Rhode Island, New York, and New Jersey adopted the English rule that \xe2\x80\x9ccopies of the poll must be delivered on demand to persons who were willing to pay a reasonable charge for the labor of writing them.\xe2\x80\x9d Bishop 186. Some colonies allowed candidates to demand a copy of the poll, ibid., and required the legislature to examine the poll in a contested election, id., at 188\xe2\x80\x93189. Thus, as in this case, the government not only publicly collected identifying information about who voted and for which candidate, it also disclosed that information to the public.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Any suggestion that viva voce voting infringed the accepted understanding of the pre-existing freedom of speech to which the First Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s text refers is refuted by the fact that several state constitutions that required or authorized viva voce voting also explicitly guaranteed the freedom of speech. See, e.g., Ky. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0X, \xc2\xa77, Art.\xc2\xa0VI, \xc2\xa716 (1799); Ill. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0VIII, \xc2\xa722, Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa728 (1818). Surely one constitutional provision did not render the other invalid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course the practice of viva voce voting was gradually replaced with the paper ballot, which was thought to reduce fraud and undue influence. See Evans 1\xe2\x80\x936; Dinkin 101\xe2\x80\x93106. There is no indication that the shift resulted from a sudden realization that public voting infringed voters\xe2\x80\x99 freedom of speech, and the manner in which it occurred suggests the contrary. States adopted the paper ballot at different times, and some States changed methods multiple times. New York\xe2\x80\x99s 1777 Constitution, for example, explicitly provided for the State to switch between methods. Art.\xc2\xa0VI. Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s 1792 Constitution required paper ballots, Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa72, but its 1799 Constitution required viva voce voting, Art.\xc2\xa0VI, \xc2\xa716. The different voting methods simply reflected different views about how democracy should function. One scholar described Virginia\xe2\x80\x99s and Kentucky\xe2\x80\x99s steadfast use of viva voce voting through the Civil War as follows: \xe2\x80\x9c[I]n the appeal to unflinching manliness at the polls these two states insisted still that every voter should show at the hustings the courage of his personal conviction.\xe2\x80\x9d Schouler, Evolution of the American Voter, 2 The American Historical Review 665, 671 (1897). See also id., at 666\xe2\x80\x93667 (\xe2\x80\x9cIn Virginia and the other states in close affiliation with her this oral expression was vaunted as the privilege of the free-born voter, to show the faith that was in him by an outspoken announcement of his candidate\xe2\x80\x9d).\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The new paper ballots did not make voting anonymous. See Evans 10 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he ballot was not secret\xe2\x80\x9d); Argersinger 48 (\xe2\x80\x9cCertainly there were no legal provisions to ensure secrecy\xe2\x80\x9d). Initially, many States did not regulate the form of the paper ballot. See Evans 10; Argersinger 48\xe2\x80\x9349. Taking advantage of this, political parties began printing ballots with their candidates\xe2\x80\x99 names on them. They used brightly colored paper and other distinctive markings so that the ballots could be recognized from a distance, making the votes public. See Burson, supra, at 200\xe2\x80\x93201; Evans 10\xe2\x80\x9311. Abuse of these unofficial paper ballots was rampant. The polling place had become an \xe2\x80\x9copen auction place\xe2\x80\x9d where votes could be freely bought or coerced. Burson, supra, at 202. Employers threatened employees. Party workers kept voters from the other party away from the ballot box. Ballot peddlers paid voters and then watched them place the ballot in the box. See L. Fredman, The Australian Ballot: The Story of an American Reform 22\xe2\x80\x9329 (1968); Argersinger 48\xe2\x80\x9350. Thus, although some state courts said that voting by ballot was meant to be more secret than the public act of viva voce voting; and although some state constitutional requirements of ballot voting were held to guarantee ballot secrecy, thus prohibiting the numbering of ballots for voter identification purposes, see Williams v. Stein, 38 Ind. 89 (1871); Brisbin v. Cleary, 26 Minn. 107, 1 N.\xc2\xa0W. 825 (1879); in general, voting by ballot was by no means secret. Most important of all for present purposes, I am aware of no assertion of ballot secrecy that relied on federal or state constitutional guarantees of freedom of speech.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It was precisely discontent over the nonsecret nature of ballot voting, and the abuses that produced, which led to the States\xe2\x80\x99 adoption of the Australian secret ballot. New York and Massachusetts began that movement in 1888, and almost 90 percent of the States had followed suit by 1896. Burson, 504 U. S., at 203\xe2\x80\x93205. But I am aware of no contention that the Australian system was required by the First Amendment (or the state counterparts). That would have been utterly implausible, since the inhabitants of the Colonies, the States, and the United States had found public voting entirely compatible with \xe2\x80\x9cthe freedom of speech\xe2\x80\x9d for several centuries.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The long history of public legislating and voting contradicts plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claim that disclosure of petition signatures having legislative effect violates the First Amendment. As I said in McIntyre, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here the meaning of a constitutional text (such as \xe2\x80\x98the freedom of speech\xe2\x80\x99) is unclear, the widespread and long-accepted practices of the American people are the best indication of what fundamental beliefs it was intended to enshrine.\xe2\x80\x9d 514 U. S., at 378 (dissenting opinion). Just as the century-old practice of States\xe2\x80\x99 prohibiting anonymous electioneering was sufficient for me to reject the First Amendment claim to anonymity in McIntyre, the many-centuries-old practices of public legislating and voting are sufficient for me to reject plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claim.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs raise concerns that the disclosure of petition signatures may lead to threats and intimidation. Of course nothing prevents the people of Washington from keeping petition signatures secret to avoid that\xe2\x80\x94just as nothing prevented the States from moving to the secret ballot. But there is no constitutional basis for this Court to impose that course upon the States\xe2\x80\x94or to insist (as today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion does) that it can only be avoided by the demonstration of a \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently important governmental interest,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7 (internal quotation marks omitted). And it may even be a bad idea to keep petition signatures secret. There are laws against threats and intimidation; and harsh criticism, short of unlawful action, is a price our people have traditionally been willing to pay for self-governance. Requiring people to stand up in public for their political acts fosters civic courage, without which democracy is doomed. For my part, I do not look forward to a society which, thanks to the Supreme Court, campaigns anonymously (McIntyre) and even exercises the direct democracy of initiative and referendum hidden from public scrutiny and protected from the accountability of criticism. This does not resemble the Home of the Brave.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Breyer joins, concurring in part and concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is not a hard case. It is not about a restriction on voting or on speech and does not involve a classic disclosure requirement. Rather, the case concerns a neutral, nondiscriminatory policy of disclosing information already in the State\xe2\x80\x99s possession that, it has been alleged, might one day indirectly burden petition signatories. The burden imposed by Washington\xe2\x80\x99s application of the Public Records Act (PRA) to referendum petitions in the vast majority, if not all, its applications is not substantial. And the State has given a more than adequate justification for its choice.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For a number of reasons, the application of the PRA to referendum petitions does not substantially burden any individual\xe2\x80\x99s expression. First, it is not \xe2\x80\x9ca regulation of pure speech.\xe2\x80\x9d McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 514 U. S. 334, 345 (1995); cf. United States v. O\xe2\x80\x99Brien, 391 U. S. 367, 377 (1968). It does not prohibit expression, nor does it require that any person signing a petition disclose or say anything at all. See McIntyre, 514 U. S. 334. Nor does the State\xe2\x80\x99s disclosure alter the content of a speaker\xe2\x80\x99s message. See id., at 342\xe2\x80\x93343.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, any effect on speech that disclosure might have is minimal. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The PRA does not necessarily make it more difficult to circulate or obtain signatures on a petition, see Buckley v. American Constitutional Law Foundation, Inc., 525 U. S. 182, 193\xe2\x80\x93196 (1999); Meyer v. Grant, 486 U. S. 414, 422\xe2\x80\x93423 (1988), or to communicate one\xe2\x80\x99s views generally. Regardless of whether someone signs a referendum petition, that person remains free to say anything to anyone at any time. If disclosure indirectly burdens a speaker, \xe2\x80\x9cthe amount of speech covered\xe2\x80\x9d is small\xe2\x80\x94only a single, narrow message conveying one fact in one place, Watchtower Bible & Tract Soc. of N.\xc2\xa0Y., Inc. v. Village of Stratton, 536 U. S. 150, 165 (2002); cf. Cox v. New Hampshire, 312 U. S. 569 (1941). And while the democratic act of casting a ballot or signing a petition does serve an expressive purpose, the act does not involve any \xe2\x80\x9cinteractive communication,\xe2\x80\x9d Meyer, 486 U. S., at 422, and is \xe2\x80\x9cnot principally\xe2\x80\x9d a method of \xe2\x80\x9cindividual expression of political sentiment,\xe2\x80\x9d Timmons v. Twin Cities Area New Party, 520 U. S. 351, 373 (1997) (Stevens, J., dissenting); cf. O\xe2\x80\x99Brien, 391 U. S., at 377.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Weighed against the possible burden on constitutional rights are the State\xe2\x80\x99s justifications for its rule. In this case, the State has posited a perfectly adequate justification: an interest in deterring and detecting petition fraud.[Footnote 2] Given the pedigree of this interest and of similar regulations, the State need not produce concrete evidence that the PRA is the best way to prevent fraud. See Crawford v. Marion County Election Bd., 553 U. S. 181, 191\xe2\x80\x93200 (2008) (opinion of Stevens, J.) (discussing voting fraud); Nixon v. Shrink Missouri Government PAC, 528 U. S. 377, 391 (2000) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe quantum of empirical evidence needed to satisfy heightened judicial scrutiny of legislative judgments will vary up or down with the novelty and plausibility of the justification raised\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Timmons, 520 U. S., at 375 (Stevens, J., dissenting) (rejecting \xe2\x80\x9cimaginative [and] theoretical\xe2\x80\x9d justification supported only by \xe2\x80\x9cbare assertion\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 3] And there is more than enough evidence to support the State\xe2\x80\x99s election-integrity justification. See ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9310 (opinion of the Court).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There remains the issue of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 as-applied challenge. As a matter of law, the Court is correct to keep open the possibility that in particular instances in which a policy such as the PRA burdens expression \xe2\x80\x9cby the public enmity attending publicity,\xe2\x80\x9d Brown v. Socialist Workers \xe2\x80\x9974 Campaign Comm. (Ohio), 459 U. S. 87, 98 (1982), speakers may have a winning constitutional claim. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[F]rom time to time throughout history,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d persecuted groups have been able \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to criticize oppressive practices and laws either anonymously or not at all.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d McIntyre, 514 U. S., at 342.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, this is unlikely to occur in cases involving the PRA. Any burden on speech that petitioners posit is speculative as well as indirect. For an as-applied challenge to a law such as the PRA to succeed, there would have to be a significant threat of harassment directed at those who sign the petition that cannot be mitigated by law enforcement measures.[Footnote 5] Moreover, the character of the law challenged in a referendum does not, in itself, affect the analysis. Debates about tax policy and regulation of private property can become just as heated as debates about domestic partnerships. And as a general matter, it is very difficult to show that by later disclosing the names of petition signatories, individuals will be less willing to sign petitions. Just as we have in the past, I would demand strong evidence before concluding that an indirect and speculative chain of events imposes a substantial burden on speech.[Footnote 6] A statute \xe2\x80\x9cis not to be upset upon hypothetical and unreal possibilities, if it would be good upon the facts as they are.\xe2\x80\x9d Pullman Co. v. Knott, 235 U. S. 23, 26 (1914).

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Alito, concurring.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court holds that the disclosure under the Washington Public Records Act (PRA), Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa742.56.001 et seq. (2008), of the names and addresses of persons who sign referendum petitions does not as a general matter violate the First Amendment, ante, at 13, and I agree with that conclusion. Many referendum petitions concern relatively uncontroversial matters, see ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312, and plaintiffs have provided no reason to think that disclosure of signatory information in those contexts would significantly chill the willingness of voters to sign. Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 facial challenge therefore must fail. See ante, at 2, 5.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nonetheless, facially valid disclosure requirements can impose heavy burdens on First Amendment rights in individual cases. Acknowledging that reality, we have long held that speakers can obtain as-applied exemptions from disclosure requirements if they can show \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable probability that the compelled disclosure of [personal information] will subject them to threats, harassment, or reprisals from either Government officials or private parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Buckley v. Valeo, 424 U. S. 1, 74 (1976) (per curiam); see also Citizens United v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 52); McConnell v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 540 U. S. 93, 197\xe2\x80\x93198 (2003); Brown v. Socialist Workers \xe2\x80\x9974 Campaign Comm. (Ohio), 459 U. S. 87, 93 (1982). Because compelled disclosure can \xe2\x80\x9cburden the ability to speak,\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens United, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 51), and \xe2\x80\x9cseriously infringe on privacy of association and belief guaranteed by the First Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d Buckley, supra, at 64, the as-applied exemption plays a critical role in safeguarding First Amendment rights.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The possibility of prevailing in an as-applied challenge provides adequate protection for First Amendment rights only if (1) speakers can obtain the exemption sufficiently far in advance to avoid chilling protected speech and (2) the showing necessary to obtain the exemption is not overly burdensome. With respect to the first requirement, the as-applied exemption becomes practically worthless if speakers cannot obtain the exemption quickly and well in advance of speaking. To avoid the possibility that a disclosure requirement might chill the willingness of voters to sign a referendum petition (and thus burden a circulator\xe2\x80\x99s ability to collect the necessary number of signatures, cf. Meyer v. Grant, 486 U. S. 414, 423 (1988)), voters must have some assurance at the time when they are presented with the petition that their names and identifying information will not be released to the public. The only way a circulator can provide such assurance, however, is if the circulator has sought and obtained an as-applied exemption from the disclosure requirement well before circulating the petition. Otherwise, the best the circulator could do would be to tell voters that an exemption might be obtained at some point in the future. Such speculation would often be insufficient to alleviate voters\xe2\x80\x99 concerns about the possibility of being subjected to threats, harassment, or reprisals. Cf. Citizens United, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 5\xe2\x80\x936) (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Additionally, speakers must be able to obtain an as-applied exemption without clearing a high evidentiary hurdle. We acknowledged as much in Buckley, where we noted that \xe2\x80\x9cunduly strict requirements of proof could impose a heavy burden\xe2\x80\x9d on speech. 424 U. S., at 74. Recognizing that speakers \xe2\x80\x9cmust be allowed sufficient flexibility in the proof of injury to assure a fair consideration of their claim,\xe2\x80\x9d we emphasized that speakers \xe2\x80\x9cneed show only a reasonable probability\xe2\x80\x9d that disclosure will lead to threats, harassment, or reprisals. Ibid. (emphasis added). We stated that speakers could rely on a wide array of evidence to meet that standard, including \xe2\x80\x9cspecific evidence of past or present harassment of [group] members,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9charassment directed against the organization itself,\xe2\x80\x9d or a \xe2\x80\x9cpattern of threats or specific manifestations of public hostility.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Significantly, we also made clear that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]ew [groups] that have no history upon which to draw may be able to offer evidence of reprisals and threats directed against individuals or organizations holding similar views.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. From its inception, therefore, the as-applied exemption has not imposed onerous burdens of proof on speakers who fear that disclosure might lead to harassment or intimidation.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of those principles, the plaintiffs in this case have a strong argument that the PRA violates the First Amendment as applied to the Referendum 71 petition.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consider first the burdens on plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 First Amendment rights. The widespread harassment and intimidation suffered by supporters of California\xe2\x80\x99s Proposition 8 provides strong support for an as-applied exemption in the present case. See Buckley, supra, at 74 (explaining that speakers seeking as-applied relief from a disclosure requirement can rely on \xe2\x80\x9cevidence of reprisals and threats directed against individuals or organizations holding similar views\xe2\x80\x9d). Proposition 8 amended the California Constitution to provide that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]nly marriage between a man and a woman is valid or recognized in California,\xe2\x80\x9d Cal. Const., Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa77.5, and plaintiffs submitted to the District Court substantial evidence of the harassment suffered by Proposition 8 supporters, see Declaration of Scott F. Bieniek in No. C:09\xe2\x80\x935456 (WD Wash.), Exhs. 12, 13. Members of this Court have also noted that harassment. See Hollingsworth v. Perry, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (per curiam) (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x933); Citizens United, 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 2\xe2\x80\x933) (opinion of Thomas, J.). Indeed, if the evidence relating to Proposition 8 is not sufficient to obtain an as-applied exemption in this case, one may wonder whether that vehicle provides any meaningful protection for the First Amendment rights of persons who circulate and sign referendum and initiative petitions.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What is more, when plaintiffs return to the District Court, they will have the opportunity to develop evidence of intimidation and harassment of Referendum 71 supporters\xe2\x80\x94an opportunity that was pretermitted because of the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to grant a preliminary injunction on count 1 of plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 complaint. See 661 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1194, 1205\xe2\x80\x931206 (WD Wash. 2009); Tr. of Oral Arg. 40\xe2\x80\x9341. For example, plaintiffs allege that the campaign manager for one of the plaintiff groups received threatening e-mails and phone calls, and that the threats were so severe that the manager filed a complaint with the local sheriff and had his children sleep in an interior room of his home. App. 9\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The inadequacy of the State\xe2\x80\x99s interests in compelling public disclosure of referendum signatory information further confirms that courts should be generous in granting as-applied relief in this context. See Buckley, supra, at 71 (recognizing that the weakness of the State\xe2\x80\x99s interests in an individual case can require exempting speakers from compelled disclosure); Brown, 459 U. S., at 92\xe2\x80\x9393 (same). As the Court notes, respondents rely on two interests to justify compelled disclosure in this context: (1) providing information to voters about who supports a referendum petition; and (2) preserving the integrity of the referendum process by detecting fraudulent and mistaken signatures. Ante, at 8.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my view, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 asserted informational interest will not in any case be sufficient to trump the First Amendment rights of signers and circulators who face a threat of harassment. Respondents maintain that publicly disclosing the names and addresses of referendum signatories provides the voting public with \xe2\x80\x9cinsight into whether support for holding a vote comes predominantly from particular interest groups, political or religious organizations, or other group[s] of citizens,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus allows voters to draw inferences about whether they should support or oppose the referendum. Brief for Respondent Washington Families Standing Together 58; see also Brief for Respondent Reed 46\xe2\x80\x9348. Additionally, respondents argue that disclosure \xe2\x80\x9callows Washington voters to engage in discussion of referred measures with persons whose acts secured the election and suspension of state law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 45; see also Brief for Respondent Washington Families Standing Together 58.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The implications of accepting such an argument are breathtaking. Were we to accept respondents\xe2\x80\x99 asserted informational interest, the State would be free to require petition signers to disclose all kinds of demographic information, including the signer\xe2\x80\x99s race, religion, political affiliation, sexual orientation, ethnic background, and interest-group memberships. Requiring such disclosures, however, runs headfirst into a half century of our case law, which firmly establishes that individuals have a right to privacy of belief and association. See Rumsfeld v. Forum for Academic and Institutional Rights, Inc., 547 U. S. 47, 69 (2006); Brown, supra, at 91; Buckley, 424 U. S., at 64; DeGregory v. Attorney General of N. H., 383 U. S. 825, 829 (1966); Gibson v. Florida Legislative Investigation Comm., 372 U. S. 539, 544 (1963); NAACP v. Alabama ex rel. Patterson, 357 U. S. 449, 462 (1958). Indeed, the State\xe2\x80\x99s informational interest paints such a chilling picture of the role of government in our lives that at oral argument the Washington attorney general balked when confronted with the logical implications of accepting such an argument, conceding that the State could not require petition signers to disclose their religion or ethnicity. Tr. of Oral Arg. 37, 56.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 informational interest is no more legitimate when viewed as a means of providing the public with information needed to locate and contact supporters of a referendum. In the name of pursuing such an interest, the State would be free to require petition signers to disclose any information that would more easily enable members of the voting public to contact them and engage them in discussion, including telephone numbers, e-mail addresses, and Internet aliases. Once again, permitting the government to require speakers to disclose such information runs against the current of our associational privacy cases. But more important, when speakers are faced with a reasonable probability of harassment or intimidation, the State no longer has any interest in enabling the public to locate and contact supporters of a particular measure\xe2\x80\x94for in that instance, disclosure becomes a means of facilitating harassment that impermissibly chills the exercise of First Amendment rights.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, two groups proposed to place on the Internet the names and addresses of all those who signed Referendum 71, and it is alleged that their express aim was to encourage \xe2\x80\x9cuncomfortable conversation[s].\xe2\x80\x9d 661 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 1199 (internal quotation marks omitted). If this information is posted on the Internet, then anyone with access to a computer could compile a wealth of information about all of those persons, including in many cases all of the following: the names of their spouses and neighbors, their telephone numbers, directions to their homes, pictures of their homes, information about their homes (such as size, type of construction, purchase price, and mortgage amount), information about any motor vehicles that they own, any court case in which they were parties, any information posted on a social networking site, and newspaper articles in which their names appeared (including such things as wedding announcements, obituaries, and articles in local papers about their children\xe2\x80\x99s school and athletic activities). The potential that such information could be used for harassment is vast.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondents also maintain that the State has an interest in preserving the integrity of the referendum process and that public disclosure furthers that interest by helping the State detect fraudulent and mistaken signatures. I agree with the Court that preserving the integrity of the referendum process constitutes a sufficiently important state interest. Ante, at 8. But I harbor serious doubts as to whether public disclosure of signatory information serves that interest in a way that always \xe2\x80\x9creflect[s] the seriousness of the actual burden on First Amendment rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Davis v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 554 U. S. ___, ___ (2008) (slip op., at 18).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the realities of Washington law undermine the State\xe2\x80\x99s argument that public disclosure is necessary to ensure the integrity of the referendum process. The State of Washington first authorized voter initiatives via constitutional amendment in 1912, and the following year the Washington Legislature passed a statute specifying the particulars of the referendum process. See State ex rel. Case v. Superior Ct. for Thurston Cty., 81 Wash. 623, 628, 143 P. 461, 462 (1914). Significantly, Washington\xe2\x80\x99s laws pertaining to initiatives and referenda did not then and do not now authorize the public disclosure of signatory information. See Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa729A.72.010 et seq.; 1913 Wash. Laws. pp. 418\xe2\x80\x93437. Instead, the public disclosure requirement stems from the PRA, which was enacted in 1972 and which requires the public disclosure of state documents generally, not referendum documents specifically. See Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa742.56.001 et seq. Indeed, if anything, Washington\xe2\x80\x99s referenda and initiative laws suggest that signatory information should remain confidential: Outside observers are permitted to observe the secretary of state\xe2\x80\x99s verification and canvassing process only \xe2\x80\x9cso long as they make no record of the names, addresses, or other information on the petitions or related records during the verification process,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa729A.72.230, and the State is required to destroy all those petitions that fail to qualify for the ballot, \xc2\xa729A.72.200.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the State fails to come to grips with the fact that public disclosure of referendum signatory information is a relatively recent practice in Washington. Prior to the adoption of the PRA in 1972, the Washington attorney general took the view that referendum petitions were not subject to public disclosure. See Op. Wash. Atty. Gen. 55\xe2\x80\x9357 No. 274, pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x932 (May 28, 1956), online at http://www.atg.wa.gov/AGOOpinions/opinion.aspx?section=topic&id=10488 (all Internet materials as visited June 17, 2010, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file) (declaring that public disclosure of initiative petitions would be \xe2\x80\x9ccontrary to public policy\xe2\x80\x9d and would run contrary to \xe2\x80\x9ca tendency on the part of the legislature to regard the signing of an initiative petition as a matter concerning only the individual signers except in so far as necessary to safeguard against abuses of the privilege\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, the secretary of state represents on his Web site that even after the PRA was enacted, \xe2\x80\x9cvarious Secretary of State administrations took the position, from 1973 to 1998, that the personal information on petition sheets were NOT subject to disclosure.\xe2\x80\x9d B. Zylstra, The Disclosure History of Petition Sheets (Sept. 17, 2009), online at http://blogs.sos.wa.gov/FromOurCorner/index.php/2009/09/the-disclosure-history-of-petition-sheets/. Although the secretary of state apparently changed this policy in the late 1990\xe2\x80\x99s, it appears that the secretary did not release any initiative petitions until 2006. Ibid. And to date, the secretary has released only a handful of petitions. Ibid.; App. 26. That history substantially undermines the State\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that public disclosure is necessary to ensure the integrity of the referendum process. For nearly a century, Washington\xe2\x80\x99s referendum process operated\xe2\x80\x94and apparently operated successfully\xe2\x80\x94without the public disclosure of signatory information. The State has failed to explain how circumstances have changed so dramatically in recent years that public disclosure is now\nrequired.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, the experiences of other States demonstrates that publicly disclosing the names and identifying information of referendum signatories is not necessary to protect against fraud and mistake. To give but one example, California has had more initiatives on the ballot than any other State save Oregon. See Initiative and Referendum Institute, Initiative Use, p.\xc2\xa01 (Feb. 2009), online at http://www.iandrinstitute.org/IRI%20Initiative%20Use%20%281904=2008%29.pdf. Nonetheless, California law explicitly protects the privacy of initiative and referendum signatories. See Cal. Elec. Code Ann. \xc2\xa718650 (West 2003); Cal. Govt. Code Ann. \xc2\xa76253.5 (West 2008). It is thus entirely possible for a State to keep signatory information private and maintain a referendum and initiative process free from fraud.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, Washington could easily and cheaply employ alternative mechanisms for protecting against fraud and mistake that would be far more protective of circulators\xe2\x80\x99 and signers\xe2\x80\x99 First Amendment rights. For example, the Washington attorney general represented to us at oral argument that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Secretary of State\xe2\x80\x99s first step after receiving submitted petitions is to take them to his archiving section and to have them digitized.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 30. With a digitized list, it should be relatively easy for the secretary to check for duplicate signatures on a referendum petition. And given that the secretary maintains a \xe2\x80\x9ccentralized, uniform, interactive computerized statewide voter registration list that contains the name and registration information of every registered voter in the state,\xe2\x80\x9d Wash. Rev. Code Ann. \xc2\xa729A.08.125(1) (West Supp. 2010), the secretary could use a computer program to cross-check the names and addresses on the petition with the names and addresses on the voter registration roles, thus ensuring the accuracy and legitimacy of each signature.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Additionally, using the digitized version of the referendum petition, the State could set up a simple system for Washington citizens to check whether their names have been fraudulently signed to a petition. For example, on his Web site, the secretary maintains an interface that allows voters to confirm their voter registration information simply by inputting their name and date of birth. See http://wei.secstate.wa.gov/osos/VoterVault/Pages/MyVote.aspx. Presumably the secretary could set up a similar interface for referendum petitions. Indeed, the process would seem to be all the more simple given that Washington requires a \xe2\x80\x9cunique identifier [to] be assigned to each registered voter in the state.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa729A.08.125(4).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As-applied challenges to disclosure requirements play a critical role in protecting First Amendment freedoms. To give speech the breathing room it needs to flourish, prompt judicial remedies must be available well before the relevant speech occurs and the burden of proof must be low. In this case\xe2\x80\x94both through analogy and through their own experiences\xe2\x80\x94plaintiffs have a strong case that they are entitled to as-applied relief, and they will be able to pursue such relief before the District Court.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I write separately to emphasize a point implicit in the opinion of the Court and the concurring opinions of Justice Stevens, Justice Scalia, and Justice Breyer: In assessing the countervailing interests at stake in this case, we must be mindful of the character of initiatives and referenda. These mechanisms of direct democracy are not compelled by the Federal Constitution. It is instead up to the people of each State, acting in their sovereign capacity, to decide whether and how to permit legislation by popular action. States enjoy \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderable leeway\xe2\x80\x9d to choose the subjects that are eligible for placement on the ballot and to specify the requirements for obtaining ballot access (e.g., the number of signatures required, the time for submission, and the method of verification). Buckley v. American Constitutional Law Foundation, Inc., 525 U. S. 182, 191 (1999). As the Court properly recognizes, each of these structural decisions \xe2\x80\x9cinevitably affects\xe2\x80\x94at least to some degree\xe2\x80\x94the individual\xe2\x80\x99s right\xe2\x80\x9d to speak about political issues and \xe2\x80\x9cto associate with others for political ends.\xe2\x80\x9d Anderson v. Celebrezze, 460 U. S. 780, 788 (1983). For instance, requiring petition signers to be registered voters or to use their real names no doubt limits the ability or willingness of some individuals to undertake the expressive act of signing a petition. Regulations of this nature, however, stand \xe2\x80\x9ca step removed from the communicative aspect of petitioning,\xe2\x80\x9d and the ability of States to impose them can scarcely be doubted. Buckley, 525 U. S., at 215 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment in part and dissenting in part); see also McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 514 U. S. 334, 345 (1995) (contrasting measures to \xe2\x80\x9ccontrol the mechanics of the electoral process\xe2\x80\x9d with the \xe2\x80\x9cregulation of pure speech\xe2\x80\x9d). It is by no means necessary for a State to prove that such \xe2\x80\x9creasonable, nondiscriminatory restrictions\xe2\x80\x9d are narrowly tailored to its interests. Anderson, 460 U. S., at 788.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court today confirms that the State of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s decision to make referendum petition signatures available for public inspection falls squarely within the realm of permissible election-related regulations. Cf. Buckley, 525 U. S., at 200 (describing a state law requiring petition circulators to submit affidavits containing their names and addresses as \xe2\x80\x9cexemplif[ying] the type of regulation\xe2\x80\x9d that States may adopt). Public disclosure of the identity of petition signers, which is the rule in the overwhelming majority of States that use initiatives and referenda, advances States\xe2\x80\x99 vital interests in \xe2\x80\x9c[p]reserving the integrity of the electoral process, preventing corruption, and sustaining the active, alert responsibility of the individual citizen in a democracy for the wise conduct of government.\xe2\x80\x9d First Nat. Bank of Boston v. Bellotti, 435 U. S. 765, 788\xe2\x80\x93789 (1978) (internal quotation marks and alterations omitted); see also Citizens United v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 55) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]ransparency enables the electorate to make informed decisions and give proper weight to different speakers and messages\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Respondent Washington Families Standing Together 34 (reporting that only one State exempts initiative and referendum petitions from public disclosure). In a society \xe2\x80\x9cin which the citizenry is the final judge of the proper conduct of public business,\xe2\x80\x9d openness in the democratic process is of \xe2\x80\x9ccritical importance.\xe2\x80\x9d Cox Broadcasting Corp. v. Cohn, 420 U. S. 469, 495 (1975); see also post, at 4 (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he public nature of federal lawmaking is constitutionally required\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On the other side of the ledger, I view the burden of public disclosure on speech and associational rights as minimal in this context. As this Court has observed with respect to campaign-finance regulations, \xe2\x80\x9cdisclosure requirements \xe2\x80\xa6 \xe2\x80\x98do not prevent anyone from speaking.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens United, 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 51). When it comes to initiatives and referenda, the impact of public disclosure on expressive interests is even more attenuated. While campaign-finance disclosure injects the government into what would otherwise have been private political activity, the process of legislating by referendum is inherently public. To qualify a referendum for the ballot, citizens are required to sign a petition and supply identifying information to the State. The act of signing typically occurs in public, and the circulators who collect and submit signatures ordinarily owe signers no guarantee of confidentiality. For persons with the \xe2\x80\x9ccivic courage\xe2\x80\x9d to participate in this process, post, at 10 (opinion of Scalia, J.), the State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to make accessible what they voluntarily place in the public sphere should not deter them from engaging in the expressive act of petition signing. Disclosure of the identity of petition signers, moreover, in no way directly impairs the ability of anyone to speak and associate for political ends either publicly or privately.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given the relative weight of the interests at stake and the traditionally public nature of initiative and referendum processes, the Court rightly rejects petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional challenge to the State of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s petition disclosure regulations. These same considerations also mean that any party attempting to challenge particular applications of the State\xe2\x80\x99s regulations will bear a heavy burden. Even when a referendum involves a particularly controversial subject and some petition signers fear harassment from nonstate actors, a State\xe2\x80\x99s important interests in \xe2\x80\x9cprotect[ing] the integrity and reliability of the initiative process\xe2\x80\x9d remain undiminished, and the State retains significant discretion in advancing those interests. Buckley, 525 U. S., at 191. Likewise, because the expressive interests implicated by the act of petition signing are always modest, I find it difficult to see how any incremental disincentive to sign a petition would tip the constitutional balance. Case-specific relief may be available when a State selectively applies a facially neutral petition disclosure rule in a manner that discriminates based on the content of referenda or the viewpoint of petition signers, or in the rare circumstance in which disclosure poses a reasonable probability of serious and widespread harassment that the State is unwilling or unable to control. Cf. NAACP v. Alabama ex rel. Patterson, 357 U. S. 449 (1958). Allowing case-specific invalidation under a more forgiving standard would unduly diminish the substantial breathing room States are afforded to adopt and implement reasonable, nondiscriminatory measures like the disclosure requirement now at issue. Accordingly, courts presented with an as-applied challenge to a regulation authorizing the disclosure of referendum petitions should be deeply skeptical of any assertion that the Constitution, which embraces political transparency, compels States to conceal the identity of persons who seek to participate in lawmaking through a state-created referendum process. With this understanding, I join the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Just as \xe2\x80\x9c[c]onfidence in the integrity of our electoral processes is essential to the functioning of our participatory democracy,\xe2\x80\x9d Purcell v. Gonzalez, 549 U. S. 1, 4 (2006) (per curiam), so too is citizen participation in those processes, which necessarily entails political speech and association under the First Amendment. In my view, compelled disclosure of signed referendum and initiative petitions[Footnote 1] under the Washington Public Records Act (PRA), Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa742.56.001, et seq. (2008), severely burdens those rights and chills citizen participation in the referendum process. Given those burdens, I would hold that Washington\xe2\x80\x99s decision to subject all referendum petitions to public disclosure is unconstitutional because there will always be a less restrictive means by which Washington can vindicate its stated interest in preserving the integrity of its referendum process. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns the interaction of two distinct sets of Washington statutes. The first set, codified in Washington\xe2\x80\x99s Election Code, regulates the referendum and initiative process. These statutes require, among other things, that referendum signers write their names and addresses on petition sheets, and mandate that this information\nbe disclosed to Washington\xe2\x80\x99s secretary of state for canvassing and verification. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa729A.72.130, 29A.72.230 (2008). Petitioners do not contend that these requirements violate their First Amendment rights; that is, they do not argue that the Constitution allows them to support a referendum measure without disclosing their names to the State.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second set of statutes\xe2\x80\x94the PRA\xe2\x80\x94is not a referendum or election regulation. Rather, the PRA requires disclosure of all nonexempt \xe2\x80\x9cpublic records\xe2\x80\x9d upon request by any person. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa742.56.010(2), 42.56.070. Washington has concluded that signed referendum petitions are \xe2\x80\x9cpublic records\xe2\x80\x9d subject to disclosure under the PRA, and has \xe2\x80\x9croutinely disclosed petitions in response to public records requests.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent Reed 5\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners do not challenge the constitutionality of the PRA generally. They contend only that Washington violates their First Amendment rights by construing the PRA to apply to signed referendum petitions. See Brief for Petitioners 35\xe2\x80\x9339. As the Court notes, the parties dispute whether this challenge is best conceived as a facial challenge or an as-applied challenge. See ante, at 5. In my view, the Court correctly concludes that petitioners must \xe2\x80\x9csatisfy our standards for a facial challenge\xe2\x80\x9d because their claim, and the relief that they seek, \xe2\x80\x9creach beyond\xe2\x80\x9d their \xe2\x80\x9cparticular circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We typically disfavor facial challenges. See Washington State Grange v. Washington State Republican Party, 552 U. S. 442, 449 (2008). They \xe2\x80\x9coften rest on speculation,\xe2\x80\x9d can lead courts unnecessarily to anticipate constitutional questions or formulate broad constitutional rules, and may prevent governmental officers from implementing laws \xe2\x80\x9cin a manner consistent with the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 450\xe2\x80\x93451. For those reasons, we rejected in Washington State Grange political parties\xe2\x80\x99 pre-enforcement facial challenge to a Washington initiative that allowed candidates in a primary election to self-designate their political party preference on the primary election ballot. See id., at 458\xe2\x80\x93459. Because the challenge was a pre-enforcement one, Washington \xe2\x80\x9chad no opportunity to implement\xe2\x80\x9d the initiative, id., at 450, so the political parties\xe2\x80\x99 arguments that it violated their association rights all depended \xe2\x80\x9con the possibility that voters will be confused as to the meaning of the party-preference designation,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 454. Moreover, a facial challenge was inappropriate because the regulation did \xe2\x80\x9cnot on its face impose a severe burden on political parties\xe2\x80\x99 associational rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 444.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Those considerations point in the opposite direction here. Washington\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the PRA \xe2\x80\x9con its face impose[s] a severe burden,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid.\xe2\x80\x94compelled disclosure of privacy in political association protected by the First Amendment, see infra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x94on all referendum signers. And Washington has had several \xe2\x80\x9copportunit[ies] to implement\xe2\x80\x9d the PRA\xe2\x80\x99s disclosure requirements with respect to initiative petitions. Washington State Grange, supra, at 450. Indeed, Washington admits that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll petitions for initiatives, referendum, recall, and candidate nomination are public records subject to disclosure.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent Reed 59; see also App. 26 (listing six completed requests for disclosure of signed initiative petitions since 2006). Washington thus has eliminated any \xe2\x80\x9cpossibility\xe2\x80\x9d that referendum petition signers \xe2\x80\x9cwill be confused as to\xe2\x80\x9d how the State will respond to a request under the PRA to disclose their names and addresses. Washington State Grange, 552 U. S., at 454.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, I would consider petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 facial challenge here. For purposes of this case, I will assume that to prevail, petitioners must satisfy our most rigorous standard, and show that there is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98no set of circumstances \xe2\x80\xa6 under which the\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d PRA could be constitutionally applied to a referendum or initiative petition, \xe2\x80\x9ci.e., that the [PRA] is unconstitutional in all of its applications,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 449 (quoting United States v. Salerno, 481 U. S. 739, 745 (1987)).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court correctly concludes that \xe2\x80\x9can individual expresses\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cpolitical view\xe2\x80\x9d by signing a referendum petition. Ante, at 5. The Court also rightly rejects the baseless argument that such expressive activity falls \xe2\x80\x9coutside the scope of the First Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d merely because \xe2\x80\x9cit has legal effect in the electoral process.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. Yet, the Court does not acknowledge the full constitutional implications of these conclusions.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The expressive political activity of signing a referendum petition is a paradigmatic example of \xe2\x80\x9cthe practice of persons sharing common views banding together to achieve a common end.\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens Against Rent Control/Coalition for Fair Housing v. Berkeley, 454 U. S. 290, 294 (1981). A referendum supported by only one person\xe2\x80\x99s signature is a nullity; it will never be placed on the ballot. The Doe petitioners recognized as much when they\xe2\x80\x94and more than 120,000 other Washingtonians, see ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x94joined with petitioner Protect Marriage Washington, \xe2\x80\x9ca state political action committee\xe2\x80\x9d organized under \xc2\xa742.17.040, to effect Protect Marriage Washington\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmajor purpose\xe2\x80\x9d of collecting enough valid signatures to place Referendum 71 on the general election ballot. App. to Pet. for Cert. 29a. For these reasons, signing a referendum petition amounts to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98political association\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d protected by the First Amendment. Citizens Against Rent Control, supra, at 295 (quoting Buckley v. Valeo, 424 U. S. 1, 15 (1976) (per curiam)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has long recognized the \xe2\x80\x9cvital relationship between\xe2\x80\x9d political association \xe2\x80\x9cand privacy in one\xe2\x80\x99s associations,\xe2\x80\x9d NAACP v. Alabama ex rel. Patterson, 357 U. S. 449, 462 (1958), and held that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Constitution protects against the compelled disclosure of political associations and beliefs,\xe2\x80\x9d Brown v. Socialist Workers \xe2\x80\x9974 Campaign Comm. (Ohio), 459 U. S. 87, 91 (1982). This constitutional protection \xe2\x80\x9cyield[s] only to a subordinating interest of the State that is compelling, and then only if there is a substantial relation between the information sought and an overriding and compelling state interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 91\xe2\x80\x9392 (internal quotation marks, citations, and brackets omitted). Thus, unlike the Court, I read our precedents to require application of strict scrutiny to laws that compel disclosure of protected First Amendment association. Buckley v. American Constitutional Law Foundation, Inc., 525 U. S. 182, 206, 212 (1999) (ACLF) (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). Under that standard, a disclosure requirement passes constitutional muster only if it is narrowly tailored\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the least restrictive means\xe2\x80\x94to serve a compelling state interest. See id., at 206.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Washington\xe2\x80\x99s application of the PRA to a referendum petition does not survive strict scrutiny.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Washington first contends that it has a compelling interest in \xe2\x80\x9ctransparency and accountability,\xe2\x80\x9d which it claims encompasses several subordinate interests: preserving the integrity of its election process, preventing corruption, deterring fraud, and correcting mistakes by the secretary of state or by petition signers. See Brief for Respondent Reed 40\xe2\x80\x9342; 57\xe2\x80\x9359.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true that a State has a substantial interest in regulating its referendum and initiative processes \xe2\x80\x9cto protect the[ir] integrity and reliability.\xe2\x80\x9d ACLF, 525 U. S., at 191. But Washington points to no precedent from this Court recognizing \xe2\x80\x9ccorrecting errors\xe2\x80\x9d as a distinct compelling interest that could support disclosure regulations. And our cases strongly suggest that preventing corruption and deterring fraud bear less weight in this particular electoral context: the signature-gathering stage of a referendum or initiative drive. The Court has twice observed that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the risk of fraud or corruption, or the appearance thereof, is more remote at the petition stage of an initiative than at the time of balloting.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 203 (quoting Meyer v. Grant, 486 U. S. 414, 427 (1988)). Similarly, because \xe2\x80\x9c[r]eferenda are held on issues, not candidates for public office,\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9crisk of corruption perceived in cases involving candidate elections simply is not present in a popular vote on a public issue.\xe2\x80\x9d First Nat. Bank of Boston v. Bellotti, 435 U. S. 765, 790 (1978) (citations omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We should not abandon those principles merely because Washington and its amici can point to a mere eight instances of initiative-related fraud, see Brief for Respondent Reed 42; Brief for State of Ohio et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 22\xe2\x80\x9324, among the 809 initiative measures placed on\nstate ballots in this country between 1988 and 2008,\nsee Initiative and Referendum Institute, Initiative Use\n2 (Feb. 2009), online at http://www.iandrinstitute.org/ IRI%20Initiative%20Use%20(1904-2008).pdf (as visited June 21, 2010, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). If anything, these meager figures reinforce the conclusion that the risks of fraud or corruption in the initiative and referendum process are remote and thereby undermine Washington\xe2\x80\x99s claim that those two interests should be considered compelling for purposes of strict scrutiny.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, I am not persuaded that Washington\xe2\x80\x99s interest in protecting the integrity and reliability of its referendum process, as the State has defined that interest, is compelling. But I need not answer that question here. Even assuming the interest is compelling, on-demand disclosure of a referendum petition to any person under the PRA is \xe2\x80\x9ca blunderbuss approach\xe2\x80\x9d to furthering that interest, Colorado Republican Federal Campaign Comm. v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 518 U. S. 604, 642 (1996) (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment and dissenting in part) (internal quotation marks omitted), not the least restrictive means of doing so. The events that prompted petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 complaint in this case demonstrate as much.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As Washington explained during oral argument, after the secretary of state receives signed referendum petitions, his \xe2\x80\x9cfirst step \xe2\x80\xa6 is to take them to his archiving section and to have them digitized. As soon as they\xe2\x80\x99re digitized, they\xe2\x80\x99re available on disks for anyone who requests them\xe2\x80\x9d under the PRA. Tr. of Oral Arg. 30. In this case, two organizations announced their intention to obtain the digitized names and addresses of referendum signers and post them \xe2\x80\x9conline, in a searchable format.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no apparent reason why Washington must broadly disclose referendum signers\xe2\x80\x99 names and addresses in this manner to vindicate the interest that it invokes here. Washington\xe2\x80\x94which is in possession of that information because of referendum regulations that petitioners do not challenge, see supra, at 2\xe2\x80\x94could put the names and addresses of referendum signers into a similar electronic database that state employees could search without subjecting the name and address of each signer to wholesale public disclosure. The secretary could electronically cross-reference the referendum database against the \xe2\x80\x9cstatewide voter registration list\xe2\x80\x9d contained in Washington\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cstatewide voter registration database,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa729A.08.651(1),[Footnote 2] to ensure that each referendum signer meets Washington\xe2\x80\x99s residency and voter registration requirements, see \xc2\xa729A.72.130. Doing so presumably would drastically reduce or eliminate possible errors or mistakes that Washington argues the secretary might make, see Brief for Respondent Reed 42, since it would allow the secretary to verify virtually all of the signatures instead of the mere \xe2\x80\x9c3 to 5%\xe2\x80\x9d he \xe2\x80\x9cordinarily checks,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9 (internal quotation marks omitted).[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An electronic referendum database would also enable the secretary to determine whether multiple entries correspond to a single registered voter, thereby detecting whether a voter had signed the petition more than once. In addition, the database would protect victims of \xe2\x80\x9cforgery\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98bait and switch\xe2\x80\x99 fraud.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. In Washington, \xe2\x80\x9ca unique identifier is assigned to each legally registered voter in the state.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa729A.08.651(4). Washington could create a Web site, linked to the electronic referendum database, where a voter concerned that his name had been fraudulently signed could conduct a search using his unique identifier to ensure that his name was absent from the database\xe2\x80\x94without requiring disclosure of the names and addresses of all the voluntary, legitimate signers.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Washington admits that creating this sort of electronic referendum database \xe2\x80\x9ccould be done.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 51. Implementing such a system would not place a heavy burden on Washington; \xe2\x80\x9cthe Secretary of State\xe2\x80\x99s staff\xe2\x80\x9d already uses an \xe2\x80\x9celectronic voter registration database\xe2\x80\x9d in its \xe2\x80\x9cverification process.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 50.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Washington nevertheless contends that its citizens must \xe2\x80\x9chave access to public records \xe2\x80\xa6 to independently evaluate whether the Secretary properly determined to certify or not to certify a referendum to the ballot.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent Reed 41. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]ithout the access to signed petitions that the PRA provides,\xe2\x80\x9d Washington argues, its \xe2\x80\x9ccitizens could not fulfill their role as the final judge of public business.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But Washington\xe2\x80\x99s Election Code already gives Washington voters access to referendum petition data. Under \xc2\xa729A.72.230, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he verification and canvass of signatures on the [referendum] petition may be observed by persons representing the advocates and opponents of the proposed measure so long as they make no record of the names, addresses, or other information on the petitions or related records except upon\xe2\x80\x9d court order. Each side is entitled to at least two such observers, although the secretary may increase that number if, in his opinion, doing so would not \xe2\x80\x9ccause undue delay or disruption of the verification process.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Washington does not explain why this existing access, which petitioners do not challenge here, is insufficient to permit its citizens to oversee the verification process under \xc2\xa729A.72.230, or to decide intelligently whether to pursue a court challenge under \xc2\xa729A.72.240. Moreover, if Washington had implemented the more narrowly tailored electronic referendum database discussed above, observers could see the secretary of state\xe2\x80\x99s employees examine the data using exactly the same techniques they would use if the data were released to them under the PRA. Obtaining a digitized list to navigate on their own computer would not allow an observer to learn any additional information.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Washington law also contains several other measures that preserve the integrity of the referendum process. First, it is a crime in Washington to forge a signature on a referendum petition, or to knowingly sign one more than once. See \xc2\xa729A.84.230. Second, referendum supporters must gather a large number of valid signatures\xe2\x80\x94four percent of the votes cast for Governor in the immediately preceding gubernatorial election\xe2\x80\x94to place a referendum petition on the ballot. \xc2\xa729A.72.150. Third, Washington\xe2\x80\x99s required referendum petition form limits each petition to a single subject. See \xc2\xa729A.72.130. Fourth, a large, plain-English warning must appear at the top of the referendum petition, alerting signers to the law\xe2\x80\x99s requirements. See \xc2\xa729A.72.140. Fifth, Washington prescribes the text of the declaration that a circulator must submit along with the signed petition sheets. See \xc2\xa729A.72.130. Sixth, Washington prescribes verification and canvassing methods. See \xc2\xa729A.72.230.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s dismissive treatment of those provisions, see ante, at 9, is perplexing, given the analysis that the Court endorsed in ACLF. There, the Court held that two disclosure requirements governing Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s initiative process were unconstitutional, see 525 U. S., at 186\xe2\x80\x93187, specifically finding that they were \xe2\x80\x9cnot warranted by the state interests (administrative efficiency, fraud detection, informing voters) alleged to justify\xe2\x80\x9d them, and emphasizing that its \xe2\x80\x9cjudgment [wa]s informed by other means Colorado employs to accomplish its regulatory purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 192. The entire last section of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion detailed those \xe2\x80\x9cless problematic measures\xe2\x80\x9d by which Colorado \xe2\x80\x9ccan and d[id] meet\xe2\x80\x9d its \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial interests in regulating the ballot-initiative process.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 204 (emphasis added). With one exception\xe2\x80\x94a law deeming an initiative void if the circulator violated any law applicable to the circulation process\xe2\x80\x94those Colorado laws correspond exactly to the Washington regulatory requirements listed above. See id., at 205. Including the observer provision, \xc2\xa729A.72.230, and the provision permitting court review of the secretary\xe2\x80\x99s decision to certify (or not to certify) a referendum petition, \xc2\xa729A.72.240, Washington thus appears to provide even more of the \xe2\x80\x9cless problematic measures\xe2\x80\x9d than Colorado did to \xe2\x80\x9cprotect the integrity of the initiative process,\xe2\x80\x9d ACLF, supra, at 204, and I see no reason why Washington\xe2\x80\x99s identical provisions should not \xe2\x80\x9cinform\xe2\x80\x9d the analysis here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is readily apparent that Washington can vindicate its stated interest in \xe2\x80\x9ctransparency and accountability\xe2\x80\x9d through a number of more narrowly tailored means than wholesale public disclosure. Accordingly, this interest cannot justify applying the PRA to a referendum petition.

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Washington also contends that it has a compelling interest in \xe2\x80\x9cproviding relevant information to Washington voters,\xe2\x80\x9d and that on-demand disclosure to the public is a narrowly tailored means of furthering that interest. Brief for Respondent Reed 44. This argument is easily dispatched, since this Court has already rejected it in a similar context.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 514 U. S. 334 (1995), the Court held that an Ohio law prohibiting anonymous political pamphleting violated the First Amendment. One of the interests Ohio had invoked to justify that law was identical to Washington\xe2\x80\x99s here: the \xe2\x80\x9cinterest in providing the electorate with relevant information.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 348. The Court called that interest \xe2\x80\x9cplainly insufficient to support the constitutionality of [Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s] disclosure requirement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 349. \xe2\x80\x9cThe simple interest in providing voters with additional relevant information does not justify a state requirement that a writer make statements or disclosures she would otherwise omit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 348. \xe2\x80\x9cDon\xe2\x80\x99t underestimate the common man,\xe2\x80\x9d we advised. Id., at 348, n.\xc2\xa011 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cPeople are intelligent enough to evaluate the source of an anonymous writing. They can see it is anonymous. They know it is anonymous. They can evaluate its anonymity along with its message. \xe2\x80\xa6 And then, once they have done so, it is for them to decide what is \xe2\x80\x98responsible,\xe2\x80\x99 what is valuable, and what is truth.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\nSee also Bellotti, 435 U. S., at 777 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe inherent worth of the speech in terms of its capacity for informing the public does not depend upon the identity of its source\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This observation applies equally to referendum measures. People are intelligent enough to evaluate the merits of a referendum without knowing who supported it. Thus, just as this informational interest did not justify the Ohio law in McIntyre, it does not justify applying the PRA to referendum petitions.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The foregoing analysis applies in every case involving disclosure of a referendum measure\xe2\x80\x99s supporters, as it must for petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 facial challenge to succeed. See Washington State Grange, 552 U. S., at 449 (quoting Salerno, 481 U. S., at 745). Washington does not argue that the strength of its transparency and accountability interest rises or falls based on the topic of a referendum. Nor would such an argument be convincing. We have no basis to assume that Washington\xe2\x80\x99s interest in maintaining the integrity of its referendum process is high for a charter-school referendum but low for an unemployment insurance referendum, or that a library or land-use referendum is more likely to be a target of fraud or corruption than a referendum on insurance coverage and benefits. See ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. The strength of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s interest remains constant across all types of referendum measures.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0So too does the strength of a signer\xe2\x80\x99s First Amendment interest. The First Amendment rights at issue here are associational rights, and a long, unbroken line of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents holds that privacy of association is protected under the First Amendment. See supra, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. The loss of associational privacy that comes with disclosing referendum petitions to the general public under the PRA constitutes the same harm as to each signer of each referendum, regardless of the topic. To be sure, a referendum signer may be more willing to disclose to the general public his political association with persons signing certain referendum measures than his association with others. But that choice belongs to the voter; the State may not make it for him by ascribing a lower level of First Amendment protection to an associational interest that some think a voter may be (or should be) more willing to disclose. Cf. Rosenberger v. Rector and Visitors of Univ. of Va., 515 U. S. 819, 828 (1995) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn the realm of private speech or expression, government regulation may not favor one speaker over another\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the less restrictive means available to vindicate Washington\xe2\x80\x99s transparency and accountability interest can be employed for all referendum measures, regardless of topic. There is nothing measure-specific about an electronic database or additional observers. And the forgery prohibition and other existing requirements in Washington law that help \xe2\x80\x9cprotect the integrity of the initiative process,\xe2\x80\x9d ACLF, 525 U. S., at 204, apply equally to all referendum measures.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the strength of Washington\xe2\x80\x99s interest in transparency and a signer\xe2\x80\x99s individual First Amendment interest in privacy of political association remain constant across all referendum topics, and because less restrictive means to protect the integrity of the referendum process are not topic specific, I would hold that on-demand public disclosure of referendum petitions under the PRA is not narrowly tailored for any referendum.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Significant practical problems will result from requiring as-applied challenges to protect referendum signers\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional rights.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s approach will \xe2\x80\x9crequire substantial litigation over an extended time\xe2\x80\x9d before a potential signer of any referendum will learn whether, if he signs a referendum, his associational privacy right will remain intact. Citizens United v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 9). And the tenacious litigant\xe2\x80\x99s reward for trying to protect his First Amendment rights? An \xe2\x80\x9cinterpretive process [that] itself would create an inevitable, pervasive, and serious risk of chilling protected speech pending the drawing of fine distinctions that, in the end, would themselves be questionable.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310). The large number of such fine and questionable distinctions in these types of cases reinforces my view that as-applied challenges provide no more than \xe2\x80\x9ca hollow assurance\xe2\x80\x9d that referendum signers\xe2\x80\x99 First Amendment rights will be protected. Id., at __ (slip op., at 5) (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). Consider just a few examples.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Washington, a referendum sponsor must file the proposed referendum with the secretary of state before collecting signatures. See \xc2\xa729A.72.010. May the sponsor seek an injunction against disclosure through an as-applied challenge before filing the proposed measure, or simultaneously with its filing? Because signature-gathering will not have started, the sponsor will not be able to present any evidence specific to signers or potential signers of that particular referendum showing \xe2\x80\x9ca reasonable probability that the compelled disclosure [of personal information] will subject them to threats, harassment, or reprisals from either Government officials or private parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11 (internal quotation marks omitted). Thus, to succeed at that stage of litigation, plaintiffs must point to (at least) one other instance of harassment arising from a similar referendum. The Court has never held that such evidence would be acceptable; but if it is, that necessarily means that some signers, at some point, will have suffered actual \xe2\x80\x9cthreats, harassment, and reprisals\xe2\x80\x9d for engaging in protected First Amendment activity.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If the sponsor must wait at least until signature-gathering has started on his referendum to file an as-applied challenge, it is still unclear what sort of evidence of \xe2\x80\x9cthreats, harassment, or reprisals\xe2\x80\x9d directed toward his supporters would satisfy the Court\xe2\x80\x99s standard. How many instances of \xe2\x80\x9cthreats, harassment or reprisals\xe2\x80\x9d must a signer endure before a court may grant relief on an as-applied challenge? And how dispersed throughout the group of the necessary 120,000 signers, see ante, at 3, must these threats be?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0More importantly, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s standard does not appear to require actual \xe2\x80\x9cthreats, harassment, or reprisals,\xe2\x80\x9d but merely a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonable probability\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that disclosure of the signers\xe2\x80\x99 names and addresses will lead to such activity. Ante, at 11 (emphasis added). What sort of evidence suffices to satisfy this apparently more relaxed, though perhaps more elusive, standard? Does one instance of actual harassment directed toward one signer mean that the \xe2\x80\x9creasonable probability\xe2\x80\x9d requirement is met? And again, how widespread must this \xe2\x80\x9creasonable probability\xe2\x80\x9d be? The Court does not answer any of these questions, leaving a vacuum to be filled on a case-by-case basis. This will, no doubt, result in the \xe2\x80\x9cdrawing of\xe2\x80\x9d arbitrary and \xe2\x80\x9cquestionable\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cfine distinctions\xe2\x80\x9d by even the most well-intentioned district or circuit judge. Citizens United, 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 9\xe2\x80\x9310).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, as I have previously explained, the state of technology today creates at least some probability that signers of every referendum will be subjected to threats, harassment, or reprisals if their personal information is disclosed. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[T]he advent of the Internet\xe2\x80\x99 enables\xe2\x80\x9d rapid dissemination of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the information needed\xe2\x80\x99 to\xe2\x80\x9d threaten or harass every referendum signer. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 6) (opinion of Thomas, J.). \xe2\x80\x9cThus, \xe2\x80\x98disclosure permits citizens \xe2\x80\xa6 to react to the speech of [their political opponents] in a proper\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x94or undeniably improper\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x98way\xe2\x80\x99 long before a plaintiff could prevail on an as-applied challenge.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court apparently disagrees, asserting that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no reason to assume that any burdens imposed by disclosure of typical referendum petitions would be remotely like the burdens plaintiffs fear in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. That conclusion rests on the premise that some referendum measures are so benign that the fact of public dis-closure will not chill protected First Amendment activity. I am not convinced that this premise is correct.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The historical evidence shows that the referendum and initiative process first gained popularity as a means of \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[ing] an occasional safety valve for interests that failed to get a fair hearing in the legislatures.\xe2\x80\x9d T. Cronin, Direct Democracy: The Politics of Initiative, Referendum, and Recall 59 (1989). Unsurprisingly, such interests tended to be controversial by nature. Early examples include \xe2\x80\x9cthe single tax, prohibition, women\xe2\x80\x99s suffrage, prolabor legislation, and the graduated income tax.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 58. And proponents of initiative measures tended to include politically marginalized groups such as the \xe2\x80\x9cFarmer\xe2\x80\x99s Alliance\xe2\x80\x9d in rural states; \xe2\x80\x9c[t]housands of labor federations, notably the miners\xe2\x80\x9d; and \xe2\x80\x9cthe Women\xe2\x80\x99s Suffrage Association,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9csaw the initiative and referendum as a possible new means to overcome\xe2\x80\x9d repeated failed attempts in state legislatures to secure for women the right to vote. Id., at 50\xe2\x80\x9351.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These characteristics of initiative and referendum drives persist today. Consider, for example, the goal of increasing ethics in government\xe2\x80\x94a seemingly laudable and unobjectionable goal. So thought some citizens of Utah, who, frustrated with the state legislature\xe2\x80\x99s failure to pass ethics laws commensurate with their preferences, filed a \xe2\x80\x9c21-page initiative target[ing] legislative conduct with a broad array of reforms that would significantly change how business gets done on Utah\xe2\x80\x99s Capitol Hill.\xe2\x80\x9d McKitrick, Suit Demands Secrecy for Ethics Petition Signers, Salt Lake Tribune, Apr. 15, 2010, p.\xc2\xa0A4 (hereinafter Salt Lake Tribune). But Utah law provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]nitiative packets,\xe2\x80\x9d which contain the names and addresses (and, in some cases, birthdates) of petition signers, \xe2\x80\x9care public once they are delivered to the county clerks\xe2\x80\x9d for verification and canvassing. Utah Code Ann. \xc2\xa720A\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x93206(7) (2009 Lexis Supp. Pamphlet).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The attorneys sponsoring that initiative moved for an injunction to prevent disclosure of the initiative packets under \xc2\xa720A\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x93206(7) because, they claimed, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]he [state] Republican Party has said it will target our folks.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Salt Lake Tribune, at A4. According to these attorneys, a facially benign initiative may well result in political retribution and retaliation in a State where Republicans currently hold the offices of Governor, Lieutenant Governor, attorney general, state treasurer, state auditor, and a supermajority in both the Utah House of Representatives (71%) and the Utah Senate (72%), see State Yellow Book: Who\xe2\x80\x99s Who in the Executive and Legislative Branches of the 50 State Governments 650\xe2\x80\x93651, 1292\xe2\x80\x931294 (Spring 2010), as well as four of the five seats in the State\xe2\x80\x99s delegation to the United States Congress, see GPO, 2009\xe2\x80\x932010 Official Congressional Directory, 111th Cong., pp.\xc2\xa0299, 307 (2009).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The difficulty in predicting which referendum measures will prove controversial\xe2\x80\x94combined with Washington\xe2\x80\x99s default position that signed referendum petitions will be disclosed on-demand, thereby allowing anyone to place this information on the Internet for broad dissemination\xe2\x80\x94raises the significant probability that today\xe2\x80\x99s decision will \xe2\x80\x9cinhibit the exercise of legitimate First Amendment activity\xe2\x80\x9d with respect to referendum and initiative petitions. Colorado Republican, 518 U. S., at 634 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment and dissenting in part). \xe2\x80\x9c[D]isclosure requirements enable private citizens and elected officials to implement political strategies specifically calculated to curtail campaign-related activity and prevent the lawful, peaceful exercise of First Amendment rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens United, 558 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 5) (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). Our cases have long recognized this reality;[Footnote 4] as the Court recently reiterated, the First Amendment does not require \xe2\x80\x9ccase-by-case determinations\xe2\x80\x9d if \xe2\x80\x9carchetypical\xe2\x80\x9d First Amendment rights \xe2\x80\x9cwould be chilled in the meantime.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op.,\nat 12).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This chill in protected First Amendment activity harms others besides the dissuaded signer. We have already expressed deep skepticism about restrictions that \xe2\x80\x9cmak[e] it less likely that\xe2\x80\x9d a referendum \xe2\x80\x9cwill garner the number of signatures necessary to place the matter on the ballot, thus limiting [the] ability to make the matter the focus of statewide discussion.\xe2\x80\x9d Meyer, 486 U. S., at 423. Such restrictions \xe2\x80\x9cinevitabl[y] \xe2\x80\xa6 reduc[e] the total quantum of speech on a public issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The very public that the PRA is supposed to serve is thus harmed by the way Washington implements that statute here.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners do not argue that the Constitution gives supporters of referendum petitions a right to act without anyone knowing their identities. Thus, Washington\xe2\x80\x99s requirements that referendum supporters sign their names and addresses to a referendum petition, and that this information be disclosed to the State for canvassing and verification, see Wash. Rev. Code \xc2\xa729A.72.230, are not at issue. And, petitioners do not contend that Washington\xe2\x80\x99s citizens may never obtain access to referendum data. Thus, Washington\xe2\x80\x99s rules allowing access to at least two representative observers from each side, see ibid., and authorizing courts to review the secretary of state\xe2\x80\x99s verification and canvassing decision if those observers are dissatisfied with the secretary\xe2\x80\x99s decision, see \xc2\xa729A.72.240, are also not in question.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is asked to assess the constitutionality of the PRA only with regard to referendum petitions. The question before us is whether all signers of all referendum petitions must resort to \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial litigation over an extended time,\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens United, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 9), to prevent Washington from trenching on their protected First Amendment rights by subjecting their referendum-petition signatures to on-demand public disclosure. In my view, they need not.
