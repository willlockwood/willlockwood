\nNORTHERN INS. CO. OF N.&NBSP;Y. V. CHATHAM COUNTY
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n

NORTHERN INSURANCE COMPANY OF NEW YORK v. CHATHAM COUNTY, GEORGIA

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x931618.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 1, 2006\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 25, 2006

\n

Petitioner insurance company filed this admiralty suit against respondent County seeking damages resulting from a collision between a malfunctioning County drawbridge and a boat insured by petitioner. Granting the County summary judgment, the District Court recognized that Eleventh Amendment immunity from suit does not extend to counties, but relied on Circuit precedent to conclude that sovereign immunity extends to counties and municipalities that, as here, exercise power delegated from the State. The Eleventh Circuit, which was bound by that same precedent, affirmed. It acknowledged that the County did not assert an Eleventh Amendment immunity defense, which would fail because, under other Circuit precedent, the County did not qualify as an \xe2\x80\x9carm of the State.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court of Appeals nonetheless concluded that common law has carved out a \xe2\x80\x9cresidual immunity\xe2\x80\x9d that protects political subdivisions such as the County from suit.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0An entity that does not qualify as an \xe2\x80\x9carm of the State\xe2\x80\x9d for Eleventh Amendment purposes cannot assert sovereign immunity as a defense to an admiralty suit. Pp. 3\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Immunity from suit \xe2\x80\x9cis a fundamental aspect of the sovereignty which the States enjoyed before the ratification of the Constitution, and which they retain today \xe2\x80\xa6 except as altered by the plan of the Convention or certain constitutional Amendments.\xe2\x80\x9d Alden v. Maine, 527 U. S. 706, 713. Thus, the phrase \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Eleventh Amendment immunity\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 is convenient shorthand but something of a misnomer, for the sovereign immunity of the States neither derives from, nor is limited by, the terms of the Eleventh Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 713. Because preratification sovereignty is the source of immunity from suit, only States and arms of the State possess immunity from suits authorized by federal law. See, e.g., id., at 740. Accordingly, sovereign immunity does not extend to counties, see, e.g., Lake Country Estates, Inc. v. Tahoe Regional Planning Agency, 440 U. S. 391, 401, and n.\xc2\xa019, even when they \xe2\x80\x9cexercise a \xe2\x80\x98slice of state power,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 401. The County argues unconvincingly that this Court has recognized a distinct \xe2\x80\x9cresidual\xe2\x80\x9d immunity that permits adoption of a broader test than it applies in the Eleventh Amendment context to determine whether an entity is acting as an arm of the State entitled to immunity. The Court has referenced only the States\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cresiduary and inviolable sovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d that survived the Constitution. See, e.g., Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. South Carolina Ports Authority, 535 U. S. 743, 751. Because the County may claim immunity neither based upon its identity as a county nor under an expansive arm-of-the-State test, it is subject to suit unless it was acting as an arm of the State, as delineated by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, in operating the drawbridge. E.g., Alden, supra, at 756. The County conceded below that it was not entitled to Eleventh Amendment immunity, and both the County and the Eleventh Circuit appear to have understood this concession to be based on the County\xe2\x80\x99s failure to qualify as an \xe2\x80\x9carm of the State\xe2\x80\x9d under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent. Moreover, certiorari was granted in this case premised on the conclusion that the County is not an arm of the State for Eleventh Amendment purposes, and this Court presumes that to be the case. The County\xe2\x80\x99s concession and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s presumption are dispositive. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The County\xe2\x80\x99s alternative argument that the Court should recognize a distinct sovereign immunity against in personam admiralty suits that bars cases arising from a county\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of core state functions with regard to navigable waters is rejected. Such recognition cannot be reconciled with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, which applied the general principle that sovereign immunity does not bar a suit against a city to an admiralty suit as early as Workman v. New York City, 179 U. S. 552, 570. The Court disagrees with the County\xe2\x80\x99s contention that Workman does not govern the instant case under Ex parte New York, 256 U. S. 490, 498, where, in extending sovereign immunity beyond cases \xe2\x80\x9cin law or equity\xe2\x80\x9d to admiralty cases, the Court concluded that Workman involved only substantive admiralty law, not the power of the Court to exercise jurisdiction over a particular defendant. But Workman did so precisely because the Court there held that admiralty courts have jurisdiction over municipal corporations. See 179 U. S., at 565. The Workman Court accordingly distinguished between the question before it\xe2\x80\x94whether admiralty courts may, notwithstanding state law, \xe2\x80\x9credress a wrong committed by one over whom such courts have adequate jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 566, such as a municipal corporation\xe2\x80\x94and the question not before it, but before the Court in Ex parte New York\xe2\x80\x94whether admiralty courts may \xe2\x80\x9cgive redress in a case where jurisdiction over the person or property cannot be exerted,\xe2\x80\x9d 179 U. S., at 566. In the former circumstance, the court should apply general admiralty principles, while in the latter the court lacks the power to do so. See id., at 570; Ex parte New York, supra, at 499\xe2\x80\x93500, 502\xe2\x80\x93503. Because here, as in Workman and in contrast to Ex parte New York, the defendant was an entity generally within the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction, Ex parte New York is inapposite, and Workman compels the conclusion that the County is unprotected by sovereign immunity. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

129 Fed Appx. 602, reversed.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nNORTHERN INS. CO. OF N.&NBSP;Y. V. CHATHAM COUNTY
\n547 U. S. ____ (2006)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 04-1618
\n
\n

NORTHERN INSURANCE COMPANY OF NEW YORK,\nPETITIONER v. CHATHAM COUNTY, GEORGIA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[April 25, 2006]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Northern Insurance Company of New York (Northern) filed suit against respondent Chatham County, Georgia (County), in the United States District Court for the Southern District of Georgia, seeking damages resulting from an alleged tort committed by employees of the County. The District Court granted the County\xe2\x80\x99s motion for summary judgment on the ground that the suit was barred by sovereign immunity. Relying on Circuit precedent, the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed. We granted certiorari to consider \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hether an entity that does not qualify as an \xe2\x80\x98arm of the State\xe2\x80\x99 for Eleventh Amendment purposes can nonetheless assert sovereign immunity as a defense to an admiralty suit.\xe2\x80\x9d 546 U. S. ___ (2005).

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The County owns, operates, and maintains the Causton Bluff Bridge, a drawbridge over the Wilmington River. On October 6, 2002, James Ludwig requested that the bridge be raised to allow his boat to pass. The bridge malfunctioned, a portion falling and colliding with Mr. Ludwig\xe2\x80\x99s boat. As a result of the collision, Mr. Ludwig and his wife incurred damages in excess of $130,000.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ludwigs submitted a claim for those damages to their insurer, Northern, which paid in accordance with the terms of their insurance policy. Northern then sought to recover its costs by filing suit in admiralty against the County in the District Court. The County sought summary judgment, arguing that Northern\xe2\x80\x99s claims were barred by sovereign immunity. The County conceded that Eleventh Amendment immunity did not extend to counties, but nonetheless contended that it was immune under \xe2\x80\x9cthe universal rule of state immunity from suit without the state\xe2\x80\x99s consent.\xe2\x80\x9d Defendant\xe2\x80\x99s Brief in Support of Motion for Summary Judgment, Case No. CV403\xe2\x80\x93099, App. 33a. The District Court agreed, relying on Broward County v. Wickman, 195 F.\xc2\xa02d 614 (CA5 1952), to conclude that sovereign immunity extends to counties and municipalities that, as here, \xe2\x80\x9cexercis[e] power delegated from the State.\xe2\x80\x9d Zurich Ins. Co. v. Chatham County, No. CV403\xe2\x80\x9399, App. 77a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Eleventh Circuit, which was bound to follow Wickman as Circuit precedent, affirmed.[Footnote 1] The Court of Appeals acknowledged that the County did not assert an Eleventh Amendment immunity defense, which would fail because, under Circuit precedent, the County did not qualify as an arm of the State. Zurich Ins. Co. v. Chatham County, No. 04\xe2\x80\x9313308 (Jan. 28, 2005), App. 83a, n.\xc2\xa01, judgt. order reported at 129 Fed. Appx. 602. The Court of Appeals nonetheless concluded that \xe2\x80\x9ccommon law has carved out a \xe2\x80\x98residual immunity,\xe2\x80\x99 which would protect a political subdivision such as Chatham County from suit.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 83a. We granted certiorari to review the judgment of the Court of Appeals. 546 U. S. ___ (2005).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases have recognized that the immunity of States from suit \xe2\x80\x9cis a fundamental aspect of the sovereignty which the States enjoyed before the ratification of the Constitution, and which they retain today \xe2\x80\xa6 except as altered by the plan of the Convention or certain constitutional Amendments.\xe2\x80\x9d Alden v. Maine, 527 U. S. 706, 713 (1999); see Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Florida, 517 U. S. 44, 55\xe2\x80\x9356 (1996); Principality of Monaco v. Mississippi, 292 U. S. 313, 322\xe2\x80\x93323 (1934). Consistent with this recognition, which no party asks us to reexamine today, we have observed that the phrase \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Eleventh Amendment immunity\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 is convenient shorthand but something of a misnomer, for the sovereign immunity of the States neither derives from, nor is limited by, the terms of the Eleventh Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Alden, supra, at 713.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A consequence of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s recognition of preratification sovereignty as the source of immunity from suit is that only States and arms of the State possess immunity from suits authorized by federal law. See Alden, supra, at 740; Mt. Healthy City Bd. of Ed. v. Doyle, 429 U. S. 274, 280 (1977). Accordingly, this Court has repeatedly refused to extend sovereign immunity to counties. See Lake Country Estates, Inc. v. Tahoe Regional Planning Agency, 440 U. S. 391, 401 (1979); id., at 401, n.\xc2\xa019 (gathering cases); Workman v. New York City, 179 U. S. 552, 565 (1900); Lincoln County v. Luning, 133 U. S. 529, 530 (1890). See also Jinks v. Richland County, 538 U. S. 456, 466 (2003) (\xe2\x80\x9c[M]unicipalities, unlike States, do not enjoy a constitutionally protected immunity from suit\xe2\x80\x9d). This is true even when, as respondent alleges here, \xe2\x80\x9csuch entities exercise a \xe2\x80\x98slice of state power.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Lake Country Estates, supra, at 401.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The County argues that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s cases recognize a distinct \xe2\x80\x9cresidual\xe2\x80\x9d immunity that permits adoption of a broader test than we apply in the Eleventh Amendment context to determine whether an entity is acting as an arm of the State and is accordingly entitled to immunity.[Footnote 2] Brief for Respondent 28. But this Court\xe2\x80\x99s use of that term does not suggest the County\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion; instead, this Court has referenced only the States\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cresiduary and inviolable sovereignty\xe2\x80\x9d that survived the Constitution. See The Federalist No. 39, p.\xc2\xa0245 (C. Rossiter ed. 1961) (J. Madison); Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. South Carolina Ports Authority, 535 U. S. 743, 751 (2002).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the County may claim immunity neither based upon its identity as a county nor under an expansive arm-of-the-State test, the County is subject to suit unless it was acting as an arm of the State, as delineated by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, in operating the drawbridge. Alden, supra, at 756; Lake Country Estates, supra, at 400\xe2\x80\x93401. The County conceded below that it was not entitled to Eleventh Amendment immunity, and both the County and the Court of Appeals appear to have understood this concession to be based on the County\xe2\x80\x99s failure to qualify as an arm of the State under our precedent. See App. 83a, n.\xc2\xa01 (recognizing that the County rightly disclaimed an Eleventh Amendment immunity defense because such a defense would be inconsistent with the court\xe2\x80\x99s holding in Vierling v. Celebrity Cruises, Inc., 339 F.\xc2\xa03d 1309 (CA11 2003), that the Broward County Port Authority was not an arm of the State); Brief of Appellee Chatham County in No. 04\xe2\x80\x9313308DD (CA11), p.\xc2\xa013 (distinguishing Vierling in part because it dealt with the question of Eleventh Amendment immunity); see also Brief for Respondent 8 (implicitly conceding that respondent is not an arm of the State under our Eleventh Amendment jurisprudence). Moreover, the question on which we granted certiorari is premised on the conclusion that the County is not \xe2\x80\x9can \xe2\x80\x98arm of the State\xe2\x80\x99 for Eleventh Amendment purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d 546 U. S. ___ (2005), and we presume that to be the case. Accordingly, the County\xe2\x80\x99s concession and the presumption un-\nderlying the question on which we granted review are dispositive.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As an alternative ground for affirmance, the County asks the Court to recognize a distinct sovereign immunity against in personam admiralty suits that bars cases arising from a county\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of core state functions with regard to navigable waters. Recognition of a distinct immunity in admiralty cases cannot be reconciled with our precedents. Immunity in admiralty, like other sovereign immunity, is simply an application of \xe2\x80\x9cthe fundamental rule\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cthe entire judicial power granted by the Constitution does not embrace authority to entertain a suit brought by private parties against a State without consent given.\xe2\x80\x9d Ex parte New York, 256 U. S. 490, 497\xe2\x80\x93500 (1921). Accordingly, this Court has resolved sovereign immunity questions in admiralty by relying upon principles set out in this Court\xe2\x80\x99s sovereign immunity cases, rather than by examining the history or jurisprudence specific to suits in admiralty. See Federal Maritime Comm\xe2\x80\x99n v. South Carolina Ports Authority, supra, at 754\xe2\x80\x93769 (an admiralty suit relying heavily on Alden, supra (plaintiff raised a Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 claim), and Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Florida, 517 U. S. 44 (1996) (plaintiff alleged violation of the Indian Gaming Regulatory Act)). Indeed, the Court applied the general principle that sovereign immunity does not bar a suit against a city to an admiralty suit as early as Workman v. New York City, 179 U. S. 552, which held that such immunity \xe2\x80\x9cafforded no reason for denying redress in a court of admiralty for the wrong which \xe2\x80\xa6 [had] been committed\xe2\x80\x9d by the city of New York, id., at 570.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The County nonetheless contends\xe2\x80\x94and the Eleventh Circuit, in reliance upon the Fifth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis in Wickman, held\xe2\x80\x94that the reach of Workman is limited, and that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Ex parte New York, supra, demonstrates that Workman does not govern the instant case. See Wickman, 195 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 615. We disagree. Ex parte New York extended sovereign immunity beyond cases \xe2\x80\x9cin law or equity\xe2\x80\x9d to cases in admiralty. As the County points out, Ex parte New York concluded that Workman involved only the substantive law of admiralty, and not the power of the Court to exercise jurisdiction over a particular defendant. Ex parte New York, supra, at 498. But Workman dealt only with the substantive law of admiralty precisely because the Workman Court held that admiralty courts have jurisdiction over municipal corporations. See 179 U. S., at 565 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]s a general rule, municipal corporations, like individuals, may be sued; in other words \xe2\x80\xa6 they are amenable to judicial process for the purpose of compelling performance of their obligations\xe2\x80\x9d). The Workman Court accordingly distinguished between the question before it\xe2\x80\x94whether courts of admiralty may, notwithstanding state law, \xe2\x80\x9credress a wrong committed by one over whom such courts have adequate jurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 566, such as a municipal corporation\xe2\x80\x94and the question not before it, but before the Court in Ex parte New York\xe2\x80\x94whether courts of admiralty may \xe2\x80\x9cgive redress in a case where jurisdiction over the person or property cannot be exerted,\xe2\x80\x9d 179 U. S., at 566. In the former circumstance, the court should apply general admiralty principles, while in the latter the court lacks the power to do so. See id., at 570; Ex parte New York, supra, at 499\xe2\x80\x93500, 502\xe2\x80\x93503. Because here, as in Workman and in contrast to Ex parte New York, the defendant was an entity generally within the jurisdiction of the District Court, Ex parte New York is inapposite, and Workman compels the conclusion that the County is unprotected by sovereign immunity.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the County has failed to demonstrate that it was acting as an arm of the State when it operated the Causton Bluff Bridge, the County is not entitled to immunity from Northern\xe2\x80\x99s suit. Accordingly, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.
