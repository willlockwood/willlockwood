CUTTER et\xc2\xa0al. v. WILKINSON, DIRECTOR, OHIO\nDEPARTMENT OF REHABILITATION AND\nCORRECTION, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 03\xe2\x80\x939877.Argued March 21, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 31, 2005

\n

Section 3 of the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act of 2000 (RLUIPA), 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2), provides in part: \xe2\x80\x9cNo government shall impose a substantial burden on the religious exercise of a person residing in or confined to an institution,\xe2\x80\x9d unless the burden furthers \xe2\x80\x9ca compelling governmental interest,\xe2\x80\x9d and does so by \xe2\x80\x9cthe least restrictive means.\xe2\x80\x9d Petitioners, current and former inmates of Ohio state institutions, allege, inter alia, that respondent prison officials violated \xc2\xa73 by failing to accommodate petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 exercise of their \xe2\x80\x9cnonmainstream\xe2\x80\x9d religions in a variety of ways. Respondents moved to dismiss that claim, arguing, among other things, that \xc2\xa73, on its face, improperly advances religion in violation of the First Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Establishment Clause. Rejecting that argument, the District Court stated that RLUIPA permits safety and security\xe2\x80\x94undisputedly compelling state interests\xe2\x80\x94to outweigh an inmate\xe2\x80\x99s claim to a religious accommodation. On the thin record before it, the court could not find that enforcement of RLUIPA, inevitably, would compromise prison security. Reversing on interlocutory appeal, the Sixth Circuit held that \xc2\xa73 impermissibly advances religion by giving greater protection to religious rights than to other constitutionally protected rights, and suggested that affording religious prisoners superior rights might encourage prisoners to become religious.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section 3 of RLUIPA, on its face, qualifies as a permissible accommodation that is not barred by the Establishment Clause. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Foremost, \xc2\xa73 is compatible with the Establishment Clause because it alleviates exceptional government-created burdens on private religious exercise. See, e.g., Board of Ed. of Kiryas Joel Village School Dist. v. Grumet, 512 U. S. 687, 705. Furthermore, the Act on its face does not founder on shoals the Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior decisions have identified: Properly applying RLUIPA, courts must take adequate account of the burdens a requested accommodation may impose on nonbeneficiaries, see Estate of Thornton v. Caldor, Inc., 472 U. S. 703; and they must be satisfied that the Act\xe2\x80\x99s prescriptions are and will be administered neutrally among different faiths, see Kiryas Joel, 512 U. S. 687. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he \xe2\x80\x98exercise of religion\xe2\x80\x99 often involves not only belief and profession but the performance of \xe2\x80\xa6 physical acts [such as] assembling with others for a worship service [or] participating in sacramental use of bread and wine \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Employment Div., Dept. of Human Resources of Ore. v. Smith, 494 U. S. 872, 877. Section 3 covers state-run institutions\xe2\x80\x94mental hospitals, prisons, and the like\xe2\x80\x94in which the government exerts a degree of control unparalleled in civilian society and severely disabling to private religious exercise. 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931(a); \xc2\xa71997. RLUIPA thus protects institutionalized persons who are unable freely to attend to their religious needs and are therefore dependent on the government\xe2\x80\x99s permission and accommodation for exercise of their religion. But the Act does not elevate accommodation of religious observances over an institution\xe2\x80\x99s need to maintain order and safety. An accommodation must be measured so that it does not override other significant interests. See Caldor, 472 U. S., at 709\xe2\x80\x93710. There is no reason to believe that RLUIPA would not be applied in an appropriately balanced way, with particular sensitivity to security concerns. While the Act adopts a \xe2\x80\x9ccompelling interest\xe2\x80\x9d standard, \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931(a), \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ontext matters\xe2\x80\x9d in the application of that standard, see Grutter v. Bollinger, 539 U. S. 306, 327. Lawmakers supporting RLUIPA were mindful of the urgency of discipline, order, safety, and security in penal institutions and anticipated that courts would apply the Act\xe2\x80\x99s standard with due deference to prison administrators\xe2\x80\x99 experience and expertise. Finally, RLUIPA does not differentiate among bona fide faiths. It confers no privileged status on any particular religious sect. Cf. Kiryas Joel, 512 U. S., at 706. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Sixth Circuit misread this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents to require invalidation of RLUIPA as impermissibly advancing religion by giving greater protection to religious rights than to other constitutionally protected rights. Corporation of Presiding Bishop of Church of Jesus Christ of Latter&nbhyph;day Saints v. Amos, 483 U. S. 327, counsels otherwise. There, in upholding against an Establishment Clause challenge a provision exempting religious organizations from the prohibition against religion-based employment discrimination in Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Court held that religious accommodations need not \xe2\x80\x9ccome packaged with benefits to secular entities.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 338. Were the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 view correct, all manner of religious accommodations would fall. For example, Ohio could not, as it now does, accommodate traditionally recognized religions by providing chaplains and allowing worship services. In upholding \xc2\xa73, the Court emphasizes that respondents have raised a facial challenge and have not contended that applying RLUIPA would produce unconstitutional results in any specific case. There is no reason to anticipate that abusive prisoner litigation will overburden state and local institutions. However, should inmate requests for religious accommodations become excessive, impose unjustified burdens on other institutionalized persons, or jeopardize an institution\xe2\x80\x99s effective functioning, the facility would be free to resist the imposition. In that event, adjudication in as-applied challenges would be in order. Pp.\xc2\xa013\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

349 F.\xc2\xa03d 257, reversed and remanded.

JON B. CUTTER, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. REGINALD\nWILKINSON, DIRECTOR, OHIO DEPARTMENT OF\nREHABILITATION AND CORRECTION, et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[May 31, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 3 of the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act of 2000 (RLUIPA), 114 Stat. 804, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2), provides in part: \xe2\x80\x9cNo government shall impose a substantial burden on the religious exercise of a person residing in or confined to an institution,\xe2\x80\x9d unless the burden furthers \xe2\x80\x9ca compelling governmental interest,\xe2\x80\x9d and does so by \xe2\x80\x9cthe least restrictive means.\xe2\x80\x9d Plaintiffs below, petitioners here, are current and former inmates of institutions operated by the Ohio Department of Rehabilitation and Correction and assert that they are adherents of \xe2\x80\x9cnonmainstream\xe2\x80\x9d religions: the Satanist, Wicca, and Asatru religions, and the Church of Jesus Christ Christian.[Footnote 1] They complain that Ohio prison officials (respondents here), in violation of RLUIPA, have failed to accommodate their religious exercise

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cin a variety of different ways, including retaliating and discriminating against them for exercising their nontraditional faiths, denying them access to religious literature, denying them the same opportunities for group worship that are granted to adherents of mainstream religions, forbidding them to adhere to the dress and appearance mandates of their religions, withholding religious ceremonial items that are substantially identical to those that the adherents of mainstream religions are permitted, and failing to provide a chaplain trained in their faith.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 5.

\n

\nFor purposes of this litigation at its current stage, respondents have stipulated that petitioners are members of bona fide religions and that they are sincere in their beliefs. Gerhardt v. Lazaroff, 221 F. Supp. 2d 827, 833 (SD Ohio 2002).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In response to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 complaints, respondent prison officials have mounted a facial challenge to the institutionalized-persons provision of RLUIPA; respondents contend, inter alia, that the Act improperly advances religion in violation of the First Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Establishment Clause. The District Court denied respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion to dismiss petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 complaints, but the Court of Appeals reversed that determination. The appeals court held, as the prison officials urged, that the portion of RLUIPA applicable to institutionalized persons, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931, violates the Establishment Clause. We reverse the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThis Court has long recognized that the government may \xe2\x80\xa6 accommodate religious practices \xe2\x80\xa6 without violating the Establishment Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Hobbie v. Unemployment Appeals Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Fla., 480 U. S. 136, 144\xe2\x80\x93145 (1987). Just last Term, in Locke v. Davey, 540 U. S. 712 (2004), the Court reaffirmed that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is room for play in the joints between\xe2\x80\x9d the Free Exercise and Establishment Clauses, allowing the government to accommodate religion beyond free exercise requirements, without offense to the Establishment Clause. Id., at 718 (quoting Walz v. Tax Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of City of New York, 397 U. S. 664, 669 (1970)). \xe2\x80\x9cAt some point, accommodation may devolve into \xe2\x80\x98an unlawful fostering of religion.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Corporation of Presiding Bishop of Church of Jesus Christ of Latter&nbhyph;day Saints v. Amos, 483 U. S. 327, 334\xe2\x80\x93335 (1987) (quoting Hobbie, 480 U. S., at 145). But \xc2\xa73 of RLUIPA, we hold, does not, on its face, exceed the limits of permissible government accommodation of religious practices.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0RLUIPA is the latest of long-running congressional efforts to accord religious exercise heightened protection from government-imposed burdens, consistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents. Ten years before RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, the Court held, in Employment Div., Dept. of Human Resources of Ore. v. Smith, 494 U. S. 872, 878\xe2\x80\x93882 (1990), that the First Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s Free Exercise Clause does not inhibit enforcement of otherwise valid laws of general application that incidentally burden religious conduct. In particular, we ruled that the Free Exercise Clause did not bar Oregon from enforcing its blanket ban on peyote possession with no allowance for sacramental use of the drug. Accordingly, the State could deny unemployment benefits to persons dismissed from their jobs because of their religiously inspired peyote use. Id., at 874, 890. The Court recognized, however, that the political branches could shield religious exercise through legislative accommodation, for example, by making an exception to proscriptive drug laws for sacramental peyote use. Id., at 890.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Responding to Smith, Congress enacted the Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993 (RFRA), 107 Stat. 1488, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000bb et seq. RFRA \xe2\x80\x9cprohibits \xe2\x80\x98[g]overnment\xe2\x80\x99 from \xe2\x80\x98substantially burden[ing]\xe2\x80\x99 a person\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of religion even if the burden results from a rule of general applicability unless the government can demonstrate the burden \xe2\x80\x98(1) is in furtherance of a compelling governmental interest; and (2) is the least restrictive means of furthering that compelling governmental interest.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d City of Boerne v. Flores, 521 U. S. 507, 515\xe2\x80\x93516 (1997) (brackets in original) (quoting \xc2\xa72000bb\xe2\x80\x931). \xe2\x80\x9c[U]niversal\xe2\x80\x9d in its coverage, RFRA \xe2\x80\x9capplie[d] to all Federal and State law,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 516 (quoting former \xc2\xa72000bb\xe2\x80\x933(a)), but notably lacked a Commerce Clause underpinning or a Spending Clause limitation to recipients of federal funds. In City of Boerne, this Court invalidated RFRA as applied to States and their subdivisions, holding that the Act exceeded Congress\xe2\x80\x99 remedial powers under the Fourteenth Amendment. Id., at 532\xe2\x80\x93536.[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress again responded, this time by enacting RLUIPA. Less sweeping than RFRA, and invoking federal authority under the Spending and Commerce Clauses, RLUIPA targets two areas: Section 2 of the Act concerns land-use regulation, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc;[Footnote 3] \xc2\xa73 relates to religious exercise by institutionalized persons, \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931. Section 3, at issue here, provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o [state or local] government shall impose a substantial burden on the religious exercise of a person residing in or confined to an institution,\xe2\x80\x9d unless the government shows that the burden furthers \xe2\x80\x9ca compelling governmental interest\xe2\x80\x9d and does so by \xe2\x80\x9cthe least restrictive means.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2). The Act defines \xe2\x80\x9creligious exercise\xe2\x80\x9d to include \xe2\x80\x9cany exercise of religion, whether or not compelled by, or central to, a system of religious belief.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x935(7)(A). Section 3 applies when \xe2\x80\x9cthe substantial burden [on religious exercise] is imposed in a program or activity that receives Federal financial assistance,\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 4] or \xe2\x80\x9cthe substantial burden affects, or removal of that substantial burden would affect, commerce with foreign nations, among the several States, or with Indian tribes.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2). \xe2\x80\x9cA person may assert a violation of [RLUIPA] as a claim or defense in a judicial proceeding and obtain appropriate relief against a government.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x932(a).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before enacting \xc2\xa73, Congress documented, in hearings spanning three years, that \xe2\x80\x9cfrivolous or arbitrary\xe2\x80\x9d barriers impeded institutionalized persons\xe2\x80\x99 religious exercise. See 146 Cong. Rec. S7774, S7775 (July 27, 2000) (joint statement of Senator Hatch and Senator Kennedy on RLUIPA) (hereinafter Joint Statement) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhether from indifference, ignorance, bigotry, or lack of resources, some institutions restrict religious liberty in egregious and unnecessary ways.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 5] To secure redress for inmates who encountered undue barriers to their religious observances, Congress carried over from RFRA the \xe2\x80\x9ccompelling governmental interest\xe2\x80\x9d/\xe2\x80\x9cleast restrictive means\xe2\x80\x9d standard. See id., at S7774. Lawmakers anticipated, however, that courts entertaining complaints under \xc2\xa73 would accord \xe2\x80\x9cdue deference to the experience and expertise of prison and jail administrators.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at S7775 (quoting S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 103\xe2\x80\x93111, p. 10 (1993)).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners initially filed suit against respondents asserting claims under the First and Fourteenth Amendments. After RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment, petitioners amended their complaints to include claims under \xc2\xa73. Respondents moved to dismiss the statutory claims, arguing, inter alia, that \xc2\xa73 violates the Establishment Clause. 221 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 846. Pursuant to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72403(a), the United States intervened in the District Court to defend RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality. 349 F.\xc2\xa03d 257, 261 (CA6 2003).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Adopting the report and recommendation of the Magistrate Judge, the District Court rejected the argument that \xc2\xa73 conflicts with the Establishment Clause. 221 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 846\xe2\x80\x93848. As to the Act\xe2\x80\x99s impact on a prison\xe2\x80\x99s staff and general inmate population, the court stated that RLUIPA \xe2\x80\x9cpermits safety and security\xe2\x80\x94which are undisputedly compelling state interests\xe2\x80\x94to outweigh an inmate\xe2\x80\x99s claim to a religious accommodation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 848. On the thin record before it, the court declined to find, as respondents had urged, that enforcement of RLUIPA, inevitably, would compromise prison security. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On interlocutory appeal pursuant to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71292(b), the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit reversed. Citing Lemon v. Kurtzman, 403 U. S. 602 (1971),[Footnote 6] the Court of Appeals held that \xc2\xa73 of RLUIPA \xe2\x80\x9cimpermissibly advanc[es] religion by giving greater protection to religious rights than to other constitutionally protected rights.\xe2\x80\x9d 349 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 264. Affording \xe2\x80\x9creligious prisoners rights superior to those of nonreligious prisoners,\xe2\x80\x9d the court suggested, might \xe2\x80\x9cencourag[e] prisoners to become religious in order to enjoy greater rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 266.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari to resolve the conflict among Courts of Appeals on the question whether RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s institutionalized-persons provision, \xc2\xa73 of the Act, is consistent with the Establishment Clause of the First Amendment. 543 U. S. ___ (2004).[Footnote 7] Compare 349 F.\xc2\xa03d 257, with Madison v. Riter, 355 F.\xc2\xa03d 310, 313 (CA4 2003) (\xc2\xa73 of RLUIPA does not violate the Establishment Clause); Charles v. Verhagen, 348 F.\xc2\xa03d 601, 610\xe2\x80\x93611 (CA7 2003) (same); Mayweathers v. Newland, 314 F.\xc2\xa03d 1062, 1068\xe2\x80\x931069 (CA9 2002) (same). We now reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Religion Clauses of the First Amendment provide: \xe2\x80\x9cCongress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof.\xe2\x80\x9d The first of the two Clauses, commonly called the Establishment Clause, commands a separation of church and state. The second, the Free Exercise Clause, requires government respect for, and noninterference with, the religious beliefs and practices of our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s people. While the two Clauses express complementary values, they often exert conflicting pressures. See Locke, 540 U. S., at 718 (\xe2\x80\x9cThese two Clauses \xe2\x80\xa6 are frequently in tension.\xe2\x80\x9d); Walz, 397 U. S., at 668\xe2\x80\x93669 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court has struggled to find a neutral course between the two Religion Clauses, both of which are cast in absolute terms, and either of which, if expanded to a logical extreme, would tend to clash with the other.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our decisions recognize that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is room for play in the joints\xe2\x80\x9d between the Clauses, id., at 669, some space for legislative action neither compelled by the Free Exercise Clause nor prohibited by the Establishment Clause. See, e.g., Smith, 494 U. S., at 890 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] society that believes in the negative protection accorded to religious belief can be expected to be solicitous of that value in its legislation\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d); Amos, 483 U. S., at 329\xe2\x80\x93330 (Federal Government may exempt secular nonprofit activities of religious organizations from Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on religious discrimination in employment); Sherbert v. Verner, 374 U. S. 398, 422 (1963) (Harlan, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe constitutional obligation of \xe2\x80\x98neutrality\xe2\x80\x99 is not so narrow a channel that the slightest deviation from an absolutely straight course leads to condemnation.\xe2\x80\x9d (citation omitted)). In accord with the majority of Courts of Appeals that have ruled on the question, see supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938, we hold that \xc2\xa73 of RLUIPA fits within the corridor between the Religion Clauses: On its face, the Act qualifies as a permissible legislative accommodation of religion that is not barred by the Establishment Clause.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Foremost, we find RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s institutionalized-persons provision compatible with the Establishment Clause because it alleviates exceptional government-created burdens on private religious exercise. See Board of Ed. of Kiryas Joel Village School Dist. v. Grumet, 512 U. S. 687, 705 (1994) (government need not \xe2\x80\x9cbe oblivious to impositions that legitimate exercises of state power may place on religious belief and practice\xe2\x80\x9d); Amos, 483 U. S., at 349 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment) (removal of government-imposed burdens on religious exercise is more likely to be perceived \xe2\x80\x9cas an accommodation of the exercise of religion rather than as a Government endorsement of religion\xe2\x80\x9d). Furthermore, the Act on its face does not founder on shoals our prior decisions have identified: Properly applying RLUIPA, courts must take adequate account of the burdens a requested accommodation may impose on nonbeneficiaries, see Estate of Thornton v. Caldor, Inc., 472 U. S. 703 (1985); and they must be satisfied that the Act\xe2\x80\x99s prescriptions are and will be administered neutrally among different faiths, see Kiryas Joel, 512 U. S. 687.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he \xe2\x80\x98exercise of religion\xe2\x80\x99 often involves not only belief and profession but the performance of \xe2\x80\xa6 physical acts [such as] assembling with others for a worship service [or] participating in sacramental use of bread and wine \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Smith, 494 U. S., at 877. Section 3 covers state-run institutions\xe2\x80\x94mental hospitals, prisons, and the like\xe2\x80\x94in which the government exerts a degree of control unparalleled in civilian society and severely disabling to private religious exercise. 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931(a); \xc2\xa71997; see Joint Statement S7775 (\xe2\x80\x9cInstitutional residents\xe2\x80\x99 right to practice their faith is at the mercy of those running the institution.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 9] RLUIPA thus protects institutionalized persons who are unable freely to attend to their religious needs and are therefore dependent on the government\xe2\x80\x99s permission and accommodation for exercise of their religion.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We note in this regard the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s accommodation of religious practice by members of the military. See, e.g., 10 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73073 (referring to Army chaplains); Katcoff v. Marsh, 755 F.\xc2\xa02d 223, 225\xe2\x80\x93229 (CA2 1985) (describing the Army chaplaincy program). In Goldman v. Weinberger, 475 U. S. 503 (1986), we held that the Free Exercise Clause did not require the Air Force to exempt an Orthodox Jewish officer from uniform dress regulations so that he could wear a yarmulke indoors. In a military community, the Court observed, \xe2\x80\x9cthere is simply not the same [individual] autonomy as there is in the larger civilian community.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 507 (brackets in original; internal quotation marks omitted). Congress responded to Goldman by prescribing that \xe2\x80\x9ca member of the armed forces may wear an item of religious apparel while wearing the uniform,\xe2\x80\x9d unless \xe2\x80\x9cthe wearing of the item would interfere with the performance [of] military duties [or] the item of apparel is not neat and conservative.\xe2\x80\x9d 10 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7774(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not read RLUIPA to elevate accommodation of religious observances over an institution\xe2\x80\x99s need to maintain order and safety. Our decisions indicate that an accommodation must be measured so that it does not override other significant interests. In Caldor, the Court struck down a Connecticut law that \xe2\x80\x9carm[ed] Sabbath observers with an absolute and unqualified right not to work on whatever day they designate[d] as their Sabbath.\xe2\x80\x9d 472 U. S., at 709. We held the law invalid under the Establishment Clause because it \xe2\x80\x9cunyielding[ly] weigh[ted]\xe2\x80\x9d the interests of Sabbatarians \xe2\x80\x9cover all other interests.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 710.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have no cause to believe that RLUIPA would not be applied in an appropriately balanced way, with particular sensitivity to security concerns. While the Act adopts a \xe2\x80\x9ccompelling governmental interest\xe2\x80\x9d standard, see supra, at 5, \xe2\x80\x9c[c]ontext matters\xe2\x80\x9d in the application of that standard. See Grutter v. Bollinger, 539 U. S. 306, 327 (2003).[Footnote 11] Lawmakers supporting RLUIPA were mindful of the urgency of discipline, order, safety, and security in penal institutions. See, e.g., 139 Cong. Rec. 26190 (1993) (remarks of Senator Hatch). They anticipated that courts would apply the Act\xe2\x80\x99s standard with \xe2\x80\x9cdue deference to the experience and expertise of prison and jail administrators in establishing necessary regulations and procedures to maintain good order, security and discipline, consistent with consideration of costs and limited resources.\xe2\x80\x9d Joint Statement S7775 (quoting S.\xc2\xa0Rep. No. 103\xe2\x80\x93111, p. 10 (1993)).[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, RLUIPA does not differentiate among bona fide faiths. In Kiryas Joel, we invalidated a state law that carved out a separate school district to serve exclusively a community of highly religious Jews, the Satmar Hasidim. We held that the law violated the Establishment Clause, 512 U. S., at 690, in part because it \xe2\x80\x9csingle[d] out a particular religious sect for special treatment,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 706 (footnote omitted). RLUIPA presents no such defect. It confers no privileged status on any particular religious sect, and singles out no bona fide faith for disadvantageous treatment.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sixth Circuit misread our precedents to require invalidation of RLUIPA as \xe2\x80\x9cimpermissibly advancing religion by giving greater protection to religious rights than to other constitutionally protected rights.\xe2\x80\x9d 349 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 264. Our decision in Amos counsels otherwise. There, we upheld against an Establishment Clause challenge a provision exempting \xe2\x80\x9creligious organizations from Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition against discrimination in employment on the basis of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d 483 U. S., at 329. The District Court in Amos, reasoning in part that the exemption improperly \xe2\x80\x9csingle[d] out religious entities for a benefit,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 338, had \xe2\x80\x9cdeclared the statute unconstitutional as applied to secular activity,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 333. Religious accommodations, we held, need not \xe2\x80\x9ccome packaged with benefits to secular entities.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 338; see Madison, 355 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 318 (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is no requirement that legislative protections for fundamental rights march in lockstep.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Were the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 view the correct reading of our decisions, all manner of religious accommodations would fall. Congressional permission for members of the military to wear religious apparel while in uniform would fail, see 10 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7774, as would accommodations Ohio itself makes. Ohio could not, as it now does, accommodate \xe2\x80\x9ctraditionally recognized\xe2\x80\x9d religions, 221 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 832: The State provides inmates with chaplains \xe2\x80\x9cbut not with publicists or political consultants,\xe2\x80\x9d and allows \xe2\x80\x9cprisoners to assemble for worship, but not for political rallies.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for United States 5.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In upholding RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s institutionalized-persons provision, we emphasize that respondents \xe2\x80\x9chave raised a facial challenge to [the Act\xe2\x80\x99s] constitutionality, and have not contended that under the facts of any of [petitioners\xe2\x80\x99] specific cases \xe2\x80\xa6 [that] applying RLUIPA would produce unconstitutional results.\xe2\x80\x9d 221 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 831. The District Court, noting the underdeveloped state of the record, concluded: A finding \xe2\x80\x9cthat it is factually impossible to provide the kind of accommodations that RLUIPA will require without significantly compromising prison security or the levels of service provided to other inmates\xe2\x80\x9d cannot be made at this juncture. Id., at 848 (emphasis added).[Footnote 13] We agree.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cFor more than a decade, the federal Bureau of Prisons has managed the largest correctional system in the Nation under the same heightened scrutiny standard as RLUIPA without compromising prison security, public safety, or the constitutional rights of other prisoners.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 24 (citation omitted). The Congress that enacted RLUIPA was aware of the Bureau\xe2\x80\x99s experience. See Joint Statement S7776 (letter from Department of Justice to Senator Hatch) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e do not believe [RLUIPA] would have an unreasonable impact on prison operations. RFRA has been in effect in the Federal prison system for six years and compliance with that statute has not been an unreasonable burden to the Federal prison system.\xe2\x80\x9d). We see no reason to anticipate that abusive prisoner litigation will overburden the operations of state and local institutions. The procedures mandated by the Prison Litigation Reform Act of 1995, we note, are designed to inhibit frivolous filings.[Footnote 14]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Should inmate requests for religious accommodations become excessive, impose unjustified burdens on other institutionalized persons, or jeopardize the effective functioning of an institution, the facility would be free to resist the imposition. In that event, adjudication in as-applied challenges would be in order.

\n

***

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated, the judgment of the United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas, concurring.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join the opinion of the Court. I agree with the Court that the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act of 2000 (RLUIPA) is constitutional under our modern Establishment Clause case law.[Footnote 1] I write to explain why a proper historical understanding of the Clause as a federalism provision leads to the same conclusion.[Footnote 2]

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Establishment Clause provides that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d Amdt. 1. As I have explained, an important function of the Clause was to \xe2\x80\x9cma[ke] clear that Congress could not interfere with state establishments.\xe2\x80\x9d Elk Grove Unified School Dist. v. Newdow, 542 U. S. 1, 50 (2004) (opinion concurring in judgment). The Clause, then, \xe2\x80\x9cis best understood as a federalism provision\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cprotects state establishments from federal interference.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also Zelman v. Simmons-Harris, 536 U. S. 639, 677\xe2\x80\x93680 (2002) (Thomas, J., concurring); Lee v. Weisman, 505 U. S. 577, 641 (1992) (Scalia, J., dissenting). Ohio contends that this federalism understanding of the Clause prevents federal oversight of state choices within the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98play in the joints\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d between the Free Exercise and Establishment Clauses. Locke v. Davey, 540 U. S. 712, 718\xe2\x80\x93719 (2004). In other words, Ohio asserts that the Clause protects the States from federal interference with otherwise constitutionally permissible choices regarding religious policy. In Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s view, RLUIPA intrudes on such state policy choices and hence violates the Clause.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s vision of the range of protected state authority overreads the Clause. Ohio and its amici contend that, even though \xe2\x80\x9cStates can no longer establish preferred churches\xe2\x80\x9d because the Clause has been incorporated against the States through the Fourteenth Amendment,[Footnote 3] \xe2\x80\x9cCongress is as unable as ever to contravene constitutionally permissible State choices regarding religious policy.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 26 (emphasis added); Brief for Commonwealth of Virginia et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 6\xe2\x80\x9313. That is not what the Clause says. The Clause prohibits Congress from enacting legislation \xe2\x80\x9crespecting an establishment of religion\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added); it does not prohibit Congress from enacting legislation \xe2\x80\x9crespecting religion\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ctaking cognizance of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d P. Hamburger, Separation of Church and State 106\xe2\x80\x93107 (2002). At the founding, establishment involved \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98coercion of religious orthodoxy and of financial support by force of law and threat of penalty,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Newdow, supra, at 52 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment) (quoting Lee, supra, at 640\xe2\x80\x93641 (Scalia, J., dissenting, in turn citing L. Levy, The Establishment Clause 4 (1986))), including \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98governmental preferences for particular religious faiths,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 542 U. S., at 53 (quoting Rosenberger v. Rector and Visitors of Univ. of Va., 515 U. S. 819, 856 (1995) (Thomas, J., concurring)). In other words, establishment at the founding involved, for example, mandatory observance or mandatory payment of taxes supporting ministers. See 542 U. S., at 52 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment); Lee, supra, at 640\xe2\x80\x93641 (Scalia, J., dissenting); McConnell 2131; Levy, The Establishment Clause 10 (2d ed. 1994). To proscribe Congress from making laws \xe2\x80\x9crespecting an establishment of religion,\xe2\x80\x9d therefore, was to forbid legislation respecting coercive state establishments, not to preclude Congress from legislating on religion generally.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0History, at least that presented by Ohio, does not show that the Clause hermetically seals the Federal Government out of the field of religion. Ohio points to, among other things, the words of James Madison in defense of the Constitution at the Virginia Ratifying Convention: \xe2\x80\x9cThere is not a shadow of right in the general government to intermeddle with religion. Its least interference with it would be a most flagrant usurpation.\xe2\x80\x9d General Defense of the Constitution (June 12, 1788), reprinted in 11 Papers of James Madison 130 (R. Rutland, C. Hobson, W. Rachal, & J.\xc2\xa0Sisson eds. 1977). Ohio also relies on James Iredell\xe2\x80\x99s statement discussing the Religious Test Clause at the North Carolina Ratifying Convention:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Congress] certainly [has] no authority to interfere in the establishment of any religion whatsoever\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. Is there any power given to Congress in matters of religion? Can they pass a single act to impair our religious liberties? If they could, it would be a just cause of alarm\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. If any future Congress should pass an act concerning the religion of the country, it would be an act which they are not authorized to pass, by the Constitution, and which the people would not obey.\xe2\x80\x9d Debate in North Carolina Ratifying Convention (June 30, 1788), in 5 Founders\xe2\x80\x99 Constitution 90 (P. Kurland & R. Lerner eds. 1987).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These quotations do not establish the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 beliefs about the scope of the Establishment Clause. Instead, they demonstrate only that some of the Framers may have believed that the National Government had no authority to legislate concerning religion, because no enumerated power gave it that authority. Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s Spending Clause and Commerce Clause challenges, therefore, may well have merit. See n.\xc2\xa02, supra.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In any event, Ohio has not shown that the Establishment Clause codified Madison\xe2\x80\x99s or Iredell\xe2\x80\x99s view that the Federal Government could not legislate regarding religion. An unenacted version of the Clause, proposed in the House of Representatives, demonstrates the opposite. It provided that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress shall make no laws touching religion, or infringing the rights of conscience.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 Annals of Cong. 731 (1789); see also Wallace v. Jaffree, 472 U. S. 38, 96\xe2\x80\x9397 (1985) (Rehnquist, J., dissenting). The words ultimately adopted, \xe2\x80\x9cCongress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cidentified a position from which [Madison] had once sought to distinguish his own,\xe2\x80\x9d Hamburger, supra, at 106. Whatever he thought of those words, \xe2\x80\x9che clearly did not mind language less severe than that which he had [previously] used.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The version of the Clause finally adopted is narrower than Ohio claims.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does the other historical evidence on which Ohio relies\xe2\x80\x94Joseph Story\xe2\x80\x99s Commentaries on the Constitution\xe2\x80\x94prove its theory. Leaving aside the problems with relying on this source as an indicator of the original understanding, see U. S. Term Limits, Inc. v. Thornton, 514 U. S. 779, 856 (1995) (Thomas, J., dissenting), it is unpersuasive in its own right. Justice Story did say that \xe2\x80\x9cthe whole power over the subject of religion is left exclusively to the state governments, to be acted upon according to their own sense of justice, and the state constitutions.\xe2\x80\x9d Commentaries on the Constitution of the United States 702\xe2\x80\x93703 (1833) (reprinted 1987). In context, however, his statement concerned only Congress\xe2\x80\x99 inability to legislate with respect to religious establishment. See id., at 701 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe real object of the amendment was \xe2\x80\xa6 to prevent any national ecclesiastical establishment, which should give to an hierarchy the exclusive patronage of the national government\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 702 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t was deemed advisable to exclude from the national government all power to act upon the subject\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. It was impossible, that there should not arise perpetual strife and perpetual jealousy on the subject of ecclesiastical ascendancy, if the national government were left free to create a religious establishment\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, the view that the Establishment Clause precludes Congress from legislating respecting religion lacks historical provenance, at least based on the history of which I am aware. Even when enacting laws that bind the States pursuant to valid exercises of its enumerated powers, Congress need not observe strict separation between church and state, or steer clear of the subject of religion. It need only refrain from making laws \xe2\x80\x9crespecting an establishment of religion\xe2\x80\x9d; it must not interfere with a state establishment of religion. For example, Congress presumably could not require a State to establish a religion any more than it could preclude a State from establishing a religion.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On its face\xe2\x80\x94the relevant inquiry, as this is a facial challenge\xe2\x80\x94RLUIPA is not a law \xe2\x80\x9crespecting an establishment of religion.\xe2\x80\x9d RLUIPA provides, as relevant: \xe2\x80\x9cNo government shall impose a substantial burden on the religious exercise of a person residing in or confined to an institution, \xe2\x80\xa6 even if the burden results from a rule of general applicability, unless the government demonstrates that imposition of the burden on that person,\xe2\x80\x9d first, \xe2\x80\x9cfurther[s] a compelling governmental interest,\xe2\x80\x9d and second, \xe2\x80\x9cis the least restrictive means of furthering that compelling governmental interest.\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2). This provision does not prohibit or interfere with state establishments, since no State has established (or constitutionally could establish, given an incorporated Clause) a religion. Nor does the provision require a State to establish a religion: It does not force a State to coerce religious observance or payment of taxes supporting clergy, or require a State to prefer one religious sect over another. It is a law respecting religion, but not one respecting an establishment of religion.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, RLUIPA\xe2\x80\x99s text applies to all laws passed by state and local governments, including \xe2\x80\x9crule[s] of general applicability,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., whether or not they concern an establishment of religion. State and local governments obviously have many laws that have nothing to do with religion, let alone establishments thereof. Numerous applications of RLUIPA therefore do not contravene the Establishment Clause, and a facial challenge based on the Clause must fail. See United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. __, __ (2005) (slip op., at 2) (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part); United States v. Salerno, 481 U. S. 739, 745 (1987).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It also bears noting that Congress, pursuant to its Spending Clause authority, conditioned the States\xe2\x80\x99 receipt of federal funds on their compliance with RLUIPA. \xc2\xa72000cc\xe2\x80\x931(b)(1) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis section applies in any case in which \xe2\x80\xa6 the substantial burden is imposed in a program or activity that receives Federal financial assistance\xe2\x80\x9d). As noted above, n.\xc2\xa02, supra, RLUIPA may well exceed the spending power. Nonetheless, while Congress\xe2\x80\x99 condition stands, the States subject themselves to that condition by voluntarily accepting federal funds. The States\xe2\x80\x99 voluntary acceptance of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 condition undercuts Ohio\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Congress is encroaching on its turf.
