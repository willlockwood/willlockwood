\nSYLLABUS
\nOCTOBER TERM, 2009
\nCHRISTIAN LEGAL SOC. CHAPTER OF UNIV. OF CAL.,HASTINGS COLLEGE OF LAW V. MARTINEZ
\n
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\n
\n

CHRISTIAN LEGAL SOCIETY CHAPTER OF THE UNIVERSITY OF CALIFORNIA, HASTINGS COLLEGE OF THE LAW, aka HASTINGS CHRISTIAN FELLOWSHIP v. MARTINEZ et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x931371.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 19, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 28, 2010

\n

Respondent Hastings College of the Law (Hastings), a school within the University of California public-school system, extends official recognition to student groups through its \xe2\x80\x9cRegistered Student Organization\xe2\x80\x9d (RSO) program. Several benefits attend this school-approved status, including the use of school funds, facilities, and channels of communication, as well as Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 name and logo. In exchange for recognition, RSOs must abide by certain conditions. Critical here, all RSOs must comply with the school\xe2\x80\x99s Nondiscrimination Policy, which tracks state law barring discrimination on a number of bases, including religion and sexual orientation. Hastings interprets this policy, as it relates to the RSO program, to mandate acceptance of all comers: RSOs must allow any student to participate, become a member, or seek leadership positions, regardless of her status or beliefs.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the beginning of the 2004\xe2\x80\x932005 academic year, the leaders of an existing Christian RSO formed petitioner Christian Legal Society (CLS) by affiliating with a national Christian association that charters student chapters at law schools throughout the country. These chapters must adopt bylaws that, inter alia, require members and officers to sign a \xe2\x80\x9cStatement of Faith\xe2\x80\x9d and to conduct their lives in accord with prescribed principles. Among those tenets is the belief that sexual activity should not occur outside of marriage between a man and a woman. CLS interprets its bylaws to exclude from affiliation anyone who engages in \xe2\x80\x9cunrepentant homosexual conduct\xe2\x80\x9d or holds religious convictions different from those in the Statement of Faith. Hastings rejected CLS\xe2\x80\x99s application for RSO status on the ground that the group\xe2\x80\x99s bylaws did not comply with Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 open-access policy because they excluded students based on religion and sexual orientation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CLS filed this suit for injunctive and declaratory relief under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983, alleging that Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 refusal to grant the group RSO status violated its First and Fourteenth Amendment rights to free speech, expressive association, and free exercise of religion. On cross-motions for summary judgment, the District Court ruled for Hastings. The court held that the all-comers condition on access to a limited public forum was both reasonable and viewpoint neutral, and therefore did not violate CLS\xe2\x80\x99s right to free speech. Nor, in the court\xe2\x80\x99s view, did Hastings impermissibly impair CLS\xe2\x80\x99s right to expressive association: Hastings did not order CLS to admit any student, nor did the school proscribe any speech; Hastings merely placed conditions on the use of school facilities and funds. The court also rejected CLS\xe2\x80\x99s free exercise argument, stating that the Nondiscrimination Policy did not single out religious beliefs, but rather was neutral and of general applicability. The Ninth Circuit affirmed, ruling that the all-comers condition on RSO recognition was reasonable and viewpoint neutral.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The Court considers only whether a public institution\xe2\x80\x99s conditioning access to a student-organization forum on compliance with an all-comers policy violates the Constitution. CLS urges the Court to review, instead, the Nondiscrimination Policy as written\xe2\x80\x94prohibiting discrimination on enumerated bases, including religion and sexual orientation. The policy\xe2\x80\x99s written terms, CLS contends, target solely those groups that organize around religious beliefs or that disapprove of particular sexual behavior, and leave other associations free to limit membership to persons committed to the group\xe2\x80\x99s ideology. This argument flatly contradicts the joint stipulation of facts the parties submitted at the summary-judgment stage, which specified: \xe2\x80\x9cHastings requires that [RSOs] allow any student to participate, \xe2\x80\xa6 regardless of [her] status or beliefs. For example, the Hastings Democratic Caucus cannot bar students holding Republican political beliefs \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d This Court has long recognized that parties are bound by, and cannot contradict, their stipulations. See, e.g., Board of Regents of Univ. of Wis. System v. Southworth, 529 U. S. 217, 226. The Court therefore rejects CLS\xe2\x80\x99s attempt to escape from the stipulation and shift its target to Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy as written. Pp. 8\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0The all-comers policy is a reasonable, viewpoint-neutral condition on access to the RSO forum; it therefore does not transgress First Amendment limitations. Pp. 12\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s limited public forum decisions supply the appropriate framework for assessing both CLS\xe2\x80\x99s free-speech and expressive-association claims; those decisions recognize that a governmental entity, in regulating property in its charge, may impose restrictions on speech that are reasonable in light of the purposes of the forum and viewpoint neutral, e.g., Rosenberger v. Rector and Visitors of Univ. of Va., 515 U. S. 819, 829. CLS urges the Court to apply to its expressive-association claim a different line of cases\xe2\x80\x94decisions in which the Court has rigorously reviewed restrictions on associational freedom in the context of public accommodations, e.g., Roberts v. United States Jaycees, 468 U. S. 609, 623. But, because CLS\xe2\x80\x99s expressive-association and free-speech arguments merge\xe2\x80\x94who speaks on its behalf, CLS reasons, colors what concept is conveyed\xe2\x80\x94it makes little sense to treat the claims as discrete. Instead, three observations lead the Court to analyze CLS\xe2\x80\x99s arguments under limited-public-forum precedents.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the same considerations that have led the Court to apply a less restrictive level of scrutiny to speech in limited public forums, as compared to other environments, apply with equal force to expressive association occurring in a limited public forum. Speech and expressive-association rights are closely linked. See id., at 622. When these intertwined rights arise in exactly the same context, it would be anomalous for a speech restriction to survive constitutional review under the limited-public-forum test only to be invalidated as an impermissible infringement of expressive association. Second, the strict scrutiny the Court has applied in some settings to laws that burden expressive association would, in practical effect, invalidate a defining characteristic of limited public forums\xe2\x80\x94the State\xe2\x80\x99s authority to \xe2\x80\x9creserv[e] [them] for certain groups.\xe2\x80\x9d Rosenberger, 515 U. S., at 829. Third, this case fits comfortably within the limited-public-forum category, for CLS may exclude any person for any reason if it forgoes the benefits of official recognition. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s expressive-association decisions, in contrast, involved regulations that compelled a group to include unwanted members, with no choice to opt out. See, e.g., Boy Scouts of America v. Dale, 530 U. S. 640, 648. Application of the less-restrictive limited-public-forum analysis better accounts for the fact that Hastings, through its RSO program, is dangling the carrot of subsidy, not wielding the stick of prohibition. Pp. 12\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0In three cases, this Court held that public universities had unconstitutionally singled out student groups for disfavored treatment because of their points of view. See Healy v. James, 408 U. S. 169; Widmar v. Vincent, 454 U. S. 263; and Rosenberger. Most recently and comprehensively, in Rosenberger, the Court held that a university generally may not withhold benefits from student groups because of their religious outlook. \xe2\x80\x9cOnce it has opened a limited [public] forum,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court emphasized, \xe2\x80\x9cthe State must respect the lawful boundaries it has itself set.\xe2\x80\x9d 515 U. S. at 829. It may \xe2\x80\x9cnot exclude speech where its distinction is not reasonable in light of the purpose served by the forum, \xe2\x80\xa6 nor may it discriminate against speech on the basis of \xe2\x80\xa6 viewpoint.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Pp. 17\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 all-comers policy is reasonable, taking into account the RSO forum\xe2\x80\x99s function and \xe2\x80\x9call the surrounding circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Cornelius v. NAACP Legal Defense & Ed. Fund, Inc., 473 U. S. 788, 809. Pp. 19\xe2\x80\x9328.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry is shaped by the educational context in which it arises: \xe2\x80\x9cFirst Amendment rights must be analyzed in light of the special characteristics of the school environment.\xe2\x80\x9d Widmar, 454 U. S., at 268, n.\xc2\xa05. This Court is the final arbiter of whether a public university has exceeded constitutional constraints. The Court has, however, cautioned courts to resist \xe2\x80\x9csubstitut[ing] their own notions of sound educational policy for those of \xe2\x80\xa6 school authorities,\xe2\x80\x9d for judges lack the on-the-ground expertise and experience of school administrators. Board of Ed. of Hendrick Hudson Central School Dist., Westchester Cty. v. Rowley, 458 U. S. 176, 206. Because schools enjoy \xe2\x80\x9ca significant measure of authority over the type of officially recognized activities in which their students participate,\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Ed. of Westside Community Schools (Dist. 66) v. Mergens, 496 U. S. 226, 240, the Court approaches its task here mindful that Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 decisions about the character of its student-group program are due decent respect. Pp. 19\xe2\x80\x9321.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0The justifications Hastings asserts in support of its all-comers policy are reasonable in light of the RSO forum\xe2\x80\x99s purposes. First, the policy ensures that the leadership, educational, and social opportunities afforded by RSOs are available to all students. RSOs are eligible for financial assistance drawn from mandatory student-activity fees; the policy ensures that no Hastings student is forced to fund a group that would reject her as a member. Second, the policy helps Hastings police the written terms of its Nondiscrimination Policy without inquiring into an RSO\xe2\x80\x99s motivation for membership restrictions. CLS\xe2\x80\x99s proposal that Hastings permit exclusion because of belief but forbid discrimination due to status would impose on Hastings the daunting task of trying to determine whether a student organization cloaked prohibited status exclusion in belief-based garb. Third, Hastings reasonably adheres to the view that its policy, to the extent it brings together individuals with diverse backgrounds and beliefs, encourages tolerance, cooperation, and learning among students. Fourth, the policy incorporates state-law discrimination proscriptions, thereby conveying Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 decision to decline to subsidize conduct disapproved by the State. So long as a public school does not contravene constitutional limits, its choice to advance state-law goals stands on firm footing. Pp. 21\xe2\x80\x9324.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(3)\xc2\xa0Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy is all the more creditworthy in light of the \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial alternative channels that remain open for [CLS-student] communication to take place.\xe2\x80\x9d Perry Ed. Assn. v. Perry Local Educators\xe2\x80\x99 Assn., 460 U. S. 37, 53. Hastings offered CLS access to school facilities to conduct meetings and the use of chalkboards and certain bulletin boards to advertise events. Although CLS could not take advantage of RSO-specific methods of communication, the advent of electronic media and social-networking sites lessens the importance of those channels. Private groups, such as fraternities and sororities, commonly maintain a presence at universities without official school affiliation. CLS was similarly situated: It hosted a variety of activities the year after Hastings denied it recognition, and the number of students attending those meetings and events doubled. \xe2\x80\x9cThe variety and type of alternative modes of access present here,\xe2\x80\x9d in short, \xe2\x80\x9ccompare favorably with those in other [limited public] forum cases where [the Court has] upheld restrictions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 53\xe2\x80\x9354. Pp. 24\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(4)\xc2\xa0CLS\xe2\x80\x99s arguments that the all-comers policy is not reasonable are unavailing. CLS contends that there can be no diversity of viewpoints in a forum when groups are not permitted to form around viewpoints, but this argument confuses CLS\xe2\x80\x99s preferred policy with constitutional limitation\xe2\x80\x94the advisability of Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy does not control its permissibility. A State\xe2\x80\x99s restriction on access to a limited public forum, moreover, \xe2\x80\x9cneed not be the most reasonable or the only reasonable limitation.\xe2\x80\x9d Cornelius, 473 U. S., at 808. CLS\xe2\x80\x99s contention that Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy will facilitate hostile takeovers of RSOs by student saboteurs bent on subverting a group\xe2\x80\x99s mission is more hypothetical than real; there is no history or prospect of RSO-hijackings at Hastings. Cf. National Endowment for Arts v. Finley, 524 U. S. 569, 584. Finally, CLS\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that Hastings lacks any legitimate interest in urging religious groups not to favor co-religionists erroneously focuses on the benefits the group must forgo, while ignoring the interests of those it seeks to fence out. Hastings, caught in the crossfire between a group\xe2\x80\x99s desire to exclude and students\xe2\x80\x99 demand for equal access, may reasonably draw a line in the sand permitting all organizations to express what they wish but no group to discriminate in membership. Pp. 25\xe2\x80\x9328.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 all-comers policy is viewpoint neutral. Pp. 28\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0The policy draws no distinction between groups based on their message or perspective; its requirement that all student groups accept all comers is textbook viewpoint neutral. Pp.\xc2\xa028\xe2\x80\x9329.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0Conceding that the policy is nominally neutral, CLS asserts that it systematically\xe2\x80\x94and impermissibly\xe2\x80\x94burdens most heavily those groups whose viewpoints are out of favor with the campus mainstream. This argument fails because \xe2\x80\x9c[a] regulation that serves purposes unrelated to the content of expression is deemed neutral, even if it has an incidental effect on some speakers or messages but not others.\xe2\x80\x9d Ward v. Rock Against Racism, 491 U. S. 781, 791. Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 requirement that RSOs accept all comers, the Court is satisfied, is \xe2\x80\x9cjustified without reference to the content [or viewpoint] of the regulated speech.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 791. It targets the act of rejecting would-be group members without reference to the reasons motivating that behavior. Pp. 29\xe2\x80\x9331.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa03.\xc2\xa0Neither lower court addressed CLS\xe2\x80\x99s argument that Hastings selectively enforces its all-comers policy. This Court is not the proper forum to air the issue in the first instance. On remand, the Ninth Circuit may consider this argument if, and to the extent, it is preserved. Pp. 31\xe2\x80\x9332.

\n

319 Fed. Appx. 645, affirmed and remanded.

CHRISTIAN LEGAL SOCIETY CHAPTER OF THE UNI-\nVERSITY OF CALIFORNIA, HASTINGS COLLEGE OF\nTHE LAW, aka HASTINGS CHRISTIAN FELLOW-\nSHIP, PETITIONER v. LEO P. MARTINEZ et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[June 28, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In a series of decisions, this Court has emphasized that the First Amendment generally precludes public universities from denying student organizations access to school-sponsored forums because of the groups\xe2\x80\x99 viewpoints. See Rosenberger v. Rector and Visitors of Univ. of Va., 515 U. S. 819 (1995); Widmar v. Vincent, 454 U. S. 263 (1981); Healy v. James, 408 U. S. 169 (1972). This case concerns a novel question regarding student activities at public universities: May a public law school condition its official recognition of a student group\xe2\x80\x94and the attendant use of school funds and facilities\xe2\x80\x94on the organization\xe2\x80\x99s agreement to open eligibility for membership and leadership to all students?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the view of petitioner Christian Legal Society (CLS), an accept-all-comers policy impairs its First Amendment rights to free speech, expressive association, and free exercise of religion by prompting it, on pain of relinquishing the advantages of recognition, to accept members who do not share the organization\xe2\x80\x99s core beliefs about religion and sexual orientation. From the perspective of respondent Hastings College of the Law (Hastings or the Law School), CLS seeks special dispensation from an across-the-board open-access requirement designed to further the reasonable educational purposes underpinning the school\xe2\x80\x99s student-organization program.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In accord with the District Court and the Court of Appeals, we reject CLS\xe2\x80\x99s First Amendment challenge. Compliance with Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 all-comers policy, we conclude, is a reasonable, viewpoint-neutral condition on access to the student-organization forum. In requiring CLS\xe2\x80\x94in common with all other student organizations\xe2\x80\x94to choose between welcoming all students and forgoing the benefits of official recognition, we hold, Hastings did not transgress constitutional limitations. CLS, it bears emphasis, seeks not parity with other organizations, but a preferential exemption from Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy. The First Amendment shields CLS against state prohibition of the organization\xe2\x80\x99s expressive activity, however exclusionary that activity may be. But CLS enjoys no constitutional right to state subvention of its selectivity.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Founded in 1878, Hastings was the first law school in the University of California public-school system. Like many institutions of higher education, Hastings encourages students to form extracurricular associations that \xe2\x80\x9ccontribute to the Hastings community and experience.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 349. These groups offer students \xe2\x80\x9copportunities to pursue academic and social interests outside of the classroom [to] further their education\xe2\x80\x9d and to help them \xe2\x80\x9cdevelo[p] leadership skills.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Through its \xe2\x80\x9cRegistered Student Organization\xe2\x80\x9d (RSO) program, Hastings extends official recognition to student groups. Several benefits attend this school-approved status. RSOs are eligible to seek financial assistance from the Law School, which subsidizes their events using funds from a mandatory student-activity fee imposed on all students. Id., at 217. RSOs may also use Law-School channels to communicate with students: They may place announcements in a weekly Office-of-Student-Services newsletter, advertise events on designated bulletin boards, send e-mails using a Hastings-organization address, and participate in an annual Student Organizations Fair designed to advance recruitment efforts. Id., at 216\xe2\x80\x93219. In addition, RSOs may apply for permission to use the Law School\xe2\x80\x99s facilities for meetings and office space. Id., at 218\xe2\x80\x93219. Finally, Hastings allows officially recognized groups to use its name and logo. Id., at 216.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In exchange for these benefits, RSOs must abide by certain conditions. Only a \xe2\x80\x9cnon-commercial organization whose membership is limited to Hastings students may become [an RSO].\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 83a. A prospective RSO must submit its bylaws to Hastings for approval, id., at 83a\xe2\x80\x9384a; and if it intends to use the Law School\xe2\x80\x99s name or logo, it must sign a license agreement, App. 219. Critical here, all RSOs must undertake to comply with Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cPolicies and Regulations Applying to College Activities, Organizations and Students.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Law School\xe2\x80\x99s Policy on Nondiscrimination (Nondiscrimination Policy), which binds RSOs, states:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Hastings] is committed to a policy against legally impermissible, arbitrary or unreasonable discriminatory practices. All groups, including administration, faculty, student governments, [Hastings]-owned student residence facilities and programs sponsored by [Hastings], are governed by this policy of nondiscrimination. [Hasting\xe2\x80\x99s] policy on nondiscrimination is to comply fully with applicable law.

\n

\n\n\xe2\x80\x9c[Hastings] shall not discriminate unlawfully on the basis of race, color, religion, national origin, ancestry, disability, age, sex or sexual orientation. This nondiscrimination policy covers admission, access and treatment in Hastings-sponsored programs and activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 220.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hastings interprets the Nondiscrimination Policy, as it relates to the RSO program, to mandate acceptance of all comers: School-approved groups must \xe2\x80\x9callow any student to participate, become a member, or seek leadership positions in the organization, regardless of [her] status or beliefs.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 221.[Footnote 2] Other law schools have adopted similar all-comers policies. See, e.g., Georgetown University Law Center, Office of Student Life: Student Organi\nzations, available at http://www.law.georgetown.edu/\nStudentLife/StudentOrgs/NewGroup.htm (All Internet materials as visited June 24, 2010, and included in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file) (Membership in registered groups must be \xe2\x80\x9copen to all students.\xe2\x80\x9d); Hofstra Law School Student Handbook 2009\xe2\x80\x932010, p. 49, available at http://\nlaw.hofstra.edu/pdf/StudentLife/StudentAffairs/Handbook/\nstuhb_handbook.pdf (\xe2\x80\x9c[Student] organizations are open to all students.\xe2\x80\x9d). From Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 adoption of its Nondiscrimination Policy in 1990 until the events stirring this litigation, \xe2\x80\x9cno student organization at Hastings \xe2\x80\xa6 ever sought an exemption from the Policy.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 221.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 2004, CLS became the first student group to do so. At the beginning of the academic year, the leaders of a predecessor Christian organization\xe2\x80\x94which had been an RSO at Hastings for a decade\xe2\x80\x94formed CLS by affiliating with the national Christian Legal Society (CLS-National). Id., at 222\xe2\x80\x93223, 225. CLS-National, an association of Christian lawyers and law students, charters student chapters at law schools throughout the country. Id., at 225. CLS chapters must adopt bylaws that, inter alia, require members and officers to sign a \xe2\x80\x9cStatement of Faith\xe2\x80\x9d and to conduct their lives in accord with prescribed principles. Id., at 225\xe2\x80\x93226; App. to Pet. for Cert. 101a.[Footnote 3] Among those tenets is the belief that sexual activity should not occur outside of marriage between a man and a woman; CLS thus interprets its bylaws to exclude from affiliation anyone who engages in \xe2\x80\x9cunrepentant homosexual conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 226. CLS also excludes students who hold religious convictions different from those in the Statement of Faith. Id., at 227.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On September 17, 2004, CLS submitted to Hastings an application for RSO status, accompanied by all required documents, including the set of bylaws mandated by CLS-National. Id., at 227\xe2\x80\x93228. Several days later, the Law School rejected the application; CLS\xe2\x80\x99s bylaws, Hastings explained, did not comply with the Nondiscrimination Policy because CLS barred students based on religion and sexual orientation. Id., at 228.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CLS formally requested an exemption from the Nondiscrimination Policy, id., at 281, but Hastings declined to grant one. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]o be one of our student-recognized organizations,\xe2\x80\x9d Hastings reiterated, \xe2\x80\x9cCLS must open its membership to all students irrespective of their religious beliefs or sexual orientation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 294. If CLS instead chose to operate outside the RSO program, Hastings stated, the school \xe2\x80\x9cwould be pleased to provide [CLS] the use of Hastings facilities for its meetings and activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. CLS would also have access to chalkboards and generally available campus bulletin boards to announce its events. Id., at 219, 233. In other words, Hastings would do nothing to suppress CLS\xe2\x80\x99s endeavors, but neither would it lend RSO-level support for them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Refusing to alter its bylaws, CLS did not obtain RSO status. It did, however, operate independently during the 2004\xe2\x80\x932005 academic year. CLS held weekly Bible-study meetings and invited Hastings students to Good Friday and Easter Sunday church services. Id., at 229. It also hosted a beach barbeque, Thanksgiving dinner, campus lecture on the Christian faith and the legal practice, several fellowship dinners, an end-of-year banquet, and other informal social activities. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On October 22, 2004, CLS filed suit against various Hastings officers and administrators under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. Its complaint alleged that Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 refusal to grant the organization RSO status violated CLS\xe2\x80\x99s First and Fourteenth Amendment rights to free speech, expressive association, and free exercise of religion. The suit sought injunctive and declaratory relief.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On cross-motions for summary judgment, the U. S. District Court for the Northern District of California ruled in favor of Hastings. The Law School\xe2\x80\x99s all-comers condition on access to a limited public forum, the court held, was both reasonable and viewpoint neutral, and therefore did not violate CLS\xe2\x80\x99s right to free speech. App. to Pet. for Cert. 27a\xe2\x80\x9338a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor, in the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, did the Law School impermissibly impair CLS\xe2\x80\x99s right to expressive association. \xe2\x80\x9cHastings is not directly ordering CLS to admit [any] studen[t],\xe2\x80\x9d the court observed, id., at 42a; \xe2\x80\x9c[r]ather, Hastings has merely placed conditions on\xe2\x80\x9d the use of its facilities and funds, ibid. \xe2\x80\x9cHastings\xe2\x80\x99 denial of official recognition,\xe2\x80\x9d the court added, \xe2\x80\x9cwas not a substantial impediment to CLS\xe2\x80\x99s ability to meet and communicate as a group.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 49a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The court also rejected CLS\xe2\x80\x99s Free Exercise Clause argument. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Nondiscrimination Policy does not target or single out religious beliefs,\xe2\x80\x9d the court noted; rather, the policy \xe2\x80\x9cis neutral and of general applicability.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 63a. \xe2\x80\x9cCLS may be motivated by its religious beliefs to exclude students based on their religion or sexual orientation,\xe2\x80\x9d the court explained, \xe2\x80\x9cbut that does not convert the reason for Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 [Nondiscrimination Policy] to be one that is religiously-based.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 63a\xe2\x80\x9364a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On appeal, the Ninth Circuit affirmed in an opinion that stated, in full:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe parties stipulate that Hastings imposes an open membership rule on all student groups\xe2\x80\x94all groups must accept all comers as voting members even if those individuals disagree with the mission of the group. The conditions on recognition are therefore viewpoint neutral and reasonable. Truth v. Kent Sch. Dist., 542 F.\xc2\xa03d 634, 649\xe2\x80\x9350 (9th Cir. 2008).\xe2\x80\x9d Christian Legal Soc. Chapter of Univ. of Cal. v. Kane, 319 Fed. Appx. 645, 645\xe2\x80\x93646 (CA9 2009).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 558 U. S. ____ (2009), and now affirm the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Before considering the merits of CLS\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional arguments, we must resolve a preliminary issue: CLS urges us to review the Nondiscrimination Policy as written\xe2\x80\x94prohibiting discrimination on several enumerated bases, including religion and sexual orientation\xe2\x80\x94and not as a requirement that all RSOs accept all comers. The written terms of the Nondiscrimination Policy, CLS contends, \xe2\x80\x9ctarge[t] solely those groups whose beliefs are based on religion or that disapprove of a particular kind of sexual behavior,\xe2\x80\x9d and leave other associations free to limit membership and leadership to individuals committed to the group\xe2\x80\x99s ideology. Brief for Petitioner 19 (internal quotation marks omitted). For example, \xe2\x80\x9c[a] political \xe2\x80\xa6 group can insist that its leaders support its purposes and beliefs,\xe2\x80\x9d CLS alleges, but \xe2\x80\x9ca religious group cannot.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 20.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CLS\xe2\x80\x99s assertion runs headlong into the stipulation of facts it jointly submitted with Hastings at the summary-judgment stage. In that filing, the parties specified:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cHastings requires that registered student organizations allow any student to participate, become a member, or seek leadership positions in the organization, regardless of [her] status or beliefs. Thus, for example, the Hastings Democratic Caucus cannot bar students holding Republican political beliefs from becoming members or seeking leadership positions in the organization.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 221 (Joint Stipulation \xc2\xb618) (emphasis added; citations omitted).[Footnote 5]

\n

\nUnder the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s local rules, stipulated facts are deemed \xe2\x80\x9cundisputed.\xe2\x80\x9d Civil Local Rule 56\xe2\x80\x932 (ND Cal. 2010). See also Pet. for Cert. 2 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe material facts of this case are undisputed.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Litigants, we have long recognized, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]re entitled to have [their] case tried upon the assumption that \xe2\x80\xa6 facts, stipulated into the record, were established.\xe2\x80\x9d H. Hackfeld & Co. v. United States, 197 U. S. 442, 447 (1905).[Footnote 7] This entitlement is the bookend to a party\xe2\x80\x99s undertaking to be bound by the factual stipulations it submits. See post, at 10 (Alito, J., dissenting) (agreeing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe parties must be held to their Joint Stipulation\xe2\x80\x9d). As a leading legal reference summarizes:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Factual stipulations are] binding and conclusive \xe2\x80\xa6 , and the facts stated are not subject to subsequent variation. So, the parties will not be permitted to deny the truth of the facts stated, \xe2\x80\xa6 or to maintain a contention contrary to the agreed statement, \xe2\x80\xa6 or to suggest, on appeal, that the facts were other than as stipulated or that any material fact was omitted. The burden is on the party seeking to recover to show his or her right from the facts actually stated.\xe2\x80\x9d 83 C.\xc2\xa0J.\xc2\xa0S., Stipulations \xc2\xa793 (2000) (footnotes omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has accordingly refused to consider a party\xe2\x80\x99s argument that contradicted a joint \xe2\x80\x9cstipulation [entered] at the outset of th[e] litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Regents of Univ. of Wis. System v. Southworth, 529 U. S. 217, 226 (2000). Time and again, the dissent races away from the facts to which CLS stipulated. See, e.g., post, at 2, 3, 5, 6, 7, 8, 11, 24.[Footnote 8] But factual stipulations are \xe2\x80\x9cformal concessions \xe2\x80\xa6 that have the effect of withdrawing a fact from issue and dispensing wholly with the need for proof of the fact. Thus, a judicial admission \xe2\x80\xa6 is conclusive in the case.\xe2\x80\x9d 2 K.\xc2\xa0Broun, McCormick on Evidence \xc2\xa7254, p. 181 (6th ed. 2006) (footnote omitted). See also, e.g., Oscanyan v. Arms Co., 103 U. S. 261, 263 (1881) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe power of the court to act in the disposition of a trial upon facts conceded by counsel is as plain as its power to act upon the evidence produced.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of the joint stipulation, both the District Court and the Ninth Circuit trained their attention on the constitutionality of the all-comers requirement, as described in the parties\xe2\x80\x99 accord. See 319 Fed. Appx., at 645\xe2\x80\x93646; App. to Pet. for Cert. 32a; id., at 36a. We reject CLS\xe2\x80\x99s unseemly attempt to escape from the stipulation and shift its target to Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy as written. This opinion, therefore, considers only whether conditioning access to a student-organization forum on compliance with an all-comers policy violates the Constitution.[Footnote 10]

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In support of the argument that Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 all-comers policy treads on its First Amendment rights to free speech and expressive association, CLS draws on two lines of decisions. First, in a progression of cases, this Court has employed forum analysis to determine when a governmental entity, in regulating property in its charge, may place limitations on speech.[Footnote 11] Recognizing a State\xe2\x80\x99s right \xe2\x80\x9cto preserve the property under its control for the use to which it is lawfully dedicated,\xe2\x80\x9d Cornelius v. NAACP Legal Defense & Ed. Fund, Inc., 473 U. S. 788, 800 (1985) (internal quotation marks omitted), the Court has permitted restrictions on access to a limited public forum, like the RSO program here, with this key caveat: Any access barrier must be reasonable and viewpoint neutral, e.g., Rosenberger, 515 U. S., at 829. See also, e.g., Good News Club v. Milford Central School, 533 U. S. 98, 106\xe2\x80\x93107 (2001); Lamb\xe2\x80\x99s Chapel v. Center Moriches Union Free School Dist., 508 U. S. 384, 392\xe2\x80\x93393 (1993); Perry Ed. Assn. v. Perry Local Educators\xe2\x80\x99 Assn., 460 U. S. 37, 46 (1983).[Footnote 12]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, as evidenced by another set of decisions, this Court has rigorously reviewed laws and regulations that constrain associational freedom. In the context of public accommodations, we have subjected restrictions on that freedom to close scrutiny; such restrictions are permitted only if they serve \xe2\x80\x9ccompelling state interests\xe2\x80\x9d that are \xe2\x80\x9cunrelated to the suppression of ideas\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94interests that cannot be advanced \xe2\x80\x9cthrough \xe2\x80\xa6 significantly less restrictive [means].\xe2\x80\x9d Roberts v. United States Jaycees, 468 U. S. 609, 623 (1984). See also, e.g., Boy Scouts of America v. Dale, 530 U. S. 640, 648 (2000). \xe2\x80\x9cFreedom of association,\xe2\x80\x9d we have recognized, \xe2\x80\x9cplainly presupposes a freedom not to associate.\xe2\x80\x9d Roberts, 468 U. S., at 623. Insisting that an organization embrace unwelcome members, we have therefore concluded, \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly and immediately affects associational rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Dale, 530 U. S., at 659.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CLS would have us engage each line of cases independently, but its expressive-association and free-speech arguments merge: Who speaks on its behalf, CLS reasons, colors what concept is conveyed. See Brief for Petitioner 35 (expressive association in this case is \xe2\x80\x9cthe functional equivalent of speech itself\xe2\x80\x9d). It therefore makes little sense to treat CLS\xe2\x80\x99s speech and association claims as discrete. See Citizens Against Rent Control/Coalition for Fair Housing v. Berkeley, 454 U. S. 290, 300 (1981). Instead, three observations lead us to conclude that our limited-public-forum precedents supply the appropriate framework for assessing both CLS\xe2\x80\x99s speech and association rights.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the same considerations that have led us to apply a less restrictive level of scrutiny to speech in limited public forums as compared to other environments, see supra, at 12\xe2\x80\x9313, and n. 11, apply with equal force to expressive association occurring in limited public forums. As just noted, speech and expressive-association rights are closely linked. See Roberts, 468 U. S., at 622 (Associational freedom is \xe2\x80\x9cimplicit in the right to engage in activities protected by the First Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d). When these intertwined rights arise in exactly the same context, it would be anomalous for a restriction on speech to survive constitutional review under our limited-public-forum test only to be invalidated as an impermissible infringement of expressive association. Accord Brief for State Universities and State University Systems as Amici Curiae 37\xe2\x80\x9338. That result would be all the more anomalous in this case, for CLS suggests that its expressive-association claim plays a part auxiliary to speech\xe2\x80\x99s starring role. See Brief for Petitioner 18.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, and closely related, the strict scrutiny we have applied in some settings to laws that burden expressive association would, in practical effect, invalidate a defining characteristic of limited public forums\xe2\x80\x94the State may \xe2\x80\x9creserv[e] [them] for certain groups.\xe2\x80\x9d Rosenberger, 515 U. S., at 829. See also Perry Ed. Assn., 460 U. S., at 49 (\xe2\x80\x9cImplicit in the concept\xe2\x80\x9d of a limited public forum is the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cright to make distinctions in access on the basis of \xe2\x80\xa6 speaker identity.\xe2\x80\x9d); Cornelius, 473 U. S., at 806 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] speaker may be excluded from\xe2\x80\x9d a limited public forum \xe2\x80\x9cif he is not a member of the class of speakers for whose especial benefit the forum was created.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An example sharpens the tip of this point: Schools, including Hastings, see App. to Pet. for Cert. 83a, ordinarily, and without controversy, limit official student-group recognition to organizations comprising only students\xe2\x80\x94even if those groups wish to associate with nonstudents. See, e.g., Volokh, Freedom of Expressive Association and Government Subsidies, 58 Stan. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1919, 1940 (2006). The same ground rules must govern both speech and association challenges in the limited-public-forum context, lest strict scrutiny trump a public university\xe2\x80\x99s ability to \xe2\x80\x9cconfin[e] a [speech] forum to the limited and legitimate purposes for which it was created.\xe2\x80\x9d Rosenberger, 515 U. S., at 829. See also Healy, 408 U. S., at 189 (\xe2\x80\x9cAssociational activities need not be tolerated where they infringe reasonable campus rules.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, this case fits comfortably within the limited-public-forum category, for CLS, in seeking what is effectively a state subsidy, faces only indirect pressure to modify its membership policies; CLS may exclude any person for any reason if it forgoes the benefits of official recognition.[Footnote 13] The expressive-association precedents on which CLS relies, in contrast, involved regulations that compelled a group to include unwanted members, with no choice to opt out. See, e.g., Dale, 530 U. S., at 648 (regulation \xe2\x80\x9cforc[ed] [the Boy Scouts] to accept members it [did] not desire\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Roberts, 468 U. S., at 623 (\xe2\x80\x9cThere can be no clearer example of an intrusion into the internal structure or affairs of an association than\xe2\x80\x9d forced inclusion of unwelcome participants.).[Footnote 14]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In diverse contexts, our decisions have distinguished between policies that require action and those that withhold benefits. See, e.g., Grove City College v. Bell, 465 U. S. 555, 575\xe2\x80\x93576 (1984); Bob Jones Univ. v. United States, 461 U. S. 574, 602\xe2\x80\x93604 (1983). Application of the less-restrictive limited-public-forum analysis better accounts for the fact that Hastings, through its RSO program, is dangling the carrot of subsidy, not wielding the stick of prohibition. Cf. Norwood v. Harrison, 413 U. S. 455, 463 (1973) (\xe2\x80\x9cThat the Constitution may compel toleration of private discrimination in some circumstances does not mean that it requires state support for such discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, we are persuaded that our limited-public-forum precedents adequately respect both CLS\xe2\x80\x99s speech and expressive-association rights, and fairly balance those rights against Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 interests as property owner and educational institution. We turn to the merits of the instant dispute, therefore, with the limited-public-forum decisions as our guide.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As earlier pointed out, supra, at 1, 12\xe2\x80\x9313, we do not write on a blank slate; we have three times before considered clashes between public universities and student groups seeking official recognition or its attendant benefits. First, in Healy, a state college denied school affiliation to a student group that wished to form a local chapter of Students for a Democratic Society (SDS). 408 U. S., at 170. Characterizing SDS\xe2\x80\x99s mission as violent and disruptive, and finding the organization\xe2\x80\x99s philosophy repugnant, the college completely banned the SDS chapter from campus; in its effort to sever all channels of communication between students and the group, university officials went so far as to disband a meeting of SDS members in a campus coffee shop. Id., at 174\xe2\x80\x93176. The college, we noted, could require \xe2\x80\x9cthat a group seeking official recognition affirm in advance its willingness to adhere to reasonable campus law,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9creasonable standards respecting conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 193. But a public educational institution exceeds constitutional bounds, we held, when it \xe2\x80\x9crestrict[s] speech or association simply because it finds the views expressed by [a] group to be abhorrent.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 187\xe2\x80\x93188.[Footnote 15]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We later relied on Healy in Widmar. In that case, a public university, in an effort to avoid state support for religion, had closed its facilities to a registered student group that sought to use university space for religious worship and discussion. 454 U. S., at 264\xe2\x80\x93265. \xe2\x80\x9cA university\xe2\x80\x99s mission is education,\xe2\x80\x9d we observed, \xe2\x80\x9cand decisions of this Court have never denied a university\xe2\x80\x99s authority to impose reasonable regulations compatible with that mission upon the use of its campus and facilities.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 268, n.\xc2\xa05. But because the university singled out religious organizations for disadvantageous treatment, we subjected the university\xe2\x80\x99s regulation to strict scrutiny. Id., at 269\xe2\x80\x93270. The school\xe2\x80\x99s interest \xe2\x80\x9cin maintaining strict separation of church and State,\xe2\x80\x9d we held, was not \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently compelling to justify \xe2\x80\xa6 [viewpoint] discrimination against \xe2\x80\xa6 religious speech.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 270, 276 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Most recently and comprehensively, in Rosenberger, we reiterated that a university generally may not withhold benefits from student groups because of their religious outlook. The officially recognized student group in Rosenberger was denied student-activity-fee funding to distribute a newspaper because the publication discussed issues from a Christian perspective. 515 U. S., at 825\xe2\x80\x93827. By \xe2\x80\x9cselect[ing] for disfavored treatment those student journalistic efforts with religious editorial viewpoints,\xe2\x80\x9d we held, the university had engaged in \xe2\x80\x9cviewpoint discrimination, which is presumed impermissible when directed against speech otherwise within the forum\xe2\x80\x99s limitations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 831, 830.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In all three cases, we ruled that student groups had been unconstitutionally singled out because of their points of view. \xe2\x80\x9cOnce it has opened a limited [public] forum,\xe2\x80\x9d we emphasized, \xe2\x80\x9cthe State must respect the lawful boundaries it has itself set.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 829. The constitutional constraints on the boundaries the State may set bear repetition here: \xe2\x80\x9cThe State may not exclude speech where its distinction is not reasonable in light of the purpose served by the forum, \xe2\x80\xa6 nor may it discriminate against speech on the basis of \xe2\x80\xa6 viewpoint.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We first consider whether Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy is reasonable taking into account the RSO forum\xe2\x80\x99s function and \xe2\x80\x9call the surrounding circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d Cornelius, 473 U. S., at 809.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our inquiry is shaped by the educational context in which it arises: \xe2\x80\x9cFirst Amendment rights,\xe2\x80\x9d we have observed, \xe2\x80\x9cmust be analyzed in light of the special characteristics of the school environment.\xe2\x80\x9d Widmar, 454 U. S., at 268, n.\xc2\xa05 (internal quotation marks omitted). This Court is the final arbiter of the question whether a public university has exceeded constitutional constraints, and we owe no deference to universities when we consider that question. Cf. Pell v. Procunier, 417 U. S. 817, 827 (1974) (\xe2\x80\x9cCourts cannot, of course, abdicate their constitutional responsibility to delineate and protect fundamental liberties.\xe2\x80\x9d). Cognizant that judges lack the on-the-ground expertise and experience of school administrators, however, we have cautioned courts in various contexts to resist \xe2\x80\x9csubstitut[ing] their own notions of sound educational policy for those of the school authorities which they review.\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Ed. of Hendrick Hudson Central School Dist., Westchester Cty. v. Rowley, 458 U. S. 176, 206 (1982). See also, e.g., Hazelwood School Dist. v. Kuhlmeier, 484 U. S. 260, 273 (1988) (noting our \xe2\x80\x9coft-expressed view that the education of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s youth is primarily the responsibility of parents, teachers, and state and local school officials, and not of federal judges\xe2\x80\x9d); Healy, 408 U. S., at 180 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]his Court has long recognized \xe2\x80\x98the need for affirming the comprehensive authority of the States and of school officials, consistent with fundamental constitutional safeguards, to prescribe and control conduct in the schools.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Tinker v. Des Moines Independent Community School Dist., 393 U. S. 503, 507 (1969))).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A college\xe2\x80\x99s commission\xe2\x80\x94and its concomitant license to choose among pedagogical approaches\xe2\x80\x94is not confined to the classroom, for extracurricular programs are, today, essential parts of the educational process. See Board of Ed. of Independent School Dist. No. 92 of Pottawatomie Cty. v. Earls, 536 U. S. 822, 831, n. 4 (2002) (involvement in student groups is \xe2\x80\x9ca significant contributor to the breadth and quality of the educational experience\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Schools, we have emphasized, enjoy \xe2\x80\x9ca significant measure of authority over the type of officially recognized activities in which their students participate.\xe2\x80\x9d Board of Ed. of Westside Community Schools (Dist. 66) v. Mergens, 496 U. S. 226, 240 (1990). We therefore \xe2\x80\x9capproach our task with special caution,\xe2\x80\x9d Healy, 408 U. S., at 171, mindful that Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 decisions about the character of its student-group program are due decent respect.[Footnote 16]

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With appropriate regard for school administrators\xe2\x80\x99 judgment, we review the justifications Hastings offers in defense of its all-comers requirement.[Footnote 17] First, the open-access policy \xe2\x80\x9censures that the leadership, educational, and social opportunities afforded by [RSOs] are available to all students.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Hastings 32; see Brief for American Civil Liberties Union et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 11. Just as \xe2\x80\x9cHastings does not allow its professors to host classes open only to those students with a certain status or belief,\xe2\x80\x9d so the Law School may decide, reasonably in our view, \xe2\x80\x9cthat the \xe2\x80\xa6 educational experience is best promoted when all participants in the forum must provide equal access to all students.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Hastings 32. RSOs,\nwe count it significant, are eligible for financial assist-\nance drawn from mandatory student-activity fees, see supra, at 3; the all-comers policy ensures that no Hastings student is forced to fund a group that would reject her as a member.[Footnote 18]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the all-comers requirement helps Hastings police the written terms of its Nondiscrimination Policy without inquiring into an RSO\xe2\x80\x99s motivation for membership restrictions. To bring the RSO program within CLS\xe2\x80\x99s view of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s limits, CLS proposes that Hastings permit exclusion because of belief but forbid discrimination due to status. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 18. But that proposal would impose on Hastings a daunting labor. How should the Law School go about determining whether a student organization cloaked prohibited status exclusion in belief-based garb? If a hypothetical Male-Superiority Club barred a female student from running for its presidency, for example, how could the Law School tell whether the group rejected her bid because of her sex or because, by seeking to lead the club, she manifested a lack of belief in its fundamental philosophy?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case itself is instructive in this regard. CLS contends that it does not exclude individuals because of sexual orientation, but rather \xe2\x80\x9con the basis of a conjunction of conduct and the belief that the conduct is not wrong.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 35\xe2\x80\x9336 (emphasis deleted). Our decisions have declined to distinguish between status and conduct in this context. See Lawrence v. Texas, 539 U. S. 558, 575 (2003) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen homosexual conduct is made criminal by the law of the State, that declaration in and of itself is an invitation to subject homosexual persons to discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); id., at 583 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhile it is true that the law applies only to conduct, the conduct targeted by this law is conduct that is closely correlated with being homosexual. Under such circumstances, [the] law is targeted at more than conduct. It is instead directed toward gay persons as a class.\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. Bray v. Alexandria Women\xe2\x80\x99s Health Clinic, 506 U. S. 263, 270 (1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cA tax on wearing yarmulkes is a tax on Jews.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Brief for Lambda Legal Defense and Education Fund, Inc., et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 7\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, the Law School reasonably adheres to the view that an all-comers policy, to the extent it brings together individuals with diverse backgrounds and beliefs, \xe2\x80\x9cencourages tolerance, cooperation, and learning among students.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 349.[Footnote 19] And if the policy sometimes produces discord, Hastings can rationally rank among RSO-program goals development of conflict-resolution skills, toleration, and readiness to find common ground.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fourth, Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy, which incorporates\xe2\x80\x94in fact, subsumes\xe2\x80\x94state-law proscriptions on discrimination, conveys the Law School\xe2\x80\x99s decision \xe2\x80\x9cto decline to subsidize with public monies and benefits conduct of which the people of California disapprove.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Hastings 35; id., at 33\xe2\x80\x9334 (citing Cal. Educ. Code \xc2\xa766270 (prohibiting discrimination on various bases)). State law, of course, may not command that public universities take action impermissible under the First Amendment. But so long as a public university does not contravene constitutional limits, its choice to advance state-law goals through the school\xe2\x80\x99s educational endeavors stands on firm footing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, the several justifications Hastings asserts in support of its all-comers requirement are surely reasonable in light of the RSO forum\xe2\x80\x99s purposes.[Footnote 20]

\n

3

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Law School\xe2\x80\x99s policy is all the more creditworthy in view of the \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial alternative channels that remain open for [CLS-student] communication to take place.\xe2\x80\x9d Perry Ed. Assn., 460 U. S., at 53. If restrictions on access to a limited public forum are viewpoint discriminatory, the ability of a group to exist outside the forum would not cure the constitutional shortcoming. But when access barriers are viewpoint neutral, our decisions have counted it significant that other available avenues for the group to exercise its First Amendment rights lessen the burden created by those barriers. See ibid.; Cornelius, 473 U. S., at 809; Greer v. Spock, 424 U. S. 828, 839 (1976); Pell, 417 U. S., at 827\xe2\x80\x93828.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, Hastings offered CLS access to school facilities to conduct meetings and the use of chalkboards and generally available bulletin boards to advertise events. App. 232\xe2\x80\x93233. Although CLS could not take advantage of RSO-specific methods of communication, see supra, at 3, the advent of electronic media and social-networking sites reduces the importance of those channels. See App. 114\xe2\x80\x93115 (CLS maintained a Yahoo! message group to disseminate information to students.); Christian Legal Society v. Walker, 453 F.\xc2\xa03d 853, 874 (CA7 2006) (Wood, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cMost universities and colleges, and most college-aged students, communicate through email, websites, and hosts like MySpace\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. If CLS had its own website, any student at the school with access to Google\xe2\x80\x94that is, all of them\xe2\x80\x94could easily have found it.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Brief for Associated Students of the University of California, Hastings College of Law as Amicus Curiae 14\xe2\x80\x9318 (describing host of ways CLS could communicate with Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 students outside official channels).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Private groups, from fraternities and sororities to social clubs and secret societies, commonly maintain a presence at universities without official school affiliation.[Footnote 21] Based on the record before us, CLS was similarly situated: It hosted a variety of activities the year after Hastings denied it recognition, and the number of students attending those meetings and events doubled. App. 224, 229\xe2\x80\x93230. \xe2\x80\x9cThe variety and type of alternative modes of access present here,\xe2\x80\x9d in short, \xe2\x80\x9ccompare favorably with those in other [limited public] forum cases where we have upheld restrictions on access.\xe2\x80\x9d Perry Ed. Assn., 460 U. S., at 53\xe2\x80\x9354. It is beyond dissenter\xe2\x80\x99s license, we note again, see supra, at 21, n.\xc2\xa017, constantly to maintain that nonrecognition of a student organization is equivalent to prohibiting its members from speaking.

\n

4

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CLS nevertheless deems Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 all-comers policy \xe2\x80\x9cfrankly absurd.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 49. \xe2\x80\x9cThere can be no diversity of viewpoints in a forum,\xe2\x80\x9d it asserts, \xe2\x80\x9cif groups are not permitted to form around viewpoints.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 50; accord post, at 25 (Alito, J., dissenting). This catchphrase confuses CLS\xe2\x80\x99s preferred policy with constitutional limitation\xe2\x80\x94the advisability of Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy does not control its permissibility. See Wood v. Strickland, 420 U. S. 308, 326 (1975). Instead, we have repeatedly stressed that a State\xe2\x80\x99s restriction on access to a limited public forum \xe2\x80\x9cneed not be the most reasonable or the only reasonable limitation.\xe2\x80\x9d Cornelius, 473 U. S., at 808.[Footnote 22]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CLS also assails the reasonableness of the all-comers policy in light of the RSO forum\xe2\x80\x99s function by forecasting that the policy will facilitate hostile takeovers; if organizations must open their arms to all, CLS contends, saboteurs will infiltrate groups to subvert their mission and message. This supposition strikes us as more hypothetical than real. CLS points to no history or prospect of RSO-hijackings at Hastings. Cf. National Endowment for Arts v. Finley, 524 U. S. 569, 584 (1998) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e are reluctant \xe2\x80\xa6 to invalidate legislation on the basis of its hypothetical application to situations not before the Court.\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). Students tend to self-sort and presumably will not endeavor en masse to join\xe2\x80\x94let alone seek leadership positions in\xe2\x80\x94groups pursuing missions wholly at odds with their personal beliefs. And if a rogue student intent on sabotaging an organization\xe2\x80\x99s objectives nevertheless attempted a takeover, the members of that group would not likely elect her as an officer.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0RSOs, moreover, in harmony with the all-comers policy, may condition eligibility for membership and leadership on attendance, the payment of dues, or other neutral requirements designed to ensure that students join because of their commitment to a group\xe2\x80\x99s vitality, not its demise. See supra, at 4, n. 2. Several RSOs at Hastings limit their membership rolls and officer slates in just this way. See, e.g., App. 192 (members must \xe2\x80\x9c[p]ay their dues on a timely basis\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cattend meetings regularly\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 173 (members must complete an application and pay dues; \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny active member who misses a semester of regularly scheduled meetings shall be dropped from rolls\xe2\x80\x9d); App. to Pet. for Cert. 129a (\xe2\x80\x9cOnly Hastings students who have held membership in this organization for a minimum of one semester shall be eligible to be an officer.\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 23]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hastings, furthermore, could reasonably expect more from its law students than the disruptive behavior CLS hypothesizes\xe2\x80\x94and to build this expectation into its educational approach. A reasonable policy need not anticipate and preemptively close off every opportunity for avoidance or manipulation. If students begin to exploit an all-comers policy by hijacking organizations to distort or destroy their missions, Hastings presumably would revisit and revise its policy. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 41 (counsel for Hastings); Brief for Hastings 38.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, CLS asserts (and the dissent repeats, post, at 29) that the Law School lacks any legitimate interest\xe2\x80\x94let alone one reasonably related to the RSO forum\xe2\x80\x99s purposes\xe2\x80\x94in urging \xe2\x80\x9creligious groups not to favor co-religionists for purposes of their religious activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 43; id., at 50. CLS\xe2\x80\x99s analytical error lies in focusing on the benefits it must forgo while ignoring the interests of those it seeks to fence out: Exclusion, after all, has two sides. Hastings, caught in the crossfire between a group\xe2\x80\x99s desire to exclude and students\xe2\x80\x99 demand for equal access, may reasonably draw a line in the sand permitting all organizations to express what they wish but no group to discriminate in membership.[Footnote 24]

\n

D

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We next consider whether Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 all-comers policy is viewpoint neutral.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although this aspect of limited-public-forum analysis has been the constitutional sticking point in our prior decisions, as earlier recounted, supra, at 17\xe2\x80\x9319, we need not dwell on it here. It is, after all, hard to imagine a more viewpoint-neutral policy than one requiring all student groups to accept all comers. In contrast to Healy, Widmar, and Rosenberger, in which universities singled out organizations for disfavored treatment because of their points of view, Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 all-comers requirement draws no distinction between groups based on their message or perspective. An all-comers condition on access to RSO status, in short, is textbook viewpoint neutral.[Footnote 25]

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Conceding that Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 all-comers policy is \xe2\x80\x9cnominally neutral,\xe2\x80\x9d CLS attacks the regulation by pointing to its effect: The policy is vulnerable to constitutional assault, CLS contends, because \xe2\x80\x9cit systematically and predictably burdens most heavily those groups whose viewpoints are out of favor with the campus mainstream.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 51; cf. post, at 1 (Alito, J., dissenting) (charging that Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy favors \xe2\x80\x9cpolitical[ly] correc[t]\xe2\x80\x9d student expression). This argument stumbles from its first step because \xe2\x80\x9c[a] regulation that serves purposes unrelated to the content of expression is deemed neutral, even if it has an incidental effect on some speakers or messages but not others.\xe2\x80\x9d Ward v. Rock Against Racism, 491 U. S. 781, 791 (1989). See also Madsen v. Women\xe2\x80\x99s Health Center, Inc., 512 U. S. 753, 763 (1994) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he fact that the injunction covered people with a particular viewpoint does not itself render the injunction content or viewpoint based.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if a regulation has a differential impact on groups wishing to enforce exclusionary membership policies, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]here the [State] does not target conduct on the basis of its expressive content, acts are not shielded from regulation merely because they express a discriminatory idea or philosophy.\xe2\x80\x9d R.\xc2\xa0A.\xc2\xa0V. v. St. Paul, 505 U. S. 377, 390 (1992). See also Roberts, 468 U. S., at 623 (State\xe2\x80\x99s nondiscrimination law did not \xe2\x80\x9cdistinguish between prohibited and permitted activity on the basis of viewpoint.\xe2\x80\x9d); Board of Directors of Rotary Int\xe2\x80\x99l v. Rotary Club of Duarte, 481 U. S. 537, 549 (1987) (same).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 requirement that student groups accept all comers, we are satisfied, \xe2\x80\x9cis justified without reference to the content [or viewpoint] of the regulated speech.\xe2\x80\x9d Ward, 491 U. S., at 791 (internal quotation marks and emphasis omitted). The Law School\xe2\x80\x99s policy aims at the act of rejecting would-be group members without reference to the reasons motivating that behavior: Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cdesire to redress th[e] perceived harms\xe2\x80\x9d of exclusionary membership policies \xe2\x80\x9cprovides an adequate explanation for its [all-comers condition] over and above mere disagreement with [any student group\xe2\x80\x99s] beliefs or biases.\xe2\x80\x9d Wisconsin v. Mitchell, 508 U. S. 476, 488 (1993). CLS\xe2\x80\x99s conduct\xe2\x80\x94not its Christian perspective\xe2\x80\x94is, from Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 vantage point, what stands between the group and RSO status. \xe2\x80\x9cIn the end,\xe2\x80\x9d as Hastings observes, \xe2\x80\x9cCLS is simply confusing its own viewpoint-based objections to \xe2\x80\xa6 nondiscrimination laws (which it is entitled to have and [to] voice) with viewpoint discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Hastings 31.[Footnote 26]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finding Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 open-access condition on RSO status reasonable and viewpoint neutral, we reject CLS\xe2\x80\x99 free-speech and expressive-association claims.[Footnote 27]

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In its reply brief, CLS contends that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he peculiarity, incoherence, and suspect history of the all-comers policy all point to pretext.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 23. Neither the District Court nor the Ninth Circuit addressed an argument that Hastings selectively enforces its all-comers policy, and this Court is not the proper forum to air the issue in the first instance.[Footnote 28] On remand, the Ninth Circuit may consider CLS\xe2\x80\x99s pretext argument if, and to the extent, it is\npreserved.[Footnote 29]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the foregoing reasons, we affirm the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 ruling that the all-comers policy is constitutional and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, concurring.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court correctly confines its discussion to the narrow issue presented by the record, see ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9312, and correctly upholds the all-comers policy. I join its opinion without reservation. Because the dissent has volunteered an argument that the school\xe2\x80\x99s general Nondiscrimination Policy would be \xe2\x80\x9cplainly\xe2\x80\x9d unconstitutional if applied to this case, post, at 18 (opinion of Alito, J.), a brief response\nis appropriate. In my view, both policies are plainly\nlegitimate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Hastings College of Law\xe2\x80\x99s (Hastings) Nondiscrimination Policy contains boilerplate language used by institutions and workplaces across the country: It prohibits \xe2\x80\x9cunlawfu[l]\xe2\x80\x9d discrimination \xe2\x80\x9con the basis of race, color, religion, national origin, ancestry, disability, age, sex or sexual orientation.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 220. Petitioner, the Hastings chapter of the Christian Legal Society (CLS), refused to comply. As the Court explains, ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, CLS was unwilling to admit members unless they affirmed their belief in certain Christian doctrines and refrained from \xe2\x80\x9cparticipation in or advocacy of a sexually immoral lifestyle,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 146. CLS, in short, wanted to receive the school\xe2\x80\x99s formal recognition\xe2\x80\x94and the benefits that attend formal recognition\xe2\x80\x94while continuing to exclude gay and non-Christian students (as well as, it seems, students who advocate for gay rights).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view, by refusing to grant CLS an exemption from the Nondiscrimination Policy, Hastings violated CLS\xe2\x80\x99s rights, for by proscribing unlawful discrimination on the basis of religion, the policy discriminates unlawfully on the basis of religion. There are numerous reasons why this counterintuitive theory is unsound. Although the First Amendment may protect CLS\xe2\x80\x99s discriminatory practices off campus, it does not require a public university to validate or support them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As written, the Nondiscrimination Policy is content and viewpoint neutral. It does not reflect a judgment by school officials about the substance of any student group\xe2\x80\x99s speech. Nor does it exclude any would-be groups on the basis of their convictions. Indeed, it does not regulate expression or belief at all. The policy is \xe2\x80\x9cdirected at the organization\xe2\x80\x99s activities rather than its philosophy,\xe2\x80\x9d Healy v. James, 408 U. S. 169, 188 (1972). Those who hold religious beliefs are not \xe2\x80\x9csingled out,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 19 (Alito, J., dissenting); those who engage in discriminatory conduct based on someone else\xe2\x80\x99s religious status and belief are singled out.[Footnote 1] Regardless of whether they are the product of secular or spiritual feeling, hateful or benign motives, all acts of religious discrimination are equally covered. The discriminator\xe2\x80\x99s beliefs are simply irrelevant. There is, moreover, no evidence that the policy was adopted because of any reason related to the particular views that religious individuals or groups might have, much less because of a desire to suppress or distort those views. The policy\xe2\x80\x99s religion clause was plainly meant to promote, not to undermine, religious freedom.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To be sure, the policy may end up having greater consequence for religious groups\xe2\x80\x94whether and to what extent it will is far from clear ex ante\xe2\x80\x94inasmuch as they are more likely than their secular counterparts to wish to exclude students of particular faiths. But there is likewise no evidence that the policy was intended to cause harm to religious groups, or that it has in practice caused significant harm to their operations. And it is a basic tenet of First Amendment law that disparate impact does not, in itself, constitute viewpoint discrimination.[Footnote 2] The dissent has thus given no reason to be skeptical of the basic\ndesign, function, or rationale of the Nondiscrimination Policy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What the policy does reflect is a judgment that discrimination by school officials or organizations on the basis of certain factors, such as race and religion, is less tolerable than discrimination on the basis of other factors. This approach may or may not be the wisest choice in the context of a Registered Student Organization (RSO) program. But it is at least a reasonable choice. Academic administrators routinely employ antidiscrimination rules to promote tolerance, understanding, and respect, and to safeguard students from invidious forms of discrimination, including sexual orientation discrimination.[Footnote 3] Applied to the RSO context, these values can, in turn, advance numerous pedagogical objectives. See post, at 3\xe2\x80\x934 (Kennedy, J., concurring).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is critical, in evaluating CLS\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to the Nondiscrimination Policy, to keep in mind that an RSO program is a limited forum\xe2\x80\x94the boundaries of which may be delimited by the proprietor. When a religious association, or a secular association, operates in a wholly public setting, it must be allowed broad freedom to control its membership and its message, even if its decisions cause offense to outsiders. Profound constitutional problems would arise if the State of California tried to \xe2\x80\x9cdemand that all Christian groups admit members who believe that Jesus was merely human.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 27 (Alito, J., dissenting). But the CLS chapter that brought this lawsuit does not want to be just a Christian group; it aspires to be a recognized student organization. The Hastings College of Law is not a legislature. And no state actor has demanded that anyone do anything outside the confines of a discrete, voluntary academic program. Although it may be the case that to some \xe2\x80\x9cuniversity students, the campus is their world,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 13 (internal quotation marks omitted), it does not follow that the campus ought to be equated with the public square.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The campus is, in fact, a world apart from the public square in numerous respects, and religious organizations, as well as all other organizations, must abide by certain norms of conduct when they enter an academic community. Public universities serve a distinctive role in a modern democratic society. Like all specialized government entities, they must make countless decisions about how to allocate resources in pursuit of their role. Some of those decisions will be controversial; many will have differential effects across populations; virtually all will entail value judgments of some kind. As a general matter, courts should respect universities\xe2\x80\x99 judgments and let them manage their own affairs.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The RSO forum is no different. It is not an open commons that Hastings happens to maintain. It is a mechanism through which Hastings confers certain benefits and pursues certain aspects of its educational mission. Having exercised its discretion to establish an RSO program, a university must treat all participants evenhandedly. But the university need not remain neutral\xe2\x80\x94indeed it could not remain neutral\xe2\x80\x94in determining which goals the program will serve and which rules are best suited to facilitate those goals. These are not legal questions but policy questions; they are not for the Court but for the university to make. When any given group refuses to comply with the rules, the RSO sponsor need not admit that group at the cost of undermining the program and the values reflected therein. On many levels, a university administrator has a \xe2\x80\x9cgreater interest in the content of student activities than the police chief has in the content of a soapbox oration.\xe2\x80\x9d Widmar v. Vincent, 454 U. S. 263, 280 (1981) (Stevens, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, petitioner excludes students who will not sign its Statement of Faith or who engage in \xe2\x80\x9cunrepentant homosexual conduct,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 226. The expressive association argument it presses, however, is hardly limited to these facts. Other groups may exclude or mistreat Jews, blacks, and women\xe2\x80\x94or those who do not share their contempt for Jews, blacks, and women. A free society must tolerate such groups. It need not subsidize them, give them its official imprimatur, or grant them equal access to law school facilities.

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Rosenberger v. Rector and Visitors of Univ. of Va., 515 U. S. 819 (1995), the essential purpose of the limited forum was to facilitate the expression of differing views in the context of student publications. The forum was limited because it was confined: first, to student-run groups; and second, to publications. The forum was created in the long tradition of using newspapers and other publications to express differing views and also in the honored tradition of a university setting that stimulates the free exchange of ideas. See id., at 835 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]n the University setting, \xe2\x80\xa6 the State acts against a background and tradition of thought and experiment that is at the center of our intellectual and philosophic tradition\xe2\x80\x9d). These considerations supported the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that, under the First Amendment, a limited forum for student-run publications did not permit the exclusion of a paper for the reason that it was devoted to expressing religious views.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rosenberger is distinguishable from the instant case in various respects. Not least is that here the school policy in question is not content based either in its formulation or evident purpose; and were it shown to be otherwise, the case likely should have a different outcome. Here, the policy applies equally to all groups and views. And, given the stipulation of the parties, there is no basis for an allegation that the design or purpose of the rule was, by subterfuge, to discriminate based on viewpoint.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An objection might be that the all-comers policy, even if not so designed or intended, in fact makes it difficult for certain groups to express their views in a manner essential to their message. A group that can limit membership to those who agree in full with its aims and purposes may be more effective in delivering its message or furthering its expressive objectives; and the Court has recognized that this interest can be protected against governmental interference or regulation. See Boy Scouts of America v. Dale, 530 U. S. 640 (2000). By allowing like-minded students to form groups around shared identities, a school creates room for self-expression and personal development. See Board of Regents of Univ. of Wis. System v. Southworth, 529 U. S. 217, 229 (2000) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe University\xe2\x80\x99s whole justification for [its student activity program] is that it springs from the initiative of the students, who alone give it purpose and content in the course of their extracurricular endeavors\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the instant case, however, if the membership qualification were enforced, it would contradict a legitimate purpose for having created the limited forum in the first place. Many educational institutions, including respondent Hastings College of Law, have recognized that the process of learning occurs both formally in a classroom setting and informally outside of it. See id., at 233. Students may be shaped as profoundly by their peers as by their teachers. Extracurricular activities, such as those in the Hastings \xe2\x80\x9cRegistered Student Organization\xe2\x80\x9d program, facilitate interactions between students, enabling them to explore new points of view, to develop interests and talents, and to nurture a growing sense of self. See Board of Ed. of Independent School Dist. No. 92 of Pottawatomie Cty. v. Earls, 536 U. S. 822, 831, n.\xc2\xa04 (2002) (participation in extracurricular activities is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a significant contributor to the breadth and quality of the educational experience\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). The Hasting program is designed to allow all students to interact with their colleagues across a broad, seemingly unlimited range of ideas, views, and activities. See Regents of Univ. of Cal. v. Bakke, 438 U. S. 265, 312, 313, n.\xc2\xa048 (1978) (opinion of Powell, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] great deal of learning \xe2\x80\xa6 occurs through interactions among students \xe2\x80\xa6 who have a wide variety of interests, talents, and perspectives; and who are able, directly or indirectly, to learn from their differences and to stimulate one another to reexamine even their most deeply held assumptions about themselves and their world\xe2\x80\x9d (alteration in original; internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Law students come from many backgrounds and have but three years to meet each other and develop their skills. They do so by participating in a community that teaches them how to create arguments in a convincing, rational, and respectful manner and to express doubt and disagreement in a professional way. A law school furthers these objectives by allowing broad diversity in registered student organizations. But these objectives may be better achieved if students can act cooperatively to learn from and teach each other through interactions in social and intellectual contexts. A vibrant dialogue is not possible if students wall themselves off from opposing points of view.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The school\xe2\x80\x99s objectives thus might not be well served if, as a condition to membership or participation in a group, students were required to avow particular personal beliefs or to disclose private, off-campus behavior. Students whose views are in the minority at the school would likely fare worse in that regime. Indeed, were those sorts of requirements to become prevalent, it might undermine the principle that in a university community\xe2\x80\x94and in a law school community specifically\xe2\x80\x94speech is deemed persuasive based on its substance, not the identity of the speaker. The era of loyalty oaths is behind us. A school quite properly may conclude that allowing an oath or belief-affirming requirement, or an outside conduct requirement, could be divisive for student relations and inconsistent with the basic concept that a view\xe2\x80\x99s validity should be tested through free and open discussion. The school\xe2\x80\x99s policy therefore represents a permissible effort to preserve the value of its forum.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to a circumstance, already noted, in which it could be demonstrated that a school has adopted or enforced its policy with the intent or purpose of discriminating or disadvantaging a group on account of its views, petitioner also would have a substantial case on the merits if it were shown that the all-comers policy was either designed or used to infiltrate the group or challenge its leadership in order to stifle its views. But that has not been shown to be so likely or self-evident as a matter of group dynamics in this setting that the Court can declare the school policy void without more facts; and if there were a showing that in a particular case the purpose or effect of the policy was to stifle speech or make it ineffective, that, too, would present a case different from the one before us.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These observations are offered to support the analysis set forth in the opinion of the Court, which I join.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The proudest boast of our free speech jurisprudence is that we protect the freedom to express \xe2\x80\x9cthe thought that we hate.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Schwimmer, 279 U. S. 644, 654\xe2\x80\x93655 (1929) (Holmes, J., dissenting). Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision rests on a very different principle: no freedom for expression that offends prevailing standards of political correctness in our country\xe2\x80\x99s institutions of higher learning.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Hastings College of the Law, a state institution, permits student organizations to register with the law school and severely burdens speech by unregistered groups. Hastings currently has more than 60 registered groups and, in all its history, has denied registration to exactly one: the Christian Legal Society (CLS). CLS claims that Hastings refused to register the group because the law school administration disapproves of the group\xe2\x80\x99s viewpoint and thus violated the group\xe2\x80\x99s free speech rights.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rejecting this argument, the Court finds that it has been Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy for 20 years that all registered organizations must admit any student who wishes to join. Deferring broadly to the law school\xe2\x80\x99s judgment about the permissible limits of student debate, the Court concludes that this \xe2\x80\x9caccept-all-comers\xe2\x80\x9d policy, ante, at 1, is both viewpoint-neutral and consistent with Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 proclaimed policy of fostering a diversity of viewpoints among registered student groups.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s treatment of this case is deeply disappointing. The Court does not address the constitutionality of the very different policy that Hastings invoked when it denied CLS\xe2\x80\x99s application for registration. Nor does the Court address the constitutionality of the policy that Hastings now purports to follow. And the Court ignores strong evidence that the accept-all-comers policy is not viewpoint neutral because it was announced as a pretext to justify viewpoint discrimination. Brushing aside inconvenient precedent, the Court arms public educational institutions with a handy weapon for suppressing the speech of unpopular groups\xe2\x80\x94groups to which, as Hastings candidly puts it, these institutions \xe2\x80\x9cdo not wish to \xe2\x80\xa6 lend their name[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent Hastings College of Law 11; see also id., at 35.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court provides a misleading portrayal of this case. As related by the Court, (1) Hastings, for the past 20 years, has required any student group seeking registration to admit any student who wishes to join, ante, at 5; (2) the effects of Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 refusal to register CLS have been of questionable importance, see ante, at 24\xe2\x80\x9325; and (3) this case is about CLS\xe2\x80\x99s desire to obtain \xe2\x80\x9ca state subsidy,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15. I begin by correcting the picture.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court bases all of its analysis on the proposition that the relevant Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy is the so-called accept-all-comers policy. This frees the Court from the difficult task of defending the constitutionality of either the policy that Hastings actually\xe2\x80\x94and repeatedly\xe2\x80\x94invoked when it denied registration, i.e., the school\xe2\x80\x99s written Nondiscrimination Policy, or the policy that Hastings belatedly unveiled when it filed its brief in this Court. Overwhelming evidence, however, shows that Hastings denied CLS\xe2\x80\x99s application pursuant to the Nondiscrimination Policy and that the accept-all-comers policy was nowhere to be found until it was mentioned by a former dean in a deposition taken well after this case began.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The events that gave rise to this litigation began in 2004, when a small group of Hastings students sought to register a Hastings chapter of CLS, a national organization of Christian lawyers and law students. All CLS members must sign a Statement of Faith affirming belief in fundamental Christian doctrines, including the belief that the Bible is \xe2\x80\x9cthe inspired Word of God.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 226. In early 2004, the national organization adopted a resolution stating that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n view of the clear dictates of Scripture, unrepentant participation in or advocacy of a sexually immoral lifestyle is inconsistent with an affirmation of the Statement of Faith, and consequently may be regarded by CLS as disqualifying such an individual from CLS membership.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 146. The resolution made it clear that \xe2\x80\x9ca sexually immoral lifestyle,\xe2\x80\x9d in CLS\xe2\x80\x99s view, includes engaging in \xe2\x80\x9cacts of sexual conduct outside of God\xe2\x80\x99s design for marriage between one man and one woman.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. It was shortly after this resolution was passed that the Hastings chapter of CLS applied to register with the law school.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hastings sponsors an active program of \xe2\x80\x9cregistered student organizations\xe2\x80\x9d (RSOs) pursuant to the law school\xe2\x80\x99s avowed responsibility to \xe2\x80\x9censure an opportunity for the expression of a variety of viewpoints\xe2\x80\x9d and promote \xe2\x80\x9cthe highest standards of . . . freedom of expression,\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 82a, 74a. During the 2004\xe2\x80\x932005 school year, Hastings had more than 60 registered groups, including political groups (e.g., the Hastings Democratic Caucus and the Hastings Republicans), religious groups (e.g., the Hastings Jewish Law Students Association and the Hastings Association of Muslim Law Students), groups that promote social causes (e.g., both pro-choice and pro-life groups), groups organized around racial or ethnic identity (e.g., the Black Law Students Association, the Korean American Law Society, La Raza Law Students Association, and the Middle Eastern Law Students Association), and groups that focus on gender or sexuality (e.g., the Clara Foltz Feminist Association and Students Raising Consciousness at Hastings). See App. 236\xe2\x80\x93245; Brief for Petitioner 3\xe2\x80\x934.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Not surprisingly many of these registered groups were and are dedicated to expressing a message. For example, Silenced Right, a pro-life group, taught that \xe2\x80\x9call human life from the moment of conception until natural death is sacred and has inherent dignity,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 244, while Law Students for Choice aimed to \xe2\x80\x9cdefend and expand reproductive rights,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 243. The American Constitution Society sought \xe2\x80\x9cto counter . . . a narrow conservative vision\xe2\x80\x9d of American law,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 236, and the UC Hastings Student Animal Defense Fund aimed \xe2\x80\x9cat protecting the lives and advancing the interests of animals through the legal system,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 245.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Groups that are granted registration are entitled to meet on university grounds and to access multiple channels for communicating with students and faculty\xe2\x80\x94including posting messages on designated bulletin boards, sending mass e-mails to the student body, distributing material through the Student Information Center, and participating in the annual student organizations fair. App. to Pet. for Cert. 7a, 85a. They may also apply for limited travel funds, id., at 7a, which appear to total about $4,000 to $5,000 per year, App. 217\xe2\x80\x94or less than $85 per registered group. Most of the funds available to RSOs come from an annual student activity fee that every student must pay. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 89a\xe2\x80\x9393a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When CLS applied for registration, Judy Hansen Chapman, the Director of Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 Office of Student Services, sent an e-mail to an officer of the chapter informing him that \xe2\x80\x9cCLS\xe2\x80\x99s bylaws did not appear to be compliant\xe2\x80\x9d with the Hastings Nondiscrimination Policy, App. 228, 277, a written policy that provides in pertinent part that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he University of California, Hastings College of the Law shall not discriminate unlawfully on the basis of race, color, religion, national origin, ancestry, disability, age, sex or sexual orientation,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 220. As far as the record reflects, Ms. Chapman made no mention of an accept-all-applicants policy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A few days later, three officers of the chapter met with Ms. Chapman, and she reiterated that the CLS bylaws did not comply with \xe2\x80\x9cthe religion and sexual orientation provisions of the Nondiscrimination Policy and that they would need to be amended in order for CLS to become a registered student organization.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 228. About a week later, Hastings sent CLS a letter to the same effect. Id., at 228\xe2\x80\x93229, 293\xe2\x80\x93295. On both of these occasions, it appears that not a word was said about an accept-all-comers\npolicy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When CLS refused to change its membership requirements, Hastings denied its request for registration\xe2\x80\x94thus making CLS the only student group whose application for registration has ever been rejected. Brief in Opposition 4.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In October 2004, CLS brought this action under 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 against the law school\xe2\x80\x99s dean and other school officials, claiming, among other things, that the law school, by enacting and enforcing the Nondiscrimination Policy, had violated CLS\xe2\x80\x99s First Amendment right to freedom of speech. App. 78.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In May 2005, Hastings filed an answer to CLS\xe2\x80\x99s first amended complaint and made an admission that is significant for present purposes. In its complaint, CLS had alleged that the Nondiscrimination Policy discriminates against religious groups because it prohibits those groups \xe2\x80\x9cfrom selecting officers and members dedicated to a particular set of religious ideals or beliefs\xe2\x80\x9d but \xe2\x80\x9cpermits political, social and cultural student organizations to select officers and members dedicated to their organization\xe2\x80\x99s ideals and beliefs.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 79. In response, Hastings admitted that its Nondiscrimination Policy \xe2\x80\x9cpermits political, social, and cultural student organizations to select officers and members who are dedicated to a particular set of ideals or beliefs.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 93. The Court states that \xe2\x80\x9cHastings interprets the Nondiscrimination Policy, as it relates to the RSO program, to mandate acceptance of all comers.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4. But this admission in Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 answer shows that Hastings had not adopted this interpretation when its answer was filed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Within a few months, however, Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 position changed. In July 2005, Mary Kay Kane, then the dean of the law school, was deposed, and she stated: \xe2\x80\x9cIt is my view that in order to be a registered student organization you have to allow all of our students to be members and full participants if they want to.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 343. In a declaration filed in October 2005, Ms. Chapman provided a more developed explanation, stating: \xe2\x80\x9cHastings interprets the Nondiscrimination Policy as requiring that student organizations wishing to register with Hastings allow any Hastings student to become a member and/or seek a leadership position in the organization.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 349.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hastings claims that this accept-all-comers policy has existed since 1990 but points to no evidence that the policy was ever put in writing or brought to the attention of members of the law school community prior to the dean\xe2\x80\x99s deposition. Indeed, Hastings has adduced no evidence of the policy\xe2\x80\x99s existence before that date. And while Dean Kane and Ms. Chapman stated, well after this litigation had begun, that Hastings had such a policy, neither they nor any other Hastings official has ever stated in a deposition, affidavit, or declaration when this policy took effect.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 effort to portray the accept-all-comers policy as merely an interpretation of the Nondiscrimination Policy runs into obvious difficulties. First, the two policies are simply not the same: The Nondiscrimination Policy proscribes discrimination on a limited number of specified grounds, while the accept-all-comers policy outlaws all selectivity. Second, the Nondiscrimination Policy applies to everything that Hastings does, and the law school does not follow an accept-all-comers policy in activities such as admitting students and hiring faculty.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In an effort to circumvent this problem, the Court writes that \xe2\x80\x9cHastings interprets the Nondiscrimination Policy, as it relates to the RSO program, to mandate acceptance of all comers.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4 (emphasis added). This puts Hastings in the implausible position of maintaining that the Nondiscrimination Policy means one thing as applied to the RSO program and something quite different as applied to all of Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 other activities. But the Nondiscrimination Policy by its terms applies fully to all components of the law school, \xe2\x80\x9cincluding administration [and] faculty.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 220.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, the record is replete with evidence that, at least until Dean Kane unveiled the accept-all-comers policy in July 2005, Hastings routinely registered student groups with bylaws limiting membership and leadership positions to those who agreed with the groups\xe2\x80\x99 viewpoints. For example, the bylaws of the Hastings Democratic Caucus provided that \xe2\x80\x9cany full-time student at Hastings may become a member of HDC so long as they do not exhibit a consistent disregard and lack of respect for the objective of the organization as stated in Article 3, Section 1.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 118a (emphasis added). The constitution of the Association of Trial Lawyers of America at Hastings provided that every member must \xe2\x80\x9cadhere to the objectives of the Student Chapter as well as the mission of ATLA.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 110a. A student could become a member of the Vietnamese American Law Society so long as the student did not \xe2\x80\x9cexhibit a consistent disregard and lack of respect for the objective of the organization,\xe2\x80\x9d which centers on a \xe2\x80\x9ccelebrat[ion] [of] Vietnamese culture.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 146a\xe2\x80\x93147a. Silenced Right limited voting membership to students who \xe2\x80\x9care committed\xe2\x80\x9d to the group\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmission\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9cspread[ing] the pro-life message.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 142a\xe2\x80\x93143a. La Raza limited voting membership to \xe2\x80\x9cstudents of Raza background.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 192. Since Hastings requires any student group applying for registration to submit a copy of its bylaws, see id., at 249\xe2\x80\x93250, Hastings cannot claim that it was unaware of such provisions. And as noted, CLS was denied registration precisely because Ms. Chapman reviewed its bylaws and found them unacceptable.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are told that, when CLS pointed out these discrepancies during this litigation, Hastings took action to ensure that student groups were in fact complying with the law school\xe2\x80\x99s newly disclosed accept-all-comers policy. For example, Hastings asked La Raza to revise its bylaws to allow all students to become voting members. App. to Pet. for Cert. 66a. See also Brief for State of Michigan et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 2, n.\xc2\xa01 (relating anecdotally that Hastings recently notified the Hastings Democrats that \xe2\x80\x9cto maintain the Club\xe2\x80\x99s standing as a student organization,\xe2\x80\x9d it must \xe2\x80\x9copen its membership to all students, regardless of party affiliation\xe2\x80\x9d). These belated remedial efforts suggest, if anything, that Hastings had no accept-all-comers policy until this litigation was well under way.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, when Hastings filed its brief in this Court, its policy, which had already evolved from a policy prohibiting certain specified forms of discrimination into an accept-all-comers policy, underwent yet another transformation. Now, Hastings claims that it does not really have an accept-all-comers policy; it has an accept-some-comers policy. Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 current policy, we are told, \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not foreclose neutral and generally applicable membership requirements unrelated to \xe2\x80\x98status or beliefs.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent Hastings College of Law 5. Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 brief goes on to note with seeming approval that some registered groups have imposed \xe2\x80\x9ceven conduct requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Hastings, however, has not told us which \xe2\x80\x9cconduct requirements\xe2\x80\x9d are allowed and which are not\xe2\x80\x94although presumably requirements regarding sexual conduct fall into the latter category.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When this case was in the District Court, that court took care to address both the Nondiscrimination Policy and the accept-all-comers policy. See, e.g., App. to Pet. for Cert. 8a\xe2\x80\x939a, 16a\xe2\x80\x9317a, 21a\xe2\x80\x9324a, 26a, 27a, 32a, 44a, 63a. On appeal, however, a panel of the Ninth Circuit, like the Court today, totally ignored the Nondiscrimination Policy. CLS\xe2\x80\x99s argument in the Ninth Circuit centered on the Nondiscrimination Policy, and CLS argued strenuously, as it had in the District Court, that prior to the former dean\xe2\x80\x99s deposition, numerous groups had been permitted to restrict membership to students who shared the groups\xe2\x80\x99 views.[Footnote 1] Nevertheless, the Ninth Circuit disposed of CLS\xe2\x80\x99s appeal with a two-sentence, not-precedential opinion that solely addressed the accept-all-comers policy. Christian Legal Soc. Chapter of Univ. of Cal. v. Kane, 319 Fed. Appx. 645\xe2\x80\x93646 (2009).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Like the majority of this Court, the Ninth Circuit relied on the following Joint Stipulation, which the parties filed in December 2005, well after Dean Kane\xe2\x80\x99s deposition:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cHastings requires that registered student organizations allow any student to participate, become a member, or seek leadership positions in the organization, regardless of their status or beliefs.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 221.

\n

\nCiting the binding effect of stipulations, the majority sternly rejects what it terms \xe2\x80\x9cCLS\xe2\x80\x99s unseemly attempt to escape from the stipulation and shift its target to [the Nondiscrimination Policy].\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree that the parties must be held to their Joint Stipulation, but the terms of the stipulation should be respected. What was admitted in the Joint Stipulation filed in December 2005 is that Hastings had an accept-all-comers policy. CLS did not stipulate that its application had been denied more than a year earlier pursuant to such a policy. On the contrary, the Joint Stipulation notes that the reason repeatedly given by Hasting at that time was that the CLS bylaws did not comply with the Nondiscrimination Policy. See App. 228\xe2\x80\x93229. Indeed, the parties did not even stipulate that the accept-all-comers policy existed in the fall of 2004. In addition, Hastings itself is now attempting to walk away from this stipulation by disclosing that its real policy is an accept-some-comers policy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s insistence on the binding effect of stipulations contrasts sharply with its failure to recognize the binding effect of a party\xe2\x80\x99s admissions in an answer. See American Title Insurance Co. v. Lacelaw Corp., 861 F.\xc2\xa02d 224, 226 (CA9 1988) (\xe2\x80\x9cFactual assertions in pleadings and pretrial orders, unless amended, are considered judicial admissions conclusively binding on the party who made them\xe2\x80\x9d); Bakersfield Westar Ambulance, Inc. v. Community First Bank, 123 F.\xc2\xa03d 1243, 1248 (CA9 1997) (quoting Lacelaw, supra). As noted above, Hastings admitted in its answer, which was filed prior to the former dean\xe2\x80\x99s deposition, that at least as of that time, the law school did not follow an accept-all-comers policy and instead allowed \xe2\x80\x9cpolitical, social, and cultural student organizations to select officers and members who are dedicated to a particular set of ideals or beliefs.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 93.\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court also distorts the record with respect to the effect on CLS of Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 decision to deny registration. The Court quotes a letter written by Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 general counsel in which she stated that Hastings \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98would be pleased to provide [CLS] the use of Hastings facilities for its meetings and activities.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6 (quoting App. 294). Later in its opinion, the Court reiterates that \xe2\x80\x9cHastings offered CLS access to school facilities to conduct meetings,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 24, but the majority does not mention that this offer was subject to important qualifications. As Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 attorney put it in the District Court, Hastings told CLS: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Hastings allows community groups to some degree to use its facilities, sometimes on a pay basis, I understand, if they\xe2\x80\x99re available after priority is given to registered organizations\xe2\x80\x99. We offered that.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 442.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court also fails to mention what happened when CLS attempted to take advantage of Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 offer. On August 19, 2005, the local CLS president sent an e-mail to Ms. Chapman requesting permission to set up an \xe2\x80\x9cadvice table\xe2\x80\x9d on a campus patio on August 23 and 24 so that members of CLS could speak with students at the beginning of the fall semester. Id., at 298. This request\xe2\x80\x94merely to set up a table on a patio\xe2\x80\x94could hardly have interfered with any other use of the law school\xe2\x80\x99s premises or cost the school any money. But although the request was labeled \xe2\x80\x9ctime sensitive,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., Ms. Chapman did not respond until the dates in question had passed, and she then advised the student that all further inquiries should be made through CLS\xe2\x80\x99s attorney. Id., at 297\xe2\x80\x93298.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In September 2005, CLS tried again. Through counsel, CLS sought to reserve a room on campus for a guest speaker who was scheduled to appear on a specified date. Id., at 302\xe2\x80\x93303. Noting Ms. Chapman\xe2\x80\x99s tardy response on the prior occasion, the attorney asked to receive a response before the scheduled date, but once again no answer was given until after the date had passed. Id., at 300.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Other statements in the majority opinion make it seem as if the denial of registration did not hurt CLS at all. The Court notes that CLS was able to hold Bible-study meetings and other events. Ante, at 6. And \xe2\x80\x9c[a]lthough CLS could not take advantage of RSO-specific methods of communication,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court states, \xe2\x80\x9cthe advent of electronic media and social-networking sites reduces the importance of those channels.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 24.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the beginning of the 2005 school year, the Hastings CLS group had seven members, App. to Pet. for Cert. 13a, so there can be no suggestion that the group flourished. And since one of CLS\xe2\x80\x99s principal claims is that it was subjected to discrimination based on its viewpoint, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on CLS\xe2\x80\x99s ability to endure that discrimination\xe2\x80\x94by using private facilities and means of communication\xe2\x80\x94is quite amazing.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court does not customarily brush aside a claim of unlawful discrimination with the observation that the effects of the discrimination were really not so bad. We have never before taken the view that a little viewpoint discrimination is acceptable. Nor have we taken this approach in other discrimination cases.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, I must comment on the majority\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on funding. According to the majority, CLS is \xe2\x80\x9cseeking what is effectively a state subsidy,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 15, and the question presented in this case centers on the \xe2\x80\x9cuse of school funds,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 1. In fact, funding plays a very small role in this case. Most of what CLS sought and was denied\xe2\x80\x94such as permission to set up a table on the law school patio\xe2\x80\x94would have been virtually cost free. If every such activity is regarded as a matter of funding, the First Amendment rights of students at public universities will be at the mercy of the administration. As CLS notes, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o university students, the campus is their world. The right to meet on campus and use campus channels of communication is at least as important to university students as the right to gather on the town square and use local communication forums is to the citizen.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioner 13.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To appreciate how far the Court has strayed, it is instructive to compare this case with Healy v. James, 408 U. S. 169 (1972), our only First Amendment precedent involving a public college\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to recognize a student group. The group in Healy was a local chapter of the Students for a Democratic Society (SDS). When the students who applied for recognition of the chapter were asked by a college committee whether they would \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98respond to issues of violence as other S.D.S. chapters have,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d their answer was that their \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98action would have to be dependent upon each issue.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 172\xe2\x80\x93173. They similarly refused to provide a definitive answer when asked whether they would be willing to \xe2\x80\x9cuse any means possible\xe2\x80\x9d to achieve their aims. Id., at 173. The president of the college refused to allow the group to be recognized, concluding that the philosophy of the SDS was \xe2\x80\x9cantithetical to the school\xe2\x80\x99s policies\xe2\x80\x9d and that it was doubtful that the local chapter was independent of the national organization, the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98published aims and philosophy\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of which included \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98disruption and violence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 174\xe2\x80\x93175, and n.\xc2\xa04.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The effects of nonrecognition in Healy were largely the same as those present here. The SDS was denied the use of campus facilities, as well as access to the customary means used for communication among the members of the college community. Id., at 176, 181\xe2\x80\x93182.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The lower federal courts held that the First Amendment rights of the SDS chapter had not been violated, and when the case reached this Court, the college, much like today\xe2\x80\x99s majority, sought to minimize the effects of nonrecognition, arguing that the SDS members \xe2\x80\x9cstill may meet as a group off campus, that they still may distribute written material off campus, and that they still may meet together informally on campus \xe2\x80\xa6 as individuals.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 182\xe2\x80\x93183.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court took a different view. The Court held that the denial of recognition substantially burdened the students\xe2\x80\x99 right to freedom of association. After observing that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he primary impediment to free association flowing from nonrecognition is the denial of use of campus facilities for meetings and other appropriate purposes,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 181, the Court continued:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 associational interests also were circumscribed by the denial of the use of campus bulletin boards and the school newspaper. If an organization is to remain a viable entity in a campus community in which new students enter on a regular basis, it must possess the means of communicating with these students. Moreover, the organization\xe2\x80\x99s ability to participate in the intellectual give and take of campus debate, and to pursue its stated purposes, is limited by denial of access to the customary media for communicating with the administration, faculty members, and other students. Such impediments cannot be viewed as insubstantial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 181\xe2\x80\x93182.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is striking that all of these same burdens are now borne by CLS. CLS is prevented from using campus facilities\xe2\x80\x94unless at some future time Hastings chooses to provide a timely response to a CLS request and allow the group, as a favor or perhaps in exchange for a fee, to set up a table on the patio or to use a room that would otherwise be unoccupied. And CLS, like the SDS in Healy, has been cut off from \xe2\x80\x9cthe customary media for communicating with the administration, faculty members, and other students.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 181\xe2\x80\x93182.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is also telling that the Healy Court, unlike today\xe2\x80\x99s majority, refused to defer to the college president\xe2\x80\x99s judgment regarding the compatibility of \xe2\x80\x9csound educational policy\xe2\x80\x9d and free speech rights. The same deference arguments that the majority now accepts were made in defense of the college president\xe2\x80\x99s decision to deny recognition in Healy. Respondents in that case emphasized that the college president, not the courts, had the responsibility of administering the institution and that the courts should allow him \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98wide discretion \xe2\x80\xa6 in determining what actions are most compatible with its educational objectives.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents in Healy v. James, O. T. 1971, No. 71\xe2\x80\x93452, pp. 7\xe2\x80\x938. A supporting amicus contended that college officials \xe2\x80\x9cmust be allowed a very broad discretion in formulating and implementing policies.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Board of Trustees, California State Colleges 6. Another argued that universities should be permitted to impose restrictions on speech that would not be tolerated elsewhere. Brief for American Association of Presidents of Independent Colleges and Universities 11\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Healy Court would have none of this. Unlike the Court today, the Healy Court emphatically rejected the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9cFirst Amendment protections should apply with less force on college campuses than in the community at large.\xe2\x80\x9d 408 U. S., at 180. And on one key question after another\xe2\x80\x94whether the local SDS chapter was independent of the national organization, whether the group posed a substantial threat of material disruption, and whether the students\xe2\x80\x99 responses to the committee\xe2\x80\x99s questions about violence and disruption signified a willingness to engage in such activities\xe2\x80\x94the Court drew\nits own conclusions, which differed from the college\npresident\xe2\x80\x99s.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Healy Court was true to the principle that when it comes to the interpretation and application of the right to free speech, we exercise our own independent judgment. We do not defer to Congress on such matters, see Sable Communications of Cal., Inc. v. FCC, 492 U. S. 115, 129 (1989), and there is no reason why we should bow to university administrators.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the end, I see only two possible distinctions between Healy and the present case. The first is that Healy did not involve any funding, but as I have noted, funding plays only a small part in this case. And if Healy would otherwise prevent Hastings from refusing to register CLS, I see no good reason why the potential availability of funding should enable Hastings to deny all of the other rights that go with registration.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This leaves just one way of distinguishing Healy: the identity of the student group. In Healy, the Court warned that the college president\xe2\x80\x99s views regarding the philosophy of the SDS could not \xe2\x80\x9cjustify the denial of First Amendment rights.\xe2\x80\x9d 408 U. S., at 187. Here, too, disapproval of CLS cannot justify Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 actions.[Footnote 2]

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court pays little attention to Healy and instead focuses solely on the question whether Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 registration policy represents a permissible regulation in a limited public forum. While I think that Healy is largely controlling, I am content to address the constitutionality of Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 actions under our limited public forum cases, which lead to exactly the same conclusion.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, the forum consists of the RSO program. Once a public university opens a limited public forum, it \xe2\x80\x9cmust respect the lawful boundaries it has itself set.\xe2\x80\x9d Rosenberger v. Rector and Visitors of Univ. of Va., 515 U. S. 819, 829 (1995). The university \xe2\x80\x9cmay not exclude speech where its distinction is not \xe2\x80\x98reasonable in light of the purpose served by the forum.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Cornelius v. NAACP Legal Defense & Ed. Fund, Inc., 473 U. S. 788, 806 (1985)). And the university must maintain strict viewpoint neutrality. Board of Regents of Univ. of Wis. System v. Southworth, 529 U. S. 217, 234 (2000); Rosenberger, supra, at 829.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This requirement of viewpoint neutrality extends to the expression of religious viewpoints. In an unbroken line of decisions analyzing private religious speech in limited public forums, we have made it perfectly clear that \xe2\x80\x9c[r]eligion is [a] viewpoint from which ideas are conveyed.\xe2\x80\x9d Good News Club v. Milford Central School, 533 U. S. 98, 112, and n. 4 (2001). See Rosenberger, supra, at 831; Lamb\xe2\x80\x99s Chapel v. Center Moriches Union Free School Dist., 508 U. S. 384, 393\xe2\x80\x93394 (1993); Widmar v. Vincent, 454 U. S. 263, 277 (1981).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have applied this analysis in cases in which student speech was restricted because of the speaker\xe2\x80\x99s religious viewpoint, and we have consistently concluded that such restrictions constitute viewpoint discrimination. E.g., Rosenberger, supra, at 845\xe2\x80\x93846; Widmar, supra, at 267, n.\xc2\xa05, 269, 277; see also Good News Club, supra, at 106\xe2\x80\x93107, 109\xe2\x80\x93110; Lamb\xe2\x80\x99s Chapel, supra, at 392\xe2\x80\x93393, 394. We have also stressed that the rules applicable in a limited public forum are particularly important in the university setting, where \xe2\x80\x9cthe State acts against a background of tradition of thought and experiment that is at the center of our intellectual and philosophic tradition.\xe2\x80\x9d Rosenberger, supra, at 835.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Analyzed under this framework, Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 refusal to register CLS pursuant to its Nondiscrimination Policy plainly fails.[Footnote 3] As previously noted, when Hastings refused to register CLS, it claimed that the CLS bylaws impermissibly discriminated on the basis of religion and sexual orientation. As interpreted by Hastings and applied\nto CLS, both of these grounds constituted viewpoint\ndiscrimination.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Religion. The First Amendment protects the right of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98expressive association\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, \xe2\x80\x9cthe right to associate for the purpose of speaking.\xe2\x80\x9d Rumsfeld v. Forum for Academic and Institutional Rights, Inc., 547 U. S. 47, 68 (2006) (quoting Boy Scouts of America v. Dale, 530 U. S. 640, 644 (2000)). And the Court has recognized that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he forced inclusion of an unwanted person in a group infringes the group\xe2\x80\x99s freedom of expressive association if the presence of that person affects in a significant way the group\xe2\x80\x99s ability to advocate public or private viewpoints.\xe2\x80\x9d Dale, supra, at 648.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With one important exception, the Hastings Nondiscrimination Policy respected that right. As Hastings stated in its answer, the Nondiscrimination Policy \xe2\x80\x9cpermit[ted] political, social, and cultural student organizations to select officers and members who are dedicated to a particular set of ideals or beliefs.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 93. But the policy singled out one category of expressive associations for disfavored treatment: groups formed to express a religious message. Only religious groups were required to admit students who did not share their views. An environmentalist group was not required to admit students who rejected global warming. An animal rights group was not obligated to accept students who supported the use of animals to test cosmetics. But CLS was required to admit avowed atheists. This was patent viewpoint discrimination. \xe2\x80\x9cBy the very terms of the [Nondiscrimination Policy], the University \xe2\x80\xa6 select[ed] for disfavored treatment those student [groups] with religious \xe2\x80\xa6 viewpoints.\xe2\x80\x9d Rosenberger, 515 U. S., at 831. It is no wonder that the Court makes no attempt to defend the constitutionality of the Nondiscrimination Policy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unlike the Court, Justice Stevens attempts a defense, contending that the Nondiscrimination Policy is viewpoint neutral. But his arguments are squarely contrary to established precedent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens first argues that the Nondiscrimination Policy is viewpoint neutral because it \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not regulate expression or belief at all\xe2\x80\x9d but instead regulates conduct. See ante, at 2 (concurring opinion). This Court has held, however, that the particular conduct at issue here constitutes a form of expression that is protected by the First Amendment. It is now well established that the First Amendment shields the right of a group to engage in expressive association by limiting membership to persons whose admission does not significantly interfere with the group\xe2\x80\x99s ability to convey its views. See Dale, supra, at 648; Roberts v. United States Jaycees, 468 U. S. 609, 623 (1984); see also New York State Club Assn., Inc. v. City of New York, 487 U. S. 1, 13 (1988) (acknowledging that an \xe2\x80\x9cassociation might be able to show that it is organized for specific expressive purposes and that it will not be able to advocate its desired viewpoints nearly as effectively if it cannot confine its membership to those who share the same sex, for example, or the same religion\xe2\x80\x9d); Widmar, supra, at 268\xe2\x80\x93269 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he First Amendment rights of speech and association extend to the campuses of state universities\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, the opinion of the Court, which Justice Stevens joins, acknowledges this rule. See ante, at 13.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens also maintains that the Nondiscrimination Policy is viewpoint neutral because it prohibits all groups, both religious and secular, from engaging in religious speech. See ante, at 3. This argument is also contrary to established law. In Rosenberger, the dissent, which Justice Stevens joined, made exactly this argument. See 515 U. S., at 895\xe2\x80\x93896 (opinion of Souter, J.). The Court disagreed, holding that a policy that treated secular speech more favorably than religious speech discriminated on the basis of viewpoint.[Footnote 4] 515 U. S., at 831. The Court reaffirmed this holding in Good News Club, 533 U. S., at 112, and n.\xc2\xa04.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, the Nondiscrimination Policy permitted membership requirements that expressed a secular viewpoint. See App. 93. (For example, the Hastings Democratic Caucus and the Hastings Republicans were allowed to exclude members who disagreed with their parties\xe2\x80\x99 platforms.) But religious groups were not permitted to express a religious viewpoint by limiting membership to students who shared their religious viewpoints. Under established precedent, this was viewpoint discrimination.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It bears emphasis that permitting religious groups to limit membership to those who share the groups\xe2\x80\x99 beliefs would not have the effect of allowing other groups to discriminate on the basis of religion. It would not mean, for example, that fraternities or sororities could exclude students on that basis. As our cases have recognized, the right of expressive association permits a group to exclude an applicant for membership only if the admission of that person would \xe2\x80\x9caffec[t] in a significant way the group\xe2\x80\x99s ability to advocate public or private viewpoints.\xe2\x80\x9d Dale, 530 U. S., at 648. Groups that do not engage in expressive association have no such right. Similarly, groups that are dedicated to expressing a viewpoint on a secular topic (for example, a political or ideological viewpoint) would have no basis for limiting membership based on religion because the presence of members with diverse religious beliefs would have no effect on the group\xe2\x80\x99s ability to express its views. But for religious groups, the situation is very different. This point was put well by a coalition of Muslim, Christian, Jewish, and Sikh groups: \xe2\x80\x9cOf course there is a strong interest in prohibiting religious discrimination where religion is irrelevant. But it is fundamentally confused to apply a rule against religious discrimination to a religious association.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for American Islamic Congress et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 3.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sexual orientation. The Hastings Nondiscrimination Policy, as interpreted by the law school, also discriminated on the basis of viewpoint regarding sexual morality. CLS has a particular viewpoint on this subject, namely, that sexual conduct outside marriage between a man and a woman is wrongful. Hastings would not allow CLS to express this viewpoint by limiting membership to persons willing to express a sincere agreement with CLS\xe2\x80\x99s views. By contrast, nothing in the Nondiscrimination Policy prohibited a group from expressing a contrary viewpoint by limiting membership to persons willing to endorse\nthat group\xe2\x80\x99s beliefs. A Free Love Club could require members to affirm that they reject the traditional view of sexual morality to which CLS adheres. It is hard to see\nhow this can be viewed as anything other than viewpoint discrimination.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 current policy, as announced for the first time in the brief filed in this Court, fares no better than the policy that the law school invoked when CLS\xe2\x80\x99s application was denied. According to Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 brief, its new policy, contrary to the position taken by Hastings officials at an earlier point in this litigation, really does not require a student group to accept all comers. Now, Hastings explains, its policy allows \xe2\x80\x9cneutral and generally applicable membership requirements unrelated to \xe2\x80\x98status or beliefs.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent Hastings College of Law 5. As examples of permissible membership requirements, Hastings mentions academic standing, writing ability, \xe2\x80\x9cdues, attendance, and even conduct requirements.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It seems doubtful that Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 new policy permits registered groups to condition membership eligibility on whatever \xe2\x80\x9cconduct requirements\xe2\x80\x9d they may wish to impose. If that is the school\xe2\x80\x99s current policy, it is hard to see why CLS may not be registered, for what CLS demands is that members foreswear \xe2\x80\x9cunrepentant participation in or advocacy of a sexually immoral lifestyle.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 146. That should qualify as a conduct requirement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If it does not, then what Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 new policy must mean is that registered groups may impose some, but not all, conduct requirements. And if that is the case, it is incumbent on Hastings to explain which conduct requirements are acceptable, which are not, and why CLS\xe2\x80\x99s requirement is not allowed. Hastings has made no effort to provide such an explanation.[Footnote 6]

\n

VI

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I come now to the version of Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy that the Court has chosen to address. This is not the policy that Hastings invoked when CLS was denied registration. Nor is it the policy that Hastings now proclaims\xe2\x80\x94and presumably implements. It is a policy that, as far as the record establishes, was in force only from the time when it was first disclosed by the former dean in July 2005 until Hastings filed its brief in this Court in March 2010. Why we should train our attention on this particular policy and not the other two is a puzzle. But in any event, it is clear that the accept-all-comers policy is not reasonable in light of the purpose of the RSO forum, and it is impossible to say on the present record that it is viewpoint neutral.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Once a state university opens a limited forum, it \xe2\x80\x9cmust respect the lawful boundaries it has itself set.\xe2\x80\x9d Rosenberger, 515 U. S., at 829. Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 regulations on the registration of student groups impose only two substantive limitations: A group seeking registration must have student members and must be non-commercial. App. to Pet. for Cert. 82a\xe2\x80\x9383a, Hastings Board of Directors, Policies and Regulations Applying to College Activities, Organizations and Students \xc2\xa734.10 (June 22, 1990) (hereinafter Hastings Regulations). Access to the forum is not limited to groups devoted to particular purposes. The regulations provide that a group applying for registration must submit an official document including \xe2\x80\x9ca statement of its purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 83a (Hastings Regulations \xc2\xa734.10.A.1 (emphasis added)), but the regulations make no attempt to define the limits of acceptable purposes. The regulations do not require a group seeking registration to show that it has a certain number of members or that its program is of interest to any particular number of Hastings students. Nor do the regulations require that a group serve a need not met by existing groups.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The regulations also make it clear that the registration program is not meant to stifle unpopular speech. They proclaim that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is the responsibility of the Dean to ensure an ongoing opportunity for the expression of a variety of viewpoints.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 82a (Hastings Regulations \xc2\xa733.11). They also emphatically disclaim any endorsement of or responsibility for views that student groups may express. Id., at 85a (Hastings Regulations \xc2\xa734.10.D).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Taken as a whole, the regulations plainly contemplate the creation of a forum within which Hastings students are free to form and obtain registration of essentially the same broad range of private groups that nonstudents may form off campus. That is precisely what the parties in this case stipulated: The RSO forum \xe2\x80\x9cseeks to promote a diversity of viewpoints among registered student organizations, including viewpoints on religion and human sexuality.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 216 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The way in which the RSO forum actually developed corroborates this design. As noted, Hastings had more than 60 RSOs in 2004\xe2\x80\x932005, each with its own independently devised purpose. Some addressed serious social issues; others\xe2\x80\x94for example, the wine appreciation and ultimate Frisbee clubs\xe2\x80\x94were simply recreational. Some organizations focused on a subject but did not claim to promote a particular viewpoint on that subject (for example, the Association of Communications, Sports & Entertainment Law); others were defined, not by subject, but by viewpoint. The forum did not have a single Party Politics Club; rather, it featured both the Hastings Democratic Caucus and the Hastings Republicans. There was no Reproductive Issues Club; the forum included separate pro-choice and pro-life organizations. Students did not see fit to create a Monotheistic Religions Club, but they have formed the Hastings Jewish Law Students Association and the Hastings Association of Muslim Law Students. In short, the RSO forum, true to its design, has allowed Hastings students to replicate on campus a broad array of private, independent, noncommercial organizations that is very similar to those that nonstudents have formed in the outside world.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The accept-all-comers policy is antithetical to the design of the RSO forum for the same reason that a state-imposed accept-all-comers policy would violate the First Amendment rights of private groups if applied off campus. As explained above, a group\xe2\x80\x99s First Amendment right of expressive association is burdened by the \xe2\x80\x9cforced inclusion\xe2\x80\x9d of members whose presence would \xe2\x80\x9caffec[t] in a significant way the group\xe2\x80\x99s ability to advocate public or private viewpoints.\xe2\x80\x9d Dale, 530 U. S., at 648. The Court has therefore held that the government may not compel a group that engages in \xe2\x80\x9cexpressive association\xe2\x80\x9d to admit such a member unless the government has a compelling interest, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98unrelated to the suppression of ideas, that cannot be achieved through means significantly less restrictive of associational freedoms.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Roberts, 468 U. S., at 623).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There can be no dispute that this standard would not permit a generally applicable law mandating that private religious groups admit members who do not share the groups\xe2\x80\x99 beliefs. Religious groups like CLS obviously engage in expressive association, and no legitimate state interest could override the powerful effect that an accept-all-comers law would have on the ability of religious groups to express their views. The State of California surely could not demand that all Christian groups admit members who believe that Jesus was merely human. Jewish groups could not be required to admit anti-Semites and Holocaust deniers. Muslim groups could not be forced to admit persons who are viewed as slandering Islam.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While there can be no question that the State of California could not impose such restrictions on all religious groups in the State, the Court now holds that Hastings, a state institution, may impose these very same requirements on students who wish to participate in a forum that is designed to foster the expression of diverse viewpoints. The Court lists four justifications offered by Hastings in defense of the accept-all-comers policy and, deferring to the school\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, ante, at 21, the Court finds all those justifications satisfactory, ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9324. If we carry\nout our responsibility to exercise our own independent judgment, however, we must conclude that the justifications offered by Hastings and accepted by the Court are insufficient.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court first says that the accept-all-comers policy is reasonable because it helps Hastings to ensure that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98leadership, educational, and social opportunities\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d are afforded to all students. Ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322 (quoting Brief for Respondent Hastings College of Law 32). The RSO forum, however, is designed to achieve these laudable ends in a very different way\xe2\x80\x94by permitting groups of students, no matter how small, to form the groups they want. In this way, the forum multiplies the opportunity for students to serve in leadership positions; it allows students to decide which educational opportunities they wish to pursue through participation in extracurricular activities; and it permits them to create the \xe2\x80\x9csocial opportunities\xe2\x80\x9d they desire by forming whatever groups they wish to create.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the Court approves the accept-all-comers policy because it is easier to enforce than the Nondiscrimination Policy that it replaced. It would be \xe2\x80\x9ca daunting labor,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court warns, for Hastings to try to determine whether\na group excluded a member based on belief as opposed to status. Ante, at 22; see also ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, n.\xc2\xa01 (opinion\nof Stevens, J.) (referring to the \xe2\x80\x9cimpossible task of\nseparating out belief-based from status-based religious\ndiscrimination\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is a strange argument, since the Nondiscrimination Policy prohibits discrimination on substantially the same grounds as the antidiscrimination provisions of many States,[Footnote 7] including California, and except for the inclusion of the prohibition of discrimination based on sexual orientation, the Nondiscrimination Policy also largely tracks federal antidiscrimination laws.[Footnote 8] Moreover, Hastings now willingly accepts greater burdens under its latest policy, which apparently requires the school to distinguish between certain \xe2\x80\x9cconduct requirements\xe2\x80\x9d that are allowed and others that are not. Nor is Hastings daunted by the labor of determining whether a club admissions exam legitimately tests knowledge or is a pretext for screening out students with disfavored beliefs. Asked at oral argument whether CLS could require applicants to pass a test on the Bible, Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 attorney responded: \xe2\x80\x9cIf it were truly an objective knowledge test, it would be okay.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 52. The long history of disputes about the meaning of Bible passages belies any suggestion that it would be an easy task to determine whether the grading of such a test was \xe2\x80\x9cobjective.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, the Court argues that the accept-all-comers policy, by bringing together students with diverse views, encourages tolerance, cooperation, learning, and the development of conflict-resolution skills. Ante, at 23. These are obviously commendable goals, but they are not undermined by permitting a religious group to restrict membership to persons who share the group\xe2\x80\x99s faith. Many religious groups impose such restrictions. See, e.g., Brief for Agudath Israel of America as Amicus Curiae 3 (\xe2\x80\x9c[B]ased upon millennia-old Jewish laws and traditions, Orthodox Jewish institutions \xe2\x80\xa6 regularly differentiate between Jews and non-Jews\xe2\x80\x9d). Such practices are not manifestations of \xe2\x80\x9ccontempt\xe2\x80\x9d for members of other faiths. Cf. ante, at 6 (opinion of Stevens, J.) (invoking groups that have \xe2\x80\x9ccontempt for Jews, blacks, and women\xe2\x80\x9d). Nor do they thwart the objectives that Hastings endorses. Our country as a whole, no less than the Hastings College of Law, values tolerance, cooperation, learning, and the amicable resolution of conflicts. But we seek to achieve those goals through \xe2\x80\x9c[a] confident pluralism that conduces to civil peace and advances democratic consensus-building,\xe2\x80\x9d not by abridging First Amendment rights. Brief for Gays and Lesbians for Individual Liberty as Amicus Curiae 35.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Fourth, the Court observes that Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 policy \xe2\x80\x9cincorporates\xe2\x80\x94in fact, subsumes\xe2\x80\x94state-law proscriptions on discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 23. Because the First Amendment obviously takes precedence over any state law, this would not justify the Hastings policy even if it were true\xe2\x80\x94but it is not. The only Hastings policy considered by the Court\xe2\x80\x94the accept-all-comers policy\xe2\x80\x94goes far beyond any California antidiscrimination law. Neither Hastings nor the Court claims that California law demands that state entities must accept all comers. Hastings itself certainly does not follow this policy in hiring or student admissions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor is it at all clear that California law requires Hastings to deny registration to a religious group that limits membership to students who share the group\xe2\x80\x99s religious beliefs. Hastings cites no California court decision or administrative authority addressing this question. Instead, Hastings points to a statute prohibiting discrimination on specified grounds, including religion or sexual orientation, \xe2\x80\x9cin any program or activity conducted by\xe2\x80\x9d certain postsecondary educational institutions. Cal. Educ. Code Ann. \xc2\xa766270 (West Supp. 2010) (emphasis added). Hastings, however, does not conduct the activities of the student groups it registers. Indeed, Hastings disclaims such responsibility, stating both in its regulations and its Handbook for Student Organizations that it \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not sponsor student organizations and therefore does not accept liability for activities of student organizations.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 85a (Hastings Regulations \xc2\xa734.10.D); App. 250. In addition, as CLS notes, another provision of California law specifically exempts \xe2\x80\x9cany funds that are used directly or indirectly for the benefit of student organizations\xe2\x80\x9d from a ban on state funding of private groups that discriminate on any of the grounds listed in \xc2\xa766270. See \xc2\xa792150 (West Supp. 2010).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The authority to decide whether \xc2\xa766270 or any other provision of California law requires religious student groups at covered institutions to admit members who do not share the groups\xe2\x80\x99 religious views is of course a question of state law that we cannot resolve. The materials that have been brought to our attention, however, provide little support for the majority\xe2\x80\x99s suggested interpretation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 accept-all-comers policy is not reasonable in light of the stipulated purpose of the RSO forum: to promote a diversity of viewpoints \xe2\x80\x9camong\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94not within\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cregistered student organizations.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 216 (emphasis added).[Footnote 9]

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court is also wrong in holding that the accept-all-comers policy is viewpoint neutral. The Court proclaims that it would be \xe2\x80\x9chard to imagine a more viewpoint-neutral policy,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 28, but I would not be so quick to jump to this conclusion. Even if it is assumed that the policy is viewpoint neutral on its face,[Footnote 10] there is strong evidence in the record that the policy was announced as a pretext.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The adoption of a facially neutral policy for the purpose of suppressing the expression of a particular viewpoint is viewpoint discrimination. See Crawford v. Board of Ed. of Los Angeles, 458 U. S. 527, 544 (1982) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] law neutral on its face still may be unconstitutional if motivated by a discriminatory purpose\xe2\x80\x9d). A simple example illustrates this obvious point. Suppose that a hated student group at a state university has never been able to attract more than 10 members. Suppose that the university administration, for the purpose of preventing that group from using the school grounds for meetings, adopts a new rule under which the use of its facilities is restricted to groups with more than 25 members. Although this rule would be neutral on its face, its adoption for a discriminatory reason would be illegal.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, CLS has made a strong showing that Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 sudden adoption and selective application of its accept-all-comers policy was a pretext for the law school\xe2\x80\x99s unlawful denial of CLS\xe2\x80\x99s registration application under the Nondiscrimination Policy.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Shifting policies. When Hastings denied CLS\xe2\x80\x99s application in the fall of 2004, the only policy mentioned was the Nondiscrimination Policy. In July 2005, the former dean suggested in a deposition that the law school actually followed the very different accept-all-comers policy. In March of this year, Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 brief in this Court rolled out still a third policy. As is recognized in the employment discrimination context, where issues of pretext regularly arise, \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ubstantial changes over time in [an] employer\xe2\x80\x99s proffered reason for its employment decision support a finding of pretext.\xe2\x80\x9d Kobrin v. University of Minnesota, 34 F.\xc2\xa03d 698, 703 (CA8 1994); see also, e.g., Aragon v. Republic Silver State Disposal Inc., 292 F.\xc2\xa03d 654, 661 (CA9 2002); Cicero v. Borg-Warner Automotive, Inc., 280 F.\xc2\xa03d 579, 592 (CA6 2001).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Timing. The timing of Hastings\xe2\x80\x99 revelation of its new policies closely tracks the law school\xe2\x80\x99s litigation posture. When Hastings denied CLS registration, it cited only the Nondiscrimination Policy. Later, after CLS alleged that the Nondiscrimination Policy discriminated against religious groups, Hastings unveiled its accept-all-comers policy. Then, after we granted certiorari and CLS\xe2\x80\x99s opening brief challenged the constitutionality\xe2\x80\x94and the plausibility\xe2\x80\x94of the accept-all-comers policy, Hastings disclosed a new policy. As is true in the employment context, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen the justification for an adverse . . . action changes during litigation, that inconsistency raises an issue whether the proffered reason truly motivated the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Cicero, supra, at 592.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lack of documentation. When an employer has a written policy and then relies on a rule for which there is no written documentation, that deviation may support an inference of pretext. See, e.g., Diaz v. Eagle Produce Ltd. Partnership, 521 F.\xc2\xa03d 1201, 1214 (CA9 2008); Rudin v. Lincoln Land Community College, 420 F.\xc2\xa03d 712, 727 (CA7 2005); Machinchick v. PB Power, Inc., 398 F.\xc2\xa03d 345, 354, n.\xc2\xa029 (CA5 2005); Russell v. TG Missouri Corp., 340 F.\xc2\xa03d 735, 746 (CA8 2003); Mohammed v. Callaway, 698 F.\xc2\xa02d 395, 399\xe2\x80\x93400, 401 (CA10 1983).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, Hastings claims that it has had an accept-all-comers policy since 1990, but it has not produced a single written document memorializing that policy. Nor has it cited a single occasion prior to the dean\xe2\x80\x99s deposition when this putative policy was orally disclosed to either student groups interested in applying for registration or to the Office of Student Services, which was charged with reviewing the bylaws of applicant groups to ensure that they were in compliance with the law school\xe2\x80\x99s policies.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nonenforcement. Since it appears that no one was told about the accept-all-comers policy before July 2005, it is not surprising that the policy was not enforced. The record is replete with evidence that Hastings made no effort to enforce the all-comers policy until after it was proclaimed by the former dean. See, e.g., App. to Pet. for Cert. 118a (Hastings Democratic Caucus); id., at 110a (Association of Trial Lawyers of America at Hastings); id., at 146a\xe2\x80\x93147a (Vietnamese American Law Society); id., at 142a\xe2\x80\x93143a (Silent Right); App. 192 (La Raza). See generally supra, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. If the record here is not sufficient to permit a finding of pretext, then the law of pretext is dead.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x94understandably\xe2\x80\x94sidesteps this issue. The Court states that the lower courts did not address the \xe2\x80\x9cargument that Hastings selectively enforces its all-comer policy,\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 11] that \xe2\x80\x9cthis Court is not the proper forum to air the issue in the first instance,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]n remand, the Ninth Circuit may consider CLS\xe2\x80\x99s pretext argument if, and to the extent, it is preserved.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 31\xe2\x80\x9332.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the Court affirms the entry of summary judgment in favor of respondents, it is not clear how CLS will be able to ask the Ninth Circuit on remand to review its claim of pretext. And the argument that we should not address this issue of pretext because the Ninth Circuit did not do so is hard to take, given that the Ninth Circuit barely addressed anything, disposing of this case in precisely two sentences.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Neither of those two sentences addressed the \xe2\x80\x9cnovel question,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 1, to which the bulk of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is devoted, i.e., whether the accept-all-comers policy is reasonable in light of the purposes of the RSO forum and is viewpoint neutral, see ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9331. If it is appropriate for us to consider that issue, then the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s failure to address the issue of pretext should not stand in the way of review by this Court.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One final aspect of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision warrants comment. In response to the argument that the accept-all-comers-policy would permit a small and unpopular group to be taken over by students who wish to silence its message, the Court states that the policy would permit a registered group to impose membership requirements \xe2\x80\x9cdesigned to ensure that students join because of their commitment to a group\xe2\x80\x99s vitality, not its demise.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 27. With this concession, the Court tacitly recognizes that Hastings does not really have an accept-all-comers policy\xe2\x80\x94it has an accept-some-dissident-comers policy\xe2\x80\x94and the line between members who merely seek to change a group\xe2\x80\x99s message (who apparently must be admitted) and those who seek a group\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdemise\xe2\x80\x9d (who may be kept out) is hopelessly vague.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here is an example. Not all Christian denominations agree with CLS\xe2\x80\x99s views on sexual morality and other matters. During a recent year, CLS had seven members. Suppose that 10 students who are members of denominations that disagree with CLS decided that CLS was misrepresenting true Christian doctrine. Suppose that these students joined CLS, elected officers who shared their views, ended the group\xe2\x80\x99s affiliation with the national organization, and changed the group\xe2\x80\x99s message. The new leadership would likely proclaim that the group was \xe2\x80\x9cvital\xe2\x80\x9d but rectified, while CLS, I assume, would take the view that the old group had suffered its \xe2\x80\x9cdemise.\xe2\x80\x9d Whether a change represents reform or transformation may depend very much on the eye of the beholder.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy takes a similarly mistaken tack. He contends that CLS \xe2\x80\x9cwould have a substantial case on the merits if it were shown that the all-comers policy was \xe2\x80\xa6 used to infiltrate the group or challenge its leadership in order to stifle its views,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 4 (concurring opinion), but he does not explain on what ground such a claim could succeed. The Court holds that the accept-all-comers policy is viewpoint neutral and reasonable in light of the purposes of the RSO forum. How could those characteristics be altered by a change in the membership of one of the forum\xe2\x80\x99s registered groups? No explanation is apparent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the end, the Court refuses to acknowledge the consequences of its holding. A true accept-all-comers policy permits small unpopular groups to be taken over by students who wish to change the views that the group expresses. Rules requiring that members attend meetings, pay dues, and behave politely, see ante, at 27, would not eliminate this threat.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The possibility of such takeovers, however, is by no means the most important effect of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding. There are religious groups that cannot in good conscience agree in their bylaws that they will admit persons who do not share their faith, and for these groups, the consequence of an accept-all-comers policy is marginalization. See Brief for Evangelical Scholars (Officers and 24 Former Presidents of the Evangelical Theological Society) et al. as Amici Curiae 19 (affirmance in this case \xe2\x80\x9cwill allow every public college and university in the United States to exclude all evangelical Christian organizations\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Agudath Israel of America as Amicus Curiae 3, 8 (affirmance would \xe2\x80\x9cpoint a judicial dagger at the heart of the Orthodox Jewish community in the United States\xe2\x80\x9d and permit that community to be relegated to the status of \xe2\x80\x9ca second-class group\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for Union of Orthodox Jewish Congregations of America as Amicus Curiae 3 (affirmance \xe2\x80\x9ccould significantly affect the ability of [affiliated] student clubs and youth movements \xe2\x80\xa6 to prescribe requirements for their membership and leaders based on religious beliefs and commitments\xe2\x80\x9d). This is where the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision leads.

Our First Amendment reflects a \xe2\x80\x9cprofound national commitment to the principle that debate on public issues should be uninhibited, robust, and wide-open.\xe2\x80\x9d New York Times Co. v. Even those who find CLS\xe2\x80\x99s views objectionable should be concerned about the way the group has been treated\xe2\x80\x94by Hastings, the Court of Appeals, and now this Court. I can only hope that this decision will turn out to be an aberration.
