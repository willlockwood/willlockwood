\n

FOSTER v. CHATMAN, WARDEN

\n

certiorari to the supreme court of georgia

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x938349.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 2, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 23, 2016

\n

Petitioner Timothy Foster was convicted of capital murder and sentenced to death in a Georgia court. During jury selection at his trial, the State used peremptory challenges to strike all four black prospective jurors qualified to serve on the jury. Foster argued that the State\xe2\x80\x99s use of those strikes was racially motivated, in violation of Batson v. Kentucky,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 79\n. The trial court rejected that claim, and the Georgia Supreme Court affirmed. Foster then renewed his Batson claim in a state habeas proceeding. While that proceeding was pending, Foster, through the Georgia Open Records Act, obtained from the State copies of the file used by the prosecution during his trial. Among other documents, the file contained (1) copies of the jury venire list on which the names of each black prospective juror were highlighted in bright green, with a legend indicating that the highlighting \xe2\x80\x9crepresents Blacks\xe2\x80\x9d; (2) a draft affidavit from an investigator comparing black prospective jurors and concluding, \xe2\x80\x9cIf it comes down to having to pick one of the black jurors, [this one] might be okay\xe2\x80\x9d; (3) notes identifying black prospective jurors as \xe2\x80\x9cB#1,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cB#2,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cB#3\xe2\x80\x9d; (4) notes with \xe2\x80\x9cN\xe2\x80\x9d (for \xe2\x80\x9cno\xe2\x80\x9d) appearing next to the names of all black prospective jurors; (5) a list titled \xe2\x80\x9c[D]efinite NO\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x9d containing six names, including the names of all of the qualified black prospective jurors; (6)\xc2\xa0a document with notes on the Church of Christ that was annotated \xe2\x80\x9cNO. No Black Church\xe2\x80\x9d; and (7) the questionnaires filled out by five prospective black jurors, on which each juror\xe2\x80\x99s response indicating his or her race had been circled.

\n

The state habeas court denied relief. It noted that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim had been adjudicated on direct appeal. Because Foster\xe2\x80\x99s renewed Batson claim \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ed] to demonstrate purposeful discrimination,\xe2\x80\x9d the court concluded that he had failed to show \xe2\x80\x9cany change in the facts sufficient to overcome\xe2\x80\x9d the state law doctrine of res judicata. The Georgia Supreme Court denied Foster the Certificate of Probable Cause necessary to file an appeal.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0This Court has jurisdiction to review the judgment of the Georgia Supreme Court denying Foster a Certificate of Probable Cause on his Batson claim. Although this Court cannot ascertain the grounds for that unelaborated judgment, there is no indication that it rested on a state law ground that is both \xe2\x80\x9cindependent of the merits\xe2\x80\x9d of Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim and an \xe2\x80\x9cadequate basis\xe2\x80\x9d for that decision, so as to preclude jurisdiction. Harris v. Reed,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 255\n. The state habeas court held that the state law doctrine of res judicata barred Foster\xe2\x80\x99s claim only by examining the entire record and determining that Foster had not alleged a change in facts sufficient to overcome the bar. Based on this lengthy \xe2\x80\x9cBatson analysis,\xe2\x80\x9d the state habeas court concluded that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s renewed Batson claim was \xe2\x80\x9cwithout merit.\xe2\x80\x9d Because the state court\xe2\x80\x99s application of res judicata thus \xe2\x80\x9cdepend[ed] on a federal constitutional ruling, [that] prong of the court\xe2\x80\x99s holding is not independent of federal law, and [this Court\xe2\x80\x99s] jurisdiction is not precluded.\xe2\x80\x9d Ake v. Oklahoma,\n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 68\n; see also Three Affiliated Tribes of Fort Berthold Reservation v. Wold Engineering, P.\xc2\xa0C.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 138\n. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0The decision that Foster failed to show purposeful discrimination was clearly erroneous. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Batson provides a three-step process for adjudicating claims such as Foster\xe2\x80\x99s. \xe2\x80\x9cFirst, a defendant must make a prima facie showing that a preemptory challenge has been exercised on the basis of race; second, if that showing has been made, the prosecution must offer a race-neutral basis for striking the juror in question; and third, in light of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 submissions, the trial court must determine whether the defendant has shown purposeful discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Snyder v. Louisiana,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 472\n (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted). Only Batson\xe2\x80\x99s third step is at issue here. That step turns on factual findings made by the lower courts, and this Court will defer to those findings unless they are clearly erroneous. See ibid. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Foster established purposeful discrimination in the State\xe2\x80\x99s strikes of two black prospective jurors: Marilyn Garrett and Eddie Hood. Though the trial court accepted the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s justifications for both strikes, the record belies much of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9322.

\n

(i)\xc2\xa0The prosecution explained to the trial court that it made a last-minute decision to strike Garrett only after another juror, Shirley Powell, was excused for cause on the morning that the strikes were exercised. That explanation is flatly contradicted by evidence showing that Garrett\xe2\x80\x99s name appeared on the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s list of \xe2\x80\x9c[D]efinite NO\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the six prospective jurors whom the prosecution was intent on striking from the outset. The record also refutes several of the reasons the prosecution gave for striking Garrett instead of Arlene Blackmon, a white prospective juror. For example, while the State told the trial court that it struck Garrett because the defense did not ask her for her thoughts about such pertinent trial issues as insanity, alcohol, or pre-trial publicity, the record reveals that the defense asked Garrett multiple questions on each topic. And though the State gave other facially reasonable justifications for striking Garrett, those are difficult to credit because of the State\xe2\x80\x99s willingness to accept white jurors with the same characteristics. For example, the prosecution claims that it struck Garrett because she was divorced and, at age 34, too young, but three out of four divorced white prospective jurors and eight white prospective jurors under age 36 were allowed to serve. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

(ii)\xc2\xa0With regard to prospective juror Hood, the record similarly undermines the justifications proffered by the State to the trial court for the strike. For example, the prosecution alleged in response to Foster\xe2\x80\x99s pretrial Batson challenge that its only concern with Hood was the fact that his son was the same age as the defendant. But then, at a subsequent hearing, the State told the court that its chief concern was with Hood\xe2\x80\x99s membership in the Church of Christ. In the end, neither of those reasons for striking Hood withstands scrutiny. As to the age of Hood\xe2\x80\x99s son, the prosecution allowed white prospective jurors with sons of similar age to serve, including one who, in contrast to Hood, equivocated when asked whether Foster\xe2\x80\x99s age would be a factor at sentencing. And as to Hood\xe2\x80\x99s religion, the prosecution erroneously claimed that three white Church of Christ members were excused for cause because of their opposition to the death penalty, when in fact the record shows that those jurors were excused for reasons unrelated to their views on the death penalty. Moreover, a document acquired from the State\xe2\x80\x99s file contains a handwritten note stating, \xe2\x80\x9cNO. NO Black Church,\xe2\x80\x9d while asserting that the Church of Christ does not take a stand on the death penalty. Other justifications for striking Hood fail to withstand scrutiny because no concerns were expressed with regard to similar white prospective jurors. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0Evidence that a prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for striking a black prospective juror apply equally to an otherwise similar nonblack prospective juror who is allowed to serve tends to suggest purposeful discrimination. Miller-El v. Dretke,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 231\n. Such evidence is compelling with respect to Garrett and Hood and, along with the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s shifting explanations, misrepresentations of the record, and persistent focus on race, leads to the conclusion that the striking of those prospective jurors was \xe2\x80\x9cmotivated in substantial part by discriminatory intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Snyder, 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 485. P.\xc2\xa023.

\n

(d)\xc2\xa0Because Batson was decided only months before Foster\xe2\x80\x99s trial, the State asserts that the focus on black prospective jurors in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file was an effort to develop and maintain a detailed account should the prosecution need a defense against any suggestion that its reasons were pretextual. That argument, having never before been raised in the 30 years since Foster\xe2\x80\x99s trial, \xe2\x80\x9creeks of afterthought.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller-El, 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 246. And the focus on race in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file plainly demonstrates a concerted effort to keep black prospective jurors off the jury. Pp.\xc2\xa023\xe2\x80\x9325.

\n

Reversed and remanded.

\n

TIMOTHY TYRONE FOSTER, PETITIONER v. BRUCE CHATMAN, WARDEN

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of georgia

\n

[May 23, 2016]

\n

Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Petitioner Timothy Foster was convicted of capital murder and sentenced to death in a Georgia court. During jury selection at his trial, the State exercised peremptory strikes against all four black prospective jurors qualified to serve. Foster argued that the State\xe2\x80\x99s use of those strikes was racially motivated, in violation of our decision in Batson v. Kentucky,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 79 (1986)\n. The trial court and the Georgia Supreme Court rejected Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim.

\n

Foster then sought a writ of habeas corpus from the Superior Court of Butts County, Georgia, renewing his Batson objection. That court denied relief, and the Georgia Supreme Court declined to issue the Certificate of Probable Cause necessary under Georgia law for Foster to pursue an appeal. We granted certiorari and now reverse.

\n

I

\n

On the morning of August 28, 1986, police found Queen Madge White dead on the floor of her home in Rome, Georgia. White, a 79-year-old widow, had been beaten, sexually assaulted, and strangled to death. Her home had been burglarized. Timothy Foster subsequently confessed to killing White, and White\xe2\x80\x99s possessions were recovered from Foster\xe2\x80\x99s home and from Foster\xe2\x80\x99s two sisters. The State indicted Foster on charges of malice murder and burglary. He faced the death penalty. Foster v. State, 258 Ga. 736, 374 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 188 (1988).

\n

District Attorney Stephen Lanier and Assistant District Attorney Douglas Pullen represented the State at trial. Jury selection proceeded in two phases: removals for cause and peremptory strikes. In the first phase, each prospective juror completed a detailed questionnaire, which the prosecution and defense reviewed. The trial court then conducted a juror-by-juror voir\xc2\xa0dire of approximately 90 prospective jurors. Throughout this process, both parties had the opportunity to question the prospective jurors and lodge challenges for cause. This first phase whittled the list down to 42 \xe2\x80\x9cqualified\xe2\x80\x9d prospective jurors. Five were black.

\n

In the second phase, known as the \xe2\x80\x9cstriking of the jury,\xe2\x80\x9d both parties had the opportunity to exercise peremptory strikes against the array of qualified jurors. Pursuant to state law, the prosecution had ten such strikes; Foster twenty. See Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa715\xe2\x80\x9312\xe2\x80\x93165 (1985). The process worked as follows: The clerk of the court called the qualified prospective jurors one by one, and the State had the option to exercise one of its peremptory strikes. If the State declined to strike a particular prospective juror, Foster then had the opportunity to do so. If neither party exercised a peremptory strike, the prospective juror was selected for service. This second phase continued until 12 jurors had been accepted.

\n

The morning the second phase began, Shirley Powell, one of the five qualified black prospective jurors, notified the court that she had just learned that one of her close friends was related to Foster. The court removed Powell for cause. That left four black prospective jurors: Eddie Hood, Evelyn Hardge, Mary Turner, and Marilyn Garrett.

\n

The striking of the jury then commenced. The State exercised nine of its ten allotted peremptory strikes, removing all four of the remaining black prospective jurors. Foster immediately lodged a Batson challenge. The trial court rejected the objection and empaneled the jury. The jury convicted Foster and sentenced him to death.

\n

Following sentencing, Foster renewed his Batson claim in a motion for a new trial. After an evidentiary hearing, the trial court denied the motion. The Georgia Supreme Court affirmed, 258 Ga., at 747, 374 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 197, and we denied certiorari, Foster v. Georgia,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1085 (1989)\n.

\n

Foster subsequently sought a writ of habeas corpus from the Superior Court of Butts County, Georgia, again pressing his Batson claim. While the state habeas proceeding was pending, Foster filed a series of requests under the Georgia Open Records Act, see Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa750\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x9370 to 50\xe2\x80\x9318\xe2\x80\x9377 (2002), seeking access to the State\xe2\x80\x99s file from his 1987 trial. In response, the State disclosed documents related to the jury selection at that trial. Over the State\xe2\x80\x99s objections, the state habeas court admitted those documents into evidence. They included the following:

\n

(1) Four copies of the jury venire list. On each copy, the names of the black prospective jurors were highlighted in bright green. A legend in the upper right corner of the lists indicated that the green highlighting \xe2\x80\x9crepresents Blacks.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., App. 253. The letter \xe2\x80\x9cB\xe2\x80\x9d also appeared next to each black prospective juror\xe2\x80\x99s name. See, e.g., ibid. According to the testimony of Clayton Lundy, an investigator who assisted the prosecution during jury selection, these highlighted venire lists were circulated in the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office during jury selection. That allowed \xe2\x80\x9ceverybody in the office\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94approximately \xe2\x80\x9c10 to 12 people,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ecretaries, investigators, [and] district attorneys\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94to look at them, share information, and contribute thoughts on whether the prosecution should strike a particular juror. Pl. Exh. 1, 2 Record 190, 219 (Lundy deposition) (hereinafter Tr.). The documents, Lundy testified, were returned to Lanier before jury selection. Id., at 220.

\n

(2) A draft of an affidavit that had been prepared by Lundy \xe2\x80\x9cat Lanier\xe2\x80\x99s request\xe2\x80\x9d for submission to the state trial court in response to Foster\xe2\x80\x99s motion for a new trial. Id., at 203. The typed draft detailed Lundy\xe2\x80\x99s views on ten black prospective jurors, stating \xe2\x80\x9c[m]y evaluation of the jurors are a[s] follows.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 343. Under the name of one of those jurors, Lundy had written:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf it comes down to having to pick one of the black jurors, [this one] might be okay. This is solely my opinion.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Upon picking of the jury after listening to all of the jurors we had to pick, if we had to pick a black juror I recommend that [this juror] be one of the jurors.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 345 (paragraph break omitted).

\n

That text had been crossed out by hand; the version of the affidavit filed with the trial court did not contain the crossed-out language. See id., at 127\xe2\x80\x93129. Lundy testified that he \xe2\x80\x9cguess[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d the redactions had been done by Lanier. Tr. 203.

\n

(3) Three handwritten notes on black prospective jurors Eddie Hood, Louise Wilson, and Corrie Hinds. Annotations denoted those individuals as \xe2\x80\x9cB#1,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cB#2,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cB#3,\xe2\x80\x9d respectively. App. 295\xe2\x80\x93297. Lundy testified that these were examples of the type of \xe2\x80\x9cnotes that the team\xe2\x80\x94the State would take down during voir\xc2\xa0dire to help select the jury in Mr. Foster\xe2\x80\x99s case.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 208\xe2\x80\x93210.

\n

(4) A typed list of the qualified jurors remaining after voir\xc2\xa0dire. App. 287\xe2\x80\x93290. It included \xe2\x80\x9cNs\xe2\x80\x9d next to ten jurors\xe2\x80\x99 names, which Lundy told the state habeas court \xe2\x80\x9csignif[ied] the ten jurors that the State had strikes for during jury selection.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 211. Such an \xe2\x80\x9cN\xe2\x80\x9d appeared alongside the names of all five qualified black prospective jurors. See App. 287\xe2\x80\x93290. The file also included a handwritten version of the same list, with the same markings. Id., at 299\xe2\x80\x93300; see Tr. 212. Lundy testified that he was unsure who had prepared or marked the two lists.

\n

(5) A handwritten document titled \xe2\x80\x9cdefinite NO\xe2\x80\x99s,\xe2\x80\x9d listing six names. The first five were those of the five qualified black prospective jurors. App. 301. The State concedes that either Lanier or Pullen compiled the list, which Lundy testified was \xe2\x80\x9cused for preparation in jury selection.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 215; Tr. of Oral Arg. 45.

\n

(6) A handwritten document titled \xe2\x80\x9cChurch of Christ.\xe2\x80\x9d A notation on the document read: \xe2\x80\x9cNO. No Black Church.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 302.

\n

(7) The questionnaires that had been completed by several of the black prospective jurors. On each one, the juror\xe2\x80\x99s response indicating his or her race had been circled. Id., at 311, 317, 323, 329, 334.

\n

In response to the admission of this evidence, the State introduced short affidavits from Lanier and Pullen. Lanier\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit stated:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cI did not make any of the highlighted marks on the jury venire list. It was common practice in the office to highlight in yellow those jurors who had prior case experience. I did not instruct anyone to make the green highlighted marks. I reaffirm my testimony made during the motion for new trial hearing as to how I used my peremptory jury strikes and the basis and reasons for those strikes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 169 (paragraph numeral omitted).

\n

Pullen\xe2\x80\x99s affidavit averred:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cI did not make any of the highlighted marks on the jury venire list, and I did not instruct anyone else to make the highlighted marks. I did not rely on the highlighted jury venire list in making my decision on how to use my peremptory strikes.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 170\xe2\x80\x93171 (paragraph numeral omitted).

\n

Neither affidavit provided further explanation of the documents, and neither Lanier nor Pullen testified in the habeas proceeding.

\n

After considering the evidence, the state habeas court denied relief. The court first stated that, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]s a preliminary matter,\xe2\x80\x9d Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim was \xe2\x80\x9cnot reviewable based on the doctrine of res judicata\xe2\x80\x9d because it had been \xe2\x80\x9craised and litigated adversely to [Foster] on his direct appeal to the Georgia Supreme Court.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 175. The court nonetheless announced that it would \xe2\x80\x9cmak[e] findings of fact and conclusions of law\xe2\x80\x9d on that claim. Id., at 191. Based on what it referred to as a \xe2\x80\x9cBatson .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. analysis,\xe2\x80\x9d the court concluded that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crenewed Batson claim is without merit,\xe2\x80\x9d because he had \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ed] to demonstrate purposeful discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 192, 195, 196.

\n

The Georgia Supreme Court denied Foster the \xe2\x80\x9cCertificate of Probable Cause\xe2\x80\x9d necessary under state law for him to pursue an appeal, determining that his claim had no \xe2\x80\x9carguable merit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 246; see Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9314\xe2\x80\x9352 (2014); Ga. Sup. Ct. Rule 36 (2014). We granted certiorari. 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

II

\n

Before turning to the merits of Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim, we address a threshold issue. Neither party contests our jurisdiction to review Foster\xe2\x80\x99s claims, but we \xe2\x80\x9chave an independent obligation to determine whether subject-matter jurisdiction exists, even in the absence of a challenge from any party.\xe2\x80\x9d Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp.,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 500,\n514 (2006)\n.

\n

This Court lacks jurisdiction to entertain a federal claim on review of a state court judgment \xe2\x80\x9cif that judgment rests on a state law ground that is both \xe2\x80\x98independent\xe2\x80\x99 of the merits of the federal claim and an \xe2\x80\x98adequate\xe2\x80\x99 basis for the court\xe2\x80\x99s decision.\xe2\x80\x9d Harris v. Reed,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 255,\n260 (1989)\n.

\n

The state habeas court noted that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim was \xe2\x80\x9cnot reviewable based on the doctrine of res judicata\xe2\x80\x9d under Georgia law. App. 175. The Georgia Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s unelaborated order on review provides no reasoning for its decision.[1] That raises the question whether the Georgia Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s order\xe2\x80\x94the judgment from which Foster sought certiorari[2]\xe2\x80\x94rests on an adequate and independent state law ground so as to preclude our jurisdiction over Foster\xe2\x80\x99s federal claim.

\n

We conclude that it does not. When application of a state law bar \xe2\x80\x9cdepends on a federal constitutional ruling, the state-law prong of the court\xe2\x80\x99s holding is not independent of federal law, and our jurisdiction is not precluded.\xe2\x80\x9d Ake v. Oklahoma,\n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 68,\n75 (1985)\n; see also Three Affiliated Tribes of Fort Berthold Reservation v. Wold Engineering, P.\xc2\xa0C.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 138,\n152 (1984)\n.

\n

In this case, the Georgia habeas court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis in the section of its opinion labeled \xe2\x80\x9cBatson claim\xe2\x80\x9d proceeded as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe [State] argues that this claim is not reviewable due to the doctrine of res judicata. However, because [Foster] claims that additional evidence allegedly supporting this ground was discovered subsequent to the Georgia Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling [on direct appeal], this court will review the Batson claim as to whether [Foster] has shown any change in the facts sufficient to overcome the res judicata bar.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 192.

\n

To determine whether Foster had alleged a sufficient \xe2\x80\x9cchange in the facts,\xe2\x80\x9d the habeas court engaged in four pages of what it termed a \xe2\x80\x9cBatson .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. analysis,\xe2\x80\x9d in which it evaluated the original trial record and habeas record, including the newly uncovered prosecution file. Id., at 192\xe2\x80\x93196. Ultimately, that court concluded that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9crenewed Batson claim is without merit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 196 (emphasis added).

\n

In light of the foregoing, it is apparent that the state habeas court\xe2\x80\x99s application of res judicata to Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim was not independent of the merits of his federal constitutional challenge.[3] That court\xe2\x80\x99s invocation of res judicata therefore poses no impediment to our review of Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim. See Ake, 470 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 75.[4]

\n

III

\n

A

\n

The \xe2\x80\x9cConstitution forbids striking even a single prospective juror for a discriminatory purpose.\xe2\x80\x9d Snyder v. Louisiana,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 472,\n478 (2008)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). Our decision in Batson v. Kentucky,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 79\n, provides a three-step process for determining when a strike is discriminatory:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cFirst, a defendant must make a prima facie showing that a peremptory challenge has been exercised on the basis of race; second, if that showing has been made, the prosecution must offer a race-neutral basis for striking the juror in question; and third, in light of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 submissions, the trial court must determine whether the defendant has shown purposeful discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Snyder, 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 476\xe2\x80\x93477 (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted).

\n

Both parties agree that Foster has demonstrated a prima facie case, and that the prosecutors have offered race-neutral reasons for their strikes. We therefore address only Batson\xe2\x80\x99s third step. That step turns on factual determinations, and, \xe2\x80\x9cin the absence of exceptional circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d we defer to state court factual findings unless we conclude that they are clearly erroneous. Synder, 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 477.

\n

Before reviewing the factual record in this case, a brief word is in order regarding the contents of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file that Foster obtained through his Georgia Open Records Act requests. Pursuant to those requests, Foster received a \xe2\x80\x9ccertif[ied] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. true and correct copy of 103 pages of the State\xe2\x80\x99s case file\xe2\x80\x9d from his 1987 trial. App. 247. The State argues that \xe2\x80\x9cbecause [Foster] did not call either of the prosecutors to the stand\xe2\x80\x9d to testify in his state habeas proceedings, \xe2\x80\x9che can only speculate as to the meaning of various markings and writings\xe2\x80\x9d on thosepages, \xe2\x80\x9cthe author of many of them, and whether the twoprosecutors at trial (District Attorney Lanier and Assistant District Attorney Pullen) even saw many of them.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 20. For these reasons, the State argues, \xe2\x80\x9cnone of the specific pieces of new evidence [found in the file] shows an intent to discriminate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (capitalization omitted). For his part, Foster argues that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here is no question that the prosecutors used the lists and notes, which came from the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file and were certified as such,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore the \xe2\x80\x9csource of the lists and notes, their timing, and their purpose is hardly \xe2\x80\x98unknown\xe2\x80\x99 or based on \xe2\x80\x98conjecture.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief 4\xe2\x80\x935 (quoting Brief for Respondent 27\xe2\x80\x9328).

\n

The State concedes that the prosecutors themselves authored some documents, see, e.g., Tr. of Oral Arg. 45 (admitting that one of the two prosecutors must have written the list titled \xe2\x80\x9cdefinite NO\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x9d), and Lundy\xe2\x80\x99s testimony strongly suggests that the prosecutors viewed others, see, e.g., Tr. 220 (noting that the highlighted jury venire lists were returned to Lanier prior to jury selection). There are, however, genuine questions that remain about the provenance of other documents. Nothing in the record, for example, identifies the author of the notes that listed three black prospective jurors as \xe2\x80\x9cB#1,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cB#2,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cB#3.\xe2\x80\x9d Such notes, then, are not necessarily attributable directly to the prosecutors themselves. The state habeas court was cognizant of those limitations, but nevertheless admitted the file into evidence, reserving \xe2\x80\x9ca determination as to what weight the Court is going to put on any of [them]\xe2\x80\x9d in light of the objections urged by the State. 1 Record 20.

\n

We agree with that approach. Despite questions about the background of particular notes, we cannot accept the State\xe2\x80\x99s invitation to blind ourselves to their existence. We have \xe2\x80\x9cmade it clear that in considering a Batson objection, or in reviewing a ruling claimed to be Batson error, all of the circumstances that bear upon the issue of racial animosity must be consulted.\xe2\x80\x9d Snyder, 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 478. As we have said in a related context, \xe2\x80\x9c[d]etermining whether invidious discriminatory purpose was a motivating factor demands a sensitive inquiry into such circumstantial .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. evidence of intent as may be available.\xe2\x80\x9d Arlington Heights v. Metropolitan Housing Development Corp.,\n\n429 U.\xc2\xa0S. 252,\n266 (1977)\n. At a minimum, we are comfortable that all documents in the file were authored by someone in the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office. Any uncertainties concerning the documents are pertinent only as potential limits on their probative value.

\n

B

\n

Foster centers his Batson claim on the strikes of two black prospective jurors, Marilyn Garrett and Eddie Hood. We turn first to Marilyn Garrett. According to Lanier, on the morning that the State was to use its strikes he had not yet made up his mind to remove Garrett. Rather, he decided to strike her only after learning that he would not need to use a strike on another black prospective juror, Shirley Powell, who was excused for cause that morning.

\n

Ultimately, Lanier did strike Garrett. In justifying that strike to the trial court, he articulated a laundry list of reasons. Specifically, Lanier objected to Garrett because she: (1) worked with disadvantaged youth in her job as a teacher\xe2\x80\x99s aide; (2) kept looking at the ground during voir\xc2\xa0dire; (3) gave short and curt answers during voir\xc2\xa0dire; (4) appeared nervous; (5) was too young; (6) misrepresented her familiarity with the location of the crime; (7) failedto disclose that her cousin had been arrested on a drug charge; (8) was divorced; (9) had two children and two jobs; (10) was asked few questions by the defense; and (11) did not ask to be excused from jury service. See App. 55\xe2\x80\x9357 (pretrial hearing); id., at 93\xe2\x80\x9398, 105, 108, 110\xe2\x80\x93112 (new trial hearing); Record in No. 45609 (Ga. 1988), pp. 439\xe2\x80\x93440 (hereinafter Trial Record) (brief in opposition to new trial).

\n

The trial court accepted Lanier\xe2\x80\x99s justifications, concluding that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n the totality of circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d there was \xe2\x80\x9cno discriminatory intent, and that there existed reasonably clear, specific, and legitimate reasons\xe2\x80\x9d for the strike. App. 143. On their face, Lanier\xe2\x80\x99s justifications for the strike seem reasonable enough. Our independent examination of the record, however, reveals that much of the reasoning provided by Lanier has no grounding in fact.

\n

Lanier\xe2\x80\x99s misrepresentations to the trial court began with an elaborate explanation of how he ultimately came to strike Garrett:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he prosecution considered this juror [to have] the most potential to choose from out of the four remaining blacks in the 42 [member] panel venire. However, a system of events took place on the morning of jury selection that caused the excusal of this juror. The [S]tate had, in his jury notes, listed this juror as questionable. The four negative challenges were allocated for Hardge, Hood, Turner and Powell.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. But on the morning of jury selection, Juror Powell was excused for cause with no objections by [d]efense counsel. She was replaced by Juror Cadle [who] was acceptable to the State. This left the State with an additional strike it had not anticipated or allocated. Consequently, the State had to choose between [white] Juror Blackmon or Juror Garrett, the only two questionable jurors the State had left on the list.\xe2\x80\x9d Trial Record 438\xe2\x80\x93440 (brief in opposition to new trial) (emphasis added and citations omitted).

\n

Lanier then offered an extensive list of reasons for striking Garrett and explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]hese factors, with no reference to race, were considered by the prosecutor in this particular case to result in a juror less desirable from the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s viewpoint than Juror Blackmon.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 441 (emphasis deleted).

\n

Lanier then compared Blackmon to Garrett. In contrast to Garrett, Juror Blackmon

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cwas 46 years old, married 13 years to her husband who works at GE, buying her own home and [was recommended by a third party to] this prosecutor. She was no longer employed at Northwest Georgia Regional Hospital and she attended Catholic church on an irregular basis. She did not hesitate when answering the questions concerning the death penalty, had good eye contact with the prosecutor and gave good answers on the insanity issue. She was perceived by the prosecutor as having a stable home environment, of the right age and no association with any disadvantaged youth organizations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

Lanier concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe chances of [Blackmon] returning a death sentence were greater when all these factors were considered than Juror Garrett. Consequently, Juror Garrett was excused.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

The trial court accepted this explanation in denying Foster\xe2\x80\x99s motion for a new trial. See App. 142\xe2\x80\x93143. But the predicate for the State\xe2\x80\x99s account\xe2\x80\x94that Garrett was \xe2\x80\x9clisted\xe2\x80\x9d by the prosecution as \xe2\x80\x9cquestionable,\xe2\x80\x9d making that strike a last-minute race-neutral decision\xe2\x80\x94was false.

\n

During jury selection, the State went first. As a consequence, the defense could accept any prospective juror not struck by the State without any further opportunity for the State to use a strike against that prospective juror. Accordingly, the State had to \xe2\x80\x9cpretty well select the ten specific people [it] intend[ed] to strike\xe2\x80\x9d in advance. Id., at 83 (pretrial hearing); accord, ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he ten people that we felt very uncomfortable with, we have to know up front.\xe2\x80\x9d (Lanier testimony)). The record evidence shows that Garrett was one of those \xe2\x80\x9cten specific people.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

That much is evident from the \xe2\x80\x9cdefinite NO\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x9d list in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file. Garrett\xe2\x80\x99s name appeared on that list, which the State concedes was written by one of the prosecutors. Tr. of Oral Arg. 45. That list belies Lanier\xe2\x80\x99s assertion that the State considered allowing Garrett to serve. The title of the list meant what it said: Garrett was a \xe2\x80\x9cdefinite NO.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 301 (emphasis added). The State from the outset was intent on ensuring that none of the jurors on that list would serve.

\n

The first five names on the \xe2\x80\x9cdefinite NO\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x9d list were Eddie Hood, Evelyn Hardge, Shirley Powell, Marilyn Garrett, and Mary Turner. All were black. The State struck each one except Powell (who, as discussed, was excused for cause at the last minute\xe2\x80\x94though the prosecution informed the trial court that the \xe2\x80\x9cState was not, under any circumstances, going to take [Powell],\xe2\x80\x9d Trial Record 439 (brief in opposition to new trial)). Only in the number six position did a white prospective juror appear, and she had informed the court during voir\xc2\xa0dire that she could not \xe2\x80\x9csay positively\xe2\x80\x9d that she could impose the death penalty even if the evidence warranted it. 6 Tr. in No. 86\xe2\x80\x932218\xe2\x80\x932 (Super. Ct. Floyd Cty., Ga., 1987), p. 1152 (hereinafter Trial Transcript); see also id., at 1153\xe2\x80\x931158. In short, contrary to the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s submissions, the State\xe2\x80\x99s resolve to strike Garrett was never in doubt. See also App. 290 (\xe2\x80\x9cN\xe2\x80\x9d appears next to Garrett\xe2\x80\x99s name on juror list); id., at 300 (same).

\n

The State attempts to explain away the contradiction between the \xe2\x80\x9cdefinite NO\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x9d list and Lanier\xe2\x80\x99s statements to the trial court as an example of a prosecutor merely \xe2\x80\x9cmisspeak[ing].\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 51. But this was not some off-the-cuff remark; it was an intricate story expounded by the prosecution in writing, laid out over three single-spaced pages in a brief filed with the trial court.

\n

Moreover, several of Lanier\xe2\x80\x99s reasons for why he chose Garrett over Blackmon are similarly contradicted by the record. Lanier told the court, for example, that he struck Garrett because \xe2\x80\x9cthe defense did not ask her questions about\xe2\x80\x9d pertinent trial issues such as her thoughts on \xe2\x80\x9cinsanity\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9calcohol,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cmuch questions on publicity.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 56 (pretrial hearing). But the trial transcripts reveal that the defense asked her several questions on all three topics. See 5 Trial Transcript 955\xe2\x80\x93956 (two questions on insanity and one on mental illness); ibid. (four questions on alcohol); id., at 956\xe2\x80\x93957 (five questions on publicity).

\n

Still other explanations given by the prosecution, while not explicitly contradicted by the record, are difficult to credit because the State willingly accepted white jurors with the same traits that supposedly rendered Garrett an unattractive juror. Lanier told the trial court that he struck Garrett because she was divorced. App. 56 (pre-trial hearing). But he declined to strike three out of the four prospective white jurors who were also divorced. SeeJuror Questionnaire in No. 86\xe2\x80\x932218\xe2\x80\x932 (Super. Ct. Floyd Cty., Ga., 1987) (hereinafter Juror Questionnaire), for Juror No. 23, p. 2 (juror Coultas, divorced); id., No. 33, p. 2 (juror Cochran, divorced); id., No. 107, p. 2 (juror Hatch, divorced); App. 23\xe2\x80\x9324, 31 (State accepting jurors Coultas, Cochran, and Hatch). Additionally, Lanier claimed that he struck Garrett because she was too young, and the \xe2\x80\x9cState was looking for older jurors that would not easily identify with the defendant.\xe2\x80\x9d Trial Record 439; see App. 55 (pretrial hearing). Yet Garrett was 34, and the State declined to strike eight white prospective jurors under the age of 36. See Trial Record 439; Juror Questionnaire No. 4, p. 1; id., No. 10, p. 1; id., No. 23, p. 1; id., No. 48, p. 1; id., No. 70, p. 1; id., No. 71, p. 1; id., No. 92, p. 1; id., No. 106, p. 1; see App. 22\xe2\x80\x9331. Two of those white jurors served on the jury; one of those two was only 21 years old. See id., at 35.

\n

Lanier also explained to the trial court that he struck Garrett because he \xe2\x80\x9cfelt that she was less than truthful\xe2\x80\x9d in her answers in voir\xc2\xa0dire. Id., at 108 (new trial hearing). Specifically, the State pointed the trial court to the following exchange:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Court]: Are you familiar with the neighborhood where [the victim] lived, North Rome?

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Garrett]: No.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 Trial Transcript 950\xe2\x80\x93951.

\n

Lanier, in explaining the strike, told the trial court that in apparent contradiction to that exchange (which represented the only time that Garrett was asked about the topic during voir\xc2\xa0dire), he had \xe2\x80\x9cnoted that [Garrett] attended Main High School, which is only two blocks from where [the victim] lived and certainly in the neighborhood. She denied any knowledge of the area.\xe2\x80\x9d Trial Record 439 (brief in opposition to new trial).

\n

We have no quarrel with the State\xe2\x80\x99s general assertion that it \xe2\x80\x9ccould not trust someone who gave materially untruthful answers on voir\xc2\xa0dire.\xe2\x80\x9d Foster, 258 Ga., at 739, 374 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 192. But even this otherwise legitimate reason is difficult to credit in light of the State\xe2\x80\x99s acceptance of (white) juror Duncan. Duncan gave practically the same answer as Garrett did during voir\xc2\xa0dire:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Court]: Are you familiar with the neighborhood in which [the victim] live[d]?

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[Duncan]: No. I live in Atteiram Heights, but it\xe2\x80\x99s not\xe2\x80\x94I\xe2\x80\x99m not familiar with up there, you know.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 Trial Transcript 959.

\n

But, as Lanier was aware, Duncan\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cresidence [was] less than a half a mile from the murder scene\xe2\x80\x9d and her workplace was \xe2\x80\x9clocated less than 250 yards\xe2\x80\x9d away. Trial Record 430 (brief in opposition to new trial).

\n

In sum, in evaluating the strike of Garrett, we are not faced with a single isolated misrepresentation.

\n

C

\n

We turn next to the strike of Hood. According to Lanier, Hood \xe2\x80\x9cwas exactly what [the State] was looking for in terms of age, between forty and fifty, good employment and married.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 44 (pretrial hearing). The prosecution nonetheless struck Hood, giving eight reasons for doing so. Hood: (1) had a son who was the same age as the defendant and who had previously been convicted of a crime; (2) had a wife who worked in food service at the local mental health institution; (3) had experienced food poisoning during voir\xc2\xa0dire; (4) was slow in responding to death penalty questions; (5) was a member of the Church of Christ; (6) had a brother who counseled drug offenders; (7) was not asked enough questions by the defense during voir\xc2\xa0dire; and (8) asked to be excused from jury service. See id., at 44\xe2\x80\x9347; id., at 86, 105, 110\xe2\x80\x93111 (new trial hearing); Trial Record 433\xe2\x80\x93435 (brief in opposition to new trial). An examination of the record, however, convinces us that many of these justifications cannot be credited.

\n

As an initial matter, the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s principal reasons for the strike shifted over time, suggesting that those reasons may be pretextual. In response to Foster\xe2\x80\x99s pre-trial Batson challenge, District Attorney Lanier noted all eight reasons, but explained:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe only thing I was concerned about, and I will state it for the record. He has an eighteen year old son which is about the same age as the defendant.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn my experience prosecuting over twenty-five murder cases .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. individuals having the same son as [a] defendant who is charged with murder [have] serious reservations and are more sympathetic and lean toward that particular person.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIt is ironic that his son, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. Darrell Hood[,] has been sentenced .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by the Court here, to theft by taking on April 4th, 1982.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [T]heft by taking is basi-cally the same thing that this defendant is chargedwith.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 44\xe2\x80\x9345 (pretrial hearing; emphasis added).

\n

But by the time of Foster\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent motion for a new trial, Lanier\xe2\x80\x99s focus had shifted. He still noted the similarities between Hood\xe2\x80\x99s son and Foster, see id., at 105 (new trial hearing), but that was no longer the key reason behind the strike. Lanier instead told the court that his paramount concern was Hood\xe2\x80\x99s membership in the Church of Christ: \xe2\x80\x9cThe Church of Christ people, while they may not take a formal stand against the death penalty, they are very, very reluctant to vote for the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 84 (new trial hearing); accord, Trial Record 434\xe2\x80\x93435 (\xe2\x80\x9cIt is the opinion of this prosecutor that in a death penalty case, Church of Christ affiliates are reluctant to return a verdict of death.\xe2\x80\x9d (brief in opposition to new trial)). Hood\xe2\x80\x99s religion, Lanier now explained, was the most important factor behind the strike: \xe2\x80\x9cI evaluated the whole Eddie Hood.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. And the bottom line on Eddie Hood is the Church of Christ affiliation.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 110\xe2\x80\x93111 (new trial hearing; emphasis added).

\n

Of course it is possible that Lanier simply misspoke in one of the two proceedings. But even if that were so, we would expect at least one of the two purportedly principal justifications for the strike to withstand closer scrutiny. Neither does.

\n

Take Hood\xe2\x80\x99s son. If Darrell Hood\xe2\x80\x99s age was the issue, why did the State accept (white) juror Billy Graves, who had a 17-year-old son? Juror Questionnaire No. 31, p.\xc2\xa03; see App. 24. And why did the State accept (white) juror Martha Duncan, even though she had a 20-year-old son? Juror Questionnaire No. 88, p.\xc2\xa03; see App. 30.

\n

The comparison between Hood and Graves is particu-larly salient. When the prosecution asked Hood if Foster\xe2\x80\x99sage would be a factor for him in sentencing, he answered \xe2\x80\x9cNone whatsoever.\xe2\x80\x9d Trial Transcript 280. Graves, on the other hand, answered the same question \xe2\x80\x9cprobably so.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 446. Yet the State struck Hood and accepted Graves.

\n

The State responds that Duncan and Graves were not similar to Hood because Hood\xe2\x80\x99s son had been convicted of theft, while Graves\xe2\x80\x99s and Duncan\xe2\x80\x99s sons had not. See Brief for Respondent 34\xe2\x80\x9335; see also App. 135\xe2\x80\x93136 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhile the defense asserts that the state used different standards for white jurors, insofar as many of them had children near the age of the Defendant, the Court believes that [Darrell Hood\xe2\x80\x99s] conviction is a distinction that makes the difference.\xe2\x80\x9d (trial court opinion denying new trial)). Lanier had described Darrell Hood\xe2\x80\x99s conviction to the trial court as being for \xe2\x80\x9cbasically the same thing that this defendant is charged with.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 45 (pretrial hearing). Nonsense. Hood\xe2\x80\x99s son had received a 12-month suspended sentence for stealing hubcaps from a car in a mall parking lot five years earlier. Trial Record 446. Foster was charged with capital murder of a 79-year-old widow after a brutal sexual assault. The \xe2\x80\x9cimplausible\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cfantastic\xe2\x80\x9d assertion that the two had been charged with \xe2\x80\x9cbasically the same thing\xe2\x80\x9d supports our conclusion that the focus on Hood\xe2\x80\x99s son can only be regarded as pretextual. Miller-El v. Cockrell,\n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 322,\n339 (2003)\n; see also ibid. (\xe2\x80\x9cCredibility can be measured by, among other factors, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. how reasonable, or how improbable, the [State\xe2\x80\x99s] explanations are.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s second principal justification for striking Hood\xe2\x80\x94his affiliation with the Church of Christ, and that church\xe2\x80\x99s alleged teachings on the death penalty\xe2\x80\x94fares no better. Hood asserted no fewer than four times during voir\xc2\xa0dire that he could impose the death penalty.[5] A prosecutor is entitled to disbelieve a juror\xe2\x80\x99s voir\xc2\xa0dire answers, of course. But the record persuades us that Hood\xe2\x80\x99s race, and not his religious affiliation, was Lanier\xe2\x80\x99s true motivation.

\n

The first indication to that effect is Lanier\xe2\x80\x99s mischaracterization of the record. On multiple occasions, Lanier asserted to the trial court that three white prospective jurors who were members of the Church of Christ had been struck for cause due to their opposition to the death penalty. See App. 46 (\xe2\x80\x9c[Hood\xe2\x80\x99s] religious preference is Church of Christ. There have been [three] other jurors that have been excused for cause by agreement that belong to the Church of Christ, Juror No. 35, 53, and 78.\xe2\x80\x9d (pretrial hearing)); id., at 114 (\xe2\x80\x9cThree out of four jurors who professed to be members of the Church of Christ, went off for [cause related to opposition to the death penalty].\xe2\x80\x9d (new trial hearing)); Trial Record 435 (\xe2\x80\x9cChurch of Christ jurors Terry (#35), Green (#53), and Waters (#78) [were] excused for cause due to feeling[s] against the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d (brief in opposition to new trial)).

\n

That was not true. One of those prospective jurors was excused before even being questioned during voir\xc2\xa0dire because she was five-and-a-half months pregnant. 5 Trial Transcript 893. Another was excused by the agreement of both parties because her answers on the death penalty made it difficult to ascertain her precise views on capital punishment. See Brief for Respondent 39 (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t was entirely unclear if [this juror] understood any of the trial court\xe2\x80\x99squestions and her answers are equivocal at best.\xe2\x80\x9d). And the judge found cause to dismiss the third because she had already formed an opinion about Foster\xe2\x80\x99s guilt. See 3 Trial Transcript 558 (\xe2\x80\x9c[Court]: And you have made up your mind already as to the guilt of the accused? A: Yes, sir. [Court]: I think that\xe2\x80\x99s cause.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

The prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file fortifies our conclusion that any reliance on Hood\xe2\x80\x99s religion was pretextual. The file contains a handwritten document titled \xe2\x80\x9cChurch of Christ.\xe2\x80\x9d The document notes that the church \xe2\x80\x9cdoesn\xe2\x80\x99t take a stand on [the] Death Penalty,\xe2\x80\x9d and that the issue is \xe2\x80\x9cleft for each individual member.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 302. The document then states: \xe2\x80\x9cNO. NO Black Church.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The State tries to downplay the significance of this document by emphasizing that the document\xe2\x80\x99s author is unknown. That uncertainty is pertinent. But we think the document is nonetheless entitled to significant weight, especially given that it is consistent with our serious doubts about the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s account of the strike.

\n

Many of the State\xe2\x80\x99s secondary justifications similarly come undone when subjected to scrutiny. Lanier told the trial court that Hood \xe2\x80\x9cappeared to be confused and slow in responding to questions concerning his views on the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Trial Record 434 (brief in opposition to new trial). As previously noted, however, Hood unequivocally voiced his willingness to impose the death penalty, and a white juror who showed similar confusion served on the jury. Compare 5 Trial Transcript 1100\xe2\x80\x931101 (white juror Huffman\xe2\x80\x99s answers) with 2 id., at 269\xe2\x80\x93278 (Hood\xe2\x80\x99s answers); see App. 35. According to the record, such confusion was not uncommon. See id., at 138 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Court notes that [Hood\xe2\x80\x99s] particular confusion about the death penalty questions was not unusual.\xe2\x80\x9d); accord, 5 Trial Transcript 994 (\xe2\x80\x9c[Court]: I think these questions should be reworded. \n

Lanier also stated that he struck Hood because Hood\xe2\x80\x99s wife worked at Northwest Regional Hospital as a food services supervisor. App. 45 (pretrial hearing). That hospital, Lanier explained, \xe2\x80\x9cdeals a lot with mentally disturbed, mentally ill people,\xe2\x80\x9d and so people associated with it tend \xe2\x80\x9cto be more sympathetic to the underdog.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. D

\n

As we explained in Miller-El v. Dretke, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f a prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s proffered reason for striking a black panelist applies just as well to an otherwise-similar nonblack [panelist] who is permitted to serve, that is evidence tending to prove purposeful discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 231,\n241 (2005)\n. With respect to both Garrett and Hood, such evidence is compelling. But that is not all. There are also the shifting explanations, the misrepresentations of the record, and the persistent focus on race in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file. Considering all of the circumstantial evidence that \xe2\x80\x9cbear[s] upon the issue of racial animosity,\xe2\x80\x9d we are left with the firm conviction that the strikes of Garrett and Hood were \xe2\x80\x9cmotivated in substantial part by discriminatory intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Snyder, 552 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 478, 485.[6]

\n

IV

\n

Throughout all stages of this litigation, the State has strenuously objected that \xe2\x80\x9crace [was] not a factor\xe2\x80\x9d in its jury selection strategy. App. 41 (pretrial hearing); but see id., at 120 (Lanier testifying that the strikes were \xe2\x80\x9cbased on many factors and not purely on race.\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added) (new trial hearing)). Indeed, at times the State has been downright indignant. See Trial Record 444 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Defenses\xe2\x80\x99s [sic] misapplication of the law and erroneous distortion of the facts are an attempt to discredit the pro-secutor.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. The State and this community demand an apology.\xe2\x80\x9d (brief in opposition to new trial)).

\n

The contents of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file, however, plainly belie the State\xe2\x80\x99s claim that it exercised its strikes in a \xe2\x80\x9ccolor-blind\xe2\x80\x9d manner. App. 41, 60 (pretrial hearing). The sheer number of references to race in that file is arresting. The State, however, claims that things are not quite as bad as they seem. The focus on black prospective jurors, it contends, does not indicate any attempt to exclude them from the jury. It instead reflects an effort to ensure that the State was \xe2\x80\x9cthoughtful and non-discriminatory in [its] consideration of black prospective jurors [and] to develop and maintain detailed information on those prospective jurors in order to properly defend against any suggestion that decisions regarding [its] selections were pretextual.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 6. Batson, after all, had come down only months before Foster\xe2\x80\x99s trial. The prosecutors, according to the State, were uncertain what sort of showing might be demanded of them and wanted to be prepared.

\n

This argument falls flat. To begin, it \xe2\x80\x9creeks of afterthought,\xe2\x80\x9d Miller-El, 545 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 246, having never before been made in the nearly 30-year history of this litigation: not in the trial court, not in the state habeas court, and not even in the State\xe2\x80\x99s brief in opposition to Foster\xe2\x80\x99s petition for certiorari.

\n

In addition, the focus on race in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file plainly demonstrates a concerted effort to keep black prospective jurors off the jury. The State argues that it \xe2\x80\x9cwas actively seeking a black juror.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 12; see also App. 99 (new trial hearing). But this claim is not credible. An \xe2\x80\x9cN\xe2\x80\x9d appeared next to each of the black prospective jurors\xe2\x80\x99 names on the jury venire list. See, e.g., id., at 253. An \xe2\x80\x9cN\xe2\x80\x9d was also noted next to the name of each black prospective juror on the list of the 42 qualified prospective jurors; each of those names also appeared on the \xe2\x80\x9cdefinite NO\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x9d list. See id., 299\xe2\x80\x93301. And a draft affidavit from the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s investigator stated his view that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f it comes down to having to pick one of the black jurors, [Marilyn] Garrett, might be okay.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 345 (emphasis added); see also ibid. (recommending Garrett \xe2\x80\x9cif we had to pick a black juror\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). Such references are inconsistent with attempts to \xe2\x80\x9cactively see[k]\xe2\x80\x9d a black juror.

\n

The State\xe2\x80\x99s new argument today does not dissuade us from the conclusion that its prosecutors were motivated in substantial part by race when they struck Garrett and Hood from the jury 30 years ago. Two peremptory strikes on the basis of race are two more than the Constitution allows.

\n

The order of the Georgia Supreme Court is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

It is so ordered.

Justice Alito, concurring in the judgment.

\n

I agree with the Court that the decision of the Supreme Court of Georgia cannot be affirmed and that the case must be remanded. I write separately to explain my understanding of the role of state law in the proceedings that must be held on remand.

\n

I

\n

As the Court recounts, in August 1986, Queen Madge White, a 79-year-old retired schoolteacher, was sexually assaulted and brutally murdered in her home in Rome, Georgia. Her home was ransacked, and various household items were stolen. Foster v. State, 258 Ga. 736, 374 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 188 (1988). About a month after the murder, police officers were called to respond to a local disturbance. The complainant, Lisa Stubbs, told them that her boyfriend, petitioner Timothy Foster, had killed White and had distributed the goods stolen from White\xe2\x80\x99s home to Stubbs and family members. Tr. 1719\xe2\x80\x931723. Officers arrested Foster, who confessed to the murder and robbery, 258 Ga., at 736, 374 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 190, and the police recovered some of the stolen goods.

\n

Foster was put on trial for White\xe2\x80\x99s murder, convicted, and sentenced to death. Before, during, and after his trial, Foster argued that the prosecution violated his rights under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s then-recent decision in Batson v. Kentucky,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 79 (1986)\n, by peremptorily challenging all the prospective jurors who were black. After the Georgia Supreme Court rejected Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson argument on direct appeal, he filed a petition for a writ of certiorari in this Court, but his petition did not raise a Batson claim,[1] and the petition was denied. Foster v. Georgia,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1085 (1989)\n.

\n

In July 1989, Foster filed a state habeas petition in the Superior Court of Butts County, Georgia. For the next 10 years, most of Foster\xe2\x80\x99s claims (including his Batson claim) were held in abeyance while the Georgia courts adjudi-cated Foster\xe2\x80\x99s claim that he is \xe2\x80\x9cmentally retarded\xe2\x80\x9d and thus cannot be executed under Georgia law. Zant v. Foster, 261 Ga. 450, 406 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 74 (1991). After extensive court proceedings, including two visits to the State Supreme Court,[2] additional petitions for certiorari to this Court,[3] and a jury trial on the issue of intellectual disability, Foster was denied relief on that claim. He then amended his habeas petition, and the Superior Court considered the many other claims asserted in his petition, including his Batson claim. In support of that claim, Foster offered new evidence, namely, the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s jury selection notes, which he had obtained through a Georgia open-records request. These notes showed that someone had highlighted the names of black jurors and had written the letter \xe2\x80\x9cB\xe2\x80\x9d next to their names.

\n

The Superior Court issued a written decision in which it evaluated Foster\xe2\x80\x99s habeas claims. The opinion began by noting that many of his claims were barred by res judi-cata. The opinion stated: \xe2\x80\x9c[T]his court notes .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the following claims are not reviewable based on the doctrine of res judicata, as the claims were raised and litigated adversely to the petitioner on his direct appeal to the Georgia Supreme Court.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 175. Included in the list of barred claims was \xe2\x80\x9cPetitioner[\xe2\x80\x99s] alleg[ation] that the State used peremptory challenges in a racially discriminatory manner in violation of Batson.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 175\xe2\x80\x93176.

\n

Later in its opinion, the Superior Court again referred to the Batson claim and wrote as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe Respondent argues that this claim is not reviewable due to the doctrine of res judicata. However, because the Petitioner claims that additional evidence allegedly supporting this ground was discovered subsequent to the Georgia Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling in Foster v. State, 258 Ga. 736 (1988) [the decision affirming Foster\xe2\x80\x99s conviction on direct appeal], this court will review the Batson claim as to whether Petitioner has shown any change in the facts sufficient to overcome the res judicata bar.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 192.

\n

The court then reviewed the evidence and concluded that it \xe2\x80\x9c[could not] find that the highlighting of the names of black jurors and the notation of their race can serve to override this previous consideration [on direct appeal].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 193. Because \xe2\x80\x9call jurors in this case, regardless of race, were thoroughly investigated and considered before the State exercised its peremptory challenges,\xe2\x80\x9d the court found that \xe2\x80\x9cPetitioner fail[ed] to demonstrate purposeful discrimination on the basis that the race of prospective jurors was either circled, highlighted or otherwise noted on various lists.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 195. Thus, the court held that the Batson claim was \xe2\x80\x9cwithout merit.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 196.

\n

Foster subsequently sought review of the Superior Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in the Georgia Supreme Court, but that court refused to issue a certificate of probable cause (CPC) to appeal. In its entirety, the State Supreme Court order states:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cUpon consideration of the Application for Certificate of Probable Cause to appeal the denial of habeas corpus, it is ordered that it be hereby denied. All the Justices concur, except Benham, J., who dissents.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 246.

\n

Foster sought review of this decision, and this Court granted certiorari to review the decision of the Georgia Supreme Court. 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015).

\n

II

\n

The decision of the Georgia Supreme Court was a decision on the merits of Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim, as presented in his state habeas petition. See Ga. Sup.\xc2\xa0Ct. Rule 36 (2016) (a CPC to appeal a final judgment in a habeas corpus case involving a criminal conviction \xe2\x80\x9cwill be issued where there is arguable merit\xe2\x80\x9d); Hittson v. Warden, 759 F. 3d 1210, 1232 (CA11 2014) (The Georgia Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s standard for denying a CPC \xe2\x80\x9cclearly constitutes an adjudication on the merits\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, what the Georgia Supreme Court held was that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim, as presented in his state habeas petition, lacked arguable merit.

\n

That holding was likely based at least in part on state law. As noted, the Superior Court quite clearly held that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim was barred by res judicata. That conclusion, to be sure, was not entirely divorced from the merits of his federal constitutional claim, since the court went on to discuss the evidence advanced by petitioner in support of his argument that the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s strikes of black members of the venire were based on race. Rather, it appears that the Superior Court understood state law to permit Foster to obtain reconsideration of his previously rejected Batson claim only if he was able to show that a \xe2\x80\x9cchange in the facts\xe2\x80\x9d was \xe2\x80\x9csufficient to overcome the res judicata bar.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 192.

\n

In concluding that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s renewed Batson claim was required to meet a heightened standard, the Superior Court appears to have been following established Georgia law. Some Georgia cases seem to stand for the proposition that the bar is absolute, at least in some circumstances. See, e.g., Roulain v. Martin, 266 Ga. 353, 466 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 837, 839 (1996) (\xe2\x80\x9cSince this issue was raised and resolved in Martin\xe2\x80\x99s direct appeal, it should not have been readdressed by the habeas court\xe2\x80\x9d); Davis v. Thomas, 261 Ga. 687, 689, 410 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 110, 112 (1991) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis issue was raised on direct appeal, and this court determined that it had no merit. Davis recognizes the principle that one who had an issue decided adversely to him on direct appeal is precluded from relitigating that issue on habeas corpus\xe2\x80\x9d); Gunter v. Hickman, 256 Ga. 315, 316, 348 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 644, 645 (1986) (\xe2\x80\x9cThis issue was actually litigated, i.e., raised and decided, in the appellant\xe2\x80\x99s direct appeal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. For this reason, the issue cannot be reasserted in habeas-corpus proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d); Elrod v. Ault, 231 Ga. 750, 204 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 176 (1974) (\xe2\x80\x9cAfter an appellate review the same issues will not be reviewed on habeas corpus\xe2\x80\x9d). Other decisions, however, allow a defendant to overcome res judicata if he can produce newly discovered evidence that was not \xe2\x80\x9creasonably available\xe2\x80\x9d to him on direct review. Gibson v. Head, 282 Ga. 156, 159, 646 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 257, 260 (2007); see also Gibson v. Ricketts, 244 Ga. 482, 483, 260 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 877, 878 (1979).[4]

\n

In restricting the relitigation of previously rejected claims, Georgia is not alone. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]e have long and consistently affirmed that a collateral challenge may not do service for an appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Frady,\n\n456 U.\xc2\xa0S. 152,\n165 (1982)\n. Accordingly, at least as a general rule, federal prisoners may not use a motion under\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255 to relitigate a claim that was previously rejected on direct appeal. See, e.g., Reed v. Farley,\n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 339,\n358 (1994)\n (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]laims will ordinarily not be entertained under \xc2\xa72255 that have already been rejected on direct review\xe2\x80\x9d); Withrow v. Williams,\n\n507 U.\xc2\xa0S. 680,\n721 (1993)\n (Scalia, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]bsent countervailing considerations, district courts may refuse to reach the merits of a constitutional claim previously raised and rejected on direct appeal\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. Lee, 715 F.\xc2\xa03d 215, 224 (CA8 2013); Rozier v. United States, 701 F.\xc2\xa03d 681, 684 (CA11 2012); United States v. Roane, 378 F.\xc2\xa03d 382, 396, n. 7 (CA4 2004); United States v. Webster, 392 F.\xc2\xa03d 787, 791 (CA5 2004); White v. United States, 371 F.\xc2\xa03d 900, 902 (CA7 2004); United States v. Jones, 918 F.\xc2\xa02d 9, 10\xe2\x80\x9311 (CA2 1990); United States v. Prichard, 875 F.\xc2\xa02d 789, 790\xe2\x80\x93791 (CA10 1989). Cf. Davis v. United States,\n\n417 U.\xc2\xa0S. 333,\n342 (1974)\n. As we have said, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t has, of course, long been settled law that an error that may justify reversal on direct appeal will not necessarily support a collateral attack on a final judgment. The reasons for narrowly limiting the grounds for collateral attack on final judgments are well known and basic to our adversary system of justice.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Addonizio,\n\n442 U.\xc2\xa0S. 178,\n184 (1979)\n (footnote omitted).

\n

In accordance with this principle, federal law provides that a state prisoner may not relitigate a claim that was rejected in a prior federal habeas petition. See 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72244(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(3). And even when a state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s second or successive federal habeas petition asserts a new federal constitutional claim based on what is asserted to be new evidence, the claim must be dismissed unless a very demanding test is met. See \xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(B) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he factual predicate for the claim could not have been discovered previously through the exercise of due diligence\xe2\x80\x9d; and the facts must \xe2\x80\x9cbe sufficient to establish by clear and convincing evidence that .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. no reasonable factfinder would have found the applicant guilty\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he principle of finality\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cessential to the operation of our criminal justice system.\xe2\x80\x9d Teague v. Lane,\n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 288,\n309 (1989)\n (plurality opinion). Thus, once a criminal conviction becomes final\xe2\x80\x94as Foster\xe2\x80\x99s did 30 years ago\xe2\x80\x94state courts need not remain open indefinitely to relitigate claims related to that conviction which were raised and decided on direct review. States are under no obligation to permit collateral attacks on convictions that have become final, and if they allow such attacks, they are free to limit the circumstances in which claims may be relitigated.

\n

To the extent that the decision of the Georgia Supreme Court was based on a state rule restricting the relitigation of previously rejected claims, the decision has a state-law component, and we have no jurisdiction to review a state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision on a question of state law. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71257(a). This Court, no less than every other federal court, has \xe2\x80\x9can independent obligation to ensure that [we] do not exceed the scope of [our] jurisdiction, and therefore [we] must raise and decide jurisdictional questions that the parties either overlook or elect not to press.\xe2\x80\x9d Henderson v. Shinseki,\n\n562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 428,\n434 (2011)\n.

\n

III

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThis Court long has held that it will not consider an issue of federal law on direct review from a judgment of a state court if that judgment rests on a state-law ground that is both \xe2\x80\x98independent\xe2\x80\x99 of the merits of the federal claim and an \xe2\x80\x98adequate\xe2\x80\x99 basis for the court\xe2\x80\x99s decision,\xe2\x80\x9d Harris v. Reed, \n\n489 U.\xc2\xa0S. 255,\n260 (1989)\n, and like the Court (and both petitioner and respondent) I agree that we cannot conclude from the brief order issued by the Supreme Court of Georgia that its decision was based wholly on state law. It is entirely possible that the State Supreme Court reached a conclusion about the effect of the state res judicata bar based in part on as assessment of the strength of Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim or the extent to which the new evidence bolstered that claim. And if that is what the State Supreme Court held, the rule that the court applied was an amalgam of state and federal law.

\n

By the same token, however, the state-law res judicata rule applied by the Superior Court is clearly not like the rule in Ake v. Oklahoma,\n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 68 (1985)\n, which appears to have been entirely dependent on federal law. In Ake, a prisoner argued that due process entitled him to obtain the services of a psychiatrist in order to prove that he was insane at the time when he committed a murder. The Oklahoma courts concluded that Ake\xe2\x80\x99s claim was waived, but the Oklahoma waiver rule essentially made an exception for any case in which there was a violation of a fundamental federal constitutional right. See id., at 74\xe2\x80\x9375 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Oklahoma waiver rule does not apply to fundamental trial error,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9cfederal constitutional errors [that] are \xe2\x80\x98fundamental\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, the state waiver rule was entirely dependent on federal law, and this Court therefore held that it had jurisdiction to review the underlying constitutional question\xe2\x80\x94whether Ake was entitled to a psychiatrist. Then, having found a constitu-tional violation, the Court remanded for a new trial. Id., at 86\xe2\x80\x9387.

\n

The res judicata rule applied by the Superior Court in this case is quite different. That court obviously did not think that Georgia law included an Ake-like exception that would permit a defendant to overcome res judicata simply by making the kind of showing of federal constitutional error that would have been sufficient when the claim was first adjudicated. Accordingly, Ake does not mean that we can simply disregard the possibility that the decision under review may have a state-law component.

\n

Our cases chart the path that we must follow in a situation like the one present here. When \xe2\x80\x9ca state court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of state law has been influenced by an accompanying interpretation of federal law,\xe2\x80\x9d the proper course is for this Court to \xe2\x80\x9crevie[w] the federal question on which the state-law determination appears to have been premised. If the state court has proceeded on an incorrect perception of federal law, it has been this Court\xe2\x80\x99s practice to vacate the judgment of the state court and remand the case so that the court may reconsider the state-law question free of misapprehensions about the scope of federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Three Affiliated Tribes of Fort Berthold Reservation v. Wold Engineering, P. C.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 138,\n152 (1984)\n. See also S. Shapiro, K. Geller, T. Bishop, E. Hartnett, & D. Himmelfarb, Supreme Court Practice 212 (10th ed. 2013). In a situation like the one presented here, the correct approach is for us to decide the question of federal law and then to remand the case to the state court so that it can reassess its decision on the state-law question in light of our decision on the underlying federal issue.[5]

\n

IV

\n

I agree with the Court that the totality of the evidence now adduced by Foster is sufficient to make out a Batson violation. On remand, the Georgia Supreme Court is bound to accept that evaluation of the federal question, but whether that conclusion justifies relief under state res judicata law is a matter for that court to decide.

\n

Compliance with Batson is essential to ensure that defendants receive a fair trial and to preserve the public confidence upon which our system of criminal justice depends. But it is also important that this Court respect the authority of state courts to structure their systems of postconviction review in a way that promotes the expeditious and definitive disposition of claims of error.

\n

Until recently, this Court rarely granted review of state-court decisions in collateral review proceedings, preferring to allow the claims adjudicated in such proceedings to be decided first in federal habeas proceedings. See Lawrence v. Florida,\n\n549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 327,\n335 (2007)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]his Court rarely grants review at this stage of the litigation even when the application for state collateral relief is supported by arguably meritorious federal constitutional claims, choosing instead to wait for federal habeas proceedings\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Kyles v. Whitley,\n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 931,\n932 (1990)\n (Stevens, J., concurring in denial of stay of execution); Huffman v. Florida,\n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1014\n\xe2\x80\x931018 (1978) (Stevens, J., respecting denial of certiorari). When cases reach this Court after habeas review in the lower federal courts, the standards of review set out in the Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72254, apply. Recently, this Court has evidenced a predilection for granting review of state-court decisions denying postconviction relief, see, e.g., Wearry v. Cain, 577 U.\xc2\xa0S. __ (2016) (per curiam). Particularly in light of that trend, it is important that we do not lightly brush aside the States\xe2\x80\x99 legitimate interest in structuring their systems of postconviction review in a way that militates against repetitive litigation and endless delay.

TIMOTHY TYRONE FOSTER, PETITIONER v. BRUCE CHATMAN, WARDEN

\n

on writ of certiorari to the supreme court of georgia

\n

[May 23, 2016]

\n

\n

Justice Thomas, dissenting.

\n

Thirty years ago, Timothy Foster confessed to murdering Queen Madge White after sexually assaulting her with a bottle of salad dressing. In the decades since, Foster has sought to vacate his conviction and death sentence on the ground that prosecutors violated Batson v. Kentucky,\n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 79 (1986)\n, when they struck all black prospective jurors before his trial. Time and again, the state courts have rejected that claim. The trial court twice rejected it, and the Supreme Court of Georgia unequivocally rejected it when Foster directly appealed his conviction and sentence. Foster v. State, 258 Ga. 736, 736, n.\xc2\xa01, 738\xe2\x80\x93739, 374 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 188, 190, n.\xc2\xa01, 192 (1988), cert. denied,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1085 (1989)\n. A state habeas court rejected it in 2013. App. 175\xe2\x80\x93176, 192\xe2\x80\x93196. And most recently, the Supreme Court of Georgia again rejected it as lacking \xe2\x80\x9carguable merit,\xe2\x80\x9d Ga. Sup. Ct. Rule 36 (2001). See App. 246.

\n

Yet, today\xe2\x80\x94nearly three decades removed from voir\xc2\xa0dire\xe2\x80\x94the Court rules in Foster\xe2\x80\x99s favor. It does so without adequately grappling with the possibility that we lack jurisdiction. Moreover, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s ruling on the merits, based, in part, on new evidence that Foster procured decades after his conviction, distorts the deferential Batson inquiry. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

Federal law authorizes us to review the \xe2\x80\x9cjudgments or decrees rendered by the highest court of a State in which a decision could be had,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71257(a), but only if such a judgment or decree raises a question of federal law, Michigan v. Long,\n\n463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1032,\n1038 (1983)\n. The Court today errs by assuming that the Supreme Court of Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s one-line order\xe2\x80\x94the \xe2\x80\x9cjudgmen[t] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. rendered by the highest court of a State in which a decision could be had,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71257\xe2\x80\x94raises such a question. See ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. The far more likely explanation for the court\xe2\x80\x99s denial of habeas relief is that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s claim is procedurally barred. This disposition is ordinarily a question of state law that this Court is powerless to review. Before addressing the merits of Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim, the Court should have sought clarification that the resolution of a federal question was implicated in the Georgia high court\xe2\x80\x99s decision.

\n

A

\n

The Supreme Court of Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s order in this case states in full: \xe2\x80\x9cUpon consideration of the Application for Certificate of Probable Cause to appeal the denial of habeas corpus, it is ordered that it be hereby denied.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 246. Neither that order nor Georgia law provides adequate assurance that this case raises a federal question.

\n

Under Georgia law, a state prisoner may file a state habeas petition in a state superior court. Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9314\xe2\x80\x9341 to 9\xe2\x80\x9314\xe2\x80\x9343 (2015). If the state superior court denies the petition, then the prisoner may appeal to the Supreme Court of Georgia, which has exclusive jurisdiction over habeas corpus cases, by timely filing a notice of appeal in the superior court and applying for a certificate of probable cause in the supreme court. See Fullwood v. Sivley, 271 Ga. 248, 250\xe2\x80\x93251, 517 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 511, 513\xe2\x80\x93515 (1999) (discussing requirements of \xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9314\xe2\x80\x9352). Much like certificates of appealability in federal court, Miller-El v. Cockrell,\n\n537 U.\xc2\xa0S. 322,\n336 (2003)\n, a Georgia prisoner must establish in his application that at least one of his claims has \xe2\x80\x9carguable merit.\xe2\x80\x9d Ga. Sup. Ct. Rule 36. If he cannot, the Supreme Court of Georgia summarily denies relief by denying the certificate of probable cause. Ibid.; see also \xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9314\xe2\x80\x9352(b); Hittson v. GDCP Warden, 759 F. 3d 1210, 1231\xe2\x80\x931232 (CA11 2014). If he can, then the court affords plenary review of the arguably meritorious claim. See, e.g., Sears v. Humphrey, 294 Ga. 117, 117\xe2\x80\x93118, 751 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 365, 368 (2013); Hillman v. Johnson, 297 Ga. 609, 611, 615, n.\xc2\xa05, 774 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 615, 617, 620, n.\xc2\xa05 (2015). The most we can glean, therefore, from the summary denial of Foster\xe2\x80\x99s state habeas petition is that the Supreme Court of Georgia concluded that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s claim lacked \xe2\x80\x9carguable merit.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

The most obvious ground for deciding that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s claim lacked \xe2\x80\x9carguable merit\xe2\x80\x9d is that the Supreme Court of Georgia already considered that claim and rejected it decades ago.[1] Georgia law prohibits Foster from raising the same claim anew in his state habeas petition. See, e.g., Davis v. Thomas, 261 Ga. 687, 689, 410 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 110, 112 (1991). \xe2\x80\x9cIt is axiomatic\xe2\x80\x9d in the Georgia courts \xe2\x80\x9cthat a habeas court is not to be used as a substitute for an appeal, or as a second appeal.\xe2\x80\x9d Walker v. Penn, 271 Ga. 609, 612, 523 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 325, 327 (1999). Without such procedural bars, state prisoners could raise old claims again and again until they are declared victorious, and finality would mean nothing. See Friendly, Is Innocence Irrelevant? Collateral Attack on Criminal Judgments, 38 U. Chi. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 142, 145 (1970) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe proverbial man from Mars would surely think we must consider our system of criminal justice terribly bad if we are willing to tolerate such efforts at undoing judgments of conviction\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

I would think that this state-law defect in Foster\xe2\x80\x99s state habeas petition would be the end of the matter: \xe2\x80\x9cBecause this Court has no power to review a state law determination that is sufficient to support the judgment, resolution of any independent federal ground for the decision could not affect the judgment and would therefore be advisory.\xe2\x80\x9d Coleman v. Thompson,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 722,\n729 (1991)\n. It is fundamental that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9conly power over state judgments is to correct them to the extent that they incorrectly adjudge federal rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Herb v. Pitcairn,\n\n324 U.\xc2\xa0S. 117\n\xe2\x80\x93126 (1945). If an adequate and independent state-law ground bars Foster\xe2\x80\x99s claim, then the Court today has done nothing more than issue an impermissible advisory opinion.

\n

B

\n

To assure itself of jurisdiction, the Court wrongly assumes that the one-line order before us implicates a federal question. See ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. The lurking state-law procedural bar, according to the Court, is not an independent state-law ground because it \xe2\x80\x9cdepends on a federal constitutional ruling.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

I would not so hastily assume that the State Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s unelaborated order depends on the resolution of a federal question without first seeking clarification from the Supreme Court of Georgia. To be sure, we often presume that a \xe2\x80\x9cstate court decide[s] the case the way it did because it believed that federal law required it to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d Long, 463 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 1040\xe2\x80\x931041. But there still exist \xe2\x80\x9ccertain circumstances in which clarification [from the state court] is necessary or desirable\xe2\x80\x9d before delving into the merits of a state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. Id., at 1041, n.\xc2\xa06.

\n

This case presents such a circumstance. The Long presumption assumes that the ambiguous state-court ruling will come in the form of a reasoned decision: It applies in cases in which \xe2\x80\x9cit is not clear from the opinion itself that the state court relied upon an adequate and independent state ground and when it fairly appears that the state court rested its decision primarily on federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1042 (emphasis added). But here, when the decision is a one-line judgment, it hardly makes sense to invoke the Long presumption. There is neither an \xe2\x80\x9copinion\xe2\x80\x9d nor any resolution of federal law that \xe2\x80\x9cfairly appears\xe2\x80\x9d on the face of the unexplained order. Ibid.

\n

Confronted with cases like this in the past, this Court has vacated and remanded for clarification from the state court before proceeding to decide the merits of the underlying claim. I would follow that path instead of assuming that the one-line order implicates a federal question. We have \xe2\x80\x9cdecline[d] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to review the federal questions asserted to be present\xe2\x80\x9d when \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98there is considerable uncertainty as to the precise grounds for the [state court\xe2\x80\x99s] decision.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Bush v. Palm Beach County Canvassing Bd.,\n\n531 U.\xc2\xa0S. 70,\n78 (2000)\n (per curiam) (quoting Minnesota v. National Tea Co.,\n\n309 U.\xc2\xa0S. 551,\n555 (1940)\n). A\xc2\xa0fortiori, when a State\xe2\x80\x99s highest court has denied relief without any explanation, the proper course is to vacate and remand for clarification before reaching the merits of a federal question that might have nothing to do with the state court\xe2\x80\x99s decision. See, e.g., Capital Cities Media, Inc. v. Toole,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 378 (1984)\n (per curiam); see also, e.g., Johnson v. Risk,\n\n137 U.\xc2\xa0S. 300\n\xe2\x80\x93307 (1890). This course respects weighty federalism concerns. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is fundamental that state courts be left free and unfettered by us\xe2\x80\x9d in interpreting their own law, National Tea Co., supra, at 557, especially when a state prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s long-final conviction is at stake.

\n

Clarification is especially warranted here. Nothing in the reported decisions of the Supreme Court of Georgia suggests that federal law figures in how Georgia applies its res judicata procedural bar. Those decisions state that \xe2\x80\x9cnew law or new facts\xe2\x80\x9d could \xe2\x80\x9cjustify the reconsideration of the claims .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. raised on direct appeal,\xe2\x80\x9d Hall v. Lance, 286 Ga. 365, 376\xe2\x80\x93377, 687 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 809, 818 (2010), as might a showing that the prisoner is actually innocent, Walker, supra, at 611, 523 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d, at 327. But it is for the Supreme Court of Georgia\xe2\x80\x94not this Court\xe2\x80\x94to decide what new facts suffice to reopen a claim already decided against a state habeas petitioner. It is up to the Georgia courts, for example, to decide whether a petitioner was diligent in discovering those new facts, see, e.g., Gibson v. Head, 282 Ga. 156, 159, 646 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 257, 260 (2007) (noting that whether a petitioner could overcome the procedural bar \xe2\x80\x9cdepend[ed] on factual findings\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9cthe precise timing of [his] discovery of\xe2\x80\x9d the new evidence), or whether the new facts are \xe2\x80\x9cmaterial,\xe2\x80\x9d Rollf v. Carter, 298 Ga. 557, 558, ___ S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d ___, ___ (2016).

\n

Instead of leaving the application of Georgia law to the Georgia courts, the Court takes it upon itself to decide that the procedural bar implicates a federal question. Worse still, the Court surmises that Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s procedural bar depends on the resolution of a federal question by parsing the wrong court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, the opinion of the Superior Court of Butts County. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. Invoking Ake v. Oklahoma,\n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 68,\n75 (1985)\n, the Court reasons that \xe2\x80\x9cthe state habeas court\xe2\x80\x99s application of res judicata to Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim was not independent of the merits of his federal constitutional challenge.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8. (emphasis added). Accordingly, whether Foster has alleged a sufficient \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98change in the facts\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to overcome the Georgia procedural bar depends on whether Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim would succeed in light of those changed facts. Ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. But the State Superior Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion is not the \xe2\x80\x9cjudgmen[t] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by the highest court of [Georgia] in which a decision could be had\xe2\x80\x9d subject to our certiorari jurisdiction.\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71257. The unexplained denial of relief by the Supreme Court of Georgia is.

\n

I cannot go along with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to assure itself of its jurisdiction by attributing snippets of the State Superior Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning to the Supreme Court of Georgia. The reported decisions of the Supreme Court of Georgia do not resolve what \xe2\x80\x9ctype of new alleged facts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. could ever warrant setting aside the procedural bar,\xe2\x80\x9d Hall, supra, at 377, 687 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d, at 818, let alone intimate that a prisoner may relitigate a claim already decided against him merely because he might win this second time around. Cf. Roulain v. Martin, 266 Ga. 353, 354, 466 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d 837, 839 (1996) (opining that a state habeas court \xe2\x80\x9cwould certainly be bound by the ruling [in the petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s direct appeal] regardless of whether that ruling may be erroneous\xe2\x80\x9d). I therefore refuse to presume that the unexplained denial of relief by the Supreme Court of Georgia presents a federal question.[2]

\n

The Court today imposes an opinion-writing requirement on the States\xe2\x80\x99 highest courts. Lest those high courts be subject to lengthy digressions on constitutional claims that might (or might not) be at issue, they must offer reasoned opinions why\xe2\x80\x94after rejecting the same claim decades ago\xe2\x80\x94they refuse to grant habeas relief now. But \xe2\x80\x9c[o]pinion-writing practices in state courts are influenced by considerations other than avoiding scrutiny by collateral attack in federal court,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9cconcentrat[ing their] resources on the cases where opinions are most needed.\xe2\x80\x9d Harrington v. Richter,\n\n562 U.\xc2\xa0S. 86,\n99 (2011)\n. Rather than demand detailed opinions of overburdened state courts, the Court should vacate and remand cases such as this one to assure itself of its jurisdiction.

\n

II

\n

The Court further errs by deciding that Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim has arguable merit. Because the adjudication of his Batson claim is, at bottom, a credibility determination, we owe \xe2\x80\x9cgreat deference\xe2\x80\x9d to the state court\xe2\x80\x99s initial finding that the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s race-neutral reasons for striking veniremen Eddie Hood and Marilyn Garrett were credible. Batson, 476 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 98, n.\xc2\xa021. On a record far less cold than today\xe2\x80\x99s, the Supreme Court of Georgia long ago (on direct appeal) rejected that claim by giving great deference to the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility determinations. Evaluating the strike of venireman Hood, the court highlighted that his son had been convicted of a misdemeanor and that both his demeanor and religious affiliation indicated that he might be reluctant to impose the death penalty. Foster, 258 Ga., at 738, 374 S.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa02d, at 192. And the prosecution reasonably struck venireman Garrett, according to the court, because it feared that she would sympathize with Foster given her work with \xe2\x80\x9clow-income, underprivileged children\xe2\x80\x9d and because she was \xe2\x80\x9crelated to someone with a drug or alcohol problem.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 739, 374 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 192. That should have been the last word on Foster\xe2\x80\x99s Batson claim.

\n

But now, Foster has access to the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file. By allowing Foster to relitigate his Batson claim by bringing this newly discovered evidence to the fore, the Court upends Batson\xe2\x80\x99s deferential framework. Foster\xe2\x80\x99s new evidence does not justify this Court\xe2\x80\x99s reassessment of who was telling the truth nearly three decades removed from voir\xc2\xa0dire.

\n

A

\n

The new evidence sets the tone for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, but a closer look reveals that it has limited probative value. For this reason, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the prosecution violated Batson rests mostly on arguments at Foster\xe2\x80\x99s disposal decades ago. See ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9316 (concluding that trial transcripts belie proffered reasons for striking Garrett); ante, at 17\xe2\x80\x9322 (relying on transcripts and briefs as evidence of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s shifting explanations for striking Hood). The new evidence is no excuse for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reversal of the state court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility determinations.

\n

As even the Court admits, ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, we do not know who wrote most of the notes that Foster now relies upon as proof of the prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 race-based motivations. We do know, however, that both prosecutors averred that they \xe2\x80\x9cdid not make any of the highlighted marks on the jury venire list\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdid not instruct anyone to make the green highlighted marks.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 168\xe2\x80\x93169, 171. In particular, prosecutor Stephen Lanier reaffirmed his earlier testimony, given during Foster\xe2\x80\x99s hearing for a new trial, that he relied only on race-neutral factors in striking the jury. Id., at 169; see also id., at 80\xe2\x80\x93125. And, prosecutor Douglas Pullen swore that he \xe2\x80\x9cdid not rely on the highlighted jury venire list.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 171.

\n

The hazy recollections of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s investigator, Clayton Lundy, are not to the contrary. As part of the postconviction proceedings, Lundy testified that he \xe2\x80\x9c[v]aguely\xe2\x80\x9d remembered parts of jury selection, he \xe2\x80\x9ckind of remember[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d some of the documents used during jury selection, and cautioned that he \xe2\x80\x9cain\xe2\x80\x99t done this in a long time.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. 181\xe2\x80\x93182. (When Lundy testified in 2006, nearly 20 years had passed since Foster\xe2\x80\x99s trial and he had changed careers. Id., at 174.) He thought others at the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office \xe2\x80\x9cprobably\xe2\x80\x9d passed venire lists around the office and \xe2\x80\x9cguess[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d that everyone would make notations. Id., at 182, 190.

\n

As for the other documents in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file, Lundy could not identify who authored any of them, with two exceptions.[3] First, Lundy said he prepared handwritten lists describing seven veniremen, including Garrett, but her race is not mentioned. See id., at 205; App. 293\xe2\x80\x93294. Second, Lundy \xe2\x80\x9cguess[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d that prosecutor Lanier suggested the handwritten edits to a draft of an affidavit that Lundy later submitted to the trial court. Tr. 203; see App. 343\xe2\x80\x93347 (draft affidavit); id., at 127\xe2\x80\x93129 (final affidavit). The relevant edits suggested deleting two statements that, \xe2\x80\x9csolely [in Lundy\xe2\x80\x99s] opinion,\xe2\x80\x9d prosecutors ought to pick Garrett \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f it comes down to having to pick one of the black jurors.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 345 (emphasis added). Perhaps this look inside the district attorney\xe2\x80\x99s office reveals that the office debated internally who would be the best black juror. Or perhaps it reveals only Lundy\xe2\x80\x99s personal thoughts about selecting black jurors, an \xe2\x80\x9copinion\xe2\x80\x9d with which (we can \xe2\x80\x9cguess\xe2\x80\x9d) Lanier disagreed.

\n

The notion that this \xe2\x80\x9cnewly discovered evidence\xe2\x80\x9d could warrant relitigation of a Batson claim is flabbergasting. In Batson cases, the \xe2\x80\x9cdecisive question will be whether counsel\xe2\x80\x99s race-neutral explanation for a peremptory challenge should be believed.\xe2\x80\x9d Hernandez v. New York,\n\n500 U.\xc2\xa0S. 352,\n365 (1991)\n (plurality opinion). And because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here will seldom be much evidence bearing on that issue,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cthe best evidence often will be the demeanor of the attorney who exercises the challenge.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Time and again, we have said that the credibility of the attorney is best judged by the trial court and can be overturned only if it is clearly erroneous. See ibid.; see also Snyder v. Louisiana,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 472,\n477 (2008)\n; Miller-El, 537 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 339; Hernandez, supra, at 375 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

But the Court today invites state prisoners to go searching for new \xe2\x80\x9cevidence\xe2\x80\x9d by demanding the files of the prosecutors who long ago convicted them. If those prisoners succeed, then apparently this Court\xe2\x80\x99s doors are open to conduct the credibility determination anew. Alas, \xe2\x80\x9cevery end is instead a new beginning\xe2\x80\x9d for a majority of this Court. Welch v. United States, ante, at 15 (Thomas, J., dissenting). I cannot go along with that \xe2\x80\x9csort of sandbagging of state courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Miller-El v. Dretke,\n\n545 U.\xc2\xa0S. 231,\n279 (2005)\n (Thomas, J., dissenting). New evidence should not justify the relitigation of Batson claims.

\n

B

\n

Perhaps the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to reconsider a decades-old Batson claim based on newly discovered evidence would be less alarming if the new evidence revealed that the trial court had misjudged the prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 reasons for striking Garrett and Hood. It does not. Not only is the probative value of the evidence severely limited, supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x9311, but also pieces of the new evidence corroborate the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that the race-neutral reasons were valid. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s substitution of its judgment for the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s credibility determinations is flawed both as a legal and factual matter.

\n

1

\n

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis with respect to Hood is unavailing. The Court first compares Hood with other jurors who had similarly aged children, ante, at 18\xe2\x80\x9319, just as the trial court did decades ago, App. 135\xe2\x80\x93136. The trial court was well aware that Hood\xe2\x80\x99s son\xe2\x80\x99s conviction was for theft, not murder. But in the words of the trial court, \xe2\x80\x9cthe conviction is a distinction that makes the difference\xe2\x80\x9d between Hood and the other jurors, and the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9capprehension that this would tend to, perhaps only subconsciously, make the venireman sympathetic to [Foster] was a rational one.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Because \xe2\x80\x9cthe trial court believe[d] the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s nonracial justification, and that finding is not clearly erroneous, that [should be] the end of the matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Hernandez, supra, at 375 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

The Court also second-guesses the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s strike of Hood because of his questionable stance on the death penalty. The Court concludes that Hood\xe2\x80\x99s transcribed statements at voir dire \xe2\x80\x9cunequivocally voiced [Hood\xe2\x80\x99s] willingness to impose the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 22. There is nothing unequivocal about a decades-old record. Our case law requires the Court to defer to the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that the State\xe2\x80\x99s race-neutral concerns about Hood\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9csoft-spoken[ness] and slow[ness] in responding to the death penalty questions\xe2\x80\x9d were \xe2\x80\x9ccredible.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 138; see Snyder, supra, at 477 (\xe2\x80\x9c[R]ace-neutral reasons for peremptory challenges often invoke a juror\xe2\x80\x99s demeanor (e.g., nervousness, inattention), making the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s firsthand observations of even greater importance\xe2\x80\x9d). The \xe2\x80\x9cevaluation of the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s state of mind based on demeanor and credibility lies peculiarly within a trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s province.\xe2\x80\x9d Hernandez, supra, at 365 (plurality opinion) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

The new evidence, moreover, supports the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s concern about Hood\xe2\x80\x99s views on capital punishment. A handwritten document in the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s file stated that the Church of Christ \xe2\x80\x9cdoesn\xe2\x80\x99t take a stand on [the] Death Penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 302. Perplexingly, the Court considers this proof that the prosecution misled the trial court about its reasons for striking Hood. Ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9321. Hardly. That document further states that capital punishment is an issue \xe2\x80\x9cleft for each individual member,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 302, and thus in no way discredits the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s statement that, in his experience, \xe2\x80\x9cChurch of Christ people, while they may not take a formal stand against the death penalty, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. are very, very reluctant to vote for the death penalty.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 84. And other notes in the file say that Hood gave \xe2\x80\x9cslow D[eath] P[enalty] answers\xe2\x80\x9d and that he \xe2\x80\x9chesitated .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. when asked about [the] D[eath] P[enalty].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 295, 303. This new evidence supports the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s stated reason for striking Hood\xe2\x80\x94that he, as a member of the Church of Christ, had taken an uncertain stance on capital punishment.

\n

2

\n

Likewise, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s evaluation of the strike of Garrett is riddled with error. The Court is vexed by a single misrepresentation about the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s decision to strike Garrett\xe2\x80\x94the prosecution stated that Garrett was listed as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98questionable\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d but the new evidence reveals that Garrett was on the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98definite NO\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d list from the beginning. Ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. But whether the prosecution planned to strike Garrett all along or only at the last minute seems irrelevant to the more than 10 race-neutral reasons the prosecution supplied for striking Garrett.

\n

The prosecution feared that Garrett might sympathize with Foster at sentencing. She worked with disadvantaged children, she was young, and she failed to disclose that her cousin had been recently arrested. See App. 55\xe2\x80\x9357, 105. And prosecutors were concerned that she gave short answers, appeared nervous, and did not ask to be off the jury even though she was a divorced mother of two children and worked more than 70 hours per week. See id., at 55\xe2\x80\x9356, 93\xe2\x80\x9394. The prosecution also stated repeatedly that they were concerned about female jurors, who \xe2\x80\x9cappear to be more sympathetic .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. death penalty case[s] than men.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 42; see id., at 57.[4]

\n

Pieces of the new evidence support some of these concerns. The notes in the prosecutors\xe2\x80\x99 file reveal that someone on the prosecution team was aware that Garrett\xe2\x80\x99s cousin was Angela Garrett (who had been arrested for drug-related charges and fired from her job on the eve of trial, id., at 105, 129), that Garrett \xe2\x80\x9cwould not look a[t] [the] C[our]t during V[oir] D[ire],\xe2\x80\x9d that she gave \xe2\x80\x9cvery short answers,\xe2\x80\x9d and that she \xe2\x80\x9c[l]ooked @ floor during D[eath] P[enalty]\xe2\x80\x9d questioning. Id., at 293, 308.

\n

Nevertheless, the Court frets that these indisputably race-neutral reasons were pretextual. The Court engages in its own comparison of the jurors to highlight the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to strike white jurors with similar characteristics. Ante, at 14\xe2\x80\x9316. But as with venireman Hood, the Georgia courts were faced with the same contentions regarding Garrett decades ago, and the Supreme Court of Georgia rightly decided that the trial court\xe2\x80\x99s findings were worthy of deference. After conducting a post-trial hearing in which one of the prosecutors testified, App. 80\xe2\x80\x93125, the trial court credited the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s concerns. The trial court, for example, agreed that Garrett\xe2\x80\x99s association with Head Start might be troubling and \xe2\x80\x9cbelieve[d] that the state [was] honest in voicing its concern that the combination of holding down two jobs and being the divorced mother of two indicates a less stable home environment,\xe2\x80\x9d which \xe2\x80\x9cwas the prime defense in [Foster\xe2\x80\x99s] case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 142; see id., at 141. Again, that should be \xe2\x80\x9cthe end of the matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Hernandez, 500 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 375 (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment).

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

Today, without first seeking clarification from Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s highest court that it decided a federal question, the Court affords a death-row inmate another opportunity to relitigate his long-final conviction. In few other circumstances could I imagine the Court spilling so much ink over a factbound claim arising from a state postconviction proceeding. It was the trial court that observed the veniremen firsthand and heard them answer the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s questions, and its evaluation of the prosecution\xe2\x80\x99s credibility on this point is certainly far better than this Court\xe2\x80\x99s nearly 30 years later. See Hernandez, supra, at 365 (plurality opinion). I respectfully dissent.
