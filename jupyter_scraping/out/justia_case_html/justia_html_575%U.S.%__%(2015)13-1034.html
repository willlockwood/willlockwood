MELLOULI v. LYNCH, ATTORNEY GENERAL

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

No. 13\xe2\x80\x931034.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 14, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 1, 2015

\n

Petitioner Moones Mellouli, a lawful permanent resident, pleaded guilty to a misdemeanor offense under Kansas law, the possession of drug paraphernalia \xe2\x80\x9cto .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. store [or] conceal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a controlled substance.\xe2\x80\x9d Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa721\xe2\x80\x935709(b)(2). The sole \xe2\x80\x9cparaphernalia\xe2\x80\x9d Mellouli was charged with possessing was a sock in which he had placed four unidentified orange tablets. Citing Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s misdemeanor conviction, an Immigration Judge ordered him deported under\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i), which authorizes the deportation (removal) of an alien \xe2\x80\x9cconvicted of a violation of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any law or regulation of a State, the United States, or a foreign country relating to a controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of Title 21).\xe2\x80\x9d Section 802, in turn, limits the term \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substance\xe2\x80\x9d to a \xe2\x80\x9cdrug or other substance\xe2\x80\x9d included in one of five federal schedules.\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7802(6). Kansas defines \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substance\xe2\x80\x9d as any drug included on its own schedules, without reference to \xc2\xa7802. Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa721\xe2\x80\x935701(a). At the time of Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 schedules included at least nine substances not on the federal lists. The Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA) affirmed Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s deportation order, and the Eighth Circuit denied his petition for review.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s Kansas conviction for concealing unnamed pills in his sock did not trigger removal under \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i). Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The categorical approach historically taken in determining whether a state conviction renders an alien removable looks to the statutory definition of the offense of conviction, not to the particulars of the alien\xe2\x80\x99s conduct. The state conviction triggers removal only if, by definition, the underlying crime falls within a category of removable offenses defined by federal law. The BIA has long applied the categorical approach to assess whether a state drug conviction triggers removal under successive versions of what is now \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i). Matter of Paulus, 11 I. & N. Dec. 274, is illustrative. At the time the BIA decided Paulus, California controlled certain \xe2\x80\x9cnarcotics\xe2\x80\x9d not listed as \xe2\x80\x9cnarcotic drugs\xe2\x80\x9d under federal law. Id., at 275. The BIA concluded that an alien\xe2\x80\x99s California conviction for offering to sell an unidentified \xe2\x80\x9cnarcotic\xe2\x80\x9d was not a deportable offense, for it was possible that the conviction involved a substance controlled only under California, not federal, law. Under the Paulus analysis, Mellouli would not be deportable. The state law involved in Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, like the California statute in Paulus, was not confined to federally controlled substances; it also included substances controlled only under state, not federal, law.

\n

The BIA, however, announced and applied a different approach to drug-paraphernalia offenses (as distinguished from drug possession and distribution offenses) in Matter of Martinez Espinoza, 25 I. & N. Dec. 118. There, the BIA ranked paraphernalia statutes as relating to \xe2\x80\x9cthe drug trade in general,\xe2\x80\x9d reasoning that a paraphernalia conviction \xe2\x80\x9crelates to\xe2\x80\x9d any and all controlled substances, whether or not federally listed, with which the paraphernalia can be used. Id., at 120\xe2\x80\x93121. Under this reasoning, there is no need to show that the type of controlled substance involved in a paraphernalia conviction is one defined in \xc2\xa7802.

\n

The BIA\xe2\x80\x99s disparate approach to drug possession and distribution offenses and paraphernalia possession offenses finds no home in \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i)\xe2\x80\x99s text and \xe2\x80\x9cleads to consequences Congress could not have intended.\xe2\x80\x9d Moncrieffe v. Holder, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. That approach has the anomalous result of treating less grave paraphernalia possession misdemeanors more harshly than drug possession and distribution offenses. The incongruous upshot is that an alien is not removable for possessing a substance controlled only under Kansas law, but he is removable for using a sock to contain that substance. Because it makes scant sense, the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is owed no deference under the doctrine described in Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837\n. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the statute is similarly flawed. The Government argues that aliens who commit any drug crime, not just paraphernalia offenses, in States whose drug schedules substantially overlap the federal schedules are deportable, for \xe2\x80\x9cstate statutes that criminalize hundreds of federally controlled drugs and a handful of similar substances, are laws \xe2\x80\x98relating to\xe2\x80\x99 federally controlled substances.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 17. While the words \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d are broad, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s reading stretches the construction of \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) to the breaking point, reaching state-court convictions, like Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s, in which \xe2\x80\x9c[no] controlled substance (as defined in [\xc2\xa7802])\xe2\x80\x9d figures as an element of the offense. Construction of \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) must be faithful to the text, which limits the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substance,\xe2\x80\x9d for removal purposes, to the substances controlled under \xc2\xa7802. Accordingly, to trigger removal under \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i), the Government must connect an element of the alien\xe2\x80\x99s conviction to a drug \xe2\x80\x9cdefined in [\xc2\xa7802].\xe2\x80\x9d Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

719 F.\xc2\xa03d 995, reversed.

\n

MOONES MELLOULI, PETITIONER v. LORETTA E.LYNCH, ATTORNEY GENERAL

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

[June 1, 2015]

\n

Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

This case requires us to decide how immigration judges should apply a deportation (removal) provision, defined with reference to federal drug laws, to an alien convicted of a state drug-paraphernalia misdemeanor.

\n

Lawful permanent resident Moones Mellouli, in 2010, pleaded guilty to a misdemeanor offense under Kansas law, the possession of drug paraphernalia to \xe2\x80\x9cstore, contain, conceal, inject, ingest, inhale or otherwise introduce a controlled substance into the human body.\xe2\x80\x9d Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa721\xe2\x80\x935709(b)(2) (2013 Cum. Supp.). The sole \xe2\x80\x9cparaphernalia\xe2\x80\x9d Mellouli was charged with possessing was a sock in which he had placed four orange tablets. The criminal charge and plea agreement did not identify the controlled substance involved, but Mellouli had acknowledged, prior to the charge and plea, that the tablets were Adderall. Mellouli was sentenced to a suspended term of 359 days and 12 months\xe2\x80\x99 probation.

\n

In February 2012, several months after Mellouli successfully completed probation, Immigration and Customs Enforcement officers arrested him as deportable under 8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) based on his Kansas misde-meanor conviction. Section 1227(a)(2)(B)(i) authorizes the removal of an alien \xe2\x80\x9cconvicted of a violation of . . . any law or regulation of a State, the United States, or a foreign country relating to a controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of Title 21).\xe2\x80\x9d We hold that Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s Kansas conviction for concealing unnamed pills in his sock didnot trigger removal under \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i). The drug-paraphernalia possession law under which he was convicted, Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa721\xe2\x80\x935709(b), by definition, related to a controlled substance: The Kansas statute made it unlawful \xe2\x80\x9cto use or possess with intent to use any drug paraphernalia to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. store [or] conceal .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. a controlled substance.\xe2\x80\x9d But it was immaterial under that law whether the substance was defined in\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7802. Nor didthe State charge, or seek to prove, that Mellouli possessed a substance on the \xc2\xa7802 schedules. Federal law (\xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i)), therefore, did not authorize Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s removal.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

This case involves the interplay between several federal and state statutes. Section 1227(a)(2)(B)(i), a provision of the Immigration and Nationality Act,\n\n66Stat.\n163, as amended, authorizes the removal of an alien \xe2\x80\x9cconvicted of a violation of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any law or regulation of a State, the United States, or a foreign country relating to a controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of Title 21), other than a single offense involving possession for one\xe2\x80\x99s own use of 30 grams or less of marijuana.\xe2\x80\x9d Section 1227(a)(2)(B)(i) incorporates\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7802, which limits the term \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substance\xe2\x80\x9d to a \xe2\x80\x9cdrug or other substance\xe2\x80\x9d included in one of five federal schedules. \xc2\xa7802(6).

\n

The statute defining the offense to which Mellouli pleaded guilty, Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa721\xe2\x80\x935709(b), proscribes \xe2\x80\x9cpossess[ion] with intent to use any drug paraphernalia to,\xe2\x80\x9d among other things, \xe2\x80\x9cstore\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cconceal\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substance.\xe2\x80\x9d Kansas defines \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substance\xe2\x80\x9d as any drug included on its own schedules, and makes no reference to \xc2\xa7802 or any other federal law. \xc2\xa721\xe2\x80\x935701(a).[1] At the time of Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 schedules included at least nine substances not included in the federal lists. See \xc2\xa765\xe2\x80\x934105(d)(30), (31), (33), (34), (36) (2010 Cum. Supp.); \xc2\xa765\xe2\x80\x934111(g) (2002); \xc2\xa765\xe2\x80\x934113(d)(1), (e), (f\xc2\xa0) (2010 Cum. Supp.); see also Brief for Respondent 9, n.\xc2\xa02.

\n

The question presented is whether a Kansas conviction for using drug paraphernalia to store or conceal a controlled substance, \xc2\xa721\xe2\x80\x935709(b), subjects an alien to deportation under \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i), which applies to an alien \xe2\x80\x9cconvicted of a violation of [a state law] relating to a controlled substance (as defined in [\xc2\xa7802]).\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

B

\n

Mellouli, a citizen of Tunisia, entered the United States on a student visa in 2004. He attended U.\xc2\xa0S. universities, earning a bachelor of arts degree, magna cum laude, as well as master\xe2\x80\x99s degrees in applied mathematics and economics. After completing his education, Mellouli worked as an actuary and taught mathematics at the University of Missouri-Columbia. In 2009, he became a conditional permanent resident and, in 2011, a lawful permanent resident. Since December 2011, Mellouli has been engaged to be married to a U.\xc2\xa0S. citizen.

\n

In 2010, Mellouli was arrested for driving under the influence and driving with a suspended license. During a postarrest search in a Kansas detention facility, deputies discovered four orange tablets hidden in Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s sock. According to a probable-cause affidavit submitted in the state prosecution, Mellouli acknowledged that the tablets were Adderall and that he did not have a prescription for the drugs. Adderall, the brand name of an amphetamine-based drug typically prescribed to treat attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder,[2] is a controlled substance under both federal and Kansas law. See 21 CFR \xc2\xa71308.12(d)(1) (2014) (listing \xe2\x80\x9camphetamine\xe2\x80\x9d and its \xe2\x80\x9csalts\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cisomers\xe2\x80\x9d); Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa765\xe2\x80\x934107(d)(1) (2013 Cum. Supp.) (same). Based on the probable-cause affidavit, a criminal complaint was filed charging Mellouli with trafficking contraband in jail.

\n

Ultimately, Mellouli was charged with only the lesser offense of possessing drug paraphernalia, a misdemeanor. The amended complaint alleged that Mellouli had \xe2\x80\x9cuse[d] or possess[ed] with intent to use drug paraphernalia, to-wit: a sock, to store, contain, conceal, inject, ingest, inhale or otherwise introduce into the human body a controlled substance.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 23. The complaint did not identify the substance contained in the sock. Mellouli pleaded guilty to the paraphernalia possession charge; he also pleaded guilty to driving under the influence. For both offenses, Mellouli was sentenced to a suspended term of 359 days and 12 months\xe2\x80\x99 probation.

\n

In February 2012, several months after Mellouli successfully completed probation, Immigration and Customs Enforcement officers arrested him as deportable under \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) based on his paraphernalia possession conviction. An Immigration Judge ordered Mellouli deported, and the Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA) affirmed the order. Mellouli was deported in 2012.

\n

Under federal law, Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s concealment of controlled-substance tablets in his sock would not have qualified as a drug-paraphernalia offense. Federal law criminalizes the sale of or commerce in drug paraphernalia, but possession alone is not criminalized at all. See\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7863(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b). Nor does federal law define drug paraphernalia to include common household or ready-to-wear items like socks; rather, it defines paraphernalia as any \xe2\x80\x9cequipment, product, or material\xe2\x80\x9d which is \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily intended or designed for use\xe2\x80\x9d in connection with various drug-related activities. \xc2\xa7863(d) (emphasis added). In 19 States as well, the conduct for which Mellouli was convicted\xe2\x80\x94use of a sock to conceal a controlled substance\xe2\x80\x94is not a criminal offense. Brief for National Immigrant Justice Center et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 7. At most, it is a low-level infraction, often not attended by a right to counsel. Id., at 9\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

The Eighth Circuit denied Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s petition for review. 719 F.\xc2\xa03d 995 (2013). We granted certiorari, 573 U.\xc2\xa0S.___ (2014), and now reverse the judgment of the EighthCircuit.

\n

II

\n

We address first the rationale offered by the BIA and affirmed by the Eighth Circuit, which differentiates paraphernalia offenses from possession and distribution offenses. Essential background, in evaluating the rationale shared by the BIA and the Eighth Circuit, is the categorical approach historically taken in determining whether a state conviction renders an alien removable under the immigration statute.[3] Because Congress predicated de-portation \xe2\x80\x9con convictions, not conduct,\xe2\x80\x9d the approach looks to the statutory definition of the offense of conviction, not to the particulars of an alien\xe2\x80\x99s behavior. Das, The Immigration Penalties of Criminal Convictions: Resurrecting Categorical Analysis in Immigration Law, 86 N. Y. U. L. Rev. 1669, 1701, 1746 (2011). The state conviction triggers removal only if, by definition, the underlying crime falls within a category of removable offenses defined by federal law. Ibid. An alien\xe2\x80\x99s actual conduct is irrelevant to the inquiry, as the adjudicator must \xe2\x80\x9cpresume that the conviction rested upon nothing more than the least of the acts criminalized\xe2\x80\x9d under the state statute. Moncrieffe v. Holder, 569 U. S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 5) (internal quotation marks and alterations omitted).[4]

\n

The categorical approach \xe2\x80\x9chas a long pedigree in our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s immigration law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 6). As early as 1913, courts examining the federal immigration statute concluded that Congress, by tying immigration penalties to convictions, intended to \xe2\x80\x9climi[t] the immigration adjudicator\xe2\x80\x99s assessment of a past criminal conviction to a legal analysis of the statutory offense,\xe2\x80\x9d and to disallow \xe2\x80\x9c[examination] of the facts underlying the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Das, supra, at 1688, 1690.

\n

Rooted in Congress\xe2\x80\x99 specification of conviction, not conduct, as the trigger for immigration consequences, the categorical approach is suited to the realities of the system. Asking immigration judges in each case to determine the circumstances underlying a state conviction would burden a system in which \xe2\x80\x9clarge numbers of cases [are resolved by] immigration judges and front-line immigration officers, often years after the convictions.\xe2\x80\x9d Koh, The Whole Better than the Sum: A Case for the Categorical Approach to Determining the Immigration Consequences of Crime, 26 Geo. Immigration L. J. 257, 295 (2012). By focusing on the legal question of what a conviction necessarily established, the categorical approach ordinarily works to promote efficiency, fairness, and predictability in the administration of immigration law. See id., at 295\xe2\x80\x93310; Das, supra, at 1725\xe2\x80\x931742. In particular, the approach enables aliens \xe2\x80\x9cto anticipate the immigration consequences of guilty pleas in criminal court,\xe2\x80\x9d and to enter \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98safe harbor\xe2\x80\x99 guilty pleas [that] do not expose the [alien defendant] to the risk of immigration sanctions.\xe2\x80\x9d Koh, supra, at 307. See Das, supra, at 1737\xe2\x80\x931738.[5]

\n

The categorical approach has been applied routinely to assess whether a state drug conviction triggers removal under the immigration statute. As originally enacted, the removal statute specifically listed covered offenses and covered substances. It made deportable, for example, any alien convicted of \xe2\x80\x9cimport[ing],\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cbuy[ing],\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9csell[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d any \xe2\x80\x9cnarcotic drug,\xe2\x80\x9d defined as \xe2\x80\x9copium, coca leaves, cocaine, or any salt, derivative, or preparation of opium or coca leaves, or cocaine.\xe2\x80\x9d Ch. 202,\n\n42Stat.\n596\xe2\x80\x93597. Over time, Congress amended the statute to include additional offenses and additional narcotic drugs.[6] Ultimately, the Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986 replaced the increasingly long list of controlled substances with the now familiar reference to \xe2\x80\x9ca controlled substance (as defined in [\xc2\xa7802]).\xe2\x80\x9d See \xc2\xa71751,\n\n100Stat.\n3207\xe2\x80\x9347. In interpreting successive versions of the removal statute, the BIA inquired whether the state statute under which the alien was convicted covered federally controlled substances and not others.[7]

\n

Matter of Paulus, 11 I.\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0N. Dec. 274 (1965), is illustrative. At the time the BIA decided Paulus, the immigration statute made deportable any alien who had been \xe2\x80\x9cconvicted of a violation of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any law or regulation relating to the illicit possession of or traffic in narcotic drugs or mari-huana.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 275. California controlled certain \xe2\x80\x9cnarcotics,\xe2\x80\x9d such as peyote, not listed as \xe2\x80\x9cnarcotic drugs\xe2\x80\x9d under federal law. Ibid. The BIA concluded that an alien\xe2\x80\x99s California conviction for offering to sell an unidentified \xe2\x80\x9cnarcotic\xe2\x80\x9d was not a deportable offense, for it was possible that the conviction involved a substance, such as peyote, controlled only under California law. Id., at 275\xe2\x80\x93276. Because the alien\xe2\x80\x99s conviction was not necessarily predicated upon a federally controlled \xe2\x80\x9cnarcotic drug,\xe2\x80\x9d the BIA concluded that the conviction did not establish the alien\xe2\x80\x99s deportability. Id., at 276.

\n

Under the Paulus analysis, adhered to as recently as 2014 in Matter of Ferreira, 26 I. & N. Dec. 415 (BIA 2014),[8] Mellouli would not be deportable. Mellouli pleaded guilty to concealing unnamed pills in his sock. At the time of Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 schedules of controlled substances included at least nine substances\xe2\x80\x94e.g., salvia and jimson weed\xe2\x80\x94not defined in \xc2\xa7802. See Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa765\xe2\x80\x934105(d)(30), (31). The state law involved in Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s conviction, therefore, like the California statute in Paulus, was not confined to federally controlled substances; it required no proof by the prosecutor that Mellouli used his sock to conceal a substance listed under \xc2\xa7802, as opposed to a substance controlled only under Kansas law. Under the categorical approach applied in Paulus, Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s drug-paraphernalia conviction does not render him deportable. In short, the state law under which he was charged categorically \xe2\x80\x9crelat[ed] to a controlled substance,\xe2\x80\x9d but was not limited to substances \xe2\x80\x9cdefined in [\xc2\xa7802].\xe2\x80\x9d[9]

\n

The BIA, however, announced and applied a different approach to drug-paraphernalia offenses (as distinguished from drug possession and distribution offenses) in Matter of Martinez Espinoza, 25 I. & N. Dec. 118 (2009). There, the BIA ranked paraphernalia statutes as relating to \xe2\x80\x9cthe drug trade in general.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 121. The BIA rejected the argument that a paraphernalia conviction should not count at all because it targeted implements, not controlled substances. Id., at 120. It then reasoned that a paraphernalia conviction \xe2\x80\x9crelates to\xe2\x80\x9d any and all controlled substances, whether or not federally listed, with which the paraphernalia can be used. Id., at 121. Under this reasoning, there is no need to show that the type of controlled substance involved in a paraphernalia conviction is one defined in \xc2\xa7802.

\n

The Immigration Judge in this case relied upon Martinez Espinoza in ordering Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s removal, quoting that decision for the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the requirement of a correspondence between the Federal and State controlled substance schedules, embraced by Matter of Paulus . . . has never been extended\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to paraphernalia offenses. App. to Pet. for Cert. 32 (quoting Martinez Espinoza, 25 I.\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0N. Dec., at 121). The BIA affirmed, reasoning that Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s conviction for possession of drug paraphernalia \xe2\x80\x9cinvolves drug trade in general and, thus, is covered under [\xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i)].\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 18. Denying Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s petition for review, the Eighth Circuit deferred to the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Martinez Espinoza, and held that a Kansas paraphernalia conviction \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98relates to\xe2\x80\x99 a federal controlled substance because it is a crime .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x98associated with the drug trade in general.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 719 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1000.

\n

The disparate approach to state drug convictions, devised by the BIA and applied by the Eighth Circuit, finds no home in the text of \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i). The approach, moreover, \xe2\x80\x9cleads to consequences Congress could not have intended.\xe2\x80\x9d Moncrieffe, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 15). Statutes should be interpreted \xe2\x80\x9cas a symmetrical and coherent regulatory scheme.\xe2\x80\x9d FDA v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp.,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 120,\n133 (2000)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). The BIA, however, has adopted conflicting positions on the meaning of \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i), distinguishing drug possession and distribution offenses from offenses involving the drug trade in general, with the anomalous result that minor paraphernalia possession offenses are treated more harshly than drug possession and distribution offenses. Drug possession and distribution convictions trigger removal only if they necessarily involve a federally controlled substance, see Paulus, 11 I.\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0N. Dec. 274, while convictions for paraphernalia possession, an offense less grave than drug possession and distribution, trigger removal whether or not they necessarily implicate a federally controlled substance, see Martinez Espinoza, 25 I. & N. Dec. 118. The incongruous upshot is that an alien is not removable for possessing a substance controlled only under Kansas law, but he is removable for using a sock to contain that substance. Because it makes scant sense, the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, we hold, is owed no deference under the doctrine described in Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,\n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837,\n843 (1984)\n.

\n

III

\n

Offering an addition to the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s rationale, the Eighth Circuit reasoned that a state paraphernalia possession conviction categorically relates to a federally controlled substance so long as there is \xe2\x80\x9cnearly a complete overlap\xe2\x80\x9d between the drugs controlled under state and federal law. 719 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1000.[10] The Eighth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s analysis, however, scarcely explains or ameliorates the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s anomalous separation of paraphernalia possession offenses from drug possession and distribution offenses.

\n

Apparently recognizing this problem, the Government urges, as does the dissent, that the overlap between state and federal drug schedules supports the removal of aliens convicted of any drug crime, not just paraphernalia of-fenses. As noted, \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) authorizes the removal of any alien \xe2\x80\x9cconvicted of a violation of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any law or reg-ulation of a State, the United States, or a foreigncountry relating to a controlled substance (as defined in [\xc2\xa7802]).\xe2\x80\x9d According to the Government, the words \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d modify \xe2\x80\x9claw or regulation,\xe2\x80\x9d rather than \xe2\x80\x9cviolation.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 25\xe2\x80\x9326 (a limiting phrase ordinarily modifies the last antecedent). Therefore, the Government argues, aliens who commit \xe2\x80\x9cdrug crimes\xe2\x80\x9d in States whose drug schedules substantially overlap the federal schedules are removable, for \xe2\x80\x9cstate statutes that criminalize hundreds of federally controlled drugs and a handful of similar substances, are laws \xe2\x80\x98relating to\xe2\x80\x99 federally controlled substances.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 17.

\n

We do not gainsay that, as the Government urges, the last reasonable referent of \xe2\x80\x9crelating to,\xe2\x80\x9d as those words appear in \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i), is \xe2\x80\x9claw or regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d The removal provision is thus satisfied when the elements that make up the state crime of conviction relate to a federally controlled substance. As this case illustrates, however, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s construction of the federal removal statute stretches to the breaking point, reaching state-court convictions, like Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s, in which \xe2\x80\x9c[no] controlled substance (as defined in [\xc2\xa7802])\xe2\x80\x9d figures as an element of the offense. We recognize, too, that the \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) words to which the dissent attaches great weight, i.e., \xe2\x80\x9crelating to,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 2\xe2\x80\x933, are \xe2\x80\x9cbroad\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cindeterminate.\xe2\x80\x9d Maracich v. Spears, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 9) (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted).[11] As we cautioned in New York State Conference of Blue Cross & Blue Shield Plans v. Travelers Ins. Co.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 645,\n655 (1995)\n, those words, \xe2\x80\x9cextend[ed] to the furthest stretch of [their] indeterminacy, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. stop nowhere.\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c[C]ontext,\xe2\x80\x9d therefore, may \xe2\x80\x9ctu[g] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in favor of a narrower reading.\xe2\x80\x9d Yates v. United States, 574 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2015) (slip op., at 10). Context does so here.

\n

The historical background of \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) demonstrates that Congress and the BIA have long required a direct link between an alien\xe2\x80\x99s crime of conviction and a particular federally controlled drug. Supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s position here severs that link by authorizing deportation any time the state statute of conviction bears some general relation to federally controlled drugs. The Government offers no cogent reason why its position is limited to state drug schedules that have a \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial overlap\xe2\x80\x9d with the federal schedules. Brief for Respondent 31. A statute with any overlap would seem to be related to federally controlled drugs. Indeed, the Government\xe2\x80\x99s position might well encompass convictions for offenses related to drug activity more generally, such as gun possession, even if those convictions do not actually involve drugs (let alone federally controlled drugs). The Solicitor General, while resisting this particular example, acknowledged that convictions under statutes \xe2\x80\x9cthat have some connection to drugs indirectly\xe2\x80\x9d might fall within \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i). Tr. of Oral Arg. 36. This sweeping interpretation departs so sharply from the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text and history that it cannot be considered a permissible reading.

\n

In sum, construction of \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) must be faithful to the text, which limits the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substance,\xe2\x80\x9d for removal purposes, to the substances controlled under \xc2\xa7802. We therefore reject the argument that any drug offense renders an alien removable, without regard to the appearance of the drug on a \xc2\xa7802 schedule. Instead, to trigger removal under \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i), the Government must connect an element of the alien\xe2\x80\x99s conviction to a drug \xe2\x80\x9cdefined in [\xc2\xa7802].\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

For the reasons stated, the judgment of the U.\xc2\xa0S. Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit is reversed.

\n

It is so ordered.

MOONES MELLOULI, PETITIONER v. LORETTA E. LYNCH, ATTORNEY GENERAL

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eighth circuit

\n

[June 1, 2015]

\n

Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Alito joins, dissenting.

\n

The Court reverses the decision of the United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit on the ground that it misapplied the federal removal statute. It rejects the Government\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of that statute, which would supply an alternative ground for affirmance. Yet it offers no interpretation of its own. Lower courts are thus left to guess which convictions qualify an alien for removal under\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i), and the majority has deprived them of their only guide: the statutory text itself. Because the statute renders an alien removable whenever he is convicted of violating a law \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d a federally controlled substance, I would affirm.

\n

I

\n

With one exception not applicable here, \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) makes removable \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny alien who at any time after admission has been convicted of a violation of (or a conspir-acy or attempt to violate) any law or regulation of a State, the United States, or a foreign country relating to a controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of title 21).\xe2\x80\x9d I would hold, consistent with the text, that the provision requires that the conviction arise under a \xe2\x80\x9claw or regulation of a State, the United States, or a foreign country relating to a controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of title 21).\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, Mellouli was properly subject to removal if the Kansas statute of conviction \xe2\x80\x9crelat[es] toa controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of title 21),\xe2\x80\x9d regardless of whether his particular conduct would also have subjected him to prosecution under federal controlled-substances laws. See ante, at 6 (\xe2\x80\x9cAn alien\xe2\x80\x99s actual conduct is irrelevant to the inquiry\xe2\x80\x9d). The majority\xe2\x80\x99s 12 references to the sock that Mellouli used to conceal the pills are thus entirely beside the point.[1]

\n

The critical question, which the majority does not di-rectly answer, is what it means for a law or regulation to \xe2\x80\x9crelat[e] to a controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of title 21).\xe2\x80\x9d At a minimum, we know that this phrase does not require a complete overlap between the substances controlled under the state law and those controlled un-der\n\n21 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7802. To \xe2\x80\x9crelate to\xe2\x80\x9d means \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to stand in some relation; to have bearing or concern; to pertain; refer; to bring into association with or connection with.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Morales v. Trans World Airlines, Inc.,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 374,\n383 (1992)\n (quoting Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1158 (5th ed. 1979)). In ordinary parlance, one thing can \xe2\x80\x9crelate to\xe2\x80\x9d another even if it also relates to other things. As ordinar-ily understood, therefore, a state law regulating various controlled substances may \xe2\x80\x9crelat[e] to a controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of title 21)\xe2\x80\x9d even if the statute also controls a few substances that do not fall within the federal definition.

\n

The structure of the removal statute confirms this interpretation. Phrases like \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d have broad but indeterminate meanings that must be understood in the context of \xe2\x80\x9cthe structure of the statute and its other provisions.\xe2\x80\x9d Maracich v. Spears, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2013) (slip op., at 9) (\xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d); see also New York State Conference of Blue Cross & Blue Shield Plans v. Travelers Ins. Co.,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 645,\n655 (1995)\n (\xe2\x80\x9crelate to\xe2\x80\x9d); see generally California Div. of Labor Standards Enforcement v. Dillingham Constr., N. A., Inc.,\n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 316,\n324 (1997)\n (describing the Court\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to interpret the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98clearly expansive\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9crelate to\xe2\x80\x9d language in the pre-emption provision of the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974). In interpreting such phrases, we must be careful to honor Congress\xe2\x80\x99 choice to use expansive language. Maracich, supra, at ___ (Ginsburg, J., dissenting) (slip op., at 7) (noting that a statute should be interpreted broadly in light of Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to use sweeping language like \xe2\x80\x9cin connection with\xe2\x80\x9d); see also, e.g., Alaska Dept. of Environmental Conservation v. EPA,\n\n540 U.\xc2\xa0S. 461,\n484 (2004)\n (Ginsburg, J.) (interpreting Environmental Protection Agency\xe2\x80\x99s authority in light of the \xe2\x80\x9cnotably capacious terms\xe2\x80\x9d contained in its authorizing statute).

\n

Here, the \xe2\x80\x9cstructure of the statute and its other provisions\xe2\x80\x9d indicate that Congress understood this phrase to sweep quite broadly. Several surrounding subsections of the removal statute reveal that when Congress wanted to define with greater specificity the conduct that subjects an alien to removal, it did so by omitting the expansive phrase \xe2\x80\x9crelating to.\xe2\x80\x9d For example, a neighboring provision makes removable \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny alien who .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is convicted un-der any law of purchasing, selling, offering for sale, exchanging, using, owning, possessing, or carrying .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any weapon, part, or accessory which is a firearm or destructive device (as defined in section 921(a) of title 18).\xe2\x80\x9d 8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(C) (emphasis added). This language explicitly requires that the object of the offense fit within a federal definition. Other provisions adopt similar requirements. See, e.g., \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(E)(i) (making removable \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny alien who .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is convicted of a crime of domestic violence,\xe2\x80\x9d where \xe2\x80\x9cthe term \xe2\x80\x98crime of domestic violence\xe2\x80\x99 means any crime of violence (as defined in section 16 of title 18) .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. committed by\xe2\x80\x9d a person with a specified family relationship with the victim); see generally \xc2\xa71101(a)(43) (defining certain aggravated felonies using federal definitions as elements). That Congress, in this provision, required only that a law relate to a federally controlled substance, as opposed to involve such a substance, suggests that it understood \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d as having its ordinary and expansive meaning. See, e.g., Russello v. United States,\n\n464 U.\xc2\xa0S. 16,\n23 (1983)\n.

\n

Applying this interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9crelating to,\xe2\x80\x9d a conviction under Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 drug paraphernalia statute qualifies as a predicate offense under \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i). That state statute prohibits the possession or use of drug paraphernalia to \xe2\x80\x9cstore, contain, conceal, inject, ingest, inhale or otherwise introduce a controlled substance into the human body.\xe2\x80\x9d Kan. Stat. Ann. \xc2\xa721\xe2\x80\x935709(b)(2) (2013 Cum. Supp.). And, as used in this statute, a \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substance\xe2\x80\x9d is a substance that appears on Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 schedules, \xc2\xa721\xe2\x80\x935701(a), which in turn consist principally of federally controlled substances. Ante, at 3; see also Brief for Petitioner 3 (listing nine substances on Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 schedules that were not on the federal schedules at the time of Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s arrest); Brief for Respondent 8 (noting that, at the time of Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s arrest, more than 97 percent of the named substances on Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 schedules were federally controlled). The law certainly \xe2\x80\x9crelat[es] to a controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of title 21)\xe2\x80\x9d because it prohibits conduct involving controlled substances falling within the federal definition in \xc2\xa7802.

\n

True, approximately three percent of the substances appearing on Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 lists of \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substances\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s conviction did not fall within the federal definition, ante, at 3, meaning that an individual convicted of possessing paraphernalia may never have used his paraphernalia with a federally controlled substance. But that fact does not destroy the relationship between the law and federally controlled substances. Mellouli was convicted for violating a state law \xe2\x80\x9crelating to a controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of title 21),\xe2\x80\x9d so he was properly removed under\n\n8 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

The majority rejects this straightforward interpretation because it \xe2\x80\x9creach[es] state-court convictions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in which \xe2\x80\x98[no] controlled substance (as defined in [\xc2\xa7802])\xe2\x80\x99 figures as an element of the offense.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. This assumes the answer to the question at the heart of this case: whether the removal statute does in fact reach such convictions. To answer that question by assuming the answer iscircular.

\n

The majority hints that some more limited definition of \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d is suggested by context. See ibid. I wholeheartedly agree that we must look to context to understand indeterminate terms like \xe2\x80\x9crelating to,\xe2\x80\x9d which is why I look to surrounding provisions of the removal statute. These \xe2\x80\x9creveal that when Congress wanted to define with greater specificity the conduct that subjects an alien to removal, it did so by omitting the expansive phrase \xe2\x80\x98relating to.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Supra, at 3. For its part, the majority looks to the context of other provisions referring to \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substances\xe2\x80\x9d without a definitional parenthetical, ante, at 13, n.\xc2\xa011, and rejoins that the most natural reading of the statute \xe2\x80\x9cshrinks to the vanishing point the words \xe2\x80\x98as defined in [\xc2\xa7802],\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310, n.\xc2\xa09. But the definition of controlled substances does play a role in my interpretation, by requiring that the law bear some relationship to federally controlled substances. Although we need not establish the precise boundaries of that relationship in this case given that Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 paraphernalia law clearly qualifies under any reasonable definition of \xe2\x80\x9crelating to,\xe2\x80\x9d the definition of controlled substances imposes a meaningful limit on the statutes that qualify.

\n

B

\n

The majority appears to conclude that a statute \xe2\x80\x9crelates to\xe2\x80\x9d a federally controlled substance if its \xe2\x80\x9cdefinition of the offense of conviction\xe2\x80\x9d necessarily includes as an element of that offense a federally controlled substance. Ante, at 6. The text will not bear this meaning.

\n

The first problem with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is that it converts a removal provision expressly keyed to features of the statute itself into one keyed to features of the underlying generic offense. To understand the difference, one need look no further than this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Moncrieffe v. Holder, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013). In that case, removal was predicated on the generic offense of \xe2\x80\x9cillicit trafficking in a controlled substance.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at ___ (slip op., at 2). Thus, in order to satisfy the federal criteria, it was necessary for the state offense at issue to have as elements the same elements that make up that generic offense. Id., at ___ (slip op., at 5). By contrast, \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) does not refer to a generic offense for which we must discern the relevant criteria from its nature. Instead, it establishes the relevant criteria explicitly, and does so for the lawof conviction itself rather than for some underlying generic offense\xe2\x80\x94that is, the law of conviction must \xe2\x80\x9crelat[e] to\xe2\x80\x9d a federally controlled substance.

\n

The only plausible way of reading the text here to refer to a generic offense that has as one element the involve-ment of a federally controlled substance would be to read \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d as modifying \xe2\x80\x9cviolation\xe2\x80\x9d instead of \xe2\x80\x9claw.\xe2\x80\x9d Under that reading, the statute would attach immigration consequences to a \xe2\x80\x9cviolation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. relating to a controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of title 21),\xe2\x80\x9d rather than a violation of a \xe2\x80\x9claw .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. relating to a controlled substance (as defined in section 802 of title 21).\xe2\x80\x9d Yet the majority expressly\xe2\x80\x94and correctly\xe2\x80\x94rejects as grammatically incorrect Mellouli\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d clause modifies \xe2\x80\x9cviolation.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12.

\n

Having done so, the majority can reconcile its outcome with the text only by interpreting the words \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cregulating only.\xe2\x80\x9d It should be obvious why the majority does not make this argument explicit. Even assuming \xe2\x80\x9cregulating only\xe2\x80\x9d were a permissible interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9crelating to\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94for it certainly is not the most natural one\xe2\x80\x94that interpretation would be foreclosed by Congress\xe2\x80\x99 pointed word choice in the surrounding provisions. And given the logical upshot of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, it is it even more understandable that it avoids offering an explicit exegesis. For unless the Court ultimately adopts the modified categorical approach for statutes, like the one at issue here, that define offenses with reference to \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substances\xe2\x80\x9d generally, and treats them as divisible by each separately listed substance, ante, at 6, n.\xc2\xa04, its interpretation would mean that no conviction under a controlled-substances regime more expansive than the Federal Government\xe2\x80\x99s would trigger removal.[2] Thus, whenever a State moves first in subjecting some newly discovered drug to regulation, every alien convicted during the lag between state and federal regulation would be immunized from the immigration consequences of his conduct. Cf. Brief for Respondent 10 (explaining that two of the nine nonfederally controlled substances on Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 schedules at the time Mellouli was arrested became federally controlled within a year of his arrest). And the Government could never, under \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i), remove an alien convicted of violating the controlled-substances law of a State that defines \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled substances\xe2\x80\x9d with reference to a list containing even one substance that does not appear on the federal schedules.

\n

Finding no support for its position in the text, the majority relies on the historical background, ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314, and especially the Board of Immigration Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 (BIA) decision in Matter of Paulus, 11 I.\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0N. Dec. 274 (1965)\xe2\x80\x94a surprising choice, given that the majority concludes its discussion of that history by acknowledging that the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s atextual approach to the statute makes \xe2\x80\x9cscant sense,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11. To the extent that the BIA\xe2\x80\x99s approach to \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) and its predecessors is consistent with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s, it suffers from the same flaw: It fails to account for the text of the removal provision because it looks at whether the conviction itself necessarily involved a substance regulated under federal law, not at whether the statute related to one. See Paulus, 11 I.\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0N. Dec., at 276 (\xe2\x80\x9c[O]nly a conviction for illicit possession of or traffic in a substance which is defined as a narcotic drug under federal laws can be the basis for deportation\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); Matter of Ferreira, 26 I.\xc2\xa0&\xc2\xa0N. Dec. 415, 418\xe2\x80\x93419 (BIA 2014) (modeling its categorical approach to \xc2\xa71227(a)(2)(B)(i) after the analysis in Moncrieffe, which, as explained above, keyed removal to the characteristics of the offense).

\n

Section 1227(a)(2)(B)(i) requires only that the state law itself, not the \xe2\x80\x9cgeneric\xe2\x80\x9d offense defined by the law, \xe2\x80\x9crelat[e] to\xe2\x80\x9d a federally controlled substance. The majority has not offered a textual argument capable of supporting a different conclusion.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

The statutory text resolves this case. True, faithfully applying that text means that an alien may be deported for committing an offense that does not involve a federally controlled substance. Nothing about that consequence, however, is so outlandish as to call this application into doubt. An alien may be removed only if he is convicted of violating a law, and I see nothing absurd about removing individuals who are unwilling to respect the drug laws of the jurisdiction in which they find themselves.

\n

The majority thinks differently, rejecting the only plausible reading of this provision and adopting an interpretation that finds no purchase in the text. I fail to understand why it chooses to do so, apart from a gut instinct that an educated professional engaged to an American citizen should not be removed for concealing unspecified orange tablets in his sock. Or perhaps the majority just disapproves of the fact that Kansas, exercising its police powers, has decided to criminalize conduct that Congress, exercising its limited powers, has decided not to criminalize, ante, at 4\xe2\x80\x935. Either way, that is not how we should go about interpreting statutes, and I respectfully dissent.
