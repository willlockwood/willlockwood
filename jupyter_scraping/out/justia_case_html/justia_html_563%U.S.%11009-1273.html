ASTRA USA, INC., et\xc2\xa0al. v. SANTA CLARA COUNTY, CALIFORNIA

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x931273.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 19, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 29, 2011

\n

Section 340B of the Public Health Services Act imposes ceilings on prices drug manufacturers may charge for medications sold to specified health care facilities (340B or covered entities), dominantly, local providers of medical care for the poor. The \xc2\xa7340B ceiling-price program (340B Program) is superintended by the Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA), part of the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS). It is tied to the earlier-enacted, much larger Medicaid Drug Rebate Program, under which manufacturers gain Medicaid coverage for their drugs. To qualify for participation in this program, a manufacturer must enter into a standardized agreement with HHS undertaking to provide rebates to States on their Medicaid drug purchases. The amount of the rebates depends on a manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9caverage\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cbest\xe2\x80\x9d prices, as defined by legislation and regulation. The 340B Program, like the Medicaid Rebate Program, uses a form contract as an opt-in mechanism. The 340B Program also draws on the larger scheme\xe2\x80\x99s pricing methodology. In the 340B Program\xe2\x80\x99s contract, called the Pharmaceutical Pricing Agreement (PPA), manufacturers agree to charge covered entities no more than predetermined ceiling prices, derived from the \xe2\x80\x9caverage\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cbest\xe2\x80\x9d prices and rebates calculated under the Medicaid Rebate Program.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0HRSA may require a manufacturer who overcharges a covered entity to reimburse that entity. HRSA may also terminate the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s PPA, which terminates as well the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility for Medicaid coverage of its drugs. Currently, HRSA handles overcharge complaints through informal procedures, but the 2010 Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (PPACA) directs the Secretary to develop formal procedures. Once those procedures are in place, HRSA will reach an \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative resolution,\xe2\x80\x9d which will be subject to judicial review under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA). In addition to authorizing compensation awards to overcharged entities, the PPACA provides for the imposition of monetary penalties payable to the Government.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Santa Clara County (County), operator of several 340B entities, filed suit against Astra and eight other pharmaceutical companies, alleging that they were overcharging 340B entities in violation of the PPAs. Asserting that 340B entities are the PPAs\xe2\x80\x99 intended beneficiaries, the County sought compensatory damages for breach of contract. The District Court dismissed the complaint, concluding that the PPAs conferred no enforceable rights on 340B entities. Reversing, the Ninth Circuit held that, while 340B entities have no right to sue under the statute, they could proceed against drug manufacturers as third-party beneficiaries of the PPAs.

\n

Held: Suits by 340B entities to enforce ceiling-price contracts running between drug manufacturers and the Secretary of HHS are incompatible with the statutory regime. As the County has conceded, covered entities have no right of action under \xc2\xa7340B itself. Congress vested authority to oversee compliance with the 340B Program in HHS and assigned no auxiliary enforcement role to covered entities. Nonetheless, the County maintains that the PPAs are contracts enforceable by covered entities as third-party beneficiaries. This argument overlooks that the PPAs simply incorporate statutory obligations and record the manufacturers\xe2\x80\x99 agreement to abide by them. The agreements have no negotiable terms. Like the Medicaid Rebate Program agreements, the PPAs provide the means by which drug manufacturers opt into the statutory scheme. A third-party suit to enforce an HHS-drug manufacturer agreement, therefore, is in essence a suit to enforce the statute itself. Telling in this regard, the County based its suit on allegations that the manufacturers charged more than the \xc2\xa7340B ceiling price, not that they violated an independent substantive obligation arising from the PPAs.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit reasoned that suits like the County\xe2\x80\x99s would spread the enforcement burden instead of placing it entirely on the Government. But spreading the enforcement burden is hardly what Congress contemplated when it made HHS administrator of the interdependent Medicaid Rebate Program and 340B Program. Suits by 340B entities would undermine the agency\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to administer these two programs harmoniously and uniformly. Notably, the Medicaid Rebate Program\xe2\x80\x99s statute prohibits HHS from disclosing pricing information that could reveal the prices a manufacturer charges for its drugs. Had Congress meant to leave open the prospect of third-party beneficiary suits by 340B entities, it likely would not have barred them from obtaining the very information necessary to determine whether their asserted rights have been violated.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit noted that HHS\xe2\x80\x99s Office of the Inspector General has reported on HRSA\xe2\x80\x99s inadequate enforcement authority. But Congress did not respond to the reports of lax enforcement by inviting 340B entities to launch lawsuits. Instead, Congress opted to strengthen and formalize HRSA\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement authority, to make the new adjudicative framework the proper remedy for covered entities\xe2\x80\x99 complaints, and to render the agency\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of those complaints binding, subject to judicial review under the APA. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

588 F.\xc2\xa03d 1237, reversed.

ASTRA USA, INC., et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. SANTA CLARA COUNTY, CALIFORNIA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[March 29, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Ginsburg delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 340B of the Public Health Services Act, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7256b (Oct. 2010 Supp.), imposes ceilings on prices drug manufacturers may charge for medications sold to specified health care facilities. Those facilities, here called \xe2\x80\x9c340B\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccovered\xe2\x80\x9d entities, include public hospitals and community health centers, many of them providers of safety-net services to the poor. The \xc2\xa7340B ceiling-price program (340B Program) is superintended by the Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA), a unit of the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS). Drug manufacturers opt into the 340B Program by signing a form Pharmaceutical Pricing Agreement (PPA) used nationwide. PPAs are not transactional, bargained-for contracts. They are uniform agreements that recite the responsibilities \xc2\xa7340B imposes, respectively, on drug manufacturers and the Secretary of HHS. Manufacturers\xe2\x80\x99 eligibility to participate in state Medicaid programs is conditioned on their entry into PPAs for covered drugs purchased by 340B entities.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is conceded that Congress authorized no private right of action under \xc2\xa7340B for covered entities who claim they have been charged prices exceeding the statutory ceiling. This case presents the question whether 340B entities, though accorded no right to sue for overcharges under the statute itself, may nonetheless sue allegedly overcharging manufacturers as third-party beneficiaries of the PPAs to which the manufacturers subscribed. We hold that suits by 340B entities to enforce ceiling-price contracts running between drug manufacturers and the Secretary of HHS are incompatible with the statutory regime.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress placed the Secretary (acting through her designate, HRSA) in control of \xc2\xa7340B\xe2\x80\x99s drug-price prescriptions. That control could not be maintained were potentially thousands of covered entities permitted to bring suits alleging errors in manufacturers\xe2\x80\x99 price calculations. If 340B entities may not sue under the statute, it would make scant sense to allow them to sue on a form contract implementing the statute, setting out terms identical to those contained in the statute. Though labeled differently, suits to enforce \xc2\xa7340B and suits to enforce PPAs are in substance one and the same. Their treatment, therefore, must be the same, \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o matter the clothing in which [340B entities] dress their claims.\xe2\x80\x9d Tenet v. Doe, 544 U. S. 1, 8 (2005).

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The 340B Program is tied to the earlier-enacted, much larger Medicaid Drug Rebate Program. Adopted by Congress in 1990, the Medicaid Rebate Program covers a significant portion of drug purchases in the United States. See GAO, J. Dicken, Prescription Drugs: Oversight of Drug Pricing in Federal Programs 1 (GAO\xe2\x80\x9307\xe2\x80\x93481T, 2007) (testimony before the Committee on Oversight and Government Reform, House of Representatives).[Footnote 1] To gain payment under Medicaid for covered drugs, a manufacturer must enter a standardized agreement with HHS; in the agreement, the manufacturer undertakes to provide rebates to States on their Medicaid drug purchases. 104 Stat. 1388\xe2\x80\x93143, as amended, 124 Stat. 3290, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa71396r\xe2\x80\x938(a). The amount of the rebates depends on the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9caverage\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cbest\xe2\x80\x9d prices, as defined by legislation and regulation. \xc2\xa71396r\xe2\x80\x938(c), (k).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Calculation of a manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9caverage\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cbest\xe2\x80\x9d prices, undertaken by the pharmaceutical company, is a complex enterprise requiring recourse to detailed information about the company\xe2\x80\x99s sales and pricing. \xc2\xa71396r\xe2\x80\x938(k); 42 CFR \xc2\xa7447.500\xe2\x80\x93520 (2010). To enable HHS to calcu-\nlate the rebate rate for each drug, manufacturers submit the relevant data to HHS on a quarterly basis. \xc2\xa71396r\xe2\x80\x938(b)(3). With exceptions set out in the legislation, HHS is prohibited from disclosing the submitted information \xe2\x80\x9cin a form which discloses the identity of a specific manufacturer \xe2\x80\xa6 [or] prices charged for drugs by such manu-facturer.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71396r\xe2\x80\x938(b)(3)(D).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under \xc2\xa7340B, added in 1992, 106 Stat. 4967, as amended, 124 Stat. 823, manufacturers participating in Medicaid must offer discounted drugs to covered entities, dominantly, local facilities that provide medical care for the poor. See \xc2\xa7256b(a); \xc2\xa71396r\xe2\x80\x938(a)(1). The 340B Program, like the Medicaid Drug Rebate Program, employs a form contract as an opt-in mechanism. The 340B Program also draws on the larger scheme\xe2\x80\x99s pricing methodology. In their 340B Program contracts with HHS, called Pharmaceutical Pricing Agreements (PPAs), see supra, at 1, manufacturers agree to charge covered entities no more than predetermined ceiling prices, derived from the \xe2\x80\x9caverage\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cbest\xe2\x80\x9d prices and rebates calculated under the Medicaid Drug Rebate Program. \xc2\xa7256b(a)(1); see App. to Pet. for Cert. 165a\xe2\x80\x93171a (PPA \xc2\xa7I\xe2\x80\x93II).[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If a manufacturer overcharges a covered entity, HRSA may require the manufacturer to reimburse the covered entity; HRSA may also terminate the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s PPA, \xc2\xa71396r\xe2\x80\x938(b)(4)(B)(i), (v); App. to Pet. for Cert. 174a (PPA \xc2\xa7IV(c)), which terminates as well the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility for Medicaid coverage of its drugs, \xc2\xa71396r\xe2\x80\x938(a)(1), (5). Currently, HRSA handles overcharge complaints through informal procedures. Manufacturer Audit Guidelines and Dispute Resolution Process, 61 Fed. Reg. 65412 (1996). The 2010 Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (PPACA), Pub. L. 111\xe2\x80\x93148, 124 Stat. 119, provides for more rigorous enforcement. The PPACA directs the Secretary to develop formal procedures for resolving overcharge claims. Id., at 826, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7256b(d)\n(3)(A). Under those procedures, which are not yet in place, HRSA will reach an \xe2\x80\x9cadministrative resolution\xe2\x80\x9d that is subject to judicial review under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA), 5 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7701 et seq. See 124 Stat. 827, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7256b(d)(3)(C). In addition to authorizing compensation awards to overcharged entities, the PPACA provides for the imposition of monetary penalties pay-\nable to the Government. Id., at 824\xe2\x80\x93825, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7256b(d)(1)(B)(ii), (vi).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent Santa Clara County (County), operator of several 340B entities, commenced suit against Astra and eight other pharmaceutical companies, alleging that the companies were overcharging 340B health care facilities in violation of the PPAs to which the companies subscribed. The County styled its suit a class action on behalf of both 340B entities in California and the counties that fund those entities. Asserting that the 340B entities and the counties that fund them are the intended beneficiaries of the PPAs, the County sought compensatory damages for the pharmaceutical companies\xe2\x80\x99 breach of contract.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court dismissed the complaint, concluding that the PPAs conferred no enforceable rights on 340B entities. Reversing the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s judgment, the Ninth Circuit held that covered entities, although they have no right to sue under the statute, could maintain the action as third-party beneficiaries of the PPAs. 588 F.\xc2\xa03d 1237, 1241 (2009).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 561 U. S. ___ (2010),[Footnote 3] and now reverse the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the County conceded below and before this Court, see 588 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1249; Tr. of Oral Arg. 45, covered entities have no right of action under \xc2\xa7340B itself. \xe2\x80\x9c[R]ecognition of any private right of action for violating a federal statute,\xe2\x80\x9d currently governing decisions instruct, \xe2\x80\x9cmust ultimately rest on congressional intent to provide a private remedy.\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia Bankshares, Inc. v. Sandberg, 501 U. S. 1083, 1102 (1991). See also Stoneridge Investment Partners, LLC v. Scientific-Atlanta, Inc., 552 U. S. 148, 164 (2008); Alexander v. Sandoval, 532 U. S. 275, 286 (2001). Congress vested authority to oversee compliance with the 340B Program in HHS and assigned no auxiliary enforcement role to covered entities.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Notwithstanding its inability to assert a statutory right of action, the County maintains that the PPAs implementing the 340B Program are agreements enforceable by covered entities as third-party beneficiaries. A nonparty becomes legally entitled to a benefit promised in a\ncontract, the County recognizes, only if the contracting parties so intend. Brief for Respondent 31 (citing Restatement (Second) of Contracts \xc2\xa7302(1)(b) (1979)). The PPAs \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically nam[e]\xe2\x80\x9d covered entities as the recipients of discounted drugs, the County observes; indeed the very object of the agreements is to ensure that those entities would be \xe2\x80\x9ccharge[d] \xe2\x80\xa6 no more than the ceiling price.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 33. When the Government uses a contract to secure a benefit, the County urges, the intended recipient acquires a right to the benefit enforceable under federal common law. Id., at 30. But see 9 J. Murray, Corbin on Contracts \xc2\xa745.6, p.\xc2\xa092 (rev. ed. 2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe distinction between an intention to benefit a third party and an intention that the third party should have the right to enforce that intention is emphasized where the promisee is a governmental entity.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The County\xe2\x80\x99s argument overlooks that the PPAs simply incorporate statutory obligations and record the manufacturers\xe2\x80\x99 agreement to abide by them. The form agreements, composed by HHS, contain no negotiable terms. Like the Medicaid Drug Rebate Program agreements, see supra, at 3, the 340B Program agreements serve as the means by which drug manufacturers opt into the statutory scheme. A third-party suit to enforce an HHS-drug manufacturer agreement, therefore, is in essence a suit to enforce the statute itself. The absence of a private right to enforce the statutory ceiling price obligations would be rendered meaningless if 340B entities could overcome that obstacle by suing to enforce the contract\xe2\x80\x99s ceiling price obligations instead. The statutory and contractual obligations, in short, are one and the same. See Grochowski v. Phoenix Construction, 318 F.\xc2\xa03d 80, 86 (CA2 2003) (when\na government contract confirms a statutory obligation, \xe2\x80\x9ca third-party private contract action [to enforce that obligation] would be inconsistent with \xe2\x80\xa6 the legislative scheme \xe2\x80\xa6 to the same extent as would a cause of action directly under the statute\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Telling in this regard, the County based its suit on allegations that the manufacturers charged more than the \xc2\xa7340B ceiling price, see, e.g., Third Amended Complaint in No. 3:05\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9303740 (ND Cal.), \xc2\xb61, 65, not that they violated any independent substantive obligation arising only from the PPAs.[Footnote 4] Repeatedly, the County acknowledged that \xc2\xa7340B is the source of the contractual term allegedly breached. See, e.g., id., \xc2\xb628 (\xe2\x80\x9c[Section] 340B requires pharmaceutical manufacturers to ensure that \xc2\xa7340B Participants pay no more than the \xe2\x80\x98ceiling price\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\xa6 for any pharmaceutical product.\xe2\x80\x9d); id., \xc2\xb636 (\xe2\x80\x9cUnder both \xc2\xa7340B and the PPA, [drug manufacturers] are required to ensure that the \xc2\xa7340B Participants \xe2\x80\xa6 pay no more for any product than the \xc2\xa7340B ceiling price.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit determined that \xe2\x80\x9c[p]ermitting covered entities to sue as intended beneficiaries of the PPA is \xe2\x80\xa6 wholly compatible with the Section 340B program\xe2\x80\x99s objectives\xe2\x80\x9d to ensure \xe2\x80\x9cthat drug companies comply with their obligations under the program and provide [the required] discounts.\xe2\x80\x9d 588 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1251. Suits like the County\xe2\x80\x99s, the Court of Appeals reasoned, would spread the enforcement burden instead of placing it \xe2\x80\x9c[entirely] on the government.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (citing Price v. Pierce, 823 F.\xc2\xa02d 1114, 1121 (CA7 1987)). But spreading the enforcement burden, the United States stressed, both in the Ninth Circuit and in this Court, is hardly what Congress contemplated when it \xe2\x80\x9ccentralized enforcement in the government.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 32; see Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae in No. 09\xe2\x80\x9315216 (CA9), p.\xc2\xa013 (County\xe2\x80\x99s challenge is at odds with Congress\xe2\x80\x99 unitary administrative and enforcement scheme).[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress made HHS administrator of both the Medicaid Drug Rebate Program and the 340B Program, the United States observed, Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 33\xe2\x80\x9334, and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he interdependent nature of the two programs\xe2\x80\x99 requirements means that an adjudication of rights under one program must proceed with an eye towards any implications for the other,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 34. Far from assisting HHS, suits by 340B entities would undermine the agency\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to administer both Medicaid and \xc2\xa7340B harmoniously and on a uniform, nationwide basis.[Footnote 6] Recognizing the County\xe2\x80\x99s right to proceed in court could spawn a multitude of dispersed and uncoordinated lawsuits by 340B entities. With HHS unable to hold the control rein, the risk of conflicting adjudications would be substantial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As earlier noted, see supra, at 3, the Medicaid Rebate Program\xe2\x80\x99s statute prohibits HHS from disclosing pricing information in a form that could reveal the prices a manufacturer charges for drugs it produces. \xc2\xa71396r\xe2\x80\x938(b)(3)(D).[Footnote 7] This ban on disclosure is a further indication of the incompatibility of private suits with the statute Congress enacted. If Congress meant to leave open the prospect of third-party beneficiary suits by 340B entities, it likely would not have barred the potential suitors from obtaining the very information necessary to determine whether their asserted rights have been violated.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true, as the Ninth Circuit observed, that HHS\xe2\x80\x99s Office of the Inspector General (OIG) has published reports finding that \xe2\x80\x9cHRSA lacks the oversight mechanisms and authority to ensure that [covered] entities pay at or below the \xe2\x80\xa6 ceiling price.\xe2\x80\x9d 588 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1242 (quoting OIG, D. Levinson, Deficiencies in the Oversight of the 340B Drug Pricing Program ii (OEI\xe2\x80\x9305\xe2\x80\x9302\xe2\x80\x9300072, Oct. 2005)). See also 588 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1242\xe2\x80\x931243 (citing OIG, D. Levinson, Review of 340B Prices 11 (OEI\xe2\x80\x9305\xe2\x80\x9302\xe2\x80\x9300073, July 2006) (estimating that covered entities overpaid $3.9 million in June 2005 alone)). But Congress did not respond to the reports of inadequate HRSA enforcement by inviting 340B entities to launch lawsuits in district courts across the country. Instead, in the PPACA, Congress directed HRSA to create a formal dispute resolution procedure, institute refund and civil penalty systems, and perform audits of manufacturers. 124 Stat. 823\xe2\x80\x93827, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7256b(d). Congress thus opted to strengthen and formalize HRSA\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement authority, to make the new adjudicative framework the proper remedy for covered entities complaining of \xe2\x80\x9covercharges and other violations of the discounted pricing requirements,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 823, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7256b(d)(1)(A), and to render the agency\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of covered entities\xe2\x80\x99 complaints binding, subject to judicial review under the APA, id., at 827, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7256b(d)(3)(C).

\xc2\xa0Whether a contracting agency may authorize third-party suits to enforce a Government contract is not at issue in this case. Cf. Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 22.
