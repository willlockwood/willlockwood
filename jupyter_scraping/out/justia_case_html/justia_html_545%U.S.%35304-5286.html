DODD v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 04\xe2\x80\x935286.Argued March 22, 2005\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 20, 2005

\n

On April 4, 2001, petitioner Dodd filed a pro se motion under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255, claiming that his conviction for knowingly and intentionally engaging in a continuing criminal enterprise, in violation of 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7841 and 846, should be set aside because it was contrary to Richardson v. United States, 526 U. S. 813, 815, which held that a jury must agree unanimously that a defendant is guilty of each of the specific violations that together constitute the continuing criminal enterprise. The District Court held that, because Richardson had been decided more than one year before Dodd filed his motion, the motion was untimely under \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3), which provides that \xc2\xa72255\xe2\x80\x99s 1-year limitation period begins to run on \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the right asserted was initially recognized by the Supreme Court, if that right has been newly recognized by the Supreme Court and made retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d On appeal, Dodd argued that \xc2\xb66(3)\xe2\x80\x99s limitation period began to run on April 19, 2002, the date the Eleventh Circuit recognized Richardson\xe2\x80\x99s retroactive application to cases on collateral review. The Eleventh Circuit held that the period began to run on June 1, 1999, the date that this Court initially decided Richardson.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0The 1-year limitation period under \xc2\xb66(3) begins to run on the date on which this Court \xe2\x80\x9cinitially recognized\xe2\x80\x9d the right asserted in an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s motion, not the date on which that right was made retroactive. The text of \xc2\xb66(3) unequivocally identifies one, and only one, date from which the limitation period is measured: \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the right asserted was initially recognized by the Supreme Court.\xe2\x80\x9d This Court presumes that a legislature says what it means and means what it says in a statute. Dodd\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on \xc2\xb66(3)\xe2\x80\x99s second clause to identify the operative date is misplaced. That clause merely limits the subsection\xe2\x80\x99s applicability to cases in which applicants assert rights \xe2\x80\x9cnewly recognized by the Supreme Court and made retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, \xc2\xb66(3)\xe2\x80\x99s date\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the right asserted was initially recognized by the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94does not apply at all unless the conditions in the second clause are satisfied. This result may make it difficult for applicants filing second or successive \xc2\xa72255 motions to obtain relief, since this Court rarely announces a new rule of constitutional law and makes it retroactive within a year, but the Court is not free to rewrite the statute that Congress has enacted. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0Because Dodd\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa72255 motion was filed more than a year after this Court decided Richardson, his motion was untimely. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

365 F.\xc2\xa03d 1273, affirmed.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nDODD V. UNITED STATES
\n545 U. S. ____ (2005)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 04-5286
\n
\n

MICHAEL DONALD DODD, PETITIONER v. UNITED\nSTATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[June 20, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice O\xe2\x80\x99Connor delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Title 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255 establishes a \xe2\x80\x9c1-year period of limitation\xe2\x80\x9d within which a federal prisoner may file a motion to vacate, set aside, or correct his sentence under that section. That period runs from \xe2\x80\x9cthe latest\xe2\x80\x9d of a number of events, which are enumerated in subparagraphs (1) through (4) of \xc2\xb66 of that section. This case involves subparagraph (3), which provides that the limitation period begins to run on \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the right asserted was initially recognized by the Supreme Court, if that right has been newly recognized by the Supreme Court and made retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d We must decide whether the date from which the limitation period begins to run under \xc2\xb66(3) is the date on which this Court \xe2\x80\x9cinitially recognized\xe2\x80\x9d the right asserted in an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa72255 motion, or whether, instead, it is the date on which the right is \xe2\x80\x9cmade retroactiv[e].\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Michael Donald Dodd was indicted on June 25, 1993, for knowingly and intentionally engaging in a continuing criminal enterprise in violation of 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7841 and 846, conspiring to possess with intent to distribute marijuana in violation of \xc2\xa7841(a)(1), conspiring to possess with intent to distribute cocaine in violation of \xc2\xa7841(a)(1), and 16 counts of using and possessing a passport obtained by false statement in violation of 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71546(a). He was convicted of all counts except the cocaine charge, and was sentenced to 360 months\xe2\x80\x99 imprisonment followed by five years of supervised release. The Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed on May 7, 1997. 111 F. 3d 867 (per curiam). Because Dodd did not file a petition for certiorari, his conviction became final on August 6, 1997. See Clay v. United States, 537 U. S. 522, 525 (2003).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On April 4, 2001, more than three years after his conviction became final, Dodd filed a pro se motion under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255 seeking to set aside his conviction for knowingly and intentionally engaging in a continuing criminal enterprise, based on our decision in Richardson v. United States, 526 U. S. 813 (1999). Richardson held that a jury must agree unanimously that a defendant is guilty of each of the specific violations that together constitute the continuing criminal enterprise. Id., at 815. Dodd argued, among other things, that he was entitled to relief because his jury had not been instructed that they had to agree unanimously on each predicate violation. App. 9. The District Court dismissed Dodd\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa72255 motion as time barred. Id., at 11\xe2\x80\x9315. Because Richardson had been decided more than one year before Dodd filed his motion, the court held that the motion was untimely; it also rejected Dodd\xe2\x80\x99s request for equitable tolling. App. 13\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dodd appealed, arguing that the limitation period in \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3), did not begin to run until April 19, 2002, when the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit held in Ross v. United States, 289 F.\xc2\xa03d 677, that the right recognized in Richardson applies retroactively to cases on collateral review. The Eleventh Circuit held that the limitation period began to run on \xe2\x80\x9cthe date the Supreme Court initially recognizes the right\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the date Richardson was decided\xe2\x80\x94and accordingly affirmed the dismissal of Dodd\xe2\x80\x99s motion as time barred. 365 F.\xc2\xa03d 1273, 1283 (2004).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari, 543 U. S. __ (2004), to resolve a conflict in the Courts of Appeals over when the limitation period in \xc2\xb66(3) begins to run. Compare, e.g., 365 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1283 (case below) (period runs from date of Supreme Court decision initially recognizing right asserted); and United States v. Lopez, 248 F.\xc2\xa03d 427, 432\xe2\x80\x93433 (CA5 2001) (same), with Pryor v. United States, 278 F.\xc2\xa03d 612, 616 (CA6 2002) (period does not begin to run until right has been held retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review); and United States v. Valdez, 195 F.\xc2\xa03d 544, 547\xe2\x80\x93548 (CA9 1999) (same).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 2255, \xc2\xb66, provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cA 1-year period of limitation shall apply to a motion under this section. The limitation period shall run from the latest of\xe2\x80\x94

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(1) the date on which the judgment of conviction becomes final;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2) the date on which the impediment to making a motion created by governmental action in violation of the Constitution or laws of the United States is removed, if the movant was prevented from making a motion by such governmental action;

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(3) the date on which the right asserted was initially recognized by the Supreme Court, if that right has been newly recognized by the Supreme Court and made retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review; or

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(4) the date on which the facts supporting the claim or claims presented could have been discovered through the exercise of due diligence.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nIn most cases, the operative date from which the limitation period is measured will be the one identified in \xc2\xb66(1): \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the judgment of conviction becomes final.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.; see also Clay, supra, at 524. But later filings are permitted where subparagraphs (2)\xe2\x80\x93(4) apply. This case involves \xc2\xb66(3), which gives \xc2\xa72255 applicants one year from \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the right asserted was initially recognized by the Supreme Court, if that right has been newly recognized by the Supreme Court and made retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d Dodd contends that under subparagraph (3), the limitation period runs from the date on which the right asserted was made retroactively applicable. The United States, on the other hand, argues that it runs from the date on which this Court initially recognized the right asserted.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We believe that the text of \xc2\xb66(3) settles this dispute. It unequivocally identifies one, and only one, date from which the 1-year limitation period is measured: \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the right asserted was initially recognized by the Supreme Court.\xe2\x80\x9d We \xe2\x80\x9cmust presume that [the] legislature says in a statute what it means and means in a statute what it says there.\xe2\x80\x9d Connecticut Nat. Bank v. Germain, 503 U. S. 249, 253\xe2\x80\x93254 (1992). What Congress has said in \xc2\xb66(3) is clear: an applicant has one year from the date on which the right he asserts was initially recognized by this Court.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dodd urges us to adopt a different interpretation. He contends that the second clause in \xc2\xb66(3) affects the applicable date under that provision. He reads \xc2\xb66(3) as containing \xe2\x80\x9cthree distinct prerequisites\xe2\x80\x9d that \xe2\x80\x9cmust be satisfied before the limitation period begins.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 8. Those three prerequisites are: (1) the right asserted by the applicant \xe2\x80\x9cwas initially recognized\xe2\x80\x9d by this Court; (2) this Court \xe2\x80\x9cnewly recognized\xe2\x80\x9d the right; and (3) a court must have \xe2\x80\x9cmade\xe2\x80\x9d the right \xe2\x80\x9cretroactively applicable to cases on collateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (internal quotation marks omitted). Because the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit did not hold the right recognized in Richardson v. United States, 526 U. S. 813 (1999), retroactively applicable until April 19, 2002, when it decided Ross, 289 F. 3d 677, Dodd contends that he had until April 19, 2003\xe2\x80\x94one year from the date when all three prerequisites were satisfied\xe2\x80\x94to file his \xc2\xa72255\nmotion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dodd\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation does not square with the only natural reading of the text. Paragraph 6(3) identifies one date and one date only as the date from which the 1-year limitation period runs: \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the right asserted was initially recognized by the Supreme Court.\xe2\x80\x9d Dodd\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the second clause to identify the operative date is misplaced. That clause\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cif that right has been newly recognized by the Supreme Court and made retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94imposes a condition on the applicability of this subsection. See Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 1124 (1993) (the definition of \xe2\x80\x9cif\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cin the event that\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9con condition that\xe2\x80\x9d). It therefore limits \xc2\xb66(3)\xe2\x80\x99s application to cases in which applicants are seeking to assert rights \xe2\x80\x9cnewly recognized by the Supreme Court and made retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3). That means that \xc2\xb66(3)\xe2\x80\x99s date\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the right asserted was initially recognized by the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94does not apply at all if the conditions in the second clause\xe2\x80\x94the right \xe2\x80\x9chas been newly recognized by the Supreme Court and made retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94have not been satisfied. As long as the conditions in the second clause are satisfied so that \xc2\xb66(3) applies in the first place, that clause has no impact whatsoever on the date from which the 1-year limitation period in \xc2\xb66(3) begins to run. Thus, if this Court decides a case recognizing a new right, a federal prisoner seeking to assert that right will have one year from this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision within which to file his \xc2\xa72255 motion. He may take advantage of the date in the first clause of \xc2\xb66(3) only if the conditions in the second clause are met.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We recognize that the statute of limitations in \xc2\xb66(3) makes it difficult for applicants filing second or successive \xc2\xa72255 motions to obtain relief. The limitation period in \xc2\xb66(3) applies to \xe2\x80\x9call motions\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa72255, initial motions as well as second or successive ones. Section 2255, \xc2\xb68(2), narrowly restricts an applicant\xe2\x80\x99s ability to file a second or successive motion. An applicant may file a second or successive motion only in limited circumstances, such as where he seeks to take advantage of \xe2\x80\x9ca new rule of constitutional law, made retroactive to cases on collateral review by the Supreme Court, that was previously unavailable.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb68(2). Dodd points out that this Court rarely decides that a new rule is retroactively applicable within one year of initially recognizing that right. Thus, because of the interplay between \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb68(2) and 6(3), an applicant who files a second or successive motion seeking to take advantage of a new rule of constitutional law will be time barred except in the rare case in which this Court announces a new rule of constitutional law and makes it retroactive within one year.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although we recognize the potential for harsh results in some cases, we are not free to rewrite the statute that Congress has enacted. \xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language is plain, the sole function of the courts\xe2\x80\x94at least where the disposition required by the text is not absurd\xe2\x80\x94is to enforce it according to its terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Hartford Underwriters Ins. Co. v. Union Planters Bank, N.\xc2\xa0A., 530 U. S. 1, 6 (2000) (internal quotation marks omitted). See also Tyler v. Cain, 533 U. S. 656, 663, n.\xc2\xa05 (2001) (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven if we disagreed with the legislative decision to establish stringent procedural requirements for retroactive application of new rules, we do not have license to question the decision on policy grounds\xe2\x80\x9d). The disposition required by the text here, though strict, is not absurd. It is for Congress, not this Court, to amend the statute if it believes that the interplay of \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb68(2) and 6(3) of \xc2\xa72255 unduly restricts federal prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 ability to file second or successive\nmotions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens would hold, contrary to the plain text, that the limitation period in \xc2\xb66(3) begins to run when the right asserted is made retroactive, see post, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310 (dissenting opinion), because he assumes that \xe2\x80\x9cthe most natural reading of the statutory text would make it possible for the limitations period to expire before the cause of action accrues,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 1. Justice Stevens analogizes this case to Graham County Soil & Water Conservation Dist. v. United States, ex rel. Wilson, post, p. __, see post, at 1 (dissenting opinion), but Graham County is distinguishable. The text of the statute at issue in Graham County is ambiguous, justifying the Court\xe2\x80\x99s partial reliance on \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98standard rule that the limitations period commences when the plaintiff has a complete and present cause of action.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d See Graham County, post, at 5\xe2\x80\x938, and n. 2. Here, there is no such ambiguity; \xc2\xb66(3) clearly specifies the date on which the limitation period begins to run.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Dodd\xe2\x80\x99s \xc2\xa72255 motion sought to benefit from our holding in Richardson, supra, which was decided on June 1, 1999. Thus, he had one year from that date within which to file his motion. Because he did not file his motion until April 4, 2001, the motion was untimely. We therefore affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit.

\n

It is so ordered.

\nSTEVENS, J., DISSENTING

\nNO. 04-5286

\n

MICHAEL DONALD DODD, PETITIONER v. UNITED\nSTATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[June 20, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, with whom Justice Souter, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Breyer join as to Part II, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the same anomalous factor is present in both this case and in Graham County Soil & Water Conservation Dist. v. United States ex rel. Wilson, post, p. ___, and is decisive in my analysis of both cases, it is appropriate to explain my views in a single opinion. In each case the most natural reading of the statutory text would make it possible for the limitations period to expire before the cause of action accrues. Whether the source of this possible result is merely the use of careless wording or an incorrect assumption by Congress concerning the timing of two relevant events, I am convinced that Congress did not intend to authorize such a perverse result in either case. Thus, while I agree with much of the reasoning in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s cogent opinion in Graham County, I write separately because I would agree with the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 reading of the text of 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73731(b)(1) were it not for this anomaly. In this case, however, because that same factor provides an even stronger reason for rejecting the interpretation of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3), that the Court endorses, I would reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Graham County, respondent and the Government argue (and the Court of Appeals held) that the 6-year limitations period applicable to a \xe2\x80\x9ccivil action under section 3730,\xe2\x80\x9d 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73731(b)(1), applies to the retaliation action authorized by \xc2\xa73730(h). That argument is supported by a literal reading of the statutory text; for \xc2\xa73730(h) plainly qualifies as \xe2\x80\x9ca civil action under section 3730.\xe2\x80\x9d Moreover, that reading derives strong support from the interest in having a uniform federal statute of limitations govern the litigation of federal causes of action. Cf. Jones v. R.\xc2\xa0R. Donnelley & Sons Co., 541 U. S. 369, 377\xe2\x80\x93383 (2004). Nevertheless, I agree with the Court that another reading of the text is far more plausible, and with its conclusion that when choosing between two constructions of a statute of limitations, whenever possible we should prefer the construction that starts the time limit running when the cause of action accrues.[Footnote 1]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Graham County that choice is compelled by the interaction between two relevant events: the \xe2\x80\x9cviolation of \xc2\xa73729\xe2\x80\x9d and the retaliatory act against the whistle-blower. Section 3731(b)(1) provides that a \xe2\x80\x9ccivil action under section 3730\xe2\x80\x9d must be brought within six years of a \xe2\x80\x9cviolation of section 3729.\xe2\x80\x9d If this section were read to encompass retaliation claims under \xc2\xa73730(h), as held by the Court of Appeals, the statute of limitations would be triggered by the \xe2\x80\x9cviolation of \xc2\xa73729\xe2\x80\x9d; that is, the limitations period would begin to run before the cause of action for retaliation accrues, and could potentially expire before an actionable retaliation claim even exists. See Graham County, post, at 11; United States ex rel. Wilson v. Graham County Soil & Water Conservation Dist., 367 F. 3d 245, 260\xe2\x80\x93261 (CA4 2004) (Wilkinson, J., dissenting). Thus, the potentially prolonged time period between the two relevant events\xe2\x80\x94the violation of \xc2\xa73729 (triggering the limitations period) and the retaliation against the whistle-blower (giving rise to an actionable claim)\xe2\x80\x94could leave the well-intentioned whistle-blower without any recourse under \xc2\xa73730(h), the very statute designed to provide such protection.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court rightly avoids that harsh and counterintuitive result by adopting a construction of the statute that would generally start the running of the limitations period from the date the cause of action accrues, i.e., when the act or acts of retaliation occur. As Justice Thomas explains, that is not only the prevailing rule applied throughout the country to analogous state-law claims, Graham County, post, at 9\xe2\x80\x9311, n.\xc2\xa02; it is also the background norm against which Congress legislates, Graham County, post, at 8\xe2\x80\x939. Because Congress surely did not intend to create a cause of action for retaliation with one hand, and impose with the other a premature trigger date for the limitations period with the potential to bar retaliation claims altogether, I concur in the judgment in Graham County.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The same potential for premature expiration of a statute of limitations is the primary reason why I cannot join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s anomalous construction of the statute in this case. The statute we are called upon to interpret provides a 1-year period of limitation for a habeas petition that has as its basis a new rule of criminal law or criminal procedure that has retroactive application. Title 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3), provides that \xe2\x80\x9cthe limitation period shall run from \xe2\x80\xa6 the date on which the right asserted was initially recognized by the Supreme Court, if that right has been newly recognized by the Supreme Court and made retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d There are two possible interpretations of when the period should start to run: from the date that this Court recognizes the new right, or from the date that both conditions[Footnote 2] in \xc2\xb66(3) are met.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If, as I believe Congress thought to be the case, this Court made a decision concerning a new rule\xe2\x80\x99s retroactive application at the same time it recognized the new right, the statutory scheme would make perfect sense: Petitioners, whether filing an initial habeas petition or a second or successive petition, would have one year from this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to file a petition for a writ taking advantage of that decision. Within a relatively short amount of time, those claims would be adjudicated, and the statute\xe2\x80\x99s goals of finality would be duly served. In practice, however, this Court does not ordinarily make retroactivity judgments at the time a new right is recognized.[Footnote 3] See, e.g., Ring v. Arizona, 536 U. S. 584 (2002) (applying Apprendi v. New Jersey, 530 U. S. 466 (2000), to determinations of death penalty eligibility); Schriro v. Summerlin, 542 U. S. 348 (2004) (concluding Ring was not retroactive). Thus, as in Graham County, the statute implicates two relevant events: this Court\xe2\x80\x99s recognition of a new right (which, according to the majority, triggers the limitation period) and the declaration that the right can be applied retroactively (which allows a petitioner to proceed with the claim). Because a significant amount of time may elapse during the interval between the triggering event and the point at which a petitioner may actually be able to file an action seeking relief under the statute, there is a real risk that the 1-year limitation period will expire before the cause of action accrues. In my judgment, the probable explanation for statutory text that creates this risk is Congress\xe2\x80\x99 apparent assumption that our recognition of the new right and our decision to apply it retroactively would be made at the same time. Otherwise it seems nonsensical to assume that Congress deliberately enacted a statute that recognizes a cause of action, but wrote the limitation period in a way that precludes an individual from ever taking advantage of the cause of action.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We are thus faced with the same decision as in Graham County: Do we interpret the statute in such a way as to allow prisoners such as Dodd to take advantage of the full year Congress provided for such claims, or do we interpret the statute in such a way that the limitation period will begin to run before a prisoner may take advantage of \xc2\xb66(3)? As an initial matter, the text here certainly permits both readings, just as it did in Graham County. Paragraph 6(3) requires that two prerequisites must be met before a habeas petitioner can take advantage of that date as the starting point for the statute of limitations. Both requirements are in the past tense, and both must be satisfied before \xc2\xb66(3) is applicable. Furthermore, just as the clause \xe2\x80\x9cif that right has been newly recognized by the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x9d describes the date indicated in the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the right asserted was initially recognized,\xe2\x80\x9d it is possible to read the subordinate clause \xe2\x80\x9cif that right has been\xe2\x80\xa6made retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review\xe2\x80\x9d as amplifying the description of the date in the provision\xe2\x80\x99s main clause, rather than adding an additional qualifier. Consequently, while the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reading of \xc2\xb66(3)\xe2\x80\x94requiring that the statute of limitations begin to run when this Court recognizes a new rule\xe2\x80\x94may be the more natural reading of the text, that advocated by petitioner\xe2\x80\x94starting the statute of limitations when the new rule is held to be retroactive\xe2\x80\x94is by no means implausible.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, the potential for claims to be prematurely barred by the statute of limitations is even greater than in Graham County. There, the possibility that the 6-year statute of limitations period could run before the cause of action accrued, while plausible, was not particularly likely, since in most cases the retaliatory conduct that would form the basis of the cause of action under 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73730(h) would probably occur within six years of the violation of \xc2\xa73729.[Footnote 5] In this case, owing to the substantially shorter 1-year statute of limitation period, both requirements of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3), will often not be met before the statute of limitation period has expired if it is triggered by the decision of the Supreme Court announcing a new rule.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That result is certainly true for Dodd himself. Richardson v. United States, 526 U. S. 813, was decided on June 1, 1999. Under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, the statute of limitations thus expired on June 1, 2000, one year after we recognized the new rule. The Eleventh Circuit, however, did not decide whether Richardson was retroactive until April 19, 2002.[Footnote 6] See Ross v. United States, 289 F.\xc2\xa03d 677 (CA11 2002). Thus, Dodd would not, under the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, have been able to raise his claim at all, since the statute of limitations expired before he could have taken advantage of \xc2\xb66(3)\xe2\x80\x99s 1-year grace period.[Footnote 7] Even for those prisoners who are incarcerated in a jurisdiction in which the new rule is quickly held to be retroactive, at least part of the 1-year period in which to file a claim taking advantage of the retroactive rule will run before the petition raising the claim can be filed.[Footnote 8]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, the admonition in Graham County that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress generally drafts statutes of limitations to begin when the cause of action accrues,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 11, applies with special force in this case. Paragraph 6(3) both recognizes a basis for habeas relief by allowing an otherwise barred claim to go forward if certain conditions are met, and also sets forth a 1-year statute of limitation for such claims. It would make no sense for Congress, in the same provision, both to recognize a potential basis for habeas relief and also to make it highly probable that the statute of limitation would bar relief before the claim can be brought. Again, this is not simply a remote possibility: it is true for Dodd himself, and in 6 of the 7 Circuits to have addressed whether Richardson is retroactive. See n.\xc2\xa07, supra. It is this absurd result that convinces me that Congress could not have intended that \xc2\xb66(3) should be read in this manner. Even if the text is as clear as the majority claims (a proposition I reject), we should still interpret the text in a manner that would avoid such an absurd result. See, e.g., Clinton v. City of New York, 524 U. S. 417, 429 (1998); Church of Holy Trinity v. United States, 143 U. S. 457, 459 (1892).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To avoid this result, I would interpret \xc2\xb66(3) to begin to run only when the Supreme Court has initially recognized the new right and when that right has been held to be retroactive. Under this interpretation, the statute of limitation would not begin to run until the prisoner was actually able to file a petition under \xc2\xb66(3), which is the only interpretation Congress could have intended. Although in enacting AEDPA Congress was clearly concerned with finality, see Duncan v. Walker, 533 U. S. 167, 179 (2001), \xc2\xb66(3) is an explicit exception to that general preference. Congress surely intended to allow habeas petitioners to take advantage of the new rights that this Court deems retroactive. Otherwise, there would have been no reason to include that section in the statute. That is why, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]bsent other indication, a statue of limitations begins to run at the time the plaintiff has the right to apply to the court for relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Graham County, post, at 8\xe2\x80\x939 (quoting TRW Inc. v. Andrews, 534 U. S. 19, 37 (2001) (Scalia, J., concurring in judgment) (internal quotation marks and citation omitted)).[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition to creating the perverse result that the statute of limitations will run before a prisoner can file an initial habeas petition, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s myopic reading of \xc2\xb66(3) effectively nullifies 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72244(b)(2)(A), which allows prisoners to file second or successive applications based on a retroactive rule.[Footnote 10] As the majority recognizes in what amounts to a dramatic understatement, its interpretation of \xc2\xb66(3) \xe2\x80\x9cmakes it difficult for applicants filing second or successive \xc2\xa72255 motions to obtain relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. Because of the way \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb66(3) and 8(2) interact, a prisoner can only file a second or successive petition based on a newly recognized rule that has been made retroactive if this Court has held the rule to be retroactive within one year of recognizing it. Unfortunately for such prisoners, however, this Court has never done so since Teague v. Lane, 489 U. S. 288 (1989), was decided.[Footnote 11] Because of the need for percolation, and the time it takes for cases to come to this Court from the courts below, it seems unlikely (to say the least) that we would ever do so. Therefore, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xb66(3) effectively nullifies \xc2\xb68(2). It is, of course, a basic canon of statutory construction that we will not interpret a congressional statute in such a manner as to effectively nullify an entire section. See, e.g., Duncan, 533 U. S., at 174 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] statute ought, upon the whole, to be so construed that, if it can be prevented, no clause, sentence, or word shall be superfluous, void, or insignificant\xe2\x80\x9d (citations omitted)). It is a strange principle that requires strict adherence to the text of one provision while allowing another to have virtually no real world application. It would seem far wiser to give both sections the meaning that Congress obviously intended.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, while I concur in the judgment in Graham County, I respectfully dissent in Dodd.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Essentially for reasons stated by Justice Stevens, I conclude that 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3), is most sensibly read to start the time clock on the date a right is \xe2\x80\x9cmade retroactively applicable to cases on collateral review.\xe2\x80\x9d I therefore join, in principal part, Part II of Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 dissenting opinion.*

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation\xe2\x80\x94that the limitation period begins on \xe2\x80\x9cthe date on which the right asserted was initially recognized by [this] Court,\xe2\x80\x9d 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3)\xe2\x80\x94presents \xe2\x80\x9ca real risk that the 1-year limitation period will expire before [a \xc2\xa72255 petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s] cause of action accrues,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 5 (Stevens, J., dissenting). By contrast, as Justice Breyer explains in his dissenting opinion in Graham County Soil & Water Conservation Dist. v. United States ex rel. Wilson, post, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, a determination that the False Claims Act\xe2\x80\x99s six-year statute of limitations, see 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73731(b)(1), governs civil suits for retaliation under the Act, see \xc2\xa73730(h), ordinarily would work no claim deprivation. See ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938 (Stevens, J., dissenting) (In Graham County, \xe2\x80\x9cthe possibility that the 6-year statute of limitations period could run before the cause of action accrued, while plausible, was not particularly likely, since in most cases the retaliatory conduct that would form the basis of the cause of action under 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73730(h) would probably occur within six years of the violation of \xc2\xa73729. [In cases like Dodd, on the other hand,] owing to the substantially shorter 1-year statute of limitation period, both requirements of 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3), will often not be met before the statute of limitation period has expired if it is triggered by the decision of the Supreme Court announcing a new rule.\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nearly 20 years have passed since Congress amended 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73731(b)(1) to provide that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] civil action under section 3730 may not be brought \xe2\x80\xa6 more than 6 years after the date on which the violation of section 3729 is committed.\xe2\x80\x9d See Graham County, post, at 2. Yet petitioner Graham County District has been unable to cite a single instance in which a suit has been time barred because the alleged retaliation proscribed by \xc2\xa73730(h) fell outside the period triggered by submission of a false claim in violation of \xc2\xa73729. See Tr. of Oral Arg. in No. 04\xe2\x80\x93169, pp. 4\xe2\x80\x936; Brief for Respondent in No. 04\xe2\x80\x93169, pp. 4, 15\xe2\x80\x9316; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae in No. 04\xe2\x80\x93169, pp. 27\xe2\x80\x9328; see also Graham County, post, at 5 (Breyer, J., dissenting). As Dodd\xe2\x80\x99s case illustrates, however, on the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading, it is \xe2\x80\x9chighly probable\xe2\x80\x9d that the 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3), limitation \xe2\x80\x9cwould bar relief before the claim can be brought.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9 (Stevens, J., dissenting). Accordingly, I would not, as Justice Stevens does, bracket the instant case with Graham County.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0True, the limitation period in Graham County, like the \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3), limitation, is triggered by an event that may precede the accrual date of a claim. But the resemblance ends there. The generous six-year span in 31 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73731(b)(1), in practical effect, will give the plaintiff leeway to commence suit she likely will not have under the typically shorter state limitation. See Graham County, post, at 5 (Breyer, J., dissenting). The opposite effect would attend 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255, \xc2\xb66(3). The one-year limitation specified there, if triggered by the date on which this Court \xe2\x80\x9cinitially recognized\xe2\x80\x9d the right asserted, bars Dodd and will bar most \xe2\x80\x9cnew rule\xe2\x80\x9d petitioners from presenting their claims. It exalts form over reality to equate the two statutes and cases for time-bar purposes.
