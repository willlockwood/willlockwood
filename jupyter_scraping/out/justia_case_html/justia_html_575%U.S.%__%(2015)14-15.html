ARMSTRONG et\xc2\xa0al. v. EXCEPTIONAL CHILD CENTER, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

No. 14\xe2\x80\x9315.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 20, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 31, 2015

Providers of \xe2\x80\x9chabilitation services\xe2\x80\x9d under Idaho\xe2\x80\x99s Medicaid plan are reimbursed by the State\xe2\x80\x99s Department of Health and Welfare. Section 30(A) of the Medicaid Act requires Idaho\xe2\x80\x99s plan to \xe2\x80\x9cassure that payments are consistent with efficiency, economy, and quality of care\xe2\x80\x9d while \xe2\x80\x9csafeguard[ing] against unnecessary utilization of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. care and services.\xe2\x80\x9d 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396a(a)(30)(A). Respondents, providers of habilitation services, sued petitioners, Idaho Health and Welfare Department officials, claiming that Idaho reimbursed them at rates lower than \xc2\xa730(A) permits, and seeking to enjoin petitioners to increase these rates. The District Court entered summary judgment for the providers. The Ninth Circuit affirmed, concluding that the Supremacy Clause gave the providers an implied right of action, and that they could sue under this implied right of action to seek an injunction requiring Idaho to comply with \xc2\xa730(a).

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is reversed.

567 Fed. Appx. 496, reversed.

Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court, except as to Part IV, concluding that the Supremacy Clause does not confer a private right of action, and that Medicaid providers cannot sue for an injunction requiring compliance with \xc2\xa730(a). Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9310.

(a)\xc2\xa0The Supremacy Clause instructs courts to give federal law priority when state and federal law clash. Gibbons v. Ogden, 9 Wheat. 1, 210. But it is not the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98source of any federal rights,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Golden State Transit Corp. v. Los Angeles, 493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103 , and certainly does not create a cause of action. Nothing in the Clause\xe2\x80\x99s text suggests otherwise, and nothing suggests it was ever understood as conferring a private right of action. Article I vests Congress with broad discretion over the manner of implementing its enumerated powers. Art I., \xc2\xa78; McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 421. It is unlikely that the Constitution gave Congress broad discretion with regard to the enactment of laws, while simultaneously limiting Congress\xe2\x80\x99s power over the manner of their implementation, making it impossible to leave the enforcement of federal law to federal actors. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

(b) Reading the Supremacy Clause not to confer a private right of action is consistent with this Court\xe2\x80\x99s preemption jurisprudence. The ability to sue to enjoin unconstitutional actions by state and federal officers is the creation of courts of equity, and reflects a long history of judicial review of illegal executive action, tracing back to England. This Court has never held nor suggested that this judge-made remedy, in its application to state officers, rests upon an implied right of action contained in the Supremacy Clause. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.

(c)\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suit cannot proceed in equity. The power of federal courts of equity to enjoin unlawful executive action is subject to express and implied statutory limitations. See, e.g., Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Florida, 517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 44 . Here, the express provision of a single remedy for a State\xe2\x80\x99s failure to comply with Medicaid\xe2\x80\x99s requirements\xe2\x80\x94the withholding of Medicaid funds by the Secretary of Health and Human Services, 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396c\xe2\x80\x94and the sheer complexity associated with enforcing \xc2\xa730(A) combine to establish Congress\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cintent to foreclose\xe2\x80\x9d equitable relief, Verizon Md. Inc. v. Public Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Md., 535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635 . Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310.

Scalia, J., delivered the opinion of the Court with respect to Parts I, II, and III, in which Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., and Thomas, Breyer, and Alito, JJ., joined, and an opinion with respect to Part IV, in which Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., and Thomas and Alito, JJ., joined. Breyer, J., filed an opinion concurring in part and concurring in the judgment. Sotomayor, J., filed a dissenting opinion, in which Kennedy, Ginsburg, and Kagan, JJ., joined.

RICHARD ARMSTRONG, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. EXCEPTIONAL CHILD CENTER, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[March 31, 2015]

\n

\n \n Justice Scalia delivered the opinion of the Court, except as to Part IV.

\n

\n \n We consider whether Medicaid providers can sue to enforce \xc2\xa7(30)(A) of the Medicaid Act.\n\n81Stat.\n911 (codified as amended at\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396a(a)(30)(A)).

\n

I

\n

\n \n Medicaid is a federal program that subsidizes the States\xe2\x80\x99 provision of medical services to \xe2\x80\x9cfamilies with dependent children and of aged, blind, or disabled individuals, whose income and resources are insufficient to meet the costs of necessary medical services.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71396\xe2\x80\x931. Like other Spending Clause legislation, Medicaid offers the States a bargain: Congress provides federal funds in exchange for the States\xe2\x80\x99 agreement to spend them in accordance with congressionally imposed conditions.

\n

\n \n In order to qualify for Medicaid funding, the State of Idaho adopted, and the Federal Government approved, a Medicaid \xe2\x80\x9cplan,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71396a(a), which Idaho administers through its Department of Health and Welfare. Idaho\xe2\x80\x99s plan includes \xe2\x80\x9chabilitation services\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94in-home care for individuals who, \xe2\x80\x9cbut for the provision of such services .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. would require the level of care provided in a hospital or a nursing facility or intermediate care facility for the mentally retarded the cost of which could be reimbursed under the State plan,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71396n(c) and (c)(1). Providers of these services are reimbursed by the Department of Health and Welfare.

\n

\n \n Section 30(A) of the Medicaid Act requires Idaho\xe2\x80\x99s plan to:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cprovide such methods and procedures relating to the utilization of, and the payment for, care and services available under the plan .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as may be necessary to safeguard against unnecessary utilization of such care and services and to assure that payments are consistent with efficiency, economy, and quality of care and are sufficient to enlist enough providers so that care and services are available under the plan at least to the extent that such care and services are available to the general population in the geographic area .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396a(a)(30)(A).

\n

Respondents are providers of habilitation services to persons covered by Idaho\xe2\x80\x99s Medicaid plan. They sued petitioners\xe2\x80\x94two officials in Idaho\xe2\x80\x99s Department of Health and Welfare\xe2\x80\x94in the United States District Court for the District of Idaho, claiming that Idaho violates \xc2\xa730(A) by reimbursing providers of habilitation services at rates lower than \xc2\xa730(A) permits. They asked the court to enjoin petitioners to increase these rates.

\n

\n \n The District Court entered summary judgment for the providers, holding that Idaho had not set rates in a manner consistent with \xc2\xa730(A). Inclusion, Inc. v. Armstrong, 835 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 960 (2011). The Ninth Circuit affirmed. 567 Fed. Appx. 496 (2014). It said that the providers had \xe2\x80\x9can implied right of action under the Supremacy Clause to seek injunctive relief against the enforcement or implementation of state legislation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 497 (citing Independent Living Center of Southern Cal. v. Shewry, 543 F.\xc2\xa03d 1050, 1065 (CA9 2008)). We granted certiorari. 573 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2014).

\n

II

\n

\n \n The Supremacy Clause, Art.\xc2\xa0VI, cl.\xc2\xa02, reads:

\n

\n \n \xe2\x80\x9cThis Constitution, and the Laws of the United States which shall be made in Pursuance thereof; and all Treaties made, or which shall be made, under the Authority of the United States, shall be the supreme Law of the Land; and the Judges in every State shall be bound thereby, any Thing in the Constitution or Laws of any State to the Contrary notwithstanding.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

It is apparent that this Clause creates a rule of decision: Courts \xe2\x80\x9cshall\xe2\x80\x9d regard the \xe2\x80\x9cConstitution,\xe2\x80\x9d and all laws \xe2\x80\x9cmade in Pursuance thereof,\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cthe supreme Law of the Land.\xe2\x80\x9d They must not give effect to state laws that conflict with federal laws. Gibbons v. Ogden, 9 Wheat. 1, 210 (1824). It is equally apparent that the Supremacy Clause is not the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98source of any federal rights,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Golden State Transit Corp. v. Los Angeles,\n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103,\n107 (1989)\n (quoting Chapman v. Houston Welfare Rights Organization,\n\n441 U.\xc2\xa0S. 600,\n613 (1979)\n), and certainly does not create a cause of action. It instructs courts what to do when state and federal law clash, but is silent regarding who may enforce federal laws in court, and in what circumstances they may do so.

\n

\n \n Hamilton wrote that the Supremacy Clause \xe2\x80\x9conly declares a truth, which flows immediately and necessarily from the institution of a Federal Government.\xe2\x80\x9d The Federalist No. 33, p. 207 (J. Cooke ed. 1961). And Story described the Clause as \xe2\x80\x9ca positive affirmance of that, which is necessarily implied.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 Commentaries on the Constitution of the United States \xc2\xa71831, p. 693 (1833). These descriptions would have been grossly inapt if the Clause were understood to give affected parties a constitutional (and hence congressionally unalterable) right to enforce federal laws against the States. And had it been understood to provide such significant private rights against the States, one would expect to find that mentioned in the preratification historical record, which contained ample discussion of the Supremacy Clause by both supporters and opponents of ratification. See C. Drahozal, The Supremacy Clause: A Reference Guide to the United States Constitution 25 (2004); The Federalist No. 44, at 306 (J. Madison). We are aware of no such mention, and respondents have not provided any. Its conspicuous absence militates strongly against their position.

\n

\n \n Additionally, it is important to read the Supremacy Clause in the context of the Constitution as a whole. Article I vests Congress with broad discretion over the manner of implementing its enumerated powers, giving it authority to \xe2\x80\x9cmake all Laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying [them] into Execution.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78. We have said that this confers upon the Legislature \xe2\x80\x9cthat discretion, with respect to the means by which the powers [the Constitution] confers are to be carried into execution, which will enable that body to perform the high duties assigned to it,\xe2\x80\x9d McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 421 (1819). It is unlikely that the Constitution gave Congress such broad discretion with regard to the enactment of laws, while simultaneously limiting Congress\xe2\x80\x99s power over the manner of their implementation, making it impossible to leave the enforcement of federal law to federal actors. If the Supremacy Clause includes a private right of action, then the Constitution requires Congress to permit the enforcement of its laws by private actors, significantly curtailing its ability to guide the implementation of fed-eral law. It would be strange indeed to give a clause that makes federal law supreme a reading that limits Congress\xe2\x80\x99s power to enforce that law, by imposing mandatory private enforcement\xe2\x80\x94a limitation unheard-of with regard to state legislatures.

\n

\n \n To say that the Supremacy Clause does not confer a right of action is not to diminish the significant role that courts play in assuring the supremacy of federal law. For once a case or controversy properly comes before a court, judges are bound by federal law. Thus, a court may not convict a criminal defendant of violating a state law that federal law prohibits. See, e.g., Pennsylvania v. Nelson,\n\n350 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497,\n499,\n509 (1956)\n. Similarly, a court may not hold a civil defendant liable under state law for conduct federal law requires. See, e.g., Mutual Pharmaceutical Co. v. Bartlett, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2013) (slip op., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314). And, as we have long recognized, if an individual claims federal law immunizes him from state regulation, the court may issue an injunction upon finding the state regulatory actions preempted. Ex parte Young,\n\n209 U.\xc2\xa0S. 123\n\xe2\x80\x93156 (1908).

\n

\n \n Respondents contend that our preemption jurisprudence\xe2\x80\x94specifically, the fact that we have regularly considered whether to enjoin the enforcement of state laws that are alleged to violate federal law\xe2\x80\x94demonstrates that the Supremacy Clause creates a cause of action for its violation. They are incorrect. It is true enough that we have long held that federal courts may in some circumstances grant injunctive relief against state officers who are violating, or planning to violate, federal law. See, e.g., Osborn v. Bank of United States, 9 Wheat. 738, 838\xe2\x80\x93839, 844 (1824); Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young, supra, at 150\xe2\x80\x93151 (citing Davis v. Gray, 16 Wall. 203, 220 (1873)). But that has been true not only with respect to violations of federal law by state officials, but also with respect to violations of federal law by federal officials. See American School of Magnetic Healing v. McAnnulty,\n\n187 U. S. 94,\n110 (1902)\n; see generally L. Jaffe, Judicial Control of Administrative Action 152\xe2\x80\x93196 (1965). Thus, the Supremacy Clause need not be (and in light of our textual analysis above, cannot be) the explanation. What our cases demonstrate is that, \xe2\x80\x9cin a proper case, relief may be given in a court of equity .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to prevent an injurious act by a public officer.\xe2\x80\x9d Carroll v. Safford, 3 How. 441, 463 (1845).

\n

\n \n The ability to sue to enjoin unconstitutional actions by state and federal officers is the creation of courts of equity, and reflects a long history of judicial review of illegal executive action, tracing back to England. See Jaffe & Henderson, Judicial Review and the Rule of Law: Historical Origins, 72 L.\xc2\xa0Q. Rev. 345 (1956). It is a judge-made remedy, and we have never held or even suggested that, in its application to state officers, it rests upon an implied right of action contained in the Supremacy Clause. That is because, as even the dissent implicitly acknowledges, post, at 4 (opinion of Sotomayor,\xc2\xa0J.) it does not. The Ninth Circuit erred in holding otherwise.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n \n We turn next to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 contention that, quite apart from any cause of action conferred by the Supre-macy Clause, this suit can proceed against Idaho in equity.

\n

\n \n The power of federal courts of equity to enjoin unlawful executive action is subject to express and implied statutory limitations. See, e.g., Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Flor-ida,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 44,\n74 (1996)\n. \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Courts of equity can no more disregard statutory and constitutional requirements and provisions than can courts of law.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d INS v. Pangilinan,\n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 875,\n883 (1988)\n (quoting Hedges v. DixonCounty,\n\n150 U.\xc2\xa0S. 182,\n192 (1893)\n; brackets omitted). In our view the Medicaid Act implicitly precludes private enforcement of \xc2\xa730(A), and respondents cannot, by invoking our equitable powers, circumvent Congress\xe2\x80\x99s exclusion of private enforcement. See Douglas v. Independent Living Center of Southern Cal., Inc., 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2012) (Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting) (slip op., at 4\xe2\x80\x935).

\n

\n \n Two aspects of \xc2\xa730(A) establish Congress\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cintent to foreclose\xe2\x80\x9d equitable relief. Verizon Md. Inc. v. Public Serv. Comm\xe2\x80\x99n of Md.,\n\n535 U.\xc2\xa0S. 635,\n647 (2002)\n. First, the sole remedy Congress provided for a State\xe2\x80\x99s failure to comply with Medicaid\xe2\x80\x99s requirements\xe2\x80\x94for the State\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cbreach\xe2\x80\x9d of the Spending Clause contract\xe2\x80\x94is the withholding of Medicaid funds by the Secretary of Health and Human Services.\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396c. As we have elsewhere explained, the \xe2\x80\x9cexpress provision of one method of enforcing a substantive rule suggests that Congress intended to preclude others.\xe2\x80\x9d Alexander v. Sandoval,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 275,\n290 (2001)\n.

\n

\n \n The provision for the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement by withholding funds might not, by itself, preclude the availability of equitable relief. See Virginia Office for Protection and Advocacy v. Stewart,\n\n563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 247\n, ___\xe2\x80\x93___, n. 3 (2011) (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x938, n.\xc2\xa03). But it does so when combined with the judicially unadministrable nature of \xc2\xa730(A)\xe2\x80\x99s text. It is difficult to imagine a requirement broader and less specific than \xc2\xa730(A)\xe2\x80\x99s mandate that state plans provide for payments that are \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent with efficiency, economy, and quality of care,\xe2\x80\x9d all the while \xe2\x80\x9csafeguard[ing] against unnecessary utilization of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. care and services.\xe2\x80\x9d Explicitly conferring enforcement of this judgment-laden standard upon the Secretary alone establishes, we think, that Congress \xe2\x80\x9cwanted to make the agency remedy that it provided exclusive,\xe2\x80\x9d thereby achieving \xe2\x80\x9cthe expertise, uniformity, widespread consultation, and resulting administrative guidance that can accompany agency decisionmaking,\xe2\x80\x9d and avoiding \xe2\x80\x9cthe comparative risk of inconsistent interpretations and misincentives that can arise out of an occasional inappropriate application of the statute in a private action.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzaga Univ. v. Doe,\n\n536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 273,\n292 (2002)\n (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment). The sheer complexity associated with enforcing \xc2\xa730(A), coupled with the express provision of an administrative remedy, \xc2\xa71396c, shows that the Medicaid Act precludes private enforcement of \xc2\xa730(A) in the courts.

\n

B

\n

\n \n The dissent agrees with us that the Supremacy Clause does not provide an implied right of action, and that Congress may displace the equitable relief that is traditionally available to enforce federal law. It disagrees only with our conclusion that such displacement has occurred here.

\n

\n \n The dissent insists that, \xe2\x80\x9cbecause Congress is undoubtedly aware of the federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 long-established practice of enjoining preempted state action, it should generally be presumed to contemplate such enforcement unless it affirmatively manifests a contrary intent.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 4 (emphasis added). But a \xe2\x80\x9clong-established practice\xe2\x80\x9d does not justify a rule that denies statutory text its fairest reading. Section 30(A), fairly read in the context of the Medicaid Act, \xe2\x80\x9cdisplay[s] a[n] intent to foreclose\xe2\x80\x9d the availability of equitable relief. Verizon, supra, at 647. We have no warrant to revise Congress\xe2\x80\x99s scheme simply because it did not \xe2\x80\x9caffirmatively\xe2\x80\x9d preclude the availability of a judge-made action at equity. See Seminole Tribe, supra, at 75 (inferring, in the absence of an \xe2\x80\x9caffirmative\xe2\x80\x9d statement by Congress, that equitable relief was unavailable).

\n

\n \n Equally unavailing is the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on \xc2\xa730(A)\xe2\x80\x99s history. Section 30(A) was amended, on December 19, 1989, to include what the dissent calls the \xe2\x80\x9cequal access mandate,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 9\xe2\x80\x94the requirement that reimbursement rates be \xe2\x80\x9csufficient to enlist enough providers so that care and services are available under the plan at least to the extent that such care and services are available to the general population in the geographic area.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa76402(a),\n\n103Stat.\n2260. There existed at the time another provision, known as the \xe2\x80\x9cBoren Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d that likewise imposed broad requirements on state Medicaid plans. 42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396a(a)(13)(A) (1982 ed., Supp. V). Lower courts had interpreted the Boren Amendment to be privately enforceable under \xc2\xa71983. From this, the dissent infers that, when Congress amended \xc2\xa730(A), it could not \xe2\x80\x9chave failed to anticipate\xe2\x80\x9d that \xc2\xa730(A)\xe2\x80\x99s broad language\xe2\x80\x94or at least that of the equal access mandate\xe2\x80\x94would be interpreted as enforceable in a private action. Thus, concludes the dissent, Congress\xe2\x80\x99s failure to expressly preclude the private enforcement of \xc2\xa730(A) suggests it intended not to preclude private enforcement. Post, at 10.

\n

\n \n This argument appears to rely on the prior-construction canon; the rule that, when \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial interpretations have settled the meaning of an existing statutory provision, repetition of the same language in a new statute\xe2\x80\x9d is presumed to incorporate that interpretation. Bragdon v. Abbott,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 624,\n645 (1998)\n. But that canon has no application here. The language of the two provisions is nowhere near identical; and even if it had been, the question whether the Boren Amendment permitted private actions was far from \xe2\x80\x9csettled.\xe2\x80\x9d When Congress amended \xc2\xa730(A) in 1989, this Court had already granted certiorari to decide, but had not yet decided, whether the Boren Amendment could be enforced through a \xc2\xa71983 suit. See Baliles v. Virginia Hospital Assn.,\n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 808\n (Oct. 2, 1989) (granting certiorari). Our decision permitting a \xc2\xa71983 action did not issue until June 14, 1990\xe2\x80\x94almost six months after the amendment to \xc2\xa730(A). Wilder v. Virginia Hospital Assn.,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 498\n.[1]* The existence of a granted petition for certiorari demonstrates quite clearly that the question whether the Boren Amendment could be pri-vately enforced was unsettled at the time of \xc2\xa730(A)\xe2\x80\x99s 1989 amendment\xe2\x80\x94so that if Congress was aware of the parallel (which is highly doubtful) the course that awareness would have prompted (if any) would not have been legislative silence but rather express specification of the availability of private enforcement (if that was what Congress intended).

\n

\n \n Finally, the dissent speaks as though we leave these plaintiffs with no resort. That is not the case. Their relief must be sought initially through the Secretary rather than through the courts. The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s complaint that the sanction available to the Secretary (the cut-off of funding) is too massive to be a realistic source of relief seems to us mistaken. We doubt that the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s notice to aState that its compensation scheme is inadequate will be ignored.

\n

IV

\n

\n \n The last possible source of a cause of action for respondents is the Medicaid Act itself. They do not claim that, and rightly so. Section 30(A) lacks the sort of rights-creating language needed to imply a private right of action. Sandoval, supra at 286\xe2\x80\x93287. It is phrased as a directive to the federal agency charged with approving state Medicaid plans, not as a conferral of the right to sue upon the beneficiaries of the State\xe2\x80\x99s decision to participate in Medicaid. The Act says that the \xe2\x80\x9cSecretary shall approve any plan which fulfills the conditions specified in subsection (a),\xe2\x80\x9d the subsection that includes \xc2\xa730(A).\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396a(b). We have held that such language \xe2\x80\x9creveals no congressional intent to create a private right of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Sandoval, supra at 289; see also Universities Research Assn., Inc. v. Coutu,\n\n450 U.\xc2\xa0S. 754,\n772 (1981)\n. And again, the explicitly conferred means of enforcing compliance with \xc2\xa730(A) by the Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s withholding funding, \xc2\xa71396c, suggests that other means of enforcement are precluded, Sandoval, supra, at 290.

\n

\n \n Spending Clause legislation like Medicaid \xe2\x80\x9cis much in the nature of a contract.\xe2\x80\x9d Pennhurst State School and Hospital v. Halderman,\n\n451 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n17 (1981)\n. The notion that respondents have a right to sue derives, perhaps, from the fact that they are beneficiaries of the federal-state Medicaid agreement, and that intended beneficiaries, in modern times at least, can sue to enforce the obli-gations of private contracting parties. See 13 R. Lord, Williston on Contracts \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa737:12\xe2\x80\x9337.13, pp. 123\xe2\x80\x93135 (4th ed. 2013). We doubt, to begin with, that providers are intended beneficiaries (as opposed to mere incidental beneficiar-ies) of the Medicaid agreement, which was concluded for the benefit of the infirm whom the providers were to serve, rather than for the benefit of the providers themselves. See Pharmaceutical Research and Mfrs. of America v. Walsh,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 644,\n683 (2003)\n (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). More fundamentally, however, the modern jurisprudence permitting intended beneficiaries to sue does not generally apply to contracts between a private party and the government, Astra USA, Inc. v. Santa Clara County, 563 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 6); see Williston, supra, at \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa737:35\xe2\x80\x9337:36, at 256\xe2\x80\x93271; 9 J. Murray, Corbin on Contracts \xc2\xa745.6, p. 92 (rev. ed. 2007)\xe2\x80\x94much less to contracts between two governments. Our precedents establish that a private right of action under federal law is not created by mere implication, but must be \xe2\x80\x9cunambiguously conferred,\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzaga, 536 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 283. Nothing in the Medicaid Act suggests that Congress meant to change that for the commitments made under \xc2\xa730(A).

RICHARD ARMSTRONG, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. EXCEPTIONAL CHILD CENTER, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

[March 31, 2015]

Justice Breyer, concurring in part and concurring in the judgment.

I join Parts I, II, and III of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

Like all other Members of the Court, I would not characterize the question before us in terms of a Supremacy Clause \xe2\x80\x9ccause of action.\xe2\x80\x9d Rather, I would ask whether \xe2\x80\x9cfederal courts may in [these] circumstances grant injunctive relief against state officers who are violating, or planning to violate, federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5; post, at 4 (Sotomayor, J., dissenting). I believe the answer to this question is no.

That answer does not follow from the application of a simple, fixed legal formula separating federal statutes that may underlie this kind of injunctive action from those that may not. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]he statute books are too many, the laws too diverse, and their purposes too complex, for any single legal formula to offer\xe2\x80\x9d courts \xe2\x80\x9cmore than general guidance.\xe2\x80\x9d Gonzaga Univ. v. Doe,536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 273,291 (2012) (Breyer, J., concurring in judgment). Rather, I believe that several characteristics of the federal statute before us, when taken together, make clear that Congress intended to foreclose respondents from bringing this particular action for injunctive relief.

For one thing, as the majority points out, \xc2\xa730(A) of the Medicaid Act,42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396a(a)(30)(A), sets forth a federal mandate that is broad and nonspecific. See ante, at 7. But, more than that, \xc2\xa730(A) applies its broad standards to the setting of rates. The history of ratemaking demonstrates that administrative agencies are far better suited to this task than judges. More than a century ago, Congress created the Interstate Commerce Commission, the first great federal regulatory rate-setting agency, and endowed it with authority to set \xe2\x80\x9creasonable\xe2\x80\x9d railroad rates. Ch. 104,24Stat.379 (1887). It did so in part because judicial efforts to maintain reasonable rate levels had proved inadequate. See I. Sharfman, Railway Regulation: An Analysis of the Underlying Problems in Railway Economics from the Standpoint of Government Regulation 43\xe2\x80\x9344 (1915).

Reading \xc2\xa730(A) underscores the complexity and nonjudicial nature of the rate-setting task. That provision requires State Medicaid plans to \xe2\x80\x9cassure that payments are consistent with efficiency, economy, and quality of care and are sufficient to enlist enough providers\xe2\x80\x9d to assure \xe2\x80\x9ccare and services\xe2\x80\x9d equivalent to that \xe2\x80\x9cavailable to the general population in the geographic area.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71396a(a)(30)(A). The methods that a state agency, such as Idaho\xe2\x80\x99s Department of Health and Welfare, uses to make this kind of determination may involve subsidiary determinations of, for example, the actual cost of providing quality services, including personnel and total operating expenses; changes in public expectations with respect to delivery of services; inflation; a comparison of rates paid in neighboring States for comparable services; and a comparison of any rates paid for comparable services in other public or private capacities. See App. to Reply to Brief in Opposition 16; Idaho Code Ann. \xc2\xa756\xe2\x80\x93118 (2012).

At the same time, \xc2\xa730(A) applies broadly, covering reimbursements provided to approximately 1.36 million doctors, serving over 69 million patients across the Nation. See Dept. of Health and Human Servs., Office of Inspector General, Access to Care: Provider Availability in Medicaid Managed Care 1, 5 (Dec. 2014). And States engage in time-consuming efforts to obtain public input on proposed plan amendments. See, e.g., Kansas Medicaid: Design and Implementation of a Public Input and Stakeholder Consult Process (Sept. 16, 2011) (prepared by Deloitte Consulting, LLP) (describing public input on Kansas\xe2\x80\x99 proposed Medicaid amendments).

I recognize that federal courts have long become accustomed to reviewing for reasonableness or constitutionality the rate-setting determinations made by agencies. See5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7706; FPC v. Hope Natural Gas Co.,320 U.\xc2\xa0S. 591\xe2\x80\x93606 (1944). But this is not such an action. Instead, the lower courts here, relying on the rate-setting standard articulated in Orthopaedic Hospital v. Belshe, 103 F. 3d 1491 (CA9 1997), required the State to set rates that \xe2\x80\x9capproximate the cost of quality care provided efficiently and economically.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1496. See Inclusion, Inc. v. Armstrong, 835 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 960, 963\xe2\x80\x93964 (Idaho 2011), aff\xe2\x80\x99d, 567 Fed. Appx. 496 (CA9 2014). To find in the law a basis for courts to engage in such direct rate-setting could set a precedent for allowing other similar actions, potentially resulting in rates set by federal judges (of whom there are several hundred) outside the ordinary channel of federal judicial review of agency decisionmaking. The consequence, I fear, would be increased litigation, inconsistent results, and disorderly administration of highly complex federal programs that demand public consultation, administrative guidance and coherence for their success. I do not believe Congress intended to allow a statute-based injunctive action that poses such risks (and that has the other features I mention).

I recognize that courts might in particular instances be able to resolve rate-related requests for injunctive relief quite easily. But I see no easy way to separate in advance the potentially simple sheep from the more harmful rate-making goats. In any event, this case, I fear, belongs in the latter category. See Belshe, supra, at 1496. Compare Brief for Respondents 2, n.\xc2\xa01 (claiming that respondents seek only to enforce federally approved methodology), with Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 5, n.\xc2\xa02 (the relevant methodology has not been approved). See also Idaho Code Ann. \xc2\xa756\xe2\x80\x93118 (describing in general terms what appears to be a complex rate-setting methodology, while leaving unclear the extent to which Idaho is bound to use, rather than merely consider, actual provider costs).

For another thing, like the majority, I would ask why, in the complex rate-setting area, other forms of relief are inadequate. If the Secretary of Health and Human Services concludes that a State is failing to follow legally required federal rules, the Secretary can withhold federal funds. See ante, at 7 (citing42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396c). If withholding funds does not work, the federal agency may be able to sue a State to compel compliance with federal rules. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 23, 52 (Solicitor General and respondents acknowledging that the Federal Government might be able to sue a State to enjoin it from paying less than what \xc2\xa730(A) requires). Cf., e.g., Arizona v. United States, 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. __ (2012) (allowing similar action in another context).

Moreover, why could respondents not ask the federal agency to interpret its rules to respondents\xe2\x80\x99 satisfaction, to modify those rules, to promulgate new rules or to enforce old ones? See5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7553(e). Normally, when such requests are denied, an injured party can seek judicial review of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s refusal on the grounds that it is \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7702, 706(2)(A). And an injured party can ask the court to \xe2\x80\x9ccompel agency action unlawfully withheld or unreasonably delayed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7702, 706(1). See also Tr. of Oral Arg. 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (arguing that providers can bring an action under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) whenever a waiver program is renewed or can seek new agency rulemaking); Japan Whaling Assn. v. American Cetacean Soc.,478 U.\xc2\xa0S. 221, n.\xc2\xa04, 231 (1986) (APA challenge to the Secretary of Commerce\xe2\x80\x99s failure to act).

I recognize that the law may give the federal agency broad discretionary authority to decide when and how to exercise or to enforce statutes and rules. See Massachusetts v. EPA,549 U.\xc2\xa0S. 497,527 (2007). As a result, it may be difficult for respondents to prevail on an APA claim unless it stems from an agency\xe2\x80\x99s particularly egregious failure to act. But, if that is so, it is because Congress decided to vest broad discretion in the agency to interpret and to enforce \xc2\xa730(A). I see no reason for this Court to circumvent that congressional determination by allowing this action to proceed.

SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

_________________

No. 14\xe2\x80\x9315

_________________

RICHARD ARMSTRONG, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. EXCEPTIONAL CHILD CENTER, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

[March 31, 2015]

Justice Sotomayor, with whom Justice Kennedy, Justice Ginsburg, and Justice Kagan join, dissenting.

Suits in federal court to restrain state officials from executing laws that assertedly conflict with the Constitution or with a federal statue are not novel. To the contrary, this Court has adjudicated such requests for equitable relief since the early days of the Republic. Nevertheless, today the Court holds that Congress has foreclosed private parties from invoking the equitable powers of the federal courts to require States to comply with \xc2\xa730(A) of the Medicaid Act,42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396a(a)(30)(A). It does so without pointing to the sort of detailed remedial scheme we have previously deemed necessary to establish congressional intent to preclude resort to equity. Instead, the Court relies on Congress\xe2\x80\x99 provision for agency enforcement of \xc2\xa730(A)\xe2\x80\x94an enforcement mechanism of the sort we have already definitively determined not to foreclose private actions\xe2\x80\x94and on the mere fact that \xc2\xa730(A) contains relatively broad language. As I cannot agree that these statutory provisions demonstrate the requisite congressional intent to restrict the equitable authority of the federal courts, I respectfully dissent.

I

A

That parties may call upon the federal courts to enjoin unconstitutional government action is not subject to serious dispute. Perhaps the most famous exposition of this principle is our decision in Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young,209 U.\xc2\xa0S. 123 (1908), from which the doctrine derives its usual name. There, we held that the shareholders of a railroad could seek an injunction preventing the Minnesota attorney general from enforcing a state law setting maximum railroad rates because the Eleventh Amendment did not provide the officials with immunity from such an action and the federal court had the \xe2\x80\x9cpower\xe2\x80\x9d in equity to \xe2\x80\x9cgrant a temporary injunction.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 148. This Court had earlier recognized similar equitable authority in Osborn v. Bank of United States, 9 Wheat. 738 (1824), in which a federal court issued an injunction prohibiting an Ohio official from executing a state law taxing the Bank of the United States. Id., at 838\xe2\x80\x93839. We affirmed in relevant part, concluding that the case was \xe2\x80\x9ccognizable in a Court of equity,\xe2\x80\x9d and holding it to be \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d to grant equitable relief insofar as the state tax was \xe2\x80\x9crepugnant\xe2\x80\x9d to the federal law creating the national bank. Id., at 839, 859. More recently, we confirmed the vitality of this doctrine in Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Bd.,561 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477 (2010). There, we found no support for the argument that a challenge to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98governmental action under the Appointments Clause or separation-of-powers principles\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d should be treated \xe2\x80\x9cdifferently than every other constitutional claim\xe2\x80\x9d for which \xe2\x80\x9cequitable relief \xe2\x80\x98has long been recognized as the proper means for preventing entities from acting unconstitutionally.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 491, n.\xc2\xa02.

A suit, like this one, that seeks relief against state officials acting pursuant to a state law allegedly preempted by a federal statute falls comfortably within this doc-trine. A claim that a state law contravenes a federal statute is \xe2\x80\x9cbasically constitutional in nature, deriving its force from the operation of the Supremacy Clause,\xe2\x80\x9d Douglas v. Seacoast Products, Inc.,431 U.\xc2\xa0S. 265\xe2\x80\x93272 (1977), and the application of preempted state law is therefore \xe2\x80\x9cunconstitutional,\xe2\x80\x9d Crosby v. National Foreign Trade Council,530 U.\xc2\xa0S. 363,388 (2000); accord, e.g., McCulloch v. Maryland, 4 Wheat. 316, 436 (1819) (that States have \xe2\x80\x9cno power\xe2\x80\x9d to enact laws interfering with \xe2\x80\x9cthe operations of the constitutional laws enacted by Congress\xe2\x80\x9d is the \xe2\x80\x9cunavoidable consequence of that supremacy which the constitution has declared\xe2\x80\x9d; such a state law \xe2\x80\x9cis unconstitutional and void\xe2\x80\x9d). We have thus long entertained suits in which a party seeks prospective equitable protection from an injurious and preempted state law without regard to whether the federal statute at issue itself provided a right to bring an action. See, e.g., Foster v. Love,522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 67 (1997) (state election law that permitted the winner of a state primary to be deemed the winner of election to Congress held preempted by federal statute setting date of congressional elections); Shaw v. Delta Air Lines, Inc.,463 U.\xc2\xa0S. 85 (1983) (state law preempted in part by the federal Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974); Railroad Transfer Service, Inc. v. Chicago,386 U.\xc2\xa0S. 351 (1967) (city ordinance imposing licensing requirements on motor carrier transporting railroad passengers held preempted by federal Interstate Commerce Act); Campbell v. Hussey,368 U.\xc2\xa0S. 297 (1961) (state law requiring labeling of certain strains of tobacco held preempted by the federal Tobacco Inspection Act); Railway Co. v. McShane, 22 Wall. 444 (1875) (state taxation of land possessed by railroad company held invalid under federal Act of July 2, 1864). Indeed, for this reason, we have characterized \xe2\x80\x9cthe availability of prospective relief of the sort awarded in Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young\xe2\x80\x9d as giving \xe2\x80\x9clife to the Supremacy Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Green v. Mansour,474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 64 (1985).

Thus, even though the Court is correct that it is somewhat misleading to speak of \xe2\x80\x9can implied right of action contained in the Supremacy Clause,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6, that does not mean that parties may not enforce the Supremacy Clause by bringing suit to enjoin preempted state action. As the Court also recognizes, we \xe2\x80\x9chave long held that federal courts may in some circumstances grant injunctive relief against state officers who are violating, or planning to violate, federal law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5.

B

Most important for purposes of this case is not the mere existence of this equitable authority, but the fact that it is exceedingly well established\xe2\x80\x94supported, as the Court puts it, by a \xe2\x80\x9clong history.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. Congress may, if it so chooses, either expressly or implicitly preclude Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young enforcement actions with respect to a particular statute or category of lawsuit. See, e.g., 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71341 (prohibiting federal judicial restraints on the collection of state taxes); Seminole Tribe of Fla. v. Florida,517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 44\xe2\x80\x9376 (1996) (comprehensive alternative remedial scheme can establish Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to foreclose Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young actions). But because Congress is undoubtedly aware of the federal courts\xe2\x80\x99 long-established practice of enjoining preempted state action, it should generally be presumed to contemplate such enforcement unless it affirmatively manifests a contrary intent. \xe2\x80\x9cUnless a statute in so many words, or by a necessary and inescapable inference, restricts the court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction in equity, the full scope of that jurisdiction is to be recognized and applied.\xe2\x80\x9d Porter v. Warner Holding Co.,328 U.\xc2\xa0S. 395,398 (1946).

In this respect, equitable preemption actions differ from suits brought by plaintiffs invoking42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983 or an implied right of action to enforce a federal statute. Suits for \xe2\x80\x9credress designed to halt or prevent the constitutional violation rather than the award of money damages\xe2\x80\x9d seek \xe2\x80\x9ctraditional forms of relief.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Stanley,483 U.\xc2\xa0S. 669,683 (1987). By contrast, a plaintiff invoking \xc2\xa71983 or an implied statutory cause of action may seek a variety of remedies\xe2\x80\x94including damages\xe2\x80\x94from a potentially broad range of parties. Rather than simply pointing to background equitable principles authorizing the action that Congress presumably has not overridden, such a plaintiff must demonstrate specific congressional intent to create a statutory right to these remedies. See Gonzaga Univ. v. Doe,536 U.\xc2\xa0S. 273,290 (2002); Alexander v. Sandoval,532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 275,286 (2001); see also Golden State Transit Corp. v. Los Angeles,493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103,114 (1989) (Kennedy, J., dissenting) (Because a preemption claim does not seek to enforce a statutory right, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he injured party does not need \xc2\xa71983 to vest in him a right to assert that an attempted exercise of jurisdiction or control violates the proper distribution of powers within the federal system\xe2\x80\x9d). For these reasons, the principles that we have developed to determine whether a statute creates an implied right of action, or is enforceable through \xc2\xa71983, are not transferable to the Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young context.

II

In concluding that Congress has \xe2\x80\x9cimplicitly preclude[d] private enforcement of \xc2\xa730(A),\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6, the Court ignores this critical distinction and threatens the vitality of our Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young jurisprudence. The Court identifies only a single prior decision\xe2\x80\x94Seminole Tribe\xe2\x80\x94in which we have ever discerned such congressional intent to foreclose equitable enforcement of a statutory mandate. Ante, at 6. Even the most cursory review of that decision reveals how far afield it is from this case.

In Seminole Tribe, the plaintiff Indian Tribe had invoked Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young in seeking to compel the State of Florida to \xe2\x80\x9cnegotiate in good faith with [the] tribe toward the formation of a compact\xe2\x80\x9d governing certain gaming activities, as required by a provision of the Indian Gaming Regulatory Act,25 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72710(d)(3). 517 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 47. We rejected this effort, observing that \xe2\x80\x9cCongress passed \xc2\xa72710(d)(3) in conjunction with the carefully crafted and intricate remedial scheme set forth in \xc2\xa72710(d)(7).\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 73\xe2\x80\x9374. That latter provision allowed a tribe to sue for violations of the duty to negotiate 180 days after requesting such negotiations, but specifically limited the remedy that a court could grant to \xe2\x80\x9can order directing the State and the Indian tribe to conclude a compact within 60 days,\xe2\x80\x9d and provided that the only sanction for the violation of such an order would be to require the parties to \xe2\x80\x9csub-mit a proposed compact to a mediator.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 74; \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(B)(i), (iii), (iv). The statute further directed that if the State should fail to abide by the mediator\xe2\x80\x99s selected compact, the sole remedy would be for the Secretary of the Interior, in consultation with the tribe, to prescribe regulations governing gaming. See 517 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 74\xe2\x80\x9375; \xc2\xa72710(d)(7)(B)(vii). We concluded that Congress must have intended this procedural route to be the exclusive means of enforcing \xc2\xa72710(d)(3). As we explained: \xe2\x80\x9cIf \xc2\xa72710(d)(3) could be enforced in a suit under Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young, \xc2\xa72710(d)(7) would have been superfluous; it is difficult to see why an Indian tribe would suffer through the intricate scheme of \xc2\xa72710(d)(7) when more complete and more immediate relief would be available under Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young.\xe2\x80\x9d 517 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 75.

What is the equivalent \xe2\x80\x9ccarefully crafted and intricate remedial scheme\xe2\x80\x9d for enforcement of \xc2\xa730(A)? The Court relies on two aspects of the Medicaid Act, but, whether considered separately or in combination, neither suffices.

First, the Court cites42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71396c, which authorizes the Secretary of Health and Human Services (HHS) to withhold federal Medicaid payments to a State in whole or in part if the Secretary determines that the State has failed to comply with the obligations set out in \xc2\xa71396a, including \xc2\xa730(A). See ante, at 7\xe2\x80\x938. But in striking contrast to the remedial provision set out in the Indian Gaming Regulatory Act, \xc2\xa71396c provides no specific procedure that parties actually affected by a State\xe2\x80\x99s violation of its statutory obligations may invoke in lieu of Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young\xe2\x80\x94leaving them without any other avenue for seeking relief from the State. Nor will \xc2\xa71396c always provide a particularly effective means for redressing a State\xe2\x80\x99s violations: If the State has violated \xc2\xa730(A) by refusing to reimburse medical providers at a level \xe2\x80\x9csufficient to enlist enough providers so that care and services are available\xe2\x80\x9d to Medicaid beneficiaries to the same extent as they are available to \xe2\x80\x9cthe general population,\xe2\x80\x9d agency action resulting in a reduced flow of federal funds to that State will often be self-defeating. \xc2\xa71396a(30)(A); see Brief for Former HHS Officials as Amici Curiae 18 (noting that HHS is often reluctant to initiate compliance actions because a \xe2\x80\x9cstate\xe2\x80\x99s non-compliance creates a damned-if-you-do, damned-if-you-don\xe2\x80\x99t scenario where the withholding of state funds will lead to depriving the poor of essential medical assistance\xe2\x80\x9d). Far from rendering \xc2\xa71396c \xe2\x80\x9csuperfluous,\xe2\x80\x9d then, Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young actions would seem to be an anticipated and possibly necessary supplement to this limited agency-enforcement mechanism. Seminole Tribe, 517 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 75. Indeed, presumably for these reasons, we recently rejected the very contention the Court now accepts, holding that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he fact that the Federal Government can exercise oversight of a federal spending program and even withhold or withdraw funds . . . does not demonstrate that Congress has displayed an intent not to provide the more complete and more immediate relief that would otherwise be available under Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young.\xe2\x80\x9d Virginia Office for Protection and Advocacy v. Stewart,563 U.\xc2\xa0S. 247, ____\xe2\x80\x93____, n.\xc2\xa03 (2011) (slip op., at 7\xe2\x80\x938, n.\xc2\xa03) (internal quotation marks omitted).

Section 1396c also parallels other provisions scattered throughout the Social Security Act that likewise authorize the withholding of federal funds to States that fail to fulfill their obligations. See, e.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7609(a), 1204, 1354. Yet, we have consistently authorized judicial enforcement of the Act. See Maine v. Thiboutot,448 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,6 (1980) (collecting cases). Rosado v. Wyman,397 U.\xc2\xa0S. 397 (1970), provides a fitting illustration. There, we considered a provision of the Social Security Act mandating that, in calculating benefits for participants in the Aid to Families with Dependent Children Program, States make adjustments \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to reflect fully changes in living costs.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 412 (quoting \xc2\xa7602(a)(23) (1964 ed., Supp. IV)). We expressed no hesitation in concluding that federal courts could require compliance with this obligation, explaining: \xe2\x80\x9cIt is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. peculiarly part of the duty of this tribunal, no less in the welfare field than in other areas of the law, to resolve disputes as to whether federal funds allocated to the States are being expended in consonance with the conditions that Congress has attached to their use.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 422\xe2\x80\x93423. We so held notwithstanding the existence of an enforcement provision permitting a federal agency to \xe2\x80\x9cmake a total or partial cutoff of federal funds.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 406, n.\xc2\xa08 (citing \xc2\xa71316).

Second, perhaps attempting to reconcile its treatment of \xc2\xa71396c (2012 ed.) with this longstanding precedent, the Court focuses on the particular language of \xc2\xa730(A), contending that this provision, at least, is so \xe2\x80\x9cjudicially unadministrable\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress must have intended to preclude its enforcement in private suits. Ante, at 7. Admittedly, the standard set out in \xc2\xa730(A) is fairly broad, requiring that a state Medicaid plan:

\xe2\x80\x9cprovide such methods and procedures relating to the utilization of, and the payment for, care and services available under the plan .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. as may be necessary to safeguard against unnecessary utilization of such care and services and to assure that payments are consistent with efficiency, economy, and quality of care and are sufficient to enlist enough providers so that care and services are available under the plan at least to the extent that such care and services are available to the general population in the geographic area.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71396a(a)(30)(A).

But mere breadth of statutory language does not require the Court to give up all hope of judicial enforcement\xe2\x80\x94or, more important, to infer that Congress must have done so.

In fact, the contention that \xc2\xa730(A)\xe2\x80\x99s language was intended to foreclose private enforcement actions entirely is difficult to square with the provision\xe2\x80\x99s history. The spe-cific equal access mandate invoked by the plaintiffs in this case\xe2\x80\x94that reimbursement rates be \xe2\x80\x9csufficient to enlist enough providers so that care and services are available under the plan at least to the extent that such care and services are available to the general population in the geographic area\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94was added to \xc2\xa730(A) in 1989.103Stat.2260. At that time, multiple Federal Courts of Appeals had held that the so-called Boren Amendment to the Medicaid Act was enforceable pursuant to \xc2\xa71983\xe2\x80\x94as we soon thereafter concluded it was. See Wilder v. Virginia Hospital Assn.,496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 498\xe2\x80\x93505, 524 (1990). The Boren Amendment employed language quite similar to that used in \xc2\xa730(A), requiring that a state plan:

\xe2\x80\x9cprovide .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for payment .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of the hospital services, nursing facility services, and services in an intermediate care facility for the mentally retarded provided under the plan through the use of rates .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. which the State finds, and makes assurances satisfactory to the Secretary, are reasonable and adequate to meet the costs which must be incurred by efficiently and economically operated facilities in order to provide care and services in conformity with applicable State and Federal laws, regulations, and quality and safety standards and to assure that individuals eligible for medical assistance have reasonable access .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to in-patient hospital services of adequate quality.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71396a(a)(13)(A) (1982 ed., Supp. V).

It is hard to believe that the Congress that enacted the operative version of \xc2\xa730(A) could have failed to anticipate that it might be similarly enforceable. Even if, as the Court observes, the question whether the Boren Amendment was enforceable under \xc2\xa71983 was \xe2\x80\x9cunsettled at the time,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 10 (emphasis deleted), surely Congress would have spoken with far more clarity had it actually intended to preclude private enforcement of \xc2\xa730(A) through not just \xc2\xa71983 but also Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young.

Of course, the broad scope of \xc2\xa730(A)\xe2\x80\x99s language is not irrelevant. But rather than compelling the conclusion that the provision is wholly unenforceable by private parties, its breadth counsels in favor of interpreting \xc2\xa730(A) to provide substantial leeway to States, so that only in rare and extreme circumstances could a State actually be held to violate its mandate. The provision\xe2\x80\x99s scope may also often require a court to rely on HHS, which is \xe2\x80\x9ccomparatively expert in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s subject matter.\xe2\x80\x9d Douglas v. Independent Living Center of Southern Cal., Inc., 565 U.\xc2\xa0S ___, ___ (2012) (slip op., at 7). When the agency has made a determination with respect to what legal standard should apply, or the validity of a State\xe2\x80\x99s procedures for implementing its Medicaid plan, that determination should be accorded the appropriate deference. See, e.g., Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc.,467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984); Skidmore v. Swift & Co.,323 U.\xc2\xa0S. 134 (1944). And if faced with a question that presents a special demand for agency expertise, a court might call for the views of the agency, or refer the question to the agency under the doctrine of primary jurisdiction. See Rosado, 397 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 406\xe2\x80\x93407; Pharmaceutical Research and Mfrs. of America v. Walsh,538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 644,673 (2003) (Breyer, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). Finally, because the authority invoked for enforcing \xc2\xa730(A) is equitable in nature, a plaintiff is not entitled to relief as of right, but only in the sound discretion of the court. See Amoco Production Co. v. Gambell,480 U.\xc2\xa0S. 531,542 (1987). Given the courts\xe2\x80\x99 ability to both respect States\xe2\x80\x99 legitimate choices and defer to the federal agency when necessary, I see no basis for presuming that Congress believed the Judiciary to be completely incapable of enforcing \xc2\xa730(A).[1]*

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

In sum, far from identifying a \xe2\x80\x9ccarefully crafted .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. remedial scheme\xe2\x80\x9d demonstrating that Congress intended to foreclose Ex\xc2\xa0parte Young enforcement of \xc2\xa730(A), Seminole Tribe, 517 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 73\xe2\x80\x9374, the Court points only to two provisions. The first is \xc2\xa71396c, an agency-enforcement provision that, given our precedent, cannot preclude private actions. The second is \xc2\xa730(A) itself, which, while perhaps broad, cannot be understood to mani-fest congressional intent to preclude judicial involvement.

The Court\xe2\x80\x99s error today has very real consequences. Previously, a State that set reimbursement rates so low that providers were unwilling to furnish a covered service for those who need it could be compelled by those affected to respect the obligation imposed by \xc2\xa730(A). Now, it must suffice that a federal agency, with many programs to oversee, has authority to address such violations through the drastic and often counterproductive measure of withholding the funds that pay for such services. Because a faithful application of our precedents would have led to a contrary result, I respectfully dissent.
