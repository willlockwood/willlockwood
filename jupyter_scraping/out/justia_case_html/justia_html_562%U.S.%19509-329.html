CHASE BANK USA, N.\xc2\xa0A. v. McCOY, individually and on behalf of all others similarly situated

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x93329.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 8, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 24, 2011

\n

Regulation Z\xe2\x80\x94promulgated by the Federal Reserve Board (Board) pursuant to its authority under the Truth in Lending Act\xe2\x80\x94requires credit card issuers to disclose certain information to cardholders. The version of the regulation in effect at the time this dispute arose obliges issuers to provide to cardholders an \xe2\x80\x9c[i]nitial disclosure statement,\xe2\x80\x9d 12 CFR \xc2\xa7226.6, specifying \xe2\x80\x9ceach periodic rate that may be used to compute the finance charge,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7226.6(a)(2). It also imposes \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ubsequent disclosure requirements,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7226.9, including notice to cardholders \xe2\x80\x9c[w]henever any term required to be disclosed under \xc2\xa7226.6 is changed,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1). When \xe2\x80\x9ca periodic rate or other finance charge is increased because of the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s delinquency or default,\xe2\x80\x9d notice must be given \xe2\x80\x9cbefore the effective date of the change.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the time respondent McCoy filed suit, he was the holder of a credit card issued by petitioner Chase Bank. The cardholder agreement provided, in relevant part, that McCoy was eligible for \xe2\x80\x9cPreferred rates\xe2\x80\x9d as long as he met certain conditions. If any of those conditions were not met, Chase reserved the right to raise the rate, up to a pre-set maximum, and to apply the change to both existing and new balances. McCoy alleges that Chase increased his interest rate due to his delinquency or default and applied that increase retroactively, and that this action violated Regulation Z because Chase did not notify him of the increase until after it had taken effect. The District Court dismissed his complaint, holding that because the increase did not constitute a \xe2\x80\x9cchange in terms\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7226.9(c), Chase was not required to notify him of the increase before implementing it. The Ninth Circuit reversed in relevant part, holding that Regulation Z requires issuers to provide notice of an interest-rate increase prior to its effective date.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0At the time of the transactions at issue, Regulation Z did not require Chase to provide McCoy with a change-in-terms notice before implementing the agreement term allowing it to raise his interest rate, up to a pre-set maximum, following delinquency or default. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0This case requires the Court to determine the meaning of a regulation promulgated by the Board under its statutory authority. However, Regulation Z\xe2\x80\x99s text is unclear with respect to the crucial interpretive question at issue: whether a change to an interest rate, pursuant to previously-disclosed contractual provision, constitutes a change to a \xe2\x80\x9cterm required to be disclosed under \xc2\xa7226.6\xe2\x80\x9d requiring a subsequent disclosure under \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1). Because of this ambiguity, the Court must look to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s own interpretation of the regulation for guidance in deciding this case. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Board has made clear in its amicus brief to this Court that, in its view, Chase was not required to give McCoy notice of the interest rate increase under the applicable version of Regulation Z. This Court defers to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its own regulation, advanced in a legal brief, unless that interpretation is \xe2\x80\x9cplainly erroneous or inconsistent with the regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Auer v. Robbins, 519 U. S. 452, 461 (internal quotation marks omitted). In Auer, the Court deferred to the Secretary of Labor\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of his own regulation, presented in an amicus brief submitted by the agency at the Court\xe2\x80\x99s invitation. The Court held that the fact that the interpretation came in a legal brief did not, \xe2\x80\x9cin the circumstances of th[at] case, make it unworthy of deference.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 462. The interpretation was \xe2\x80\x9cin no sense a post hoc rationalization advanced by an agency seeking to defend past agency action against attack,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted), and there was \xe2\x80\x9cno reason to suspect that the interpretation [did] not reflect the agency\xe2\x80\x99s fair and considered judgment on the matter in question,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. The brief submitted by the Board here, at the Court\xe2\x80\x99s invitation, is no different. As in Auer, there is no reason to believe that the Board\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is a \xe2\x80\x9cpost hoc rationalization\xe2\x80\x9d taken as a litigation position, for the Board is not a party to this case. And its interpretation is neither \xe2\x80\x9cplainly erroneous\xe2\x80\x9d nor \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with\xe2\x80\x9d the indeterminate text of Regulation Z. Thus, there is no reason to suspect that the Board\xe2\x80\x99s position in its amicus brief reflects anything other than its fair and considered judgment as to what the regulation required at the time this dispute arose. That Congress and the Board may currently hold a different view does not mean that deference is not warranted to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s understanding of what the applicable version of Regulation Z required. Under Auer, therefore, it is clear that deference to the interpretation in the agency amicus brief is warranted. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0McCoy errs in arguing that deference to a legal brief is inappropriate because the interpretation of Regulation Z in the Official Staff Commentary commands a different result. While Commentary promulgated by the Board as an interpretation of Regulation Z may warrant deference as a general matter, the Commentary explaining the requirements at issue in this case largely replicates the ambiguity present in the regulatory text, and therefore offers no reason to disregard the interpretation advanced in the Board\xe2\x80\x99s amicus brief. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

559 F.\xc2\xa03d 963, reversed and remanded.

CHASE BANK USA, N. A., PETITIONER v. JAMES A.\nMcCOY, individually and on behalf of all \nothers similarly situated

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[January 24, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As applicable to this case, Regulation Z\xe2\x80\x94promulgated by the Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System (Board) pursuant to its authority under the Truth in Lending Act (TILA), 82 Stat. 146, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71601 et seq.\xe2\x80\x94requires that issuers of credit cards provide cardholders with an \xe2\x80\x9c[i]nitial disclosure statement\xe2\x80\x9d specifying, inter alia, \xe2\x80\x9ceach periodic rate\xe2\x80\x9d associated with the account. 12 CFR \xc2\xa7226.6(a)(2) (2008). The regulation also imposes \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ubsequent disclosure requirements,\xe2\x80\x9d including notice to cardholders \xe2\x80\x9c[w]henever any term required to be disclosed under \xc2\xa7226.6 is changed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1). This case presents the question whether Regulation Z requires an issuer to notify a cardholder of an interest-rate increase instituted pursuant to a provision of the cardholder agreement giving the issuer discretion to increase the rate, up to a stated maximum, in the event of the cardholder\xe2\x80\x99s delinquency or default. We conclude that the version of Reg-\nulation Z applicable in this case does not require such notice.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress passed TILA to promote consumers\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cinformed use of credit\xe2\x80\x9d by requiring \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningful disclosure of credit terms,\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71601(a), and granted the Board the authority to issue regulations to achieve TILA\xe2\x80\x99s purposes, \xc2\xa71604(a). Pursuant to this authority, the Board promulgated Regulation Z, which requires credit card issuers to disclose certain information to consumers.[Footnote 1] Two provisions of Regulation Z are at issue in this case. The first, 12 CFR \xc2\xa7226.6, explains what information credit card issuers are obliged to provide to cardholders in the \xe2\x80\x9c[i]nitial disclosure statement,\xe2\x80\x9d including \xe2\x80\x9ceach periodic rate that may be used to compute the finance charge.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7226.6(a)(2). The second, \xc2\xa7226.9, imposes upon issuers certain \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ubsequent disclosure requirements,\xe2\x80\x9d including a requirement to provide notice \xe2\x80\x9c[w]henever any term required to be disclosed under \xc2\xa7226.6 is changed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1). As a general matter, notice of a change in terms has to be provided 15 days in advance of the effective date of the change. Ibid. When \xe2\x80\x9ca periodic rate or other finance charge is increased because of the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s delinquency or default,\xe2\x80\x9d however, notice only need be given \xe2\x80\x9cbefore the effective date of the change.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Regulation Z also explains that no notice is required under \xc2\xa7226.9 when the change in terms \xe2\x80\x9cresults from \xe2\x80\xa6 the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s default or delinquency (other than an increase in the periodic rate or other finance charge).\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(2).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The official interpretation of Regulation Z (Official Staff Commentary or Commentary) promulgated by the Board explains these requirements further: Section 226.9(c)(1)\xe2\x80\x99s notice-of-change requirement does not apply \xe2\x80\x9cif the specific change is set forth initially, such as \xe2\x80\xa6 an increase that occurs when the consumer has been under an agreement to maintain a certain balance in a savings account in order to keep a particular rate and the account balance falls below the specified minimum.\xe2\x80\x9d 12 CFR pt. 226, Supp. I, Comment 9(c)\xe2\x80\x931, p.\xc2\xa0506 (2008) (hereinafter Comment 9(c)\xe2\x80\x931). On the other hand, the Commentary explains, \xe2\x80\x9cnotice must be given if the contract allows the creditor to increase the rate at its discretion but does not include specific terms for an increase (for example, when an increase may occur under the creditor\xe2\x80\x99s contract reservation right to increase the periodic rate).\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. As to the timing requirements, the Commentary states: \xe2\x80\x9c[A] notice of change in terms is required, but it may be mailed or delivered as late as the effective date of the change . . . [i]f there is an increased periodic rate or any other finance charge attributable to the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s delinquency or default.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., Comment 9(c)(1)\xe2\x80\x933, at 507 (hereinafter Comment 9(c)(1)\xe2\x80\x933).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At least as early as 2004, the Board began considering revisions to Regulation Z. The new regulations the Board eventually issued do not apply to the present case, but the details of their promulgation provides useful background in considering the parties\xe2\x80\x99 arguments with respect to the version of Regulation Z we address here. In 2004 the Board issued an advance notice of proposed rulemaking announcing its intent to consider revisions. 69 Fed. Reg. 70925 (2004). In so doing, the Board described how it understood the notice requirements to function at that time:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[A]dvance notice is not required in all cases. For example, if the interest rate or other finance charge increases due to a consumer\xe2\x80\x99s default or delinquency, notice is required, but need not be given in advance. 12 CFR 226.9(c)(1); comment 9(c)(1)\xe2\x80\x933. And no change-in-terms notice is required if the creditor specifies in advance the circumstances under which an increase to the finance charge or an annual fee will occur. Comment 9(c)\xe2\x80\x931. For example, some credit card account agreements permit the card issuer to increase the interest rate if the consumer pays late . . . . Under Regulation Z, because the circumstances are specified in advance in the account agreement, the creditor need not provide a change-in-terms notice 15 days in advance of the increase; the new rate will appear on the periodic statement for the cycle in which the increase occurs.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 70931\xe2\x80\x9370932.

\n

\nThe Board asked for public comment on whether these \xe2\x80\x9cexisting disclosure rules\xe2\x80\x9d were \xe2\x80\x9cadequate to enable consumers to make timely decisions about how to manage their accounts.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 70932.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Subsequently, in 2007, the Board published proposed amendments to Regulation Z and the Commentary. 72 Fed. Reg. 32948. One amendment would have required 45 days\xe2\x80\x99 advance written notice when \xe2\x80\x9c(i) [a] rate is increased due to the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s delinquency or default; or (ii) [a] rate is increased as a penalty for one or more events spec-\nified in the account agreement, such as making a late payment or obtaining an extension of credit that exceeds the credit limit.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 33058 (proposed 12 CFR \xc2\xa7226.9(g)). The Board explained that, under the amendments, \xe2\x80\x9ccreditors would no longer be permitted to provide for the immediate application of penalty pricing upon the occurrence of certain events specified in the contract.\xe2\x80\x9d 72 Fed. Reg. 33012.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In January 2009, the Board promulgated a final rule implementing many of the proposed changes, scheduled to be effective July 1, 2010. 74 Fed. Reg. 5244. Most saliently, the Board included a new provision, \xc2\xa7226.9(g), which requires 45 days\xe2\x80\x99 advance notice of increases in rates due to cardholder delinquency or default, or as a penalty, including penalties for \xe2\x80\x9cevents specified in the account agreement, such as making a late payment \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d 12 CFR \xc2\xa7226.9(g)(1)(2010). In May 2009, Congress enacted the Credit Card Accountability Responsibility and Disclosure Act (Credit CARD Act or Act), 123 Stat. 1734. The Act amended TILA, in relevant part, to require 45 days\xe2\x80\x99 advance notice of most increases in credit card annual percentage rates. 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71637(i) (2006 ed., Supp. III). Because the Credit CARD Act\xe2\x80\x99s notice requirements with respect to interest-rate increases largely mirror the requirements in the new version of the regu-\nlation, the Board changed the effective date of those\nrequirements to August 20, 2009, to coincide with the statutory schedule. See 74 Fed. Reg. 36077\xe2\x80\x9336079. The transactions giving rise to the dispute at issue in this case, however, arose prior to enactment of the Act and the promulgation of the new regulatory provisions.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Respondent James A. McCoy brought this action in the Superior Court of Orange County, California on behalf of himself and others similarly situated against petitioner Chase Bank USA, N.\xc2\xa0A.; Chase removed the action to the United States District Court for the Central District of California under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71441. At the time of the transactions at issue, McCoy was the holder of a credit card issued by Chase. The cardholder agreement between the parties (Agreement) provides, in relevant part, that McCoy is eligible for \xe2\x80\x9cPreferred rates,\xe2\x80\x9d but that to keep such rates he has to meet certain conditions, including making \xe2\x80\x9cat least the required minimum payments when due on [his] Account and on all other loans or accounts with [Chase] and [his] other creditors.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 8, n.\xc2\xa02; see also 559 F.\xc2\xa03d 963, 972, n.\xc2\xa01 (CA9 2009) (Cudahy, J., dissenting). If any of the conditions in the Agreement are not met, Chase reserves the right to \xe2\x80\x9cchange [McCoy\xe2\x80\x99s] interest rate and impose a Non-Preferred rate up to the maximum Non-Preferred rate described in the Pricing Schedule\xe2\x80\x9d and to apply any changes \xe2\x80\x9cto existing as well as new balances \xe2\x80\xa6 effective with the billing cycle ending on the review date.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 8, n.\xc2\xa02.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0McCoy\xe2\x80\x99s complaint alleges that Chase increased his interest rate due to his delinquency or default, and applied that increase retroactively. McCoy asserts that the rate increase violates Regulation Z because, pursuant to the Agreement, Chase did not notify him of the increase until after it had taken effect.[Footnote 2] The District Court dismissed McCoy\xe2\x80\x99s complaint, holding that because the increase did not constitute a \xe2\x80\x9cchange in terms\xe2\x80\x9d as contemplated by \xc2\xa7226.9(c), Chase was not required to notify him of the increase before implementing it. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 37a\xe2\x80\x9347a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A divided panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed in relevant part, holding that Regulation Z requires issuers to provide notice of an interest-rate increase prior to its effective date. See 559 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 969. Concluding that the text of Regulation Z is ambiguous and that the agency commentary accompanying the 2004 request for comments and the 2007 proposed amendments favors neither party\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation, the court relied primarily on the Official Staff Commentary; in particular, the court noted that Comment 9(c)\xe2\x80\x931 requires no notice of a change in terms if the \xe2\x80\x9cspecific change\xe2\x80\x9d at issue is set forth in the initial agreement. See id., at 965\xe2\x80\x93967. The court found, however, that because the Agreement vests Chase with discretion to impose any Non-Preferred rate it chooses (up to the specified maximum) upon McCoy\xe2\x80\x99s default, the Agreement \xe2\x80\x9cprovides McCoy with no basis for predicting in advance what retroactive interest rate Chase will choose to charge him if he defaults.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 967. Accordingly, the court held that because the Agreement does not alert McCoy to the \xe2\x80\x9cspecific change\xe2\x80\x9d that will occur if he defaults, Chase was obliged to give notice of that change prior to its effective date. Ibid. Relying primarily on the 2004 notice of proposed rulemaking and the 2007 proposed amendments, the dissenting judge concluded that Regulation Z does not require notice of an interest-rate increase in the circumstances of this case. See id., at 972\xe2\x80\x93979 (opinion of Cudahy, J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s ruling, the United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit decided the same question in Chase\xe2\x80\x99s favor. See Shaner v. Chase Bank USA, N.\xc2\xa0A., 587 F.\xc2\xa03d 488 (2009). The First Circuit relied in part on an amicus brief submitted by the Board at the court\xe2\x80\x99s request, in which the agency advanced the same interpretation of Regulation Z that it now does before this Court. Id., at 493. We granted certiorari to resolve this division in authority.[Footnote 3] 561 U. S. ___ (2010).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In order to decide this case, we must determine whether an interest-rate increase constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9cchange in terms\xe2\x80\x9d under Regulation Z, when the change is made pursuant to a provision in the cardholder agreement allowing the issuer to increase the rate, up to a stated maximum, in the event of the cardholder\xe2\x80\x99s delinquency or default. Accordingly, this case calls upon us to determine the meaning of a regulation promulgated by the Board under its statutory authority. The parties dispute the proper interpretation of the regulation itself, as well as whether we should accord deference to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its regulation. As explained below, we conclude that the text of the regulation is ambiguous, and that deference is warranted to the interpretation of that text advanced by the Board in its amicus brief.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our analysis begins with the text of Regulation Z in effect at the time this dispute arose. First, \xc2\xa7226.6 requires an \xe2\x80\x9c[i]nitial disclosure statement\xe2\x80\x9d:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe creditor shall disclose to the consumer, in terminology consistent with that to be used on the periodic statement, each of the following items, to the\nextent applicable:

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(a) Finance charge. The circumstances under which a finance charge will be imposed and an explanation of how it will be determined, as follows:

\n

.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2) A disclosure of each periodic rate that may be used to compute the finance charge, the range of balances to which it is applicable, and the corresponding annual percentage rate. When different periodic rates apply to different types of transactions, the types of transactions to which the periodic rates apply shall also be disclosed.\xe2\x80\x9d (Footnotes omitted.)

\n

\nSecond, \xc2\xa7226.9(c) requires certain \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ubsequent disclosure requirements\xe2\x80\x9d:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cChange in terms\xe2\x80\x94(1) Written notice required. Whenever any term required to be disclosed under \xc2\xa7226.6 is changed or the required minimum periodic payment is increased, the creditor shall mail or deliver written notice of the change to each consumer who may be affected. The notice shall be mailed or delivered at least 15 days prior to the effective date of the change. The 15-day timing requirement does not apply if the change has been agreed to by the consumer, or if a periodic rate or other finance charge is increased because of the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s delinquency or default; the notice shall be given, however, before the effective date of the change.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9c(2) Notice not required. No notice under this section is required when the change \xe2\x80\xa6 results from \xe2\x80\xa6 the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s default or delinquency (other than an increase in the periodic rate or other finance charge).\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question is whether the increase in McCoy\xe2\x80\x99s interest rate constitutes a change to a \xe2\x80\x9cterm required to be disclosed under \xc2\xa7226.6,\xe2\x80\x9d requiring a subsequent disclosure under \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1). One of the initial terms that must be disclosed under \xc2\xa7226.6 is \xe2\x80\x9ceach periodic rate that may be used to compute the finance charge \xe2\x80\xa6 and the corresponding annual percentage rate.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7226.6(a)(2). McCoy argues that, because an increase in the interest rate increases the \xe2\x80\x9cperiodic rate\xe2\x80\x9d applicable to his account, such an increase constitutes a change in terms within the meaning of \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1). As further support, McCoy points to two provisions in \xc2\xa7226.9(c): first, that notice of an increase in the interest rate must be provided \xe2\x80\x9cbefore the effective date of the change\xe2\x80\x9d when the increase is due to \xe2\x80\x9cthe consumer\xe2\x80\x99s delinquency or default,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1); and second, that no notice is required of a change resulting \xe2\x80\x9cfrom the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s default or delinquency (other than an increase in the periodic rate or other finance charge),\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(2). Accordingly, because \xc2\xa7226.9(c) includes interest-rate increases due to delinquency or default, McCoy argues that the plain text of the regulation indicates that a change in the periodic rate due to such default is a \xe2\x80\x9cchange in terms\xe2\x80\x9d requiring notice under \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We recognize that McCoy\xe2\x80\x99s argument has some force; read in isolation, the language quoted above certainly suggests that credit card issuers must provide notice of an interest-rate increase imposed pursuant to cardholder delinquency or default. But McCoy\xe2\x80\x99s analysis begs the key question: whether the increase actually changed a \xe2\x80\x9cterm\xe2\x80\x9d of the Agreement that was \xe2\x80\x9crequired to be disclosed under \xc2\xa7226.6.\xe2\x80\x9d If not, \xc2\xa7226.9(c)\xe2\x80\x99s subsequent notice requirement with respect to a \xe2\x80\x9cchange in terms\xe2\x80\x9d does not apply. Chase argues precisely this: The increase did not change a term in the Agreement, but merely implemented one that had been initially disclosed, as required. This interpretation, though not commanded by the text of the regulation, is reasonable. Section 226.6(a)(2) requires initial disclosure of \xe2\x80\x9ceach periodic rate that may be used to compute the finance charge.\xe2\x80\x9d The Agreement itself discloses both the initial rate (Preferred rate) and the maximum rate to be imposed in the event of default (Non-Preferred rate). See Brief for Respondent 8, n.\xc2\xa02; Brief for Petitioner 13\xe2\x80\x9314.[Footnote 4] Accordingly, it is plausible to understand the Agreement to initially disclose \xe2\x80\x9ceach periodic rate\xe2\x80\x9d to be applied to the account, and Chase arguably did not \xe2\x80\x9cchange\xe2\x80\x9d those rates as a result of McCoy\xe2\x80\x99s default. Instead, Chase merely implemented the previously disclosed term specifying the Non-Preferred rate.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This reading still leaves the question why \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1) refers to interest-rate increases resulting from delinquency or default if such increases do not constitute a \xe2\x80\x9cchange in terms.\xe2\x80\x9d One reasonable explanation Chase offers is that \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1) refers to interest-rate increases that were not specifically outlined in the agreement\xe2\x80\x99s initial terms (unlike those in the present Agreement). For example, credit card agreements routinely include a \xe2\x80\x9creservation of rights\xe2\x80\x9d provision giving the issuer discretion to change the terms of the contract, often as a means of responding to events that raise doubts about the cardholder\xe2\x80\x99s creditworthiness. An issuer may exercise this general contract-modification authority and raise the interest rate applicable to the account to address any heightened risk. See Brief for Petitioner 6. In such a case, \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1) is best read to require that notice must be given prior to the effective date of the increase, because the unilateral increase instituted by the issuer actually changed a term\xe2\x80\x94the interest rate\xe2\x80\x94in a manner not specifically contemplated by the agreement.[Footnote 6] See Comment 9(c)\xe2\x80\x931 (providing that notice is required if the agreement \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not include specific terms for an increase (for example, when an increase may occur under the creditor\xe2\x80\x99s contract reservation right to increase the periodic rate)\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, Regulation Z is unclear with respect to the crucial interpretive question: whether the interest-rate increase at issue in this case constitutes a \xe2\x80\x9cchange in terms\xe2\x80\x9d requiring notice. We need not decide which party\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is more persuasive, however; both are plausible, and the text alone does not permit a more definitive reading. Accordingly, we find Regulation Z to be ambiguous as to the question presented, and must therefore look to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s own interpretation of the regulation for guidance in deciding this case. See Coeur Alaska, Inc. v. Southeast Alaska Conservation Council, 557 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 14) (stating that when an agency\xe2\x80\x99s regulations construing a statute \xe2\x80\x9care ambiguous \xe2\x80\xa6 we next turn to the agencies\xe2\x80\x99 subsequent interpretation of those regulations\xe2\x80\x9d for guidance); Ford Motor Credit Co. v. Milhollin, 444 U. S. 555, 560 (1980) (stating that when the question presented \xe2\x80\x9cis not governed by clear expression in the \xe2\x80\xa6 regulation \xe2\x80\xa6 it is appropriate to defer to the Federal Reserve Board and staff in determining what resolution of that issue\xe2\x80\x9d is appropriate).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Board has made clear in the amicus brief it has submitted to this Court that, in the Board\xe2\x80\x99s view, Chase was not required to give McCoy notice of the interest rate increase under the version of Regulation Z applicable at the time. Under Auer v. Robbins, 519 U. S. 452 (1997), we defer to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its own regulation, advanced in a legal brief, unless that interpretation is \xe2\x80\x9cplainly erroneous or inconsistent with the regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 461 (internal quotation marks omitted). Because the interpretation the Board presents in its brief is consistent with the regulatory text, we need look no further in deciding this case.[Footnote 7]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In its brief to this Court, the Board explains that the Ninth Circuit \xe2\x80\x9cerred in concluding that, at the time of\nthe transactions at issue in this case, Regulation Z required credit card issuers to provide a change-in-terms notice be-\nfore implementing a contractual default-rate provision.\xe2\x80\x9d See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 11; see also ibid. (stating that when a term of an agreement authorized the credit provider \xe2\x80\x9cto increase a consumer\xe2\x80\x99s interest rate if the consumer failed to make timely payments . . . any resulting rate increase did not represent a \xe2\x80\x98change in terms,\xe2\x80\x99 but rather the implementation of terms already set forth in the initial disclosure statement\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 15\xe2\x80\x9316 (stating that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a cardholder agreement identifies a contingency that triggers a rate increase, and the maximum possible rate that the issuer may charge if that contingency occurs,\xe2\x80\x9d then \xe2\x80\x9cno change-in-terms notice is required\xe2\x80\x9d under Regulation Z).[Footnote 8] Under the principles set forth in Auer, we give deference to this interpretation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Auer we deferred to the Secretary of Labor\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of his own regulation, presented in an amicus brief submitted by the agency at our invitation. 519 U. S., at 461\xe2\x80\x93462. Responding to the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 objection that the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation came in a legal brief, we held that this fact did not, \xe2\x80\x9cin the circumstances of this case, make it unworthy of deference.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 462. We observed that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s position is in no sense a \xe2\x80\x98post hoc rationalizatio[n]\xe2\x80\x99 advanced by an agency seeking to defend past agency action against attack.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Bowen v. Georgetown Univ. Hospital, 488 U. S. 204, 212 (1988)). We added: \xe2\x80\x9cThere is simply no reason to suspect that the interpretation does not reflect the agency\xe2\x80\x99s fair and considered judgment on the matter in question.\xe2\x80\x9d Auer, 519 U. S., at 462.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The brief submitted by the Board in the present case, at our invitation, is no different. As in Auer, there is no reason to believe that the interpretation advanced by the Board is a \xe2\x80\x9cpost hoc rationalization\xe2\x80\x9d taken as a litigation position. The Board is not a party to this case. And as is evident from our discussion of Regulation Z itself, see Part II\xe2\x80\x93A, supra, the Board\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is neither \xe2\x80\x9cplainly erroneous\xe2\x80\x9d nor \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with\xe2\x80\x9d the indeterminate text of the regulation. In short, there is no reason to suspect that the position the Board takes in its amicus brief reflects anything other than the agency\xe2\x80\x99s fair and considered judgment as to what the regulation required at the time this dispute arose.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0McCoy may well be correct in asserting that it is better policy to oblige credit-card issuers to give advance notice\nof a rate increase; after all, both Congress and the Board have recently indicated that such a requirement is warranted. See Credit CARD Act, \xc2\xa7101(a)(1), 123 Stat. 1735\xe2\x80\x931736; 12 CFR \xc2\xa7226.9(g) (2009). That Congress and the Board may currently hold such views does not mean, however, that deference is not warranted to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s different understanding of what the pre-2009 version of Regulation Z required. To the contrary, the interpretation the Board advances in its amicus brief is entirely consistent with its past views. The 2004 notice of rulemaking and the 2007 proposed amendments to Regulation Z make clear that, prior to 2009, the Board\xe2\x80\x99s fair and considered judgment was that \xe2\x80\x9cno change-in-terms notice is required if the creditor specifies in advance the circumstances under which an increase \xe2\x80\xa6 will occur,\xe2\x80\x9d 69 Fed. Reg. 70931, and \xe2\x80\x9cimmediate application of penalty pricing upon the occurrence of certain events specified in the contract\xe2\x80\x9d was permissible, 72 Fed. Reg. 33012.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Under Auer, therefore, it is clear that deference to the interpretation in the Board\xe2\x80\x99s amicus brief is warranted. The cases McCoy cites in which we declined to apply Auer do not suggest that deference is unwarranted here. In Gonzales v. Oregon, 546 U. S. 243 (2006), we declined to defer because\xe2\x80\x94in sharp contrast to the present case\xe2\x80\x94\nthe regulation in question did \xe2\x80\x9clittle more than restate the terms of the statute\xe2\x80\x9d pursuant to which the regulation was promulgated. Id., at 257. Accordingly, no deference was warranted to an agency interpretation of what were, in fact, Congress\xe2\x80\x99 words. Ibid. In contrast, at the time of the transactions in this case, TILA itself included no requirements with respect to the disclosure of a change in credit terms. In Christensen v. Harris County, 529 U. S. 576 (2000), we declined to apply Auer deference because the regulation in question was unambiguous, and adopting the agency\xe2\x80\x99s contrary interpretation would \xe2\x80\x9cpermit the agency, under the guise of interpreting a regulation, to create de facto a new regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d 529 U. S., at 588. In light of Regulation Z\xe2\x80\x99s ambiguity, there is no such danger here. And our statement in Christensen that \xe2\x80\x9cdeference is warranted only when the language of the regulation is ambiguous,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., is perfectly consonant with Auer itself; if the text of a regulation is unambiguous, a conflicting agency interpretation advanced in an amicus brief will necessarily be \xe2\x80\x9cplainly erroneous or inconsistent with the regulation\xe2\x80\x9d in question. Auer, 519 U. S., at 461 (internal quotation marks omitted). Accordingly, under our precedent deference to the Board\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its own regulation, as presented in the agency\xe2\x80\x99s amicus brief, is wholly appropriate.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0McCoy further argues that deference to a legal brief is inappropriate because the interpretation of Regulation Z in the Official Staff Commentary commands a different result. To be sure, the Official Staff Commentary promulgated by the Board as an interpretation of Regulation Z may warrant deference as a general matter. See Anderson Bros. Ford v. Valencia, 452 U. S. 205, 219 (1981) (holding that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Board\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its own regulation\xe2\x80\x9d should generally \xe2\x80\x9cbe accepted by the courts\xe2\x80\x9d); Milhollin, 444 U. S., at 565 (\xe2\x80\x9cUnless demonstrably irrational, Federal Reserve Board staff opinions construing [TILA] or Regulation [Z] should be dispositive\xe2\x80\x9d). We find, however, that the Commentary at issue here largely replicates the ambiguity present in the regulatory text, and therefore it offers us nothing to which we can defer with respect to the precise interpretive question before us.[Footnote 9] Cf. Smith v. City of Jackson, 544 U. S. 228, 248 (2005) (O\xe2\x80\x99Connor, J., concurring in judgment) (noting that deference is not warranted when \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no reasoned agency reading of the text to which we might defer\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Ninth Circuit relied primarily on Comment 9(c)(1)\xe2\x80\x933, which states in relevant part that \xe2\x80\x9ca notice of change in terms is required, but it may be mailed or delivered as late as the effective date of the change . . . [i]f there is an\nincreased periodic rate or any other finance charge attri-\nbutable to the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s delinquency or default.\xe2\x80\x9d This exposition of the regulation does not add any clarity to the regulatory text, which expresses the same requirement. See \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1) (2008) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]f a periodic rate or other finance charge is increased because of the consumer\xe2\x80\x99s delinquency or default \xe2\x80\xa6 notice shall be given \xe2\x80\xa6 before the effective date of the change\xe2\x80\x9d). And like \xc2\xa7226.9(c), Comment 9(c) is entitled \xe2\x80\x9cChange in terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Accordingly, Chase\xe2\x80\x99s plausible interpretation of \xc2\xa7226.9(c)(1) is equally applicable to Comment 9(c)(1)\xe2\x80\x933: On Chase\xe2\x80\x99s view, because the interest-rate increase at issue in this case did not constitute a \xe2\x80\x9cchange in terms,\xe2\x80\x9d the disclosure requirements in the regulation and Commentary simply do not come into play. See supra, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Comment 9(c)\xe2\x80\x931 is also ambiguous, though the most plausible reading supports Chase\xe2\x80\x99s position more than it does McCoy\xe2\x80\x99s. The Comment begins: \xe2\x80\x9cNo notice of a change in terms need be given if the specific change is set forth initially\xe2\x80\x9d in the agreement. We do not find that the Comment\xe2\x80\x99s addition of the modifier \xe2\x80\x9cspecific\xe2\x80\x9d to the word \xe2\x80\x9cchange\xe2\x80\x9d enables us to determine, any more than we could in light of the text of the regulation, see supra, at 12, whether the interest-rate increase at issue in this case was a \xe2\x80\x9cchange in terms\xe2\x80\x9d requiring notice. According to Chase, as long as the agreement explains that delinquency or default might trigger an increased interest rate and states the maximum level to which the rate could be increased, the \xe2\x80\x9cspecific change\xe2\x80\x9d that ensues upon default has been set forth initially and no additional notice is required before implementation. McCoy argues to the contrary: Under Comment 9(c)\xe2\x80\x931, any new rate imposed after delinquency or default must be disclosed prior to the effective date, if the particular rate (rather than the maximum rate) was not specifically mentioned in the agreement. On the whole, then, the Official Staff Commentary\xe2\x80\x99s explanation of Regulation Z does not resolve the uncertainty in the regulatory text, and offers us no reason to disregard\nthe interpretation advanced in the Board\xe2\x80\x99s amicus brief.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0McCoy further contends that our reliance here on an agency interpretation presented outside the four corners of the Official Staff Commentary will require future litigants, as well as the Board, to expend time and resources \xe2\x80\x9cto comb through \xe2\x80\xa6 correspondence, publications, and the agency\xe2\x80\x99s website to determine the agency\xe2\x80\x99s position.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 37\xe2\x80\x9338. We are not convinced. McCoy may be correct that the Board established the Official Staff Commentary so as to centralize its opinion-making process and avoid \xe2\x80\x9coverburdening the industry with excessive detail and multiple research sources.\xe2\x80\x9d 46 Fed. Reg. 50288. But his suggestion that, if we accord deference to an amicus brief, all other \xe2\x80\x9cunofficial\xe2\x80\x9d sources will be fair game is of no moment. Today we decide only that the amicus brief submitted by the Board is entitled to deference in light of \xe2\x80\x9cthe circumstances of this case.\xe2\x80\x9d Auer, 519 U. S., at 462.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, we conclude that, at the time of the transactions at issue in this case, Regulation Z did not require Chase to provide McCoy with a change-in-terms notice before it implemented the Agreement term allowing it to raise his interest rate following delinquency or default.

\n
