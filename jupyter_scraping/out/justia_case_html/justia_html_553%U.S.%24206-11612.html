GONZALEZ v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the fifth circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x9311612.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 8, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 12, 2008

\n

If the parties consent, a federal magistrate judge may preside over the voir dire and jury selection in a felony criminal trial. Peretz v. United States, 501 U. S. 923, 933. Before petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s federal trial on felony drug charges, his counsel consented to the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s presiding over jury selection. Petitioner was not asked for his own consent. After the Magistrate Judge supervised voir dire without objection, a District Judge presided at trial, and the jury returned a guilty verdict on all counts. Petitioner contended for the first time on appeal that it was error not to obtain his own consent to the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s voir dire role. The Fifth Circuit affirmed the convictions, concluding, inter alia, that the right to have a district judge preside over voir dire could be waived by counsel.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Express consent by counsel suffices to permit a magistrate judge to preside over jury selection in a felony trial, pursuant to the Federal Magistrates Act, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7636(b)(3), which states: \xe2\x80\x9cA magistrate judge may be assigned such additional duties as are not inconsistent with the Constitution and laws of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d Under Gomez v. United States, 490 U. S. 858, 870, 875\xe2\x80\x93876, and Peretz, supra, at 933, 935\xe2\x80\x93936, such \xe2\x80\x9cadditional duties\xe2\x80\x9d include presiding at voir dire if the parties consent, but not if there is an objection. Generally, where there is a full trial, there are various points at which rights either can be asserted or waived. This Court has indicated that some of these rights require the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own consent to waive. See, e.g., New York v. Hill, 528 U. S. 110, 114\xe2\x80\x93115. The Court held in Hill, however, that an attorney, acting without indication of particular consent from his client, could waive his client\xe2\x80\x99s statutory right to a speedy trial because \xe2\x80\x9c[s]cheduling matters are plainly among those for which agreement by counsel generally controls.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Similar to the scheduling matter in Hill, acceptance of a magistrate judge at the jury selection phase is a tactical decision well suited for the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s own decision. The presiding judge has significant discretion over jury selection both as to substance\xe2\x80\x94the questions asked\xe2\x80\x94and tone\xe2\x80\x94formal or informal\xe2\x80\x94and the judge\xe2\x80\x99s approach may be relevant in light of the approach of the attorney, who may decide whether to accept a magistrate judge based in part on these factors. As with other tactical decisions, requiring personal, on-the-record approval from the client could necessitate a lengthy explanation that the client might not understand and that might distract from more pressing matters as the attorney seeks to prepare the best defense. Petitioner argues unconvincingly that the decision to have a magistrate judge for voir dire is a fundamental choice, cf. Hill, supra, at 114, or, at least, raises a question of constitutional significance so that the Act should be interpreted to require explicit consent. Serious concerns about the Act\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality are not present here, and petitioner concedes that magistrate judges are capable of competent and impartial performance when presiding over jury selection. Gomez, supra, at 876, distinguished. Pp. 2\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

483 F. 3d 390, affirmed.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If the parties consent, federal magistrate judges may preside over the voir dire and selection of prospective jurors in a felony criminal trial. Peretz v. United States, 501 U. S. 923, 933 (1991). This case presents the question whether it suffices for counsel alone to consent to the magistrate judge\xe2\x80\x99s role in presiding over voir dire and jury selection or whether the defendant must give his or her own consent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Homero Gonzalez was charged in the United States District Court for the Southern District of Texas on five felony drug offense counts. He is the petitioner here. At the outset of jury selection, the parties appeared before a Magistrate Judge. The Magistrate Judge asked the attorneys to approach the bench. After they complied, the Magistrate Judge said: \xe2\x80\x9cI need to ask the parties at this time if they are going to consent to having the United States Magistrate Judge proceed in assisting in the jury selection of this case.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 16. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel responded: \xe2\x80\x9cYes, your Honor, we are.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The Magistrate Judge asked if petitioner was present and if he needed an interpreter. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel answered yes to both questions. Petitioner was not asked if he consented to the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s presiding. The record does not permit us to infer this or even to infer that petitioner knew there was a right to be waived. The Magistrate Judge then supervised voir dire and jury selection. Petitioner made no objections to the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s rulings or her conduct of the proceedings. A District Judge presided at the ensuing jury trial, and the jury returned a verdict of guilty on all counts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner appealed, contending, for the first time, that it was error not to obtain his own consent to the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s presiding at voir dire. The United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit affirmed the convictions. The court concluded petitioner could not show the error was plain and, furthermore, there was no error at all. It held the right to have an Article III judge preside over voir dire could be waived by petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel. 483 F.\xc2\xa03d 390, 394 (2007). The Courts of Appeals differ on this issue. Compare ibid., with United States v. Maragh, 174 F.\xc2\xa03d 1202, 1206 (CA11 1999) (requiring personal and explicit consent from the defendant); see also United States v. Desir, 273 F.\xc2\xa03d 39, 44 (CA1 2001) (magistrate judge may conduct jury selection unless the defendant or his attorney registers an objection). We granted certiorari. 551 U. S. ___ (2007). We agree that there was no error and hold that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel had full authority to consent to the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s role.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Federal Magistrates Act, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7631 et seq. (2000 ed. and Supp. V), permits district courts to assign designated functions to magistrate judges. For example, magistrate judges are authorized to: issue orders concerning release or detention of persons pending trial; take acknowledgments, affidavits, and depositions; and enter sentences for petty offenses. \xc2\xa7636(a) (2000 ed. and Supp. V). They also may hear and determine, when designated to do so, any pretrial matter pending before the district court, with the exception of certain specified motions. Magistrate judges may also conduct hearings and propose recommendations for those motions, applications for post-trial criminal relief, and conditions of confinement petitions. \xc2\xa7636(b)(1) (2000 ed.). If the parties consent, they may conduct misdemeanor criminal trials and civil trials. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7636(a)(3) and (c)(1).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The statutory provision of direct applicability in the present case is \xc2\xa7636(b)(3). It states: \xe2\x80\x9cA magistrate judge may be assigned such additional duties as are not inconsistent with the Constitution and laws of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d The general, nonspecific terms of this paragraph, preceded by text that sets out permissible duties in more precise terms, constitute a residual or general category that must not be interpreted in terms so expansive that the paragraph overshadows all that goes before.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In two earlier cases the Court considered the question of magistrate judges presiding over the jury selection process in felony trials. In Gomez v. United States, 490 U. S. 858 (1989), the District Judge delegated the task of selecting a jury to a Federal Magistrate Judge. Defense counsel objected, but the objection was overruled. The Court noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] critical limitation on [the magistrate judge\xe2\x80\x99s] expanded jurisdiction is consent,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 870, and held that presiding, over an objection, at the preliminary selection phase of a jury trial in felony cases is not among the additional duties that a magistrate judge may assume, id., at 875\xe2\x80\x93876.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Peretz v. United States, supra, the Court again considered whether a magistrate judge could preside over voir dire in a felony case. In that instance, however, defendant\xe2\x80\x99s counsel, upon being asked by the District Court at a pretrial conference (with the defendant present) if there was any objection to having jury selection before a magistrate judge, responded, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98I would love the opportunity.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 925. Defense counsel later advised the Magistrate Judge that the defendant consented to the process. The Court clarified that in a felony trial neither the Act nor Article III forbids supervision of voir dire by a magistrate judge if both parties consent. Id., at 935\xe2\x80\x93936.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Taken together, Gomez and Peretz mean that \xe2\x80\x9cthe additional duties\xe2\x80\x9d the statute permits the magistrate judge to undertake include presiding at voir dire and jury selection provided there is consent but not if there is an objection. We now consider whether the consent can be given by counsel acting on behalf of the client but without the client\xe2\x80\x99s own express consent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At first reading it might seem that our holding here is dictated by the holding in Peretz. In Peretz, it would appear the accused was aware of the colloquy between the District Judge and defense counsel and the formal waiver before the Magistrate Judge. On this premise Peretz might be read narrowly to hold that a defendant may signal consent by failing to object; and indeed, the petitioner here seeks to distinguish Peretz on this ground. Brief for Petitioner 41\xe2\x80\x9342. We decide this case, however, on the assumption that the defendant did not hear, or did not understand, the waiver discussions. This addresses what, at least in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s view, Peretz did not. It should be noted that we do not have before us an instance where a defendant instructs the lawyer or advises the court in an explicit, timely way that he or she demands that a district judge preside in this preliminary phase.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There are instances in federal criminal proceedings where the procedural requisites for consent are specified and a right cannot be waived except with a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own informed consent. Under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 11(b), for example, the district court is required, as a precondition to acceptance of a guilty plea, to inform the defendant in person of the specified rights he or she may claim in a full criminal trial and then verify that the plea is voluntary by addressing the defendant. The requirement is satisfied by a colloquy between judge and defendant, reviewing all of the rights listed in Rule 11.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Statutes may also address this subject. Under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73401(b), for example, a magistrate judge may preside over the whole trial and sentencing in a misdemeanor case but only with the express, personal consent of the defendant. The provision requires that the magistrate judge

\n

\xe2\x80\x9ccarefully explain to the defendant that he has a right to trial, judgment, and sentencing by a district judge and that he may have a right to trial by jury before\na district judge or magistrate judge. The magistrate judge may not proceed to try the case unless the defendant, after such explanation, expressly consents to be tried before the magistrate judge and expressly and specifically waives trial, judgment, and sentencing by a district judge. Any such consent and waiver shall be made in writing or orally on the record.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The controlling statute in this case has a different design, however. Title 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) does not state that consent to preside over felony voir dire must be granted by following a procedure of similar clarity. As a general matter, where there is a full trial there are various points in the pretrial and trial process when rights either can be asserted or waived; and there is support in our cases for concluding that some of these rights cannot be waived absent the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own consent. Whether the personal consent must be explicit and on the record or can be determined from a course of conduct may be another matter, but for now it suffices to note that we have acknowledged that some rights cannot be waived by the attorney alone. See New York v. Hill, 528 U. S. 110, 114\xe2\x80\x93115 (2000).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Citing some of our precedents on point, the Court in Hill gave this capsule discussion:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cWhat suffices for waiver depends on the nature of the right at issue. \xe2\x80\x98[W]hether the defendant must participate personally in the waiver; whether certain procedures are required for waiver; and whether the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s choice must be particularly informed or voluntary, all depend on the right at stake.\xe2\x80\x99 United States v. Olano, 507 U. S. 725, 733 (1993). For certain fundamental rights, the defendant must personally make an informed waiver. See, e.g., Johnson v. Zerbst, 304 U. S. 458, 464\xe2\x80\x93465 (1938) (right to counsel); Brookhart v. Janis, 384 U. S. 1, 7\xe2\x80\x938 (1966) (right to plead not guilty). For other rights, however, waiver may be effected by action of counsel. \xe2\x80\x98Although there are basic rights that the attorney cannot waive without the fully informed and publicly acknowledged consent of the client, the lawyer has\xe2\x80\x94and must have\xe2\x80\x94full authority to manage the conduct of the trial.\xe2\x80\x99 Taylor v. Illinois, 484 U. S. 400, 417\xe2\x80\x93418 (1988). As to many decisions pertaining to the conduct of the trial, the defendant is \xe2\x80\x98deemed bound by the acts of his lawyer-agent and is considered to have \xe2\x80\x9cnotice of all facts, notice of which can be charged upon the attorney.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99 Link v. Wabash R. Co., 370 U. S. 626, 634 (1962) (quoting Smith v. Ayer, 101 U. S. 320, 326 (1880)). Thus, decisions by counsel are generally given effect as to what arguments to pursue, see Jones v. Barnes, 463 U. S. 745, 751 (1983), what evidentiary objections to raise, see Henry v. Mississippi, 379 U. S. 443, 451 (1965), and what agreements to conclude regarding the admission of evidence, see United States v. McGill, 11 F. 3d 223, 226\xe2\x80\x93227 (CA1 1993). Absent a demonstration of ineffectiveness, counsel\xe2\x80\x99s word on such matters is the last.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\nThe issue in Hill was whether the attorney, acting without indication of particular consent from his client, could waive his client\xe2\x80\x99s statutory right to a speedy trial pursuant to the Interstate Agreement on Detainers. The Court held that the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s statement, without any showing of the client\xe2\x80\x99s explicit consent, could waive the speedy trial right: \xe2\x80\x9cScheduling matters are plainly among those for which agreement by counsel generally controls.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 115.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Giving the attorney control of trial management matters is a practical necessity. \xe2\x80\x9cThe adversary process could not function effectively if every tactical decision required client approval.\xe2\x80\x9d Taylor v. Illinois, 484 U. S. 400, 418 (1988). The presentation of a criminal defense can be a mystifying process even for well-informed laypersons. This is one of the reasons for the right to counsel. See Powell v. Alabama, 287 U. S. 45, 68\xe2\x80\x9369 (1932); ABA Standards for Criminal Justice, Defense Function 4\xe2\x80\x935.2, Commentary, p. 202 (3d ed. 1993) (\xe2\x80\x9cMany of the rights of an accused, including constitutional rights, are such that only trained experts can comprehend their full significance, and an explanation to any but the most sophisticated client would be futile\xe2\x80\x9d). Numerous choices affecting conduct of the trial, including the objections to make, the witnesses to call, and the arguments to advance, depend not only upon what is permissible under the rules of evidence and procedure but also upon tactical considerations of the moment and the larger strategic plan for the trial. These matters can be difficult to explain to a layperson; and to require in all instances that they be approved by the client could risk compromising the efficiencies and fairness that the trial process is designed to promote. In exercising professional judgment, moreover, the attorney draws upon the expertise and experience that members of the bar should bring to the trial process. In most instances the attorney will have a better understanding of the procedural choices than the client; or at least the law should so assume. See Jones v. Barnes, 463 U. S. 745, 751 (1983); see also Tollett v. Henderson, 411 U. S. 258, 267\xe2\x80\x93268 (1973); cf. ABA Standards, supra, at 202 (\xe2\x80\x9cEvery experienced advocate can recall the disconcerting experience of trying to conduct the examination of a witness or follow opposing arguments or the judge\xe2\x80\x99s charge while the client \xe2\x80\x98plucks at the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s sleeve\xe2\x80\x99 offering gratuitous suggestions\xe2\x80\x9d). To hold that every instance of waiver requires the personal consent of the client himself or herself would be impractical.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similar to the scheduling matter in Hill, acceptance of a magistrate judge at the jury selection phase is a tactical decision that is well suited for the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s own decision. Under Rule 24 of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure, the presiding judge has significant discretion over the structure of voir dire. The judge may ask questions of the jury pool or, as in this case, allow the attorneys for the parties to do so. Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 24(a); App. 20. A magistrate judge\xe2\x80\x99s or a district judge\xe2\x80\x99s particular approach to voir dire both in substance\xe2\x80\x94the questions asked\xe2\x80\x94and in tone\xe2\x80\x94formal or informal\xe2\x80\x94may be relevant in light of the attorney\xe2\x80\x99s own approach. The attorney may decide whether to accept the magistrate judge based in part on these factors. As with other tactical decisions, requiring personal, on-the-record approval from the client could necessitate a lengthy explanation the client might not understand at the moment and that might distract from more pressing matters as the attorney seeks to prepare the best defense. For these reasons we conclude that express consent by counsel suffices to permit a magistrate judge to preside over jury selection in a felony trial, pursuant to the authorization in \xc2\xa7636(b)(3).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our holding is not inconsistent with reading other precedents to hold that some basic trial choices are so important that an attorney must seek the client\xe2\x80\x99s consent in order to waive the right. See, e.g., Florida v. Nixon, 543 U. S. 175, 187 (2004) (identifying the choices \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98to plead guilty, waive a jury, testify in his or her own behalf, or take an appeal\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d as examples (quoting Jones, supra, at 751)). Petitioner argues that the decision to have a magistrate judge rather than an Article III judge preside at jury selection is a fundamental choice, cf. Hill, 528 U. S., at 114, or, at least, raises a question of constitutional significance so that we should interpret the Act to require an explicit personal statement of consent before the magistrate judge can proceed with jury selection.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We conclude otherwise. Under the avoidance canon, \xe2\x80\x9cwhen \xe2\x80\x98a statute is susceptible of two constructions, by one of which grave and doubtful constitutional questions arise and by the other of which such questions are avoided, our duty is to adopt the latter.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Harris v. United States, 536 U. S. 545, 555 (2002) (quoting United States ex rel. Attorney General v. Delaware & Hudson Co., 213 U. S. 366, 408 (1909)). The canon, however, does not apply unless there are \xe2\x80\x9cserious concerns about the statute\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality.\xe2\x80\x9d Harris, supra, at 555; see also Reno v. Flores, 507 U. S. 292, 314, n. 9 (1993).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Those concerns are not present here. Petitioner concedes that a magistrate judge is capable of competent and impartial performance of the judicial tasks involved in jury examination and selection. Reply Brief for Petitioner 12\xe2\x80\x9313; see also Peretz, 501 U. S., at 935 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Act evinces a congressional belief that magistrates are well qualified to handle matters of similar importance to jury selection\xe2\x80\x9d). The Act contains some features to ensure impartiality. See, e.g., 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7631(i) (establishing requirements for removal), 632 (limiting concurrent employment), 634(b) (providing salary protection during the term). And \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98the district judge\xe2\x80\x94insulated by life tenure and irreducible salary\xe2\x80\x94is waiting in the wings, fully able to correct errors.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Peretz, supra, at 938 (quoting United States v. Raddatz, 447 U. S. 667, 686 (1980) (Blackmun, J., concurring)). Here petitioner made no objections to the rulings by the Magistrate Judge. Had objections been made, nothing in the record or the rules indicates that the District Judge could not have ruled on the issues, all with no delay or prejudice to any trial that had commenced. See Peretz, supra, at 935, n. 12, 939. These factors support our determination that consent of counsel suffices to allow a magistrate judge to supervise voir dire. This is not a case where the magistrate judge is asked to preside or make determinations after the trial has commenced and it is arguably difficult or disruptive for a district judge to review any objections that might have been made to the magistrate judge\xe2\x80\x99s rulings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner notes that Peretz considered supervision over entire civil and misdemeanor trials comparable to presiding over voir dire at a felony trial. 501 U. S., at 933. It follows, he argues, that \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) must require, as does\n18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73401(b), express personal consent by the defendant before a magistrate judge may preside over voir dire. But it is not obvious that Congress would have thought these matters required the same form of consent. Aside from the fact that the statutory text is different, there are relevant differences between presiding over a full trial and presiding over voir dire. Were petitioner correct, one would think the Act would require at least\nthe same form of consent to authorize a magistrate judge to preside over either a civil or a misdemeanor trial\n(which Peretz also deemed to be of comparable importance). Our interpretation of the Act indicates otherwise. Compare \xc2\xa73401(b), with Roell v. Withrow, 538 U. S. 580, 590 (2003) (concluding that parties may authorize a\nfull-time magistrate judge to preside over a civil trial via implied consent).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner argues that our view of the issue should be informed by Gomez\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that having a magistrate judge during jury selection without consent is structural error, not subject to harmless-error review. See 490 U. S., at 876. The exemption of certain errors from harmless-error review \xe2\x80\x9crecognizes that some errors necessarily render a trial fundamentally unfair.\xe2\x80\x9d Rose v. Clark, 478 U. S. 570, 577 (1986); see also id., at 577\xe2\x80\x93578. In petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s view, Gomez establishes that the issue in this case is of sufficient gravity or concern that personal consent must be required.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court held in Gomez that imposition of a magistrate judge over objection was structural error, violating the basic right to a trial conducted at all critical stages by a judicial officer with appropriate jurisdiction. 490 U. S., at 876. It does not follow, however, that this structural aspect requires an insistence on personal consent. Here, jurisdiction turns on consent; and for the reasons discussed above an attorney, acting on the client\xe2\x80\x99s behalf, can make an informed decision to allow the magistrate judge to exercise the jurisdiction Congress permits.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Although a criminal defendant may demand that an Article III judge preside over the selection of a jury, the choice to do so reflects considerations more significant to the realm of the attorney than to the accused. Requiring the defendant to consent to a magistrate judge only by way of an on-the-record personal statement is not dictated by precedent and would burden the trial process, with little added protection for the defendant.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Pursuant to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) a magistrate judge may preside over jury examination and jury selection only if the parties, or the attorneys for the parties, consent. Consent from an attorney will suffice. We do not have before us, and we do not address, an instance where the attorney states consent but the party by express and timely objection seeks to override his or her counsel. We need not decide, moreover, if consent may be inferred from a failure by a party and his or her attorney to object to the presiding by a magistrate judge. These issues are not presented here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court that no statute or rule requires that petitioner personally participate in the waiver of his right to have an Article III judge oversee voir dire. As to whether the Constitution requires that, the Court holds that it does not because it is a decision more tactical than fundamental\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cmore significant to the realm of the attorney than to the accused.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion, but not with the tactical-vs.-fundamental test on which it is based.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner and the Government do not dispute that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel consented to have a magistrate judge oversee voir dire. The issue is whether that consent\xe2\x80\x94consent of counsel alone\xe2\x80\x94effected a valid waiver of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s right to an Article III judge. It is important to bear in mind that we are not speaking here of action taken by counsel over his client\xe2\x80\x99s objection\xe2\x80\x94which would have the effect of revoking the agency with respect to the action in question. See Brookhart v. Janis, 384 U. S. 1, 7\xe2\x80\x938 (1966). There is no suggestion of that. The issue is whether consent expressed by counsel alone is ineffective simply because the defendant himself did not express to the court his consent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I think not. Our opinions have sometimes said in passing that, under the Constitution, certain \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cbasic\xe2\x80\x9d rights cannot be waived unless a defendant personally participates in the waiver. See, e.g., Taylor v. Illinois, 484 U. S. 400, 417\xe2\x80\x93418 (1988); United States v. Olano, 507 U. S. 725, 733 (1993). We have even repeated the suggestion in cases that actually involved the question whether a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s attorney could waive a certain right\xe2\x80\x94but never in a case where the suggestion governed the disposition. In New York v. Hill, 528 U. S. 110 (2000), although we noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or certain fundamental rights, the defendant must personally make an informed waiver,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 114, we in fact found such a requirement inapplicable; and even that determination can be viewed as resting upon an interpretation of the statute creating the right that counsel had waived, see id., at 115. And in Florida v. Nixon, 543 U. S. 175 (2004), although we said that \xe2\x80\x9ccounsel lacks authority to consent to a guilty plea on [his] client\xe2\x80\x99s behalf,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 187, our holding was simply that counsel\xe2\x80\x99s concession of guilt to the jury did not amount to a guilty plea, id., at 188, and did not constitute ineffective assistance of counsel, id., at 192. As detailed in the margin, the decisions often cited for the principle of attorney incapacity are inapposite;[Footnote 1] except for one line of precedent, no decision of this Court holds that, as a constitutional matter, a defendant must personally waive certain of his \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental\xe2\x80\x9d rights\xe2\x80\x94which typically are identified as the rights to trial, jury, and counsel. The exceptional line of precedent involves the right to counsel. See Johnson v. Zerbst, 304 U. S. 458, 464\xe2\x80\x93465 (1938). But that right is essentially sui generis, since an unrepresented defendant cannot possibly waive his right to counsel except in person. Cases involving that right therefore provide no support for the principle that the Constitution sometimes forbids attorney waiver.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Since a formula repeated in dictum but never the basis for judgment is not owed stare decisis weight, see Lingle v. Chevron U. S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc., 544 U. S. 528, 545\xe2\x80\x93546 (2005), our precedents have not established the rule of decision applicable in this case. I would not adopt the tactical-vs.-fundamental approach, which is vague and derives from nothing more substantial than this Court\xe2\x80\x99s say-so. One respected authority has noted that the approach has a \xe2\x80\x9cpotential for uncertainty,\xe2\x80\x9d and that our precedents purporting to apply it \xe2\x80\x9chave been brief and conclusionary.\xe2\x80\x9d 3 W. LaFave, J. Israel, N. King, & O. Kerr, Criminal Procedure \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa711.6(a), (c), pp. 784, 796 (3d ed. 2007). That is surely an understatement. What makes a right tactical? Depending on the circumstances, waiving any right can be a tactical decision. Even pleading guilty, which waives the right to trial, is highly tactical, since it usually requires balancing the prosecutor\xe2\x80\x99s plea bargain against the prospect of better and worse outcomes at trial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whether a right is \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental\xe2\x80\x9d is equally mysterious. One would think that any right guaranteed by the Constitution would be fundamental. But I doubt many think that the Sixth Amendment right to confront witnesses cannot be waived by counsel. See Diaz v. United States, 223 U. S. 442, 444, 452\xe2\x80\x93453 (1912). Perhaps, then, specification in the Constitution is a necessary, but not sufficient, condition for \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental\xe2\x80\x9d status. But if something more is necessary, I cannot imagine what it might be. Apart from constitutional guarantee, I know of no objective criterion for ranking rights. The Court concludes that the right to have an Article III judge oversee voir dire is not a fundamental right, ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9310, without answering whether it is even a constitutional right,[Footnote 2] and without explaining what makes a right fundamental in the first place. The essence of \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental\xe2\x80\x9d rights continues to elude.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I would therefore adopt the rule that, as a constitutional matter, all waivable rights (except, of course, the right to counsel) can be waived by counsel. There is no basis in the Constitution, or as far as I am aware in common-law practice, for distinguishing in this regard between a criminal defendant and his authorized representative. In fact, the very notion of representative litigation suggests that the Constitution draws no distinction between them. \xe2\x80\x9cA prisoner \xe2\x80\xa6 who defends by counsel, and silently acquiesces in what they agree to, is bound as any other principal by the act of his agent.\xe2\x80\x9d People v. Rathbun, 21 Wend. 509, 543 (N.\xc2\xa0Y. Sup. Ct. 1839). The Rathbun opinion, far from being the outlier view of a state court, was adopted as the common law position by eminent jurists of the 19th century, including Chief Justice Shaw of the Supreme Judicial Court of Massachusetts. See Commonwealth v. Dailey, 66 Mass. 80, 83 (1853) (discussing Rathbun with approval in a case involving waiver of the right to a 12-man jury).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It may well be desirable to require a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s personal waiver with regard to certain rights. Rule 11(c) of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure, for example, provides that before accepting a guilty plea the court must \xe2\x80\x9caddress the defendant personally in open court,\xe2\x80\x9d advise him of the consequences of his plea, and assure that the plea is voluntary. See also Rule 10(b) (waiver of right to appear at arraignment must be in writing signed by counsel and defendant). I do not contend that the Sixth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s right to assistance of counsel prohibits such requirements of personal participation, at least where they do not impair counsel\xe2\x80\x99s expert assistance.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even without such rules it is certainly prudent, to forestall later challenges to counsel\xe2\x80\x99s conduct, for a trial court to satisfy itself of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s personal consent to certain actions, such as entry of a guilty plea or waiver of jury trial, for which objective norms require an attorney to seek his client\xe2\x80\x99s authorization. See, e.g., ABA Model Rule of Professional Conduct 1.2(a) (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cIn a criminal case, the lawyer shall abide by the client\xe2\x80\x99s decision, after consultation with the lawyer, as to a plea to be entered, whether to waive jury trial and whether the client will testify\xe2\x80\x9d). But I know of no basis for saying that the Constitution automatically invalidates any trial action not taken by the defendant personally, though taken by his authorized counsel. I know of no way of determining, except by sheer prescription, which trial rights are ex ante and by law subject to such a limitation upon waiver. Assuredly the tactical-fundamental dichotomy does not do the trick. I would leave this matter of placing reasonable limits upon the right of agency in criminal trials to be governed by positive law, in statutes and rules of procedure.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I would hold that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s counsel\xe2\x80\x99s waiver was effective because no rule or statute provides that the waiver come from the defendant personally.

\nFootnote 1

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court holds today that neither the Federal Magistrates Act, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7631 et seq., nor the Constitution requires that a criminal defendant on trial for a felony personally give his informed consent before a magistrate judge may preside over jury selection. The Court proceeds from the premise, established in Peretz v. United States, 501 U. S. 923 (1991), that the Federal Magistrates Act authorizes magistrate judges to preside over felony jury selection if the parties consent. I reject that premise, and for the reasons set forth below, would overrule Peretz and hold that the delegation of voir dire in this case was statutory error. I further conclude that the error may be corrected despite petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s failure to raise a timely objection in the District Court. Accordingly, I would reverse the judgment below.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This is the third time the Court has addressed the circumstances under which a district judge may delegate felony voir dire proceedings to a magistrate judge under the \xe2\x80\x9cadditional duties\xe2\x80\x9d clause of the Federal Magistrates Act, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7636(b)(3). In Gomez v. United States, 490 U. S. 858 (1989), the Court unanimously held that \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) does not authorize delegation of felony voir dire proceedings to a magistrate judge. Although the defendants in Gomez had objected to the delegation, neither the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning nor its conclusion turned on that fact. Rather, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) rested primarily on two inferences drawn from the statutory scheme. First, the Court reasoned that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9ccarefully defined grant of authority to conduct trials of civil matters and of minor criminal cases should be construed as an implicit withholding of the authority to preside at a felony trial.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 872. Second, the Court found it \xe2\x80\x9cincongruous\xe2\x80\x9d to assume that Congress intended felony jury selection to be among magistrate judges\xe2\x80\x99 additional duties but failed to provide an explicit standard of review as it had done for other duties described in the statute. Id., at 874. Neither of these inferences depended on the presence or absence of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 consent, and the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion was accordingly unqualified: \xe2\x80\x9cCongress \xe2\x80\xa6 did not contemplate inclusion of jury selection in felony trials among a magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s additional duties.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 872; see also ibid., n. 25 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e decide that the Federal Magistrates Act does not allow the delegation of jury selection to magistrates\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 875\xe2\x80\x93876 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe absence of a specific reference to jury selection in the statute, or indeed, in the legislative history, persuades us that Congress did not intend the additional duties clause to embrace this function\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Two years later, the Court decided Peretz. Peretz\xe2\x80\x99s trial took place before this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Gomez, and his attorney had agreed to the delegation of voir dire, assuring the Magistrate Judge that his client had consented. During the pendency of Peretz\xe2\x80\x99s appeal the Court decided Gomez, and Peretz argued that Gomez required reversal of his conviction. The Court of Appeals disagreed, concluding that he had waived any challenge to the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s supervision of voir dire. Before this Court, the Government defended the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 holding as to waiver but confessed error with respect to the delegation of voir dire, \xe2\x80\x9cagree[ing] with petitioner \xe2\x80\xa6 that Gomez foreclose[d] the argument that the statute may be read to authorize magistrate-conducted voir dire when the defendant consents.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States in Peretz v. United States, O. T. 1990, No. 90\xe2\x80\x93615, p. 9.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite the Government\xe2\x80\x99s confession of error, the Court, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n an amazing display of interpretive gymnastics,\xe2\x80\x9d Peretz, supra, at 940\xe2\x80\x93941 (Marshall, J., dissenting), held in a 5-to-4 decision that \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) does, after all, permit magistrate judges to conduct felony voir dire proceedings, so long as the parties consent. There is no need here to reproduce Peretz\xe2\x80\x99s flawed reasoning or to rehash the debate between the majority and dissenting opinions. Suffice it to say that, in my view, Gomez correctly interpreted \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) not to authorize delegation of felony jury selection regardless of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 consent, and I agree with the dissenters in Peretz that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s contrary conclusion in that case was based on a patently \xe2\x80\x9crevisionist construction of the Act.\xe2\x80\x9d 501 U. S., at 947 (opinion of Marshall, J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The only question, then, is whether to give stare decisis effect to Peretz\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous conclusion that \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) authorizes magistrate judges to conduct felony jury selection if the parties consent. Although \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is true that we give stronger stare decisis effect to our holdings in statutory cases than in constitutional cases,\xe2\x80\x9d that rule \xe2\x80\x9cis not absolute, and we should not hesitate to allow our precedent to yield to the true meaning of an Act of Congress when our statutory precedent is \xe2\x80\x98unworkable\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98badly reasoned.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Clark v. Martinez, 543 U. S. 371, 401\xe2\x80\x93402 (2005) (Thomas, J., dissenting). Peretz is both. Two considerations in particular convince me that Peretz should be overruled.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, Peretz leaves the Court with no principled way to decide the statutory question presented in this case. Contrary to the Court\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion, the question presented here is not whether \xe2\x80\x9cevery instance of waiver requires the personal consent of the client,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8; rather, it is the far narrower question whether \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) requires the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s personal consent before felony jury selection may be delegated to a magistrate judge. The Court answers this question in the negative, but does not point to anything in \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) or in the broader statutory scheme that supports its conclusion. It does not because it cannot. Not having provided for delegation of felony voir dire proceedings under the additional duties clause, Congress of course did not specify whether the parties\xe2\x80\x99 consent is required. And \xe2\x80\x9c[b]ecause the additional duties clause contains no language predicating delegation of an additional duty upon litigant consent, it likewise contains nothing indicating what constitutes \xe2\x80\x98consent\xe2\x80\x99 to the delegation of an additional duty.\xe2\x80\x9d Peretz, supra, at 947, n.\xc2\xa06 (Marshall, J., dissenting).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress\xe2\x80\x99 silence is particularly telling in the context of the Federal Magistrates Act. Elsewhere in the Act, Congress took great care to specify whether and in what manner the litigants must consent before a magistrate judge may assume significant duties. In \xc2\xa7636(c)(1), for example, Congress provided that full-time magistrate judges may conduct civil trials \xe2\x80\x9c[u]pon the consent of the parties,\xe2\x80\x9d and that part-time magistrate judges may do so \xe2\x80\x9c[u]pon the consent of the parties, pursuant to their specific written request.\xe2\x80\x9d Congress further provided in \xc2\xa7636(c)(2) that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he decision of the parties shall be communicated to the clerk of court,\xe2\x80\x9d and, if the parties do not consent, the district judge may not raise the matter again without \xe2\x80\x9cadvis[ing] the parties that they are free to withhold consent without adverse substantive consequences.\xe2\x80\x9d I have previously explained at length why \xc2\xa7636(c) is best read to require the express consent of the parties, see Roell v. Withrow, 538 U. S. 580, 591\xe2\x80\x93597 (2003) (dissenting opinion), and I will not repeat that discussion here, other than to point out that Congress obviously focused on the issue and gave detailed instructions regarding the form of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 consent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even more telling is that Congress required the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s express, informed consent before a magistrate judge may conduct a misdemeanor trial. Section 636(a)(3) authorizes magistrate judges to conduct certain misdemeanor trials \xe2\x80\x9cin conformity with and subject to the limitations of\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73401, which spells out in detail the manner in which the defendant must consent:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe magistrate judge shall carefully explain to the defendant that he has a right to trial, judgment, and sentencing by a district judge and that he may have a right to trial by jury before a district judge or magistrate judge. The magistrate judge may not proceed to try the case unless the defendant, after such explanation, expressly consents to be tried before the magistrate judge and expressly and specifically waives trial, judgment, and sentencing by a district judge. Any such consent and waiver shall be made in writing or orally on the record.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73401(b).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court recites the language of \xc2\xa73401(b), but gives it no weight in its analysis. It recognizes that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here are instances in federal criminal proceedings where the procedural requisites for consent are specified and a right cannot be waived except with a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own informed consent.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4. But it is given no pause by the fact that the Federal Magistrates Act, the very statute it interprets to permit delegation of felony proceedings without the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cown informed consent,\xe2\x80\x9d expressly requires such consent before a magistrate judge may conduct a misdemeanor trial. Instead, the Court worries that \xe2\x80\x9crequiring personal, on-the-record approval from the client could necessitate a lengthy explanation the client might not understand at the moment and that might distract from more pressing matters,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8, heedless of the fact that Congress plainly viewed any such \xe2\x80\x9cburden[s] [on] the trial process,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11, as outweighed by the need to obtain the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s personal consent before a magistrate judge may preside, even over a misdemeanor trial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court glides over this glaring anomaly, asserting that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he controlling statute in this case has a different design,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not state that consent to preside over felony voir dire must be granted by following a procedure of similar clarity.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. But there is only one statute at issue here\xe2\x80\x94the Federal Magistrates Act expressly incorporates 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73401(b)\xe2\x80\x94and the fact that it does not mandate \xe2\x80\x9ca procedure of similar clarity\xe2\x80\x9d for delegation of felony jury selection is hardly surprising, since \xc2\xa7636(b)(3)\xe2\x80\x94which provides in its entirety that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] magistrate judge may be assigned such additional duties as are not inconsistent with the Constitution and laws of the United States\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94says not a word about delegation of felony jury selection, much less about whether and in what form the parties must consent.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court further suggests that \xc2\xa73401(b) is inconsequential because \xe2\x80\x9cthere are relevant differences between presiding over a full trial and presiding over voir dire.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 10. But even Peretz recognized that \xe2\x80\x9csupervision of entire civil and misdemeanor trials\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9ccomparable in responsibility and importance to presiding over voir dire at a felony trial.\xe2\x80\x9d 501 U. S., at 933. And of course, it was Congress\xe2\x80\x99 omission of any mode of consent for delegation of felony proceedings, in contradistinction to its detailed treatment of the consent required for delegation of civil and misdemeanor trials, that drove Gomez\xe2\x80\x99s analysis. 490 U. S., at 872 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he carefully defined grant of authority to conduct trials of civil matters and of minor criminal cases should be construed as an implicit withholding of the authority to preside at a felony trial\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Peretz, supra, at 955 (Scalia, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cBy specifically authorizing magistrates to perform duties in civil and misdemeanor trials, and specifying the manner in which parties were to express their consent in those situations, the statute suggested absence of authority to preside over felony trials through some (unspecified) mode of consent\xe2\x80\x9d). Today\xe2\x80\x99s decision is truly an ironic reversal. The Court once thought that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 differential treatment of felony jury selection and misdemeanor trials was a reason to believe that Congress had entirely withheld authority to preside over felony jury selection. Today, however, the Court says that the \xe2\x80\x9crelevant differences\xe2\x80\x9d between these responsibilities support the conclusion that Congress permitted delegation of felony jury selection upon a lesser showing of consent than that required for delegation of a misdemeanor trial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the end, I am sympathetic to petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s argument that \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) should be read in pari materia with \xc2\xa73401(b). See Brief for Petitioner 38 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf, in enacting the [Federal Magistrates Act] Congress believed a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s explicit, personal consent was constitutionally necessary to bestow authority upon a magistrate judge in federal misdemeanor cases, then a fortiori Congress would have believed that such explicit, personal consent is necessary to permit a magistrate judge to conduct felony jury selection\xe2\x80\x9d). And I share his view that Congress undoubtedly would have adopted something akin to \xc2\xa73401(b)\xe2\x80\x99s requirements had it authorized delegation of felony jury selection. See Peretz, supra, at 947, n.\xc2\xa06 (Marshall, J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9cI would think, however, that the standard governing a party\xe2\x80\x99s consent to delegation of a portion of a felony trial under the additional duties clause should be at least as strict as that governing delegation of a misdemeanor trial to a magistrate\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nonetheless, I do not believe that Peretz\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous interpretation of \xc2\xa7636(b)(3) gives me license to rewrite the Federal Magistrates Act to reflect what I think Congress would have done had it contemplated delegation of felony jury selection or foreseen the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Peretz. Cf. Brief for Petitioner 33 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhat the Court is left to do in petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s case is to fill the gap by determining what Congress would have done in enacting the [Federal Magistrates Act] had it expressly addressed the \xe2\x80\x98crucial\xe2\x80\x99 consent issue\xe2\x80\x9d (footnote omitted)). Where, as here, a mistaken interpretation of a statute leaves the Court with no principled way to answer subsequent questions that arise under the statute, it seems to me that the better course is simply to acknowledge and correct the error. Cf. Kimbrough v. United States, 552 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 3) (Thomas, J., dissenting).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0A second reason why I would not give stare decisis effect to Peretz is that it requires us to wade into a constitutional morass. In Gomez, the Court declined to decide whether the Constitution permits delegation of felony jury selection to a magistrate judge. 490 U. S., at 872, n. 25. Peretz simply brushed aside that difficult constitutional question. See 501 U. S., at 936 (\xe2\x80\x9cThere is no constitutional infirmity in the delegation of felony trial jury selection to a magistrate when the litigants consent\xe2\x80\x9d); cf. id., at 948\xe2\x80\x93952 (Marshall, J., dissenting) (discussing the \xe2\x80\x9cserious constitutional question\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cwhether jury selection by a magistrate\xe2\x80\x94even when a defendant consents\xe2\x80\x94is consistent with Article III\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 956 (Scalia, J., dissenting) (not resolving \xe2\x80\x9cthe serious and difficult constitutional questions raised by the [majority\xe2\x80\x99s] construction,\xe2\x80\x9d but suggesting that the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning rendered \xe2\x80\x9cthe doctrine of unconstitutional delegation\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9ca dead letter\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Today the Court\xe2\x80\x99s result requires it to go even further. In addition to reaffirming Peretz\xe2\x80\x99s questionable holding that the Constitution permits delegation of felony voir dire proceedings to a non-Article III judge, the Court decides that a criminal defendant\xe2\x80\x99s waiver of his right to an Article III judge need not be personal and informed. The Court treats this as an easy question, concluding that the choice between an Article III judge and a magistrate judge is not among those \xe2\x80\x9cbasic trial choices,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8, that require a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s personal consent because \xe2\x80\x9ca magistrate judge is capable of competent and impartial performance of the judicial tasks involved in jury examination and selection,\xe2\x80\x9d and because magistrate judges are supervised by Article III judges, ante, at 9, 10. Under our precedents, however, the question is not so easily dispatched.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our cases shed little light on whether and when a criminal defendant must personally waive a constitutional right. Although we have previously stated that, \xe2\x80\x9c[f]or certain fundamental rights, the defendant must personally make an informed waiver,\xe2\x80\x9d New York v. Hill, 528 U. S. 110, 114 (2000), many of the cases we have cited for that proposition do not in fact stand for it. For example, we have cited Brookhart v. Janis, 384 U. S. 1 (1966), for the proposition that the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s personal consent is required for a waiver of his right to plead not guilty. See, e.g., Hill, supra, at 114. But Brookhart\xe2\x80\x99s holding was narrower. The only question presented there was \xe2\x80\x9cwhether counsel has power to enter a plea which is inconsistent with his client\xe2\x80\x99s expressed desire and thereby waive his client\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional right to plead not guilty and have a trial in which he can confront and cross-examine the witnesses against him.\xe2\x80\x9d 384 U. S., at 7 (emphasis added). Brookhart thus did not decide whether the Constitution prohibits counsel from entering a guilty plea on his client\xe2\x80\x99s behalf in cases where the defendant has not expressed a contrary desire.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, Patton v. United States, 281 U. S. 276 (1930), a case often cited for the proposition that the right to a jury trial can be waived only by the defendant personally, does not draw a clear distinction between the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s own consent and that of his attorney. The Court stated in dicta that \xe2\x80\x9cthe express and intelligent consent of the defendant\xe2\x80\x9d is required \xe2\x80\x9cbefore any waiver [of the right to a jury trial] can become effective.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 312. But that requirement appears to have been satisfied in Patton by counsel\xe2\x80\x99s representation to the trial court that he had conferred with his clients and obtained their consent. Id., at 286\xe2\x80\x93287.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our cases thus provide little relevant guidance. Justice Scalia may well be correct that, as a matter of first principles, there is no right (other than perhaps the sui generis right to counsel) that cannot be waived by a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s attorney, acting as the duly authorized agent of his client. See ante, at 4 (opinion concurring in judgment). But if I were to accept the Court\xe2\x80\x99s oft-repeated dictum that there are certain fundamental rights that can be waived only by the defendant personally, see, e.g., Florida v. Nixon, 543 U. S. 175, 187 (2004) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ertain decisions regarding the exercise or waiver of basic trial rights are of such moment that they cannot be made for the defendant by a surrogate\xe2\x80\x9d); Hill, supra, at 114; Taylor v. Illinois, 484 U. S. 400, 417\xe2\x80\x93418 (1988) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]here are basic rights that the attorney cannot waive without the fully informed and publicly acknowledged consent of the client\xe2\x80\x9d), I see no reason why the right to an Article III judge should not be among them.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is no apparent reason, for example, why the right to an Article III judge should be deemed any less fundamental, or its exercise any more \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98tactical,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7 (opinion of the Court) (quoting Taylor, supra, at 418), than the right to a jury trial. The Framers viewed independent judges, no less than the right to a jury of one\xe2\x80\x99s peers, as indispensable to a fair trial. See, e.g., United States v. Hatter, 532 U. S. 557, 568\xe2\x80\x93569 (2001) (discussing the Framers\xe2\x80\x99 overriding concern for an independent Judiciary and quoting Chief Justice Marshall\xe2\x80\x99s statement that the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98greatest scourge \xe2\x80\xa6 ever inflicted\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98was an ignorant, a corrupt, or a dependent Judiciary\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Proceedings and Debates of the Virginia State Convention, of 1829\xe2\x80\x931830, p. 619 (1830))). For that reason, the Constitution affords Article III judges the structural protections of life tenure and salary protection. Art.\xc2\xa0III, \xc2\xa71. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s observation that \xe2\x80\x9ca magistrate judge is capable of competent and impartial performance\xe2\x80\x9d of judicial duties, ante, at 9, is thus beside the point.* Magistrate judges do not enjoy the structural protections of Article III: They serve 8-year terms and may be removed for cause, 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7631(e), (i), and they are subject to diminution of their salaries and outright abolition of their offices by Congress. Accordingly, whatever their virtues, magistrate judges are no substitute for Article III judges in the eyes of the Constitution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In short, if I accepted the Court\xe2\x80\x99s dictum that the right to a jury trial may be waived only by the defendant personally, see, e.g., Nixon, supra, at 187, I would be hard pressed to conclude that waiver of the right to an Article III judge during a critical stage of a felony trial requires anything less. That said, I include this brief discussion of the constitutional issues this case presents not because I would decide them, but to point out that the Court gives them short shrift. These are serious constitutional questions, see Roell, 538 U. S., at 595 (Thomas, J., dissenting), and they are posed only because of Peretz\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous interpretation of the Federal Magistrates Act. Indeed, I suspect that Congress withheld from magistrate judges the authority to preside during felony trials precisely in order to avoid the constitutional questions Peretz now thrusts upon us. Again, rather than plow headlong into this constitutional thicket, the better choice is simply to overrule Peretz. Cf. Peretz, 501 U. S., at 952 (Marshall, J., dissenting) (finding the Court\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of difficult Article III questions \xe2\x80\x9cparticularly unfortunate\xe2\x80\x9d where \xe2\x80\x9cthe most coherent reading of the Federal Magistrates Act avoids these problems entirely\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because I conclude that Peretz should be overruled, and that the District Court therefore erred in delegating voir dire to the Magistrate Judge, I must also address the Government\xe2\x80\x99s alternative argument that petitioner forfeited his claim by failing to object to the delegation. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s failure to object, the Government contends, means that he cannot prevail unless he satisfies the requirements of the plain-error rule. It is true that petitioner did not raise a timely objection to the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s delegation of voir dire. And petitioner cannot satisfy the plain-error rule because the statutory error below\xe2\x80\x94unauthorized delegation of voir dire to a magistrate judge\xe2\x80\x94was not \xe2\x80\x9cplain\xe2\x80\x9d under Peretz.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Not all uncontested errors, however, are subject to the plain-error rule. In limited circumstances, we have \xe2\x80\x9cagreed to correct, at least on direct review, violations of a statutory provision that embodies a strong policy concerning the proper administration of judicial business even though the defect was not raised in a timely manner.\xe2\x80\x9d Nguyen v. United States, 539 U. S. 69, 78 (2003) (internal quotation marks omitted). In Nguyen, a non-Article III judge sat by designation on the Ninth Circuit panel that affirmed petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 convictions. Petitioners failed to object to the composition of the panel in the Ninth Circuit and raised the issue for the first time in their petitions for certiorari. Because the Ninth Circuit panel \xe2\x80\x9ccontravened the statutory requirements set by Congress for the composition of the federal courts of appeals,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 80, and because those requirements \xe2\x80\x9cembodie[d] weighty congressional policy concerning the proper organization of the federal courts,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 79, we held that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 failure to object did not preclude relief. We specifically declined to apply the plain-error rule:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIt is true, as the Government observes, that a failure to object to trial error ordinarily limits an appellate court to review for plain error. See 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72111; Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 52(b). But to ignore the violation of the designation statute in these cases would incorrectly suggest that some action (or inaction) on petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 part could create authority Congress has quite carefully withheld. Even if the parties had expressly stipulated to the participation of a non-Article III judge in the consideration of their appeals, no matter how distinguished and well qualified the judge might be, such a stipulation would not have cured the plain defect in the composition of the panel.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 80\xe2\x80\x9381.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I see no reason to treat this case differently than Nguyen. Just as \xe2\x80\x9cCongress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to preserve the Article III character of the courts of appeals [was] more than a trivial concern\xe2\x80\x9d in that case, id., at 80, so too here Congress\xe2\x80\x99 decision to preserve the Article III character of felony trials \xe2\x80\x9cembodies weighty congressional policy concerning the proper organization of the federal courts,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 79. Accordingly, as in Nguyen, the Court can and should correct the error in this case despite petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s failure to raise a timely objection below.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For the reasons stated, I would reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand for a new trial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0*\xc2\xa0Equally beside the point is the fact that magistrate judges are appointed by, and subject to the supervision of, district judges. The Court reassures itself by hypothesizing that the District Court could have ruled on any objections to the Magistrate Judge\xe2\x80\x99s rulings. Ante, at 10. But the Court once \xe2\x80\x9charbor[ed] serious doubts\xe2\x80\x9d that a district judge who was not present during jury selection could \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningfully\xe2\x80\x9d review a magistrate judge\xe2\x80\x99s rulings, Gomez v. United States, 490 U. S. 858, 874 (1989), because \xe2\x80\x9cno transcript can recapture the atmosphere of the voir dire,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 875. The Court does not explain what has intervened to dispel those doubts. And even if district judges could meaningfully review magistrate judges\xe2\x80\x99 voir dire rulings, that would not change the fact that magistrate judges are subject to outside influences in ways that Article III judges are not. As Judge Posner has explained:

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe fact that the appointing power has been given to Article III judges is the opposite of reassuring. It makes magistrates beholden to judges as well as to Congress\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. The Constitution built internal checks and balances into the legislative branch by making Congress bicameral and into the judicial branch by guaranteeing all federal judges\xe2\x80\x94not just Supreme Court Justices, or appellate judges generally\xe2\x80\x94tenure during good behavior and protection against pay cuts. Appellate judges can reverse district judges, can mandamus them, can criticize them, can remand a case to another judge, but cannot fire district judges, cow them, or silence them\xe2\x80\x94cannot prevent them from making independent judgments and expressing independent views. \xe2\x80\xa6 [A]s long as [district judges] enjoy the tenure and compensation protections of Article III, they are independent of [those] who appointed them \xe2\x80\xa6 . Magistrates do not have those protections; the judges control their reappointment.\xe2\x80\x9d Geras v. Lafayette Display Fixtures, Inc., 742 F.\xc2\xa02d 1037, 1053 (CA7 1984) (dissenting opinion).
