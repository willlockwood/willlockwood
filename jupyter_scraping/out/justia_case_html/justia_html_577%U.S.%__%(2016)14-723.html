Syllabus

MONTANILE v. BOARD OF TRUSTEES OF THE NATIONAL ELEVATOR INDUSTRY HEALTH BENEFIT PLAN

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 14\xe2\x80\x93723.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 9, 2015\xe2\x80\x94Decided January 20, 2016

\n

Employee benefits plans regulated by the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA or Act) often contain subrogation clauses requiring a plan participant to reimburse the plan for medical expenses if the participant later recovers money from a third party for his injuries. Here, petitioner Montanile was seriously injured by a drunk driver, and his ERISA plan paid more than $120,000 for his medical expenses. Montanile later sued the drunk driver, obtaining a $500,000 settlement. Pursuant to the plan\xe2\x80\x99s subrogation clause, respondent plan administrator (the Board of Trustees of the National Elevator Industry Health Benefit Plan, or Board), sought reimbursement from the settlement. Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s attorney refused that request and subsequently informed the Board that the fund would be transferred from a client trust account to Montanile unless the Board objected. The Board did not respond, and Montanile received the settlement.

\n

Six months later, the Board sued Montanile in Federal District Court under \xc2\xa7502(a)(3) of ERISA, which authorizes plan fiduciaries to file suit \xe2\x80\x9cto obtain .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. appropriate equitable relief .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to enforce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the terms of the plan.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71132(a)(3). The Board sought an equitable lien on any settlement funds or property in Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s possession and an order enjoining Montanile from dissipating any such funds. Montanile argued that because he had already spent almost all of the settlement, no identifiable fund existed against which to enforce the lien. The District Court rejected Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s argument, and the Eleventh Circuit affirmed, holding that even if Montanile had completely dissipated the fund, the plan was entitled to reimbursement from Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s general assets.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0When an ERISA-plan participant wholly dissipates a third-party settlement on nontraceable items, the plan fiduciary may not bring suit under \xc2\xa7502(a)(3) to attach the participant\xe2\x80\x99s separate assets. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Plan fiduciaries are limited by \xc2\xa7502(a)(3) to filing suits \xe2\x80\x9cto obtain .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. equitable relief.\xe2\x80\x9d Whether the relief requested \xe2\x80\x9cis legal or equitable depends on [1] the basis for [the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s] claim and [2] the nature of the underlying remedies sought.\xe2\x80\x9d Sereboff v. Mid Atlantic Medical Services, Inc.,\n\n547 U. S. 356\n. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(1)\xc2\xa0This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents establish that the basis for the Board\xe2\x80\x99s claim\xe2\x80\x94the enforcement of a lien created by an agreement to convey a particular fund to another party\xe2\x80\x94is equitable. See Sereboff, 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 363\xe2\x80\x93364. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents also establish that the nature of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s underlying remedy\xe2\x80\x94enforcement of a lien against \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically identifiable funds that were within [Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s] possession and control,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 362\xe2\x80\x93363\xe2\x80\x94would also have been equitable had the Board immediately sued to enforce the lien against the fund. But those propositions do not resolve the question here: whether a plan is still seeking an equitable remedy when the defendant has dissipated all of a separate settlement fund, and the plan then seeks to recover out of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

(2)\xc2\xa0This Court holds today that a plan is not seeking equitable relief under those circumstances. In premerger equity courts, a plaintiff could ordinarily enforce an equitable lien, including, as here, an equitable lien by agreement, only against specifically identified funds that remained in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s possession or against traceable items that the defendant purchased with the funds. See 4 S. Symons, Pomeroy\xe2\x80\x99s Equity Jurisprudence \xc2\xa71234, pp. 692\xe2\x80\x93695. If a defendant dissipated the entire fund on nontraceable items, the lien was eliminated and the plaintiff could not attach the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets instead. See Restatement of Restitution, \xc2\xa7215(1), p. 866. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The Board\xe2\x80\x99s arguments in favor of the enforcement of an equitable lien against Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s general assets are unsuccessful. Sereboff does not contain an exception to the general asset-tracing requirement for equitable liens by agreement. See 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 365. Nor does historical equity practice support the enforcement of an equitable lien against general assets. And the Board\xe2\x80\x99s claim that ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s objectives are best served by allowing plans to enforce such liens is a \xe2\x80\x9cvague notio[n] of [the] statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98basic purpose\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. inadequate to overcome the words of its text regarding the specific issue under consideration.\xe2\x80\x9d Mertens v. Hewitt Associates, \n\n508 U. S. 248\n. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The case is remanded for the District Court to determine, in the first instance, whether Montanile kept his settlement fund separate from his general assets and whether he dissipated the entire fund on nontraceable assets. P.\xc2\xa014.

\n

593 Fed. Appx. 903, reversed and remanded.

ROBERT MONTANILE, PETITIONER v. BOARD OF TRUSTEES of the NATIONAL ELEVATOR INDUSTRY HEALTH BENEFIT PLAN

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[January 20, 2016]

\n

Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.[1]*

\n

When a third party injures a participant in an employee benefits plan under the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA),\n\n88Stat.\n829, as amended,\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71001 et seq., the plan frequently pays covered medical expenses. The terms of these plans often include a subrogation clause requiring a participant to reimburse the plan if the participant later recovers money from the third party for his injuries. And under ERISA \xc2\xa7502(a)(3),\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71132(a)(3), plan fiduciaries can file civil suits \xe2\x80\x9cto obtain .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. appropriate equitable relief .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to enforce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the terms of the plan.\xe2\x80\x9d[2]

\n

In this case, we consider what happens when a participant obtains a settlement fund from a third party, but spends the whole settlement on nontraceable items (for instance, on services or consumable items like food). We evaluate in particular whether a plan fiduciary can sue under \xc2\xa7502(a)(3) to recover from the participant\xe2\x80\x99s remaining assets the medical expenses it paid on the participant\xe2\x80\x99s behalf. We hold that, when a participant dissipates the whole settlement on nontraceable items, the fiduciary cannot bring a suit to attach the participant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets under \xc2\xa7502(a)(3) because the suit is not one for \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate equitable relief.\xe2\x80\x9d In this case, it is unclear whether the participant dissipated all of his settlement in this manner, so we remand for further proceedings.

\n

I

\n

Petitioner Robert Montanile was a participant in a health benefits plan governed by ERISA and administered by respondent, the Board of Trustees of the National Elevator Industry Health Benefit Plan (Board of Trustees or Board). The plan must pay for certain medical ex-penses that beneficiaries or participants incur. The plan may demand reimbursement, however, when a participant recovers money from a third party for medical expenses. The plan states: \xe2\x80\x9cAmounts that have been recovered by a [\xc2\xa0participant] from another party are assets of the Plan .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and are not distributable to any person or entity without the Plan\xe2\x80\x99s written release of its subrogation interest.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 45. The plan also provides that \xe2\x80\x9cany amounts\xe2\x80\x9d that a participant \xe2\x80\x9crecover[s] from another party by award, judgment, settlement or otherwise .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. will promptly be applied first to reimburse the Plan in full for benefits advanced by the Plan .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and without reduction for attorneys\xe2\x80\x99 fees, costs, expenses or damages claimed by the covered person.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 46. Participants must notify the plan and obtain its consent before settling claims.

\n

In December 2008, a drunk driver ran through a stop sign and crashed into Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle. The accident severely injured Montanile, and the plan paid at least $121,044.02 for his initial medical care. Montanile signed a reimbursement agreement reaffirming his obligation to reimburse the plan from any recovery he obtained \xe2\x80\x9cas a result of any legal action or settlement or otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 51 (emphasis deleted).

\n

Thereafter, Montanile filed a negligence claim against the drunk driver and made a claim for uninsured motorist benefits under Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s car insurance. He obtained a $500,000 settlement. Montanile then paid his attorneys $200,000 and repaid about $60,000 that they had advanced him. Thus, about $240,000 remained of the settlement. Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s attorneys held most of that sum in a client trust account. This included enough money to satisfy Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s obligations to the plan.

\n

The Board of Trustees sought reimbursement from Montanile on behalf of the plan, and Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s attorney argued that the plan was not entitled to any recovery. The parties attempted but failed to reach an agreement about reimbursement. After discussions broke down, Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s attorney informed the Board that he would distribute the remaining settlement funds to Montanile unless the Board objected within 14 days. The Board did not respond within that time, so Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s attorney gave Montanile the remainder of the funds.

\n

Six months after negotiations ended, the Board sued Montanile in District Court under ERISA \xc2\xa7502(a)(3),\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71132(a)(3), seeking repayment of the $121,044.02 the plan had expended on his medical care. The Board asked the court to enforce an equitable lien upon any settlement funds or any property which are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in [\xc2\xa0Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s] actual or constructive possession.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 593 Fed. Appx. 903, 906 (CA11 2014) (quoting complaint). Because Montanile had already taken possession of the settlement funds, the Board also sought an order enjoining Montanile from dissipating any such funds. Montanile then stipulated that he still possessed some of the settlement proceeds.

\n

The District Court granted summary judgment to the Board. No. 12\xe2\x80\x9380746\xe2\x80\x93Civ. (SD Fla., Apr. 18, 2014), 2014 WL 8514011, *1. The court rejected Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s argument that, because he had by that time spent almost all of the settlement funds, there was no specific, identifiable fund separate from his general assets against which the Board\xe2\x80\x99s equitable lien could be enforced. Id., at *8\xe2\x80\x93*11. The court held that, even if Montanile had dissipated some or all of the settlement funds, the Board was entitled to reimbursement from Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s general assets. Id., at *10\xe2\x80\x93*11. The court entered judgment for the Board in the amount of $121,044.02.

\n

The Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed. It reasoned that a plan can always enforce an equitable lien once the lien attaches, and that dissipation of the specific fund to which the lien attached cannot destroy the underlying reimbursement obligation. The court therefore held that the plan can recover out of a participant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets when the participant dissipates the specifically identified fund. 593 Fed. Appx., at 908.

\n

We granted certiorari to resolve a conflict among the Courts of Appeals over whether an ERISA fiduciary can enforce an equitable lien against a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets under these circumstances.[3] 575 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2015). We hold that it cannot, and accordingly reverse the judgment of the Eleventh Circuit and remand for further proceedings.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

As previously stated, \xc2\xa7502(a)(3) of ERISA authorizes plan fiduciaries like the Board of Trustees to bring civil suits \xe2\x80\x9cto obtain other appropriate equitable relief .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to enforce .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the terms of the plan.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71132(a)(3). Our cases explain that the term \xe2\x80\x9cequitable relief\xe2\x80\x9d in \xc2\xa7502(a)(3) is limited to \xe2\x80\x9cthose categories of relief that were typically available in equity\xe2\x80\x9d during the days of the divided bench (meaning, the period before 1938 when courts oflaw and equity were separate). Mertens v. Hewitt Associates,\n\n508 U.\xc2\xa0S. 248,\n256 (1993)\n. Under this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents, whether the remedy a plaintiff seeks \xe2\x80\x9cis legal or equitable depends on [(1)] the basis for [the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s] claim and [(2)] the nature of the underlying remedies sought.\xe2\x80\x9d Sereboff v. Mid Atlantic Medical Services, Inc.,\n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 356,\n363 (2006)\n (internal quotation marks omitted). Our precedents also prescribe a framework for resolving this inquiry. To determine how to characterize the basis of a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim and the nature of the remedies sought, we turn to standard treatises on equity, which establish the \xe2\x80\x9cbasic contours\xe2\x80\x9d of what equitable relief was typically available in premerger equity courts. Great-West Life & Annuity Ins. Co. v. Knudson,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 204,\n217 (2002)\n.

\n

We have employed this approach in three earlier cases where, as here, the plan fiduciary sought reimbursement for medical expenses after the plan beneficiary or participant recovered money from a third party. Under these precedents, the basis for the Board\xe2\x80\x99s claim is equitable. But our cases do not resolve whether the remedy the Board now seeks\xe2\x80\x94enforcement of an equitable lien by agreement against the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets\xe2\x80\x94is equitable in nature.

\n

First, in Great-West, we held that a plan with a claim for an equitable lien was\xe2\x80\x94in the circumstances presented- seeking a legal rather than an equitable remedy. In that case, a plan sought to enforce an equitable lien by obtaining a money judgment from the defendants. The plan could not enforce the lien against the third-party settlement that the defendants had obtained because the defendants never actually possessed that fund; the fund went directly to the defendants\xe2\x80\x99 attorneys and to a restricted trust. We held that the plan sought a legal rem-edy, not an equitable one, even though the plan claimed that the money judgment was a form of restitution. Id., at 208\xe2\x80\x93209, 213\xe2\x80\x93214. We explained that restitution in equity typically involved enforcement of \xe2\x80\x9ca constructive trust or an equitable lien, where money or property identified as belonging in good conscience to the plaintiff could clearly be traced to particular funds or property in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s possession.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 213. But the restitution sought in Great-West was legal\xe2\x80\x94not equitable\xe2\x80\x94because the specific funds to which the fiduciaries \xe2\x80\x9cclaim[ed] an entitlement .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [we]re not in [the defendants\xe2\x80\x99] possession.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 214. Since both the basis for the claim and the particular remedy sought were not equitable, the plan could not sue under \xc2\xa7502(a)(3).

\n

Next, in Sereboff, we held that both the basis for the claim and the remedy sought were equitable. The plan there sought reimbursement from beneficiaries who had retained their settlement fund in a separate account. 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 359\xe2\x80\x93360. We held that the basis for the plan\xe2\x80\x99s claim was equitable because the plan sought to enforce an equitable lien by agreement, a type of equitable lien created by an agreement to convey a particular fund to another party. See id., at 363\xe2\x80\x93364. The lien existed in Sereboff because of the beneficiaries\xe2\x80\x99 agreement with the plan to convey the proceeds of any third-party settlement. We explained that a claim to enforce such a lien is equitable because the plan \xe2\x80\x9ccould rely on a familiar rul[e] of equity\xe2\x80\x9d to collect\xe2\x80\x94specifically, the rule \xe2\x80\x9cthat a contract to convey a specific object even before it is acquired will make the contractor a trustee as soon as he gets a title to the thing.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted; alteration in original). The underlying remedies that the plan sought also were equitable, because the plan \xe2\x80\x9csought specifically identifiable funds that were within the possession and control\xe2\x80\x9d of the beneficiaries\xe2\x80\x94not recovery from the beneficiaries\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cassets generally.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 362\xe2\x80\x93363 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Finally, in US Airways, Inc. v. McCutchen, 569 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013), we reaffirmed our analysis in Sereboff and again concluded that a plan sought to enforce an equitable claim by seeking equitable remedies. As in Sereboff, \xe2\x80\x9cthe basis for [the plan\xe2\x80\x99s] claim was equitable\xe2\x80\x9d because the plan\xe2\x80\x99s terms created an equitable lien by agreement on a third-party settlement. See 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 5) (internal quotation marks omitted). And, as in Sereboff, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he nature of the recovery requested\xe2\x80\x9d by the plan \xe2\x80\x9cwas equitable because [it] claimed specifically identifiable funds within the [beneficiaries\xe2\x80\x99] control\xe2\x80\x94that is, a portion of the settlement they had gotten.\xe2\x80\x9d 569 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ (slip op., at 5) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Under these principles, the basis for the Board\xe2\x80\x99s claim here is equitable: The Board had an equitable lien by agreement that attached to Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s settlement fund when he obtained title to that fund. And the nature of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s underlying remedy would have been equitable had it immediately sued to enforce the lien against the settlement fund then in Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s possession. That does not resolve this case, however. Our prior cases do not address whether a plan is still seeking an equitable remedy when the defendant, who once possessed the settlement fund, has dissipated it all, and the plan then seeks to recover out of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets.

\n

B

\n

To resolve this issue, we turn to standard equity trea-tises. As we explain below, those treatises make clear that a plaintiff could ordinarily enforce an equitable lien only against specifically identified funds that remain in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s possession or against traceable items that the defendant purchased with the funds (e.g., identifiable property like a car). A defendant\xe2\x80\x99s expenditure of the entire identifiable fund on nontraceable items (like food or travel) destroys an equitable lien. The plaintiff then may have a personal claim against the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets\xe2\x80\x94but recovering out of those assets is a legal remedy, not an equitable one.

\n

Equitable remedies \xe2\x80\x9care, as a general rule, directed against some specific thing; they give or enforce a right to or over some particular thing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. rather than a right to recover a sum of money generally out of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s assets.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 S. Symons, Pomeroy\xe2\x80\x99s Equity Jurisprudence \xc2\xa71234, p. 694 (5th ed. 1941) (Pomeroy). Equitable liens thus are ordinarily enforceable only against a specifically identified fund because an equitable lien \xe2\x80\x9cis simply a right of a special nature over the thing .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. so that the very thing itself may be proceeded against in an equitable action.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., \xc2\xa71233, at 692; see also Restatement of Restitution \xc2\xa7215, Comment a, p. 866 (1936) (Restatement) (enforcement of equitable lien requires showing that the defendant \xe2\x80\x9cstill holds the property or property which is in whole or in part its product\xe2\x80\x9d); 1 D. Dobbs, Law of Remedies \xc2\xa71.4, p. 19 (2d ed. 1993) (Dobbs) (similar). This general rule\xe2\x80\x99s application to equitable liens includes equitable liens by agreement, which depend on \xe2\x80\x9cthe notion .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the contract creates some right or interest in or over specific property,\xe2\x80\x9d and are enforceable only if \xe2\x80\x9cthe decree of the court can lay hold of\xe2\x80\x9d that specific property. 4 Pomeroy \xc2\xa71234, at 694\xe2\x80\x93695.

\n

If, instead of preserving the specific fund subject to the lien, the defendant dissipated the entire fund on nontraceable items, that complete dissipation eliminated the lien. Even though the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s conduct was wrongful, the plaintiff could not attach the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets instead. Absent specific exceptions not relevant here, \xe2\x80\x9cwhere a person wrongfully dispose[d] of the property of another but the property cannot be traced into any product, the other .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. cannot enforce a constructive trust or lien upon any part of the wrongdoer\xe2\x80\x99s property.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement \xc2\xa7215(1), at 866 (emphasis added); see also Great-West, 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 213\xe2\x80\x93214 (citing Restatement \xc2\xa7160). The plaintiff had \xe2\x80\x9cmerely a personal claim against the wrongdoer\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94a quintessential action at law. Id., \xc2\xa7215(1), at 866.

\n

In sum, at equity, a plaintiff ordinarily could not enforce any type of equitable lien if the defendant once possessed a separate, identifiable fund to which the lien attached, but then dissipated it all. The plaintiff could not attach the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets instead because those assets were not part of the specific thing to which the lien attached. This rule applied to equitable liens by agreement as well as other types of equitable liens.

\n

III

\n

The Board of Trustees nonetheless maintains that it can enforce its equitable lien against Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s general assets. We consider the Board\xe2\x80\x99s arguments in turn.

\n

A

\n

First, the Board argues that, while equity courts ordinarily required plaintiffs to trace a specific, identifiable fund in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s possession to which the lien attached, there is an exception for equitable liens by agreement. The Board asserts that equitable liens by agreement require no such tracing, and can be enforced against a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets. According to the Board, we recognized this exception in Sereboff by distinguishing between equitable restitution (where a lien attaches because the defendant misappropriated property from the plaintiff\xc2\xa0) and equitable liens by agreement.

\n

The Board misreads Sereboff, which left untouched the rule that all types of equitable liens must be enforced against a specifically identified fund in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s possession. See 1 Dobbs \xc2\xa74.3(3), at 601, 603. The question we faced in Sereboff was whether plaintiffs seeking an equitable lien by agreement must \xe2\x80\x9cidentify an asset they originally possessed, which was improperly acquired and converted into property the defendant held.\xe2\x80\x9d 547 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 365. We observed that such a requirement, although characteristic of restitutionary relief, does not \xe2\x80\x9cappl[y] to equitable liens by agreement or assignment.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (discussing Barnes v. Alexander,\n\n232 U.\xc2\xa0S. 117 (1914)\n). That is because the basic premise of an equitable lien by agreement is that, rather than physically taking the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s property, the defendant constructively possesses a fund to which the plaintiff is entitled. But the plaintiff must still identify a specific fund in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s possession to enforce the lien. See id., at 123 (\xe2\x80\x9cHaving a lien upon the fund, as soon as it was identified they could follow it into the hands of the appellant\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

Second, the Board contends that historical equity practice supports enforcement of its equitable lien against Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s general assets. The Board identifies three methods that equity courts purportedly employed to effectuate this principle: substitute money decrees, deficiency judgments, and the swollen assets doctrine. This argument also fails.

\n

We have long rejected the argument that \xe2\x80\x9cequitable relief\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7502(a)(3) means \xe2\x80\x9cwhatever relief a court of equity is empowered to provide in the particular case atissue,\xe2\x80\x9d including ancillary legal remedies. Mertens, 508 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 256. In \xe2\x80\x9cmany situations .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. an equity court could establish purely legal rights and grant legal remedies which would otherwise be beyond the scope of its authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). But these legal remedies were not relief \xe2\x80\x9ctypically available in equity,\xe2\x80\x9d and interpreting them as such would eliminate any limit on the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9cequitable relief\xe2\x80\x9d and would \xe2\x80\x9crender the modifier superfluous.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 256, 258 (emphasis deleted); see also Great-West, supra, at 210. As we have explained\xe2\x80\x94and as the Board conceded at oral argument\xe2\x80\x94as a general rule, plaintiffs cannot enforce an equitable lien against a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, supra. The Board contends that there is an exception if the defendant wrongfully dissipates the equitable lien to thwart its enforcement. But none of the Board\xe2\x80\x99s examples show that such relief was \xe2\x80\x9ctypically available\xe2\x80\x9d in equity.[4]

\n

The specific methods by which equity courts might have awarded relief from a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets only confirm that the Board seeks legal, not equitable, remedies. While equity courts sometimes awarded money decrees as a substitute for the value of the equitable lien, they were still legal remedies, because they were \xe2\x80\x9cwholly pecuniary and personal.\xe2\x80\x9d 4 Pomeroy \xc2\xa71234, at 694. The same is true with respect to deficiency judgments. Equity courts could award both of these remedies as part of their ancillary jurisdiction to award complete relief. But the treatises make clear that when equity courts did so, \xe2\x80\x9cthe rights of the parties are strictly legal, and the final remedy granted is of the kind which might be conferred by a court of law.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 id., \xc2\xa7231, at 410; see also 1 Dobbs \xc2\xa72.7, at 180\xe2\x80\x93181, and \xc2\xa74.3(3), at 602 (similar); New Federal Equity Rules 10 (rev. 5th ed. 1925) (authorizing equity courts to award such relief). But legal remedies\xe2\x80\x94even legal remedies that a court of equity could sometimes award\xe2\x80\x94are not \xe2\x80\x9cequitable relief\xe2\x80\x9d under \xc2\xa7502(a)(3). See Mertens, supra, at 256\xe2\x80\x93258.

\n

The swollen assets doctrine also does not establish that the relief the Board seeks is equitable. Under the Board\xe2\x80\x99s view of this doctrine, even if a defendant spends all of a specifically identified fund, the mere fact that the defendant wrongfully had assets that belonged to another increased the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s available assets, and justifies recovery from his general assets. But most equity courts and treatises rejected that theory. See Taft, Note, A Defense of a Limited Use of the Swollen Assets Theory Where Money Has Wrongfully Been Mingled With Other Money, 39 Colum. L. Rev. 172, 175 (1939) (describing the swollen assets doctrine as \xe2\x80\x9coften .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. rejected by the courts\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Oesterle, Deficiencies of the Restitutionary Right to Trace Misappropriated Property in Equity and in UCC \xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x93306, 68 Cornell L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 172, 189, and n.\xc2\xa033 (1983) (similar). To the extent that courts endorsed any version of the swollen assets theory, they adopted a more limited rule: that commingling a specifically identified fund\xe2\x80\x94to which a lien attached\xe2\x80\x94with a different fund of the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s did not destroy the lien. Instead, that commingling allowed the plaintiff to recover the amount of the lien from the entire pot of money. See Restatement \xc2\xa7209, at 844; Scott, The Right To Follow Money Wrong-fully Mingled With Other Money, 27 Harv. L. Rev. 125, 125\xe2\x80\x93126 (1913). Thus, even under the version of the swollen assets doctrine adopted by some courts, recovery out of Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s general assets\xe2\x80\x94in the absence of commingling\xe2\x80\x94would not have been \xe2\x80\x9ctypically available\xe2\x80\x9d relief.

\n

C

\n

Finally, the Board argues that ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s objectives\xe2\x80\x94of enforcing plan documents according to their terms and of protecting plan assets\xe2\x80\x94would be best served by allowing plans to enforce equitable liens against a participant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets. The Board also contends that, unless plans can enforce reimbursement provisions against a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s general assets, plans will lack effective or cost-efficient remedies, and participants will dissipate any settlement as quickly as possible, before fiduciaries can sue.

\n

We have rejected these arguments before, and do so again. \xe2\x80\x9c[\xc2\xa0V\xc2\xa0]ague notions of a statute\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98basic purpose\xe2\x80\x99 are .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. inadequate to overcome the words of its text regarding the specific issue under consideration.\xe2\x80\x9d Mertens, supra, at 261. Had Congress sought to prioritize the Board\xe2\x80\x99s policy arguments, it could have drafted \xc2\xa7502(a)(3) to mirror ERISA provisions governing civil actions. One of those provisions, for instance, allows participants and beneficiaries to bring civil actions \xe2\x80\x9cto enforce [their] rights under the terms of the plan\xe2\x80\x9d and does not limit them to equitable relief. Great-West, 534 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 221 (quoting\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71132(a)(1)(B) (1994 ed.)).

\n

In any event, our interpretation of \xc2\xa7502(a)(3) promotes ERISA\xe2\x80\x99s purposes by \xe2\x80\x9callocat[ing] liability for plan-related misdeeds in reasonable proportion to respective actors\xe2\x80\x99 power to control and prevent the misdeeds.\xe2\x80\x9d Mertens, supra, at 262. More than a decade has passed since we decided Great-West, and plans have developed safeguards against participants\xe2\x80\x99 and beneficiaries\xe2\x80\x99 efforts to evade reimbursement obligations. Plans that cover medical expenses know how much medical care that participants and beneficiaries require, and have the incentive to investigate and track expensive claims. Plan provisions\xe2\x80\x94like the ones here\xe2\x80\x94obligate participants and beneficiaries to notify the plan of legal process against third parties and to give the plan a right of subrogation.

\n

The Board protests that tracking and participating in legal proceedings is hard and costly, and that settlements are often shrouded in secrecy. The facts of this case undercut that argument. The Board had sufficient notice of Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s settlement to have taken various steps to preserve those funds. Most notably, when negotiations broke down and Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s lawyer expressed his intent to disburse the remaining settlement funds to Montanile unless the plan objected within 14 days, the Board could have\xe2\x80\x94but did not\xe2\x80\x94object. Moreover, the Board could have filed suit immediately, rather than waiting half a year.

\n

IV

\n

Because the lower courts erroneously held that the plan could recover out of Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s general assets, they did not determine whether Montanile kept his settlement fund separate from his general assets or dissipated the entire fund on nontraceable assets. At oral argument, Montanile\xe2\x80\x99s counsel acknowledged \xe2\x80\x9ca genuine issue of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. material fact on how much dissipation there was\xe2\x80\x9d anda lack of record evidence as to whether Montanile mixed the settlement fund with his general assets. A remandis necessary so that the District Court can make that determination.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

We reverse the judgment of the Eleventh Circuit and remand the case for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.
