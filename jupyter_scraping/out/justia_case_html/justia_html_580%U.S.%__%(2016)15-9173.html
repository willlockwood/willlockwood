SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

\n

SHAUN MICHAEL BOSSE v. OKLAHOMA

\n

on petition for writ of certiorari to the court of criminal appeals of oklahoma

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x939173.\xe2\x80\x83Decided October 11, 2016

\n

\n

Per Curiam.

\n

In Booth v. Maryland,\n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 496 (1987)\n, this Court held that \xe2\x80\x9cthe\n \nEighth Amendment prohibits a capital sentencing jury from considering victim impact evidence\xe2\x80\x9d that does not \xe2\x80\x9crelate directly to the circumstances of the crime.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 501\xe2\x80\x93502, 507, n. 10. Four years later, in Payne v. Tennessee,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 808 (1991)\n, the Courtgranted certiorari to reconsider that ban on \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98victim impact\xe2\x80\x99evidence relating to the personal characteristics of the victim and the emotional impact of the crimes on the victim\xe2\x80\x99s family.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 817. The Court held that Booth was wrong to conclude that the\n \nEighth Amendment required such a ban. Payne, 501 U.\xc2\xa0S. at 827. That holding was expressly \xe2\x80\x9climited to\xe2\x80\x9d this particular type of victim impact testimony. Id., at 830, n. 2. \xe2\x80\x9cBooth also held that the admission of a victim\xe2\x80\x99s family members\xe2\x80\x99 characterizations and opinions about the crime, the defendant, and the appropriate sentence violates the\n \nEighth Amendment,\xe2\x80\x9d but no such evidence was presented in Payne, so the Court had no occasion to reconsider that aspect of the decision. Ibid.

\n

The Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals has held that Payne \xe2\x80\x9cimplicitly overruled that portion of Booth regarding characterizations of the defendant and opinions of the sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Conover v. State, 933 P. 2d 904, 920 (1997) (emphasis added); see also Ledbetter v. State, 933 P. 2d 880, 890\xe2\x80\x93891 (Okla. Crim. App. 1997). The decision below presents a straightforward application of that interpretation of Payne. A jury convicted petitioner Shaun Michael Bosse of three counts of first-degree murder for the 2010 killing of Katrina Griffin and her two children. The State of Oklahoma sought the death penalty. Over Bosse\xe2\x80\x99s objection, the State asked three of the victims\xe2\x80\x99 relatives to recommend a sentence to the jury. All three recommended death, and the jury agreed. Bosse appealed, arguing that this testimony about the appropriate sentence violated the\n \nEighth Amendment under Booth. The Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals affirmed his sentence, concluding that there was \xe2\x80\x9cno error.\xe2\x80\x9d 2015 OK CR 14, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb6 57\xe2\x80\x9358, 360 P. 3d 1203, 1226\xe2\x80\x931227. We grant certiorari and the motion for leave to proceed in forma pauperis, and now vacate the judgment of the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals.

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is this Court\xe2\x80\x99s prerogative alone to overrule one of its precedents.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Hatter,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 557,\n567 (2001)\n (quoting State Oil Co. v. Khan,\n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 3,\n20 (1997)\n; internal quotation marks omitted); see Rodriguez de Quijas v. Shearson/American Express, Inc.,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 477,\n484 (1989)\n. The Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals has recognized that Payne \xe2\x80\x9cspecifically acknowledged its holding did not affect\xe2\x80\x9d Booth\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on opinions about the crime, the defendant, and the appropriate punishment. Ledbetter, 933 P. 2d at 890\xe2\x80\x93891. That should have ended its inquiry into whether the\n \nEighth Amendment bars such testimony; the court was wrong to go further and conclude that Payne implicitly overruled Booth in its entirety. \xe2\x80\x9cOur decisions remain binding precedent until we see fit to reconsider them, regardless of whether subsequent cases have raised doubts about their continuing vitality.\xe2\x80\x9d Hohn v. United States,\n\n524 U.\xc2\xa0S. 236\n\xe2\x80\x93253 (1998).

\n

The Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals remains bound by Booth\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition on characterizations and opinions from a victim\xe2\x80\x99s family members about the crime, the defendant, and the appropriate sentence unless this Court reconsiders that ban. The state court erred in concluding otherwise.

\n

The State argued in opposing certiorari that, even if the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals was wrong in its victim impact ruling, that error did not affect the jury\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing determination, and the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s rights were in any event protected by the mandatory sentencing review in capital cases required under Oklahoma law. See Brief in Opposition 14\xe2\x80\x9315. Those contentions may be addressed on remand to the extent the court below deems appropriate.

\n

The judgment of the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals is vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings not inconsistent with this opinion.

\n

\n

It is so ordered.

Justice Thomas, with whom Justice Alito joins, concurring.

\n

We held in Booth v. Maryland,\n\n482 U.\xc2\xa0S. 496 (1987)\n, that the\n \nEighth Amendment prohibits a court from admitting the opinions of the victim\xe2\x80\x99s family members about the appropriate sentence in a capital case. The Court today correctly observes that our decision in Payne v. Tennessee,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 808 (1991)\n, did not expressly overrule this aspect of Booth. Because \xe2\x80\x9cit is this Court\xe2\x80\x99s prerogative alone to overrule one of its precedents,\xe2\x80\x9d State Oil Co. v. Khan,\n\n522 U.\xc2\xa0S. 3,\n20 (1997)\n, the Oklahoma Court of Criminal Appeals erred in holding that Payne invalidated Booth in its entirety. In vacating the decision below, this Court says nothing about whether Booth was correctly decided or whether Payne swept away its analytical foundations. I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion with this understanding.
