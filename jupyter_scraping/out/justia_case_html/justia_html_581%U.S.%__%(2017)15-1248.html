Syllabus

\n

McLANE CO., INC. v. EQUAL EMPLOYMENT OPPORTUNITY COMMISSION

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x931248.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 21, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 3, 2017

\n

Damiana Ochoa worked for eight years in a physically demanding job for petitioner McLane Co., a supply-chain services company. McLane requires employees in those positions\xe2\x80\x94both new employees and those returning from medical leave\xe2\x80\x94to take a physical evaluation. When Ochoa returned from three months of maternity leave, she failed the evaluation three times and was fired. She then filed a sex discrimination charge under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. The Equal Employment Opportunity (EEOC) began an investigation, but McLane declined its request for so-called \xe2\x80\x9cpedigree information\xe2\x80\x9d: names, Social Security numbers, addresses, and telephone numbers of employees asked to take the evaluation. After the EEOC expanded the investigation\xe2\x80\x99s scope both geographically (to cover McLane\xe2\x80\x99s national operations) and substantively (to investigate possible age discrimination), it issued subpoenas, as authorized by\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x939, requesting pedigree information relating to its new investigation. When McLane refused to provide the information, the EEOC filed two actions in Federal District Court\xe2\x80\x94one arising out of Ochoa\xe2\x80\x99s charge and one arising out of the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s own age-discrimination charge\xe2\x80\x94seeking enforcement of its subpoenas. The District Judge declined to enforce the subpoenas, finding that the pedigree information was not relevant to the charges, but the Ninth Circuit reversed. Reviewing the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to quash the subpoena de\xc2\xa0novo, the court concluded that the lower court erred in finding the pedigree information irrelevant.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision whether to enforce or quash an EEOC subpoena should be reviewed for abuse of discretion, not de novo. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0Both factors that this Court examines when considering whether such decision should be subject to searching or deferential appellate review point toward abuse-of-discretion review. First, the longstanding practice of the courts of appeals is to review a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to enforce or quash an administrative subpoena for abuse of discretion. Title VII confers on the EEOC the same authority to issue subpoenas that the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) confers on the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB). During the three decades between the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment and the incorporation of its subpoena-enforcement provisions into Title VII, every Circuit to consider the question had held that a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision on enforcement of an NLRB subpoena is subject to abuse-of-discretion review. Congress amended Title VII to authorize EEOC subpoenas against this uniform backdrop of deferential appellate review, and today, nearly every Court of Appeals reviews a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision whether to enforce an EEOC subpoena for abuse of discretion. This \xe2\x80\x9clong history of appellate practice,\xe2\x80\x9d Pierce v. Underwood, \n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 552\n, carries significant persuasive weight.

\n

Second, basic principles of institutional capacity counsel in favor of deferential review. In most cases, the district court\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement decision will turn either on whether the evidence sought is relevant to the specific charge or whether the subpoena is unduly burdensome in light of the circumstances. Both of these tasks are well suited to a district judge\xe2\x80\x99s expertise. The first requires the district court to evaluate the relationship between the particular materials sought and the particular matter under investigation\xe2\x80\x94an analysis \xe2\x80\x9cvariable in relation to the nature, purposes and scope of the inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Press Publishing Co. v. Walling,\n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 186\n. And whether a subpoena is overly burdensome turns on the nature of the materials sought and the difficulty the employer will face in producing them\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fact-intensive, close calls\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d better suited to resolution by the district court than the court of appeals. Cooter & Gell v. Hartmarx Corp.,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 384\n.

\n

Other functional considerations also show the appropriateness of abuse-of-discretion review. For one, the district courts\xe2\x80\x99 considerable experience in making similar decisions in other contexts, see Buford v. United States,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 59\n, gives them the \xe2\x80\x9cinstitutional advantag[e],\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 64, that comes with greater experience. Deferential review also \xe2\x80\x9cstreamline[s] the litigation process by freeing appellate courts from the duty of reweighing evidence and reconsidering facts already weighed and considered by the district court,\xe2\x80\x9d Cooter & Gell, 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 404, something particularly important in a proceeding designed only to facilitate the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s investigation. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Court-appointed amicus\xe2\x80\x99 arguments in support of de\xc2\xa0novo review are not persuasive. Amicus claims that the district court\xe2\x80\x99s primary task is to test a subpoena\xe2\x80\x99s legal sufficiency and thus requires no exercise of discretion. But that characterization is not inconsistent with abuse-of-discretion review, which may be employed to insulate the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision from appellate review for the same kind of functional concerns that underpin the Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that abuse of discretion is the appropriate standard.

\n

It is also unlikely that affording deferential review to a district court\xe2\x80\x99s subpoena decision would clash with Court of Appeals decisions that instructed district courts to defer to the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s determination about the relevance of evidence to the charge at issue. Such decisions are better read as resting on the established rule that the term \xe2\x80\x9crelevant\xe2\x80\x9d be understood \xe2\x80\x9cgenerously\xe2\x80\x9d to permit the EEOC \xe2\x80\x9caccess to virtually any material that might cast light on the allegations against the employer.\xe2\x80\x9d EEOC v. Shell Oil Co.,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 54\n\xe2\x80\x9369. Nor do the constitutional underpinnings of the Shell Oil standard require a different result. While this Court has described a subpoena as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98constructive\xe2\x80\x99 search,\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Press, 327 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 202, and implied that the\n \nFourth Amendment is the source of the requirement that a subpoena not be \xe2\x80\x9ctoo indefinite,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Morton Salt Co.,\n\n338 U.\xc2\xa0S. 632\n, not every decision touching on the\n \nFourth Amendment is subject to searching review. See, e.g., United States v. Nixon,\n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 683\n. Cf. Illinois v. Gates,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213\n; Ornelas v. United States,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 690\n, distinguished. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

(c)\xc2\xa0The case is remanded so that the Court of Appeals can review the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision under the appropriate standard in the first instance. In doing so, the Court of Appeals may consider, as and to the extent it deems appropriate, any of McLane\xe2\x80\x99s arguments regarding the burdens imposed by the subpoena. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

804 F.\xc2\xa03d 1051, vacated and remanded.

\n

McLANE COMPANY, INC., PETITIONER v. EQUAL EMPLOYMENT OPPORTUNITY COMMISSION

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[April 3, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 permits the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to issue a subpoena to obtain evidence from an employer that is relevant to a pending investigation. The statute autho-rizes a district court to issue an order enforcing such a subpoena. The question presented here is whether a court of appeals should review a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to enforce or quash an EEOC subpoena de\xc2\xa0novo or for abuse of discretion. This decision should be reviewed for abuse of discretion.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibits employment discrimination on the basis of \xe2\x80\x9crace, color, religion, sex, or national origin.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7703(a),\n\n78Stat.\n255,\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(a). The statute entrusts the enforcement of that prohibition to the EEOC. See \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(a); EEOC v. Shell Oil Co.,\n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 54\n\xe2\x80\x9362 (1984). The EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s responsibilities \xe2\x80\x9care triggered by the filing of a specific sworn charge of discrimination,\xe2\x80\x9d University of Pa. v. EEOC,\n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 182,\n190 (1990)\n, which can be filed either by the person alleging discrimination or by the EEOC itself, see \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(b). When it receives a charge, the EEOC must first notify the employer, ibid., and must then investigate \xe2\x80\x9cto determine whether there is reasonable cause to believe that the charge is true,\xe2\x80\x9d University of Pa., 493 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 190 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

This case is about one of the tools the EEOC has at its disposal in conducting its investigation: a subpoena. In order \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o enable the [EEOC] to make informed decisions at each stage of the enforcement process,\xe2\x80\x9d Title VII \xe2\x80\x9cconfers a broad right of access to relevant evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 191. It provides that the EEOC \xe2\x80\x9cshall .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. have access to, for the purposes of examination, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. any evidence of any person being investigated or proceeded against that relates to unlawful employment practices covered by\xe2\x80\x9d Title VII and \xe2\x80\x9cis relevant to the charge under investigation.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x938(a). And the statute enables the EEOC to obtain that evidence by \xe2\x80\x9cauthoriz[ing] [it] to issue a subpoena and to seek an order enforcing [the subpoena].\xe2\x80\x9d University of Pa., 493 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 191; see \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x939.[1] Under that authority, the EEOC may issue \xe2\x80\x9csubp[o]enas requiring the attendance and testimony of witnesses or the production of any evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7161(1). An employer may petition the EEOC to revoke the subpoena, see ibid., but if the EEOC rejects the petition and the employer still \xe2\x80\x9crefuse[s] to obey [the] subp[o]ena,\xe2\x80\x9d the EEOC may ask a district court to issue an order enforcing it, see \xc2\xa7161(2).

\n

A district court\xe2\x80\x99s role in an EEOC subpoena enforcement proceeding, we have twice explained, is a straightforward one. See University of Pa., 493 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 191; Shell Oil, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 72, n. 26. A district court is not to use an enforcement proceeding as an opportunity to test the strength of the underlying complaint. Ibid. Rather, a district court should \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98satisfy itself that the charge is valid and that the material requested is \xe2\x80\x9crelevant\xe2\x80\x9d to the charge.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d University of Pa., 493 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 191. It should do so cognizant of the \xe2\x80\x9cgenerou[s]\xe2\x80\x9d construction that courts have given the term \xe2\x80\x9crelevant.\xe2\x80\x9d Shell Oil, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 68\xe2\x80\x9369 (\xe2\x80\x9cvirtually any material that might cast light on the allegations against the employer\xe2\x80\x9d). If the charge is proper and the material requested is relevant, the district court should enforce the subpoena unless the employer establishes that the subpoena is \xe2\x80\x9ctoo indefinite,\xe2\x80\x9d has been issued for an \xe2\x80\x9cillegitimate purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d or is unduly burdensome. Id., at 72, n. 26. See United States v. Morton Salt Co.,\n\n338 U.\xc2\xa0S. 632\n\xe2\x80\x93653 (1950) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe gist of the protection is in the requirement .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that the disclosure sought shall not be unreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

B

\n

This case arises out of a Title VII suit filed by a woman named Damiana Ochoa. Ochoa worked for eight years as a \xe2\x80\x9ccigarette selector\xe2\x80\x9d for petitioner McLane Co., a supply-chain services company. According to McLane, the job is a demanding one: Cigarette selectors work in distribution centers, where they are required to lift, pack, and move large bins containing products. McLane requires employees taking physically demanding jobs\xe2\x80\x94both new employees and employees returning from medical leave\xe2\x80\x94to take a physical evaluation. According to McLane, the evaluation \xe2\x80\x9ctests .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. range of motion, resistance, and speed\xe2\x80\x9dand \xe2\x80\x9cis designed, administered, and validated by a third party.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioner 6. In 2007, Ochoa took threemonths of maternity leave. When she attempted to return to work, McLane asked her to take the evaluation. Ochoa attempted to pass the evaluation three times, but failed. McLane fired her.

\n

Ochoa filed a charge of discrimination, alleging (among other things) that she had been fired on the basis of her gender. The EEOC began an investigation, and\xe2\x80\x94at its request\xe2\x80\x94McLane provided it with basic information about the evaluation, as well as a list of anonymous employees that McLane had asked to take the evaluation. McLane\xe2\x80\x99s list included each employee\xe2\x80\x99s gender, role at the company, and evaluation score, as well as the reason each employee had been asked to take the evaluation. But the company refused to provide what the parties call \xe2\x80\x9cpedigree information\xe2\x80\x9d: the names, Social Security numbers, last known addresses, and telephone numbers of the employees who had been asked to take the evaluation. Upon learning that McLane used the evaluation nationwide, the EEOC expanded the scope of its investigation, both geographi-cally (to focus on McLane\xe2\x80\x99s nationwide operations) and sub-stantively (to investigate whether McLane had discriminated against its employees on the basis of age). It issued subpoenas requesting pedigree information as it related to its new investigation. But McLane refused to providethe pedigree information, and so the EEOC filed two actions in Federal District Court\xe2\x80\x94one arising out of Ochoa\xe2\x80\x99s charge and one arising out of a separate age-discrimination charge the EEOC itself had filed\xe2\x80\x94seeking enforcement of its subpoenas.

\n

The enforcement actions were assigned to the same District Judge, who, after a hearing, declined to enforce the subpoenas to the extent that they sought the pedigree information. See EEOC v. McLane Co., 2012 WL 1132758, *5 (D Ariz., Apr. 4, 2012) (age discrimination charge); Civ. No. 12\xe2\x80\x932469 (D Ariz., Nov. 19, 2012), App. to Pet. for Cert. 28\xe2\x80\x9330 (Title VII charge).[2] In the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s view, the pedigree information was not \xe2\x80\x9crelevant\xe2\x80\x9d to the charges because \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98an individual\xe2\x80\x99s name, or even an interview he or she could provide if contacted, simply could not shed light on whether the [evaluation] represents a tool of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. discrimination.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 29 (quoting 2012 WL 1132758, at *5; some internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

The Ninth Circuit reversed. See 804 F.\xc2\xa03d 1051 (2015). Consistent with Circuit precedent, the panel reviewed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to quash the subpoena de\xc2\xa0novo, and concluded that the District Court had erred in finding the pedigree information irrelevant. Id., at 1057. But the panel questioned in a footnote why de\xc2\xa0novo review applied, observing that its sister Circuits \xe2\x80\x9cappear[ed] to review issues related to enforcement of administrative subpoenas for abuse of discretion.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1056, n.\xc2\xa03; see infra, at 7 (reviewing Court of Appeals authority).

\n

This Court granted certiorari to resolve the disagreement between the Courts of Appeals over the appropriate standard of review for the decision whether to enforce an EEOC subpoena. 579 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016). Because the United States agrees with McLane that such a decision shouldbe reviewed for abuse of discretion, Stephen B. Kinnard was appointed as amicus curiae to defend the judgment below. 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016). He has ably discharged his duties.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

When considering whether a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision should be subject to searching or deferential appellate review\xe2\x80\x94at least absent \xe2\x80\x9cexplicit statutory command\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94we traditionally look to two factors. Pierce v. Underwood,\n\n487 U.\xc2\xa0S. 552,\n558 (1988)\n. First, we ask whether the \xe2\x80\x9chistory of appellate practice\xe2\x80\x9d yields an answer. Ibid. Second, at least where \xe2\x80\x9cneither a clear statutory prescription nor a historical tradition exists,\xe2\x80\x9d we ask whether, \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98as a matter of the sound administration of justice, one judicial actor is better positioned than another to decide the issue in question.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 558, 559\xe2\x80\x93560 (quoting Miller v. Fenton,\n\n474 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104,\n114 (1985)\n). Both factors point toward abuse-of-discretion review here.

\n

First, the longstanding practice of the courts of appeals in reviewing a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to enforce or quash an administrative subpoena is to review that decision for abuse of discretion. That practice predates even Title VII itself. As noted, Title VII confers on the EEOC the same authority to issue subpoenas that the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) confers on the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB). See n.\xc2\xa01, supra. During the three decades between the enactment of the NLRA and the incorporation of the NLRA\xe2\x80\x99s subpoena-enforcement provisions into Title VII, every Circuit to consider the question had held that a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision whether to enforce an NLRB subpoena should be reviewed for abuse of discretion. See NLRB v. Consolidated Vacuum Corp., 395 F.\xc2\xa02d 416, 419\xe2\x80\x93420 (CA2 1968); NLRB v. Friedman, 352 F.\xc2\xa02d 545, 547 (CA3 1965); NLRB v. Northern Trust Co., 148 F.\xc2\xa02d 24, 29 (CA7 1945); Goodyear Tire & Rubber Co. v. NLRB, 122 F.\xc2\xa02d 450, 453\xe2\x80\x93454 (CA6 1941). By the time Congress amended Title VII to authorize EEOC subpoenas in 1972, it did so against this uniform backdrop of deferential appellate review.

\n

Today, nearly as uniformly, the Courts of Appeals apply the same deferential review to a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision as to whether to enforce an EEOC subpoena. Almost every Court of Appeals reviews such a decision for abuse of discretion. See, e.g., EEOC v. Kronos Inc., 620 F.\xc2\xa03d 287, 295\xe2\x80\x93296 (CA3 2010); EEOC v. Randstad, 685 F.\xc2\xa03d 433, 442 (CA4 2012); EEOC v. Roadway Express, Inc., 261 F.\xc2\xa03d 634, 638 (CA6 2001); EEOC v. United Air Lines, Inc., 287 F. 3d 643, 649 (CA7 2002); EEOC v. Technocrest Systems, Inc., 448 F.\xc2\xa03d 1035, 1038 (CA8 2006); EEOC v. Dillon Companies, Inc., 310 F.\xc2\xa03d 1271, 1274 (CA10 2002); EEOC v. Royal Caribbean Cruises, Ltd., 771 F.\xc2\xa03d 757, 760 (CA11 2014) (per curiam). As Judge Watford\xe2\x80\x94writing for the panel below\xe2\x80\x94recognized, the Ninth Circuit alone applies a more searching form of review. See 804 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1056, n.\xc2\xa03 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhy we review questions of relevance and undue burden de\xc2\xa0novo is unclear\xe2\x80\x9d); see also EPA v. Alyeska Pipeline Serv. Co., 836 F.\xc2\xa02d 443, 445\xe2\x80\x93446 (CA9 1988) (holding that de\xc2\xa0novo review applies). To be sure, the inquiry into the appropriate standard of review cannot be resolved by a head-counting exercise. But the \xe2\x80\x9clong his-tory of appellate practice\xe2\x80\x9d here, Pierce, 487 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 558, carries significant persuasive weight.

\n

Second, basic principles of institutional capacity counsel in favor of deferential review. The decision whether to enforce an EEOC subpoena is a case-specific one that turns not on \xe2\x80\x9ca neat set of legal rules,\xe2\x80\x9d Illinois v. Gates,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213,\n232 (1983)\n, but instead on the application of broad standards to \xe2\x80\x9cmultifarious, fleeting, special, narrow facts that utterly resist generalization,\xe2\x80\x9d Pierce, 487 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 561\xe2\x80\x93562 (internal quotation marks omitted). In the mine run of cases, the district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision whether to enforce a subpoena will turn either on whether the evidence sought is relevant to the specific charge before it or whether the subpoena is unduly burdensome in light of the circumstances. Both tasks are well suited to a district judge\xe2\x80\x99s expertise. The decision whether evidence sought is relevant requires the district court to evaluate the relationship between the particular materials sought and the particular matter under investigation\xe2\x80\x94an analysis \xe2\x80\x9cvariable in relation to the nature, purposes and scope of the inquiry.\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Press Publishing Co. v. Walling,\n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 186,\n209 (1946)\n. Similarly, the decision whether a subpoena is overly burdensome turns on the nature of the materials sought and the difficulty the employer will face in producing them. These inquiries are \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally not amenable to broad per\xc2\xa0se rules,\xe2\x80\x9d Sprint/United Management Co. v. Mendelsohn,\n\n552 U.\xc2\xa0S. 379,\n387 (2008)\n; rather, they are the kind of \xe2\x80\x9cfact-intensive, close calls\xe2\x80\x9d better suited to resolution by the district court than the court of appeals, Cooter & Gell v. Hartmarx Corp.,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 384,\n404 (1990)\n (internal quotation marks omitted).[3]

\n

Other functional considerations also show that abuse-of-discretion review is appropriate here. For one, district courts have considerable experience in other contexts making decisions similar\xe2\x80\x94though not identical\xe2\x80\x94to those they must make in this one. See Buford v. United States,\n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 59,\n66 (2001)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he comparatively greater expertise\xe2\x80\x9d of the district court may counsel in favor of deferential review). District courts decide, for instance, whether evidence is relevant at trial, Fed. Rule Evid. 401; whether pretrial criminal subpoenas are unreasonable in scope, Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 16(c)(2); and more. These decisions are not the same as the decisions a district court must make in enforcing an administrative subpoena. But they are similar enough to give the district court the \xe2\x80\x9cinstitutional advantag[e],\xe2\x80\x9d Buford, 532 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 64, that comes with greater experience. For another, as we noted in Cooter & Gell, deferential review \xe2\x80\x9cstreamline[s] the litigation process by freeing appellate courts from the duty of reweighing evidence and reconsidering facts already weighed and considered by the district court,\xe2\x80\x9d 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 404\xe2\x80\x94a particularly important consideration in a \xe2\x80\x9csatellite\xe2\x80\x9d proceeding like this one, ibid., designed only to facilitate the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s investigation.

\n

B

\n

Amicus\xe2\x80\x99 arguments to the contrary have aided our consideration of this case. But they do not persuade us that de\xc2\xa0novo review is appropriate.

\n

Amicus\xe2\x80\x99 central argument is that the decision whether a subpoena should be enforced does not require the exercise of discretion on the part of the district court, and so it should not be reviewed for abuse of discretion. On amicus\xe2\x80\x99 view, the district court\xe2\x80\x99s primary role is to test the legal sufficiency of the subpoena, not to weigh whether it should be enforced as a substantive matter. Cf. Shell Oil, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 72, n. 26 (rejecting the argument that the district court should assess the validity of the underlying claim in a proceeding to enforce a subpoena). Even accepting amicus\xe2\x80\x99 view of the district court\xe2\x80\x99s task, however, this understanding of abuse-of-discretion review is too narrow. As commentators have observed, abuse-of-discretion review is employed not only where a decisionmaker has \xe2\x80\x9ca wide range of choice as to what he decides, free from the constraints which characteristically attach whenever legal rules enter the decision[making] process\xe2\x80\x9d; it is also employed where the trial judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision is given \xe2\x80\x9can unu-sual amount of insulation from appellate revision\xe2\x80\x9d for func-tional reasons. Rosenberg, Judicial Discretion of the Trial Court, Viewed From Above, 22 Syracuse L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 635, 637 (1971); see also 22 C. Wright & K. Graham, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa75166.1 (2d ed. 2012). And as we have explained, it is in large part due to functional concerns that we conclude the district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision should be reviewed for abuse of discretion. Even if the district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision can be characterized in the way that amicus suggests, that characterization would not be inconsistent with abuse-of-discretion review.

\n

Nor are we persuaded by amicus\xe2\x80\x99 remaining arguments. Amicus argues that affording deferential review to a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision would clash with Court of Appeals decisions instructing district courts to defer themselves to the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s determination that evidence is relevant to the charge at issue. See Director, Office of Thrift Supervision, v. Vinson & Elkins, LLP, 124 F.\xc2\xa03d 1304, 1307 (CADC 1997) (district courts should defer to agency appraisals of relevance unless they are \xe2\x80\x9cobviously wrong\xe2\x80\x9d); EEOC v. Lockheed Martin Corp., Aero & Naval Systems, 116 F.\xc2\xa03d 110, 113 (CA4 1997) (same). In amicus\xe2\x80\x99 view, it is \xe2\x80\x9canalytically impossible\xe2\x80\x9d for the court of appeals to defer to the district court if the district court must itself defer to the agency. Tr. of Oral Arg. 29. We think the better reading of those cases is that they rest on the established rule that the term \xe2\x80\x9crelevant\xe2\x80\x9d be understood \xe2\x80\x9cgenerously\xe2\x80\x9d to permit the EEOC \xe2\x80\x9caccess to virtually any material that might cast light on the allegations against the employer.\xe2\x80\x9d Shell Oil, 466 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 68\xe2\x80\x9369. A district court deciding whether evidence is \xe2\x80\x9crelevant\xe2\x80\x9d under Title VII need not defer to the EEOC\xe2\x80\x99s decision on that score; it must simply answer the question cognizant of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s broad authority to seek and obtain evidence. Because the statute does not set up any scheme of double deference, amicus\xe2\x80\x99 arguments as to the infirmities of such a scheme are misplaced.

\n

Nor do we agree that, as amicus suggests, the constitutional underpinnings of the Shell Oil standard require a different result. To be sure, we have described a subpoena as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98constructive\xe2\x80\x99 search,\xe2\x80\x9d Oklahoma Press, 327 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 202, and implied that the\n \nFourth Amendment is the source of the requirement that a subpoena not be \xe2\x80\x9ctoo indefinite,\xe2\x80\x9d Morton Salt, 338 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 652. But not every decision that touches on the\n \nFourth Amendment is subject to searching review. Subpoenas in a wide variety of other contexts also implicate the privacy interests protected by the\n \nFourth Amendment, but courts routinely review the enforcement of such subpoenas for abuse of discretion. See, e.g., United States v. Nixon,\n\n418 U.\xc2\xa0S. 683,\n702 (1974)\n (pretrial subpoenas duces tecum); In\xc2\xa0re Grand Jury Subpoena, 696 F.\xc2\xa03d 428, 432 (CA5 2012) (grand jury subpoenas); In\xc2\xa0re Grand Jury Proceedings, 616 F.\xc2\xa03d 1186, 1201 (CA10 2010) (same). And this Court has emphasized that courts should pay \xe2\x80\x9cgreat deference\xe2\x80\x9d to a magistrate judge\xe2\x80\x99s determination of probable cause, Gates, 462 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 236 (internal quotation marks omitted)\xe2\x80\x94a decision more akin to a district court\xe2\x80\x99s preenforcement review of a subpoena than the warrantless searches and seizures we considered in Ornelas v. United States,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 690 (1996)\n, on which amicus places great weight. The constitutional pedigree of Shell Oil does not change our view of the correct standard of review.

\n

III

\n

For these reasons, a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to enforce an EEOC subpoena should be reviewed for abuse of discretion, not de\xc2\xa0novo.

\n

The United States also argues that the judgment below can be affirmed because it is clear that the District Court abused its discretion. But \xe2\x80\x9cwe are a court of review, not of first view,\xe2\x80\x9d Cutter v. Wilkinson,\n\n544 U.\xc2\xa0S. 709\n, n.\xc2\xa07 (2005), and the Court of Appeals has not had the chance to review the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision under the appropriate standard. That task is for the Court of Appeals in the first instance. As part of its analysis, the Court of Appeals may also consider, as and to the extent it deems appropriate, any arguments made by McLane regarding the burdens imposed by the subpoena.

\n

The judgment of the Court of Appeals is hereby vacated, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

While I agree with the Court that \xe2\x80\x9cabuse of discretion\xe2\x80\x9d is generally the proper review standard for district court decisions reviewing agency subpoenas, I would nevertheless affirm the Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment in this case. As the Court of Appeals explained, the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to enforce the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission\xe2\x80\x99s (EEOC) subpoena for pedigree information rested on a legal error. Lower court resolution of a question of law is ordinarily reviewable de\xc2\xa0novo on appeal. Highmark Inc. v. Allcare Health Management System, Inc., 572 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___, and n.\xc2\xa02 (2014) (slip op., at 4, and n.\xc2\xa02). According to the District Court, it was not yet \xe2\x80\x9cnecessary [for the EEOC] to seek such information.\xe2\x80\x9d 2012 WL 5868959, *6 (D Ariz., Nov. 19, 2012). As the Ninth Circuit correctly conveyed, however: \xe2\x80\x9cThe EEOC does not have to show a \xe2\x80\x98particularized necessity of access, beyond a showing of mere relevance,\xe2\x80\x99 to obtain evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d 804 F.\xc2\xa03d 1051, 1057 (2015) (quoting University of Pa. v. EEOC,\n\n493 U.\xc2\xa0S. 182,\n188 (1990)\n). Because the District Court erred as a matter of law in demanding that the EEOC show more than relevance in order to gain enforcement of its sub-poena, I would not disturb the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment.
