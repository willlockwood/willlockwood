\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

No. 07\xe2\x80\x931437.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 24, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 4, 2009

\n

Respondents filed a state-court suit alleging that petitioner had violated state and federal law in connection with a patent dispute. After removing the case to Federal District Court under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71441(c), which allows removal if the case includes at least one claim over which the federal court has original jurisdiction, petitioner moved to dismiss the suit\xe2\x80\x99s only federal claim, which arose under the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (RICO). Agreeing that respondents had failed to state a RICO claim upon which relief could be granted, the District Court dismissed the claim; declined to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over the remaining state-law claims under \xc2\xa71367(c)(3), which allows such a course if the court \xe2\x80\x9chas dismissed all claims over which it has original jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d; and remanded the case to state court. The Federal Circuit dismissed petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s appeal, finding that the remand order could be colorably characterized as based on a \xe2\x80\x9clack of subject matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d over the state-law claims, \xc2\xa71447(c), and was therefore \xe2\x80\x9cnot reviewable on appeal,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71447(d).

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0A district court\xe2\x80\x99s order remanding a case to state court after declining to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over state-law claims is not a remand for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction for which appellate review is barred by \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71447(c) and (d). With respect to supplemental jurisdiction, a federal court has subject-matter jurisdiction over specified state-law claims, see \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71367(a), (c), and its decision whether to exercise that jurisdiction after dismissing every claim over which it had original jurisdiction is purely discretionary, see, e.g., Osborn v. Haley, 549 U. S. 225, 245. It is undisputed that when this case was removed, the District Court had original jurisdiction over the federal RICO claim under \xc2\xa71331 and supplemental jurisdiction over the state-law claims, which were \xe2\x80\x9cso related to claims \xe2\x80\xa6 within such original jurisdiction that they form[ed] part of the same case or controversy,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71367(a). On dismissing the RICO claim, the court retained its statutory supplemental jurisdiction over the state-law claims. Its decision not to exercise that statutory authority was not based on a jurisdictional defect, but on its discretionary choice. See Chicago v. International College of Surgeons, 522 U. S. 156, 173. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x936.

\n

508 F. 3d 659, reversed and remanded.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In this case, we decide whether a federal court of appeals has jurisdiction to review a district court\xe2\x80\x99s order that remands a case to state court after declining to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over state-law claims under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71367(c). The Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit held that appellate review of such an order is barred by \xc2\xa71447(d) because it viewed the remand order in this case as resting on the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s lack of subject-matter jurisdiction over the state-law claims. We disagree and reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 2005, respondents filed a complaint against petitioner and others in California state court, alleging that petitioner had violated state and federal law in connection with a patent dispute. Petitioner removed the case to the United States District Court for the Central District of California pursuant to \xc2\xa71441(c), which allows removal of an \xe2\x80\x9centire case\xe2\x80\x9d when it includes at least one claim over which the federal district court has original jurisdiction. Petitioner then filed a motion to dismiss the only federal claim in the lawsuit, which arose under the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act (RICO), 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71961\xe2\x80\x931968, for failure to adequately allege a pattern of racketeering. HIF Bio, Inc. v. Yung Shin Pharmaceuticals Indus. Co., 508 F.\xc2\xa03d 659, 662 (CA Fed. 2007). The District Court agreed that respondents had failed to state a RICO claim upon which relief could be granted and dismissed the claim pursuant to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6). The District Court also declined to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over the remaining state-law claims pursuant to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71367(c)(3), which provides that a district court \xe2\x80\x9cmay decline to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over a claim\xe2\x80\x9d if \xe2\x80\x9cthe district court has dismissed all claims over which it has original jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d The District Court then remanded the case to state court as authorized by this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Carnegie-Mellon Univ. v. Cohill, 484 U. S. 343 (1988).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner appealed to the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit, arguing that the District Court should have exercised supplemental jurisdiction over the state-law claims because they implicate federal patent-law rights. 508 F. 3d, at 663. The Court of Appeals dismissed the appeal, finding that the remand order could \xe2\x80\x9cbe colorably characterized as a remand based on lack of subject matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d and, therefore, could not be reviewed under \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71447(c) and (d), which provide in part that remands for \xe2\x80\x9clack of subject matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d are \xe2\x80\x9cnot reviewable on appeal or otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d See id., at 667.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court has not yet decided whether a district court\xe2\x80\x99s order remanding a case to state court after declining to exercise supplemental jurisdiction is a remand for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction for which appellate review is barred by \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71447(c) and (d). See Powerex Corp. v. Reliant Energy Services, Inc., 551 U. S. 224, 235, n. 4 (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe have never passed on whether Cohill remands are subject-matter jurisdictional for purposes of \xe2\x80\xa6 \xc2\xa71447(c) and \xc2\xa71447(d)\xe2\x80\x9d). We granted certiorari to resolve this question, 555 U. S. ___ (2008), and now hold that such remand orders are not based on a lack of subject-matter jurisdiction. Accordingly, we reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remand for further proceedings.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Appellate review of remand orders is limited by 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71447(d), which states:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cAn order remanding a case to the State court from which it was removed is not reviewable on appeal or otherwise, except that an order remanding a case to the State court from which it was removed pursuant to section 1443 of this title shall be reviewable by appeal or otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nThis Court has consistently held that \xc2\xa71447(d) must be read in pari materia with \xc2\xa71447(c), thus limiting the remands barred from appellate review by \xc2\xa71447(d) to those that are based on a ground specified in \xc2\xa71447(c). See Thermtron Products, Inc. v. Hermansdorfer, 423 U. S. 336, 345\xe2\x80\x93346 (1976); see also Powerex, supra, at 229; Quackenbush v. Allstate Ins. Co., 517 U. S. 706, 711\xe2\x80\x93712 (1996); Things Remembered, Inc. v. Petrarca, 516 U. S. 124, 127 (1995).*

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0One type of remand order governed by \xc2\xa71447(c)\xe2\x80\x94the type at issue in this case\xe2\x80\x94is a remand order based on a lack of \xe2\x80\x9csubject matter jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71447(c) (providing, in relevant part, that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f at any time before final judgment it appears that the district court lacks subject matter jurisdiction, the case shall be remanded\xe2\x80\x9d). The question presented in this case is whether the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s remand order, which rested on its decision declining to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-law claims, is a remand based on a \xe2\x80\x9clack of subject matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71447(c) and (d). It is not.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cSubject matter jurisdiction defines the court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to hear a given type of case,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Morton, 467 U. S. 822, 828 (1984); it represents \xe2\x80\x9cthe extent to which a court can rule on the conduct of persons or the status of things.\xe2\x80\x9d Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 870 (8th ed. 2004). This Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedent makes clear that whether a court has subject-matter jurisdiction over a claim is distinct from whether a court chooses to exercise that jurisdiction. See, e.g., Quackenbush, supra, at 712 (holding that an abstention-based remand is not a remand for \xe2\x80\x9clack of subject matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71447(c) and (d)); Ankenbrandt v. Richards, 504 U. S. 689, 704 (1992) (questioning whether, \xe2\x80\x9ceven though subject matter jurisdiction might be proper, sufficient grounds exist to warrant abstention from the exercise of that jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d); Iowa Mut. Ins. Co. v. LaPlante, 480 U. S. 9, 16, n. 8 (1987) (referring to exhaustion requirement as \xe2\x80\x9ca matter of comity\xe2\x80\x9d that does \xe2\x80\x9cnot deprive the federal courts of subject-matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d but does \xe2\x80\x9crende[r] it appropriate for\nthe federal courts to decline jurisdiction in certain\ncircumstances\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With respect to supplemental jurisdiction in particular, a federal court has subject-matter jurisdiction over specified state-law claims, which it may (or may not) choose to exercise. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71367(a), (c). A district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision whether to exercise that jurisdiction after dismissing every claim over which it had original jurisdiction is purely discretionary. See \xc2\xa71367(c) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe district courts may decline to exercise supplemental jurisdiction over a claim \xe2\x80\xa6 if \xe2\x80\xa6 the district court has dismissed all claims over which it has original jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); Osborn v. Haley, 549 U. S. 225, 245 (2007) (\xe2\x80\x9cEven if only state-law claims remained after resolution of the federal question, the District Court would have discretion, consistent with Article III, to retain jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d); Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., 546 U. S. 500, 514 (2006) (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]hen a court grants a motion to dismiss for failure to state a federal claim, the court generally retains discretion to exercise supplemental jurisdiction, pursuant to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71367, over pendent state-law claims\xe2\x80\x9d); see also 13D C. Wright, A. Miller, E. Cooper, & R. Freer, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa73567.3, pp. 428\xe2\x80\x93432 (3d ed. 2008) (\xe2\x80\x9cOnce it has dismissed the claims that invoked original bases of subject matter jurisdiction, all that remains before the federal court are state-law claims\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. The district court retains discretion to exercise supplemental jurisdiction [over them]\xe2\x80\x9d). As a result, \xe2\x80\x9cthe [district] court\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of its discretion under \xc2\xa71367(c) is not a jurisdictional matter. Thus, the court\xe2\x80\x99s determination may be reviewed for abuse of discretion, but may not be raised at any time as a jurisdictional defect.\xe2\x80\x9d 16 J. Moore et\xc2\xa0al., Moore\xe2\x80\x99s Federal Practice \xc2\xa7106.05[4], p. 106\xe2\x80\x9327 (3d ed. 2009).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is undisputed that when this case was removed to federal court, the District Court had original jurisdiction over the federal RICO claim pursuant to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331 and supplemental jurisdiction over the state-law claims because they were \xe2\x80\x9cso related to claims in the action within such original jurisdiction that they form[ed] part of the same case or controversy under Article III of the United States Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71367(a). Upon dismissal of the federal claim, the District Court retained its statutory supplemental jurisdiction over the state-law claims. Its decision declining to exercise that statutory authority was not based on a jurisdictional defect but on its discretionary choice not to hear the claims despite its subject-matter jurisdiction over them. See Chicago v. International College of Surgeons, 522 U. S. 156, 173 (1997) (\xe2\x80\x9cDepending on a host of factors, then\xe2\x80\x94including the circumstances of the particular case, the nature of the state law claims, the character of the governing state law, and the relationship between the state and federal claims\xe2\x80\x94district courts may decline to exercise jurisdiction over supplemental state law claims\xe2\x80\x9d). The remand order, therefore, is not based on a \xe2\x80\x9clack of subject matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d for purposes of the bar to appellate review created by \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71447(c) and (d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals held to the contrary based on its conclusion that \xe2\x80\x9cevery \xc2\xa71367(c) remand necessarily involves a predicate finding that the claims at issue lack an independent basis of subject matter jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d 508 F. 3d, at 667. But, as explained above, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71367(a) and (c) provide a basis for subject-matter jurisdiction over any properly removed state claim. See Osborn, supra, at 245; Arbaugh, supra, at 514. We thus disagree with the Court of Appeals that the remand at issue here \xe2\x80\x9ccan be colorably characterized as a lack of subject matter jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d 508 F. 3d, at 667.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When a district court remands claims to a state court after declining to exercise supplemental jurisdiction, the remand order is not based on a lack of subject-matter jurisdiction for purposes of \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71447(c) and (d). The judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\n

Neither the brief for petitioner nor the brief for respondents explicitly asked the Court to do so here, and counsel for both parties clearly stated at oral argument that they were not asking for Thermtron to be overruled. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 16, 22; cf. South Central Bell Telephone Co. v. Alabama, 526 U. S. 160, 171 (1999). We also note that the parties in Powerex, Quackenbush, and Things Remembered did not ask for Thermtron to be overruled.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, concurring.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In his dissenting opinion in Thermtron Products, Inc. v. Hermansdorfer, 423 U. S. 336, 360 (1976), then-Justice Rehnquist remarked that he could \xe2\x80\x9cperceive no justification for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to ignore the express directive of Congress in favor of what it personally perceives to be \xe2\x80\x98justice\xe2\x80\x99 in this case.\xe2\x80\x9d He began his dissent with a comment that is also applicable to the case before us today: \xe2\x80\x9cThe Court of Appeals not unreasonably believed that 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71447(d) means what it says. It says:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98An order remanding a case to the State court from which it was removed is not reviewable on appeal or otherwise\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 354.

\n

\nToday, as in Thermtron, the Court holds that \xc2\xa71447(d) does not mean what it says.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0If we were writing on a clean slate, I would adhere to the statute\xe2\x80\x99s text. But Thermtron\xe2\x80\x99s limiting construction applies equally to this case as it did to Powerex Corp. v. Reliant Energy Services, Inc., 551 U. S. 224, 229\xe2\x80\x93230 (2007), Quackenbush v. Allstate Ins. Co., 517 U. S. 706, 711\xe2\x80\x93712 (1996), and Things Remembered, Inc. v. Petrarca, 516 U. S. 124, 127\xe2\x80\x93128 (1995), and stare decisis compels the conclusion that the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s remand order is reviewable notwithstanding \xc2\xa71447(d)\xe2\x80\x99s unambiguous contrary command. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s adherence to precedent in this case represents a welcome departure from its sometimes single-minded focus on literal text. Accordingly, I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Souter joins, concurring.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. I write separately to note an anomaly about the way 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71447 works. In this case, we consider a District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision not to retain on its docket a case that once contained federal law issues but now contains only state law issues. All agree that the law grants the District Court broad discretion to determine whether it should keep such cases on its docket, that a decision to do so (or not to do so) rarely involves major legal questions, and that (even if wrong) a district court decision of this kind will not often have major adverse consequences. We now hold that \xc2\xa71447 permits appellate courts to review a district court decision of this kind, even if only for abuse of discretion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Contrast today\xe2\x80\x99s decision with our decision two Terms ago in Powerex Corp. v. Reliant Energy Services, Inc., 551 U. S. 224 (2007). In that case, we considered a District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision to remand a case in which a Canadian province-owned power company had sought removal\xe2\x80\x94a matter that the Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 specifically authorizes federal judges (in certain instances) to decide. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71441(d); 1603(a). The case presented a difficult legal question involving the commercial activities of a foreign sovereign; and the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision (if wrong) had potentially serious adverse consequences, namely preventing a sovereign power from obtaining the federal trial to which the law (in its view) entitled it. We nonetheless held that \xc2\xa71447 forbids appellate courts from reviewing a district court decision of this kind. Id., at 238\xe2\x80\x93239.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, we have held that \xc2\xa71447 permits review of a district court decision in an instance where that decision is unlikely to be wrong and where a wrong decision is unlikely to work serious harm. And we have held that \xc2\xa71447 forbids review of a district court decision in an instance where that decision may well be wrong and where a wrong decision could work considerable harm. Unless the circumstances I describe are unusual, something is wrong. And the fact that we have read other exceptions in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s absolute-sounding language suggests that such circumstances are not all that unusual. See Osborn v. Haley, 549 U. S. 225, 240\xe2\x80\x93244 (2007); Thermtron Products, Inc. v. Hermansdorfer, 423 U. S. 336, 350\xe2\x80\x93352 (1976).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consequently, while joining the majority, I suggest that experts in this area of the law reexamine the matter with an eye toward determining whether statutory revision is appropriate.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Scalia, concurring.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court today does nothing more than accurately apply to the facts of this case our holding in Thermtron Products, Inc. v. Hermansdorfer, 423 U. S. 336 (1976). Ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x936.* As the Court notes, neither party has asked us to reconsider Thermtron, and we thus have no occasion to revisit that decision here, see ante, at 3, n.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I write separately, though, to note that our decision in Thermtron was questionable in its day and is ripe for reconsideration in the appropriate case. Title 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71447(d) states that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n order remanding a case to the State court from which it was removed is not reviewable on appeal or otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d The statute provides a single exception\xe2\x80\x94not remotely implicated in this case\xe2\x80\x94for certain civil rights cases removed under \xc2\xa71443. See \xc2\xa71447(d). As then-Justice Rehnquist understatingly observed in his Thermtron dissent, it would not be \xe2\x80\x9cunreasonabl[e] [to] believ[e] that 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71447(d) means what it says,\xe2\x80\x9d 423 U. S., at 354; and what it says is no appellate review of remand orders. See also Osborn v. Haley, 549 U. S. 225, 263 (2007) (Scalia, J., dissenting). Since the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order in this case \xe2\x80\x9cremand[ed] a case to the State court from which it was removed,\xe2\x80\x9d it should be\xe2\x80\x94in the words of \xc2\xa71447(d)\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cnot reviewable on appeal or otherwise.\xe2\x80\x9d Q.\xc2\xa0E.\xc2\xa0D.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Over the years, the Court has replaced the statute\xe2\x80\x99s clear bar on appellate review with a hodgepodge of jurisdictional rules that have no evident basis even in common sense. Under our decisions, there is no appellate jurisdiction to review remands for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction, see Powerex Corp. v. Reliant Energy Services, Inc., 551 U. S. 224, 232 (2007), though with exception, see Osborn v. Haley, supra, at 243\xe2\x80\x93244; there is jurisdiction to review remands of supplemental state-law claims, and other remands based on abstention, see Quackenbush v. Allstate Ins. Co., 517 U. S. 706, 711\xe2\x80\x93712 (1996), though presumably no jurisdiction to review remands based on the \xe2\x80\x9cdefects\xe2\x80\x9d referenced in \xc2\xa71447(c). See also post, at 2 (Breyer, J., concurring) (discussing similar anomalies). If this muddle represents a welcome departure from the literal text, see ante, at 2 (Stevens, J., concurring), the world is mad.
