PRADO NAVARETTE et\xc2\xa0al. v. CALIFORNIA

\n

certiorari to the court of appeal of california, first appellate district

\n

No. 12\xe2\x80\x939490.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 21, 2014\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 22, 2014

\n

A California Highway Patrol officer stopped the pickup truck occupied by petitioners because it matched the description of a vehicle that a 911 caller had recently reported as having run her off the road. As he and a second officer approached the truck, they smelled marijuana. They searched the truck\xe2\x80\x99s bed, found 30 pounds of marijuana, and arrested petitioners. Petitioners moved to suppress the evidence, arguing that the traffic stop violated the\n \nFourth Amendment. Their motion was denied, and they pleaded guilty to transporting marijuana. The California Court of Appeal affirmed, concluding that the officer had reasonable suspicion to conduct an investigative stop.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The traffic stop complied with the\n \nFourth Amendment because, under the totality of the circumstances, the officer had reasonable suspicion that the truck\xe2\x80\x99s driver was intoxicated. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (a)\xc2\xa0The\n \nFourth Amendment permits brief investigative stops when an officer has \xe2\x80\x9ca particularized and objective basis for suspecting the particular person stopped of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. criminal activity.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Cortez,\n\n449 U.\xc2\xa0S. 411\n\xe2\x80\x93418. Reasonable suspicion takes into account \xe2\x80\x9cthe totality of the circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 417, and depends \xe2\x80\x9cupon both the content of information possessed by police and its degree of reliability,\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama v. White,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 325\n. An anonymous tip alone seldom demonstrates sufficient reliability, White, 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 329, but may do so under appropriate circumstances, id., at 327. Pp.\xc2\xa03\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (b)\xc2\xa0The 911 call in this case bore adequate indicia of reliability for the officer to credit the caller\xe2\x80\x99s account. By reporting that she had been run off the road by a specific vehicle, the caller necessarily claimed an eyewitness basis of knowledge. The apparently short time between the reported incident and the 911 call suggests that the caller had little time to fabricate the report. And a reasonable officer could conclude that a false tipster would think twice before using the 911 system, which has several technological and regulatory features that safeguard against making false reports with immunity. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n (c)\xc2\xa0Not only was the tip here reliable, but it also created reasonable suspicion of drunk driving. Running another car off the road suggests the sort of impairment that characterizes drunk driving. While that conduct might be explained by another cause such as driver distraction, reasonable suspicion \xe2\x80\x9cneed not rule out the possibility of innocent conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Arvizu,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 266\n. Finally, the officer\xe2\x80\x99s failure to observe additional suspicious conduct during the short period that he followed the truck did not dispel the reasonable suspicion of drunk driving, and the officer was not required to surveil the truck for a longer period. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

Affirmed.

LORENZO PRADO NAVARETTE AND JOSE PRADONAVARETTE, PETITIONERS v. CALIFORNIA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the court of appeal of california, first appellate district

\n

[April 22, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n After a 911 caller reported that a vehicle had run her off the road, a police officer located the vehicle she identified during the call and executed a traffic stop. We hold that the stop complied with the\n \nFourth Amendment because, under the totality of the circumstances, the officer had reasonable suspicion that the driver was intoxicated.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n On August 23, 2008, a Mendocino County 911 dispatch team for the California Highway Patrol (CHP) received a call from another CHP dispatcher in neighboring Humboldt County. The Humboldt County dispatcher relayed a tip from a 911 caller, which the Mendocino County team recorded as follows: \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Showing southbound Highway 1 at mile marker 88, Silver Ford 150 pickup. Plate of 8-David-94925. Ran the reporting party off the roadway and was last seen approximately five [minutes] ago.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. 36a. The Mendocino County team then broadcast that information to CHP officers at 3:47 p.m.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A CHP officer heading northbound toward the reported vehicle responded to the broadcast. At 4:00 p.m., the officer passed the truck near mile marker 69. At about 4:05 p.m., after making a U-turn, he pulled the truck over. A second officer, who had separately responded to the broadcast, also arrived on the scene. As the two officers approached the truck, they smelled marijuana. A search of the truck bed revealed 30 pounds of marijuana. The officers arrested the driver, petitioner Lorenzo Prado Navarette, and the passenger, petitioner Jos\xc3\xa9 Prado Navarette.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Petitioners moved to suppress the evidence, arguing that the traffic stop violated the\n \nFourth Amendment because the officer lacked reasonable suspicion of criminal activity. Both the magistrate who presided over the suppression hearing and the Superior Court disagreed.[1] Petitioners pleaded guilty to transporting marijuana and were sentenced to 90 days in jail plus three years ofprobation.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The California Court of Appeal affirmed, concluding that the officer had reasonable suspicion to conduct an investigative stop. 2012 WL 4842651 (Oct. 12, 2012). The court reasoned that the content of the tip indicated that it came from an eyewitness victim of reckless driving, and that the officer\xe2\x80\x99s corroboration of the truck\xe2\x80\x99s description, location, and direction established that the tip was reliable enough to justify a traffic stop. Id., at *7. Finally, the court concluded that the caller reported driving that was sufficiently dangerous to merit an investigative stop without waiting for the officer to observe additional reckless driving himself. Id., at *9. The California Supreme Court denied review. We granted certiorari, 570 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2013), and now affirm.

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The\n \nFourth Amendment permits brief investigative stops\xe2\x80\x94such as the traffic stop in this case\xe2\x80\x94when a law enforcement officer has \xe2\x80\x9ca particularized and objective basis for suspecting the particular person stopped of criminal activity.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Cortez,\n\n449 U.\xc2\xa0S. 411\n\xe2\x80\x93418 (1981); see also Terry v. Ohio,\n\n392 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n\xe2\x80\x9322 (1968). The \xe2\x80\x9creasonable suspicion\xe2\x80\x9d necessary to justify such a stop \xe2\x80\x9cis dependent upon both the content of information possessed by police and its degree of reliability.\xe2\x80\x9d Alabama v. White,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 325,\n330 (1990)\n. The standard takes into account \xe2\x80\x9cthe totality of the circumstances\xe2\x80\x94the whole picture.\xe2\x80\x9d Cortez, supra, at 417. Although a mere \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98hunch\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d does not create reasonable suspicion, Terry, supra, at 27, the level of suspicion the standard requires is \xe2\x80\x9cconsiderably less than proof of wrongdoing by a preponderance of the evidence,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cobviously less\xe2\x80\x9d than is necessary for probable cause, United States v. Sokolow,\n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n7 (1989)\n.

\n

A

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n These principles apply with full force to investigative stops based on information from anonymous tips. We have firmly rejected the argument \xe2\x80\x9cthat reasonable cause for a[n investigative stop] can only be based on the officer\xe2\x80\x99s personal observation, rather than on information supplied by another person.\xe2\x80\x9d Adams v. Williams,\n\n407 U.\xc2\xa0S. 143,\n147 (1972)\n. Of course, \xe2\x80\x9can anonymous tip alone seldom demonstrates the informant\xe2\x80\x99s basis of knowledge or veracity.\xe2\x80\x9d White, 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 329 (emphasis added). That is because \xe2\x80\x9cordinary citizens generally do not provide extensive recitations of the basis of their everyday observations,\xe2\x80\x9d and an anonymous tipster\xe2\x80\x99s veracity is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98by hypoth-esis largely unknown, and unknowable.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. But under appropriate circumstances, an anonymous tip can demonstrate \xe2\x80\x9csufficient indicia of reliability to provide reasonable suspicion to make [an] investigatory stop.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 327.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Our decisions in Alabama v. White,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 325 (1990)\n, and Florida v. J.\xc2\xa0L.,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 266 (2000)\n, are useful guides. In White, an anonymous tipster told the police that a woman would drive from a particular apartment building to a particular motel in a brown Plymouth station wagon with a broken right tail light. The tipster further asserted that the woman would be transporting cocaine. 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 327. After confirming the innocent details, officers stopped the station wagon as it neared the motel and found cocaine in the vehicle. Id., at 331. We held that the officers\xe2\x80\x99 corroboration of certain details made the anonymous tip sufficiently reliable to create reasonable suspicion of criminal activity. By accurately predicting future behavior, the tipster demonstrated \xe2\x80\x9ca special familiarity with respondent\xe2\x80\x99s affairs,\xe2\x80\x9d which in turn implied that the tipster had \xe2\x80\x9caccess to reliable information about that individual\xe2\x80\x99s illegal activities.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 332. We also recognized that an informant who is proved to tell the truth about some things is more likely to tell the truth about other things, \xe2\x80\x9cincluding the claim that the object of the tip is engaged in criminal activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 331 (citing Illinois v. Gates,\n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 213,\n244 (1983)\n).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In J.\xc2\xa0L., by contrast, we determined that no reasonable suspicion arose from a bare-bones tip that a young black male in a plaid shirt standing at a bus stop was carrying a gun. 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 268. The tipster did not explain how he knew about the gun, nor did he suggest that he had any special familiarity with the young man\xe2\x80\x99s affairs. Id., at 271. As a result, police had no basis for believing \xe2\x80\x9cthat the tipster ha[d] knowledge of concealed criminal activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 272. Furthermore, the tip included no predictions of future behavior that could be corroborated to assess the tipster\xe2\x80\x99s credibility. Id., at 271. We accordingly concluded that the tip was insufficiently reliable to justify a stop and frisk.

\n

B

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The initial question in this case is whether the 911 call was sufficiently reliable to credit the allegation that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 truck \xe2\x80\x9cran the [caller] off the roadway.\xe2\x80\x9d Even assuming for present purposes that the 911 call was anonymous, see n.\xc2\xa01, supra, we conclude that the call bore adequate indicia of reliability for the officer to credit the caller\xe2\x80\x99s account. The officer was therefore justified in proceeding from the premise that the truck had, in fact, caused the caller\xe2\x80\x99s car to be dangerously diverted from the highway.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n By reporting that she had been run off the road by a specific vehicle\xe2\x80\x94a silver Ford F-150 pickup, license plate 8D94925\xe2\x80\x94the caller necessarily claimed eyewitness knowledge of the alleged dangerous driving. That basis of knowledge lends significant support to the tip\xe2\x80\x99s reliability. See Gates, supra, at 234 (\xe2\x80\x9c[An informant\xe2\x80\x99s] explicit and detailed description of alleged wrongdoing, along with a statement that the event was observed firsthand, entitles his tip to greater weight than might otherwise be the case\xe2\x80\x9d); Spinelli v. United States,\n\n393 U.\xc2\xa0S. 410,\n416 (1969)\n (a tip of illegal gambling is less reliable when \xe2\x80\x9cit is not alleged that the informant personally observed [the defendant] at work or that he had ever placed a bet with him\xe2\x80\x9d). This is in contrast to J.\xc2\xa0L., where the tip provided no basis for concluding that the tipster had actually seen the gun. 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 271. Even in White, where we upheld the stop, there was scant evidence that the tipster had actually observed cocaine in the station wagon. We called White a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98close case\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d because \xe2\x80\x9c[k]nowledge about a person\xe2\x80\x99s future movements indicates some familiarity with that person\xe2\x80\x99s affairs, but having such knowledge does not necessarily imply that the informant knows, in particular, whether that person is carrying hidden contraband.\xe2\x80\x9d 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 271. A driver\xe2\x80\x99s claim that another vehicle ran her off the road, however, necessarily implies that the informant knows the other car was driven dangerously.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n There is also reason to think that the 911 caller in this case was telling the truth. Police confirmed the truck\xe2\x80\x99s location near mile marker 69 (roughly 19 highway miles south of the location reported in the 911 call) at 4:00 p.m. (roughly 18 minutes after the 911 call). That timeline of events suggests that the caller reported the incident soon after she was run off the road. That sort of contemporaneous report has long been treated as especially reliable. In evidence law, we generally credit the proposition that statements about an event and made soon after perceiving that event are especially trustworthy because \xe2\x80\x9csubstantial contemporaneity of event and statement negate the likelihood of deliberate or conscious misrepresentation.\xe2\x80\x9d Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on Fed. Rule Evid. 803(1), 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 371 (describing the rationale for the hearsay exception for \xe2\x80\x9cpresent sense impression[s]\xe2\x80\x9d). A similar rationale applies to a \xe2\x80\x9cstatement relating to a startling event\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94such as getting run off the road\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cmade while the declarant was under the stress of excitement that it caused.\xe2\x80\x9d Fed. Rule Evid. 803(2) (hearsay exception for \xe2\x80\x9cexcited utterances\xe2\x80\x9d). Unsurprisingly, 911 calls that would otherwise be inadmissible hearsay have often been admitted on those grounds. See D. Binder, Hearsay Handbook \xc2\xa78.1, pp. 257\xe2\x80\x93259 (4th ed. 2013\xe2\x80\x932014) (citing cases admitting 911 calls as present sense impressions); id., \xc2\xa79.1, at 274\xe2\x80\x93275 (911 calls admitted as excited utterances). There was no indication that the tip in J.\xc2\xa0L. (or even in White) was contemporaneous with the observation of criminal activity or made under the stress of excitement caused by a startling event, but those considerations weigh in favor of the caller\xe2\x80\x99s veracity here.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Another indicator of veracity is the caller\xe2\x80\x99s use of the 911 emergency system. See Brief for Respondent 40\xe2\x80\x9341, 44; Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 16\xe2\x80\x9318. A 911 call has some features that allow for identifying and tracing callers, and thus provide some safeguards against making false reports with immunity. See J.\xc2\xa0L., supra, at 276 (Kennedy, J., concurring). As this case illustrates, see n.\xc2\xa01, supra, 911 calls can be recorded, which provides victims with an opportunity to identify the false tipster\xe2\x80\x99s voice and subject him to prosecution, see, e.g., Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa7653x (West 2010) (makes \xe2\x80\x9ctelephon[ing] the 911 emergency line with the intent to annoy or harass\xe2\x80\x9d punishable by imprisonment and fine); see also \xc2\xa7148.3 (2014 West Cum. Supp.) (prohibits falsely reporting \xe2\x80\x9cthat an \xe2\x80\x98emergency\xe2\x80\x99 exists\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa7148.5 (prohibits falsely reporting \xe2\x80\x9cthat a felony or misdemeanor has been committed\xe2\x80\x9d). The 911 system also permits law enforcement to verify important information about the caller. In 1998, the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) began to require cellular carriers to relay the caller\xe2\x80\x99s phone number to 911 dispatchers. 47 CFR \xc2\xa720.18(d)(1) (2013) (FCC\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cPhase I enhanced 911 services\xe2\x80\x9d requirements). Beginning in 2001, carriers have been required to identify the caller\xe2\x80\x99s geographic location with increasing specificity. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa720.18(e)\xe2\x80\x93(h) (\xe2\x80\x9cPhase II enhanced 911 service\xe2\x80\x9d requirements). And although callers may ordinarily block call recipients from obtaining their identifying information, FCC regulations exempt 911 calls from that privilege. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa764.1601(b), (d)(4)(ii) (\xe2\x80\x9c911 emergency services\xe2\x80\x9d exemption from rule that, when a caller so requests, \xe2\x80\x9ca carrier may not reveal that caller\xe2\x80\x99s number or name\xe2\x80\x9d). None of this is to suggest that tips in 911 calls are per\xc2\xa0se reliable. Given the foregoing technological and regulatory developments, however, a reasonable officer could conclude that a false tipster would think twice before using such a system. The caller\xe2\x80\x99s use of the 911 system is therefore one of the relevant circum-stances that, taken together, justified the officer\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the information reported in the 911 call.

\n

C

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Even a reliable tip will justify an investigative stop only if it creates reasonable suspicion that \xe2\x80\x9ccriminal activity may be afoot.\xe2\x80\x9d Terry, 392 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 30. We must therefore determine whether the 911 caller\xe2\x80\x99s report of being run off the roadway created reasonable suspicion of an ongoing crime such as drunk driving as opposed to an isolated episode of past recklessness. See Cortez, 449 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 417 (\xe2\x80\x9cAn investigatory stop must be justified by some objective manifestation that the person stopped is, or is about to be, engaged in criminal activity\xe2\x80\x9d). We conclude that the behavior alleged by the 911 caller, \xe2\x80\x9cviewed from the standpoint of an objectively reasonable police officer, amount[s] to reasonable suspicion\xe2\x80\x9d of drunk driving. Ornelas v. United States,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 690,\n696 (1996)\n. The stop was therefore proper.[2]

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Reasonable suspicion depends on \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cthe factual and practical considerations of everyday life on which reason-able and prudent men, not legal technicians, act.\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 695. Under that commonsense approach, we can appropriately recognize certain driving behaviors as sound indicia of drunk driving. See, e.g., People v. Wells, 38 Cal. 4th 1078, 1081, 136 P.\xc2\xa03d 810, 811 (2006) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98weaving all over the roadway\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); State v. Prendergast, 103 Haw. 451, 452\xe2\x80\x93453, 83 P.\xc2\xa03d 714, 715\xe2\x80\x93716 (2004) (\xe2\x80\x9ccross[ing] over the center line\xe2\x80\x9d on a highway and \xe2\x80\x9calmost caus[ing] several head-on collisions\xe2\x80\x9d); State v. Golotta, 178 N.\xc2\xa0J. 205, 209, 837 A.\xc2\xa02d 359, 361 (2003) (driving \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98all overthe road\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98weaving back and forth\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d); State v. Walshire, 634 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 625, 626 (Iowa 2001) (\xe2\x80\x9cdriving in the median\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, the accumulated experience of thousands of officers suggests that these sorts of erratic behaviors are strongly correlated with drunk driving. See Nat. Highway Traffic Safety Admin., The Visual Detection of DWI Motorists 4\xe2\x80\x935 (Mar. 2010), online at http://nhtsa.gov/staticfiles/nti/pdf/808677.pdf (as visited Apr. 18, 2014, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). Of course, not all traffic infractions imply intoxication. Unconfirmed reports of driving without a seatbelt or slightly over the speed limit, for example, are so tenuously connected to drunk driving that a stop on those grounds alone would be constitutionally suspect. But a reliable tip alleging the dangerous behaviors discussed above gener-ally would justify a traffic stop on suspicion of drunk driving.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The 911 caller in this case reported more than a minor traffic infraction and more than a conclusory allegation of drunk or reckless driving. Instead, she alleged a specific and dangerous result of the driver\xe2\x80\x99s conduct: running another car off the highway. That conduct bears too great a resemblance to paradigmatic manifestations of drunk driving to be dismissed as an isolated example of recklessness. Running another vehicle off the road suggests lane-positioning problems, decreased vigilance, impaired judgment, or some combination of those recognized drunk driving cues. See Visual Detection of DWI Motorists 4\xe2\x80\x935. And the experience of many officers suggests that a driver who almost strikes a vehicle or another object\xe2\x80\x94the exact scenario that ordinarily causes \xe2\x80\x9crunning [another vehicle] off the roadway\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94is likely intoxicated. See id., at 5, 8.As a result, we cannot say that the officer acted unreasonably under these circumstances in stopping a driverwhose alleged conduct was a significant indicator of drunk driving.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 attempts to second-guess the officer\xe2\x80\x99s rea-sonable suspicion of drunk driving are unavailing. It is true that the reported behavior might also be explained by, for example, a driver responding to \xe2\x80\x9can unruly child or other distraction.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 21. But we have consistently recognized that reasonable suspicion \xe2\x80\x9cneed not rule out the possibility of innocent conduct.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Arvizu,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 266,\n277 (2002)\n.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Nor did the absence of additional suspicious conduct, after the vehicle was first spotted by an officer, dispel the reasonable suspicion of drunk driving. Brief for Petitioners 23\xe2\x80\x9324. It is hardly surprising that the appearance of a marked police car would inspire more careful driving for a time. Cf. Arvizu, supra, at 275 (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[s]lowing down after spotting a law enforcement vehicle\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d does not dispel reasonable suspicion of criminal activity). Extended observation of an allegedly drunk driver might eventually dispel a reasonable suspicion of intoxication, but the 5-minute period in this case hardly sufficed in that regard. Of course, an officer who already has such a reasonable suspicion need not surveil a vehicle at length in order to personally observe suspicious driving. See Adams v. Williams, 407 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 147 (repudiating the argument that \xe2\x80\x9creasonable cause for a[n investigative stop] can only be based on the officer\xe2\x80\x99s personal observation\xe2\x80\x9d). Once reasonable suspicion of drunk driving arises, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he reasonableness of the officer\xe2\x80\x99s decision to stop a suspect does not turn on the availability of less intrusive investigatory techniques.\xe2\x80\x9d Sokolow, 490 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 11. This would be a particularly inappropriate context to depart from that settled rule, because allowing a drunk driver a second chance for dangerous conduct could have disastrousconsequences.

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Like White, this is a \xe2\x80\x9cclose case.\xe2\x80\x9d 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 332. As in that case, the indicia of the 911 caller\xe2\x80\x99s reliability here are stronger than those in J.\xc2\xa0L., where we held that a bare-bones tip was unreliable. 529 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 271. Although the indicia present here are different from those we found sufficient in White, there is more than one way to demonstrate \xe2\x80\x9ca particularized and objective basis for suspecting the particular person stopped of criminal activity.\xe2\x80\x9d Cortez, 449 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 417\xe2\x80\x93418. Under the totality of the circumstances, we find the indicia of reliability in this case sufficient to provide the officer with reasonable suspicion that the driver of the reported vehicle had run another vehicle off the road. That made it reasonable under the circumstances for the officer to execute a traffic stop. We accordingly affirm.

\n

It is so ordered.

LORENZO PRADO NAVARETTE AND JOSE PRADONAVARETTE, PETITIONERS v. CALIFORNIA

\n

on writ of certiorari to the court of appeal of california, first appellate district

\n

[April 22, 2014]

\n

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Justice Scalia, with whom Justice Ginsburg, Justice Sotomayor, and Justice Kagan join, dissenting.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The California Court of Appeal in this case relied on jurisprudence from the California Supreme Court (adopted as well by other courts) to the effect that \xe2\x80\x9can anonymous and uncorroborated tip regarding a possibly intoxicated highway driver\xe2\x80\x9d provides without more the reasonable suspicion necessary to justify a stop. People v. Wells, 38 Cal. 4th l078, 1082, 136 P.\xc2\xa03d 810, 812, (2006). See also, e.g., United States v. Wheat, 278 F. 3d 722, 729\xe2\x80\x93730 (CA8 2001); State v. Walshire, 634 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 625, 626\xe2\x80\x93627, 630 (Iowa 2001). Today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion does not explicitly adopt such a departure from our normal\n \nFourth Amendment requirement that anonymous tips must be corroborated; it purports to adhere to our prior cases, such as Florida v. J.\xc2\xa0L.,\n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 266 (2000)\n, and Alabama v. White,\n\n496 U.\xc2\xa0S. 325 (1990)\n. Be not deceived.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Law enforcement agencies follow closely our judgments on matters such as this, and they will identify at once our new rule: So long as the caller identifies where the car is, anonymous claims of a single instance of possibly careless or reckless driving, called in to 911, will support a traffic stop. This is not my concept, and I am sure would not be the Framers\xe2\x80\x99, of a people secure from unreasonable searches and seizures. I would reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeal of California.

\n

I

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The California Highway Patrol in this case knew nothing about the tipster on whose word\xe2\x80\x94and that alone\xe2\x80\x94they seized Lorenzo and Jos\xc3\xa9 Prado Navarette. They did not know her name.[1] They did not know her phone number or address. They did not even know where she called from (she may have dialed in from a neighboring county, App. 33a\xe2\x80\x9334a).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The tipster said the truck had \xe2\x80\x9c[run her] off the roadway,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 36a, but the police had no reason to credit that charge and many reasons to doubt it, beginning with the peculiar fact that the accusation was anonymous. \xe2\x80\x9c[E]liminating accountability .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is ordinarily the very purpose of anonymity.\xe2\x80\x9d McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Comm\xe2\x80\x99n,\n\n514 U.\xc2\xa0S. 334,\n385 (1995)\n (Scalia, J., dissenting). The unnamed tipster \xe2\x80\x9ccan lie with impunity,\xe2\x80\x9d J.\xc2\xa0L., supra, at 275 (Kennedy, J., concurring). Anonymity is especially suspicious with respect to the call that is the subject of the present case. When does a victim complain to the police about an arguably criminal act (running the victim off the road) without giving his identity, so that he can accuse and testify when the culprit is caught?

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The question before us, the Court agrees, ante, at 8, is whether the \xe2\x80\x9ccontent of information possessed by police and its degree of reliability,\xe2\x80\x9d White, 496 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 330, gave the officers reasonable suspicion that the driver of the truck (Lorenzo) was committing an ongoing crime. When the only source of the government\xe2\x80\x99s information is an informant\xe2\x80\x99s tip, we ask whether the tip bears sufficient \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98indicia of reliability,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 328, to establish \xe2\x80\x9ca particularized and objective basis for suspecting the particular person stopped of criminal activity,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Cortez,\n\n449 U.\xc2\xa0S. 411\n\xe2\x80\x93418 (1981).

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The most extreme case, before this one, in which an anonymous tip was found to meet this standard was White, supra. There the reliability of the tip was established by the fact that it predicted the target\xe2\x80\x99s behavior in the finest detail\xe2\x80\x94a detail that could be known only by someone familiar with the target\xe2\x80\x99s business: She would, the tipster said, leave a particular apartment building, get into a brown Plymouth station wagon with a broken right tail light, and drive immediately to a particular motel. Id., at 327. Very few persons would have such intimate knowledge, and hence knowledge of the unobservable fact that the woman was carrying unlawful drugs was plausible. Id., at 332. Here the Court makes a big deal of the fact that the tipster was dead right about the fact that a silver Ford F-150 truck (license plate 8D94925) was traveling south on Highway 1 somewhere near mile marker 88. But everyone in the world who saw the car would have that knowledge, and anyone who wanted the car stopped would have to provide that information. Unlike the situation in White, that generally available knowledge in no way makes it plausible that the tipster saw the car run someone off the road.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court says, ante, at 5, that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]y reporting that she had been run off the road by a specific vehicle .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the caller necessarily claimed eyewitness knowledge.\xe2\x80\x9d So what? The issue is not how she claimed to know, but whether what she claimed to know was true. The claim to \xe2\x80\x9ceyewitness knowledge\xe2\x80\x9d of being run off the road supports not at all its veracity; nor does the amazing, mystifying prediction (so far short of what existed in White) that the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 truck would be heading south on Highway 1.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court finds \xe2\x80\x9creason to think\xe2\x80\x9d that the informant \xe2\x80\x9cwas telling the truth\xe2\x80\x9d in the fact that police observation confirmed that the truck had been driving near the spot at which, and at the approximate time at which, the tipster alleged she had been run off the road. Ante, at 6. According to the Court, the statement therefore qualifies as a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98present sense impression\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98excited utterance,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d kinds of hearsay that the law deems categorically admissible given their low likelihood of reflecting \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98deliberate or conscious misrepresentation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on Fed. Rule Evid. 803(1), 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p. 371). So, the Court says, we can fairly suppose that the accusation was true.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n No, we cannot. To begin with, it is questionable whether either the \xe2\x80\x9cpresent sense impression\xe2\x80\x9d or the \xe2\x80\x9cexcited utterance\xe2\x80\x9d exception to the hearsay rule applies here. The classic \xe2\x80\x9cpresent sense impression\xe2\x80\x9d is the recounting of an event that is occurring before the declarant\xe2\x80\x99s eyes, as the declarant is speaking (\xe2\x80\x9cI am watching the Hindenburg explode!\xe2\x80\x9d). See 2 K. Broun, McCormick on Evidence 362 (7th ed. 2013) (hereinafter McCormick). And the classic \xe2\x80\x9cexcited utterance\xe2\x80\x9d is a statement elicited, almost involuntarily, by the shock of what the declarant is immediately witnessing (\xe2\x80\x9cMy God, those people will be killed!\xe2\x80\x9d). See id., at 368\xe2\x80\x93369. It is the immediacy that gives the statement some credibility; the declarant has not had time to dissemble or embellish. There is no such immediacy here. The declarant had time to observe the license number of the offending vehicle, 8D94925 (a difficult task if she was forced off the road and the vehicle was speeding away), to bring her car to a halt, to copy down the observed license number (presumably), and (if she was using her own cell phone) to dial a call to the police from the stopped car. Plenty of time to dissemble or embellish.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Moreover, even assuming that less than true immediacy will suffice for these hearsay exceptions to apply, the tipster\xe2\x80\x99s statement would run into additional barriers to admissibility and acceptance. According to the very Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes from which the Court quotes, cases addressing an unidentified declarant\xe2\x80\x99s present sense impression \xe2\x80\x9cindicate hesitancy in upholding the statement alone as sufficient\xe2\x80\x9d proof of the reported event. 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App., at 371; see also 7 M. Graham, Handbook of Federal Evidence 19\xe2\x80\x9320 (7th ed. 2012). For excited utterances as well, the \xe2\x80\x9cknotty theoretical\xe2\x80\x9d question of statement-alone admissibility persists\xe2\x80\x94seemingly even when the declarant is known. 2 McCormick 368. \xe2\x80\x9cSome courts .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. have taken the position that an excited utterance is admissible only if other proof is presented which supports a finding of fact that the exciting event did occur. The issue has not yet been resolved under the Federal Rules.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 367\xe2\x80\x93368 (footnote omitted). It is even unsettled whether excited utterances of an unknown declarant are ever admissible. A leading treatise reports that \xe2\x80\x9cthe courts have been reluctant to admit such statements, principally because of uncertainty that foundational requirements, including the impact of the event on the declarant, have been satisfied.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 372. In sum, it is unlikely that the law of evidence would deem the mystery caller in this case \xe2\x80\x9cespecially trustworthy,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Finally, and least tenably, the Court says that another \xe2\x80\x9cindicator of veracity\xe2\x80\x9d is the anonymous tipster\xe2\x80\x99s mere \xe2\x80\x9cuse of the 911 emergency system,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 7. Because, you see, recent \xe2\x80\x9ctechnological and regulatory developments\xe2\x80\x9d suggest that the identities of unnamed 911 callers are increasingly less likely to remain unknown. Ibid. Indeed, the systems are able to identify \xe2\x80\x9cthe caller\xe2\x80\x99s geographic location with increasing specificity.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Amici disagree with this, see Brief for National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers et\xc2\xa0al. 8\xe2\x80\x9312, and the present case surely suggests that amici are right\xe2\x80\x94since we know neither the identity of the tipster nor even the county from which the call was made. But assuming the Court is right about the ease of identifying 911 callers, it proves absolutely nothing in the present case unless the anonymous caller was aware of that fact. \xe2\x80\x9cIt is the tipster\xe2\x80\x99s belief in anonymity, not its reality, that will control his behavior.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 10 (emphasis added). There is no reason to believe that your average anonymous 911 tipster is aware that 911 callers are readily identifiable.[2]

\n

II

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n All that has been said up to now assumes that the anonymous caller made, at least in effect, an accusation of drunken driving. But in fact she did not. She said that the petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 truck \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[r]an [me] off the roadway.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d App. 36a. That neither asserts that the driver was drunk nor even raises the likelihood that the driver was drunk. The most it conveys is that the truck did some apparently nontypical thing that forced the tipster off the roadway, whether partly or fully, temporarily or permanently. Who really knows what (if anything) happened? The truck might have swerved to avoid an animal, a pothole, or a jaywalking pedestrian.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n But let us assume the worst of the many possibilities: that it was a careless, reckless, or even intentional maneuver that forced the tipster off the road. Lorenzo might have been distracted by his use of a hands-free cell phone, see Strayer, Drews, & Crouch, A Comparison of the Cell Phone Driver and the Drunk Driver, 48 Human Factors 381, 388 (2006), or distracted by an intense sports argument with Jos\xc3\xa9, see D. Strayer et\xc2\xa0al., AAA Foundation for TrafficSafety, Measuring Cognitive Distraction in the Automobile 28 (June 2013), online at https://www.aaafoundation.org/sites/default/files/MeasuringCognitiveDistractions.pdf as visited Apr. 17, 2014, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). Or, indeed, he might have intentionally forced the tipster off the road because of some personal animus, or hostility to her \xe2\x80\x9cMake Love, Not War\xe2\x80\x9d bumper sticker. I fail to see how reasonable suspicion of a discrete instance of irregular or hazardous driving generates a reasonable suspicion of ongoing intoxicated driving. What proportion of the hundreds of thousands\xe2\x80\x94perhaps millions\xe2\x80\x94of careless, reckless, or intentional traffic violations committed each day is attributable to drunken drivers? I say 0.1 percent. I have no basis for that except my own guesswork. But unless the Court has some basis in reality to believe that the proportion is many orders of magnitude above that\xe2\x80\x94say 1 in 10 or at least 1 in 20\xe2\x80\x94it has no grounds for its unsupported assertion that the tipster\xe2\x80\x99s report in this case gave rise to a reasonable suspicion of drunken driving.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Bear in mind that that is the only basis for the stop that has been asserted in this litigation.[3] The stop required suspicion of an ongoing crime, not merely suspicion of having run someone off the road earlier. And driving while being a careless or reckless person, unlike driving while being a drunk person, is not an ongoing crime. In other words, in order to stop the petitioners the officers here not only had to assume without basis the accuracy of the anonymous accusation but also had to posit an unlikely reason (drunkenness) for the accused behavior.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n In sum, at the moment the police spotted the truck, it was more than merely \xe2\x80\x9cpossib[le]\xe2\x80\x9d that the petitioners were not committing an ongoing traffic crime. United States v. Arvizu,\n\n534 U.\xc2\xa0S. 266,\n277 (2002)\n (emphasis added). It was overwhelmingly likely that they were not.

\n

III

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n It gets worse. Not only, it turns out, did the police have no good reason at first to believe that Lorenzo was driving drunk, they had very good reason at last to know that he was not. The Court concludes that the tip, plus confirmation of the truck\xe2\x80\x99s location, produced reasonable suspicion that the truck not only had been but still was barreling dangerously and drunkenly down Highway 1. Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x9310. In fact, alas, it was not, and the officers knew it. They followed the truck for five minutes, presumably to see if it was being operated recklessly. And that was good police work. While the anonymous tip was not enough to support a stop for drunken driving under Terry v. Ohio,\n\n392 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (1968)\n, it was surely enough to counsel observation of the truck to see if it was driven by a drunken driver. But the pesky little detail left out of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s reason-able-suspicion equation is that, for the five minutes that the truck was being followed (five minutes is a long time), Lorenzo\xe2\x80\x99s driving was irreproachable. Had the officers witnessed the petitioners violate a single traffic law, they would have had cause to stop the truck, Whren v. United States,\n\n517 U.\xc2\xa0S. 806,\n810 (1996)\n, and this case would not be before us. And not only was the driving irreproachable, but the State offers no evidence to suggest that the petitioners even did anything suspicious, such as suddenly slowing down, pulling off to the side of the road, or turning somewhere to see whether they were being followed. Cf. Arvizu, supra, at 270\xe2\x80\x93271, 277 (concluding that an officer\xe2\x80\x99s suspicion of criminality was enhanced when the driver, upon seeing that he was being followed, \xe2\x80\x9cslowed dramatically,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cappeared stiff,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cseemed to be trying to pretend\xe2\x80\x9d that the patrol car was not there). Consequently, the tip\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion of ongoing drunken driving (if it could be deemed to suggest that) not only went uncorroborated; it was affirmatively undermined.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n A hypothetical variation on the facts of this case illustrates the point. Suppose an anonymous tipster reports that, while following near mile marker 88 a silver FordF-150, license plate 8D949925, traveling southbound on Highway 1, she saw in the truck\xe2\x80\x99s open cab several five-foot-tall stacks of what was unmistakably baled cannabis. Two minutes later, a highway patrolman spots the truck exactly where the tip suggested it would be, begins following it, but sees nothing in the truck\xe2\x80\x99s cab. It is not enough to say that the officer\xe2\x80\x99s observation merely failed to corroborate the tipster\xe2\x80\x99s accusation. It is more precise to say that the officer\xe2\x80\x99s observation discredited the informant\xe2\x80\x99s accusation: The crime was supposedly occurring (and would continue to occur) in plain view, but the police saw nothing. Similarly, here, the crime supposedly suggested by the tip was ongoing intoxicated driving, the hallmarks of which are many, readily identifiable, and difficult to conceal. That the officers witnessed nary a minor traffic violation nor any other \xe2\x80\x9csound indici[um] of drunk driving,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 8, strongly suggests that the suspected crime was not occurring after all. The tip\xe2\x80\x99s implication of continuing criminality, already weak, grew even weaker.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Resisting this line of reasoning, the Court curiously asserts that, since drunk drivers who see marked squad cars in their rearview mirrors may evade detection simply by driving \xe2\x80\x9cmore careful[ly],\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cabsence of additional suspicious conduct\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9chardly surprising\xe2\x80\x9d and thus largely irrelevant. Ante, at 10. Whether a drunk driver drives drunkenly, the Court seems to think, is up to him. That is not how I understand the influence of alcohol. I subscribe to the more traditional view that the dangers of intoxi-cated driving are the intoxicant\xe2\x80\x99s impairing effects on the body\xe2\x80\x94effects that no mere act of the will can resist. See, e.g., A. Dasgupta, The Science of Drinking: How Alcohol Affects Your Body and Mind 39 (explaining that the physi-ological effect of a blood alcohol content between 0.08 and 0.109, for example, is \xe2\x80\x9csever[e] impair[ment]\xe2\x80\x9d of \xe2\x80\x9c[b]alance, speech, hearing, and reaction time,\xe2\x80\x9d as well as one\xe2\x80\x99s general \xe2\x80\x9cability to drive a motor vehicle\xe2\x80\x9d). Consistent with this view, I take it as a fundamental premise of our intoxicated-driving laws that a driver soused enough to swerve once can be expected to swerve again\xe2\x80\x94and soon. If he does not, and if the only evidence of his first episode of irregular driving is a mere inference from an uncorroborated, vague, and nameless tip, then the\n \nFourth Amendment requires that he be left alone.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n The Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion serves up a freedom-destroying cocktail consisting of two parts patent falsity: (1)\xc2\xa0that anonymous 911 reports of traffic violations are reliable so long as they correctly identify a car and its location, and (2)\xc2\xa0that a single instance of careless or reckless driving necessarily supports a reasonable suspicion of drunkenness. All the malevolent 911 caller need do is assert a traffic violation, and the targeted car will be stopped, forcibly if necessary, by the police. If the driver turns out not to be drunk (which will almost always be the case), the caller need fear no consequences, even if 911 knows his identity. After all, he never alleged drunkenness, but merely called in a traffic violation\xe2\x80\x94and on that point his word is as good as his victim\xe2\x80\x99s.

\n

\n \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n Drunken driving is a serious matter, but so is the loss of our freedom to come and go as we please without police interference. To prevent and detect murder we do not allow searches without probable cause or targeted Terry stops without reasonable suspicion. We should not do so for drunken driving either. After today\xe2\x80\x99s opinion all of us on the road, and not just drug dealers, are at risk of having our freedom of movement curtailed on suspicion of drunkenness, based upon a phone tip, true or false, of a single instance of careless driving. I respectfully dissent.
