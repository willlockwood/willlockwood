\nSTOLT-NIELSEN S.&NBSP;A. V. ANIMALFEEDS INT\'L CORP.

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

STOLT-NIELSEN S.\xc2\xa0A. et\xc2\xa0al. v. ANIMALFEEDS INTERNATIONAL CORP.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x931198.\xe2\x80\x83Argued December 9, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided April 27, 2010

\n

Petitioner shipping companies serve much of the world market for parcel tankers\xe2\x80\x94seagoing vessels with compartments that are separately chartered to customers, such as respondent (AnimalFeeds), who wish to ship liquids in small quantities. AnimalFeeds ships its goods pursuant to a standard contract known in the maritime trade as a charter party. The charter party that AnimalFeeds uses contains an arbitration clause. AnimalFeeds brought a class action antitrust suit against petitioners for price fixing, and that suit was consolidated with similar suits brought by other charterers, including one in which the Second Circuit subsequently reversed a lower court ruling that the charterers\xe2\x80\x99 claims were not subject to arbitration. As a consequence, the parties in this case agree that they must arbitrate their antitrust dispute. AnimalFeeds sought arbitration on behalf of a class of purchasers of parcel tanker transportation services. The parties agreed to submit the question whether their arbitration agreement allowed for class arbitration to a panel of arbitrators, who would be bound by rules (Class Rules) developed by the American Arbitration Association following Green Tree Financial Corp. v. Bazzle, 539 U. S. 444. One Class Rule requires an arbitrator to determine whether an arbitration clause permits class arbitration. The parties selected an arbitration panel, designated New York City as the arbitration site, and stipulated that their arbitration clause was \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d on the class arbitration issue. The panel determined that the arbitration clause allowed for class arbitration, but the District Court vacated the award. It concluded that the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 award was made in \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest disregard\xe2\x80\x9d of the law, for had the arbitrators conducted a choice-of-law analysis, they would have applied the rule of federal maritime law requiring contracts to be interpreted in light of custom and usage. The Second Circuit reversed, holding that because petitioners had cited no authority applying a maritime rule of custom and usage against class arbitration, the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 decision was not in manifest disregard of maritime law; and that the arbitrators had not manifestly disregarded New York law, which had not established a rule against class arbitration.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Imposing class arbitration on parties who have not agreed to authorize class arbitration is inconsistent with the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA), 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 et seq. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The arbitration panel exceeded its powers by imposing its own policy choice instead of identifying and applying a rule of decision derived from the FAA or from maritime or New York law. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0An arbitration decision may be vacated under FAA \xc2\xa710(a)(4) on the ground that the arbitrator exceeded his powers, \xe2\x80\x9conly when [an] arbitrator strays from interpretation and application of the agreement and effectively \xe2\x80\x98dispense[s] his own brand of industrial justice,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Major League Baseball Players Assn. v. Garvey, 532 U. S. 504, 509 (per curiam), for an arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s task is to interpret and enforce a contract, not to make public policy. P. 7.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0The arbitration panel appears to have rested its decision on AnimalFeeds\xe2\x80\x99 public policy argument for permitting class arbitration under the charter party\xe2\x80\x99s arbitration clause. However, because the parties agreed that their agreement was \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d on the class arbitration issue, the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 proper task was to identify the rule of law governing in that situation. Instead, the panel based its decision on post-Bazzle arbitral decisions without mentioning whether they were based on a rule derived from the FAA or on maritime or New York law. Rather than inquiring whether those bodies of law contained a \xe2\x80\x9cdefault rule\xe2\x80\x9d permitting an arbitration clause to allow class arbitration absent express consent, the panel proceeded as if it had a common-law court\xe2\x80\x99s authority to develop what it viewed as the best rule for such a situation. Finding no reason to depart from its perception of a post-Bazzle consensus among arbitrators that class arbitration was beneficial in numerous settings, the panel simply imposed its own conception of sound policy and permitted class arbitration. The panel\xe2\x80\x99s few references to intent do not show that the panel did anything other than impose its own policy preference. Thus, under FAA \xc2\xa710(b), this Court must either \xe2\x80\x9cdirect a rehearing by the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x9d or decide the question originally referred to the panel. Because there can be only one possible outcome on the facts here, there is no need to direct a rehearing by the arbitrators. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Bazzle did not control resolution of the question whether the instant charter party permits arbitration to proceed on behalf of this class. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0No single rationale commanded a majority in Bazzle, which concerned contracts between a commercial lender and its customers that had an arbitration clause that did not expressly mention class arbitration. The plurality decided only the question whether the court or arbitrator should decide whether the contracts were \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d on the class arbitration issue, concluding that it was the arbitrator. Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 opinion bypassed that question, resting instead on his resolution of the questions of what standard the appropriate decisionmaker should apply in determining whether a contract allows class arbitration, and whether, under whatever standard is appropriate, class arbitration had been properly ordered in the case at hand. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0The Bazzle opinions appear to have baffled these parties at their arbitration proceeding. For one thing, the parties appear to have believed that Bazzle requires an arbitrator, not a court, to decide whether a contract permits class arbitration, a question addressed only by the plurality. That question need not be revisited here because the parties expressly assigned that issue to the arbitration panel, and no party argues that this assignment was impermissible. Both the parties and the arbitration panel also seem to have misunderstood Bazzle as establishing the standard to be applied in deciding whether class arbitration is permitted. However, Bazzle left that question open. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9317.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Imposing class arbitration here is inconsistent with the FAA. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(1)\xc2\xa0The FAA imposes rules of fundamental importance, including the basic precept that arbitration \xe2\x80\x9cis a matter of consent, not coercion.\xe2\x80\x9d Volt v. Board of Trustees of Leland Stanford Junior Univ., 489 U. S. 468, 479. The FAA requires that a \xe2\x80\x9cwritten provision in any maritime transaction\xe2\x80\x9d calling for the arbitration of a controversy arising out of such transaction \xe2\x80\x9cshall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract,\xe2\x80\x9d 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72, and permits a party to an arbitration agreement to petition a federal district court for an order directing that arbitration proceed \xe2\x80\x9cin the manner provided for in such agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa74. Thus, this Court has said that the FAA\xe2\x80\x99s central purpose is to ensure that \xe2\x80\x9cprivate agreements to arbitrate are enforced according to their terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Volt, 489 U. S., at 479. Whether enforcing an agreement to arbitrate or construing an arbitration clause, courts and arbitrators must \xe2\x80\x9cgive effect to the [parties\xe2\x80\x99] contractual rights and expectations.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The parties\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cintentions control,\xe2\x80\x9d Mitsubishi Motors Corp. v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth, Inc., 473 U. S. 614, 626, and the parties are \xe2\x80\x9cgenerally free to structure their arbitration agreements as they see fit,\xe2\x80\x9d Mastrobuono v. Shearson Lehman Hutton, Inc., 514 U. S. 52, 57. They may agree to limit the issues arbitrated and may agree on rules under which an arbitration will proceed. They may also specify with whom they choose to arbitrate their disputes. See EEOC v. Waffle House, Inc., 534 U. S. 279, 289. Pp.\xc2\xa017\xe2\x80\x9320.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(2)\xc2\xa0It follows that a party may not be compelled under the FAA to submit to class arbitration unless there is a contractual basis for concluding that the party agreed to do so. Here, the arbitration panel imposed class arbitration despite the parties\xe2\x80\x99 stipulation that they had reached \xe2\x80\x9cno agreement\xe2\x80\x9d on that issue. The panel\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion is fundamentally at war with the foundational FAA principle that arbitration is a matter of consent. It may be appropriate to presume that parties to an arbitration agreement implicitly authorize the arbitrator to adopt those procedures necessary to give effect to the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement. See Howsam v. Dean Witter Reynolds, Inc., 537 U. S. 79, 84. But an implicit agreement to authorize class action arbitration is not a term that the arbitrator may infer solely from the fact of an agreement to arbitrate. The differences between simple bilateral and complex class action arbitration are too great for such a presumption. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

548 F.\xc2\xa03d 85, reversed and remanded.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari in this case to decide whether imposing class arbitration on parties whose arbitration clauses are \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d on that issue is consistent with the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA), 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 et seq.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners are shipping companies that serve a large share of the world market for parcel tankers\xe2\x80\x94seagoing vessels with compartments that are separately chartered to customers wishing to ship liquids in small quantities. One of those customers is AnimalFeeds International Corp. (hereinafter AnimalFeeds), which supplies raw ingredients, such as fish oil, to animal-feed producers around the world. AnimalFeeds ships its goods pursuant to a standard contract known in the maritime trade as a charter party.[Footnote 1] Numerous charter parties are in regular use, and the charter party that AnimalFeeds uses is known as the \xe2\x80\x9cVegoilvoy\xe2\x80\x9d charter party. Petitioners assert, without contradiction, that charterers like AnimalFeeds, or their agents\xe2\x80\x94not the shipowners\xe2\x80\x94typically select the particular charter party that governs their shipments. Accord, Trowbridge, Admiralty Law Institute: Symposium on Charter Parties: The History, Development, and Characteristics of the Charter Concept, 49 Tulane L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 743, 753 (1975) (\xe2\x80\x9cVoyage charter parties are highly standardized, with many commodities and charterers having their own specialized forms\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Adopted in 1950, the Vegoilvoy charter party contains the following arbitration clause:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cArbitration. Any dispute arising from the making, performance or termination of this Charter Party shall be settled in New York, Owner and Charterer each appointing an arbitrator, who shall be a merchant, broker or individual experienced in the shipping business; the two thus chosen, if they cannot agree, shall nominate a third arbitrator who shall be an Admiralty lawyer. Such arbitration shall be conducted in conformity with the provisions and procedure of the United States Arbitration Act [i.e., the FAA], and a judgment of the Court shall be entered upon any award made by said arbitrator.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 69a.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 2003, a Department of Justice criminal investigation revealed that petitioners were engaging in an illegal price-fixing conspiracy. When AnimalFeeds learned of this, it brought a putative class action against petitioners in the District Court for the Eastern District of Pennsylvania, asserting antitrust claims for supracompetitive prices that petitioners allegedly charged their customers over a period of several years.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Other charterers brought similar suits. In one of these, the District Court for the District of Connecticut held that the charterers\xe2\x80\x99 claims were not subject to arbitration under the applicable arbitration clause, but the Second Circuit reversed. See JLM Industries, Inc. v. Stolt-Nielsen S.\xc2\xa0A., 387 F.\xc2\xa03d 163, 183 (2004). While that appeal was pending, the Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation ordered the consolidation of then-pending actions against petitioners, including AnimalFeeds\xe2\x80\x99 action, in the District of Connecticut. See In\xc2\xa0re Parcel Tanker Shipping Services Antitrust Litigation, 296 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1370, 1371, and n.\xc2\xa01 (JPML 2003). The parties agree that as a consequence of these judgments and orders, AnimalFeeds and petitioners must arbitrate their antitrust dispute.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 2005, AnimalFeeds served petitioners with a demand for class arbitration, designating New York City as the place of arbitration and seeking to represent a class of \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll direct purchasers of parcel tanker transportation services globally for bulk liquid chemicals, edible oils, acids, and other specialty liquids from [petitioners] at any time during the period from August 1, 1998, to November 30, 2002.\xe2\x80\x9d 548 F.\xc2\xa03d 85, 87 (CA2 2008) (internal quotation marks omitted). The parties entered into a supplemental agreement providing for the question of class arbitration to be submitted to a panel of three arbitrators who were to \xe2\x80\x9cfollow and be bound by Rules 3 through 7 of the American Arbitration Association\xe2\x80\x99s Supplementary Rules for Class Arbitrations (as effective Oct. 8, 2003).\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 59a. These rules (hereinafter Class Rules) were developed by the American Arbitration Association (AAA) after our decision in Green Tree Financial Corp. v. Bazzle, 539 U. S. 444 (2003), and Class Rule 3, in accordance with the plurality opinion in that case, requires an arbitrator, as a threshold matter, to determine \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the applicable arbitration clause permits the arbitration to proceed on behalf of or against a class.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 56a.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The parties selected a panel of arbitrators and stipulated that the arbitration clause was \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d with respect to class arbitration. Counsel for AnimalFeeds explained to the arbitration panel that the term \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d did not simply mean that the clause made no express reference to class arbitration. Rather, he said, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ll the parties agree that when a contract is silent on an issue there\xe2\x80\x99s been no agreement that has been reached on that issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Id.,\nat 77a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After hearing argument and evidence, including testimony from petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 experts regarding arbitration customs and usage in the maritime trade, the arbitrators concluded that the arbitration clause allowed for class arbitration. They found persuasive the fact that other arbitrators ruling after Bazzle had construed \xe2\x80\x9ca wide variety of clauses in a wide variety of settings as allowing for class arbitration,\xe2\x80\x9d but the panel acknowledged that none of these decisions was \xe2\x80\x9cexactly comparable\xe2\x80\x9d to the present dispute. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 49a\xe2\x80\x9350a. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 expert evidence did not show an \xe2\x80\x9cinten[t] to preclude class arbitration,\xe2\x80\x9d the arbitrators reasoned, and petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument would leave \xe2\x80\x9cno basis for a class action absent express agreement among all parties and the putative class members.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 51a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The arbitrators stayed the proceeding to allow the parties to seek judicial review, and petitioners filed an application to vacate the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 award in the District Court for the Southern District of New York. See 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa710(a)(4) (authorizing a district court to \xe2\x80\x9cmake an order vacating the award upon the application of any party to the arbitration . . . where the arbitrators exceeded their powers\xe2\x80\x9d); Petition to Vacate Arbitration Award, No. 1:06\xe2\x80\x93CV\xe2\x80\x9300420\xe2\x80\x93JSR (SDNY) in App. in No. 06\xe2\x80\x933474\xe2\x80\x93cv (CA2), p. A\xe2\x80\x9317, \xc2\xb616 (citing \xc2\xa710(a)(4) as a ground for vacatur of the award); see also id., at A\xe2\x80\x9315 to A\xe2\x80\x9316, \xc2\xb69 (invoking the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction under 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7203 and 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71331 and 1333). The District Court vacated the award, concluding that the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 decision was made in \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest disregard\xe2\x80\x9d of the law insofar as the arbitrators failed to conduct a choice-of-law analysis. 435 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 382, 384\xe2\x80\x93385 (SDNY 2006). See Wilko v. Swan, 346 U. S. 427, 436\xe2\x80\x93437 (1953) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he interpretations of the law by the arbitrators in contrast to manifest disregard are not subject, in the federal courts, to judicial review for error in interpretation\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Petition to Vacate Arbitration Award, supra, at A\xe2\x80\x9317, \xc2\xb617 (alleging that the arbitration panel \xe2\x80\x9cmanifestly disregarded the law\xe2\x80\x9d). Had such an analysis been conducted, the District Court held, the arbitrators would have applied the rule of federal maritime law requiring that contracts be interpreted in light of custom and usage. 435 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 385\xe2\x80\x93386.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0AnimalFeeds appealed to the Court of Appeals, which reversed. See 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa716(a)(1)(E) (\xe2\x80\x9cAn appeal may be taken from . . . an order . . . vacating an award\xe2\x80\x9d). As an initial matter, the Court of Appeals held that the \xe2\x80\x9cmanifest disregard\xe2\x80\x9d standard survived our decision in Hall Street Associates, L. L. C. v. Mattel, Inc., 552 U. S. 576 (2008), as a \xe2\x80\x9cjudicial gloss\xe2\x80\x9d on the enumerated grounds for vacatur of arbitration awards under 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa710. 548 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 94. Nonetheless, the Court of Appeals concluded that, because petitioners had cited no authority applying a federal maritime rule of custom and usage against class arbitration, the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 decision was not in manifest disregard of federal maritime law. Id., at 97\xe2\x80\x9398. Nor had the arbitrators manifestly disregarded New York law, the Court of Appeals continued, since nothing in New York case law established a rule against class arbitration. Id., at 98\xe2\x80\x9399.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari. 557 U. S. ___ (2009).[Footnote 2]

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioners contend that the decision of the arbitration panel must be vacated, but in order to obtain that relief, they must clear a high hurdle. It is not enough for petitioners to show that the panel committed an error\xe2\x80\x94or even a serious error. See Eastern Associated Coal Corp. v. Mine Workers, 531 U. S. 57, 62 (2000); Paperworkers v. Misco, Inc., 484 U. S. 29, 38 (1987). \xe2\x80\x9cIt is only when [an] arbitrator strays from interpretation and application of the agreement and effectively \xe2\x80\x98dispense[s] his own brand of industrial justice\xe2\x80\x99 that his decision may be unenforceable.\xe2\x80\x9d Major League Baseball Players Assn. v. Garvey, 532 U. S. 504, 509 (2001) (per curiam) (quoting Steelworkers v. Enterprise Wheel & Car Corp., 363 U. S. 593, 597 (1960)). In that situation, an arbitration decision may be vacated under \xc2\xa710(a)(4) of the FAA on the ground that the arbitrator \xe2\x80\x9cexceeded [his] powers,\xe2\x80\x9d for the task of an arbitrator is to interpret and enforce a contract, not to make public policy. In this case, we must conclude that what the arbitration panel did was simply to impose its own view of sound policy regarding class arbitration.[Footnote 3]

\n

B

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In its memorandum of law filed in the arbitration proceedings, AnimalFeeds made three arguments in support of construing the arbitration clause to permit class arbitration:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe parties\xe2\x80\x99 arbitration clause should be construed to allow class arbitration because (a) the clause is silent on the issue of class treatment and, without express prohibition, class arbitration is permitted under Bazzle; (b) the clause should be construed to permit class arbitration as a matter of public policy; and (c) the clause would be unconscionable and unenforceable if it forbade class arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d App. in No. 06\xe2\x80\x933474\xe2\x80\x93cv (CA2), at A\xe2\x80\x93308 to A\xe2\x80\x93309 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The arbitrators expressly rejected AnimalFeeds\xe2\x80\x99 first argument, see App. to Pet. for Cert. 49a, and said nothing about the third. Instead, the panel appears to have rested its decision on AnimalFeeds\xe2\x80\x99 public policy argument. Because the parties agreed their agreement was \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d in the sense that they had not reached any agreement on the issue of class arbitration, the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 proper task was to identify the rule of law that governs in that situation. Had they engaged in that undertaking, they presumably would have looked either to the FAA itself or to one of the two bodies of law that the parties claimed were governing, i.e., either federal maritime law or New York law. But the panel did not consider whether the FAA provides the rule of decision in such a situation; nor did the panel attempt to determine what rule would govern under either maritime or New York law in the case of a \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d contract. Instead, the panel based its decision on post-Bazzle arbitral decisions that \xe2\x80\x9cconstrued a wide variety of clauses in a wide variety of settings as allowing for class arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 49a\xe2\x80\x9350a. The panel did not mention whether any of these decisions were based on a rule derived from the FAA or on maritime or New York law.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rather than inquiring whether the FAA, maritime law, or New York law contains a \xe2\x80\x9cdefault rule\xe2\x80\x9d under which an arbitration clause is construed as allowing class arbitration in the absence of express consent, the panel proceeded as if it had the authority of a common-law court to develop what it viewed as the best rule to be applied in such a situation. Perceiving a post-Bazzle consensus among arbitrators that class arbitration is beneficial in \xe2\x80\x9ca wide variety of settings,\xe2\x80\x9d the panel considered only whether there was any good reason not to follow that consensus in this case. App. to Pet. for Cert. 49a\xe2\x80\x9350a. The panel was not persuaded by \xe2\x80\x9ccourt cases denying consolidation of arbitrations,\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 5] by undisputed evidence that the Vegoilvoy charter party had \xe2\x80\x9cnever been the basis of a class action,\xe2\x80\x9d or by expert opinion that \xe2\x80\x9csophisticated, multinational commercial parties of the type that are sought to be included in the class would never intend that the arbitration clauses would permit a class arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 6] Id., at 50a\xe2\x80\x9351a. Accordingly, finding no convincing ground for departing from the post-Bazzle arbitral consensus, the panel held that class arbitration was permitted in this case. App. to Pet. for Cert. 52a. The conclusion is inescapable that the panel simply imposed its own conception of sound policy.[Footnote 7]

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is true that the panel opinion makes a few references to intent, but none of these shows that the panel did anything other than impose its own policy preference. The opinion states that, under Bazzle, \xe2\x80\x9carbitrators must look to the language of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement to ascertain the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intention whether they intended to permit or to preclude class action,\xe2\x80\x9d and the panel added that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his is also consistent with New York law.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 49a. But the panel had no occasion to \xe2\x80\x9cascertain the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intention\xe2\x80\x9d in the present case because the parties were in complete agreement regarding their intent. In the very next sentence after the one quoted above, the panel acknowledged that the parties in this case agreed that the Vegoilvoy charter party was \xe2\x80\x9csilent on whether [it] permit[ted] or preclude[d] class arbitration,\xe2\x80\x9d but that the charter party was \xe2\x80\x9cnot ambiguous so as to call for parol evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. This stipulation left no room for an inquiry regarding the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent, and any inquiry into that settled question would have been outside the panel\xe2\x80\x99s assigned task.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The panel also commented on the breadth of the language in the Vegoilvoy charter party, see id., at 50a, but since the only task that was left for the panel, in light of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 stipulation, was to identify the governing rule applicable in a case in which neither the language of the contract nor any other evidence established that the parties had reached any agreement on the question of class arbitration, the particular wording of the charter party was quite beside the point.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In sum, instead of identifying and applying a rule of decision derived from the FAA or either maritime or New York law, the arbitration panel imposed its own policy choice and thus exceeded its powers. As a result, under \xc2\xa710(b) of the FAA, we must either \xe2\x80\x9cdirect a rehearing by the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x9d or decide the question that was originally referred to the panel. Because we conclude that there can be only one possible outcome on the facts before us, we see no need to direct a rehearing by the arbitrators.

\n

III

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The arbitration panel thought that Bazzle \xe2\x80\x9ccontrolled\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cresolution\xe2\x80\x9d of the question whether the Vegoilvoy charter party \xe2\x80\x9cpermit[s] this arbitration to proceed on behalf of a class,\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 48a\xe2\x80\x9349a, but that understanding was incorrect.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bazzle concerned contracts between a commercial lender (Green Tree) and its customers. These contracts con-\ntained an arbitration clause but did not expressly mention class arbitration. Nevertheless, an arbitrator conducted class arbitration proceedings and entered awards for the customers.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The South Carolina Supreme Court affirmed the awards. Bazzle v. Green Tree Financial Corp., 351 S.\xc2\xa0C. 244, 569 S. E. 2d 349 (2002). After discussing both Seventh Circuit precedent holding that a court lacks authority to order classwide arbitration under \xc2\xa74 of the FAA, see Champ v. Siegel Trading Co., 55 F. 3d 269 (1995), and conflicting California precedent, see Keating v. Superior Court of Alameda Cty., 31 Cal. 3d 584, 645 P.\xc2\xa02d 1192 (1982), the State Supreme Court elected to follow the California approach, which it characterized as permitting a trial court to \xe2\x80\x9corder class-wide arbitration under adhesive but enforceable franchise contracts,\xe2\x80\x9d 351 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 259, 266, 569 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 357, 360. Under this approach, the South Carolina court observed, a trial judge must \xe2\x80\x9c[b]alanc[e] the potential inequities and inefficiencies\xe2\x80\x9d of requiring each aggrieved party to proceed on an individual basis against \xe2\x80\x9cresulting prejudice to the drafting party\xe2\x80\x9d and should take into account factors such as \xe2\x80\x9cefficiency\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cequity.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 260, and n.\xc2\xa015, 569 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 357, and n.\xc2\xa015.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Applying these standards to the case before it, the South Carolina Supreme Court found that the arbitration clause in the Green Tree contracts was \xe2\x80\x9csilent regarding class-wide arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 263, 569 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 359 (emphasis deleted). The Court described its holding as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e \xe2\x80\xa6 hold that class-wide arbitration may be ordered when the arbitration agreement is silent if it would serve efficiency and equity, and would not result in prejudice. If we enforced a mandatory, adhesive arbitration clause, but prohibited class actions in arbitration where the agreement is silent, the drafting party could effectively prevent class actions against\nit without having to say it was doing so in the\nagreement.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 266, 569 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 360 (footnote omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When Bazzle reached this Court, no single rationale commanded a majority. The opinions of the Justices who joined the judgment\xe2\x80\x94that is, the plurality opinion and Justice Stevens\xe2\x80\x99 opinion\xe2\x80\x94collectively addressed three separate questions. The first was which decision maker (court or arbitrator) should decide whether the contracts in question were \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d on the issue of class arbitration. The second was what standard the appropriate decision maker should apply in determining whether a contract allows class arbitration. (For example, does the FAA entirely preclude class arbitration? Does the FAA permit class arbitration only under limited circumstances, such as when the contract expressly so provides? Or is this question left entirely to state law?) The final question was whether, under whatever standard is appropriate, class arbitration had been properly ordered in the case\nat hand.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality opinion decided only the first question, concluding that the arbitrator and not a court should decide whether the contracts were indeed \xe2\x80\x9csilent\xe2\x80\x9d on the issue of class arbitration. The plurality noted that, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n certain limited circumstances,\xe2\x80\x9d involving \xe2\x80\x9cgateway matters, such as whether the parties have a valid arbitration agreement at all or whether a concededly binding arbitration clause applies to a certain type of controversy,\xe2\x80\x9d it is assumed \xe2\x80\x9cthat the parties intended courts, not arbitrators,\xe2\x80\x9d to make the decision. 539 U. S., at 452. But the plurality opined that the question whether a contract with an arbitration clause forbids class arbitration \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not fall into this narrow exception.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The plurality therefore concluded that the decision of the State Supreme Court should be vacated and that the case should be remanded for a decision by the arbitrator on the question whether the contracts were indeed \xe2\x80\x9csilent.\xe2\x80\x9d The plurality did not decide either the second or the third question noted above.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens concurred in the judgment vacating and remanding because otherwise there would have been \xe2\x80\x9cno controlling judgment of the Court,\xe2\x80\x9d but he did not endorse the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s rationale. Id., at 455 (opinion concurring in judgment and dissenting in part). He did not take a definitive position on the first question, stating only that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]rguably the interpretation of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement should have been made in the first instance by the arbitrator.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). But because he did not believe that Green Tree had raised the question of the appropriate decision maker, he preferred not to reach that question and, instead, would have affirmed the decision of the State Supreme Court on the ground that \xe2\x80\x9cthe decision to conduct a class-action arbitration was correct as a matter of law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Accordingly, his analysis bypassed the first question noted above and rested instead on his resolution of the second and third questions. Thus, Bazzle did not yield a majority decision on any of the three questions.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unfortunately, the opinions in Bazzle appear to have baffled the parties in this case at the time of the arbitration proceeding. For one thing, the parties appear to have believed that the judgment in Bazzle requires an arbitrator, not a court, to decide whether a contract permits class arbitration. See App. 89a (transcript of argument before arbitration panel) (counsel for Stolt-Nielsen states: \xe2\x80\x9cWhat [Bazzle] says is that the contract interpretation issue is left up to the arbitrator, that\xe2\x80\x99s the rule in [Bazzle]\xe2\x80\x9d). In fact, however, only the plurality decided that question. But we need not revisit that question here because the parties\xe2\x80\x99 supplemental agreement expressly assigned this issue to the arbitration panel, and no party argues that this assignment was impermissible.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Unfortunately, however, both the parties and the arbitration panel seem to have misunderstood Bazzle in another respect, namely, that it established the standard to be applied by a decision maker in determining whether a contract may permissibly be interpreted to allow class arbitration. The arbitration panel began its discussion by stating that the parties \xe2\x80\x9cdiffer regarding the rule of interpretation to be gleaned from [the Bazzle] decision.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 49a (emphasis added). The panel continued:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cClaimants argue that Bazzle requires clear language that forbids class arbitration in order to bar a class action. The Panel, however, agrees with Respondents that the test is a more general one\xe2\x80\x94arbitrators must look to the language of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement to ascertain the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intention whether they intended to permit or to preclude class action.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\nAs we have explained, however, Bazzle did not establish the rule to be applied in deciding whether class arbitration is permitted.[Footnote 8] The decision in Bazzle left that question open, and we turn to it now.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While the interpretation of an arbitration agreement is generally a matter of state law, see Arthur Andersen LLP v. Carlisle, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 6); Perry v. Thomas, 482 U. S. 483, 493, n. 9 (1987), the FAA imposes certain rules of fundamental importance, including the basic precept that arbitration \xe2\x80\x9cis a matter of consent, not coercion,\xe2\x80\x9d Volt Information Sciences, Inc. v. Board of\nTrustees of Leland Stanford Junior Univ., 489 U. S. 468, 479 (1989).

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1925, Congress enacted the United States Arbitration Act, as the FAA was formerly known, for the express purpose of making \xe2\x80\x9cvalid and enforceable written provisions or agreements for arbitration of disputes arising out of contracts, maritime transactions, or commerce among the States or Territories or with foreign nations.\xe2\x80\x9d 43 Stat. 883. Reenacted and codified in 1947, see 61 Stat. 669,[Footnote 9] the FAA provides, in pertinent part, that a \xe2\x80\x9cwritten provision in any maritime transaction\xe2\x80\x9d calling for the arbitration of a controversy arising out of such transaction \xe2\x80\x9cshall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract,\xe2\x80\x9d 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72. Under the FAA, a party to an arbitration agreement may petition a United States district court for an order directing that \xe2\x80\x9carbitration proceed in the manner provided for in such agreement.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa74. Consistent with these provisions, we have said on numerous occasions that the central or \xe2\x80\x9cprimary\xe2\x80\x9d purpose of the FAA is to ensure that \xe2\x80\x9cprivate agreements to arbitrate are enforced according to their terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Volt, supra, at 479; Mastrobuono v. Shearson Lehman Hutton, Inc., 514 U. S. 52, 57, 58 (1995); see also Doctor\xe2\x80\x99s Associates, Inc. v. Casarotto, 517 U. S. 681, 688 (1996). See generally 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa74.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whether enforcing an agreement to arbitrate or construing an arbitration clause, courts and arbitrators must \xe2\x80\x9cgive effect to the contractual rights and expectations of the parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Volt, supra, at 479. In this endeavor, \xe2\x80\x9cas with any other contract, the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intentions control.\xe2\x80\x9d Mitsubishi Motors Corp. v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth, Inc., 473 U. S. 614, 626 (1985). This is because an arbitrator derives his or her powers from the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement to forgo the legal process and submit their disputes to private dispute resolution. See AT&T Technologies, Inc. v. Communications Workers, 475 U. S. 643, 648\xe2\x80\x93649 (1986) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]rbitrators derive their authority to resolve disputes only because the parties have agreed in advance to submit such grievances to arbitration\xe2\x80\x9d); Mitsubishi Motors, supra, at 628 (\xe2\x80\x9cBy agreeing to arbitrate \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0, [a party] trades the procedures and opportunity for review of the courtroom for the simplicity, informality, and expedition of arbitration\xe2\x80\x9d); see also Steelworkers v. Warrior & Gulf Nav. Co., 363 U. S. 574, 581 (1960) (an arbitrator \xe2\x80\x9chas no general charter to administer justice for a community which transcends the parties\xe2\x80\x9d but rather is \xe2\x80\x9cpart of a system of self-government created by and confined to the parties\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Underscoring the consensual nature of private dispute resolution, we have held that parties are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98generally free to structure their arbitration agreements as they see fit.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Mastrobuono, supra, at 57; see also AT&T Technologies, supra, at 648\xe2\x80\x93649. For example, we have held that parties may agree to limit the issues they choose to arbitrate, see Mitsubishi Motors, supra, at 628, and may agree on rules under which any arbitration will proceed, Volt, supra, at 479. They may choose who will resolve specific disputes. E.g., App. 30a; Alexander v. Gardner-Denver Co., 415 U. S. 36, 57 (1974); Burchell v. Marsh, 17 How. 344, 349 (1855); see also International Produce, Inc. v. A/S Rosshavet, 638 F.\xc2\xa02d 548, 552 (CA2) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe most sought-after arbitrators are those who are prominent and experienced members of the specific business community in which the dispute to be arbitrated arose\xe2\x80\x9d), cert. denied, 451 U. S. 1017 (1981).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We think it is also clear from our precedents and the contractual nature of arbitration that parties may specify with whom they choose to arbitrate their disputes. See EEOC v. Waffle House, Inc., 534 U. S. 279, 289 (2002) (\xe2\x80\x9c[N]othing in the [FAA] authorizes a court to compel arbitration of any issues, or by any parties, that are not already covered in the agreement\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); Moses H. Cone Memorial Hospital v. Mercury Constr. Corp., 460 U. S. 1, 20 (1983) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]n arbitration agreement must be enforced notwithstanding the presence of other persons who are parties to the underlying dispute but not to the arbitration agreement\xe2\x80\x9d); Steelworkers, supra, at 581 (an arbitrator \xe2\x80\x9chas no general charter to administer justice for a community which transcends the parties\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); accord, First Options of Chicago, Inc. v. Kaplan, 514 U. S. 938, 943 (1995) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A]rbitration is simply a matter of contract between the parties; it is a way to resolve those disputes\xe2\x80\x94but only those disputes\xe2\x80\x94that the parties have agreed to submit to arbitration\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). It falls to courts and arbitrators to give effect to these contractual limitations, and when doing so, courts and arbitrators must not lose sight of the purpose of the exercise: to give effect to the intent of the parties. Volt, 489 U. S., at 479.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From these principles, it follows that a party may not be compelled under the FAA to submit to class arbitration unless there is a contractual basis for concluding that the party agreed to do so. In this case, however, the arbitration panel imposed class arbitration even though the parties concurred that they had reached \xe2\x80\x9cno agreement\xe2\x80\x9d on that issue, see App. 77a. The critical point, in the view of the arbitration panel, was that petitioners did not \xe2\x80\x9cestablish that the parties to the charter agreements intended to preclude class arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 51a. Even though the parties are sophisticated business entities, even though there is no tradition of class arbitration under maritime law, and even though AnimalFeeds does not dispute that it is customary for the shipper to choose the charter party that is used for a particular shipment, the panel regarded the agreement\xe2\x80\x99s silence on the question of class arbitration as dispositive. The panel\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion is fundamentally at war with the foundational FAA principle that arbitration is a matter of consent.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In certain contexts, it is appropriate to presume that parties that enter into an arbitration agreement implicitly authorize the arbitrator to adopt such procedures as are necessary to give effect to the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement. Thus, we have said that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cprocedural\xe2\x80\x9d questions which grow out of the dispute and bear on its final disposition\xe2\x80\x99 are presumptively not for the judge, but for an arbitrator, to decide.\xe2\x80\x9d Howsam v. Dean Witter Reynolds, Inc., 537 U. S. 79, 84 (2002) (quoting John Wiley & Sons, Inc. v. Livingston, 376 U. S. 543, 557 (1964)). This recognition is grounded in the background principle that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen the parties to a bargain sufficiently defined to be a contract have not agreed with respect to a term which is essential to a determination of their rights and duties, a term which is reasonable in the circumstances is supplied by the court.\xe2\x80\x9d Restatement (Second) of Contracts \xc2\xa7204 (1979).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0An implicit agreement to authorize class-action arbitration, however, is not a term that the arbitrator may infer solely from the fact of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement to arbitrate. This is so because class-action arbitration changes the nature of arbitration to such a degree that it cannot be presumed the parties consented to it by simply agreeing to submit their disputes to an arbitrator. In bilateral arbitration, parties forgo the procedural rigor and appellate review of the courts in order to realize the benefits of private dispute resolution: lower costs, greater efficiency and speed, and the ability to choose expert adjudicators to resolve specialized disputes. See Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp., 500 U. S. 20, 31 (1991); Mitsubishi Motors, 473 U. S., at 628; see also 14 Penn Plaza LLC v. Pyett, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip. op., at 7\xe2\x80\x938) (\xe2\x80\x9cParties generally favor arbitration precisely because of the economics of dispute resolution\xe2\x80\x9d (citing Circuit City Stores, Inc. v. Adams, 532 U. S. 105, 123 (2001)); Gardner-Denver, supra, at 57 (\xe2\x80\x9cParties usually choose an arbitrator because they trust his knowledge and judgment concerning the demands and norms of industrial relations\xe2\x80\x9d). But the relative benefits of class-action arbitration are much less assured, giving reason to doubt the parties\xe2\x80\x99 mutual consent to resolve disputes through class-wide arbitration. Cf. First Options, supra, at 945 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cone can understand why courts might hesitate to interpret silence or ambiguity on the \xe2\x80\x98who should decide arbitrability\xe2\x80\x99 point as giving the arbitrators that power, for doing so might too often force unwilling parties to arbitrate\xe2\x80\x9d contrary to their expectations).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consider just some of the fundamental changes brought about by the shift from bilateral arbitration to class-action arbitration. An arbitrator chosen according to an agreed-upon procedure, see, e.g., supra, at 2, no longer resolves a single dispute between the parties to a single agreement, but instead resolves many disputes between hundreds or perhaps even thousands of parties. See App. 86a (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]e believe domestic class members could be in the hundreds\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]here could be class members that ship to and from the U. S. who are not domestic who we think would be covered\xe2\x80\x9d); see also, e.g., Bazzle, 351 S.\xc2\xa0C., at 251, 569 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d, at 352\xe2\x80\x93353 (involving a class of 1,899 individuals that was awarded damages, fees, and costs of more than $14 million by a single arbitrator). Under the Class Rules, \xe2\x80\x9cthe presumption of privacy and confidentiality\xe2\x80\x9d that applies in many bilateral arbitrations \xe2\x80\x9cshall not apply in class arbitrations,\xe2\x80\x9d see Addendum to Brief for American Arbitration Association as Amicus Curiae 10a (Class Rule 9(a)), thus potentially frustrating the parties\xe2\x80\x99 assumptions when they agreed to arbitrate. The arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s award no longer purports to bind just the parties to a single arbitration agreement, but adjudicates the rights of absent parties as well. Cf. Ortiz v. Fibreboard Corp., 527 U. S. 815, 846 (1999) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cthe burden of justification rests on the exception\xe2\x80\x9d to the general rule that \xe2\x80\x9cone is not bound by a judgment in personam in a litigation in which he is not designated as a party or to which he has not been made a party by service of process\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). And the commercial stakes of class-action arbitration are comparable to those of class-action litigation, cf. App. in No. 06\xe2\x80\x933474\xe2\x80\x93cv (CA2), at A\xe2\x80\x9377, A\xe2\x80\x9379, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb630, 31, 40, even though the scope of judicial review is much more limited, see Hall Street, 552 U. S., at 588. We think that the differences between bilateral and class-action arbitration are too great for arbitrators to presume, consistent with their limited powers under the FAA, that the parties\xe2\x80\x99 mere silence on the issue of class-action arbitration constitutes consent to resolve their disputes in class proceedings.[Footnote 10]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dissent minimizes these crucial differences by characterizing the question before the arbitrators as being merely what \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural mode\xe2\x80\x9d was available to present AnimalFeeds\xe2\x80\x99 claims. Post, at 9. If the question were that simple, there would be no need to consider the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent with respect to class arbitration. See Howsam, supra, at 84 (committing \xe2\x80\x9cprocedural questions\xe2\x80\x9d presumptively to the arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s discretion (internal quotation marks omitted)). But the FAA requires more. Contrary to the dissent, but consistent with our precedents emphasizing the consensual basis of arbitration, we see the question as being whether the parties agreed to authorize class arbitration. Here, where the parties stipulated that there was \xe2\x80\x9cno agreement\xe2\x80\x9d on this question, it follows that the parties cannot be compelled to submit their dispute to class arbitration.

\n

V

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For these reasons, the judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

See National Park Hospitality Assn., supra, at 808 (\xe2\x80\x9c[E]ven in a case raising only prudential concerns, the question of ripeness may be considered on a court\xe2\x80\x99s own motion\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When an arbitration clause is silent on the question, may arbitration proceed on behalf of a class? The Court prematurely takes up that important question and, indulging in de novo review, overturns the ruling of experienced arbitrators.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court errs in addressing an issue not ripe for judicial review. Compounding that error, the Court substitutes its judgment for that of the decisionmakers chosen by the parties. I would dismiss the petition as improvidently granted.[Footnote 2] Were I to reach the merits, I would adhere to the strict limitations the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA), 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 et seq., places on judicial review of arbitral awards. \xc2\xa710. Accordingly, I would affirm the judgment of the Second Circuit, which rejected petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 plea for vacation of the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 decision.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As the Court recounts, ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x936, this case was launched as a class action in federal court charging named ocean carriers (collectively, Stolt-Nielsen) with a conspiracy to extract supracompetitive prices from their customers (buyers of ocean-transportation services). That court action terminated when the Second Circuit held, first, that the parties\xe2\x80\x99 transactions were governed by contracts (charter parties) with enforceable arbitration clauses, and second, that the antitrust claims were arbitrable. JLM Industries, Inc. v. Stolt-Nielsen S.\xc2\xa0A., 387 F.\xc2\xa03d 163, 175, 181 (2004).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Cargo-shipper AnimalFeeds International Corp. (AnimalFeeds) thereupon filed a demand for class arbitration of the antitrust-conspiracy claims.[Footnote 3] Stolt-Nielsen contested AnimalFeeds\xe2\x80\x99 right to proceed on behalf of a class, but agreed to submission of that threshold dispute to a panel of arbitrators. Thus, the parties entered into a supplemental agreement to choose arbitrators and instruct them to \xe2\x80\x9cfollow \xe2\x80\xa6 Rul[e] 3 \xe2\x80\xa6 of the American Arbitration Association\xe2\x80\x99s Supplementary Rules for Class Arbitrations.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 59a. Rule 3, in turn, directed the panel to \xe2\x80\x9cdetermine \xe2\x80\xa6 whether the applicable arbitration clause permits the arbitration to proceed on behalf of \xe2\x80\xa6 a class.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 56a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After receiving written submissions and hearing arguments, the arbitration panel rendered a clause-construction award. It decided unanimously\xe2\x80\x94and only\xe2\x80\x94that the \xe2\x80\x9carbitration claus[e] [used in the parties\xe2\x80\x99 standard-form shipping contracts] permit[s] this \xe2\x80\xa6 arbitration to proceed as a class arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 52a. Stolt-Nielsen petitioned for court review urging vacatur of the clause-construction award on the ground that \xe2\x80\x9cthe arbitrators [had] exceeded their powers.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710(a)(4). The Court of Appeals upheld the award: \xe2\x80\x9cBecause the parties specifically agreed that the arbitration panel would decide whether the arbitration claus[e] permitted class arbitration,\xe2\x80\x9d the Second Circuit reasoned, \xe2\x80\x9cthe arbitration panel did not exceed its authority in deciding that issue\xe2\x80\x94irrespective of whether it decided the issue correctly.\xe2\x80\x9d 548 F.\xc2\xa03d 85, 101 (2008).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I consider, first, the fitness of the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 clause-construction award for judicial review. The arbitrators decided the issue, in accord with the parties\xe2\x80\x99 supplemental agreement, \xe2\x80\x9cas a threshold matter.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 56a. Their decision that the charter-party arbitration clause permitted class arbitration was abstract and highly interlocutory. The panel did not decide whether the particular claims AnimalFeeds advanced were suitable for class resolution, see App. to Pet. for Cert. 48a\xe2\x80\x9349a; much less did it delineate any class or consider whether, \xe2\x80\x9cif a class is certified, \xe2\x80\xa6 members of the putative class should be required to \xe2\x80\x98opt in\xe2\x80\x99 to th[e] proceeding,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 52a.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court, ante, at 6, n.\xc2\xa02, does not persuasively justify judicial intervention so early in the game or convincingly reconcile its adjudication with the firm final-judgment rule prevailing in the federal court system. See, e.g., 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71257 (providing for petitions for certiorari from \xe2\x80\x9c[f]inal judgments or decrees\xe2\x80\x9d of state courts); \xc2\xa71291 (providing for Court of Appeals review of district court \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decisions\xe2\x80\x9d); Catlin v. United States, 324 U. S. 229, 233 (1945) (describing \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decision\xe2\x80\x9d generally as \xe2\x80\x9cone which ends the litigation on the merits and leaves nothing for the court to do but execute the judgment\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have equated to \xe2\x80\x9cfinal decisions\xe2\x80\x9d a slim set of \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral orders\xe2\x80\x9d that share these characteristics: They \xe2\x80\x9care conclusive, [they] resolve important questions separate from the merits, and [they] are effectively unreviewable on appeal from the final judgment in the underlying action.\xe2\x80\x9d Mohawk Industries, Inc. v. Carpenter, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 4\xe2\x80\x935) (quoting Swint v. Chambers County Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 514 U. S. 35, 42 (1995)). \xe2\x80\x9c[O]rders relating to class certification\xe2\x80\x9d in federal court, it is settled, do not fit that bill. Coopers & Lybrand v. Livesay, 437 U. S. 463, 470 (1978).[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress, of course, can provide exceptions to the \xe2\x80\x9cfinal-decision\xe2\x80\x9d rule. Prescriptions in point include \xc2\xa71292 (immediately appealable \xe2\x80\x9c[i]nterlocutory decisions\xe2\x80\x9d); \xc2\xa72072(c) (authorizing promulgation of rules defining when a district court ruling is final for purposes of appeal under \xc2\xa71291); Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 23(f) (pursuant to \xc2\xa71292(e), accords Courts of Appeals discretion to permit appeals from district court orders granting or denying class-action certification); Fed. Rule Civ. Proc. 54(b) (providing for \xe2\x80\x9centry of a final judgment as to one or more, but fewer than all, of the claims or parties\xe2\x80\x9d). Did Congress provide for immediate review of the preliminary ruling in question here?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 16 of the FAA, governing appellate review of district court arbitration orders, lists as an appealable disposition a district court decision \xe2\x80\x9cconfirming or denying confirmation of an award or partial award.\xe2\x80\x9d 9 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa716(a)(1)(D). Notably, the arbitrators in the matter at hand labeled their decision \xe2\x80\x9cPartial Final Clause Construction Award.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 45a. It cannot be true, however, that parties or arbitrators can gain instant review by slicing off a preliminary decision or a procedural order and declaring its resolution a \xe2\x80\x9cpartial award.\xe2\x80\x9d Cf. Hall Street Associates, L. L. C. v. Mattel, Inc., 552 U. S. 576, 588 (2008) (FAA \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa79\xe2\x80\x9311, which provide for expedited review to confirm, vacate, or modify arbitration awards, \xe2\x80\x9csubstantiat[e] a national policy favoring arbitration with just the limited review needed to maintain arbitration\xe2\x80\x99s essential virtue of resolving disputes straightaway.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lacking this Court\xe2\x80\x99s definitive guidance, some Courts of Appeals have reviewed arbitration awards \xe2\x80\x9cfinally and definitely dispos[ing] of a separate independent claim.\xe2\x80\x9d E.g., Metallgesellschaft A. G. v. M/V Capitan Constante, 790 F.\xc2\xa02d 280, 283 (CA2 1986).[Footnote 5] Others have considered \xe2\x80\x9cpartial award[s]\xe2\x80\x9d that finally \xe2\x80\x9cdetermin[e] liability, but \xe2\x80\xa6 not \xe2\x80\xa6 damages.\xe2\x80\x9d E.g., Hart Surgical, Inc. v. Ultracision, Inc., 244 F.\xc2\xa03d 231, 234 (CA1 2001).[Footnote 6] Another confirmed an interim ruling on a \xe2\x80\x9cseparate, discrete, independent, severable issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Island Creek Coal Sales Co. v. Gainesville, 729 F.\xc2\xa02d 1046, 1049 (CA6 1984) (internal quotation marks omitted), abrogated on other grounds by Cortez Byrd Chips, Inc. v. Bill Harbert Constr. Co., 529 U. S. 193 (2000).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Receptivity to review of preliminary rulings rendered by arbitrators, however, is hardly universal. See Dealer Computer Servs., Inc. v. Dub Herring Ford, 547 F.\xc2\xa03d 558 (CA6 2008) (arbitration panel\xe2\x80\x99s preliminary ruling that contract did not bar class proceedings held not ripe for review; arbitrators had not yet determined that arbitration should proceed on behalf of a class); Metallgesellschaft A. G., 790 F.\xc2\xa02d, at 283, 285 (Feinberg, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting) (\xe2\x80\x9c[Piecemeal review] will make arbitration more like litigation, a result not to be desired. It would be better to minimize the number of occasions the parties to arbitration can come to court; on the whole, this benefits the parties, the arbitration process and the courts.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While lower court opinions are thus divided, this much is plain: No decision of this Court, until today, has ever approved immediate judicial review of an arbitrator\xe2\x80\x99s decision as preliminary as the \xe2\x80\x9cpartial award\xe2\x80\x9d made in this case.[Footnote 7]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even if Stolt-Nielsen had a plea ripe for judicial review, the Court should reject it on the merits. Recall that the parties jointly asked the arbitrators to decide, initially, whether the arbitration clause in their shipping contracts permitted class proceedings. See supra, at 2. The panel did just what it was commissioned to do. It construed the broad arbitration clause (covering \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny dispute arising from the making, performance or termination of this Charter Party,\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 47a) and ruled, expressly and only, that the clause permitted class arbitration. The Court acts without warrant in allowing Stolt-Nielsen essentially to repudiate its submission of the contract-construction issue to the arbitration panel, and to gain, in place of the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 judgment, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s de novo determination.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The controlling FAA prescription, \xc2\xa710(a),[Footnote 8] authorizes a court to vacate an arbitration panel\xe2\x80\x99s decision \xe2\x80\x9conly in very unusual circumstances.\xe2\x80\x9d First Options of Chicago, Inc. v. Kaplan, 514 U. S. 938, 942 (1995). The four grounds for vacatur codified in \xc2\xa710(a) restate the longstanding rule that, \xe2\x80\x9c[i]f [an arbitration] award is within the submission, and contains the honest decision of the arbitrators, after a full and fair hearing of the parties, a court \xe2\x80\xa6 will not set [the award] aside for error, either in law or fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Burchell v. Marsh, 17 How. 344, 349 (1855).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The sole \xc2\xa710 ground Stolt-Nielsen invokes for vacating the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 decision is \xc2\xa710(a)(4). The question under that provision is \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the arbitrators had the power, based on the parties\xe2\x80\x99 submissions or the arbitration agreement, to reach a certain issue, not whether the arbitrators correctly decided that issue.\xe2\x80\x9d DiRussa v. Dean Witter Reynolds Inc., 121 F.\xc2\xa03d 818, 824 (CA2 1997); Comprehensive Accounting Corp. v. Rudell, 760 F.\xc2\xa02d 138, 140 (CA7 1985). The parties\xe2\x80\x99 supplemental agreement, referring the class-arbitration issue to an arbitration panel, undoubtedly empowered the arbitrators to render their clause-construction decision. That scarcely debatable point should resolve this case.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of the arbitration panel\xe2\x80\x99s decision as resting on \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy,\xe2\x80\x9d not law, is hardly fair comment, for \xe2\x80\x9cpolicy\xe2\x80\x9d is not so much as mentioned in the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 award. Instead, the panel tied its conclusion that the arbitration clause permitted class arbitration, App. to Pet. for Cert. 52a, to New York law, federal maritime law, and decisions made by other panels pursuant to Rule 3 of the American Arbitration Association\xe2\x80\x99s Supplementary Rules for Class Arbitrations. Id., at 49a\xe2\x80\x9350a.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the outset of its explanation, the panel rejected the argument, proffered by AnimalFeeds, that this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Green Tree Financial Corp. v. Bazzle, 539 U. S. 444 (2003), settled the matter by \xe2\x80\x9crequir[ing] clear language that forbids class arbitration in order to bar a class action.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 49a (emphasis added). Agreeing with Stolt-Nielsen in this regard, the panel said that the test it employed looked to the language of the particular agreement to gauge whether the parties \xe2\x80\x9cintended to permit or to preclude class action[s].\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Concentrating on the wording of the arbitration clause, the panel observed, is \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent with New York law as articulated by the [New York] Court of Appeals \xe2\x80\xa6 and with federal maritime law.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[Footnote 9]

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Emphasizing the breadth of the clause in question\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98any dispute arising from the making, performance or termination of this Charter Party\xe2\x80\x99 shall be put to arbitration,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 50a\xe2\x80\x94the panel noted that numerous other partial awards had relied on language similarly comprehensive to permit class proceedings \xe2\x80\x9cin a wide variety of settings.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 49a\xe2\x80\x9350a. The panel further noted \xe2\x80\x9cthat many of the other panels [had] rejected arguments similar to those advanced by [Stolt-Nielsen].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 50a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court features a statement counsel for AnimalFeeds made at the hearing before the arbitration panel, and maintains that it belies any argument that the clause in question permits class arbitration: \xe2\x80\x9cAll the parties agree that when a contract is silent on an issue there\xe2\x80\x99s been no agreement that has been reached on that issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4 (quoting App. 77a); see ante, at 8, 11\xe2\x80\x9312, 20, 23, and n.\xc2\xa010. The sentence quoted from the hearing transcript concluded: \xe2\x80\x9ctherefore there has been no agreement to bar class arbitrations.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 77a (emphasis added). Counsel quickly clarified his position: \xe2\x80\x9cIt\xe2\x80\x99s also undisputed that the arbitration clause here contains broad language and this language should be interpreted to permit class arbitrations.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 79a. See also id., at 80a (noting consistent recognition by arbitration panels that \xe2\x80\x9ca silent broadly worded arbitration clause, just like the one at issue here, should be construed to permit class arbitration\xe2\x80\x9d); id., at 88a (\xe2\x80\x9c[B]road \xe2\x80\xa6 language \xe2\x80\xa6 silent as to class proceedings should be interpreted to permit a class proceeding.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Stolt-Nielsen, the panel acknowledged, had vigorously argued, with the support of expert testimony, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe bulk of international shippers would never intend to have their disputes decided in a class arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 52a. That concern, the panel suggested, might be met at a later stage; \xe2\x80\x9cif a class is certified,\xe2\x80\x9d the panel noted, class membership could be confined to those who affirmatively \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98opt in\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to the proceeding. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question properly before the Court is not whether the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 ruling was erroneous, but whether the arbitrators \xe2\x80\x9cexceeded their powers.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa710(a)(4). The arbitrators decided a threshold issue, explicitly committed to them, see supra, at 2, about the procedural mode available for presentation of AnimalFeeds\xe2\x80\x99 antitrust claims. Cf. Shady Grove Orthopedic Associates, P.\xc2\xa0A. v. Allstate Ins. Co., 559 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (plurality opinion) (slip op., at 13\xe2\x80\x9314) (\xe2\x80\x9c[R]ules allowing multiple claims (and claims by or against multiple parties) to be litigated together \xe2\x80\xa6 neither change plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 separate entitlements to relief nor abridge defendants\xe2\x80\x99 rights; they alter only how the claims are processed.\xe2\x80\x9d). That the arbitrators endeavored to perform their assigned task honestly is not contested. \xe2\x80\x9cCourts \xe2\x80\xa6 do not sit to hear claims of factual or legal error by an arbitrator as an appellate court does in reviewing decisions of lower courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Paperworkers v. Misco, Inc., 484 U. S. 29, 38 (1987). The arbitrators here not merely \xe2\x80\x9carguably,\xe2\x80\x9d but certainly, \xe2\x80\x9cconstru[ed] \xe2\x80\xa6 the contract\xe2\x80\x9d with fidelity to their commission. Ibid. This Court, therefore, may not disturb the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 judgment, even if convinced that \xe2\x80\x9cserious error\xe2\x80\x9d infected the panel\xe2\x80\x99s award. Ibid.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court not only intrudes on a decision the parties referred to arbitrators. It compounds the intrusion by according the arbitrators no opportunity to clarify their decision and thereby to cure the error the Court perceives. Section 10(b), the Court asserts, invests in this tribunal authority to \xe2\x80\x9cdecide the question that was originally referred to the panel.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 12. The controlling provision, however, says nothing of the kind. Section 10(b) reads, in full: \xe2\x80\x9cIf an award is vacated and the time within which the agreement required the award to be made has not expired, the court may, in its discretion, direct a rehearing by the arbitrators.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) Just as \xc2\xa710(a)(4) provides no justification for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s disposition, see supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x939 and this page, so, too, \xc2\xa710(b) provides no grounding for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s peremptory action.

\n

IV

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For arbitrators to consider whether a claim should proceed on a class basis, the Court apparently demands contractual language one can read as affirmatively authorizing class arbitration. See ante, at 20 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] party may not be compelled under the FAA to submit to class arbitration unless there is a contractual basis for concluding that the party agreed to do so.\xe2\x80\x9d); ante, at 23. The breadth of the arbitration clause, and the absence of any provision waiving or banning class proceedings,[Footnote 10] will not do. Ante, at 20\xe2\x80\x9323.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court ties the requirement of affirmative authorization to \xe2\x80\x9cthe basic precept that arbitration \xe2\x80\x98is a matter of consent, not coercion.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 17 (quoting Volt Information Sciences, Inc. v. Board of Trustees of Leland Stanford Junior Univ., 489 U. S. 468, 479 (1989)). Parties may \xe2\x80\x9cspecify with whom they choose to arbitrate,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court observes, just as they may \xe2\x80\x9climit the issues they choose to arbitrate.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 19. But arbitrators, in delineating an appropriate class, need not, and should not, disregard such contractual constraints. In this case, for example, AnimalFeeds proposes to pursue, on behalf of a class, only \xe2\x80\x9cclaims \xe2\x80\xa6 arising out of any [charter party agreement] \xe2\x80\xa6 that provides for arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 56a (emphasis added). Should the arbitrators certify the proposed class, they would adjudicate only the rights of persons \xe2\x80\x9cwith whom\xe2\x80\x9d Stolt-Nielsen agreed to arbitrate, and only \xe2\x80\x9cissues\xe2\x80\x9d subject to arbitration. Ante, at 19 (emphasis omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court also links its affirmative-authorization requirement to the parties\xe2\x80\x99 right to stipulate rules under which arbitration may proceed. See ibid. The question, however, is the proper default rule when there is no stipulation. Arbitration provisions, this Court has noted, are a species of forum-selection clauses. See Scherk v. Alberto-Culver Co., 417 U. S. 506, 519 (1974). Suppose the parties had chosen a New York judicial forum for resolution of \xe2\x80\x9cany dispute\xe2\x80\x9d involving a contract for ocean carriage of goods. There is little question that the designated court, state or federal, would have authority to conduct claims like AnimalFeeds\xe2\x80\x99 on a class basis. Why should the class-action prospect vanish when the \xe2\x80\x9cany dispute\xe2\x80\x9d clause is contained in an arbitration agreement? Cf. Connecticut General Life Ins. Co. v. Sun Life Assurance Co. of Canada, 210 F.\xc2\xa03d 771, 774\xe2\x80\x93776 (CA7 2000) (reading contract\xe2\x80\x99s authorization to arbitrate \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny dispute\xe2\x80\x9d to permit consolidation of arbitrations). If the Court is right that arbitrators ordinarily are not equipped to manage class proceedings, see ante, at 21\xe2\x80\x9322, then the claimant should retain its right to proceed in that format in court.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When adjudication is costly and individual claims are no more than modest in size, class proceedings may be \xe2\x80\x9cthe thing,\xe2\x80\x9d i.e., without them, potential claimants will have little, if any, incentive to seek vindication of their rights. Amchem Products, Inc. v. Windsor, 521 U. S. 591, 617 (1997); Carnegie v. Household Int\xe2\x80\x99l, Inc., 376 F. 3d 656, 661 (CA7 2004) (\xe2\x80\x9cThe realistic alternative to a class action is not 17 million individual suits, but zero individual suits, as only a lunatic or a fanatic sues for $30.\xe2\x80\x9d). Mindful that disallowance of class proceedings severely shrinks the dimensions of the case or controversy a claimant can mount, I note some stopping points in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the Court does not insist on express consent to class arbitration. Class arbitration may be ordered if \xe2\x80\x9cthere is a contractual basis for concluding that the part[ies] agreed\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cto submit to class arbitration\xe2\x80\x9d. Ante, at 20; see ante, at 23, n.\xc2\xa010 (\xe2\x80\x9cWe have no occasion to decide what contractual basis may support a finding that the parties agreed to authorize class-action arbitration.\xe2\x80\x9d). Second, by observing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe parties [here] are sophisticated business entities,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cthat it is customary for the shipper to choose the charter party that is used for a particular shipment,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court apparently spares from its affirmative-authorization requirement contracts of adhesion presented on a take-it-or-leave-it basis. Ante, at 20. While these qualifications limit the scope of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision, I remain persuaded that the arbitrators\xe2\x80\x99 judgment should not have been disturbed.
