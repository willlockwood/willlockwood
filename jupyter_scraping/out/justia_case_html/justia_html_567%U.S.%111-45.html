\n\t\t\t\tELGIN et\xc2\xa0al. v. DEPARTMENT OF THE TREASURY et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x9345.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 27, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 11, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 (CSRA) \xe2\x80\x9cestablished a comprehensive system for reviewing personnel action taken against federal employees,\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Fausto, \n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 439\n, including removals, \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77512. A qualifying employee has the right to a hearing before the Merit Systems Protection Board (MSPB), \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77513(d), 7701(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2), which is authorized to order reinstatement, backpay, and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71204(a)(2), 7701(g). An employee who is dissatisfied with the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s decision is entitled to judicial review in the Federal Circuit. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77703(a)(1), (b)(1).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners were federal employees discharged pursuant to \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73328, which bars from Executive agency employment anyone who has knowingly and willfully failed to register for the Selective Service as required by the Military Selective Service Act, 50 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App. \xc2\xa7453. Petitioner Elgin challenged his removal before the MSPB, claiming that \xc2\xa73328 is an unconstitutional bill of attainder and unconstitutionally discriminates based on sex when combined with the Military Selective Service Act\xe2\x80\x99s male-only registration requirement. The MSPB referred the case to an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ), who dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction, concluding that an employee is not entitled to MSPB review of agency action that is based on an absolute statutory bar to employment. The ALJ also concluded that the MSPB lacked authority to determine the constitutionality of a federal statute. Rather than seeking further MSPB review or appealing to the Federal Circuit, Elgin joined other petitioners raising the same constitutional challenges to their removals in a suit in Federal District Court. The District Court found that it had jurisdiction and denied petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims on the merits. The First Circuit vacated and remanded with instructions to dismiss for lack of jurisdiction. The First Circuit held that petitioners were employees entitled to MSPB review despite the statutory bar to their employment. The court further concluded that challenges to a removal are not exempt from the CSRA review scheme simply because an employee challenges the constitutionality of the statute authorizing the removal.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0The CSRA precludes district court jurisdiction over petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims because it is fairly discernible that Congress intended the statute\xe2\x80\x99s review scheme to provide the exclusive avenue to judicial review for covered employees who challenge covered adverse employment actions, even when those employees argue that a federal statute is unconstitutional. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9320.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0Relying on Webster v. Doe, \n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 592\n, petitioners claim that \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331\xe2\x80\x99s general grant of federal-question jurisdiction to district courts remains undisturbed unless Congress explicitly directs otherwise. But Webster\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cheightened showing\xe2\x80\x9d applies only when a statute purports to \xe2\x80\x9cdeny any judicial forum for a colorable constitutional claim,\xe2\x80\x9d 486 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 603, not when Congress channels judicial review of a constitutional claim to a particular court, see Thunder Basin Coal Co. v. Reich, \n\n510 U.\xc2\xa0S. 200\n. Here, where the claims can be \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningfully addressed in the\xe2\x80\x9d Federal Circuit, id., at 215, the proper inquiry is whether Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to preclude district court jurisdiction was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fairly discernible in the statutory scheme,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 207. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x936.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0It is \xe2\x80\x9cfairly discernible\xe2\x80\x9d from the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s text, structure, and purpose that Congress precluded district court jurisdiction over petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0Just as the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9celaborate\xe2\x80\x9d framework demonstrated Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to entirely foreclose judicial review to employees to whom the CSRA denies statutory review in Fausto, 484 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 443, the CSRA indicates that extrastatutory review is not available to those employees to whom the CSRA grants administrative and judicial review. It \xe2\x80\x9cprescribes in great detail the protections and remedies applicable to\xe2\x80\x9d adverse personnel actions against federal employees, ibid., specifically enumerating the major adverse actions and employee classifications to which the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s procedural protections and review provisions apply, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77511, 7512, setting out the procedures due an employee prior to final agency action, \xc2\xa77513, and exhaustively detailing the system of review before the MSPB and the Federal Circuit, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77701, 7703. Petitioners and the Government do not dispute that petitioners are removed employees to whom CSRA review is provided, but petitioners claim that there is an exception to the CSRA review scheme for employees who bring constitutional challenges to federal statutes; this claim finds no support in the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s text and structure. The availability of administrative and judicial review under the CSRA generally turns on the type of civil service employee and adverse employment action at issue. Nothing in the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s text suggests that its exclusive review scheme is inapplicable simply because a covered employee raises a constitutional challenge. And \xc2\xa77703(b)(2)\xe2\x80\x94which expressly exempts from Federal Circuit review challenges alleging that a covered action was based on discrimination prohibited by enumerated federal employment laws\xe2\x80\x94demonstrates that Congress knew how to provide alternative forums for judicial review based on the nature of an employee\xe2\x80\x99s claim. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9310.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0The CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s purpose also supports the conclusion that the statutory review scheme is exclusive, even for constitutional challenges. The CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s objective of creating an integrated review scheme to replace inconsistent decisionmaking and duplicative judicial review would be seriously undermined if a covered employee could challenge a covered employment action first in a district court, and then again in a court of appeals, simply by challenging the constitutionality of the statutory authorization for the action. Claim-splitting and preclusion doctrines would not necessarily eliminate the possibility of parallel proceedings before the MSPB and the district court, and petitioners point to nothing in the CSRA to support the notion that Congress intended to allow employees to pursue constitutional claims in district court at the expense of forgoing other, potentially meritorious claims before the MSPB. Pp.\xc2\xa010\xe2\x80\x9312.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Petitioners invoke the \xe2\x80\x9cpresum[ption] that Congress does not intend to limit [district court] jurisdiction if \xe2\x80\x98a finding of preclusion could foreclose all meaningful judicial review\xe2\x80\x99; if the suit is \xe2\x80\x98wholly collateral to a statute\xe2\x80\x99s review provisions\xe2\x80\x99; and if the claims are \xe2\x80\x98outside the agency\xe2\x80\x99s expertise.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Bd., 561 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___. But none of those characteristics is present here. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9320.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims can receive meaningful review within the CSRA scheme even if the MSPB, as it claims, is not authorized to decide a federal law\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality. Their claims can be \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningfully addressed\xe2\x80\x9d in the Federal Circuit, which has held that it can determine the constitutionality of a statute upon which an employee\xe2\x80\x99s removal was based, notwithstanding the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s professed lack of authority to decide the question. The CSRA review scheme also fully accommodates the potential need for a factual record. Even without factfinding capabilities, the Federal Circuit may take judicial notice of facts relevant to the constitutional question. If further development is necessary, the CSRA empowers the MSPB to take evidence and find facts for Federal Circuit review. See 5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71204(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2). Petitioners err in arguing that the MSPB will invariably dismiss a case without ever reaching the factfinding stage in an appeal such as theirs. The MSPB may determine that it lacks authority to decide the issue; but absent another infirmity in the adverse action, it will affirm the employing agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision. The Federal Circuit can then review the decision, including any factual record developed by the MSPB. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument is not illustrated by Elgin\xe2\x80\x99s case, which was dismissed on the threshold ground that he was not an \xe2\x80\x9cemployee\xe2\x80\x9d with a right to appeal because his employment was absolutely barred by statute. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9318.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims are also not \xe2\x80\x9cwholly collateral\xe2\x80\x9d to the CSRA scheme. Their constitutional claims are the vehicle by which they seek to reverse the removal decisions, to return to federal employment, and to receive lost compensation. A challenge to removal is precisely the type of personnel action regularly adjudicated by the MSPB and the Federal Circuit within the CSRA scheme, and reinstatement, backpay, and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees are precisely the kinds of relief that the CSRA empowers the MSPB and the Federal Circuit to provide. Pp.\xc2\xa018\xe2\x80\x9319.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(3)\xc2\xa0Finally, in arguing that their constitutional claims are not the sort that Congress intended to channel through the MSPB because they are beyond the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s expertise, petitioners overlook the many threshold questions that may accompany a constitutional claim and to which the MSPB can apply its expertise, e.g., whether a resignation, as in petitioner Tucker\xe2\x80\x99s case, amounts to a constructive discharge. Pp.\xc2\xa019\xe2\x80\x9320.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t[June 11, 2012]\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder the Civil Service Reform Act of 1978 (CSRA), \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71101 et\xc2\xa0seq., certain federal employees may obtain administrative and judicial review of specified ad- verse employment actions. The question before us is whether the CSRA provides the exclusive avenue to judicial review when a qualifying employee challenges an adverse employment action by arguing that a federal sta- tute is unconstitutional. We hold that it does.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe CSRA \xe2\x80\x9cestablished a comprehensive system for reviewing personnel action taken against federal employees.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Fausto, \n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 439, \n455 (1988)\n. As relevant here, Subchapter II of Chapter 75 governs review of major adverse actions taken against employees \xe2\x80\x9cfor such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service.\xe2\x80\x9d 5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77503(a), 7513(a). Employees entitled to review are those in the \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitive service\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cexcepted service\xe2\x80\x9d who meet certain requirements regarding probationary periods and years of service.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \xc2\xa77511(a)(1). The re- viewable agency actions are removal, suspension for more than 14 days, reduction in grade or pay, or furlough for 30 days or less. \xc2\xa77512.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhen an employing agency proposes a covered action against a covered employee, the CSRA gives the employee the right to notice, representation by counsel, an opportunity to respond, and a written, reasoned decision from the agency. \xc2\xa77513(b). If the agency takes final adverse action against the employee, the CSRA gives the employee the right to a hearing and to be represented by an attorney or other representative before the Merit Systems Pro- tection Board (MSPB). \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77513(d), 7701(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2). The MSPB is authorized to order relief to prevailing employees, including reinstatement, backpay, and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71204(a)(2), 7701(g).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAn employee who is dissatisfied with the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s decision is entitled to judicial review in the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit. That court \xe2\x80\x9cshall review the record and hold unlawful and set aside any agency action, findings, or conclusions\xe2\x80\x9d that are \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary, capricious, an abuse of discretion, or otherwise not in accordance with law,\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cobtained without procedures required by law, rule, or regulation having been followed,\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cunsupported by substantial evidence.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77703(a)(1), (c). The Federal Circuit has \xe2\x80\x9cexclusive jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d over appeals from a final decision of the MSPB. \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71295(a)(9); see also \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77703(b)(1) (judicial review of an MSPB decision \xe2\x80\x9cshall be\xe2\x80\x9d in the Federal Circuit).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners are former federal competitive service employees who failed to comply with the Military Selective Service Act, 50 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. App. \xc2\xa7453. That Act requires male citizens and permanent-resident aliens of the United States between the ages of 18 and 26 to register for the Selective Service. Another federal statute, \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73328 (hereinafter Section 3328), bars from employment by an Executive agency anyone who has knowingly and willfully failed to register. Pursuant to Section 3328, petitioners were discharged (or allegedly constructively discharged) by respondents, their employing agencies.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAmong petitioners, only Michael Elgin appealed his removal to the MSPB. Elgin argued that Section 3328 is an unconstitutional bill of attainder and unconstitution-ally discriminates on the basis of sex when combined with the registration requirement of the Military Selective Service Act. The MSPB referred Elgin\xe2\x80\x99s appeal to an ad- ministrative law judge (ALJ) for an initial decision.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The ALJ dismissed the appeal for lack of jurisdiction, concluding that an employee is not entitled to MSPB review of agency action that is based on an absolute statutory bar to employment. App. to Pet. for Cert. 100a\xe2\x80\x93101a. The ALJ also held that Elgin\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional claims could not \xe2\x80\x9cconfer jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d on the MSPB because it \xe2\x80\x9clacks authority to determine the constitutionality of a statute.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 101a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tElgin neither petitioned for review by the full MSPB nor appealed to the Federal Circuit. Instead, he joined the other petitioners in filing suit in the United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts, raising the same constitutional challenges to Section 3328 and the Military Selective Service Act. App. 4, 26\xe2\x80\x9328, 29. Petitioners sought equitable relief in the form of a declaratory judgment that the challenged statutes are unconstitutional, an injunction prohibiting enforcement of Section 3328, reinstatement to their former positions, backpay, benefits, and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees. Id., at 29\xe2\x80\x9330.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe District Court rejected respondents\xe2\x80\x99 argument that it lacked jurisdiction and denied petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims on the merits. See Elgin v. United States, 697 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 187 (Mass. 2010). The District Court held that the CSRA did not preclude it from hearing petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims, because the MSPB had no authority to determine the constitutionality of a federal statute. Id., at 193. Hence, the District Court concluded that it retained jurisdiction under the general grant of federal-question jurisdiction in \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331. 697 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d, at 194.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit vacated the judgment and remanded with instructions to dismiss for lack of jurisdiction. See 641 F.\xc2\xa03d 6 (2011). The Court of Appeals held that challenges to a removal are not exempted from the CSRA review scheme simply because the employee argues that the statute authorizing the removal is unconstitutional. Id., at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. According to the Court of Appeals, the CSRA provides a forum\xe2\x80\x94the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x94that may adjudicate the constitutionality of a federal statute, and petitioners \xe2\x80\x9cwere obliged to use it.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 12\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari to decide whether the CSRA pre- cludes district court jurisdiction over petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims even though they are constitutional claims for equitable relief. See 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011). We conclude that it does, and we therefore affirm.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe begin with the appropriate standard for determining whether a statutory scheme of administrative and judicial review provides the exclusive means of review for constitutional claims. Petitioners argue that even if they may obtain judicial review of their constitutional claims before the Federal Circuit, they are not precluded from pursuing their claims in federal district court. According to petitioners, the general grant of federal-question jurisdiction in \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331, which gives district courts authority over constitutional claims, remains undisturbed unless Congress explicitly directs otherwise. In support of this argument, petitioners rely on Webster v. Doe, \n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 592, \n603 (1988)\n, which held that \xe2\x80\x9cwhere Congress intends to preclude judicial review of constitutional claims[,] its intent to do so must be clear.\xe2\x80\x9d The Webster Court noted that this \xe2\x80\x9cheightened showing\xe2\x80\x9d was required \xe2\x80\x9cto avoid the \xe2\x80\x98serious constitutional question\xe2\x80\x99 that would arise if a federal statute were construed to deny any judicial forum for a colorable constitutional claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (quoting Bowen v. Michigan Academy of Family Physicians, \n\n476 U.\xc2\xa0S. 667\n, n.\xc2\xa012 (1986)). Petitioners contend that the CSRA does not meet this standard because it does not expressly bar suits in district court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 argument overlooks a necessary predicate to the application of Webster\xe2\x80\x99s heightened standard: a statute that purports to \xe2\x80\x9cdeny any judicial forum for a colorable constitutional claim.\xe2\x80\x9d 486 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 603. Webster\xe2\x80\x99s standard does not apply where Congress simply channels judicial review of a constitutional claim to a particular court. We held as much in Thunder Basin Coal Co. v. Reich, \n\n510 U.\xc2\xa0S. 200 (1994)\n. In that case, we considered whether a statutory scheme of administrative review followed by judicial review in a federal appellate court precluded district court jurisdiction over a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s statutory and constitutional claims. Id., at 206. We noted that the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claims could be \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningfully addressed in the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x9d and that the case therefore did \xe2\x80\x9cnot present the \xe2\x80\x98serious constitutional question\xe2\x80\x99 that would arise if an agency statute were construed to preclude all judicial review of a constitutional claim.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 215, and n.\xc2\xa020 (quoting Bowen, supra, at 681, n.\xc2\xa012). Accordingly, we did not require Webster\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cheightened showing,\xe2\x80\x9d but instead asked only whether Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to preclude district court jurisdiction was \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98fairly discernible in the statutory scheme.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 510 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 207 (quoting Block v. Community Nutrition Institute, \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 340, \n351 (1984)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLike the statute in Thunder Basin, the CSRA does not foreclose all judicial review of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims, but merely directs that judicial review shall occur in the Federal Circuit. Moreover, as we explain below, the Federal Circuit is fully capable of providing meaningful review of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims. See infra, at 12\xe2\x80\x9317. Accordingly, the appropriate inquiry is whether it is \xe2\x80\x9cfairly discernible\xe2\x80\x9d from the CSRA that Congress intended covered employees appealing covered agency actions to proceed exclusively through the statutory review scheme, even in cases in which the employees raise constitutional challenges to federal statutes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo determine whether it is \xe2\x80\x9cfairly discernible\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress precluded district court jurisdiction over petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims, we examine the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s text, structure, and purpose. See Thunder Basin, supra, at 207; Fausto, 484 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 443.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis is not the first time we have addressed the impact of the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s text and structure on the availability of judicial review of a federal employee\xe2\x80\x99s challenge to an employment decision. In Fausto, we considered whether a so-called \xe2\x80\x9cnonpreference excepted service employe[e]\xe2\x80\x9d could challenge his suspension in the United States Claims Court, even though the CSRA did not then afford him a right to review in the MSPB or the Federal Circuit.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Id., at 440\xe2\x80\x93441, 448. Citing \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he comprehensive nature of the CSRA, the attention that it gives throughout to the rights of nonpreference excepted service employees, and the fact that it does not include them in provisions for administrative and judicial review contained in Chapter 75,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court concluded that \xe2\x80\x9cthe absence of provision for these employees to obtain judicial review\xe2\x80\x9d was a \xe2\x80\x9cconsidered congressional judgment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 448. The Court thus found it \xe2\x80\x9cfairly discernible\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress intended to preclude all judicial review of Fausto\xe2\x80\x99s statutory claims.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Id., at 452 (citing Block, supra, at 349).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJust as the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9celaborate\xe2\x80\x9d framework, 484 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 443, demonstrates Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to entirely foreclose judicial review to employees to whom the CSRA denies statutory review, it similarly indicates that extrastatutory review is not available to those employees to whom the CSRA grants administrative and judicial review. Indeed, in Fausto we expressly assumed that \xe2\x80\x9ccompetitive service employees, who are given review rights by Chapter 75, cannot expand these rights by resort to\xe2\x80\x9d judicial review outside of the CSRA scheme. See id., at 450, n.\xc2\xa03. As Fausto explained, the CSRA \xe2\x80\x9cprescribes in great detail the protections and remedies applicable to\xe2\x80\x9d adverse personnel actions against federal employees. Id., at 443. For example, Subchapter II of Chapter 75, the portion of the CSRA relevant to petitioners, specifically enumerates the major adverse actions and employee classifications to which the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s procedural protections and review provisions apply. 5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77511, 7512. The subchapter then sets out the procedures due an employee prior to final agency action. \xc2\xa77513. And, Chapter 77 of the CSRA exhaustively details the system of review before the MSPB and the Federal Circuit. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77701, 7703; see also Fausto, supra, at 449 (emphasizing that the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s structure evinces \xe2\x80\x9cthe primacy\xe2\x80\x9d of review by the MSPB and the Federal Circuit). Given the painstaking detail with which the CSRA sets out the method for covered employees to obtain review of adverse employment actions, it is fairly discernible that Congress intended to deny such employees an additional avenue of review in district court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners do not dispute that they are employees who suffered adverse actions covered by the foregoing provisions of the CSRA. Nor do they contest that the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s text and structure support implied preclusion of district court jurisdiction, at least as a general matter. Petitioners even acknowledge that the MSPB routinely adjudicates some constitutional claims, such as claims that an agency took adverse employment action in violation of an em- ployee\xe2\x80\x99s First or \n \nFourth Amendment rights, and that these claims must be brought within the CSRA scheme. See Brief for Petitioners 33; Tr. of Oral Arg. 7\xe2\x80\x9311, 15, 21; see also, e.g., Smith v. Department of Transp., 106 MSPR 59, 78\xe2\x80\x9379 (2007) (applying Pickering v. Board of Ed. of Township High School Dist. 205, Will Cty., \n\n391 U.\xc2\xa0S. 563 (1968)\n, to an employee\xe2\x80\x99s claim that he was suspended in retal- iation for the exercise of his \n \nFirst Amendment rights); Garrison v. Department of Justice, 67 MSPR 154 (1995) (considering whether an order directing an employee to submit to a drug test was reasonable under the \n \nFourth Amendment). Nevertheless, petitioners seek to carve out an exception to CSRA exclusivity for facial or as-applied constitutional challenges to federal statutes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe text and structure of the CSRA, however, provide no support for such an exception. The availability of ad- ministrative and judicial review under the CSRA gen- erally turns on the type of civil service employee and adverse employment action at issue. See, e.g., 5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77511(a)(1) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cemployee\xe2\x80\x9d), 7512 (defining \xe2\x80\x9c[a]c- tions covered\xe2\x80\x9d), 7513(d) (providing that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n employee against whom an action is taken under this section is entitled to appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board\xe2\x80\x9d), 7703(a)(1) (providing that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny employee .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. adversely affected or aggrieved by a final order or decision of the Merit Systems Protection Board may obtain judicial review of the order or decision\xe2\x80\x9d in the Federal Circuit). Nothing in the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s text suggests that its exclusive review scheme is inapplicable simply because a covered employee challenges a covered action on the ground that the statute authorizing that action is unconstitutional. As the Government correctly notes, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he plain language of [the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s] provisions applies to an employee who challenges his removal on the ground that the statute requiring it is unconstitutional no less than it applies to an employee who challenges his removal on any other ground.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 33\xe2\x80\x9334.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn only one situation does the CSRA expressly exempt a covered employee\xe2\x80\x99s appeal of a covered action from Federal Circuit review based on the type of claim at issue. When a covered employee \xe2\x80\x9calleges that a basis for the action was discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d prohibited by enumerated federal employment laws, \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77702(a)(1)(B), the CSRA allows the employee to obtain judicial review of an unfavorable MSPB decision by filing a civil action as provided by the applicable employment law. See \xc2\xa77703(b)(2). Each of the cross-referenced employment laws authorizes an action in federal district court. See \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(f); \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7633a(c); \xc2\xa7216(b). Title \n\n5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa77703(b)(2) demonstrates that Congress knew how to provide alternative forums for judicial review based on the nature of an employee\xe2\x80\x99s claim. That Congress declined to include an exemption from Federal Circuit review for challenges to a statute\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality indicates that Congress intended no such exception.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe purpose of the CSRA also supports our conclusion that the statutory review scheme is exclusive, even for employees who bring constitutional challenges to federal statutes. As we have previously explained, the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cintegrated scheme of administrative and judicial review\xe2\x80\x9d for aggrieved federal employees was designed to replace an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98outdated patchwork of statutes and rules\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that afforded employees the right to challenge employing agency actions in district courts across the country. Fausto, 484 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 444\xe2\x80\x93445. Such widespread judicial review, which included appeals in all of the Federal Courts of Appeals produced \xe2\x80\x9cwide variations in the kinds of decisions .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. issued on the same or similar matters\xe2\x80\x9d and a double layer of judicial review that was \xe2\x80\x9cwasteful and irrational.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 445 (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s objective of creating an integrated scheme of review would be seriously undermined if, as petitioners would have it, a covered employee could challenge a covered employment action first in a district court, and then again in one of the courts of appeals, simply by alleging that the statutory authorization for such action is unconstitutional. Such suits would reintroduce the very po- tential for inconsistent decisionmaking and duplicative judicial review that the CSRA was designed to avoid. Moreover, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position would create the possibility of parallel litigation regarding the same agency action before the MSPB and a district court. An employee could challenge the constitutionality of the statute authorizing an agency\xe2\x80\x99s action in district court, but the MSPB would remain the exclusive forum for other types of challenges to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision. See Tr. of Oral Arg. 4\xe2\x80\x937, 9, 15\xe2\x80\x9316.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners counter that doctrines regarding claim splitting and preclusion would bar parallel suits before the MSPB and the district court. But such doctrines would not invariably eliminate the possibility of simultaneous proceedings, for a tribunal generally has discretion to decide whether to dismiss a suit when a similar suit is pending elsewhere. See 18 C. Wright et al., Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa74406 (2d ed. 2002 and Supp. 2011). In any event, petitioners point to nothing in the CSRA to support the odd notion that Congress intended to allow employees to pursue constitutional claims in district court at the cost of forgoing other, potentially meritorious claims before the MSPB.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, we note that a jurisdictional rule based on the nature of an employee\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional claim would deprive the aggrieved employee, the MSPB, and the district court of clear guidance about the proper forum for the employee\xe2\x80\x99s claims at the outset of the case. For example, petitioners contend that facial and as-applied constitutional challenges to statutes may be brought in district court, while other constitutional challenges must be heard by the MSPB. See supra, at 8\xe2\x80\x939; infra, at 13, n.\xc2\xa05. But, as we explain below, that line is hazy at best and incoherent at worst. See ibid. The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approach fares no better. The dissent carves out for district court adjudication only facial constitutional challenges to statutes, but we have previously stated that \xe2\x80\x9cthe distinction between facial and as-applied challenges is not so well defined that it has some automatic effect or that it must always control the pleadings and disposition in every case involving a constitutional challenge.\xe2\x80\x9d Citizens United v. Federal Election Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 558 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 14). By contrast, a jurisdictional rule based on the type of em- ployee and adverse agency action at issue does not involve such amorphous distinctions. Accordingly, we conclude that the better interpretation of the CSRA is that its exclusivity does not turn on the constitutional nature of an employee\xe2\x80\x99s claim, but rather on the type of the employee and the challenged employment action.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners raise three additional factors in arguing that their claims are not the type that Congress intended to be reviewed within the CSRA scheme. Specifically, petitioners invoke our \xe2\x80\x9cpresum[ption] that Congress does not intend to limit [district court] jurisdiction if \xe2\x80\x98a finding of preclusion could foreclose all meaningful judicial review\xe2\x80\x99; if the suit is \xe2\x80\x98wholly collateral to a statute\xe2\x80\x99s review provisions\xe2\x80\x99; and if the claims are \xe2\x80\x98outside the agency\xe2\x80\x99s expertise.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Bd., 561 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 8) (quoting Thunder Basin, 510 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 212\xe2\x80\x93213). Contrary to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 suggestion, none of those characteristics are present here.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, petitioners argue that the CSRA review scheme provides no meaningful review of their claims because the MSPB lacks authority to declare a federal statute un- constitutional. Petitioners are correct that the MSPB has repeatedly refused to pass upon the constitutionality of legislation. See, e.g., Malone v. Department of Justice, 13 M.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0P.\xc2\xa0B. 81, 83 (1983) (\xe2\x80\x9c[I]t is well settled that administrative agencies are without authority to determine the constitutionality of statutes\xe2\x80\x9d). This Court has also stated that \xe2\x80\x9cadjudication of the constitutionality of congressional enactments has generally been thought beyond the jurisdiction of administrative agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d Thunder Basin, 510 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 215 (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe need not, and do not, decide whether the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s view of its power is correct, or whether the oft-stated principle that agencies cannot declare a statute unconstitutional is truly a matter of jurisdiction. See ibid. (describing this rule as \xe2\x80\x9cnot mandatory\xe2\x80\x9d). In Thunder Basin, we held that Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to preclude district court jurisdiction was fairly discernible in the statutory scheme \xe2\x80\x9c[e]ven if\xe2\x80\x9d the administrative body could not decide the constitutionality of a federal law. Ibid. That issue, we reasoned, could be \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningfully addressed in the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress had authorized to conduct judicial review. Ibid.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Likewise, the CSRA provides review in the Federal Circuit, an Article III court fully competent to adjudicate petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims that Section 3328 and the Military Selective Service Act\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement are unconstitutional.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners insist, however, that the Federal Circuit cannot decide their constitutional claims either. Emphasizing the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s holdings that its jurisdiction over employee appeals is coextensive with the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction, petitioners argue that the Federal Circuit likewise lacks jurisdiction to decide their challenge to the constitutionality of a federal statute. Petitioners are incorrect.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs we have explained, the CSRA makes MSPB jurisdiction over an appeal dependent only on the nature of the employee and the employment action at issue. See supra, at 1\xe2\x80\x932, 8\xe2\x80\x939; see also 5 CFR \xc2\xa71201.3(a) (stating that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he Board has jurisdiction over appeals from agency actions\xe2\x80\x9d and enumerating covered actions); Todd v. Merit Systems Protection Bd., 55 F.\xc2\xa03d 1574, 1576 (CA Fed. 1995) (explaining that the employee \xe2\x80\x9chas the burden of establishing that she and the action she seeks to appeal [are] within the [MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s] jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d). Accordingly, as the cases cited by petitioners demonstrate, the Federal Circuit has questioned its jurisdiction when an employee appeals from a type of adverse action over which the MSPB lacked jurisdiction.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t But the Federal Circuit has never held, in an appeal from agency action within the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction, that its authority to decide particular legal questions is derivative of the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s authority. To the contrary, in Briggs v. MSPB, 331 F.\xc2\xa03d 1307, 1312\xe2\x80\x931313 (2003), the Federal Circuit concluded that it could determine the constitutionality of a statute upon which an employee\xe2\x80\x99s removal was based, notwithstanding the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s professed lack of authority to decide the question.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners next contend that even if the Federal Circuit could consider their claims in the first instance, resolution of the claims requires a factual record that neither the MSPB (because it lacks authority to decide the legal question) nor the Federal Circuit (because it is an appellate court) can create. To the contrary, we think the CSRA review scheme fully accommodates an employee\xe2\x80\x99s potential need to establish facts relevant to his constitutional challenge to a federal statute. Even without factfinding capabilities, the Federal Circuit may take judicial notice of facts relevant to the constitutional question. See, e.g., Rothe Development Corp. v. Department of Defense, 545 F.\xc2\xa03d 1023, 1045\xe2\x80\x931046 (CA Fed. 2008) (judicially noticing facts relevant to equal protection challenge). And, if resolution of a constitutional claim requires the development of facts beyond those that the Federal Circuit may judicially notice, the CSRA empowers the MSPB to take evidence and find facts for Federal Circuit review. See 5 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71204(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2) (providing that the MSPB may administer oaths, examine witnesses, take depositions, issue interrogatories, subpoena testimony and documents, and otherwise receive evidence when a covered employee appeals a covered adverse employment action). Unlike petitioners, we see nothing extraordinary in a statutory scheme that vests reviewable factfinding authority in a non-Article III entity that has jurisdiction over an action but cannot finally decide the legal question to which the facts pertain. Congress has authorized magistrate judges, for example, to conduct evidentiary hearings and make findings of fact relevant to dispositive pretrial motions, although they are powerless to issue a final ruling on such motions. See 28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7636(b)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x93(B); United States v. Raddatz, \n\n447 U.\xc2\xa0S. 667, \n673 (1980)\n.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners nonetheless insist that the MSPB will never reach the factfinding stage in an appeal challenging the constitutionality of a federal statute, pointing to the ALJ\xe2\x80\x99s dismissal for lack of jurisdiction in petitioner Elgin\xe2\x80\x99s case. Again, petitioners are incorrect. When a covered employee appeals a covered adverse action, the CSRA grants the MSPB jurisdiction over the appeal. See supra, at 14. If the employee attacks the adverse action on the ground that a statute is unconstitutional, the MSPB may determine that it lacks authority to decide that particular issue; but absent another infirmity in the adverse action, the MSPB will affirm the employing agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision rather than dismiss the appeal. See, e.g., Briggs, supra, at 1311. The Federal Circuit can then review the MSPB decision, including any factual record developed by the MSPB in the course of its decision on the merits.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tContrary to petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 suggestion, Elgin\xe2\x80\x99s case does not illustrate that the MSPB will invariably dismiss an appeal challenging the constitutionality of a federal statute before reaching the factfinding stage. The ALJ dismissed Elgin\xe2\x80\x99s case on the threshold jurisdictional ground that he was not an \xe2\x80\x9cemployee\xe2\x80\x9d with a right to appeal to the MSPB because his employment was absolutely barred by statute. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 100a\xe2\x80\x93101a. The Government conceded before the First Circuit that this jurisdictional argument was incorrect, see Brief for United States 10, and the Court of Appeals agreed, see 641 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 10\xe2\x80\x9311. The parties do not raise that issue here, and we do not address it. What matters for present purposes is that the particular circumstances of Elgin\xe2\x80\x99s case do not demonstrate that the MSPB will dismiss an appeal that is otherwise within its jurisdiction merely because it lacks the authority to decide a particular claim.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, the CSRA grants the MSPB and the Federal Circuit jurisdiction over petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 appeal because they are covered employees challenging a covered adverse employment action. Within the CSRA review scheme, the Federal Circuit has authority to consider and decide petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims. To the extent such challenges require factual development, the CSRA equips the MSPB with tools to create the necessary record. Thus, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims can receive meaningful review within the CSRA scheme.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t11\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners next contend that the CSRA does not preclude district court jurisdiction over their claims because they are \xe2\x80\x9cwholly collateral\xe2\x80\x9d to the CSRA scheme. According to petitioners, their bill-of-attainder and sex discrimination claims \xe2\x80\x9chave nothing to do with the types of day-to-day personnel actions adjudicated by the MSPB,\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Petitioners 29, and petitioners \xe2\x80\x9care not seeking the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98protections and remedies.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioners 3. We disagree.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs evidenced by their district court complaint, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims are the vehicle by which they seek to reverse the removal decisions, to return to federal employment, and to receive the compensation they would have earned but for the adverse employment action. See App. 29\xe2\x80\x9330. A challenge to removal is precisely the type of personnel action regularly adjudicated by the MSPB and the Federal Circuit within the CSRA scheme. Likewise, reinstatement, backpay, and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees are precisely the kinds of relief that the CSRA empowers the MSPB and the Federal Circuit to provide. See supra, at 2; see also Heckler v. Ringer, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 602, \n614 (1984)\n (holding that plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claims were not wholly collateral to a statutory scheme of administrative and judicial review of Medicare payment decisions, where plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional and statutory challenge to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s procedure for reaching payment decisions was \xe2\x80\x9cat bottom\xe2\x80\x9d an attempt to reverse the agency\xe2\x80\x99s decision to deny payment). Far from a suit wholly collateral to the CSRA scheme, the case before us is a challenge to CSRA-covered employment action brought by CSRA-covered employees requesting relief that the CSRA routinely affords.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRelatedly, petitioners argue that their constitutional claims are not the sort that Congress intended to channel through the MSPB because they are outside the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s expertise. But petitioners overlook the many threshold questions that may accompany a constitutional claim and to which the MSPB can apply its expertise. Of particular relevance here, preliminary questions unique to the employment context may obviate the need to address the constitutional challenge. For example, petitioner Henry Tucker asserts that his resignation amounted to a constructive discharge. That issue falls squarely within the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s expertise, and its resolution against Tucker would avoid the need to reach his constitutional claims. In addition, the challenged statute may be one that the MSPB regularly construes, and its statutory interpretation could alleviate constitutional concerns. Or, an employee\xe2\x80\x99s appeal may involve other statutory or constitutional claims that the MSPB routinely considers, in addition to a constitutional challenge to a federal statute. The MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s resolution of those claims in the employee\xe2\x80\x99s favor might fully dispose of the case. Thus, because the MSPB\xe2\x80\x99s expertise can otherwise be \xe2\x80\x9cbrought to bear\xe2\x80\x9d on employee appeals that challenge the constitutionality of a statute, we see no reason to conclude that Congress intended to exempt such claims from exclusive review before the MSPB and the Federal Circuit. See Thunder Basin, 510 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 214\xe2\x80\x93215 (concluding that, where administrative Commission\xe2\x80\x99s expertise \xe2\x80\x9ccould be brought to bear\xe2\x80\x9d on appeal, Commission\xe2\x80\x99s exclusive review of alleged statutory violation was appropriate despite its lack of expertise in interpreting a particular statute (internal quotation marks and brackets omitted)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor the foregoing reasons, we conclude that it is fairly discernible that the CSRA review scheme was intended to preclude district court jurisdiction over petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Alito, with whom Justice Ginsburg and Justice Kagan join, dissenting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners are former federal employees who were discharged for failing to register for the military draft as required under \n\n5 U. S. C. \xc2\xa73328. They filed a putative class-action suit in Federal District Court, arguing that the registration requirement is facially unconstitutional because it discriminates on the basis of gender and operates as a bill of attainder. Their complaint sought backpay as well as declaratory and injunctive relief reinstating their employment and preventing the Government from enforcing \xc2\xa73328 against them.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court affirms the dismissal of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 suit on the ground that the Civil Service Reform Act (CSRA) provides an exclusive administrative remedy for claims of wrongful termination brought by covered federal employees. Because the CSRA provides an avenue for employees to pursue their grievances through the Merit Systems Protection Board, the majority concludes, Congress must have intended to remove petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims from the ordinary ambit of the federal courts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe problem with the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning is that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims are a far cry from the type of claim that Congress intended to channel through the Board. The Board\xe2\x80\x99s mission is to adjudicate fact-specific employment disputes within the existing statutory framework. By contrast, petitioners argue that one key provision of that framework is facially unconstitutional. Not only does the Board lack authority to adjudicate facial constitutional challenges, but such challenges are wholly collateral to the type of claims that the Board is authorized to hear.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority attempts to defend its holding by noting that, although the Board cannot consider petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims, petitioners may appeal from the Board to the Federal Circuit, which does have authority to address facial constitutional claims. But that does not cure the oddity of requiring such claims to be filed initially before the Board, which can do nothing but pass them along unaddressed, leaving the Federal Circuit to act as a court of first review, but with little capacity for factfinding.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBecause I doubt that Congress intended to channel petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims into an administrative tribunal that is powerless to decide them, I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs a general matter, federal district courts have \xe2\x80\x9coriginal jurisdiction of all civil actions arising under the Constitution, laws, or treaties of the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331. Under this provision, it has long been \xe2\x80\x9cestablished practice for this Court to sustain the jurisdiction of federal courts to issue injunctions to protect rights safeguarded by the Constitution.\xe2\x80\x9d Bell v. Hood, \n\n327 U. S. 678, \n684 (1946)\n. In light of \xc2\xa71331, the question is not whether Congress has specifically conferred jurisdiction, but whether it has taken it away. See Whitman v. Department of Transportation, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 512, \n514 (2006)\n (per\xc2\xa0curiam).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress may remove certain claims from the general jurisdiction of the federal courts in order to channel these claims into a system of statutory review. For example, in Shalala v. Illinois Council on Long Term Care, Inc., \n\n529 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1 (2000)\n, we considered a clause providing that \xe2\x80\x9cno action .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to recover on any claim\xe2\x80\x9d arising under the Medicare laws \xe2\x80\x9cshall be brought under section 1331 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. of title 28,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 5 (quoting \n\n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7405(h) (brackets omitted)). When dealing with an express preclusion clause like this, we determine the scope of preclusion simply by interpreting the words Congress has chosen.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have also recognized that preclusion can be implied. When Congress creates an administrative process to handle certain types of claims, it impliedly removes those claims from the ordinary jurisdiction of the federal courts. Under these circumstances, the test is whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98statu- tory scheme\xe2\x80\x99 displays a \xe2\x80\x98fairly discernible\xe2\x80\x99 intent to limit jur- isdiction and the claims at issue \xe2\x80\x98are of the type Congress intended to be reviewed within th[e] statutory structure.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Free Enterprise Fund v. Public Company Accounting Oversight Bd., 561 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 8) (quoting Thunder Basin Coal Co. v. Reich, \n\n510 U.\xc2\xa0S. 200, \n207, \n212 (1994)\n; alteration in Free Enterprise). In making this determination, we look to \xe2\x80\x9cthe statute\xe2\x80\x99s language, structure, and purpose, its legislative history, and whether the claims can be afforded meaningful review\xe2\x80\x9d through the alternative administrative process that Congress has established. Thunder Basin, supra, at 207 (citation omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have emphasized two important factors for determining whether Congress intended an agency to have exclusive original jurisdiction over a claim. The first is whether the claim falls within the agency\xe2\x80\x99s area of ex- pertise, which would give the agency a comparative advantage over the courts in resolving the claim. \xe2\x80\x9cGenerally, when Congress creates procedures \xe2\x80\x98designed to permit agency expertise to be brought to bear on particular problems,\xe2\x80\x99 those procedures \xe2\x80\x98are to be exclusive.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Free Enterprise Fund, supra, at ___(slip op., at 8) (quoting Whitney Nat. Bank in Jefferson Parish v. Bank of New Orleans & Trust Co., \n\n379 U.\xc2\xa0S. 411, \n420 (1965)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, even if a claim would not benefit from agency expertise, we nonetheless consider whether the claim is le- gally or factually related to the type of dispute the agency is authorized to hear. If so, the claim may be channeled through the administrative process to guard against claim-splitting, which could involve redundant analysis of overlapping issues of law and fact. But for claims that fall outside the agency\xe2\x80\x99s expertise and are \xe2\x80\x9cwholly collateral\xe2\x80\x9d to the type of dispute the agency is authorized to hear, the interest in requiring unified administrative review is considerably reduced. Thunder Basin, supra, at 212 (internal quotation marks omitted); see also Free Enterprise Fund, supra, at ___ \xe2\x80\x93 ___ (slip op., at 8\xe2\x80\x9310).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe CSRA was enacted to \xe2\x80\x9cprovide the people of the United States with a competent, honest, and productive Federal work force reflective of the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s diversity, and to improve the quality of public service.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73(1), \n\n92Stat.\n1112. To that end, the Act created an \xe2\x80\x9cintegrated scheme of administrative and judicial review [of personnel actions], designed to balance the legitimate interests of the various categories of federal employees with the needs of sound and efficient administration.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Fausto, \n\n484 U.\xc2\xa0S. 439, \n445 (1988)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tChapter 75 of Title 5 sets forth detailed procedures for adverse actions taken against certain covered employees \xe2\x80\x9cfor such cause as will promote the efficiency of the service.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n5 U. S. C. \xc2\xa77513(a). When an agency takes such an action, it must provide the employee with advance written notice of the action and the specific reasons for it, give the employee an opportunity to respond, allow the employee to be represented by an attorney, and provide the employee with a final written decision. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77513(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(4). Following these internal agency procedures, an aggrieved employee may appeal to the Merit Systems Protection Board. \xc2\xa77513(d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Board\xe2\x80\x99s mission is \xe2\x80\x9cto ensure that Federal employees are protected against abuses by agency management, that Executive branch agencies make employment decisions in accordance with the merit system principles, and that Federal merit systems are kept free of prohibited personnel practices.\xe2\x80\x9d Merit Systems Protection Board, An Introduction to the Merit Systems Protection Board 5 (1999). The Board adjudicates employment disputes in accordance with applicable federal laws and regulations, including the \xe2\x80\x9c[m]erit system principles\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9c[p]rohibited personnel principles\xe2\x80\x9d identified in \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72301, 2302. After the Board renders a decision, the United States Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit has exclusive jurisdiction on appeal. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa77703(a)(1), (b)(1); \n\n28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71295(a)(9).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe parties agree that petitioners are covered employees who may file an appeal to the Board protesting their re- moval from federal employment. The parties also agree, however, that the Board lacks authority to adjudicate claims like those asserted by petitioners, which attack the validity of a federal statute as a facial matter. As this Court has noted, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]djudication of the constitutionality of congressional enactments has generally been thought beyond the jurisdiction of administrative agencies.\xe2\x80\x9d Thun- der Basin, supra, at 214 (alteration in original; inter- nal quotation marks omitted). The Board itself has consistently taken the position that it lacks \xe2\x80\x9cauthority to determine the constitutionality of statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Malone v. Department of Justice, 14 MSPR 403, 406 (1983) (citing Montana Ch. of Assn. of Civilian Technicians, Inc. v. Young, 514 F.\xc2\xa02d 1165, 1167 (CA9 1975)). Thus, the Board\xe2\x80\x99s own self-described role in the administrative process is simply to apply the relevant statutes as written, without addressing any facial challenges to the validity of those statutes.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere is no basis for the majority\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that petitioners must file their constitutional challenges be- fore the Board instead of a federal district court. Congress has not expressly curtailed the jurisdiction of the federal courts to consider facial constitutional claims relating to federal employment, and no such limitation can be fairly discerned from the CSRA. Not only are petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims \xe2\x80\x9cwholly collateral to [the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s] review provisions and outside the agency\xe2\x80\x99s expertise,\xe2\x80\x9d Thunder Basin, 510 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 212 (internal quotation marks omitted), but the Board itself admits that it is completely powerless to consider the merits of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arguments. In short, neither efficiency nor agency expertise can explain why Congress would want the Board to have exclusive jurisdiction over claims like these. To the contrary, imposing a scheme of exclusive administrative review in this context breeds inefficiency and creates a procedural framework that is needlessly vexing.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners argue that registration for the military draft violates the Equal Protection and Bill of Attainder Clauses. These facial constitutional arguments are entirely outside the Board\xe2\x80\x99s power to decide, and they do not remotely implicate the Board\xe2\x80\x99s administrative expertise. They have nothing to do with the statutory rules of federal employment, and nothing to do with any application of the \xe2\x80\x9cmerit system principles\xe2\x80\x9d or the \xe2\x80\x9cprohibited personnel practices\xe2\x80\x9d that the Board administers.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims also have no relation to any of the facts that might be relevant to a proceeding before the Board. The Board typically addresses factual issues pertaining to the specific circumstances in which employee grievances arise. For example: Why was a particular employee removed from federal employment? Does the employer have a sound, nonprohibited basis for the employment action in question? See, e.g., Davis v. Department of Veterans Affairs, 106 MSPR 654, 657\xe2\x80\x93658 (2007).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBy contrast, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims involve general factual issues pertaining to the facial constitutionality of the military draft. The equal protection question is whether men and women are sufficiently different to justify disparate treatment under the Selective Service Act. Rostker v. Goldberg, \n\n453 U.\xc2\xa0S. 57, \n78 (1981)\n. The factual record that petitioners wish to develop would address issues of gender difference that might be considered relevant to military service. See Brief for Petitioners 48 (alleging that \xe2\x80\x9cwomen\xe2\x80\x99s role in the military has changed dramatically in the past thirty years\xe2\x80\x9d). Likewise, under the Bill of Attainder Clause, the key question is whether requiring draft registration as a condition of federal employment amounts to the singling out of a particular person or group for punishment without trial. See Nixon v. Administrator of General Services, \n\n433 U.\xc2\xa0S. 425\n\xe2\x80\x93469. Whatever the relevant facts may be on either claim, it is clear that they can have no conceivable bearing on any matter the Board is authorized to address.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAdministrative agencies typically do not adjudicate facial constitutional challenges to the laws that they administer. Such challenges not only lie outside the realm of special agency expertise, but they are also wholly collateral to other types of claims that the agency is empowered to consider. When \xe2\x80\x9cthe administrative appeals process does not address the kind of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. constitutional claims\xe2\x80\x9d at issue, we cannot infer that Congress intended to \xe2\x80\x9climit[t] judicial review of these claims to the procedures set forth in [the statutory scheme].\xe2\x80\x9d McNary v. Haitian Refugee Center, Inc., \n\n498 U.\xc2\xa0S. 479, \n493 (1991)\n.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSeveral other cases confirm this basic principle. In Free Enterprise Fund, for example, the plaintiffs were not required to pursue their constitutional claims through the Public Accounting Company Oversight Board, because they were challenging the very existence of the Board it- self. 561 U.\xc2\xa0S., at ___ \xe2\x80\x93 ___ (slip op., at 8\xe2\x80\x9310). Likewise, in Johnson v. Robison, \n\n415 U.\xc2\xa0S. 361\n\xe2\x80\x93374 (1974). Where petitioners brought claims \xe2\x80\x9cchallenging the constitutionality of laws providing benefits,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court held that these claims were not precluded by a statute creating exclusive administrative review over how those benefits were administered. And in Mathews v. Eldridge, \n\n424 U. S. 319\n\xe2\x80\x93332 (1976), we held that although a party challenging the denial of statutory benefits was generally required to proceed through the statutory process of administrative review, a constitutional challenge to the ad-ministrative process itself could still be brought directly in federal court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe present case follows the same pattern: Petitioners are challenging the facial validity of a law that the Board is bound to apply to them, and so it makes little sense for them to seek review before the Board.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe wholly collateral nature of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims makes them readily distinguishable from claims that this Court has held to be impliedly excluded from the original jurisdiction of the federal courts. In Fausto, for example, we held that the CSRA precluded a statutory Back Pay Act claim involving a dispute over whether an employee had engaged in unauthorized use of a Government vehicle. 484 U. S., at 455. The plaintiff in that case did not challenge the constitutional validity of the applicable legal framework, but argued instead that the framework had been improperly applied to him. He argued that he had been wrongfully suspended from work, and that he was entitled to backpay as a result. Id., at 440. For that type of fact-specific personnel dispute, we determined, Congress had intended for the CSRA\xe2\x80\x99s comprehensive administrative scheme to provide the exclusive avenue of relief. Id., at 455.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSimilarly, in Bush v. Lucas, \n\n462 U.\xc2\xa0S. 367 (1983)\n, we declined to allow a claim under Bivens v. Six Unknown Fed. Narcotics Agents, \n\n403 U.\xc2\xa0S. 388 (1971)\n, brought by an employee seeking money damages for an alleged \xe2\x80\x9cretaliatory demotion or discharge because he ha[d] exercised his \n \nFirst Amendment rights.\xe2\x80\x9d 462 U. S., at 381. Although the claim was constitutional in nature, we noted that it \xe2\x80\x9car[o]se out of an employment relationship that is governed by comprehensive procedural and substantive provisions\xe2\x80\x9d that had been enacted by Congress. Id., at 368. The employee was pursuing an as-applied challenge that depended on the case-specific facts of why he had been fired. The gravamen of the employee\xe2\x80\x99s claim was that he had been \xe2\x80\x9cunfairly disciplined for making critical comments about [his agency].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 386. Under the statu- tory scheme that Congress had created, the employee could have pursued a very similar statutory claim for wrongful removal within the administrative process. Id., at 386\xe2\x80\x93388. Under these circumstances, we found that Congress did not intend to allow a duplicative nonstatutory claim for damages based on the same set of underlying facts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on Thunder Basin is entirely misplaced. See ante, at 13. In that case, we found that a statutory scheme impliedly precluded a pre-enforcement challenge brought by a mining company seeking to enjoin an order issued by the Mine Safety and Health Administration. 510 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 205. Importantly, the plaintiff company was seeking review of purely statutory claims that were reviewable in the first instance by the administrative commission that Congress had established. The only constitutional issue was a matter of timing: The company argued that it had a due process right to immediate judicial review of its statutory claims, because it would suffer irreparable harm if it were forced to wait until after the agency initiated an enforcement action. Ibid. The Court disagreed, holding that the statutory scheme was \xe2\x80\x9cconsistent with due process\xe2\x80\x9d even though it provided for only postenforcement review. Id., at 218. Thus, the Court rejected the company\xe2\x80\x99s constitutional claim \xe2\x80\x9cnot on preclusion grounds but on the merits.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 219 (Scalia, J., concurring in part and concurring in judgment). The heart of the preclusion analysis was that the company could not use a preenforcement challenge to obtain judicial review of statutory claims that Congress had clearly intended to channel into administrative review.*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBy requiring facial constitutional claims to be filed before the Board, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s holding sets up an odd sequence of procedural hoops for petitioners to jump through. As the Government concedes, the Board is powerless to adjudicate facial constitutional claims, and so these claims cannot be addressed on the merits until they reach the Federal Circuit on appeal. As a result, the Federal Circuit will be forced to address the claims in the first instance, without the benefit of any relevant factfinding at the administrative level. This is a strange result, because \xe2\x80\x9cstatutes that provide for only a single level of judicial review in the courts of appeals are traditionally viewed as warranted only in circumstances where district court factfinding would unnecessarily duplicate an adequate administrative record.\xe2\x80\x9d McNary, 498 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 497 (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Government admits that the absence of first-tier factfinding might very well result in \xe2\x80\x9cthe initial record\xe2\x80\x9d being \xe2\x80\x9cinsufficient to permit meaningful consideration of a constitutional claim,\xe2\x80\x9d but suggests that the court could always \xe2\x80\x9cremand the case to the [Board] for further factual development.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 41. The majority accepts this solution, ante, at 15, but it is hard to see how it will work in practice. Without any authority to decide merits issues, the Board may find it difficult to adjudicate disputes about the relevancy of evidence sought in discovery. Nor will the Board find it easy to figure out which facts it must find before sending the case back to the Federal Circuit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven if these problems can be overcome, that will not resolve the needless complexity of the majority\xe2\x80\x99s approach. According to the majority, petitioners should file their claims with the Board, which must then kick the claims up to the Federal Circuit, which must then remand the claims back to the Board, which must then develop the record and send the case back to the Federal Circuit, which can only then consider the constitutional issues.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo be sure, this might be sufficient to afford \xe2\x80\x9cmeaningful review\xe2\x80\x9d of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims, ante, at 16\xe2\x80\x9317, but that is not the only consideration. The question is whether it is \xe2\x80\x9cfairly discernible\xe2\x80\x9d that Congress intended to impose these pinball procedural requirements instead of permitting petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims to be decided in a regular lawsuit in federal district court. And why would it? As already noted, the benefits of preventing claim-splitting are considerably reduced with respect to facial constitutional claims that are wholly collateral to an administrative proceeding. Because collateral constitutional claims have no overlap with the issues of law and fact that will pertain to the administrative proceeding, allowing the constitutional claims to be adjudicated separately before a district court does not invite wasteful or duplicative review. It simply allows the district court to develop the factual record and then provide a first-tier legal analysis, thereby enhancing both the quality and efficiency of appellate review.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo the extent that there is some need to prevent claim-splitting, that purpose is already served by ordinary principles of claim preclusion. Plaintiffs generally must bring all claims arising out of a common set of facts in a single lawsuit, and federal district courts have discretion to enforce that requirement as necessary \xe2\x80\x9cto avoid duplicative litigation.\xe2\x80\x9d Colorado River Water Conservation Dist. v. United States, \n\n424 U.\xc2\xa0S. 800, \n817 (1976)\n; Stone v. Department of Aviation, 453 F.\xc2\xa03d 1271, 1278 (CA10 2006) (\xe2\x80\x9cA plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s obligation to bring all related claims together in the same action arises under the common-law rule of claim preclusion prohibiting the splitting of actions\xe2\x80\x9d). See also 18 C. Wright et\xc2\xa0al., Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa74406, p. 40 (2d ed. 2002, Supp. 2011) (discussing \xe2\x80\x9cprinciples of \xe2\x80\x98claim splitting\xe2\x80\x99 that are similar to claim preclusion, but that do not require prior judgment\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, if an aggrieved employee goes to a district court with claims that would duplicate the factfinding or legal analysis of a separate Board proceeding, the district court would be free to dismiss the case.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe majority suggests that its approach will allow the Board to resolve some cases on nonconstitutional grounds, thus avoiding needless adjudication of constitutional is- sues. See ante, at 18. But achieving that goal does not require the blunt instrument of jurisdictional preclusion. District courts have broad discretion to manage their dockets, including the power to refrain from reviewing a constitutional claim pending adjudication of a nonconstitutional claim that might moot the case. See Kerotest Mfg. Co. v. C-O-Two Fire Equipment Co., \n\n342 U. S. 180, \n183 (1952)\n (acknowledging the equitable discretion of courts, in furtherance of \xe2\x80\x9c[w]ise judicial administration\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cconservation of judicial resources,\xe2\x80\x9d to stay proceedings to prevent \xe2\x80\x9ctwo litigations where one will suffice\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). In short, the district courts are well equipped to guard against piecemeal litigation without any help from the majority\xe2\x80\x99s holding.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the majority contends that channeling facial constitutional claims through the Board is necessary to provide \xe2\x80\x9cclear guidance about the proper forum for the employee\xe2\x80\x99s claims at the outset of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 11. Because it can be hard to tell the difference between facial and as-applied challenges, the majority argues, it is less confusing simply to require that all claims must be brought before the Board. This is a red herring. Labels aside, the most sensible rule would be to allow initial judicial review of constitutional claims that attack the validity of a statute based on its inherent characteristics, not as a result of how the statute has been applied. That line is bright enough, and the distinction is already one that the Board must draw based on its own determination that it can hear some as-applied challenges but lacks \xe2\x80\x9cauthority to determine the constitutionality of statutes.\xe2\x80\x9d Malone, 14 MSPR, at 406. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe presumptive power of the federal courts to hear constitutional challenges is well established. In this case, however, the majority relies on a very weak set of inferences to strip the courts of their original jurisdiction over petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 claims. Because I believe Congress would have been very surprised to learn that it implied this result when it passed the CSRA, I respectfully dissent.\n\t\t\t
