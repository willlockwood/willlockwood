IRIZARRY v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

No. 06\xe2\x80\x937517.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 15, 2008\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 12, 2008

\n

Petitioner pleaded guilty to making a threatening interstate communication to his ex-wife, in violation of federal law. Although the presentence report recommended a Federal Sentencing Guidelines range of 41-to-51 months in prison, the court imposed the statutory maximum sentence\xe2\x80\x9460 months in prison and 3 years of supervised release\xe2\x80\x94rejecting petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s objection that he was entitled to notice that the court was contemplating an upward departure. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed, reasoning that Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 32(h), which states that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]efore the court may depart from the applicable sentencing range on a ground not identified \xe2\x80\xa6 either in the presentence report or in a party\xe2\x80\x99s pre-hearing submission, the court must give the parties reasonable notice that it is contemplating such a departure,\xe2\x80\x9d did not apply because the sentence was a variance, not a Guidelines departure.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Rule 32(h) does not apply to a variance from a recommended Guidelines range. At the time that Burns v. United States, 501 U. S. 129, was decided, prompting Rule 32(h)\xe2\x80\x99s promulgation, the Guidelines were mandatory; the Sentencing Reform Act of 1984 prohibited district courts from disregarding most of the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cmechanical dictates,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 133. Confronted with the constitutional problems that might otherwise arise, the Burns Court held that the Rule 32 provision allowing parties to comment on the appropriate sentence\xe2\x80\x94now Rule 32(i)(1)(C)\xe2\x80\x94would be \xe2\x80\x9crender[ed] meaningless\xe2\x80\x9d unless the defendant were given notice of a contemplated departure. Id. at 135\xe2\x80\x93136. Any constitutionally protected expectation that a defendant will receive a sentence within the presumptively applicable Guidelines range did not, however, survive United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220, which invalidated the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x99 mandatory features. Faced with advisory Guidelines, neither the Government nor the defendant may place the same degree of reliance on the type of \xe2\x80\x9cexpectancy\xe2\x80\x9d that gave rise to a special need for notice in Burns. Indeed, a sentence outside the Guidelines carries no presumption of unreasonableness. Gall v. United States, 552 U. S. ___, ___. Thus, the due process concerns motivating the Court to require notice in a mandatory Guidelines world no longer provide a basis for extending the Burns rule either through an interpretation of Rule 32(h) itself or through Rule 32(i)(C)(1). Nor does the rule apply to 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553 variances by its terms. Although the Guidelines, as the \xe2\x80\x9cstarting point and the initial benchmark,\xe2\x80\x9d continue to play a role in the sentencing determination, see Gall, 552 U. S., at ___, there is no longer a limit comparable to the one in Burns on variances from Guidelines ranges that a district court may find justified. This Court is confident that district judges and counsel have the ability\xe2\x80\x94especially in light of Rule 32\xe2\x80\x99s other procedural protections\xe2\x80\x94to make sure that all relevant matters relating to a sentencing decision have been considered before a final determination is made. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x938.

\n

458 F.\xc2\xa03d 1208, affirmed.

\n

RICHARD IRIZARRY, PETITIONER v. UNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit

\n

[June 12, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rule 32(h) of the Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure, promulgated in response to our decision in Burns v. United States, 501 U. S. 129 (1991), states that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]efore the court may depart from the applicable sentencing range on a ground not identified for departure either in the presentence report or in a party\xe2\x80\x99s prehearing submission, the court must give the parties reasonable notice that it is contemplating such a departure.\xe2\x80\x9d The question presented by this case is whether that Rule applies to every sentence that is a variance from the recommended Federal Sen-tencing Guidelines range even though not considered a \xe2\x80\x9cdeparture\xe2\x80\x9d as that term was used when Rule 32(h) was promulgated.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner, Richard Irizarry, pleaded guilty to one count of making a threatening interstate communication, in violation of 18 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7875(c). Petitioner made the following admissions in the factual resume accompanying his plea: (1) On November 5, 2003, he sent an e-mail threatening to kill his ex-wife and her new husband; (2) he had sent \xe2\x80\x9cdozens\xe2\x80\x9d of similar e-mails in violation of a restraining order; (3) he intended the e-mails to \xe2\x80\x9cconvey true threats to kill or injure multiple persons\xe2\x80\x9d; and (4) at all times he acted knowingly and willfully. App. 273\xe2\x80\x93275.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The presentence report (PSR), in addition to describing the threatening e-mails, reported that petitioner had asked another inmate to kill his ex-wife\xe2\x80\x99s new husband. Brief for United States 6. The PSR advised against an adjustment for acceptance of responsibility and recommended a Guidelines sentencing range of 41-to-51 months of imprisonment, based on enhancements for violating court protective orders, making multiple threats, and intending to carry out those threats. Brief for Petitioner 9. As possible grounds for a departure, the probation officer stated that petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s criminal history category might not adequately reflect his \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98past criminal conduct or the likelihood that [petitioner] will commit other crimes.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government made no objection to the PSR, but advised the court that it intended to call petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s ex-wife as a witness at the sentencing hearing. App. 293. Petitioner objected to the PSR\xe2\x80\x99s application of the enhancement based on his intention to carry out the threats and its rejection of an adjustment for acceptance of responsibility. Id., at 295\xe2\x80\x93296.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Four witnesses testified at the sentencing hearing. Id., at 299. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s ex-wife described incidents of domestic violence, the basis for the restraining order against petitioner, and the threats petitioner made against her and her family and friends. Id., at 307, 309, 314. She emphasized at some length her genuine concern that petitioner fully intended to carry out his threats. Id., at 320. A special agent of the Federal Bureau of Investigation was called to describe documents recovered from petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s vehicle when he was arrested; those documents indicated he intended to track down his ex-wife and their children. Id., at 326\xe2\x80\x93328. Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s cellmate next testified that petitioner \xe2\x80\x9cwas obsessed with the idea of getting rid of\xe2\x80\x9d his ex-wife\xe2\x80\x99s husband. Id., at 336. Finally, petitioner testified at some length, stating that he accepted responsibility for the e-mails, but that he did not really intend to carry out his threats. Id., at 361. Petitioner also denied speaking to his cellmate about killing his ex-wife\xe2\x80\x99s husband. Id., at 356\xe2\x80\x93357.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After hearing from counsel, the trial judge delivered a thoughtful oral decision, which included findings resolving certain disputed issues of fact. She found that petitioner had deliberately terrorized his ex-wife, that he intended to carry out one or more of his threats, \xe2\x80\x9cthat he still intends to terrorize Ms. Smith by whatever means he can and that he does not accept responsibility for what he has done.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 372. After giving both petitioner and counsel\nan opportunity to make further comment, the judge\nconcluded:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cI\xe2\x80\x99ve considered all of the evidence presented today, I\xe2\x80\x99ve considered everything that\xe2\x80\x99s in the presentence report, and I\xe2\x80\x99ve considered the statutory purpose of sentencing and the sentencing guideline range. I find the guideline range is not appropriate in this case. I find Mr. Irizarry\xe2\x80\x99s conduct most disturbing. I am sincerely convinced that he will continue, as his ex-wife testified, in this conduct regardless of what this court does and regardless of what kind of supervision he\xe2\x80\x99s under. And based upon that, I find that the maximum time that he can be incapacitated is what is best for society, and therefore the guideline range, I think, is not high enough.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cThe guideline range goes up to 51 months, which is only nine months shorter than the statutory maximum. But I think in Mr. Irizarry\xe2\x80\x99s case the statutory maximum is what\xe2\x80\x99s appropriate, and that\xe2\x80\x99s what I\xe2\x80\x99m going to sentence him.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 374\xe2\x80\x93375.

\n

\nThe court imposed a sentence of 60 months of imprisonment to be followed by a 3-year term of supervised release. Id., at 375.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Defense counsel then raised the objection that presents the issue before us today. He stated, \xe2\x80\x9cWe didn\xe2\x80\x99t have notice of [the court\xe2\x80\x99s] intent to upwardly depart. What the law is on that now with\xe2\x80\x94,\xe2\x80\x9d to which the Court responded, \xe2\x80\x9cI think the law on that is out the window\xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0You had notice that the guidelines were only advisory and the court could sentence anywhere within the statutory range.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 377.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s sentence, reasoning that Rule 32(h) did not apply because \xe2\x80\x9cthe above-guidelines sentence imposed by the district court in this case was a variance, not a guidelines departure.\xe2\x80\x9d 458 F.\xc2\xa03d 1208, 1211 (2006) (per curiam). The Court of Appeals declined to extend the rule to variances. \xe2\x80\x9cAfter [United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220 (2005),] parties are inherently on notice that the sentencing guidelines range is advisory. . . . Given Booker, parties cannot claim unfair surprise or inability to present informed comment.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1212.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the Courts of Appeals are divided with respect to the applicability of Rule 32(h) to Guidelines variances,[Footnote 1] we granted certiorari. 552 U. S. ___ (2008). We now affirm.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the time of our decision in Burns, the Guidelines were mandatory; the Sentencing Reform Act of 1984, \xc2\xa7211 et seq., 98 Stat. 1987, prohibited district courts from disregarding \xe2\x80\x9cthe mechanical dictates of the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x9d except in narrowly defined circumstances. 501 U. S., at 133. Confronted with the constitutional problems that might otherwise arise, we held that the provision of Rule 32 that allowed parties an opportunity to comment on the appropriate sentence\xe2\x80\x94now Rule 32(i)(1)(C)\xe2\x80\x94would be \xe2\x80\x9crender[ed] meaningless\xe2\x80\x9d unless the defendant were given notice of any contemplated departure. Id., at 135\xe2\x80\x93136. Justice Souter disagreed with our conclusion with respect to the text of Rule 32 and conducted a due process analysis. Id., at 147 (dissenting opinion).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Any expectation subject to due process protection at the time we decided Burns that a criminal defendant would receive a sentence within the presumptively applicable guideline range did not survive our decision in United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220 (2005), which invalidated the mandatory features of the Guidelines. Now faced with advisory Guidelines, neither the Government nor the defendant may place the same degree of reliance on the type of \xe2\x80\x9cexpectancy\xe2\x80\x9d that gave rise to a special need for notice in Burns. Indeed, a sentence outside the Guidelines carries no presumption of unreasonableness. Gall v. United States, 552 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 12); see also Rita v. United States, 551 U. S. ___ (2007).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is, therefore, no longer the case that \xe2\x80\x9cwere we to read Rule 32 to dispense with notice [of a contemplated non-Guidelines sentence], we would then have to confront the serious question whether [such] notice in this setting is mandated by the Due Process Clause.\xe2\x80\x9d Burns, 501 U. S., at 138. The due process concerns that motivated the Court to require notice in a world of mandatory Guidelines no longer provide a basis for this Court to extend the rule set forth in Burns either through an interpretation of Rule 32(h) itself or through Rule 32(i)(1)(C). And contrary to what the dissent argues, post, at 1\xe2\x80\x932 (opinion of Breyer, J.), the rule does not apply to \xc2\xa73553 variances by its terms. \xe2\x80\x9cDeparture\xe2\x80\x9d is a term of art under the Guidelines and refers only to non-Guidelines sentences imposed under the framework set out in the Guidelines.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The notice requirement set out in Burns applied to a narrow category of cases. The only relevant departures were those authorized by 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(b) (1988 ed.), which required \xe2\x80\x9can aggravating or mitigating circumstance of a kind, or to a degree, not adequately taken into consideration by the Sentencing Commission in formulating the guidelines that should result in a sentence different from that described.\xe2\x80\x9d That determination could only be made based on \xe2\x80\x9cthe sentencing guidelines, policy statements, and official commentary of the Sentencing Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And the notice requirement only applied to the subcategory of those departures that were based on \xe2\x80\x9ca ground not identified as a ground for . . . departure either in the presentence report or in a pre-hearing submission.\xe2\x80\x9d Burns, 501 U. S., at 138\xe2\x80\x93139; see also Fed. Rule Crim. Proc.\xc2\xa032(h). Although the Guidelines, as the \xe2\x80\x9cstarting point and the initial benchmark,\xe2\x80\x9d continue to play a role in the sentencing determination, see Gall, 552 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 11), there is no longer a limit comparable to the one at issue in Burns on the variances from Guidelines ranges that a District Court may find justified under the sentencing factors set forth in 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a) (2000 ed. and Supp. V).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rule 32(i)(1)(C) requires the district court to allow the parties to comment on \xe2\x80\x9cmatters relating to an appropriate sentence,\xe2\x80\x9d and given the scope of the issues that may be considered at a sentencing hearing, a judge will normally be well-advised to withhold her final judgment until after the parties have had a full opportunity to present their evidence and their arguments. Sentencing is \xe2\x80\x9ca fluid and dynamic process and the court itself may not know until the end whether a variance will be adopted, let alone on what grounds.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Vega-Santiago, 519 F.\xc2\xa03d 1, 4 (CA1 2008) (en banc). Adding a special notice requirement whenever a judge is contemplating a variance may create unnecessary delay; a judge who concludes during the sentencing hearing that a variance is appropriate may be forced to continue the hearing even where the content of the Rule 32(h) notice would not affect the parties\xe2\x80\x99 presentation of argument and evidence. In the case before us today, even if we assume that the judge had contemplated a variance before the sentencing hearing began, the record does not indicate that a statement announcing that possibility would have changed the parties\xe2\x80\x99 presentations in any material way; nor do we think it would in most cases. The Government admits as much in arguing that the error here was harmless. Brief for United States 37\xe2\x80\x9338.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sound practice dictates that judges in all cases should make sure that the information provided to the parties in advance of the hearing, and in the hearing itself, has given them an adequate opportunity to confront and debate the relevant issues. We recognize that there will be some cases in which the factual basis for a particular sentence will come as a surprise to a defendant or the Government. The more appropriate response to such a problem is not to extend the reach of Rule 32(h)\xe2\x80\x99s notice requirement categorically, but rather for a district judge to consider granting a continuance when a party has a legitimate basis for claiming that the surprise was prejudicial. As Judge Boudin has noted,

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn the normal case a competent lawyer . . . will anticipate most of what might occur at the sentencing hearing\xe2\x80\x94based on the trial, the pre-sentence report, the exchanges of the parties concerning the report, and the preparation of mitigation evidence. Garden variety considerations of culpability, criminal history, likelihood of re-offense, seriousness of the crime, nature of the conduct and so forth should not generally come as a surprise to trial lawyers who have prepared for sentencing.\xe2\x80\x9d Vega-Santiago, 519 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 5.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The fact that Rule 32(h) remains in effect today does not justify extending its protections to variances; the justification for our decision in Burns no longer exists and such an extension is apt to complicate rather than to simplify sentencing procedures. We have confidence in the ability of district judges and counsel\xe2\x80\x94especially in light of Rule 32\xe2\x80\x99s other procedural protections[Footnote 2]\xe2\x80\x94to make sure that all relevant matters relating to a sentencing decision have been considered before the final sentencing determination is made.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is affirmed.

\n

It is so ordered.

Mejia-Huerta, 480 F.\xc2\xa03d 713 (CA5 2007); United States v. Long Soldier, 431 F.\xc2\xa03d 1120 (CA8 2005); and United States v. Walker, 447 F.\xc2\xa03d 999, 1006 (CA7 2006), with United States v. Anati, 457 F.\xc2\xa03d 233 (CA2 2006); United States v. Davenport, 445 F.\xc2\xa03d 366 (CA4 2006); United States v. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas, concurring.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Earlier this Term, I explained that because \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no principled way to apply the Booker remedy,\xe2\x80\x9d it is \xe2\x80\x9cbest to apply the statute as written, including 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(b), which makes the [Federal Sentencing] Guidelines mandatory.\xe2\x80\x9d Kimbrough v. United States, 552 U. S. ___, ___ (2007) (slip op., at 3) (dissenting opinion) (referencing United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220, 258\xe2\x80\x93265 (2005)); see also Gall v. United States, 552 U. S. ___ (2007) (slip op., at 1) (Thomas, J., dissenting) (applying the Guidelines as mandatory). Consistent with that view, I would hold that the District Court committed statutory error when it imposed a sentence at \xe2\x80\x9cvariance\xe2\x80\x9d with the Guidelines in a manner not authorized by the text of the Guidelines, which permit sentences outside the Guidelines, or \xe2\x80\x9cdepartures,\xe2\x80\x9d only when certain aggravating or mitigating circumstances are present. See United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa71B1.1 (Nov. 2007). But the issue whether such post-Booker \xe2\x80\x9cvariances\xe2\x80\x9d are permissible is not currently before us.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rather, we are presented with the narrow question whether Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 32(h) requires a judge to give notice before he imposes a sentence outside the Guidelines on a ground not identified in the presentence report or in a prehearing submission by the Government. I agree with the Court that neither Rule 32(h) nor Burns v. United States, 501 U. S. 129 (1991), compels a judge to provide notice before imposing a sentence at \xe2\x80\x9cvariance\xe2\x80\x9d with the post-Booker advisory Guidelines, ante, at 8. Each addresses only \xe2\x80\x9cdepartures\xe2\x80\x9d under the mandatory Guidelines and does not contemplate the drastic changes to federal sentencing wrought by the Booker remedy. For this reason, I join the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion.

[June 12, 2008]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Breyer, with whom Justice Kennedy, Justice Souter, and Justice Ginsburg join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 32(h) says:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cBefore the court may depart from the applicable sentencing range on a ground not identified for departure either in the presentence report or in a party\xe2\x80\x99s prehearing submission, the court must give the parties reasonable notice that it is contemplating such a departure.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\nThe question before us is whether this Rule applies when a sentencing judge decides, pursuant to 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a) (2000 ed. and Supp. V), to impose a sentence that is a \xe2\x80\x9cvariance\xe2\x80\x9d from the advisory Guidelines, but is not a \xe2\x80\x9cdeparture\xe2\x80\x9d within the Guidelines. The Court says that the Rule does not apply. I disagree.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court creates a legal distinction without much of a difference. The Rule speaks specifically of \xe2\x80\x9cdeparture[s],\xe2\x80\x9d but I see no reason why that term should not be read to encompass what the Court calls \xc2\xa73553(a) \xe2\x80\x9cvariances.\xe2\x80\x9d The Guidelines define \xe2\x80\x9cdeparture\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cimposition of a sentence outside the applicable guideline range or of a sentence that is otherwise different from the guideline sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual (USSG), \xc2\xa71B1.1, comment., n.\xc2\xa01(E) (Nov. 2007). So-called variances fall comfortably within this definition. Variances are also consistent with the ordinary meaning of the term \xe2\x80\x9cdeparture.\xe2\x80\x9d See, e.g., Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 604 (1993) (defining \xe2\x80\x9cdeparture\xe2\x80\x9d to mean a \xe2\x80\x9cdeviation or divergence esp. from a rule\xe2\x80\x9d (def. 5a)). And conceptually speaking, the substantive difference between a \xe2\x80\x9cvariance\xe2\x80\x9d and a \xe2\x80\x9cdeparture\xe2\x80\x9d is nonexistent, as this Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinions themselves make clear. See, e.g., Gall v. United States, 552 U. S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2007) (slip op., at 7\xc5\xbb8) (using the term \xe2\x80\x9cdeparture\xe2\x80\x9d to describe any non-Guideline sentence); Rita v. United States, 551 U. S. ___, ___\xe2\x80\x93___ (2007) (slip op., at 10) (stating that courts \xe2\x80\x9cmay depart (either pursuant to the Guidelines or, since Booker, by imposing a non-Guidelines\nsentence)\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, when Rule 32(h) was written, its drafters had only Guidelines-authorized departures in mind: Rule 32(h) was written after the Guidelines took effect but before this Court decided United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220 (2005). Yet the language of a statute or a rule, read in light of its purpose, often applies to circumstances that its authors did not then foresee. See, e.g., Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc., 523 U. S. 75, 79\xe2\x80\x9380 (1998).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0And here, the purpose behind Rule 32(h) requires that the Rule be construed to apply to variances. That Rule was added to \xe2\x80\x9creflect\xe2\x80\x9d our decision in Burns v. United States, 501 U. S. 129 (1991). See Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 32, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p.\xc2\xa01141 (2000 ed., Supp. II). (2002 Amendments). In Burns, the Court focused upon \xe2\x80\x9cthe extraordinary case in which the district court, on its own initiative and contrary to the expectations both the defendant and the Government, decides that the factual and legal predicates for a departure are satisfied.\xe2\x80\x9d 501 U. S., at 135. The Court held that \xe2\x80\x9cbefore a district court can depart upward on a ground not identified as a ground for upward departure either in the presentence report or in a prehearing submission by the Government \xe2\x80\xa6 the district court [must] give the parties reasonable notice that it is contemplating such a ruling.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 138.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our holding in Burns was motivated, in part, by a desire to avoid due process concerns. See 501 U. S., at 138 (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]ere we to read Rule 32 to dispense with notice, we would then have to confront the serious question whether notice in this setting is mandated by the Due Process Clause\xe2\x80\x9d). That is perhaps why the majority today suggests that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]ny expectation subject to due process protection at the time we decided Burns\xe2\x80\x9d failed to survive Booker. Ante, at 5. But the due process concern was not the only reason for our holding in Burns, nor was it even the primary one. Rather, the Court principally based its decision upon Rule 32\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that parties be given \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98an opportunity to comment upon \xe2\x80\xa6 matters relating to the appropriate sentence.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 501 U. S., at 135 (citing then-Rule 32(a)(1)). \xe2\x80\x9cObviously,\xe2\x80\x9d the Court said, whether a sU. S.onte departure was warranted was a \xe2\x80\x9cmatter relating to the appropriate sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (internal quotation marks omitted). To deprive the parties of notice of such a departure would thus \xe2\x80\x9crende[r] meaningless\xe2\x80\x9d their right to comment on \xe2\x80\x9cmatters relating to the appropriate sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 136 (internal quotation marks omitted). Notice, the Court added, was \xe2\x80\x9cessential to assuring procedural fairness.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 138.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Burns also relied on what the Court described as Rule 32\xe2\x80\x99s overall purpose of \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[ing] for focused, adversarial development of the factual and legal issues\xe2\x80\x9d related to sentencing. Id., at 134. This could be gleaned, inter alia, from the requirement that parties be given an opportunity to file responses or objections to the presentence report and from the requirement that parties be given an opportunity to speak at the sentencing proceeding. Ibid. Construing Rule 32 not to require notice of sU. S.onte departures, the Court reasoned, would be \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with Rule 32\xe2\x80\x99s purpose of promoting focused, adversarial resolution\xe2\x80\x9d of sentencing issues. Id., at 137.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The primary grounds for the Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Burns apply with equal force to the variances we consider here. Today, Rule 32(i)(1)(C) provides a virtually identical requirement that the district court \xe2\x80\x9callow the parties\xe2\x80\x99 attorneys to comment on the probation officer\xe2\x80\x99s determinations and other matters relating to an appropriate sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d (Emphasis added.) To deprive the parties of notice of previously unidentified grounds for a variance would today \xe2\x80\x9crende[r] meaningless\xe2\x80\x9d the parties\xe2\x80\x99 right to comment on \xe2\x80\x9cmatters relating to [an] appropriate sentence.\xe2\x80\x9d Burns, 501 U. S., at 136 (internal quotation marks omitted). To deprive the parties of notice would today subvert Rule 32\xe2\x80\x99s purpose of \xe2\x80\x9cpromoting focused, adversarial resolution\xe2\x80\x9d of sentencing issues. In a word, it is not fair. Id., at 137.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Seeking to overcome the fact that text, purpose, and precedent are not on its side, the majority makes two practical arguments in its defense. First, it says that notice is unnecessary because \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no longer a limit comparable to the one at issue in Burns\xe2\x80\x9d as to the number of reasons why a district court might sU. S.onte impose a sentence outside the applicable range. Ante, at 6. Is that so? Courts, while now free to impose sentences that vary from a Guideline-specified range, have always been free to depart from such a range. See USSG ch.\xc2\xa01, pt.\xc2\xa0A, \xc2\xa74(b) (Nov. 1987), reprinted in \xc2\xa71A1.1 comment., editorial note (Nov. 2007) (suggesting broad departure authority). Indeed, even Burns recognized that \xe2\x80\x9cthe Guidelines place essentially no limit on the number of potential factors that may warrant a departure.\xe2\x80\x9d 501 U. S., 136\xe2\x80\x93137 (citing USSG ch.\xc2\xa01, pt.\xc2\xa0A, \xc2\xa74(b) (1990)). Regardless, if Booker expanded the number of grounds on which a district court may impose a non-Guideline sentence, that would seem to be an additional argument in favor of, not against, giving the parties notice of the district court\xe2\x80\x99s intention to impose a non-Guideline sentence for some previously unidentified reason. Notice, after all, would promote \xe2\x80\x9cfocused, adversarial\xe2\x80\x9d litigation at sentencing. Burns, supra, at 134, 137.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, the majority fears that a notice requirement would unnecessarily \xe2\x80\x9cdelay\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccomplicate\xe2\x80\x9d sentencing. Ante, at 7, 8. But this concern seems exaggerated. Rule 32(h) applies only where the court seeks to depart on a ground not previously identified by the presentence report or the parties\xe2\x80\x99 presentencing submissions. And the Solicitor General, after consulting with federal prosecutors, tells us that \xe2\x80\x9cin the vast majority of cases in which a district court imposes a sentence outside the Guidelines range, the grounds for the variance have previously been identified by the [presentence report] or the parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 32.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the remaining cases, notice does not necessarily mean delay. The parties may well be prepared to address the point and a meaningful continuance of sentencing would likely be in order only where a party would adduce additional evidence or brief an unconsidered legal issue. Further, to the extent that district judges find a notice requirement to complicate sentencing, those judges could make use of Rule 32(d)(2)(F), which enables them to require that presentence reports address the sentence that would be appropriate in light of the \xc2\xa73553(a) factors (including, presumably, whether there exist grounds for imposing a non-Guidelines sentence). If a presentence report includes a section on whether a variance would be appropriate under \xc2\xa73553(a), that would likely eliminate the possibility that the district court would wind up imposing a non-Guidelines sentence for some reason not previously identified.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, if notice still produced some burdens and delay, fairness justifies notice regardless. Indeed, the Government and the defendant here\xe2\x80\x94the parties most directly affected by sentencing\xe2\x80\x94both urge the Court to find a notice requirement. Clearly they recognize, as did the Court in Burns, that notice is \xe2\x80\x9cessential to assuring procedural fairness\xe2\x80\x9d at sentencing. 501 U. S., at 138.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I believe that Rule 32(h) provides this procedural safeguard. And I would vacate and remand to the Court of Appeals so that it could determine whether the petitioner received the required notice and, if not, act accordingly.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I respectfully dissent.
