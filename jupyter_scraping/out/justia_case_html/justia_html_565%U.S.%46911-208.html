\n\t\t\t\tSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tDARIN RYBURN, et\xc2\xa0al. v. GEORGE R. HUFF, et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\ton petition for writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x93208.\xe2\x80\x83Decided January 23, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPer Curiam.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners Darin Ryburn and Edmundo Zepeda, along with two other officers from the Burbank Police Department, responded to a call from Bellarmine-Jefferson High School in Burbank, California. When the officers arrived at the school, the principal informed them that a stu- dent, Vincent Huff, was rumored to have written a letter threatening to \xe2\x80\x9cshoot up\xe2\x80\x9d the school. App. to Pet. for Cert. 2. The principal reported that many parents, after hearing the rumor, had decided to keep their children at home. Ibid. The principal expressed concern for the safety of her students and requested that the officers investigate the threat. Id., at 42, 54\xe2\x80\x9355.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the course of conducting interviews with the principal and two of Vincent\xe2\x80\x99s classmates, the officers learned that Vincent had been absent from school for two days and that he was frequently subjected to bullying. Id., at 2. The officers additionally learned that one of Vincent\xe2\x80\x99s classmates believed that Vincent was capable of carrying out the alleged threat. Id., at 44. The officers found Vincent\xe2\x80\x99s absences from school and his history of being subjected to bullying as cause for concern. The officers had received training on targeted school violence and were aware that these characteristics are common among perpetrators of school shootings. Id., at 56\xe2\x80\x9358, 63.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe officers decided to continue the investigation by interviewing Vincent. When the officers arrived at Vincent\xe2\x80\x99s house, Officer Zepeda knocked on the door and announced several times that the officers were with the Burbank Police Department. No one answered the door or otherwise responded to Officer Zepeda\xe2\x80\x99s knocks. Sergeant Ryburn then called the home telephone. The officers could hear the phone ringing inside the house, but no one answered. Id., at 2.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSergeant Ryburn next tried calling the cell phone of Vincent\xe2\x80\x99s mother, Mrs. Huff. When Mrs. Huff answered the phone, Sergeant Ryburn identified himself and inquired about her location. Mrs. Huff informed Sergeant Ryburn that she was inside the house. Sergeant Ryburn then inquired about Vincent\xe2\x80\x99s location, and Mrs. Huff informed him that Vincent was inside with her. Sergeant Ryburn told Mrs. Huff that he and the other officers were outside and requested to speak with her, but Mrs. Huff hung up the phone. Id., at 2\xe2\x80\x933.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOne or two minutes later, Mrs. Huff and Vincent walked out of the house and stood on the front steps. Officer Zepeda advised Vincent that he and the other officers were there to discuss the threats. Vincent, apparently aware of the rumor that was circulating at his school, responded, \xe2\x80\x9cI can\xe2\x80\x99t believe you\xe2\x80\x99re here for that.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3. Sergeant Ryburn asked Mrs. Huff if they could continue the discussion inside the house, but she refused. Ibid. In Sergeant Ryburn\xe2\x80\x99s experience as a juvenile bureau sergeant, it was \xe2\x80\x9cextremely unusual\xe2\x80\x9d for a parent to decline an officer\xe2\x80\x99s request to interview a juvenile inside. Id., at 3, 73\xe2\x80\x9374. Sergeant Ryburn also found it odd that Mrs. Huff never asked the officers the reason for their visit. Id., at 73\xe2\x80\x9374.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter Mrs. Huff declined Sergeant Ryburn\xe2\x80\x99s request to continue the discussion inside, Sergeant Ryburn asked her if there were any guns in the house. Mrs. Huff responded by \xe2\x80\x9cimmediately turn[ing] around and r[unning] into the house.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 3. Sergeant Ryburn, who was \xe2\x80\x9cscared because [he] didn\xe2\x80\x99t know what was in that house\xe2\x80\x9d and had \xe2\x80\x9cseen too many officers killed,\xe2\x80\x9d entered the house behind her. Id., at 75. Vincent entered the house behind Sergeant Ryburn, and Officer Zepeda entered after Vincent. Officer Zepeda was concerned about \xe2\x80\x9cofficer safety\xe2\x80\x9d and did not want Sergeant Ryburn to enter the house alone. Id., at 3. The two remaining officers, who had been standing out of earshot while Sergeant Ryburn and Officer Zepeda talked to Vincent and Mrs. Huff, entered the house last, on the assumption that Mrs. Huff had given Sergeant Ryburn and Officer Zepeda permission to enter. Id., at 3\xe2\x80\x934.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUpon entering the house, the officers remained in the living room with Mrs. Huff and Vincent. Eventually, Vincent\xe2\x80\x99s father entered the room and challenged the officers\xe2\x80\x99 authority to be there. The officers remained in-side the house for a total of 5 to 10 minutes. During that time, the officers talked to Mr. Huff and Vincent. They did not conduct any search of Mr. Huff, Mrs. Huff, or Vincent, or any of their property. The officers ultimately concluded that the rumor about Vincent was false, and they reported their conclusion to the school. Id., at 4.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Huffs brought this action against the officers under Rev. Stat. \xc2\xa71979, \n42 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71983. The complaint alleges that the officers violated the Huffs\xe2\x80\x99 \n \nFourth Amendment rights by entering their home without a warrant. Following a 2-day bench trial, the District Court entered judgment in favor of the officers. The District Court resolved conflicting testimony regarding Mrs. Huff\xe2\x80\x99s response to Sergeant Ryburn\xe2\x80\x99s inquiry about guns by finding that Mrs. Huff \xe2\x80\x9cimmediately turned around and ran into the house.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 3. The District Court concluded that the officers were entitled to qualified immunity because Mrs. Huff\xe2\x80\x99s odd behavior, combined with the information the officers gathered at the school, could have led reason-able officers to believe \xe2\x80\x9cthat there could be weapons inside the house, and that family members or the officers themselves were in danger.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 6. The District Court noted that \xe2\x80\x9c[w]ithin a very short period of time, the officers were confronted with facts and circumstances giving rise to grave concern about the nature of the danger they were confronting.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 6\xe2\x80\x937. With respect to this kind of \xe2\x80\x9crapidly evolving incident,\xe2\x80\x9d the District Court explained, courts should be especially reluctant \xe2\x80\x9cto fault the police for not obtaining a warrant.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 7.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA divided panel of the Ninth Circuit affirmed the District Court as to the two officers who entered the house on the assumption that Mrs. Huff had consented, but reversed as to petitioners. The majority upheld the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s findings of fact, but disagreed with the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that petitioners were entitled to qualified immunity. The majority acknowledged that police officers are allowed to enter a home without a warrant if they reasonably believe that immediate entry is necessary to protect themselves or others from serious harm, even if the officers lack probable cause to believe that a crime has been or is about to be committed. Id., at 24. But the majority determined that, in this case, \xe2\x80\x9cany belief that the officers or other family members were in serious, im-minent harm would have been objectively unreasonable\xe2\x80\x9d given that \xe2\x80\x9c[Mrs. Huff] merely asserted her right to end her conversation with the officers and returned to her home.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 25.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJudge Rawlinson dissented. She explained that \xe2\x80\x9cthe discrete incident that precipitated the entry in this case was Mrs. Huff\xe2\x80\x99s response to the question regarding whether there were guns in the house.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 31. She faulted the majority for \xe2\x80\x9crecit[ing] a sanitized account of this event\xe2\x80\x9d that differed markedly from the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s findings of fact, which the majority had conceded must be credited. Judge Rawlinson looked to \xe2\x80\x9ccases that specifi- cally address the scenario where officer safety concerns prompted the entry\xe2\x80\x9d and concluded that, under the rationale articulated in those cases, \xe2\x80\x9ca police officer could have reasonably believed that he was justified in making a warrantless entry to ensure that no one inside the house had a gun after Mrs. Huff ran into the house without answering the question of whether anyone had a weapon.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 31, 33, 37.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJudge Rawlinson\xe2\x80\x99s analysis of the qualified immunity issue was correct. No decision of this Court has found a \n \nFourth Amendment violation on facts even roughly comparable to those present in this case. On the contrary, some of our opinions may be read as pointing in the opposition direction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Brigham City v. Stuart, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 398, \n400 (2006)\n, we held that officers may enter a residence without a warrant when they have \xe2\x80\x9can objectively reasonable basis for believing that an occupant is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. imminently threatened with [serious injury].\xe2\x80\x9d We explained that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[t]he need to protect or preserve life or avoid serious injury is justification for what would be otherwise illegal absent an exigency or emergency.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 403 (quoting Mincey v. Arizona, \n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 385, \n392 (1978)\n). In addition, in Georgia v. Randolph, \n\n547 U.\xc2\xa0S. 103, \n118 (2006)\n, the Court stated that \xe2\x80\x9cit would be silly to suggest that the police would commit a tort by entering [a residence] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to determine whether violence .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is about to (or soon will) occur.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA reasonable police officer could read these decisions to mean that the \n \nFourth Amendment permits an officer to en- ter a residence if the officer has a reasonable basis for concluding that there is an imminent threat of violence. In this case, the District Court concluded that petitioners had such an objectively reasonable basis for reaching such a conclusion. The District Court wrote:\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he officers testified that a number of factors led them to be concerned for their own safety and for the safety of other persons in the residence: the unusual behavior of the parents in not answering the door or the telephone; the fact that Mrs. Huff did not inquire about the reason for their visit or express concern that they were investigating her son; the fact that she hung up the telephone on the officer; the fact that she refused to tell them whether there were guns in the house; and finally, the fact that she ran back into the house while being questioned. That behavior, combined with the information obtained at the school\xe2\x80\x94that Vincent was a student who was a victim of bullying, who had been absent from school for two days, and who had threatened to \xe2\x80\x98shoot up\xe2\x80\x99 the school\xe2\x80\x94led the officers to believe that there could be weapons inside the house, and that family members or the officers themselves were in danger.\xe2\x80\x9d App. to Pet. for Cert. 6.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThis belief, the District Court held, was \xe2\x80\x9cobjectively reasonable,\xe2\x80\x9d particularly since the situation was \xe2\x80\x9crapidly evolving\xe2\x80\x9d and the officers had to make quick decisions. Id., at 6\xe2\x80\x937.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe panel majority\xe2\x80\x94far removed from the scene and with the opportunity to dissect the elements of the situation\xe2\x80\x94confidently concluded that the officers really had no reason to fear for their safety or that of anyone else. As the panel majority saw things, it was irrelevant that the Huffs did not respond when the officers knocked on the door and announced their presence and when they called the home phone because the Huffs had no legal obligation to respond to a knock on the door or to answer the phone. The majority attributed no significance to the fact that, when the officers finally reached Mrs. Huff on her cell phone, she abruptly hung up in the middle of their conversation. And, according to the majority, the officers should not have been concerned by Mrs. Huff\xe2\x80\x99s reaction when they asked her if there were any guns in the house because Mrs. Huff \xe2\x80\x9cmerely asserted her right to end her conversation with the officers and returned to her home.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 25.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tConfronted with the facts found by the District Court, reasonable officers in the position of petitioners could have come to the conclusion that there was an imminent threat to their safety and to the safety of others. The Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s contrary conclusion was flawed for numerous reasons.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFirst, although the panel majority purported to accept the findings of the District Court, it changed those findings in several key respects. As Judge Rawlinson correctly observed, \xe2\x80\x9cthe discrete incident that precipitated the entry in this case was Mrs. Huff\xe2\x80\x99s response to the question regarding whether there were guns in the house.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 31. The District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding that Mrs. Huff \xe2\x80\x9cimmediately turned around and ran into the house\xe2\x80\x9d implicitly rejected Mrs. Huff\xe2\x80\x99s contrary testimony that she walked into the house after telling the officers that she was going to get her husband. Id., at 3. The panel majority upheld the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s findings of fact and acknowledged that it could not reverse the District Court simply because it \xe2\x80\x9cmay have weighed the testimony of the witnesses and other evidence in another manner.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 15. But the panel majority\xe2\x80\x99s determination that petitioners were not entitled to qualified immunity rested on an account of the facts that differed markedly from the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s finding. According to the panel majority, Mrs. Huff \xe2\x80\x9cmerely asserted her right to end her conversation with the officers and returned to her home\xe2\x80\x9d after telling the officers \xe2\x80\x9cthat she would go get her husband.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 12, 25.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, the panel majority appears to have taken the view that conduct cannot be regarded as a matter of concern so long as it is lawful. Accordingly, the panel ma- jority concluded that Mrs. Huff\xe2\x80\x99s response to the question whether there were any guns in the house (immediately turning around and running inside) was not a reason for alarm because she was under no legal obligation to continue her conversation with the police. It should go without saying, however, that there are many circumstances in which lawful conduct may portend imminent violence.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThird, the panel majority\xe2\x80\x99s method of analyzing the string of events that unfolded at the Huff residence was entirely unrealistic. The majority looked at each separate event in isolation and concluded that each, in itself, did not give cause for concern. But it is a matter of common sense that a combination of events each of which is mundane when viewed in isolation may paint an alarming picture.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFourth, the panel majority did not heed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s wise admonition that judges should be cautious about second-guessing a police officer\xe2\x80\x99s assessment, made on the scene, of the danger presented by a particular situation. With the benefit of hindsight and calm deliberation, the panel majority concluded that it was unreasonable for petitioners to fear that violence was imminent. But we have instructed that reasonableness \xe2\x80\x9cmust be judged from the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene, rather than with the 20/20 vision of hindsight\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he calculus of reasonableness must embody allowance for the fact that police officers are often forced to make split-second judgments\xe2\x80\x94in circumstances that are tense, uncertain, and rapidly evolving.\xe2\x80\x9d Graham v. Connor, \n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 386\n\xe2\x80\x93397 (1989). Judged from the proper perspective of a reasonable officer forced to make a split-second decision in response to a rapidly unfolding chain of events that culminated with Mrs. Huff turning and running into the house after refusing to answer a question about guns, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 belief that entry was necessary to avoid injury to themselves or others was im- minently reasonable.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn sum, reasonable police officers in petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 position could have come to the conclusion that the \n \nFourth Amendment permitted them to enter the Huff residence if there was an objectively reasonable basis for fearing that violence was imminent. And a reasonable officer could have come to such a conclusion based on the facts as found by the District Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe petition for certiorari is granted, the judgment of the Ninth Circuit is reversed, and the case is remanded for the entry of judgment in favor of petitioners.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\nIt is so ordered.\n\t\t\t
