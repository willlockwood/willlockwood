\n\t\t\t\tFEDERAL TRADE COMMISSION v. PHOEBE PUTNEY HEALTH SYSTEM, INC., et\xc2\xa0al.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the eleventh circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x931160.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 26, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 19, 2013\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tUnder Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s Hospital Authorities Law (Law), political subdivisions may create special-purpose public entities called hospital authorities to provide \xe2\x80\x9cfor the operation and maintenance of needed health care facilities in the several counties and municipalities of th[e] state.\xe2\x80\x9d The Law permits authorities to \xe2\x80\x9cexercise public and essential governmental functions\xe2\x80\x9d and delegates to them numerous general powers, including the ability to acquire and lease hospitals and other public health facilities. Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Hospital Authority of Albany-Dougherty County (Authority) owns Phoebe Putney Memorial Hospital (Memorial), one of two hospitals in the county. The Authority formed two private nonprofit corporations to manage Memorial: Phoebe Putney Health System, Inc. (PPHS) and Phoebe Putney Memorial Hospital, Inc. (PPMH). After the Authority decided to purchase the second hospital in the county and lease it to a subsidiary of PPHS, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) issued an administrative complaint alleging that the transaction would substantially reduce competition in the market for acute-care hospital services, in violation of \xc2\xa75 of the Federal Trade Commission Act and \xc2\xa77 of the Clayton Act. The FTC and Georgia subsequently sued the Authority, PPHS, PPMH, and others (collectively respondents), seeking to enjoin the transaction pending administrative proceedings. The District Court denied the request for a preliminary injunction and granted respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion to dismiss, holding that respondents are immune from antitrust liability under the state-action doctrine. The Eleventh Circuit affirmed. It concluded that the Authority, as a local governmental entity, was entitled to state-action immunity because the challenged anticompetitive conduct was a foreseeable result of the Law. The court reasoned that the state legislature could have readily anticipated an anticompetitive effect, given the breadth of the powers delegated to hospital authorities, particularly leasing and acquisition powers that could lead to consolidation of hospital ownership.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Because Georgia has not clearly articulated and affirmatively expressed a policy allowing hospital authorities to make acquisitions that substantially lessen competition, state-action immunity does not apply. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9319.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0This Court recognized in Parker v. Brown, \n\n317 U.\xc2\xa0S. 341\n\xe2\x80\x93352, that the federal antitrust laws do not prevent States from imposing market restraints \xe2\x80\x9cas an act of government .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. .\xe2\x80\x9d Under the state-action doctrine, immunity from federal antitrust law may extend to nonstate actors carrying out the State\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory program. See, e.g., Patrick v. Burget, \n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 94\n\xe2\x80\x93100. But given the antitrust laws\xe2\x80\x99 values of free enterprise and economic competition, \xe2\x80\x9cstate-action immunity is disfavored,\xe2\x80\x9d FTC v. Ticor Title Ins. Co., \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 621\n, and is recognized only when it is clear that the challenged anticompetitive conduct is undertaken pursuant to the \xe2\x80\x9cState\xe2\x80\x99s own\xe2\x80\x9d regulatory scheme, id., at 635. Immunity will attach only to activities of substate governmental entities that are undertaken pursuant to a \xe2\x80\x9cclearly articulated and affirmatively expressed\xe2\x80\x9d state policy to displace competition. Community Communications Co. v. Boulder, \n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 40\n. A state legislature need not \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly state\xe2\x80\x9d that intent, Hallie v. Eau Claire, \n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 34\n, but the anticompetitive effect must have been the \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeable result\xe2\x80\x9d of what the State authorized, id., at 42. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x939.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 state-action immunity defense fails under the clear-articulation test because there is no evidence the State affirmatively contemplated that hospital authorities would displace competition by consolidating hospital ownership. The Authority\xe2\x80\x99s powers, including its acquisition and leasing powers, mirror general powers routinely conferred by state law on private corporations. More is required to establish state-action immunity; the Authority must show that it has been delegated authority not just to act, but to act or regulate anticompetitively. Columbia v. Omni Outdoor Advertising, Inc., \n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 365\n. In Boulder, this Court concluded that a Colorado law granting municipalities the power to enact ordinances governing local affairs did not satisfy the clear-articulation test, 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 55\xe2\x80\x9356, because, when a State\xe2\x80\x99s position \xe2\x80\x9cis one of mere neutrality respecting the municipal actions challenged as anticompetitive,\xe2\x80\x9d the State cannot be said to have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98contemplated\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d those anticompetitive actions, id., at 55.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat principle controls here. Grants of general corporate power allowing substate governmental entities to participate in a competitive marketplace are typically used without raising federal antitrust concerns, so a State cannot be said to have contemplated that such powers will be used anticompetitively. Here, though the Law allows the Authority to acquire hospitals, it does not clearly articulate and affirmatively express a state policy empowering the Authority to make acquisitions of existing hospitals that will substantially lessen competition. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9310.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0In concluding otherwise, the Eleventh Circuit applied the concept of \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeability\xe2\x80\x9d too loosely. This Court, recognizing that no legislature \xe2\x80\x9ccan be expected to catalog all of the anticipated effects\xe2\x80\x9d of a statute delegating authority to a substate governmental entity, Hallie, 471 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 43, has approached the clear-articulation inquiry practically, but without diluting the ultimate requirement that the State must have affirmatively contemplated the displacement of competition such that the challenged anticompetitive effects can be attributed to the \xe2\x80\x9cstate itself,\xe2\x80\x9d Parker, 317 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 352. Thus, the Court has found a state policy to displace federal antitrust law was sufficiently expressed where the displacement of competition was the inherent, logical, or ordinary result of the exercise of authority delegated by the state legislature. In that scenario, the State must have foreseen and implicitly endorsed the anticompetitive effects as consistent with its policy goals. See Hallie, 471 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 41; Omni, 499 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 373. By contrast, when a State grants an entity a general power to act, it does so against the backdrop of federal antitrust law. Entities might transgress antitrust requirements by exercising their powers anticompetitively, but a reasonable legislature\xe2\x80\x99s ability to anticipate that possibility falls well short of clearly articulating an affirmative state policy to displace competition. The Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s argument, echoed by respondents, that the case falls within the foreseeability standard used in Hallie and Omni is rejected. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(d)\xc2\xa0Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 additional arguments are also unpersuasive. They contend that because hospital authorities are granted unique powers and responsibilities to fulfill Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s objective of providing access to adequate and affordable health care, it was foreseeable that they would decide that the best way to serve their communities was to acquire an existing local hospital, instead of incurring the additional expense and regulatory burden of expanding, or constructing, a facility. But even though the authorities may differ from private corporations offering hospital services, neither the Law nor any other state-law provision clearly articulates a state policy allowing authorities to exercise their general corporate powers without regard to anticompetitive effects. Respondents also contend that when there is doubt about whether the clear-articulation test is satisfied, federal courts should err on the side of recognizing immunity to avoid improper interference with state policy choices. But the Law here is not ambiguous, and respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suggestion is inconsistent with the principle that \xe2\x80\x9cstate-action immunity is disfavored,\xe2\x80\x9d Ticor Title, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 636. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9319.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t663 F.\xc2\xa03d 1369, reversed and remanded.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Sotomayor delivered the opinion of the Court.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder this Court\xe2\x80\x99s state-action immunity doctrine, when a local governmental entity acts pursuant to a clearly articulated and affirmatively expressed state policy to displace competition, it is exempt from scrutiny under the federal antitrust laws. In this case, we must decide whether a Georgia law that creates special-purpose public entities called hospital authorities and gives those entities general corporate powers, including the power to acquire hospitals, clearly articulates and affirmatively expresses a state policy to permit acquisitions that substantially lessen competition. Because Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s grant of general cor- porate powers to hospital authorities does not include permission to use those powers anticompetitively, we hold that the clear-articulation test is not satisfied and state-action immunity does not apply.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 1941, the State of Georgia amended its Constitution to allow political subdivisions to provide health care services. 1941 Ga. Laws p. 50. The State concurrently enacted the Hospital Authorities Law (Law), id., at 241, Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9370 et\xc2\xa0seq. (2012), \xe2\x80\x9cto provide a mechanism for the operation and maintenance of needed health care facilities in the several counties and municipalities of th[e] state.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9376(a). \xe2\x80\x9cThe purpose of the constitutional provision and the statute based thereon was to .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. create an organization which could carry out and make more workable the duty which the State owed to its in- digent sick.\xe2\x80\x9d DeJarnette v. Hospital Auth. of Albany, 195 Ga. 189, 200, 23 S.\xc2\xa0E. 2d 716, 723 (1942) (citations omitted). As amended, the Law authorizes each county and municipality, and certain combinations of counties or municipalities, to create \xe2\x80\x9ca public body corporate and politic\xe2\x80\x9d called a \xe2\x80\x9chospital authority.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9372(a), (d). Hospital authorities are governed by 5- to 9-member boards that are appointed by the governing body of the county or municipality in their area of operation. \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9372(a).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnder the Law, a hospital authority \xe2\x80\x9cexercise[s] public and essential governmental functions\xe2\x80\x9d and is delegated \xe2\x80\x9call the powers necessary or convenient to carry out and effectuate\xe2\x80\x9d the Law\xe2\x80\x99s purposes. \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375. Giving more content to that general delegation, the Law enumerates 27 powers conferred upon hospital authorities, including the power \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o acquire by purchase, lease, or otherwise and to operate projects,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(4), which are defined to include hospitals and other public health facilities, \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9371(5); \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o construct, reconstruct, improve, alter, and repair projects,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(5); \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o lease .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. for operation by others any project\xe2\x80\x9d provided certain conditions are satisfied, \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(7); and \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o establish rates and charges for the services and use of the facilities of the authority,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(10). Hospital authorities may not operate or construct any project for profit, and accordingly they must set rates so as only to cover operating expenses and create reasonable reserves. \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9377.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn the same year that the Law was adopted, the city of Albany and Dougherty County established the Hospital Authority of Albany-Dougherty County (Authority) and the Authority promptly acquired Phoebe Putney Memorial Hospital (Memorial), which has been in operation in Al- bany since 1911. In 1990, the Authority restructured its operations by forming two private nonprofit corporations to manage Memorial: Phoebe Putney Health System, Inc. (PPHS), and its subsidiary, Phoebe Putney Memorial Hospital, Inc. (PPMH). The Authority leased Memorial to PPMH for $1 per year for 40 years. Under the lease, PPMH has exclusive authority over the operation of Memorial, including the ability to set rates for services. Consistent with \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(7), PPMH is subject to lease conditions that require provision of care to the indigent sick and limit its rate of return.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMemorial is one of two hospitals in Dougherty County. The second, Palmyra Medical Center (Palmyra), was estab- lished in Albany in 1971 and is located just two miles from Memorial. At the time suit was brought in this case, Palmyra was operated by a national for-profit hospital network, HCA, Inc. (HCA). Together, Memorial and Palmyra account for 86 percent of the market for acute-care hospital services provided to commercial health care plans and their customers in the six counties surrounding Al- bany. Memorial accounts for 75 percent of that market on its own.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn 2010, PPHS began discussions with HCA about acquiring Palmyra. Following negotiations, PPHS presented the Authority with a plan under which the Authority would purchase Palmyra with PPHS controlled funds and then lease Palmyra to a PPHS subsidiary for $1 per year under the Memorial lease agreement. The Authority unanimously approved the transaction.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Federal Trade Commission (FTC) shortly there- after issued an administrative complaint alleging that the proposed purchase-and-lease transaction would create a virtual monopoly and would substantially reduce competition in the market for acute-care hospital services, in violation of \xc2\xa75 of the Federal Trade Commission Act, \n\n38Stat.\n719, \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa745, and \xc2\xa77 of the Clayton Act, \n\n38Stat.\n731, \n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa718. The FTC, along with the State of Georgia,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t subsequently filed suit against the Authority, HCA, Palmyra, PPHS, PPMH, and the new PPHS subsidiary created to manage Palmyra (collectively respondents), seeking to enjoin the transaction pending administrative proceedings. See 15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa726, 53(b).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe United States District Court for the Middle District of Georgia denied the request for a preliminary injunction and granted respondents\xe2\x80\x99 motion to dismiss. 793 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1356 (2011). The District Court held that respondents are immune from antitrust liability under the state-action doctrine. See\xc2\xa0id., at 1366\xe2\x80\x931381.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit affirmed. 663 F.\xc2\xa03d 1369 (2011). As an initial matter, the court \xe2\x80\x9cagree[d] with the [FTC] that, on the facts alleged, the joint operation of Memorial and Palmyra would substantially lessen competition or tend to create, if not create, a monopoly.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1375. But the court con-cluded that the transaction was immune from antitrust liability. See id., at 1375\xe2\x80\x931378. The Court of Appeals explained that as a local governmental entity, the Authority was entitled to state-action immunity if the challenged anticompetitive conduct was a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98foreseeable result\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d of Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s legislation. Id., at 1375. According to the court, anticompetitive conduct is foreseeable if it could have been \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98reasonably anticipated\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d by the state legislature; it is not necessary, the court reasoned, for an anticompetitive effect to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0be \xe2\x80\x98one that ordinarily occurs, routinely occurs, or is inherently likely to occur as a result of the empowering legislation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1375\xe2\x80\x931376 (quoting FTC v. Hospital Bd. of Directors of Lee Cty., 38 F.\xc2\xa03d 1184, 1188, 1190\xe2\x80\x931191 (CA11 1994)). Applying that standard, the Court of Appeals concluded that the Law contemplated the anticompetitive conduct challenged by the FTC. The court noted the \xe2\x80\x9cimpressive breadth\xe2\x80\x9d of the powers given to hospital authorities, which include traditional powers of private corporations and a few additional capabilities, such as the power to exercise eminent domain. See 663 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1376. More specifically, the court reasoned that the Georgia Legislature must have anticipated that the grant of power to hospital authorities to acquire and lease projects would produce anticompetitive effects because \xe2\x80\x9c[f]oreseeably, acquisitions could consolidate ownership of competing hospitals, eliminating competition between them.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 1377.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals also rejected the FTC\xe2\x80\x99s alternative argument that state-action immunity did not apply because the transaction in substance involved a transfer of control over Palmyra from one private entity to another, with the Authority acting as a mere conduit for the sale to evade antitrust liability. See id., at 1376, n.\xc2\xa012.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe granted certiorari on two questions: whether the Georgia Legislature, through the powers it vested in hospital authorities, clearly articulated and affirmatively expressed a state policy to displace competition in the market for hospital services; and if so, whether state-action immunity is nonetheless inapplicable as a result of the Authority\xe2\x80\x99s minimal participation in negotiating the terms of the sale of Palymra and the Authority\xe2\x80\x99s limited supervision of the two hospitals\xe2\x80\x99 operations. See 567 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2012). Concluding that the answer to the first question is \xe2\x80\x9cno,\xe2\x80\x9d we reverse without reaching the second question.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Parker v. Brown, \n\n317 U.\xc2\xa0S. 341 (1943)\n, this Court held that because \xe2\x80\x9cnothing in the language of the Sherman Act [\n\n15 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71 et\xc2\xa0seq.] or in its history\xe2\x80\x9d suggested that Congress intended to restrict the sovereign capacity of the States to regulate their economies, the Act should not be read to bar States from imposing market restraints \xe2\x80\x9cas an act of government.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 350, 352. Following Parker, we have held that under certain circumstances, immunity from the federal antitrust laws may extend to nonstate actors carrying out the State\xe2\x80\x99s regulatory program. See Patrick v. Burget, \n\n486 U.\xc2\xa0S. 94\n\xe2\x80\x93100 (1988); Southern Motor Carriers Rate Conference, Inc. v. United States, \n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 48\n\xe2\x80\x9357 (1985).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBut given the fundamental national values of free enterprise and economic competition that are embodied in the federal antitrust laws, \xe2\x80\x9cstate-action immunity is disfavored, much as are repeals by implication.\xe2\x80\x9d FTC v. Ticor Title Ins. Co., \n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 621, \n636 (1992)\n. Consistent with this preference, we recognize state-action immunity only when it is clear that the challenged anticompetitive conduct is undertaken pursuant to a regulatory scheme that \xe2\x80\x9cis the State\xe2\x80\x99s own.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 635. Accordingly, \xe2\x80\x9c[c]loser analysis is required when the activity at issue is not directly that of\xe2\x80\x9d the State itself, but rather \xe2\x80\x9cis carried out by others pursuant to state authorization.\xe2\x80\x9d Hoover v. Ronwin, \n\n466 U.\xc2\xa0S. 558, \n568 (1984)\n. When determining whether the anticompetitive acts of private parties are entitled to immunity, we employ a two-part test, requiring first that \xe2\x80\x9cthe challenged restraint .\xc2\xa0. . be one clearly articu- lated and affirmatively expressed as state policy,\xe2\x80\x9d and second that \xe2\x80\x9cthe policy . . . be actively supervised by the State.\xe2\x80\x9d California Retail Liquor Dealers Assn. v. Midcal Aluminum, Inc., \n\n445 U.\xc2\xa0S. 97, \n105 (1980)\n (internal quotation marks omitted).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case involves allegedly anticompetitive conduct undertaken by a substate governmental entity. Because municipalities and other political subdivisions are not themselves sovereign, state-action immunity under Parker does not apply to them directly. See Columbia v. Omni Outdoor Advertising, Inc., \n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 365, \n370 (1991)\n; Lafay- ette v. Louisiana Power & Light Co., \n\n435 U.\xc2\xa0S. 389\n\xe2\x80\x93413 (1978) (plurality opinion). At the same time, however, substate governmental entities do receive immunity from antitrust scrutiny when they act \xe2\x80\x9cpursuant to state policy to displace competition with regulation or monopoly public service.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 413.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t This rule \xe2\x80\x9cpreserves to the States their freedom .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to use their municipalities to administer state regulatory policies free of the inhibitions of the federal antitrust laws without at the same time permitting purely parochial interests to disrupt the Nation\xe2\x80\x99s free-market goals.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 415\xe2\x80\x93416.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs with private parties, immunity will only attach to the activities of local governmental entities if they are undertaken pursuant to a \xe2\x80\x9cclearly articulated and affirmatively expressed\xe2\x80\x9d state policy to displace competition. Community Communications Co. v. Boulder, \n\n455 U.\xc2\xa0S. 40, \n52 (1982)\n. But unlike private parties, such entities are not subject to the \xe2\x80\x9cactive state supervision requirement\xe2\x80\x9d because they have less of an incentive to pursue their own self-interest under the guise of implementing state policies. Hallie v. Eau Claire, \n\n471 U.\xc2\xa0S. 34\n\xe2\x80\x9347 (1985).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9c[T]o pass the \xe2\x80\x98clear articulation\xe2\x80\x99 test,\xe2\x80\x9d a state legislature need not \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly state in a statute or its legislative history that the legislature intends for the delegated action to have anticompetitive effects.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 43. Rather, we explained in Hallie that state-action immunity applies if the anticompetitive effect was the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0foreseeable result\xe2\x80\x9d of what the State authorized. Id., at 42. We applied that principle in Omni, where we concluded that the clear-articulation test was satisfied because the suppression of competition in the billboard market was the foreseeable result of a state statute authorizing municipalities to adopt zoning ordinances regulating the construction of buildings and other structures. 499 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 373.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tApplying the clear-articulation test to the Law before us, we conclude that respondents\xe2\x80\x99 claim for state-action immunity fails because there is no evidence the State affirmatively contemplated that hospital authorities would displace competition by consolidating hospital ownership. The acquisition and leasing powers exercised by the Authority in the challenged transaction, which were the principal powers relied upon by the Court of Appeals in finding state-action immunity, see 663 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1377, mirror general powers routinely conferred by state law upon private corporations.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Other powers possessed by hospital authorities that the Court of Appeals characterized as having \xe2\x80\x9cimpressive breadth,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 1376, also fit this pattern, including the ability to make and execute contracts, \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(3), to set rates for services, \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(10), to sue and be sued, \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(1), to borrow money, \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(17), and the residual authority to exercise any or all powers possessed by private corporations, \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(21).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur case law makes clear that state-law authority to act is insufficient to establish state-action immunity; the substate governmental entity must also show that it has been delegated authority to act or regulate anticompetitively. See Omni, 499 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 372. In Boulder, we held that Colorado\xe2\x80\x99s Home Rule Amendment allowing municipalities to govern local affairs did not satisfy the clear-articulation test. 455 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 55\xe2\x80\x9356. There was no doubt in that case that the city had authority as a matter of state law to pass an ordinance imposing a moratorium on a cable provider\xe2\x80\x99s expansion of service. Id., at 45\xe2\x80\x9346. But we rejected the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9cthe general grant of power to enact ordinances necessarily implies state authorization to enact specific anticompetitive ordinances\xe2\x80\x9d because such an approach \xe2\x80\x9cwould wholly eviscerate the concepts of \xe2\x80\x98clear articulation and affirmative expression\xe2\x80\x99 that our precedents require.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 56. We explained that when a State\xe2\x80\x99s position \xe2\x80\x9cis one of mere neutrality respecting the municipal actions challenged as anticompetitive,\xe2\x80\x9d the State cannot be said to have \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98contemplated\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d those anticompetitive actions. Id., at 55.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe principle articulated in Boulder controls this case. Grants of general corporate power that allow substate governmental entities to participate in a competitive marketplace should be, can be, and typically are used in ways that raise no federal antitrust concerns. As a result, a State that has delegated such general powers \xe2\x80\x9ccan hardly be said to have \xe2\x80\x98contemplated\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d that they will be used anticompetitively. Ibid. See also 1A P. Areeda & H. Hovenkamp, Antitrust Law \xc2\xb6225a, p. 131 (3d ed. 2006) (hereinafter Areeda & Hovenkamp) (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen a state grants power to an inferior entity, it presumably grants the power to do the thing contemplated, but not to do so anticompetitively\xe2\x80\x9d). Thus, while the Law does allow the Authority to acquire hospitals, it does not clearly articulate and affirmatively express a state policy empowering the Authority to make acquisitions of existing hospitals that will substantially lessen competition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn concluding otherwise, and specifically in reasoning that the Georgia Legislature \xe2\x80\x9cmust have anticipated\xe2\x80\x9d that acquisitions by hospital authorities \xe2\x80\x9cwould produce anticompetitive effects,\xe2\x80\x9d 663 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1377, the Court of Appeals applied the concept of \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeability\xe2\x80\x9d from our clear-articulation test too loosely.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn Hallie, we recognized that it would \xe2\x80\x9cembod[y] an unrealistic view of how legislatures work and of how statutes are written\xe2\x80\x9d to require state legislatures to explicitly authorize specific anticompetitive effects before state-action immunity could apply. 471 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 43. \xe2\x80\x9cNo legislature,\xe2\x80\x9d we explained, \xe2\x80\x9ccan be expected to catalog all of the anticipated effects\xe2\x80\x9d of a statute delegating authority to a substate governmental entity. Ibid.\xc2\xa0 Instead, we have approached the clear-articulation inquiry more practically, but without diluting the ultimate requirement that the State must have affirmatively contemplated the displacement of competition such that the challenged anticompetitive effects can be attributed to the \xe2\x80\x9cstate itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Parker, 317 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 352. Thus, we have concluded that a state policy to displace federal antitrust law was sufficiently expressed where the displacement of competition was the inherent, logical, or ordinary result of the exercise of authority delegated by the state legislature. In that scenario, the State must have foreseen and implicitly endorsed the anticompetitive effects as consistent with its policy goals.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor example, in Hallie, Wisconsin statutory law regulating the municipal provision of sewage services expressly permitted cities to limit their service to surrounding unincorporated areas. See 471 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 41. While unincorporated towns alleged that the city\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of that power constituted an unlawful tying arrangement, an unlawful refusal to deal, and an abuse of monopoly power, we had no trouble concluding that these alleged anticompetitive effects were affirmatively contemplated by the State because it was \xe2\x80\x9cclear\xe2\x80\x9d that they \xe2\x80\x9clogically would result\xe2\x80\x9d from the grant of authority. Id., at 42. As described by the Wisconsin Supreme Court, the state legislature \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98viewed annexation by the city of a surrounding unincorporated area as a reasonable quid pro quo that a city could require before extending sewer services to the area.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 44\xe2\x80\x9345, n.\xc2\xa08 (quoting Hallie v. Chippewa Falls, 105 Wis. 2d 533, 540\xe2\x80\x93541, 314 N.\xc2\xa0W. 2d 321, 325 (1982)). Without immunity, federal antitrust law could have undermined that arrangement and taken completely off the table the policy option that the State clearly intended for cities to have.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSimilarly, in Omni, where the respondents alleged that the city had used its zoning power to protect an incumbent billboard provider against competition, we found that the clear-articulation test was easily satisfied even though the state statutes delegating zoning authority to the city did not explicitly permit the suppression of competition. We explained that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he very purpose of zoning regulation is to displace unfettered business freedom in a manner that regularly has the effect of preventing normal acts of competition\xe2\x80\x9d and that a zoning ordinance regulating the size, location, and spacing of billboards \xe2\x80\x9cnecessarily protects existing billboards against some competition from newcomers.\xe2\x80\x9d 499 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 373. Other cases in which we have found a \xe2\x80\x9cclear articulation\xe2\x80\x9d of the State\xe2\x80\x99s intent to displace competition without an explicit statement have also involved authorizations to act or regulate in ways that were inherently anticompetitive.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBy contrast, \xe2\x80\x9csimple permission to play in a market\xe2\x80\x9d does not \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeably entail permission to roughhouse in that market unlawfully.\xe2\x80\x9d Kay Elec. Cooperative v. Newkirk, 647 F.\xc2\xa03d 1039, 1043 (CA10 2011). When a State grants some entity general power to act, whether it is a private corporation or a public entity like the Authority, it does so against the backdrop of federal antitrust law. See Ticor Title, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 632. Of course, both private parties and local governmental entities conceivably may transgress antitrust requirements by exercising their general powers in anticompetitive ways. But a reasonable legislature\xe2\x80\x99s ability to anticipate that (potentially undesirable) possibility falls well short of clearly articulating an affirmative state policy to displace competition with a regulatory alternative.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tBelieving that this case falls within the scope of the foreseeability standard applied in Hallie and Omni, the Court of Appeals stated that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t defies imagination to suppose the [state] legislature could have believed that every geographic market in Georgia was so replete with hospitals that authorizing acquisitions by the authorities could have no serious anticompetitive consequences.\xe2\x80\x9d 663 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1377. Respondents echo this argument, noting that each of Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s 159 counties covers a small geographical area and that most of them are sparsely populated, with nearly three-quarters having fewer than 50,000 residents as of the 2010 Census. Brief for Respondents 46.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven accepting, arguendo, the premise that facts about a market could make the anticompetitive use of general corporate powers \xe2\x80\x9cforeseeable,\xe2\x80\x9d we reject the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 and respondents\xe2\x80\x99 conclusion because only a relatively small subset of the conduct permitted as a matter of state law by Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(4) has the potential to negatively affect competition. Contrary to the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 and respondents\xe2\x80\x99 characterization, \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(4) is not principally concerned with hospital authorities\xe2\x80\x99 ability to acquire multiple hospitals and consolidate their operations. Section 31\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(4) allows authorities to acquire \xe2\x80\x9cprojects,\xe2\x80\x9d which includes not only \xe2\x80\x9chospitals,\xe2\x80\x9d but also \xe2\x80\x9chealth care facilities, dormitories, office buildings, clinics, housing accommodations, nursing homes, rehabilitation centers, extended care facilities, and other public health facilities.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9371(5). Narrowing our focus to the market for hospital services, the power to acquire hospitals still does not ordinarily produce anticompetitive effects. Section 31\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(4) was, after all, the source of power for newly formed hospital authorities to acquire a hospital in the first instance\xe2\x80\x94a transaction that was unlikely to raise any antitrust concerns even in small markets because the transfer of ownership from private to public hands does not increase market concentration. See 1A Areeda & Hovenkamp \xc2\xb6224e(c), at 126 (\xe2\x80\x9c[S]ubstitution of one monopolist for another is not an antitrust violation\xe2\x80\x9d). While subsequent acquisitions by authorities have the potential to reduce competition, they will raise federal antitrust concerns only in markets that are large enough to support more than one hospital but sufficiently small that the merger of competitors would lead to a significant increase in market concentration. This is too slender a reed to support the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 and respondents\xe2\x80\x99 inference.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIV\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTaking a somewhat different approach than the Court of Appeals, respondents insist that the Law should not be read as a mere authorization for hospital authorities to participate in the hospital-services market and exercise general corporate powers. Rather, they contend that hos- pital authorities are granted unique powers and respon- sibilities to fulfill the State\xe2\x80\x99s objective of providing all residents with access to adequate and affordable health and hospital care. See, e.g., Ga. Code Ann. \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(22). Respondents argue that in view of hospital authorities\xe2\x80\x99 statutory objective, their specific attributes, and the regulatory context in which they operate, it was foreseeable that authorities facing capacity constraints would decide they could best serve their communities\xe2\x80\x99 needs by acquiring an existing local hospital rather than incur the additional expense and regulatory burden of expanding a facility or constructing a new one. See\xc2\xa0Brief for Respondents 33\xe2\x80\x9339.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn support of this argument, respondents observe that hospital authorities are simultaneously empowered to act in ways private entities cannot while also being subject to significant regulatory constraints. On the power side, as the Court of Appeals noted, 663 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1376\xe2\x80\x931377, hospital authorities may acquire through eminent domain property that is \xe2\x80\x9cessential to the [authority\xe2\x80\x99s] purposes.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(12).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t On the restraint side, hospital authorities are managed by a publicly accountable board, \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9374.1, 31\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9376, they must operate on a nonprofit basis, \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9377, and they may only lease a project for others to operate after determining that doing so will promote the community\xe2\x80\x99s public health needs and that the lessee will not receive more than a reasonable rate of return on its investment, \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x937\xe2\x80\x9375(7). Moreover, hospital authorities operate within a broader regulatory context in which Georgia requires any party seeking to establish or significantly expand certain medical facilities, including hospitals, to obtain a certificate of need from state regulators. See \xc2\xa731\xe2\x80\x936\xe2\x80\x9340 et\xc2\xa0seq.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have no doubt that Georgia\xe2\x80\x99s hospital authorities differ materially from private corporations that offer hospital services. But nothing in the Law or any other provision of Georgia law clearly articulates a state policy to allow authorities to\xc2\xa0exercise their general corporate powers, including their acquisition power, without regard to negative effects on competition. The state legislature\xe2\x80\x99s objective of improving access to affordable health care does not logically suggest that the State intended that hospital authorities pursue that end through mergers that create monopolies. Nor do the restrictions imposed on hospital authorities, including the requirement that they operate on a nonprofit basis, reveal such a policy. Particularly in light of our national policy favoring competition, these restrictions should be read to reflect more modest aims. The legislature may have viewed profit generation as incompatible with its goal of providing care for the indigent sick. In addition, the legislature may have believed that some hospital authorities would operate in markets with characteristics of natural monopolies, in which case the legislature could not rely on competition to control prices. See Cantor v. Detroit Edison Co., \n\n428 U.\xc2\xa0S. 579\n\xe2\x80\x93596 (1976).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe recognize that Georgia, particularly through its certificate of need requirement, does limit competition in the market for hospital services in some respects. But regulation of an industry, and even the authorization of discrete forms of anticompetitive conduct pursuant to a regulatory structure, does not establish that the State has affirmatively contemplated other forms of anticompetitive conduct that are only tangentially related. Thus, in Goldfarb v. Virginia State Bar, \n\n421 U.\xc2\xa0S. 773 (1975)\n, we re- jected a state-action defense to price-fixing claims where a state bar adopted a compulsory minimum fee schedule. Although the State heavily regulated the practice of law, we found no evidence that it had adopted a policy to displace price competition among lawyers. Id., at 788\xe2\x80\x93792. And in Cantor, we concluded that a state commission\xe2\x80\x99s regulation of rates for electricity charged by a public utility did not confer state-action immunity for a claim that the utility\xe2\x80\x99s free distribution of light bulbs restrained trade in the light-bulb market. 428 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 596.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, the fact that Georgia imposes limits on entry into the market for medical services, which apply to both hospital authorities and private corporations, does not clearly articulate a policy favoring the consolidation of existing hospitals that are engaged in active competition. Accord, FTC v. University Health, Inc., 938 F.\xc2\xa02d 1206, 1213, n.\xc2\xa013 (CA11 1991). As to the Authority\xe2\x80\x99s eminent domain power, it was not exercised here and we do not find it relevant to the question whether the State authorized hospital authorities to consolidate market power through potentially anticompetitive acquisitions of existing hospitals.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, respondents contend that to the extent there is any doubt about whether the clear-articulation test is satisfied in this context, federal courts should err on the side of recognizing immunity to avoid improper interference with state policy choices. See Brief for Respondents 43\xe2\x80\x9344. But we do not find the Law ambiguous on the question whether it clearly articulates a policy authorizing anticompetitive acquisitions; it does not.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tMore fundamentally, respondents\xe2\x80\x99 suggestion is inconsistent with the principle that \xe2\x80\x9cstate-action immunity is disfavored.\xe2\x80\x9d Ticor Title, 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 636. Parker and its progeny are premised on an understanding that respect for the States\xe2\x80\x99 coordinate role in government counsels against reading the federal antitrust laws to restrict the States\xe2\x80\x99 sovereign capacity to regulate their economies and provide services to their citizens. But federalism and state sovereignty are poorly served by a rule of construction that would allow \xe2\x80\x9cessential national policies\xe2\x80\x9d embodied in the antitrust laws to be displaced by state delegations of authority \xe2\x80\x9cintended to achieve more limited ends.\xe2\x80\x9d 504 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 636. As an amici brief filed by 20 States in support of the FTC contends, loose application of the clear-articulation test would attach significant unintended consequences to States\xe2\x80\x99 frequent delegations of corporate authority to local bodies, effectively requiring States to disclaim any intent to displace competition to avoid inadvertently authorizing anticompetitive conduct. Brief for State of Illinois et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 12\xe2\x80\x9317; see also Surgical Care Center of Hammond, L. C. v. Hospital Serv. Dist. No. 1, 171 F.\xc2\xa03d 231, 236 (CA5 1999) (en banc). We decline to set such a trap for unwary state legislatures.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe hold that Georgia has not clearly articulated and affirmatively expressed a policy to allow hospital authorities to make acquisitions that substantially lessen competition. The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.\n\t\t\t

\n
