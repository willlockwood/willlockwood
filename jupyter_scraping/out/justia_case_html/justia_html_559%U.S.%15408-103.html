\nREED ELSEVIER, INC. V. MUCHNICK

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

REED ELSEVIER, INC., et\xc2\xa0al. v. MUCHNICK et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the second circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x93103.\xe2\x80\x83Argued October 7, 2009\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 2, 2010

\n

The Copyright Act (Act) generally requires copyright holders to register their works before suing for copyright infringement. 17 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7411(a). The complaint in this consolidated, class-action copyright infringement suit alleged that the named plaintiffs each own at least one copyright, typically in a freelance article written for a newspaper or magazine, that they had registered in accordance with \xc2\xa7411(a). The class, however, included both authors who had registered their works and authors who had not. The parties moved the District Court to certify a settlement class and approve a settlement agreement. The District Court did so over the objections of some freelance authors. On appeal, the Second Circuit sU. S.onte raised the question whether \xc2\xa7411(a) deprives federal courts of subject-matter jurisdiction over infringement claims involving unregistered copyrights, concluding that the District Court lacked jurisdiction to certify the class or approve the settlement.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Section 411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement is a precondition to filing a copyright infringement claim. A copyright holder\xe2\x80\x99s failure to comply with that requirement does not restrict a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction over infringement claims involving unregistered works. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cJurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d refers to \xe2\x80\x9ca court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudicatory authority,\xe2\x80\x9d Kontrick v. Ryan, 540 U. S. 443, 455. Thus, \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d properly applies only to \xe2\x80\x9cprescriptions delineating the classes of cases (subject-matter jurisdiction) and the persons (personal jurisdiction)\xe2\x80\x9d implicating that authority. Ibid. Because the distinction between jurisdictional conditions and claim-processing rules can be confusing in practice, federal courts and litigants should use the term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d only when it is apposite. Ibid. A statutory requirement is considered jurisdictional if Congress \xe2\x80\x9cclearly states that [it] count[s] as jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d; a condition \xe2\x80\x9cnot rank[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d as such should be treated \xe2\x80\x9cas nonjurisdictional in character.\xe2\x80\x9d Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., 546 U. S. 500, 515\xe2\x80\x93516. In Arbaugh, the Court held that the employee-numerosity coverage requirement of Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 was not a jurisdictional requirement because the provision did not \xe2\x80\x9cclearly stat[e]\xe2\x80\x9d that the numerosity rule counted as jurisdictional, this Court\xe2\x80\x99s prior Title VII cases did not compel the conclusion that the rule nonetheless was jurisdictional, and the requirement\xe2\x80\x99s location in a provision separate from Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction-granting section indicated that Congress had not ranked the rule as jurisdictional. Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Like the Title VII numerosity requirement in Arbaugh, \xc2\xa7411(a) does not \xe2\x80\x9cclearly stat[e]\xe2\x80\x9d that its registration requirement is \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d 546 U. S., at 515. Although \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s last sentence contains the word \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d that sentence speaks to a court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudicatory authority to determine a copyright claim\xe2\x80\x99s registrability and says nothing about whether a federal court has subject-matter jurisdiction to adjudicate claims for infringement of unregistered works. Moreover, \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement, like Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s employee-numerosity requirement, is located in a provision \xe2\x80\x9cseparate\xe2\x80\x9d from those granting federal courts subject-matter jurisdiction over those respective claims, ibid., and no other factor suggests that \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement can be read to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98speak in jurisdictional terms or refer in any way to the jurisdiction of the district courts,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. This conclusion is not affected by the fact that the employee-numerosity requirement in Arbaugh was considered an element of a Title VII claim rather than a prerequisite to initiating a lawsuit. See Zipes v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 455 U. S. 385, 393. Pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0A contrary result is not required by Bowles v. Russell, 551 U. S. 205. There, in finding that Congress had ranked as jurisdictional 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that parties in a civil action file a notice of appeal within 30 days of the judgment, this Court analyzed \xc2\xa72107\xe2\x80\x99s specific language and the historical treatment accorded to that type of limitation. That analysis is consistent with the Arbaugh framework because context is relevant to whether a statute \xe2\x80\x9crank[s]\xe2\x80\x9d a requirement as jurisdictional. Pp.\xc2\xa011\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(d)\xc2\xa0The Court declines to apply judicial estoppel to affirm the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment vacating the settlement. While some of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arguments below are in tension with those made in this Court, accepting their arguments here does not create the type of \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent court determinations\xe2\x80\x9d in their favor that estoppel is meant to address. See New Hampshire v. Maine, 532 U. S. 742. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(e)\xc2\xa0Because \xc2\xa7411(a) does not restrict a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction, this Court need not address the question whether the District Court had authority to approve the settlement under the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s erroneous reading of \xc2\xa7411. The Court also declines to decide whether \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement is a mandatory precondition to suit that district courts may or should enforce sU. S.onte by dismissing copyright infringement claims involving unregistered works. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

509 F.\xc2\xa03d 116, reversed and remanded.

\n

Ginsburg, J., filed an opinion concurring in part and concurring in the judgment, in which Stevens and Breyer, JJ., joined. Sotomayor, J., took no part in the consideration or decision of the case.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT

\n559 U. S. ____ (2010)
\n
\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 08-103
\n
\n

REED ELSEVIER, INC., et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. IRVIN\n MUCHNICK et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the second circuit

\n

[March 2, 2010]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Subject to certain exceptions, the Copyright Act (Act) requires copyright holders to register their works before suing for copyright infringement. 17 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7411(a) (Supp. 2009). In this case, the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit held that a copyright holder\xe2\x80\x99s failure to comply with \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement deprives a federal court of jurisdiction to adjudicate his copyright infringement claim. We disagree. Section 411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement is a precondition to filing a claim\nthat does not restrict a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Constitution grants Congress the power \xe2\x80\x9c[t]o promote the Progress of Science and useful Arts, by securing for limited Times to Authors \xe2\x80\xa6 the exclusive Right to \xe2\x80\xa6 their \xe2\x80\xa6 Writings.\xe2\x80\x9d Art.\xc2\xa0I, \xc2\xa78, cl.\xc2\xa08. Exercising this power, Congress has crafted a comprehensive statutory scheme governing the existence and scope of \xe2\x80\x9c[c]opyright protection\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9coriginal works of authorship fixed in any tangible medium of expression.\xe2\x80\x9d 17 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7102(a). This scheme gives copyright owners \xe2\x80\x9cthe exclusive rights\xe2\x80\x9d (with specified statutory exceptions) to distribute, reproduce, or publicly perform their works. \xc2\xa7106. \xe2\x80\x9cAnyone who violates any of the exclusive rights of the copyright owner as provided\xe2\x80\x9d in the Act \xe2\x80\x9cis an infringer of the copyright.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7501(a). When such infringement occurs, a copyright owner \xe2\x80\x9cis entitled, subject to the requirements of section 411, to institute an action\xe2\x80\x9d for copyright infringement. \xc2\xa7501(b) (emphasis added).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns \xe2\x80\x9cthe requirements of section 411\xe2\x80\x9d to which \xc2\xa7501(b) refers. Section 411(a) provides, inter alia and with certain exceptions, that \xe2\x80\x9cno civil action for infringement of the copyright in any United States work shall be instituted until preregistration or registration of the copyright claim has been made in accordance with this title.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 1] This provision is part of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s remedial scheme. It establishes a condition\xe2\x80\x94copyright registration\xe2\x80\x94that plaintiffs ordinarily must satisfy before filing an infringement claim and invoking the Act\xe2\x80\x99s remedial provisions. We address whether \xc2\xa7411(a) also deprives federal courts of subject-matter jurisdiction to adjudicate infringement claims involving unregistered works.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The relevant proceedings in this case began after we issued our opinion in New York Times Co. v. Tasini, 533 U. S. 483 (2001). In Tasini, we agreed with the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit that several owners of online databases and print publishers had infringed the copyrights of six freelance authors by reproducing the authors\xe2\x80\x99 works electronically without first securing their permission. See id., at 493. In so holding, we affirmed the principal theory of liability underlying copyright infringement suits that other freelance authors had filed after the Court of Appeals had issued its opinion in Tasini. These other suits, which were stayed pending our decision in Tasini, resumed after we issued our opinion and were consolidated in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York by the Judicial Panel on Multidistrict Litigation.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The consolidated complaint alleged that the named plaintiffs each own at least one copyright, typically in a freelance article written for a newspaper or a magazine, that they had registered in accordance with \xc2\xa7411(a). The class, however, included both authors who had registered their copyrighted works and authors who had not. See App. 94.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because of the growing size and complexity of the lawsuit, the District Court referred the parties to mediation. For more than three years, the freelance authors, the publishers (and their insurers), and the electronic databases (and their insurers) negotiated. Finally, in March 2005, they reached a settlement agreement that the parties intended \xe2\x80\x9cto achieve a global peace in the publishing industry.\xe2\x80\x9d In\xc2\xa0re Literary Works in Electronic Databases Copyright Litigation, 509 F.\xc2\xa03d 116, 119 (CA2 2007).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The parties moved the District Court to certify a class for settlement and to approve the settlement agreement. Ten freelance authors, including Irvin Muchnick (hereinafter Muchnick respondents), objected. The District Court overruled the objections; certified a settlement class of freelance authors under Federal Rules of Civil Procedure 23(a) and (b)(3); approved the settlement as fair, reasonable, and adequate under Rule 23(e); and entered final judgment. At no time did the Muchnick respondents or any other party urge the District Court to dismiss the case, or to refuse to certify the class or approve the settlement, for lack of subject-matter jurisdiction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Muchnick respondents appealed, renewing their objections to the settlement on procedural and substantive grounds. Shortly before oral argument, the Court of Appeals sU. S.onte ordered briefing on the question whether \xc2\xa7411(a) deprives federal courts of subject-matter jurisdiction over infringement claims involving unregistered copyrights. All parties filed briefs asserting that the District Court had subject-matter jurisdiction to approve the settlement agreement even though it included unregistered works.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Relying on two Circuit precedents holding that \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement was jurisdictional, see 509 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 121 (citing Well-Made Toy Mfg. Corp. v. Goffa Int\xe2\x80\x99l Corp., 354 F.\xc2\xa03d 112, 114\xe2\x80\x93115 (CA2 2003); Morris v. Business Concepts, Inc., 259 F.\xc2\xa03d 65, 72\xe2\x80\x9373 (CA2 2001)), the Court of Appeals concluded that the District Court lacked jurisdiction to certify a class of claims arising from the infringement of unregistered works, and also lacked jurisdiction to approve a settlement with respect to those claims, 509 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 121 (citing \xe2\x80\x9cwidespread agreement among the circuits that section 411(a) is jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Judge Walker dissented. He concluded \xe2\x80\x9cthat \xc2\xa7411(a) is more like the [nonjurisdictional] employee-numerosity requirement in Arbaugh [v. Y & H Corp., 546 U. S. 500 (2006)]\xe2\x80\x9d than the jurisdictional statutory time limit in Bowles v. Russell, 551 U. S. 205 (2007). 509 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 129. Accordingly, he reasoned that \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement does not limit federal subject-matter jurisdiction over infringement suits involving unregistered works. Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted the owners\xe2\x80\x99 and publishers\xe2\x80\x99 petition for a writ of certiorari, and formulated the question presented to ask whether \xc2\xa7411(a) restricts the subject-matter jurisdiction of the federal courts over copyright infringement actions. 555 U. S. ____ (2009). Because no party supports the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 jurisdictional holding, we appointed an amicus curiae to defend the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment.[Footnote 3] 556 U. S. ____ (2009). We now reverse.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cJurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d refers to \xe2\x80\x9ca court\xe2\x80\x99s adjudicatory authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Kontrick v. Ryan, 540 U. S. 443, 455 (2004). Accordingly, the term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d properly applies only to \xe2\x80\x9cprescriptions delineating the classes of cases (subject-matter jurisdiction) and the persons (personal jurisdiction)\xe2\x80\x9d implicating that authority. Ibid.; see also Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U. S. 83, 89 (1998) (\xe2\x80\x9csubject-matter jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d refers to \xe2\x80\x9cthe courts\xe2\x80\x99 statutory or constitutional power to adjudicate the case\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis in original)); Landgraf v. USI Film Products, 511 U. S. 244, 274 (1994) (\xe2\x80\x9c[J]urisdictional statutes \xe2\x80\x98speak to the power of the court rather than to the rights or obligations of the parties\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Republic Nat. Bank of Miami v. United States, 506 U. S. 80, 100 (1992) (Thomas, J., concurring))).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While perhaps clear in theory, the distinction between jurisdictional conditions and claim-processing rules can be confusing in practice. Courts\xe2\x80\x94including this Court\xe2\x80\x94have sometimes mischaracterized claim-processing rules or elements of a cause of action as jurisdictional limitations, particularly when that characterization was not central to the case, and thus did not require close analysis. See Arbaugh, supra, at 511\xe2\x80\x93512 (citing examples); Steel Co., 523 U. S., at 91 (same). Our recent cases evince a marked desire to curtail such \xe2\x80\x9cdrive-by jurisdictional rulings,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., which too easily can miss the \xe2\x80\x9ccritical difference[s]\xe2\x80\x9d between true jurisdictional conditions and nonjurisdictional limitations on causes of action, Kontrick, supra, at 456; see also Arbaugh, 546 U. S., at 511.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In light of the important distinctions between jurisdictional prescriptions and claim-processing rules, see, e.g., id., at 514, we have encouraged federal courts and litigants to \xe2\x80\x9cfacilitat[e]\xe2\x80\x9d clarity by using the term \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d only when it is apposite, Kontrick, supra, at 455. In Arbaugh, we described the general approach to distinguish \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d conditions from claim-processing requirements or elements of a claim:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf the Legislature clearly states that a threshold limitation on a statute\xe2\x80\x99s scope shall count as jurisdictional, then courts and litigants will be duly instructed and will not be left to wrestle with the issue. But when Congress does not rank a statutory limitation on coverage as jurisdictional, courts should treat the restriction as nonjurisdictional in character.\xe2\x80\x9d 546 U. S., at 515\xe2\x80\x93516 (citation and footnote omitted).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plaintiff in Arbaugh brought a claim under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, which makes it unlawful \xe2\x80\x9cfor an employer . . . to discriminate,\xe2\x80\x9d inter alia, on the basis of sex. 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1). But employees can bring Title VII claims only against employers that have \xe2\x80\x9cfifteen or more employees.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72000e(b). Arbaugh addressed whether that employee numerosity requirement \xe2\x80\x9caffects federal-court subject-matter jurisdiction or, instead, delineates a substantive ingredient of a Title VII claim for relief.\xe2\x80\x9d 546 U. S., at 503. We held that it does the latter.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our holding turned principally on our examination of the text of \xc2\xa72000e(b), the section in which Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s numerosity requirement appears. Section 2000e(b) does not \xe2\x80\x9cclearly stat[e]\xe2\x80\x9d that the employee numerosity threshold on Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s scope \xe2\x80\x9ccount[s] as jurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 515\xe2\x80\x93516, and n.\xc2\xa011. And nothing in our prior Title VII cases compelled the conclusion that even though the numerosity requirement lacks a clear jurisdictional label, it nonetheless imposed a jurisdictional limit. See id., at 511\xe2\x80\x93513. Similarly, \xc2\xa72000e(b)\xe2\x80\x99s text and structure did not demonstrate that Congress \xe2\x80\x9crank[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d that requirement as jurisdictional. See id., at 513\xe2\x80\x93516. As we observed, the employee numerosity requirement is located in a provision \xe2\x80\x9cseparate\xe2\x80\x9d from \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(f)(3), Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdiction-granting section, distinguishing it from the \xe2\x80\x9camount-in-controversy threshold ingredient of subject-matter jurisdiction \xe2\x80\xa6 in diversity-of-jurisdiction under 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71332.\xe2\x80\x9d Arbaugh, 546 U. S., at 514\xe2\x80\x93515. Accordingly, the numerosity requirement could not fairly be read to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98speak in jurisdictional terms or in any way refer to the jurisdiction of the district courts.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 515 (quoting Zipes v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 455 U. S. 385, 394 (1982)). We thus \xe2\x80\x9crefrain[ed] from\xe2\x80\x9d construing the numerosity requirement to \xe2\x80\x9cconstric[t] \xc2\xa71331 or Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional provision.\xe2\x80\x9d Arbaugh, supra, at 515 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We now apply this same approach to \xc2\xa7411(a).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 411(a) provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cExcept for an action brought for a violation of the rights of the author under section 106A(a), and subject to the provisions of subsection (b), no civil action for infringement of the copyright in any United States work shall be instituted until preregistration or registration of the copyright claim has been made in accordance with this title. In any case, however, where the deposit, application, and fee required for registration have been delivered to the Copyright Office in proper form and registration has been refused, the applicant is entitled to institute a civil action for infringement if notice thereof, with a copy of the complaint, is served on the Register of Copyrights. The Register may, at his or her option, become a party to the action with respect to the issue of registrability of the copyright claim by entering an appearance within sixty days after such service, but the Register\xe2\x80\x99s failure to become a party shall not deprive the court of jurisdiction to determine that issue.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We must consider whether \xc2\xa7411(a) \xe2\x80\x9cclearly states\xe2\x80\x9d that its registration requirement is \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Arbaugh, supra, at 515. It does not. \xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Amicus disagrees, pointing to the presence of the word \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d in the last sentence of \xc2\xa7411(a) and contending that the use of the term there indicates the jurisdictional cast of \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s first sentence as well. Brief for Court-Appointed Amicus Curiae in support of Judgment Below 18 (hereinafter Amicus Brief). But this reference to \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d cannot bear the weight that amicus places upon it. The sentence upon which amicus relies states:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cThe Register [of Copyrights] may, at his or her option, become a party to the [copyright infringement] action with respect to the issue of registrability of the copyright claim by entering an appearance within sixty days after such service, but the Register\xe2\x80\x99s failure to become a party shall not deprive the court of jurisdiction to determine that issue.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7411(a) (emphasis added).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Congress added this sentence to the Act in 1976, 90 Stat. 2583, to clarify that a federal court can determine \xe2\x80\x9cthe issue of registrability of the copyright claim\xe2\x80\x9d even if the Register does not appear in the infringement suit. That clarification was necessary because courts had interpreted \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s precursor provision,[Footnote 4] which imposed a similar registration requirement, as prohibiting copyright owners who had been refused registration by the Register of Copyrights from suing for infringement until the owners first sought mandamus against the Register. See Vacheron & Constantin-Le Coultre Watches, Inc. v. Benrus Watch Co., 260 F.\xc2\xa02d 637, 640\xe2\x80\x93641 (CA2 1958) (construing \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s precursor). The 1976 amendment made it clear that a federal court plainly has adjudicatory authority to determine \xe2\x80\x9cthat issue,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7411(a) (emphasis added)\xe2\x80\x94i.e., the issue of registrability\xe2\x80\x94regardless of whether the Register is a party to the infringement suit. The word \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction,\xe2\x80\x9d as used here, thus says nothing about whether a federal court has subject-matter jurisdiction to adjudicate claims for infringement of unregistered works.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement, like Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s numerosity requirement, is located in a provision \xe2\x80\x9cseparate\xe2\x80\x9d from those granting federal courts subject-matter jurisdiction over those respective claims. See Arbaugh, supra, at 514\xe2\x80\x93515. Federal district courts have subject-matter jurisdiction over copyright infringement actions based on 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71331 and 1338. But neither \xc2\xa71331, which confers subject-matter jurisdiction over questions of federal law, nor \xc2\xa71338(a), which is specific to copyright claims, conditions its jurisdictional grant on whether copyright holders have registered their works before suing for infringement. Cf. Arbaugh, supra, at 515 (\xe2\x80\x9cTitle VII\xe2\x80\x99s jurisdictional provision\xe2\x80\x9d does not \xe2\x80\x9cspecif[y] any threshold ingredient akin to 28 U. S. C. \xc2\xa71332\xe2\x80\x99s monetary floor\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nor does any other factor suggest that 17 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement can be read to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98speak in jurisdictional terms or refer in any way to the jurisdiction of the district courts.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d\xc2\xa0 Arbaugh, 546 U. S., at 515 (quoting Zipes, 455 U. S., at 394). First, and most significantly, \xc2\xa7411(a) expressly allows courts to adjudicate infringement claims involving unregistered works in three circumstances: where the work is not a U. S. work, where the infringement claim concerns rights of attribution and integrity under \xc2\xa7106A, or where the holder attempted to register the work and registration was refused. Separately, \xc2\xa7411(c) permits courts to adjudicate infringement actions over certain kinds of unregistered works where the author \xe2\x80\x9cdeclare[s] an intention to secure copyright in the work\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cmakes registration for the work, if required by subsection (a), within three months after [the work\xe2\x80\x99s] first transmission.\xe2\x80\x9d 17 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7411(c)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2). It would be at least unusual to ascribe jurisdictional significance to a condition subject to these sorts of exceptions.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0That the numerosity requirement in Arbaugh could be considered an element of a Title VII claim, rather than a prerequisite to initiating a lawsuit, does not change this conclusion, as our decision in Zipes demonstrates. Zipes (upon which Arbaugh relied) held that Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s requirement that sex-discrimination claimants timely file a discrimination charge with the EEOC before filing a civil action in federal court was nonjurisdictional. See 455 U. S., at 393; 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x935(f)(1) (establishing specific time periods within which a discrimination claimant must file a lawsuit after filing a charge with the EEOC). A statutory condition that requires a party to take some action before filing a lawsuit is not automatically \xe2\x80\x9ca jurisdictional prerequisite to suit.\xe2\x80\x9d Zipes, 455 U. S., at 393 (emphasis added). Rather, the jurisdictional analysis must focus on the \xe2\x80\x9clegal character\xe2\x80\x9d of the requirement, id., at 395, which we discerned by looking to the condition\xe2\x80\x99s text, context, and relevant historical treatment, id., at 393\xe2\x80\x93395; see also National Railroad Passenger Corporation v. Morgan, 536 U. S. 101, 119\xe2\x80\x93121 (2002). We similarly have treated as nonjurisdictional other types of threshold requirements that claimants must complete, or exhaust, before filing a lawsuit.[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The registration requirement in 17 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7411(a) fits in this mold. Section 411(a) imposes a precondition to filing a claim that is not clearly labeled jurisdictional, is not located in a jurisdiction-granting provision, and admits of congressionally authorized exceptions. See \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x93(c). Section 411(a) thus imposes a type of precondition to suit that supports nonjurisdictional treatment under our precedents.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Amicus insists that our decision in Bowles, 551 U. S. 205, compels a conclusion contrary to the one we reach today. Amicus cites Bowles for the proposition that where Congress did not explicitly label a statutory condition as jurisdictional, a court nevertheless should treat it as such if that is how the condition consistently has been interpreted and if Congress has not disturbed that interpretation. Amicus Brief 26. Specifically, amicus relies on a footnote in Bowles to argue that here, as in Bowles, it would be improper to characterize the statutory condition as nonjurisdictional because doing so would override \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a century\xe2\x80\x99s worth of precedent\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d treating \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement as jurisdictional. Amicus Brief 26 (quoting Bowles, supra, at 209, n.\xc2\xa02). This argument focuses\non the result in Bowles, rather than on the analysis we employed.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bowles did not hold that any statutory condition devoid of an express jurisdictional label should be treated as jurisdictional simply because courts have long treated it as such. Nor did it hold that all statutory conditions imposing a time limit should be considered jurisdictional.[Footnote 7] Rather, Bowles stands for the proposition that context, including this Court\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of similar provisions in many years past, is relevant to whether a statute ranks a requirement as jurisdictional.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Bowles, we considered 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107, which requires parties in a civil action to file a notice of appeal within 30 days of the judgment being appealed, and Rule 4 of the Federal Rules of Appellate Procedure, which \xe2\x80\x9ccarries \xc2\xa72107 into practice.\xe2\x80\x9d 551 U. S., at 208. After analyzing \xc2\xa72107\xe2\x80\x99s specific language and this Court\xe2\x80\x99s historical treatment of the type of limitation \xc2\xa72107 imposes (i.e., statutory deadlines for filing appeals), we concluded that Congress had ranked the statutory condition as jurisdictional. Our focus in Bowles on the historical treatment of statutory conditions for taking an appeal is thus consistent with the Arbaugh framework. Indeed, Bowles emphasized that this Court had long treated such conditions as jurisdictional, including in statutes other than \xc2\xa72107, and specifically in statutes that predated the creation of the courts of appeals. See 551 U. S., at 209\xe2\x80\x93210, and n.\xc2\xa02.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bowles therefore demonstrates that the relevant question here is not (as amicus puts it) whether \xc2\xa7411(a) itself has long been labeled jurisdictional, but whether the type of limitation that \xc2\xa7411(a) imposes is one that is properly ranked as jurisdictional absent an express designation. The statutory limitation in Bowles was of a type that we had long held did \xe2\x80\x9cspeak in jurisdictional terms\xe2\x80\x9d even absent a \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d label, and nothing about \xc2\xa72107\xe2\x80\x99s text or context, or the historical treatment of that type of limitation, justified a departure from this view. That was not the case, however, for the types of conditions in Zipes and Arbaugh.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Here, that same analysis leads us to conclude that \xc2\xa7411(a) does not implicate the subject-matter jurisdiction of federal courts. Although \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s historical treatment as \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d is a factor in the analysis, it is not dispositive. The other factors discussed above demonstrate that \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement is more analogous to the nonjurisdictional conditions we considered in Zipes and Arbaugh than to the statutory time limit at issue in Bowles.[Footnote 8] We thus conclude that \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement is nonjurisdictional, notwithstanding its prior jurisdictional treatment.[Footnote 9]

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Amicus argues that even if \xc2\xa7411(a) is nonjurisdictional, we should nonetheless affirm on estoppel grounds the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 judgment vacating the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order approving the settlement and dismissing the case. According to amicus, petitioners asserted previously in these proceedings that copyright registration was jurisdictional, and this assertion should estop them from now asserting a right to waive objections to the authors\xe2\x80\x99 failure to register. Amicus urges us to prevent the parties \xe2\x80\x9cfrom \xe2\x80\x98playing fast and loose with the courts\xe2\x80\x99 by \xe2\x80\x98deliberately changing positions according to the exigencies of the moment.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Amicus Brief 58 (quoting New Hampshire v. Maine, 532 U. S. 742, 750 (2001)).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We agree that some statements in the parties\xe2\x80\x99 submissions to the District Court and the Court of Appeals are in tension with their arguments here. But we decline to apply judicial estoppel. As we explained in New Hampshire, that doctrine typically applies when, among other things, a \xe2\x80\x9cparty has succeeded in persuading a court to accept that party\xe2\x80\x99s earlier position, so that judicial acceptance of an inconsistent position in a later proceeding would create the perception that either the first or the second court was misled.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 750 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Such circumstances do not exist here for two reasons. First, the parties made their prior statements when negotiating or defending the settlement agreement. We do not fault the parties\xe2\x80\x99 lawyers for invoking in the negotiations binding Circuit precedent that supported their clients\xe2\x80\x99 positions. Perhaps more importantly, in approving the settlement, the District Court did not adopt petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 interpretation of \xc2\xa7411(a) as jurisdictional. Second, when the Court of Appeals asked petitioners to brief whether \xc2\xa7411(a) restricted the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction, they argued that it did not, and the Court of Appeals rejected their arguments. See App. to Reply Brief for Petitioners 3a\xe2\x80\x935a, and n.\xc2\xa02. Accepting petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 arguments here thus cannot create \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent court determinations\xe2\x80\x9d in their favor. New Hampshire, supra, at 751 (internal quotation marks omitted). We therefore hold that the District Court had authority to adjudicate the parties\xe2\x80\x99 request to approve their settlement.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our holding that \xc2\xa7411(a) does not restrict a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction precludes the need for us to address the parties\xe2\x80\x99 alternative arguments as to whether the District Court had authority to approve the settlement even under the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 erroneous reading of \xc2\xa7411. In concluding that the District Court had jurisdiction to approve the settlement, we express no opinion on the settlement\xe2\x80\x99s merits.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We also decline to address whether \xc2\xa7411(a)\xe2\x80\x99s registration requirement is a mandatory precondition to suit that\xe2\x80\x94like the threshold conditions in Arizona v. California, 530 U. S. 392, 412\xe2\x80\x93413 (2000) (res judicata defense); Day v. McDonough, 547 U. S. 198, 205\xe2\x80\x93206 (2006) (habeas statute of limitations); and Hallstrom v. Tillamook County, 493 U. S. 20, 26, 31 (1989) (Resource Conservation and Recovery Act of 1976 notice provision)\xe2\x80\x94district courts may or should enforce sU. S.onte by dismissing copyright infringement claims involving unregistered works.

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We reverse the judgment of the Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit and remand this case for proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s characterization of 17 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.\xc2\xa0A. \xc2\xa7411(a) (Supp. 2009). That provision, which instructs authors to register their copyrights before commencing suit for infringement, \xe2\x80\x9cis a precondition to filing a claim that does not restrict a federal court\xe2\x80\x99s subject-matter jurisdiction.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1. I further agree that Arbaugh v. Y & H Corp., 546 U. S. 500 (2006), is the controlling precedent, see ante, at 6, and that Bowles v. Russell, 551 U. S. 205 (2007), does not counsel otherwise. There is, however, undeniable tension between the two decisions. Aiming to stave off continuing controversy over what qualifies as \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d and what does not, I set out my understanding of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinions in Arbaugh and Bowles, and the ground on which I would reconcile those rulings.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Arbaugh, we held nonjurisdictional a prescription confining Title VII\xe2\x80\x99s coverage to employers with 15 or more employees, 42 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72000e\xe2\x80\x932(a)(1). After observing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe 15-employee threshold \xe2\x80\xa6 \xe2\x80\x98d[id] not speak in jurisdictional terms or refer in any way to the jurisdiction of the district courts,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 546 U. S., at 515 (quoting Zipes v. Trans World Airlines, Inc., 455 U. S. 385, 394 (1982)), the Arbaugh opinion announced and applied a \xe2\x80\x9creadily administrable bright line\xe2\x80\x9d:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIf the Legislature clearly states that a threshold limitation on a statute\xe2\x80\x99s scope shall count as jurisdictional, then courts and litigants will be duly instructed and will not be left to wrestle with the issue. But when Congress does not rank a statutory limitation on coverage as jurisdictional, courts should treat the restriction as nonjurisdictional in character. Applying that readily administrable bright line to this case, we hold that the threshold number of employees for application of Title VII is an element of a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claim for relief, not a jurisdictional issue.\xe2\x80\x9d 546 U. S., at 515\xe2\x80\x93516 (citation and footnote omitted).

\n

\nAs the above-quoted passage indicates, the unanimous Arbaugh Court anticipated that all federal courts would thereafter adhere to the \xe2\x80\x9cbright line\xe2\x80\x9d held dispositive that day.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bowles moved in a different direction. A sharply divided Court there held \xe2\x80\x9cmandatory and jurisdictional\xe2\x80\x9d the time limits for filing a notice of appeal stated in 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72107(a), (c). 551 U. S., at 209 (internal quotation marks omitted). Bowles mentioned Arbaugh only to distinguish it as involving a statute setting \xe2\x80\x9can employee-numerosity requirement, not a time limit.\xe2\x80\x9d 551 U. S., at 211. Section 2107\xe2\x80\x99s time limits were \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x9d Bowles explained, because they were contained in a statute, not merely a rule, id., at 210\xe2\x80\x93213, and because \xe2\x80\x9c[t]his Court ha[d] long held that the taking of an appeal within the prescribed time is \xe2\x80\x98mandatory and jurisdictional,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 209. Fidelity to Arbaugh and similarly reasoned decisions,* the dissent in Bowles observed, would have yielded the conclusion that statutory time limits \xe2\x80\x9care only jurisdictional if Congress says so.\xe2\x80\x9d 551 U. S., at 217 (opinion of Souter, J.).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bowles and Arbaugh can be reconciled without distorting either decision, however, on the ground that Bowles \xe2\x80\x9crel[ied] on a long line of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s decisions left undisturbed by Congress.\xe2\x80\x9d Union Pacific R.\xc2\xa0Co. v. Locomotive Engineers and Trainmen Gen. Comm. of Adjustment, Central Region, 558 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 13) (citing Bowles, 551 U. S., at 209\xe2\x80\x93211). The same is true of our decision, subsequent to Bowles, in John R. Sand & Gravel Co. v. United States, 552 U. S. 130 (2008). There the Court concluded, largely on stare decisis grounds, that the Court of Federal Claims statute of limitations requires sua sponte consideration of a lawsuit\xe2\x80\x99s timeliness. Id., at 136 (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]etitioner can succeed only by convincing us that this Court has overturned, or that it should now overturn, its earlier precedent.\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Plainly read, Arbaugh and Bowles both point to the conclusion that \xc2\xa7411(a) is nonjurisdictional. Section 411(a) \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not speak in jurisdictional terms or refer in any way to the jurisdiction of the district courts.\xe2\x80\x9d Zipes, 455 U. S., at 394. Arbaugh\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9creadily administrable bright line\xe2\x80\x9d is therefore controlling. 546 U. S., at 516.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Bowles does not detract from that determination. Amicus, reading Bowles as I do, urges on its authority that we hold \xc2\xa7411(a) jurisdictional lest we disregard \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98a century\xe2\x80\x99s worth of precedent.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Court-Appointed Amicus Curiae in Support of Judgment Below 26 (quoting Bowles, 551 U. S., at 209, n.\xc2\xa02); see ante, at 12. But in Bowles and John R. Sand & Gravel Co., as just explained, we relied on longstanding decisions of this Court typing the relevant prescriptions \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional.\xe2\x80\x9d Bowles, 551 U. S., at 209\xe2\x80\x93210 (citing, inter alia, Scarborough v. Pargoud, 108 U. S. 567 (1883), and United States v. Curry, 6 How. 106 (1848)); John R. Sand & Gravel Co., 552 U. S., at 136. Amicus cites well over 200 opinions that characterize \xc2\xa7411(a) as jurisdictional, but not one is from this Court, and most are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98drive-by jurisdictional rulings\xe2\x80\x99 that should be accorded \xe2\x80\x98no precedential effect,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Arbaugh, 546 U. S., at 511 (quoting Steel Co. v. Citizens for Better Environment, 523 U. S. 83, 91 (1998)); see Arbaugh, 546 U. S., at 511\xe2\x80\x93513; ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936.
