SUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

Syllabus

\n

IMPRESSION PRODUCTS, INC. v. LEXMARK INTERNATIONAL, INC.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x931189.\xe2\x80\x83Argued March 21, 2017\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 30, 2017

\n

A United States patent entitles the patent holder to \xe2\x80\x9cexclude others from making, using, offering for sale, or selling [its] invention throughout the United States or importing the invention into the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7154(a). Whoever engages in one of these acts \xe2\x80\x9cwithout authority\xe2\x80\x9d from the patentee may face liability for patent infringement. \xc2\xa7271(a). When a patentee sells one of its products, however, the patentee can no longer control that item through the patent laws\xe2\x80\x94its patent rights are said to \xe2\x80\x9cexhaust.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Respondent Lexmark International, Inc. designs, manufactures, and sells toner cartridges to consumers in the United States and abroad. It owns a number of patents that cover components of those cartridges and the manner in which they are used. When Lexmark sells toner cartridges, it gives consumers two options: One option is to buy a toner cartridge at full price, with no restrictions. The other option is to buy a cartridge at a discount through Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cReturn Program.\xe2\x80\x9d In exchange for the lower price, customers who buy through the Return Program must sign a contract agreeing to use the cartridge only once and to refrain from transferring the cartridge to anyone but Lexmark.

\n

Companies known as remanufacturers acquire empty Lexmark toner cartridges\xe2\x80\x94including Return Program cartridges\xe2\x80\x94from purchasers in the United States, refill them with toner, and then resell them. They do the same with Lexmark cartridges that they acquire from purchasers overseas and import into the United States. Lexmark sued a number of these remanufacturers, including petitioner Impression Products, Inc., for patent infringement with respect to two groups of cartridges. The first group consists of Return Program cartridges that Lexmark had sold within the United States. Lexmark argued that, because it expressly prohibited reuse and resale of these cartridges, Impression Products infringed the Lexmark patents when it refurbished and resold them. The second group consists of all toner cartridges that Lexmark had sold abroad and that Impression Products imported into the country. Lexmark claimed that it never gave anyone authority to import these cartridges, so Impression Products infringed its patent rights by doing just that.

\n

Impression Products moved to dismiss on the grounds that Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s sales, both in the United States and abroad, exhausted its patent rights in the cartridges, so Impression Products was free to refurbish and resell them, and to import them if acquired overseas. The District Court granted the motion to dismiss as to the domestic Return Program cartridges, but denied the motion as to the cartridges sold abroad. The Federal Circuit then ruled for Lexmark with respect to both groups of cartridges. Beginning with the Return Program cartridges that Lexmark sold domestically, the Federal Circuit held that a patentee may sell an item and retain the right to enforce, through patent infringement lawsuits, clearly communicated, lawful restrictions on post-sale use or resale. Because Impression Products knew about Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s restrictions and those restrictions did not violate any laws, Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s sales did not exhaust its patent rights, and it could sue Impression Products for infringement. As for the cartridges that Lexmark sold abroad, the Federal Circuit held that, when a patentee sells a product overseas, it does not exhaust its patent rights over that item. Lexmark was therefore free to sue for infringement when Impression Products imported cartridges that Lexmark had sold abroad. Judge Dyk, joined by Judge Hughes, dissented.

\n

Held:

\n

1.\xc2\xa0Lexmark exhausted its patent rights in the Return Program cartridges that it sold in the United States. A patentee\xe2\x80\x99s decision to sell a product exhausts all of its patent rights in that item, regardless of any restrictions the patentee purports to impose. As a result, even if the restrictions in Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s contracts with its customers were clear and enforceable under contract law, they do not entitle Lexmark to retain patent rights in an item that it has elected to sell. Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The Patent Act grants patentees the \xe2\x80\x9cright to exclude others from making, using, offering for sale, or selling [their] invention[s].\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7154(a). For over 160 years, the doctrine of patent exhaustion has imposed a limit on that right to exclude: When a patentee sells an item, that product \xe2\x80\x9cis no longer within the limits of the [patent] monopoly\xe2\x80\x9d and instead becomes the \xe2\x80\x9cprivate, individual property\xe2\x80\x9d of the purchaser. Bloomer v. McQuewan, 14 How. 539, 549\xe2\x80\x93550. If the patentee negotiates a contract restricting the purchaser\xe2\x80\x99s right to use or resell the item, it may be able to enforce that restriction as a matter of contract law, but may not do so through a patent infringement lawsuit.

\n

The exhaustion rule marks the point where patent rights yield to the common law principle against restraints on alienation. The Patent Act promotes innovation by allowing inventors to secure the financial rewards for their inventions. Once a patentee sells an item, it has secured that reward, and the patent laws provide no basis for restraining the use and enjoyment of the product. Allowing further restrictions would run afoul of the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon law\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to permit restraints on the alienation of chattels.\xe2\x80\x9d Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc.,\n\n568 U. S. 519\n. As Lord Coke put it in the 17th century, if an owner restricts the resale or use of an item after selling it, that restriction \xe2\x80\x9cis voide, because . . . it is against Trade and Traffique, and bargaining and contracting betweene man and man.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 E. Coke, Institutes of the Laws of England \xc2\xa7360, p. 223 (1628). Congress enacted and has repeatedly revised the Patent Act against the backdrop of this hostility toward restraints on alienation, which is reflected in the exhaustion doctrine.

\n

This Court accordingly has long held that, even when a patentee sells an item under an express, otherwise lawful restriction, the patentee does not retain patent rights in that product. See, e.g., Quanta Computer, Inc. v. LG Electronics, Inc.,\n\n553 U. S. 617\n. And that well-settled line of precedent allows for only one answer in this case: Lexmark cannot bring a patent infringement suit against Impression Products with respect to the Return Program cartridges sold in the United States because, once Lexmark sold those cartridges, it exhausted its right to control them through the patent laws. Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0The Federal Circuit reached a different result because it started from the premise that the exhaustion doctrine is an interpretation of the patent infringement statute, which prohibits anyone from using or selling a patented article \xe2\x80\x9cwithout authority\xe2\x80\x9d from the patentee. According to the Federal Circuit, exhaustion reflects a default rule that selling an item \xe2\x80\x9cpresumptively grant[s] \xe2\x80\x98authority\xe2\x80\x99 for the purchaser to use it and resell it.\xe2\x80\x9d 816 F. 3d 721, 742. But if a patentee withholds some authority by expressly limiting the purchaser\xe2\x80\x99s rights, the patentee may enforce that restriction through patent infringement lawsuits. See id., at 741.

\n

The problem with the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s logic is that the exhaustion doctrine is not a presumption about the authority that comes along with a sale; it is a limit on the scope of the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s rights. The Patent Act gives patentees a limited exclusionary power, and exhaustion extinguishes that power. A purchaser has the right to use, sell, or import an item because those are the rights that come along with ownership, not because it purchased authority to engage in those practices from the patentee. Pp. 9\xe2\x80\x9313.

\n

2.\xc2\xa0Lexmark also sold toner cartridges abroad, which Impression Products acquired from purchasers and imported into the United States. Lexmark cannot sue Impression Products for infringement with respect to these cartridges. An authorized sale outside the United States, just as one within the United States, exhausts all rights under the Patent Act.

\n

The question about international exhaustion of intellectual property rights has arisen in the context of copyright law. Under the first sale doctrine, when a copyright owner sells a lawfully made copy of its work, it loses the power to restrict the purchaser\xe2\x80\x99s right \xe2\x80\x9cto sell or otherwise dispose of . . . that copy.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n17 U. S. C. \xc2\xa7109(a). In Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc.,\n\n568 U. S. 519\n, this Court held that the first sale doctrine applies to copies of works made and sold abroad. Central to that decision was the fact that the first sale doctrine has its roots in the common law principle against restraints on alienation. Because that principle makes no geographical distinctions and the text of the Copyright Act did not provide such a distinction, a straightforward application of the first sale doctrine required concluding that it applies overseas.

\n

Applying patent exhaustion to foreign sales is just as straightforward. Patent exhaustion, too, has its roots in the antipathy toward restraints on alienation, and nothing in the Patent Act shows that Congress intended to confine that principle to domestic sales. Differentiating between the patent exhaustion and copyright first sale doctrines would also make little theoretical or practical sense: The two share a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong similarity . . . and identity of purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d Bauer & Cie v. O\xe2\x80\x99Donnell,\n\n229 U. S. 1\n, and many everyday products are subject to both patent and copyright protections.

\n

Lexmark contends that a foreign sale does not exhaust patent rights because the Patent Act limits a patentee\xe2\x80\x99s power to exclude others from making, using, selling, or importing its products to acts that occur in the United States. Because those exclusionary powers do not apply abroad, the patentee may not be able to sell its products overseas for the same price as it could in the United States, and therefore is not sure to receive the reward guaranteed by American patent laws. Without that reward, says Lexmark, there should be no exhaustion.

\n

The territorial limit on patent rights is no basis for distinguishing copyright protections; those do not have extraterritorial effect either. Nor does the territorial limit support Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s argument. Exhaustion is a distinct limit on the patent grant, which is triggered by the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s decision to give a patented item up for whatever fee it decides is appropriate. The patentee may not be able to command the same amount for its products abroad as it does in the United States. But the Patent Act does not guarantee a particular price. Instead, the Patent Act just ensures that the patentee receives one reward\xe2\x80\x94of whatever it deems to be satisfactory compensation\xe2\x80\x94for every item that passes outside the scope of its patent monopoly.

\n

This Court\xe2\x80\x99s decision in Boesch v. Gr\xc3\xa4ff,\n\n133 U. S. 697\n, is not to the contrary. That decision did not, as Lexmark contends, exempt all foreign sales from patent exhaustion. Instead, it held that a sale abroad does not exhaust a patentee\xe2\x80\x99s rights when the patentee had nothing to do with the transaction. That just reaffirms the basic premise that only the patentee can decide whether to make a sale that exhausts its patent rights in an item.

\n

Finally, the United States advocates what it views as a middle-ground position: that a foreign sale exhausts patent rights unless the patentee expressly reserves those rights. This express-reservation rule is based on the idea that overseas buyers expect to be able to use and resell items freely, so exhaustion should be the presumption. But, at the same time, lower courts have long allowed patentees to expressly reserve their rights, so that option should remain open to patentees. The sparse and inconsistent decisions the Government cites, however, provide no basis for any expectation, let alone a settled one, that patentees can reserve rights when they sell abroad. The theory behind the express-reservation rule also wrongly focuses on the expectations of the patentee and purchaser during a sale. More is at stake when it comes to patent exhaustion than the dealings between the parties, which can be addressed through contracts. Instead, exhaustion occurs because allowing patent rights to stick to an already-sold item as it travels through the market would violate the principle against restraints on alienation. As a result, restrictions and location are irrelevant for patent exhaustion; what matters is the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s decision to make a sale. Pp. 13\xe2\x80\x9318.

\n

816 F.\xc2\xa03d 721, reversed and remanded.

IMPRESSION PRODUCTS, INC., PETITIONER v. LEXMARK INTERNATIONAL, INC.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

[May 30, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Ginsburg, concurring in part and dissenting in part.

\n

I concur in the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding regarding domestic exhaustion\xe2\x80\x94a patentee who sells a product with an express restriction on reuse or resale may not enforce that restriction through an infringement lawsuit, because the U.\xc2\xa0S. sale exhausts the U.\xc2\xa0S. patent rights in the product sold. See ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x9313. I dissent, however, from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s holding on international exhaustion. A foreign sale, I would hold, does not exhaust a U.\xc2\xa0S. inventor\xe2\x80\x99s U.\xc2\xa0S. patent rights.

\n

Patent law is territorial. When an inventor receives a U.\xc2\xa0S. patent, that patent provides no protection abroad. See Deepsouth Packing Co. v. Laitram Corp.,\n\n406 U.\xc2\xa0S. 518,\n531 (1972)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cOur patent system makes no claim to extraterritorial effect.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(a) (establishing liability for acts of patent infringement \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the United States\xe2\x80\x9d and for \xe2\x80\x9cimport[ation] into the United States [of] any patented invention\xe2\x80\x9d). A U.\xc2\xa0S. pat-entee must apply to each country in which she seeks the exclusive right to sell her invention. Microsoft Corp. v. AT&T Corp.,\n\n550 U.\xc2\xa0S. 437,\n456 (2007)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[F]oreign law alone, not United States law, currently governs the manufacture and sale of components of patented inventions in foreign countries.\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Convention at Brussels, An Additional Act Modifying the Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property of Mar. 20, 1883, Dec. 14, 1900, Art.\xc2\xa0I,\n\n32Stat.\n1940 (\xe2\x80\x9cPatents applied for in the different contracting States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall be independent of the patents obtained for the same invention in the other States.\xe2\x80\x9d). And patent laws vary by country; each country\xe2\x80\x99s laws \xe2\x80\x9cmay embody different policy judgments about the relative rights of inventors, competitors, and the public in patented inventions.\xe2\x80\x9d Microsoft, 550 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 455 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Because a sale abroad operates independently of the U.\xc2\xa0S. patent system, it makes little sense to say that such a sale exhausts an inventor\xe2\x80\x99s U.\xc2\xa0S. patent rights. U.\xc2\xa0S. patent protection accompanies none of a U.\xc2\xa0S. patentee\xe2\x80\x99s sales abroad\xe2\x80\x94a competitor could sell the same patented product abroad with no U.\xc2\xa0S.-patent-law consequence. Accordingly, the foreign sale should not diminish the protections of U.\xc2\xa0S. law in the United States.

\n

The majority disagrees, in part because this Court decided, in Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc.,\n\n568 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519,\n525 (2013)\n, that a foreign sale exhausts U.\xc2\xa0S. copyright protections. Copyright and patent exhaustion, the majority states, \xe2\x80\x9cshare a strong similarity.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 14 (internal quotation marks omitted). I dissented from our decision in Kirtsaeng and adhere to the view that a foreign sale should not exhaust U.\xc2\xa0S. copyright protections. See 568 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 557.

\n

But even if I subscribed to Kirtsaeng\xe2\x80\x99s reasoning with respect to copyright, that decision should bear little weight in the patent context. Although there may be a \xe2\x80\x9chistorical kinship\xe2\x80\x9d between patent law and copyright law, Sony Corp. of America v. Universal City Studios, Inc.,\n\n464 U.\xc2\xa0S. 417,\n439 (1984)\n, the two \xe2\x80\x9care not identical twins,\xe2\x80\x9d id, at 439, n.\xc2\xa019. The Patent Act contains no analogue to\n\n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7109(a), the Copyright Act first-sale provision analyzed in Kirtsaeng. See ante, at 13\xe2\x80\x9314. More importantly, copyright protections, unlike patent protections, are harmonized across countries. Under the Berne Convention, which 174 countries have joined,[1] members \xe2\x80\x9cagree to treat authors from other member countries as well as they treat their own.\xe2\x80\x9d Golan v. Holder,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 302,\n308 (2012)\n (citing Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works, Sept. 9, 1886, as revised at Stockholm on July 14, 1967, Arts. 1, 5(1), 828 U.\xc2\xa0N.\xc2\xa0T.\xc2\xa0S. 225, 231\xe2\x80\x93233). The copyright protections one receives abroad are thus likely to be similar to those received at home, even if provided under each country\xe2\x80\x99s separate copyright regime.

\n

For these reasons, I would affirm the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s judgment with respect to foreign exhaustion.

IMPRESSION PRODUCTS, INC., PETITIONER v. LEXMARK INTERNATIONAL, INC.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the federal circuit

\n

[May 30, 2017]

\n

\n

Chief Justice Roberts delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

A United States patent entitles the patent holder (the \xe2\x80\x9cpatentee\xe2\x80\x9d), for a period of 20 years, to \xe2\x80\x9cexclude others from making, using, offering for sale, or selling [its] invention throughout the United States or importing the invention into the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7154(a). Whoever engages in one of these acts \xe2\x80\x9cwithout authority\xe2\x80\x9d from the patentee may face liability for patent infringement. \xc2\xa7271(a).

\n

When a patentee sells one of its products, however, the patentee can no longer control that item through the patent laws\xe2\x80\x94its patent rights are said to \xe2\x80\x9cexhaust.\xe2\x80\x9d The purchaser and all subsequent owners are free to use or resell the product just like any other item of personal property, without fear of an infringement lawsuit.

\n

This case presents two questions about the scope of the patent exhaustion doctrine: First, whether a patentee that sells an item under an express restriction on the purchaser\xe2\x80\x99s right to reuse or resell the product may enforce that restriction through an infringement lawsuit. And second, whether a patentee exhausts its patent rights by selling its product outside the United States, where American patent laws do not apply. We conclude that a patentee\xe2\x80\x99s decision to sell a product exhausts all of its patent rights in that item, regardless of any restrictions the patentee purports to impose or the location of the sale.

\n

I

\n

The underlying dispute in this case is about laser printers\xe2\x80\x94or, more specifically, the cartridges that contain the powdery substance, known as toner, that laser printers use to make an image appear on paper. Respondent Lexmark International, Inc. designs, manufactures, and sells toner cartridges to consumers in the United States and around the globe. It owns a number of patents that cover components of those cartridges and the manner in which they are used.

\n

When toner cartridges run out of toner they can be refilled and used again. This creates an opportunity for other companies\xe2\x80\x94known as remanufacturers\xe2\x80\x94to acquire empty Lexmark cartridges from purchasers in the United States and abroad, refill them with toner, and then resell them at a lower price than the new ones Lexmark puts on the shelves.

\n

Not blind to this business problem, Lexmark structures its sales in a way that encourages customers to return spent cartridges. It gives purchasers two options: One is to buy a toner cartridge at full price, with no strings attached. The other is to buy a cartridge at roughly 20-percent off through Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cReturn Program.\xe2\x80\x9d A customer who buys through the Return Program still owns the cartridge but, in exchange for the lower price, signs a contract agreeing to use it only once and to refrain from transferring the empty cartridge to anyone but Lexmark. To enforce this single-use/no-resale restriction, Lexmark installs a microchip on each Return Program cartridge that prevents reuse once the toner in the cartridge runs out.

\n

Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s strategy just spurred remanufacturers toget more creative. Many kept acquiring empty Return Program cartridges and developed methods to counteract the effect of the microchips. With that technologicalobstacle out of the way, there was little to prevent the re-manufacturers from using the Return Program cartridges in their resale business. After all, Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s contractual single-use/no-resale agreements were with the initial customers, not with downstream purchasers like the remanufacturers.

\n

Lexmark, however, was not so ready to concede that its plan had been foiled. In 2010, it sued a number of remanufacturers, including petitioner Impression Products, Inc., for patent infringement with respect to two groups of cartridges. One group consists of Return Program cartridges that Lexmark sold within the United States. Lexmark argued that, because it expressly prohibited reuse and resale of these cartridges, the remanufacturers infringed the Lexmark patents when they refurbished and resold them. The other group consists of all toner cartridges that Lexmark sold abroad and that remanufacturers imported into the country. Lexmark claimed that it never gave anyone authority to import these cartridges, so the remanufacturers ran afoul of its patent rights by doing just that.

\n

Eventually, the lawsuit was whittled down to one defendant, Impression Products, and one defense: that Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s sales, both in the United States and abroad, exhausted its patent rights in the cartridges, so Impression Products was free to refurbish and resell them, and to import them if acquired abroad. Impression Products filed separate motions to dismiss with respect to both groups of cartridges. The District Court granted the motion as to the domestic Return Program cartridges, but denied the motion as to the cartridges Lexmark sold abroad. Both parties appealed.

\n

The Federal Circuit considered the appeals en banc and ruled for Lexmark with respect to both groups of cartridges. The court began with the Return Program cartridgesthat Lexmark sold in the United States. Relying on its decision in Mallinckrodt, Inc. v. Medipart, Inc., 976 F.\xc2\xa02d 700 (1992), the Federal Circuit held that a patentee may sell an item and retain the right to enforce, through patent infringement lawsuits, \xe2\x80\x9cclearly communicated, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. lawful restriction[s] as to post-sale use or resale.\xe2\x80\x9d 816 F.\xc2\xa03d 721, 735 (2016). The exhaustion doctrine, the court reasoned, derives from the prohibition on making, using, selling, or importing items \xe2\x80\x9cwithout authority.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 734 (quoting\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(a)). When you purchase an item you presumptively also acquire the authority to use or resell the item freely, but that is just a presumption; the same authority does not run with the item when the seller restricts post-sale use or resale. 816 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 742. Because the parties agreed that Impression Products knew about Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s restrictions and that those restrictions did not violate any laws, the Federal Circuit concluded that Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s sales had not exhausted all of its patent rights, and that the company could sue for infringement when Impression Products refurbished and resold Return Program cartridges.

\n

As for the cartridges that Lexmark sold abroad, the Federal Circuit once again looked to its precedent. In Jazz Photo Corp. v. International Trade Commission, 264 F.\xc2\xa03d 1094 (2001), the court had held that a patentee\xe2\x80\x99s decision to sell a product abroad did not terminate its ability to bring an infringement suit against a buyer that \xe2\x80\x9cimport[ed] the article and [sold] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. it in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d 816 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 726\xe2\x80\x93727. That rule, the court concluded, makes good sense: Exhaustion is justified when a patentee receives \xe2\x80\x9cthe reward available from [selling in] American markets,\xe2\x80\x9d which does not occur when the patentee sells overseas, where the American patent offers no protection and therefore cannot bolster the price of the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s goods. Id., at 760\xe2\x80\x93761. As a result, Lexmark was free to exercise its patent rights to sue Impression Products for bringing the foreign-sold cartridges to market in the United States.

\n

Judge Dyk, joined by Judge Hughes, dissented. In their view, selling the Return Program cartridges in the United States exhausted Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s patent rights in those items because any \xe2\x80\x9cauthorized sale of a patented article .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. free[s] the article from any restrictions on use or sale based on the patent laws.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 775\xe2\x80\x93776. As for the foreign cartridges, the dissenters would have held that a sale abroad also results in exhaustion, unless the seller \xe2\x80\x9cexplicitly reserve[s] [its] United States patent rights\xe2\x80\x9d at the time of sale. Id., at 774, 788. Because Lexmark failed to make such an express reservation, its foreign sales exhausted its patent rights.

\n

We granted certiorari to consider the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decisions with respect to both domestic and international exhaustion, 580 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2016), and now reverse.

\n

II

\n

A

\n

First up are the Return Program cartridges that Lexmark sold in the United States. We conclude that Lexmark exhausted its patent rights in these cartridges the moment it sold them. The single-use/no-resale restrictions in Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s contracts with customers may have been clear and enforceable under contract law, but they do not entitle Lexmark to retain patent rights in an item that it has elected to sell.

\n

The Patent Act grants patentees the \xe2\x80\x9cright to exclude others from making, using, offering for sale, or selling [their] invention[s].\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7154(a). For over 160 years, the doctrine of patent exhaustion has imposed a limit on that right to exclude. See Bloomer v. McQuewan, 14 How. 539 (1853). The limit functions automatically: When a patentee chooses to sell an item, that product \xe2\x80\x9cis no longer within the limits of the monopoly\xe2\x80\x9d and instead becomes the \xe2\x80\x9cprivate, individual property\xe2\x80\x9d of the purchaser, with the rights and benefits that come along with ownership. Id., at 549\xe2\x80\x93550. A patentee is free to set the price and negotiate contracts with purchasers, but may not, \xe2\x80\x9cby virtue of his patent, control the use or disposition\xe2\x80\x9d of the product after ownership passes to the purchaser. United States v. Univis Lens Co.,\n\n316 U.\xc2\xa0S. 241,\n250 (1942)\n (emphasis added). The sale \xe2\x80\x9cterminates all patent rights to that item.\xe2\x80\x9d Quanta Computer, Inc. v. LG Electronics, Inc.,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 617,\n625 (2008)\n.

\n

This well-established exhaustion rule marks the point where patent rights yield to the common law principle against restraints on alienation. The Patent Act \xe2\x80\x9cpromote[s] the progress of science and the useful arts by granting to [inventors] a limited monopoly\xe2\x80\x9d that allows them to \xe2\x80\x9csecure the financial rewards\xe2\x80\x9d for their inventions. Univis, 316 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 250. But once a patentee sells an item, it has \xe2\x80\x9cenjoyed all the rights secured\xe2\x80\x9d by that limited monopoly. Keeler v. Standard Folding Bed Co.,\n\n157 U.\xc2\xa0S. 659,\n661 (1895)\n. Because \xe2\x80\x9cthe purpose of the patent law is fulfilled .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. when the patentee has received his reward for the use of his invention,\xe2\x80\x9d that law furnishes \xe2\x80\x9cno basis for restraining the use and enjoyment of the thing sold.\xe2\x80\x9d Univis, 316 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 251.

\n

We have explained in the context of copyright law that exhaustion has \xe2\x80\x9can impeccable historic pedigree,\xe2\x80\x9d tracing its lineage back to the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon law\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to permit restraints on the alienation of chattels.\xe2\x80\x9d Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc.,\n\n568 U.\xc2\xa0S. 519,\n538 (2013)\n. As Lord Coke put it in the 17th century, if an owner restricts the resale or use of an item after selling it, that restriction \xe2\x80\x9cis voide, because .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. it is against Trade and Traffique, and bargaining and contracting betweene man and man.\xe2\x80\x9d 1 E. Coke, Institutes of the Laws of England \xc2\xa7360, p. 223 (1628); see J. Gray, Restraints on the Alienation of Prop-erty \xc2\xa727, p.\xc2\xa018 (2d\xc2\xa0ed. 1895) (\xe2\x80\x9cA condition or conditional limitation on alienation attached to a transfer of the entire interest in personalty is as void as if attached to a fee simple in land\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

This venerable principle is not, as the Federal Circuit dismissively viewed it, merely \xe2\x80\x9cone common-law jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x99s general judicial policy at one time toward anti-alienation restrictions.\xe2\x80\x9d 816 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 750. Congress enacted and has repeatedly revised the Patent Act against the backdrop of the hostility toward restraints on alienation. That enmity is reflected in the exhaustion doctrine. The patent laws do not include the right to \xe2\x80\x9crestrain[\xc2\xa0] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. further alienation\xe2\x80\x9d after an initial sale; such conditions have been \xe2\x80\x9chateful to the law from Lord Coke\xe2\x80\x99s day to ours\xe2\x80\x9d and are \xe2\x80\x9cobnoxious to the public interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Straus v. Victor Talking Machine Co.,\n\n243 U.\xc2\xa0S. 490,\n501 (1917)\n. \xe2\x80\x9cThe inconvenience and annoyance to the public that an opposite conclusion would occasion are too obvious to require illustration.\xe2\x80\x9d Keeler, 157 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 667.

\n

But an illustration never hurts. Take a shop that restores and sells used cars. The business works because the shop can rest assured that, so long as those bringing in the cars own them, the shop is free to repair and resell those vehicles. That smooth flow of commerce would sputter if companies that make the thousands of parts that go into a vehicle could keep their patent rights after the first sale. Those companies might, for instance, restrict resale rights and sue the shop owner for patent infringement. And even if they refrained from imposing such restrictions, the very threat of patent liability would force the shop to invest in efforts to protect itself from hidden lawsuits. Either way, extending the patent rights beyond the first sale would clog the channels of commerce, with little benefit from the extra control that the patentees retain. And advances in technology, along with increasingly complex supply chains, magnify the problem. See Brief for Costco Wholesale Corp. et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 7\xe2\x80\x939; Brief for Intel Corp. et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 17, n.\xc2\xa05 (\xe2\x80\x9cA generic smartphone assembled from various high-tech components could practice an estimated 250,000 patents\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

This Court accordingly has long held that, even when a patentee sells an item under an express restriction, the patentee does not retain patent rights in that product. In Boston Store of Chicago v. American Graphophone Co., for example, a manufacturer sold graphophones\xe2\x80\x94one of the earliest devices for recording and reproducing sounds\xe2\x80\x94to retailers under contracts requiring those stores to resell at a specific price.\n\n246 U.\xc2\xa0S. 8\n\xe2\x80\x9318 (1918). When the manufacturer brought a patent infringement suit against a retailer who sold for less, we concluded that there was \xe2\x80\x9cno room for controversy\xe2\x80\x9d about the result: By selling the item, the manufacturer placed it \xe2\x80\x9cbeyond the confines of the patent law, [and] could not, by qualifying restrictions as to use, keep [it] under the patent monopoly.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 20, 25.

\n

Two decades later, we confronted a similar arrangement in United States v. Univis Lens Co. There, a company that made eyeglass lenses authorized an agent to sell its products to wholesalers and retailers only if they promised to market the lenses at fixed prices. The Government filed an antitrust lawsuit, and the company defended its arrangement on the ground that it was exercising authority under the Patent Act. We held that the initial sales \xe2\x80\x9crelinquish[ed] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the patent monopoly with respect to the article[s] sold,\xe2\x80\x9d so the \xe2\x80\x9cstipulation .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. fixing resale prices derive[d] no support from the patent and must stand on the same footing\xe2\x80\x9d as restrictions on unpatented goods. 316 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 249\xe2\x80\x93251.

\n

It is true that Boston Store and Univis involved resale price restrictions that, at the time of those decisions, violated the antitrust laws. But in both cases it was the sale of the items, rather than the illegality of the restrictions, that prevented the patentees from enforcing those resale price agreements through patent infringement suits. And if there were any lingering doubt that patent exhaustion applies even when a sale is subject to an express, otherwise lawful restriction, our recent decision in Quanta Computer, Inc. v. LG Electronics, Inc. settled the matter. In that case, a technology company\xe2\x80\x94with authorization from the patentee\xe2\x80\x94sold microprocessors under contracts requiring purchasers to use those processors with other parts that the company manufactured. One buyer disregarded the restriction, and the patentee sued for infringement. Without so much as mentioning the lawfulness of the contract, we held that the patentee could not bring an infringement suit because the \xe2\x80\x9cauthorized sale .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. took its products outside the scope of the patent monopoly.\xe2\x80\x9d 553 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 638.

\n

Turning to the case at hand, we conclude that this well-settled line of precedent allows for only one answer: Lexmark cannot bring a patent infringement suit against Impression Products to enforce the single-use/no-resale provision accompanying its Return Program cartridges. Once sold, the Return Program cartridges passed outside of the patent monopoly, and whatever rights Lexmark retained are a matter of the contracts with its purchasers, not the patent law.

\n

B

\n

The Federal Circuit reached a different result largely because it got off on the wrong foot. The \xe2\x80\x9cexhaustion doctrine,\xe2\x80\x9d the court believed, \xe2\x80\x9cmust be understood as an interpretation of\xe2\x80\x9d the infringement statute, which prohibits anyone from using or selling a patented article \xe2\x80\x9cwithout authority\xe2\x80\x9d from the patentee. 816 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 734 (quoting\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7271(a)). Exhaustion reflects a default rule that a patentee\xe2\x80\x99s decision to sell an item \xe2\x80\x9cpresumptively grant[s] \xe2\x80\x98authority\xe2\x80\x99 to the purchaser to use it and resell it.\xe2\x80\x9d 816 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 742. But, the Federal Circuit explained, the patentee does not have to hand over the full \xe2\x80\x9cbundle of rights\xe2\x80\x9d every time. Id., at 741 (internal quotation marks omitted). If the patentee expressly withholds a stick from the bundle\xe2\x80\x94perhaps by restricting the purchaser\xe2\x80\x99s resale rights\xe2\x80\x94the buyer never acquires that withheld authority, and the patentee may continue to enforce its right to exclude that practice under the patent laws.

\n

The misstep in this logic is that the exhaustion doctrine is not a presumption about the authority that comes along with a sale; it is instead a limit on \xe2\x80\x9cthe scope of the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s rights.\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. General Elec. Co.,\n\n272 U.\xc2\xa0S. 476,\n489 (1926)\n (emphasis added). The right to use, sell, or import an item exists independently of the Patent Act. What a patent adds\xe2\x80\x94and grants exclusively to the pat-entee\xe2\x80\x94is a limited right to prevent others from engaging in those practices. See Crown Die & Tool Co. v. Nye Tool & Machine Works,\n\n261 U.\xc2\xa0S. 24,\n35 (1923)\n. Exhaustion extinguishes that exclusionary power. See Bloomer, 14 How., at 549 (the purchaser \xe2\x80\x9cexercises no rights created by the act of Congress, nor does he derive title to [the item] by virtue of the .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. exclusive privilege granted to the patentee\xe2\x80\x9d). As a result, the sale transfers the right to use, sell, or import because those are the rights that come along with ownership, and the buyer is free and clear of an infringement lawsuit because there is no exclusionary right left to enforce.

\n

The Federal Circuit also expressed concern that preventing patentees from reserving patent rights when they sell goods would create an artificial distinction between such sales and sales by licensees. Patentees, the court explained, often license others to make and sell their products, and may place restrictions on those licenses. A computer developer could, for instance, license a manufacturer to make its patented devices and sell them only for non-commercial use by individuals. If a licensee breaches the license by selling a computer for commercial use, the patentee can sue the licensee for infringement. And, in the Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s view, our decision in General Talking Pictures Corp. v. Western Elec. Co.,\n\n304 U.\xc2\xa0S. 175\n, aff\xe2\x80\x99d on reh\xe2\x80\x99g,\n\n305 U.\xc2\xa0S. 124 (1938)\n, established that\xe2\x80\x94when a patentee grants a license \xe2\x80\x9cunder clearly stated restrictions on post-sale activities\xe2\x80\x9d of those who purchase products from the licensee\xe2\x80\x94the patentee can also sue for infringement those purchasers who knowingly violate the restrictions. 816 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 743\xe2\x80\x93744. If patentees can employ licenses to impose post-sale restrictions on purchasers that are enforceable through infringement suits, the court concluded, it would make little sense to prevent patentees from doing so when they sell directly to consumers.

\n

The Federal Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s concern is misplaced. A patentee can impose restrictions on licensees because a license does not implicate the same concerns about restraints on alienation as a sale. Patent exhaustion reflects the principle that, when an item passes into commerce, it should not be shaded by a legal cloud on title as it moves through the marketplace. But a license is not about passing title to a product, it is about changing the contours of the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s monopoly: The patentee agrees not to exclude a licensee from making or selling the patented invention, expanding the club of authorized producers and sellers. See General Elec. Co., 272 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 489\xe2\x80\x93490. Because the patentee is exchanging rights, not goods, it is free to relinquish only a portion of its bundle of patent protections.

\n

A patentee\xe2\x80\x99s authority to limit licensees does not, as the Federal Circuit thought, mean that patentees can use licenses to impose post-sale restrictions on purchasers that are enforceable through the patent laws. So long as a licensee complies with the license when selling an item, the patentee has, in effect, authorized the sale. That licensee\xe2\x80\x99s sale is treated, for purposes of patent exhaustion, as if the patentee made the sale itself. The result: The sale exhausts the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s rights in that item. See Hobbie v. Jennison,\n\n149 U.\xc2\xa0S. 355\n\xe2\x80\x93363 (1893). A license may require the licensee to impose a restriction on purchasers, like the license limiting the computer manufacturer to selling for non-commercial use by individuals. But if the licensee does so\xe2\x80\x94by, perhaps, having each customer sign a contract promising not to use the computers in business\xe2\x80\x94the sale nonetheless exhausts all patent rights in the item sold. See Motion Picture Patents Co. v. Universal Film Mfg. Co.,\n\n243 U.\xc2\xa0S. 502\n\xe2\x80\x93507, 516 (1917). The purchasers might not comply with the restriction, but the only recourse for the licensee is through contract law, just as if the patentee itself sold the item with a restriction.

\n

General Talking Pictures involved a fundamentally different situation: There, a licensee \xe2\x80\x9cknowingly ma[de] .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. sales .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. outside the scope of its license.\xe2\x80\x9d 304 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 181\xe2\x80\x93182 (emphasis added). We treated the sale \xe2\x80\x9cas if no license whatsoever had been granted\xe2\x80\x9d by the patentee, which meant that the patentee could sue both the licensee and the purchaser\xe2\x80\x94who knew about the breach\xe2\x80\x94for infringement. General Talking Pictures Corp. v. Western Elec. Co.,\n\n305 U.\xc2\xa0S. 124,\n127 (1938)\n. This does not mean that patentees can use licenses to impose post-sale restraints on purchasers. Quite the contrary: The licensee infringed the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s rights because it did not comply with the terms of its license, and the patentee could bring a patent suit against the purchaser only because the purchaser participated in the licensee\xe2\x80\x99s infringement. General Talking Pictures, then, stands for the modest principle that, if a patentee has not given authority for a licensee to make a sale, that sale cannot exhaust the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s rights.

\n

In sum, patent exhaustion is uniform and automatic. Once a patentee decides to sell\xe2\x80\x94whether on its own or through a licensee\xe2\x80\x94that sale exhausts its patent rights, regardless of any post-sale restrictions the patentee purports to impose, either directly or through a license.

\n

III

\n

Our conclusion that Lexmark exhausted its patent rights when it sold the domestic Return Program cartridges goes only halfway to resolving this case. Lexmark also sold toner cartridges abroad and sued Impression Products for patent infringement for \xe2\x80\x9cimporting [Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s] invention into the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7154(a). Lexmark contends that it may sue for infringement with respect to all of the imported cartridges\xe2\x80\x94not just those in the Return Program\xe2\x80\x94because a foreign sale does not trigger patent exhaustion unless the patentee \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly or implicitly transfer[s] or license[s]\xe2\x80\x9d its rights. Brief for Respondent 36\xe2\x80\x9337. The Federal Circuit agreed, but we do not. An authorized sale outside the United States, just as one within the United States, exhausts all rights under the Patent Act.

\n

This question about international exhaustion of intellectual property rights has also arisen in the context of copyright law. Under the \xe2\x80\x9cfirst sale doctrine,\xe2\x80\x9d which is codified at\n\n17 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7109(a), when a copyright owner sells a lawfully made copy of its work, it loses the power to restrict the purchaser\xe2\x80\x99s freedom \xe2\x80\x9cto sell or otherwise dispose of .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. that copy.\xe2\x80\x9d In Kirtsaeng v. John Wiley & Sons, Inc., we held that this \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98first sale\xe2\x80\x99 [rule] applies to copies of a copyrighted work lawfully made [and sold] abroad.\xe2\x80\x9d 568 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 525. We began with the text of \xc2\xa7109(a), but it was not decisive: The language neither \xe2\x80\x9crestrict[s] the scope of [the] \xe2\x80\x98first sale\xe2\x80\x99 doctrine geographically,\xe2\x80\x9d nor clearly embraces international exhaustion. Id., at 528\xe2\x80\x93533. What helped tip the scales for global exhaustion was the fact that the first sale doctrine originated in \xe2\x80\x9cthe common law\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to permit restraints on the alienation of chattels.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 538. That \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-law doctrine makes no geographical distinctions.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 539. The lack of any textual basis for distinguishing between domestic and international sales meant that \xe2\x80\x9ca straightforward application\xe2\x80\x9d of the first sale doctrine required the conclusion that it applies overseas. Id., at 540 (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

Applying patent exhaustion to foreign sales is just as straightforward. Patent exhaustion, too, has its roots in the antipathy toward restraints on alienation, see supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x938, and nothing in the text or history of the Patent Act shows that Congress intended to confine that borderless common law principle to domestic sales. In fact, Congress has not altered patent exhaustion at all; it remains an unwritten limit on the scope of the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s monopoly. See Astoria Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn. v. Solimino,\n\n501 U.\xc2\xa0S. 104,\n108 (1991)\n (\xe2\x80\x9c[W]here a common-law principle is well established, .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. courts may take it as given that Congress has legislated with an expectation that the principle will apply except when a statutory purpose to the contrary is evident\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)). And differentiating the patent exhaustion and copyright first sale doctrines would make little theoretical or practical sense: The two share a \xe2\x80\x9cstrong similarity .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. and identity of purpose,\xe2\x80\x9d Bauer & Cie v. O\xe2\x80\x99Donnell,\n\n229 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1,\n13 (1913)\n, and many everyday products\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cautomobiles, microwaves, calculators, mobile phones, tablets, and personal computers\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94are subject to both patent and copyright protections, see Kirtsaeng, 568 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 545; Brief for Costco Wholesale Corp. et\xc2\xa0al. as Amici Curiae 14\xe2\x80\x9315. There is a \xe2\x80\x9chistoric kinship between patent law and copyright law,\xe2\x80\x9d Sony Corp. of America v. Universal City Studios, Inc.,\n\n464 U.\xc2\xa0S. 417,\n439 (1984)\n, and the bond between the two leaves no room for a rift on the question of international exhaustion.

\n

Lexmark sees the matter differently. The Patent Act, it points out, limits the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cright to exclude others\xe2\x80\x9d from making, using, selling, or importing its products to acts that occur in the United States.\n\n35 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7154(a). A domestic sale, it argues, triggers exhaustion because the sale compensates the patentee for \xe2\x80\x9csurrendering [those] U.\xc2\xa0S. rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondent 38. A foreign sale is different: The Patent Act does not give patentees exclusionary powers abroad. Without those powers, a patentee selling in a foreign market may not be able to sell its product for the same price that it could in the United States, and therefore is not sure to receive \xe2\x80\x9cthe reward guaranteed by U.\xc2\xa0S. patent law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 39 (internal quotation marks omitted). Absent that reward, says Lexmark, there should be no exhaustion. In short, there is no patent exhaustion from sales abroad because there are no patent rights abroad to exhaust.

\n

The territorial limit on patent rights is, however, no basis for distinguishing copyright protections; those protections \xe2\x80\x9cdo not have any extraterritorial operation\xe2\x80\x9d either. 5 M. Nimmer & D. Nimmer, Copyright \xc2\xa717.02, p. 17\xe2\x80\x9326 (2017). Nor does the territorial limit support the premise of Lexmark\xe2\x80\x99s argument. Exhaustion is a separate limit on the patent grant, and does not depend on the patentee receiving some undefined premium for selling the right to access the American market. A purchaser buys an item, not patent rights. And exhaustion is triggered by the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s decision to give that item up and receive whatever fee it decides is appropriate \xe2\x80\x9cfor the article and the invention which it embodies.\xe2\x80\x9d Univis, 316 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 251. The patentee may not be able to command the same amount for its products abroad as it does in the United States. But the Patent Act does not guarantee a particular price, much less the price from selling to American consumers. Instead, the right to exclude just ensures that the patentee receives one reward\xe2\x80\x94of whatever amount the patentee deems to be \xe2\x80\x9csatisfactory compensation,\xe2\x80\x9d Keeler, 157 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 661\xe2\x80\x94for every item that passes outside the scope of the patent monopoly.

\n

This Court has addressed international patent exhaustion in only one case, Boesch v. Gr\xc3\xa4ff, decided over 125 years ago. All that case illustrates is that a sale abroad does not exhaust a patentee\xe2\x80\x99s rights when the patentee had nothing to do with the transaction. Boesch\xe2\x80\x94from the days before the widespread adoption of electrical lighting\xe2\x80\x94involved a retailer who purchased lamp burners from a manufacturer in Germany, with plans to sell them in the United States. The manufacturer had authority to make the burners under German law, but there was a hitch: Two individuals with no ties to the German manufacturer held the American patent to that invention. These patentees sued the retailer for infringement when the retailer imported the lamp burners into the United States, and we rejected the argument that the German manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s sale had exhausted the American patentees\xe2\x80\x99 rights. The German manufacturer had no permission to sell in the United States from the American patentees, and the American patentees had not exhausted their patent rights in the products because they had not sold them to anyone, so \xe2\x80\x9cpurchasers from [the German manufacturer] could not be thereby authorized to sell the articles in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n133 U.\xc2\xa0S. 697,\n703 (1890)\n.

\n

Our decision did not, as Lexmark contends, exempt all foreign sales from patent exhaustion. See Brief for Respondent 44\xe2\x80\x9345. Rather, it reaffirmed the basic premise that only the patentee can decide whether to make a sale that exhausts its patent rights in an item. The American patentees did not do so with respect to the German products, so the German sales did not exhaust their rights.

\n

Finally, the United States, as an amicus, advocates what it views as a middle-ground position: that \xe2\x80\x9ca foreign sale authorized by the U.\xc2\xa0S. patentee exhausts U.\xc2\xa0S. pat-ent rights unless those rights are expressly reserved.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States 7\xe2\x80\x938. Its position is largely based on policy rather than principle. The Government thinks that an overseas \xe2\x80\x9cbuyer\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate expectation\xe2\x80\x9d is that a \xe2\x80\x9csale conveys all of the seller\xe2\x80\x99s interest in the patented article,\xe2\x80\x9d so the presumption should be that a foreign sale triggers exhaustion. Id., at 32\xe2\x80\x9333. But, at the same time, \xe2\x80\x9clower courts long ago coalesced around\xe2\x80\x9d the rule that \xe2\x80\x9ca patentee\xe2\x80\x99s express reservation of U.\xc2\xa0S. patent rights at the time of a foreign sale will be given effect,\xe2\x80\x9d so that option should remain open to the patentee. Id., at 22 (emphasis deleted).

\n

The Government has little more than \xe2\x80\x9clong ago\xe2\x80\x9d on its side. In the 1890s, two circuit courts\xe2\x80\x94in cases involving the same company\xe2\x80\x94did hold that patentees may use express restrictions to reserve their patent rights in connection with foreign sales. See Dickerson v. Tinling, 84 F. 192, 194\xe2\x80\x93195 (CA8 1897); Dickerson v. Matheson, 57 F. 524, 527 (CA2 1893). But no \xe2\x80\x9ccoalesc[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d ever took place: Over the following hundred-plus years, only a smattering of lower court decisions mentioned this express-reservation rule for foreign sales. See, e.g., Sanofi, S.\xc2\xa0A. v. Med-Tech Veterinarian Prods., Inc., 565 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 931, 938 (NJ 1983). And in 2001, the Federal Circuit adopted its blanket rule that foreign sales do not trigger exhaustion, even if the patentee fails to expressly reserve its rights. Jazz Photo, 264 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1105. These sparse and inconsistent decisions provide no basis for any expectation, let alone a settled one, that patentees can reserve patent rights when they sell abroad.

\n

The theory behind the Government\xe2\x80\x99s express-reservation rule also wrongly focuses on the likely expectations of the patentee and purchaser during a sale. Exhaustion does not arise because of the parties\xe2\x80\x99 expectations about how sales transfer patent rights. More is at stake when it comes to patents than simply the dealings between the parties, which can be addressed through contract law. Instead, exhaustion occurs because, in a sale, the patentee elects to give up title to an item in exchange for payment. Allowing patent rights to stick remora-like to that item as it flows through the market would violate the principle against restraints on alienation. Exhaustion does not depend on whether the patentee receives a premium for selling in the United States, orthe type of rights that buyers expect to receive. As a result, restrictions and location are irrelevant; what matters is the patentee\xe2\x80\x99s decision to make a sale.
