\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES

JOHNSON v. CALIFORNIA et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit

\n

No. 03\xe2\x80\x93636.Argued November 2, 2004\xe2\x80\x94Decided February 23, 2005

\n

The California Department of Corrections\xe2\x80\x99 (CDC) unwritten policy of racially segregating prisoners in double cells for up to 60 days each time they enter a new correctional facility is based on the asserted rationale that it prevents violence caused by racial gangs. Petitioner Johnson, an African-American inmate who has been intermittently double-celled under the policy\xe2\x80\x99s terms ever since his 1987 incarceration, filed this suit alleging that the policy violates his Fourteenth Amendment right to equal protection. The District Court ultimately granted defendant former CDC officials summary judgment on grounds that they were entitled to qualified immunity. The Ninth Circuit affirmed, holding that the policy\xe2\x80\x99s constitutionality should be reviewed under the deferential standard articulated in Turner v. Safley, 482 U. S. 78, not under strict scrutiny, and that the policy survived Turner scrutiny.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0Strict scrutiny is the proper standard of review for an equal protection challenge to the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy. Pp.\xc2\xa04\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0Because the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy is \xe2\x80\x9cimmediately suspect\xe2\x80\x9d as an express racial classification, Shaw v. Reno, 509 U. S. 630, 642, the Ninth Circuit erred in failing to apply strict scrutiny and thereby to require the CDC to demonstrate that the policy is narrowly tailored to serve a compelling state interest, see Adarand Constructors, Inc. v. Pe\xc5\x84a, 515 U. S. 200, 227. \xe2\x80\x9c[A]ll racial classifications [imposed by government] \xe2\x80\xa6 must be analyzed \xe2\x80\xa6 under strict scrutiny,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., in order to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98smoke out\xe2\x80\x99 illegitimate uses of race by assuring that [government] is pursuing a goal important enough to warrant [such] a highly suspect tool,\xe2\x80\x9d Richmond v. J.\xc2\xa0A. Croson Co., 488 U. S. 469, 493. The CDC\xe2\x80\x99s claim that its policy should be exempt from this categorical rule because it is \xe2\x80\x9cneutral\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94i.e., because all prisoners are \xe2\x80\x9cequally\xe2\x80\x9d segregated\xe2\x80\x94ignores this Court\xe2\x80\x99s repeated command that \xe2\x80\x9cracial classifications receive close scrutiny even when they may be said to burden or benefit the races equally,\xe2\x80\x9d Shaw, supra, at 651. Indeed, the Court rejected the notion that separate can ever be equal\xe2\x80\x94or \xe2\x80\x9cneutral\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x9450 years ago in Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U. S. 483, and refuses to resurrect it today. The Court has previously applied a heightened standard of review in evaluating racial segregation in prisons. Lee v. Washington, 390 U. S. 333. The need for strict scrutiny is no less important here. By perpetuating the notion that race matters most, racial segregation of inmates \xe2\x80\x9cmay exacerbate the very patterns of [violence that it is] said to counteract.\xe2\x80\x9d Shaw, supra, at 648. Virtually all other States and the Federal Government manage their prison systems without reliance on racial segregation. In fact, the United States argues that it is possible to address prison security concerns through individualized consideration without using racial segregation, unless it is warranted as a necessary and temporary response to a serious threat of race-related violence. As to transferees, in particular, whom the CDC has already evaluated at least once, it is not clear why more individualized determinations are not possible. Pp. 4\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The Court declines the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s invitation to make an exception to the categorical strict scrutiny rule and instead to apply Turner\xe2\x80\x99s deferential review standard on the ground that the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy applies only in the prison context. The Court has never applied the Turner standard\xe2\x80\x94which asks whether a regulation that burdens prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 fundamental rights is \xe2\x80\x9creasonably related\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate penological interests,\xe2\x80\x9d 482 U. S., at 89\xe2\x80\x94to racial classifications. Turner itself did not involve such a classification, and it cast no doubt on Lee. That is unsurprising, as the Court has applied the Turner test only to rights that are \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with proper incarceration.\xe2\x80\x9d Overton v. Bazzetta, 539 U. S. 126, 131. The right not to be discriminated against based on one\xe2\x80\x99s race is not susceptible to Turner\'s logic because it is not a right that need necessarily be compromised for the sake of proper prison administration. On the contrary, compliance with the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s ban on racial discrimination is not only consistent with proper prison administration, but also bolsters the legitimacy of the entire criminal justice system. Cf. Batson v. Kentucky, 476 U. S. 79, 99. Deference to the particular expertise of officials managing daily prison operations does not require a more relaxed standard here. The Court did not relax the standard of review for racial classifications in prison in Lee, and it refuses to do so today. Rather, it explicitly reaffirms that the \xe2\x80\x9cnecessities of prison security and discipline,\xe2\x80\x9d Lee, supra, at 334, are a compelling government interest justifying only those uses of race that are narrowly tailored to address those necessities, see, e.g., Grutter v. Bollinger, 539 U. S. 306, 353. Because Turner\xe2\x80\x99s standard would allow prison officials to use race-based policies even when there are race-neutral means to accomplish the same goal, and even when the race-based policy does not in practice advance that goal, it is too lenient a standard to ferret out invidious uses of race. Contrary to the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s protest, strict scrutiny will not render prison administrators unable to address legitimate problems of race-based violence in prisons. On remand, the CDC will have the burden of demonstrating that its policy is narrowly tailored with regard to new inmates as well as transferees. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The Court does not decide whether the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy violates equal protection, but leaves it to the Ninth Circuit, or the District Court, to apply strict scrutiny in the first instance. See, e.g., Consolidated Rail Corporation v. Gottshall, 512 U. S. 532, 557\xe2\x80\x93558. P.\xc2\xa015.

\n

321 F.\xc2\xa03d 791, reversed and remanded.

\nOPINION OF THE COURT
\nJOHNSON V. CALIFORNIA
\n543 U. S. ____ (2005)

\nSUPREME COURT OF THE UNITED STATES
\nNO. 03-636

GARRISON S. JOHNSON, PETITIONER v. \nCALIFORNIA et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of\nappeals for the ninth circuit

\n

[February 23, 2005]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice O\xe2\x80\x99Connor delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The California Department of Corrections (CDC) has an unwritten policy of racially segregating prisoners in double cells in reception centers for up to 60 days each time they enter a new correctional facility. We consider whether strict scrutiny is the proper standard of review for an equal protection challenge to that policy.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0CDC institutions house all new male inmates and all male inmates transferred from other state facilities in reception centers for up to 60 days upon their arrival. During that time, prison officials evaluate the inmates to determine their ultimate placement. Double-cell assignments in the reception centers are based on a number of factors, predominantly race. In fact, the CDC has admitted that the chances of an inmate being assigned a cellmate of another race are \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[p]retty close\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to zero percent. App. to Pet. for Cert. 3a. The CDC further subdivides prisoners within each racial group. Thus, Japanese-Americans are housed separately from Chinese-Americans, and Northern California Hispanics are separated from Southern California Hispanics.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The CDC\xe2\x80\x99s asserted rationale for this practice is that it is necessary to prevent violence caused by racial gangs. Brief for Respondents 1\xe2\x80\x936. It cites numerous incidents of racial violence in CDC facilities and identifies five major prison gangs in the State: Mexican Mafia, Nuestra Familia, Black Guerilla Family, Aryan Brotherhood, and Nazi Low Riders. Id., at 2. The CDC also notes that prison-gang culture is violent and murderous. Id., at 3. An associate warden testified that if race were not considered in making initial housing assignments, she is certain there would be racial conflict in the cells and in the yard. App. 215a. Other prison officials also expressed their belief that violence and conflict would result if prisoners were not segregated. See, e.g., id., at 305a\xe2\x80\x93306a. The CDC claims that it must therefore segregate all inmates while it determines whether they pose a danger to others. See Brief for Respondents 29.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0With the exception of the double cells in reception areas, the rest of the state prison facilities\xe2\x80\x94dining areas, yards, and cells\xe2\x80\x94are fully integrated. After the initial 60-day period, prisoners are allowed to choose their own cellmates. The CDC usually grants inmate requests to be housed together, unless there are security reasons for denying them.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Garrison Johnson is an African-American inmate in the custody of the CDC. He has been incarcerated since 1987 and, during that time, has been housed at a number of California prison facilities. Fourth Amended Complaint 3, Record, Doc. No. 78. Upon his arrival at Folsom prison in 1987, and each time he was transferred to a new facility thereafter, Johnson was double-celled with another African-American inmate. See ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Johnson filed a complaint pro se in the United States District Court for the Central District of California on February 24, 1995, alleging that the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s reception-center housing policy violated his right to equal protection under the Fourteenth Amendment by assigning him cellmates on the basis of his race. He alleged that, from 1987 to 1991, former CDC Director James Rowland instituted and enforced an unconstitutional policy of housing inmates according to race. Second Amended Complaint 2\xe2\x80\x934, Record, Doc. No. 21. Johnson made the same allegations against former Director James Gomez for the period from 1991 until the filing of his complaint. Ibid. The District Court dismissed his complaint for failure to state a claim. The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit reversed and remanded, holding that Johnson had stated a claim for racial discrimination in violation of the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. Johnson v. California, 207 F.\xc2\xa03d 650, 655 (2000).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0On remand, Johnson was appointed counsel and granted leave to amend his complaint. On July 5, 2000, he filed his Fourth Amended Complaint. Record, Doc. No. 81. Johnson claimed that the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy of racially segregating all inmates in reception-center cells violated his rights under the Equal Protection Clause. Johnson sought damages, alleging that former CDC Directors Rowland and Gomez, in their individual capacities, violated his constitutional rights by formulating and implementing the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s housing policy. He also sought injunctive relief against former CDC Director Stephen Cambra.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Johnson has consistently challenged, and the CDC has consistently defended, the policy as a whole\xe2\x80\x94as it relates to both new inmates and inmates transferred from other facilities. Johnson was first segregated in 1987 as a new inmate when he entered the CDC facility at Folsom. Since 1987, he has been segregated each time he has been transferred to a new facility. Thus, he has been subject to the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy both as a new inmate and as an inmate transferred from one facility to another.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After discovery, the parties moved for summary judgment. The District Court granted summary judgment to the defendants on grounds that they were entitled to qualified immunity because their conduct was not clearly unconstitutional. The Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed. 321 F.\xc2\xa03d 791 (2003). It held that the constitutionality of the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy should be reviewed under the deferential standard we articulated in Turner v. Safley, 482 U. S. 78 (1987)\xe2\x80\x94not strict scrutiny. 321 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 798\xe2\x80\x93799. Applying Turner, it held that Johnson had the burden of refuting the \xe2\x80\x9ccommon-sense connection\xe2\x80\x9d between the policy and prison violence. 321 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 802. Though it believed this was a \xe2\x80\x9cclose case,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 798, the Court of Appeals concluded that the policy survived Turner\xe2\x80\x99s deferential standard, 321 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 807.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals denied Johnson\xe2\x80\x99s petition for rehearing en banc. Judge Ferguson, joined by three others, dissented on grounds that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he panel\xe2\x80\x99s decision ignore[d] the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s repeated and unequivocal command that all racial classifications imposed by the government must be analyzed by a reviewing court under strict scrutiny, and fail[ed] to recognize that [the] Turner analysis is inapplicable in cases, such as this one, in which the right asserted is not inconsistent with legitimate penological objectives.\xe2\x80\x9d 336 F.\xc2\xa03d 1117 (2003) (internal quotation marks and citations omitted). We granted certiorari to decide which standard of review applies. 540 U. S. 1217 (2004).

\n

II

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have held that \xe2\x80\x9call racial classifications [imposed by government] \xe2\x80\xa6 must be analyzed by a reviewing court under strict scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x9d Adarand Constructors, Inc. v. Pe\xc5\x84a, 515 U. S. 200, 227 (1995) (emphasis added). Under strict scrutiny, the government has the burden of proving that racial classifications \xe2\x80\x9care narrowly tailored measures that further compelling governmental interests.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. We have insisted on strict scrutiny in every context, even for so-called \xe2\x80\x9cbenign\xe2\x80\x9d racial classifications, such as race-conscious university admissions policies, see Grutter v. Bollinger, 539 U. S. 306, 326 (2003), race-based preferences in government contracts, see Adarand, supra, at 226, and race-based districting intended to improve minority representation, see Shaw v. Reno, 509 U. S. 630, 650 (1993).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The reasons for strict scrutiny are familiar. Racial classifications raise special fears that they are motivated by an invidious purpose. Thus, we have admonished time and again that, \xe2\x80\x9c[a]bsent searching judicial inquiry into the justification for such race-based measures, there is simply no way of determining \xe2\x80\xa6 what classifications are in fact motivated by illegitimate notions of racial inferiority or simple racial politics.\xe2\x80\x9d Richmond v. J.\xc2\xa0A. Croson Co., 488 U. S. 469, 493 (1989) (plurality opinion). We therefore apply strict scrutiny to all racial classifications to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98smoke out\xe2\x80\x99 illegitimate uses of race by assuring that [government] is pursuing a goal important enough to warrant use of a highly suspect tool.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The CDC claims that its policy should be exempt from our categorical rule because it is \xe2\x80\x9cneutral\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94that is, it \xe2\x80\x9cneither benefits nor burdens one group or individual more than any other group or individual.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Respondents 16. In other words, strict scrutiny should not apply because all prisoners are \xe2\x80\x9cequally\xe2\x80\x9d segregated. The CDC\xe2\x80\x99s argument ignores our repeated command that \xe2\x80\x9cracial classifications receive close scrutiny even when they may be said to burden or benefit the races equally.\xe2\x80\x9d Shaw, supra, at 651. Indeed, we rejected the notion that separate can ever be equal\xe2\x80\x94or \xe2\x80\x9cneutral\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x9450 years ago in Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U. S. 483 (1954), and we refuse to resurrect it today. See also Powers v. Ohio, 499 U. S. 400, 410 (1991) (rejecting the argument that race-based peremptory challenges were permissible because they applied equally to white and black jurors and holding that \xe2\x80\x9c[i]t is axiomatic that racial classifications do not become legitimate on the assumption that all persons suffer them in equal degree\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have previously applied a heightened standard of review in evaluating racial segregation in prisons. In Lee v. Washington, 390 U. S. 333 (1968) (per curiam), we upheld a three-judge court\xe2\x80\x99s decision striking down Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s policy of segregation in its prisons. Id., at 333\xe2\x80\x93334. Alabama had argued that desegregation would undermine prison security and discipline, id., at 334, but we rejected that contention. Three Justices concurred \xe2\x80\x9cto make explicit something that is left to be gathered only by implication from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthat prison authorities have the right, acting in good faith and in particularized circumstances, to take into account racial tensions in maintaining security, discipline, and good order in prisons and jails.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added). The concurring Justices emphasized that they were \xe2\x80\x9cunwilling to assume that state or local prison authorities might mistakenly regard such an explicit pronouncement as evincing any dilution of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s firm commitment to the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition of racial discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The need for strict scrutiny is no less important here, where prison officials cite racial violence as the reason for their policy. As we have recognized in the past, racial classifications \xe2\x80\x9cthreaten to stigmatize individuals by reason of their membership in a racial group and to incite racial hostility.\xe2\x80\x9d Shaw, supra, at 643 (citing J.\xc2\xa0A. Croson Co., supra, at 493 (plurality opinion) (emphasis added)). Indeed, by insisting that inmates be housed only with other inmates of the same race, it is possible that prison officials will breed further hostility among prisoners and reinforce racial and ethnic divisions. By perpetuating the notion that race matters most, racial segregation of inmates \xe2\x80\x9cmay exacerbate the very patterns of [violence that it is] said to counteract.\xe2\x80\x9d Shaw, supra, at 648; see also Trulson & Marquart, The Caged Melting Pot: Toward an Understanding of the Consequences of Desegregation in Prisons, 36 Law & Soc. Rev. 743, 774 (2002) (in a study of prison desegregation, finding that \xe2\x80\x9cover [10 years] the rate of violence between inmates segregated by race in double cells surpassed the rate among those racially integrated\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Brief for Former State Corrections Officials as Amici Curiae 19 (opinion of former corrections officials from six States that \xe2\x80\x9cracial integration of cells tends to diffuse racial tensions and thus diminish interracial violence\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9ca blanket policy of racial segregation of inmates is contrary to sound prison management\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy is unwritten. Although California claimed at oral argument that two other States follow a similar policy, see Tr. of Oral Arg. 30\xe2\x80\x9331, this assertion was unsubstantiated, and we are unable to confirm or deny its accuracy.[Footnote 2] Virtually all other States and the Federal Government manage their prison systems without reliance on racial segregation. See Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 24. Federal regulations governing the Federal Bureau of Prisons (BOP) expressly prohibit racial segregation. 28 CFR \xc2\xa7551.90 (2004) (\xe2\x80\x9c[BOP] staff shall not discriminate against inmates on the basis of race, religion, national origin, sex, disability, or political belief. This includes the making of administrative decisions and providing access to work, housing and programs\xe2\x80\x9d). The United States contends that racial integration actually \xe2\x80\x9cleads to less violence in BOP\xe2\x80\x99s institutions and better prepares inmates for re-entry into society.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 25. Indeed, the United States argues, based on its experience with the BOP, that it is possible to address \xe2\x80\x9cconcerns of prison security through individualized consideration without the use of racial segregation, unless warranted as a necessary and temporary response to a race riot or other serious threat of race-related violence.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 24. As to transferees, in particular, whom the CDC has already evaluated at least once, it is not clear why more individualized determinations are not possible.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Because the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy is an express racial classification, it is \xe2\x80\x9cimmediately suspect.\xe2\x80\x9d Shaw, 509 U. S., at 642; see also Washington v. Seattle School Dist. No. 1, 458 U. S. 457, 485 (1982). We therefore hold that the Court of Appeals erred when it failed to apply strict scrutiny to the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy and to require the CDC to demonstrate that its policy is narrowly tailored to serve a compelling state interest.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The CDC invites us to make an exception to the rule that strict scrutiny applies to all racial classifications, and instead to apply the deferential standard of review articulated in Turner v. Safley, 482 U. S. 78 (1987), because its segregation policy applies only in the prison context. We decline the invitation. In Turner, we considered a claim by Missouri prisoners that regulations restricting inmate marriages and inmate-to-inmate correspondence were unconstitutional. Id., at 81. We rejected the prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 argument that the regulations should be subject to strict scrutiny, asking instead whether the regulation that burdened the prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 fundamental rights was \xe2\x80\x9creasonably related\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate penological interests.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 89.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We have never applied Turner to racial classifications. Turner itself did not involve any racial classification, and it cast no doubt on Lee. We think this unsurprising, as we have applied Turner\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable-relationship test only to rights that are \xe2\x80\x9cinconsistent with proper incarceration.\xe2\x80\x9d Overton v. Bazzetta, 539 U. S. 126, 131 (2003); see also Pell v. Procunier, 417 U. S. 817, 822 (1974) (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] prison inmate retains those First Amendment rights that are not inconsistent with his status as a prisoner or with the legitimate penological objectives of the corrections system\xe2\x80\x9d). This is because certain privileges and rights must necessarily be limited in the prison context. See O\xe2\x80\x99Lone v. Estate of Shabazz, 482 U. S. 342, 348 (1987) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[L]awful incarceration brings about the necessary withdrawal or limitation of many privileges and rights, a retraction justified by the considerations underlying our penal system\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Price v. Johnston, 334 U. S.266, 285 (1948))). Thus, for example, we have relied on Turner in addressing First Amendment challenges to prison regulations, including restrictions on freedom of association, Overton, supra; limits on inmate correspondence, Shaw v. Murphy, 532 U. S. 223 (2001); restrictions on inmates\xe2\x80\x99 access to courts, Lewis v. Casey, 518 U. S. 343 (1996); restrictions on receipt of subscription publications, Thornburgh v. Abbott, 490 U. S. 401 (1989); and work rules limiting prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 attendance at religious services, Shabazz, supra. We have also applied Turner to some due process claims, such as involuntary medication of mentally ill prisoners, Washington v. Harper, 494 U. S. 210 (1990); and restrictions on the right to marry, Turner, supra.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The right not to be discriminated against based on one\xe2\x80\x99s race is not susceptible to the logic of Turner. It is not a right that need necessarily be compromised for the sake of proper prison administration. On the contrary, compliance with the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s ban on racial discrimination is not only consistent with proper prison administration, but also bolsters the legitimacy of the entire criminal justice system. Race discrimination is \xe2\x80\x9cespecially pernicious in the administration of justice.\xe2\x80\x9d Rose v. Mitchell, 443 U. S. 545, 555 (1979). And public respect for our system of justice is undermined when the system discriminates based on race. Cf. Batson v. Kentucky, 476 U. S. 79, 99 (1986) (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]ublic respect for our criminal justice system and the rule of law will be strengthened if we ensure that no citizen is disqualified from jury service because of his race\xe2\x80\x9d). When government officials are permitted to use race as a proxy for gang membership and violence without demonstrating a compelling government interest and proving that their means are narrowly tailored, society as a whole suffers. For similar reasons, we have not used Turner to evaluate Eighth Amendment claims of cruel and unusual punishment in prison. We judge violations of that Amendment under the \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x9d standard, rather than Turner\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9creasonably related\xe2\x80\x9d standard. See Hope v. Pelzer, 536 U. S. 730, 738 (2002) (asking whether prison officials displayed \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98deliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x99 to the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s health or safety\xe2\x80\x9d where an inmate claimed that they violated his rights under the Eighth Amendment (quoting Hudson v. McMillian, 503 U. S. 1, 8 (1992))). This is because the integrity of the criminal justice system depends on full compliance with the Eighth Amendment. See Spain v. Procunier, 600 F.\xc2\xa02d 189, 193\xe2\x80\x93194 (CA9 1979) (Kennedy, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he full protections of the eighth amendment most certainly remain in force [in prison]. The whole point of the amendment is to protect persons convicted of crimes. \xe2\x80\xa6 Mechanical deference to the findings of state prison officials in the context of the eighth amendment would reduce that provision to a nullity in precisely the context where it is most necessary\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the prison context, when the government\xe2\x80\x99s power is at its apex, we think that searching judicial review of racial classifications is necessary to guard against invidious discrimination. Granting the CDC an exemption from the rule that strict scrutiny applies to all racial classifications would undermine our \xe2\x80\x9cunceasing efforts to eradicate racial prejudice from our criminal justice system.\xe2\x80\x9d McCleskey v. Kemp, 481 U. S. 279, 309 (1987) (internal quotation marks omitted).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The CDC argues that \xe2\x80\x9c[d]eference to the particular expertise of prison officials in the difficult task of managing daily prison operations\xe2\x80\x9d requires a more relaxed standard of review for its segregation policy. Brief for Respondents 18. But we have refused to defer to state officials\xe2\x80\x99 judgments on race in other areas where those officials traditionally exercise substantial discretion. For example, we have held that, despite the broad discretion given to prosecutors when they use their peremptory challenges, using those challenges to strike jurors on the basis of their race is impermissible. See Batson, supra, at 89\xe2\x80\x9396. Similarly, in the redistricting context, despite the traditional deference given to States when they design their electoral districts, we have subjected redistricting plans to strict scrutiny when States draw district lines based predominantly on race. Compare generally Vieth v. Jubilier, 541 U. S. 267 (2004) (partisan gerrymandering), with Shaw v. Reno, 509 U. S. 630 (1993) (racial gerrymandering).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We did not relax the standard of review for racial classifications in prison in Lee, and we refuse to do so today. Rather, we explicitly reaffirm what we implicitly held in Lee: The \xe2\x80\x9cnecessities of prison security and discipline,\xe2\x80\x9d 390 U. S., at 334, are a compelling government interest justifying only those uses of race that are narrowly tailored to address those necessities. See Grutter, 539 U. S., at 353 (Thomas,\xc2\xa0J., concurring in part and dissenting in part) (citing Lee for the principle that \xe2\x80\x9cprotecting prisoners from violence might justify narrowly tailored racial discrimination\xe2\x80\x9d); J.\xc2\xa0A. Croson Co., 488 U. S., at 521 (Scalia,\xc2\xa0J., concurring) (citing Lee for the proposition that \xe2\x80\x9conly a social emergency rising to the level of imminent danger to life or limb\xe2\x80\x94for example, a prison race riot, requiring temporary segregation of inmates\xe2\x80\x94can justify an exception to the principle embodied in the Fourteenth Amendment that \xe2\x80\x98[o]ur Constitution is color-blind, and neither knows nor tolerates classes among citizens\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Plessy v. Ferguson, 163 U. S. 537, 559 (1896) (Harlan, J., dissenting))); see also Pell, 417 U. S., at 823 (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]entral to all other corrections goals is the institutional consideration of internal security within the correctional facilities themselves\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Thomas would subject race-based policies in prisons to Turner\xe2\x80\x99s deferential standard of review because, in his view, judgments about whether race-based policies are necessary \xe2\x80\x9care better left in the first instance to the officials who run our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s prisons.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 20. But Turner is too lenient a standard to ferret out invidious uses of race. Turner requires only that the policy be \xe2\x80\x9creasonably related\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9clegitimate penological interests.\xe2\x80\x9d 482 U. S., at 89. Turner would allow prison officials to use race-based policies even when there are race-neutral means to accomplish the same goal, and even when the race-based policy does not in practice advance that goal. See, e.g., 321 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 803 (case below) (reasoning that, under Turner, the Court of Appeals did \xe2\x80\x9cnot have to agree that the policy actually advances the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s legitimate interest, but only [that] \xe2\x80\x98defendants might reasonably have thought that the policy would advance its interests\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d). See also Turner, supra, at 90 (warning that Turner is not a \xe2\x80\x9cleast restrictive alternative test\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0For example, in Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x99 world, prison officials could segregate visiting areas on the ground that racial mixing would cause unrest in the racially charged prison atmosphere. Under Turner, \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he prisoner would have to prove that there would not be a riot. [But] [i]t is certainly \xe2\x80\x98plausible\xe2\x80\x99 that such a riot could ensue: our society, as well as our prisons, contains enough racists that almost any interracial interaction could potentially lead to conflict.\xe2\x80\x9d 336 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1120 (case below) (Ferguson, J., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc). Indeed, under Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x99 view, there is no obvious limit to permissible segregation in prisons. It is not readily apparent why, if segregation in reception centers is justified, segregation in the dining halls, yards, and general housing areas is not also permissible. Any of these areas could be the potential site of racial violence. If Justice Thomas\xe2\x80\x99 approach were to carry the day, even the blanket segregation policy struck down in Lee might stand a chance of survival if prison officials simply asserted that it was necessary to prison management. We therefore reject the Turner standard for racial classifications in prisons because it would make rank discrimination too easy to defend.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The CDC protests that strict scrutiny will handcuff prison administrators and render them unable to address legitimate problems of race-based violence in prisons. See also post, at 9, 24\xe2\x80\x9325 (Thomas, J., dissenting). Not so. Strict scrutiny is not \xe2\x80\x9cstrict in theory, but fatal in fact.\xe2\x80\x9d Adarand, 515 U. S., at 237 (internal quotation marks omitted); Grutter, 539 U. S., at 326\xe2\x80\x93327 (\xe2\x80\x9cAlthough all governmental uses of race are subject to strict scrutiny, not all are invalidated by it\xe2\x80\x9d). Strict scrutiny does not preclude the ability of prison officials to address the compelling interest in prison safety. Prison administrators, however, will have to demonstrate that any race-based policies are narrowly tailored to that end. See id., at 327 (\xe2\x80\x9cWhen race-based action is necessary to further a compelling governmental interest, such action does not violate the constitutional guarantee of equal protection so long as the narrow-tailoring requirement is also satisfied\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The fact that strict scrutiny applies \xe2\x80\x9csays nothing about the ultimate validity of any particular law; that determination is the job of the court applying strict scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x9d Adarand, supra, at 229\xe2\x80\x93230. At this juncture, no such determination has been made. On remand, the CDC will have the burden of demonstrating that its policy is narrowly tailored with regard to new inmates as well as transferees. Prisons are dangerous places, and the special circumstances they present may justify racial classifications in some contexts. Such circumstances can be considered in applying strict scrutiny, which is designed to take relevant differences into account.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We do not decide whether the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy violates the Equal Protection Clause. We hold only that strict scrutiny is the proper standard of review and remand the case to allow the Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, or the District Court, to apply it in the first instance. See Consolidated Rail Corporation v. Gottshal, 512 U. S. 532, 557\xe2\x80\x93558 (1994) (reversing and remanding for the lower court to apply the correct legal standard in the first instance); Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council, 505 U. S. 1003, 1031\xe2\x80\x931032 (1992) (same). The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed, and the case is remanded for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.

\n

It is so ordered.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Stevens, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In my judgment a state policy of segregating prisoners by race during the first 60 days of their incarceration, as well as the first 60 days after their transfer from one facility to another, violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The California Department of Corrections (CDC) has had an ample opportunity to justify its policy during the course of this litigation, but has utterly failed to do so whether judged under strict scrutiny or the more deferential standard set out in Turner v. Safley, 482 U. S. 78 (1987). The CDC had no incentive in the proceedings below to withhold evidence supporting its policy; nor has the CDC made any offer of proof to suggest that a remand for further factual development would serve any purpose other than to postpone the inevitable. I therefore agree with the submission of the United States as amicus curiae that the Court should hold the policy unconstitutional on the current record.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The CDC\xe2\x80\x99s segregation policy[Footnote 1] is based on a conclusive presumption that housing inmates of different races together creates an unacceptable risk of racial violence. Under the policy\xe2\x80\x99s logic, an inmate\xe2\x80\x99s race is a proxy for gang membership, and gang membership is a proxy for violence. The CDC, however, has offered scant empirical evidence or expert opinion to justify this use of race under even a minimal level of constitutional scrutiny. The presumption underlying the policy is undoubtedly overbroad. The CDC has made no effort to prove what fraction of new or transferred inmates are members of race-based gangs, nor has it shown more generally that interracial violence is disproportionately greater than intraracial violence in its prisons. Proclivity toward racial violence unquestionably varies from inmate to inmate, yet the CDC applies its blunderbuss policy to all new and transferred inmates housed in double cells regardless of their criminal histories or records of previous incarceration. Under the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy, for example, two car thieves of different races\xe2\x80\x94neither of whom has any history of gang involvement, or of violence, for that matter\xe2\x80\x94would be barred from being housed together during their first two months of prison. This result derives from the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s inflexible judgment that such integrated living conditions are simply too dangerous. This Court has never countenanced such racial prophylaxis.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To establish a link between integrated cells and violence, the CDC relies on the views of two state corrections officials. They attested to their belief that double-celling members of different races would lead to violence and that this violence would spill out into the prison yards. One of these officials, an associate warden, testified as follows:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[W]ith the Asian population, the control sergeants have to be more careful than they do with Blacks, Whites, and Hispanics because, for example, you cannot house a Japanese inmate with a Chinese inmate. You cannot. They will kill each other. They won\xe2\x80\x99t even tell you about it. They will just do it. The same with Laotians, Vietnamese, Cambodians, Filipinos. You have to be very careful about housing other Asians with other Asians. It\xe2\x80\x99s very culturally heavy.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 189a.

\n

\nSuch musings inspire little confidence. Indeed, this comment supports the suspicion that the policy is based on racial stereotypes and outmoded fears about the dangers of racial integration. This Court should give no credence to such cynical, reflexive conclusions about race. See, e.g., Palmore v. Sidoti, 466 U. S. 429, 432 (1984) (\xe2\x80\x9cClassifying persons according to their race is more likely to reflect racial prejudice than legitimate public concerns; the race, not the person, dictates the category\xe2\x80\x9d); Watson v. Memphis, 373 U. S. 526, 536 (1963) (rejecting the city\xe2\x80\x99s plea for delay in desegregating public facilities when \xe2\x80\x9cneither the asserted fears of violence and tumult nor the asserted inability to preserve the peace was demonstrated at trial to be anything more than personal speculations or vague disquietudes of city officials\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The very real risk that prejudice (whether conscious or not) partly underlies the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy counsels in favor of relaxing the usual deference we pay to corrections officials in these matters. We should instead insist on hard evidence, especially given that California\xe2\x80\x99s policy is an outlier when compared to nationwide practice. The Federal Bureau of Prisons administers 104 institutions; no similar policy is applied in any of them. Countless state penal institutions are operated without such a policy. An amici brief filed by six former state corrections officials with an aggregate of over 120 years of experience managing prison systems in Wisconsin, Georgia, Oklahoma, Kansas, Alaska, and Washington makes clear that a blanket policy of even temporary segregation runs counter to the great weight of professional opinion on sound prison management. See Brief for Former State Corrections Officials as Amici Curiae 19. Tellingly, the CDC can only point to two other States, Texas and Oklahoma, that use racial status in assigning inmates in prison reception areas. It is doubtful from the record that these States\xe2\x80\x99 policies have the same broad and inflexible sweep as California\xe2\x80\x99s, and this is ultimately beside the point. What is important is that the Federal Government and the vast majority of States address the threat of interracial violence in prisons without resorting to the expedient of segregation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In support of its policy, the CDC offers poignant evidence that its prisons are infested with violent race-based gangs. The most striking of this evidence involves a series of riots that took place between 1998 and 2001 at Pelican Bay State Prison. That prison houses some of the State\xe2\x80\x99s most violent criminal offenders, including \xe2\x80\x9cvalidated\xe2\x80\x9d gang members who have been transferred from other prisons. The riots involved both interracial and intraracial violence. In the most serious incident, involving 250\xe2\x80\x93300 inmates, \xe2\x80\x9cSouthern Hispanic\xe2\x80\x9d gang members, joined by some white inmates, attacked a number of black inmates.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our judicial role, however, requires that we scratch below the surface of this evidence, lest the sheer gravity of a threat be allowed to authorize any policy justified in its name. Upon inspection, the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s post hoc, generalized evidence of gang violence is only tenuously related to its segregation policy. Significantly, the CDC has not cited a single specific incident of interracial violence between cellmates\xe2\x80\x94much less a pattern of such violence\xe2\x80\x94that prompted the adoption of its unique policy years ago. Nor is there any indication that antagonism between cellmates played any role in the more recent riots the CDC mentions. And despite the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s focus on prison gangs and its suggestion that such gangs will recruit new inmates into committing racial violence during their 60-day stays in the reception centers, the CDC has cited no evidence of such recruitment, nor has it identified any instances in which new inmates committed racial violence against other new inmates in the common areas, such as the yard or the cafeteria. Perhaps the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s evidence might provide a basis for arguing that at Pelican Bay and other facilities that have experienced similar riots, some race-conscious measures are justified if properly tailored. See Lee v. Washington, 390 U. S. 333, 334 (1968) (Black, J., concurring). But even if the incidents cited by the CDC, which occurred in the general prison population, were relevant to the conditions in the reception centers, they provide no support for the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s decision to apply its segregation policy to all of its reception centers, without regard for each center\xe2\x80\x99s security level or history of racial violence. Nor do the incidents provide any support for a policy applicable only to cellmates, while the common areas of the prison in which the disturbances occurred remain fully integrated.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Given the inherent indignity of segregation and its shameful historical connotations, one might assume that the CDC came to its policy only as a last resort. Distressingly, this is not so: There is no evidence that the CDC has ever experimented with, or even carefully considered, race-neutral methods of achieving its goals. That the policy is unwritten reflects, I think, the evident lack of deliberation that preceded its creation.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Specifically, the CDC has failed to explain why it could not, as an alternative to automatic segregation, rely on an individualized assessment of each inmate\xe2\x80\x99s risk of violence when assigning him to a cell in a reception center. The Federal Bureau of Prisons and other state systems do so without any apparent difficulty. For inmates who are being transferred from one facility to another\xe2\x80\x94who represent approximately 85% of those subject to the segregation policy\xe2\x80\x94the CDC can simply examine their prison records to determine if they have any known gang affiliations or if they have ever engaged in or threatened racial violence. For example, the CDC has had an opportunity to observe the petitioner for almost 20 years; surely the CDC could have determined his placement without subjecting him to a period of segregation.[Footnote 2] For new inmates, assignments can be based on their presentence reports, which contain information about offense conduct, criminal record, and personal history\xe2\x80\x94including any available information about gang affiliations. In fact, state law requires the county probation officer to transmit a presentence report to the CDC along with an inmate\xe2\x80\x99s commitment papers. See Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa71203c (West 2004); Cal. Rule of Court 4.411(d) (Criminal Cases) (West Supp. 2004).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Despite the rich information available in these records, the CDC considers these records only rarely in assigning inmates to cells in the reception centers. The CDC\xe2\x80\x99s primary explanation for this is administrative inefficiency\xe2\x80\x94the records, it says, simply do not arrive in time. The CDC\xe2\x80\x99s counsel conceded at oral argument that presentence reports \xe2\x80\x9chave a fair amount of information,\xe2\x80\x9d but she stated that, \xe2\x80\x9cin California, the presentence report does not always accompany the inmate and frequently does not. It follows some period of time later from the county.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 33. Despite the state-law requirement to the contrary, counsel informed the Court that the counties are not preparing the presentence reports \xe2\x80\x9cin a timely fashion.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Similarly, with regard to transferees, counsel stated that their prison records do not arrive at the reception centers in time to make cell assignments. Id., at 28. Even if such inefficiencies might explain a temporary expedient in some cases, they surely do not justify a system-wide policy. When the State\xe2\x80\x99s interest in administrative convenience is pitted against the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s ban on racial segregation, the latter must prevail. When there has been no \xe2\x80\x9cserious, good faith consideration of workable race-neutral alternatives that will achieve the [desired goal],\xe2\x80\x9d Grutter v. Bollinger, 539 U. S. 306, 339 (2003), and when \xe2\x80\x9cobvious, easy alternatives\xe2\x80\x9d are available, Turner, 482 U. S., at 90, the conclusion that CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy is unconstitutional is inescapable regardless of the standard of review that the Court chooses to apply.[Footnote 3]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In fact, the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s failure to demand timely presentence reports and prison records undercuts the sincerity of its concern for inmate security during the reception process. Race is an unreliable and necessarily underinclusive predictor of violence. Without the inmate-specific information found in the records, there is a risk that corrections officials will, for example, house together inmates of the same race who are nevertheless members of rival gangs, such as the Bloods and Crips.[Footnote 4]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Accordingly, while I agree that a remand is appropriate for a resolution of the issue of qualified immunity, I respectfully dissent from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to decide, on the basis of the record before us, that the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy is unconstitutional.

\nFootnote 1

\xc2\xa0The CDC operates 32 prisons, 7 of which house reception centers. All new inmates and all inmates transferring between prisons are funneled through one of these reception centers before they are permanently placed. At the centers, inmates are housed either in dormitories, double cells, or single cells (of which there are few). Under the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s segregation policy, race is a determinative factor in placing inmates in double cells, regardless of the other factors considered in such decisions. While a corrections official with 24 years of experience testified that an exception to this policy was once granted to a Hispanic inmate who had been \xe2\x80\x9craised with Crips,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 184a, the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that its policy is therefore flexible, see Brief for Respondents 9, strains credulity. There is no evidence that the CDC routinely allows inmates to opt-out of segregation, much less evidence that the CDC informs inmates of their supposed right to do so.

\nFootnote 2

\xc2\xa0In explaining why it cannot prescreen new inmates, the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s brief all but concedes that segregating transferred inmates is unnecessary. See Brief for Respondents 42 (\xe2\x80\x9cIf the officials had all of the necessary information to assess the inmates\xe2\x80\x99 violence potential when the inmates arrived, perhaps a different practice could be used. But unlike the federal system, where the inmates are generally in federal custody from the moment they are arrested, state inmates are in county custody until they are convicted and later transferred to the custody of the CDC\xe2\x80\x9d).

\nFootnote 3

\xc2\xa0Because the Turner factors boil down to a tailoring test, and I conclude that the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy is, at best, an \xe2\x80\x9cexaggerated response\xe2\x80\x9d to its asserted security concerns, see Turner v. Safley, 482 U. S. 78, 90 (1987), I find it unnecessary to address specifically the other factors, such as whether new and transferred inmates have \xe2\x80\x9calternative means\xe2\x80\x9d of exercising their right to equal protection during their period of housing segregation, id., at 89. Indeed, this case demonstrates once again that \xe2\x80\x9c[h]ow a court describes its standard of review when a prison regulation infringes fundamental constitutional rights often has far less consequences for the inmates than the actual showing that the court demands of the State in order to uphold the regulation.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 100 (Stevens, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part)

\nFootnote 4

\xc2\xa0The CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy may be counterproductive in other ways. For example, an official policy of segregation may initiate new arrivals into a corrosive culture of prison racial segregation, lending credence to the view that members of other races are to be feared and that racial alliances are necessary. While integrated cells encourage inmates to gain valuable cross-racial experiences, segregated cells may well facilitate the formation of race-based gangs. See Brief for Former State Corrections Officials as Amici Curiae 19 (citing evidence and experience suggesting that the racial integration of cells on balance decreases interracial violence).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The questions presented in this case require us to resolve two conflicting lines of precedent. On the one hand, as the Court stresses, this Court has said that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98all racial classifications reviewable under the Equal Protection Clause must be strictly scrutinized.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Gratz v. Bollinger, 539 U. S. 244, 270 (2003) (emphasis added) (quoting Adarand Constructors, Inc. v. Pe\xc5\x84a, 515 U. S. 200, 224 (1995)). On the other, this Court has no less categorically said that \xe2\x80\x9cthe [relaxed] standard of review we adopted in Turner [v. Safley, 482 U. S. 78 (1987),] applies to all circumstances in which the needs of prison administration implicate constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Washington v. Harper, 494 U. S. 210, 224 (1990) (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Emphasizing the former line of cases, the majority resolves the conflict in favor of strict scrutiny. I disagree. The Constitution has always demanded less within the prison walls. Time and again, even when faced with constitutional rights no less \xe2\x80\x9cfundamental\xe2\x80\x9d than the right to be free from state-sponsored racial discrimination, we have deferred to the reasonable judgments of officials experienced in running this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s prisons. There is good reason for such deference in this case. California oversees roughly 160,000 inmates, in prisons that have been a breeding ground for some of the most violent prison gangs in America\xe2\x80\x94all of them organized along racial lines. In that atmosphere, California racially segregates a portion of its inmates, in a part of its prisons, for brief periods of up to 60 days, until the State can arrange permanent housing. The majority is concerned with sparing inmates the indignity and stigma of racial discrimination. Ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937. California is concerned with their safety and saving their lives. I respectfully dissent.

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To understand this case, one must understand just how limited the policy at issue is. That requires more factual background than the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion provides. Petitioner Garrison Johnson is a black inmate in the California Department of Corrections (CDC), currently serving his sentence for murder, robbery, and assault with a deadly weapon. App. 255a\xe2\x80\x93256a, 259a. Johnson began serving his sentence in June 1987 at the California Institution for Men in Chino, California. Id., at 79a, 264a. Since that time he has been transferred to a number of other facilities within the CDC. Id., at 79a\xe2\x80\x9382a.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When an inmate like Johnson is admitted into the California prison system or transferred between the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s institutions, he is housed initially for a brief period\xe2\x80\x94usually no more than 60 days\xe2\x80\x94in one of California\xe2\x80\x99s prison reception centers for men. Id., at 303a\xe2\x80\x93305a. CDC, Department Operations Manual \xc2\xa761010.3 (2004) (hereinafter CDC Operations Manual), available at http://www.corr.ca.gov/\nRegulationsPolicies/PDF/DOM/00_dept_ops_manual.pdf (all Internet materials as visited Feb. 18, 2005, and available in the Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). In 2003, the centers processed more than 40,000 newly admitted inmates, almost 72,000 inmates returned from parole, over 14,000 inmates admitted for other reasons, and some portion of the 254,000 inmates who were transferred from one prison to another. California Dept. of Corrections, Movement of Prison Population 3 (2003) (hereinafter Movement of Prison Population).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the reception center, prison officials have limited information about an inmate, \xe2\x80\x9cparticularly if he has never been housed in any CDC facility.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 303a. The inmate therefore is classified so that prison officials can place the inmate in appropriate permanent housing. During this process, the CDC evaluates the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cphysical, mental and emotional health.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. The CDC also reviews the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s criminal history and record in jail to assess his security needs and classification level. Id., at 304a. Finally, the CDC investigates whether the inmate has any enemies in prison. Ibid. This process determines the inmate\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate housing placement and has nothing to do with race.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0While the process is underway, the CDC houses the inmate in a one-person cell, a two-person cell, or a dormitory. Id., at 305a. The few single cells available at reception centers are reserved for inmates who present special security problems, including those convicted of especially heinous crimes or those in need of protective custody. See, e.g., CDC Operations Manual \xc2\xa761010.11.3. At the other end of the spectrum, lower risk inmates are assigned to dormitories. App. 189a\xe2\x80\x93190a. Placement in either a single cell or a dormitory has nothing to do with race, except that prison officials attempt to maintain a racial balance within each dormitory. Id., at 250a. Inmates placed in single cells or dormitories lead fully integrated lives: The CDC does not distinguish based on race at any of its facilities when it comes to jobs, meals, yard and recreation time, or vocational and educational assignments. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Yet some prisoners, like Johnson, neither require confinement in a single cell nor may be safely housed in a dormitory. The CDC houses these prisoners in double cells during the 60-day period. In pairing cellmates, race is indisputably the predominant factor. Id., at 305a, 309a. California\xe2\x80\x99s reason is simple: Its prisons are dominated by violent gangs. Brief for Respondents 1\xe2\x80\x935. And as the largest gangs\xe2\x80\x99 names indicate\xe2\x80\x94the Aryan Brotherhood, the Black Guerrilla Family, the Mexican Mafia, the Nazi Low Riders, and La Nuestra Familia\xe2\x80\x94they are organized along racial lines. See Part II\xe2\x80\x93B, infra.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0According to the State, housing inmates in double cells without regard to race threatens not only prison discipline, but also the physical safety of inmates and staff. App. 305a\xe2\x80\x93306a, 310a\xe2\x80\x93311a. That is because double cells are especially dangerous. The risk of racial violence in public areas of prisons is high, and the tightly confined, private conditions of cells hazard even more violence. Prison staff cannot see into the cells without going up to them, and inmates can cover the windows to prevent the staff from seeing inside the cells. Id., at 306a. The risk of violence caused by this privacy is grave, for inmates are confined to their cells for much of the day. Ibid.; id., at 187a\xe2\x80\x93188a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Nevertheless, while race is the predominant factor in pairing cellmates, it is hardly the only one. After dividing this subset of inmates based on race, the CDC further divides them based on geographic or national origin. As an example, Hispanics from Northern and Southern California are not housed together in reception centers, because they often belong to rival gangs\xe2\x80\x94La Nuestra Familia and the Mexican Mafia, respectively. Id., at 185a. Likewise, Chinese and Japanese inmates are not housed together, nor are Cambodians, Filipinos, Laotians, or Vietnamese. Id., at 189a. In addition to geographic and national origin, prison officials consider a host of other factors, including inmates\xe2\x80\x99 age, mental health, medical needs, criminal history, and gang affiliation. Id., at 304a, 309a. For instance, when Johnson was admitted in 1987, he was a member of the Crips, a black street gang. Id., at 93a. He was therefore ineligible to be housed with\nnonblack inmates. Id., at 183a; Brief for Respondents 12, n.\xc2\xa09.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, while prison officials consider race in assigning inmates to double cells, the record shows that inmates are not necessarily housed with other inmates of the same race during that 60-day period. When a Hispanic inmate affiliated with the Crips asked to be housed at the reception center with a black inmate, for example, prison administrators granted his request. App. at 183a\xe2\x80\x93184a, 199a. Such requests are routinely granted after the 60-day period, when prison officials complete the classification process and transfer an inmate from the reception center to a permanent placement at that prison or another one.[Footnote 1] Id., at 311a\xe2\x80\x93312a.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Traditionally, federal courts rarely involved themselves in the administration of state prisons, \xe2\x80\x9cadopt[ing] a broad hands-off attitude toward problems of prison administration.\xe2\x80\x9d[Footnote 2] Procunier v. Martinez, 416 U. S. 396, 404 (1974). For most of this Nation\xe2\x80\x99s history, only law-abiding citizens could claim the cover of the Constitution: Upon conviction and incarceration, defendants forfeited their constitutional rights and possessed instead only those rights that the State chose to extend them. See, e.g., Shaw v. Murphy, 532 U. S. 223, 228 (2001); Ruffin v. Commonwealth, 62 Va. 790, 796 (1871). In recent decades, however, this Court has decided that incarceration does not divest prisoners of all constitutional protections. See, e.g., Wolff v. McDonnell, 418 U. S. 539, 555\xe2\x80\x93556 (1974) (the right to due process); Cruz v. Beto, 405 U. S. 319, 322 (1972) (per curiam) (the right to free exercise of religion).[Footnote 3]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At the same time, this Court quickly recognized that the extension of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s demands behind prison walls had to accommodate the needs of prison administration. This Court reached that accommodation in Turner v. Safley, 482 U. S. 78 (1987), which \xe2\x80\x9cadopted a unitary, deferential standard for reviewing prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional claims,\xe2\x80\x9d Shaw, supra, at 229. That standard should govern Johnson\xe2\x80\x99s claims, as it has governed a host of other claims challenging conditions of confinement, even when restricting the rights at issue would otherwise have occasioned strict scrutiny. Under the Turner standard, the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy passes constitutional muster, because it is reasonably related to legitimate penological interests.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Well before Turner, this Court recognized that experienced prison administrators, and not judges, are in the best position to supervise the daily operations of prisons across this country. See, e.g., Jones v. North Carolina Prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 Labor Union, Inc., 433 U. S. 119, 125 (1977) (courts must give \xe2\x80\x9cappropriate deference to the decisions of prison administrators\xe2\x80\x9d); Procunier, supra, at 405 (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ourts are ill equipped to deal with the increasingly urgent problems of prison administration and reform\xe2\x80\x9d). Turner made clear that a deferential standard of review would apply across-the-board to inmates\xe2\x80\x99 constitutional challenges to prison policies.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0At issue in Turner was the constitutionality of a pair of Missouri prison regulations limiting inmate-to-inmate correspondence and inmate marriages. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s analysis proceeded in two steps. First, the Court recognized that prisoners are not entirely without constitutional rights. As proof, it listed certain constitutional rights retained by prisoners, including the right to be \xe2\x80\x9cprotected against invidious racial discrimination . . . , Lee v. Washington, 390 U. S. 333 (1968).\xe2\x80\x9d Turner, 482 U. S., at 84. Second, the Court concluded that for prison administrators rather than courts to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98make the difficult judgments concerning institutional operations,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 89 (quoting Jones, supra, at 128), courts should uphold prison regulations that impinge on those constitutional rights if they reasonably relate to legitimate penological interests, 482 U. S., at 89. Nowhere did the Court suggest that Lee\xe2\x80\x99s right to be free from racial discrimination was immune from Turner\xe2\x80\x99s deferential standard of review. To the contrary, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]e made quite clear that the standard of review we adopted in Turner applies to all circumstances in which the needs of prison administration implicate constitutional rights.\xe2\x80\x9d Harper, 494 U. S., at 224 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Consistent with that understanding, this Court has applied Turner\xe2\x80\x99s standard to a host of constitutional claims by prisoners, regardless of the standard of review that would apply outside prison walls.[Footnote 4] And this Court has adhered to Turner despite being urged to adopt different standards of review based on the constitutional provision at issue. See Harper, supra, at 224 (Turner\xe2\x80\x99s standard of review \xe2\x80\x9cappl[ies] in all cases in which a prisoner asserts that a prison regulation violates the Constitution, not just those in which the prisoner invokes the First Amendment\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)); O\xe2\x80\x99Lone v. Estate of Shabazz, 482 U. S. 342, 353 (1987) (\xe2\x80\x9cWe take this opportunity to reaffirm our refusal, even where claims are made under the First Amendment, to substitute our judgment on . . . difficult and sensitive matters of institutional administration for the determinations of those charged with the formidable task of running a prison\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks and citation omitted; emphasis added)). Our steadfast adherence makes sense: If Turner is our accommodation of the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s demands to those of prison administration, see supra, at 7, we should apply it uniformly to prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 challenges to their conditions of confinement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After all, Johnson\xe2\x80\x99s claims, even more than other claims to which we have applied Turner\xe2\x80\x99s test, implicate Turner\xe2\x80\x99s rationale. In fact, in a passage that bears repeating, the Turner Court explained precisely why deference to the judgments of California\xe2\x80\x99s prison officials is necessary:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cSubjecting the day-to-day judgments of prison officials to an inflexible strict scrutiny analysis would seriously hamper their ability to anticipate security problems and to adopt innovative solutions to the intractable problems of prison administration. The rule would also distort the decisionmaking process, for every administrative judgment would be subject to the possibility that some court somewhere would conclude that it had a less restrictive way of solving the problem at hand. Courts inevitably would become the primary arbiters of what constitutes the best solution to every administrative problem, thereby unnecessarily perpetuating the involvement of the federal courts in affairs of prison administration.\xe2\x80\x9d 482 U. S., at 89 (internal quotation marks and alteration omitted).

\n

\nThe majority\xe2\x80\x99s failure to heed that advice is inexplicable, especially since Turner itself recognized the \xe2\x80\x9cgrowing problem with prison gangs.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 91. In fact, there is no more \xe2\x80\x9cintractable problem\xe2\x80\x9d inside America\xe2\x80\x99s prisons than racial violence, which is driven by race-based prison gangs. See, e.g., Dawson v. Delaware, 503 U. S. 159, 172\xe2\x80\x93173, and n. 1 (1992) (Thomas, J., dissenting); Stefanow v. McFadden, 103 F.\xc2\xa03d 1466, 1472 (CA9 1996) (\xe2\x80\x9cAnyone familiar with prisons understands the seriousness of the problems caused by prison gangs that are fueled by actively virulent racism and religious bigotry\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority decides this case without addressing the problems that racial violence poses for wardens, guards, and inmates throughout the federal and state prison systems. But that is the core of California\xe2\x80\x99s justification for its policy: It maintains that, if it does not racially separate new cellmates thrown together in close confines during their initial admission or transfer, violence will erupt.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dangers California seeks to prevent are real. See Brief for National Association of Black Law Enforcement Officers, Inc. as Amicus Curiae 12. Controlling prison gangs is the central challenge facing correctional offi-\ncers and administrators. Carlson, Prison Interventions:\nEvolving Strategies to Control Security Threat Groups, 5 Corrections Mgmt. Q. 10 (Winter 2001) (hereinafter Carlson). The worst gangs are highly regimented and\nsophisticated organizations that commit crimes rang-\ning from drug trafficking to theft and murder. Id.,\nat 12; California Dept. of Justice, Division of Law En-\nforcement, Organized Crime in California Annual Report to the California Legislature 2003, p.\xc2\xa015, available at http://caag.state.ca.us/publications/org_crime.pdf. In fact, street gangs are often just an extension of prison gangs, their \xe2\x80\x9cfoot soldiers\xe2\x80\x9d on the outside. Ibid.; Willens, Structure, Content and the Exigencies of War: American Prison Law After Twenty-Five Years 1962\xe2\x80\x931987, 37 Am. U. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 41, 55\xe2\x80\x9356 (1987). And with gang membership on the rise, the percentage of prisoners affiliated with prison gangs more than doubled in the 1990\xe2\x80\x99s.[Footnote 5]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The problem of prison gangs is not unique to California,[Footnote 6] but California has a history like no other. There are at least five major gangs in this country\xe2\x80\x94the Aryan Brotherhood, the Black Guerrilla Family, the Mexican Mafia, La Nuestra Familia, and the Texas Syndicate\xe2\x80\x94all of which originated in California\xe2\x80\x99s prisons.[Footnote 7] Unsurprisingly, then, California has the largest number of gang-related inmates of any correctional system in the country, including the Federal Government. Carlson 16.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As their very names suggest, prison gangs like the Aryan Brotherhood and the Black Guerrilla Family organize themselves along racial lines, and these gangs perpetuate hate and violence. Irwin 182, 184. Interracial murders and assaults among inmates perpetrated by these gangs are common.[Footnote 8] And, again, that brutality is particularly severe in California\xe2\x80\x99s prisons. See, e.g., Walker v. Gomez, 370 F.\xc2\xa03d 969, 971 (CA9 2004) (describing \xe2\x80\x9chistory of significant racial tension and violence\xe2\x80\x9d at Calipatria State Prison); id., at 979\xe2\x80\x93980 (Rymer, J., dissenting) (same); App. 297a\xe2\x80\x93299a (describing 2-year span at Pelican Bay Prison, during which there were no fewer than nine major riots that left at least one inmate dead and many more wounded).

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0It is against this backdrop of pervasive racial violence that California racially segregates inmates in the reception centers\xe2\x80\x99 double cells, for brief periods of up to 60 days, until such time as the State can assign permanent housing. Viewed in that context and in light of the four factors enunciated in Turner, California\xe2\x80\x99s policy is constitutional: The CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy is reasonably related to a legitimate penological interest; alternative means of exercising the restricted right remain open to inmates; racially integrating double cells might negatively impact prison inmates, staff, and administrators; and there are no obvious, easy alternatives to the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy.

\n

1

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0First, the policy is reasonably related to a legitimate penological interest. Turner, 482 U. S., at 89. The protection of inmates and staff is undeniably a legitimate penological interest. See Bell v. Wolfish, 441 U. S. 520, 546\xe2\x80\x93547 (1979). The evidence shows, and Johnson has never contested, that the objective of California\xe2\x80\x99s policy is reducing violence among the inmates and against the staff. No cells are designated for, nor are special privileges afforded to, any racial group. App. 188a, 305a. Because prison administrators use race as a factor in making initial housing assignments \xe2\x80\x9csolely on the basis of [its] potential implications for prison security,\xe2\x80\x9d the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s cell assignment practice is neutral. Thornburgh v. Abbott, 490 U. S. 401, 415 (1989); Turner, supra, at 90.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0California\xe2\x80\x99s policy bears a valid, rational connection to this interest. The racial component to prison violence is impossible for prison administrators to ignore. Johnson himself testified that he is afraid of violence\xe2\x80\x94based solely on the color of his skin.[Footnote 9] In combating that violence, an inmate\xe2\x80\x99s arrival or transfer into a new prison setting is a critical time for inmate and staff alike. The policy protects an inmate from other prisoners, and they from him, while prison officials gather more information, including his gang affiliation, about his compatibility with other inmates. App. 249a. This connection between racial violence and the policy makes it far from \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary or irrational.\xe2\x80\x9d Turner, supra, at 89\xe2\x80\x9390.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, Johnson concedes that it would be perfectly constitutional for California to take account of race \xe2\x80\x9cas part of an overall analysis of proclivity to violence based upon a series of facts existing in that prison.\xe2\x80\x9d Tr. of Oral Arg. 15. But that is precisely what California does. It takes into account a host of factors in addition to race: geographic or national origin, age, physical size, mental health, medical needs, criminal history, and, of course, gang affiliation. Supra, at 4. California does not simply assign inmates to double cells in the reception centers based on race\xe2\x80\x94it also separates intraracially (for example, northern from southern Hispanics or violent from nonviolent offenders).

\n

2

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Second, alternative means of exercising the restricted right remain open to inmates like Johnson. Turner, supra, at 90. The CDC submits, and Johnson does not contest, that all other facets of prison life are fully integrated: work, vocational, and educational assignments; dining halls; and exercise yards and recreational facilities. App. 250a. And after a brief detention period at the reception center, inmates may select their own cellmates regardless of race in the absence of overriding security concerns. Id., at 311a\xe2\x80\x93312a. Simply put, Johnson has spent, and will continue to spend, the vast bulk of his sentence free from any limitation on the race of his cellmate.

\n

3

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Third, Johnson fails to establish that the accommodation he seeks\xe2\x80\x94i.e., assigning inmates to double cells without regard to race\xe2\x80\x94would not significantly impact prison personnel, other inmates, and the allocation of prison resources. Harper, 494 U. S., at 226\xe2\x80\x93227; Turner, supra, at 90. Prison staff cannot see into the double cells without going up to them, and inmates can cover the windows so that staff cannot see inside the cells at all. App. 306a. Because of the limited number of staff to oversee the many cells, it \xe2\x80\x9cwould be very difficult to assist inmates if the staff were needed in several places at one time.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Coordinated gang attacks against nongang cellmates could leave prison officials unable to respond effectively. In any event, diverting prison resources to monitor cells disrupts services elsewhere.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Then, too, fights in the cells are likely to spill over to the exercise yards and common areas. Ibid.; see also id., at 187a. As Turner made clear: \xe2\x80\x9cWhen accommodation of an asserted right will have a significant \xe2\x80\x98ripple effect\xe2\x80\x99 on fellow inmates or on prison staff, courts should be particularly deferential to the informed discretion of corrections officials.\xe2\x80\x9d 482 U. S., at 90; see also White v. Morris, 832 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 1129, 1130 (SD Ohio 1993) (racially integrated double celling contributed to a race riot in which 10 people were murdered). California prison officials are united in the view that racially integrating double cells in the reception centers would lead to serious violence.[Footnote 10] This is precisely the sort of testimony that the Court found persuasive in Turner itself. Turner, supra, at 92.

\n

4

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, Johnson has not shown that there are \xe2\x80\x9cobvious, easy alternatives\xe2\x80\x9d to the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy. Turner, supra, at 90. Johnson contends that, for newly admitted inmates, prison officials need only look to the information available in the presentence report that must accompany a convict to prison. See Cal. Penal Code Ann. \xc2\xa71203(c) (West 2004); Cal. Rules of Court, Criminal Cases, Rule 4.411(d) (West 2004). But prison officials already do this to the extent that they can. Indeed, gang affiliation, not race, is the first factor in determining initial housing assignments. App. 315a. Race becomes the predominant factor only because gang affiliation is often not known, especially with regard to newly admitted inmates. As the Court of Appeals pointed out: \xe2\x80\x9cThere is little chance that inmates will be forthcoming about their past violent episodes or criminal gang activity so as to provide an accurate and dependable picture of the inmate.\xe2\x80\x9d 321 F.\xc2\xa03d 791, 806 (CA9 2003); see also App. 185a, 189a. Even if the CDC had the manpower and resources to prescreen the more than 40,000 new inmates it receives yearly, leafing through presentence reports would not tell prison officials\nwhat they need to know. See ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937 (Stevens, J., dissenting).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Johnson presents a closer case with regard to the segregation of prisoners whom the CDC transfers between facilities. As I understand it, California has less need to segregate prisoners about whom it already knows a great deal (since they have undergone the initial classification process and been housed for some period of time). However, this does not inevitably mean that racially integrating transferred inmates, while obvious and easy, is a true alternative. For instance, an inmate may have affiliated with a gang since the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s last official assessment, or his past lack of racial violence may have been due to the absence of close confinement with members of other races. The CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy does not appear to arise from laziness or neglect; California is a leader in institutional intelligence-gathering. See Carlson 16 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe CDC devotes 75 intelligence staff to gathering and verifying inmate-related information,\xe2\x80\x9d both in prisons and on the streets). In\nshort, applying the policy to transfers is not \xe2\x80\x9carbitrary or irrational,\xe2\x80\x9d requiring that we set aside the considered contrary judgment of prison administrators. Turner, supra, at 89\xe2\x80\x9390.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority claims that strict scrutiny is the applicable standard of review based on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s precedents and its general skepticism of racial classifications. It is wrong on both scores.

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Only once before, in Lee v. Washington, 390 U. S. 333 (1968) (per curiam), has this Court considered the constitutionality of racial classifications in prisons. The majority claims that Lee applied \xe2\x80\x9ca heightened standard of review.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. But Lee did not address the applicable standard of review. And even if it bore on the standard of review, Lee would support the State here.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In Lee, a three-judge District Court ordered Alabama to desegregate its prisons under Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U. S. 483 (1954). Washington v. Lee, 263 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 327, 331\xe2\x80\x93332 (MD Ala. 1966). In so doing, the District Court rejected any notion that \xe2\x80\x9cconsideration[s] of prison security or discipline\xe2\x80\x9d justified the \xe2\x80\x9ccomplete and permanent segregation of the races in all the Alabama penal facilities.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 331. However, the District Court noted \xe2\x80\x9cthat in some isolated instances prison security and discipline necessitates segregation of the races for a limited period.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (footnote omitted). It provided only one example\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98tank\xe2\x80\x99 used in \xe2\x80\xa6 large municipal jails where intoxicated persons are placed upon their initial incarceration and kept until they become sober,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 331, n.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x94and the court left unmentioned why it would have been necessary to separate drunk whites from blacks on a Birmingham Saturday night.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court, in a per curiam, one-paragraph opinion, affirmed the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s order. It found \xe2\x80\x9cunexceptionable\xe2\x80\x9d not only the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s general rule that wholesale segregation of penal facilities was unconstitutional, but also the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9callowance for the necessities of prison security and discipline.\xe2\x80\x9d Lee, 390 U. S., at 334. Indeed, Justices Black, Harlan, and Stewart concurred

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cto make explicit something that is left to be gathered only by implication from the Court\xe2\x80\x99s opinion. This is that prison authorities have the right, acting in good faith and in particularized circumstances, to take into account racial tensions in maintaining security, discipline, and good order in prisons and jails.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (emphasis added).

\n

\nThose Justices were \xe2\x80\x9cunwilling to assume\xe2\x80\x9d that such an \xe2\x80\x9cexplicit pronouncement [would] evinc[e] any dilution of this Court\xe2\x80\x99s firm commitment to the Fourteenth Amendment\xe2\x80\x99s prohibition of racial discrimination.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Lee said nothing about the applicable standard of review, for there was no need. Surely Alabama\xe2\x80\x99s wholesale segregation of its prisons was unconstitutional even under the more deferential standard of review that applies within prisons. This Court\xe2\x80\x99s brief, per curiam opinion in Lee simply cannot bear the weight or interpretation the majority places on it. See U. S. Bancorp Mortgage Co. v. Bonner Mall Partnership, 513 U. S. 18, 24 (1994) (noting \xe2\x80\x9cour customary skepticism toward per curiam dispositions that lack the reasoned consideration of a full opinion\xe2\x80\x9d); Edelman v. Jordan, 415 U. S. 651, 670\xe2\x80\x93671 (1974).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Yet even if Lee had announced a heightened standard of review for prison policies that pertain to race, Lee also carved out an exception to the standard that California\xe2\x80\x99s policy would certainly satisfy. As the Lee concurrence explained without objection, the Court\xe2\x80\x99s exception for \xe2\x80\x9cthe necessities of prison security and discipline\xe2\x80\x9d meant that \xe2\x80\x9cprison authorities have the right, acting in good faith and in particularized circumstances, to take into account racial tensions in maintaining security, discipline, and good order in prisons and jails.\xe2\x80\x9d Lee, supra, at 334 (opinion of Black, Harlan, and Stewart, JJ., concurring) (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0California\xe2\x80\x99s policy\xe2\x80\x94which is a far cry from the wholesale segregation at issue in Lee\xe2\x80\x94would fall squarely within Lee\xe2\x80\x99s exception. Johnson has never argued that California\xe2\x80\x99s policy is motivated by anything other than a desire to protect inmates and staff. And the \xe2\x80\x9cparticularized\xe2\x80\x9d nature of the policy is evident: It applies only to new inmates and transfers, only in a handful of prisons, only to double cells, and only then for a period of no more than two months. In the name of following a test that Lee\ndid not create, the majority opts for a more demanding standard of review than Lee\xe2\x80\x99s language even arguably supports.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority heavily relies on this Court\xe2\x80\x99s statement that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98all racial classifications [imposed by government] \xe2\x80\xa6 must be analyzed by a reviewing court under strict scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 4 (quoting Adarand Constructors, Inc., 515 U. S., at 227). Adarand has nothing to do with this case. Adarand\xe2\x80\x99s statement that \xe2\x80\x9call racial classifications\xe2\x80\x9d are subject to strict scrutiny addressed the contention that classifications favoring rather than disfavoring blacks are exempt. Id., at 226\xe2\x80\x93227; accord, Grutter v. Bollinger, 539 U. S. 306, 353 (2003) (Thomas, J., concurring in part and dissenting in part). None of these statements overruled, sub silentio, Turner and its progeny, especially since the Court has repeatedly held that constitutional demands are diminished in the unique context of prisons. See, e.g., Harper, 494 U. S., at 224; Abbott, 490 U. S., at 407; Turner, 482 U. S., at 85; see also Webster v. Fall, 266 U. S. 507, 511 (1925) (\xe2\x80\x9cQuestions\nwhich merely lurk in the record, neither brought to the attention of the court nor ruled upon, are not to be considered as having been so decided as to constitute precedents\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority offers various other reasons for applying strict scrutiny. None is persuasive. The majority\xe2\x80\x99s main reason is that \xe2\x80\x9cTurner\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable-relationship test [applies] only to rights that are \xe2\x80\x98inconsistent with proper incarceration.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939 (quoting Overton v. Bazzetta, 539 U. S. 126, 131 (2003)). According to the majority, the question is thus whether a right \xe2\x80\x9cneed necessarily be compromised for the sake of proper prison administration.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 9. This inconsistency-with-proper-prison-administration test begs the question at the heart of this case. For a court to know whether any particular right is inconsistent with proper prison administration, it must have some implicit notion of what a proper prison ought to look like and how it ought to be administered. Overton, supra, at 139 (Thomas, J., concurring in judgment). But the very issue in this case is whether such second-guessing is permissible.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s test eviscerates Turner. Inquiring whether a given right is consistent with \xe2\x80\x9cproper prison administration\xe2\x80\x9d calls for precisely the sort of judgments that Turner said courts were ill equipped to make. In none of the cases in which the Court deferred to the judgments of prison officials under Turner did it examine whether \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d prison security and discipline permitted greater speech or associational rights (Abbott, supra; Shaw, 532 U. S. 223; and Overton, supra); expanded access to the courts (Lewis v. Casey, 518 U. S. 343 (1996)); broader freedom from bodily restraint (Harper, supra); or additional free exercise rights (O\xe2\x80\x99Lone, 482 U. S. 342). The Court has steadfastly refused to undertake the threshold standard-of-review inquiry that Turner settled, and that the majority today resurrects. And with good reason: As Turner pointed out, these judgments are better left in the first instance to the officials who run our Nation\xe2\x80\x99s prisons, not to the judges who run its courts.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In place of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s usual deference, the majority gives conclusive force to its own guesswork about \xe2\x80\x9cproper\xe2\x80\x9d prison administration. It hypothesizes that California\xe2\x80\x99s policy might incite, rather than diminish, racial hostility.[Footnote 11] Ante, at 6\xe2\x80\x937; see also ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, and n. 2 (Stevens, J., dissenting). The majority\xe2\x80\x99s speculations are implausible. New arrivals have a strong interest in promptly convincing other inmates of their willingness to use violent force. See Brief for National Association of Black Law Enforcement Officers, Inc., as Amicus Curiae 13\xe2\x80\x9314 (citing commentary and congressional findings); cf. United States v. Santiago, 46 F.\xc2\xa03d 885, 888 (CA9 1995) (describing one Hispanic inmate\xe2\x80\x99s murder of another in order to join the Mexican Mafia); United States v. Silverstein, 732 F.\xc2\xa02d 1338, 1341 (CA7 1984) (prospective members of the Aryan Brotherhood must \xe2\x80\x9cmake bones,\xe2\x80\x9d or commit a murder, to be eligible for membership). In any event, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s guesswork falls far short of the compelling showing needed to overcome the deference we owe to prison\nadministrators.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority contends that the Court \xe2\x80\x9c[has] put the burden on state actors to demonstrate that their race-based policies are justified,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 5, n.\xc2\xa01, and \xe2\x80\x9c[has] refused to defer to state officials\xe2\x80\x99 judgments on race in other areas where those officials traditionally exercise substantial discretion,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312. Yet two Terms ago, in upholding the University of Michigan Law School\xe2\x80\x99s affirmative-action program, this Court deferred to the judgment by the law school\xe2\x80\x99s faculty and administrators on their need for diversity in the student body. See Grutter, supra, at 328 (\xe2\x80\x9cThe Law School\xe2\x80\x99s educational judgment that \xe2\x80\xa6 diversity is essential to its educational mission is one to which we defer\xe2\x80\x9d). Deference would seem all the more warranted in the prison context, for whatever the Court knows of administering educational institutions, it knows much less about administering penal ones. The potential consequences of second-guessing the judgments of prison administrators are also much more severe. See White v. Morris, 832 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 1129, 1130 (SD Ohio 1993) (racially integrated double celling that resulted from federal consent decree was a factor in the worst prison riot in Ohio history). More important, as I have explained, the Court has recognized that the typically exacting review it applies to restrictions on fundamental rights must be relaxed in the unique context of prisons. See, e.g., Harper, supra, at 224; Abbott, 490 U. S., at 407; Turner, 482 U. S., at 85. The majority cannot fall back on the Constitution\xe2\x80\x99s usual demands, because those demands have always been lessened inside the prison walls. See supra, at 6\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority also mentions that California\xe2\x80\x99s policy may be the only one of its kind, as virtually all other States and the Federal Government manage their prison systems without racially segregating inmates. Ante, at 7. This is both irrelevant and doubtful. It is irrelevant because the number of States that have followed California\xe2\x80\x99s lead matters not to the applicable standard of review (the only issue the Court today decides), but to whether California satisfies whatever standard applies, a question the majority leaves to be addressed on remand. In other words, the uniqueness of California\xe2\x80\x99s policy might show whether the policy is reasonable or narrowly tailored\xe2\x80\x94but deciding whether to apply Turner or strict scrutiny in the first instance must depend on something else, like the majority\xe2\x80\x99s inconsistency-with-proper-prison-administration test. The commonness of California\xe2\x80\x99s housing policy is further irrelevant because strict scrutiny now applies to all claims of racial discrimination in prisons, regardless of whether the policies being challenged are unusual.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority\xe2\x80\x99s assertion is doubtful, because at least two other States apply similar policies to newly admitted inmates. Both Oklahoma and Texas, like California, assign newly admitted inmates to racially segregated cells in their prison reception centers.[Footnote 12] The similarity is not surprising: States like California and Texas have historically had the most severe problems with prison gangs. However, even States with less severe problems maintain that policies like California\xe2\x80\x99s are necessary to deal with race-related prison violence. See Brief of the States of Utah, Alabama, Alaska, Delaware, Idaho, Nevada, New Hampshire and North Dakota as Amici Curiae 16. Relatedly, 10.3% of all wardens at maximum security facilities in the United States report that their inmates are assigned to racially segregated cells\xe2\x80\x94apparently on a permanent basis. M. Henderson, F. Cullen, L. Carroll, & W. Feinberg, Race, Rights, and Order in Prison: A National Survey of Wardens on the Racial Integration of Prison Cells, 80 Prison J. 295, 304 (Sept. 2000). In the same survey, 4.3% of the wardens report that their States have an official policy against racially integrating male inmates in cells. Id., at 302. Presumably, for the remainder of prisons in which inmates are assigned to racially segregated cells, that policy is the result of discretionary decisions by wardens rather than of official state directives. Ibid. In any event, the ongoing debate about the best way to reduce racial violence in prisons should not be resolved by judicial decree: It is the job \xe2\x80\x9cof prison administrators \xe2\x80\xa6 and not the courts, to make the difficult judgments concerning institutional operations.\xe2\x80\x9d Jones, 433 U. S., at 128.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The majority also observes that we have already carved out an exception to Turner for Eighth Amendment claims of cruel and unusual punishment in prison. See Hope v. Pelzer, 536 U. S. 730, 738 (2002). In that context, we have held that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] prison official\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98deliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x99 to a substantial risk of serious harm to an inmate violates the Eighth Amendment.\xe2\x80\x9d Farmer v. Brennan, 511 U. S. 825, 828 (1994). Setting aside whether claims challenging inmates\xe2\x80\x99 conditions of confinement should be cognizable under the Eighth Amendment at all, see Hudson v. McMillian, 503 U. S. 1, 18\xe2\x80\x9319 (1992) (Thomas, J., dissenting), the \xe2\x80\x9cdeliberate indifference\xe2\x80\x9d standard does not bolster the majority\xe2\x80\x99s argument. If anything, that standard is more deferential to the judgments of prison administrators than Turner\xe2\x80\x99s reasonable-relationship test: It subjects prison officials to liability only when they are subjectively aware of the risk to the inmate, and they fail to take reasonable measures to abate the risk. Farmer, supra, at 847. It certainly does not demonstrate the wisdom of an exception that imposes a heightened standard of review on the actions of prison officials.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Moreover, the majority\xe2\x80\x99s decision subjects prison officials to competing and perhaps conflicting demands. In this case, California prison officials have uniformly averred that random double-celling poses a substantial risk of serious harm to the celled inmates. App. 245a\xe2\x80\x93246a, 251a. If California assigned inmates to double cells without regard to race, knowing full well that violence might result, that would seem the very definition of deliberate indifference. See Robinson v. Prunty, 249 F.\xc2\xa03d 862, 864\xe2\x80\x93865 (CA9 2001) (prisoner alleged an Eighth Amendment violation because administrators had failed to consider race when releasing inmates into the yards); Jensen v. Clarke, 94 F.\xc2\xa03d 1191, 1201, 1204 (CA8 1996) (court held that random double celling by prison officials constituted deliberate indifference, and affirmed an injunction and attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees awarded against the officials). Nor would a victimized inmate need to prove that prison officials had anticipated any particular attack; it would be sufficient that prison officials had ignored a dangerous condition that was chronic and ongoing\xe2\x80\x94like interracial housing in closely confined quarters within prisons dominated by racial gangs. Farmer, supra, at 843\xe2\x80\x93844. Under Farmer, prison officials could have been ordered to take account of the very thing to which they may now have to turn a blind eye: inmates\xe2\x80\x99 race.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, the majority presents a parade of horribles designed to show that applying the Turner standard would grant prison officials unbounded discretion to segregate inmates throughout prisons. See ante, at 13. But we have never treated Turner as a blank check to prison officials. Quite to the contrary, this Court has long had \xe2\x80\x9cconfidence that . . . a reasonableness standard is not toothless.\xe2\x80\x9d Abbott, 490 U. S., at 414 (internal quotation marks omitted). California prison officials segregate only double cells, because only those cells are particularly difficult to monitor\xe2\x80\x94unlike \xe2\x80\x9cdining halls, yards, and general housing areas.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 13. Were California\xe2\x80\x99s policy not so narrow, the State might well have race-neutral means at its disposal capable of accommodating prisoners\xe2\x80\x99 rights without sacrificing their safety. See Turner, 482 U. S., at 90\xe2\x80\x9391. The majority does not say why Turner\xe2\x80\x99s standard ably polices all other constitutional infirmities, just not racial discrimination. In any event, it is not the refusal to apply\xe2\x80\x94for the first time ever\xe2\x80\x94a strict standard of review in the prison context that is \xe2\x80\x9cfundamentally at odds\xe2\x80\x9d with our constitutional jurisprudence. Ante, at 5, n.\xc2\xa01. Instead, it is the majority\xe2\x80\x99s refusal\xe2\x80\x94for the first time ever\xe2\x80\x94to defer to the expert judgment of prison officials.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even under strict scrutiny analysis, \xe2\x80\x9cit is possible, even likely, that prison officials could show that the current policy meets the test.\xe2\x80\x9d 336 F.\xc2\xa03d 1117, 1121 (CA9 2003) (Ferguson, J., joined by Pregerson, Nelson, and Reinhardt, JJ., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc). As Johnson concedes, all States have a compelling interest in maintaining order and internal security within their prisons. See Reply Brief for Petitioner 18; see also Procunier, 416 U. S., at 404. Thus the question on remand will be whether the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy is narrowly tailored to serve California\xe2\x80\x99s compelling interest.[Footnote 13] The other dissent notes the absence of evidence on that question, see ante, at 3\xe2\x80\x934 (opinion of Stevens, J.), but that is hardly California\xe2\x80\x99s fault.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0From the outset, Johnson himself has alleged, in terms taken from Turner, that the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy is \xe2\x80\x9cnot related to a legitimate penological interest.\xe2\x80\x9d Johnson v. California, 207 F.\xc2\xa03d 650, 655 (CA9 2000) (discussing Johnson\xe2\x80\x99s Third Amended Complaint). In reinstating Johnson\xe2\x80\x99s equal protection claim following the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s dismissal, the Court of Appeals repeated Johnson\xe2\x80\x99s allegation, without indicating that strict scrutiny should apply on remand before the District Court.[Footnote 14] Ibid. And on remand, again Johnson alleged only that the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy \xe2\x80\x9cis not reasonably related to the legitimate penological interests of the CDC.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 51a (Fourth Amended Complaint \xc2\xb623).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After the District Court granted qualified immunity to some of the defendants, Johnson once again appealed. In his brief before the Court of Appeals, Johnson assumed that both Lee and Turner applied, without arguing that there was any tension between them; indeed, nowhere in his brief did Johnson even mention the words \xe2\x80\x9cstrict scrutiny.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Appellant in No. 01\xe2\x80\x9356436 (CA9), pp. 20, 26; 2001 WL 34091249. Perhaps as a result, the Court of Appeals did not discuss strict scrutiny in its second decision, the one currently before this Court. The Court of Appeals did find tension between Lee and Turner; however, it resolved this tension in Turner\xe2\x80\x99s favor. 321 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 799. Yet the Court of Appeals accepted Lee\xe2\x80\x99s test at face value: Prison officials may only make racial classifications \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in good faith and in particularized circumstances.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d 321 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 797. The Court of Appeals, like Johnson, did not equate Lee\xe2\x80\x99s test with strict scrutiny, and in fact it mentioned strict scrutiny only when it quoted the portion of Turner that rejects strict scrutiny as the proper standard of review in the prison context. 321 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 798. Even Johnson did not make the leap equating Lee with strict scrutiny when he requested that the Court of Appeals rehear his case. Appellant\xe2\x80\x99s Petition for Panel Rehearing with Suggestion for Rehearing En Banc in No. 01\xe2\x80\x9356436 (CA9), pp. 4\xe2\x80\x935. That leap was first made by the judges who dissented from the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 denial of rehearing en banc. 336 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 1118 (Ferguson, J., joined by Pregerson, Nelson, and Reinhardt, JJ., dissenting from denial of rehearing en banc).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, California is now, after the close of discovery, subject to a more stringent standard than it had any reason to anticipate from Johnson\xe2\x80\x99s pleadings, the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 initial decision, or even the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision below. In such circumstances, California should be allowed to present evidence of narrow tailoring, evidence it was never obligated to present in either appearance before the District Court. See Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council, 505 U. S. 1003, 1031\xe2\x80\x931032 (1992) (remanding for consideration under the correct legal standard); id., at 1033 (Kennedy, J., concurring in judgment) (\xe2\x80\x9cAlthough we establish a framework for remand, \xe2\x80\xa6 we do not decide the ultimate [constitutional] question [because] [t]he facts necessary to the determination have not been developed in the record\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

***

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Garrison Johnson challenges not permanent, but temporary, segregation of only a portion of California\xe2\x80\x99s prisons. Of the 17 years Johnson has been incarcerated, California has assigned him a cellmate of the same race for no more than a year (and probably more like four months); Johnson has had black cellmates during the other 16 years, but by his own choice. Nothing in the record demonstrates that if Johnson (or any other prisoner) requested to be housed with a person of a different race, it would be denied (though Johnson\xe2\x80\x99s gang affiliation with the Crips might stand in his way). Moreover, Johnson concedes that California\xe2\x80\x99s prisons are racially violent places, and that he lives in fear of being attacked because of his race. Perhaps on remand the CDC\xe2\x80\x99s policy will survive strict scrutiny, but in the event that it does not, Johnson may well have won a Pyrrhic victory.

App. 106a, 112a\xe2\x80\x93113a, 175a.
