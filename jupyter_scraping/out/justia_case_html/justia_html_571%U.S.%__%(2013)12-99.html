\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tJustice Breyer, with whom Justice Sotomayor and Justice Kagan join, dissenting.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSection 302(a) of the Labor Management Relations Act, 1947, \n\n61Stat.\n157, as amended, an antibribery provision, makes it a crime for an employer \xe2\x80\x9cto pay, lend, or deliver, or agree to pay, lend, or deliver, any money or other thing of value\xe2\x80\x9d to a labor union that represents or seeks to represent its employees. \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7186(a)(2). Section 302(b) makes it a crime \xe2\x80\x9cfor any person to request [or] demand .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, or agree to receive or accept, any payment, loan, or delivery of any money or other thing of value prohibited by subsection (a).\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7186(b)(1). The question in this case is whether an employer violates \xc2\xa7302(a) by making the following promises to a union that seeks to represent its employees: (1) that the employer will remain neutral in respect to the union\xe2\x80\x99s efforts to organize its employees, (2) that the union will be given access (for organizing purposes) to nonpublic areas of the employer\xe2\x80\x99s premises, and (3) that the union will receive a list of employees\xe2\x80\x99 names and contact information (also for organizing purposes). A further question (the other side of the same coin) is whether a union violates \xc2\xa7302(b) by requesting that the employer perform its contractual obligations to fulfill these promises.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Eleventh Circuit held that these items are \xe2\x80\x9cthing[s] of value\xe2\x80\x9d and that an employer\xe2\x80\x99s promise to \xe2\x80\x9cpay\xe2\x80\x9d them in return for something of value from the union violates the Act if the employer intends to use the payment to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrupt\xe2\x80\x9d the union; the Eleventh Circuit also held that a union\xe2\x80\x99s request that an employer make such a payment violates \xc2\xa7302(b) if the union intends to \xe2\x80\x9cextort\xe2\x80\x9d the benefit from the employer. 667 F.\xc2\xa03d 1211, 1215\xe2\x80\x931216 (2012). Other Circuits have held to the contrary, reasoning that similar promises by an employer to assist a union\xe2\x80\x99s orga- nizing campaign (or merely to avoid opposing the campaign) fall outside the scope of \xc2\xa7302. See Adcock v. Freightliner LLC, 550 F.\xc2\xa03d 369 (CA4 2008); Hotel Employees & Restaurant Employees Union, Local 57 v. Sage Hospitality Resources, LLC, 390 F.\xc2\xa03d 206 (CA3 2004). We granted certiorari to resolve the conflict.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe have received briefs on the issue, and we have heard oral argument. But in considering the briefs and argument, we became aware of two logically antecedent questions that could prevent us from reaching the question of the correct interpretation of \xc2\xa7302. First, it is possible that the case is moot because the contract between the employer and union that contained the allegedly criminal promises appears to have expired by the end of 2011, before the Eleventh Circuit rendered its decision on the scope of \xc2\xa7302. Second, it is arguable that respondent Mulhall, the sole plaintiff in this case, lacks Article III standing.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn my view, rather than dismiss the writ of certiorari as improvidently granted, the Court should simply ask for additional briefs addressing these two questions. If it turns out that the federal courts lack jurisdiction either because the case is moot or because Mulhall lacks standing, then we cannot reach the merits. But if that is the case, then we should likely order the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision vacated, thereby removing its precedential effect and leaving the merits question open to be resolved in a later case that does fall within the jurisdiction of the federal courts.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tI believe we should also ask for further briefing on a third question: the question whether \xc2\xa7302 authorizes a private right of action. I recognize that the Court said, long ago and in passing, that \xc2\xa7302(e) \xe2\x80\x9cpermit[s] private litigants to obtain injunctions\xe2\x80\x9d for violations of \xc2\xa7302. Sinclair Refining Co. v. Atkinson, \n\n370 U.\xc2\xa0S. 195, \n205 (1962)\n, overruled in part on other grounds, Boys Markets, Inc. v. Retail Clerks, \n\n398 U.\xc2\xa0S. 235\n\xe2\x80\x93238 (1970). But, in light of the Court\xe2\x80\x99s more restrictive views on private rights of action in recent decades, see, e.g., Alexander v. Sandoval, \n\n532 U.\xc2\xa0S. 275\n\xe2\x80\x93287 (2001), the legal status of Sinclair Refining\xe2\x80\x99s dictum is uncertain. And if \xc2\xa7302 in fact does not provide a right of action to private parties like Mulhall, then courts will not need to reach difficult questions about the scope of \xc2\xa7302, as happened in this case, unless the Federal Government decides to prosecute such cases rather than limit its attention to cases that clearly fall within the statute\xe2\x80\x99s core antibribery purpose.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnless resolved, the differences among the Courts of Appeals could negatively affect the collective-bargaining process. This is because the Eleventh Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision raises the specter that an employer or union official could be found guilty of a crime that carries a 5-year maximum sentence, see \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7186(d), if the employer or union official is found to have made certain commonplace orga- nizing assistance agreements with the intent to \xe2\x80\x9ccorrupt\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cextort.\xe2\x80\x9d In my view, given the importance of the question presented to the collective-bargaining process, further briefing, rather than dismissal, is the better course of action.\n\t\t\t
