\n\t\t\t\tCHRISTOPHER et\xc2\xa0al. v. SMITHKLINE BEECHAM CORP., dba GLAXOSMITHKLINE\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tcertiorari to the united states court of appeals for the ninth circuit\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tNo. 11\xe2\x80\x93204.\xe2\x80\x83Argued April 16, 2012\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 18, 2012\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\tThe Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) requires employers to pay employees overtime wages, see \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7207(a), but this requirement does not apply with respect to workers employed \xe2\x80\x9cin the capacity of outside salesman,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(a)(1). Congress did not elaborate on the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman,\xe2\x80\x9d but it delegated authority to the Department of Labor (DOL) to issue regulations to define the term. Three of the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s regulations are relevant to this case. First, 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.500 defines \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman\xe2\x80\x9d to mean \xe2\x80\x9cany employee .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [w]hose primary duty is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. making sales within the meaning of [\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7203(k)].\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7541.500(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2). Section 203(k), in turn, states that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[s]ale\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98sell\xe2\x80\x99 includes any sale, exchange, contract to sell, consignment for sale, shipment for sale, or other disposition.\xe2\x80\x9d Second, \xc2\xa7541.501 clarifies that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ales within the meaning of [\xc2\xa7203(k)] include the transfer of title to tangible property.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7541.501(b). Third, \xc2\xa7541.503 provides that promotion work that is \xe2\x80\x9cperformed incidental to and in conjunction with an employee\xe2\x80\x99s own outside sales or solicitations is exempt work,\xe2\x80\x9d whereas promotion work that is \xe2\x80\x9cincidental to sales made, or to be made, by someone else is not.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7541.503(a). The DOL provided additional guidance in connection with its promulgation of these regulations, stressing that an employee is an \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman\xe2\x80\x9d when the employee \xe2\x80\x9cin some sense, has made sales.\xe2\x80\x9d 69 Fed. Reg. 22162.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe prescription drug industry is subject to extensive federal regulation, including the requirement that prescription drugs be dispensed only upon a physician\xe2\x80\x99s prescription. In light of this requirement, pharmaceutical companies have long focused their direct marketing efforts on physicians. Pharmaceutical companies promote their products to physicians through a process called \xe2\x80\x9cdetailing,\xe2\x80\x9d whereby employees known as \xe2\x80\x9cdetailers\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpharmaceutical sales representatives\xe2\x80\x9d try to persuade physicians to write prescriptions for the products in appropriate cases. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners were employed by respondent as pharmaceutical sales representatives for roughly four years, and during that time their primary objective was to obtain a nonbinding commitment from physicians to prescribe respondent\xe2\x80\x99s products in appropriate cases. Each week, petitioners spent about 40 hours in the field calling on physicians during normal business hours and an additional 10 to 20 hours attending events and performing other miscellaneous tasks. Petitioners were not required to punch a clock or report their hours, and they were subject to only minimal supervision. Petitioners were well compensated for their efforts, and their gross pay included both a base salary and incentive pay. The amount of incentive pay was determined based on the performance of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 assigned portfolio of drugs in their assigned sales territories. It is undisputed that petitioners were not paid time-and-a-half wages when they worked more than 40 hours per week. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners filed suit, alleging that respondent violated the FLSA by failing to compensate them for overtime. Respondent moved for summary judgment, arguing that petitioners were \xe2\x80\x9cemployed in the capacity of outside salesman,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(a)(1), and therefore were exempt from the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s overtime compensation requirement. The District Court agreed and granted summary judgment to respondent. Petitioners filed a motion to alter or amend the judgment, contending that the District Court had erred in failing to accord controlling deference to the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the pertinent regulations, which the DOL had announced in an amicus brief filed in a similar action. The District Court rejected this argument and denied the motion. The Ninth Circuit, agreeing that the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation was not entitled to controlling deference, affirmed. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tHeld:\xc2\xa0Petitioners qualify as outside salesmen under the most reasonable interpretation of the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s regulations. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9325.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(a)\xc2\xa0The DOL filed amicus briefs in the Second Circuit and the Ninth Circuit in which it took the view that \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98sale\xe2\x80\x99 for the purposes of the outside sales exemption requires a consummated transaction directly involving the employee for whom the exemption is sought.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for Secretary of Labor as Amicus Curiae in In\xc2\xa0re Novartis Wage and Hour Litigation, No. 09\xe2\x80\x930437 (CA2), p.\xc2\xa011. The DOL changed course after the Court granted certiorari in this case, however, and now maintains that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n employee does not make a \xe2\x80\x98sale\xe2\x80\x99 . . . unless he actually transfers title to the property at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 12\xe2\x80\x9313. The DOL\xe2\x80\x99s current interpretation of its regulations is not entitled to deference under Auer v. Robbins, \n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452\n. Although Auer ordinarily calls for deference to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its own ambiguous regulation, even when that interpretation is advanced in a legal brief, see, id., at 461\xe2\x80\x93462, this general rule does not apply in all cases. Deference is inappropriate, for example, when the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98plainly erroneous or inconsistent with the regulation,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 461, or when there is reason to suspect that the interpretation \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not reflect the agency\xe2\x80\x99s fair and considered judgment on the matter,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 462. There are strong reasons for withholding Auer deference in this case. Petitioners invoke the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation to impose potentially massive liability on respondent for conduct that occurred well before the interpretation was announced. To defer to the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation would result in precisely the kind of \xe2\x80\x9cunfair surprise\xe2\x80\x9d against which this Court has long warned. See, e.g., Long Island Care at Home, Ltd. v. Coke, \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 158\n\xe2\x80\x93171. Until 2009, the pharmaceutical industry had little reason to suspect that its longstanding practice of treating detailers as exempt outside salesmen transgressed the FLSA. The statute and regulations do not provide clear notice. Even more important, despite the industry\xe2\x80\x99s decades-long practice, the DOL never initiated any enforcement actions with respect to detailers or otherwise suggested that it thought the industry was acting unlawfully. The only plausible explanation for the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s inaction is acquiescence. Whatever the general merits of Auer deference, it is unwarranted here. The DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation should instead be given a measure of deference proportional to its power to persuade. See United States v. Mead Corp., \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218\n. Pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9314.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(b)\xc2\xa0The DOL\xe2\x80\x99s current interpretation\xe2\x80\x94that a sale demands a transfer of title\xe2\x80\x94is quite unpersuasive. It plainly lacks the hallmarks of thorough consideration. Because the DOL first announced its view that pharmaceutical sales representatives are not outside salesmen in a series of amicus briefs, there was no opportunity for public comment, and the interpretation that initially emerged from the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s internal decisionmaking process proved to be untenable. The interpretation is also flatly inconsistent with the FLSA. The statute defines \xe2\x80\x9csale\xe2\x80\x9d to mean, inter alia, a \xe2\x80\x9cconsignment for sale,\xe2\x80\x9d and a \xe2\x80\x9cconsignment for sale\xe2\x80\x9d does not involve the transfer of title. The DOL relies heavily on 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.501, which provides that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ales . . . include the transfer of title to tangible property,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7541.501(b), but it is apparent that this regulation does not mean that a sale must include a transfer of title, only that transactions involving a transfer of title are included within the term \xe2\x80\x9csale.\xe2\x80\x9d The DOL\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cexplanation that obtaining a non-binding commitment to prescribe a drug constitutes promotion, and not sales,\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioners 17, is also unconvincing. Since promotion work that is performed incidental to an employee\xe2\x80\x99s own sales is exempt, the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that detailers perform only nonexempt promotion work is only as strong as the reasoning underlying its conclusion that those employees do not make sales. Pp.\xc2\xa014\xe2\x80\x9316. \n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(c)\xc2\xa0Because the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is neither entitled to Auer deference nor persuasive in its own right, traditional tools of interpretation must be employed to determine whether petitioners are exempt outside salesmen. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9324.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(1)\xc2\xa0The FLSA does not furnish a clear answer to this question, but it provides at least one interpretive clue by exempting anyone \xe2\x80\x9cemployed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the capacity of [an] outside salesman.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(1). The statute\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on \xe2\x80\x9ccapacity\xe2\x80\x9d counsels in favor of a functional, rather than a formal, inquiry, one that views an employee\xe2\x80\x99s responsibilities in the context of the particular industry in which the employee works. The DOL\xe2\x80\x99s regulations provide additional guidance. Section 541.500 defines an outside salesman as an employee whose primary duty is \xe2\x80\x9cmaking sales\xe2\x80\x9d and adopts the statu- tory definition of \xe2\x80\x9csale.\xe2\x80\x9d This statutory definition contains at least three important textual clues. First, the definition is introduced with the verb \xe2\x80\x9cincludes,\xe2\x80\x9d which indicates that the examples enumerated in the text are illustrative, not exhaustive. See Burgess v. United States, \n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 124\n, n.\xc2\xa03. Second, the list of transactions included in the statutory definition is modified by \xe2\x80\x9cany,\xe2\x80\x9d which, in the context of \xc2\xa7203(k), is best read to mean \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98one or some indiscriminately of whatever kind,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Gonzales, \n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1\n. Third, the definition includes the broad catchall phrase \xe2\x80\x9cother disposition.\xe2\x80\x9d Under the rule of ejusdem generis, the catchall phrase is most reasonably interpreted as including those arrangements that are tantamount, in a particular industry, to a paradigmatic sale of a commodity. Nothing in the remaining regulations requires a narrower construction. Pp.\xc2\xa016\xe2\x80\x9320.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t(2)\xc2\xa0Given this interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cother disposition,\xe2\x80\x9d it follows that petitioners made sales under the FLSA and thus are exempt outside salesmen within the meaning of the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s regulations. Petitioners obtain nonbinding commitments from physicians to prescribe respondent\xe2\x80\x99s drugs. This kind of arrangement, in the unique regula- tory environment within which pharmaceutical companies operate, comfortably falls within the catchall category of \xe2\x80\x9cother disposition.\xe2\x80\x9d That petitioners bear all of the external indicia of salesmen provides further support for this conclusion. And this holding also comports with the apparent purpose of the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s exemption. The exemption is premised on the belief that exempt employees normally earn salaries well above the minimum wage and perform a kind of work that is difficult to standardize to a particular time frame and that cannot easily be spread to other workers. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x94each of whom earned an average of more than $70,000 per year and spent 10 to 20 hours outside normal business hours each week performing work related to his assigned portfolio of drugs in his assigned sales territory\xe2\x80\x94are hardly the kind of employees that the FLSA was intended to protect. Pp.\xc2\xa020\xe2\x80\x9322.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) imposes minimum wage and maximum hours requirements on employers, see 29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7206\xe2\x80\x93207 (2006 ed. and Supp. IV), but those requirements do not apply to workers employed \xe2\x80\x9cin the capacity of outside salesman,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(a)(1). This case requires us to decide whether the term \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman,\xe2\x80\x9d as defined by Department of Labor (DOL or Department) regulations, encompasses pharmaceutical sales representatives whose primary duty is to obtain nonbinding commitments from physicians to prescribe their employ- er\xe2\x80\x99s prescription drugs in appropriate cases. We conclude that these employees qualify as \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesm[e]n.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress enacted the FLSA in 1938 with the goal of \xe2\x80\x9cprotect[ing] all covered workers from substandard wages and oppressive working hours.\xe2\x80\x9d Barrentine v. Arkansas-Best Freight System, Inc., \n\n450 U.\xc2\xa0S. 728, \n739 (1981)\n; see also \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7202(a). Among other requirements, the FLSA obligates employers to compensate employees for hours in excess of 40 per week at a rate of 1\xc2\xbd times the employees\xe2\x80\x99 regular wages. See \xc2\xa7207(a). The overtime compensation requirement, however, does not apply with respect to all employees. See \xc2\xa7213. As relevant here, the statute exempts workers \xe2\x80\x9cemployed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the capacity of outside salesman.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(a)(1).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tCongress did not define the term \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman,\xe2\x80\x9d but it delegated authority to the DOL to issue regulations \xe2\x80\x9cfrom time to time\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9cdefin[e] and delimi[t]\xe2\x80\x9d the term. Ibid. The DOL promulgated such regulations in 1938, 1940, and 1949. In 2004, following notice-and-comment procedures, the DOL reissued the regulations with minor amendments. See 69 Fed. Reg. 22122 (2004). The current regulations are nearly identical in substance to the regulations issued in the years immediately following the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s enactment. See 29 CFR \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7541.500\xe2\x80\x93541.504 (2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThree of the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s regulations are directly relevant to this case: \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7541.500, 541.501, and 541.503. We refer to these three regulations as the \xe2\x80\x9cgeneral regulation,\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9csales regulation,\xe2\x80\x9d and the \xe2\x80\x9cpromotion-work regulation,\xe2\x80\x9d respectively.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe general regulation sets out the definition of the statutory term \xe2\x80\x9cemployee employed in the capacity of outside salesman.\xe2\x80\x9d It defines the term to mean \xe2\x80\x9cany employee .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [w]hose primary duty is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. making sales within the meaning of [\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7203(k)]\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and \xe2\x80\x9c[w]ho is customarily and regularly engaged away from the employer\xe2\x80\x99s place or places of business in performing such primary duty.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7541.500(a)(1)\xe2\x80\x93(2). The referenced statutory provision, \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7203(k), states that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[s]ale\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98sell\xe2\x80\x99 includes any sale, exchange, contract to sell, consignment for sale, shipment for sale, or other disposition.\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, un- der the general regulation, an outside salesman is any employee whose primary duty is making any sale, exchange, contract to sell, consignment for sale, shipment for sale, or other disposition.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe sales regulation restates the statutory definition of sale discussed above and clarifies that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ales within the meaning of [\n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7203(k)] include the transfer of title to tangible property, and in certain cases, of tangible and valuable evidences of intangible property.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.501(b).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the promotion-work regulation identifies \xe2\x80\x9c[p]romotion work\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cone type of activity often performed by persons who make sales, which may or may not be exempt outside sales work, depending upon the circumstances under which it is performed.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7541.503(a). Promotion work that is \xe2\x80\x9cperformed incidental to and in conjunction with an employee\xe2\x80\x99s own outside sales or solicitations is exempt work,\xe2\x80\x9d whereas promotion work that is \xe2\x80\x9cincidental to sales made, or to be made, by someone else is not exempt outside sales work.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAdditional guidance concerning the scope of the outside salesman exemption can be gleaned from reports issued in connection with the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s promulgation of regulations in 1940 and 1949, and from the preamble to the 2004 regulations. See Dept. of Labor, Wage and Hour Division, Report and Recommendations of the Presiding Officer at Hearings Preliminary to Redefinition (1940) (hereinafter 1940 Report); Dept. of Labor, Wage and Hour Division, Report and Recommendations on Proposed Revisions of Regulations, Part 541 (1949) (hereinafter 1949 Report); 69 Fed. Reg. 22160\xe2\x80\x9322163 (hereinafter Preamble). Although the DOL has rejected proposals to eliminate or dilute the requirement that outside salesmen make their own sales, the Department has stressed that this requirement is met whenever an employee \xe2\x80\x9cin some sense make[s] a sale.\xe2\x80\x9d 1940 Report 46; see also Preamble 22162 (reiterating that the exemption applies only to an employee who \xe2\x80\x9cin some sense, has made sales\xe2\x80\x9d). And the DOL has made it clear that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xempt status should not depend\xe2\x80\x9d on technicalities, such as \xe2\x80\x9cwhether it is the sales employee or the customer who types the order into a computer system and hits the return button,\xe2\x80\x9d Preamble 22163, or whether \xe2\x80\x9cthe order is filled by [a] jobber rather than directly by [the employee\xe2\x80\x99s] own employer,\xe2\x80\x9d 1949 Report 83.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent SmithKline Beecham Corporation is in the business of developing, manufacturing, and selling prescription drugs. The prescription drug industry is subject to extensive federal regulation, including the now-familiar requirement that prescription drugs be dispensed only upon a physician\xe2\x80\x99s prescription.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t4\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t In light of this requirement, pharmaceutical companies have long focused their direct marketing efforts, not on the retail pharmacies that dispense prescription drugs, but rather on the medi- cal practitioners who possess the authority to prescribe the drugs in the first place. Pharmaceutical companies promote their prescription drugs to physicians through a process called \xe2\x80\x9cdetailing,\xe2\x80\x9d whereby employees known as \xe2\x80\x9cdetailers\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cpharmaceutical sales representatives\xe2\x80\x9d provide information to physicians about the company\xe2\x80\x99s products in hopes of persuading them to write prescriptions for the products in appropriate cases. See Sorrell v. IMS Health Inc., 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 1\xe2\x80\x932) (describing the process of \xe2\x80\x9cdetailing\xe2\x80\x9d). The position of \xe2\x80\x9cdetailer\xe2\x80\x9d has existed in the pharmaceutical industry in substantially its current form since at least the 1950\xe2\x80\x99s, and in recent years the industry has employed more than 90,000 detailers nationwide. See 635 F.\xc2\xa03d 383, 387, and n.\xc2\xa05, 396 (CA9 2011).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tRespondent hired petitioners Michael Christopher and Frank Buchanan as pharmaceutical sales representatives in 2003. During the roughly four years when petitioners were employed in that capacity,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t5\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t they were responsible for calling on physicians in an assigned sales territory to discuss the features, benefits, and risks of an assigned portfolio of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s prescription drugs. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 primary objective was to obtain a nonbinding commitment\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t6\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t from the physician to prescribe those drugs in appropriate cases, and the training that petitioners received underscored the importance of that objective.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners spent about 40 hours each week in the field calling on physicians. These visits occurred during normal business hours, from about 8:30 a.m. to 5 p.m. Outside of normal business hours, petitioners spent an additional 10 to 20 hours each week attending events, reviewing product information, returning phone calls, responding to e-mails, and performing other miscellaneous tasks. Petitioners were not required to punch a clock or report their hours, and they were subject to only minimal supervision.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners were well compensated for their efforts. On average, Christopher\xe2\x80\x99s annual gross pay was just over $72,000, and Buchanan\xe2\x80\x99s was just over $76,000.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t7\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 gross pay included both a base salary and incentive pay. The amount of petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 incentive pay was based on the sales volume or market share of their assigned drugs in their assigned sales territories,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t8\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t and this amount was uncapped. Christopher\xe2\x80\x99s incentive pay exceeded 30 percent of his gross pay during each of his years of employment; Buchanan\xe2\x80\x99s exceeded 25 percent. It is undisputed that respondent did not pay petitioners time-and-a-half wages when they worked in excess of 40 hours per week.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tC\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners brought this action in the United States District Court for the District of Arizona under \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7216(b). Petitioners alleged that respondent violated the FLSA by failing to compensate them for overtime, and they sought both backpay and liquidated damages as relief. Respondent moved for summary judgment, arguing that petitioners were \xe2\x80\x9cemployed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the capacity of outside salesman,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7213(a)(1), and therefore were exempt from the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s overtime compensation requirement.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t9\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t The District Court agreed and granted summary judgment to respondent. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 37a\xe2\x80\x9347a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAfter the District Court issued its order, petitioners filed a motion to alter or amend the judgment, contending that the District Court had erred in failing to accord control- ling deference to the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the pertinent regulations. That interpretation had been announced in an uninvited amicus brief filed by the DOL in a similar action then pending in the Second Circuit. See Brief for Secretary of Labor as Amicus Curiae in In\xc2\xa0re Novartis Wage and Hour Litigation, No. 09\xe2\x80\x930437 (hereinafter Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s Novartis Brief). The District Court rejected this argument and denied the motion. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 48a\xe2\x80\x9352a.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed. See 635 F.\xc2\xa03d 383. The Court of Appeals agreed that the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t10\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t was not entitled to controlling deference. See id., at 393\xe2\x80\x93395. It held that, because the commitment that petitioners obtained from physicians was the maximum possible under the rules applicable to the pharmaceutical industry, petitioners made sales within the meaning of the regulations. See id., at 395\xe2\x80\x93397. The court found it significant, moreover, that the DOL had previously interpreted the regulations as requiring only that an employee \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in some sense\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d make a sale, see id., at 395\xe2\x80\x93396 (emphasis deleted), and had \xe2\x80\x9cacquiesce[d] in the sales practices of the drug industry for over seventy years,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 399.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Ninth Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision conflicts with the Second Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s decision in In\xc2\xa0re Novartis Wage and Hour Litigation, 611 F.\xc2\xa03d 141, 153\xe2\x80\x93155 (2010) (holding that the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is entitled to controlling deference). We granted certiorari to resolve this split, 565 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___ (2011), and we now affirm the judgment of the Ninth Circuit.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe must determine whether pharmaceutical detailers are outside salesmen as the DOL has defined that term in its regulations. The parties agree that the regulations themselves were validly promulgated and are therefore entitled to deference under Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984)\n. But the parties disagree sharply about whether the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the regulations is owed deference under Auer v. Robbins, \n\n519 U.\xc2\xa0S. 452 (1997)\n. It is to that question that we now turn.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tA\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe DOL first announced its view that pharmaceutical detailers are not exempt outside salesmen in an amicus brief filed in the Second Circuit in 2009, and the Department has subsequently filed similar amicus briefs in other cases, including the case now before us.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t11\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t While the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s ultimate conclusion that detailers are not exempt has remained unchanged since 2009, the same cannot be said of its reasoning. In both the Second Circuit and the Ninth Circuit, the DOL took the view that \xe2\x80\x9ca \xe2\x80\x98sale\xe2\x80\x99 for the purposes of the outside sales exemption requires a con- summated transaction directly involving the employee for whom the exemption is sought.\xe2\x80\x9d Secretary\xe2\x80\x99s Novartis Brief 11; see also Brief for Secretary of Labor as Amicus Curiae in No. 10\xe2\x80\x9315257 (CA9), p. 12. Perhaps because of the nebulous nature of this \xe2\x80\x9cconsummated transaction\xe2\x80\x9d test,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t12\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t the Department changed course after we granted certiorari in this case. The Department now takes the position that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n employee does not make a \xe2\x80\x98sale\xe2\x80\x99 for purposes of the \xe2\x80\x98outside salesman\xe2\x80\x99 exemption unless he actually transfers title to the property at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 12\xe2\x80\x9313 (hereinafter U.\xc2\xa0S. Brief).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t13\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Petitioners and the DOL assert that this new interpretation of the regulations is entitled to controlling deference. See Brief for Petitioners 31\xe2\x80\x9342; U.\xc2\xa0S. Brief 30\xe2\x80\x9334.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t14\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAlthough Auer ordinarily calls for deference to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its own ambiguous regulation, even when that interpretation is advanced in a legal brief, see Chase Bank USA, N.\xc2\xa0A. v. McCoy, 562 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (slip op., at 12); Auer, 519 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 461\xe2\x80\x93462, this general rule does not apply in all cases. Deference is undoubtedly inappropriate, for example, when the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98plainly erroneous or inconsistent with the regulation.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 461 (quoting Robertson v. Methow Valley Citizens Council, \n\n490 U.\xc2\xa0S. 332, \n359 (1989)\n). And deference is likewise unwarranted when there is reason to suspect that the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not reflect the agency\xe2\x80\x99s fair and considered judgment on the matter in question.\xe2\x80\x9d Auer, supra, at 462; see also, e.g., Chase Bank, supra, at ___ (slip op., at 14). This might occur when the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation conflicts with a prior interpretation, see, e.g., Thomas Jefferson Univ. v. Shalala, \n\n512 U.\xc2\xa0S. 504, \n515 (1994)\n, or when it appears that the interpretation is nothing more than a \xe2\x80\x9cconvenient litigating position,\xe2\x80\x9d Bowen v. Georgetown Univ. Hospital, \n\n488 U.\xc2\xa0S. 204, \n213 (1988)\n, or a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98post\xc2\xa0hoc rationalizatio[n]\xe2\x80\x99 advanced by an agency seeking to defend past agency action against attack,\xe2\x80\x9d Auer, supra, at 462 (quoting Bowen, supra, at 212; alteration in original).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn this case, there are strong reasons for withholding the deference that Auer generally requires. Petitioners invoke the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of ambiguous regulations to impose potentially massive liability on respondent for conduct that occurred well before that interpretation was announced. To defer to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation in this circumstance would seriously undermine the principle that agencies should provide regulated parties \xe2\x80\x9cfair warning of the conduct [a regulation] prohibits or requires.\xe2\x80\x9d Gates & Fox Co. v. Occupational Safety and Health Review Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 790 F.\xc2\xa02d 154, 156 (CADC 1986) (Scalia, J.).\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t15\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t Indeed, it would result in precisely the kind of \xe2\x80\x9cunfair surprise\xe2\x80\x9d against which our cases have long warned. See Long Island Care at Home, Ltd. v. Coke, \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 158\n\xe2\x80\x93171 (2007) (deferring to new interpretation that \xe2\x80\x9ccreate[d] no unfair surprise\xe2\x80\x9d because agency had pro- ceeded through notice-and-comment rulemaking); Martin v. Occupational Safety and Health Review Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, \n\n499 U.\xc2\xa0S. 144, \n158 (1991)\n (identifying \xe2\x80\x9cadequacy of notice to regulated parties\xe2\x80\x9d as one factor relevant to the reasonableness of the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation); NLRB v. Bell Aerospace Co., \n\n416 U.\xc2\xa0S. 267, \n295 (1974)\n (suggesting that an agency should not change an interpretation in an adjudicative proceeding where doing so would impose \xe2\x80\x9cnew liability .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. on individuals for past actions which were taken in good-faith reliance on [agency] pronouncements\xe2\x80\x9d or in a case involving \xe2\x80\x9cfines or damages\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis case well illustrates the point. Until 2009, the pharmaceutical industry had little reason to suspect that its longstanding practice of treating detailers as exempt outside salesmen transgressed the FLSA. The statute and regulations certainly do not provide clear notice of this. The general regulation adopts the broad statutory definition of \xe2\x80\x9csale,\xe2\x80\x9d and that definition, in turn, employs the broad catchall phrase \xe2\x80\x9cother disposition.\xe2\x80\x9d See 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.500(a)(1). This catchall phrase could reasonably be construed to encompass a nonbinding commitment from a physician to prescribe a particular drug, and nothing in the statutory or regulatory text or the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s prior guidance plainly requires a contrary reading. See Preamble 22162 (explaining that an employee must \xe2\x80\x9cin some sense\xe2\x80\x9d make a sale); 1940 Report 46 (same).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tEven more important, despite the industry\xe2\x80\x99s decades-long practice of classifying pharmaceutical detailers as exempt employees, the DOL never initiated any enforcement actions with respect to detailers or otherwise suggested that it thought the industry was acting unlawfully.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t16\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t We acknowledge that an agency\xe2\x80\x99s enforcement decisions are informed by a host of factors, some bearing no relation to the agency\xe2\x80\x99s views regarding whether a violation has occurred. See, e.g., Heckler v. Chaney, \n\n470 U.\xc2\xa0S. 821, \n831 (1985)\n (noting that \xe2\x80\x9can agency decision not to enforce often involves a complicated balancing of a number of factors which are peculiarly within its expertise\xe2\x80\x9d). But where, as here, an agency\xe2\x80\x99s announcement of its interpretation is preceded by a very lengthy period of conspicuous inaction, the potential for unfair surprise is acute. As the Seventh Circuit has noted, while it may be \xe2\x80\x9cpossible for an entire industry to be in violation of the [FLSA] for a long time without the Labor Department noticing,\xe2\x80\x9d the \xe2\x80\x9cmore plausible hypothesis\xe2\x80\x9d is that the Department did not think the industry\xe2\x80\x99s practice was un- lawful. Yi v. Sterling Collision Centers, Inc., 480 F.\xc2\xa03d 505, 510\xe2\x80\x93511 (2007). There are now approximately 90,000 pharmaceutical sales representatives; the nature of their work has not materially changed for decades and is well known; these employees are well paid; and like quintessential outside salesmen, they do not punch a clock and often work more than 40 hours per week. Other than acquiescence, no explanation for the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s inaction is plausible.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\t Our practice of deferring to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its own ambiguous regulations undoubtedly has important advantages,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t17\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t but this practice also creates a risk that agencies will promulgate vague and open-ended regulations that they can later interpret as they see fit, thereby \xe2\x80\x9cfrustrat[ing] the notice and predictability purposes of rulemaking.\xe2\x80\x9d Talk America, Inc. v. Michigan Bell Telephone Co., 564 U.\xc2\xa0S. ___, ___ (2011) (Scalia, J., concurring) (slip op., at 3); see also Stephenson & Pogoriler, Seminole Rock\xe2\x80\x99s Domain, 79 Geo. Wash. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 1449, 1461\xe2\x80\x931462 (2011); Manning, Constitutional Structure and Judicial Deference to Agency Interpretations of Agency Rules, 96 Colum. L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 612, 655\xe2\x80\x93668 (1996). It is one thing to expect regulated parties to conform their conduct to an agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretations once the agency announces them; it is quite another to require regulated parties to divine the agency\xe2\x80\x99s interpretations in advance or else be held liable when the agency announces its interpretations for the first time in an enforcement proceeding and demands deference.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAccordingly, whatever the general merits of Auer deference, it is unwarranted here. We instead accord the Department\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation a measure of deference proportional to the \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98thoroughness evident in its consideration, the validity of its reasoning, its consistency with earlier and later pronouncements, and all those factors which give it power to persuade.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Mead Corp., \n\n533 U.\xc2\xa0S. 218, \n228 (2001)\n (quoting Skidmore v. Swift & Co., \n\n323 U.\xc2\xa0S. 134, \n140 (1944)\n).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tB\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe find the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of its regulations quite unpersuasive. The interpretation to which we are now asked to defer\xe2\x80\x94that a sale demands a transfer of title\xe2\x80\x94plainly lacks the hallmarks of thorough consideration. Because the DOL first announced its view that pharmaceutical sales representatives do not qualify as outside salesmen in a series of amicus briefs, there was no opportunity for public comment, and the interpretation that initially emerged from the Department\xe2\x80\x99s internal decisionmaking process proved to be untenable. After arguing successfully in the Second Circuit and then unsucess- fully in the Ninth Circuit that a sale for present purposes simply requires a \xe2\x80\x9cconsummated transaction,\xe2\x80\x9d the DOL advanced a different interpretation in this Court. Here, the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s brief states unequivocally that \xe2\x80\x9c[a]n employee does not make a \xe2\x80\x98sale\xe2\x80\x99 for purposes of the \xe2\x80\x98outside salesman\xe2\x80\x99 exemption unless he actually transfers title to the property at issue.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0S. Brief 12\xe2\x80\x9313.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThis new interpretation is flatly inconsistent with the FLSA, which defines \xe2\x80\x9csale\xe2\x80\x9d to mean, inter alia, a \xe2\x80\x9cconsignment for sale.\xe2\x80\x9d A \xe2\x80\x9cconsignment for sale\xe2\x80\x9d does not involve the transfer of title. See, e.g., Sturm v. Boker, \n\n150 U.\xc2\xa0S. 312, \n330 (1893)\n (\xe2\x80\x9cThe agency to sell and return the proceeds, or the specific goods if not sold .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. does not involve a change of title\xe2\x80\x9d); Hawkland, Consignment Selling Under the Uniform Commercial Code, 67 Com. L.\xc2\xa0J. 146, 147 (1962) (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98[a] consignment of goods for sale does not pass the title at any time, nor does it contemplate that it should be passed\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (quoting Rio Grande Oil Co. v. Miller Rubber Co. of N.\xc2\xa0Y., 31 Ariz. 84, 87, 250 P. 564, 565 (1926))).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe DOL cannot salvage its interpretation by arguing that a \xe2\x80\x9cconsignment for sale\xe2\x80\x9d may eventually result in the transfer of title (from the consignor to the ultimate purchaser if the consignee in fact sells the good). Much the same may be said about a physician\xe2\x80\x99s nonbinding commitment to prescribe a particular product in an appropriate case. In that situation, too, agreement may eventually result in the transfer of title (from the manufacturer to a pharmacy and ultimately to the patient for whom the drug is prescribed).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn support of its new interpretation, the DOL relies heavily on its sales regulation, which states in part that \xe2\x80\x9c[s]ales [for present purposes] include the transfer of title to tangible property,\xe2\x80\x9d 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.501(b) (emphasis added). This regulation, however, provides little support for the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s position. The DOL reads the sales regulation to mean that a \xe2\x80\x9csale\xe2\x80\x9d necessarily includes the transfer of title, but that is not what the regulation says. And it seems clear that that is not what the regulation means. The sentence just subsequent to the one on which the DOL relies, echoing the terms of the FLSA, makes clear that a \xe2\x80\x9cconsignment for sale\xe2\x80\x9d qualifies as a sale. Since a consignment for sale does not involve a transfer of title, it is apparent that the sales regulation does not mean that a sale must include a transfer of title, only that transactions involving a transfer of title are included within the term \xe2\x80\x9csale.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners invite us to look past the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cdetermination that a sale must involve the transfer of title\xe2\x80\x9d and instead defer to the Department\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cexplanation that obtaining a non-binding commitment to prescribe a drug constitutes promotion, and not sales.\xe2\x80\x9d Reply Brief for Petitioners 17. The problem with the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation of the promotion-work regulation, however, is that it depends almost entirely on the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s flawed transfer-of-title interpretation. The promotion-work regulation does not distinguish between promotion work and sales; rather, it distinguishes between exempt promotion work and nonexempt promotion work. Since promotion work that is performed incidental to an employee\xe2\x80\x99s own sales is exempt, the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s conclusion that pharmaceutical detailers perform only nonexempt promotion work is only as strong as the reasoning underlying its conclusion that those employees do not make sales. For the reasons already discussed, we find this reasoning wholly unpersuasive.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tIn light of our conclusion that the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation is neither entitled to Auer deference nor persuasive in its own right, we must employ traditional tools of interpretation to determine whether petitioners are exempt outside salesmen.\n\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t1\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWe begin with the text of the FLSA. Although the provision that establishes the overtime salesman exemption does not furnish a clear answer to the question before us, it provides at least one interpretive clue: It exempts anyone \xe2\x80\x9cemployed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the capacity of [an] outside salesman.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(1) (emphasis added). \xe2\x80\x9cCapacity,\xe2\x80\x9d used in this sense, means \xe2\x80\x9c[o]utward condition or circumstances; relation; character; position.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 396 (2d ed. 1934); see also 2 Oxford English Dictionary 89 (def. 9) (1933) (\xe2\x80\x9cPosition, condition, character, relation\xe2\x80\x9d). The statute\xe2\x80\x99s emphasis on the \xe2\x80\x9ccapacity\xe2\x80\x9d of the employee counsels in favor of a functional, rather than a formal, inquiry, one that views an employee\xe2\x80\x99s responsibilities in the context of the particular industry in which the employee works.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe DOL\xe2\x80\x99s regulations provide additional guidance. The general regulation defines an outside salesman as an employee whose primary duty is \xe2\x80\x9cmaking sales,\xe2\x80\x9d and it adopts the statutory definition of \xe2\x80\x9csale.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.500(a)(1)(i). This definition contains at least three important textual clues. First, the definition is introduced with the verb \xe2\x80\x9cincludes\xe2\x80\x9d instead of \xe2\x80\x9cmeans.\xe2\x80\x9d This word choice is significant because it makes clear that the examples enumerated in the text are intended to be illustrative, not exhaustive. See Burgess v. United States, \n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 124\n, n.\xc2\xa03 (2008) (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9c[a] term whose statutory definition declares what it \xe2\x80\x98includes\xe2\x80\x99 is more susceptible to extension of meaning .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. than where .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. the definition declares what a term \xe2\x80\x98means\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d (alteration in original; some internal quotation marks omitted)). Indeed, Congress used the narrower word \xe2\x80\x9cmeans\xe2\x80\x9d in other provisions of the FLSA when it wanted to cabin a definition to a specific list of enumerated items. See, e.g., \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7203(a) (\xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Person\xe2\x80\x99 means an individual, partnership, association, corporation, business trust, legal representative, or any organized group of persons\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, the list of transactions included in the statu- tory definition of sale is modified by the word \xe2\x80\x9cany.\xe2\x80\x9d We have recognized that the modifier \xe2\x80\x9cany\xe2\x80\x9d can mean \xe2\x80\x9cdifferent things depending upon the setting,\xe2\x80\x9d Nixon v. Missouri Municipal League, \n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 125, \n132 (2004)\n, but in the context of \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7203(k), it is best read to mean \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98one or some indiscriminately of whatever kind,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d United States v. Gonzales, \n\n520 U.\xc2\xa0S. 1, \n5 (1997)\n (quoting Webster\xe2\x80\x99s Third New International Dictionary 97 (1976)). That is so because Congress defined \xe2\x80\x9csale\xe2\x80\x9d to include both the unmodified word \xe2\x80\x9csale\xe2\x80\x9d and transactions that might not be considered sales in a technical sense, including exchanges and consignments for sale.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t18\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThird, Congress also included a broad catchall phrase: \xe2\x80\x9cother disposition.\xe2\x80\x9d Neither the statute nor the regulations define \xe2\x80\x9cdisposition,\xe2\x80\x9d but dictionary definitions of the term range from \xe2\x80\x9crelinquishment or alienation\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9carrangement.\xe2\x80\x9d See Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 644 (def. 1(b)) (1927) (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]he getting rid, or making over, of anything; relinquishment or alienation\xe2\x80\x9d); ibid. (def. 1(a)) (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]he ordering, regulating, or administering of anything\xe2\x80\x9d); 3 Oxford English Dictionary, supra, at 493 (def. 4) (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]he action of disposing of, putting away, getting rid of, making over, etc.\xe2\x80\x9d); ibid. (def. 1) (\xe2\x80\x9c[t]he action of setting in order, or condition of being set in order; arrangement, order\xe2\x80\x9d). We agree with the DOL that the rule of ejusdem generis should guide our interpretation of the catchall phrase, since it follows a list of specific items.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t19\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t But the limit the DOL posits, one that would confine the phrase to dispositions involving \xe2\x80\x9ccontract[s] for the exchange of goods or services in return for value,\xe2\x80\x9d see U.\xc2\xa0S. Brief 20, is much too narrow, as is petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 view that a sale requires a \xe2\x80\x9cfirm agreement\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cfirm commitment\xe2\x80\x9d to buy, see Tr. of Oral Arg. 64, 66. These interpretations would defeat Congress\xe2\x80\x99 intent to define \xe2\x80\x9csale\xe2\x80\x9d in a broad manner and render the general statutory language meaningless. See United States v. Alpers, \n\n338 U.\xc2\xa0S. 680, \n682 (1950)\n (instructing that rule of ejusdem generis cannot be employed to \xe2\x80\x9cobscure and defeat the intent and purpose of Congress\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9crender general words meaningless\xe2\x80\x9d). Indeed, we are hard pressed to think of any contract for the exchange of goods or services in return for value or any firm agreement to buy that would not also fall within one of the specifically enumerated categories.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t20\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe specific list of transactions that precedes the phrase \xe2\x80\x9cother disposition\xe2\x80\x9d seems to us to represent an attempt to accommodate industry-by-industry variations in methods of selling commodities. Consequently, we think that the catchall phrase \xe2\x80\x9cother disposition\xe2\x80\x9d is most reasonably interpreted as including those arrangements that are tantamount, in a particular industry, to a paradigmatic sale of a commodity.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tNothing in the remaining regulations requires a narrower construction.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t21\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t As discussed above, the sales regulation instructs that sales within the meaning of \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7203(k) \xe2\x80\x9cinclude the transfer of title to tangible property,\xe2\x80\x9d 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.501(b) (emphasis added), but this regulation in no way limits the broad statutory definition of \xe2\x80\x9csale.\xe2\x80\x9d And although the promotion-work regulation distinguishes between promotion work that is incidental to an employee\xe2\x80\x99s own sales and work that is incidental to sales made by someone else, see \xc2\xa7541.503(a), this distinction tells us nothing about the meaning of \xe2\x80\x9csale.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t22\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t2\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tGiven our interpretation of \xe2\x80\x9cother disposition,\xe2\x80\x9d it follows that petitioners made sales for purposes of the FLSA and therefore are exempt outside salesmen within the meaning of the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s regulations. Obtaining a nonbinding commitment from a physician to prescribe one of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s drugs is the most that petitioners were able to do to ensure the eventual disposition of the products that respondent sells.\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t23\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t This kind of arrangement, in the unique regulatory environment within which pharmaceutical companies must operate, comfortably falls within the catch- all category of \xe2\x80\x9cother disposition.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThat petitioners bear all of the external indicia of salesmen provides further support for our conclusion. Petitioners were hired for their sales experience. They were trained to close each sales call by obtaining the maximum commitment possible from the physician. They worked away from the office, with minimal supervision, and they were rewarded for their efforts with incentive compensation. It would be anomalous to require respondent to compensate petitioners for overtime, while at the same time exempting employees who function identically to petitioners in every respect except that they sell physician-administered drugs, such as vaccines and other inject- able pharmaceuticals, that are ordered by the physician directly rather than purchased by the end user at a pharmacy with a prescription from the physician.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tOur holding also comports with the apparent purpose of the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s exemption for outside salesmen. The exemption is premised on the belief that exempt employees \xe2\x80\x9ctypically earned salaries well above the minimum wage\xe2\x80\x9d and enjoyed other benefits that \xe2\x80\x9cse[t] them apart from the nonexempt workers entitled to overtime pay.\xe2\x80\x9d Preamble 22124. It was also thought that exempt employees performed a kind of work that \xe2\x80\x9cwas difficult to standardize to any time frame and could not be easily spread to other workers after 40 hours in a week, making compliance with the overtime provisions difficult and generally precluding the potential job expansion intended by the FLSA\xe2\x80\x99s time-and-a-half overtime premium.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x94each of whom earned an average of more than $70,000 per year and spent between 10 and 20 hours outside normal business hours each week performing work related to his assigned portfolio of drugs in his assigned sales territory\xe2\x80\x94are hardly the kind of employees that the FLSA was intended to protect. And it would be challenging, to say the least, for pharmaceutical companies to compensate detailers for overtime going forward without significantly changing the nature of that position. See, e.g., Brief for PhRMA as Amicus Curiae 14\xe2\x80\x9320 (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9ckey aspects of [detailers\xe2\x80\x99] jobs as they are currently structured are fundamentally incompatible with treating [detailers] as hourly employees\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t3\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe remaining arguments advanced by petitioners and the dissent are unavailing. Petitioners contend that detailers are more naturally classified as nonexempt promotional employees who merely stimulate sales made by others than as exempt outside salesmen. They point out that respondent\xe2\x80\x99s prescription drugs are not actually sold until distributors and retail pharmacies order the drugs from other employees. See Reply Brief for Petitioners 7. Those employees,\n\t\t\t\t[\n\t\t\t\t\t24\n\t\t\t\t]\n\t\t\t\t they reason, are the true salesmen in the industry, not detailers. This formalistic argument is inconsistent with the realistic approach that the outside salesman exemption is meant to reflect.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners\xe2\x80\x99 theory seems to be that an employee is properly classified as a nonexempt promotional employee whenever there is another employee who actually makes the sale in a technical sense. But, taken to its extreme, petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 theory would require that we treat as a nonexempt promotional employee a manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s representative who takes an order from a retailer but then transfers the order to a jobber\xe2\x80\x99s employee to be filled, or a car salesman who receives a commitment to buy but then asks his or her assistant to enter the order into the computer. This formalistic approach would be difficult to reconcile with the broad language of the regulations and the statutory definition of \xe2\x80\x9csale,\xe2\x80\x9d and it is in significant ten- sion with the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s past practice. See 1949 Report 83 (explaining that the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s representative was clearly \xe2\x80\x9cperforming sales work regardless of the fact that the order is filled by the jobber rather than directly by his own employer\xe2\x80\x9d); Preamble 22162 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9ctechnological changes in how orders are taken and processed should not preclude the exemption for employees who in some sense make the sales\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tPetitioners additionally argue that detailers are the functional equivalent of employees who sell a \xe2\x80\x9cconcept,\xe2\x80\x9d and they point to Wage and Hour Division opinion letters, as well as lower court decisions, deeming such employees nonexempt. See Brief for Petitioners 47\xe2\x80\x9348. Two of these opinions, however, concerned employees who were more analogous to buyers than to sellers. See Clements v. Serco, Inc., 530 F.\xc2\xa03d 1224, 1229\xe2\x80\x931230, n. 4 (CA10 2008) (explaining that, although military recruiters \xe2\x80\x9c[i]n a loose sense\xe2\x80\x9d were \xe2\x80\x9cselling the Army\xe2\x80\x99s services,\xe2\x80\x9d it was the Army that would \xe2\x80\x9cpa[y] for the services of the recruits who enlist\xe2\x80\x9d); Opinion Letter from Dept. of Labor, Wage and Hour Division (Aug. 19, 1994), 1994 WL 1004855 (explaining that selling the \xe2\x80\x9cconcept\xe2\x80\x9d of organ donation \xe2\x80\x9cis similar to that of outside buyers who in a very loose sense are sometimes described as selling their employer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x98service\xe2\x80\x99 to the person for whom they obtain their goods\xe2\x80\x9d). And the other two opinions are likewise inapposite. One concerned employees who were not selling a good or service at all, see Opinion Letter from Dept. of Labor, Wage and Hour Division (May 22, 2006), 2006 WL 1698305 (concluding that employees who solicit charitable contributions are not exempt), and the other concerned employees who were incapable of selling any good or service because their employer had yet to extend an offer, see Opinion Letter from Dept. of Labor, Wage and Hour Division (Apr. 20, 1999), 1999 WL 1002391 (concluding that college recruiters are not exempt because they merely induce qualified customers to apply to the college, and the college \xe2\x80\x9cin turn decides whether to make a contractual offer of its educational services to the applicant\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the dissent posits that the \xe2\x80\x9cprimary duty\xe2\x80\x9d of a pharmaceutical detailer is not \xe2\x80\x9cto obtain a promise to prescribe a particular drug,\xe2\x80\x9d but rather to \xe2\x80\x9cprovid[e] information so that the doctor will keep the drug in mind with an eye toward using it when appropriate.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 6. But the record in this case belies that contention. Petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 end goal was not merely to make physicians aware of the medically appropriate uses of a particular drug. Rather, it was to convince physicians actually to prescribe the drug in appropriate cases. See App. to Pet. for Cert. 40a (finding that petitioners\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9cprimary objective was convincing physicians to prescribe [respondent\xe2\x80\x99s] products to their patients\xe2\x80\x9d).\n\t\t\t

\n\n13\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0When pressed to clarify its position at oral argument, the DOL suggested that a \xe2\x80\x9ctransfer of possession in contemplation of a transfer of title\xe2\x80\x9d might also suffice. Tr. of Oral Arg. 17.\n\t\n

See Estate of Cowart v. \n\n15\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Accord, Phelps Dodge Corp. v. Federal Mine Safety and Health Review Comm\xe2\x80\x99n, 681 F.\xc2\xa02d 1189, 1192 (CA9 1982) (recognizing that \xe2\x80\x9cthe application of a regulation in a particular situation may be challenged on the ground that it does not give fair warning that the allegedly violative conduct was prohibited\xe2\x80\x9d); Kropp Forge Co. v. \n\n20\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s approach suffers from the same flaw. The dissent contends that, in order to make a sale, an employee must at least obtain a \xe2\x80\x9cfirm commitment to buy.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 10 (opinion of Breyer, J.). But when an employee who has extended an offer to sell obtains a \xe2\x80\x9cfirm commitment to buy,\xe2\x80\x9d that transaction amounts to a \xe2\x80\x9ccontract to sell.\xe2\x80\x9d Given that a \xe2\x80\x9ccontract to sell\xe2\x80\x9d already falls within the statutory definition of \xe2\x80\x9csale,\xe2\x80\x9d the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s interpretation would strip the catchall phrase of independent meaning.\n\t\n
\n\n21\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0In the past, we have stated that exemptions to the FLSA must be \xe2\x80\x9cnarrowly construed against the employers seeking to assert them and their application limited to those [cases] plainly and unmistakably within their terms and spirit.\xe2\x80\x9d Arnold v. Ben Kanowsky, Inc., \n. Petitioners and the DOL contend that Arnold requires us to construe the outside salesman exemption narrowly, but Arnold is inapposite where, as here, we are interpreting a general definition that applies throughout the FLSA.\n\t\n
\n\n22\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0The dissent\xe2\x80\x99s view that pharmaceutical detailers are more naturally characterized as nonexempt promotional employees than as exempt outside salesmen relies heavily on the DOL\xe2\x80\x99s explanation in its 1940 Re-port that \xe2\x80\x9csales promotion men\xe2\x80\x9d are not salesmen. See post, at 7; see also 1940 Report 46. There, the Department described a \xe2\x80\x9csales promotion man\xe2\x80\x9d as an employee who merely \xe2\x80\x9cpav[es] the way for salesmen\xe2\x80\x9d and who frequently \xe2\x80\x9cdeals with retailers who are not customers of his own employer but of his employer\xe2\x80\x99s customer\xe2\x80\x9d and is \xe2\x80\x9cinterested in sales by the retailer, not to the retailer.\xe2\x80\x9d 1940 Report 46. The dissent asserts that detailers are analogous to \xe2\x80\x9csales promotion men\xe2\x80\x9d because they deal with \xe2\x80\x9cindividuals, namely doctors, \xe2\x80\x98who are not customers\xe2\x80\x99 of their own employer\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9care primarily interested in sales authorized by the doctor, not to the doctor.\xe2\x80\x9d Post, at 7. But this comparison is inapt. The equivalent of a \xe2\x80\x9csales promotion man\xe2\x80\x9d in the pharmaceutical industry would be an employee who promotes a manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s products to the retail pharmacies that sell the products after purchasing them from a wholesaler or distributor. Detailers, by contrast, obtain nonbinding commitments from the gatekeepers who must prescribe the product if any sale is to take place at all.\n\t\n
\n\n23\n\n\n\t\t\xc2\xa0Our point is not, as the dissent suggests, that any employee who does the most that he or she is able to do in a particular position to ensure the eventual sale of a product should qualify as an exempt outside salesman. See post, at 9 (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cthe \xe2\x80\x98most\xe2\x80\x99 a California firm\xe2\x80\x99s marketing employee may be able \xe2\x80\x98to do\xe2\x80\x99 to secure orders from New York customers is to post an advertisement on the Internet\xe2\x80\x9d). Rather, our point is that, when an entire industry is constrained by law or regulation from selling its products in the ordinary manner, an employee who functions in all relevant respects as an outside salesman should not be excluded from that category based on technicalities.\n\t\n

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) exempts from federal maximum hour and minimum wage requirements \xe2\x80\x9cany employee employed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the capacity of outside salesman.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(1). The question is whether drug company detailers fall within the scope of the term \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman.\xe2\x80\x9d In my view, they do not.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tI\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court describes the essential aspects of the detailer\xe2\x80\x99s job as follows: First, the detailer \xe2\x80\x9cprovide[s] information to physicians about the company\xe2\x80\x99s products in hopes of persuading them to write prescriptions for the products in appropriate cases.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. Second, the detailers \xe2\x80\x9ccal[l] on physicians in an assigned sales terri-tory to discuss the features, benefits, and risks of an assigned portfolio of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s prescription drugs,\xe2\x80\x9d and they seek a \xe2\x80\x9cnonbinding commitment from the physician to prescribe those drugs in appropriate cases .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. \xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. (footnote omitted). Third, the detailers\xe2\x80\x99 compensation includes an \xe2\x80\x9cincentive\xe2\x80\x9d element \xe2\x80\x9cbased on the sales volume or market share of their assigned drugs in their assigned sales territories.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6. The Court adds that the detailers work with \xe2\x80\x9conly minimal supervision\xe2\x80\x9d and beyond normal business hours \xe2\x80\x9cattending events, reviewing product information, returning phone calls, responding to e-mails, and performing other miscellaneous tasks.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAs summarized, I agree with the Court\xe2\x80\x99s description of the job. In light of important, near-contemporaneous differences in the Justice Department\xe2\x80\x99s views as to the meaning of relevant Labor Department regulations, see ante, at 8\xe2\x80\x939, I also agree that we should not give the Solicitor General\xe2\x80\x99s current interpretive view any especially favorable weight. Ante, at 14. Thus, I am willing to assume, with the Court, that we should determine whether the statutory term covers the detailer\xe2\x80\x99s job as here described through our independent examination of the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language and the related Labor Department regulations. But, I conclude on that basis that a detailer is not an \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe FLSA does not itself define the term \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman.\xe2\x80\x9d Rather, it exempts from wage and hour requirements \xe2\x80\x9cany employee employed .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in the capacity of outside salesman (as such terms are defined and delimited from time to time by regulations of the Secretary).\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(1) (emphasis added). Thus, we must look to relevant Labor Department regulations to answer the question. See Chevron U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0A. Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council, Inc., \n\n467 U.\xc2\xa0S. 837 (1984)\n; see also Long Island Care at Home, Ltd. v. Coke, \n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 158, \n165 (2007)\n (explaining that \xe2\x80\x9cthe FLSA explicitly leaves gaps\xe2\x80\x9d to be filled by regulations).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThere are three relevant regulations. The first is entitled \xe2\x80\x9cGeneral rule for outside sales employees,\xe2\x80\x9d 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.500 (2011); the second is entitled \xe2\x80\x9cMaking sales or obtaining orders,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7541.501; and the third is entitled \xe2\x80\x9cPromotion work,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7541.503. The relevant language of the first two regulations is similar. The first says that the term \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98employee employed in the capacity of outside salesman\xe2\x80\x99 .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. shall mean any employee .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. [w]hose pri-mary duty is: (i) making sales within the meaning of sec-tion 3(k) of the Act, or (ii) obtaining orders or contracts for services or for the use of facilities .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7541.500(a)(1). The second regulation tells us that the first regulation \xe2\x80\x9crequires that the employee be engaged in .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. (1) Making sales within the meaning of section 3(k) of the Act, or (2) Obtaining orders or contracts for services or for the use of facilities.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7541.501(a).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe second part of these quoted passages is irrelevant here, for it concerns matters not at issue, namely \xe2\x80\x9corders or contracts for services or for the use of facilities.\xe2\x80\x9d The remaining parts of the two regulations are similarly irrelevant. See Appendix, infra. Thus, the relevant portions of the first two regulations say simply that the employee\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprimary duty\xe2\x80\x9d must be \xe2\x80\x9cmaking sales within the meaning of section 3(k) of the Act.\xe2\x80\x9d And \xc2\xa73(k) of the Act says that the word \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Sale\xe2\x80\x99 or \xe2\x80\x98sell\xe2\x80\x99 includes any sale, exchange, contract to sell, consignment for sale, shipment for sale, or other disposition.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7203(k).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tUnless we give the words of the statute and regulations some special meaning, a detailer\xe2\x80\x99s primary duty is not that of \xe2\x80\x9cmaking sales\xe2\x80\x9d or the equivalent. A detailer might convince a doctor to prescribe a drug for a particular kind of patient. If the doctor encounters such a patient, he might prescribe the drug. The doctor\xe2\x80\x99s client, the patient, might take the prescription to a pharmacist and ask the pharmacist to fill the prescription. If so, the pharmacist might sell the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s drug to the patient, or might substitute a generic version. But it is the pharmacist, not the detailer, who will have sold the drug.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo put the same fairly obvious point in the language of the regulations and of \xc2\xa73(k) of the FLSA, see \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7203(k), the detailer does not \xe2\x80\x9csell\xe2\x80\x9d anything to the doctor. See Black\xe2\x80\x99s Law Dictionary 1454 (9th ed. 2009) (defining \xe2\x80\x9csale\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he transfer of property or title for a price\xe2\x80\x9d). Nor does he, during the course of that visit or immedi-ately thereafter, \xe2\x80\x9cexchange\xe2\x80\x9d the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s product for money or for anything else. He enters into no \xe2\x80\x9ccontract to sell\xe2\x80\x9d on behalf of anyone. He \xe2\x80\x9cconsigns\xe2\x80\x9d nothing \xe2\x80\x9cfor sale.\xe2\x80\x9d He \xe2\x80\x9cships\xe2\x80\x9d nothing for sale. He does not \xe2\x80\x9cdispose\xe2\x80\x9d of any product at all.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhat the detailer does is inform the doctor about the nature of the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s drugs and explain their uses, their virtues, their drawbacks, and their limitations. The detailer may well try to convince the doctor to prescribe the manufacturer\xe2\x80\x99s drugs for patients. And if the detailer is successful, the doctor will make a \xe2\x80\x9cnonbinding commitment\xe2\x80\x9d to write prescriptions using one or more of those drugs where appropriate. If followed, that \xe2\x80\x9cnonbinding commitment\xe2\x80\x9d is, at most, a nonbinding promise to consider advising a patient to use a drug where medical indications so indicate (if the doctor encounters such a patient), and to write a prescription that will likely (but may not) lead that person to order that drug under its brand name from the pharmacy. (I say \xe2\x80\x9cmay not\xe2\x80\x9d because 30% of patients in a 2-year period have not filled a prescription given to them by a doctor. See USA Today, Kaiser Family Foundation, Harvard School of Public Health, The Public on Prescription Drugs and Pharmaceutical Companies 3 (2008), available at http://www.kff.org/kaiserpolls/upload/7748.pdf (all Internet materials as visited June 13, 2012, and available in Clerk of Court\xe2\x80\x99s case file). And when patients do fill prescriptions, 75% are filled with generic drugs. See Dept. of Health and Human Services, Office of Science & Data Policy, Expanding the Use of Generic Drugs 2 (2010).)\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tWhere in this process does the detailer sell the product? At most he obtains from the doctor a \xe2\x80\x9cnonbinding commitment\xe2\x80\x9d to advise his patient to take the drug (or perhaps a generic equivalent) as well as to write any necessary prescription. I put to the side the fact that neither the Court nor the record explains exactly what a \xe2\x80\x9cnonbinding commitment\xe2\x80\x9d is. Like a \xe2\x80\x9cdefinite maybe,\xe2\x80\x9d an \xe2\x80\x9cimpos-sible solution,\xe2\x80\x9d or a \xe2\x80\x9ctheoretical experience,\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cnonbinding commitment\xe2\x80\x9d seems to claim more than it can deliver. Regardless, other than in colloquial speech, to obtain a commitment to advise a client to buy a product is not to obtain a commitment to sell that product, no matter how often the client takes the advice (or the patient does what the doctor recommends).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe third regulation, entitled \xe2\x80\x9cPromotion work,\xe2\x80\x9d lends support to this view. That is because the detailer\xe2\x80\x99s work as described above is best viewed as promotion work. The regulation makes clear that promotion work falls within the statutory exemption only when the promotion work \xe2\x80\x9cis actually performed incidental to and in conjunction with an employee\xe2\x80\x99s own outside sales or solicitations.\xe2\x80\x9d 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.503(a) (emphasis added). But it is not exempt if it is \xe2\x80\x9cincidental to sales made, or to be made, by someone else.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe detailer\xe2\x80\x99s work, in my view, is more naturally characterized as involving \xe2\x80\x9c[p]romotional activities designed to stimulate sales .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. made by someone else,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa7541.503, e.g., the pharmacist or the wholesaler, than as involving \xe2\x80\x9c[p]romotional activities designed to stimulate\xe2\x80\x9d the detailer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cown sales.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThree other relevant documents support this reading. First, the Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America (PhRMA), of which respondent is a mem- ber, publishes a \xe2\x80\x9cCode on Interactions with Healthcare Pro-fessionals.\xe2\x80\x9d See PhRMA, Code on Interactions with Healthcare Professionals (PhRMA Code) (rev. July 2008), available at http://www.phrma.org/sites/default/files/108/ phrma_marketing_code_2008.pdf. The PhRMA Code de-scribes a detailer\xe2\x80\x99s job in some depth. It consistently refers to detailers as \xe2\x80\x9cdelivering accurate, up-to-date information to healthcare professionals,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 14, and it stresses the importance of a doctor\xe2\x80\x99s treatment decision being based \xe2\x80\x9csolely on each patient\xe2\x80\x99s medical needs\xe2\x80\x9d and the doctor\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cmedical knowledge and experience,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 2. The PhRMA Code also forbids the offering or providing of anything \xe2\x80\x9cin a manner or on conditions that would interfere with the independence of a healthcare professional\xe2\x80\x99s prescribing practices.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 13. But the PhRMA Code nowhere refers to detailers as if they were salesmen, rather than providers of information, nor does it refer to any kind of commitment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTo the contrary, the document makes clear that the pharmaceutical industry itself understands that it cannot be a detailer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprimary duty\xe2\x80\x9d to obtain a nonbinding commitment, for, in respect to many doctors, such a commitment taken alone is unlikely to make a significant difference to their doctor\xe2\x80\x99s use of a particular drug. When a particular drug, say Drug D, constitutes the best treatment for a particular patient, a knowledgeable doctor should (hence likely will) prescribe it irrespective of any nonbinding commitment to do so. Where some other drug, however, is likely to prove more beneficial for a particular patient, that doctor should not (hence likely will not) prescribe Drug D irrespective of any nonbinding commitment to the contrary.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tAt a minimum, the document explains why a detailer should not (hence likely does not) see himself as seeking primarily to obtain a promise to prescribe a particular drug, as opposed to providing information so that the doctor will keep the drug in mind with an eye toward using it when appropriate. And because the detailer\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cprimary duty\xe2\x80\x9d is informational, as opposed to sales-oriented, he fails to qualify as an outside salesman. See \xc2\xa7541.500(a)(1)(i) (restricting the outside salesman exemption to employees \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hose primary duty is .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. making sales\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). A detailer operating in accord with the PhRMA Code \xe2\x80\x9csells\xe2\x80\x9d the product only in the way advertisers (particularly very low key advertisers) \xe2\x80\x9csell\xe2\x80\x9d a product: by creating demand for the product, not by taking orders.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tSecond, a Labor Department Wage and Hour Division Report written in 1940 further describes the work of \xe2\x80\x9csales promotion men.\xe2\x80\x9d See Dept. of Labor, Wage and Hour Division, Report and Recommendations of the Presiding Officer at Hearings Preliminary to Redefinition (1940) (1940 Report). The 1940 Report says that such individuals \xe2\x80\x9cpav[e] the way\xe2\x80\x9d for sales by others. Id., at 46. \xe2\x80\x9cFrequently,\xe2\x80\x9d they deal \xe2\x80\x9cwith [the] retailers who are not custom- ers of [their] own employer but of [their] employer\xe2\x80\x99s customer.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. And they are \xe2\x80\x9cprimarily interested in sales by the retailer, not to the retailer.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. \xe2\x80\x9c[T]hey do not make actual sales,\xe2\x80\x9d and they \xe2\x80\x9care admittedly not outside salesmen.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tLike the \xe2\x80\x9csales promotion men,\xe2\x80\x9d the detailers before us deal with individuals, namely doctors, \xe2\x80\x9cwho are not customers\xe2\x80\x9d of their own employer. And the detailers are primarily interested in sales authorized by the doctor, not to the doctor. According to the 1940 Report, sales promotion men are not \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesmen,\xe2\x80\x9d primarily because they seek to bring about, not their own sales, but sales by others. Thus, the detailers in this case are not \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesmen.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThird, a Wage and Hour Division Report written in 1949 notes that where \xe2\x80\x9cwork is promotional in nature it is sometimes difficult to determine whether it is incidental to the employee\xe2\x80\x99s own sales work.\xe2\x80\x9d See Dept. of Labor, Wage and Hour Division, Report and Recommendations on Proposed Revisions of Regulations, Part 541, p. 82 (1949) (1949 Report). It adds that in borderline cases\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cthe test is whether the person is actually engaged in activities directed toward the consummation of his own sales, at least to the extent of obtaining a commitment to buy from the person to whom he is selling. If his efforts are directed toward stimulating the sales of his company generally rather than the consummation of his own specific sales his activities are not exempt.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 83 (emphasis added).\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tThe 1949 Report also refers to a\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9ccompany representative who visits chain stores, arranges the merchandise on shelves, replenishes stock .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0, consults with the manager as to the requirements of the store, fills out a requisition for the quantity wanted and leaves it with the store manager to be transmitted to the central warehouse of the chain-store company which later ships the quantity re-quested.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 84.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIt says this company representative is not an \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman\xe2\x80\x9d because he\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\xe2\x80\x9cdoes not consummate the sale nor direct his efforts toward the consummation of a sale (the store manager often has no authority to buy).\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tSee also 29 CFR \xc2\xa7541.503(c) (explaining that if an em-ployee \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not consummate the sale nor direct efforts to-ward the consummation of a sale, the work is not exempt outside sales work\xe2\x80\x9d)\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tA detailer does not take orders, he does not consummate a sale, and he does not direct his efforts towards the consummation of any eventual sale (by the pharmacist) any more than does the \xe2\x80\x9ccompany\xe2\x80\x99s representative\xe2\x80\x9d in the 1949 Report\xe2\x80\x99s example. The doctor whom the detailer visits, like the example\xe2\x80\x99s store manager, \xe2\x80\x9chas no authority to buy.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tTaken together, the statute, regulations, ethical codes, and Labor Department Reports indicate that the drug detailers do not promote their \xe2\x80\x9cown sales,\xe2\x80\x9d but rather \xe2\x80\x9csales made, or to be made, by someone else.\xe2\x80\x9d Therefore, detailers are not \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesmen.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\tIII\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court\xe2\x80\x99s different conclusion rests primarily upon its interpretation of the statutory words \xe2\x80\x9cother disposition\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cincluding those arrangements that are tantamount, in a particular industry, to a paradigmatic sale of a commod-ity.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 19. Given the fact that the doctor buys noth-ing, the fact that the detailer sells nothing to the doctor, and the fact that any \xe2\x80\x9cnonbinding commitment\xe2\x80\x9d by the doctor must, of ethical necessity, be of secondary importance, there is nothing about the detailer\xe2\x80\x99s visit with the doctor that makes the visit (or what occurs during the visit) \xe2\x80\x9ctantamount .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to a paradigmatic sale.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. See Part I, supra.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court adds that \xe2\x80\x9c[o]btaining a nonbinding commitment from a physician to prescribe one of respondent\xe2\x80\x99s drugs is the most that petitioners were able to do to ensure the eventual disposition of the products that respondent sells.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 20. And that may be so. But there is no \xe2\x80\x9cmost they are able to do\xe2\x80\x9d test. After all, the \xe2\x80\x9cmost\xe2\x80\x9d a California firm\xe2\x80\x99s marketing employee may be able \xe2\x80\x9cto do\xe2\x80\x9d to secure orders from New York customers is to post an advertisement on the Internet, but that fact does not help qualify the posting employee as a \xe2\x80\x9csalesman.\xe2\x80\x9d The Court adds that it means to apply this test only when the law precludes \xe2\x80\x9can entire industry .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. from selling its products in the ordinary manner.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 21, n.\xc2\xa023. But the law might preclude an industry from selling its products through an outside salesman without thereby leading the legal term \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman\xe2\x80\x9d to apply to whatever is the next best thing. In any event, the Court would be wrong to assume, if it does assume, that there is in nearly every industry an outside salesman lurking somewhere (if only we can find him). An industry might, after all, sell its goods through wholesalers or retailers, while using its own outside employees to encourage sales only by providing third parties with critically important information.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tThe Court expresses concern lest a holding that detailers are not \xe2\x80\x9csalesmen\xe2\x80\x9d lead to holdings that the statute forbids treating as a \xe2\x80\x9csalesman\xe2\x80\x9d an employee \xe2\x80\x9cwho takes an order from a retailer but then transfers the order to a jobber\xe2\x80\x99s employee to be filled,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 23, or \xe2\x80\x9ca car salesman who receives a commitment to buy but then asks his or her assistant to enter the order into the computer,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid. But there is no need for any such fear. Both these examples involve employees who are salesmen because they obtain a firm commitment to buy the product. See 1949 Report 83 (as to the first example, such an employee \xe2\x80\x9chas obtained a commitment from the customer\xe2\x80\x9d); 69 Fed. Reg. 22163 (2004) (as to the second example, explaining that \xe2\x80\x9c[e]xempt status should not depend on . . . who types the order into a computer,\xe2\x80\x9d but maintaining requirement that a salesman \xe2\x80\x9cobtai[n] a commitment to buy from the person to whom he is selling\xe2\x80\x9d). The problem facing the detailer is that he does not obtain any such commitment.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFinally, the Court points to the detailers\xe2\x80\x99 relatively high pay, their uncertain hours, the location of their work, their independence, and the fact that they frequently work overtime, all as showing that detailers fall within the basic purposes of the statutory provision that creates exceptions from wage and hour requirements. Ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936. The problem for the detailers, however, is that the statute seeks to achieve its general objectives by creating certain categories of exempt employees, one of which is the category of \xe2\x80\x9coutside salesman.\xe2\x80\x9d It places into that category only those who satisfy the definition of \xe2\x80\x9coutside sales- man\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9cdefined and delimited from time to time by regulations of the Secretary.\xe2\x80\x9d \n\n29 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7213(a)(1) (emphasis added). And the detailers do not fall within that category as defined by those regulations.\n\t\t\t

\n

\n\t\t\t\t\n\t\t\t\t\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\n\t\t\t\tFor these reasons, with respect, I dissent.\n\t\t\t
