MAC\xe2\x80\x99S SHELL SERVICE, INC., et\xc2\xa0al. v. SHELL OIL PRODUCTS CO. LLC et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the first circuit

\n

No. 08\xe2\x80\x93240.\xe2\x80\x83Argued January 19, 2010\xe2\x80\x94Decided March 2, 2010

\n

The Petroleum Marketing Practices Act (Act) limits the circumstances in which franchisors may \xe2\x80\x9cterminate\xe2\x80\x9d a service-station franchise or \xe2\x80\x9cfail to renew\xe2\x80\x9d a franchise relationship. 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72802, 2804. Typically, the franchisor leases the service station to the franchisee and permits the franchisee to use the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s trademark and purchase the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s fuel for resale. \xc2\xa72801(1). As relevant here, service-station franchisees (dealers) filed suit under the Act, alleging that a petroleum franchisor and its assignee had constructively \xe2\x80\x9cterminate[d]\xe2\x80\x9d their franchises and constructively \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ed] to renew\xe2\x80\x9d their franchise relationships by substantially changing the rental terms that the dealers had enjoyed for years, increasing costs for many of them. The dealers asserted these claims even though they had not been compelled to abandon their franchises, and even though they had been offered and had accepted renewal agreements. The jury found against the franchisor and assignee, and the District Court denied their requests for judgment as a matter of law. The First Circuit affirmed as to the constructive termination claims, holding that the Act does not require a franchisee to abandon its franchise to recover for such termination, and concluding that a simple breach of contract by an assignee of a franchise agreement can amount to constructive termination if the breach resulted in a material change effectively ending the lease. However, the court reversed as to the constructive nonrenewal claims, holding that such a claim cannot be maintained once a franchisee signs and operates under a renewal agreement.

\n

Held:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa01.\xc2\xa0A franchisee cannot recover for constructive termination under the Act if the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s allegedly wrongful conduct did not compel the franchisee to abandon its franchise. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9315.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The Act provides that \xe2\x80\x9cno franchisor \xe2\x80\xa6 may \xe2\x80\xa6 terminate any franchise,\xe2\x80\x9d except for an enumerated reason and after giving written notice, \xc2\xa72802(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b), and specifies that \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98termination\xe2\x80\x99 includes cancellation,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72801(17). Because it does not further define those terms, they are given their ordinary meanings: \xe2\x80\x9cput [to] an end\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cannul[ed] or destroy[ed].\xe2\x80\x9d Thus, the Act prohibits only franchisor conduct that has the effect of ending a franchise. The same conclusion follows even if Congress used \xe2\x80\x9cterminate\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccancel\xe2\x80\x9d in their technical, rather than ordinary, senses. This conclusion is also consistent with the general understanding of the constructive termination doctrine as applied in analogous legal contexts\xe2\x80\x94e.g., employment law, see Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, 542 U. S. 129, 141\xe2\x80\x93143\xe2\x80\x94where a termination is deemed \xe2\x80\x9cconstructive\xe2\x80\x9d only because the plaintiff, not the defendant, formally ends a particular legal relationship\xe2\x80\x94not because there is no end to the relationship at all. Allowing franchisees to obtain relief for conduct that does not force a franchise to end would ignore the Act\xe2\x80\x99s scope, which is limited to the circumstances in which franchisors may terminate a franchise or decline to renew a franchise relationship and leaves undisturbed state-law regulation of other types of disputes between petroleum franchisors and franchisees, see \xc2\xa72806(a). This conclusion is also informed by important practical considerations, namely, that any standard for identifying those breaches of contract that should be treated as effectively ending a franchise, even though the franchisee continues to operate, would be indeterminate and unworkable. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0The dealers\xe2\x80\x99 claim that this interpretation of the Act fails to provide franchisees with protection from unfair and coercive franchisor conduct that does not force an end to the franchise ignores the availability of state-law remedies to address such wrongful conduct. The Court\xe2\x80\x99s reading of the Act is also faithful to the statutory interpretation principle that statutes should be construed \xe2\x80\x9cin a manner that gives effect to all of their provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d United States ex rel. Eisenstein v. City of New York, 556 U. S. ___, ___, because this interpretation gives meaningful effect to the Act\xe2\x80\x99s preliminary injunction provisions and its alternative statute-of-limitations accrual dates. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9314.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa02.\xc2\xa0A franchisee who signs and operates under a renewal agreement with a franchisor may not maintain a constructive nonrenewal claim under the Act. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s text leaves no room for such an interpretation. It is violated only when a franchisor \xe2\x80\x9cfail[s] to renew\xe2\x80\x9d a franchise relationship for an enumerated reason or fails to provide the required notice, see \xc2\xa72802, and it defines \xe2\x80\x9cfail to renew\xe2\x80\x9d as a \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to reinstate, continue, or extend the franchise relationship,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72801(14). A franchisee that signs a renewal agreement cannot carry the threshold burden of showing a \xe2\x80\x9cnonrenewal of the franchise relationship,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72805(c), and thus necessarily cannot establish that the franchisor has violated the Act. Signing their renewal agreements \xe2\x80\x9cunder protest\xe2\x80\x9d did not preserve the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 ability to assert nonrenewal claims. When a franchisee signs a renewal agreement\xe2\x80\x94even \xe2\x80\x9cunder protest\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94there has been no \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ure] to renew,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus no violation of the Act. The Act\xe2\x80\x99s structure and purpose confirm this interpretation. Accepting the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 contrary reading would greatly expand the Act\xe2\x80\x99s reach. Pp.\xc2\xa015\xe2\x80\x9319.

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Petroleum Marketing Practices Act (PMPA or Act), 92 Stat. 322, 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72801 et seq., limits the circumstances in which petroleum franchisors may \xe2\x80\x9cterminate\xe2\x80\x9d a franchise or \xe2\x80\x9cfail to renew\xe2\x80\x9d a franchise relationship. \xc2\xa72802. In these consolidated cases, service-station franchisees brought suit under the Act, alleging that a franchisor had constructively \xe2\x80\x9cterminate[d]\xe2\x80\x9d their franchises and had constructively \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ed] to renew\xe2\x80\x9d their franchise relationships. They asserted these claims even though the conduct of which they complained had not compelled any of them to abandon their franchises and even though they had been offered and had accepted renewal agreements. We hold that a franchisee cannot recover for constructive termination under the PMPA if the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s allegedly wrongful conduct did not compel the franchisee to abandon its franchise. Additionally, we conclude that a franchisee who signs and operates under a renewal agreement with a franchisor may not maintain a claim for constructive nonrenewal. We therefore reverse in part and affirm in part.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petroleum refiners and distributors supply motor fuel to the public through service stations that often are operated by independent franchisees. In the typical franchise arrangement, the franchisor leases the service-station premises to the franchisee, grants the franchisee the right to use the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s trademark, and agrees to sell motor fuel to the franchisee for resale. Franchise agreements remain in effect for a stated term, after which the parties can opt to renew the franchise relationship by executing a new agreement.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Enacted in 1978, the PMPA was a response to widespread concern over increasing numbers of allegedly unfair franchise terminations and nonrenewals in the petroleum industry. See, e.g., Comment, 1980 Duke L.\xc2\xa0J. 522, 524\xe2\x80\x93531. The Act establishes minimum federal standards governing the termination and nonrenewal of petroleum franchises. Under the Act\xe2\x80\x99s operative provisions, a franchisor may \xe2\x80\x9cterminate\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cfranchise\xe2\x80\x9d during the term stated in the franchise agreement and may \xe2\x80\x9cfail to renew\xe2\x80\x9d a \xe2\x80\x9cfranchise relationship\xe2\x80\x9d at the conclusion of that term only if the franchisor provides written notice and takes the action in question for a reason specifically recognized in the statute. 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa72802, 2804. Consistent with the typical franchise arrangement, a \xe2\x80\x9cfranchise\xe2\x80\x9d is defined as \xe2\x80\x9cany contract\xe2\x80\x9d that authorizes a franchisee to use the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s trademark, as well as any associated agreement providing for the supply of motor fuel or authorizing the franchisee to occupy a service station owned by the franchisor.[Footnote 1] \xc2\xa72801(1). The Act defines a \xe2\x80\x9cfranchise relationship\xe2\x80\x9d in more general terms: the parties\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9crespective motor fuel marketing or distribution obligations and responsibilities\xe2\x80\x9d that result from the franchise arrangement. \xc2\xa72801(2).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To enforce these provisions, a franchisee may bring suit in federal court against any franchisor that fails to comply with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s restrictions on terminations and nonrenewals. See \xc2\xa72805. Successful franchisees can benefit from a wide range of remedies, including compensatory and punitive damages, reasonable attorney\xe2\x80\x99s fees and expert costs, and equitable relief. See \xc2\xa72805(b), (d). The Act also requires district courts to grant preliminary injunctive relief to aggrieved franchisees, if there are \xe2\x80\x9csufficiently serious questions going to the merits\xe2\x80\x9d that present \xe2\x80\x9ca fair ground for litigation\xe2\x80\x9d and the balance of hardships favors such relief. \xc2\xa72805(b)(2).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This litigation involves a dispute between Shell Oil Company (Shell), a petroleum franchisor, and several Shell franchisees in Massachusetts.[Footnote 2] Pursuant to their franchise agreements with Shell, each franchisee was required to pay Shell monthly rent for use of the service-station premises. For many years, Shell offered the franchisees a rent subsidy that reduced the monthly rent by a set amount for every gallon of motor fuel a franchisee sold above a specified threshold. Shell renewed the subsidy annually through notices that \xe2\x80\x9cexplicitly provided for cancellation [of the rent subsidy] with thirty days\xe2\x80\x99 notice.\xe2\x80\x9d Marcoux v. Shell Oil Prods. Co., 524 F.\xc2\xa03d 33, 38 (CA1 2008). Nonetheless, Shell representatives made various oral representations to the franchisees \xe2\x80\x9cthat the [s]ubsidy or something like it would always exist.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 1998, Shell joined with two other oil companies to create Motiva Enterprises LLC (Motiva), a joint venture that combined the companies\xe2\x80\x99 petroleum-marketing operations in the eastern United States. Id., at 37. Shell assigned to Motiva its rights and obligations under the relevant franchise agreements. Motiva, in turn, took two actions that led to this lawsuit. First, effective January 1, 2000, Motiva ended the volume-based rent subsidy, thus increasing the franchisees\xe2\x80\x99 rent. Id., at 38. Second, as each franchise agreement expired, Motiva offered the franchisees new agreements that contained a different formula for calculating rent. For some (but not all) of the franchisees, annual rent was greater under the new\nformula.

\n

C

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In July 2001, 63 Shell franchisees (hereinafter dealers) filed suit against Shell and Motiva in Federal District Court. Their complaint alleged that Motiva\xe2\x80\x99s discontinuation of the rent subsidy constituted a breach of contract under state law. Additionally, the dealers asserted two claims under the PMPA. First, they maintained that Shell and Motiva, by eliminating the rent subsidy, had \xe2\x80\x9cconstructively terminated\xe2\x80\x9d their franchises in violation of the Act. Second, they claimed that Motiva\xe2\x80\x99s offer of new franchise agreements that calculated rent using a different formula amounted to a \xe2\x80\x9cconstructive nonrenewal\xe2\x80\x9d of their franchise relationships.[Footnote 3]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0After a 2-week trial involving eight of the dealers, the jury found against Shell and Motiva on all claims. Both before and after the jury\xe2\x80\x99s verdict, Shell and Motiva moved for judgment as a matter of law on the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 two PMPA claims. They argued that they could not be found liable for constructive termination under the Act because none of the dealers had abandoned their franchises in response to Motiva\xe2\x80\x99s elimination of the rent subsidy\xe2\x80\x93\xe2\x80\x93something Shell and Motiva said was a necessary element of any constructive termination claim. Similarly, they argued that the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 constructive nonrenewal claims necessarily failed because seven of the eight dealers had signed and operated under renewal agreements with Motiva, and the eighth had sold his franchise prior to the expiration of his franchise agreement. The District Court denied these motions, and Shell and Motiva appealed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The First Circuit affirmed in part and reversed in part. In affirming the judgment on the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 constructive termination claims, the Court of Appeals held that a franchisee is not required to abandon its franchise to recover for constructive termination under the PMPA. See 524 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 45\xe2\x80\x9347. Instead, the court ruled, a simple breach of contract by an assignee of a franchise agreement can amount to constructive termination under the Act, so long as the breach resulted in \xe2\x80\x9csuch a material change that it effectively ended the lease, even though the [franchisee] continued to operate [its franchise].\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 46 (internal quotation marks omitted). Turning to the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 constructive nonrenewal claims, the First Circuit agreed with Shell and Motiva that a franchisee cannot maintain a claim for unlawful nonrenewal under the PMPA \xe2\x80\x9cwhere the franchisee has signed and operates under the renewal agreement complained of.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 49. The court thus reversed the judgment on those claims.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We granted certiorari. See 557 U. S. ___ (2009).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The first question we are asked to decide is whether a service-station franchisee may recover for constructive termination under the PMPA when the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s allegedly wrongful conduct did not force the franchisee to abandon its franchise. For the reasons that follow, we conclude that a necessary element of any constructive termination claim under the Act is that the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s conduct forced an end to the franchisee\xe2\x80\x99s use of the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s trademark, purchase of the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s fuel, or occupation of the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s service station.[Footnote 4]

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0When given its ordinary meaning, the text of the PMPA prohibits only that franchisor conduct that has the effect of ending a franchise. As relevant here, the Act provides that \xe2\x80\x9cno franchisor \xe2\x80\xa6 may \xe2\x80\xa6 terminate any franchise,\xe2\x80\x9d except for an enumerated reason and after providing written notice. 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72802(a)\xe2\x80\x93(b). The Act specifies that \xe2\x80\x9c[t]he term \xe2\x80\x98termination\xe2\x80\x99 includes cancellation,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72801(17), but it does not further define the term \xe2\x80\x9cterminate\xe2\x80\x9d or the incorporated term \xe2\x80\x9ccancel.\xe2\x80\x9d We therefore give those terms their ordinary meanings. See Asgrow Seed Co. v. Winterboer, 513 U. S. 179, 187 (1995).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The word \xe2\x80\x9cterminate\xe2\x80\x9d ordinarily means \xe2\x80\x9cput an end to.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s New International Dictionary 2605 (2d ed. 1957); see also The Random House Dictionary of the English Language 1465 (1967). The term \xe2\x80\x9ccancel\xe2\x80\x9d carries a similar meaning: to \xe2\x80\x9cannul or destroy.\xe2\x80\x9d Webster\xe2\x80\x99s, supra, at 389; see also Random House, supra, at 215 (\xe2\x80\x9cto make void; revoke; annul\xe2\x80\x9d). The object of the verb \xe2\x80\x9cterminate\xe2\x80\x9d is the noun \xe2\x80\x9cfranchise,\xe2\x80\x9d a term the Act defines as \xe2\x80\x9cany contract\xe2\x80\x9d for the provision of one (or more) of the three elements of a typical petroleum franchise. \xc2\xa72801(1). Thus, when given its ordinary meaning, the Act is violated only if an agreement for the use of a trademark, purchase of motor fuel, or lease of a premises is \xe2\x80\x9cput [to] an end\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cannul[ed] or destroy[ed].\xe2\x80\x9d Conduct that does not force an end to the franchise, in contrast, is not prohibited by the Act\xe2\x80\x99s plain terms.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The same conclusion follows even if Congress was using the words \xe2\x80\x9cterminate\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9ccancel\xe2\x80\x9d in their technical, rather than ordinary, senses. When Congress enacted the PMPA, those terms had established meanings under the Uniform Commercial Code.[Footnote 5] Under both definitions, however, a \xe2\x80\x9ctermination\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9ccancellation\xe2\x80\x9d occurs only when a contracting party \xe2\x80\x9cputs an end to the contract.\xe2\x80\x9d U.\xc2\xa0C. C. \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x93106(3)\xe2\x80\x93(4) (1972 ed.); see also U. C. C. \xc2\xa72\xe2\x80\x93106(3)\xe2\x80\x93(4), 1 U. L. A. 695, 695\xe2\x80\x93696 (2004). Thus, a franchisee who continues operating a franchise\xe2\x80\x94occupying the same premises, receiving the same fuel, and using the same trademark\xe2\x80\x94has not had the franchise \xe2\x80\x9cterminate[d]\xe2\x80\x9d in either the ordinary or technical sense of the word.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Requiring franchisees to abandon their franchises before claiming constructive termination is also consistent with the general understanding of the doctrine of constructive termination. As applied in analogous legal contexts\xe2\x80\x94both now and at the time Congress enacted the PMPA\xe2\x80\x94a plaintiff must actually sever a particular legal relationship in order to maintain a claim for constructive termination. For example, courts have long recognized a theory of constructive discharge in the field of employment law. See Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, 542 U. S. 129, 141\xe2\x80\x93143 (2004) (tracing the doctrine to the 1930\xe2\x80\x99s). To recover for constructive discharge, however, an employee generally is required to quit his or her job. See 1 B. Lindemann & P. Grossman, Employment Discrimination Law 1449 (4th ed. 2007); 3 L. Larson, Labor and Employment Law \xc2\xa759.05[8] (2009); 2 EEOC Compliance Manual \xc2\xa7612.9(a) (2008); cf. Suders, supra, at 141\xe2\x80\x93143, 148; Young v. Southwestern Savings & Loan Assn., 509 F.\xc2\xa02d 140, 144 (CA5 1975); Muller v. United States Steel Corp., 509 F.\xc2\xa02d 923, 929 (CA10 1975). Similarly, landlord-tenant law has long recognized the concept of constructive eviction. See Rapacz, Origin and Evolution of Constructive Eviction in the United States, 1 DePaul L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 69 (1951). The general rule under that doctrine is that a tenant must actually move out in order to claim constructive eviction. See id., at 75; Glendon, The Transformation of American Landlord-Tenant Law, 23 Boston College L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 503, 513\xe2\x80\x93514 (1982); 1 H. Tiffany, Real Property \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7141, 143 (3d ed. 1939).[Footnote 6]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As generally understood in these and other contexts, a termination is deemed \xe2\x80\x9cconstructive\xe2\x80\x9d because it is the plaintiff, rather than the defendant, who formally puts an end to the particular legal relationship\xe2\x80\x94not because there is no end to the relationship at all. There is no reason why a different understanding should apply to constructive termination claims under the PMPA. At the time when it enacted the statute, Congress presumably was aware of how courts applied the doctrine of constructive termination in these analogous legal contexts. See Fitzgerald v. Barnstable School Comm., 555 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op, at 11\xe2\x80\x9312). And in the absence of any contrary evidence, we think it reasonable to interpret the Act in a way that is consistent with this well-established body of law.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Court of Appeals was of the view that analogizing to doctrines of constructive termination in other contexts was inappropriate because \xe2\x80\x9csunk costs, optimism, and the habit of years might lead franchisees to try to make the new arrangements work, even when the terms have changed so materially as to make success impossible.\xe2\x80\x9d 524 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 46. But surely these same factors compel employees and tenants\xe2\x80\x94no less than service-station franchisees\xe2\x80\x94to try to make their changed arrangements work. Nonetheless, courts have long required plaintiffs asserting such claims to show an actual severance of the relevant legal relationship. We see no reason for a different rule here.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Additionally, allowing franchisees to obtain PMPA relief for conduct that does not force an end to a franchise would extend the reach of the Act much further than its text and structure suggest. Prior to 1978, the regulation of petroleum franchise agreements was largely a matter of state law. See Dersch Energies, Inc. v. Shell Oil Co., 314 F.\xc2\xa03d 846, 861 (CA7 2002); Comment, 32 Emory L.\xc2\xa0J. 273, 277\xe2\x80\x93283 (1983). In enacting the PMPA, Congress did not regulate every aspect of the petroleum franchise relationship but instead federalized only the two parts of that relationship with which it was most concerned: the circumstances in which franchisors may terminate a franchise or decline to renew a franchise relationship. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72802; Dersch Energies, supra, at 861\xe2\x80\x93862. Congress left undisturbed state-law regulation of other types of disputes between petroleum franchisors and franchisees. See \xc2\xa72806(a) (pre-empting only those state laws governing franchise terminations or nonrenewals).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dealers would have us interpret the PMPA in a manner that ignores the Act\xe2\x80\x99s limited scope. On their view, and in the view of the Court of Appeals, the PMPA prohibits, not just unlawful terminations and nonrenewals, but also certain serious breaches of contract that do not cause an end to the franchise. See Brief for Respondents in No. 08\xe2\x80\x93372, pp. 28\xe2\x80\x9335 (hereinafter Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 Brief); 524 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 44\xe2\x80\x9347. Reading the Act to prohibit simple breaches of contract, however, would be inconsistent with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s limited purpose and would further expand federal law into a domain traditionally reserved for the States. Without a clearer indication that Congress intended to federalize such a broad swath of the law governing petroleum franchise agreements, we decline to adopt an interpretation of the Act that would have such sweeping consequences. See, e.g., United States v. Bass, 404 U. S. 336, 349 (1971).[Footnote 7]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, important practical considerations inform our decision. Adopting the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 reading of the PMPA would require us to articulate a standard for identifying those breaches of contract that should be treated as effectively ending a franchise, even though the franchisee in fact continues to use the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s trademark, purchase the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s fuel, and occupy the service-station premises.[Footnote 8] We think any such standard would be indeterminate and unworkable. How is a court to determine whether a breach is serious enough effectively to end a franchise when the franchisee is still willing and able to continue its operations? And how is a franchisor to know in advance which breaches a court will later determine to have been so serious? The dealers have not provided answers to these questions. Nor could they. Any standard for identifying when a simple breach of contract amounts to a PMPA termination, when all three statutory elements remain operational, simply evades coherent formulation.

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dealers suggest that this interpretation of the PMPA fails to provide franchisees with much-needed protection from unfair and coercive franchisor conduct that does not force an end to the franchise. That argument, however, ignores the fact that franchisees still have state-law remedies available to them. The pre-emptive scope of the PMPA is limited: The Act pre-empts only those state or local laws that govern the termination of petroleum franchises or the nonrenewal of petroleum franchise relationships. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72806(a). Outside of those areas, therefore, franchisees can still rely on state-law remedies to address wrongful franchisor conduct that does not have the effect of ending the franchise. Indeed, that happened in this very lawsuit. The dealers argued in the District Court that Motiva\xe2\x80\x99s elimination of the rent subsidy not only constructively terminated their franchises in violation of the PMPA but also amounted to a breach of contract under state law. The jury found in their favor on their state-law claims and awarded them almost $1.3 million in damages. See App. 376\xe2\x80\x93379. Thus, the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 own experience demonstrates that franchisees do not need a PMPA remedy to have meaningful protection from abusive franchisor conduct.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dealers also charge that this interpretation of the PMPA cannot be correct because it renders other provisions of the Act meaningless. Respondents\xe2\x80\x99 Brief 21\xe2\x80\x9322, 24\xe2\x80\x9325. While we agree that we normally should construe statutes \xe2\x80\x9cin a manner that gives effect to all of their provisions,\xe2\x80\x9d we believe our interpretation is faithful to this \xe2\x80\x9cwell-established principl[e] of statutory interpretation.\xe2\x80\x9d United States ex rel. Eisenstein v. City of New York, 556 U. S. ___, ___ (2009) (slip op., at 5)

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To begin, the dealers insist that our reading of the term \xe2\x80\x9cterminate\xe2\x80\x9d will require franchisees to go out of business before they can obtain preliminary relief and thus will render useless the Act\xe2\x80\x99s preliminary injunction mechanism. We disagree. To obtain a preliminary injunction, it is true, a franchisee must show, among other things, that \xe2\x80\x9cthe franchise of which he is a party has been terminated.\xe2\x80\x9d 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72805(b)(2)(A)(i) (emphasis added). But that does not necessarily mean that a franchisee must go out of business before obtaining an injunction. For example, in cases of actual termination, the Act requires franchisors to provide franchisees with written notice of termination well in advance of the date on which the termination \xe2\x80\x9ctakes effect.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72804(a). A franchisee that receives notice of termination \xe2\x80\x9chas been terminated\xe2\x80\x9d within the meaning of \xc2\xa72805(b)(2)(A)(i), even though the termination \xe2\x80\x9ctakes effect\xe2\x80\x9d on a later date, just as an employee who receives notice of discharge can be accurately described as having been discharged, even though the employee\xe2\x80\x99s last day at work may perhaps be weeks later. Thus, franchisees that receive notice of impending termination can invoke the protections of the Act\xe2\x80\x99s preliminary injunction mechanism well before having to go out of business.[Footnote 9] Contrary to the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 assertions, therefore, our interpretation of the Act gives meaningful effect to the PMPA\xe2\x80\x99s preliminary injunction provisions.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Our interpretation also gives effect to the Act\xe2\x80\x99s alternative statute-of-limitations accrual dates. The 1-year limitations period governing PMPA claims runs from the later of either (1) \xe2\x80\x9cthe date of termination of the franchise\xe2\x80\x9d or (2) \xe2\x80\x9cthe date the franchisor fails to comply with the requirements of\xe2\x80\x9d the Act. \xc2\xa72805(a). Some violations of the PMPA, however, cannot occur until after a franchise has been terminated. See, e.g., \xc2\xa72802(d)(1) (franchisor must share with a franchisee certain parts of a condemnation award when the termination was the result of a condemnation or taking); \xc2\xa72802(d)(2) (franchisor must grant a franchisee a right of first refusal if the franchise was terminated due to the destruction of the service station and the station subsequently is rebuilt). The second accrual date listed in \xc2\xa72805(a), therefore, shows only that the limitations period runs from the date of these types of post-termination violations. It does not suggest that Congress intended franchisees to maintain claims under the PMPA to redress franchisor conduct that does not force an end to the franchise.

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We therefore hold that a necessary element of any constructive termination claim under the PMPA is that the complained-of conduct forced an end to the franchisee\xe2\x80\x99s use of the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s trademark, purchase of the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s fuel, or occupation of the franchisor\xe2\x80\x99s service station. Because none of the dealers in this litigation abandoned any element of their franchise operations in response to Motiva\xe2\x80\x99s elimination of the rent subsidy,[Footnote 10] they cannot maintain a constructive termination claim on the basis of that conduct.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The second question we are asked to decide is whether a franchisee who is offered and signs a renewal agreement can nonetheless maintain a claim for \xe2\x80\x9cconstructive nonrenewal\xe2\x80\x9d under the PMPA. For reasons similar to those given above, we agree with the Court of Appeals that a franchisee that chooses to accept a renewal agreement cannot thereafter assert a claim for unlawful nonrenewal under the Act.[Footnote 11]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plain text of the statute leaves no room for a franchisee to claim that a franchisor has unlawfully declined to renew a franchise relationship\xe2\x80\x94constructively or otherwise\xe2\x80\x94when the franchisee has in fact accepted a new franchise agreement. As relevant here, a franchisor violates the PMPA only when it \xe2\x80\x9cfail[s] to renew\xe2\x80\x9d a franchise relationship for a reason not provided for in the Act or after not providing the required notice. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72802. The Act defines the term \xe2\x80\x9cfail to renew,\xe2\x80\x9d in turn, as a \xe2\x80\x9cfailure to reinstate, continue, or extend the franchise relationship.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72801(14). Thus, the threshold requirement of any unlawful nonrenewal action\xe2\x80\x94a requirement the franchisee bears the burden of establishing, see \xc2\xa72805(c)\xe2\x80\x94is that the franchisor did not \xe2\x80\x9creinstate, continue, or renew\xe2\x80\x9d the franchise relationship once a franchise agreement expired. But if a franchisee signs a renewal agreement, the franchisor clearly has \xe2\x80\x9creinstate[d], continue[d], or extend[ed]\xe2\x80\x9d the franchise relationship. True, the franchisee might find some of the terms in the new agreement objectionable. But the Act prohibits only unlawful \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ures] to renew\xe2\x80\x9d a franchise relationship, not renewals of a franchise relationship on terms that are less than favorable to the franchisee. A franchisee that signs a renewal agreement, in short, cannot carry the threshold burden of showing a \xe2\x80\x9cnonrenewal of the franchise relationship,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa72805(c), and thus necessarily cannot establish that the franchisor has violated the Act.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The dealers point out that several of them signed their renewal agreements \xe2\x80\x9cunder protest,\xe2\x80\x9d and they argue that they thereby explicitly preserved their ability to assert a claim for unlawful nonrenewal under the PMPA. That argument misunderstands the legal significance of signing a renewal agreement. Signing a renewal agreement does not constitute a waiver of a franchisee\xe2\x80\x99s legal rights\xe2\x80\x94something that signing \xe2\x80\x9cunder protest\xe2\x80\x9d can sometimes help avoid. See, e.g., U. C. C. \xc2\xa71\xe2\x80\x93207, 1 U. L.\xc2\xa0A. 318. Instead, signing a renewal agreement negates the very possibility of a violation of the PMPA. When a franchisee signs a renewal agreement\xe2\x80\x94even \xe2\x80\x9cunder protest\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94there has been no \xe2\x80\x9cfail[ure] to renew,\xe2\x80\x9d and thus the franchisee has no cause of action under the Act. See 15 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72805(a).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Act\xe2\x80\x99s structure and purpose confirm this interpretation. By requiring franchisors to renew only the \xe2\x80\x9cfranchise relationship,\xe2\x80\x9d as opposed to the same franchise agreement, see \xc2\xa72802; see also \xc2\xa72801(2), the PMPA contemplates that franchisors can respond to market demands by proposing new and different terms at the expiration of a franchise agreement. To that end, the Act authorizes franchisors to decline to renew a franchise relationship if the franchisee refuses to accept changes or additions that are proposed \xe2\x80\x9cin good faith and in the normal course of business\xe2\x80\x9d and that are not designed to convert the service station to direct operation by the franchisor. \xc2\xa72802(b)(3)(A). Additionally, the Act creates a procedural mechanism for resolving disputes over the legality of proposed new terms. If the parties cannot agree, the franchisor has the option of either modifying the objectionable terms or pursuing nonrenewal, in which case it must provide the franchisee with written notice well in advance of the date when the nonrenewal takes effect. \xc2\xa72804(a)(2). Once the franchisee receives notice of nonrenewal, it can seek a preliminary injunction under the Act\xe2\x80\x99s relaxed injunctive standard, maintaining the status quo while a court determines the lawfulness of the proposed changes. See \xc2\xa72805(b)(2); supra, at 13.[Footnote 12]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Allowing franchisees to pursue nonrenewal claims even after they have signed renewal agreements would undermine this procedural mechanism and, in the process, would frustrate franchisors\xe2\x80\x99 ability to propose new terms. Under the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 theory, franchisees have no incentive to object to burdensome new terms and seek a preliminary injunction if a franchisor pursues nonrenewal. Instead, a franchisee could simply sign the new franchise agreement and decide later whether to sue under the PMPA. Franchisees would then have the option of either continuing to operate under the new agreement or, if the terms of the agreement later proved unfavorable, bringing suit under the PMPA alleging that the newly imposed terms are unlawful. And because the PMPA has a 1-year statute of limitations, see \xc2\xa72805(a), franchisees would retain that option for the entire first year of a new franchise agreement. Accepting the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 argument, therefore, would cast a cloud of uncertainty over all renewal agreements and could chill franchisors from proposing new terms in response to changing market conditions and consumer needs.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, accepting the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 argument would greatly expand the PMPA\xe2\x80\x99s reach. Under the balance struck by the plain text of the statute, a franchisee faced with objectionable new terms must decide whether challenging those terms is worth risking the nonrenewal of the franchise relationship; if the franchisee rejects the terms and the franchisor seeks nonrenewal, the franchisee runs the risk that a court will ultimately determine that the proposed terms were lawful under the PMPA. See \xc2\xa72802(b)(3)(A). That risk acts as a restraint, limiting the scope of franchisor liability under the Act to that with which Congress was most concerned: the imposition of arbitrary and unreasonable new terms on a franchisee that are designed to force an end to the petroleum franchise relationship. See, e.g., ibid.; Comment, 32 Emory L.\xc2\xa0J., at 277\xe2\x80\x93283. Allowing franchisees both to sign a franchise agreement and to pursue a claim under the PMPA would eliminate that restraint and thus permit franchisees to challenge a much broader range of franchisor conduct\xe2\x80\x94conduct to which the dealer might object but not consider so serious as to risk the nonrenewal of the franchise by mounting a legal challenge. As explained, the PMPA was enacted to address the narrow areas of franchise terminations and nonrenewals, not to govern every aspect of the petroleum franchise relationship. See supra, at 10; Dersch Energies, 314 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 861. We thus decline to adopt an interpretation that would expand the Act in such a fashion.[Footnote 13]

\n

*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*\xe2\x80\x83\xe2\x80\x83*

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0We hold that a franchisee who is offered and signs a renewed franchise agreement cannot maintain a claim for unlawful nonrenewal under the PMPA. We therefore affirm the judgment of the Court of Appeals with respect to the dealers\xe2\x80\x99 nonrenewal claims.

\n

\n
