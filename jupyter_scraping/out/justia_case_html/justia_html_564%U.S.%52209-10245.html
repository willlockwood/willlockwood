FREEMAN v. UNITED STATES

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

No. 09\xe2\x80\x9310245.\xe2\x80\x83Argued February 23, 2011\xe2\x80\x94Decided June 23, 2011

\n

In order to reduce unwarranted federal sentencing disparities, the Sentencing Reform Act of 1984 authorizes the United States Sentencing Commission to create, and to retroactively amend, Sentencing Guidelines to inform judicial discretion. Title 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) permits a defendant who was sentenced to a term of imprisonment \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d a Guidelines sentencing range that has subsequently been lowered by retroactive amendment to move for a sentence reduction. This case concerns \xc2\xa73582(c)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s application to cases in which the defendant and the Government have entered into a plea agreement under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 11(c)(1)(C), which permits the parties to \xe2\x80\x9cagree that a specific sentence or sentencing range is the appropriate disposition of the case,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cbinds the court [to the agreed-upon sentence] once [it] accepts the plea agreement.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner Freeman was indicted for various crimes, including possessing with intent to distribute cocaine base. 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7841(a)(1). He entered into an 11(c)(1)(C) agreement to plead guilty to all charges; in return the Government agreed to a 106-month sentence. The agreement states that the parties independently reviewed the applicable Guidelines, noted that Freeman agreed to have his sentence determined under the Guidelines, and reflected the parties\xe2\x80\x99 understanding that the agreed-to sentence corresponded with the minimum sentence suggested by the applicable Guidelines range of 46 to 57 months, along with a consecutive mandatory minimum of 60 months for possessing a firearm in furtherance of a drug-trafficking crime under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A). Three years after the District Court accepted the plea agreement, the Commission issued a retroactive Guidelines amendment to remedy the significant disparity between the penalties for cocaine base and powder cocaine offenses. Because the amendment\xe2\x80\x99s effect was to reduce Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s applicable sentencing range to 37 to 46 months plus the consecutive 60-month mandatory minimum, he moved for a sentence reduction under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). However, the District Court denied the motion, and the Sixth Circuit affirmed because its precedent rendered defendants sentenced pursuant to 11(c)(1)(C) agreements ineligible for \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) relief, barring a miscarriage of justice or mutual mistake.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The judgment is reversed, and the case is remanded.

\n

355 Fed. Appx. 1, reversed and remanded.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy, joined by Justice Ginsburg, Justice Breyer, and Justice Kagan, concluded that defendants who enter into 11(c)(1)(C) agreements that specify a particular sentence as a condition of the guilty plea may be eligible for relief under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The text and purpose of the statute, Rule 11(c)(1)(C), and the governing Guidelines policy statements compel the conclusion that the district court has authority to entertain \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) motions when sentences are imposed in light of the Guidelines, even if the defendant enters into an 11(c)(1)(C) agreement. The district judge must, in every case, impose \xe2\x80\x9ca sentence sufficient, but not greater than necessary, to comply with\xe2\x80\x9d the purposes of federal sentencing, in light of the Guidelines and other relevant factors. \xc2\xa73553(a). The Guidelines provide a framework or starting point\xe2\x80\x94a basis, in the term\xe2\x80\x99s commonsense meaning\xe2\x80\x94for the judge\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of discretion. Rule 11(c)(1)(C) permits the defendant and the prosecutor to agree on a specific sentence, but that agreement does not discharge the district court\xe2\x80\x99s independent obligation to exercise its discretion. In the usual sentencing, whether following trial or plea, the judge\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the Guidelines will be apparent when the judge uses the Guidelines range as the starting point in the analysis and imposes a sentence within the range. Gall v. United States, 552 U. S. 38, 49. Even where the judge varies from the recommended range, id., at 50, if the judge uses the sentencing range as the beginning point to explain the deviation, then the Guidelines are in a real sense a basis for the sentence. The parties\xe2\x80\x99 recommended sentence binds the court \xe2\x80\x9conce the court accepts the plea agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d Rule 11(c)(1)(C), but the relevant policy statement forbids the judge to accept an agreement without first giving due consideration to the applicable Guidelines sentencing range, even if the parties recommend a specific sentence as a condition of the guilty plea, see U. S. Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual \xc2\xa76B1.2. This approach finds further support in the policy statement applicable to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) motions, which instructs the district court in modifying a sentence to substitute the retroactive amendment, but to leave all original Guidelines determinations in place, \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(1). Pp. 5\xe2\x80\x937.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0Petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing hearing transcript reveals that the District Court expressed its independent judgment that the sentence was appropriate in light of the applicable Guidelines range. Its decision was therefore \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d that range within \xc2\xa73582(c)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s meaning. P. 7.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0The Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument that sentences that follow an 11(c)(1)(C) agreement are based only on the agreement itself and not the Guidelines, and are therefore ineligible for \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) reduction, must be rejected. Even when a defendant enters into an 11(c)(1)(C) agreement, the judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision to accept the plea and impose the recommended sentence is likely to be based on the Guidelines; and when it is, the defendant should be eligible to seek \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) relief. Pp. 7\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor concluded that if an agreement under Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 11(c)(1)(C) ((C) agreement) expressly uses a Guidelines sentencing range applicable to the charged offense to establish the term of imprisonment, and that range is subsequently lowered by the Sentencing Commission, the prison term is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d the range employed and the defendant is eligible for sentence reduction under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x9311.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(a)\xc2\xa0The term of imprisonment imposed by a district court pursuant to a (C) agreement is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d the agreement itself, not on the judge\xe2\x80\x99s calculation of the Guidelines sentencing range. To hold otherwise would be to contravene the very purpose of (C) agreements\xe2\x80\x94to bind the district court and allow the Government and the defendant to determine what sentence he will receive. Pp.\xc2\xa01\xe2\x80\x935.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(b)\xc2\xa0This does not mean, however, that a term of imprisonment imposed under a (C) agreement can never be reduced under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). Because the very purpose of a (C) agreement is to allow the parties to determine the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence, when the agreement itself employs a particular Guidelines sentencing range applicable to the charged offenses in establishing the term of imprisonment imposed by the district court, the defendant is eligible to have his sentence reduced under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). Pp.\xc2\xa05\xe2\x80\x939.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0(c)\xc2\xa0Freeman is eligible. The offense level and criminal history category set forth in his (C) agreement produce a sentencing range of 46 to 57 months; it is evident that the parties combined the 46-month figure at the low end of the range with the 60-month mandatory minimum sentence under \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A) to establish the 106-month sentence called for in the agreement. Under the amended Guidelines, however, the applicable sentencing range is now 37 to 46 months. Therefore, Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s prison term is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d a sentencing range that \xe2\x80\x9chas subsequently been lowered by the Sentencing Commission,\xe2\x80\x9d rendering him eligible for sentence reduction. Pp.\xc2\xa09\xe2\x80\x9311.

\nOPINION OF KENNEDY, J.

WILLIAM FREEMAN, PETITIONER v.\nUNITED STATES

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the sixth circuit

\n

[June 23, 2011]

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Kennedy announced the judgment of the Court and delivered an opinion, in which Justice Ginsburg, Justice Breyer, and Justice Kagan join.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Sentencing Reform Act of 1984, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73551\net seq., calls for the creation of Sentencing Guidelines to inform judicial discretion in order to reduce unwarranted disparities in federal sentencing. The Act allows retro-active amendments to the Guidelines for cases where\nthe Guidelines become a cause of inequality, not a bulwark against it. When a retroactive Guideline amendment is adopted, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) permits defendants sentenced based on a sentencing range that has been modified to move for a reduced sentence.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The question here is whether defendants who enter into plea agreements that recommend a particular sentence\nas a condition of the guilty plea may be eligible for relief under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). See Fed. R. Crim. Proc. 11(c)(1)(C) (authorizing such plea agreements). The Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit held that, barring a miscarriage of justice or mutual mistake, defendants who enter into 11(c)(1)(C) agreements cannot benefit from retroactive Guideline amendments.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Five Members of the Court agree that this judgment must be reversed. The Justices who join this plurality opinion conclude that the categorical bar enacted by the Court of Appeals finds no support in \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), Rule 11(c)(1)(C), or the relevant Guidelines policy statements. In every case the judge must exercise discretion to im-\npose an appropriate sentence. This discretion, in turn, is framed by the Guidelines. And the Guidelines must be consulted, in the regular course, whether the case is one in which the conviction was after a trial or after a plea, including a plea pursuant to an agreement that recommends a particular sentence. The district judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision to impose a sentence may therefore be based on the Guidelines even if the defendant agrees to plead guilty under Rule 11(c)(1)(C). Where the decision to impose a sentence is based on a range later subject to retroactive amendment, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) permits a sentence reduction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 3582(c)(2) empowers district judges to correct sen-tences that depend on frameworks that later prove un-justified. There is no reason to deny \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) relief to defendants who linger in prison pursuant to sentences that would not have been imposed but for a since-rejected, excessive range.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor would reverse the judgment on a different ground set out in the opinion concurring in the judgment. That opinion, like the dissent, would hold that sentences following 11(c)(1)(C) agreement are based on the agreement rather than the Guidelines, and therefore that \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) relief is not available in the typical case. But unlike the dissent she would permit the petitioner here to seek a sentence reduction because his plea agreement in express terms ties the recommended sentence to the Guidelines sentencing range.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The reasons that lead those Members of the Court who join this plurality opinion may be set forth as follows.

\n

I

\n

A

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Federal courts are forbidden, as a general matter, to \xe2\x80\x9cmodify a term of imprisonment once it has been imposed,\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c); but the rule of finality is subject to a few narrow exceptions. Here, the exception is contained in a statutory provision enacted to permit defendants whose Guidelines sentencing range has been lowered by retro-active amendment to move for a sentence reduction if the terms of the statute are met. The statute provides:

\n

\xe2\x80\x9cIn the case of a defendant who has been sentenced to a term of imprisonment based on a sentencing range that has subsequently been lowered by the Sentencing Commission pursuant to 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. 994(o) \xe2\x80\xa6 the court may reduce the term of imprisonment, after considering the factors set forth in section 3553(a) to the extent that they are applicable, if such a reduction is consistent with applicable policy statements issued by the Sentencing Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73582(c)(2).

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This case concerns the application of the statute to cases in which defendants enter into plea agreements under Rule 11(c)(1)(C). That Rule permits the parties to \xe2\x80\x9cagree that a specific sentence or sentencing range is the appropriate disposition of the case, \xe2\x80\xa6 [a request which] binds the court once the court accepts the plea agreement.\xe2\x80\x9d The question is whether defendants who enter into 11(c)(1)(C) agreements that specify a particular sentence may be said to have been sentenced \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d a Guidelines sentencing range, making them eligible for relief under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2).

\n

B

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Petitioner William Freeman was indicted in 2005 for various crimes, including possessing with intent to distribute cocaine base. 21 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa7841(a)(1); (b)(1)(C). He entered into an agreement under Rule 11(c)(1)(C) in which he agreed to plead guilty to all charges. In return the Government \xe2\x80\x9cagree[d] that a sentence of 106 months\xe2\x80\x99 in-carceration is the appropriate disposition of this case.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 26a. The agreement states that \xe2\x80\x9c[b]oth parties have independently reviewed the Sentencing Guidelines applicable in this case,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9c[Freeman] agrees to have his sentence determined pursuant to the Sentencing Guidelines.\xe2\x80\x9d The agreement reflects the parties\xe2\x80\x99 expectation that Freeman would face a Guidelines range of 46 to 57 months, id., at 27a\xe2\x80\x9328a (Offense Level 19, Criminal History Category IV), along with a consecutive mandatory minimum of 60 months for possessing a firearm in furtherance of a drug-trafficking crime under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A). The recommended sentence of 106 months thus corresponded with the minimum sentence suggested by the Guidelines, in addition to the 60-month \xc2\xa7924(c)\n(1)(A) sentence.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The District Court accepted the plea agreement. At the sentencing hearing, the court \xe2\x80\x9cadopt[ed] the findings of the probation officer disclosed in the probation report and application of the guidelines as set out therein.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 47a. \xe2\x80\x9c[H]aving considered the advisory guidelines and 18 USC 3553(a),\xe2\x80\x9d the court imposed the recommended 106-month sentence, which was \xe2\x80\x9cwithin the guideline ranges\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94the 46- to 57-month range the parties had anticipated plus the mandatory 60 months under \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A)\xe2\x80\x94and \xe2\x80\x9csufficient to meet the objectives of the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id. at 48a\xe2\x80\x9349a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Three years later, the Commission issued a retroactive amendment to the Guidelines to remedy the significant disparity between the penalties for cocaine base and powder cocaine offenses. See United States Sentencing Commission, Guidelines Manual Supp. App. C, Amdt. 706 (Nov. 2010) (USSG) (effective Nov. 1, 2007) (adjusting Guidelines); id., Amdt. 713 (effective Mar. 3, 2008) (making Amendment 706 retroactive). Its effect was to reduce Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s applicable sentencing range to 37 to 46 months, again with the consecutive 60-month mandatory minimum. App. 142a\xe2\x80\x93144a (Sealed).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Freeman moved for a sentence reduction under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). The District Court, however, denied the motion, and the Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit affirmed. United States v. Goins, 355 Fed. Appx. 1 (2009). Adhering to its decision in United States v. Peveler, 359 F.\xc2\xa03d 369 (2004), the Court of Appeals held that defendants sentenced following 11(c)(1)(C) agreements that specify a particular sentence are ineligible for \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) relief, barring a miscarriage of justice or mutual mistake.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This Court granted certiorari. 561 U. S. __ (2010).

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Federal sentencing law requires the district judge in every case to impose \xe2\x80\x9ca sentence sufficient, but not greater than necessary, to comply with\xe2\x80\x9d the purposes of federal sentencing, in light of the Guidelines and other \xc2\xa73553(a) factors. 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a). The Guidelines provide a framework or starting point\xe2\x80\x94a basis, in the commonsense meaning of the term\xe2\x80\x94for the judge\xe2\x80\x99s exercise of discretion. E.g., 1 Oxford English Dictionary 977 (2d ed. 1989). Rule 11(c)(1)(C) permits the defendant and the prosecutor to agree that a specific sentence is appropriate, but that agreement does not discharge the district court\xe2\x80\x99s independent obligation to exercise its discretion. In the usual sentencing, whether following trial or plea, the judge\xe2\x80\x99s reliance on the Guidelines will be apparent, for the judge will use the Guidelines range as the starting point in the analysis and impose a sentence within the range. Gall v. United States, 552 U. S. 38, 49 (2007). Even where the judge varies from the recommended range, id., at 50, if the judge uses the sentencing range as the beginning point to explain the decision to deviate from it, then the Guidelines are in a real sense a basis for the sentence.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Rule 11(c)(1)(C) makes the parties\xe2\x80\x99 recommended sentence binding on the court \xe2\x80\x9conce the court accepts the plea agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d but the governing policy statement confirms that the court\xe2\x80\x99s acceptance is itself based on the Guidelines. See USSG \xc2\xa76B1.2. That policy statement forbids the district judge to accept an 11(c)(1)(C) agreement without first evaluating the recommended sentence in light\nof the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s applicable sentencing range. The commentary to \xc2\xa76B1.2 advises that a court may accept an 11(c)(1)(C) agreement \xe2\x80\x9conly if the court is satisfied either that such sentence is an appropriate sentence within the applicable guideline range or, if not, that the sentence de-parts from the applicable guideline range for justifiable reasons.\xe2\x80\x9d Cf. Stinson v. United States, 508 U. S. 36 (1993) (Guidelines commentary is authoritative). Any bargain between the parties is contingent until the court accepts the agreement. The Guidelines require the district judge to give due consideration to the relevant sentencing range, even if the defendant and prosecutor recommend a specific sentence as a condition of the guilty plea.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This approach finds further support in the policy statement that applies to \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) motions. See USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10. It instructs the district court in modifying a sentence to substitute only the retroactive amendment and then leave all original Guidelines determinations in place. \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(1). In other words, the policy statement seeks to isolate whatever marginal effect the since-rejected Guideline had on the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. Working backwards from this purpose, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) modification proceedings should be available to permit the district court to revisit a prior sentence to whatever extent the sentencing range in question was a relevant part of the analytic framework the judge used to determine the sentence or to approve the agreement. This is the only rule consistent with the governing policy statement, a statement that rests on the premise that a Guideline range may be one of many factors that determine the sentence imposed.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Thus, the text and purpose of the three relevant sources\xe2\x80\x94the statute, the Rule, and the governing policy statements\xe2\x80\x94require the conclusion that the district court has authority to entertain \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) motions when sentences are imposed in light of the Guidelines, even if the defendant enters into an 11(c)(1)(C) agreement.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The transcript of petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s sentencing hearing reveals that his original sentence was based on the Guidelines. The District Court first calculated the sentencing range, as both \xc2\xa73553(a)(4) and \xc2\xa76B1.2(c) require. App. 47a, 49a. It explained that it \xe2\x80\x9cconsidered the advisory guidelines and 18 USC 3553(a),\xe2\x80\x9d and that \xe2\x80\x9cthe sentence imposed \xe2\x80\xa6 fall[s] within the guideline rang[e] and [is] sufficient to meet the objectives of the law.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 48a\xe2\x80\x9349a. Apart from the defense attorney\xe2\x80\x99s initial statement that the case involved a \xe2\x80\x9c(C) plea,\xe2\x80\x9d id., at 47a, the hearing proceeded as if the agreement did not exist. The court expressed its independent judgment that the sentence was appropriate in light of the applicable Guidelines range, and its decision was therefore \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d that range.

\n

IV

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government asks this Court to hold that sentences like petitioner\xe2\x80\x99s, which follow an 11(c)(1)(C) agreement, are based only on the agreement and not the Guidelines, and therefore that defendants so sentenced are ineligible for \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) relief. The Government\xe2\x80\x99s position rests in part on the concern that the conclusion reached here will upset the bargain struck between prosecutor and defendant. See Brief for United States 42\xe2\x80\x9343. That, however, has nothing to do with whether a sentence is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d the Guidelines under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). And in any event, the concern is overstated. Retroactive reductions to sentencing ranges are infrequent, so the problem will not arise often. Thompson, DOJ\xe2\x80\x99s Attack on Federal Judicial \xe2\x80\x9cLeniency,\xe2\x80\x9d the Supreme Court\xe2\x80\x99s Response, and the Future of Criminal Sentencing, 44 Tulsa L.\xc2\xa0Rev. 519, 535 (2009). More important, the district court\xe2\x80\x99s authority under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) is subject to significant constraints, constraints that can be enforced by appellate review.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The binding policy statement governing \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) motions places considerable limits on district court discretion. All Guidelines decisions from the original sentencing remain in place, save the sentencing range that was altered by retroactive amendment. USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(1). In an initial sentencing hearing, a district court can vary below the Guidelines; but, by contrast, below-Guidelines modifications in \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) proceedings are forbidden, USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2)(A), except where the original sentence was itself a downward departure. \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(2)(B). And the court must always \xe2\x80\x9cconsider the nature and seriousness of the danger to any person or the community that may be posed by a reduction in the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s term of imprisonment.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71B1.10, comment., n. 1(B)(ii). The district court\xe2\x80\x99s authority is limited; and the Courts of Appeals, and ultimately this Court, can ensure that district courts do not overhaul plea agreements, thereby abusing their authority under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). See Dillon v. United States, 560 U. S. ___ (2010) (reviewing and affirming a \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) sentence reduction); Gall, 552 U. S., at 49 (all sentences are reviewable for abuse of discretion).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Government would enact a categorical bar on \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) relief. But such a bar would prevent district courts from making an inquiry that is within their own special knowledge and expertise. What is at stake in this case is a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility for relief, not the extent of that relief. Indeed, even where a defendant is permitted to seek a reduction, the district judge may conclude that a reduction would be inappropriate. District judges have a continuing professional commitment, based on scholarship and accumulated experience, to a consistent sentencing policy. They can rely on the frameworks they have devised to determine whether and to what extent a sentence reduction is warranted in any particular case. They may, when considering a \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) motion, take into account\na defendant\xe2\x80\x99s decision to enter into an 11(c)(1)(C) agreement. If the district court, based on its experience and informed judgment, concludes the agreement led to a more lenient sentence than would otherwise have been imposed, it can deny the motion, for the statute permits but does not require the court to reduce a sentence. This discretion ensures that \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) does not produce a windfall.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As noted, the opinion concurring in the judgment suggests an intermediate position. That opinion argues that in general defendants sentenced following 11(c)(1)(C) agreements are ineligible for \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) relief, but relief may be sought where the plea agreement itself contemplates sentence reduction. The statute, however, calls for an inquiry into the reasons for a judge\xe2\x80\x99s sentence, not the reasons that motivated or informed the parties. If, as the Government suggests, the judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision to impose a sentence is based on the agreement, then \xc2\xa73582(c)(2)\ndoes not apply. The parties cannot by contract upset an otherwise-final sentence. And the consequences of this erroneous rule would be significant. By allowing modification only when the terms of the agreement contemplate it, the proposed rule would permit the very disparities the Sentencing Reform Act seeks to eliminate.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The Act aims to create a comprehensive sentencing scheme in which those who commit crimes of similar severity under similar conditions receive similar sentences. See 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a)(6); K. Stith & J. Cabranes, Fear of Judging 104\xe2\x80\x93105 (1998). Section 3582(c)(2) contributes to that goal by ensuring that district courts may adjust sentences imposed pursuant to a range that the Commission concludes are too severe, out of step with the seriousness of the crime and the sentencing ranges of analogous offenses, and inconsistent with the Act\xe2\x80\x99s purposes.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The crack-cocaine range here is a prime example of an unwarranted disparity that \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) is designed to cure. The Commission amended the crack-cocaine Guidelines\nto effect a \xe2\x80\x9cpartial remedy\xe2\x80\x9d for the \xe2\x80\x9curgent and compel-ling\xe2\x80\x9d problem of crack-cocaine sentences, which, the Commission concluded, \xe2\x80\x9csignificantly undermines the various congressional objectives set forth in the Sentencing Reform Act.\xe2\x80\x9d United States Sentencing Commission, Report to Congress: Cocaine and Federal Sentencing Policy, pp.\xc2\xa08\xe2\x80\x9310 (May 2007); see also USSG Supp. App. C, Amdt. 706; Kimbrough v. United States, 552 U. S. 85, 99\xe2\x80\x93100 (2007). The Commission determined that those Guidelines were flawed, and therefore that sentences that relied on them ought to be reexamined. There is no good reason to extend the benefit of the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s judgment only to an arbitrary subset of defendants whose agreed sentences were accepted in light of a since-rejected Guidelines range based on whether their plea agreements refer to the Guidelines. Congress enacted \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) to remedy systemic injustice, and the approach outlined in the opinion concurring in the judgment would undercut a systemic solution.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Even when a defendant enters into an 11(c)(1)(C) agreement, the judge\xe2\x80\x99s decision to accept the plea and impose the recommended sentence is likely to be based on the Guidelines; and when it is, the defendant should be eligible to seek \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) relief. This straightforward analysis would avoid making arbitrary distinctions between similar defendants based on the terms of their plea agreements. And it would also reduce unwarranted disparities in federal sentencing, consistent with the purposes of the Sentencing Reform Act.

\n

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The judgment of the Court of Appeals is reversed and this case is remanded for further proceedings.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor, concurring in the judgment.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with the plurality that petitioner William Freeman is eligible for sentence reduction under 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), but I differ as to the reason why. In my view, the term of imprisonment imposed by a district court pursuant to an agreement authorized by Federal Rule of Criminal Procedure 11(c)(1)(C) ((C) agreement) is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d the agreement itself, not on the judge\xe2\x80\x99s calculation of the Sentencing Guidelines. However, I believe that if a (C) agreement expressly uses a Guidelines sentencing range applicable to the charged offense to establish the term of imprisonment, and that range is subsequently lowered by the United States Sentencing Commission, the term of imprisonment is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d the range employed and\nthe defendant is eligible for sentence reduction under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2).

\n

I

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To ask whether a particular term of imprisonment is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d a Guidelines sentencing range is to ask whether that range serves as the basis or foundation for the term of imprisonment. No term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x94\n\nwhether derived from a (C) agreement or otherwise\xe2\x80\x94has legal effect until the court enters judgment imposing it. As a result, in applying \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) a court must discern the foundation for the term of imprisonment imposed by the sentencing judge. As the plurality explains, in the normal course the district judge\xe2\x80\x99s calculation of the Guidelines range applicable to the charged offenses will serve as the basis for the term of imprisonment imposed. See ante, at 5; see also Gall v. United States, 552 U. S. 38, 49 (2007).

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Sentencing under (C) agreements, however, is different. At the time of sentencing, the term of imprisonment imposed pursuant to a (C) agreement does not involve the court\xe2\x80\x99s independent calculation of the Guidelines or consideration of the other 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73553(a) factors. The court may only accept or reject the agreement, and if\nit chooses to accept it, at sentencing the court may only impose the term of imprisonment the agreement calls for; the court may not change its terms. See Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 11(c)(3)(A) (\xe2\x80\x9cTo the extent the plea agreement is of the type specified in [Rule 11(c)(1)(C)], the court may accept the agreement, reject it, or defer a decision until the court has reviewed the presentence report\xe2\x80\x9d); Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on 1979 Amendments to Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 11, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App., pp. 583\xe2\x80\x93584 (1982 ed.) (\xe2\x80\x9c[C]ritical to a \xe2\x80\xa6 (C) agreement is that the defendant receive the \xe2\x80\xa6 agreed-to sentence\xe2\x80\x9d); accord, United States v. Rivera-Mart\xc3\xadnez, 607 F.\xc2\xa03d 283, 286 (CA1 2010); United States v. Green, 595 F.\xc2\xa03d 432, 438 (CA2 2010).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the (C) agreement context, therefore, it is the binding plea agreement that is the foundation for the term of im-prisonment to which the defendant is sentenced. At the moment of sentencing, the court simply implements the terms of the agreement it has already accepted. Contrary to the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s view, see ante, at 5\xe2\x80\x936, the fact that USSG \xc2\xa76B1.2(c) (Nov. 2010) instructs a district court to use the Guidelines as a yardstick in deciding whether to accept a (C) agreement does not mean that the term of imprisonment imposed by the court is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d a particular Guidelines sentencing range. The term of imprisonment imposed by the sentencing judge is dictated by the terms of the agreement entered into by the parties, not the judge\xe2\x80\x99s Guidelines calculation. In short, the term of imprisonment imposed pursuant to a (C) agreement is, for purposes of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d the agreement itself.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0To hold otherwise would be to contravene the very purpose of (C) agreements\xe2\x80\x94to bind the district court and allow the Government and the defendant to determine what sentence he will receive. Although district courts ordinarily have significant discretion in determining the appropriate sentence to be imposed on a particular defendant, see Gall, 552 U. S., at 46, under Rule 11(c)(1)(C) it\nis the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement that determines the sentence to be imposed, see Advisory Committee\xe2\x80\x99s Notes on 1999 Amendments to Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 11, 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. App., p.\xc2\xa01570 (2000 ed.) (noting that, under a (C) agreement, \xe2\x80\x9cthe government and defense have actually agreed on what amounts to an appropriate sentence \xe2\x80\xa6\xc2\xa0. [T]his agreement is binding on the court once the court accepts it\xe2\x80\x9d). To be sure, the court \xe2\x80\x9cretains absolute discretion whether to accept a plea agreement,\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., but once it does it is bound at sentencing to give effect to the parties\xe2\x80\x99 agreement as to the appropriate term of imprisonment.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Allowing district courts later to reduce a term of im-prisonment simply because the court itself considered\nthe Guidelines in deciding whether to accept the agreement would transform \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) into a mechanism by which courts could rewrite the terms of (C) agreements in ways not contemplated by the parties. At the time that \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) was enacted in 1984, it was already well understood that, under Rule 11, the term of imprisonment stipulated in a (C) agreement bound the district court once it accepted the agreement. See Fed. Rule Crim. Proc. 11(e)(1) (1982) (specifying that the parties to a (C) agreement may \xe2\x80\x9cagree that a specific sentence is the appropriate disposition of the case\xe2\x80\x9d); United States v. French, 719 F.\xc2\xa02d 387, 389, n.\xc2\xa02 (CA11 1983) (per curiam) (noting that a Rule 11(e)(1)(C) plea agreement was a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98binding\xe2\x80\x99 plea bargain\xe2\x80\x9d).[Footnote 1]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the absence of any indication from the statutory text or legislative history that \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) was meant to fundamentally alter the way in which Rule 11(c)(1)(C) operates, I cannot endorse the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s suggestion that \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) should be understood \xe2\x80\x9cto permit the district court to revisit a prior sentence to whatever extent the sentencing range in question was a relevant part of the analytic framework the judge used to determine the sentence or to approve the agreement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6; cf. Dillon v. United States, 560 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 8) (\xe2\x80\x9cCongress intended [\xc2\xa73582(c)(2)] to authorize only a limited adjustment to an otherwise final sentence\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0By the same token, the mere fact that the parties to a (C) agreement may have considered the Guidelines in the course of their negotiations does not empower the court under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) to reduce the term of imprisonment they ultimately agreed upon, as Freeman argues. Undoubtedly, he is correct that in most cases the Government and the defendant will negotiate the term of imprisonment in a (C) agreement by reference to the applicable Guidelines provisions. See Brief for Petitioner 30\xe2\x80\x9331 (\xe2\x80\x9c[T]he Guidelines are \xe2\x80\xa6 the starting point and initial benchmark for plea negotiations\xe2\x80\x9d); Brief for United States 33 (noting the \xe2\x80\x9cconcededly strong likelihood that the parties will \xe2\x80\xa6 calculat[e] and conside[r] potential Guidelines ranges in the course of negotiating a plea agreement and selecting a specific sentence\xe2\x80\x9d). This only makes sense; plea bargaining necessarily occurs in the shadow of the sentencing scheme to which the defendant would otherwise be subject. See United States v. Booker, 543 U. S. 220, 255 (2005) (\xe2\x80\x9c[P]lea bargaining takes place in the shadow of \xe2\x80\xa6 a potential trial\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis deleted)).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The term of imprisonment imposed by the district court, however, is not \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d those background negotia-\ntions; instead, as explained above, it is based on the binding agreement produced by those negotiations. I therefore cannot agree with Freeman that \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) calls upon district courts to engage in a free-ranging search through the parties\xe2\x80\x99 negotiating history in search of a Guidelines sentencing range that might have been relevant to the agreement or the court\xe2\x80\x99s acceptance of it. Nor can I agree with the plurality that the district judge\xe2\x80\x99s calculation of the Guidelines provides the basis for the term of imprisonment imposed pursuant to a (C) agreement.

\n

II

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0These conclusions, however, do not mean that a term of imprisonment imposed pursuant to a (C) agreement can never be reduced under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), as the Government contends. For example, Rule 11(c)(1)(C) allows the parties to \xe2\x80\x9cagree that a specific \xe2\x80\xa6 sentencing range is the appropriate disposition of the case.\xe2\x80\x9d In delineating the agreed-upon term of imprisonment, some (C) agreements may\ncall for the defendant to be sentenced within a particular Guidelines sentencing range. In such cases, the district court\xe2\x80\x99s acceptance of the agreement obligates the court to sentence the defendant accordingly, and there can be no doubt that the term of imprisonment the court imposes is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d the agreed-upon sentencing range within the meaning of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). If that Guidelines range is sub-sequently lowered by the Sentencing Commission, the de-fendant is eligible for sentence reduction.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Similarly, a plea agreement might provide for a specific term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x94such as a number of months\xe2\x80\x94but also make clear that the basis for the specified term is\na Guidelines sentencing range applicable to the offense\nto which the defendant pleaded guilty. As long as that sentencing range is evident from the agreement itself, for purposes of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) the term of imprisonment imposed by the court in accordance with that agreement is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d that range. Therefore, when a (C) agreement expressly uses a Guidelines sentencing range to establish the term of imprisonment, and that range is subsequently lowered by the Commission, the defendant is eligible for sentence reduction under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2).[Footnote 2]

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In so holding, I necessarily reject the categorical rule advanced by the Government and endorsed by the dissent, which artificially divorces a (C) agreement from its express terms.[Footnote 3] Because the very purpose of a (C) agreement is to allow the parties to determine the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence, when the agreement itself employs the particular Guidelines sentencing range applicable to the charged offenses in establishing the term of imprisonment, the defendant is eligible to have his sentence reduced under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2).[Footnote 4] In such cases, the district court\xe2\x80\x99s reduction of the sentence does not rewrite the plea agreement; instead, it enforces the agreement\xe2\x80\x99s terms.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Like the plurality, I am not persuaded by the Government\xe2\x80\x99s argument that allowing a term of imprisonment imposed pursuant to a (C) agreement to be reduced under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) deprives the Government of the benefit of the bargain it struck with the defendant. When a (C) agreement explicitly employs a particular Guidelines sentencing range to establish the term of imprisonment, the agreement itself demonstrates the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent that\nthe imposed term of imprisonment will be based on that range, as required for sentence reduction under the statute.[Footnote 5] The Government\xe2\x80\x99s concern that application of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) to (C) agreements will result in certain defendants receiving an \xe2\x80\x9cunjustified windfall\xe2\x80\x9d is therefore misplaced. See Brief for United States 40, 43.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Furthermore, in cases where the Government believes that even the limited sentence reduction authorized by \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) and USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10 improperly benefits the defendant, it can argue to the district court that the court should not exercise its discretion under the statute to reduce the sentence.[Footnote 6] See Dillon, 560 U. S., at ___ (slip op., at 9) (noting that, in applying \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), the court must \xe2\x80\x9cconsider whether the authorized reduction is warranted, either in whole or in part, according to the factors set forth in [18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C.] \xc2\xa73553(a)\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, if the Government wants to ensure ex ante that a particular defendant\xe2\x80\x99s term of imprisonment will not be reduced later, the solution is simple enough: Nothing prevents the Government from negotiating with a defendant to secure a waiver of his statutory right to seek sentence reduction under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), just as it often does with respect to a defendant\xe2\x80\x99s rights to appeal and collaterally attack the conviction and sentence.[Footnote 7] See 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73742; 28 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa72255 (2006 ed., Supp. III); see also App. 28a\xe2\x80\x9329a (provision in Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s agreement expressly waiving both rights). In short, application of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) to an eligible defendant does not\xe2\x80\x94and will not\xe2\x80\x94deprive the Government of the benefit of its bargain.

\n

III

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In order to conclude that Freeman is eligible for sentence reduction under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), the plea agreement between Freeman and the Government must use a Guidelines sentencing range that has subsequently been lowered by the Sentencing Commission to establish the term of imprisonment imposed by the District Court. Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s agreement does.

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The agreement states that Freeman \xe2\x80\x9cagrees to have his sentence determined pursuant to the Sentencing Guidelines,\xe2\x80\x9d App. 28a, and that 106 months is the total term of imprisonment to be imposed, id., at 26a. The agreement also makes clear that the \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A) count to which Freeman agrees to plead guilty carries a minimum sentence of 60 months, \xe2\x80\x9cwhich must be served consecutively to\xe2\x80\x9d any other sentence imposed. Id., at 27a. This leaves 46 months unaccounted for. The agreement sets Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s offense level at 19, as determined by the quantity of drugs and his acceptance of responsibility, and states that the parties anticipate a criminal history category of IV. Id., at 27a\xe2\x80\x9328a. Looking to the Sentencing Guidelines,\nan offense level of 19 and a criminal history category of\nIV produce a sentencing range of 46 to 57 months.[Footnote 8] See USSG ch. 5, pt. A (sentencing table). Therefore, contrary to the dissent\xe2\x80\x99s curious suggestion that \xe2\x80\x9cthere is no way\nof knowing what th[e] sentence was \xe2\x80\x98based on,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 6, it is evident that Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s agreement employed the\n46-month figure at the bottom end of this sentencing range, in combination with the 60-month mandatory minimum sentence under \xc2\xa7924(c)(1)(A), to establish his 106-month sentence.[Footnote 9] Thus the first of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s conditions is satisfied\xe2\x80\x94Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s term of imprisonment is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d a Guidelines sentencing range.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In 2007 the Commission amended the Guidelines provisions applicable to cocaine base offenses, such that the offense level applicable to the quantity of drugs for which Freeman was charged was lowered from 22 to 20. See App. 142a\xe2\x80\x93143a (Sealed); USSG Supp. App. C, Amdt. 706. Taking into account the three-level reduction for acceptance of responsibility, Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s recalculated offense level is 17, resulting in an amended sentencing range of 37 to 46 months. Thus there can be no doubt that the Guidelines sentencing range originally used to establish Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s term of imprisonment \xe2\x80\x9chas subsequently been lowered by the Sentencing Commission,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), such that the amendment \xe2\x80\x9cha[s] the effect of lowering [Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s] applicable guideline range,\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71B1.10(a)(2)(B). As\na result, Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s term of imprisonment satisfies the second of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2)\xe2\x80\x99s conditions. I therefore concur in the plurality\xe2\x80\x99s judgment that he is eligible for sentence reduction.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Of course, if a (C) agreement \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not contain any references to the Guidelines,\xe2\x80\x9d post, at 8 (Roberts, C.\xc2\xa0J., dissenting), there is no way of knowing whether the agreement \xe2\x80\x9cuse[d] a Guidelines sentencing range to establish the term of imprisonment,\xe2\x80\x9d supra, at 6, and a prisoner sentenced under such an agreement would not be eligible. It is therefore unclear why the dissent believes that the straightforward inquiry called for by the rule I apply today will \xe2\x80\x9cfoster confusion\xe2\x80\x9d among the lower courts. Post, at 7. This approach is consistent with the one already taken by most Courts of Appeals, see n.\xc2\xa03, supra, and there\nis no indication that they have found it unpalatable, cf. post, at 9.

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Chief Justice Roberts, with whom Justice Scalia, Justice Thomas, and Justice Alito join, dissenting.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The plurality and the opinion concurring in the judgment agree on very little except the judgment. I on the other hand agree with much of each opinion, but disagree on the judgment. I agree with the concurrence that the sentence imposed under a Rule 11(c)(1)(C) plea agreement is based on the agreement, not the Sentencing Guidelines. I would, however, adhere to that logic regardless whether the agreement could be said to \xe2\x80\x9cuse\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cemploy\xe2\x80\x9d a Guidelines range in arriving at the particular sentence specified in the agreement. Ante, at 1 (opinion of Sotomayor, J.). In that respect I agree with the plurality that the approach of the concurrence to determining when a Rule 11(c)(1)(C) sentence may be reduced is arbitrary and unworkable. Ante, at 9\xe2\x80\x9310.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Section 3582(c)(2) provides that \xe2\x80\x9cin the case of a defendant who has been sentenced to a term of imprisonment based on a sentencing range that has subsequently been lowered by the Sentencing Commission,\xe2\x80\x9d a district court \xe2\x80\x9cmay reduce the term of imprisonment . . . if such a reduction is consistent with applicable policy statements issued by the Sentencing Commission.\xe2\x80\x9d 18 U. S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). The lone issue here is whether petitioner William Freeman meets the initial prerequisite of having been sentenced to a term of imprisonment \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d a subsequently reduced sentencing range.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I agree with Justice Sotomayor that \xe2\x80\x9cthe term of im-prisonment imposed pursuant to a (C) agreement is, for purposes of \xc2\xa73582(c)(2), \xe2\x80\x98based on\xe2\x80\x99 the agreement itself.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3. In this case, Freeman executed a written plea agreement in which the parties \xe2\x80\x9cagree[d] that a sentence of 106 months\xe2\x80\x99 incarceration [was] the appropriate disposition.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 26a. Because the plea agreement was entered pursuant to Rule 11(c)(1)(C), that proposed sentence became binding on the District Court once it accepted the agreement. See Fed. Rule Crim. Proc.\xc2\xa011(c)(1)(C) (the parties\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x9crequest\xe2\x80\x9d for \xe2\x80\x9ca specific sentence\xe2\x80\x9d \xe2\x80\x9cbinds the court once the court accepts the plea agreement\xe2\x80\x9d). As a result, when determining the sentence to impose on Freeman, the District Court needed to consult one thing and one thing only\xe2\x80\x94the plea agreement. See ante, at 2 (opinion of\nSotomayor, J.) (\xe2\x80\x9cAt the moment of sentencing, the court simply implements the terms of the agreement it has al-ready accepted\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0I also agree with Justice Sotomayor that the \xe2\x80\x9cterm of imprisonment imposed by the sentencing judge is dictated by the terms of the agreement entered into by the par-\nties, not the judge\xe2\x80\x99s Guidelines calculation,\xe2\x80\x9d and that \n\xe2\x80\x9c[a]l-lowing district courts later to reduce a term of impris-onment simply because the court itself considered the Guidelines in deciding whether to accept the agreement would transform \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) into a mechanism by which courts could rewrite the terms of (C) agreements in ways not contemplated by the parties.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 3.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0But then comes the O. Henry twist: After cogently explaining why a Rule 11(c)(1)(C) sentence is based on the plea agreement, Justice Sotomayor diverges from that straightforward conclusion and holds that Freeman nevertheless satisfies the threshold requirement in \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). According to her opinion, if a Rule 11(c)(1)(C) \xe2\x80\x9cagreement expressly uses a Guidelines sentencing range applicable\nto the charged offense to establish the term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94or if such use is \xe2\x80\x9cevident from the agreement\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94then the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cterm of imprisonment is \xe2\x80\x98based on\xe2\x80\x99 the range employed and the defendant is eligible for sentence reduction under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2).\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 1, 6. This exception is in my view as mistaken as the position of the plurality\xe2\x80\x94and basically for the same reasons.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor begins the departure from her\nown rule innocently enough. As she explains, \xe2\x80\x9csome (C) agreements may call for the defendant to be sentenced within a particular Guidelines sentencing range.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. In such a case, according to Justice Sotomayor, there can be \xe2\x80\x9cno doubt\xe2\x80\x9d that the prison term the court imposes is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d the agreed-upon sentencing range, and therefore the defendant is eligible for sentence reduction. Ibid.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Whether or not that is true, it provides no support for the next step:

\n

\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9cSimilarly, a plea agreement might provide for a specific term of imprisonment\xe2\x80\x94such as a number of months\xe2\x80\x94but also make clear that the basis for the specified term is a Guidelines sentencing range applicable to the offense to which the defendant pleaded guilty. As long as that sentencing range is evident from the agreement itself \xe2\x80\xa6 the term of imprisonment imposed by the court in accordance with that agreement is \xe2\x80\x98based on\xe2\x80\x99 that range.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 6.

\n

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This category of cases is not \xe2\x80\x9csimilar\xe2\x80\x9d to the first at all. It is one thing to say that a sentence imposed pursuant to an agreement expressly providing that the court will sentence the defendant within an applicable Guidelines range is \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d that range. It is quite another to conclude that an agreement providing for a specific term is \xe2\x80\x9csimilarly\xe2\x80\x9d based on a Guidelines range, simply because the specified term can be said to reflect that range.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0According to the concurrence, if the parties simply \xe2\x80\x9ccon-sider[ ] the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cnegotiate \xe2\x80\xa6 by reference\xe2\x80\x9d\nto them, the defendant is not eligible for a sentence reduction. Ante, at 4. If, however, the agreement sets forth a specific term but it is somehow \xe2\x80\x9cclear that the basis for the specified term is a Guidelines sentencing range,\xe2\x80\x9d then the defendant is eligible for a sentence reduction. Ante, at 6. This head-scratching distinction between negotiating by reference to the Guidelines and using them as a basis for the specified term makes for an unworkable test that can yield only arbitrary results.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The confusion is compounded by the varying standards in the concurrence. Sometimes the test is whether an agreement \xe2\x80\x9cexpressly uses\xe2\x80\x9d a Guidelines sentencing range, ante, at 1, 6; see ante, at 7 (\xe2\x80\x9cexplicitly employs\xe2\x80\x9d). Other times the test is whether such use is \xe2\x80\x9cevident,\xe2\x80\x9d ante, at 6, 9; see ante, at 10, n.\xc2\xa09 (\xe2\x80\x9cclear\xe2\x80\x9d). A third option is whether the agreement \xe2\x80\x9cindicate[s] the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent to base the term of imprisonment on a particular Guideline range.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7, n.\xc2\xa05 (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The error in the concurring opinion is largely attributable to a mistaken shift in analysis. In the first half of the opinion, the inquiry properly looks to what the judge does: He is, after all, the one who imposes the sentence. After approving the agreement, the judge considers only the fixed term in the agreement, so the sentence he actually imposes is not \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d the Guidelines.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In the second half of the opinion, however, the analysis suddenly shifts, and focuses on the parties: Did they \xe2\x80\x9cuse\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cemploy\xe2\x80\x9d the Guidelines in arriving at the term in their agreement? But \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) is concerned only with whether a defendant \xe2\x80\x9chas been sentenced to a term of imprisonment based on a sentencing range.\xe2\x80\x9d Only a court can sentence a defendant, so there is no basis for examining why the parties settled on a particular prison term.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This conclusion dovetails with USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(1)\xe2\x80\x94\nthe Sentencing Commission\xe2\x80\x99s policy statement governing whether a defendant is eligible for a reduction under \xc2\xa73582(c)(2). As we explained last Term, \xc2\xa73582(c)(2) requires a district court \xe2\x80\x9cto follow the Commission\xe2\x80\x99s instructions in \xc2\xa71B1.10 to determine the prisoner\xe2\x80\x99s eligibility\nfor a sentence modification.\xe2\x80\x9d Dillon v. United States,\n560 U. S. ___, ___ (2010) (slip op., at 9). According to \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(1), the court must first determine \xe2\x80\x9cthe amended guideline range that would have been applicable to the defendant\xe2\x80\x9d if the retroactively amended provision had been in effect at the time of his sentencing. \xe2\x80\x9cIn making such determination, the court shall substitute only\nthe amendments \xe2\x80\xa6 for the corresponding guideline pro-visions that were applied when the defendant was sentenced.\xe2\x80\x9d USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10(b)(1), p. s. (emphasis added).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As noted, the District Court sentenced Freeman pursuant to the term specified by his plea agreement; it never \xe2\x80\x9capplied\xe2\x80\x9d a Guidelines provision in imposing his term of imprisonment. The fact that the court may have \xe2\x80\x9cuse[d] the Guidelines as a yardstick in deciding whether to accept a (C) agreement does not mean that the term of imprisonment imposed by the court is \xe2\x80\x98based on\xe2\x80\x99 a particular Guidelines sentencing range.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 2\xe2\x80\x933 (opinion of Sotomayor, J.). Even if the Guidelines were \xe2\x80\x9cused\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cemployed\xe2\x80\x9d by the parties in arriving at the Rule 11(c)(1)(C) sentencing term, they were not \xe2\x80\x9capplied when the defendant was sentenced.\xe2\x80\x9d Once the District Court accepted the agreement, all that was later \xe2\x80\x9capplied\xe2\x80\x9d was the sentence set forth in that agreement.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Justice Sotomayor is wrong to assert that her standard \xe2\x80\x9cdoes not rewrite the plea agreement\xe2\x80\x9d but rather \xe2\x80\x9cenforces the agreement\xe2\x80\x99s terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7. According to the concurrence, \xe2\x80\x9c[w]hen a (C) agreement explicitly employs a particular Guidelines sentencing range to establish the term of imprisonment, the agreement itself demonstrates the parties\xe2\x80\x99 intent that the imposed term of imprisonment will be based on that range,\xe2\x80\x9d and therefore subject to reduction if the Commission subsequently lowers that range. Ibid. In this case, Justice Sotomayor concludes that Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s agreement contemplated such a reduction, even though the parties had \xe2\x80\x9cagree[d] that a sentence of 106 months\xe2\x80\x99 incarceration is the appropriate disposition of this case.\xe2\x80\x9d App. 26a.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0There is, however, no indication whatever that the par-ties to the agreement contemplated the prospect of lowered sentencing ranges. And it is fanciful to suppose that the parties would have said \xe2\x80\x9c106 months\xe2\x80\x9d if what they really meant was \xe2\x80\x9ca sentence at the lowest end of the applicable Guidelines range.\xe2\x80\x9d Cf. App. 25a (parties in this case recommending \xe2\x80\x9ca fine at the lowest end of the applicable Guideline Range\xe2\x80\x9d). In concluding otherwise, the concurrence \xe2\x80\x9cignore[s] the agreement\xe2\x80\x99s express terms.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 7, n.\xc2\xa04.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0The reality is that whenever the parties choose a fixed term, there is no way of knowing what that sentence was \xe2\x80\x9cbased on.\xe2\x80\x9d The prosecutor and the defendant could well have had quite different reasons for concluding that 106 months was a good deal. Perhaps the prosecutor wanted to devote the limited resources of his office to a different area of criminal activity, rather than try this case. Perhaps the defendant had reason to question the credibility of one of his key witnesses, and feared a longer sentence if the case went to trial.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Indeed, the fact that there may be uncertainty about how to calculate the appropriate Guidelines range could be the basis for agreement on a fixed term in a plea under Rule 11(c)(1)(C). Here the agreement made clear that there was some doubt about the Guidelines calculations. See App. 28a (\xe2\x80\x9cBoth parties reserve the right to object to the USSG \xc2\xa74A1.1 calculation of defendant\xe2\x80\x99s criminal history\xe2\x80\x9d); ibid. (the parties acknowledge that their Guidelines calculations \xe2\x80\x9care not binding upon the Court\xe2\x80\x9d and that the \xe2\x80\x9cdefendant understands the Court will independently calculate the Guidelines at sentencing and defendant may not withdraw the plea of guilty solely because the Court does not agree with \xe2\x80\xa6 [the] Sentencing Guideline application\xe2\x80\x9d).

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0In addition, parties frequently enter plea agreements that reflect prosecutorial decisions not to pursue particular counts. If a defendant faces three counts, and agrees to plead to one if the prosecutor does not pursue the other two, is the sentence reflected in the Rule 11(c)(1)(C) agreement in any sense \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d the Guidelines sentencing range for the one count to which the defendant pleaded? Surely not. The concurrence tacitly concedes as much when it suggests that an agreement to \xe2\x80\x9cdrop[ ] a charge or forgo[ ] a future charge\xe2\x80\x9d could ultimately be grounds for not reducing the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s sentence. Ante, at 8, n.\xc2\xa06. But what this really shows is a basic flaw in the \xe2\x80\x9cbased on\xe2\x80\x9d test adopted by that opinion.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0Finally, Justice Sotomayor\xe2\x80\x99s approach will foster con-fusion in an area in need of clarity. As noted, courts\nwill be hard pressed to apply the distinction between referring to and relying on a Guidelines range. Other questions abound:

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What if the agreement contains a particular Guidelines calculation but the agreement\xe2\x80\x99s stipulated sentence is out-side the parties\xe2\x80\x99 predicted Guidelines range? The test\nin the concurring opinion is whether the agreement \xe2\x80\x9cuses\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cemploys\xe2\x80\x9d a Guidelines sentencing range to establish the term of imprisonment, ante, at 1, not whether that term falls within the range. In this case, what if the term was 53 months\xe2\x80\x94exactly half the low end of the sentencing range anticipated by the parties? Is it \xe2\x80\x9cevident\xe2\x80\x9d in that case that the Guidelines were used or employed to establish the agreed-upon sentence?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What if the plea agreement does not contain any references to the Guidelines\xe2\x80\x94not even the partial and tentative Guidelines calculations in Freeman\xe2\x80\x99s agreement\xe2\x80\x94but the binding sentence selected by the parties corresponds exactly to the low end of the applicable Guidelines range? Is it \xe2\x80\x9cevident\xe2\x80\x9d in that case that the agreement is based on a sentencing range?

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0What if the District Court calculates the applicable Guidelines range differently than the parties? This is no academic hypothetical. See, e.g., United States v. Franklin, 600 F.\xc2\xa03d 893, 896\xe2\x80\x93897 (CA7 2010) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9cthe district court settled on a higher guidelines range than that contemplated in the [Rule 11(c)(1)(C)] plea agreement\xe2\x80\x9d). Is a Rule 11(c)(1)(C) sentence still subject to reduction if the parties relied on the wrong sentencing range? Justice Sotomayor\xe2\x80\x99s surprising answer is \xe2\x80\x9cyes,\xe2\x80\x9d see ante, at 9, n.\xc2\xa08, even though the governing Guide-\nlines provision specifies that a defendant is only eligible for sentence reduction if the amended Guideline has \xe2\x80\x9cthe effect of lowering the defendant\xe2\x80\x99s applicable guideline range\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94presumably the correct applicable guideline range. See USSG \xc2\xa71B1.10(a)(2), p. s. Relying on error is just one unforeseen consequence of looking not to the specified term in a Rule 11(c)(1)(C) agreement, but instead trying to reconstruct what led the parties to agree to that term in the first place.

\n

\n\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0This confusion will invite the very thing Justice Sotomayor claims to disavow: a \xe2\x80\x9cfree-ranging search\xe2\x80\x9d by district courts \xe2\x80\x9cthrough the parties\xe2\x80\x99 negotiating history in search of a Guidelines sentencing range that might have been relevant to the agreement.\xe2\x80\x9d Ante, at 5. This is particularly unfortunate given that the whole point of Rule 11(c)(1)(C) agreements is to provide the parties with certainty about sentencing.

\n\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0As with any negotiation, parties entering a Rule 11(c)\n(1)(C) plea agreement must take the bitter with the\nsweet. Because of today\xe2\x80\x99s decision, however, Freeman gets more sweet and the Government more bitter than either side bargained for. But those who will really be left with a sour taste after today\xe2\x80\x99s decision are the lower courts charged with making sense of it going forward.
