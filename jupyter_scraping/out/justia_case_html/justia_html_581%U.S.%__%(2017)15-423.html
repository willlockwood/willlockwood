BOLIVARIAN REPUBLIC OF VENEZUELA et\xc2\xa0al. v. HELMERICH & PAYNE INTERNATIONAL DRILLING CO. et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

No. 15\xe2\x80\x93423.\xe2\x80\x83Argued November 2, 2016\xe2\x80\x94Decided May 1, 2017

\n

The Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act (FSIA) shields foreign states from suits in United States Courts,\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71604, with specified exceptions. The expropriation exception applies to \xe2\x80\x9cany case .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in which rights in property taken in violation of international law are in issue and that property .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is owned or operated by an agency or instrumentality of the foreign state .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. engaged in a commercial activity in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71605(a)(3).

\n

A wholly owned Venezuelan subsidiary (Subsidiary) of an American company (Parent) has long supplied oil rigs to oil development entities that were part of the Venezuelan Government. The American Parent and its Venezuelan Subsidiary (plaintiffs) filed suit in federal court against those entities (Venezuela), claiming that Venezuela had unlawfully expropriated the Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s rigs by nationalizing them. Venezuela moved to dismiss the case on the ground that its sovereign immunity deprived the District Court of jurisdiction. Plaintiffs argued that the case falls within the expropriation exception, but Venezuela claimed that international law did not cover the expropriation of property belonging to a country\xe2\x80\x99s nationals like the Subsidiary and that the American Parent did not have property rights in the Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s assets. The District Court agreed as to the Subsidiary, dismissing its claim on jurisdictional grounds. But it rejected the claim that the Parent had no rights in the Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s property. The District of Columbia Circuit reversed in part and affirmed in part, finding that both claims fell within the exception. With respect to the Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s claim, it concluded that a sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s taking of its own nationals\xe2\x80\x99 property would violate international law if the expropriation unreasonably discriminated based on a company\xe2\x80\x99s shareholders\xe2\x80\x99 nationality. With respect to the Parent\xe2\x80\x99s claim, it held that the exception applied because the Parent had raised its rights in a nonfrivolous way. The court decided only whether the plaintiffs might have a nonfrivolous expropriation claim, making clear that, under its standard, a nonfrivolous argument would be sufficient to bring a case within the scope of the exception. Given the factual stipulations, the court concluded, the Subsidiary had satisfied that standard for purposes of surviving a motion to dismiss.

\n

Held:\xc2\xa0The nonfrivolous-argument standard is not consistent with the FSIA. A case falls within the scope of the expropriation exception only if the property in which the party claims to hold rights was indeed \xe2\x80\x9cproperty taken in violation of international law.\xe2\x80\x9d A court should decide the foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s immunity defense \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t the threshold\xe2\x80\x9d of the action, Verlinden B. V. v. Central Bank of Nigeria,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 480\n, resolving any factual disputes as near to the outset of the case as is reasonably possible. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

(a)\xc2\xa0The expropriation exception grants jurisdiction only where there is a legally valid claim that a certain kind of right is at issue (property rights) and that the relevant property was taken in a certain way (in violation of international law). Simply making a nonfrivolous argument to that effect is not sufficient. This reading is supported by the provision\xe2\x80\x99s language, which applies in a \xe2\x80\x9ccase.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in which rights in property taken in violation of international law are in issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Such language would normally foresee a judicial decision about the jurisdictional matter. This interpretation is supported by precedent. See, e.g., Permanent Mission of India to United Nations v. City of New York,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193\n\xe2\x80\x93202. It is also supported by a basic objective of the FSIA, which is to follow international law principles, namely, that granting foreign sovereigns immunity from suit both recognizes the \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute independence of every sovereign authority\xe2\x80\x9d and helps to \xe2\x80\x9cinduc[e]\xe2\x80\x9d each nation state, as a matter of \xe2\x80\x9cinternational comity,\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9crespect the independence and dignity of every other,\xe2\x80\x9d Berizzi Brothers Co. v. S. S. Pesaro,\n\n271 U.\xc2\xa0S. 562\n. Nothing in the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s history suggests that Congress intended a radical departure from these principles in codifying the mid-20th-century doctrine of \xe2\x80\x9crestrictive\xe2\x80\x9d sovereign immunity, which denies immunity in cases \xe2\x80\x9carising out of a foreign state\xe2\x80\x99s strictly commercial acts,\xe2\x80\x9d but applies immunity in \xe2\x80\x9csuits involving the foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s public acts,\xe2\x80\x9d Verlinden, supra, at 487. It is thus not surprising that the expropriation exception on its face emphasizes conformity with international law, requiring both a commercial connection with the United States and a taking of property \xe2\x80\x9cin violation of international law.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

A \xe2\x80\x9cnonfrivolous-argument\xe2\x80\x9d reading of the exception would undermine the objectives embedded in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language, history, and structure. It could also embroil a foreign sovereign in an American lawsuit for some time by adopting a standard limited only by the bounds of a lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s (nonfrivolous) imagination. And it could cause friction with other nations, leading to reciprocal actions against this country. Pp.\xc2\xa06\xe2\x80\x9312.

\n

(b)\xc2\xa0Plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 arguments to the contrary are unpersuasive. They suggest that the expropriation exception should be treated similarly to\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9carising under\xe2\x80\x9d jurisdiction, which applies if a plaintiff can make a nonfrivolous argument that a federal law provides the relief sought\xe2\x80\x94even if, in fact, it does not, Bell v. Hood,\n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678\n 685. But \xc2\xa71331 differs from the exception in language and concerns. Section 1331 often simply determines which court doors\xe2\x80\x94federal or state\xe2\x80\x94are open, and neither it nor related jurisdictional sections seek to provide a sovereign foreign nation with immunity\xe2\x80\x94the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s basic objective. Nor does the text of \xc2\xa71331 suggest that consistency with international law is of particular importance.

\n

Plaintiffs also claim that the nonfrivolous-argument approach will work little harm since the matter could be resolved by motion practice before the sovereign bears the expense of a full trial. But resolving a claim pursuant to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 12(b)(6) or summary judgment under Rule 56 may impose increased burdens of time and expense upon the foreign nation. And a district court\xe2\x80\x99s decision that there is a \xe2\x80\x9cviolation of international law\xe2\x80\x9d as a matter of jurisdiction may be immediately appealable as a collateral order, while the same decision made pursuant to a Rule 12(b)(6) or Rule 56 motion would be a decision on the \xe2\x80\x9cmerits\xe2\x80\x9d not subject to immediate appeal. Moreover, the Circuit would part with its nonfrivolous-argument standard where a \xe2\x80\x9cviolation of international law\xe2\x80\x9d is not an element of the claim to be decided on the merits. This bifurcated approach is difficult to reconcile with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language, history, or purpose; and it creates needless complexity for judges and lawyers, domestic and foreign. Pp.\xc2\xa012\xe2\x80\x9316.

\n

784 F.\xc2\xa03d 804, vacated and remanded.

BOLIVARIAN REPUBLIC OF VENEZUELA, et\xc2\xa0al., PETITIONERS v. HELMERICH & PAYNE IN-TERNATIONAL DRILLING CO., et\xc2\xa0al.

\n

on writ of certiorari to the united states court of appeals for the district of columbia circuit

\n

[May 1, 2017]

\n

\n

Justice Breyer delivered the opinion of the Court.

\n

The Foreign Sovereign Immunities Act of 1976 (FSIA or Act), provides, with specified exceptions, that a \xe2\x80\x9cforeign state shall be immune from the jurisdiction of the courts of the United States and of the States .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71604. One of the jurisdictional exceptions\xe2\x80\x94the expropriation exception\xe2\x80\x94says that

\n

\xe2\x80\x9c[a] foreign state shall not be immune from the jurisdiction of courts of the United States or of the States in any case .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. (3) in which rights in property taken in violation of international law are in issue and that property .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. is owned or operated by an agency orinstrumentality of the foreign state .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. engaged ina commercial activity in the United States.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71605(a)(3).

\n

The question here concerns the phrase \xe2\x80\x9ccase .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in which rights in property taken in violation of international law are in issue.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

Does this phrase mean that, to defeat sovereign immu-nity, a party need only make a \xe2\x80\x9cnonfrivolous\xe2\x80\x9d argument that the case falls within the scope of the exception? Once made, does the existence of that nonfrivolous argument mean that the court retains jurisdiction over the case until the court decides, say, the merits of the case? Or does a more rigorous jurisdictional standard apply? To put the question more generally: What happens in a case where the party seeking to rely on the expropriation exception makes a nonfrivolous, but ultimately incorrect, claim that his property was taken in violation of international law?

\n

In our view, a party\xe2\x80\x99s nonfrivolous, but ultimately incorrect, argument that property was taken in violation of international law is insufficient to confer jurisdiction. Rather, state and federal courts can maintain jurisdiction to hear the merits of a case only if they find that the property in which the party claims to hold rights was indeed \xe2\x80\x9cproperty taken in violation of international law.\xe2\x80\x9d Put differently, the relevant factual allegations must make out a legally valid claim that a certain kind of right is at issue (property rights) and that the relevant property was taken in a certain way (in violation of international law). A good argument to that effect is not sufficient. But a court normally need not resolve, as a jurisdictional matter, disputes about whether a party actually held rights in that prop-erty; those questions remain for the merits phase of the litigation.

\n

Moreover, where jurisdictional questions turn upon further factual development, the trial judge may take evidence and resolve relevant factual disputes. But, consistent with foreign sovereign immunity\xe2\x80\x99s basic objective, namely, to free a foreign sovereign from suit, the court should normally resolve those factual disputes and reach a decision about immunity as near to the outset of the case as is reasonably possible. See Verlinden B. V. v. Central Bank of Nigeria,\n\n461 U.\xc2\xa0S. 480\n\xe2\x80\x93494 (1983).

\n

I

\n

Since the mid-1970\xe2\x80\x99s a wholly owned Venezuela-incorporated subsidiary (Subsidiary) of an American company (Parent) supplied oil rigs to oil development entities that were part of the Venezuelan Government. In 2011 the American Parent company and its Venezuelan Subsidiary (the respondents here) brought this lawsuit in federal court against those foreign government entities. (The entities go by their initials, PDVSA, but we shall normally refer to them as \xe2\x80\x9cVenezuela\xe2\x80\x9d or the \xe2\x80\x9cVenezuelan Government.\xe2\x80\x9d) The American Parent and the Venezuelan Subsidiary claimed that the Venezuelan Government had unlawfully expropriated the Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s oil rigs. And they sought compensation.

\n

According to stipulated facts, by early 2010 the Venezuelan Government had failed to pay more than $10 million that it owed the Subsidiary. At that point the government sent troops to the equipment yard where the rigs were stored, prevented the Subsidiary from removing the rigs, and issued a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Decree of Expropriation\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d nationalizing the rigs. App. 72\xe2\x80\x9374. Subsequently, the president of the oil development entities led a rally at the Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s offices, where he referred to the Venezuelan Subsidiary as an \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98American company\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d with \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98foreign gentlemen investors.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 54.

\n

Venezuela asked the court to dismiss the case on the ground that Venezuela possessed sovereign immunity and that the court consequently lacked \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d to hear the case. See\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71604; Fed. Rules Civ. Proc. 12(b)(1) and (b)(2); Verlinden, supra, at 485, n.\xc2\xa05 (explaining that a court lacks \xe2\x80\x9csubject-matter\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cpersonal\xe2\x80\x9d jurisdiction over a foreign sovereign unless an FSIA exception applies). The companies replied that the case falls within the expropriation exception. Venezuela in turn argued that the Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s expropriation claim did not satisfy the exception because \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98international law does not cover expropriations of property belonging to a country\xe2\x80\x99s own nationals\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d; the taking was not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98in violation of international law,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d and the exception thus does not apply. Record in No. 11\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x9301735 (D DC), Doc. 22, p. 13. Venezuela further argued that the American Parent\xe2\x80\x99s nationality makes no difference because, \xe2\x80\x9cas a corporate parent, [it] does not own [the Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s] assets.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., Doc. 24,at 12.

\n

The parties agreed that the District Court should then decide whether the exception applies, and it should do so on the basis of governing law, taking all of the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 well-pleaded allegations as true and construing the complaint in the light most favorable to the plaintiffs. App. 119. The court decided, in relevant part, that the exception did not apply to the Venezuelan Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s claim because the Subsidiary was a national of Venezuela. See 971 F. Supp. 2d 49, 57\xe2\x80\x9361 (2013). The court concluded that Venezuela consequently possessed sovereign immu-nity, and it dismissed the Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s claim on jurisdictional grounds. It rejected, however, Venezuela\xe2\x80\x99s argument that the Parent had no rights in property in the Subsidiary. It concluded that Venezuela\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cactions have deprived [the Parent], individually, of its essential and unique rights as sole shareholder .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. by dismantling its voting power, destroying its ownership, and frustrating its control over the company.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 73.

\n

The Venezuelan Subsidiary appealed the dismissal of its expropriation claim, and Venezuela appealed the court\xe2\x80\x99s refusal to dismiss the Parent\xe2\x80\x99s claim. The Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit reversed in part and affirmed in part the District Court\xe2\x80\x99s conclusions. It decided that both the Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s and the Parent\xe2\x80\x99s claims fell within the exception.

\n

With respect to the Subsidiary\xe2\x80\x99s claim, the court agreed that a sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s taking of its own nationals\xe2\x80\x99 property normally does not violate international law. But, the court said, there is an \xe2\x80\x9cexception\xe2\x80\x9d to this rule. And that exception applies when a sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s expropriation unreasonably discriminates on the basis of a company\xe2\x80\x99s shareholders\xe2\x80\x99 nationality, 784 F.\xc2\xa03d 804, 812 (CADC 2015) (citing Banco Nacional de Cuba v. Sabbatino, 307 F. 2d 845 (CA2 1962)). That exception, it added, might apply here, in which case the expropriation would violate international law, the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s expropriation exception would apply, and the federal courts would possess jurisdiction over the case. 784 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 813. With respect to the Parent\xe2\x80\x99s expropriation claim, the court agreed with the District Court that the expropriation exception applied because the Parent had \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98put its rights in property in issue in a non-frivolous way.\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 816.

\n

For present purposes, it is important to keep in mind that the Court of Appeals did not decide (on the basis of the stipulated facts) that the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 allegations are sufficient to show their property was taken in violation of international law. It decided instead that the plaintiffs might have such a claim. And it made clear the legal standard that it would apply. It said that, in deciding whether the expropriation exception applies, it would set an \xe2\x80\x9cexceptionally low bar.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 812. Any possible, i.e., \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98non-frivolous,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d ibid., claim of expropriation is sufficient, in the Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 view, to bring a case within the scope of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s exception. In particular: If a plaintiff alleges facts and claims that permit the plaintiff to make an expropriation claim that is not \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98wholly insubstantial or frivolous,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d then the exception permits the suit and the sovereign loses its immunity. Ibid. (emphasis added). Given the factual stipulations, the Court of Appeals did not suggest further factfinding on this jurisdictional issue but, rather, decided that the Subsidiary had \xe2\x80\x9csatisfied this Circuit\xe2\x80\x99s forgiving standard for surviving a motion to dismiss in an FSIA case.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 813.

\n

Venezuela filed a petition for certiorari asking us to decide whether the Court of Appeals had applied the correct standard in deciding that the companies had met the expropriation exception\xe2\x80\x99s requirements. We agreed to do so.

\n

II

\n

Foreign sovereign immunity is jurisdictional in this case because explicit statutory language makes it so. See \xc2\xa71604 (\xe2\x80\x9c[A] foreign state shall be immune from the jurisdiction of the courts of the United States and of the States except as provided\xe2\x80\x9d by the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s exceptions); \xc2\xa71605(a) (\xe2\x80\x9cA foreign state shall not be immune from the jurisdiction\xe2\x80\x9d of federal and state courts if the exception at issue here is satisfied). Given the parties\xe2\x80\x99 stipulations as to all relevant facts, our inquiry poses a \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98pure question of statutory construction,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d Republic of Austria v. Altmann,\n\n541 U.\xc2\xa0S. 677,\n701 (2004)\n.In our view, the expropriation exception grants jurisdiction only where there is a valid claim that \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d has been \xe2\x80\x9ctaken in violation of international law.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71605(a)(3). A nonfrivolous argument to that effect is insufficient.

\n

For one thing, the provision\xe2\x80\x99s language, while ambiguous, supports such a reading. It says that there is jurisdiction in a \xe2\x80\x9ccase .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. in which rights in property taken in violation of international law are in issue.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. Such language would normally foresee a judicial decision about the jurisdictional matter. And that matter is whether a certain kind of \xe2\x80\x9cright\xe2\x80\x9d is \xe2\x80\x9cat issue,\xe2\x80\x9d namely, a property right taken in violation of international law. To take a purely hypothetical example, a party might assert a claim to a house in a foreign country. If the foreign country nationalized the house and, when sued, asserted sovereign immunity, then the claiming party would as a jurisdictional matter prove that he claimed \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d (which a house obviously is) and also that the property was \xe2\x80\x9ctaken in violation of international law.\xe2\x80\x9d He need not show as a jurisdictional matter that he, rather than someone else, owned the house. That question is part of the merits of the case and remains \xe2\x80\x9cat issue.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

We recognize that merits and jurisdiction will sometimes come intertwined. Suppose that the party asserted a claim to architectural plans for the house. It might be necessary to decide whether the law recognizes the kind of right that he asserts, or whether it is a right in \xe2\x80\x9cproperty\xe2\x80\x9d that was \xe2\x80\x9ctaken in violation of international law.\xe2\x80\x9d Perhaps that is the only serious issue in the case. If so, the court must still answer the jurisdictional question. If to do so, it must inevitably decide some, or all, of the merits issues, so be it.

\n

Our reading of the statute is consistent with its language. The case is one which the existence of \xe2\x80\x9crights\xe2\x80\x9d remains \xe2\x80\x9cat issue\xe2\x80\x9d until the court decides the merits of the case. But whether the rights asserted are rights of a certain kind, namely, rights in \xe2\x80\x9cproperty taken in violation of international law,\xe2\x80\x9d is a jurisdictional matter that the court must typically decide at the outset of the case, or as close to the outset as is reasonably possible.

\n

Precedent offers a degree of support for our interpretation. In Permanent Mission of India to United Nations v. City of New York,\n\n551 U.\xc2\xa0S. 193 (2007)\n, we interpreted a different FSIA exception for cases \xe2\x80\x9cin which .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. rights in immovable property situated in the United States are in issue.\xe2\x80\x9d \xc2\xa71605(a)(4). We held that there was jurisdiction over the case because the plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s lawsuit to enforce a tax lien \xe2\x80\x9cdirectly implicate[d]\xe2\x80\x9d the property rights described by the FSIA exception. See id., at 200\xe2\x80\x93201. We did not simply rely upon a finding that the plaintiff had made a nonfrivolous argument that the exception applied.

\n

For another thing, one of the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s basic objectives, as shown by its history, supports this reading. The Act for the most part embodies basic principles of international law long followed both in the United States and elsewhere. See Schooner Exchange v. McFaddon, 7 Cranch 116, 136\xe2\x80\x93137 (1812); see also Verlinden, 461 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 493 (explaining that the Act \xe2\x80\x9ccomprehensively regulat[es] the amen-ability of foreign nations to suit in the United States\xe2\x80\x9d). Our courts have understood, as international law itself understands, foreign nation states to be \xe2\x80\x9cindependent sovereign\xe2\x80\x9d entities. To grant those sovereign entities an immunity from suit in our courts both recognizes the \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute independence of every sovereign authority\xe2\x80\x9d and helps to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98induc[e]\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d each nation state, as a matter of \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98international comity,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d to \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98respect the independence and dignity of every other,\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d including our own. Berizzi Brothers Co. v. S.\xc2\xa0S. Pesaro,\n\n271 U.\xc2\xa0S. 562,\n575 (1926)\n (quoting The Parlement Belge, [1880] 5 P. D. 197, 214\xe2\x80\x93215 (appeal taken from Admiralty Div.)).

\n

In the mid-20th century, we, like many other nations, began to treat nations acting in a commercial capacity like other commercial entities. See Permanent Mission, supra, at 199\xe2\x80\x93200. And we consequently began to limit our recognition of sovereign immunity, denying that immunity in cases \xe2\x80\x9carising out of a foreign state\xe2\x80\x99s strictly commercial acts,\xe2\x80\x9d but continuing to apply that doctrine in \xe2\x80\x9csuits involving the foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s public acts,\xe2\x80\x9d Verlinden, 461 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 487 (emphasis added).

\n

At first, our courts, aware of the expertise of the Executive Branch in matters of foreign affairs, relied heavily upon the advice of that branch when deciding just when and how this \xe2\x80\x9crestrictive\xe2\x80\x9d sovereign immunity doctrine applied. Ibid. See also H.\xc2\xa0R. Rep. No. 94\xe2\x80\x931487, pp. 8\xe2\x80\x939 (1976) (similar). But in 1976, Congress, at the urging of the Department of State and Department of Justice, began to codify the doctrine. The resulting statute, the FSIA, \xe2\x80\x9cstarts from a premise of immunity and then creates exceptions to the general principle.\xe2\x80\x9d Id., at 17; Verlinden, supra, at 493. Almost all the exceptions involve commerce or immovable property located in the United States. E.g., \xc2\xa7\xc2\xa71605(a)(2) and (4); see also \xc2\xa71602 (expressing the finding that \xe2\x80\x9c[u]nder international law, states are not immune from the jurisdiction of foreign courts insofar as their commercial activities are concerned\xe2\x80\x9d). The statute thereby creates a doctrine that by and large continues to reflect basic principles of international law, in particular those principles embodied in what jurists refer to as the \xe2\x80\x9crestrictive\xe2\x80\x9d theory of sovereign immunity. See, e.g., Restatement (Third) of Foreign Relations Law of the United States \xc2\xa7451, and Comment a (1986) (describing the restrictive theory of immunity); United Nations General Assembly, Convention on Jurisdictional Immunities of States and Their Property, Res. 59/38, Arts. 5, 10\xe2\x80\x9312 (Dec. 2, 2004) (adopting a restrictive theory of immunity and withdrawing immunity for loss of property where, among other requirements, \xe2\x80\x9cthe act or omission occurred in whole or in part in the territory of th[e] other State\xe2\x80\x9d); United Nations General Assembly, Report of the Ad\xc2\xa0Hoc Committee on Jurisdictional Immunities of States and Their Property, Supp. A/59/22 No. 1, pp.\xc2\xa07\xe2\x80\x9311 (Mar. 1\xe2\x80\x935, 2004) (same).

\n

We have found nothing in the history of the statute that suggests Congress intended a radical departure from these basic principles. To the contrary, the State Department, which helped to draft the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s language (and to whose views on sovereign immunity this Court, like Congress, has paid special attention, Altmann, 541 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 696), told Congress that the Act was \xe2\x80\x9cdrafted keeping in mind what we believe to be the general state of the law internationally, so that we conform fairly closely .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. to our accepted international standards,\xe2\x80\x9d Hearing on H.\xc2\xa0R. 3493 before the Subcommittee on Claims and Governmental Relations of the House of Representatives Committee on the Judiciary, 93d Cong., 1st Sess., 18 (1973). The Department added that, by doing so, we would diminish the likelihood that other nations would each go their own way, thereby \xe2\x80\x9csubject[ing]\xe2\x80\x9d the United States \xe2\x80\x9cabroad\xe2\x80\x9d to more claims \xe2\x80\x9cthan we permit in this country .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid. It is consequently not surprising to find that the expropriation exception on its face emphasizes conformity with international law by requiring not only a commercial connection with the United States but also a taking of property \xe2\x80\x9cin violation of international law.\xe2\x80\x9d

\n

We emphasize this point, embedded in the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language, history, and structure, because doing so reveals a basic objective of our sovereign immunity doctrine, which a \xe2\x80\x9cnonfrivolous-argument\xe2\x80\x9d reading of the expropriation exception would undermine. A sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s taking or regulating of its own nationals\xe2\x80\x99 property within its own territory is often just the kind of foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s public act (a \xe2\x80\x9cjure imperii\xe2\x80\x9d) that the restrictive theory of sovereign immunity ordinarily leaves immune from suit. See Permanent Mission, 551 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 199 (describing the FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s distinction between public acts, or jure imperii, and purely commercial ones); Restatement (Third) of Foreign Relations Law of the United States \xc2\xa7712, at 196 (noting that, under international law, a state is responsible for a \xe2\x80\x9ctaking of the property of a national of another state\xe2\x80\x9d (emphasis added)). See also Restatement (Fourth) of Foreign Relations Law of the United States \xc2\xa7455, Reporter\xe2\x80\x99s Note 12, p. 9 (Tent. Draft No. 2, Mar. 22, 2016) (noting that \xe2\x80\x9c[n]o provision comparable\xe2\x80\x9d to the exception \xe2\x80\x9chas yet been adopted in the domestic immunity statutes of other countries\xe2\x80\x9d and that expropriations are considered acts jure imperii); United States v. Belmont,\n\n301 U.\xc2\xa0S. 324,\n332 (1937)\n; B. Cheng & G. Schwarzberger, General Principles of Law as Applied by International Courts and Tribunals 37\xe2\x80\x9338 (1953) (collecting cases describing \xe2\x80\x9cthe power of the sovereign State to expropriate\xe2\x80\x9d (internal quotation marks omitted)); Jurisdictional Immunities of the State (Germany v. Italy), 2012 I. C. J. 99, 123\xe2\x80\x93125, \xc2\xb6\xc2\xb656\xe2\x80\x9360 (Judgt. of Feb. 3) (noting consistent state practice in respect to the distinction between public and commercial acts and describing an international law of immunity recognizing such a difference); Altmann, supra, at 708 (Breyer, J., concurring) (describing the French Court of Appeals\xe2\x80\x99 decision about whether a King who has abdicated the throne is \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98entitled to claim .\xc2\xa0.\xc2\xa0. immunity\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d as \xe2\x80\x9c\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x98Hea[d] of State\xe2\x80\x99\xc2\xa0\xe2\x80\x9d when his sovereign status at the time of suit was in doubt (quoting Ex-King Farouk of Egypt v. Christian Dior, 84 Clunet 717, 24 I. L. R. 228, 229 (CA Paris 1957))).

\n

To be sure, there are fair arguments to be made that a sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s taking of its own nationals\xe2\x80\x99 property sometimes amounts to an expropriation that violates international law, and the expropriation exception provides that the general principle of immunity for these otherwise public acts should give way. But such arguments are about whether such an expropriation does violate international law. To find jurisdiction only where a taking does violate international law is thus consistent with basic international law and the related statutory objectives and principles that we have mentioned. But to find jurisdiction where a taking does not violate international law (e.g., where there is a nonfrivolous but ultimately incorrect argument that the taking violates international law) is inconsistent with those objectives. And it is difficult to understand why Congress would have wanted that result.

\n

Moreover, the \xe2\x80\x9cnonfrivolous-argument\xe2\x80\x9d interpretation would, in many cases, embroil the foreign sovereign in an American lawsuit for an increased period of time. It would substitute for a more workable standard (\xe2\x80\x9cviolation of international law\xe2\x80\x9d) a standard limited only by the bounds of a lawyer\xe2\x80\x99s (nonfrivolous) imagination. It would create increased complexity in respect to a jurisdictional matter where clarity is particularly important. Hertz Corp. v. Friend,\n\n559 U.\xc2\xa0S. 77\n\xe2\x80\x9395 (2010). And clarity is doubly important here where foreign nations and foreign lawyers must understand our law.

\n

Finally, the Solicitor General and the Department of State also warn us that the nonfrivolous-argument interpretation would \xe2\x80\x9caffron[t]\xe2\x80\x9d other nations, producing friction in our relations with those nations and leading some to reciprocate by granting their courts permission to embroil the United States in \xe2\x80\x9cexpensive and difficult litigation, based on legally insufficient assertions that sovereign immunity should be vitiated.\xe2\x80\x9d Brief for United States as Amicus Curiae 21\xe2\x80\x9322. (At any given time the Department of Justice\xe2\x80\x99s Office of Foreign Litigation represents the United States in about 1,000 cases in 100 courts around the world. Ibid.) See also National City Bank of N. Y. v. Republic of China,\n\n348 U.\xc2\xa0S. 356,\n362 (1955)\n (noting that our grant of immunity to foreign sovereigns dovetails with our own interest in receiving similar treatment).

\n

III

\n

The plaintiffs make two important arguments to the contrary. First, they point to the federal statute that gives federal courts jurisdiction over cases \xe2\x80\x9carising under the Constitution, laws, or treaties of the United States,\xe2\x80\x9d\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331. They note that in Bell v. Hood,\n\n327 U.\xc2\xa0S. 678 (1946)\n, this Court held that the \xe2\x80\x9carising under\xe2\x80\x9d statute confers jurisdiction if a plaintiff can make a nonfrivolous argument that a federal law provides the relief he seeks\xe2\x80\x94even if, in fact, it does not. See id., at 685 (jurisdiction exists where, if the \xe2\x80\x9cConstitution and laws of the United States are given one construction,\xe2\x80\x9d a claim will be \xe2\x80\x9csustained,\xe2\x80\x9d but if the laws are given a different construction, the claim \xe2\x80\x9cwill be defeated\xe2\x80\x9d). And the plaintiffs say we should treat the expropriation exception similarly.

\n

Section 1331, however, uses different language from the expropriation exception (\xe2\x80\x9carising under\xe2\x80\x9d) and focuses on different concerns. Section 1331 often simply determines which court\xe2\x80\x99s doors are open (federal or state). Cf. Mims v. Arrow Financial Services, LLC,\n\n565 U.\xc2\xa0S. 368\n\xe2\x80\x93379 (2012). Unlike the FSIA, neither that jurisdictional section nor related jurisdictional sections seeks to provide a sovereign foreign nation (or any party) with immunity\xe2\x80\x94the basic FSIA objective. See Dole Food Co. v. Patrickson,\n\n538 U.\xc2\xa0S. 468,\n479 (2003)\n (FSIA\xe2\x80\x99s objective is to give \xe2\x80\x9cprotection from the inconvenience of suit as a gesture of comity\xe2\x80\x9d); Republic of Philippines v. Pimentel,\n\n553 U.\xc2\xa0S. 851,\n866 (2008)\n. And unlike the expropriation exception, the \xe2\x80\x9carising under\xe2\x80\x9d statute\xe2\x80\x99s language does not suggest that consistency with international law is of particular importance.

\n

Moreover, this Court has interpreted other jurisdictional statutes differently. Where jurisdiction depends on diversity of citizenship, for example, courts will look to see whether the parties are in fact diverse, not simply whether they are arguably so. See Indianapolis v. Chase Nat. Bank,\n\n314 U.\xc2\xa0S. 63,\n69 (1941)\n; McNutt v. General Motors Acceptance Corp.,\n\n298 U.\xc2\xa0S. 178,\n189 (1936)\n; see also 13E C. Wright, A. Miller, & E. Cooper, Federal Practice and Procedure \xc2\xa73611 (2009). We do not believe either jurisdictional analogy (\n\n28 U.\xc2\xa0S.\xc2\xa0C. \xc2\xa71331 or \xc2\xa71332) is particularly helpful, but the expropriation exception\xe2\x80\x99s substantive goals suggest that the diversity jurisdiction example provides a marginally closer analogy.

\n

Second, the plaintiffs argue that the nonfrivolous-argument approach will work little harm. They say that a court faced with an arguable, but ultimately incorrect, claim of jurisdiction can simply decide the same question\xe2\x80\x94say, whether there was a \xe2\x80\x9cviolation of international law\xe2\x80\x9d\xe2\x80\x94as part of its decision on the merits. Thus a foreign sovereign defendant (in court because a plaintiff has made a nonfrivolous but incorrect argument that its property was taken in violation of international law) can simply move for judgment on the merits under Rule 12(b)(6), which provides for judgment where a plaintiff does not \xe2\x80\x9cstate a claim upon which relief can be granted.\xe2\x80\x9d Or the defendant could move for summary judgment under Rule 56. In a word, the defendant may not need to undergo a full trial and judgment, remaining in court until the bitter end.

\n

These alternatives, however, have their own problems. For one thing, they will sometimes mean increased delay, imposing increased burdens of time and expense upon the foreign nation. For another, where a district court decides that there is a \xe2\x80\x9cviolation of international law\xe2\x80\x9d as a matter of jurisdiction, then (according to the Courts of Appeals) the losing sovereign nation can immediately appeal the decision as a collateral order. But the same decision made to dispose of, say, a Rule 12(b)(6) motion or a Rule 56 motion would not be a \xe2\x80\x9ccollateral order.\xe2\x80\x9d It would be a decision on the \xe2\x80\x9cmerits.\xe2\x80\x9d And the foreign sovereign would not enjoy a right to take an immediate appeal. See Coopers & Lybrand v. Livesay,\n\n437 U.\xc2\xa0S. 463,\n468 (1978)\n (permitting interlocutory appeal of a collateral order that \xe2\x80\x9cresolve[s] an important issue completely separate from the merits of the action\xe2\x80\x9d); Will v. Hallock,\n\n546 U.\xc2\xa0S. 345,\n349 (2006)\n (same). See also Intel Corp. v. Commonwealth Scientific, 455 F.\xc2\xa03d 1364, 1366 (CA Fed. 2006) (permitting collateral appeal of an FSIA jurisdictional decision denying immunity); Rubin v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 637 F.\xc2\xa03d 783, 785 (CA7 2011) (same); Compania Mexicana de Aviacion v. Central Dist. of Cal., 859 F. 2d 1354, 1356 (CA9 1988) (per curiam) (same); Foremost-McKesson v. Islamic Republic of Iran, 905 F.\xc2\xa02d 438, 443 (CADC 1990) (same).

\n

Moreover, what is a court to do in a case where a \xe2\x80\x9cviolation of international law,\xe2\x80\x9d while a jurisdictional prerequisite, is not an element of the claim to be decided on the merits? The Circuit has suggested that they arise when the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 claim is not an \xe2\x80\x9cexpropriation claim,\xe2\x80\x9d but rather a simple common-law claim of conversion, restitution, or breach of contract, the merits of which do not involve the merits of international law. See Simon v. Republic of Hungary, 812 F. 3d 127, 141\xe2\x80\x93142 (2016). The Circuit has recognized that there are such cases, id., at 141, and a cursory survey of the principal district courts in which these cases are brought confirms the reality of the problem. See, e.g., Philipp v. Federal Republic of Ger-many, ___ F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d ___, 2017 WL 1207408 (DC, Mar. 31, 2017) (deciding whether the expropriation exception is satisfied where the complaint pleads only common-law or statutory claims for relief); De Csepel v. Hungary, 169 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d 143 (DC 2016) (similar); Pablo Star Ltd. v. Welsh Government, 170 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 3d 597 (SDNY 2016) (similar); Chettri v. Nepal, 2014 WL 4354668 (SDNY, Sept. 2, 2014) (similar); Order Granting Defendants\xe2\x80\x99 Motion To Dismiss in Lu v. Central Bank of Republic ofChina, No. 2:12\xe2\x80\x93cv\xe2\x80\x93317 (CD Cal., June 13, 2013) (similar); Orkin v. Swiss Confederation, 770 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 612 (SDNY 2011) (similar); Hammerstein v. Federal Republic of Germany, 2011 WL 9975796 (EDNY, Aug. 1, 2011) (similar); Cassirer v. Kingdom of Spain, 461 F.\xc2\xa0Supp. 2d 1157 (CD Cal. 2006) (similar). Indeed, cases in which the jurisdictional inquiry does not overlap with the elements of a plaintiff\xe2\x80\x99s claims have been the norm in cases arising under other exceptions to the FSIA. E.g., Republic of Argentina v. Weltover, Inc.,\n\n504 U.\xc2\xa0S. 607,\n610 (1992)\n (deciding whether a plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 breach-of-contractclaim satisfied the jurisdictional requirements of the commercial-activity exception, \xc2\xa71605(a)(2)).

\n

To address the problem raised by these cases in which the \xe2\x80\x9cjurisdictional and merits inquiries\xe2\x80\x9d are not fully \xe2\x80\x9coverlap[ping],\xe2\x80\x9d the Circuit has held that a district court is not to apply its nonfrivolous-argument standard in such cases. Simon, 812 F.\xc2\xa03d, at 141. Rather, a court is to ask \xe2\x80\x9cwhether the plaintiffs\xe2\x80\x99 allegations satisfy the jurisdictional standard.\xe2\x80\x9d Ibid.

\n

We can understand why the Circuit has departed from its nonfrivolous-argument standard in these latter cases. For, unless it did so, how could a foreign nation ever obtain a decision on the merits of the nonfrivolous argument that a plaintiff has advanced? But what in the statutory provision suggests that sometimes courts should, but sometimes they should not, simply look to the existence of a nonfrivolous argument when they decide whether the requirements of the expropriation exception are satisfied? It is difficult, if not impossible, to reconcile this bifurcated approach with the statute\xe2\x80\x99s language. It receives little, if any, support from the statute\xe2\x80\x99s history or purpose. And, it creates added complexity, making it more difficult for judges and lawyers, domestic and foreign, to understand the intricacies of the law.

\n

IV

\n

We conclude that the nonfrivolous-argument standard is not consistent with the statute. Where, as here, the facts are not in dispute, those facts bring the case within the scope of the expropriation exception only if they do show (and not just arguably show) a taking of property in violation of international law. Simply making a nonfrivolous argument to that effect is not sufficient. Moreover, as we have previously stated, a court should decide the foreign sovereign\xe2\x80\x99s immunity defense \xe2\x80\x9c[a]t the threshold\xe2\x80\x9d of the action. Verlinden, 461 U.\xc2\xa0S., at 493. As we have said, given the parties\xe2\x80\x99 stipulations as to all relevant facts, the question before us is purely a legal one and can be resolved at the outset of the case. If a decision about the matter requires resolution of factual disputes, the court will have to resolve those disputes, but it should do so as near to the outset of the case as is reasonably possible.
